Abstract
This project involves a detailed study of grid­forming converters and the modelling of a
grid­forming PV­BESS co­located hybrid converter system in DIgSILENT PowerFactory.
The individual components of the PV­BESS grid­forming system is studied in depth, es­
pecially the different control and modelling methods of Grid Forming Converters (GFMs).
Then the design and modelling of Hybrid Power Plants (HPPs) is reviewed in literature
focusing on the configuration, control methods and applications. Based on the review,
the control method of the hybrid power plant is designed. The performance of the HPP
towards various transient states is observed and the inferences are drawn accordingly.
The voltage, current and frequency characteristics of the HPP is in accordance to the
limits set in the Danish [1],[2] and European grid codes. The UK grid­code addendum
GC0137[3], that focuses on design and characteristics of grid­forming converters. In ad­
dition, the HPP model is tested in steady state by modelling and implementing a plant
controller, which is designed to ensure the power output at the PCC obeys a certain set
of rules, governed by the load demand and the SOC of the BESS.

Introduction
1.1
Global renewable energy scenario
In the last three decades, there has been a significant impetus towards increasing the
share of renewable energy sources in electricity generation. This paradigm shift is driven
by various factors, mainly climate change, dwindling reserves of fossil fuels (coal,oil and
natural gas) and the innovations in photovoltaic and wind turbine technologies. These
renewable energy sources are interfaced using power electronic inverter devices. The
emergence of Inverter Based Resources (IBRs) have led to a transition of the electrical
power system from being highly centralized with radial flow of electricity from the gen­
erating stations at the center to the loads located at the ends of the radial system, to a
more mesh­like system of distributed energy systems of lower generation capacity being
scattered in the network.
Figure 1.1: Renewable annual net capacity addition by Technology [4]
Among the renewable energy sources, solar PV and wind turbine generators have ob­
served the maximum growth in capacity. The rapid growth of PV generation can be at­
tributed to the steady decline in manufacturing costs of PV modules and the improvement
in efficiency of PV modules. Wind turbine generators have been steadily increasing in
size, and offshore wind turbine installations have enabled wind turbine rated power to
reach upto 15MVA. PV power plants seem to be the most cost conservative among the
renewable energy technologies [4]. Hence it is being adopted widely, especially in devel­
oping economies. Commercial and residential PV systems are projected to constitute a
major portion of the capacity expansion in the next 5 years.
1.2
Hybrid Power Plants­ Why are they trending?
The primary issue with renewable energy sources like wind and solar irradiation is the
variability and unpredictability. The time of the day of maximum generation may or may
not coincide with the period of maximum demand. The issue of reliability can be signifi­
cantly mitigated with the development of Hybrid energy systems. As defined in [5], hybrid
power system is one, which is composed of diverse energy sources, which could be a
combination of conventional and renewable energy sources, or could be entirely com­

posed of renewable energy sources. They are treated as a single source at the Point of
Connection (PoC) with the grid. Some of the advantages of hybrid energy systems are
as follows:
• The combination of multiple renewable energy sources improves the capacity factor
of the plant, defined as the ratio of the energy generated by the plant in an year to
the maximum possible energy that can be generated by the plant.
• The economic and logistical investment required to install and integrate a plant into
the grid can be offset by hybrid plants. For instance PV and storage plants can be

be reduced.
• Reduced CAPEX and OPEX of hybrid systems compared to individual energy sources.

• The integration of energy storage services in the hybrid power plants allows the
renewable energy plants to provide additional ancillary services like inertia support
and black start services,[6][7] that are especially necessary when the renewable
energy system is integrated into a weak grid.
• Hybrid systems also offer greater flexibility to the energy producers to strategically
participate in their energy markets, By being able to modify their offerings as per
their energy market, they can offer the energy produced either from a single source,
or a combination of different energy sources.
• The successful implementation of hybrid power plants have enabled the decentral­
ization of the electrical network from a radial flow of power from the generators to
the loads, to a meshed arrangement of generators and loads. Grid­forming hybrid
converters are an integral part of microgrids, defined as ”a group of interconnected
loads and distributed energy resources within clearly defined electrical boundaries
that acts as a single controllable entity with respect to the grid, and that can operate
in parallel with, or independently from, the grid” by the US Department of Energy.

The various development trends and market participation options that are enabled by
hybrid energy systems is discussed in [8]. Some of the disadvantages of hybrid energy

energy sources. For instance, the co­location of PV and wind generators may not provide
the same level of performance from the individual generation technologies.
1.3
Why is Grid­Forming control interesting?
In the initial period of integration of IBR based renewable energy sources in the power
system, when the electrical grid was dominated by conventional generators run by fossil­
fuel based energy sources, the IBRs were mainly of the grid­following type, which rely on
the synchronous generator to establish the voltage and frequency. The renewable gen­
erator injects active and reactive power into the network, while following the set voltage
and frequency. With the increase in penetration of IBRs and the consequent reduction of
synchronous generators in the grid, the stability of grid­following converters reduces. In
addition, most of the grid­following converter controllers are designed to cut­off from the
network in case of a voltage or frequency instability in the network. This jeopardizes the
network stability in high renewable energy networks and may lead to a blackout. Hence,
renewable energy generators of higher rated capacities are now expected to provide re­
active power support In order to provide voltage phase and frequency reference to the
network, a few alternate methods include the use of a synchronous condenser[9], in order
to provide Voltage phase, frequency and inertia support to the Inverter Based Resource
(IBR). This method has been successfully deployed in the UK under the Phoenix Project.
An alternative method that has been growing in popularity is the implementation of Grid
Forming Converters (GFMs).

Concepts of grid forming converters
The initial design of Inverter Based Resources (IBRs) were targeted to maximize the AC
power injection to the grid as per the availability of the renewable energy source (solar
irradiation,wind, etc) using Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT) techniques. The grid
consisted primarily of synchronous generators that maintain the voltage and frequency
of the system based on excitation control and governor control respectively. So, the ini­
tial IBRs are modelled as a current source that inject the active and reactive currents
based on the maximum power. They follow the voltage magnitude and phase set by the
synchronous generators or other voltage sources, hence they are called Grid Following
Converters (GFL). As seen in Figure 2.1a, the active and reactive power setpoints are
used to obtain the currents that are injected into the network.
(a) Grid following converter control in syn­
chronous reference frame[11]
(b) Grid following converter representation as a
current source[12]
Figure 2.1: Grid following converters
The reactive power supply requirements from GFLs have been increased in recent years,
owing to the increasing rating of IBRs causing stability issues during voltage drop and
fault situations. The dependence of GFLs on the network to provide frequency and volt­
age phase reference has also been highlighted with the increase in renewable energy
penetration. The voltage phase and frequency references for the GFLs can be provided
by various methods, but the most commonly used method is the phase locked­loop (PLL).
The use of a synchronizing device like PLL decreases the responsiveness of the converter
to the dynamic events in the network, making the system less stable. This also necessi­
tates the requirement of a stiff grid for the smooth and stable functioning of a GFL. A stiff
grid is characterized by a high short circuit ratio, which is measured as:

Hierarchical control of Hybrid Power Plants

Maximum Power Point Tracking (MPPT)
In the previous section, it was mentioned that the PV array maximizes the output of the
PV array using an technique called the maximum power point tracking (MPPT). MPPT
techniques are commonly used for renewable energy sources like solar PV and wind,
which are inherently intermittent. So, it is optimal to maximize the generation of power
for the generated output voltage and current. A comprehensive review of the different
categories of MPPT techniques is provided in [32]. The design and analysis of MPPT

reviewed in order to understand the modelling of the PV system. In the present model, it is
assumed that the PV array can deliver maximum power. The MPPT voltage and current is
corrected to the ambient temperature and irradiation data provided to the PV array block,
based on the PV panel characteristics.

An important transient scenario that needs to be considered is the synchronization of the
hybrid system with the grid before integration. [35] discusses a couple of methods, one of
which is to connected connect the 2 energy sources primarily when the frequency and the
phase difference between the grid and the hybrid system is minimal. In order to perform
this task, a new DSL model is prepared. Initially, the PV­BESS hybrid system (HPP) is
considered to be in islanded mode. In this project, it is considered that the voltage levels
of the HPP and the grid are identical. In practice, the voltage levels may slightly differ,
leading to circulating currents between the two energy systems. This can be mitigated by
comparing the voltage levels of the two systems before closing the breaker. In addition,
this project considers that the phase difference between the phases is equal, otherwise
this method is not capable of synchronizing the three phases individually.

The process of synchronization is initiated by setting the synchronization signal io as 1.
Once the synchronization process is on, the algorithm checks for the phase difference
between the HPP and the grid. The algorithm modelled for this study measures the dif­
ference between the cos(ϕt) of the voltage of the grid and the HPP. The reason behind
using the cosine factor is that the phase angle varies between 0 and 2π in one cycle,
which is difficult to track and act upon by the switch. It is much easier and convenient to
track cos(ϕt) which only oscillates between ­1 and 1. Typically, there will be significant
phase shift between the grid and the HPP. In order to change the phase difference be­
tween the 2 systems, the frequency of the 2 systems need to be significantly different.
Having different frequencies in the 2 systems enables the frequency phasors to rotate at
different speeds, thereby affecting a change in the phase difference between the 2 sys­
tems with respect to time. Realistically, the grid frequency cannot be controlled, hence
the frequency of the HPP is raised by raising the frequency setpoint(f_set) of the GFM.
Once the phase difference between the 2 systems is reduced below the threshold value,
the frequency setpoint of the HPP is controlled to be equal to the frequency of the grid.
This enables the voltage phasors of the 2 energy sources to rotate at the same speed,
thereby fixing the phase difference with respect to time. Once the frequency and phase
difference between the 2 sources is achieved within the desired limits, the breaker at the
point of contact is closed and synchronization is achieved.

5.7
Modelling the Plant controller for the PV­BESS hybrid
system
The transient scenarios are used to understand the stability of the PV­BESS hybrid sys­
tem under different disturbances. These disturbances have a very short duration, and
recovering from them takes only a few seconds. The utility of the hybrid power plant can
be further exhibited by demonstrating some of the other features can can be designed
in the system to be performed over a longer time duration. These applications are exe­
cuted by a plant controller. As discussed in chapter 3, the plant controller manages the
power output of the different energy sources. For instance, a plant controller design that
aims to maximize the power output by the PV­BESS hybrid system is discussed in [21],
considering the load demand, PV generation, SOC of the BESS in both grid­connected
and islanded modes. The function of the plant controller is to implement the setpoints
determined by the Energy Management System (EMS). It is responsible of aggregating
the energy supplied by the various energy sources and supplying energy within the ramp
rate limitations.
In small scaled hybrid systems, the plant controller is designed towards maximizing the
energy supply to the load using renewable energy sources. When the hybrid system only
forms a part of the total generation to supply the load, then the objective of the EMS
and the plant controller is to provide peak shaving services [36], where the BESS stores
energy during periods of low demand, which is released during periods of high demand.
This helps to minimize reliance on conventional energy sources for reserve power. HPP
applications also include energy arbitrage, which determine the power supplied by the
system based on the spot prices. The EMS charges the battery when the spot prices
are low, which are periods of surplus generation or low demand. When the spot prices

==PAGE:42==
increase, indicating a period of high demand. An example which combines the EMS
methods mentioned above is [37], where a multi­functional Energy Management System
(EMS) is proposed for a single phase hybrid energy system. Another application of hybrid
plant control and energy management is capacity firming proposed in [38], where the
utility of BESS to provide capacity firming service for a duration of at least 4 hours. This
application is quite useful in context of implementing the HPP as generator supplying
power to the base load in the load profile in a IBR dominated network.
In this study, the objective is to model a simple plant controller that can perform a combi­
nation of the following methods:
• Convert the varying PV power supply to a fixed value by providing fixed power output
at the PCC for a particular duration. The time duration of fixed power output depends
on the available stored energy represented by the SOC, and the load demand.
• Ensure that the renewable energy supply forms a constant fraction of the load de­
mand. For instance, if 50 percent of the network demand is expected to be supplied
by renewable energy at all times, then the power output of the HPP will be adjusted
to supply 50 percent of the expected demand at all times.
The input data obtained by the plant controller are:
• The network frequency, measured at the PCC.
• The voltage and active power measured at the PCC.
• Voltage and power measurements at the terminal of the BESS GFM terminal Terminal_sync.
• Voltage and power measurements at the terminal of the BESS GFM terminal LV.
• PV forecasted generation value.
• Expected load demand.
• SOC value of the BESS.

high value before being used for the other modes. The battery is also charged during low

coincides with low spot prices.

7.4
Minimum sizing requirement of grid­forming converter in
the hybrid system
In hybrid energy systems, the sizing of the individual systems is an important question
that determines the behaviour of the system in different scenarios. The sizing depends
on the configuration of the hybrid energy system. For instance, in many smaller scaled
systems (upto a few MW), the grid forming converter (GFM) is recommended to be the
highest rated converter. Typically, the GFM in hybrid systems is connected to a BESS
source, which needs to be capable of mitigating the voltage and frequency imbalances of
the network to which it is connected. It can be compared to the slack bus of the system. In
larger, utility­scale power systems, the cost difference between the grid­forming and grid­
following converters becomes more pronounced. In addition, the grid code requirements
for larger sized plants require them to perform functions that are only performed by GFMs
on a smaller scale. Hence, the size of grid­forming converters can be determined by
different objectives. For instance, [39] discusses the sizing of GFMs in an offshore wind
farm, with an objective to provide enough reactive power to energize the transmission
lines and transformers and establish a stable voltage phase and frequency reference for
the GFLs to integrate into the network.
In the presented study, the objective of the GFM connected to the BESS is to supplement
the operation of the PV system, and maximize the energy generated by the PV system
by minimizing the requirement of curtailment. In the studies and tests performed in the
previous sections, the size of the PV and BESS is equal, meaning that the GFM BESS
can be used to supply as much energy as the PV system. However, in order to determine
the minimize the size of the GFM required to ensure that the voltage and frequency levels
are maintained to retain the PV system into the network.
In order to find the minimum size of the grid forming converter required in the test system,
the approach that is followed is that the GFM should operate below its current limits, in grid
connected as well as standalone mode. Consequently, the voltage drop across the virtual
impedance of the GFM should be below it’s maximum limit. For this case, it is considered
that the GFM will only provide the voltage and frequency reference to the network, and
all the active power requirements can be inherently fulfilled by the GFL (PV system in this
study).
The voltage and frequency values in the default case can be seen in section 6.2. The
reactive power shared between the GFM and GFL and the voltage drop across the virtual
impedance of the GFM are shown in Figure 7.10.

Conclusions and future scope of work
The project focuses on the study of grid­forming converters and it’s implementation in a
AC coupled hybrid system, along with a grid­following PV inverter.
The concept of hybrid converters was studied with the different features and benefits
that make hybrid converters a lucrative prospect, attracting increasing investment and re­
search. The hierarchical structure of the Hybrid Power system is studied with the functions
of each level of controllers understood.
There are multiple methods of modelling the grid­forming converter. Each of the methods
is designed to implement certain characteristics of the synchronous generator. Based on
the objectives of the study, the modelling of the grid­forming converter can be selected.
The ultimate objective is to model the converter to behave as a voltage source with series
impedance, capable of generating its own voltage and frequency references. Although,
there are some characteristics that are hard to emulate by converters. One of them being
the ability to provide high inrush current (almost 6­7 times of rated current) required by
devices like transformers and induction motors.
The regulations for grid­forming converters is at a very nascent stage. Most of the regu­
lations for grid­forming converters are quite similar to that of synchronous machines. It is
expected that, with the increase in renewable energy penetration, there will be specialized
regulations for grid­forming converters, based on their flexibility in characteristics.
The system is tested for different transient scenarios in both grid­connected and islanded
modes, and its performance voltage and frequency deviations are found to be in accor­
dance with the limits prescribed by the Danish grid code regulations. The UK grid­code
for grid­forming converters is also studied and the model developed is compliant with
these grid codes as well. In order to maximize the utility of the PV energy generated,
grid­forming battery converter regulates its power injection or absorption. In addition, the
fluctuations in frequency and active power demand caused by the network transients are
handled by the grid­forming converter. In case of a standalone PV system, the surplus en­
ergy produced by the PV generator would be curtailed. In addition, the capabilities of the
2 converters to supply reactive power is utilized to increase the reactive power capacity
of the hybrid system.
Since the studied system is and AC coupled system, there is also a possibility of vary­
ing the sizes of the grid­following and grid­forming converters. This consideration is quite
pragmatic, since there is significant cost gap between grid­forming and grid­following con­
verters. In addition, once the voltage and frequency reference is established, the grid
following converter can be utilized for maximum power generation. In the study, it was
observed that one of the main factors for sizing the grid­forming converter is the reactive
power requirement to energize the network. Below a certain value of reactive power that
can ensure an acceptable range of network voltage, the grid­following converters can­
not operate. Hence, during processes like black start, the grid forming converter is used
only to partially energize the network, in order to sequentially raise the reactive power
generation capacity of the network with the available generators.
Owing to their multiple capabilities, hybrid converters can be utilized to perform roles that
were not possible by individual energy source. In this study, the variability of renewable
energy sources was reduced by developing a plant controller mechanism that can pro­

vide a fixed power value at the PCC, functioning as a base generator. They can also be
used to provide ancillary services like frequency restoration reserves and replacement
reserves. However, in the simulation tests, it was observed that having a singular renew­
able energy source would lead to oversizing of the energy storage systems to account for
sustained days of sub­optimal renewable energy generation. This issue is aggravated in
the absence of a stable grid connection. The most popular solution is to have different
energy sources like wind,solar biomass, etc that can complement one another.
The future works on this project should include the following studies:
• Explore the functionalities of the HPP. The hybrid power plant control can be ex­
panded to provide more features including active and reactive power injection sup­
port in different network scenarios. In the thesis, the load demand and the SOC
was the driving force determining the steady state setpoint of the power output at
the PCC. The energy dispatch can be further optimized by considering parameters
like energy market prices. The energy storage allows the hybrid system to be par­
ticipant in different energy markets.
• Alternate topologies: In this project, the AC­coupled hybrid converter is implemented.
It will be interesting to model other topologies of Hybrid Systems like DC­coupled
systems. DC­coupled systems are commercially favoured due to the less number of
inverter stages required in the system, reducing the system complexity and cost. In
addition, the battery can be charged even when the PV panels are operating below
the maximum power point voltage range of the inverters. The challenges would be
to implement the DC voltage and current control to maintain the DC­link voltage, in
addition to the inverter control.
• Expanded network and interaction with other devices: The HPP model and the plant
controller is modelled in a simplified network. It would be interesting to observe the
behaviour of the HPP in an expanded network with multiple HPPs. An aspect of this
study could be the voltage and frequency interactions between multiple grid­forming
HPPs in a network. In theory, if the grid­forming converters are based on droop or
a similar power sharing methodology, it is possible to have multiple grid­forming
converters in a network. In addition, the effects of reduced system inertia can be
better observed in a larger network.
