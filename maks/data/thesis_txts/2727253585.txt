Summary 
 
Economic trades at a global scale have increased the complexity of environmental impact 
attribution between emitters and consumers. Marine eutrophication, quantified by a decrease in 
oxygen levels in coastal waters, is a localized impact caused by the overabundance of reactive nitrogen 
in a marine environment. The biogeochemical cycle of nitrogen is among the Planetary Boundaries 
that are exceeded and could endanger the balance of the Earth system. Agriculture, fossil fuel burning, 
and sewage discharges emit nitrogen to the soil, to the atmosphere, and to freshwater, all contributing 
to marine eutrophication and the risk of hypoxia, a mounting threat to marine ecosystems amplified 
by global warming and ocean acidification. In order to tackle this issue that puts entire ecosystems 
and, ultimately, human lives in danger, it is crucial to be able to identify what patterns of production 
and consumption are responsible for the eutrophication observed in a specific marine area. Multi-
Regional Input-Output (MRIO) analysis, used in economics to map trade patterns between different 
regions of the world, is a framework that can be used along with environmental extensions to identify 
externalities generated along the supply chain of a commodity from production to the final consumer. 
While some MRIO databases include environmental satellites, used notably for climate change 
modeling, none of them provide a suitable framework for analyzing the spatially differentiated impacts 
of eutrophication in coastal waters. 
 
The overall goal of this study is to build a combined marine eutrophication MRIO model 
retaining the localized aspect of eutrophication, by coupling a spatially explicit impact model with an 
MRIO database. This global model would allow us to associate local eutrophication impacts with 
country- and sector-specific emissions, combining the strengths of both the input-output framework 
and the spatially explicit data available for nitrogen emissions and eutrophication. Firstly, a spatially 
explicit nitrogen emissions inventory was created for atmospheric emissions of NH3 and NOx, and soil 
emissions of NO3-, for all sectors. Then, this inventory was inputted into an impact model to compute 
spatially explicit eutrophication impacts attributable to a country and an industry. Finally, this 
environmental satellite was integrated into an MRIO model in order to trace back the calculated 
impacts to the final consumption they are associated with. As a result, it is possible to identify which 
country’s consumption drives eutrophication in any marine area of interest. To illustrate a possible use 
of the model, a case study was conducted focusing on a selection of coral reef areas that were 
investigated for hypoxic conditions. For this case, the EXIOBASE MRIO database was selected for its 
large variety in economic sectors, especially in agriculture. 
 
With this model, it is possible to compute the total impacts caused by nitrogen emissions in 
each coastal water region. The results show the highest eutrophication intensity in the Black Sea, the 
Yellow Sea, the Baltic and North Sea, and the Patagonian and South Brazil shelves. Other regions with 
higher-than-average eutrophication impacts are the Mediterranean Sea, the Chinese Sea, the east 
coast of the United States and the Gulf of Mexico. These results are mostly consistent with the 
observed eutrophication and hypoxia in the last decades, even though some hypoxic areas are missed 
by this model. Additionally, the difference between exported and imported impacts of each region can 
be computed. The results show a positive eutrophication balance for some European countries 
including the United Kingdom, Italy, and Austria, as well as the United States, Japan, and China. These 
countries are responsible for eutrophication in coastal waters of other regions due to their imports. 
On the other hand, Latin America and Germany are countries with a large negative balance, which 
reveals a large part of impacts due to production for exports: their coastal waters suffer eutrophication 
caused by consumption in other countries. 
 
For the selected coral reef areas investigated in the case study, the most impactful industries 
and countries were identified. In all cases, the impacts from soil emissions are due exclusively to the 
==PAGE:4==
4 
 
countries neighboring the marine areas, and the sectors responsible for most of the eutrophication 
are the cultivation of cereal grains, oil seeds, paddy rice, and vegetables, fruits, and nuts. The analysis 
of the final demand driving this production shows different patterns concerning the displacement of 
impacts. On the one hand, in the investigated areas of South-East Asia and Latin America as well as 
Australia, a large part of the eutrophication is caused by production destined for consumption in other 
parts of the world. The regions most responsible for these displaced impacts include richer countries 
such as Europe and the United States. On the other hand, the impacts at the site of Okinawa, near 
Japan, are caused almost exclusively by local consumption of goods produced in Japan, and not 
destined for exportation. 
 
Some limitations to this study were identified, regarding both the emissions inventory and the 
consistency of the results. Firstly, the inventory could be expanded to include a larger variety of crops 
for soil emissions, more nitrogen compounds in the atmosphere, and point source emissions to 
freshwater, to achieve better completeness. When comparing the study’s results with other studies, 
some inconsistencies were spotted, which could be due to a variety of factors such as the choice of 
eutrophication and MRIO model or the source and the year of the data used. Further investigation is 
necessary at this point to determine the cause of these differences.  
 
To conclude, the model built in this study is aimed at proposing a new framework for 
identifying the consumption causing eutrophication impacts in specific areas, as a way to attribute the 
responsibility of the displaced ecological damage in a fairer way taking into account international trade 
patterns. Backtracking from the observed eutrophication to the final demand associated with them 
opens up new policy-making regarding international cooperation to fight ecosystem destruction and 
mitigate the impacts of human activity on the environment.

1. Introduction 
The Planetary Boundaries (PB) framework provides a science-based analysis of the stress that 
human activities apply on the Earth system and the possibility that these perturbations can destabilize 
it (Rockström et al., 2009). There are nine planetary boundaries describing processes that regulate the 
stability of the Earth system: Climate Change, Novel Entities, Stratospheric Ozone Depletion, 
Atmospheric Aerosol Loading, Ocean Acidification, Biogeochemical Flows of N and P, Freshwater Use, 
Land-System Change, and Biosphere Integrity. Crossing these boundaries means reaching a state 
where the Earth system might be unable to self-regulate, causing threats to biodiversity and, 
ultimately, to the resilience of human societies (Rockström et al., 2009; Steffen et al., 2015). Therefore, 
understanding the PB framework is necessary in the context of our current societies being subject to 
existential threats such as global warming and biodiversity collapse. 
 
Figure 1. The Nine Planetary Boundaries and their Safe Operating Space, April 2022.  
(Image credit: Azote for Stockholm Resilience Centre, based on analysis in Wang-Erlandsson et al., 2022) 
At least six of these nine processes are in overshoot compared to the safe operating space 
within which human societies can thrive without compromising the stability of the Earth system, as 
shown in Figure 1 (Persson et al., 2022; Steffen et al., 2015; Wang-Erlandsson et al., 2022). The 
==PAGE:7==
7 
 
biogeochemical flow of Nitrogen is one of the most critical ones, with total emissions reaching over 
three times the estimated safe operating space (Bjørn et al., 2020; Galloway et al., 2013; Steffen et al., 
2015). Nitrogen, either emitted to the air in the form of nitrogen oxides (NOx) or ammonia (NH3) or to 
water, causes eutrophication in coastal waters, leading to oxygen depletion (Breitburg et al., 2018). 
Oxygen, a fundamental requirement for crucial processes in marine ecosystems — including both 
biological and biogeochemical cycles — when reduced, can trigger significant alterations in ocean 
biodiversity. Therefore, oxygen decline in ocean waters is likely to cause major changes in ocean 
biodiversity, causing grave disturbances in marine ecosystems. 
Hypoxia, a condition triggered by a lack of oxygen in ocean waters, is a major and escalating 
threat to marine ecosystems (Pezner et al., 2023). An important element is that the impacts of hypoxia 
are localized and thus require spatially explicit approaches using comprehensive models to assess the 
impacts of nitrogen emissions. Acknowledging this specificity and the complexity of this phenomenon 
helps in understanding its impacts and consequences on marine ecosystems (Bidoglio et al., 2023; 
Pezner et al., 2023). This is why performing a spatially informed study on hypoxia is a prerequisite for 
the development of strategic transformations to mitigate these impacts.  
Various economic sectors within human activity contribute to the overabundance of Nitrogen 
to various degrees. The largest contributing sector is agriculture, especially the meat and dairy 
industries which account for a significant portion of nitrogen emissions to water and air in the form of 
NH3. In addition, fossil fuel combustion is another large contributor through its NOx emissions to the 
atmosphere (Beylot et al., 2019). In order to contain the Nitrogen flows within the planetary 
boundaries requires allocating the responsibility for these emissions (Kander et al., 2015; Peters, 
2008). However, this is an increasingly challenging endeavor, owing largely to the intricate 
interconnections among industries and nations and the ever-expanding complexity of global supply 
chains (Beaufils et al., 2023; Peters et al., 2011). 
Input-Output analysis is an economic framework allowing to model the relationships between 
different sectors or industries in an interlinked economy (Miller & Blair, 2009). Multi-Regional Input-
Output (MRIO) models, specifically, provide an overview of economic networks at a global scale. MRIO 
tables are routinely used to identify externalities generated both directly and indirectly for supplying 
commodities to final consumers (Miller & Blair, 2009; Wiedmann & Lenzen, 2018; Wiedmann et al., 
2015); environmentally extended MRIO models (EE-MRIO) have been applied to greenhouse gas 
emissions to calculate carbon footprints (Kander et al., 2015; Peters et al., 2011).  
These models are only beginning to be used in concordance with marine eutrophication 
models, which would provide valuable information about the industries responsible for the observed 
impacts. While some MRIO databases do provide environmental extensions including nitrogen 
emissions and/or marine eutrophication (Lenzen et al., 2013; Stadler et al., 2021), the spatial aspect 
of eutrophication is left out, whereas it is crucial in order to understand the impacts, and the 
documentation is too scarce to disaggregate them. One study has recently explored this issue for 
agricultural emissions to the soil (Bidoglio et al., 2023), and the results show how trade patterns 
between countries are translated into displaced impacts. However, it only includes impacts from soil 
emissions, and is based on emission data from year 2000 and trade data from year 2010. This study 
will aim at expanding this by including emissions to the atmosphere, including all industries, and with 
more up-to-date emission and trade data. 
The overall aim of this study is to build a marine eutrophication MRIO model by coupling a 
spatially explicit impact model with an MRIO database. This would allow us to associate local 
eutrophication impacts with country and sector-specific emissions, combining the strengths of both 
==PAGE:8==
8 
 
the input-output framework and the spatially explicit data on nitrogen emissions and eutrophication. 
To achieve this goal, the following steps will be followed: 
 
Assemble a nitrogen emissions inventory, including spatially explicit emissions to the 
atmosphere and the soil, 
 
Set up an MRIO analysis model using a database fit for exploring the eutrophication 
impacts of nitrogen emissions, 
 
Link the nitrogen inventory, the eutrophication model, and the MRIO model in a way 
that ensures i) flexibility and transparency so that the model can calculate impacts at 
any level of spatial disaggregation, provided that the data is precise enough, ii) the 
ability to back-calculate and link the observed impacts to their source instead of only 
providing a country- or industry-wide footprint. 
This way, it will be possible for this model to be adapted to different data sources and MRIO models at 
any level of disaggregation, even lower than the country level, providing a basis for future studies. 
 
The following chapters will present how a spatially explicit marine eutrophication model, using 
emission data for both nitrogen emissions to air and soil, was combined with an MRIO model. Finally, 
the results of this model will be presented through the lens of a specific study case.

2. Methods 
2.1. MRIO models 
2.1.1. Background 
Input-Output Analysis was developed by the Nobel prize laureate Wassily Leontief in the 1930s 
as a framework allowing to study the interconnections between different interconnected sectors of 
the economy (Leontief, 1936). An input-output table is a matrix in which each row and each column 
represents a single industrial sector. One can read the outputs of a sector in its corresponding row, 
with all its different destinations including other sectors of the economy or as final value to consumers. 
Similarly, all the inputs into a sector are to be read in the associated column. These include 
intermediate inputs from other sectors and “value added”, or primary inputs. Most commonly these 
values are in monetary terms, and there is a balance between the sum of the total inputs and the total 
outputs of any given sector. This property allows normalizing each column by the total output of the 
respective sector, giving what is called the technical matrix, or direct requirements matrix. Each 
coefficient in this matrix represents the relative importance (in monetary terms) of the associated 
input in the production of the corresponding output. All these coefficients have a value between 0 and 
1 and the sum of each column adds up to 1.  
In the past few decades, methods using the input-output framework were developed in 
various fields including economics and industrial ecology, such as value-added analysis or carbon 
footprint calculations. These methods use Multi-Regional Input-Output tables, which include more 
than one region and are comprised of regional input-output tables as well as international trade tables, 
thus providing an overview of economic networks at a larger scale. Each regional table can have the 
same or a different sectoral decomposition, depending on the model. The international or 
interregional trade tables effectively represent the imports and exports of one country to another. 
Expanding this method to all the countries or regions allows a complete mapping of the world’s 
economy (Miller & Blair, 2009). 
In addition to the technical matrix, MRIO models can be extended via satellite matrixes to 
become an Environmentally Extended MRIO (EE-MRIO). A satellite is a vector with each value 
representing the influence of a sector on a specific process. This is especially useful for measuring the 
environmental impacts of the economy. 
One of the strengths of MRIO models is that they allow us to calculate the impacts of a given 
country on a global scale, and to allocate them in various ways. Two different accounting paradigms 
have arisen for calculating a country’s impact: Production-Based Accounting (PBA) and Consumption-
Based Accounting (CBA). In production-based accounting, all the goods that are produced within a 
country’s borders contribute to this country’s footprint, regardless of their end destination. On the 
other hand, consumption-based accounting considers all the goods that will be consumed in said 
country at the end of their life cycle, independently of their origin. The two different allocation 
frameworks will provide different results when computing the impacts of a country or sector, 
depending on the trade relationships between it and the rest of the world’s economy.  
Let’s take an example: soybeans are grown in France to feed cattle in Denmark, destined to 
produce meat to be sold in Germany. In a PBA framework, soybean production is allocated to France, 
and meat production to Denmark. Nothing is allocated to Germany as no good is produced within the 
country’s borders. In the case of a CBA, the production of soybeans is allocated to Denmark and the 
production of meat is allocated to Germany, as this is where the product is consumed. In other words, 
PBA includes local consumption as well as exports; CBA includes local consumption as well as imports.

Both these approaches have their respective strengths, and both are used for carbon 
accounting. In the case of marine eutrophication, large differences are to be expected as it is a localized 
impact, unlike climate change which is global: the place where greenhouse gases are emitted has little 
to no influence on the impacts. For this reason, a single satellite vector comprising marine 
eutrophication all over the planet is insufficient to precisely grasp the impacts of human activity in 
widely different marine environments and ecosystems: there is a need for more localized impact 
satellites reflecting the spatial variations in nitrogen flows. This would allow us to determine which 
countries or regions are responsible for the marine eutrophication impacts observed in other parts of 
the world.

2.1.3. EXIOBASE 
The model developed in this thesis is based on the input-output data from EXIOBASE. 
EXIOBASE 3 is a database providing MRIO tables for 44 countries (28 European Union member 
countries plus 16 of the largest economies) and 5 rest of the world regions, and 163 industries (Stadler 
et al., 2021). This level of sector disaggregation, especially for the agricultural processes, makes 
EXIOBASE a suitable option for studying Nitrogen flows. However, this is at the expense of a higher 
regional disaggregation that can be found in other MRIO databases such as EORA 26 (Lenzen et al., 
2012, 2013). The balance between sectoral detail and regional disaggregation is a trade-off that must 
always be made in input-output modeling due to the limitations of computing power and data 
availability when building the databases. Moreover, the recent years of EXIOBASE data are 
extrapolated from older data, most of it dating back to between 2011 and 2015 (Stadler et al., 2021). 
EXIOBASE provides an industry transactions matrix as well as the direct requirements matrix, 
the final demand matrix, and the gross output vector. In addition, it includes an environmental 
extension satellite containing, among others, nitrogen emissions (Oita et al., 2016). However, this 
extension will not be used here as it is not spatially explicit, and the documentation about it is rather 
==PAGE:12==
12 
 
scarce. This makes the data not accurate enough and then unsuitable for the analysis of marine 
eutrophication impacts in a localized approach.  
Instead, a new satellite matrix will be created, based on spatially explicit impacts derived from 
the marine eutrophication model presented in the next chapter. This will answer the main limits of the 
EXIOBASE satellite data and enable us to build a more reliable model. 
 
2.2. Marine eutrophication and nitrogen emissions inventory 
2.2.1. Marine eutrophication model 
Eutrophication in coastal areas causes a decrease in the ocean’s oxygen level, leading to 
hypoxia and the death of marine species, greatly disturbing ecosystems (Pezner et al., 2023). The main 
cause for eutrophication is an increased nitrogen loading in coastal waters, due to nitrogen carried in 
rivers and the atmosphere (Breitburg et al., 2018). The increased availability of nitrogen causes a surge 
in productivity from algae and phytoplankton, which decreases the oxygen level. The lack of oxygen in 
the ocean causes hypoxia and death of various marine species. It has been determined that 
eutrophication is a problem for the environment primarily in coastal areas, and hypoxia has been 
observed in an increasing number of places around the globe in the last century. It is expected that 
eutrophication and global warming will lead hypoxia to continue increasing exponentially (Beusen et 
al., 2022). 
A method for measuring eutrophication impacts was developed based on an LCIA model to 
translate nitrogen emissions into impacts (Cosme et al., 2018; Cosme & Hauschild, 2017). It was 
expanded to provide characterization factors with an increased spatial resolution in Vea et al. (2022, 
2023), and the impact areas were divided into Large Marine Ecosystems subsegments (LME-ss). A 
different set of characterization factors is defined for each compartment 𝑚 (atmosphere, freshwater, 
or soil).

2.2.2. Nitrogen emissions inventory 
The emissions to the atmosphere include NHx and NOx emissions from industries and NO3- 
emissions from agriculture which are then deposited on soil, freshwater, and coastal waters. The 
emissions to freshwater are due to sewage systems, and the emissions to the soil are caused by 
agriculture, mainly nitrogen fertilizers and manure application. Figure 3 presents the model’s basic 
structure. Nitrogen in the atmosphere is deposited on soil, freshwater, and coastal water. The excess 
nitrogen in the soil is into freshwater, and all the nitrogen present in freshwater is carried to a river 
mouth where it is discharged into coastal waters.

substance.

due to data availability and time limitations, it was decided not to include them in the model for the

van Puijenbroek et al., 2019).

2.3.3. MRIO coupling 
Once the satellite matrix is built, it can directly be used within the MRIO model as described 
in section 2.1. The MRIO model is coded in Python and connected directly with the outputs from the 
soil and atmospheric eutrophication models. It is then possible to determine the impact of any industry 
or country in a chosen geographic area, for this example an LME subsegment: the impact vector 𝒒 
corresponding to the area of interest is isolated and integrated into the model. This can then be 
repeated with each impact area and for each substance (NHx and NOx in the atmosphere, and N in the 
soil). 
The study also encompasses the calculation of consumption-based accounting (CBA) and 
production-based accounting (PBA) impacts attributable to a specific country or region. As outlined in 
the methodology section of this thesis, these two distinct accounting frameworks provide the means 
to assign environmental impacts to countries based on their respective economic activities and trade 
relationships. Each accounting approach yields a singular impact value, reflecting the total annual 
eutrophication instigated by the selected country 𝑐 within the designated impact area. 
In order to derive these accounting impacts, an initial decomposition of the impacts 
concerning the country of interest is required. This step is a crucial component in the process, enabling 
the accurate calculation of both CBA and PBA impacts, thereby providing a comprehensive 
understanding of a country or region's environmental footprint within the context of eutrophication.

Consumption-Based Accounting (CBA) can be interpreted, from an economic perspective, as 
the total impacts resulting from a country's consumption. This includes impacts derived from local 
consumption, referring to goods both produced and consumed within the country's borders, as well 
as impacts associated with imported goods, which are produced abroad and consumed domestically. 
In terms of greenhouse gas emissions, this is also recognized as the carbon footprint (Peters, 2008). 
In contrast, Production-Based Accounting (PBA) represents the total impacts of the country's 
production activities. The PBA encompasses both local consumption and exports, the latter referring 
to goods produced domestically but consumed outside of the country's borders (Miller & Blair, 2009). 
Additionally, this research provides the capability to examine a particular impact area and 
identify the industries contributing significantly to eutrophication. This method of analysis will be 
applied in the upcoming case study (Section 2.4), wherein the focus will be directed toward a select 
few coral reef locations that have been documented to experience hypoxia (Pezner et al., 2023). This 
aspect of the research aims to further our understanding of the major industrial contributors to the 
observed environmental phenomena. 
 
2.4. Case study on coral reefs 
2.4.1. Presentation 
Marine eutrophication, characterized by deoxygenation in the ocean’s coastal waters, has 
been inflicting widespread damage on marine ecosystems (Breitburg et al., 2018). Coral reefs are 
among the most vulnerable ecosystems in the world’s oceans, while being essential to marine 
biodiversity and ecological resilience (Knowlton et al., 2010; Moberg & Folke, 1999). These habitats, 
that house a myriad of species in diverse ecosystems, were historically viewed as possessing high 
oxygen levels. However, recent evidence contradicts this statement, showing that they are susceptible 
to hypoxia in the context of ocean warming (Pezner et al., 2023). This state, characterized by low 
oxygen levels, carries dangerous consequences for the coral reefs and the ecosystems they support 
(Perry & Alvarez-Filip, 2019). In this context, this case study will aim at identifying the main drivers of 
hypoxia in a handful of coral reef areas, tracing back the supply chains to find the countries responsible 
for the consumption that caused eutrophication in these regions. 
 
2.4.2. Methods 
The locations selected for this case study were picked from a previous study investigating 
oxygen levels and the presence of hypoxia on coral reefs (Pezner et al., 2023). Seven of them were 
retained here, as the other ones were located far away from the coasts and could not be matched with 
the Large Marine Ecosystems of the model. The list of locations and their geographic coordinates, along

Multiple reasons could explain these inconsistencies between the model’s results and the 
observations. Firstly, the model only takes emissions to the soil into account, while eutrophication can 
also be due to atmospheric and freshwater emissions. Including these would likely provide a more 
accurate overview of eutrophication in marine areas. In addition, the maps referred to in Figure 8 
include seasonal hypoxic events, while this model only covers yearly averages both in emissions and 
impacts. Therefore, some areas could be subject to momentaneous peaks of high oxygen depletion 
without being flagged as high eutrophication regions on a yearly average. Finally, the oxygen level in 
marine environments is determined by a multitude of factors not limited to human activity. Therefore, 
some areas of low oxygen concentrations could be subject to hypoxia under lower anthropogenic 
pressure than other regions. A way to investigate this would be to compare the calculated oxygen 
depletion with the safe operating space of each subsegment, as defined in Vea et al. (2022). 
 
3.1.2. Trade-embedded impacts 
The MRIO model also provides information about consumption-based impacts in the form of 
CBA. Comparing CBA and PBA can reveal trade patterns and exported impacts in certain areas of the 
world.  
In the context of greenhouse gas emissions, it has been observed that high-income countries 
often exhibit a Consumption-Based Accounting (CBA) impact that exceeds their Production-Based 
Accounting (PBA) impact, primarily attributable to their import activities. Notable examples of such 
countries include Norway, France, and the United States. Conversely, developing industrial countries 
with lower labor costs frequently display a PBA impact that surpasses their CBA impact, due to their 
substantial export of goods. This trend is evident in countries such as China, Turkey, and Brazil (Peters 
et al., 2011). 
However, the situation becomes more complex when considering eutrophication, which is 
largely induced by agricultural production. The distribution of activities rich in nitrogen, a key 
contributor to eutrophication, can significantly differ from those that are carbon intensive. Research 
indicates that countries with export-oriented agricultural production, like Canada, Australia, and 
Argentina, experience eutrophication impacts on their coastal waters as a consequence of 
==PAGE:25==
25 
 
consumption in other nations (Bidoglio et al., 2023). These repercussions, induced by trade activities, 
can be perceived as "imported impacts" from the perspective of the exporting country.  
Conversely, countries that import substantial quantities of their agricultural goods are 
accountable for "exported impacts" in other regions globally. This situation pertains to countries like 
Belgium and the Netherlands, as well as arid countries where agricultural yield is constrained, such as 
those in North Africa and the Middle East. These countries import a considerable proportion of their 
eutrophication impacts (Bidoglio et al., 2023). Consequently, it is anticipated that these countries will 
present a CBA impact that greatly surpasses their PBA impact. 
In the case of eutrophication, which is a localized impact, PBA will highlight the impacts of the 
countries neighboring the marine area of interest. However, CBA can be very interesting to study since 
it will allow us to witness the impacts of importing countries on the coastal waters of their trade 
partners. When looking at the coastal area of a country that exports a lot of nitrogen-intensive 
products, the share between this country’s local impact and other countries’ CBA will be low; in other 
words, this country will have a large share of imported impacts in its coastal waters.  
Figure 9 presents the eutrophication balance for each region defined within the EXIOBASE 
framework, as calculated by the MRIO model. The balance is calculated by subtraction between the 
impacts caused by imported and exported goods for each region. In other words, the value displayed 
is Balance = CBA −PBA. 
A positive balance indicates that a region primarily imports nitrogen-intensive goods, 
suggesting that the eutrophication impacts tied to the region's consumption largely arise from 
production activities in other areas. Essentially, a large positive balance represents a situation where 
the environmental impacts of the region's consumption are experienced as spillovers in other regions, 
illustrating an environmental burden predominantly borne by external production activities. 
On the other hand, a negative balance suggests that a region mainly exports nitrogen-intensive 
goods. This implies that the eutrophication impacts associated with the region's production activities 
are largely a consequence of consumption occurring outside its borders. In this case, a large negative 
balance means that the region's eutrophication impact is largely a spillover from the consumption 
activities in other regions, demonstrating an environmental burden transferred from the regions 
consuming its exports. 
These insights underscore the intricate interplay between trade and environmental impacts, 
emphasizing the necessity of adopting a holistic perspective when assessing environmental 
responsibility. The transference of environmental impacts across borders illuminates the shared, global 
nature of environmental challenges, and the importance of international cooperation in mitigating 
these issues.

The aforementioned example, while illustrative, is considerably simplified, operating under 
assumptions such as uniform eutrophication impacts across different nitrogen-intensive goods and 
geographically proximate eutrophication relative to the place of production. However, actual scenarios 
may present more complexity where the environmental impact manifests in a third country that is 
neither the producer nor the consumer of the nitrogen-intensive goods. 
Nevertheless, this example has been intentionally kept straightforward to clearly elucidate the 
process and principles. The application of the model provides a robust framework to navigate and 
illustrate the myriad of more complex situations, as we will observe in the results of the case study 
(Section 3.2). In a nuanced real-world context, the model serves as a potent tool for capturing the 
subtleties of transboundary environmental impacts, offering deeper insights into the global 
interconnectedness of production, consumption, and environmental consequences. 
 
Some of the results are consistent with the other study investigating trade-displacement of 
eutrophication impacts from crop production (Figure 10, Bidoglio et al., 2023). For example, Japan, 
China, and the United States are all computed to produce large eutrophication impacts linked with 
their imports, while the neighboring LMEs suffer a limited amount of eutrophication due to exports. 
This difference is what is reflected in Figure 9 as the eutrophication balance. In Europe, the United 
Kingdom, Italy, and Austria also fall under this category, as mentioned in Bidoglio et al. (2023).  
Similarly, the eutrophication associated with exports in coastal waters in South America, 
especially off the coast of Argentina, is measured to be high, while Latin American countries have low 
oxygen depletion due to their imports (excluding Brazil and Mexico, according to the EXIOBASE region). 
This is translated into a large negative balance in this study. 
However, some discrepancies are observed when comparing this model’s results with the 
aforementioned study. Germany is mentioned as a country with large impacts linked with their imports 
compared to their production, which is contrary to the negative balance calculated in this study. 
Conversely, Russia displays the same characteristics as the US in Figure 10, while the model also 
ascribes it to a negative eutrophication balance. 
These disparities in results could originate from a variety of factors including the choice of 
MRIO model, the marine eutrophication model, or the sources of data. Bidoglio et al. (2023) use a 
different dataset for soil emissions, that encompass a larger number of crops (West et al., 2014). In 
addition, they used a different equation for computing the fraction of leached nitrogen, taking into 
account climatic conditions and irrigation. This could be cause for significant differences between the 
models, especially in arid zones such as Australia. They also use a different set of characterization 
factors, based on the model by Cosme et al., 2015, 2018; Cosme & Hauschild, 2017. The difference in 
parameters, especially regarding the residence time of nitrogen in coastal waters, could be another 
source of discrepancies. Finally, their trade model is based on the study from Pendrill et al. (2019), that 
used both EXIOBASE 3 (Stadler et al., 2021) and a physical trade analysis using data from FAOSTAT (FAO, 
2023), built in Kastner et al. (2014). Finally, the data used in Bidoglio et al. (2023) is from the year 2000 
for the crop emissions, and from the year 2010 for the trade model. While the patterns are most likely 
similar to more recent data, these temporal disparities may cause slight differences in the final results.

It would be worth investigating this further to provide a better understanding of the trade patterns 
and their consequences in terms of displaced impacts.

Overall, it seems that some regions suffer from eutrophication caused by production for 
exports at the destination of other countries. This is the case in Latin America (LME-ss 12.1, 12.2) and 
in South-East Asia (LME-ss 36.13) as well as Australia (LME-ss 40.4). The consumption linked with these 
impacts is due to exports both to the neighboring countries and to other regions of the world, often 
to richer countries like the United States and Europe.  
In light of these results, the issue of displaced eutrophication impacts can also be viewed in 
part as a matter of international solidarity and environmental justice. This concept of environmental 
justice has been explored in the case of greenhouse gas emissions, regarding inequalities between rich 
and poor countries in both the vulnerability and the ability to adapt to climate change (Okereke, 2010; 
Schlosberg & Collins, 2014). The notion of displaced impacts through economic trades could therefore 
be used in environmental economics in order to attribute responsibility for the environmental and 
ecological damage caused indirectly by imports. Analogous to carbon accounting methods (Kander et 
al., 2015), this could potentially revolutionize the paradigm for assigning responsibility for 
eutrophication damage in coastal ecosystems, and possibly serve as a mechanism for impacted 
countries to seek redress for harm caused by economic trades with other countries (Folmer & Von 
Mouche, 2000). 
 
3.3. 
Remaining uncertainties and further research 
The emissions data sources used in this model are robust. Indeed, EDGAR (European 
Commission, 2022) is built from consolidated satellite data and is widely used in the scientific field. 
The soil nitrogen balance was compared to a collection of other relevant studies and it proved to be 
consistent with the literature (Jwaideh et al., 2022). 
The results displayed in this report only account for emissions to the soil. After correcting the 
calculation of the characterization factors in the atmospheric model, as presented in Section 2.3.1, the 
model will provide a more accurate outlook of eutrophication impacts in the oceans. A study by Beylot 
et al. (2019) estimated that atmospheric emissions of NHx and NOx combined are responsible for 50% 
of the total eutrophication impacts globally, contributing as much as emissions of NO3- to the soil.  
Furthermore, the inventory could be expanded to incorporate emissions of other crops in the 
soil model (Jwaideh et al., 2022). Indeed, the inventory only includes the emissions associated with 
the cultivation of 17 main crops, that account for 58% of the global cropland area and 70% of the total 
nitrogen application on cropland (West et al., 2014). Finally, the completeness of the model could be 
further increased by including point source emissions to freshwater from sewage systems (Preisner et 
al., 2021). Completing the inventory as such would allow for a better understanding of the impacts of 
human activities in coastal waters, and potentially explain the discrepancies between the model’s 
results and the observed hypoxia in the oceans.  
==PAGE:32==
32 
 
To better understand the eutrophication amounts calculated in the model, a comparison with 
the safe operating space of each subsegment could be valuable, similar to the study by Vea et al. 
(2022). This could help explain some discrepancies between the areas of high eutrophication and the 
observed hypoxic events. Moreover, coupled with the industry and region analysis allowed by the 
present model, such an analysis could be a basis for focusing emission reduction policies on the 
industries occupying the largest share of the safe operating space. 
The choice of MRIO database to use within the model also influences the results and should 
be decided carefully, depending on the goal of each project. A tradeoff between data completeness, 
and spatial and sectoral disaggregation must be addressed to choose the model fitting best the needs 
of a specific study. Adapting the model to a different MRIO model, such as EORA 26 (Lenzen et al., 
2013), would provide new information, especially regarding regions in the world that are less 
disaggregated in EXIOBASE such as South-East Asia and Latin America, where large eutrophication 
impacts are observed.

4. Conclusion  
The model presented in this report is a tool designed to better understand how trade patterns 
in the globalized world economy affect eutrophication impacts in various places of the planet’s oceans. 
It is designed to be easily adapted to different sources of data and MRIO models. As a spatially explicit 
model, it is also fit for any level of disaggregation both for the emissions and for the impacts, which 
allows it to be easily adapted and integrated into different models.  
The distinctive properties of the MRIO model provide us with insightful perspectives on the 
sources of eutrophication in any selected marine region. It offers the capability to pinpoint not only 
the industry directly inducing the impacts, but also the final demand driving them. Hence, it provides 
a measure of the extent to which eutrophication is engendered by local consumption or by imports 
from other countries. It was found for example that the eutrophication impacts observed at Bocas del 
Toro in Panama were caused for the main part by cultivation of rice and oil seeds, 40% of which is at 
the destination of the United States or other continents, including Europe. In general, richer countries 
were found to be responsible for a larger part of the impacts on marine areas in other parts of the 
world. 
The identification of displaced impacts through global economic trades could be the basis for 
assigning environmental impacts from nitrogen emissions to the importing countries, in an effort to 
increase international cooperation to mitigate the impacts of human activities on the environment. 
This model is intended as a basis for further research regarding the importance of international 
trade patterns for marine eutrophication impacts. Its ability to backtrack from the observed impacts 
to the responsible industries and all the way to the final demand allows a more thorough analysis of 
the trade patterns, both at the international scale but also within countries with more detailed input-
output models such as full EORA (Lenzen et al., 2013), thanks to the model’s spatial definition. 
Moreover, focusing on a specific marine area and rewinding to the countries responsible for the 
impacts, both from a production and a consumption point of view, can allow for new policies to protect 
sensitive marine ecosystems such as coral reefs, as was presented in the case study.

A. Atmospheric model description 
This section will present in further detail the different processes used in the atmospheric 
model. In italics are the filenames of the Alteryx workflows corresponding to each part. They refer to 
the NHx model, but the NOx model is an exact copy. 
1. NHx_gridcells_EDGAR_mapping 
The first step is to match the spatially explicit emission data, at a resolution of 0.1 degrees, 
with the grid cells from the eutrophication model for atmospheric emissions, at a resolution of 0.5 
degrees. There are 13104 grid cells, identified by a unique Cell ID, that can be reshaped in a matrix of 
91 rows and 144 columns. 
To start with, the longitude and latitude of the center of each grid cell are computed. The cells 
in the matrix cover a longitude range of [−180, 180] and a latitude range of [−90, 90]. Hence, the 
center of the cell (𝑎, 𝑏) has the following coordinates (for 𝑖 and 𝑗 indices starting from 0):

2) ∗180

2) ∗360
144 −180 
 
Then the grid cells for the eutrophication model are created using the trade area tool and the 
coordinates of their center, as shown in Figure 11. The green lines are the delimitation between grid 
cells, and the red diamond represents the coordinates of the center.

The red diamond is the center of the grid cell. 
They are then matched with the grid cells from the EDGAR emission data based on their 
center’s coordinates with the spatial match process, as shown in Figure 12. The emissions can then be 
summed for each of the model’s cells. 
 
==PAGE:41==
41 
 
 
Figure 12. Grid cell 10011 matched with EDGAR emissions grid cells. Each blue diamond corresponds to the 
center of one cell from the EDGAR data. 
Countries EEZ_extension 
Subsequently, each cell must be associated with the country it is located within. This is done 
using country shape files obtained from the Alteryx community forums, including a total of 252 
countries and territories. However, the countries’ borders first need to be extended with their coastal 
waters in order to include the impacts which are located in the ocean. The Marine Regions database 
(Flanders Marine Institute, 2023) allows downloading shape files with the EEZ (exclusive economic 
zone) corresponding to each country and territory. After matching these marine areas with the 
countries from the Alteryx files, they are combined into a single shape using a spatial process from 
Alteryx. 
Gridcells_countries_mapping + 2. NHx_gridcells_countries_mapping 
Then, using a spatial match process, the grid cells can be matched with the country or 
countries they intersect. In the case of a single grid cell intersecting with multiple countries’ borders 
or EEZ, it is split into different cells and its emissions are divided in proportion to the area within each 
country, as displayed in Figure 13. 
==PAGE:42==
42 
 
 
Figure 13. Cell 10011 is divided into four different cells separated on the borders of the countries it intersects.  
The countries are then associated with the EXIOBASE regions: they either remain as singular 
countries or are grouped in EXIOBASE’s five “Rest of the world” regions. At this step, overseas 
territories are also joined with the mainland for the relevant countries (France, United Kingdom, 
Denmark, etc.). This provides a table with each cell’s ID, its coordinates, the country and region it is 
located in, and its surface area. It is worth noting that multiple entries can have the same cell ID but in 
different countries, and they will be treated as different cells in the following. 
NH3_MRIO_Mapping 
The next step is to split each cell’s emissions into the different industries of the MRIO model. 
EDGAR provides country and sector-specific emission data. However, before using these, the 
aforementioned countries and sectors need to be converted into EXIOBASE regions and industries. The 
country match is similar to what was done previously. To match the sectors from the EDGAR data, they 
first need to be associated with one or multiple industries each. The 2006 IPCC Guidelines for National 
Greenhouse Gas Inventories (IPCC, 2006) provide a framework for dividing economic activities into 
sectors with great detail and are referred to in the EDGAR documentation. Using these, it is possible 
to map the sectors from EDGAR to the EXIOBASE industries. Each industry can have zero, one, or more 
sectors associated with it. Then, the emissions within each sector are scaled to the industries in 
proportion to their value added in the respective year and country of interest. This method 
necessitates the assumption that all the industries associated with a given sector have a similar 
==PAGE:43==
43 
 
emission intensity. While this is probably a significant approximation, it is the best method available 
without further data about sectoral emissions.  
3. NHx_gridcells_sectors_scaling 
Subsequently, this same decomposition is applied to each grid cell within the country in 
proportion to its surface. Again, this method uses the assumption that all the grid cells within a given 
country have the same emission pattern including all the industries present in the country. This is 
obviously not realistic, as different regions have different economic activities between agriculture, 
industry, or services. However, it is again the best approximation since no further geographical data is 
available on the industries. As a result, each grid cell has emissions attributed to each industry in 
EXIOBASE (some of which will be zero).  
FF_NHx_preparation + 4. NHx_impacts 
Now that the spatially explicit emissions have been mapped to regions and industries, it is time 
to convert them into eutrophication impacts. The characterization factors provided by the 
eutrophication model each are associated with an emission cell and a destination cell. Using the join 
process in an Alteryx workflow, each emission cell from the model is associated with all its destination 
cells. This step is very time-consuming and memory intensive; therefore, a threshold of 
10−6 kg O2/kg N was defined to eliminate the lowest and null characterization factors. This 
manipulation drastically reduces the number of entries necessary and therefore the time and memory 
required without affecting too much the results. 
Then, multiplying the cell’s emissions (in each country and industry) with the characterization 
factor from each destination cell gives the spatially explicit impacts. At this point, the model provides 
a table with the following columns:

The emission cells are then aggregated based on the emission region to match EXIOBASE. With 
this method, it is easy to change the aggregation scale, in case the model is to be used with a more 
detailed input-output model for example.  
Then, the destination cells can be aggregated to any geographical level. For example, here they 
were aggregated at the LME subsegments scale, but it can also be done at the country scale for 
example. Note that theoretically, it would be possible to keep the grid cell precision for the impacts in 
the input-output model. However, the eutrophication model is built based on coastal areas larger than 
the grid cell, so this level of detail would not be scientifically relevant. In addition, the more different 
areas are used, the more times the input-output model will need to run, thus requiring increasingly 
more time and memory. 
5. NHx_satellite 
Finally, a cross table is built from the previous output, such that each line of the table 
corresponds to one region and industry, and the columns correspond to each impact area (individual 
countries in this example). Then, simply transposing this table gives a satellite matrix for the 
eutrophication model. Each line of the satellite matrix describes the eutrophication impacts in one 
==PAGE:44==
44 
 
impact area, and the columns are the regions and industries like any satellite matrix specific to the 
input-output model used.  
 
B. Soil model description 
1. Soil_emission_country_mapping 
The first step is to match the emission grid cells with the countries they are located in. This is 
done in the same way as in the atmospheric model, using a spatial match. However, due to the larger 
number of cells, it is not feasible to split the cells precisely when they overlap in different countries. 
Instead, each cell is allocated to the country where its center is located. However, since the cells in this 
model are much smaller, the overlap should be less frequent and represent smaller areas. For instance, 
in the atmospheric model, no grid cell had its center located in Luxembourg, as shown in Figure 11. On 
the other hand, Figure 14 shows the grid cells located in the same area. 
 
Figure 14. The grid cells of the soil model: each red diamond is the center of one grid cell. 47 grid cells have 
their center located in Luxembourg. 
 
2. Soil_emission_scaling 
Then, the emissions are scaled using the yearly production from each country, to translate 
them from the 2000 value to the more recent one (2018 in the case of the model). To do so, production 
==PAGE:45==
45 
 
data from the Food and Agriculture Organization of the United Nations (FAO, 2023) is used. The crops 
from the model are matched to the ones from FAO data, as well as the countries. Then, each emission 
cell value is multiplied by the ratio between the 2018 production and the 2000 production for said 
crop and year (Eq. 25). If the data is unavailable for either 2000 or 2018 production, the emission value 
is unchanged.  
3. Soil_eutrophication 
Then, the emissions are joined with the characterization factors in order to calculate the 
eutrophication impact of each cell. This is done via a Join process using the cell IDs. Subsequently, 
eutrophication is simply the product between the emissions and the characterization factor (Eq. 26). 
LME_country_mapping + 4. Soil_eutrophication_basin 
Once this is done, each grid cell has to be matched to a river basin in order to know where the 
impacts are located in the coastal waters (Vea et al., 2022). Once again, a spatial match is performed 
between the center of each grid cell and the basin areas on land. Then, the basin’s river mouths are 
matched with the large marine ecosystem (LME) subsegment they are located in. Finally, each grid cell 
can be allocated to both the river basin it is located in and the LME subsegment it will cause impacts 
in.  
5. Region_eutrophication_LME 
Then, the cells have to be aggregated, similarly to the atmospheric model. The emission cells 
are aggregated to the MRIO regions level in order to build the satellite for the MRIO model. The 
destination impacts are at the scale of the LME subsegments. Alternatively, it is possible to get impacts 
per country by matching the subsegments with the EEZ from each country and dividing them based 
on area in the case of an overlap. The crops from the original data are also aggregated to match the 
industries from EXIOBASE. 
6. Soil_satellite 
 
Finally, the satellite is built in the same way as the atmospheric model, using a cross table. To 
get the complete satellite with columns corresponding to all the EXIOBASE industries, the missing 
columns are added to the cross table with zero values in all rows.

Firstly, one can observe that in most cases, the regions responsible for the production of 
impacts are responsible for a lower share of the consumption. This is explained by international trade, 
which shifts the impacts from production to other countries' consumption. This can be observed in the 
LME-ss 36.13 near the Taiping coral reef (Table 9): the eutrophication impacts are caused by emissions 
in Asia (WA) and China (CN) for a great part, but over 30% of them are destined for consumption in 
other regions of the world, including around 10% to Europe.  
It is even more visible in the case of agricultural emissions in LME-ss 12.1, where Bocas del 
Toro is located. In this case, over 90% of the eutrophication is caused by production in Latin America 
(WL). However, only 55% is destined for consumption in this region, while the rest is split between the 
United States (10%), China (7%) and other regions, including a total of 9.6% in European countries. 
On the other hand, LME-ss 10, where Kane’ohe Bay’s coral reef is found, offers a different 
outlook. In this case, the main responsible for production impacts from NOx is the US with 73%, 
followed by the rest of Asia and Pacific countries (WA) with 19%. However, the consumption is destined 
for the US in an even larger part, with 83% of the impacts being allocated to US consumption.  
Overall, it seems that some regions suffer from eutrophication caused by production at the 
destination of other countries. This is the case in Latin America (LME-ss 12.1) and in South-East Asia 
(LME-ss 36.13). The countries indirectly responsible for these impacts seem to be the more 
industrialized and richer, such as the US, European countries, and to some extent China.
