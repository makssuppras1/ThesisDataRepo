Abstract
This thesis deals with the problem of designing high-quality utility networks
with focus on telecommunication networks. Designing such networks is today
often a manual process, based on implicit knowledge of an experienced net-
work designer; however, with costs ranging between hundreds to thousands
DKK per meter of installation even small improvements is crucial. The utility
network design problem (UNDP) investigated can be formulated as a set of
demand point (customers) that must be connected to a set of serving struc-
tures (supplier), with a certain supply limit.
The objective is to minimize
the cost associated with drilling ducts and blowing ﬁber optic cable through
these ducts. The problem is NP-hard, which motivates the implementation of
a heuristic based algorithm to ﬁnd high-quality solution.
In this paper, we report on a simulated annealing based implementation
containing a regret cost based insertion heuristic for ﬁnding an initial solutions,
and two methods, swap and reroute, allowing the heuristic to move from one
solution to a another in search for improvements. Computational results on
smaller instances shows that the simulated annealing heuristic can compete
with the optimal solution. Even for larger instances the algorithm is successful
in ﬁnding better solutions than the standard LP-solver, CPLEX.

Introduction
In this chapter, a description of the problem, presented by breakoutimage
A/S, is given. The description is based on an short introduction to the current
situation, motivating the need for investigating the problem. The aim of the
thesis and related literature is also summarized here.
1.1
Background and Motivation
This thesis is written in cooperation with breakoutimage A/S. They supply
Geographical Information System (GIS) solutions to danish utility companies
based on a larger software platform, Smallworld, from General Electric (GE),
see www.breakoutimage.dk for more information.
breakoutimage have been very successful within telecommunication, espe-
cially ﬁber based solutions, where the supported solution is called Physical
Network Inventory (PNI). The PNI solution consists all the primary proce-
dures for ﬁber networks, such as strategic planning, network design, network
roll-out, maintenance and service.
Since it is extremely expensive to roll-out these ﬁber networks, primarily a
result of the physical drilling for cable ducts, an optimized planning of a utility
network is necessary. The prices for the drilling ducts and implementing ﬁber
varies a lot, depending on the infrastructure in the given area. For example,
in areas such as an bare ﬁeld, the total cost per meter varies below 250 kr/m,
while in areas with high population and a lot of traﬃc such as Kongens Ny-
torv, the total cost per meter varies above 1500 kr/m.
An optimized network design, which considered the above prices variations
and relates to the customer potential in a given area can therefor result in
large economic savings for ﬁber operators. The calculation of customer poten-
tial depends on many parameters such as their type (commercial or domestic),
socioeconomic status, etc. For example does commercial customers often have
a higher potential than private customers, and customers living in houses typ-
ically has a higher potential than customers living in apartment houses, and
ﬁnally implementing in urban areas with higher social class is often more at-
tractive than implementing in rural areas with lower social class.
This means that there is high potential for an intelligent, data-driven, al-
gorithm which considers the above challenges. The GIS systems contains a

This thesis is about planning and optimizing utility network designs the

1.2
Problem Description
Utility companies – for example, a ﬁber operator – supply services to cus-
tomers.
These customers have geographical locations, in this thesis called
demand points. These demand points form the end of the network – the con-
sumption - whereas the point of presence (POP) and core POP’s form the
supply.
As not all demand points can be supplied directly from the supply, a num-
ber of hierarchical service structures organize the network into several layers.
E.g. for a ﬁber operator, the lowest level layer or ”zone” could contain 80
demand points. These 80-demand-point zones can in turn be aggregated to
higher level zones, that again could be aggregated and so on.
Designing such supply zones today is often a manual, heuristic process
based on the implicit knowledge of an experienced network designer.
This
person visually inspects the area of interest and in the computer-aided design
(CAD) and GIS draws a polygon around for instance 80 demand points. When
satisﬁed with that, an underground utility box, in this thesis called a serving
structure, is placed in a suitable position within the zone.
The network design of physical assets, such as a ﬁber optic network, rep-
resent substantial capital expenditure investments for the ﬁber operator. It is
therefore important to design supply zones in the best possible way. As an
alternative to the heuristic approach it is assumed feasible to develop a data
driven algorithm that clusters demand points into optimal zones and optionally
places a serving structure in the optimal position within the zone.
The problem that needs to be solved is to design and implement an algo-
rithm that clusters demand points in the best possible way for a ﬁber operator.
In this case ”best possible” means in the optimal way meeting deﬁned design
criteria, such as no. of demand points pr. zone, geographical distance between
demand points, best blend of demand point types (commercial, domestic), no
crossing of barrier structures such as railway, river, etc. or even social eco-
nomic parameters characterizing the demand points. This thesis will consider
the zone size and geographical distance aspects of the problem.
Clustering demand points should be done topologically based on an in-
terconnected route network and not using polygons, as utility networks are
topological. For illustrative purpose, a simple clustering examples of 20 de-
mand points and 2 serving structures is seen in ﬁgure 1.1.
As seen in Figure 1.1, the utility network design problem consists of a net-
work deﬁning possible routes in between a number of demand points and serv-
ing structures. Each demand point must be connected to a serving structure.

1.3
Aim of this Thesis
This thesis present the utility network design problem (UNDP) which occurs
in the utility industry. The focus of this thesis is to optimize the design of
such networks the best possible way. To do so, the following goals will be ad-
dressed. This thesis will motivate and describe the UNDP and investigate liter-
ature within related topics in order to identify similarities in problem structure
and possible solution approaches. This thesis will formalize the mathematical
model of UNDP and use experiments to investigate the practical complexity of
the problem in order to determine the size of solvable instances. Next, this the-
sis will identify and construct a suitable meta-heuristic for solving the UNDP
and implement the heuristic in a programming environment. The quality of
the heuristic solutions will be compared against the best known mixed integer
programming (MIP) solutions. Last, this thesis will analyze and discuss the
solution structure and hopefully be able to work with a case study and bench-
mark the algorithm against actual utility networks provided by breakoutimage

1.4
Literature Review
In this section, a summary of the most relevant literature for this research topic
is presented. The literature contains research on topics with similar problem
structure and the solution approaches employed. This will provide insight into
which solution method might be viable. The solution approaches can be sep-
arated into two categories, exact- and heuristic-methods. These methods are
applied when the problem at hand is NP-hard, and can’t be solved by standard
LP solvers, such as CPLEX, for suﬃcient large instances. The structure of the
UNDP is very similar to the multi-commodity ﬂow problem (MCFP), which
will be further elaborated in Chapter 2. It is an NP-hard problem and the
basic idea of the MCFP is to connect ﬂow of a number of commodities with
a known demand to a number of suppliers, which could be translated into de-
mand points and serving structures for the UNDP. Comprehensive surveys of
the available multi-commodity network ﬂow solution techniques are provided
in the papers by Assad [15] and Kennington [16].
Furthermore, Barnhart et al. [14] presents a column generation and parti-
tioning approach. The target of the study is large-scale problems with many
commodities as those encountered in telecommunication industry. It seems,
however, that the test problem generated contains ”only” up to 150 com-
modities. This reﬂects no more than a small residential area in the UNDP,
which is intended for solving instances with several thousands of commodities.
Imagining a problem containing 50000 demand points, the concept of column
generation, will lose some of its strength due to a large amount of variables
initially required for using the method. In general, the literature shows that
column generation returns great results for the MCFP, but due to the fact that
UNDP is more complex and will contain thousands of demand point for large
instances it might not be as successful if you attempt to solve it all at once.
Solving a single cluster, however, might not be too bad.
Heuristic procedures have also been successful in problems related to the
MCFP. There exist many variants of such metaheuristic procedures for exam-
ple Crainic et al. [6] presents a tabu search heuristic for the ﬁxed charge ca-
pacitated network design problem and later Crainic et al. [7] a scatter search
heuristic to the similar problem. The problem investigated contains a ﬁxed
charge element which will also be included in the UNDP. It refers to the cost
for making an edge available, which in practice reﬂect the cost of drilling ducts.
For both of these papers the heuristics show great computational results for
instances containing up to 400 commodities, with a running time of up to
more than 24 hours. However, they do not indicate any performance on larger
instances.
Finally, no more than two years ago Li Shuqin et al.
[13] suggested a
simulated annealing based heuristic for the multi-source single-path multi-
commodity network ﬂow problem with a formulation as close to the UNDP
I’ve yet seen covered in the literature. The performance of the algorithm was
measured on 175 random generated instances, and the results shows the pro-
posed heuristic is an eﬃcient and eﬀective method for the given problem. But

2.1.6
Assumptions
As already mentioned the UNDP model does not impose any capacity on each
arc, but for theoretical purposes an upper bound is set to the total number of
demand points. Several other assumptions are worth noting.
I assume that the cost on each arc is linear in the ﬂow on that arc. This
means that the more congestion arises in an arc, the cost of using that arc is
not increased. This is a feature, which typically arises in lots of multicommod-
ity ﬂow problems within communication, transportation and other problem
areas, where the commodities interact in a more complicated sense.
Last to mention would be that the model assumes the serving structure
to be in a ﬁxed location. If the locations of the serving structures are to be
a decision as well, the complexity increases signiﬁcantly, but it’s a problem
worth investigating.

3.1
Introduction to Metaheuristic Algorithms
A heuristic algorithm is an approach which seeks to obtain high-quality (i.e.
near-optimal) solutions at reasonable computational cost and no commercial
license is required. Unfortunately, there is no certiﬁcate of optimality and it
is not even be possible to state how close to optimality a particular heuristic
solution is. A metaheuristic is an iterative process which guides and modiﬁes
other heuristics to produce solutions beyond those that a are normally gen-
erated in a search for local optimality. It is a master strategy consisting of a
set of available moves in order to transform one solution to another, together
with an associated evaluation rule. Metaheuristic does not guarantee a global
optimal solution but can avoid getting stuck in a local optimum by including
elements from stochastic optimization such that the solution is dependent on
a set of random variables generated. A metaheuristic may often be divided
into the following main parts.
Main parts included in a metaheuristic:
• Generation of initial solution
• Deﬁnition of neighborhood
• Selection from neighborhood
• Stopping criteria
Unless the initial solution is given as input, it is often generated from
constructive heuristics.
This refers to the process of building an initial
solution from scratch. An example could be an ”empty” utility network with
no connection between serving structures and demand points. The demand

constraint until they are all allocated. The order in which demand points are
connected may inﬂuence the quality of solution since it’s often a single-pass
approach. This, however, may characterize this constructive heuristic as fast.
The solution generated by the constructive heuristic can then be employed
as the starting point of local search heuristics. This is a heuristic mech-
anism that considers the neighborhood of the current solution for possible
replacements. A neighborhood deﬁnes a search space of solutions (neighbors)
around the current solution. If a solution from the neighborhood is accepted
then we move to that solution and consider its neighbors. The local search
process is illustrated in Figure 3.1. Note, that the deﬁnition of neighborhood
and selection from neighborhood is problem dependent, which is why it can’t
be elaborated on more until applied on a problem.

In other words local search heuristics moves from one solution to another
until the stop criteria is met. The ﬁnal solution will then be the best of all
found solutions.
Probably the most basic local search algorithm is known as hill climbing.
The idea is to search the neighborhood of the current solution for one which
has a larger objective, assuming it’s a maximization problem. Minimizing a
certain value would be just the opposite, where instead of climbing, one would
be descending. A solution is only accepted if the objective is improved, which
means the algorithm suﬀers from getting stuck in local optima, see Figure 3.2.
If the current solution is the one shown in Figure 3.2, the algorithm will
reach the local optima directly above and the algorithm will terminate without
ﬁnding the global optimum. Several methods exist to overcome this issue of
getting stuck in a local optima and I present a selection of these which i ﬁnd
essential to consider for solving the UNDP.

3.2
Simulated Annealing
This thesis will be focused on a simulated annealing (SA) based implemen-
tation in order to solve the UNDP. This method was introduced in the early
1980s by Kirkpatrick et al. [2] and independently ˇCern´y [3] introduced the
concepts of annealing in combinatorial optimization. Simulated annealing is
among the best known local search algorithms because it performs quite well
and is applicable to a large amount of problems. The basic concept of the
algorithm is, similar to the hill climber, to search the neighborhood for im-
proving solutions. However, it does also accept worse solutions to a limited
extent such that the algorithm may avoid getting stuck in local optima. By
allowing bad steps it may lead the algorithm towards another ”hill” to climb,
see Figure 3.2, with a chance to reach global optimality. Now, the probability
of which a bad move is chosen should be determined in an intelligent way in
order to lead the algorithm towards a good direction.
This is achieved by introducing a couple of parameters. First, we have
the initial temperature T0 and secondly the reduction rate α. Both of these
are initialized to some suitable value, found by a procedure called parameter
tuning which will be discussed later in Section 4.1. Now these two parameters
control the current temperature ti at each iteration i which is deﬁned as:
ti = T0 · αi
(3.1)

wards zero, such that the acceptance probability also converges towards zero.

ber, the worse solution will be accepted. Let f denote the evaluation function
and δ the diﬀerence between objective values of current solution si and the

or millions solutions within just a few minutes. The algorithm is also quite
ﬂexible, highly applicable and fairly easy to implement which is the foundation
for the choice of the algorithm for the UNDP.
3.3
Overview of other Metaheuristics
Choosing the best suitable metaheuristic to apply for the UNDP can be hard
to determine. One can only compare the performance of two algorithms once
they are both implemented to the problem hand, and yet they may outper-
form each other on diﬀerent instances. Below is a short discussion on other
candidates I found of interest for a possible implementation to the UNDP.
Fred Glover proposed in 1986 a new approach, which he called tabu search,
containing several elements that he introduced in 1977 [4]. This is, similar to
simulated annealing, a local search approach to overcome local optima. The
basic principle of the algorithm is to include memory such that whenever a
local optimum is encountered, the algorithm allows non-improving solutions
which do not cycle back to previously visited solutions. The memory can be
thought of as a list of tabu solutions, that records the history of search.
Tabu search has, like simulated annealing, been applied with great success
to a large amount of combinatorial problems such as mathematical graph-,
scheduling- and vehicle routing problem [5, p. 178]. Also a study performed
by Crainic et al. [6] proposes a modiﬁed tabu search algorithm to solve the
ﬁxed charge multicommodity network design problem which has a lot similar-
ities with the UNDP. However, even though its a powerful algorithm, many
failed tabu search implementation are found. The reason for this is that deﬁn-
ing the solution search space and related neighborhoods is by far the most
critical step in the design of any tabu search heuristic and it requires a deep
understanding and knowledge of the problem at hand.
Iterated local search (ILS) is another metaheuristic ﬁrst seen used by
==PAGE:27==
3.3. OVERVIEW OF OTHER METAHEURISTICS
20
Baxter [8]; however, the name was ﬁrst invented later in the 90’s. The essence
of the iterated local search is to iteratively build a sequence of solutions gener-
ated by an embedded heuristic, which is often a local search heuristic, leading
to far better solutions than if one were to use repeated random trials of that
heuristic. This is achieved by the concept of perturbation, which is comple-
mentary to the local search. Perturbation can be thought of as a change of
state, moving the solution search to a diﬀerent location, and is often associated
a memory such that one may not fall back to the same location. The choice
of perturbation can be diﬃcult. If it’s too small one will fall back to the same
local optima, and contrary if it’s too large, it simply becomes a random restart
of the local search algorithm.
Iterated local search has a long history of applications but has seen most
promising result within the well-known traveling salesman problem and schedul-
ing problems in general [9]. With the right choice of local search algorithm and
perturbation, the iterated local search can become very eﬀective and robust
by its biased sampling of local optima. However, I yet haven’t found literature
investigating an ILS implementation for similar problems to the UNDP which
has shown good result.
Finally, is the large neighborhood search (LNS) which was ﬁrst pro-
posed by Shaw [10]. The basic concept is to search a large neighborhood in
order to ﬁnd high-quality local optima. The large neighborhoods consist of
two heuristic methods that destroys and repairs an incumbent solution. The
destroy method often contains stochastic elements which destructs part of a
solution, while the repair method rebuilds the destroyed solution, often by
some greedy constructive heuristic, such that it yet again becomes feasible.
The repair method has much freedom in choosing, however, determining the
right degree of destruction, i.e. how much of the solution should be destroyed,
is critical. Too little and the exploration of the solution search space is limited.
Too much and the algorithm becomes very time-consuming or may even yield
poor results based on the repair method. An extension to this method is the
adaptive large neighborhood search (ALNS) proposed by Pisinger et al.
[12] which allows multiple destroy and repair method within the same search.
The choice of which methods to use are dynamically adjusted as the search
progresses so that the heuristic adapts to the state of the search and instance
at hand.
The idea of using destroy and repair methods has seen good results for
problems which can be decomposed into a master problem covering a set of
tasks and a set of sub problems which must fulﬁll some constraints.
LNS
and ALNS are in general suitable heuristics for tightly constrained problems
such as scheduling- and routing problems or others which involve some sort of
partitioning decision [11]. It does; however, reach a higher level of complexity
when it comes to an implementation.

3.4.1
Initial Solution
As mentioned earlier, ﬁnding an initial solution is often done by constructive
heuristics which builds a solution from scratch. Such a solution can either be
completely random, partially random or created by some intelligent process,
see Figure 3.3. In theory, this doesn’t matter if one has made high-quality
neighborhoods which allow the search algorithm to ﬁnd the global optimum.
However, in practice that may be diﬃcult which is why it is often desired to
give the search algorithm a ”good start”. This thesis proposes an intelligent
constructive heuristic to the UNDP.
The basic idea of the heuristic is to calculate the distance for each demand
point to each serving structure and take the demand points one by one and
assign it to the serving structure with the shortest distance by the associated
shortest path. When it occurs that a demand point’s best option is a fully
utilized serving structure it will instead take the second best alternative and
so forth until it ﬁnds an available serving structure.
However, after the implementation of this, an investigation of initial so-
lutions showed that the ﬁnal demand points could be associated with a very
poor serving structures due to the occupation of suitable ones. This motivated
an adaption of above, resulting in a regret cost based insertion heuristic,

3.4.2
Neighborhoods
Now, the choice of neighborhoods are important because that’s what drives
the algorithm in search for better solutions. As mentioned earlier, a neigh-
borhood can take various forms and sizes and the quality of a neighborhood
can be hard to deﬁne since it is problem dependent. For example, using a set
of neighborhoods on small UNDP’s may not perform as well as on larger in-
stances. This means, a neighborhood should always be modiﬁed and adapted,
such that it suits the problem. However, for this study we will be investigat-
ing problems of various size and the neighborhoods will therefore be slightly
general such that they are applicable for any UNDP. Further work could be
to improve neighborhoods according to the performance of the algorithm and
problem size.
For the utility network design problem we introduce two neighborhoods
to the search space of all feasible solutions. Given an initial solution it may
be of interest to swap two demand points, meaning the demands points swap
clusters. Furthermore, rerouting a single demand point (changing it’s path)
will be necessary. These two neighborhoods are the foundation to obtain new
solutions and are further elaborated on in the following sections.

3.4.2.1
Swap
An optimal solution will contain optimal clusters. By that we mean, the best
partitioning of demand point to a set of serving structures. The initial solution
might not contain these optimal clusters, hence, we need to be able two swap
cluster association of two demand points. This can be constructed in various
ways, by either being completely stochastic, or give it some search direction.
Since the algorithm is intended to solve larger instances with thousands of
demand points, one could imagine, in such a network, that by choosing two
demand points at random for a potential swap millions of very poor swap
iterations will be evaluated. Hence, we choose to give the swap method some
search direction.
This is implemented in two stages. First, at random choosing a cluster, C.
Within this cluster, the demand points can be sorted by the length of the their
path to the associated serving structure. This allows me to make a set of short
path demand points, S, and long path demand points L. Since the demand
points with shortest paths are most likely to be in the optimal cluster, these
will not be selected as possible swap candidates, whereas the demand points
in L will be. Choosing sizes of S and L can be diﬃcult since the algorithm is
solving networks with clusters of various sizes up to 80. In my implementation
I ensured that the set L should at least contain 2 demand points and at most
20% of the cluster size. This means for smaller networks with clusters of four
demand points, 2 of these are possible swap candidates, and in larger networks
with 80 demand points, the set L contain 16 swap candidates. From the set L,
a demand point dp1 is chosen at random, for a swap routine. The ﬁrst stage
is illustrated in ﬁgure 3.4.

3.4.2.2
Reroute
Because the problem includes ﬁxed costs for opening edges, it may not be the
optimal solution to connect every demand point to a serving structure by the
shortest path, which is how the initial solution is designed. A better option
would be to try make the demand points share the same edges, minimizing
the ﬁxed charge cost. This rerouting neighborhood can be designed in various
ways and is seen within many other heuristics for solving graph problem and
is also known as relinking, rebuilding, etc.
We chose to implement this by, at random, selecting a cluster, C, and two
demand points, dp1 and dp2, within. The reason we chose two demand points
within the same cluster is because the chance of getting a better solution is
much higher, than if two demands were chosen at random within the complete
graph. The idea is to connect dp1 to dp2’s path. The path from dp2 to its
associated serving structure contains some intermediate nodes, I. One of these
nodes is chosen, at random, as the connection point, cp, for dp1, as seen in
Figure 3.6. The number of possible connection points varies depending on the
length of dp2’s path.
Figure 3.6: The rerouting method illustrating the ﬁrst demand point, dp1,
being connected to a second demand points, dp2, path by the connection point.
Due to the fact that we pre-calculated the Bellman-Ford distances and
paths for each demand point to any other vertex, means that we can easily
ﬁnd and evaluate the shortest path from dp1 to the connection point. From
the connection point, dp1 and dp2 will obviously share the same edges in the
remaining path to the associated serving structure. If the rerouting doesn’t
result in a better objective or not accepted by the simulated annealing accep-
tance strategy, the original path of dp1 is kept.
The structure of this rerouting method ensures a lot of diversiﬁcation in
how the two demand points are being connected, since the only requirement

4.1
Parameter Tuning
According to the ”no free lunch” theorem [17] any two algorithms are equiva-
lent when their performance is averaged across all possible problems. This is
not very intuitive and precise but it simply means that an algorithms elevated
performance over one class of problems is oﬀset by the performance of an-
other class. In this thesis, we have constructed and implemented a simulated
annealing algorithm designed to produce high-quality solutions to the UNDP.
Evaluating the performance of a metaheuristic can be diﬃcult since it is
problem- and parameter dependent and contains stochastic elements. Further-
more, for suﬃcient large problems it is not possible to state how far from the
optimal solution one is. However, by a process called parameter tuning, one
can ﬁnd good parameters when optimizing the average performance on a set
of diverse test samples.
The ﬁrst step of a parameter tuning is to decide for how long the heuristic
should run. In this paper we will look into run times of 1 and 5 minutes.
Secondly, we have to determine the parameters of the my algorithm. As
in any standard simulated annealing we will have an initial temperature T0
and a temperature reduction rate α. Furthermore, in the implementation we
have a choice between two neighborhoods method. As earlier mentioned, this
choice is determined by a constant, P ∈[0; 1[, which deﬁnes the probability
of using the swap method and (1 −P) the probability of using the rerouting
method. Choosing some suitable values intelligently for these parameters can
be diﬃcult and require some investigation.
The initial temperature, T0, must be chosen suitably with respect to Equa-
tion (3.2) such that it correlates with the expected values of δ. Too large initial
temperature and the algorithm will struggle converging towards local optima
and too small the algorithm can’t escape these local optima. This parameter
tuning covers initial temperatures of 50, 200, 1000.

6.1
Conclusion
In this thesis, the utility network design problem (UNDP) is introduced. It’s
a problem consisting of a set of demand points (customers) that must be con-
nected to a set of serving structures (supplier), with a certain supply limit.
A survey of relevant literature shows that the problem contains a lot of sim-
ilarities to the NP-hard multi-commodity ﬂow problem (MCFP). Since the
problem is NP-hard, alternative methods must be implemented in order to
solve the UNDP. The literature states many diﬀerent method, both exact and
heuristic, in order to solve the MCFP; however, they do not cover problem
sizes containing thousand of demand points. Therefore, based on the practical
UNDP sizes, a heuristic based implementation was selected.
In order to benchmark the algorithm as well as identifying the complexity
of the UNDP a mathematical model is formulated. Computational experi-
ments with the model, using a standard LP-solver, CPLEX, shows that the
problem is a very complex problem with many combinatorial possibilities. Fur-
thermore, the model struggles ﬁnding optimal solution within 1800 seconds to
problems containing more than 81 nodes and 288 edges, which again, justiﬁes
the complexity of the UNDP.
To solve this issue, a simulated annealing heuristic was implemented. The
choice of suitable metaheuristic is based on the survey of relevant literature and
personal preference. The implemented simulated annealing is using the classic
framework, with an initial temperature and a reduction rate. The heuristic
includes an intelligent regret cost based insertion algorithm for ﬁnding an initial
solution and two neighborhood methods, swap and reroute, which allows the
algorithm to search for better solutions. The optimal parameter settings for the
simulated annealing implementation was found by a parameter tuning process.
Computational experiments with the simulated annealing shows that the
algorithm performs quite well when benchmarked against the MIP solutions
for smaller test instances, with solution gaps ranging between −3.20% and
5.93%, and for even larger problems the heuristic consistently outperform the

6.3
Other Extensions
The problem description from Section 1.2 covers a couple more network de-
sign criteria which yet haven’t been discussed in above models.
These are
the barrier structures such as railway, river, etc. and best blend of demand
points when considering their property of being either commercial or domestic.
Considering the barrier structures, this could in fact be fairly easy incorpo-
rated in the model such that it doesn’t actually change. Imagining a railroad
crossing a number of edges in the graph. One could either remove those edges
from the graph or make them very unattractive. The easy solution would be
to simply remove them from the graph; however, this could yield very poor
solutions where the network is sparse such that a longer route would be a worse
alternative. Therefore making the edges unattractive but still available might
==PAGE:52==
45

be the better solution. Now remember, the parameter fij determines the ﬁxed
cost of opening the arc (i, j). Now, for each edge containing a crossing railroad,
one could simply add a penalty penrailroad, such that
fij = fij + penrailroad
(6.4)
But, choosing the suitable size of penalties, making aﬀected edges unattractive
but in some cases still better options, is very diﬃcult. Hence, the reason for
not being included in the previous models, UNDP and UNDPSSL.
Last issue would be to include the best blend design criteria. One solution
could be to introduce a new matrix {Ai,j}|DP|,|DP|
i,j=1
where |DP| is the number
of demand points in the graph. The matrix describes the correlation between
each pair of demand points by a score. This makes it possible to evaluate the
score of each cluster and in general maximize the sum of all clusters scores.
The best blend criteria wouldn’t constrain the problem but only change the
deﬁnition of objective. However, this does, similar to the barrier structures,
require some pre-calculations, which can be very hard to determine, since there
is no rule on how they should be calculated, but requires the knowledge of an
expert.
