Abstract
Ingress of chlorides into a reinforced concrete structure can result in a reduction of the
structural service life. A suggested initiative to increase the durability for structures
subjected to deterioration from water-borne chloride ions is hydrophobic impregnation,
which resists the chloride ingress by its water-resistant property.
In this report, experimental research studies the resistance to chloride ingress applying
silane-based hydrophobic impregnation to concrete with age of 28 maturity days and
younger. Two surface treatments, one cream treatment and one liquid treatment, and
one admixture treatment were tested on concrete containing Blast-furnace Slag Cement
CEM III/B 42.5 N (slag cement) and Low-alkali Sulphate Resistant Cement CEM I
42.5 N (low-alkali cement) with w/c = 0.4. The effect on chloride ingress was evaluated
when specimens were exposed to either 3 % saline water or saturated saline water at
atmospheric pressure, or saturated saline water at 4 bar water pressure. The effect from
the parameters; application time, amount, and type of surface treatment was examined.
Furthermore, the chloride ingress was evaluated by different methods, and a correlation
between the impregnation penetration depth and chloride ingress was assessed. The hy-
drophobic effect including the change of penetration depth of impregnation and contact
angle was examined for different types of surface treatment as well as, concrete age for
application of impregnation and curing times of impregnation for both mould-covered
and plastic-covered surfaces.
A reduction of the chloride ingress for impregnated concrete exposed to saturated saline
water is observed for all types of treatment, where a higher effect is found for concrete
containing slag cement. However, the integral treatment additionally reduces the 28-
days compressive strength by approximately 15 to 20 %. The effect of treatment with
Creme C (creme treatment) and Faceal RF (liquid treatment), both from WACKER,
was not significantly different regarding the effect for both impregnation penetration
depth and chloride ingress. Neither was observed a clear tendency from halving the
amount of Faceal RF. Generally, the impregnation penetration depth increases for a
later application time, where only in some cases the resistance to chlorides is slightly
improved.
The findings for the specimens exposed to saturated saline water and 3 % saline water
were not consistent since no significant effect from treatment was seen in the latter. The
results regarding the effect of surface treatment on chloride ingress when exposed to a
saturated saline water pressure of 4 bar are inconclusive.
The different evaluation methods for chloride ingress all have strengths and weaknesses.
It was found that the best method depends on the performed experiment and the re-

Resumé
Kloridindtrængning i armerede beton konstruktioner kan være kritisk og resultere i en
reduceret strukturel levetid. En metode til at forlænge holdbarheden af konstruktioner
udsat for kloridholdige væsker er at benytte hydrofob imprægnering, som kan reducere
kloridindtrængning med sine vandafvisende egenskaber.
I denne rapport undersøges modstanden mod kloridindtrængning gennem forsøg ved at
påføre hydrofob silan-baseret imprægnering på beton, som er 28 modenhedsdøgn eller
yngre. To forskellige overfladebehandlinger, én creme og én væske, og et iblandingspro-
dukt blev testet på beton indeholdende enten Højovnsslagge Cement CEM III/B 42.5 N
(slagge cement) eller Lavalkali Sulfatbestandig Cement CEM I 42.5 N (lav-alkali cement)
med et v/c = 0.4.
For at vurdere hvordan imprægneringsprodukterne påvirker modstanden mod klorid-
indtrængning, blev prøverne eksponeret til enten 3 % saltvand eller mættet saltvand
ved atmosfærisk tryk, eller mættet saltvand ved vandtryk på 4 bar. Effekten fra føl-
gende parametre; påføringstidspunkt, mængde og type af imprægnering blev undersøgt.
Derudover blev kloridindtrængningen evalueret gennem flere forskellige metoder, og det
blev undersøgt, om en korrelation mellem indtrængningen af henholdsvis imprægnering
og klorid kunne findes. Den hydrofobe effekt bestående af ændringen af imprægner-
ingsindtrængningen og kontaktvinklen blev også undersøgt for de to typer af overflade
imprægnering samt påføringstidspunktet og hærdetiden for både form overfladen og den
plastik afdækkede overflade.
For alle prøver, der har været eksponeret til mættet saltvand, blev kloridindtrængnin-
gen reduceret ved brug af de forskellige typer imprægnering, - den største effekt blev
set for prøverne indeholdende slagge cement. Det blev dog endvidere fundet, at ibland-
ingsproduktet reducerer 28 døgns trykstyrken med ca. 15 til 20 %. I forhold til hvilken
overfladebehandling der var mest effektiv mod kloridindtrængning og trængte længst ind,
var der ikke nogen signifikant forskel mellem Creme C (creme) og Faceal RF (væske).
Der blev heller ikke observeret nogle klare tendenser ved at halvere den påførte mængde
af Faceal RF. Generelt kunne det ses, at imprægneringen trængte længere ind, hvis den
blev påført senere, hvorimod det kun var i nogle tilfælde at kloridindtrængningen blev
reduceret heraf.
Resultaterne fra prøverne eksponeret til mættet saltvand og 3 % saltvand stemte ikke
overens, da der ikke kunne ses nogen tydelig effekt fra imprægnering for det sidste til-
fælde. Derudover var resultaterne for effekten på kloridindtrængningen fra imprægnering
i prøverne eksponeret til mættet saltvand ved 4 bar vandtryk ikke entydige.
De forskellige metoder brugt til at evaluere kloridindtrængningen har alle fordele og
ulemper. Den bedste metode til at vurdere kloridindtrængningen afhænger af det ud-

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The service life of reinforced concrete structures is mainly assessed based on the chlo-
ride ingress from exposure to seawater or deicing salt. When a critical chloride content
threshold is reached at the reinforcement, pitting corrosion will occur, and the steel will
lose strength. For structures located in an aggressive environment, a minimum concrete
quality and cover layer to protect the reinforcement from chlorides are required to fulfill
the service life. For reinforced concrete structures, the service life can be further ex-
tended by additional initiatives, for example by use of water-repellent impregnation of
the concrete.
The effect of impregnating the concrete has been investigated and developed for many
years, both by laboratory tests and by field tests, but for impregnating concrete with age
younger than 28 days, the literature somewhat reveals very little information. However,
impregnating the young concrete is relevant to investigate as it might be used in large
projects with a tight time schedule. The impregnation might conveniently lead to early
resistance to chloride ingress and also to the easier application of the impregnation in
the construction phase compared to later on.
Despite the numerous investigations on impregnating concrete, the exact effects with
varying different parameters and conditions are not entirely known. The list of various
impregnation products offered is endless and may generates confusion due to complex
chemical compositions. Furthermore, the composition of the concrete mix and the ap-
plication method influences the water-repellent effect. Without any initial familiarity
with impregnation, it is not straightforward to identify which products are better for
the individual situation and which influencing parameters are more important to obtain
the highest effectiveness.
World-wide the use of impregnation is increasing [WACKER, 2018], and many examples
are found in Sweden and Norway. Yet in Denmark, where the resistance towards using
unfamiliar methods and products where no experience has been gained is persistent, the
use of impregnation in the construction industry is not common.

1.1
Motivation for this project
In the construction industry, climate changes have induced a major focus on reducing the
consumption of materials. Especially this involves cement, which is responsible for ap-
proximately 8 % of the world’s CO2 release [Royal Institute of International Affairs, 2018].
Water-repellent impregnation has for a long time been used for materials such as tex-
tiles and wood in daily life. Concrete, on the other hand, is often considered resisting
water itself because it can withstand water without serious deterioration for a very long
time. However, water will still during a certain period penetrate into the concrete and
potentially transport other soluble ions, such as chlorides. The intent of impregnating
concrete is to hinder the chloride ingress and in this way, delay the deterioration and
thereby the need for restoration or renewal.
The impregnation can also be applied to reduce the concrete cover layer, which will lead
to a reduction of the needed amount of concrete already during construction.
The motivation for this project is to provide more specific knowledge on the use of
water-repellent treatment of the young concrete to resist chloride ingress for a realistic
large marine concrete structure.
By testing a young concrete with mix proportions
corresponding to this kind of structure, the results will become more relevant to the
industry. With inspiration from conditions and material considerations for the Femern
project, it will be examined if impregnation applied early could be used to either prolong
the service life or to reduce the cover layer in a concrete structure. In continuation, it is of
interest to find the most optimal application process allowing for treatment of the young
concrete structure in a tight time frame before exposure to an aggressive environment.
1.2
Objectives
The main scope of this Master’s thesis is to examine and assess the effect of impregnat-
ing a young concrete through laboratory experiments.
The objectives are to find:
• The optimal concrete age for application of impregnation to achieve the highest
resistance to chloride ingress.
• The optimal concrete age for application of impregnation to achieve the greatest
impregnation penetration depth.
• A correlation between the penetration depth of water-repellent surface treatment
and chloride ingress.
• The most effective type of impregnation (two surface treatments and one integral
water-repellent treatment) regarding resistance to chloride ingress.

CHAPTER 3
Literature study
In this section, a relevant extract from the attached State-of-the-Art (STAR) The Effect
of Silane-based Surface Impregnation Before Exposure on the Durability of Concrete is
presented. The aim of the STAR was to gather information and experience on the effects
of hydrophobic treatment on concrete. The focus is on silane-based products since these
are the most commonly used. To restrict the study, only water-repellent treatment of
newly constructed intact structures to prevent water transport, rather than repair sys-
tems on existing and cracked structures, was considered. In this extract, the overall
conclusions and tendencies found in the STAR are presented even though different cir-
cumstances may apply in this project where the concrete mix including w/c and cement
type, application method and conditions, water-repellent product, and test methods are
not similar. The conclusions from the STAR are mainly for concretes older than 28
maturity days.
Resistance against chloride ingress
The majority of the numerous tests executed and presented in Table C.2 in Appendix
C shows a reduction in chloride ingress for the various impregnation products tested in
both the laboratory and the field. The effect highly depends on exposure conditions and
properties of both concrete and impregnation product. However, the chloride ingress
significantly reduces as a result of the treatments in almost all presented cases and es-
pecially for porous specimens. [Liu et al., 2005] states that this tendency appears as an
effect from the impregnation penetration depth.
Impregnation penetration depth
For the penetration depth of surface treatment, the three most important influencing
factors are the time of contact between the concrete surface and water-repellent product,
porosity and degree of saturation of the concrete element [Johansson et al., 2007]. The
penetration occurs fastest immediately after application where the viscosity of the silane
is always low because the degree of polymerisation is low [Gerdes et al., 1998]. From Ta-
ble C.4 in Appendix C, it is generally found that the impregnation penetrates deeper
with increasing porosity, increasing active content and decreasing moisture content at
the time of the treatment application.
Cement types and additives
Only limited available literature was found for the effect of cement types and additives
on the water-repellent treatment. [Vries, 1997] tests two different mix compositions with

penetration depth of the impregnation than CEM I 32.5 R, but no clear tendency of

Surface texture
Considering the effect from concrete surface texture, [Liu et al., 2005] tests the effect
on the penetration depth of impregnation applied after 3 and 7 maturity days for three
different surface textures. The impregnation penetration depth is highest for the top
surface (covered by plastic) compared to the mould and cut surfaces where the impreg-
nation penetration depth is similar. [Vries, 1997] agrees with these findings through
tests for concrete with both finished and formwork surface. In particular, the penetra-
tion depth of the hydrophobic product is deeper in finished surfaces than in formwork
surfaces. In majority of literature in Table C.4 in Appendix C the specimens are washed
thoroughly before the application according to manufactures recommendations. How-
ever, [Hosoda et al., 2010] says that only a little influence from the mould release agent
on the impregnation penetration depth and water absorption controlling effect is found.
Therefore it is stated that no special considerations about concrete surface treatment
before surface impregnation is necessary for newly constructed structures.
Moisture conditions
Literature with very few exceptions agrees that a lower relative humidity and thereby
a lower moisture content in the surface near zone of the concrete results in a higher
impregnation penetration depth regardless of both w/c and temperature.
However,
the moisture content in concrete at the time of treatment does not always seem to af-
fect the actual performance of the impregnation in reducing the water- and chloride
ingress. [Basheer et al., 1998] impregnates concrete specimens under different concrete
conditions to simulate wet climate, long dry spell, and arid conditions.
It was con-
cluded that the moisture content in the concrete at the time of application of treatment
does not have a significant effect on the reduction of chloride ingress in treated concrete.
[Liu et al., 2005], however, does not agree on the non-existing effect. For a relatively dry
concrete substrate, the hydrophobic surface treatment provided very efficient protection
of the early age concrete against chloride ingress. For a higher relative humidity, the ef-
fect is less pronounced. For a very wet concrete substrate, the ability of the hydrophobic
surface treatment to protect the concrete significantly reduces. [Liu et al., 2005] states
that this effect appears because the impregnation penetration depth is more shallow for
a more moist concrete at the application time.

Concrete age for application of impregnation
Generally, it is found that young concrete can be just as successfully impregnated as
older concrete. Related to this, and the tests performed regarding moisture conditions,
a water-repellent treatment can be successfully applied to a young concrete, if the satu-
ration of the pores is low enough. However, it is preferable to postpone the application
of a water-repellent treatment as long as possible, to minimize its impact on the hy-
dration process [Meier and Wittmann, 2001]. Contrary, [Hosoda et al., 2010] finds that
the penetration depth of the treatment increases with the age, and states that espe-
cially after 7 days it should be easier for impregnation to penetrate the concrete but
recommends to postpone the application to after the age of approximately 14 days to
low w/cs concrete (0.35 to 0.42), so that a larger penetration depth can be obtained. In
that relation, [Liu et al., 2005] finds that the impregnation penetration depth becomes
almost the same by applying the treatment after 3 or 7 maturity days.
Chemical composition of water-repellent product
The chemical reactivity and the size of the water-repellent molecules both affect the
effective penetration depth of the product. [Pan et al., 2017] states that the structure
of the alkoxy group (such as ethoxy or methoxy) is related to how silane bonds to the
concrete surface and thereby the penetration depth. [Johansson et al., 2007] finds that
silanes with methoxy groups react significantly faster than those with ethoxy groups,
which results in lower impregnation penetration depths. [Büttner and Raupach, 2008]
find the contrary when they examine the impregnation depth in concrete treated with
four different water-repellent products. There was not found any direct link between the
size of the molecules and the impregnation penetration depth observed. Furthermore,
no significant difference of the impregnation penetration depth is found when using two
hydrophobic products featuring two completely different organic residues. Lastly, the
impregnation penetration depth increased for an increase in the active content and the
contact time. Which factor is the dominant one, is a question that still needs an answer.
Texture of impregnation
The main aim of applying creams or gels is to ascertain a long contact time between the
concrete surface and the silanes [Meier and Wittmann, 2011]. Cream-based hydrophobic
treatments have a longer drying time relative to liquid systems, and this usually results
in superior penetration efficiency, and thus higher protective potential for the cream
[Sohawon and Beushausen, 2018]. It is difficult to impregnate the surface of concrete
and to reach a sufficient impregnation penetration depth with a w/c ≤0.5, in this case,
it is recommended to use pastes, creams or gels [Meier and Wittmann, 2011]. Addition-
ally, cream- and gel products are used as this is easier to handle on-site to minimize the
high risk of spillage and evaporation of the impregnation product, and there is also a
reduced waste from dripping or evaporation [Syed and Donadio, 2013].
Polymerisation of surface treatment
The rate of polymerisation depends on the moisture content, the temperature, and the

pH-value of the pore solution and also on the molecular structure of the water-repellent
product [Meier and Wittmann, 2011]. The optimum curing time of the impregnation
before exposure to a critical external environment is important since it is clear that the
polymerisation rate of the water-repellent product affects the impregnation penetration
depth [Johansson et al., 2007]. A more extended polymerisation period leads to a better
performing water-repellent treatment. As mentioned in [Büttner and Raupach, 2008]

Contact angle between concrete and water
Regarding the contact angle, the results from the tests found in the literature are pre-
sented in Table C.3 in Appendix C. It is seen that the surface treatments provide a
contact angle larger than > 90° for all cases. Several different methods are used to
determine the contact angle including Drop Shape Analysis System and CDD camera
where a computer-assisted syringe places the water drops.
Integral impregnation
Integral impregnation has not been included in the above conclusions since it is a newer
product type, and only limited literature is found.
It would appear to be more ef-
ficient and convenient to treat the bulk of the construction material itself instead of
applying a water-repellent post-treatment that requires complicated in-situ preparation
and, furthermore, climatic weathering influences it [Milenković et al., 2014]. From the
limited literature available, it is found that the silane-based admixture can reduce the
ingress of water and chloride considerably depending on the w/c. However, a reduc-
tion in strength was documented for several products. It was possible to show that
the integral water-repellent product influences the cement hydration process. However,
[Milenković et al., 2014] showed that porosity and pore structure in cement matrix does
not change due to the presence of silane emulsion.

4 Theory
4.1
Concrete as a material
Concrete is the most widely used engineering material, and for this, there are several
reasons. First, concrete possesses good resistance to water. Secondly, it can be shaped
into a variety of shapes and sizes and, thirdly, when considering the achievable compres-
sive strength, it is usually the cheapest and most readily available material. Concrete
consists of cement, water, and aggregates, that are materials available in most parts of
the world. Each of which affects the development and final properties of the concrete.
4.1.1
Cement types
The clinker composition varies intentionally for different types of cement to achieve
specific properties. In this report, the focus will be on Low-alkali Sulphate Resistant
Cement and Ground Granulated Blast-furnace Slag Cement.
Low-alkali cement is characterized by a lower content of C4AF and C3A. Sulphate and
C3A form expansive reactions and therefore the amount of C3A is of great importance
when creating sulphate resistant cement. C4AF is more resistance to sulphate than C3A,
but not to the same extent as C3S and C2S. This cement type has been frequently used
in Denmark for civil structures due to the low alkali content, which hinders the occur-
rence of alkali-silica reactions. Even though the control of reactive siliceous aggregates
is improved, it is still commonly used because of the experience and well-documented use.
Portland slag cement typically contains 30 to 70 % slag, and the remaining part con-
sists of Portland cement. Slag cement has better resistance to sulphates than ordinary
cement as well due to a lower total content of aluminates. Ground granulated blast-
furnace slag is itself cementing; however, the rate of hydration is insufficient for struc-
tural use by itself. When used in combination with Portland cement, the hydration of
slag is accelerated due to the presence of calcium hydroxide [Mehta and Monteiro, 2006].
The rates of hydration and strength development for slag cement are slower up to 28
maturity days after casting compared to an ordinary Portland cement; however, the
differences usually disappear hereafter when they achieve a similar degree of hydration
[Mehta and Monteiro, 2006]. Additionally, the pore refinement process from the reac-
tion between slag, water, and calcium hydroxide increases the final strength and lowers
the permeability. However, it has to be emphasised that the extent of these advanta-
geous effects varies since the exact characteristics of a cement containing slag varies.
Blast-furnace slag is a by-product of iron extraction from oxide ores; granulated slag
is the glassy or noncrystalline product which is formed when molten blast-furnace slag
is rapidly chilled, as by immersion in water. Since the slag is a by-product, the char-
acteristics vary, but the chemical composition of the slag typically contains 30 to 40
% SiO2, 10 to 20 % Al2O3 and 30 to 50 % CaO. Additionally, it can contain smaller
amounts of magnesium oxides, calcium sulphide, manganese oxide, iron oxide, metallic
iron, plus alkali components. The only common clinker mineral with portland cement
is C2S [Aalborg Portland, 1985]. Although particle size characteristics, the composition
==PAGE:29==

of glass, and the glass content are primary factors determining the reactivity of slags, it
may be noted that the reactivity of the glass itself varies with the thermal history.
Cement can during production be ground to a specific fineness. The reaction of cement
with water takes place on the surface of the cement grains and therefore proceeds faster
for a higher fineness. However, the cost of grinding and the heat evolved on hydration
set some limits on the fineness. The fineness is for quality control purposes determined
from sieving to obtain the particle size distribution, but it is common practice in the
industry to obtain a relative measure given by the Blaine surface area. The higher the
Blaine surface area, the finer the grains.
The fineness is furthermore related to the packing of the cement particles. Between
single cement grains electrostatic surface forces impact which hinder a free packing of
the grains. This tendency means that cement with high fineness is more loosely packed,
and thereby has a lower density than a more coarse cement. Therefore the water demand
to achieve a specific consistency increases with the fineness [Aalborg Portland, 1985]. For
two similar concretes, the one containing cement with a higher Blaine fineness will have
a lower density and a lower slump. Compared to ordinary Portland cement, slag cement
is often ground to finer particle sizes to compensate partly for the reduction of early
strength.

is low, the cement paste will have high capillary porosity and will contain a relatively
large number of big and well-connected pores resulting in the coefficient of permeability
to be high. The permeability is defined as the ease with which fluid under pressure can
flow through a solid. As hydration progresses, most of the pores will be reduced in size
and will lose their interconnections, and the permeability will drop. The discontinuity
in the capillary network is generally reached after 3 maturity days of moist curing for a
concrete with w/c = 0.4 [Mehta and Monteiro, 2006].
With the progress of hydration, as the capillary porosity decreases, so does the co-
efficient of permeability, but there is no direct proportionality between the two.
At
the beginning of the hydration, even a small decrease in the total capillary porosity
is associated with a considerable reduction of large pores. The permeability of a fully
hydrated cement paste may be of the order of 106 times less than that of a young paste
[Mehta and Monteiro, 2006].

4.1.3
Compressive Strength
An important property of concrete is the compressive strength which is the highest nu-
merical value of the nominal normal stress in a specimen subjected to one-dimensional
compression until crushing [Aalborg Portland, 1985].
Parameters affecting the compressive strength are w/c, hydration degree, air content,
pozzolan content, admixtures, the type of aggregates, and the aggregates grain size dis-
tribution. As mentioned previously, the compressive strength increases during the hy-
dration process when the reactions involving silicates proceed. Normally, the strength is
tested after 28 maturity days, since the majority of the strength has been gained at this
point for concrete with ordinary cement and no addition of pozzolans. E.g., concrete
containing cement with pozzolan or other additives and admixtures, the strength devel-
opment may be retarded, and therefore, the final strength can be significantly higher
than after 28 maturity days.
4.2
Transport of moisture in concrete
Moisture transport, as water vapor or liquid water, is not always the main reason for
the deterioration of concrete. However, it is one of the most critical parameters for the
rate of degradation progress, especially when dissolved materials as salt are transported
by moisture. The atoms in a water molecule are held together by covalent bonds while
the water molecules are held together by hydrogen bonds which is much weaker and
relatively easy to affect or break.
Transport of moisture will occur if the internal humidity in the concrete is not in equilib-
rium with the surroundings to reach a state of equilibrium. In this project, the primary
transport mechanisms occurring will be capillary suction, diffusion, and advection. If
the relative humidity is below the magnitude of 65 to 70 %, the only transport mecha-
nism will be water vapor diffusion as illustrated in Figure 4.3, where the total amount

4.2.1
Adsorption
In the hygroscopic range, water and ions can be physically bound in the cement paste
through adsorption of water molecules on the pore surface or through surface tension
effects causing capillary condensation [Selander, 2010]. Adsorption dominates at low
moisture levels (approximately up to 50 % RH) and capillary condensation at high
(approximately above 50 % RH). The adsorbed water molecules are bound temporarily
by a range of physical effects, e.g., Van der Waal forces. The capacity of the concrete to
adsorb ions will be proportional to the cement content since it is in the paste (binding
on the aluminate phase) it is bound. For chlorides, there is a good link between the
number of chloride ions in a solution and the adsorbed amount, but it will not be a
completely linear relationship.

4.2.5
Ingress of chlorides
The transport of moisture in concrete can occur with or without dissolved materials
such as salt.
For chloride ions, it requires a continuous water phase into the pore
system [Selander, 2010]. In a dry or partially dry concrete chloride is transported in
water by capillary suction until saturation is reached and the dissolved chloride ions are
transported by diffusion. The maximum rate of chloride diffusion is reached at fully
saturation, and below 50 % RH, it is close to zero. Fick’s law presented in Equation

in the formulas. Average seawater holds the presence of about 3.5 % soluble salts by
mass [Mehta and Monteiro, 2006]. However, the use of deicing salts in combination with
water from, e.g., rain can result in exposure to even higher concentrations of salt.

4.3
Silanes
Hydrophobic treatments are used to change the surface tension of concrete to keep water
and thereby aggressive water-soluble salts, for example, chlorides, out. Some impregna-
tion types also have additional properties such as changing the vapour transmission and
air permeability.
The most commonly used hydrophobic impregnation consists of silanes, siloxanes and a
mixture of both. Silanes are the smallest molecules, and can generally penetrate further
into the concrete than the larger siloxane molecules [Pincheira and Dorshorst, 2005]. In
this project, only silane-based impregnation is examined.
The silane-based surface treatment is defined as a hydrophobic impregnation. It pene-
trates concrete pores, polymerises and enlarges the contact angle to more than 90°allow-
ing water vapor to enter or exit [Pan et al., 2017] but inhibiting penetration of water
and water-born ions. When applied to concrete surfaces silanes react rapidly with the
silicates of the cement paste [Tepfers, 2009] and result in silicon resins that are chemi-
cally bound to the concrete base [Medeiros and Helene, 2008].
In this section, the structure and properties for two different silane-based impregnation
types, Iso-octyltriethoxysilane, and Iso-butyltriethoxysilane, will be covered.

size of only 1.0· 10−6 to 1.5· 10−6 mm in diameter [Medeiros and Helene, 2008]. Silanes

adsorbed by stable bonds formed by condensation of the hydroxyl groups through water
elimination [Moriconi et al., 2002]. The alkalinity of the concrete acts as a catalyst for
this reaction [Pan et al., 2017].

4.3.3
Integral treatment
One of the newest developed water-repellent treatments is a type mixed into the fresh
concrete during casting. By having relatively thin construction elements, it would ap-
pear to be more effective and convenient to treat the construction element during casting.
By postponing the application exposure to climatic weathering [Milenković et al., 2014]
and extra work hours for application which has to be planned and paid are prevented.
For water-repellent admixture, silanes are also often used. Due to their poor water sol-
ubility, they are often pretreated in order to make their dispersion into the concrete
mixture more effective [Moriconi et al., 2002].
According to [Moriconi et al., 2002] the addition of admixture can result in prolonged
setting time and loss of mechanical strength since the hydrophobic products can interact
with the cement hydration mechanism by hindering contact between water and cement
particles. Additionally, the water-repellent effectiveness can decrease with time due to
the growth of cement hydration products, which cover the surface of the water-repellent
molecules [Moriconi et al., 2002].
4.3.4
Hydrophobic effect
When a water-repellent surface treatment is applied to the concrete, it penetrates and
covers the capillary walls [Sohawon and Beushausen, 2018]. The silane molecules bond
to the capillary walls, and the concrete surface becomes devoid of ionic electrical charges
and polar molecules. This results in a change in surface tension, and thereby contact
angle since the silanes weaken the molecular attraction between water and concrete
[Sohawon and Beushausen, 2018]. The contact angle determines the ability of the sur-
face to expel water [Moriconi et al., 2002] and therefore the ability to inhibit penetration
of water and water-born ions [Pan et al., 2017]. The term hydrophobic effect does in this
report include the impregnation penetration depth and the resulting change in contact
angle.
When water comes in contact with a porous material, capillary forces are developed
according to the Washburn equation as presented in Equation (4.1). By applying a
surface treatment, the contact angle between water and the treated surface will increase
changing the negative pressure and thereby reduces the capillary rise.
According to [Aalborg Portland, 1985] a material will be called hydrophilic and become
wetted by water if θ < 90° whereas if θ > 90° the material is called hydrophobic and
the water will not wet the material. When the contact angle is enlarged to θ > 90°
a convex meniscus will occur. Subsequently, a pressure acting opposite will occur in
the capillary pores meaning that the level of liquid in the pores will be lower than that
of the surrounding liquid [Sohawon and Beushausen, 2018]. Figure 4.13 illustrates this

6.2
Casting of concrete
All the tests in this project require concrete specimens cast with the same procedure.
An elaborated experiment description is given in Appendix B.3.
After adjusting the mix water according to Appendix B.2, the final mix design was
ready.
The materials were weighed in separate buckets and readied near the mixer.
Equipment for the slump test and air content measurement was readied as well. First,
the dry materials were added to the mixer in the following order: all the crushed stones,
half of the sand, the cement and, finally, the rest of the sand. The dry materials were
mixed for 30 seconds, and the water was then added through the lid of the mixer, while
the mixing continued. The mixer was stopped 2.5 minutes after addition of water to
manually scratch away concrete from the sides followed by another 2.5 minutes of mixing
by the machine. When the water-repellent product was added as an admixture, it was
mixed with the mixing water just before the water was added to the dry materials. The
slump was measured for the fresh mix based on DS/EN 12350-2 according to Appendix
B.5 followed by determination of the air content based on C231/C231M-14 according to
Appendix B.4.
The concrete specimens were cast with 4 cylindrical moulds or a box mould, all lubricated
by mould grease, at one time. The cylinders have the dimensions Ø100x200mm, and
the box has the dimensions shown in Figure 6.3. The cylinders were filled half and
vibrated on the vibration table at 50 Hz until most of the air was released and water
appeared on the surface (assessed visually). This procedure was repeated to fill the
cylinder, whereafter the top was covered either by plastic or by a lid slid on in a sawing
motion and fastened with a clamp. The box was first filled 1/3 by concrete mix and
vibrated in the same way as the cylinders. This procedure was repeated after filling the
box 2/3. The box was then filled by a final layer, and then vibrated with a concrete
needle vibrator until just before water appeared on the surface.
All the moulds containing concrete were placed as illustrated in Figure 6.4 and 6.5, and
stored in a room with approximately 20 °C. The specimens were demoulded three days
after casting and located in a climate room with a temperature of 20 °C and relative
humidity of 85 %.

Exposure conditions

the chloride ingress depth can be measured, and from titration, the more detailed chlo-

6.6.2
Potentiometric titration
A widespread more accurate method found in the literature is to obtain and compare
the chloride profiles from, e.g., potentiometric titration. From this method, detailed
information on the chloride content throughout the specimens are obtained, where both
free and bound chlorides are included after chemical processing. For the determination
of the chloride content in a concrete powder sample, taken from the milling according
to Appendix B.14, the more time consuming potentiometric chloride titration was used
based on DS/EN 14629.
First, dust samples were milled out in specific intervals determined based on the visual
chloride ingress, as illustrated in Figure 6.22. The concrete dust sample was weighed
before and after 24 hours in the oven at 105 °C to obtain the dry sample mass. The
chloride ions were dissolved by adding acid and hot water to the dry sample only inter-
rupted by shaking. The solutions were filtered into beakers, as shown in Figure 6.23,
followed by the addition of 1 ml 0,1 M NaCl solution to ensure that the concentration
of chloride ion was high enough.

CHAPTER 7
Results
In this chapter, the most relevant results from the outlined experiments are presented
and commented. Initially, results from testing the concrete mix properties including
slump, air content, density with and without integral product have been determined.
Subsequently, results describing the behaviour of the water-repellent treatment in the
concrete and the effect on the ingress of chlorides provided by the different treatments
are introduced. Raw-data can be found in Appendix J.2 at the USB handed out.
Working with concrete mixes leads to several unavoidable uncertainties, which will influ-
ence the results. First, concrete mixes will not be completely homogeneous. Secondly,
the temperature in the laboratory and the mixing water will be fluctuating. Generally
will experiments with building materials not be executed under completely controlled
circumstances which have to be taken into account evaluating the results.
Generally, all specimens have been treated similarly, but some specimens have been
outside the climate room longer than others during the surface preparation. This devia-
tion in the preparation process can have a significant influence on the moisture content
when applying surface treatment resulting in different moisture content for the different
specimens treated. In the literature, it is found that the moisture content is crucial for
the impregnation penetration depth and the performance of resisting chloride ingress.
Based on this knowledge and the deviation in the preparation process, there is a risk of
misleading results. It was of course considered to examine the moisture content before
application of a surface treatment, but it was too complicated to include in the test
program.
In the following sections, systematic naming and colouring presented in Section 5.1 and
on the unfolding poster in Appendix A are used to distinguish between the different
combinations of concrete mixes and specimens. It is strongly recommended to unfold
the poster before continuing, and to keep it unfold while reading the following sections.

Discussion
In this section, the results from the different experiments will be compared, assessed,
and discussed. Overall, the aim is to evaluate the effect on chloride ingress when varying
several different conditions from impregnating a young concrete produced with mixes
similar to marine structures. Additionally, the development of the hydrophobic proper-
ties of the concrete after the application of surface treatment is outlined.
Initially, the mix properties and mechanical properties are discussed focusing on the in-
fluence of cement type and water-repellent admixture. Then, the hydrophobic effect on
the concrete from surface impregnating a very young concrete is assessed when varying
the treatment procedure.
For the specimens exposed to saturated saline water at atmospheric pressure, the meth-
ods for determining and comparing chloride ingress are evaluated, followed by a com-
parison of the effect on chloride ingress from the different treatment products, including
the influence of the amount of Faceal RF. Next, parameters influencing on the chloride
ingress are discussed. The optimal concrete age for application of impregnation is as-
sessed involving the influence of cement type. Simultaneously, the correlation between
chloride profiles and impregnation penetration depth is examined.
Subsequently, the acceleration of the test is discussed and, lastly, the effect of the treat-
ment on cylinders exposed to saturated saline water at 4 bar water pressure is illustrated
and the evaluation method is discussed.
The sources of error and uncertainties mentioned in Section 7 will also be discussed
throughout this section.

8.2
Compressive strength
It was seen that the concrete containing slag cement has a lower 28-days compressive
strength than the concrete containing low-alkali cement from Figure 7.4 in Section 7.2.
This finding may be attributed to the slower hydration of slag cement, which has not
been entirely balanced by a higher Blaine fineness. If the fineness had been as for the
low-alkali cement, the hydration might have developed even slower.
The addition of admixture reduced the compressive strength by 17.2 % and 19.2 % for
concrete containing slag cement and low-alkali cement, respectively. In [Wacker, 2014]
the compressive strength of a reference specimen is measured to be 45.2 MPa, which by
the addition of water-repellent admixture equal to 0.2 weight-% of the cement reduces
to 39.1 MPa. The reduction in compressive strength found by Wacker on 13 % is less
than found in this project. The reduction in compressive strength is an essential consid-
eration when using the product for a load-bearing structure.
It is important to remember that the reduction in 28-days compressive strength for
the specimens with water-repellent admixture, not necessarily results in a reduction in
the final compressive strength. The admixture may, in fact, only delay the strength
development by its hydrophobic effect. This explanation can be examined further by
a more thorough investigation, for example, by SEM analysis where it is possible to
observe the clinker and how much they have reacted. [Milenković et al., 2014] found in
a SEM analysis that the hydrophobic alkyl group in n-octylethoxysilane hindered the re-
action between C3S and water, and thereby retarded the formation of calcium hydroxide.

8.5
Assessment of chloride profiles
A more detailed description of the ingress of chlorides is obtained from the chloride
profiles presented in Section 7.5.2. For comparing the chloride profiles with each other,
both the shape of the profiles and the chloride content at various depths from the surface
can be evaluated. It is, however, beneficial to be able to compare the chloride resistance

First, the diffusion coefficients are discussed, and next, the depths, where a specified

8.5.1
Diffusion coefficients
Normally, the extended service life is predicted in the industry from the diffusion coeffi-
cient. In this report, it was aimed to do the same to compare the resistance to chlorides.
It was more difficult than expected to determine the different diffusion coefficients be-
cause of the subjectivity of the method.
A curve based on Fick’s second law is fitted to the obtained chloride profiles by changing
the relevant values; the surface chloride content and the diffusion coefficient. Regarding
the determined initial chloride content in Table 7.2, the high standard deviations might
be reduced by determining the initial chloride ingress in specimens not exposed to saline
water.
Normally, the best fit is found by determining the diffusion coefficient which results in
the lowest sum of differences in the power of 2 between the concentration according
to the fitted curve formula and the actual concentration in each interval. The ”best
fit” turned out to be far from perfect assessing the fitted graph and the actual graph
visually. Instead, the diffusion coefficient and surface concentration were adjusted until
a reasonably fitted graph was achieved. However, several good fits from this method
could be found, and they all led to different diffusion coefficients. An example in Figure

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
Through experimental research, the results have been examined and evaluated, and the
main findings in this section are presented in the same order as the objectives presented
in Section 1.2.
The results show that a higher impregnation penetration depth can be achieved by post-
poning the application of impregnation for most cases. The tendency may be because
the cement-water hydration process is continued resulting in reduction of free water and
thereby giving space inside the capillary pores for the impregnation. From the obtained
chloride profiles, the concrete age for application of impregnation seems to be of minor
importance, which may be beneficial for the contractors.
Generally, the impregnation penetration depth increases for concrete having higher ma-
turity, where it is only in some cases that the resistance to chlorides is slightly improved.
This implies that a clear correlation between the chloride ingress and impregnation depth
is not found.
A reduction of the chloride ingress was found for all types of impregnation. The dif-
ference in effect between the treatment with either Creme C and Faceal RF was not
significant. Creme C was expected to perform better since it has a higher active content,
higher molecular weight, and longer contact time than Faceal RF. The water-repellent
admixture treatment added during mixing of the concrete did also provide good resis-
tance to chloride ingress, but the compressive strength after 28 days was reduced by
approximately 15 to 20 %. This is a crucial consideration when using the product for a
load-bearing structure.
The effect of using either one or two cross-layers of Faceal RF was not measurable for
a w/c = 0.4 regarding both the impregnation penetration depth and chloride ingress.
It was found that the application of one layer of Faceal RF is as sufficient as Creme C.
However, it is not known if the second cross-layer has a positive long term effect on the
durability of the surface treatments.
Regarding the resistance against chloride ingress, both surface and integral impregna-
tion types are more effective for concrete containing slag cement compared to concrete
containing low-alkali cement. The difference in effectiveness may be explained by the
slower development of the pore system for the concrete containing slag cement.
Overall, the tests gave inconclusive results regarding the effect of surface treatment on

chloride ingress when exposed to water pressure equal to 4 bar. The path of the chloride
ingress was challenging to identify, which may be because the solution penetrates the
specimens by advection. The surface tension of the concrete is less governing for advec-
tion compared to capillary suction.
By assessing the obtained chloride profiles, the diffusion coefficient was at first deter-
mined by use of the error function. It turned out to be more complicated than expected
and furthermore the influence of the impregnated layer is not considered using Fick’s
second law. Therefore an alternative method was used where the depth reaching the
specified threshold content of chloride were compared. By this method, information
about the chloride ingress development is omitted, but it turned out to be the least
subjective method next to comparing the shape of the different chloride profiles, which
is also done. The different evaluation methods for chloride ingress all have strengths
and weaknesses, but it was found that the best method depends on the performed ex-
periment and the required level of information, such as the chloride profile to obtain a
quantitative result or ingress path for relative comparison.
The effects and tendencies found from the accelerated experiments using saturated saline
water did not agree with the findings from using 3 % saline water. No effect from the
impregnation was found which may be due to the short exposure period.
For very young treated concrete specimens not exposed to saline water, no clear tenden-
cies were found between the curing time of impregnation and the average impregnation
penetration depth nor between the concrete age for application and average impregna-
tion penetration depth. However, for most cases, the average impregnation penetration
depth is lower for the specimens treated after 7 maturity days compared to the ones
treated after 4 maturity days.
It was found that the impregnation penetration depth for a very young concrete was
higher for a finished surface compared to a mould surface. For obtaining or document-
ing a high measured impregnation penetration depth, it can generally be recommended
to apply the impregnation to the finished concrete surface, which is more porous. It was
desired to evaluate the change of the contact angle, but the method used turned out to
be too inaccurate and complicated.
The research results is used to estimate an elongation in structure service life or a reduc-
tion in the required cover layer by using hydrophobic impregnation for concrete.
It is in this limited project difficult to achieve a complete overview of the effect of
impregnating a high-quality concrete exposed to saline environments in terms of how
and when it should be applied in relation to type, curing, and amount of impregnation,
the concrete’s maturity, the type of concrete and its surface texture. It is, however,
clear that impregnated concrete may have a significantly improved resistance to chloride
ingress in the early phase.

CHAPTER 11
Future Work
The short project period forced some limitations to the configuration of experiments
beforehand, and during the analyses of the results, several issues have given rise to
questions and future improvement and investigations. In this section, some suggestions
for future work are presented.
• It has been stated that the moisture content is important and one of the parameters
affecting the ingress of impregnation. Therefore it is of interest to examine the
exact effect more thoroughly.
• The used method in this project to determine the contact angle turned out to be
very doubtful. It is therefore of interest to measure the contact angle by a more
accurate method to examine the development of differently impregnated specimens.
Alternatively, a method to determine the surface tension could be used.
• The impregnation penetration depth is in this project determined by measuring the
distance to the visible front. It is of interest to find a more thorough examination
to achieve a more detailed profile of the ingress as for the chloride profiles. If this
is possible, it will be easier to compare the ingress of impregnation and chloride.
Furthermore, this method would maybe also help to understand the found effect
of reducing the amount of Faceal RF.
• The findings from the accelerated experiment using saturated saline water and
the one using only 3 % saline water were inconsistent. It is therefore of interest
to examine the effect by exposing the specimens to 3 % saline water for a longer
period.
• The focus in this project has mainly been on the effect of impregnation regarding
resistance against chloride ingress. It is important to have in mind that the findings
might be different when examining the effect on other deterioration mechanisms.
It is, for example, found that all types of impregnation reduce the chloride ingress
and that the cover layer, therefore possibly can be reduced. For other deteriora-
tion mechanisms, this might not be the case, which therefore needs to be further
investigated.
The items mentioned above are mainly for the effect of the impregnation. During the
work with different impregnation types, several issues and concerns were raised regarding
their interaction with concrete. Suggestions for further work examining this is presented
in the following.
==PAGE:116==
102
11 Future Work
• A more thorough examination of the polymerisation of surface impregnation, when
applied to a very young concrete, is of interest to better understand which circum-
stances and conditions can be changed to achieve the best effect. It was for example
found that in some cases the impregnation penetration depth into very young con-
crete reduced when increasing the impregnation curing time. By SEM analysis
it is possible to investigate if the reason for this is hydration products forming
and covering the hydrophobic molecules and thereby hindering the water-repellent
property.
• By using admixture, it was found that the compressive strength after 28 days was
reduced. It is therefore of interest to examine the influence of the admixture during
hydration. This interaction can be investigated further by a thorough investigation
with more tests, including SEM analysis, where it is possible to observe the clinker
and how much they have reacted.
• Relating to the experiments with exposure to water pressure, it is of interest to
test if the method can be improved. Furthermore, if the same findings would be
seen if the concrete was older or more hydrated when exposed to water pressure.

B Experiments
B.1
Mix design for concrete
In this project two different concrete mix designs are used for the test specimens. The
materials used are described in Section 5.2. The process of determining the mix design
is:
1. Choose w/c.
2. Choose maximum stone size Dmax.
3. Choose slump.
According to [Aalborg Portland, 1985] the concrete consistency classes are for
slumps on 30-60 mm, 60-100 mm and 100-150 mm termed rigid plastic, plastic
and viscous, respectively.
4. Read the required water content mw of from (Tab. 8, Chap 6 in [Munch-Petersen, 2013])
by using the maximum stone size and the slump aimed for.
5. Calculate cement content mc = mw/(w/c).
6. Determine sand percent from (Tab. 4, Chap 6 in [Munch-Petersen, 2013]), based
on maximum stone size and cement content.
7. Determine the percentage of Rønne granite 4-8 mm and 11-16 mm by considering
the grain size distribution. The grain size distribution of the individual components
are given from the manufacturer and a combined distribution can be found by
guessing the amount of each stone size in percentages of the total amount of
aggregates.
The combined grain size distribution is changed so that it is located in between
the minimum and maximum limits for distribution curves [Hansen, 2016]. The
final combined grain distribution used in the mix design is shown in Figure B.1
along with the individual curves for the sand and stone, and the limit curves.
Figure B.2 shows the sand grain distribution together with the sand maximum and
minimum distribution limits. The sea sand distribution is very near the maximum
distribution limit but is assessed to be fit for use.
8. On the mixing day the mix has to be adjusted for moisture content in the aggre-
gates as described in Appendix B.2.

B Experiments
B.2
Adjustment of water content in mix
Before the mixing is started the moisture contents in the aggregates has to be determined
to adjust the amount of water in the mix. This is done as the first thing when producing
the test specimens.
Equipment
• 3 petri dishes
• Conical flask
• Vibration table
• Microwave oven
• Stirring spoon
• Weight
Chemicals/Materials
• Sea sand 0-4 mm
• Rønne granite stones 4-8 mm
• Rønne granite stones 11-16 mm
• Demineralised water
The procedure for determining the moisture in the aggregates is as follows:
1. Weight a petri dish for each aggregate size and note it down as mdish.
2. Extract representative samples of sand and stones and fill into the petri dishes.
By a representative specimen is meant a specimen that is not taken from the top
of the container. The upper layer may have a different moisture content than the
rest of the aggregate.
3. Weigh the petri dishes with aggregates and note it down as mmoist.
4. Put the dishes into a microwave oven along with a conical flask of water. The dish
with water prevents the oven from breaking down.
5. The microwave oven is run at full power for 5 minutes.
6. After the 5 minutes the oven is opened and the aggregates are stirred to release
potential trapped moisture. The petri dishes with stones are weighted.
7. Step 5-6 are repeated until each sample has a constant weight and the weight can
be noted down as mdry.
8. Calculate the moisture content u in the stones as shown below.
u = mmoist −mdry
mdry −mdish
· 100%
(B.1)
where u is the moisture content, mmoist is the mass of the in situ sample, mdry is the
mass of the dried sample, and mdish is the mass of the petri dish.
==PAGE:131==
Adjustment of water content in mix
117
The mix design recipe before adjustments lists the density, amount mdesign, and volume
of the components. With the desired amount of mix as a known factor the not adjusted
amount of each sub-component can be listed.
With the previous determined moisture contents of the sand and stone, u0−4, u4−8, and
u11−16, the recipe can now be adjusted. A table is set up in which the following is written
and calculated:
• Expected absorption, uexpected, defined in the data sheet of the aggregate from the
supplier.
• Actual absorption, u, as calculated in Equation (B.1).
• Difference between expected and actual absorption, ∆u = uexpected −u
• Adjusted amount of aggregate, madjusted =
100+u
100+uexpected · mdesign
• Adjusted amount of water, Vadjusted =
∆u
100+uexpected · mdesign
The three Vadjusted are then added together and the sum is the amount of water in need
or in surplus in order to reach the designed amount of water in the mix:
Vadjusted,water = Vadjusted,0−4 + Vadjusted,4−8 + Vadjusted,11−16
(B.2)
If Vadjusted,water is positive extra water have to be added to the mix in order to reach a
state of Saturated Surface Dry (SSD) aggregates. If Vadjusted,water is negative the amount
of water will have to be reduced. The aggregates will absorb water to reach SSD state,
and if the amount of water is not adjusted the w/c-ratio will not be as expected.
Only the content of water, sand, 4-8 stone, and 11-16 stone are affected. The water is
adjusted so that the adjusted amount of water, Vadjusted,water, is added to the original
amount:
madjusted,water = mdesign,water + Vadjusted,water
(B.3)
The final mix design might differ from the following day’s mix design even though the
recipe is the same. This means that all mix designs should have the same fresh mix
properties (air contents, slump flow, setting time, ect.) and as hardened concrete (com-
pressive strength). This applies even though some mix designs had very wet aggregates
while others had very dry. All mix designs used in this project can be found in Ap-
pendix H.1. Appendix H.1 contains tables showing the exact amount of moisture in all
experiments.

Mixing
1. Determine the moisture content in the aggregates as described in B.2 and correct
the proportions of aggregates and water in the mix design accordingly.
2. Weigh aggregates and demineralised water in individual buckets.
3. Weigh the cement (Slag or Low-alkali) and cover it to prevent hydration.
4. Prepare timer, trowels, mixer, moulds, vibration table and equipment for measur-
ing the slump according to Appendix B.5 and air content according to Appendix
B.4.
5. Add all stones and half of the sand in the mixer.
6. Add cement to the mixer followed by the rest of the sand.
7. Mix the dry materials for 30 sec.
8. Add water quickly while the mixing continues. If using water-repellent admixtures
it has to be added to the bucket with demineralised water just before adding the
water to the mixer.

B.15.2
Potentiometric Chloride Titration
For the determination of the chloride content in a concrete powder sample the potentio-
metric chloride titration is used with basis in DS/EN 14629. The potentioetric titration
is used instead of the commonly used Volhard titration is, since it is as or even better
in terms of accuracy.
Potentiometric titration is a titration technique, where no colour indicator is needed.
Instead the potential of the electrolyte solution is measured with an electrode placed in
the solution. The electrode forms an electrochemical half cell with the ions that is going
to be registered.
Equipment

Approach for preparation of the solutions
1. Weigh the conical flask and zero the scale.
2. Weigh ≥5,00 g sample in the conical flask.
3. Dry the sample in the conical flask in an oven at 105 ◦C.
4. Weigh the sample after 24 hours in the oven.
5. Add 20 mL demineralized water in the flask with the sample. Shake.
6. Add 10,0 mL conc. HNO3 to the flask in a fume cupcoard. Shake. The acid is
added by 10,0 mL dispenser. Remember gloves and safety glasses.
7. Add 50 mL approx. 50 ◦C hot water. This is achieved by mixing equal parts of
boiled demineralized water from the kettle and demineralized water from the tap.
Shake.
8. Acid test: Add 1 mL conc. HNO3 using the automatic pipette. Pour a bit of acid
in a small plactic bottle and remove by a pipette. If a buzzing sounds occur it
means that the solution is not acidic enough.
