Abstract
Greenlands supply of electricity and heat is based on hydropower plants in six of the most populous
settlements as well as imported oil products for diesel generators and oil burners in the remaining
more than 60 island energy systems. Ambitious political targets for increased deployment of re-
newable generation are in place and several well-documented renewable resources and technologies
exist. The objective of this thesis is to establish a software architecture enabling a comparison and
prioritisation of options for investments in renewable energy and to apply this to seven relevant
options across the settlements of Nuuk, Nanortalik, Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit. This is achieved
by a combination of energy system modelling in the HOMER software and socio-economic anal-
ysis in a spreadsheet model. The renewable technologies considered are hydropower plants with
reservoirs, solar PV, wind power and battery energy storage. Results show that options to expand
the hydropower capacity of Nuuk consistently outperform all other options on both environmental-
and economic indicators. Best performance is observed for an option to increase the current catch-
ment area via a new tunnel and installation of additional 55 MW turbine capacity, replacing 39
million litres of oil annually (around a ﬁfth of total imports) and generating a NPV of 1.4 billion
DKK at an IRR of 15 % in the base case. For the towns of Nanortalik, Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit
hybrid systems with diﬀerent combinations of PV, wind power and battery storage are found to be
more proﬁtable than competing hydropower options, though providing a lower renewable energy
share. Highest sensitivities are observed towards changes in CAPEX, discount rate and future oil
prices. Important non-monetised eﬀects include environmental impacts, eﬀects on labour markets
and demographic trends within settlement. It is concluded that the presented methodology pro-
vides valuable inputs for evidence-based decision making, enabling prioritisation of investments in
renewable energy.

Introduction
Despite intensiﬁed eﬀorts to increase the share of renewable energy - mainly through investments in
hydropower - the energy system of Greenland is heavily dependent on imported oil. This raises con-
cerns regarding costs, security of supply and sustainability. Fortunately, several well-documented
resources for renewable energy exist. The question therefore is; how should Greenland prioritise
investments in renewable energy to continue its transition towards a self-suﬃcient and sustainable
future in the most cost-eﬃcient way to society?
1.1
Greenland and Nukissiorﬁit
With a total area of almost 2.2 million km2 Greenland constitutes the major share (98 %) of the
Unity of the Realm (Danish: rigsfællesskabet) to which it belongs togehter with Denmark and the
Faroe Islands [1]. The 56 000 inhabitants represent around one percent of the Unities total popula-
tion, making Greenland the most sparsely populated area on earth [2].
The capital city Nuuk is home to almost 18 000 inhabitants, while the rest of the population lives
in 16 towns and approximately 50 small villages (Danish: bygder) along the coastline of the is-
land. Supplying these settlements1 with energy and water is a challenging task due to the long
distances between them and the harsh arctic climate prevailing. The utility company of Greenland,
Nukissiorﬁit, is in charge of just that. Being subject to government control, Nukissiorﬁit has a
universal service obligation2 while at the same time aiming at minimising costs and environmental
impacts of its operations. In 2018 the company employed 388 full-time equivalents and generated
a revenue of 782 million DKK [3].
The overall demographic trend of Greenland is continuous population growth in Nuuk, relatively
stable population counts in the next biggest towns and depopulation of small towns and villages.
The total number of inhabitants in Greenland has remained almost constant in the past two decades
but is expected to decline by around 15 % during the coming three decades due to an ageing popula-
tion and migration, mainly to Denmark [4]. These trends play an important role in the assessment
of future energy demands and may be decisive for the feasibility of energy projects.
Greenland has ambitious targets for the deployment of renewable energy and modernisation of
the energy system.
The governmental strategy paper for energy- and water supply from 2017
states three main goals: i) Low consumer prices for electricity and water, with equal prices in all
settlements, regardless of actual production costs. ii) By 2030, the public energy supply3 shall
be based on renewable energy, as far as possible. iii) Comprehensive modernisation of the energy
system with renewable production replacing fossil fuels. [5]
1Settlement (Danish: bebyggelse) is used as a collective term for cities, towns and villages throughout this thesis.
2The term universal service obligation refers to the fact that Nukissiorﬁit is committed to provide a basic level of
supply to all inhabitants of Greenland.
3In this context, the public energy supply is deﬁned as electricity and heat supplied by Nukissiorﬁit. The strategy
paper does not provide a clear deﬁnition on how to evaluate goal ii, making it diﬃcult to quantify the contributions
of renewable energy investments towards this goal.

1.2
Energy system of Greenland
The energy system of Greenland is special in many respects. Long distances mean that transporta-
tion between settlements is maintained by air and sea. Road transport is limited to the network of
roads within each location. For the same reason, almost all power systems are isolated grids. Most
of these grids are supplied by redundant sets of diesel generators. However, since 1993 Greenland
has built ﬁve hydropower plants, providing energy to six of the eight most populous settlements.
Thus, hydropower covered 82 % of the total electricity produced in 2017. This ﬁgure is enhanced
by the fact that in places where hydropower potential exceeds the conventional electricity demand,
the excess energy is utilised as electric heating in households and industry. Therefore, electricity
consumption in Greenland is divided into two main categories: i) conventional electricity consump-
tion for light and power needs and ii) electricity consumption for electrical heating. The latter is
supplied in various forms, as will be elaborated shortly. Consumer prices of electricity depend both
on the type of consumption and the type of consumer, with discounts granted to the ﬁsh industry.
Appendix A.1 gives an overview of current energy prices.
The arctic climate of Greenland imposes a high demand for space heating of buildings. Broadly, this
heating demand is met in two ways: i) oil boilers installed as individual units in houses or small dis-
trict heating (DH) networks of public housing companies, and ii) heat supplied by Nukissiorﬁit. The
latter can be further divided into various forms of public heat supply. Firstly DH, utilising excess
heat from electricity production and waste incineration or using central oil boilers to generate heat.
In locations with hydropower, electric boilers are also used in DH networks. Secondly, Nukissiorﬁit
sells excess electricity from hydropower as individual electric heating in buildings. Around half of
this is supplied as ﬁxed electric heating, in which the customer has no backup for heat generation,
whereas the other half is supplied as disruptible electric heating. The term disruptible refers to the
fact that Nukissiorﬁit can curtail demand remotely to allow for peak shaving and to reduce the
need for backup capacity in the case of hydropower outages. When curtailed, individual oil burners
kick in as backup automatically.
Due to the above reasons the per capita supply of primary energy is 1.25 times that of Denmark
[6, 7].
This is despite the GDP per capita being around 22 % lower than in Denmark [1, 8].
Furthermore, the energy system of Greenland depends heavily on imported oil, with a total per
head consumption of 3750 L/person/year in 20174, which is 2.7 times higher than the correspond-
ing statistic for Denmark5. As shown by ﬁgure 1.1a oil products covered all of Greenlands total
primary energy supply (TPES) until 1993, when the ﬁrst hydropower plant was commissioned at
Buksefjorden near Nuuk. Since then, four additional hydropower projects have been carried out,
increasing the total installed capacity to 91.3 MW [11].
Figure 1.1b shows a breakdown of the TPES for 2017. As seen, Nukissiorﬁit accounts for around
a third of TPES, using it to produce electricity and public heating, where applicable. It should be
noted that hydropower covers a higher percentage of ﬁnal energy consumption than indicated by
the ﬁgure due to lower conversion losses compared to diesel generators.

costs of heat and electricity for all settlements as well as consumer prices for 2019.
1.3
Objectives and research questions
The objective of this master’s thesis is to provide answers to the research questions, presented in
the following.
Overall research question. The overall research question, which this thesis aims at contributing to,
is formulated as follows:
How to establish a prioritisation of renewable energy investments in Greenland, minimising the
costs to society while contributing to the target set by the Government of Greenland, stating that
the public energy supply shall, as far as possible, be based on renewable energy in 2030?
Supporting research questions. The overall research question is formulated very broadly and needs
further clariﬁcation. Three supporting research questions provide this.
1. How can available energy system data be systematically utilised to model the current energy
system (electricity and heat) of a given settlement and to estimate the heating demand covered by
individual oil boilers?
2. How to determine the economic and technical feasibility of selected options for investments in
renewable energy, given data on renewable resources, energy demands, demographic trends and costs
of components?
3. How to compare and prioritise the options investigated in point 2 regarding their socio-economic
implications despite diﬀerences in size of investment, expected lifetime and production characteris-
tics?
1.4
Methodology
The methodology applied to address the above research questions can be summarised by the fol-
lowing points:
1. Gathering relevant background information and data. This includes a review of relevant litera-
ture on renewable energy technologies- and projects in Greenland. Data supplied by Nukissiorﬁit
and other sources are systematised and quality checked. The applicability of machine learning
techniques to extract features not inherently present in the available data, such as heat demand
covered by individual oil boilers, is investigated.
2. Modelling the current energy system of selected settlements (Nuuk, Nanortalik, Aasiaat and
Qasigiannguit) and validating model behaviour against known data for the base year. Subsequently,
alternative options for investments in renewable energy are modelled, enabling a comparison of
these. The tool for energy system modelling applied is HOMER.

1.5
Limitations
This study places itself in the early stages of decision making by investigating the pre-feasibility
of investment options.
Finding an appropriate trade-oﬀbetween technical detail and complex-
ity requires several limitations to be drawn up. Firstly, the study does not include a full energy
system model of Greenland, as the focus is on the power and heat sectors of the four selected
settlements by investigating seven investment options. However, these options span a wide range of
energy technologies and the model architecture is designed for quick expansion to other settlements.
Secondly, the overall modelling approach is to seek energy balances, which excludes detailed mod-
elling of the electrical grid and thermodynamic processes. This means that electrical design and
grid stability are not modelled and only discussed brieﬂy. Also, thermodynamic processes, e.g.
those of district heating (DH), grids are not modelled.
And thirdly, the socio-economic analysis only quantiﬁes costs and beneﬁts directly related to the
energy systems via an unweighed willingness to pay. Distributional eﬀects are not considered.

Background
This chapter provides background information, intending to frame the thesis in the ongoing eﬀorts
to increase the deployment of renewable energy in Greenland. After a brief review of the most
relevant reports and previous studies, the state of development within diﬀerent renewable energy
technologies in Greenland is addressed. Finally, the options for investments in renewable energy
considered in this thesis are presented and illustrated. A total of seven options for the settlements
of Nuuk, Nanortalik, Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit are studied.
2.1
Previous studies and reports
The idea of utilising renewable energy in Greenland is not new. Substantial work has gone into
research, data collection and pilot projects without which this thesis would not be possible.
2.1.1
Consultancy reports
As a basis for decision making, Nukissiorﬁit has commissioned several consultancy reports in recent
years. Most of these focus on investments within a certain settlement, such as Nuuk [12], Aasiaat
and Qasigiannguit [13], Nanortalik [14, 15, 16] and Ilulissat [17]. These studies vary signiﬁcantly in
underlying assumptions, methods applied and level of detail, making a direct comparison of results
diﬃcult. Instead of summarizing the ﬁndings of the above studies here, these will be woven into
the discussion and validation of results in chapter 5.
Extensive eﬀort has gone into exploring, monitoring and assessing the considerable potentials of
hydropower in Greenland. A detailed list of resources was prepared in 2005, dividing the sites into
their applications as energy supply of settlements or energy intensive industry [18]. In 2019, ﬁve of
the most promising potentials for the energy supply of towns have been updated, both with regard
to hydrological potential1 and detailed cost estimates [19]. These provide the basis for modelling
the hydropower options considered in this thesis.
2.1.2
Academia
In spite of its limited size, the energy system of Greenland has been subject to high academic
interest. Much of this has its origins in the so-called Lighthouse project, with students from DTU
carrying out projects in Greenland to set up solar PV panels in Ilulissat, erecting a small wind
turbine in Sarfannguit and similar pilot projects [20, 21].
In 2016 Jakobsen, Hansen and Vincent presented a PhD thesis on the renewable energy potential
of Greenland with focus on wind resource assessment [22]. The study ﬁnds signiﬁcant potentials for
energy savings and optimisations as well as the best resources for wind power being in the southern
part of Greenland.

2.2
Novelty of this study
The studies mentioned above mostly focus on a speciﬁc technology, project or settlement, thus rep-
resenting important contributions to the ongoing development. However no study exists directly
comparing the feasibility and performance of renewable energy projects between diﬀerent settle-
ments. Partly, this is due to a lack of consistent data handling, necessary to create energy system
models - an issue addressed in this study.
In spite of the ambitious political goals for renewable energy, no consistent roadmap for achieving
these has been developed. Furthermore, there is no consensus on how to prioritise investments
or even how to establish comparable indicators necessary for such prioritisation.
Also, to the
knowledge of the author, there exists no energy system model for Greenland yet, which may be
explained by i) the fact that the energy system consists of around 70 individual island system and
ii) the limited size of the total system. This thesis aims to address these shortcomings. Even though
only considering a limited number of Greenlandic settlements and investment options, it draws up
a methodology that can be applied to all remaining settlements in order to support decision making
in renewable energy investments. Furthermore, the study provides concrete recommendations on
how to prioritise Nukissiorﬁits pipeline of renewable energy projects.
2.3
Renewable energy technologies in Greenland
Special considerations apply to renewable energy technologies in a Greenlandic context - the fol-
lowing addresses these, technology by technology.
2.3.1
Hydropower
Hydropower is by far the most mature and proven renewable energy technology in Greenland, con-
sidering installed capacity, years of operational experience and total investments. Even though the
potential of hydropower by far exceeds the total energy demand of Greenland, the high costs of
developing these is often prohibitive for new projects [18].
Nukissiorﬁit has built up substantial competences within construction and operation of such plants,
which is of high beneﬁt for future projects. During the stay in Nuuk in connection with this thesis,
a visit was made to the Buksefjorden plant. Despite its 25 years of age, the plant appears in an
impressive condition with only limited maintenance requirements.

An essential aspect of hydropower investments is that these come in discrete sizes, mainly deﬁned
by the hydrological potential of a given site. This makes the technology less scalable compared to
other renewables.
Some technical aspects of hydropower should be addressed brieﬂy. The general principle of plant
design is adapted from an approach commonly used in Norway with regulated reservoirs, unlined
pressure tunnels and underground power stations [26, 27] . Regarding turbine design, it is assumed
that new turbines are of the Francis-type, which is the case for all but one (Tassilaq) turbines
installed by Nukissiorﬁit.
Generally, Francis turbines are well suited for low heads (especially
below 100 m) and are prone to relatively high changes in eﬃciency when operated at oﬀ-design ﬂow
rates (illustrated by ﬁgure 3 in appendix A.2). Pelton turbines, on the contrary, are well suited for
very high heads and are known for a more constant eﬃciency curve2.
2.3.2
Solar PV
Solar PV has experienced a substantial development in Greenland during recent years. There exists
no oﬃcial record of the total installed capacity and estimates3 range between 0.7-4 MWp. To put
this number into perspective, Denmark had an installed capacity of around 1000 MWp in 2018 [28].
The installed capacity per capita is thus several times higher in Denmark compared to Greenland.
Most of the PV systems in Greenland are installed on private homes or company roofs, with the
amount installed by Nukissiorﬁit totalling at a mere 139 kWp [3]. This ratio might change in the
years to come, mainly due to the following reason: Until 2018, the cost of energy was diﬀerentiated
between the settlements, to some degree reﬂecting the true cost of production - this incentiﬁed
installation of PV systems in settlements with high electricity prices, namely locations without
hydropower. However, a price reform carried out in 2018 resulted in equal energy prices all over
Greenland, with electricity prices for normal consumers at 1.6 DKK/kWh. Even though this dras-
tically reduced the attractiveness of solar PV for private customers, the incentive for Nukissiorﬁit
remains, since the marginal cost of electricity production4 is higher than 1.6 DKK/kWh in 54 of 68
Greenlandic settlements [29]. See appendix A.1 for breakdown of production costs.
Measured performance data of eight PV systems installed across Greenland have been collected
and compared in this study.

2.3.3
Wind power
Wind power is an emerging technology in a Greenlandic context, and no commercial project has
been carried out so far. Generally, distinction should be made between small-scale wind turbines
for hybrid systems in villages (< 100 kW) and larger turbines (> 800 kW) for integration into the
grids of towns.
So far, all Greenlandic experiences with wind power are related to the former category. Starting in
1983, when a ﬁrst test turbine was erected in Nuuk, several demonstration and research projects
have been carried out [22, 31]. None of these have been as successful as anticipated, mainly due to
technical challenges such as breakdowns in heavy winds, icing and grid integration.
The most recent development is the erection of two 25 kW small-scale wind turbines in Sisim-
iut, aiming at testing the technology in arctic conditions [32]. In February 2019, less than half a
year after commissioning, one of the turbines lost a wing, presumably due to an assembly error [33].
Regarding the category of larger wind turbines, the global development within the industry has
pushed towards larger turbines, which explains the lower limit of 800 kW in this category, as smaller
models have been phased out. Although this is a small turbine size in the light of today’s wind
industry, 800 kW represents a large production unit considering the size of most towns in Green-
land. Despite the limited Greenlandic experience with wind power, plans of erecting two Enercon
E53 turbines in Nanortalik are concrete and developing, with detailed foundation design currently
being carried out and environmental permits given.
Considering costs, the category of larger turbines has signiﬁcantly lower speciﬁc investment costs
compared to small turbines. Comparing realized costs of erecting the previously mentioned 25 kW
turbines in Sisimiut with detailed cost estimates for the Nanortalik project shows that the speciﬁc
cost (DKK/kWh) of the latter is only a ﬁfth of the former. This is an important insight, as it
suggests that larger turbines should be preferred whenever the size of settlement allows integration
without causing stability issues.
Overall, wind power has yet to prove its viability in Greenland. Therefore, the inherent risks related
to investments in wind power must be concluded to be higher than for both hydro and solar PV.

2.3.4
Energy storage
The only energy storage technology considered in this project are battery energy storage systems
(BESS), used as short-term storage of electricity. No Greenlandic experience with heat storage of
any kind exists, even though steel tanks for diurnal heat storage have been discussed [13]. Further-
more, pumped hydro storages are only of interest in systems that include both large amounts of
variable renewable energy and hydropower, which is not the case in Greenland.
Experiences with utility-scale BESS is limited to the pilot project of a hybrid system in the small
village of Igaliku, consisting of 100 kWp solar PV, a 473 kWh lead-acid battery and diesel engines
[3].
For future projects, Li-ion batteries are thought to be more suitable, which is why these
are included in the options considered in this thesis. However, no experience with such systems
exists in Greenland, which induces risks to options, including the technology. Some of the main
uncertainties are the lifetime of the batteries and their performance in the cold arctic climate. The
systems considered in this thesis are installed in containers, inducing a need for heating during
winter, which lowers overall eﬃciency.

energy demand during recent decades. The number of inhabitants has doubled during the last four
decades to almost 18 000 today. In 1993 Greenlands ﬁrst hydropower plant was commissioned at

total installed capacity to 3 x 15 MW. The production of 271 GWh in 2018 (corresponding to a

40 % of the heating demand. Historical data of energy production at Buksefjorden are shown in

energy demand, the annual withdrawal of water has surpassed the sustainable rate corresponding

Methods
A well-documented, structured and transparent method is the foundation of both academic and
professional writing. Without it, the results presented subsequently are questionable at best. This
chapter aims at giving the reader a brief, still precise, insight into the methods applied and the
assumptions made in this study.
3.1
Overall modelling strategy
The overall modelling strategy is best explained by ﬁgure 3.1, indicating a three-step approach with
iterations between these. In the ﬁrst step, input data are gathered and systematised. This is done
in Excel and Matlab, preparing data to serve as inputs in the energy system model. Load data for
both heat and electricity are imported as hourly data of one year (base year), while weather data
are imported at the highest available resolution (down to 5 minutes). Other parameters, such as
cost data, are given as constant values.
Figure 3.1: Overall modelling strategy. Arrows indicating iterative processes.
In the second step, the energy system is modelled using the commercial software HOMER Pro,
which is developed to model microgrids with multiple energy sources. Initially, a business as usual
(BAU) option is deﬁned for each settlement, simulating today’s energy system to which the op-
tions for investments in renewable generation are to be compared. The base-year outputs of the
BAU model are validated against known data for fuel consumption and energy production of indi-
vidual components - this ensures that the model is capable of replicating the current energy system.
Subsequently, the options for renewable energy investments are modelled. Options, for which the
capacities are predetermined (options 1.1 through 3.1), are simulated and optimised with regard
to operations only - no optimisation of capacities is necessary for these options. If, on the other
hand, capacities are unknown (option 3.2, Aas.-Qas., hybrid systems), the model is employed to
optimise both capacities and operations. The time steps applied in modelling depend on the type
of energy system; for options including wind and solar, a time step of one minute is applied whereas
hydropower options are modelled using 60 minute time steps. This is done to capture the eﬀects
15
==PAGE:25==
Master’s Thesis
2019
Mathis Backen
of intra-hour variability of wind and solar production.
The outputs of the second step are annual sums of diesel consumption and energy production by
components, with the latter being used as a proxy for the variable operational expenditures. These
annual numbers are used as inputs to the socio-economic analysis carried out in Excel. Here, a
discounted cash ﬂow (DCF) analysis is performed to determine costs and beneﬁts associated with
the individual options.
3.1.1
Scenarios of future energy demand
How will the energy demand of diﬀerent Greenlandic settlements develop in the future? The answer
to this question is highly uncertain and yet very relevant in the context of this study as it might be
decisive for whether an investment in renewable energy is feasible or not. Therefore, three scenarios

3.2
Estimation of individual heating demand
The amount of heating provided by individual oil boilers - and thus not supplied by Nukissiorﬁit - is
not easily established, since these boilers receive their fuel from retailers, which have no interest in
sharing sales data with Nukissiorﬁit. During the ﬁrst three months of this study, the only oil data
available were from the latest national energy statistic of 2016, aggregated to the country level [6].
At a later stage of this project, the government-owned oil company, Polaroil, kindly provided data
on imported oil volumes divided into the diﬀerent types of oil products as well as the settlements,
to which these are imported. Even with this improved knowledge, two problems remain: i) It is
not possible to determine how much of the oil is used for heating purposes since the same type of
fuel is used for heating, transportation (sea and land) and industry. ii) Oil is stored in big tanks
in the settlements of Greenland. Since no consecutive years of import data was provided (only
2017), ﬂuctuations in the inventory of these tanks is not apparent from data. These ﬂuctuations
are thought to be signiﬁcant, which could lead to wrong results if only relying on import data of
one year.

Despite these challenges, establishing the energy demand for heating is of high interest - especially

3.2.1
Estimation based on average speciﬁc consumption
The ﬁrst and relatively simple approach is to determine an average speciﬁc heating demand
(MWh/person/year) using national statistics and to assume that this is the same for all loca-
tions in Greenland. Even such a highly simpliﬁed number is not easily determined from available
data. The best estimate has been found to be 24.4 MWh/person/year [6, 12]. It should be noted
that this ﬁgure includes the heating demand of households as well as industry and services - i.e.
the total demand for space heating and hot water in a settlement. Households are estimated to
consume slightly more than half of this number [12]. The amount of heat covered by individual
oil boilers is then found by subtracting the amount delivered by Nukissiorﬁit from the total value.
This results in the values presented in ﬁgure 1.2 in the introduction.

3.3
Energy system modelling
This section elaborates on the methods for energy system modelling (ESM), starting with general
considerations on the chosen modelling tool, followed by the methods used to model the diﬀerent
renewable technologies. It should be mentioned that the energy system modelling is a iterative
process, requiring continuous eﬀorts to verify model behaviour and to validate base-year results
against known data. An attempt to illustrate this work is given in appendix A.10.
3.3.1
Choice of modelling tool
HOMER Pro is an energy system modelling tool, capable of optimising capacities and operations of
hybrid energy systems [41]. It is a commercial tool, developed by the National Renewable Energy
Laboratory (NREL). Simulations can be performed in time steps down to minutes and the recently
added multi-year feature allows to specify trends in fuel cost, demand growth, degradation of PV
modules and OPEX.
HOMER is one of the most commonly used tools in modelling community-scale hybrid energy sys-
tems [42, 43]. Besides this fact, the most decisive argument for choosing HOMER as a modelling
tool in this thesis is the fact that Nukissiorﬁit uses HOMER to make calculations on hybrid energy

==PAGE:30==
Master’s Thesis
2019

systems in the pre-feasibility phase of projects. Thus, models developed in connection with this
study can be discussed with Nukissiorﬁit staﬀand passed on after completion. However, the soft-
ware has weaknesses, which are highlighted throughout the following sections and ﬁnally addressed
in the discussion in chapter 6.
Due to the software being commercialised, it has not been possible to get comprehensive docu-
mentation on the underlying algorithm. This is a weakness, as it prevents the modeller from fully
understanding how the model works. As for most energy system optimisation tools, the objective
is to minimise overall costs while respecting constraints on demand and available capacities. Cur-
tailment of excess renewable generation is possible if this serves the objective. Rather than relying
on linear- or mixed integer programming, HOMER creates a search space of various systems conﬁg-
urations among which the software determines the least-cost conﬁguration of capacities [44]. This
is done by optimising the operations of each conﬁguration in the search space. If capacities are
predetermined, the user can apply HOMER as a simulation tool, only optimising operations. In
this study, the latter approach is applied in all options but option 3.2 (Aas.-Qas., hybrid systems).

Modelling of technologies
Every energy technology has special characteristics that need to be taken into account during
modelling.

3.3.2.1
Diesel generators
A main strength of HOMER compared to other energy system modelling tools is its eﬀortless and
detailed approach to modelling diesel generators. The software comes with an extensive library of
engines of diﬀerent manufacturers, deﬁning their capacities, fuel curves, ramp rates and emissions.
The electricity work of a settlement is modelled by choosing generators from the library identical
or similar to those currently in operation. Ramp rates and minimum run times are established
through deliberations with Nukissiorﬁit employees and assumed to be the same for all generators.
The next step is to simulate the base year and to validate the modelled fuel consumption against
measured data provided by Nukissiorﬁit. If necessary, the fuel curves of generators are modiﬁed
until modelled fuel consumption matches measured values.
The heat recovery ratio of a generator deﬁnes the fraction of waste heat from electricity produc-
tion utilised as DH. During validation, these ratios are adapted iteratively to align the modelled
production of DH with the actual amount of heat recovered. There are signiﬁcant variations in the
utilisation of excess heat among the modelled locations, depending on the DH grid available and the
type of engine. Despite being a simpliﬁed approach, not taking into account part-load behaviour and
annual variations in heat demand, the method is deemed to be suﬃcient in the context of this study.
During model runs, HOMER employs algorithms to optimise the operation of generators in each
scenario to minimise overall costs while meeting the electricity demand at all times. Heat is regarded
as a by-product of electricity generation and if demand exceeds the amount of heat available from
co-generation, backup oil burners are used to ﬁll the gaps. The outputs from the optimisations in

3.3.2.2
Hydropower
One of the main shortcomings of using HOMER in a Greenlandic context is its approach to mod-
elling hydropower. Firstly, HOMER does not take into account variations in turbine eﬃciency at
part-load. To illustrate this issue, the performance curves of the Sisimiut hydropower plant are
shown in appendix A.2, indicating considerable variations in eﬃciency at part-load. Based on these
data and discussions with Nukissiorﬁit, an average eﬃciency of ηhydro = 0.86 is assumed for all
hydropower plants.
Secondly, HOMER is not designed for hydro systems with reservoirs.
The software oﬀers two
built-in modules for hydropower; a hydrokinetic module for modelling tidal power (not applica-
ble here) and a hydro module for run-of-river systems. Even though none of these are designed
to model systems with reservoirs as those found in Greenland, the latter module can be adapted
to such systems. Two diﬀerent approaches to adapt the module have been investigated in this study.
Approach 1, modelling reservoir as a battery. In this approach, the run-of-river hydropower plant
is modelled in combination with a large battery, which emulates the hydro reservoir. It is inspired
by Canales, Beluco and Mendes (2017), who describe the approach in detail [45].
Basically, the hydropower plant charges the battery from where the model can strategically draw
electricity to meet the demand. The battery is large enough to allow for interseasonal storage
(just as a reservoir) and is assumed to be 100 % eﬃcient since all losses are associated with the
hydropower plant. In theory, this setup should be able to imitate the actual behaviour of a hy-
dropower plant with a reservoir.
However, the model turned out not to behave as anticipated
when using this approach. The reason for this is thought to be found in the fact that HOMER uses
heuristics to simulate battery behaviour, signiﬁcantly reducing computational time. However, these
heuristics seem to be incapable to model interseasonal storage. In typical applications of HOMER,
this would not be an issue since BESS is still too expensive for interseasonal storage. The above
observation was conﬁrmed in an e-mail correspondence with HOMER support.
Approach 2, predeﬁned patterns of reservoir discharge. The second approach predeﬁnes the monthly
reservoir discharge pattern to be monthly averages to those observed for the current hydropower
plant in Nuuk. Thus, the ﬂow rate available to the hydropower plant is assumed to be constant
throughout the month. These ﬂow patterns are illustrated in appendices A.4 through A.6.
Next, short term variation in demand (intra-day and intra-week) are taken into account by con-
necting the hydropower plant to a 100 % eﬃcient, virtual battery. This time, however, the size of
the battery is considerably smaller compared to that modelled in approach 1. Hence, the battery
does not allow for seasonal storage of electricity, eliminating the problems occurring due to the
heuristics as described in approach 1.
It is found that a battery capacity corresponding to 24 hours of full-load operation is appropriate
to get realistic model behaviour. It is important to mention that the model is constrained in such

3.3.2.3
Solar PV
The solar PV modules are modelled using a ﬁrst year eﬃciency of 16 % and a degradation in out-
put3 of 0.8 % annually [46]. This eﬃciency is relatively low compared to the values provided by
manufacturers of modules installed in Greenland in recent years, averaging around 21 %, according
to the main importer of modules. The reason for using a somewhat lower eﬃciency, however, is
that validation of model results against measured performance data of existing solar PV systems
(as presented in A.3), indicated that a ﬁrst year eﬃciency of 16 % a more realistic value. The
deviation from the lab eﬃciencies stated by manufacturers is explained by eﬀects of suboptimal
orientation of panels, shading losses, snow cover and dirt deposits.
The solar resources applied as inputs to the PV model are based on measured data for solar radiation
of a given settlement with a resolution of 5 minutes. Eﬀects of tilt and orientation of the panels are
not explicitly modelled but are included in the eﬃciency. Previous research suggests that annual
performance is not inﬂuenced signiﬁcantly if the orientation of panels is varied within reasonable
ranges from optimal tilt and orientation [30, 23].

3.3.2.6

oil boilers would enhance the feasibility of converting to electric heating.

respect to heating technologies. Therefore, all forms of electric heating are aggregated to one dump

3.4
Estimation of available roof space for PV installations
The cost of installing PV systems in Greenland depends signiﬁcantly on the way these are mounted.
Experience from previous projects suggests that systems mounted on stands in the Greenlandic
landscape have almost twice the cost compared to systems mounted on rooftops. This is in contrast
to developments in mainland Europe, where utility-scale solar PV has proven to have signiﬁcantly
lower speciﬁc costs than rooftop systems [48]. The diﬀerence between Greenland and mainland
Europe is explained by the need for blasting, concreting and heavy equipment to mount stands in
Greenland - all of which is costly in a remote location.
Due to these reasons, an alternative way for Nukissiorﬁit to install PV panels is investigated. All
settlements have roofs of considerable size, for instance those of schools, supermarkets and social
housing. If rented by Nukissiorﬁit, these could provide space for solar panels at a lower cost than
mounting them on stands in the landscape. Furthermore, this would place solar panels closer to
consumers, reducing grid losses and costs of cabling.

With this in mind, the questions on the amount of suitable roof space and how to determine it in a

1. Area of roofs. Only large roofs with areas of more than 200 m2 are considered to reduce in-

2. Orientation and tilt. Using a combination of Nukissiorﬁits GIS maps and Google street view7 the

and south-west are preferred since experience and previous studies suggest optimal orientation being
close to straight south [30, 23]. Some variation in orientation between the diﬀerent installations

should be relatively high in Greenlandic contexts with optimums around 55-60 deg for locations like

some large ﬂat roofs (such as ﬁsh factories) are included as well, assuming that panels would be
installed using stands, which are cheaper to mount on roofs than on the fells.
3. Proximity to a grid substation. Only roofs with an euclidean distance of less than 50 m to a grid
substation are considered. This criterion ensures that large installations can be connected to the
medium, voltage grid, increasing grid stability.

electrician, specialised in installing PV systems, to ensure that the suggested sites are suitable and

3.5
Socio-economic analysis
Every undertaking carried out in society is associated with costs and beneﬁts to its people and
the surrounding environment. A socio-economic analysis aims at identifying, assessing and, where
possible, quantifying these costs and beneﬁts. The purpose is to enable evidence-based decision
making and to utilise the limited resources available to society in the best possible way [49].
In the context of this thesis, a socio-economic approach is applied to answer the question of how
investments in renewable energy should be prioritised in Greenland. The reason for this being a
socio-economic question, rather than a private economic optimisation, is that Nukissiorﬁit is subject
to government control due to its monopolistic nature. Thus, Nukissiorﬁit has to comply with the
guidelines set by its owner - the government of Greenland. Furthermore, besides being the utility
of Greenland, Nukissiorﬁit also acts as the civil service with regard to energy matters in Greenland.
Thus, decisions taken by Nukissiorﬁit should reﬂect the interests of the Greenlandic society.
Many countries provide guidelines on how to carry out socio-economic analyses. Following these
rules is essential as it ensures comparability between studies carried out in diﬀerent parts of society.
Therefore, the guideline provided by the Government of Greenland is applied where possible in this
study [50]. Since Greenland has no guideline directly targeting projects within the energy sector,
the guideline provided by the Danish Energy Agency is used supplementary [51].
The analysis aims at establishing the unweighted willingness to pay, i.e. not assessing how costs and
beneﬁts are distributed within society. Distributional- and non-monetised eﬀects will be discussed
subsequently.
3.5.1
Temporal demarcation
Diﬀerent energy projects have diﬀerent time horizons. Both the time required before plants can
be commissioned as well as the technical lifetime of assets may vary signiﬁcantly between projects.
This depends on various parameters, such as the type of energy technology, operational conditions
as well as the natural environment these projects are carried out in.
A clear deﬁnition of the temporal demarcation is of utmost importance as it ensures comparability
between projects. The considered project timeline is illustrated in ﬁgure 3.3 and is based on the
following main assumptions, which have been established through discussions with Nukissiorﬁit
project managers:
1. The decision date for any of the considered investment options is set to be the 31st December
2019, assuming that all information needed for decision making is available on this date. This date
is also the evaluation date, meaning that all cash ﬂows are discounted to represent 2019 values.
2. Projects in windpower and solar PV can be planned, constructed and commissioned within two
years from the decision date. Hence, these projects are assumed to be ready for commissioning on
the 31st December 2021.
3. Hydropower projects require longer planning- and construction times and are thus considered
to be fully operational ultimo 2024.

3.5.4
Comparison of options
NPV is probably the most widely used indicator in socio-economic analysis, as it tells the decision
maker how much a given project is worth in today’s money. When used to compare options, the
main weakness of this indicator is that it fails to account for the vast diﬀerences of CAPEX between
investments. Hydropower options, for instance, require high up-front investments, enabling them
to generate high returns (measured as NPV) in absolute terms. Therefore, one critical question
remains: How can we compare the results of options, given vast diﬀerences in scale of investment
and the diﬀerent timelines of projects?
Proﬁtability index, PI. The most straight-forward way to deal with diﬀerences in size of investments
is to normalise the ∆NPV by the total investment, obtaining the so-called proﬁtability index (PI),
deﬁned in equation 3.6:
PI = 1 + ∆NPV

(3.6)
where Itot is the sum of investments in a given option. Thus, the PI provides an answer to the
question; how many times does the investment pay oﬀfor society within the analysis period?. A PI
of one indicates break-even, whereas a value above one indicates a proﬁtable investment. It must
be noticed that using Itot to normalise the investments of assets that do not reach their full lifetime
within the analysis period (e.g. hydropower) skews the PI indicator. The reader should be aware
of this when results for PI are presented.
Internal rate of return, IRR. A diﬀerent approach is taken with the internal rate of return (IRR)
deﬁned as the socio-economic discount rate at which the ∆NPV becomes zero. Obtaining an IRR
higher than the socio-economic discount rate indicates that the project is proﬁtable and vice versa.
Determining the IRR requires numerical methods, for which Excel’s goal seek function is employed.

3.5.5
Assumptions related to oil products
A number of assumptions related to oil products should be mentioned. Firstly, it is assumed that
the fuel used in electricity generation, production of DH and individual oil burners has identical
physical and chemical characteristics, such as caloriﬁc value, emission factor and density.
Secondly, throughout the study, a fuel cost of 5 DKK/L is applied, which excludes an environmental
fee of 0.1 DKK/L, charged on all oil products. The fee is disregarded since a socio-economic analysis
uses factor prices, reﬂecting prices that occur in eﬃcient markets. This assumption is challenged
by the that the government of Greenland controls imports and sets the price of oil products to be
the same in all settlements. All oil products are ﬁrst imported to four harbours8 from where they
are distributed to all other settlements of Greenland. Hence, the actual cost of oil products varies
between settlements due to expenses related to transport and storage. Therefore, strictly speaking,
fuel costs are not real factor prices. For the sake of simplicity and due to a lack of data regarding
the oil infrastructure, it is assumed that the real costs are equal in all locations, even though they
presumably are higher in small and remote locations. Monetising this eﬀect would enhance the
feasibility of oil-saving investments in small villages and remote locations.

Data is the backbone of any energy system model.
Regardless of the model applied, one key
relationship between input data and the validity of results applies: Garbage in →garbage out.
Thus, careful handling, documented manipulations and critical assessment of data is critical and
often more time consuming than the modelling itself.
4.1
Data quality assessment and validation of data
Large amounts of data from various sources are processed in connection with this thesis.
An
assessment of the data quality is carried out, inspired by the data quality assessment scheme by the
U.S. Environmental Protection Agency (US EPA) [55] and adapted to this study. The following
criteria are been taken into account in the assessment:
1. Reliability - How many outliers exist and how well can data be validated against other data?
2. Temporal correlation - How old is the data and how many years of data are available?
3. Geographical correlation - Does data exist for all settlements of interest or is it aggregated data?
4. Technological correlation - Has the energy system changed since data was collected?
5. Data collection method - How is data collected, validated and stored?
The assessment can be found in appendix A.8, presenting both a speciﬁcation of the ﬁve criteria as
well as the resulting scores, the averages of which are included in the tables of the following sections.
All data are checked for obvious outliers and validated against data from previous years and other
sources where applicable. Outliers are primarily handled through replacement with averages of
nearest neighbouring data. With regard to data provided by Nukissiorﬁit, there seems to be a
trend of production data being of lower quality compared to consumption data.
This may be
explained by the fact that consumption data are used for billing customers, thus being reviewed
more thoroughly. Another apparent trend is that data quality is correlated with the size of the
settlement, with the best data available for the largest settlements.
Generally, there is a lack of high quality data in energy sectors not serviced by Nukissiorﬁit. Espe-
cially the consumption of fossil fuels for individual oil heating and transportation is only provided
on a very aggregated level. Furthermore, the latest published energy statistic for Greenland is for
the year 2016 [6].

One might ask why changing the demand forecast alters the feasibility of options with total renew-
able energy shares signiﬁcantly below 100 % (such as options 2.2 and 3.2), as it might be reasonable
to assume that these renewable technologies would be able to oﬀset their generation even though
demand decreases somewhat. The reason lies in the fact that substituting fossil-based electricity
generation for light and power is more valuable than substituting fossil-based heat generation. This
is because the former is subject to second law eﬃciencies (Carnot) whereas the latter is not. A short
example is given to illustrate this point: Assume a diesel generator with an electrical eﬃciency of
33 % and an oil boiler with 100 % heat eﬃciency, for simplicity. Now, replacing one energy unit
of electricity from the diesel generator by renewable generation saves three energy units of diesel
oil. Substituting oil heating by electric heating, however, reduces energy from oil in a ratio of
one-to-one4. Now, as demand for light and power decreases, more of the renewable generation is
utilised as heating or curtailed, decreasing the feasibility of the investment.
Though somewhat trivial, the above recognition of substituting one kWh of electricity produced
by diesel generators being more valuable than substituting one kWh of heating produced by oil
burners is fundamental for investments in renewable energy in Greenland.

5.2.2
Constraining the renewable share
As it was shown in table 5.2, there are substantial diﬀerences in the renewable shares obtained by
the various options. This gives rise to an interesting question: Is it fair to directly compare the
performance of options that provide vastly diﬀerent renewable energy shares?
The answer to this question depends on the objective. If the objective is to identify proﬁtable
investments in renewable energy and prioritise these, the answer is yes. However, if the objective is
to achieve a certain amount of oil superseding or reduction in CO2 emissions in a given settlement,
the answer is no.
To clarify this point, we take the example of Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit, since the options consid-
ered here have the most pronounced diﬀerence in renewable share. While option 3.1 (new hydro)
achieves a combined renewable share for Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit of 72 %, option 3.2 (hybrid
systems) achieves only 6 %.
However, the former is found to be unproﬁtable in the base case
while the latter pays back economically. Seen from this perspective, one should choose to invest
in the hybrid system rather than the hydropower plant. But would the hybrid systems still be the
preferred choice if we wanted to achieve the same renewable share as seen for the hydropower plant?
To answer this question, a new series of simulations is performed by constraining the minimum
RESs of Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit in steps of 5 %. The results of this analysis are presented in
ﬁgure 5.5, along with the renewable share of the hydropower option 3.1. The y-axis of this ﬁgure
is a weighted cost of heat and electricity supply.

Firstly, there exists an optimum renewable share in both Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit, at which a
hybrid system is capable of supplying energy at a lower cost than the BAU-option. This result is
in line with previously presented ﬁndings for option 3.2 (hybrid systems) showing a positive ∆NPV.
Secondly, there are diﬀerences in the optimal levels of renewable energy for the two locations; Qasi-

5.3.1
Sensitivity towards changes in CAPEX
Despite eﬀorts to apply well-documented cost estimates for the diﬀerent options, their CAPEX
values remain uncertain. Several factors are contributing to this uncertainty. Firstly, most costs
are established through tendering processes and are therefore dependent on the current economic
situation within relevant industries as well as the degree of competition. Secondly, unforeseen costs
or reductions thereof are relatively likely to occur during construction in arctic conditions due to
extreme weather and unpredictable seasons. Thirdly, technologies that are immature in a Green-
landic context, such as wind power, lack cost references.
Due to these reasons, the ±25 % sensitivity analysis on the investment costs is performed as elab-
orated now. The range is suggested by the aforementioned Greenlandic guideline and is considered
to capture the uncertainty of CAPEX in the pre-feasibility stage [50]. The results of the analysis
are presented in ﬁgure 5.7 in terms of error bars applied to the base case PI. The reason for choosing
this type of illustration is that changes in CAPEX may occur independently from option to option,
as opposed to e.g. a change in oil price forecast. Thus, it is relevant to ask, for instance, how do
conclusions change if CAPEX of option x is increased by 25 % while CAPEX of option y is kept
constant?
Figure 5.7: Sensitivity towards changes in CAPEX of ±25 % indicated as error bars of the PI on the base
case result. Low range indicating increased CAPEX and vice versa.
As seen, changes in CAPEX greatly inﬂuence results. The highest absolute changes in PI are ob-
served for the Nuuk options since these are associated with the highest investments. However, even
if CAPEX increases by 25 % for option 1.2 while remaining unchanged for the options of all other
settlements, option 1.2 would continue to be more attractive in terms of PI.
Furthermore, it is interesting to see that the hybrid systems of both Nanortalik and Aasiaat-
Qasigiannguit are more sensitive towards changes in CAPEX compared to their hydropower coun-
terparts. This is an important observation, given that the cost estimates of the hybrid systems are
thought to be more uncertain due to a lack of experience on these.

accounted for economically.

Sensitivity towards future oil prices
Greenland has two main sources of energy; oil products and hydropower. Thus, any investment
in renewable energy aims to reduce the consumption of oil. The economic value of superseding oil
consumption depends on the future oil price, which is why this sensitivity analysis is carried out
to quantify the eﬀect of diﬀerent oil price forecasts. Figure 5.9 illustrates the considered forecasts.
It includes i) the constant forecast used in the base case, ii) linear increase and decrease of 25 %
by 2041 from today’s level, iii) the forecast applied by the Danish Energy Agency for analyses [51]
and iv) the IEA sustainable development scenario [12].
It should be noted that the constant forecast applied for the base case is considerably lower than
the forecasts provided by both DEA and IEA. Compared to the latter forecats, the performance of
options for investments in renewable energy are underestimated in the base case. It may thus be
questioned whether the chosen base case is too conservative. The argument for using it, however, is
that revisions of historical forecasts show that these consistently fail to predict long term oil prices
and generally tend to overestimate these [66]. Furthermore, there is no consensus within Nukissior-
ﬁit or in Greenland on what forecast to use for analysis. Therefore, using a constant forecast may
appear more transparent and less biased than using an arbitrary forecast7.

5.5
Implications for the energy system of Greenland
To complete this chapter, it is appropriate to ask how the presented results aﬀect the overall en-
ergy system of Greenland. Thus, the question addressed in this section is; how do the oil savings
analysed in this study compare to the country-wide oil consumption and how cost-eﬀective are the
options?
In an attempt to answer this question, ﬁgure 5.12 shows the estimated cost of abating the annual
oil imports to Greenland, totalling at 210 million litres in 2017, by dividing the consumption into
categories [40]. The approach is inspired by so-called marginal abatement cost curves (MACC),
a tool commonly used to identify and illustrate cost-eﬀective measures of reducing externalities
(e.g. CO2 emissions) [69]. Regarding the categories assessed in this study, only one option can be
included for every settlement due to the mutual exclusiveness of options. The decision to include
option 1.2, 2.2 and 3.1 is based on an assessment of their overall performance.
Before analysing the ﬁgure, it is important to stress that most of the presented data are adapted
from literature not directly applicable to the Greenlandic case, based on rough estimates or associ-
ated with considerable uncertainties. Therefore, the ﬁgure should be used only for the purpose of
putting results into perspective and no further conclusions should be drawn on it.

Discussion
Having presented and discussed the quantitative results of this study in the previous chapter, it is
now appropriate to frame these in a more general discussion addressing the applied methodology,
non-monetised eﬀects and implication of results.
6.1
Methodology of energy system modelling
6.1.1
HOMER as an energy system modelling tool
Overall, HOMER is found to be well-suited for the type of pre-feasibility modelling carried out
in this study. It accommodates the characteristics of the Greenlandic energy system with many
distributed island systems and varying access to renewable energy resources. The main strengths
of the tool are: i) intuitive user interface and quick result generation, enabling fast and eﬃcient
modelling, ii) its approach to modelling diesel generator systems, which make up the majority of the
power systems in Greenland and iii) the extensive list of pre-deﬁned components found in the library.
However, the intuitive and straight-forward modelling approach comes at the cost of reduced free-
dom for the modeller. The main disadvantages of using HOMER in the context of this study are:
i) the very simpliﬁed approach to hydropower modelling as elaborated in section 3.3.2.2 ii) the
inability to optimise the capacity of dump load (disruptible heating) and iii) its inability to model
grid stability.
For future applications, it is recommended to continue using HOMER (or similar tools) in the
pre-feasibility phase of studies, especially for hybrid systems with diesel engines being the main
power supply. However, other tools should be considered for two speciﬁc cases. Firstly, a tool ca-
pable of optimising hydropower reservoirs should be employed before investment decision in order
to optimise the operational strategy, turbine size and type of turbine of a new plant. Secondly,
for detailed modelling of hybrid systems with high penetration of renewables, such as wind-diesel
systems, a tool capable of modelling grid stability should be used in a later phase of the project.
Addressing the scientiﬁc debate of simulation versus optimisation in energy system modelling, e.g.
brought forward by Lund et al. in [41], this study applies a combination of the two approaches.
For the settlements of Nuuk and Nanortalik, sets of pre-deﬁned alternative options are simulated
and compared. For Aasiaat-Qasigiannguit, however, an optimisation approach is used to determine
the conﬁgurations of hybrid systems which are then compared to a hydropower alternative using
simulations. The study presents the results of all options, resembling the simulation approach, along
with sensitivity analysis on parameters, which, on the other hand, is typical for an optimisation
approach [41]. This combination is found to provide a comprehensive basis for decision making, as
it allows to compare options by multiple criteria.

6.1.2
Neglect of energy saving measures
The options considered in this study all focus on investments in new power generation assets. No
attention is drawn towards measures of energy saving. However, during the work on this thesis and
the stay in Greenland, it became obvious that signiﬁcant potentials for energy savings exist. Of
these, three have been identiﬁed as most important.
First, increased recovery of excess heat from electricity production by diesel engines could supersede
oil consumption for heating. There are signiﬁcant diﬀerences among settlements in how well excess
heat is being utilised in DH grids. This suggests a saving potential in settlements with low heat
recovery ratios. However, establishing or extending DH grids is expensive in Greenland given the
geological conditions. Therefore, projects need to be evaluated case-by-case.
Second, optimisations in the design and operation of diesel engines could prove beneﬁcial. Better
adaption of engine sizes to the load conditions in a settlement would make these operate closer to
their optimal eﬃciency point. Similar eﬀects can be achieved by using small battery banks, allowing
engines to operate in cycle charging mode, i.e switching between being turned oﬀand operating at
optimal eﬃciency.
Third, upgrading the thermal properties of old buildings is thought to have vast saving potential.
Building standards have increased signiﬁcantly over the last decade, though old buildings are often
neglected and continue to consume excessive amounts of heat. Retroﬁtting the old buildings with
more insulation and better windows would probably lead to the highest energy savings among the
points discussed here. However, this saving potential is outside Nukissiorﬁits limits of supply.
Evaluating the feasibility of saving potentials requires better data than presently at hand and
is often a very case-speciﬁc task. However, such measures should be examined thoroughly and
benchmarked against oil superseding through investments in renewable energy using the same cost-
beneﬁt approach as presented in this thesis.
6.1.3
Temporal demarcation and mutual exclusiveness of options
The options of this study have been formulated as mutually exclusive pathways of future energy
systems within the settlements. Though useful for the purpose of comparing the options, this for-
mulation may not reﬂect reality well, since energy systems are usually dynamic in the way they
adapt to changes of boundary conditions such as demand, fuel prices and costs of technologies.
Taking the example of Nuuk, the model is constructed in such a way that deciding for option 1.1
(tunnel only) precludes the other two options. However, several other future pathways exist, some
of which are combinations of the three analysed options. For instance, it is possible to construct the
new tunnel between ISTA and KANG (which is included in all options) for commissioning ultimo
2024 and expanding the hydropower capacity gradually as demand increases. Such strategy could
prove to be more cost-eﬀective than any of the three options investigated.
Another disadvantage of the rigid temporal demarcation applied in this study is that it may lead
to suboptimal designs of hybrid systems. To exemplify this point, consider the hybrid systems

6.2
Methodology of socio-economic analysis
This study applies the methods of socio-economic analysis to carry out a cost-beneﬁt analysis.
However, some of the points usually required by national guidelines for carrying out such analysis
have been omitted in this thesis [50, 52, 49]. These will be discussed brieﬂy in the following
6.2.1
Distributional eﬀects
The results of this thesis are presented as aggregated values of net beneﬁts to society. Thus, the
question of who gains and who loses in a certain option has not been addressed. However, due
to the uniform pricing system of energy in Greenland, it can be said that most beneﬁts from re-
duced cost in energy supply accrue to Nukissiorﬁit in the ﬁrst place. These beneﬁts may be either
redistributed through reductions in energy prices or reinvested. In both cases, this results in a real-
location of money from the settlement where a proﬁtable project is undertaken to other settlements.
An essential point to make is that the results of this study can only be obtained if consumers
are incentivised to make choices that maximise overall beneﬁts. Very concretely, this means, for
instance, that consumers in locations with excess renewable production (especially hydropower)
should be incentivised to install electric heating if the costs of doing so are lower than the beneﬁts.
This requires the price of electric heating to be lower than that of oil heating. This is not always the
case today, resulting in undesired eﬀects, such as the hydropower plant of Ilulissat having around
30 GWh of wasted potential. For future hydropower projects to obtain the results presented in this
study, it is crucial to establish a pricing scheme that creates incentives to utilise the production
potential in the most optimal way.
6.2.2
Non-monetised eﬀects
Every investment in renewable energy has eﬀects on people, nature and society. Some of these can
be easily monetised, as done in previous chapters, while others can not. This section discusses the
non-monetised eﬀects which are thought to be of most signiﬁcance.
Impact on demographic trends. Investments carried out in relatively small communities inﬂuence
peoples perception of the opportunities and future outlooks of that particular settlement. It might
even change their incentive to either stay or leave, thus inﬂuencing the demographic trend of the
settlement. This eﬀect is much discussed in Greenlandic politics and plays an important role in
decision making. Even though it is diﬃcult to conclude on the importance of this eﬀect, it seems
likely that the eﬀect is more pronounced for small settlements and for settlements that experience

depopulation, such as Nanortalik and Qasigiannguit. Therefore, investments in such places might
have beneﬁts not considered in this thesis.
Inﬂuence on the labour market. Especially large energy investments, such as hydropower projects,
inﬂuence the relatively small Greenlandic labour market. It is likely, that workforce from outside
the country needs to be imported in order to secure the supply of labour, whereas smaller hybrid
projects might draw on local labour. Furthermore, hybrid systems are likely to require more staﬀ
for operation and maintenance compared to hydropower plants, which creates jobs in the long term.
Eﬀects on local environment. Starting with visual eﬀects, there are signiﬁcant diﬀerences in the
visual expressions of renewable energy technologies. Whereas hydropower plants often are located
outside the settlements and integrated in the fells, solar PV and wind turbines have a higher visual
presence. Whether this is perceived as positive or negative varies from individual to individual.
Though challenging to monetise, such eﬀects should be taken into consideration when undertak-
ing investments in renewable energy. In general, the Greenlandic people’s perception of renewable
energy seems to be mostly positive, with only limited Nimby-eﬀects1 observed. However, this is
purely based on the (biased) observations of the author through conversations with locals. Another
beneﬁt not monetised in this study are reductions in noise and local pollution from diesel generators
when investing in renewable energy. These may be signiﬁcant in many Greenlandic settlements, as
power plants are often located close to housing.
Security of supply. All renewable energy projects reduce Greenland’s dependence on imported fossil
fuels. This has several beneﬁts. Most importantly, the economy is less exposed to the volatility
of global oil prices, reducing uncertainties in future budgeting. Secondly, security of supply has
geopolitical beneﬁts, reducing dependency on imports from other nations. Lastly, the energy sys-
tem of Greenland becomes less vulnerable towards unpredicted events in the oil supply chain.
Technical expertise within Nukissiorﬁit. Investing in renewable energy leads to acquisition of com-
petencies to Nukissiorﬁit and its suppliers. This eﬀect is especially pronounced for immature tech-
nologies, such as wind power. These competencies are of high value when considering Nukissiorﬁits
ambitions with regard to renewable energy. Though such value is diﬃcult to quantify in monetary
terms, it should not be disregarded in decision making.

not represent the real conditions Nukissiorﬁit is facing when ﬁnancing project.

6.3
Uncertainties and risks
Besides the uncertainties addressed in the sensitivity analysis, other factors entailing incertitude
should be discussed. Generally, risks associated with investments in renewable energy are project-
speciﬁc and should be treated case-by-case. However, some general risks can be mentioned here.
Uncertainty of hydrological potential. The hydrological potential and reservoir size applied in the
calculations of this study are based on measurements and estimates with inherent uncertainties
[19, 75, 18]. However, these parameters are decisive for the performance of a hydropower plant and
thus need to be readdressed before an investment decision.
Increased unpredictability of weather patterns due to climate change. Research suggests that the
consequences of human caused climate change are likely to be more pronounced in the Arctic com-
pared to other regions [76]. Measurements show that the increase in average temperature is higher
in the Arctic region compared to global averages [77]. A continuation of this trend will, ceteris
paribus, decrease the heating demand of buildings.
Furthermore, an increase in average temperature might change weather patterns of locations in
Greenland.
Three concrete examples of the eﬀect of such changes can be mentioned.

changes in how foggy or cloudy a location is have impact on the intensity of solar radiation. Sec-

Since renewable energy technologies are inherently exposed to changes in weather patterns, in-

Uncertainties related to energy demands. The scenarios for energy demand considered in this study
are all relatively moderate, following long-term population trends. The highest annual growth rate
is seen for Nuuk at 1.8 %. However, there is a chance for more rapid changes, e.g. due to unforeseen
migration patterns, economic development and electriﬁcation of transport. Furthermore, the ﬁnal
heat demand of settlements is subject to high uncertainty, as shown in section 3.2. It may be argued
that the scenario formulation of energy demands does not capture this suﬃciently. The above could
have been dealt with by conducting a dedicated sensitivity analysis applying greater variations in
energy demands. In retrospect, not including such analysis is a weakness of this study.
6.4
Generalisability
Before concluding on the ﬁndings of this thesis, it is relevant to discuss the generalisability of the
methods applied and the results generated.
Beginning with the methods, the thesis shows that the applied combination of energy system mod-
elling and socio-economic analysis generates valuable inputs to evidence-based decision making
regarding renewable energy investments. This requires accurate data processing in the beginning,
comprehensive sensitivity analysis in the end as well as iterations between all phases. Besides being
applicable to other options within Greenland, the developed model structure can be adapted to
studies on energy systems with similar characteristics; i.e. isolated island systems with diverse
renewable resources. Though prominent in Arctic regions, such systems are found in many parts
of the world.
Moving on to the results, these are undoubtedly less generalizable than the methods. It is, for
instance, wrong to conclude upon this thesis that hydropower is a better investment than other
renewable sources or that wind power is infeasible in Greenland. It all comes down to the three
questions of i) how much does it cost? ii) how much energy is produced? and iii) what other en-
ergy production is substituted? The answer to all of these questions varies by orders of magnitude
between the settlements of Greenland. Thus, there is no generic answer to the question of what an
optimal hybrid system for Greenland looks like.
Having pointed out the limits of generalisability, there are still some results that appear to be
universal in a Greenlandic context. These will be highlighted in the conclusion.

Conclusion
The objective of this thesis is to prioritise diﬀerent options for investments in renewable energy in
Greenland. This is achieved by combining energy system modelling and socio-economic analysis
in a software architecture. Subsequently, this architecture is applied to seven concrete renewable
energy projects across the towns of Nuuk, Nanortalik, Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit.
A systematic approach to data collection and validation has proven important to acquire model
inputs of high quality. One major challenge is to establish the heating demand of settlements,
which is addressed by using a series of both machine learning and analytical approaches. Unable
to conﬁrm the accuracy of these approaches, it is decided to use an average per capita heat con-
sumption.
The energy system modelling tool HOMER is applied to both simulate the options for which capac-
ities are predetermined and to optimise capacities for which this is not the case. Despite weaknesses
in HOMERs approach to modelling hydropower and electric heating, the tool is concluded to be
suitable for modelling Greenlandic power and heat systems. The simulation results are used as
inputs in the socio-economic spreadsheet model, evaluating costs and beneﬁts of all options. Dif-
ferent economic indicators are applied to compare the performance of the options.
Results show that the option of extending the hydropower plant in Nuuk by an additional reservoir
and 55 MW new capacity obtains the highest score in all regarded parameters - both economic
and environmental - and should thus be highly prioritised. The investment generates a ∆NPV of
1422 M DKK at an internal rate of return of 15 % while superseding 39 M litres of oil consumption
annually in the base case.
Regarding the options for Nanortalik, a wind-diesel hybrid system achieves better economic results
than the proposed hydropower plant - though both being economically infeasible in the base case.
Due to the considerable diﬀerences in size of investments, the hybrid plant generates losses of 3 M
DKK during the analysis period versus 157 M DKK of losses for the hydropower plant.
The energy systems of Aasiaat and Qasigiannguit are modelled simultaneously due to the proposed
hydropower plant being able to supply both towns. It is shown that individual hybrid systems
(wind, solar, battery, diesel), optimised to the conditions in the two towns, achieve better eco-
nomic performance compared to the hydropower plant. However, the total renewable energy share
of 6 % for the hybrid systems is considerably lower than the 72 % achieved with a hydropower plant.
A thorough sensitivity analysis reveals high sensitivity towards changes in CAPEX, socio-economic
discount rate and future oil prices, all of which inﬂuence the economic feasibility of options signiﬁ-
cantly. The conclusion of investing in an extension of the hydropower plant in Nuuk being superior
to all other options is shown to be robust.
It is concluded that the applied methodology provides decision makers with valuable and evidence-

results of this study as a basis for comparison.
7.1
Recommendations
1. Extending the hydropower plant of Nuuk should be prioritised. A conﬁguration including a
new tunnel and 55 MW new turbine capacity is consistently shown to be the best option. Further
investigations on the costs, technical conﬁguration and project execution should be commenced as
soon as possible. This includes an investigation on how much electric heating can be installed at
what cost.
2. In Nanortalik, a wind-diesel hybrid system should be prioritised over a hydropower plant. Pro-
ceeding with the wind-diesel project should happen with the intention to gain experience in wind
power and combating climate change, rather than for purely economic reasons.
3. In Aasiaat-Qasigiannguit, hybrid systems are economically proﬁtable and should be prioritised
over a hydropower plant if the latter is found to be mutually exclusive with extending hydropower
in Nuuk. If, however, high renewable energy shares are desired, these can only be achieved at
reasonable costs by establishing a hydropower plant.
4. The process of enhancing accessibility and quality of data within Nukissiorﬁit should be contin-
ued, especially concerning production data.
5. Hybrid systems (wind, solar, diesel) should be optimised for the lowest overall cost rather than
for highest renewable energy share. The strategy of building many hybrid systems with relatively
low RES is more proﬁtable than having few systems with high RESs, as it circumvents the need
for expensive storage and grid balancing.
6. Solar PV systems with limited capacities are proﬁtable investments in power systems supplied
by diesel generators. To avoid the need for energy storage, the installed capacities of such systems
should stay below the annual minimum load of the settlement - at least until costs of storage
solutions decrease signiﬁcantly. The possibility to rent large roof areas for installing solar PV in
towns should be investigated further, as this is shown to have several beneﬁts compared to using
ground mounted stands.
7. The feasibility of wind power is highly dependent on the local wind resource and the demand
of the considered settlement. Systems may be feasible in locations with relatively high mean wind
speeds. However, due to the limited experience with wind power in Greenland, such investments
are associated with high risks.
8. The political goals for reductions of oil imports and deployment of renewable energies should
be concretised, as the current formulation does not allow to quantify the contributions of options
towards these goals. Further, the goals should be extended to not only include the public energy
supply, as this results in suboptimal prioritisation of measures.

A.10
Validation and veriﬁcation of HOMER models
This appendix aims at illustrating the behaviour of the HOMER models as well as documenting
the process of model validation and veriﬁcation. These tasks are carried out continuously during
modelling work, which is why this appendix is only capable of providing examples on them.
Figure 12 shows results of the model for the Nuuk BAU option with hourly values in the base year.
The illustration is made in a HOMER tool for post-processing results after simulation and before
exporting these results to the socio-economic model. This is a valuable step in order to verify model
behaviour. For instance, the ﬁgure shows how the load for light and power (grey) generally exceeds
the hydropower production deﬁned by the monthly discharge pattern (red). This is because the
BAU-option for Nuuk is constrained to sustainable discharge level of 225 GWh/year, which is lower
than the electrical demand in the base year. The operation of the diesel engines is illustrated by
the green line and the state of charge of the virtual battery imitating the hydropower reservoir (see
section 3.3.2.2) is shown by the blue line. Investigating these production patterns is helpful in the
process of verifying model behaviour. Validation of the annual fuel consumption against known
data for the base year is done subsequently.
Figure 12: Analysis of the hourly HOMER outputs for the BAU option of Nuuk in the base year.
Performed in the HOMER tool Time Series Detail Analysis.
