Summary (English)
The aim of this thesis, is the exploration of how certain phenomena from clas-
sical dynamical systems theory are expressed in tropical geometry.
Tropical geometry is a variant af algebraic geometry, which, in the context
of systems of polynomial ordinary diﬀerential equations, can be used to split
the systems into to smaller subsets that evolve on vastly diﬀerent time scales,
thus simplifying the analysis.
The starting point is a transformation of the
diﬀerential equation system without use of results from tropical geometry, and
thus no knowledge of this is required.
In chapter 2 important result from geometric singular perturbation theory is
presented, assuming knowledge of basic dynamical systems theory. Follow-
ing this chapter 3 introduces tropical geometry in the context of dynamical
systems, and important concepts such as tropical ﬂow, tropical manifolds og
tropical equilibria are deﬁned. In chapter 4 tropical versions of speciﬁc dynam-
ical phenomena such as saddle-node and Hopf bifurcations and folded critical
manifolds are deﬁned. It is further shown that these correspond with analo-
gous phenomena in classical dynamical systems. Finally chapter 5 presents an
analysis of a 3D biological model from the literature, and it is shown how the
results from preceding chapters can be used to analyse the model and obtain
results which agree well with those from literature.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Natural phenomena from the largest to the smallest scale, and in as vastly diﬀer-
ent areas as biology and economics are described using diﬀerential equations.
Our understanding of the world around us, as well as within us, depends cru-
cially on our ability to model and analyse these phenomena with diﬀerential
equations. Since their inauguration in the mid 17th century, diﬀerential equa-
tions have been a major branch of applied as well as pure mathematics[1], and
the study of these has developed in to a rich area of its own called dynami-
cal systems. Our understanding of the area has grown vastly since the 17th
century, and many general results, especially for systems of a few dimensions,
have been developed. However, when a problem contains more than a few
variables and parameters, general results are rare, and analysing speciﬁc cases
analytically is often diﬃcult if not insurmountable. The rapid growth of com-
puter power alleviates this problem to an extend, making numerical analyses
both fast and precise. Many phenomena, however, escape the limited precision
of even modern computers. An important example of this is the phenomenon
of a canard explosion. In this a system modelled by diﬀerential equations, de-
velops oscillations which grow rapidly in amplitude within a parameter range
to small to consistently detect on a computer. Further, bifurcations in diﬀer-
ential equations, where a system undergoes a fundamental qualitative change
in character, are diﬃcult to determine and characterize by numerical meth-
ods. Therefore further development in the analysis of systems of diﬀerential
equations is needed.
One such development is the introduction of tropical
==PAGE:14==
2
Introduction
geometry to dynamical systems analysis. In simpliﬁed terms tropical geome-
try is the study of polynomial equations by substituting addition of two terms
with taking the max of these, and replacing multiplication with addition[16].
In recent years tropical geometry has been applied to the study of diﬀerential
equation models especially in biology[5, 19, 20]. The focus has however largely
been on studying speciﬁc models and phenomena, rather then on developing
of a more general picture of how classical dynamical systems phenomena are
expressed in tropical geometry. This thesis will aim to do just that.
This thesis presents important results on the relation between the ﬂow, equi-
libria and bifurcations of a diﬀerential equation system and its tropical coun-
terpart. Tropical models for saddle-node and Hopf bifurcations are developed,
and shown to yield analogous bifurcations in the corresponding diﬀerential
equations system. Further, the notion of a tropical manifold is deﬁned and
shown to correspond with an invariant slow manifold. This is important, as
the existence of a slow manifold allows for a dimensional reduction of the sys-
tem. The analysis further reveals that dynamics in the system of diﬀerential
equations can be "hidden" in the tropical system. Finally the tools developed
are applied to the analysis of a 3D model of beta-cell, insulin, and glucose ki-
netics from an academic paper. Here the tropical form of equations is used to
sequentially reduce the problem determining equilibria and bifurcation values,
and results are shown to agree well.
Tropical geometry is a vast area, and this thesis will not go into its intricacies.
Instead the transformation of diﬀerential equation to tropical geometry is pre-
sented as a transformation in its own right, and results on this are presented
without the use of theory from tropical geometry. We shall however stick to
the term tropical geometry throughout.

3.1.3
Tropical equilibria
From the previous section it is obvious that to have a tropical equilibria, we
must have a tropical line of sliding type. We can therefore consider Filippov-
and tropical sliding lines when looking for equilibria. Given that the ﬂow on
a Filippov sliding line can not change direction, without it intersecting another
tropical line, it can not contain tropical equilibria.
We will therefore only
consider tropical manifolds when deﬁning equilibria. We note, however, that
equilibria could exists elsewhere. We have
Deﬁnition 3.10 (Tropical equilibrium). A tropical equilibrium is an isolated point
on a tropical manifold at which two subdominant monomials with tropical coeﬃcients
with opposite sign intersect. Here isolated means that there exists a neighbourhood of
the equilibrium on the tropical manifold where the subdominant monomials are equal.
■
Remark 3.6. The criteria of the equilibrium being isolated in deﬁnition 3.10
assures that the tropical manifold, can not be parallel to the intersection of the
subdominant monomials.
We have three types of tropical equilibria: stable node, unstable node and
saddle point. We have
Deﬁnition 3.11 (Stable tropical equlibrium). A stable tropical equilibrium is a trop-
ical equilibrium on an attracting tropical manifold at which the ﬂows of the subdominant
monomials point towards the equilibrium.
■
Deﬁnition 3.12 (Tropical saddle). A tropical saddle is one of
a) A tropical equilibrium on an attracting tropical manifold at which the ﬂows of
the subdominant monomials points away from the equilibrium.
b) A tropical equilibrium on a repelling tropical manifold at which the ﬂows of the
subdominant monomials points towards the equilibrium.
■
Deﬁnition 3.13 (Unstable tropical node). An unstable tropical node is a tropical
equilibrium on a repelling tropical manifold at which the ﬂows of the subdominant
monomials points away from the equilibrium.
■
Illustrations of the tropical equilibria can be seen in ﬁg. 3.7.
We will now show, that the tropical equilibria perturb to equilibria with the
same stability for 휀> 0 suﬃciently small.

Conclusion
In this chapter we have deﬁned tropical systems, and the tropicalization of a
system of polynomial ODEs, and further described the ﬂow on the piecewise
linear parts of the a tropical system given by the tropical monomials. We have
described several cases for the non-smooth boundaries between the tropical
monomials, and deﬁned two types of ﬂow on these. Most notably we have
deﬁned the notions of tropical manifolds with tropical sliding ﬂow and tropical
equilibria. We have shown that the former correspond with critical manifolds
in the smooth system and the latter with equilibria with the same stability.
In the next chapters we will employ these results in analysing planer systems of
polynomial ODEs. We will examine a case analogous to the one described here
where tropical equilibria live on a single tropical manifold, and there determine
a tropical form of a saddle-node bifurcation. Further we examine the case of two
equilibria on two intersection tropical manifolds, and there determine several
important phenomena.

Conclusion
This work has examined the relation between dynamical systems theory and
tropical geometry.
Starting from basic deﬁnitions of tropical ﬂow tropical
equilibria, and tropical manifolds, several important results have been obtained.
It has been shown how tropical ﬂow and tropical equilibria and their stability
persist in the ODE system. The notion of a tropical manifold has been shown
to correspond with an invariant slow manifold for suﬃciently small values of
휀> 0. This is an important result, as slow manifolds allow for a reduction of the
model. Further the tropical sliding ﬂow on tropical manifolds correspond with
the slow ﬂow. A tropical model for a Sn bifurcation has been developed and
shown to correspond with a SN bifurcation in the ODE system. A model of a
folded tropical has been shown to correspond with a generic folded singularity
in the ODE system. This model further exhibits both SN, Hopf, and homoclinic
bifurcations, as well as canard cycles. Subsequently it has without proof been
proposed how this model may be modiﬁed to also exhibit relaxation cycles.
Lastly the results have been applied to the analysis of a 3D biological model
from literature. The analysis exempliﬁed how a transformation to a tropical
system allows for sub sequential reduction of the system, thus determining
bifurcation value and type of resulting equilibria, and the results are seen to
agree well with the ones from literature.
This thesis has only scratched the surface of dynamical systems in tropical
geometry, and a lot more work is needed. Besides determining the basic forms
