Abstract
In recent years climate changes has caused more extreme rain events. The city of
Odense is also aﬀected by this and has seen an increase in ﬂood events. With Odense
currently undergoing city development, the potential for increased ﬂood risk has to
be analyzed. In this study the impact of city development on ﬂood risk in Odense,
and potential adaptation measures to reduce this has been investigated.
Two MIKE URBAN (MU) models were provided for the analysis of Odense. Model
1 used runoﬀmodel A, model 2 used runoﬀmodel B. To include runoﬀfrom green
areas in Odense runoﬀmodel B has to be used. The default initial inﬁltration storage
in MU was increased from 1 mm to 10 mm to test the eﬀect. The ﬂood simulations
were conducted with 4 rain series with return periods of half a year, 2 years, 10
years and 100 years. Implementing city development in a MU model showed to have
limited impact on the ﬂood results on a large area as Odense.
Three climate adaptation measures were implemented and tested in a MU model
in a sub-catchment of Odense, local retention, retention basins and expansion of the
drainage network. Local retention was the best solution for reducing spill during
T000.5 and T002 with 78% and 31% reduced spill respectively. Storage basins and
expansion of the drainage network impacted the longer return periods events. Local
retention had insigniﬁcant impact on these events. Combining local retention and
storage basins was seen as the best solution due to signiﬁcant decrease in spill volume
for all return periods.
The 1D drainage model provided a general overview of Odense and was used
to identify areas with ﬂood problems. The 1D model was not used to implement
speciﬁc adaptation measures. This would require a more detailed investigation of the
catchment, possibly with the addition of a 2D surface model.

1. INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
In the last 10-20 years climate changes have gained increased interest as the impacts
becomes more evident with rising temperatures and more extreme weather situations
(Zolnikov, 2017). The ever changing climate requires people, cites and countries
to adapt accordingly. Urban areas are heavily impacted by climate changes. The
increase in large rain events combined with old and often too small drainage networks
increases the ﬂood risk in these areas (Viavattene and Ellis, 2013). In addition,
urban growth further raises the issue that new solutions to deal with rain water
are needed (Kinzig, 2015). Investments in climate adaptation solutions can be
comprehensive and expensive, however proper investments could easily prove to be
worth it in the long run (Baron and Petersen, 2015).
The city of Odense is also aﬀected by climate changes and in recent years the
city has witnessed an increase in ﬂood events (VandCenterSyd, 2017). Through
various climate adaptation projects, Odense is trying to address the ﬂood issues in
advance. The local utility company in Odense, VandCenterSyd (VCS), has created
ﬂood maps, storm surge maps and risk maps for the city (Odense Kommune, 2017).
These maps displays a high ﬂood risk in Odense for rain events with return periods
longer than 5 years, partially due to the high damage costs in the city center.
In recent years the population in Odense has increased (Odense kommune, 2016).
As a results the municipality of Odense has started two major projects, "Letbanen"
(stage 1 and 2) and urban city development with 10,000 new residencies. These
projects are scheduled to ﬁnish in 2025 (Odense kommune, 2015). In the city center
the development is focused on transforming areas, like old industry buildings, into
urban areas with new residencies. In the suburbs of Odense, city growth is planned
as part of the city development (Odense kommune, 2015).
The planned city development in Odense could further increase the ﬂood risks
in these areas due to increased pressure on the drainage network. Some of the city
development areas are located where ﬂooding is already a problem. Increasing the
imperviousness through city development could enhance this issue. Damage costs in
these areas are also expected to increase as a result of changing old industry to new
residencies. Due to this climate adaptation has to be considered together with the
city development in Odense.

1. INTRODUCTION
1.1
Aim
This study aims to investigate the impact of city development in Odense on ﬂood
modelling results and ﬁnd potential climate adaptation measures to limit the ﬂood
risks in Odense after completion of the current city development projects.
Through comparison of 3 diﬀerent MIKE URBAN (MU) models, the best model
is selected to test the eﬀects of city development on a large scale area. Diﬀerent
climate adaptation measures are tested in smaller scale to ﬁnd solutions that is able
to reduce the ﬂood risk in Odense. These implementations have to take the new
city development into account and also the statement made by Odense municipality
that rain water should be considered a resource rather than a problem (Odense
kommune, 2015). The diﬀerent climate adaptation measures serves as general
suggestions to what can be done in Odense to reduce the ﬂood risk, and also what
the user should be aware of when implementing the diﬀerent measures.
In summary, the aim of this project is
• Identify the runoﬀmodel best suited for ﬂood modelling in Odense.
• Investigate the default value for the initial inﬁltration parameter in MU models.
• Determine the impact of city development on ﬂood modelling in a large area
like Odense.
• Present general suggestions for climate adaptation measures in a catchment in
Odense.
• Implement the suggested climate adaptation measures in a MU model and
investigate the eﬀects these.
• Present a solution to what should be done prospectively for climate adaptation
in Odense.

2.2
Flood management
In cooperation with VCS, Odense municipality has created a climate adaptation
plan, which includes a ﬂood map, a storm surge map and a value map (Odense
kommune, 2013). The value map is shown in ﬁgure 3, the ﬂood map and storm
surge map are found in appendix A. The ﬂood map displays the potential ﬂooding
from rain events with diﬀerent return periods. The return periods shown are 5 year,
10 year, 20 year, 50 year and 100 year events. The diﬀerent shades of blue indicate
when ﬂooding occurs. Areas with light blue will ﬂood during all events while areas
with the darkest blue only will ﬂood during the 100 year event. The ﬂood model is
limited to the area inside the dotted orange line. The ﬂooding outside this line is
caused by natural depressions in the terrain.

Odense Fjord makes Odense vulnerable to storm surges in the harbor area. Large
storm surges can also aﬀect the areas close to the two streams running through
Odense. Odense Fjord has been identiﬁed as one of 10 areas in Denmark with high
risk for economical losses from damages caused by ﬂooding in the future (Odense
kommune, 2013; Odense Kommune, 2017). As recently as October 29 2017, Odense
was hit by a storm surge where water levels in the Fjord increased by 168 centimeters.
Due to the improvements Odense has done to prevent ﬂoods only minor damages
occurred (Mette Richardt, 2017; Vandcenter Syd, 2011).
In ﬁgure 3 Odense is divided into 100 m × 100 m cells to create a value map. The
value map shows the damage costs (in DKK) if ﬂooding occurs in a cell. The highest
cost cells are mainly located in the city center because of the housing density. The
value map does not take into account whether or not ﬂooding occurs in a given cell.
The value map can be combined with the ﬂood map and storm surge map to create
two risk maps (appendix A). The risk maps combines the ﬂooding potential with the
damage cost to ﬁnd the most vulnerable areas in Odense. During storm surges the
highest risk is around the harbor where both the chance of ﬂooding and the damage
costs are high. The risk map for rain events shows the highest risk in the city center.
The ﬂood chance is low, but the high damage costs makes the area very vulnerable
to ﬂooding.
2.3
City development
The municipality of Odense has a 12 year plan which is updated every third year.
The current plan covers 2016-2028 and replaced the previous plan from 2013-2025.
Both plans contain objectives to implement city development in Odense, however,
there is one big diﬀerence. Previously, city development in Odense should consist of

70% urban transformation (housing on an already used area like a parking lot or old
industrial area) and 30% should be urban growth (new housing on green areas which
were previously untouched). With the new municipality plan, this objective has
changed. The new objective is to only use urban transformation for city development,
already planned projects are not aﬀected by this (Odense kommune, 2015).
From now through 2028 Odense municipality has planned to build 10,000 new
residencies divided into three categories 1) Apartment, 2) Close/low and 3) Open/low
where "Apartment" is apartments and oﬃce buildings, "Close/low" is townhouses
and "Open/low" is townhouses with gardens. Of the 10,000 planned residencies
6,100 are already locally planned (the tender and/or construction process has begun)
(Odense kommune, 2015). All urban growth projects that are not already locally
planned are postponed for now due to the new objective in the municipality plan.
The remaining 3,900 residencies are planned but the projects has yet to be started.
All projects are divided into 3 priorities. Priority 1 is build from 2016-2019, priority
2 is build from 2020-2023 and priority 3 is build from 2024-2027. Most residencies
have priority 1 or 2. The planned construction areas can be seen in ﬁgure 4. Areas
inside the green circle are subject to city transformation, areas outside are planned
city growth. On top of the planned residencies, there is a buﬀer of 500 where the
placement and speciﬁc project are not yet known (Odense kommune, 2015).

created a set of guidelines to limit the water volume directed to the drainage network
from diﬀerent area types (table 2) (Vandcenter Syd, 2011). These guidelines are

As shown in table 2 the limits in the new wastewater plan are more strict than in

in certain areas (Odense kommune, 2015). This could be areas in the city center
where the imperviousness is higher than the limit and during city development, it
might not be possible or economically feasible to reduce the imperviousness lower
than it already is.

center consists of many small catchments and pipes, this makes it hard to see every

The two streams (blue lines in ﬁgure 5) that runs through Odense are modeled
as a series of links. The stream works as an outlet for basins, WWTP’s and other
parts of the network. Some of the basins along the stream are natural depressions in
green areas which can be used for storage during rain events. All nodes in the model
are set to spilling nodes as it simulates the process of discharge from the drainage
system to the surface well (Toke and Thrysøe, 2015). The MU model does not
cover the entire catchment described, however the areas not covered are green areas
or roads that would not have an signiﬁcant impact on the ﬂood model. Rain on the
green areas would inﬁltrate and rain on the roads would run oﬀthe roads to green
areas or ditches along the road.
2.4.1
Model A vs model B
Runoﬀmodel A uses a ﬁxed inﬁltration of 100% in pervious areas. In model A two
minor losses are included, the initial loss and a factor to account for the evapotran-
spiration (DHI, 2016b). In model A, the runoﬀcontribution from impervious areas
is described by a time-area curve which is based on the area, shape of the catchment
and the time of concentration (TOC). When the simulation time equals TOC all of
the impervious area contributes to the runoﬀ(Davidsen, 2016). In model B runoﬀ
from pervious areas is accounted for. The inﬁltration in green areas changes over
time using Horton’s inﬁltration (DHI, 2016b). This means that inﬁltration is high in
the beginning and gradually decreases because the green areas become saturated.
In both model A and model B, there are two types of areas, impervious and
pervious. In model B these two area types are split into 2 and 3 subtypes respectively.
For the impervious areas, there are ﬂat and steep, where ﬂat is used to describe
roads, parking lots and similar ﬂat surfaces, steep is mainly used to describe roofs
on houses. The pervious areas are divided into low, medium and high to diﬀerentiate
between green areas (DHI, 2016b). Table 3 outlines the overall diﬀerences between
model A and model B.

2.4.2
Model changes
The model provided by Roland was compared with the model from VCS. The
impervious areas in the two models were diﬀerent in some areas. This diﬀerence
can be attributed to how the impervious areas were determined. The model from
Roland used GIS layers with all buildings and roads in Odense to determine the
impervious areas. In the model from VCS some of the impervious areas were changed
due to already implemented climate adaptation measures. Generally the changes
were made due to VCS’s knowledge of the area. For the models to be comparable,
the impervious areas have to be identical. The impervious areas in Rolands model
were changed to match those of the VCS model as they are assumed to be accurate.
In the model in ﬁgure 5, there are some very large green areas. In model B where
the runoﬀfrom green areas is accounted for, the runoﬀfrom these large green areas
contributes with large water volumes to the drainage network in certain nodes,
which resulted in large spill in these areas. It is unrealistic that all runoﬀfrom these
catchments would end up in the drainage network. The rain would accumulate in
natural depressions following the surface slope. The runoﬀcontribution from green
areas larger than 10,000 m2 and with a imperviousness less than 10% was limited to
maximum 3 times the impervious area in that speciﬁc area. A total of 307 catchments
met this requirement. The area that should be excluded from the model was stored
in the parameter "Pervious Low" instead of "Pervious Medium" as it was initially
(table 3). The initial inﬁltration for the low areas was than set to 10 m instead of
1 mm to remove the water from the model.

3.2
Eﬀects of city development on ﬂood risk
The planned city development in Odense has to be implemented in one of the three
models. The results from the node and catchment comparison suggests that model 3
would be a good choice for further use. The ﬁxed inﬁltration in model 1 is not realistic
in a real world scenario. The inﬁltration rate should depend on the saturation of the
ground (Liu et al., 2011). Using varying inﬁltration as in model 2 and 3 leads to
estimation of more parameters and increases the uncertainty regarding inﬁltration.
Estimating the parameters incorrectly could give misleading results (Davidsen,
2016). Because of this uncertainty all the default parameters in MU has been used
except the inﬁltration storage, which was suspected to be too low.
For short return periods (T000.5 and T002) runoﬀfrom green areas is not expected
to signiﬁcantly impact the ﬂood results. Model 1 is assumed to correctly model
shorter rain events as it is created by VCS who have experience with various rain
events in Odense. Model 1 and 3 yield similar results from the short return periods.
In the two long return periods, model 2 and 3 have 2 to 10 times higher spill in all
areas. The diﬀerence is lowest in areas with high average imperviousness. Model 3
is selected as the best solution for ﬂood modeling in Odense because the model is
comparable to model 1 in the short return periods. The diﬀerences during the longer
return periods were expected, as the impact of runoﬀfrom green areas increases with
longer return periods.

4. IDENTIFYING VULNERABLE SUB-CATCHMENTS
4
Identifying vulnerable sub-catchments
After implementation of city development in model 3, the climate adaptation measures
has to be implemented in the model. The objective is to successfully implement
and solve some of the ﬂooding issues in the most vulnerable areas in Odense. The
task of implementing climate adaptation in all of Odense is too comprehensive for
this project. Vulnerable areas on a sub-catchment level have to be identiﬁed. These
sub-catchments are analyzed separately to ﬁnd areas in the catchment that causes
ﬂooding. This should provide enough information to suggest solutions for climate
adaptation in the area.
4.1
Analyzing sub-catchments
There are three diﬀerent ways to consider the spilling from the 27 sub-catchments,
1) total spill volumes, 2) spill in mm per total area (as in ﬁgure 9), 3) spill in mm
per impervious area. Besides these three options, there is also the consideration to
include/exclude the stream nodes and which area to use, the geographical area or
the model area from MU (used in ﬁgure 9).
The total spill volume can not be used to compare the sub-catchments. Naturally
large catchments with many nodes will have the highest spill volumes. The total spill
volume can be used to ﬁnd the amount of water that has to be retained or inﬁltrated
in an area to avoid spilling.
The sub-catchments can be compared with the use of spill in mm either with total
area or with impervious area. Using only the impervious area would exclude green

Implementing climate adaptation in
MIKE URBAN
To reduce spilling in the selected area climate adaptation measures have to be
implemented. Three diﬀerent adaptation measures are to be tested separately and in
combination. The tested solutions are, local retention of the water through green
roofs and rainwater harvesting, creating storage basins, and increasing pipe capacity
through separate rainwater pipes. The area has already been subject to climate
adaptation. A separate rainwater pipe has been implemented and directs water from
several sub-catchments to the Harbor, which can be seen right below the compass in
ﬁgure 12. The rainwater pipe is only used during heavy storms and is regulated by
the water volume in the upstream system, when a certain threshold is reached the
pipe opens. During normal ﬂow the water is directed to Northwest WWTP. With
a rainwater pipe already in place in the area this solution is not tested separately.
Instead an expansion of the current rainwater pipe system is tested. In this section
the implementation and eﬀects of the diﬀerent climate adaptation measures are
described.
When implementing climate adaptation measures it is important that the model
reﬂect the eﬀects and not necessarily the exact physical properties. This applies to
e.g. rain water harvesting where, in the model, the initial wetting loss of an area is
increased instead of creating an actual basin. Implementation of the three solutions
is described in detail below.
5.0.1
Local retention through green roofs and rainwater harvesting
Local retention of rainwater can be done in many diﬀerent ways. Only the implemen-
tation and eﬀects of green roofs and rainwater harvesting were investigated. Other
solution that can be considered includes, but are not limited to; wetlands, on-street
raingardens, tree pits, swales and greening of roads (Melbourne Water, 2013).
As stated by the term local retention the eﬀects from these adaptation measures
can be seen in the area where they are implemented. A green roof is only eﬀective
on the house where it is implemented, however implementing local retention i a large
area may also positively eﬀect the downstream network. Green roofs and rainwater
storage is an eﬀective measure to handle rainwater from rain events with short return
periods (D. Berghage et al., 2013; Viola et al., 2017). During larger storms, the
initial loss they provide becomes irrelevant. Implementing green roofs and rainwater
storage is simple and can be done on most building (D. Berghage et al., 2013;
Viola et al., 2017). Certain buildings might not be suitable for green roofs or
rainwater harvesting. The eﬀectiveness of these local retention measures are also
dependent on the weather. During long wet periods with continuous rain the system
will be at full capacity and unable to reduce ﬂooding when a new rain event hits.

5.0.2
Retention basins
Retention basins can be natural basins or constructed basins, implementation of
these is simple in MU. A basin with the desired volume is created and connected
to the existing network through a weir. The basin is slowly emptied by an outlet
pipe. The outlet pipe can either be connected to a downstream node or an outlet,
simulating discharge to a stream like in Odense (Löwe, 2017).
Densely populated areas, like a city center, are not suitable for a natural basin as
space is limited. Constructing underground basins in the city can be very expensive.
Open green areas are optimal for basin placement where construction is relatively
simple and cheap. Retention basins should be placed in low lying areas where the
ﬂow from the network can happen naturally without the use of pumps.
Optimally a retention basin designed to avoid spilling in a speciﬁc return period
would not allow spilling in events smaller than this. This is not always the case
because the impact of the basins are centered around the area where it is connected
to the network. The water might not be able to get to the basin if the spilling occurs
upstream. Retention basins are also often used as overﬂow basins for larger return
periods, which makes them less eﬃcient in shorter return periods. An overﬂow basin
only reduces the spilling if the connected nodes spill and that might not always be
the case.
To ﬁnd the optimal locations for retention basins the catchment is divided into
three areas that all contain spill and have a suitable green area where a basin can be
placed. The green areas are all downstream in the network which allows natural ﬂow
to the basins (ﬁgure 13). The areas are naturally separated by the drainage network.
In each of the separated areas a basin is placed (blue basins in ﬁgure 13). The
middle area is located close to Odense sportspark which includes a hockey stadium,
an athletics area and several green areas such as football ﬁelds. The green areas
could be used to delay the spilling in this area. The two other areas both have a
small park area where the basin could be placed. With these downstream locations
the water can ﬂow naturally to the basins.
In MU overﬂow water is directed to the 3 basins by weirs. Two weirs are connected
to all three basins. This is to test the best case scenario with basins where as much
water as possible is directed in the basins. It would not be possible to implement this
exact solution in Odense, but suitable areas for a second basins could be investigated.
If it proves to be a good solution it might be worth consider in implementing. All
weirs have a width of 5 m, a discharge coeﬃcient of 0.40 and the crest level is 10 cm
below the ground level of the node. The three basins are supplied with a small outlet,

Expanding the drainage network
In the selected area there is a large separate drainage pipe which directs the water to
the harbor during heavy rain events. Due to this pipe already being implemented, the
option of creating another storm water pipe is not considered. However connecting
the basins to the separate network is investigated. Increasing pipe diameters was not
considered because the area is placed upstream and all pipe diameters down to the
WWTP would have to be increased for this option to work properly.
Expanding pipe diameter can be used to handle problems where a small pipe joins
with a larger pipe and there is spilling in the small upstream pipe. Increasing the pipe
diameter in an upstream pipe where the downstream pipe has the same diameter is
rarely relevant because the problem would just be moved further downstream. In such
scenarios the pipe diameter should often be increased throughout the downstream
network to obtain results. Expanding the drainage network with a separate network
is costly and is only relevant during rain events with return periods above 10 years.
The current rainwater pipe has a diameter of 1.8 m to 2.5 m and is controlled by
the water volume in a basin. The maximum ﬂow allowed to the separate network is
8 m3/s. The water is directed to an outlet in the harbor. The pipe supports more
downstream sub-catchments during these large rain events. The pipe is almost full
running during 10 and full running at a 100 year events, with limited spilling from
the nodes. Utilizing a separate drainage pipe of this size is equal to use a basin
because the available storage volume in the pipes is signiﬁcant compared to building
a basin.

7.1
Impacts of model choice and city development
When using MIKE URBAN for ﬂood modelling, two model types are available model
A and model B. Previously model A has been the model of choice but in recent years
model B has seen more and more use (Davidsen, 2016). Model A and model B each
have their own strengths and weaknesses when it comes to ﬂood modelling. The
main diﬀerences between model A and model B are outlined in table 3.
The main diﬀerence between model A and model B is that model A ignores runoﬀ
from green areas due to a ﬁxed inﬁltration of 100%. In Odense the diﬀerence between
the models were most signiﬁcant in the suburbs. In the densely built city center
the models yielded similar results. Model B can be used to model runoﬀfrom green
areas, however this requires good estimations of diﬀerent inﬁltration parameters like
initial inﬁltration storage.
Model B requires estimation of many diﬀerent parameters. The process of correctly
estimating all these parameters is challenging and could be very time consuming.
These parameters can be seen as both a strength and a weakness for the model. The
large parameter-set allows model B to make more individual estimations within a
small catchment. As mentioned in section 2.4.1 model B can diﬀerentiate between
ﬂat and steep impervious surfaces, which roughly means roads and buildings. This
feature is useful when local retention is implemented. Local retention measures are
limited by the area type e.g. greenroofs can only be implemented on buildings (steep
impervious areas) while other measures have diﬀerent limitations.
The large number of parameters could increas the uncertainty when using model
B. With the assumption that it is too time consuming and expensive to obtain
the parameters for model B through measurements, these parameters have to be
estimated or use the default parameters in MU. The results from section 3.1 indicate
that some of the default values in MU are too low. The initial inﬁltration storage in
MU is set to 1 mm, whereas Urban Drainage and Flood Control District
(2016) and Rossman (2015) suggests an initial inﬁltration up to 10 mm. Rossman
(2015) is a manual for a modelling program called "Storm Water Management Model"
(SWMM) which is similar to MU and widely used in USA.
Model A has fewer parameters than model B but regardless of which model is
used the parameters have to be estimated. The default parameters in MU might
be ﬁne to use in some instances but they can not be used without consideration if
they apply to the speciﬁc model area. Even though model B has more parameters
than model A it might be ﬁne to limit the number of used parameters as done in the
model created by Roland (table 4). The purpose of the model was to include runoﬀ
from green areas in the model. Individual estimation of every single parameter in

the model would not make sense because the model covers all of Odense. The actual
inﬁltration capacities are likely diﬀerent in the area because of its large size. Starting
to estimate them could improve the results in one area but worsen them in another
area. It is easier to keep an overview of the model if less parameters are used, and
in a model of this size individual estimated parameters would likely not inﬂuence
the model results by a lot. Estimating parameters would also require more extensive
knowledge of the area.
All the model results are generated from CDS rains. The use of CDS rains does not
account for recent rain events. For model A this is not a problem as the no runoﬀ
is generated from pervious areas. For model B it is diﬀerent. If two relatively small
rain events occur in quick succession the second event is more likely to generate
runoﬀfrom pervious areas. With the ground already saturated from the ﬁrst event,
runoﬀfrom green areas are more likely to occur in the second event. For the to be
tested properly a long term simulation of the model has to be done. This could be a
simulation of all rain events in Odense over the last 10 years. Results from a long
term simulation would most likely increase the diﬀerences between the models as
saturation of the ground becomes a factor in model B but not in model A.
There is no correct answer to which model is the best of the two. In scenarios
where green areas are less relevant, like in a densely build city, model A might be
the better choice as fewer parameters has to be estimated. In models where green
areas are expected to impact the model results model B would be the better choice.
The choice of model has to be determined on a project to project basis, however it
is recommended to consider model B as it also provides the opportunity to make
individual estimations within a catchment, a feature that could be useful in small
models where accurate results are important. For this project model B was applied
to be able to consider runoﬀfrom green areas. The model contains a lot of green
areas and they should not be neglected.
7.1.1
City development
The city development in Odense is planned to be implemented over the next 12 years.
The majority of the city development is deﬁned as city transformation. Transforming
and rebuilding old city areas to new ones does not necessarily result in increased
imperviousness because of a set of guidelines created by VCS. If these guidelines are
complied with the imperviousness in many of the city transformation areas is more
likely to decrease than increase. In the suburbs of Odense, the city development is
focused on city growth where green areas are transformed into residencies, here the
imperviousness is expected to increase (ﬁgure 4).
Initially it was expected that city development would impact the spill results and
investigating possible solutions in these development areas would be suﬃcient to
reduce spill volumes in Odense. The model results from section 3.2 showed that city

allowed for testing the overall impacts of the diﬀerent climate adaptation measures.
The implemented measures should not be considered as the optimal solutions for the
area, as a more detailed approach is required for this. It is recommended that an
even smaller area is selected for this, it could be the city development area to the

suitable locations for outlet pipes from basins. This also includes a more speciﬁc
design of the basins, where should they exactly be build and which control measures
should be installed to obtain the optimal spill reduction. It is also possible to test

current rainwater pipe. This slightly increased the spill reduction, but only for T100

increased the pressure on the rainwater pipe and resulted in increased spill further
downstream in the network. The avoided spill upstream is larger than the created
spill downstream.
Compared to the cost of expanding the drainage network the relative small spill

damage the water quality, however with the right control measures most of the dirty
water from the ﬁrst ﬂush should be treated at the WWTP.

7. DISCUSSION
Implementing basins and expanding the drainage network should be considered
together as a solution. If it is decided that basins are needed to reduce spill volumes
in Odense, the basin types determines if network expansion is necessary. Utilizing
retention basins in natural environments such as fascines and overground basins there
is no requirement for expanding the drainage network. Implementing a closed basin
might require a connection to the current network, but it should be investigated if it
is possible to create an outlet to Odense stream. This could prove to be cheaper and
avoid increase the pressure on the network in certain areas.
With the results from 3 implemented solutions it was determined that combining
two of the climate adaptation measures would yield the best results. The drainage
network was not expanded as this solution already is in place to deal with the
largest storms in Odense. The combination of local retention and basins were used
because they would deal with a signiﬁcant amount of the issues that are not dealt
with through the rainwater pipe. Local retention was good for short return periods
and basins proved useful for medium return periods. Utilizing these two adaptation
measures divides the responsibility of climate adaptation between the citizens and
the municipality. With the subsidies the citizens are responsible for implementing
local retention to prevent spill from short return periods. The municipality can then
focus on preventing ﬂooding from longer return periods by implementing basins and
possibly expanding the drainage network as they have already done in Odense.
The exact implementation of climate adaptation is not suggested, as a more detailed
analysis of the area is required to ﬁnd areas which requires the most attention and
where it is possible to implement local retention or basins. An obvious area of interest
is the city development to the east in ﬁgure 12. There are a large number of spilling
nodes in and around the area. A signiﬁcant amount of this spilling could be dealt
with locally if local retention is implemented. Other speciﬁc areas also have to be
identiﬁed. Dealing locally with rainwater also goes a long way towards the goal in
Odense of considering rainwater as a resource instead of a problem. Potentially the
rainwater could be used for for purposes like toilet ﬂushing and washing clothes.
The suggested basins might not be the best possible solution, as there is the
possibility of using fascines along the roads or using green areas as temporary natural
basins. A low laying football ﬁeld could be used as a temporary basin where the
rainwater is stored during larger rain events before inﬁltrating to the ground. This
carries the risk of such areas being ﬂooded for extensive periods of time. With the
use of natural inﬁltration methods the basins do not have to be connected to the
current drainage network and saves costs for expanding the drainage network. Further
investigation of the area is required before these solutions can be applied.

ﬁnd the best climate adaptation solutions in an area. With the right inputs PLASK

8. CONCLUSION
8
Conclusion
In recent years Odense city has experienced more ﬂood events due to climate changes,
this has forced the city to adapt accordingly. Currently Odense is in the middle of two
large city development projects which were assumed to increased the risk of ﬂooding
in the city. This study has provided an overview of the current ﬂood situation in
Odense, which models to use and what to be aware of. General suggestions to how
the drainage network could be improved to deal with the increased ﬂood risk has
also been done. This project has reached the following conclusions.
• Flood modelling in Odense requires the use of model B to be able to include
runoﬀfrom green areas during return periods above T002.
• Model A can be used for return periods less than T002 because the runoﬀfrom
green areas are limited regardless of model use. Model A can be considered to
use in highly impervious areas where runoﬀfrom green areas have no eﬀect on
the model.
• It is important to always consider the parameters used when ﬂood modelling.
The default value for initial inﬁltration storage (1 mm) generated signiﬁcantly
more runoﬀthan the 10 mm suggested by the literature. The diﬀerences were
most evident for shorter return periods.
• City development had a negligible impact on ﬂood hazard. However, this work
did not assess the eﬀects of changing ﬂood vulnerability due to increasing
values of the urban area. Increasing vulnerabilities might lead to increased
ﬂood risk, even if the hazard remains constant.
• In smaller screening areas it would be relevant to consider city development as
a factor for increased ﬂooding.
• Local retention proved to be the best solution for reducing spill volumes for
return periods shorter than T002. No speciﬁc local retention was implemented
as the best solution requires more detailed analysis of the area.
• To avoid spilling in longer return periods it is necessary to implement storage
volume in the form of basins and possibly combine it with expanding the
drainage network.
• Combining local retention and basins storage showed good results in spill
reduction for all rain events except T100. This combination was estimated
to be the best possible solution for the selected catchment and should be
investigated further.
49
==PAGE:58==
8. CONCLUSION
• The use of only a 1D model was suﬃcient to provide a general overview of
Odense and an indication on which adaptation measures were most eﬀective.
For speciﬁc implementation suggestions a more detailed analysis of a smaller
area is required. Preferably the 1D would be combined with a 2D model to be
able to estimate damage costs.
The general approach of using only a 1D model can be used for overall ﬂood
inspection of a large area and help implementing adaptation measures for longer
return periods, like the rainwater pipe that was already implemented. Implementation
of local retention requires a more detailed approach, because the exact location of the
adaptation measures have a large impact on the eﬃciency. More details of the area
could be obtained by doing a surface analysis and identify natural depressions and
other areas that might be vulnerable to ﬂooding. To include economical considerations
it requires a combination of the current 1D model with a 2D surface model. The
combination of 1D-2D models would make it possible to determine the damage costs
associated with ﬂooding. The PLASK tool could be used to do a cost beneﬁt analysis
of selected climate adaptation measures in an area.
