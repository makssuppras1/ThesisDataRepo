Abstract 
 
Building on previous experiments conducted at the Danish Shellfish Center (DSC), DTU Aqua, Nykøbing 
Mors, Denmark, and with the aim of providing both scientific support as well as further advances in the 
protocols for breeding European flat oyster, Ostrea edulis, broodstock from the Limfjord (DK) were 
conditioned for spawning using two diets composed by four species of microalgae, Chaetoceros sp., 
Rhodomonas salina, Tisochrysis lutea and Pavlova gyrans, but with different concentrations of Chaetoceros 
sp., T. lutea and P. gyrans. One ratio consisted of the normal ratio used at DSC for several years, while the 
enrich ratio contained 3 times the levels of T. lutea and P. gyrans. By increasing the amount of T. lutea and 
P. gyrans it was attempted to increase the levels of essential fatty acids present in the diet and provide 
extra food for the early development of the larvae. In order to investigate the impacts caused by the 
increase amount of T. lutea and P. gyrans on the conditioning and fatty acid content of the broodstock and 
quantity and quality of larvae, multiple analyses were performed. Like in previous studies, there was a 
general increase in shell length (SL) but a decrease in weight, condition index (CI) and fatty acid content 
during the reproductive season. The ratios used in the experiment presented some differences in fatty acid 
content. The enriched ratio contained less 16:1 (n-7) then the normal ratio, while containing more 18:2 (n-
6), 18:3 (n-3) and 22:6 (n-3). Along the experiment, the broodstock experienced changes in its fatty acid 
composition, however, with both groups presenting the same fatty acid profile at the end of the 
experiment, with the exception of 16:1 (n-7) (normal treatment with 8.67 ± 1.16 % and enriched treatment 
with 7.57 ± 0.98 %), indicating that the increase levels of T. lutea and P. gyrans had little effect on the fatty 
acid content of the broodstock. 
A total of 48.7 million larvae were collected from the broodstock fed with the normal diet, with a SL of 
182.59 ± 8.09 µm and a dry weight (DW) of 2.63 ± 0.63 µg larvae-1. In the enriched treatment the 
broodstock produced a total of 38.3 million larvae with 181.08 ± 6.77 µm of SL and 2.56 ± 0.61 µg larvae-1 
of DW. Although no difference was found in morphological characteristics of the larvae, numbers of larvae 
were smaller in the enriched ratio. 
In order to access possible reasons for the low impacts obtained by the increased levels of T. lutea and 
P. gyrans, clearance rate (CR) and absorption efficiency (AE) experiments were conducted. Broodstock from 
O. edulis from both treatments showed no affinity with T. lutea. When fed with P. gyrans, broodstock from 
the normal treatment showed low affinity with this species, with only one oyster filtering the algae (1.33 L 
h-1 g-1 of CR and 4 % of AE). A CR of 5.47 ± 1.54 L h-1 g-1 and an AE of 37.53 ± 5.1 % were obtained when 
broodstock from the enriched treatment was fed with P. gyrans. Despite this, only 3 out of 9 oysters from 
the enriched treatment filtered P. gyrans. This was probably caused by the algae small size. Higher AE were 
obtained by the broodstock when fed the mixed diets, comparing with the individual algae; this was the 
case for both, normal and enriched treatment.  
The results obtained in this study suggest that T. lutea and P. gyrans are not ideal species to use on O. 
edulis broodstock conditioning, resulting in very low differences between tests. The small size of the algae 
could be the reason for these results. However, tests on their effect on larvae need to be conducted, as 
larvae start to feed before being released from the parental mantle cavity and therefore, their nutritional 
requirements need to be accessed.

1. Introduction 
 
1.1. History and distribution of Ostrea edulis 
 
The European flat oyster (Ostrea edulis) is a marine species native of the North Atlantic that has been 
part of the human diet for thousands of years. Oyster shells were found in the remains of Viking 
settlements and Romans were already cultivating oysters to meet the demand for this luxury (FAO, 2004-
2006). 
Natural populations of flat oysters suffered a large reduction in numbers since the 18th century. This 
reduction was caused by over-exploitation and failing recruitment. Also, extremely cold winters in the 18th 
and 19th centuries destroyed natural beds of flat oysters. Data from FAO (2016) shows a big yearly 
variation, but also a decreasing trend in the global capture production from 1950 to 2014 (Fig. 1) with the 
highest values of approximately 9 000 tonnes of O. edulis collected in 1991 and 1992. 
 
 
Figure 1 – Global capture production of Ostrea edulis (in tonnes) since 1950 until 2014 (Source: FAO, 2016). 
 
The aquaculture production of flat oysters also suffered a large reduction (Fig. 2), as it was drastically 
affected by the spread of Marteilia refringens and Bonamia ostrea in the 70’s and 80’s, two diseases that 
still affect this species. Despite all the efforts that have been done to increase the production of this 
species, production numbers never rose. Some producers of oysters changed their target for the Pacific 
oyster (Crassostrea gigas) due to the easier production and increased financial results (FAO, 2004-2006). 
In Europe, O. edulis can be currently found along the coast from Norway to Morocco, in the whole 
Mediterranean Basin and in the northern shores of the Black Sea. It was intentionally introduced in North 
America during 1940s and 1950s and it can be found along the coast from Maine to Rhode Island (FAO, 
2004-2006). 
In Denmark, one of the most important areas for the fisheries of the flat oysters is the Limfjord. The 
Limfjord is a shallow body of water with an average depth of 7 m and connected in the east to the Baltic 
Sea and in the west to the North Sea. This last connection was closed for some centuries and reopened in 
1825. It was after this opening that Ostrea edulis was detected in the Limfjord. They can be found mainly in

1.2. Biology and reproduction of O. edulis 
 
Flat oysters have a slightly oval shaped shell with the right valve flat and the left one concave and fixed 
to the substratum. An adult oyster usually measures 8 to 12 cm, but larger specimens of up to 20 cm can be 
found. 
Anatomically, the mantle encloses the animal inside the shell (Fig. 3). Located in the centre is the 
adductor muscle that keeps the two valves closed. The gonads, located in the anterior zone, can be 
developed as either male or female, but they can also contain cells of both sexes. The gills, connected to 
the digestive system, are responsible for feeding and respiration and are located in a ventral position. As 
filter feeders, the oysters filter the surrounding water, retaining microalgae particles in the gills. (Helm et 
al., 2004). 
European flat oysters have sex reversal; they may spawn originally as a male in a season and spawn as 
a female in the second spawning. The frequency of sex change is related to the duration of the 
reproduction period and the location (Helm et al., 2004). Flat oysters’ sexual maturity may be achieved as 
early as 1 year, depending on environmental parameters, like temperature(Walne, 1964).  
Ostrea edulis are larviparous, meaning that they release larvae already fully developed instead of eggs. 
Early stages of larval development occur in the inhalant chamber of the mantle cavity of the oyster. During 
this time, small quantities of undeveloped larvae can be expelled in a white mass. Larvae are released with 
the digestive system ready for exogenous food. Usually each oyster releases 500 000 to 1 million larvae per 
spawning and the same oyster may spawn twice along a breeding season (Helm et al., 2004).

Figure 3 – Partial anatomy of Ostrea edulis: a) gonads; b) gills; c) adductor muscle. 
 
Several reproductive studies with flat oysters are available (e.g. Frolov and Pankov, 1992; González-
Araya et al., 2013, 2012b; Helm et al., 1973; Walne, 1964). Usually gametogenesis starts in spring when 
temperatures start to rise. The spawning will then occur a couple of months later, during summer 
(Loosanoff, 1962). However, oysters may spawn at different temperatures, depending on place (local 
adaptability) (Helm et al., 2004). Likewise, the time for larvae development is affected by the temperature. 
Most studies on reproduction of flat oysters are conducted at 19 to 20oC (e.g. Frolov and Pankov, 1992; 
González-Araya et al., 2013, 2012b; Helm et al., 1973). At these temperatures, larvae take approximately 7 
to 10 days to develop before being released by the broodstock (Walne, 1964).  
There is a lack of scientific knowledge on the development of the larvae prior to spawning. As referred 
before, European flat oysters are larviparous and, at the time of spawning, the larvae are fully developed. 
Observations done at DSC indicate that newly spawned larvae already contain feed within their gut, 
suggesting that feeding commences before the larvae are released. Due to this fact, microalgae with small 
cell size (bellow 7 µm) should be included in the broodstock diet, so that the larvae can feed (Frolov and 
Pankov, 1992). 
Conditioning and size of the broodstock are positively related to the size of the released larvae 
(Walne, 1964), suggesting that a proper broodstock conditioning leads to the production of larger larvae. 
Although larvae can vary in size at the time of spawning, they are typically around 180 µm in shell length 
(Walne, 1964). 
 
 
1.3. Broodstock conditioning and feeding 
 
When brought from the field to the hatchery, bivalves are usually placed in systems where they can be 
acclimatized to the conditions required for cultivation. This process, called conditioning, is dependent on 
the time of the year and objectives of the study. At the time when bivalves are collected, the temperature 
==PAGE:9==
8 
 
from the water can be much lower than what is required for reproduction. The specimens are placed in 
tanks with water at the same temperature as that found in the field, reducing stress on the animals, and a 
slow warming process is then undertaken. This process can be conducted over a period of several weeks, 
resulting in a slow but steady increase of temperature up to the level required for reproduction (Frolov and 
Pankov, 1992; González-Araya et al., 2012a; Haure et al., 1998). During the entire process the animals are 
fed specific diets in order to prepare them for spawning.  
Bivalves metabolism in their natural habitat varies within seasons and depends, besides other factors, 
on temperature and food availability (Abad et al., 1995; Çelik et al., 2015). To minimize these variations and 
to be able to obtain a stable broodstock, the conditioning in the hatchery is one of the most important 
steps in the production of these species. Conditioning of broodstock has been proved to affect larval 
development and survival (Frolov and Pankov, 1992).  
Although many species have been studied in terms of conditioning, nutritional requirements of 
bivalves are species specific. Many studies have been done with European flat oysters, studying broodstock 
conditioning in the hatchery (González-Araya et al., 2013, 2012a, 2012b, 2011; Helm et al., 1973; Millican 
and Helm, 1994; Utting and Millican, 1997). Most of these studies looked at the best combination of 
microalgae species in the diet of the flat oyster and they recommend a diet with at least two different 
species, composed by diatoms and flagellates (González-Araya et al., 2013, 2012a, 2012b, 2011; Utting and 
Millican, 1997).  
At the Danish Shellfish Center (DSC), DTU Aqua, Nykøbing Mors, Denmark, the algae used to feed the 
broodstock are a species of diatom (Chaetoceros sp.) and three species of flagellates (Rhodomonas sp., 
Isochrysis sp. and Pavlova sp.) mixed in a concentration of 65, 29, 3 and 3% of each species, respectively. 
One of the most important characteristics of microalgae species, when used in a bivalves’ hatchery, is 
their lipid and fatty acid content. Studies about lipids and in particular fatty acids have been an extensive 
topic in bivalves (Abad et al., 1995; Frolov and Pankov, 1992; González-Araya et al., 2012a).  
Lipids are important reserves for gametogenesis and reproduction in bivalves (Frolov and Pankov, 
1992). The polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFA), such as eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA, 20:5 (n-3)), 
docosahexaenoic acid (DHA, 22:6 (n-3)) and arachidonic acid (ARA, 20:4 (n-6)), are very important for the 
growth and development of bivalves broodstock (Batista et al., 2014; Frolov and Pankov, 1992) and for 
bivalves gametogenesis and reproduction (Frolov and Pankov, 1992). T. lutea and P. gyrans are known to 
have high levels of this fatty acids, theoretically making their inclusion beneficial for the conditioning of the 
broodstock (González-Araya et al., 2012a, 2011). 
Different microalgae species contain different lipid composition with high variations of fatty acid 
profiles. It is due to this variation that a multispecies diet is advised, as different algae complement each 
other regarding fatty acid composition (Frolov and Pankov, 1992). Despite multiple studies conducted on 
this subject, there is still no clear answer regarding the optimal algae combination and fatty acid profile 
required by the European flat oyster broodstock. 
Although it might be a key factor, the biochemical value of each microalgae species is not the only 
important factor for a good conditioning and production of oysters. In order to be used by the broodstock, 
microalgae need to be ingested and absorbed efficiently during the conditioning of the oysters, in order to 
make algae content available for the broodstock (Helm et al., 2004). This leads to the need to consider 
other characteristics when choosing the species of microalgae to feed the broodstock, like size and how the 
oysters feed and absorb those cells. 
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
Bivalves can select the food they filter from the water (e.g. based on size) (Batista et al., 2014) and, in 
some situations, for example excess of food in the water column, oysters can reject the excess food by 
expelling it as pseudofaeces (Helm et al., 2004). This requires that the amount of food provided to the 
broodstock be determined and regulated. In order to do this, clearance rate (CR) and absorption efficiency 
(AE) experiments are generally conducted to evaluate the amount of algae ingested and absorbed, allowing 
the comparison between each microalgae species and diets regarding assimilation efficiency. 
CR is, by definition, the volume of water cleared of suspended particles per unit of time (Riisgård, 
2001). Although there are many different methods to measure CR, the indirect method, used in this 
experiment, measures the reduction of particles concentration in the water as a function of time. One of 
the down sides of using this method, because it is performed in a closed system, is the variation of the food 
level. As the oysters filter the particles in the water, extra food needs to be added, leading to a constant 
increase and decrease in food concentration. 
AE measures the organic component of the food that was absorbed by the animals, using the organic 
content from the faeces (Conover, 1966). Assimilation of food assumes that the food ingested passes 
through the digestive system and is absorbed by the animal. To calculate AE, the organic content of the 
food and the faeces are obtained to determine the percentage of the organic content of food that was 
absorbed by the animal and the percentage expelled as faeces. 
 
 
1.4 Aim of the project 
 
Despite the extensive research that has been carried out on European flat oysters, many questions are 
still unanswered. Contradicting results have been found on multiple aspects of oyster breeding and no clear 
protocol has been defined to produce O. edulis in a hatchery environment. Declining natural populations 
and aquaculture production, coupled with this species monetary value, make this species a prime candidate 
for further research regarding breeding programs. 
 
In order to improve the breeding protocols of O. edulis and building on the knowledge gathered at 
DSC, two different diets were formulated and fed to the oyster broodstock in order to access the impacts 
on the broodstock and their offspring over an entire breeding season. The hypothesis was that the increase 
inclusion of T. lutea and P. gyrans, would lead to an increase in the fatty acids DHA and EPA available for 
the broodstock, which would lead to improvements oyster conditioning and in offspring production. 
 
Tests were conducted to access broodstock condition and also quality and quantity of larvae 
produced, as well as feed assimilation by the broodstock.

2. Material and Methods 
 
All experiments were done at DSC, DTU Aqua, Nykøbing Mors (DK). Fatty acid extractions were 
performed at Section for Aquaculture, DTU Aqua, Hirtshals (DK), while fatty acid analyses were conducted 
at DTU Food, Lyngby (DK). 
 
 
2.1 Maintenance of microalgae cultures 
 
The cultures used in this experiment were acquired from the Culture Collection of Algae and Protozoa, 
UK: Chaetoceros sp. (CCAP 1010/38), Rhodomonas salina (CCAP 978/27), Tisochrysis lutea (CCAP 927/14) 
and Pavlova gyrans (CCAP 940/1C). 
A continuous production of these cultures is maintained at DSC all year round, enriched with a 
homemade media based on the Conway media (Annex I) and grown with constant light (140 µmol m-2 s-1) 
and temperature (20oC).  
The culture of the microalgae, from mother culture to the solution used to feed the oysters, follows 
several steps described as follow. First, start-up culture flasks with 80 mL of each species enriched with 
culture media (2 mL L-1) were kept without aeration and renewed every two weeks. From these flasks, the 
cultures were inoculated in to 2 L bottles filled up with seawater and 2 mL L-1 of media and aerated with 1% 
CO2/air to keep the cultures mixed and the pH stable. These cultures were then transferred to 50 L bags 
filled up with filtered seawater (0.2 µm) together with 1 mL L-1 of media. Cultures were kept mixed with 1% 
CO2/air aeration. Finally, from these bags, microalgae were collected and mixed to the desired ratio to feed 
the oysters. 
Cell count of cultures was performed daily using an AccuSizer 780 Optical Particle Sizer, Santa Barbara, 
USA, in order to supply the same concentration of food every day. 
In order to determine microalgae dry weight (DW) and organic content (OC), 20 mL of culture of a 
known concentration was filtrated through a pre-weighted 47 mm GF/C Whatman filters and washed with 
5 mL of 0.5 M (v/v) ammonium formate to remove residual salts (Lund et al., 2007). The filters were then 
dried at 80oC for 48 hours and reweighted to obtain the DW and then burned at 550oC for 4 hours to 
determine the OC.  
 
 
2.2 Broodstock conditioning 
 
O. edulis were dredged from the bottom of the Limfjord, in Nissum bay, at a depth between 3.5 to 8 
m, on the 2nd of April, 2016. The temperature at time of collection was approximately 5oC. 
A homogeneous group of 160 oysters measuring 88.9 ± 7.75 mm of shell length (SL) and weighing 
157.6 ± 35.7 g of total fresh weigh (TFW) were scrubbed and dipped in a Sodium hypochlorite bath (10 %) 
for 20 seconds to clean the shells from biofouling. From this group, 10 oysters were kept in the freezer at -
20oC for lipid and fatty acid analyses, 25 were used immediately for condition index and 124 were randomly 
separated in two equal groups and distributed in to four 40 L tanks (two per group) with a 60 L h-1 flow-
through system for acclimation in a temperature controlled room. The water from the Limfjord was 
==PAGE:12==

pumped through a sand filter (Astralpool Delta 10), followed by UV treatment (Ultra Aqua UV Compact) 
and finally filtered through two cartridge filters (100 and 1 µm, respectively) before entering the tanks. 
During this period temperature was increased by 1oC every second day from approximately 12.5 to 
18oC. However, due to large variations in temperature outside the controlled room, this was not always 
possible. The temperature variation in the controlled room during the acclimation period was recorded 
with a HOBO Pendant temperature/light data logger and is presented in figure 4.

Two different ratios of food were created based on the same species of microalgae and feed to the 
broodstock since the acclimation period (Tab. 1). The normal diet was based in the traditional ratio used in 
the DSC hatchery. This diet is composed by one species of diatoms, Chaetoceros sp., and three species of 
flagellates, R. salina, T. lutea and P. gyrans. The enriched diet was calculated so the amount of T. lutea and 
P. gyrans were three times higher than on the normal ratio while reducing the amount of Chaetoceros sp. 
in order to maintain the same amount of food supplied. The reason for the increase in T. lutea and P. 
gyrans was, both, to increase the amount of specific fatty acids needed for reproduction (DHA and EPA) and 
also to increase the amount of cells in a size range that can be ingested by the larvae. The oysters were fed 
daily an amount of algae correspondent to 4.5 % of their dry weight to minimize the level of pseudofaeces 
(González-Araya et al., 2011). The food was supplied daily by means of peristaltic pumps (Aqua Medic 
reefdoser Quadro), set to a rate of 6 mL min-1.

2.3 Larval collection and condition 
 
Larvae collectors were checked several times each day in order to ensure that larvae remained in the 
filters the shortest time possible, reducing stress to the larvae. When larvae were visually detected, a small 
sample of each spawn was collected for different analyses and an estimation of the amount of larvae was 
calculated by collecting all the remaining larvae in to a container of a known volume. Three subsamples of a 
known volume were then counted and the total number of larvae was estimated. A known number of 
larvae (approximately 500) were filtrated (total number of larvae per filter was confirmed under the 
microscope) and treated following the same protocol as for the microalgae (section 2.1), in order to 
determine DW. A second subsample of approximately 1000 larvae was filtrated through a pre-weighted 47 
mm GF/C Whatman filters as described in section 2.1 and stored at -20oC for fatty acid analyses. 
SL of larvae was measured in an Olympus IX51 microscope fitted with a Nikon Digital Sight DS-Fi1 
camera.

2.5 Biochemical procedures 
 
Samples for fatty acid composition were stored at -20oC. 
The oyster meat collected at the beginning (10 in total) and at the end of the experiment (10 per 
treatment) were ground using a Büchi Mixer B-400 and a sample of known weight (approximately 2 g) was 
used for fatty acid analyses.  
Samples of a known number of larvae from each spawn (approximately 1000) and 20 mL of a known 
concentration sample of each microalgae and each diet were filtrated through a pre-weighted 47 mm GF/C 
Whatman filter. 
The lipids were extracted in clean glass test tubes with approximately 1 mL of a mixture of 
chloroform:methanol (2:1 v/v) (Folch et al., 1957) followed by addition of 40 µL (1 mg mL-1) of internal 
standard of methyl tricosanoate (C23:0) in chloroform. The test tubes were flushed with nitrogen, closed 
with aluminium foil, sonicated for 20 minutes in a 2510 Branson ultrasound cleaner and stored at -20oC for 
24 hours to extract the lipids. After this period, the samples were unfrozen and centrifuged at 3000 rpm for 
10 minutes while kept at 5oC. The lower phases were transferred to clean 2 mL autosampler vials with 
butyl/PTFE lids. The solvent was evaporated at 60oC by applying a flow of nitrogen with a Pierce Reacti-
therm heating module over a 20 minute period. 1 mL of a reagent solution composed by toluene, methanol 
and acetyl chloride (40:50:10) was added to the vial and heated in the oven for 2 hours at 95oC. After this 
process, 0.5 mL of aqueous NaHCO3 (5% by weight) was added to the vial and the upper layer was removed 
to a new 2 mL vial (with screw caps with silicone/PTFE septa) after vigorous shaking. The remaining phase 
in the first vial was extracted twice with 0.5 mL of heptane and the upper layer collected to the new vial. 
The organic phase (second vial) was evaporated at 60oC for about 10 minutes by applying a gentle stream 
of nitrogen. To re-suspend the fatty acid methyl esters, 0.5 mL of chloroform was added to the vials and the 
samples were stored at -80oC until further analyses.  
To identify fatty acid composition, 4 µL of the aliquot of the samples was analysed by Gas 
Chromatography Flame Ionization Detection (GC-FID) and fatty acids were identified by comparison of their 
retention time with standards. Fatty acid composition of each sample is presented as a percentage. 
A total of 34 fatty acids were analysed, but only the most important ones are presented in this study.

3.1 Diet composition 
 
The four individual species of algae used on the diets presented a varied content of fatty acids. Figure 
6 shows the relative mean content of the main fatty acids in the four species of microalgae used in this 
experiment (Fig. 6 - a) and in the two diets formulated for broodstock conditioning (Fig. 6 – b). Values are 
presented as % of the total fatty acids (TFA) ± standard deviation (sd). 
Chaetoceros sp. was very rich in 16:1 (n-7) with more than 40% of the TFA and very poor in 20:4 (n-6), 
ARA, and 22:6 (n-3), DHA, with less than 1 % of each one of these PUFAs. The fatty acid 20:5 (n-3), EPA, 
represented 5.75 ± 0.71%, one of the highest content among the four species. R. salina was rich in 16:00, 
18:2 (n-6) and 18:3 (n-6) with, respectively, 17.97 ± 0.69, 18.67 ± 1.03 and 23.06 ± 1.31 % of each of these 
fatty acids, but relatively poor in 20:4 (n-6), 20:5 (n-3) and 22:6 (n-3) with 0.26 ± 0.16, 4.11 ± 0.99 and 4.11 
± 0.33 % of each fatty acid, respectively. T. lutea had a high content of 18:1 (n-9) and 18:2 (n-6), 
respectively, 9.08 ± 0.43 and 18.67 ± 0.96 %. It was also relatively rich in DHA with 6.76 ± 0.39 %, but poor 
in 20:4 (n-6) and 20:5 (n-3), with 0.31 ± 0.02 and 2.25 ± 0.26 % of each fatty acid, respectively. With 18.37 ± 
0.13 % of 20:5 (n-3), P. gyrans presented the highest content of this PUFA. It was also rich in 16:00 and 16:1 
(n-7) with, respectively, 11.61 ± 0.92 and 13.19 ± 0.27 %, but had a low content in 20:4 (n-6) and 22:6 (n-3), 
with 0.11 ± 0.11 and 3.71 ± 0.69 %, respectively. 
The normal diet used in this experiment was based in the diet already used in this hatchery while the 
enriched ratio had three times more T. lutea and P. gyrans (9 % of each microalgae, instead of 3 %) and less 
12 % of Chaetoceros sp. (53 % instead of 65). Although the enriched ratio contained three times more T. 
lutea and P. gyrans, there were just small variations in some fatty acids in this diet.  
There was a statistically significant decrease (Holm-Sidak method; p < 0.001) on fatty acid 16:1 (n-7) 
on the enriched diet due to the reduction of Chaetoceros sp. and a statistically significant increase (Holm-
Sidak method; p < 0.001) in 18:2 (n-6), 18:3 (n-6), 18:3 (n-3) and 22:6 (n-3) due to the greater amount of T. 
lutea and P. gyrans.

4. Discussion 
 
The diet used in the normal treatment was based on the diet that has been used at the DSC hatchery 
for some years, which has been improved through experiments done at DSC and elsewhere. The enriched 
ratio was chosen based on DSC previous years’ experience, where it had been found that an increase of 
both, T. lutea and P. gyrans proportion in the feed, produced higher larval survival rates, but lower total 
number of larvae (data not published). 
A more comprehensible overview of the diet used at DSC was conducted during this experiment. Due 
to the endless possible combinations of microalgae, the aim of these experiments was not to develop the 
best diet possible for O. edulis broodstock, but to compare and analyse the differences caused by the 
increase in the proportion of T. lutea and P. gyrans in the diet of the broodstock of European flat oyster and 
try to provide scientific support for the results previously obtained empirically at DSC. 
The microalgae species used in this hatchery are species with a different biochemical composition and 
are commonly used in bivalves’ hatchery for their high content in PUFAs. Chaetoceros sp., the only diatom 
species used in the oysters diet in this experiment, is commonly used for its high content of fatty acid 16:1 
(n-7) (Batista et al., 2014; Berntsson et al., 1997); this genus has been shown to promote a good gonad 
development (González-Araya et al., 2013) and a good AE in flat oysters broodstock (González-Araya et al., 
2011). Rhodomonas are used for its high percentage of EPA (González-Araya et al., 2012a) and for its good 
results with gonad development (González-Araya et al., 2013). T. lutea is usually known by its high content 
of DHA (González-Araya et al., 2012a, 2011) and the genus Pavlova is known by its high content of EPA and 
DHA (González-Araya et al., 2012a). 
Some doubts were raised by González-Araya et al. (2011) regarding the use of T. lutea when 
conditioning flat oyster broodstock. In their study this microalgae caused a decrease in DW and a weak 
fecundity, and oysters had a low assimilation of this algae. However, they found that this species presented 
a high content of DHA (15 %) and this fatty acid was well transferred to the oysters’ gonads. That makes 
this species an interesting option to use on flat oysters diet, when accompanied with other species. An 
important factor is the fact that oyster larvae start to feed while still inside the broodstock, which requires 
broodstock to be fed with small size algae species. The presence of T. lutea in the diet of O. edulis 
broodstock could therefore be beneficial because of its small size (approximately 4 - 5 µm). However, 
further studies need to be conducted to evaluate the impact that this species has on the larvae 
development. 
It was expected that the two diets had a higher difference between them than what was observed 
regarding EPA and DHA percentage, since these two PUFAs are present in higher percentage in T. lutea and 
P. gyrans than in the other two species. The enriched diet had higher percentage of important fatty acids, 
like 18:2 (n-6), 18:3 (n-6), 18:3 (n-3) and 22:6 (n-3). The higher amount of T. lutea and P. gyrans was enough 
to increase the percentage of DHA, but not EPA. A higher increase of these two species in the diet of the 
broodstock should be tested on fatty acid composition in order to determine the possibility of further 
increase of PUFAs, specially EPA and DHA. 
The fact that none of the microalgae species used in this experiment had a significant content of ARA 
needs to be taken into account. The inclusion of a different species with higher values of this fatty acid 
could also be analysed. For example, Thalassiosira pseudonana used in some studies on O. edulis 
conditioning (González-Araya et al., 2012a) is a microalgae with the same range of size of T. lutea and P. 
gyrans that contains approximately 10% of this PUFA. This study showed good results regarding ingestion 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
and absorption of this species on flat oyster broodstock, suggesting that it might be a good option for 
broodstock conditioning. Some species of Chaetoceros also have relative high content of ARA (Volkman et 
al., 1989) and might be used instead of the one used in this experiments. Further tests regarding the effects 
of these microalgae species on oysters should be performed. 
Using quantitative analysis of fatty acid to test the microalgae species used in the diets for bivalves 
production helps defining and adjusting an appropriate mixture of microalgae that can be used in a 
commercial hatchery.  
When conditioning oysters’ broodstock, there are important factors to pay attention to. Temperature 
should be relatively high, however without exceeding acceptable temperature ranges. Higher temperatures 
lead to higher filtration rates and therefore require a higher availability of food. High temperatures and low 
amounts of food cause stress to broodstock, which cause decrease of DW and CI (Gabbott and Walker, 
1971). 
An increase in SL of the broodstock fed with both diets was observed during the 90 days of the 
experiment. This result was expected, because the temperature of the system where broodstock was kept 
was higher (20°C) than the “biological zero” (11°C) for shell growth of O. edulis (Wilson, 1987). 
A general decrease in MFW and CI was observed in the broodstock of both treatments. These results 
can be explained by the investment of oysters in reproduction and spawning events. After this period, 
broodstock usually presents a fragile general condition (Çelik et al., 2015). 
In the natural environment, a series of environmental factors, where temperature is one of the most 
important ones, affect growth, mortality, reproduction and feeding of oysters (Çelik et al., 2015; Wilson, 
1987). In general, it is common to see a decrease in broodstock condition in the wild during spawning 
season and an increase after the breeding season (accumulation of reserves for winter). Another increase is 
observed after winter in preparation for spawning. These variations are mainly caused by availability 
(quantity and quality) of food in the water and spawning season (Abad et al., 1995). One event that may 
cause an increase in broodstock condition is the phytoplankton blooms, which provide extra food for the 
oysters. The results obtained in the present study followed the same pattern, with a decrease in CI and 
MDW over the duration of the experiment. This was the case for both treatments, with no difference found 
between the two. A field study conducted by Çelik et al. (2015) found similar results. A decrease in MDW 
was observed during a 90 day experiment (May to July), despite an increase in SL. Other studies conducted 
in the field presented a decrease in broodstock condition from May to August (Abad et al., 1995), and a loss 
in the meat weight of flat oyster at spawning time (Rodhouse, 1978). 
During the 110 days in which the oysters were in the hatchery (20 days of acclimation and 90 days of 
experiment) there was a total of 4 % mortality in the enrich treatment and 0 % in the normal treatment. 
These results are in agreement with previous observation for both hatchery and field experiments. Low 
mortality (2 %) was also obtained in a 2 months experiments by González-Araya et al. (2012a) in a hatchery 
setting, while Çelik et al. (2015) observed a 2.5 % mortality in the field over a 90 day period, indicating that 
the treatments and handling of the broodstock had no negative impacts in the survival rates during this 
experiment. 
Following the trend of CI and MDW, a general decrease in fatty acids was observed in broodstock from 
both treatments. These results were expected, since lipids are essential for development of gonads and for 
spawning events (Çelik et al., 2015; Frolov and Pankov, 1992). A similar result was found by Abad et al. 
(1995). According to the authors, seasonal changes in the content of fatty acids are dependent on food 
availability and the reproductive cycle. Furthermore, the amount of lipids present in gonads, and therefore  
==PAGE:31==
30 
 
available for reproduction, depends on the amount of lipids found in the food (Frolov and Pankov, 1992). 
For this reason, it is essential that the food contains the necessary lipids and fatty acid profile for 
reproduction. Lipids are a key component in determining the nutritional value of food and the condition of 
the reproductive system (Frolov and Pankov, 1992). 
Some fatty acids play a fundamental role in reproduction. Frolov and Pankov (1992) concluded that 
EPA and DHA are indispensable compounds for oysters and that the food provided should contain enough 
quantity of both this fatty acids. Abad et al. (1995) showed the variability in the content of EPA in the 
broodstock of flat oysters through one year. Oysters presented maximum values in October-December and 
March-May, leading the authors to conclude that those values were probably due to phytoplankton blooms 
with high number of diatoms that happened in autumn and spring. Other factor that may have contributed 
to the decrease in fatty acids is the temperature increase. When the broodstock was collected, it was at a 
temperature of 5°C. This meant that their metabolism was slow at that time. When acclimatized to 20°C, 
their metabolism increased, causing an increased use of fatty acids for metabolism (Chu and Greaves, 
1991). During this experiment, initial samples were collected when the oysters were brought in for 
acclimation and then at the end of the experiment. In future studies, it could be relevant to collect samples 
after the acclimation period in order to access changes caused by this process. 
In the present study, a total of 87 million larvae were collected in the 90 days of the experiment, 
which corresponds to a fecundity of approximately 0.9 million larvae oyster-1 tank-1 (0.98 million larvae 
oyster-1 in the normal treatment and 0.79 million larvae oyster-1 in the enriched treatment). These values 
are substantially higher than the ones obtained in other studies. The highest fecundity value obtained by 
González-Araya et al. (2011) was 0.04 million larvae per female, while Millican and Helm (1994) obtained 12 
million larvae from 90 oysters over a period of 70 days (approximately 0.13 million larvae oyster-1). 
Flat oyster larvae from both treatments measured between 167 to 200 µm. Looking at the results of SL 
and DW in a chronological order, it seems that there was a slight decrease in both measurements, although 
with many variations along the 90 days of experiment. Reproduction on O. edulis was studied by Walne 
(1964). In his study, larvae were released with approximately 180 µm of SL. The author observed no decline 
in fertility along the season and no clear evidence that larvae were released with a smaller size late in the 
season. In the current study, larvae showed a decrease trend in relation to size and weight, although no 
statistical significance was found. Reduction in size along the season could be related to a reduction in CI of 
the broodstock. These results may explain previous years’ observations at DSC, where larvae size and 
survival was lower late in the season. 
Larvae collected in the present study presented a high variability in some fatty acid content. That 
might have been caused by the condition of the broodstock. Broodstock with poor condition will produce 
poor quality larvae. Frolov and Pankov (1992) showed that there is a positive relation between lipid content 
in gonads and that in larvae. Broodstock provide the nutrients for the larvae. As food influence the 
broodstock and their reproduction by supplying the nutrients to larvae, a proper diet is essential to obtain 
good quality larvae. 
The main variability in fatty acids was found in EPA concentrations, with some spawnings containing 
approximately 10 % while others contained up to 30 % of this PUFA. These differences where not related to 
the different treatments, with both showing variation in the amount of EPA present.  
EPA is very important for larvae development. The content of EPA in the gonads of broodstock is 
positively correlated with that amount in larvae (Frolov and Pankov, 1992), meaning that if the broodstock 
has low content of this fatty acid, their offspring will also have a low content of EPA and their development 
==PAGE:32==
31 
 
will be affected. This factor justifies the increased inclusion of species with high levels of EPA in the diet of 
O. edulis. 
The diets that were given to the broodstock and consequently given to the larvae during their early 
development had species that are not suitable for larvae, because of their size. R. salina, with 
approximately 10 µm of size, are considered too big for larvae at spawning time, when larvae measure 
approximately 180 - 190 µm. Chaetoceros might also be too big for larvae, because they form chains. T. 
lutea and P. gyrans are within the range of sizes suitable for larvae (Frolov and Pankov, 1992) and therefore 
their inclusion may be important, as they will form the base of the larvae feeding while inside the 
broodstock. According to Frolov and Pankov (1992), microalgae with sizes above 7 µm are too big to be 
ingested by larvae in early stages of development. In the present study, only two species used in the diets 
had a smaller size than 7 µm, T. lutea and P. gyrans. Although a single cell of Chaetoceros sp. measures 
approximatelly 5 µm, they form chains and have spines (setae) that increase the total size of the algae, 
which could make it difficult to be ingested by newly released larvae. 
Despite the fact that almost no ARA was present in the diets and in the broodstock, a relatively high 
(approximately 10 %) content of this PUFA was detected in the larvae produced by both treatments. 
Although no information could be found in the literature, it might have been possible that larvae could 
elongate fatty acid 18:3 (n-3) to 20:4 (n-6). There is evidence that other bivalves are able to elongate some 
fatty acids in to PUFAs. For example, Crassostrea virginica showed a limited capacity to elongate 18:2 (n-6) 
and 18:3 (n-3) to long-chain fatty acids (C20 and C22 PUFAs) (Chu and Greaves, 1991), but they could not 
produce EPA or DHA, which means that these PUFAs are essentials to this species and must be provided by 
the diet. In this study, fatty acid composition was tested by homogenizing all the meat of the animal. A way 
of testing if larvae could elongate their own PUFAs would be to obtain the fatty acid content of gonads and 
compare it with the fatty acid content of other tissues of the broodstock and of larvae. It is possible that 
ARA was accumulated in the gonads and passed on to the larvae by the broodstock. 
While biochemical composition of the diets is essential, the oysters capability to ingest and absorb 
them is equally important. Physiological parameters, like consumption and ingestion, have been proved to 
be related to the diet in oysters (González-Araya et al., 2012b, 2011). The four microalgae used in these 
experiments showed a different impact on the broodstock regarding CR and AE. 
It was not possible to calculate CR and AE of the broodstock when fed T. lutea. 9 specimens from each 
treatment were used for CR and AE experiments, however, none of them fed on this species. The exact 
reason for these results is not entirely clear. This was not the first time that this species showed low results 
regarding ingestion and absorption. An AE of 19 % was obtained at 19°C with flat oyster broodstock in 
other study (González-Araya et al., 2011) and the authors suggested that this was not a good microalgae for 
conditioning flat oysters broodstock.  
Chaetoceros sp. promoted a higher CR and AE on the normal treatment. The higher levels of 
Chaetoceros sp. on this diet feed over a 110 day period may have caused some feeding adaptability which 
led to higher CR and AE versus the enrich treatment, where the amount of Chaetoceros sp. was reduced. 
R. salina had high results in both treatments regarding CR (8.25 ± 1.54 L h-1 g-1 for the normal 
treatment and 6.52 ± 1.73 L h-1 g-1 for the enriched treatment) and AE (33.48 ± 6.23 % for the normal 
treatment and 34.21 ± 8.49 % for the enriched treatment). This was also the conclusion obtained in a 
different study conducted by González-Araya et al. (2012a), where a AE of 47 % was obtained with R. salina, 
in a concentration of 0.9 mL L-1 at 19°C with oysters measuring approximately 5 cm. 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
In the present study, only one oyster from the normal treatment fed on P. gyrans, obtaining an AE of 4 
% and a CR of 1.33 L h-1 g-1, while broodstock from the enriched treatment obtained an AE of 37.42 ± 5.1 % 
and a CR of 5.47 ± 1.54 L h-1 g-1. The results obtained by the CR and AE of O. edulis broodstock indicate an 
active selection of food in this study, as well as adaptability to feeding. Despite having very similar sizes, T. 
lutea was not ingested by any oysters in the CR and AE experiment, while P. gyrans was absorbed by 3 
oysters from the enriched treatment, but only 1 of the oyster from the normal treatment. These results are 
highly interesting as they suggest not only active selection of food, but also some adaptability to different 
food sources dependent on feed supplied. The low levels of ingestion of both P. gyrans and T. lutea could, 
in part, explain the lack of differences between both treatments.   
In order to access adaption on feeding habits between both test groups, each treatment was tested 
for CR and AE using both ratios of food. Higher AE were obtained when broodstock was fed with a mixed 
diet, comparing with single diets. Oysters from the normal treatment obtained an AE of 42.39 ± 4.29 % and 
42.13 ± 1.17 % when fed with the normal and enriched ratio, respectively, while oysters from the enriched 
treatment obtained an AE of 39.31 ± 1.96 % and 44.84 ± 2.34 % when fed, respectively, with the normal 
and enriched diet. More interesting are the CR results. The broodstock group treated with the enriched diet 
presented very similar results when fed both ratios (6.83 ± 2.05 and 7.04 ± 1.29 L h-1 g-1, respectively, for 
the normal and enriched ratios). The broodstock from the normal treatment obtained very different 
results. When fed with the normal diet, the results were similar to the results obtained by the enrich 
treatment (7.0 ± 0.83 L h-1 g-1). However, when fed with the enriched treatment these values dropped to 
half (3.55 ± 0.81 L h-1 g-1). Like the results for the individual algae, the diet results suggest that the oyster 
experienced a feeding adaptation. However, it is hard to conclude if the adaptation happened to the 
broodstock from the enriched treatment (increase flexibility in feeding habits) or to the broodstock from 
the normal treatment (decrease flexibility in feeding habits). Nonetheless, the results are very interesting 
and could potentially impact species selection and conditioning protocols for O. edulis broodstock, 
including the time frames used for acclimation (e.g. longer or shorter periods of acclimation to 
increase/avoid changes in feeding habits). Therefore, studies should be conducted on the feeding 
adaptation of the European flat oyster. 
Unlike other bivalve species, where larvae do not feed until spawning, O. edulis larvae feed while 
inside the mother. This factor adds an increased layer of difficulty on the successful breeding of this 
species. Not only the feed supplied to the broodstock needs to be developed to supply all the requirements 
of the broodstock, it also has to supply the requirements of the larvae. This was not the main focus of this 
study; however, due to its potential implication on the development and potential survival of the larvae, 
this should be subjected to further investigation in future studies.

5. Conclusion 
 
There is no doubt regarding the importance of a good conditioning of O. edulis in a hatchery. During 
this period, a diet that can provide the nutritional value necessary for a good development and 
reproductive cycle is essential. Not only that, but the fact that oyster larvae start feeding while inside the 
broodstock means that the larvae nutrition needs to be taken into account when formulating the diet. 
Despite a 3 fold increase in the amount of T. lutea and P. gyrans and the high levels of DHA and EPA 
present in this algae species, the fatty acid content of the diets remained very similar. The comparison of 
fatty acid composition of the oysters belonging to the different treatments at the end of the experiment 
revealed no difference in terms of PUFA content of the broodstock. 
T. lutea and P. gyrans showed to be a poor addition to the diet of flat oysters’ broodstock during 
conditioning. The results obtained in this study suggest that the small size of this species may have 
hindered the broodstock capability to filtrate and assimilate them, suggesting that larger species need to be 
used during conditioning, in order to provide essential fatty acids.  
However the development of larvae needs to be considered when choosing the diet to feed the 
broodstock, as their small size prevents them from eating larger size microalgae. 
Taking into account that food should provide the essential nutrition for broodstock and that the 
broodstock condition influences directly the condition and development of larvae, choosing a diet that can 
provide it during broodstock conditioning is vital for an O. edulis hatchery. Due to the small size of the 
larvae, further studies should be conducted in order to access the impacts that the increase of small size 
algae in the diet composition of the broodstock have on larvae development and survival.
