Abstract
Methods that allow to identify the speech stream a person attends to in a competing-
talker scenario by means of electroencephalography (EEG) have been the subject of
many studies over the past decades. Identifying attended speech in such a way has po-
tential use in brain computer interface (BCI) applications, e.g., controlling beamformers
or informing hearing aids about the current acoustic conditions. Previous research has
mainly focused on controlled conditions with competing talkers (i.e. two monologues).
In the here present project, we consider attention decoding in a more realistic condition
by introducing a multi-talker target in the form of a dialogue. We measured the single-
trial EEG responses of 17 young normal-hearing subjects while they were audio-visually
presented with a monologue and a dialogue simultaneously. Additionally, both head
movements and eye-gaze data were recorded. Subjects were instructed to attend to ei-
ther monologue or dialogue in different trials. To ensure a sufficient level of difficulty,
multi-talker babble noise was used as an additional masker. The stimuli were presented
on an 88-inch wide curved screen in front of the subject, with a loudspeaker array posi-
tioned behind the screen to play back the talkers’ audio. To en-/decode attention, both
forward and backward models were applied. We showed that the attended dialogue was
separable from the attended monologue in different measurement domains. We achieved
a slightly, but significantly (on a trial-subject level) higher stimulus reconstruction accu-
racy and greater classification accuracy for the attended dialogue compared the attended
monologue by up to 13 %. Generally, we introduced a way to assess attention decoding
within a direct (monologue attended) and indirect (dialogue attended) conversational
paradigm. Throughout the project we discussed potential implications for e.g. the as-
sessment of listening effort, that can be drawn from this ecologically-valid measurement
set up. We also compared our findings to previous studies that are comparable to the
presented one. This study will provide insight into how well the stimulus reconstruction
method performs in realistic scenarios and what pitfalls need to considered for future
realistic measurement conditions within the attention decoding community. This can
be utilized in defining requirements for future BCI systems.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter introduces the project and defines the research question that is going to
be targeted. The first section describes the initial motivation behind the project and
outlines the possible contribution to current research in attention decoding.
In the
second section the research question and the hypothesis are presented. In the end of
this chapter, the structure of the thesis is outlined.
1.1
Motivation
The human sensory system has the ability to detect and separate sources with high preci-
sion. This is true even if the conditions are unfavourable. In the auditory domain those
conditions can, for instance, be multiple talkers or a low signal-to-noise ratio (SNR).
Such situations are often referred to as ”cocktail party” problem, which was first men-
tioned by Cherry (1953). How the auditory system solves the cocktail party problem is
still unclear and subject to ongoing research. However, if one of the sensory systems e.g.
the auditory system is impaired, the ability of separating sources is decreased. This is
one of the reasons why hearing impaired people find noisy environments very challenging.
Even when fitted with hearing aids, hearing impaired people struggle in ”cocktail-party”
scenarios. To support hearing impaired people is such scenarios, current hearing aids
are using adaptive noise reduction algorithms and microphone beam-forming patterns
to improve the speech intelligibility in noisy environments (Dillon, 2008). However, in
situations with multiple speech-like sources e.g. a group conversation those techniques
cannot reliably distinguish between attended and unattended sources. Thus, new solu-
tions must be developed to improve everyday life of hearing impaired people. Utilizing
electrophysiology measurements could be one good candidate for such a solution. Pre-
vious experiments by O’Sullivan et al. (2015) and Akram et al. (2016) showed that an
attended source can be predicted based on single trial electroencephalography (EEG)
or magnetoencephalography (MEG) in a competing talker scenario. These experiments
were conducted using either monaural or binaural stimulation in a controlled measure-
ment setups. To check more realistic setups Das et al. (2018) looked at the boundary
conditions for auditory attention decoding (ADD) by using additional background noise
and spatial separation of the attended and unattended streams. Furthermore, Fuglsang
et al. (2017) investigated the effect of multi-talker scenarios and different reverberation
times on the performance of cortical attention tracking. Another way to create realistic
conditions is to introduce audiovisual stimuli as reported in Crosse and Lalor (2014). In
their study it was shown that an audiovisual stimulation results in an earlier cortical
response. The authors suggest that the earlier response is driven by a quicker processing
in the auditory cortex driven by the interaction between visual signals and the auditory
features.
==PAGE:20==
2
1 Introduction
Auditory attention decoding has the potential usage in real-life applications like
Brain-Computer-Interfaces (BCI) in connection with hearing devices as described by
Dau et al. (2018). Along this line, EEG systems seem to be a suitable tool for a portable
implementation in hearing devices or any other wearables. Fiedler et al. (2017) showed
that it is possible to decode a listeners attention by using in-Ear-EEG electrodes. They
postulate, that these techniques could for instance be combined with a hearing device in
a BCI modality. However, implementations for everyday use are still under development.
A step towards real-time implementation was presented by Alickovic et al. (2016) who
developed an algorithm to determine the optimal order for a finite impulse response
FIR filter, which could be used to decode attention based on recorded EEG data and
associated audio features.
However, for a robust classification a certain duration of
input data is required which was among other studies reported by Wong et al. (2018).
Decreasing the amount of data required for a good classification is one of the current
obstacles for real-time implementation.
A good summary of available methods and
their performance can be found in Alickovic et al. (2019). Having a robust real-time
classification of attended and unattended sources could be used to steer microphone
beamformers in hearing aids as reported in Das et al. (2020) for instance.
1.2
Scope of the Project
This project introduces another step towards real-world measurement scenarios to de-
code an attended speaker(s). The real-world aspect was achieved by using audiovisual
stimulation displayed on an 88-inch wide curved screen for a reasonable visual stimulus
presentation. Furthermore, for a realistic acoustic stimulation we propose a competing
monologue versus dialogue paradigm with additional multi-talker babble noise in the
background. Such a setup introduces a lot of new variables and uncertainties that need
to be considered. However, we think that it also provides a more realistic measurement
scenario. A setup in this constellation has not been used before and has the potential to
give new insights to the performance of current ADD techniques. The acquired knowl-
edge can be useful for understanding the performance of BCI systems in future real-life
”cocktail-party” situations. The here proposed condition seems reasonable to us since a
”cocktail-party” situation often includes group conversations and not competing single
talkers.
The main goal of this project is to apply existing en-/decoding models on EEG data
collected in realistic measurement conditions. The experimental setup introduces a new
perspective and more complexity for attention decoding models and tries to fill another
gap towards real-life applications. The central research question can be formulated as
follows:
”Do current ADD techniques provide enough predictive power to identify attended and
unattended talker(s) in realistic scenarios?”
The collected data set contains subjective measures like question scores and difficulty
ratings on a trial by trial level. The main outcome are EEG and eye-tracker recordings
in response and as consequence to the presented stimuli. Based on the above formulated
research question the following hypothesis can be made:
==PAGE:21==
1.3 Outline
3
• Current attention decoding strategies CAN predict an attended dialogue target
stimulus.
• It is possible to determine if a listener is attending to a dialogue or a monologue.
Along with the formulated hypothesis, many other questions can be targeted once
the measurements are conducted. This includes the influence of eye-movements on EEG
recordings in the here proposed conditions. By recording eye-movements we have an
additional reference signal regarding the focus and behaviour of the subjects in the dif-
ferent measurement conditions. Furthermore, a potential difference in difficulty between
the two condition can be investigated by using the subjective measurement outcomes.
Yet, this project is not aiming to develop new tools to increase the decoding perfor-
mance or to solve the ”cocktail-party” problem. The project rather highlights potential
obstacles on the way towards a real-life EEG based BCI application.
1.3
Outline
Chapter 2 of this master thesis presents some theoretical background information. This
starts on a broader level, by discussing the relevant human sensory systems. Thereafter,

CHAPTER 2
Theory
This chapter presents background information and provides an overview of the tech-
niques applied in this project. The first two sections are providing a brief description
of the auditory and visual system. The third section summarizes how evoked poten-
tials are generated and measured using non-invasive EEG measurement techniques. In
the subsequent section common system identification methods, to en-/decode attention
from electrophysiological measurements are described. Here, we are focusing on regres-
sion based models to estimate temporal response functions (TRFs) and to reconstruct
stimulus features. In section five attention en-/decoding models are discussed in more
detail.
Since we collected eye-movements alongside with EEG data, the last section
describes some characteristics which can be derived using a simple velocity threshold
(VT) algorithm to differentiate between the different conditions and to draw lines to the
information obtained by the electrophysiology measures.
2.1
The Peripheral Auditory System
The human auditory system can be roughly divided into peripheral and central part.
Figure 2.1 shows the auditory periphery including the outer, middle and inner ear. The
outer ear picks up sound pressure variations in the air through the pinna and leads
them down the ear canal to the tympanic membrane (eardrum). The conical shaped
membrane transmits the structural vibrations across the ossicles, located in the middle
ear, to the oval window. The oval window is the entrance to the inner ear, which is filled
with two kinds of liquids, perilymph and endolymph. The impedance matching between
both media (air and lymph) is performed by (1) an area transformation and (2) the lever
system provided via the ossicles. The Eustachian tube provides a pressure equalization
between the surrounding aerial pressure and the pressure in the middle ear. The snail
shaped cochlea is situated in the inner ear and connects the auditory periphery with the
central auditory system via the auditory nerve. The cochlea sits in the temporal bone
and winds 2.5 times around the auditory nerve.
Figure 2.2 illustrates a cross-section of the cochlea. The cochlea is separated into
the three channels scala vestibuli, scala media and scala timpani. The Organ of Corti
sits on top of the basilar membrane, which separates the scala media and scala timpani.
When vibrations arrive through the ossicles at the oval window a travelling wave excites
the basilar membrane at a certain location according to its tonotopic organization. The
tonotopic arrangement of the basilar membrane can be simulated as mechanical trans-
mission line model with location specific mass, spring and damper elements as presented
in de Boer (1980). In the electrical analogy, the place depended frequency response of
the basilar membrane is often described by a series of band-pass filters with increas-
ing bandwidth towards higher frequencies. A common approach to digitally implement

On top of the Organ of Corti are the outer (OHC) and inner (IHC) hair cells. The

place and with a certain frequency and amplitude. Looking at the input/output function
of the basilar membrane movements, a compression can be noticed. This compressive
behaviour is attributed to the OHCs (Plack, 2013). The visible part of the hair cells
are the so called steriocilia.
The deflection of the steriocilia leads to an opening of
the ion channels and an influx of the extracellular liquid into the cell. This is then
causing the generation of a potential gradient between the inside and outside of a cell.
If the postsynaptic potential (summation of the input at all synapses) in the soma is
above a certain threshold, action potentials are generated which then propagate along
the auditory nerve to the central auditory system. In common auditory models the
deflection of the steriocilia and the IHC is often simplified as half-wave rectification and
low-pass filtering (Heinz et al., 2001).

2.2
The Visual System
The human visual system is the most dominant senses in terms of information transport
compared to others sensory systems like the auditory system (Reisberg and Snavely,
2018). Figure 2.3 shows a schematic representation of the eyeball and its components
within the human head. When reflected light beams enter the pupil, they pass through
the cornea/lens and hit the retina at the back of the eyeball. The retina is equipped
with thousands of photoreceptors. These can be grouped in rods, which are sensitive
to light intensity, and cones, which are sensitive to color. The brain receives the visual
information via the optical nerve.
In the central neural system the visual input is
projected on the V1 area (primary visual area) (Reisberg and Snavely, 2018). This area
is located in the occipital lobe at back of the head.
When recording EEG data the potentials generated by muscular eye activity are
called electrooculography (EOG). This activity can be divided into eye-blinks (short
peaks) and eye-movements (gradual changes). The potentials for the eye-movements are
generated by the positive charged cornea and the negative charged retina, whereas the
eye-blinks are muscular activity. Eye-movements change the electrical field comparable
to a dipole. In some cases eye-blinks and eye-movements are considered as artifacts
(Remijn et al., 2014) and need to be treated in the EEG records since they are not of
interest. There are multiple tools to treat EOG artifacts in EEG signal. Both Joyce
et al. (2007) and de Cheveigné and Parra (2014) presented automated ways to treat
EOG artifacts in electrophysiology recordings.

2.3
Evoked Potentials
Neural activity in response to external stimulation can be measured in forms of evoked
potentials (EPs). Evoked potentials refer to electrical changes in the sensory periphery
and central nervous system in response to any stimulation (Picton, 2010). If the stimu-
lus is sound the measured electrical activities are often called auditory evoked potentials
(AEPs). Non-invasive ways to measure brain activities in the far-field are electroen-
cephalography or magnetoencephalography. However, in this project we are focusing
on EEG measurements. As mentioned earlier (see Section 2.1), the electrical potentials
generated in the neurons have different stages e.g. postsynaptic potentials or action
potentials. These potentials can be described by different components with different
orders (o). The postsynaptic potential for instance can be specified as dipole moment
(o = 2), whereas the propagating action potential can be described by a quadruple mo-
ment (o = 3). Since the electrical potential decreases with 1
Ro , higher-order components
are weaker represented in the far-field. Thus, the electrical field picked up by electrodes
in non-invasive measurement systems is mainly driven by the early electrical components
(postsynaptic potentials). Furthermore, placing the electrodes far away from the source
has the consequence that all sorts of activities in the brain contribute to the measured
signal and not just the ones in response to the presented stimulus. This however re-
sults in a very poor signal to noise ratio SNR. The electrical field potential measured in
one single electrode rm(t) over a certain period of time t can be formulated as a linear
combination of neuronal activity as in Equation (2.1).

the electric fields or use conductive gel to lower the skin impedance for the electrodes.
Internal artifacts are related to noise coming from within the subject/brain. This can be
due to eye-blink or eye-movement also known as EOG artifacts (see Section 2.2). Other
examples are heart related artifacts (electrocardiography ECG) signals) or muscular ac-
tivity. When using a real-life measurement scenario, it is almost certain that the EEG
data contains several kinds of the here mentioned artifacts. However, there are different
methods available to process EEG data in order to separate and remove unwanted ar-
tifacts from the recordings. One very fundamental approach is to apply different types
of filters on the EEG data. However, as described by de Cheveigné and Nelken (2019)
it is important to keep delays and spreads, introduced by filter operations, in mind and
report them thoroughly. This is especially the case when looking at early response mea-
surements, where timing is a crucial factor to draw conclusions. There are also more
advanced techniques like independent component analysis (ICA) and denoising source
separation (DSS) reported in de Cheveigné and Parra (2014) to remove undesired ar-
tifacts from EEG data. In case of a manual removal of ICA components a researcher
needs to be careful not to remove signals of any interest. Furthermore, a manual data
cleaning approach is generally lowering the reproducibility of the data pre-processing
stage.

2.4
System Identification Methods
Within the last couple of years, different system identification techniques have been
used to study neuronal activity in the auditory (O’Sullivan et al. (2015), Alickovic
et al. (2016), Hausfeld et al. (2018), Fuglsang et al. (2020)) and visual (Theunissen
et al. (2001)) cortex. The basic idea of this approach is to characterize an unknown
system (brain) by its input/output response function. Under the coarse assumption of
linearity one can make use of concepts developed to describe linear time-invariant (LTI)
systems.
With this assumption a system under test (SUT) is fully described by its
impulse response (IR)/system weights. Assuming linearity the system response r(t, m)
can be calculated by convolving the stimulus input s(t) at different time-lags τ with the
system weights w(τ, m) as described in Equation (2.2).

2.5
Attention En-/Decoding
From early studies we know that AEPs in connection to selective auditory attention
introduces an increase in the LAEPs (Hillyard et al., 1973). The previously introduced
linear system identification methods are a common tool to en-/decode attention when
using continuous speech stimuli (O’Sullivan et al., 2015). For univariate attention en-
/decoding models the broadband speech envelope is often used as feature in ADD. It
was shown by Power et al. (2011) that the attended stimulus envelope is to some extent
preserved in the auditory cortex and enhanced for an attended acoustic stimulus. It is
known that the fine structure for higher frequencies of an acoustic stimulus is most likely
not preserved in the auditory cortex. However, the envelope structure is still available
at higher auditory stages. To perform a ADD, there are multiple ways to extract the
broadband speech envelope. A good overview of possible envelope extraction methods
for univariate ADD is given by Biesmans et al. (2017). Here a significant improvement
in decoding performance was shown, when using a simplified model of the auditory
periphery to obtain the envelope feature.
As previously described, there are two directions to tackle attention analysis. From
an interpretable point of view the forward model weights TRFs are comparable to the
auditory evoked potentials as shown in Figure 2.4. In Lalor et al. (2009) a quantitative
comparison between AEPs and a model driven response measure was reported. Even if
the spatial resolution of EEG measurements is disturbed, the average across a selection
of electrodes can results in a similar pattern as for AEPs. Looking at the topology of the
forward model weights ˆw at around τ = 100 ms negative activation pattern can usually
found in the fronto-central electrodes. Increasing τ to approximately 200 ms the same
electrode region shows positive forward model weights. It was shown that by taking the
average across the fronto-central electrodes the N1 and P2 are represented by the model
weights. In a selective attention task the P2 wave of the attended stream is generally
enhanced compared to the unattended stream (Fuglsang et al., 2017). This can also be
seen when looking at the time course of the averaged forward model weights. However,
from a BCI point of view it is more important to reliably classify whether someone is
paying attention to talker A or B. As reported earlier, it was shown by Wong et al. (2018)
that discriminative models outperform forward model in terms of classification accuracy.
To calculate the classification accuracy for a backward model the pearson’s correlation
coefficient between the predicted ˆs(t) and the actual acoustic stimulus envelope ˆs(t)
can be calculated and compared. For the attended stream the envelope reconstruction
accuracy is normally increased (O’Sullivan et al., 2015). By using labelled data the
classification accuracy can be obtained through a binary classification in a competing
talker scenario.

to use a permutation test. This can be done by using miss-labelled or phase-scrambled
data (Prichard and Theiler, 1994) and a sufficient number of interactions to create a
null-distribution of random classification accuracies (Noirhomme et al., 2014). Based on
this distribution the p-value can be deduced (see Equation (2.15)).

2.6
Eye-Movements
Eye-tracking systems like Tobii pro glasses II do often utilize video-oculography (VOG)
measures. This is done by using infrared lights pointing towards the pupil and record-
ing the resulting reflections.
From these recording eye-gaze parameters can then be
estimated.
A common classification of eye-movements is: fixations, saccades, smooth pursuits
(SPs) and others (Leigh and Zee, 2015). The most common calculated metrics are fix-
ations and saccades. A fixation describes a stable eye gaze onto a certain target. A
saccade denotes a rapid change in eye gaze indicated by high angular velocities. This
happens if one changes the fixation from one target to another (Komogortsev and Kar-
pov, 2013). Based on those metrics one can classify different experimental conditions.
There are algorithms available to identify saccades and fixations. For this project we
are focusing on a method called velocity threshold identification (I-VT) (Komogortsev
et al., 2010) algorithm. However, in this algorithm the velocity threshold needs to be
tuned to the used equipment and measurement condition. However, Komogortsev et al.
(2010) suggests a range of thresholds between 30◦/s and 70◦/s, which can be used to
identify saccade start and end points.

CHAPTER 3
Material and Methods
In the first section of this chapter the participants are introduced. The second section
gives a detailed description of the audiovisual stimulus material. This includes stimulus
preparation, trial design and masker generation. In the following section the experi-
mental setup is presented. Here the hardware setup, the presentation format and the
experimental conditions are described. Section four outlines the experimental procedure
including the data collection process. The fifth section explains how the different out-
come measures were prepared for the analysis. The last section describes the methods
that were used to obtain the results presented in the subsequent chapter.
3.1
Participants
Seventeen normal hearing (NH) native danish subjects (7 female, 2 left-handed) took
part in the experiment. The age range of the subjects was from 18 to 57 years. The
initial age criteria (18 to 35 years) has not been met. However, there are two age out-
liers in the data set sub-003: 57 and sub-011: 54 years. The remaining subjects are
between 18 and 32 years old, with a mean age of 26 (± 3.54 std.) years. All subjects
reported no history of neurological disorders (e.g. dementia or chronic stress). To deter-
mine the handedness of the subjects, a danish version of The Assessment and Analysis
of Handedness, The Edinburgh Inventory, was used (Oldfield, 1971). The subjects were
asked not to drink coffee or smoke within one hour prior to the experiment. All sub-
jects either had visual impairments or used visual aids. The subjects were financially
compensated for the traveling distance. The experiment was approved by the Science
Ethics Committees for the Capital Region Denmark in accordance to the Declaration of
Helsinki. The collected data were anonymised following the Data Protection Act (Act
No. 502). All subjects agreed on the data collection before the experiment by signing
the relevant consent forms.
3.2
Audiovisual Speech Stimuli
The audiovisual stimuli were recorded at Eriksholm Research Centre (ERH). Four pro-
fessional danish actors (2 male and 2 female) were recruited to create the material. For

3.5
Data Pre-processing
This section describes the computational steps used for data pre-processing. The first
section explains the stimulus feature extraction. The second section shows the EEG
pre-processing pipeline. The pre-processing pipelines for the stimulus feature extraction
and the EEG data follows a similar principle as in Fuglsang et al. (2020). The last
section describes the pre-processing of the eye-tracking data and the feature extraction.
3.5.1
Stimulus Envelope Extraction
Figure 3.9 shows the steps to extract the audio stimulus features. As explained in Sec-
tion 2.5 we are focusing on the univariate attention decoding approach using the stimulus
envelope as model input. To extract a single broadband envelope for the dialogue, the
cohesive channels were summed together. This was done to avoid numerical instabilities
due to long pauses in the separated dialogue signals. To estimate the broadband enve-
lope the audio signals were down sampled from 48 kHz to 12 kHz using a 98th order
zero-phase finite impulse response (FIR) low-pass filter with a 6 kHz cut-off frequency
to avoid aliasing. Afterwards, a gammatone filterbank with 24 ERB-spaced center fre-
quencies between 100 Hz and 4 kHz was used (Søndergaard and Majdak, 2013). The
output from each filter was then rectified by taking the absolute value |s(t)|. Each band
was then compressed using the power law relation with a factor of β = 0.3 to account for
non-linearities in the auditory periphery (see Section 2.1). Next, the 24 channels were
averaged to obtain a single vector for each signal. The signals were then down sampled
from 12 kHz to 512 Hz using a zero-phase FIR low-pass filter of 620th order and a
cut-off frequency of 256 Hz. Hereafter, a final down sampling operation from 512 Hz
to 64 Hz was performed. Again, a zero-phase FIR low-pass filter of 226th order and
a 30 Hz cut-off frequency was used. The signals were then band-pass filtered between
1 Hz and 9 Hz. This was achieved by applying a 106th order zero-phase FIR high-pass
filter with a 1 Hz cut-off frequency and a 94th order zero-phase FIR low-pass filter at a
cut-off frequency of 9 Hz. To correct for filter delays the output of each filter was time
shifted as suggested in de Cheveigné and Nelken (2019). In a final step the signal was
cropped from 2 s post-stimulus onset to 110 s post-stimulus onset. This was defined as
the time of interest (TOI). The stimulus feature for each audio stream was then saved
in a mat-file for later processing.
Attention decoding has not been performed in this kind of experimental paradigm,
thus we needed to establish a meaning full way of combining both dialogue signals. The

Next, the 64 EEG channels were re-referenced to the average of the left (Ext. 1) and

Subsequently, the EEG data were down
sampled from 8192 Hz to 128 Hz using a 226th order zero-phase low-pass FIR filter with

regress out EEG artefacts. The EEG signal was then segmented into trials based on
the timestamps found via the recorded trigger signals. Each trial was then band-pass
filtered between 1 Hz and 9 Hz to match the frequency bands which were mainly driven
by attention as reported in Section 2.5. For the band-pass filter a 106th order zero-phase
FIR high-pass filter with a cut-off frequency of 1 Hz and a 94th order zero-phase FIR
low-pass with a cut-off frequency of 9 Hz was used. In the last step, the EEG signal

3.5.3
Eye-Tracking Pre-processing
In Figure 3.13 the data pre-processing pipeline for the eye-tracking measurements can
be found. Firstly, the eye-tracking data were separated from the head-tracking data
and the individual trials were identified using the timestamps identified via the recorded
trigger signals. Thereafter, a lower and upper bound was calculated to identify eye-blink
artifacts based on a protocol from Pedrotti et al. (2011) (see Figure 3.12 A). In the same
processing step a quality index based on the ratio between the number of artifact and
total number of observations for all data streams was calculated (see Figure 3.12 B).
The artifact timestamps were extended by 50 ms before and after the blink. A linear
interpolation was used to fill up the blink artifacts. To smooth the signal a 30th order
median filter was used.
Based on the greater averaged quality index between the data streams for each eye,
either the left or right eye was chosen for further analysis. If the data quality from
both eyes was below a threshold of 50 % the trial was not analysed further. Next, the
data were down sampled from 100 Hz to 64 Hz using a 226th order zero-phase FIR
low-pass filter. The TOI for the eye-data were also defined by truncating the data to
2 s to 110 s post stimulus. Subsequently, two rotational angles (yaw and pitch) were
calculated from the unit gaze vector coordinates. Based on the yaw angle the horizontal
velocity was computed by multiplication with the sampling frequency. This horizontal
velocity was then low-pass filtered to suppress high frequency noise content. A simple
velocity threshold algorithm with VT = 60 deg/s was used to identify saccade and
fixation start/end points (see Figure 3.12 C). Based on the start and stop samples
of the saccades and fixations, the average saccade amplitude (ASA) and the average
fixation duration (AFD) was calculated (see Figure 3.12 D). To correct for outliers,
saccade amplitudes below 4 and above 50 degree have been removed. Also fixations that
are shorter then 400 ms and longer then 50 s have been discarded.
Comparing Figure 3.12 D with Figure 3.14 one can see an example of how the VT
algorithm operates in the dialogue versus monologue condition. It is clear to see that
the horizontal velocity is less peaky in the monologue condition, indicating less saccades
during the stimulus presentation. This again results in a smaller number of saccades
and fixations.

3.6
Data Analysis
The pre-processed data were analysed individually in their respective measurement do-
main. However, the general perspective was tailored towards working out differences
between the monologue and dialogue attended conditions. For the ratings and questions
the average across subjects and trials was calculated. To explore the behavioral data, de-
scriptive plots were generated and observed differences were tested for significance. The
STM measurement was analysed in respect to the behavioral data obtained from the
main experiment. This was done to see if there is a meaningful correlation of subjects
who rated the trials as more difficult and had a high threshold in the STM. Looking at
the behavioral data could reveal potential differences in the perceived difficulty between
the two monologue and dialogue attended conditions. Furthermore, outliers in STM
thresholds or ratings could be removed for further analysis or brought in perspective to
the electrophysiology measurement outcomes.
To analyse the eye-tracking data a processing pipeline was implemented in MATLAB.
In this analysis pipeline the previous discussed pre-processing steps were implemented
following some basic ideas taken from Holmqvist et al. (2011). To summarize the raw
measurement outcomes four commonly known quantities are calculated. Those quanti-
ties are the average saccade amplitude (ASA), the average number of saccades (ANS),
the average fixation duration (AFD) and the average number of fixations (ANF), which
were introduced in Section 3.5.3. Since the subject was told to sit quietly in front of the

To analyse the recorded and pre-processed EEG data, the HPC cluster at DTU, in

hyperparameters was set to λ = 100, 101, ..., 1012. A hyperparameter of λ = 104 was
often selected by the CV procedure. The 108 s long measurement trials were processed as
a whole and not separated into sub-trials. This means, that each trial can be associated
with one fold in the cross-validation description (see Subsection 2.4.2). Using one entire
trial instead of several sub-trials was found to be useful in order to avoid edge effects
(Alickovic et al., 2019).
Before the model generation the attended and unattended
stimulus envelopes and EEG data were standardized using the z-score by subtracting
the mean and divide by it standard deviation.
The analysis process of the EEG data can essentially be separated into generic and
specific models generation. For the generic forward and backward model preparation all
24 attended (MAtt.) and unattended (MUtt.) trials from the main experiment were used
at once. Those trials contained all the factors, which were presented in Section 3.2. For
the attended and unattended model calculation a separate LOOCV was performed to
find an optimal subject specific hyperparameter λopt., Att. and λopt., Utt.. Subsequently,
24 ridge regression models for both attended and unattended streams were trained.
For each model 23 trials were used in the training stage and the remaining trial was
used to evaluate the model performance in a LOOCV fashion. The performance from
each of the 24 obtained models for both forward and backward direction was evaluated
using the Pearson’s linear correlation coefficient. The correlation coefficient between the
estimated response ˆr(t, m) and the true response r(t, m) as well as the estimated stimulus
envelope ˆs(t) and the true envelope s(t) was calculated respectively.
The estimated
TRF for the forward models and the estimated model weights ˆg(τ, m) are stored across
the entire time-lags from τmin to τmax.
To determine the classification accuracy a
stimulus reconstruction model was used. Here the subject specific baseline data were
utilized. Again, a suitable hyperparameter was found before the model training stage
through LOOCV. Thereafter, the entire ≈20 min of baseline trials were used to fit a
single Tikhonov regression model. This model was trained on the 5 monologue and 5
dialogue baseline trials. Afterwards, the model was used to predict both attended and
unattended stimulus envelopes from the main experiment. For that purpose the attended
and unattended envelopes and EEG responses of the main experiment were concatenated
along the time axes. The prediction was done for different time windows sizes ranging

and model weights ˆg(τ, m)) was 600 ms. After obtaining the correlation coefficient for

higher correlation coefficient was performed. The final classification accuracy is then

attended and 12 unattended trials per iteration. After the separation the same regression
model training and testing steps as for the generic analysis were performed. However,
the available data is reduced by 50 %. In the classification analysis the same 10 baseline
trials were used to train a reconstruction model. The obtained reconstruction model
was then used in to predict the stimulus envelope of either the dialogue attended and
monologue unattended or vice versa. Based on the predictions a specific classification
accuracy could be calculated. To see if there are differences between monologue and
dialogue attended streams, a significance test on subject-trial and subject level between
both reconstruction accuracies was performed.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
In this chapter the previously presented results are discussed. This includes the dif-
ferences between monologue and dialogue attended conditions across the measurement
domains are reviewed. Furthermore, possible connections between the different measure-
ment outcomes are highlighted and discussed.
5.1
Monologue vs. Dialogue Condition
In this project we introduced a realistic measurement setup and compared a direct
(monologue) and indirect (dialogue) attended conversational paradigm throughout dif-
ferent measurement domains. We found differences between the attended monologue
and the attended dialogue in all examined measurement outcomes and on the acoustic
stimulus level. This includes behavioral data, eye-tracking, electrophysiological measure-
ments and the stimuli themselves.
Looking at the stimuli, we suggested a possibility to use two dialogue streams within
an attention decoding framework. Our proposal was supported by an analysis of the
stimulus properties used in the measurements. In the stimulus analysis we compared
the averaged monologue and the separated/summed dialogue in the frequency and mod-
ulation frequency domain (see Figure 3.10). In the stimulus analysis a level imbalance
between the monologue and dialogue recordings towards higher frequency bands was
discovered.
As a possible consequence, an inherently unequal difficulty between dia-
logue attended versus monologue attended, could have occurred. We know from Das
et al. (2018) that the SNR between attended and unattended source has an influence
on the decoding accuracy. Hence, even if the level difference is frequency specific, a
bias in favor of the attended monologue could emerge. The initial assumption that a
single summed dialogue envelope can be used is motivated by the averaged modulation
excitation pattern shown in the left graph of Figure 3.10. I was shown that the envelope
of the summed dialogue results in a modulation excitation pattern, which looks similar
to the monologue one.
For the behavioral outcomes in Figure 4.1 a significant difference in the subjective audi-
bility rating between the baseline and main experiment for both conditions was shown.
This is based on the fact, that the baseline experiment was conducted without any ad-
ditional background noise and at the same sound level as the main experiment. The
variation in difficulty is also supported by the slightly lower percent correct in the ques-
tions asked during the main experiment. However, the overall percent correct is at a
high level, which resulted in ceiling effects. Looking at the condition specific difference,
there was no significant effect found between the monologue and dialogue condition.
This indicates that both conditions had the same perceived audibility. With this in
mind, the level difference in the excitation pattern was maybe not as noticeable for the

test subjects. The frequency specific level mismatch between the two conditions was
maybe balanced out by the additional visual stimulation. Schwartz et al. (2004) showed
that the possibility of lip reading enhances the speech intelligibility. Since we presented
an audiovisual stimulus, more than just the acoustic information was available for the
subjects. Coming back to the subjective difficulty rating, the initial intention of picking
a SNR, which results in a sufficient complexity for NH subjects, could be reached.
In Section 4.2 the results for the recorded eye-tracking data were presented. The main
focus was to work out differences in respect both attended measurement conditions.
Based on the calculated characteristics a significant difference between the attended
monologue and dialogue condition in both baseline and main experiment could be shown.
However, the ASA does not match with the angle between the talker pairs in the dialogue
attended condition (see Figure 3.4). This miss-match could be due to wrongly identified
saccades or intra-talker saccades, which influence the trial by trial averaged saccade
amplitudes. However, this should be taken care of by the applying thresholds for too
small and unreasonable large angular changes in the yaw angle. Another factor that
influences the gaze angles are head movements, which are not included in the analysis.
Nevertheless, the results suggest that there is a significantly higher ocular activity in
the dialogue attended condition compared to the monologue condition. This however,
is trivial in context of the here presented experimental paradigm.
Yet, it contains
possible implications for the subsequent attention decoding analysis. By looking at the
eye-tracking data alone, one could already separate the monologue from the dialogue
condition.
In Section 4.3 the main measurement results were presented. Here we pointed out the
basic analysis of the earlier proposed measurement paradigm for the assessment of at-
tention decoding in realistic conditions. The obtained TRFs show a somewhat similar
pattern as those found in previous attention decoding studies by e.g. Crosse et al. (2016)
or Hjortkjaer et al. (2018). In order to address potential differences between measure-
ment conditions specific models were generated and analysed. Either of them showed
two positive peaks at around 50 ms and 200 ms as well as a negative amplitude at approx-
imately 125 ms post stimulus. The overall course of the obtained TRFs is comparable
to AEPs in the respective latency interval, which can be quantitatively compared as
reported by Lalor et al. (2009). However, comparing the latency of the TRFs to similar
studies with audiovisual stimulation by Crosse and Lalor (2014), a delay of about 50 ms
as well as a temporal spread of the TRFs can be noticed. This delay could originate
from a miss-alignment of the trigger signals, which normally should align the measured
response and the audio stimulus. Another reason could be an introduced delay by the
filter operation along the recording and pre-processing chain as reported in de Cheveigné
and Nelken (2019). The temporal spread and the less distinct peak at 200 ms post stim-
ulus (P219) could be due to the distractor/noise configuration in the main experiment.
Hjortkjaer et al. (2018) suggested that with decreased SNR the TRF amplitude is signifi-
cantly decreased, broader and delayed. Comparing the attended and unattended speech
streams a reduced envelope entrainment of the ignored stream was found. This was also
examined by numerous of other attention decoding studies e.g. O’Sullivan et al. (2015),
Fuglsang et al. (2017), Power et al. (2012) and Zion Golumbic et al. (2013). Looking at
the topography plots for the forward model weights a similar spatial activation pattern
as in Fuglsang et al. (2017) could be observed. When comparing the generic and specific

trained forward models the overall pattern looks similar. However, when looking at the
monologue and dialogue attended TRFs an earlier and in amplitude increased P219 for
the attended dialogue could be shown. This could be the result of the difference in
frequency specific stimulus levels reported earlier (see Figure 3.10). Another possibility
could be the difference in required listening effort and the greater attention needed to
follow the attended dialogue and answer the subsequent question. Albeit, the subjects
reported an equal amount of difficulty for the monologue and dialogue attended condi-

rather than to the perceived amount of necessary listening effort spent to attended to the
dialogue. In a verbal interview after the experiment many subjects reported that it was
harder for them to follow the information flow in the attended dialogue condition. This
is likely, since the dialogue speech stimuli consisted of short incoherent stories, where
the actors aimed to solve the diapix task described in Section 3.2. On the other hand,
the monologues were said to be interesting and good to follow. With this knowledge a
general bias in listening effort or difficulty towards the dialogue attended condition could
be postulated. In a recent publication by Jaeger et al. (2020) it was suggested to use a
EEG based attention decoding analysis as objective measure for listening effort, which
indicates a link between spent listening effort and the expended attention. Under the
assumption of a higher information exchange rate in the dialogues, a logical implication
could be that the subjects needed to pay more attention to follow the dialogue than the
monologue.
Continuing with the subject averaged and standardized backward model topologies pre-

However, for τ = −78 ms in both specific and

gression parameters can be observed. At this early latencies no clear spatial pattern is
noticeable. Locally higher amplitudes in the regression parameters could be driven by
single electrodes that are corrupted, drifting or which contain other kinds of artifacts in
the response measure. At higher negative latencies a more pronounced spatial pattern
with higher activation in the centro-temporal electrodes for the attended stream could
be shown. At similar latencies for the unattended stream, the weights show a normal-
ized amplitude, which is mostly close to zero. This spatial pattern and the difference
between attended and unattended activation is strongest in the attended dialogue - unat-
tended monologue case. These spatial activation pattern were also found for instance
in Fuglsang et al. (2017). However, the unattended reconstruction weights do not show
an inverted pattern compared to the attended weights, which is the case in Fuglsang
et al. (2017). This disagreement could be due to major differences in the experimental
design and used stimuli. There is a difference between the obtained topologies and the
ones found in relevant literature for the electrode contribution at τ = −156 ms. For the
current study higher weights from the frontopolar electrodes Fp1, FpZ and Fp2 could be
observed. Those increased weights could emerge for ocular activity e.g. eye-movements
during the attention task. However, it is not clear, if the contribution of the frontopolar
electrodes is based on more eye-movements since the same pattern can be observed in
the monologue attended backward model weights. Taking the results of the eye-tracking
measurements into account, a significant difference in ocular activity between the mono-
logue and dialogue attended condition was shown. The way how the EOG artifacts were

The reported reconstruction accuracies for both the generic (see Figure 4.11) and spe-
cific (see Figure 4.12) case show a mean rAttended, which is at the lower margin of
previous findings that used regularized regression models (Wong et al., 2018). However,
for rUnattended the mean is comparable to earlier results. A difference in variance be-
tween attended and unattended reconstruction accuracies could also be noticed in other
studies by Wong et al. (2018) for instance. The reconstruction accuracy for the attended
stream is significantly higher than the unattended one. This was also reported in other
publications e.g. Fuglsang et al. (2017) or Wong et al. (2018) who used backward mod-
els. This is mainly due to a better representation of the attended stimulus envelope
structure in the auditory cortex (Theunissen et al., 2001). Looking at Figure 4.12 the
attended and unattended stream is split further into monologue and dialogue condition.
Here a significantly higher accuracy was found in the dialogue attended condition. Yet,
the difference was small and only found to be significant when the observations were
analysed at the subject-trial level. Averaging across trials and looking at a subject level
the significance vanishes (see Figure 4.13).
The presented decoder weights, based on the collected EEG data during the baseline
experiment (see Figure 4.14), show an increasing spatial contribution from the centro-
temporal electrodes located in the left and right hemisphere. Again, the baseline trials
contained both monologue and dialogue attended trials without any further distrac-
tion. These pattern are to some extent also visible in the backward model weights in
Figure 4.10, even if they are not as pronounced. Based on this stimulus reconstruc-
tion model, a binary classifier was used to evaluate the classification accuracy of the
constructed baseline model. Here it is worth mentioning that the classification of the
labeled attended and unattended trials was done with unseen data. However, the actors
and the relative spatial talker separation was the same as in the main experiment to
some extent. Nevertheless, the absolute spatial location of the talkers were different
between baseline and main experiment. The obtained classification accuracies in the
generic and specific case increased with a growing window length. This was also found
by other studies by Wong et al. (2018) and makes good sense since the given amount of
observed data increases with the window length. In the results a high standard deviation
across subjects is clearly noticeable. This strong subject to subject variability can be
partly explained by the numerous of uncontrolled variables in a realistic measurement
setup (e.g. head-movements, eye-movements) as well as fatigue effects as a consequence
of the total duration of the experiment (3.5 h). The previous mentioned points could
be the reason for the overall lower classification accuracy compared to similar studies.
Nevertheless, the classification accuracies are above the calculated chance level as shown
in Figure 4.15. In Figure 4.15b the classification accuracies for specifically evaluated at-
tended dialogue and monologue trials are shown. An increased classification accuracy,
in respect to the lengthened observation window, for the attended dialogue compared
to the attended monologue could be obtained. This increase is in line with the previous
differences between dialogue and monologue attended.
A possible explanation could
be the contribution of eye-movements, which are likely correlated with speech envelope.

However, there are no studies which support this claim.
Overall, we found fairly similar results, compared to other studies in attention decoding.
By applying forward and backward models to the here examined realistic measurement
paradigm a difference between attending to a dialogue or to a monologue was observed
in multiple measurement domains, not just electrophysiological data. Yet, it is hard
to conclude on the exact origin of this difference due to multiple uncontrolled variables
in the experiment. In a realistic measurement setup, subjects should be able to move
freely with both body and eyes.
Furthermore, the presence of multiple stimuli and
interaction with in the stimuli introduce potential biases in the recorded EEG response
as well as biases with the stimuli. Controlling all uncertainties post hoc comes always
with assumptions that need to be made in order to clean the EEG measurements for
instance. Due to the nature of introducing more realistic conditions we are sacrificing
some degree of interpretability of the results. Therefore, we focused not on the explicit
auditory or visual attention, but rather on attention towards a single speaker or a group
of speakers. For holding up the realistic condition we avoided using a headrest or a
visual fixation point. This and other sources of noise (both externally and internally) are
contained in the recorded data. However, some noise reduction techniques were applied
in the data pre-processing. However, it is certain that there are fundamental linguistic
differences between a monologue and dialogue. The initially stated hypothesis, that we
are able to decode an attended dialogue as well as distinguish between monologue and
dialogue was verified by showing an significant classification accuracy in the executed
measurements. From a BCI system point of view the separation between monologue and
dialogue attended should be possible by means of EEG or eye-tracking measurements.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion & Outlook
The main goal of this thesis was to establish a realistic measurement paradigm to assess
the performance of attention decoding methods in such conditions. To this end, an au-
diovisual monologue versus dialogue condition was used. Continuously throughout the
thesis we could show a difference between the attended dialogue and monologue condi-
tion in different measurement domains. The most obvious difference was found for the
eye-tracking measurements. The estimated speech envelope entrainment as well as the
reconstruction performance based on the recorded EEG data also showed a difference
between the monologue and dialogue attended condition. However, this difference was
less pronounced compared to the eye-tracking results and confounded with some pre-
liminary assumptions. Yet, it seems that the attended dialogue is better decodable at
the cortical level compared to the monologue. We could show an increased classification
accuracy for the attended dialogue of up to 13 % for an observation window of 50 s.
In the discussion we listed some possible reasons for the increased performance of the
dialogue decoding. Nevertheless, some further work needs to be done to ensure a clear
difference between both conditions.
The recorded data set contains a lot of information, which can be further processed.
One key aspect for the future work with the collected data is the implementation of
more sophisticated decoding strategies like CCA or multiway CCA. Another aspect,
which could be interesting, is the analysis of frequency band specific power pattern in
the EEG data. Furthermore, it is possible to look into the recorded gaze pattern and see
if they are still preserved in the EEG signal after applying certain denoising methods.
The time shift between earlier found TRFs and the reported ones in the literature
can be addressed by using the recorded trigger signal in combination with short time
cross-correlation calculations to identify drifts or even miss-aligned data. The recorded
eye-movements could also be analysed together with the extracted stimulus envelopes.
Since we used audiovisual stimuli another step would be the extraction of visual features
to see how they are preserved in the visual cortex.
A next step towards the realisation of BCI like controlled hearing aids would be an
realistic experiment with hearing impaired subjects. Here the experiment needs to be
slightly re-designed since the length of the experiment is on the upper limit even for
NH subjects. Furthermore, when testing hearing impaired subjects some preselected
hearing aid amplification needs to be chosen. When re-designing the experiment for
hearing impaired subjects, one can also think about the assessment of listening effort
in the here presented realistic measurement conditions by means of electrophysiological
recordings. As proposed in Dau et al. (2018) and Fiedler et al. (2017) the usability of
EEG measurements in a BCI controlled hearing-aid could be a candidate for the future
generation of hearing devices.
