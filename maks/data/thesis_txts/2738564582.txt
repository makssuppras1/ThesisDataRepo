Abstract 
Precision fermentation products are authorized as novel food according to European regulations. As 
such they must not pose a risk for adverse health effects on humans based on scientific evidence. 
Products produced by genetically modified microorganisms must demonstrate the absence of the 
production strain and the recombinant genes to fall under Regulation (EU) 2015/2283 on novel 
food.  
In this thesis, two risk assessments were performed, following the “Guidance on the risk assessment 
of genetically modified microorganisms and their products intended for food and feed” provided by 
the European Food Safety Authority. One risk assessment for intracellular αs1-casein produced by a 
non-Qualified Presumption of Safety Escherichia coli SB001, and one for extracellular α-
lactalbumin produced by Corynebacterium glutamicum AA001, where the recipient organism had a 
Qualified Presumption of Safety-status. The absence of SB001 and AA001 was investigated using a 
selective broth and spread plate method in triplicates of four batches. The absence of recombinant 
DNA was investigated through Polymerase Chain Reaction, followed by gel electrophoresis with 
three replicates of four batches. The target gene coded for the milk protein sequences. Each 
investigation also included spiked samples and a negative control.  
The four batches of each protein were recovered from downstream processing. A mean of 2.9 and 
9.8 mg protein per liter of used fermentation broth were obtained for αs1-casein and α-lactalbumin, 
respectively. The secreted α-lactalbumin was more easily recovered and higher concentrations were 
obtained than the intracellular αs1-casein. However, the α-lactalbumin required longer fermentation 
time and was degraded into smaller proteins. 
The absence of genetically modified organisms was demonstrated in both proteins.  
Recombinant DNA presence with unknown concentration was observed in αs1-casein. For α-
lactalbumin absence of DNA was demonstrated with a 0.1 ng/mL limit of detection.  
Antimicrobial resistance genes were assumed present in αs1-casein, due to their presence on the 
inserted plasmid. The antimicrobial resistance genes could cause adverse environmental impacts. 
Although, the presence of genes would not have any direct adverse effect on human health. The α-
lactalbumin, free of process contaminants, was considered safe for the general population. 
Uncertainties were taken into consideration when estimating the risks.  
Both risk assessments were based on research circumstances. This provides issues that would 
normally not occur in industry settings, such as the genetically modified DNA being on a plasmid 
and having antimicrobial genes present. The missing experimental work, the research scale, the 
degree of independent batches, and the final product composition containing imidazole would make 
the proteins unacceptable for market authorization as a food ingredient.  
The risk assessments in the master thesis were intended as a learning practice and could not fulfill 
all requirements.

1 Introduction 
A worldwide risk to food security is on the rise due to several factors, including global unrest, 
increasing human population, and the impact of weather changes on human food sources. The 
researchers at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) work on a feasible production of milk 
proteins, casein and α-lactalbumin, through precision fermentation, as an alternative protein source. 
The project is in the early stages, but the team works towards upscaled and optimized production.  
However, strict requirements need to be met, especially for a product produced by a genetically 
modified microorganism (GMM), to enter the food market in most parts of the world. The risks of 
adverse effects on human health and the environmental impact must be assessed before any 
genetically modified (GM) food can be authorized to enter the European market. This includes milk 
proteins derived from recombinant bacteria. 
Even though the current versions of these proteins are not ready, this master thesis investigates how 
this risk assessment would be carried out. The master thesis is written due to the students’ interest 
in the risk assessment process and legislation within the European Union (EU).  
After this introduction, the aims of this master thesis are presented. A theoretical background 
chapter contains introductory knowledge about precision fermentation, milk proteins, genetically 
modified microorganisms (GMOs), and EU regulations. The material and methods of the 
experimental work and desk research are described in the following chapter. Afterward, two risk 
assessments are presented, one for each protein, with their investigation and conclusion, utilizing 
the experimental- and desk research results. The discussion chapter reflects on the risk assessment 
process and the necessary upscaling changes to the genetic modification and production process. 
The thesis ends with a conclusion of the work and a future aspects chapter.  
2 Aims 
The overall aim of this master thesis is to practice risk assessment based on the EFSA guideline on 
GMOs and their products intended for food. The risk assessments will be based on modified 
organisms under research circumstances.  
The aim is to produce αs1-casein and α-lactalbumin using precision fermentation from recombinant 
E. coli and C. glutamicum respectively. Further, to recover the proteins from downstream 
processing and to analyze and quantify the recovered proteins.  
Furthermore, the aim is to design and perform experimental protocols for the presence of GMM and 
recombinant DNA in the purified proteins. The effects of whether the protein is extracellular or 
intracellular on the presence of GMM and recombinant DNA will be investigated. 
The final aim is to investigate and evaluate the regulatory requirements within the EU for approval 
of GMM produced foods.

3 Theoretical background 
3.1 Precision fermentation 
Research to improve fermentation processes can be divided into three parts. The first part looks at 
the traditional fermentation process. The second part focuses on the biomass or feedstock used to 
generate desired compounds, where the goal is to utilize food waste. The last part of the research is 
precision fermentation. The main aim of precision fermentation is to use specifically designed 
microorganisms to produce targeted compounds that traditionally are not fermentation products 
(Teng et al., 2021).  
The specifically designed microorganisms can be referred to as cell factories, which through 
optimization and the right conditions, can produce functional and high-value ingredients like 
proteins, flavorings, vitamins, lipids, enzymes, etc., with high purity and yield (Chai et al., 2022).  
The rapid advancement in precision biology has made precision fermentation an emerging food 
trend (Augustin et al., 2023), though it is not a recent innovation. Precision fermentation has been 
used since the 1970s when it was utilized to produce insulin using Escherichia coli (E. coli) 
(Ronchetti et al., 2024).  
Precision fermentation technology has advanced but is faced with challenges, especially from the 
public point of view and knowledge about GMOs safety, risks, and regulations. With the use of 
precision fermentation, it is possible to produce specific and functional ingredients that rely on less 
land and water consumption and have lower emissions of greenhouse gases, compared to 
conventional production (Augustin et al., 2023).  
Precision fermentation of milk protein can contribute to solving challenges associated with climate 
change. This was already observed by a life cycle assessment made for single-cell milk protein 
produced by Perfect Day. The US-based company produces secreted whey protein called fBLG 
(fermented β-lactoglobulin) from the fermentation of recombinant fungi.   
A third-party reviewed life cycle assessment found that whey protein produced by Perfect Day 
consumed 99% less groundwater and surface water and had a 97% reduction in greenhouse gas 
emissions (Geistlinger et al., 2024).  
Selection of production strains  
The choice of the host organism depends on different factors. It is important to consider the host’s 
ability to produce the compound. For example, a native protein will be easier to express than a non-
native protein. It is also important to consider the history of using the host for similar products and 
on a similar scale. To ensure safety, the expression of toxins and allergens must also be considered. 
Feedstock quality may also affect the host selection, as well as influence the downstream processing 
(DSP) and subsequent product quality (Geistlinger et al., 2024).  
A host that can secrete the compound into the fermentation media should also be considered for 
simpler DSP, as it is not necessary to lysate the cells to release the target compound (Geistlinger et 
al., 2024).

3.2 Proteins  
3.2.1 Casein  
The αs1-casein is one of the caseins, the dominant group of proteins in bovine milk (B.T. 
O’Kennedy, 2011). Of the total casein composition (αs1-, αs2-, β-, and κ-casein) αs1-casein 
represents around 40% (Huppertz et al., 2018).  
The genetic variants of αs1-casein are A, B, C, D, E, and F (Eskin & Goff, 2013). The main variant 
of αs1-casein, B-8P, is commonly used to reference the protein’s characteristics. The αs1-casein B-
8P is composed of 199 amino acid residues (Huppertz et al., 2018), visualized in Figure 1. A highly 
charged region exists between residues 41 and 80 due to eight highly acidic phosphoseryl residues. 
The rest of the molecule has a net zero charge and contains three strong hydrophobic regions at 1-
40, 90-110, and 130-199 residues. This makes the αs1-casein an amphiphilic molecule (Eskin & 
Goff, 2013).  
The molecular mass based on the primary structure is 23.0 kDa without phosphorylation. The 
isoelectric point of αs1-casein is pH 4 (Huppertz et al., 2018).  
. 
 
Figure 1 Amino acid sequence of αs1-casein B-8P with phosphorylation on serine residues (SerP) at Ser45, Ser47, Ser64, Ser66, 
Ser67, Ser68, Ser75, and Ser115 (Farrell Jr. et al., 2004). 
Phosphorylation, a posttranslational modification (PTM), has a major impact on the physical and 
self-assembly properties of casein. Bovine caseins are almost always fully phosphorylated (Horne, 
2011).  
When αs1-casein is in the presence of the other types of caseins, in environments like milk, they 
will naturally assemble into colloidal particles, called casein micelles, with calcium phosphate and 
other minerals (Bijl et al., 2020). 
Casein can be used as a food ingredient to modify the protein content and provide structure and 
texture to a product. As a functional ingredient, casein can control the formed acid gels during 
fermentation of e.g. yogurt and it can be used as an emulsifier and stabilizer (B.T. O’Kennedy, 
2011).

The global casein and caseinate markets was valued at 1,021 million euros in 2023 with a 
compound annual growth rate (CAGR) of 4.3% (Mordor Intelligence, n.d.-a). The increasing 
demand is influenced by rising consumer awareness of the health advantages of a protein-rich diet 
and increased demand for culinary products that use casein as a functional ingredient (Mordor 
Intelligence, n.d.-b).   
3.2.2 Whey protein  
Whey proteins account for 20% of all proteins in milk and consist of α-lactalbumin, β-lactoglobulin, 
bovine serum albumin (BSA), and immunoglobulin. α-lactalbumin (13%) and β-lactoglobulin 
(58%) are the most abundant types of whey proteins (Khaire & Gogate, 2019)(Kilara & Vaghela, 
2004).  
The protein of interest, α-lactalbumin, is a globular protein. Its polypeptide chains are folded to 
predominantly have the hydrophobic sidechains on the inside and the hydrophilic sidechains on the 
outside (Moody, 2011). The tertiary structure is built upon two domains, α-helix and β–sheet, which 
are connected by a calcium-binding loop (Permyakov & Berliner, 2000). α-lactalbumin contains 
four stabilizing disulfide bridges and no phosphate groups. Its molecular weight is 14,2 kDa and it 
consists of 123 amino acids, as shown in its primary structure in Figure 2 (Kilara & Vaghela, 
2004)(Khaire & Gogate, 2019).  
 
 
Figure 2 Primary structure of α-lactalbumin, genetic variant B. Disulfide bonds are formed between the following cysteine (Cys) 
residues: 6 and 120, 28 and 111, 61 and 77, and 73 and 91 (Kilara & Vaghela, 2004). 
Generally, the whey proteins are soluble at a large range of pH values. The isoelectric point of α-
lactalbumin is pH 5.1, where the protein is least soluble (Kilara & Vaghela, 2004).  
Whey proteins are of commercial interest in the food industry due to their properties like gelation, 
thermal stability, emulsification, and foaming and they can be added directly to a wide range of 
foods (Khaire & Gogate, 2019). There is a big global market for whey proteins. China is a 
dominating importer, and the US and EU are major suppliers (Khaire & Gogate, 2019). The global 
market value for whey powder and -proteins was approximately 6.3 billion euros in 2019 and was 
estimated to reach 7.2 billion euros by 2024 (3A Business Consulting, 2020).  
Research into novel applications of whey protein in the food industry can positively influence how 
the market will evolve as the demand for whey protein has the potential to grow. For example, 
whey protein has been tested as a component of edible food coating that could replace plastic 
wrapping. It is also considered a nanoparticle carrier for encapsulation (Khaire & Gogate, 2019).

3.3 Genetically modified organisms 
Conventional breeding, the traditional form of “genome modification”, has been selecting 
organisms with useful traits and improved features for hundreds of years. When scientific 
knowledge in biotechnology improved, it became possible to shear, splice, and insert target 
deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) sequences directly into plants, fungi, and bacteria. This technology 
gives new phenotypes to organisms with precision and within a short time. The improvement in 
genetic operating tools gives the modified organisms broad applications and new functions (Nawaz 
et al., 2023)(Liu et al., 2022). The modified organisms are also referred to as GMOs or recombinant 
microorganisms.  
The European Commission (EC) defines GMO in the directive 2009/41/EC as:  
“Organisms (apart from human beings) whose genetic material has been altered in a way that does 
not occur naturally by mating and/or natural recombination” (EUR-Lex, 2009). 
Genetic modification technology raises concerns associated with risks to the environment and 
human health. Therefore, risk assessments of GMOs and their products are necessary for them to be 
authorized to enter the market. The risk assessment should be based on scientific evidence and 
should be objective and transparent (Nawaz et al., 2023).  
 
3.4 Regulation of genetically modified food in the European Union  
The established GMO legislation in the EU aims to protect human and animal health and the 
environment. Deliver harmonized procedures for risk assessment, authorization of GMOs, ensure 
clean labeling and traceability of GMOs placed on the European market (European Commission, 
n.d.). Among other GMO legislation, Regulation (EC) 1829/2003 on genetically modified food and 
feed, including food produced by GMOs (EUR-Lex, 2003). 
 
The legal framework is supported by recommendations and guidelines on more specific aspects 
(European Commission, n.d.). For food produced by GMOs, the European Food Safety Authority 
(EFSA)’s Panel on GMOs has released a “Guidance on the risk assessment of genetically modified 
microorganisms and their products intended for food and feed use” document to help applicants 
through the risk assessment necessary for authorization on the European market (EFSA GMO 
Panel, 2011).  
 
Novel food  
The “novel food” term encompasses any food newly developed or introduced after May 1997. It 
includes new foods, food from new sources, new compounds used in food, new technologies for 
producing food, and traditional food consumed outside Europe (EFSA, n.d.-a).  
 
Since precision fermentation of food proteins is a new applied technology, the product would be 
categorized as a novel food and must be authorized under Regulation (EU) 2015/2283. However, 
since it is a product produced by GMOs, it must be categorized under Regulation (EC) 1829/2003 if 
the recombinant DNA or the GMO is present in the final product (Panel for the Future of Science 
and Technology, n.d.).

Criteria for GM food  
According to Regulation (EU) 2015/2283, there are several conditions that the food must comply 
with. The food must not pose a safety risk to human health based on available scientific evidence, 
the intended use must not mislead the customer, and the food must not be nutritionally 
disadvantageous to the consumer if it is intended to replace another food product (EUR-LEX, 
2015).  
 
Criteria for food produced by GMOs falling under the Regulation (EC) 1829/2003 are similar. 
Additionally, the product must also not pose safety risks to animal health or the environment (EUR-
Lex, 2003). 
 
Labeling of GM food  
Novel food must follow the general labeling requirements as laid down in Regulation (EU) No 
1169/2011 on the provision of food information to consumers. It may be necessary to include 
additional labeling information regarding the description, source, composition, or intended use, so 
the consumers are sufficiently informed about the nature and safety of the product (EUR-LEX, 
2015).  
 
Regulation (EC) 1829/2003 also has special labeling requirements. When a food product consists of 
more than one ingredient, the concerned ingredients must be followed by the words “genetically 
modified” or “produced from genetically modified (name of the ingredient)” in brackets. The label 
must also inform the consumers about differences from the conventional counterparts, and when the 
food may give rise to ethical or religious concerns (EUR-Lex, 2003). 
 
Qualified presumption of safety  
A Qualified Presumption of Safety (QPS) is a status that can be given to a microorganism through a 
pre-assessment, where experts assess:   
 
• The taxonomic identity of the microorganism in the form of taxonomic units.    
• The cumulative scientific knowledge about the exposure of humans and animals to the 
microorganism through food and feed.    
• The risk of adverse effects on human, and animal health and the environment through 
possible safety concerns.    
 
If the microorganism is not already excluded from the QPS process and passes all three 
requirements, it receives a QPS status.    
 
When a microorganism achieves the QPS status, all strains belonging to the QPS taxonomic unit 
will have the same QPS status in an application for market authorization. The strains still require a 
safety assessment by EFSA’s scientific panels, however, the OPS status results in a fast-track 
evaluation that requires less data on potential risk.

For GMMs, the QPS status can be extended to the recombinant production strain when the species 
of the recipient strain has QPS status, and the genetic modification does not raise safety concerns. 
Whether the genetic modification raises safety concerns is assessed during the safety assessment for 
the market authorization.     
 
Microorganisms that are not well defined, or it is not possible to determine whether the organism 
has a safety concern cannot be given QPS status and a full assessment must therefore be made 
(EFSA, n.d.-b).

4 Materials and Methods 
4.1 Production strains  
Two recombinant bacterial strains were used for precision fermentation to produce αs1-casein and 
α-lactalbumin. The DTU Research Group for Microbial Biotechnology and Biorefining (MBB) 
provided these recombinant production strains.  
Recombinant E. coli BL21 (DE3), named E. coli SB001, carried plasmid pET15alpha. The plasmid 
included genes for polyhistidine-tag (his-tag) bovine αs1-casein and ampicillin resistance. The αs1-
casein was produced intracellularly.  
Recombinant Corynebacterium glutamicum (C. glutamicum) ATTC 13032, named C. glutamicum 
AA001, carried plasmid LaNa_pECCg1514. The plasmid included genes for his-tagged bovine α-
lactalbumin and kanamycin resistance. The α-lactalbumin was secreted.  
4.2 Strategy for experimental work  
The strains were first used in a practice trial of protein recovery. The protein samples were used to 
determine the location of the proteins and get familiar with the procedure. Practice trails were 
followed by production of four batches of each protein, named C1-4 for αs1-casein and W1-4 for α-
lactalbumin.  
4.3 Fermentation and recovery of αs1-casein  
Fermentation with E. coli SB001 
The E. coli SB001 was inoculated on a lysogeny broth (LB) agar plate containing 100 µg/mL 
ampicillin and incubated overnight at 37°C.  
A single colony was used to prepare an overnight culture in a terrific broth (TB) liquid broth 
containing 100 µg/mL ampicillin. The overnight culture was incubated at 37°C with shaking at 200 
rpm.  
For the fermentation, 200 mL TB liquid broth containing 100 µg/mL ampicillin was inoculated with 
a volume of the overnight culture to achieve a starting concentration of 0.1 at optical density 
(OD)600. The fermentation culture was incubated at 37°C with shaking at 200 rpm. A 0.5 mM 
isopropyl β-d-1-thiogalactopyranoside (IPTG) was added when the culture reached an OD600 of 0.6, 
and fermentation continued for an additional four hours. Absorbance measurements were taken to 
observe the bacterial growth using a V-1200 spectrophotometer and TB broth was used as 
reference.  
Recovery of intracellular αs1-casein  
The fermentation broth was centrifuged at 4000 xg for 20 min at 4°C. The supernatant was 
discarded.  
The cell pellet was suspended in a binding buffer (20 mM imidazole) and protease inhibitor, mixed 
with lysozyme, and incubated for 30 min. The mixture was homogenized using the Homogenizer 
MP FastPrep-24 at 4 m/s for 20 sec three times, along with an added glass bead.

Following homogenization, centrifugation was performed at 10,000 rpm for 20 min at 4℃. The 
collected supernatant was filtered through a 0.2 μm filter. Nickel-nitrilotriacetic acid (Ni-NTA) 
agarose was added to the filtered liquid, and the mixture was put on a rocking platform for one hour 
at 4°C.  
For the final recovery step of αs1-casein, affinity chromatography was performed. The sample 
mixed with Ni-NTA agarose was transferred to a gravity flow column. Two washes were performed 
with binding buffer and the protein was eluted in six steps with washing buffer (200 mM 
imidazole). The eluates were pooled and stored at -18°C, constituting a finished batch. The final 
volume of the product was 3 mL.  
The samples containing cells and protein were kept on ice throughout the recovery. 
4.4 Fermentation and recovery of α-lactalbumin 
Fermentation with C. glutamicum AA001 
The C. glutamicum AA001 was inoculated on a Brain Heart Infusion (BHI) agar plate containing 
1% glucose and 25 μg/mL kanamycin and the culture was incubated overnight at 30°C.  
A single colony was used to prepare an overnight culture in CGXII broth containing 1% BHI and 
25 μg/mL kanamycin. The overnight culture was incubated at 30°C with shaking at 200 rpm.  
For fermentation preparation, 200 mL CGXII broth containing 1% BHI and 25 μg/mL kanamycin 
was inoculated with a volume of the overnight culture to achieve a starting OD600 of 0.1. The 
fermentation broth in a baffled flask with aeration was incubated at 30°C with shaking at 200 rpm 
for 24 h. Absorbance measurements were taken to observe the bacterial growth using a V-1200 
spectrophotometer and CGXII broth containing 1% BHI as a reference.  
Recovery of intracellular and secreted α-lactalbumin for practice trial 
The fermentation culture was centrifuged at 4000 xg at 4°C for 20 min. The cell pellet and 
supernatant were recovered and used in intracellular and secreted protein recovery, respectively.  
The recovery of intracellular α-lactalbumin followed the procedure described in the “Recovery of 
intracellular αs1-casein” section on page 14.  
The secreted α-lactalbumin located in the supernatant from fermentation was gradually concentrated 
by centrifugation at 4000 xg at 4°C using PierceTM Protein Concentrator PES 3K MWCO. The 
concentrated supernatant was mixed with the remaining unconcentrated supernatant after a long 
period of concentration.  
A protease inhibitor was added to the sample, and the sample was filtered through a 0.45 µm pore-
sized vacuum filter.  
Immobilized metal affinity chromatography (IMAC) was performed for the final recovery step. As

5.1 Hazard identification  
The hazard identification provides information relating to the GMM and the product produced by 
the GMM.  
5.1.1 Information relating to the GMM 
The information provided in relation to the GMM touches upon the recipient microorganism, the 
characteristics of the origin of the inserted sequence, and a description of the genetic modification 
(EFSA GMO Panel, 2011). 
5.1.1.1 Characteristics of the recipient microorganism  
E. coli BL21 (DE3) is the recipient microorganism. Recombinant E. coli BL21 (DE3), the 
production strain named E. coli SB001, produces αs1-casein.  
The following sections will describe the relevant information about the recipient.  
Taxonomy and other names  
The recipient’s taxonomy is important to predict the organism's relevant characteristics and 
potential hazards. If the recipient produces toxins, even at low levels, the modification can 
unintentionally increase the release of toxins.  
The genus of the recipient bacteria is Escherichia in the family Enterobacteriaceae. The species is 
Escherichia coli (KEGG, n.d.). E. coli is a common species that is naturally present in the 
gastrointestinal tract of humans and animals, and the bacterium can also be found in the 
environment (EFSA BIOHAZ Panel, 2017). The specific strain used as a recipient is E. coli BL21 
(DE3), commercially sold as BL21 (DE3) Competent Cells.  
E. coli BL21 (DE3) is a commercial gene-modified strain suitable for the expression of non-toxic 
heterologous genes (ThermoFisher Scientific, 2019).  
Phenotypic and genetic markers  
Phenotypic and genetic markers can be used to identify a bacterial strain. Knowledge of the 
phenotypic and genetic markers is also useful when predicting the behavior and characteristics of 
the recipient microorganism and subsequently, the GMM.   
E. coli is a gram-negative, facultative anaerobic, rod-shaped bacteria. The rods are 1.1-1.5μm wide 
and 2-6μm long. The following conditions are optimal for E. coli; growth temperature between 35-
40℃, pH between 6-7, and aw of 0.995 (Desmarchelier & Fegan, 2016). When grown under aerobic 
conditions and with the optimal conditions presented above, E. coli has a doubling time of roughly 
20 minutes in a rich liquid medium (Tuttle et al., 2021).

As for the genetic markers, the “DE3” in the bacterium’s name refers to lambda DE3 lysogen, 
which the strain harbors. The DE3 lysogen carries a T7 Ribonucleic acid (RNA) polymerase gene, 
controlled by the lacUV5 promoter (Jeong et al., 2015).  
E. coli BL21 (DE3) also has a deficiency in Ion and ompT, which are two proteases, and the 
deficiency yields high levels of proteins by avoiding degradation of protein (Daegelen et al., 
2009)(ThermoFisher Scientific, n.d.-a).  
No experimental identification technique was performed to identify/confirm the strains in this 
project. The recipient microorganism was a part of the common bacterial collection in the 
laboratory.  
Genetic stability  
The recipient’s genetic stability should be verified to confirm its identity, phenotypic, and genotypic 
characteristics. A genetic fingerprinting technique could also verify the correspondence between the 
recipient and the GMM. This risk assessment did not investigate the correspondence 
experimentally.  
The modification is based on a plasmid insertion. Therefore, the chromosomal DNA is not altered.   
In organisms, genetic stability is attained by an accurate DNA repair- and DNA replication system 
of genetic information. An unintended mutation can occur, if changes are introduced to the base 
sequence of DNA. The enzyme DNA polymerase plays an important role in achieving accurate 
DNA replication, replacing a wrong base with the correct base before a mutation can occur. In E. 
coli, three different DNA polymerases, polymerase I, II, and III, have been characterized (Bhagavan 
& Ha, 2011).  
The whole genomic DNA of E. coli BL21 (DE3) is sequenced and available in online databases, 
like the gene bank NCBI. It is possible to confirm the bacteria's identity by sequencing the genome 
of the recipient strain and comparing it to the sequencing available in the online databases (NIH, 
n.d.-b).   
Additionally, the genetic stability of the recipient microorganism can be proven by looking at 
previous use and if the recipient strains often are used as founders for new GMMs. 
History of safe use  
E. coli BL21 (DE3) is a part of the E. coli B strains genealogy with a history of use for recombinant 
protein production (Daegelen et al., 2009).  
E. coli is used as a host constituting around 30% of the production of approved therapeutic proteins, 
with E. coli BL21 (DE3) being the preferred strain. It is used for production in both the industry and 
for research purposes (Hausjell et al., 2018)(EFSA BIOHAZ Panel, 2017).  
Pathogenicity, ecological, and physiological traits  
Certain strains of E. coli can cause illness in humans generally resulting in diarrheal disease, 
infection of the urinary tract, or meningitis (Kaper et al., 2004). E. coli is a common food-borne 
pathogen, and outbreaks can occur. Even though the species are well studied, there is not a 
==PAGE:27==

complete understanding of the virulence factors and therefore E. coli cannot be qualified for QPS 
status. Furthermore, one of the intrinsic characteristics of E. coli is its ability to resist several β-
lactam antimicrobials and utilize a large amount of different antimicrobial resistance genes (EFSA 
BIOHAZ Panel, 2017). However, E. coli BL21 (DE3) harbors no antimicrobial resistance 
(ThermoFisher Scientific, n.d.-a).  
To be pathogenic E. coli, the strain must carry DNA for pathogenic elements and be capable of 
expressing them in vivo. In the study by Chart H. et al., (2000), an investigation into the pathogenic 
properties of BL21, the recipient of BL21 (DE3), considered the strain to be non-pathogenic (Chart 
et al., 2000).  
E. coli BL21 was found not to express verocytotoxins, heat‐labile toxin, and heat‐stable toxin like 
most other strains of E. coli in the host’s gut. In general, it was not found that BL21 harbors the 
recognized pathogenic mechanisms required to cause enteric infections (Chart et al., 2000).  
Furthermore, EFSA has published a scientific opinion on the safety of 3-fucosyllactose produced by 
E. coli BL21 (DE3) in 2022. The scientific opinion found that E. coli BL21 (DE3) was not to be 
considered pathogenic or likely to cause disease. Also, based on a genome sequencing of the 
bacterium, it was found that there is an absence of genes encoding for invasion factors and 
enterotoxins related to virulence (EFSA NDA Panel et al., 2022b). 
Based on the evidence presented above, it is assumed that the BL21 (DE3) is non-pathogenic. No 
information relating to the possible allergenicity of E. coli BL21 (DE3) has been found and neither 
were any vulnerable population groups identified. 
The Chart et al, (2000), study investigated colonization in chickens where pathogen-free chicks 
were orally intragastric intubated with viable E. coli BL21 cells. The results detected E. coli BL21 
in the liver, and the bacterium had colonized the caeca 24 hours after the inoculation. The 
experiment further found that BL21 failed to colonize the spleen and the liver colonization only 
lasted for a short period (Chart et al., 2000).  
A safety assessment on 2-fucosyllactose from BL21 (DE3) by the Government of Canada in 2020 
states that E. coli BL21 (DE3) cannot colonize the human gastrointestinal system. The assessment 
does not mention the source of the information (Health Canada, n.d.).  
According to EU legislation Directive 2000/54/EC on the protection of workers from risks related 
to exposure to biological agents at work concerning the protection of the environment and human 
health, hazards must be classified. Biological agents are divided into four (1-4) risk groups based on 
their level of risk of infection, ranging from Group 1 being unlikely to cause human disease to 
Group 4 causing severe human disease (EUR-Lex, 2000).  
The pathogenic E. coli, and the E. coli that produces verocytotoxins, belong to Group 2, which can 
cause human disease and possibly be a hazard to workers. However, E. coli BL21 (DE3) is non-
pathogenic and does not produce verocytotoxins. This type of E. coli strain is not included on the 
list and does not have a classification. The regulation mentions that biological agents not classified 
as Group 2-4, are not implicitly classified as Group 1 (EUR-Lex, 2000).

Description of the parental strain 
E. coli B is the parental strain of SB001. An article on the ancestors and relatives of E. coli B by 
Daegelen et al., (2009) investigated documents related to the B strains. The strain was named by 
Delbrück and Luria in 1942 and was chosen by the phage research group for their common studies 
of the virulent phages T1-T7 in the 1940s.  
However, the earlier history of the strain is not well established. The article argued that E. coli B is 
a clonal descendant of Bacillus Coli, the first E. coli strain used in laboratory studies of 
bacteriophages (Daegelen et al., 2009). The Bacillus Coli was discovered by Félix d’Herelle, 
isolated from patients’ feces in 1917 (Publications service, 2007).  
Derivates of E. coli B strain are currently some of the most used bacterial hosts for the production 
of recombinant protein on an industrial scale (Daegelen et al., 2009).  
However, detailed information on the parental strain and its cultivation conditions is unavailable. 
One of the earliest articles, by Demerec & Fano, (1944), cultivated E. coli B in nutrient broth agar 
with 0.5% NaCl, and the culture was incubated at 37 °C temperature (Demerec & Fano, 1944). 
These parameters are suitable for the E. coli species. E. coli B likely has the same optimal 
conditions as its derivatives. No evidence of E. coli B being used for the production of food or food-
related ingredients was found.  
History of previous modifications  
The article, mentioned earlier, on the ancestors and relatives of E. coli B by Daegelen et al. (2009) 
put together a genealogy of the E. coli B strain with its derivates leading to the E. coli BL21 (DE3) 
strain. Nine different strains were identified between E. coli B and E. coli BL21 (DE3)(Daegelen et 
al., 2009).  
Generally, the modifications introduced to the strains were either through a P1 transduction from a 
different E. coli strain, UV radiation, or spontaneous mutation. All these previous modifications 
aimed to create a more sensitive strain that would be easier to work with. Specifically, through the 
introduction of sensitivity to the λ phage, removal of proteases, and other alterations (Daegelen et 
al., 2009). 
E. coli BL21, the E. coli BL21 (DE3) recipient, can express proteins from many different sources 
and proteins that are extremely toxic to the cells (Avantor, n.d.). BL21 is also commonly used as a 
host for high-level recombinant protein production but used especially for non-T7 expression 
(Jeong et al., 2015).  
E. coli BL21 (DE3) harbors the prophage DE3, derived from λ bacteriophage. Therefore, the strain 
is used for T7 expression. The strain was created during the same time as BL21 by Studier and 
Moffat (Jeong et al., 2015). 
From these previous modifications, that the recipient organisms cannot cause adverse health effects 
in humans originating from the modifications.

5.1.1.2 Characteristics of the origin of the inserted sequence  
It is important to know where the inserted gene originates from to trace the source and the function 
of the inserted genes. Furthermore, the information can be used to evaluate the potential toxicity 
and allergenicity of the product or any introduced traits that could affect humans, animals, or the 
environment (EFSA GMO Panel, 2011).  
The plasmid, pET-15alpha, inserted into the E. coli BL21 (DE3) has several key gene sequences. 
The two most important sequences are discussed here, the ampicillin microbial resistance gene and 
the genetic sequence coding for bovine αs1-casein expression. The “The functional elements of the 
vector” section on page 30 describes the functional elements of the plasmid.  
The ampicillin resistance gene was present in the purchased plasmid pET-15b. As the whole 
plasmid was made synthetically, this gene sequence is also synthetic.  
The coding sequence for αs1-casein has a size of 600 pb excluding the sequence for 6xHis-tag. It is 
also referred to as CSN1S1. The CSN1S1 gene inserted into the pET-15b is synthetic. The sequence 
is codon optimized. The strategy for the design of DNA sequence is to express natural bovine αs1-
casein. The original αs1-casein gene comes from Bos taurus, commonly known as cattle. The genus 
of cattle is Bos, and the species is Bos taurus (NIH, n.d.-a).

5.1.1.3 Description of the genetic modification

Functional elements of the vector  
 
Figure 5 Plasmid pET-15alpha retrieved from SnapGene 
The vector used to insert the new genes is the plasmid, pET-15alpha. The plasmid can be seen in 
Figure 5 made by the program SnapGene. The use of pET vectors inserted into E. coli BL21 (DE3) 
as a host, is one of the most common approaches in research. The use of a pET vector has also been 
cited in more than 200.000 different research publications (Heyde & Nørholm, 2021).  
The plasmid, pET-15alpha, is double-stranded, and it was constructed from the commercial plasmid 
pET-15b by a member of the MBB Research Group at DTU.  
The identified functional elements on the plasmid are the ampicillin resistance gene (AmpR) and its 
promoter (AmpR promoter), repressor of primer (rop), origin of replication (ori), the lac repressor 
(lacI) and its promoter (lacI promoter), lac operator, T7 promoter and T7 terminator, Ribosome-
binding site (RBS), 6xHist-tag, and thrombin site. The introduced gene for αs1-casein 
(Alphacasein) is inserted on the multiple cloning site (MCS).

The resistance gene, AmpR, confers resistance to ampicillin, carbenicillin, and other related 
antimicrobials through the expression of the β-lactamase (Nakano et al., 2017).  
The lac repressor gene, lacI, produces a lac repressor protein that binds to the lac operator site and 
prohibits its transcription by RNA polymerase (Swint-Kruse et al., 2021). The T7 RNA polymerase 
cannot bind to the promoter, while the repressor protein is on the site, preventing transcription of 
the region between the T7 promoter and T7 terminator. RBS, 6xHist-tag, and thrombin sides are 
present before the αs1-casein gene. RBS is responsible for ribosome binding, while the thrombin 
site can be used to cleave the affinity His-tag from the translated protein e.g. by a Thrombin 
cleavage kit (Abcam, n.d.). The His-tag is placed on the N-terminal. 
The origin of replication (ori) is where a round of replication is initiated (Wagner et al., 2007). Its 
characteristics are specific to the pET vector system.   
The final gene of interest is the repressor of the primer (rop) gene. This gene expresses a rop protein 
that helps regulate the copy number of plasmid (Castagnoli et al., 1989).  
Information relating to genetic modification  
The following description is based on an oral exchange of the insertion of the prepared pET15alpha 
plasmid into E. coli BL21 (DE3). The plasmid construction and the insertion were done previously 
by a member of the MBB Research Group at DTU.  
Plasmid pET-15b and DNA coding sequence for αs1-casein were purchased separately. After 
determining where the gene would be inserted, the gene and the plasmid were amplified using PCR. 
The plasmid was amplified from the ends of MCS, removing a part of the site, where the gene 
would be inserted. The target gene was inserted into the plasmid using a Gibson assembly kit. An 
external company verified the plasmid’s assembly and transcription to be correct. 
The finished plasmid was transferred into One Shot™ TOP10 Chemically competent E. coli, also 
referred to as E. coli Top10. As this is a commercial strain, a protocol provided by the producer was 
followed to insert the plasmid into the cells. The E. coli Top10 was opened chemically for the 
plasmid to enter the organisms. The whole mixture was then plated on plates with ampicillin. Cells 
with the plasmid had resistance genes and were able to form colonies. A plasmid with only the 
resistance gene could also be present, therefore, a colony PCR was performed with the primers from 
bovine protein-coding gene amplification to confirm the gene’s presence. When a colony with the 
modified plasmid was found, the rest of the colony was stored for further use.  
The insertion into the target organism, E. coli BL21 (DE3) followed. An overnight culture of the E. 
coli Top10 carrying the target plasmid was purified using a plasmid DNA purification kit. As the E. 
coli BL21 (DE3) is also a commercial strain, a protocol was followed for the plasmid insertion. The 
mixture was plated on plates with ampicillin and modified cells formed colonies.  
To confirm that cells were producing the target protein, fermentation, and protein recovery were 
performed similarly to the method described in the ”Fermentation and recovery of αs1-casein” 
section on page 14, except for the purification through affinity chromatography. A Western blot was 
instead used to confirm the presence of the his-tagged protein.

Characteristics of the plasmid  
A plasmid is a mobile genetic element. Therefore, information about the copy number, host range, 
stability, and transfer capabilities, must be obtained to evaluate the possibility of horizontal gene 
transfer. The plasmid genetic map contains an explanation “high-copy-number 
ColE1/pMB1/pBR322/pUC origin of replication” for the copy number on the ori sequence. 
Encyclopedia of Cell Biology by Fisher et al. (2016) suggests that a pET vector system should 
make 16-60 copies per cell, although the copy number also depends on the cell and its metabolic 
state (Fisher et al., 2016). The copy number has not been investigated experimentally during this 
project.  
Concerning the host range, the pET expression vector takes advantage of the T7 RNA polymerase 
and lac repressor. The expression gene can be found in specific E. coli strains, lysogens of 
bacteriophage DE3. IPTG must also be used to promote the expression of the target gene through 
the T7 promoter. If a host with T7 promoter is used, but it lacks the lac repressor, basic polymerase 
activity would become high enough that target genes cannot maintain stability (Novagen, 1992). 
This system narrows the host range to very few bacteria strains.  
There are three categories regarding plasmid mobility: conjugative, mobilizable, and non-
mobilizable plasmids. Conjugative plasmids and mobilizable plasmids have a mechanism for 
conjugation. The plasmids harbor the oriT and relaxase genes, among others, needed for the 
plasmids to be transmissible. Conjugative plasmids carry all the genetic material to self-transfer, 
while mobilizable plasmids can only transfer when conjugation is already ongoing. Non-
mobilizable plasmids can only spread through natural transformation or transduction (Smillie et al., 
2010).  
The pET15b plasmid is constructed without any of the necessary genes for conjugation and would, 
therefore, be categorized as non-mobilizable. 
Regarding the stability of the genetic sequence on the plasmid, pET15b is described as having 
transient stability by the Addgene database. This means that the genetic code is very stable 
(Addgene, n.d.).  
The stability of a pET expression plasmid also depends on the host strain and the selected 
antimicrobial resistance, when the plasmid is used in longer induction times. It is suggested to keep 
the induction times short and use kanamycin resistance when using BL21 (DE3) for expression 
(Khananisho et al., 2023).  
It is therefore assumed that the plasmid is very likely genetically stable during the production 
process.

5.1.1.4 Information on the GMM 
Description of new genetic traits and phenotypic characteristics expressed  
It is important to understand what kind of changes the modification does to the recipient to identify 
hazards resulting from the genetic modification.  
The pET15alpha inserted into the BL21 (DE3) is intended to express the target protein with the help 
of the T7 bacteriophage gene 10 in the recipient. As described earlier, the protein is expressed 
through T7 RNA polymerase, and this enzyme is specific for the T7 promoter. The host cell 
polymerase does not typically recognize the T7 promoter (Agilent Technologies, n.d.).  
When the cell is not induced, the SB001 has a relatively unchanged metabolism with negligible 
levels of milk protein expression.  
When the cell is induced with IPTG, the repressor protein gets removed, and the T7 RNA 
polymerase starts to transcribe the target gene. The T7 promoter is rarely found outside the T7 
phage genome. Since the plasmid is the only place in the BL21 (DE3) cell with a T7 promoter, there 
is a high level of target gene transcription. Metabolic burden on the cell is also avoided by not 
employing the cell’s RNA polymerase. The plasmid also contains the gene 10 5’ leader sequence, 
which is important for highly efficient translation (Agilent Technologies, n.d.). The SB001 will 
therefore start to express αs1-casein upon induction, which would not occur in the recipient.  
However, the plasmid can still be a metabolic burden on the cell, when its replication or 
maintenance occurs, especially in plasmids with high copy numbers. This requires a lot of energy 
and can result in slower growth and lower yields of the product (Xu et al., 2023).  
Additionally, the plasmid carries an AmpR gene, that codes for expression of β-lactamase and gives 
the recombinant organisms a resistance to ampicillin and related antimicrobials.  
E. coli BL21 (DE3) would not be able to survive in an environment with ampicillin. The purpose of 
this modification is to be able to select the microorganisms with pET15alpha during the creation of 
the SB001, as well as ensure selective growth in media in further experiments.

Harmful effects on human health  
The product produced, αs1-casein, is an allergen. The allergenicity of the milk protein is described 
in detail in the “Hazard Characterization” chapter on page 44.  
The gene for milk protein expression is considered to not pose a risk for adverse effects on human 
health or the environment. However, the antimicrobial resistance genes might have a potential 
impact on human health and the environment. The antimicrobial resistance genes are discussed 
further in the “Potential environmental impact” on page 47.  
A review study observed that small DNA fragments from bacteria could pass through the intestinal 
barrier, but there was no evidence that larger segments of DNA from food intake could pass into the 
circulatory system of humans (Spisák et al., 2013). Therefore, the antimicrobial resistance genes 
would likely not be absorbed into the human body.  
So, far nothing has indicated that other hazards with risk for adverse effects on human health are 
present based on the recipient and the modification made in the GMM.  
Survival of the GMM in the gastrointestinal tract 
The gastrointestinal tract is a harsh environment for a wide variety of cells as the body’s natural 
defense mechanism. Two types of bacteria are known for their mechanisms to survive the passage 
through the tract – probiotics and pathogens (Bezkorovainy, 2001)(Sistrunk et al., 2016).  
E. coli BL21 (DE3) is not a probiotic or pathogenic strain. However, no concrete scientific studies 
were found during literature research that provided evidence that the bacteria would survive or not 
survive the gastrointestinal tract.

the EFSA guideline. Their removal must be integrated into the production process and their absence 
demonstrated in the final product.

cells to release the product during downstream processing. Firstly, the cells are incubated with 
lysozyme for 30 minutes and subsequently homogenized using the FastPrep-24 homogenizer. The

destroy the cells thoroughly within 40 seconds. For homogenization of SB001, three cycles of 20 
sec at 4.0m/s are run constituting a homogenization of 60 sec.

chromatography through a filter is almost certain to remove any remaining cells if they are still 
present.

5.1.2.5 Description of the product 
The product is specifically bovine αs1-casein variant B. Naturally, it is a phosphoprotein, because it 
goes through phosphorylation after translation. The current protein contains 6xHis-tag on the N-
terminal, which increases its molecular weight.  
The product, αs1-casein, is not functional in its current state, as it does not go through the necessary 
PTMs. It requires phosphorylation and the addition of other caseins, to create micelles. The micelles 
could be used as a functional ingredient or to increase the protein contents of food, just as current 
casein-based ingredients are used.  
No experimental data was obtained for the product composition and the extent of batch-to-batch 
variation. However, the product obtained during this risk assessment was not in its final 
composition. Instead, the protein was solubilized in the washing buffer used during purification. 
The intent is to produce a casein powder, but the current product is not ready yet to be placed on the 
market. The physical and technological properties were also therefore not evaluated. 
The current product may contain impurities originating from the disruption of the production cells. 
Because DNA presence was detected, other impurities might also be present.  
One common impurity, when using E. coli for production strain, is the copurification of E. coli 
protein during affinity chromatography (Structural Genomics Consortium et al., 2008). Some 
natural E. coli proteins, e.g. SlyD, contain multiple histidine residues that might bind and get 
purified with αs1-casein. The presence of natural E. coli protein was not detected in the SDS-PAGE 
results.

5.2 Hazard characterization  
The hazard characterization presents a qualitative evaluation of the potential adverse effects on 
human health. 
The first step of hazard characterization is to compare the product and/or GMM with an appropriate 
comparator, called the comparative approach. For example, when assessing the GMM, it can be 
compared to the non-modified organism, and the differences from the modification can be evaluated 
for potential adverse effects. Toxicology, allergenicity, and nutritional aspects are assessed. Special 
considerations should be made towards the proteins and other metabolites produced by the GMM, 
introduced by the modification.  
The genes themselves are discussed in the environmental impact assessment for adverse effects on 
human health, including the potential for genetic transfer, and the spread of antimicrobial resistance 
genes. As discovered in the “Information on the possible presence of recombinant DNA” section on 
page 39, the αs1-casein has detectable recombinant DNA presence, which could influence the risk 
for adverse effects. However, the aim is to produce the protein as a Category 2, with DNA and 
GMM absent. It is therefore important to consider that the recombinant DNA will be absent from 
the final formula.  
The product composition mainly constitutes αs1-casein, with possible traces of native E. coli 
proteins. Besides the identified proteins, it is assumed that no new metabolites are produced.  
Toxicology  
The recombinant αs1-casein has a suitable comparator in the native bovine αs1-casein. Although, 
the recombinant protein lacks phosphorylation, and contains his-tag.  
In the “Casein” section on page 9, the primary structure of αs1-casein can be found together with 
information on the molecular weight and other characteristics.  
The history of safe use of casein is well-documented throughout history and its appliance. Bovine 
milk, naturally containing αs1-casein, has been consumed by humans for more than 8000 years 
(Vincent et al., 2016). Ever since casein was discovered in 1818 by Schübler, the protein has been 
utilized by the food industry (Fox & Brodkorb, 2008). The worldwide use of casein as a functional 
and nutritional ingredient is described in the “Casein” section on page 9.  
A literature search of articles related to the toxic effects of casein yielded no evidence of adverse 
health effects related to the toxicity of the protein. Instead, articles and scientific publications were 
found to support casein’s beneficial health effects (Pihlanto-Leppälä, 2002).  
The impact of the his-tag on the structure and function of casein is possible. The study by Booth et 
al., (2018), investigated the impact of N-terminal his-tag on protein thermal stability. The study 
described how, even for its small size, his-tag may impact the protein’s oligomeric states, and 
ability to crystalize and function, reported from several other studies. The study observed that in 4 
out of 10 tested proteins, the presence of his-tag decreased thermal stability (Booth et al., 2018).

Although, a research paper by Carson et al., (2007) and a scientific publication by Gräsland et al., 
(2008) reported that his-tag has negligible impact on crystallization and NMR structure 
determination (Gräslund et al., 2008)(Carson et al., 2007). The Gräsland et al., (2008) publication 
also suggested that the solubility of the protein can be affected by the his-tag placement. 
Completely insoluble protein with an N-terminal his-tag can be soluble when expressed with a C-

Because of the his-tag, it cannot be assumed that the purified protein would have the same functions

suggests that his-tag induces toxicity in the recombinant protein.  
The antimicrobial resistance gene AmpR codes for β-lactamase. During the literature search, no 
evidence of adverse health effects was found for β-lactamase. However, the antimicrobial resistance 
gene itself is a hazard (Nakano et al., 2017).   
Allergenicity  
Milk proteins, including casein, can induce milk allergy. Milk allergy can be either immunoglobulin 
E (IgE) or non-IgE mediated (Walsh et al., 2016).  
The IgE-mediated allergic reaction occurs immediately upon ingestion. Its pathogenicity is divided 
into two phases. In the sensitization phase, the allergen enters the body through the gastrointestinal 
tract, skin, or mucosa, and encounters the native immune system under Th2-dominant conditions. 
This results in IgE production. In the induction phase, the same allergen is taken orally and causes 
adverse effects (Matsuo et al., 2015). The acute effects typically consist of skin manifestation (e.g. 
rash, and erythema) and/or respiratory alterations (e.g. wheezing, and cough), and/or 
gastrointestinal symptoms (e.g. abdominal pain, and vomiting) or systemic disorders that affect 
more than one organ (anaphylaxis)(Martorell-Aragonés et al., 2015).  
Milk allergy is mostly prevalent in infants and young children. Most children outgrow the allergy 
by the age of 6 years. However, the allergy can sometimes persist into adulthood. A cohort study 
made by Regula et al., (2023), included three adult patients with milk allergy. The study observed 
that the predominant source of sensitization in adults was α-lactalbumin, whereas it was casein 
among children (Regula et al., 2023).  
The non-IgE mediated allergy is delayed and can take up to 48 hours to develop (Walsh et al., 
2016). In the case of milk, it typically causes a condition called food protein-induced allergic 
proctocolitis (FPIAP). FPIAP occurs in breastfed infants often by maternal ingestion of cow’s milk, 
or in formula-fed infants, where formulas contain cow’s milk. The symptoms are reported as blood-
tinged and mucous stools in otherwise healthy infants. Non-IgE mediated allergy’s effects are 
primarily localized in the gut, with occasional effects on the skin or in the lungs (Zhang et al., 
2021).

The phosphorylation may have an impact on the allergenicity of casein. The study by Cases et al., 
(2011), on the allergenicity of cow casein in children with goat and sheep milk allergies, suggests 
that phosphorylation degrees of bovine, sheep, and goat caseins could explain different allergenicity 
patterns in patients. Patients with strong IgE reactivity to goat and sheep milk (minimal 
phosphorylation) did not show a strong reaction to bovine milk (high phosphorylation)(Cases et al., 
2011).  
A study by Lebetwa et al., (2019), investigated the anti-allergic activity of milk casein peptide by 
additional phosphorylation and observed that the additional phosphorylation weakened the IgE-
mediated reaction in mice (Lebetwa et al., 2019). These observations raise a concern that the 
recombinant αs1-casein could induce a stronger allergic reaction, due to lack of phosphorylation. 
Another scientific study investigated how the IgE-mediated reactivity differs in a recombinant 
casein. The study by Schulmeister et al., (2009), observed that αs1-casein derived from recombinant 
E. coli BL21 (DE3) induced IgE recognition in 97.4% of patients (n =66), while IgE reactivity to 
native α-casein was only observed in 56.7% patients (n =66)(Schulmeister et al., 2009). This 
suggests that the αs1-casein could potentially be a stronger allergen than the native αs1-casein, but 
the study does not mention whether this might be due to phosphorylation.  
Nutritional assessment 
The nutritional value of the αs1-casein is currently being investigated. The recombinant strain 
designer confirms that the nutritional value is the same as in the native casein, through a written 
exchange. 
5.3 Exposure assessment  
The exposure assessment aims at qualitatively estimating the likely exposure to humans and the 
environment to the GMM product (EFSA GMO Panel, 2011). 
For the exposure assessment, the interest lies in establishing if the intake of the protein produced 
from the GMM is expected to differ from the conventional product.  
The recombinant casein is not ready to be placed on the market in its current state. However, the 
intended use of the protein is to act as a functional ingredient or to modify the protein content of a 
given product. The target population group for consumption would be the general population. 
If the recombinant DNA is not removed from the final composition, then GMO labeling is a 
requirement. It is expected that fewer consumers would be willing to buy the product due to GMO 
labeling. Studies have shown that consumers are skeptical about GMO safety. If a product is labeled 
“contains GMO”, the market share is reduced compared to food without GMO. GMO labels have 
also been shown to decrease the importance consumers normally place on the prices of products and 
increase consumers’ willingness to pay more for non-GMO products. The requirements for GMO 
labeling would potentially result in lower exposure to recombinant protein (Kim et al., 2022).  
An increasing amount of milk products are enriched with natural milk proteins, where the 
recombinant casein could replace the added casein. It is assumed that the recombinant casein would 
be added to the products in similar amounts as the current additional protein.

5.4 Potential Environmental Impact  
The purpose of Environmental Risk Assessment (ERA) is to identify potential environmental 
damage and the likelihood of its occurrence. An environmental damage is a measurable change in a 
natural resource or a measurable impairment of a natural resource service. Environmental damage 
can happen directly or indirectly (EFSA GMO Panel, 2011).  
In the first step of the ERA, the occurrence would be assessed for sources and routes into the 
environment. Possible adverse effects would then be evaluated in the relevant environments. ERA 
should also consider the long-term effects of interaction with biotic and abiotic components of the 
environment (EFSA GMO Panel, 2011). 
The most relevant environments in the case of products derived from the GMM are those that are 
exposed to them, e.g. human gastrointestinal tract, and aquatic ecosystem receiving wastewater 
(EFSA GMO Panel, 2011).  
According to the EFSA guideline, Category 2 products do not need to undergo an ERA, because the 
absence of recombinant DNA and the GMMs is demonstrated. Thus, the product would not affect 
the environments of concern. The environmental effects of the product are not a part of this ERA. 
In the “Information on the possible presence of recombinant DNA” section on page 39, DNA is 
present in the recombinant αs1-casein. Even though these results are not acceptable for a Category 2 
product, the intent is that the final composition of the product will be able to demonstrate DNA 
absence. Therefore, considering the recombinant αs1-casein in Category 2, a full ERA is not 
needed.  
However, the antimicrobial resistance genes are a hazard with risk for environmental impact. It is 
assumed that the presence of the milk protein genes also means that the antimicrobial resistance 
genes are present. It is also assumed that they are present in similar concentrations.  
Any remaining antimicrobial resistance can be transferred into bacterial cells in the relevant 
environment. The environments that would mostly be exposed to the recombinant genes would be 
the human gastrointestinal tract of the consumers and subsequently the wastewater with waste 
originating from the consumers’ locations. Other environments are expected to be exposed to the 
genes in negligible occurrence, e.g. if someone spills the food or feeds the food to their animal.

The consumed genes must pass through the human digestion system until they reach the local 
microbiota. A study investigated the degradation of exogenous DNA during digestion in mice. In 
general, the study found that almost all DNA was degraded when the DNA entered the small 
intestine in mice (Xing et al., 2022).  
Foreign DNA fragments have also been detected in the digestive tract of rainbow trout. The trout 
had been fed genetically modified soybeans (Chainark et al., 2008). Another review study 
investigated four independent studies. It was demonstrated, through animal studies, that small 
amounts of dietary DNA could potentially resist digestion (Spisák et al., 2013).  
It is possible for a small amount of the recombinant DNA fragments, including the antimicrobial 
resistance genes to survive digestion and have contact with the local microbiota. All genetic 
material fragments, including the whole plasmid, can be taken up through transformation into the 
gut microbiota and incorporated into their genomic DNA (Burmeister, 2015).  
Microbiota that contains the DNA, or free DNA fragments could also pass through the 
gastrointestinal tract and end up in the local waste system. Other microbiota would be exposed to 
the genes, and the antimicrobial resistance genes could potentially begin to spread.  
When different bacteria, especially pathogens, gain antimicrobial resistance, infections become 
more difficult to treat. This is a growing threat to global public health (Salam et al., 2023).  
5.5 Risk characterization  
In the risk characterization, the information from the previous chapters is evaluated considering the 
occurrence and the severity of adverse effects on human health and the environment. The 
information obtained is assessed in categories relating to the GMM, the product, and the 
environmental impact, according to the EFSA guideline. 
Uncertainties and the lack of knowledge of the risk’s inputs are listed and quantified using 
probability. These uncertainties include common uncertainties for both recombinant proteins, as 
well as specific uncertainties that are relevant for the αs1-casein. The magnitude of the risk of 
adverse effects on human health and the environment is concluded at the end.

Information relating to the GMM 
The recipient of SB001, E. coli BL21 (DE3), does not have a QPS status. The strain is commonly 
used for recombinant protein expression. This is documented in scientific studies, as well as in other 
risk assessments for novel foods. E. coli BL21 (DE3) is assumed non-pathogenic, as it comes from 
a long line of non-pathogenic strains, with its recipient microorganism, E. coli BL21, being well-
studied to be non-pathogenic.  
The genetic modification in SB001 intends to introduce the expression of αs1-casein and resistance 
to ampicillin. No evidence was found to support an unintentional increase in the expression of other 
metabolites.

The protocol for the genetic modification was the standard protocol for transforming E. coli BL21

The inserted gene is on a plasmid, and the plasmid is less genetically stable than genes inserted into

minimizing the potential for instability. Furthermore, no evidence was found indicating that 
instability could result in the expression of new hazards.  
The plasmid utilizes the T7 polymerase for the target gene transcription. T7 polymerase is

in common strains. The plasmid does not contain material to induce conjugation, making it non-

between unintentional strains and be utilized.

with samples taken from pilot scale production for the presence of GMM and recombinant DNA. 
As the risk assessment is based on a research project, not all requirements were fulfilled due to 
limited time and resources. Four batches were produced but could not be defined as independent. 
Only two people were performing the experiments, and two batches were performed simultaneously 
twice. Finally, the same resources were used in most experiments, and the production was only 
possible on a laboratory scale.

The casein in its current structure and composition is not a ready product. For now, the purified

5.5.3 Conclusion  
The current composition has a very high risk of adverse effects on human health due to the presence 
of imidazole. It is not ready to be placed on the European market as a food ingredient. 
The following risk estimations are based on an assumed product, where the process contaminants 
were removed, and the casein is used as a dry food ingredient in other products. 
The presence of antimicrobial resistance genes in the product could cause a potential adverse 
environmental impact. The concentration of the recombinant DNA in the product is unknown. It is 
also unknown, how much of it would survive the different relevant environments. Therefore, the 
local microbiota would be exposed to an unknown amount of DNA. However, it is assumed to be 
very low concentrations. Even the low concentrations could contribute to global antimicrobial 
resistance over time. Therefore, there could be adverse irreversible environmental impacts. The risk 
of adverse effects on the environment is high with high uncertainties due to lack of knowledge. The 
product would not be able to enter the European market before it demonstrates the absence of 
recombinant DNA. 
The antimicrobial resistance genes themselves would not have any direct adverse effect on human 
health. However, due to the contribution to global antimicrobial resistance, the genes would 
indirectly increase the risk of non-treatable infections in humans. The product itself is safe for 
consumption in the general population. This is due to a negligible risk of adverse effects on human 
health with some uncertainties. However, there is a high risk of allergic reactions with low 
uncertainty in the population group with milk allergies. The allergen must be highlighted on the 
product’s labeling to avoid consumption by the allergic population.  
Furthermore, the presence of recombinant DNA means that the αs1-casein would not be accepted as 
Category 2 and would not be considered under Regulation (EU) 2015/2283 on novel food. The 
current version would instead be considered under Regulation (EC) 1829/2003 on genetically 
modified food and feed.

6.1.1.1 Characteristics of the recipient microorganism  
The recombinant C. glutamicum strain, named C. glutamicum AA001, produces α-lactalbumin. A 
wild-type C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 strain was used to create AA001, therefore, C. glutamicum 
ATCC 13032 is both the recipient and the parental microorganism. C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 
will be referred to as the recipient microorganism.  
Taxonomy and other names  
The recipient microorganism can be purchased as Corynebacterium glutamicum ATCC 13032. The 
genus of this bacteria is Corynebacterium with the species being Corynebacterium glutamicum. The 
non-profit organization American Type Culture Collection (ATCC) states that the specific strain is 
called C. glutamicum 543 (NCIMB 10025), previously named Micrococcus glutamicus (ATCC, 
n.d.; NCBI, n.d.-c). 
The genus Corynebacterium includes serious human pathogenic species. However, the species C. 
glutamicum is considered safe and has had the QPS status by EFSA since the introduction of QPS 
status in 2007 (EFSA Scientific Committee, 2007). The individual strains within the species will 
continue to have QPS status as long as the strain does not harbor any acquired microbial resistance 
genes for clinically relevant antimicrobials, and if it is only used for production purposes (EFSA 
BIOHAZ Panel, 2023).  
Phenotypic and genetic markers  
C. glutamicum is gram-positive and rod-shaped. The rods are either straight or to some degree 
curved. The bacteria are further non-sporulating, non-motile, non-capsulated, and catalase-positive. 
The diameter of C. glutamicum is 0.7 μm and the length is 1-2.5 μm. When cultivated on media, C. 
glutamicum cells are characterized by being flat, small, and transparent colonies in the early growth 
phase. After a long incubation time of 15-28 days, the colonies will become large and yellow (Ray 
et al., 2022). All these phenotypic characteristics can help identify the bacterium.

For growth characteristics, C. glutamicum requires nutrient media and aerobic conditions (ATCC, 
n.d.). When using glucose as a carbon and energy source the growth rate of C. glutamicum was 
observed to be 0.41 h1 in a minimal medium in a study performed by Graf et al., (2020)(Graf et al., 
2020). pH values between 7 and 8.5 give the optimal growth rates (Follmann et al., 2009), and 
optimal temperatures lie between 25°C and 37°C (Leszczewicz & Walczak, 2019).  
The bacterium quickly grows to a high cell density. Furthermore, it has a low protease activity (Lee 
et al., 2016). The recipient C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 does not harbor known antimicrobial 
resistance genes (EFSA Scientific Committee, 2007).  
 
Potential expression of toxins can be identified from specific coding sites on the chromosomal DNA 
sequence of the recipient microorganism. If these coding sites are identified, the potential for the 
expression of toxins under the desired production conditions must be investigated. Two studies, 
Ikeda & Nakagawa (2003) and Kalinowski et al. (2003), investigated the complete genomic 
sequence for ATCC 13032 and did not mention the coding sequence for toxins in the studies (Ikeda 
& Nakagawa, 2003; Kalinowski et al., 2003).  
 
Concerning genetic markers, the genome of. C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 is composed of one 
circular chromosome with a genome size of 3.2 Mbp. The guanine-cytosine (GC) content is 53.8%. 
The genome contains more than 24 insertion sequences, which could potentially be responsible for 
horizontal gene transfer (Gopinath & Nampoothiri, 2014).  
As for E. coli BL21 (DE3), it is possible to compare the genome sequence with the C. glutamicum 
ATCC 13032 sequence available in online databases and thereby confirm the bacterial strain (Ikeda 
& Nakagawa, 2003).  
Genetic stability  
C. glutamicum has been widely used for the industrial production of amino acids, especially L-
glutamate, and L-lysine since the bacterium was discovered in the 1950s (Lee et al., 2016).  
In the 1980 the production of amino acids took off with the development and advancement in 
genetic engineering technology. Fermentation of C. glutamicum to produce L-glutamate is currently 
the biggest market for amino acids (Lee et al., 2016), and roughly 2.1 million tons is produced 
worldwide with an increase of 3-5% each year (Wachi, 2013). The long history of use of C. 
glutamicum for production purposes indicates the stability of the species.  
 
The benefit of using C. glutamicum as an industrial production strain is that the bacterium has many 
good physiological properties one being the lack of recombination repair systems, making C. 
glutamicum genetically stable (Lee et al., 2016). 
Article Nakamura et al., (2003), investigated the genome stability in Corynebacterium species and 
found that the characteristic of this species is their stable genome (Nakamura et al., 2003).  
 
The recipient microorganism was a part of the common bacterial collection in the laboratory, which 
supports its genetic stability.

6.1.1.4 Information on the GMM  
Description of new genetic traits and phenotypic characteristics expressed 
The purpose of genetic modification with LaNA_pECCg1514 inserted into C. glutamicum ATCC 
13032 is to produce secreted α-lactalbumin, and the purpose of the kanamycin resistance gene is to 
ensure selectivity during production. No other changes to the phenotypic characteristics were 
intended with this modification. The GMM can produce an allergen.  
As the GMM is a gram-positive bacterium, the proteins produced, are secreted by being transported 
across the intracellular membrane. The transport can be done with two transport mechanisms. They 
are called the twin-arginine translocation (Tat) and Sec translocon (Sec) pathways. In C. 
glutamicum the main protein exporting system is the Sec pathway. The Sec pathway transports 
proteins across the cytoplasmic membrane and the proteins can either be unfolded or partially 
folded (Balasubramanian et al., 2024).

6.2 Hazard Characterization 
In the hazard characterization, the secreted α-lactalbumin will be compared with an appropriate 
comparator, the native bovine α-lactalbumin. This part of the risk assessment will also focus on 
defining the potential health effects on humans by looking at toxicology, allergenicity, and the 
nutritional aspects of the GMM produced product.  
It is assumed that GMM does not produce new metabolites or constituents other than the recipient 
microorganism, other than α-lactalbumin.  
Toxicology  
The strategy for the gene modification was for C. glutamicum to produce α-lactalbumin via 
precision fermentation that would be the same as if the α-lactalbumin had been recovered from 
bovine milk. But currently, for α-lactalbumin to be purified, a 6xhis-tag was attached to the protein 
and used for affinity chromatography. The attachment of his-tag makes α-lactalbumin different 
from the native bovine α-lactalbumin. As mentioned in the hazard characterization for αs1-casein 
and the his-tag attachment on the protein, it cannot be ruled out that his-tag influences the function 
and structure of the protein. Experimental data are needed to identify the differences and compare 
the recombinant protein to the native protein. No evidence suggests that his-tag induces toxicity in 
the recombinant α-lactalbumin.  
 
The EFSA guideline recommends that proteins expressed by the introduced genes should be 
evaluated, and this can be done by looking into the source of the protein, its function, and history of 
consumption.

In the “Whey protein” section on page 10, the primary structure of α-lactalbumin can be found with 
information on the molecular weight and a description of the protein’s functions. As presented in 
the hazard characterization of αs1-casein, both proteins have been safely consumed through bovine 
milk for over 8000 years. No evidence suggesting that α-lactalbumin should be toxic has been 
found. On the contrary, α-lactalbumin can promote gastrointestinal function and the protein can be 
used as a supplement to support the development of infants (Layman et al., 2018).  
Allergenicity  
The α-lactalbumin can induce milk allergy. The allergy to α-lactalbumin is IgE mediated, which 
causes immediate allergic reactions. Depending on the study population the prevalence lies in 
between 27% and 63% of patients with α-lactalbumin-specific IgE allergy. Concerning food 
allergy, cow milk is the most frequent cause of food allergy, especially in kids younger than 5 years 
old (Matsuo et al., 2015).   
The composition of the final product mainly constitutes α-lactalbumin, with potential traces of 
native proteins produced by C. glutamicum. The potential traces of native proteins in the final 
product were not investigated concerning their allergenicity in this risk assessment. 
No information about increased allergenic potential associated with his-tag proteins was found 
during literature research.  
In a study by Hochwallner et al., (2010), they performed immunological characterization of α-
lactalbumin. In the study, E. coli expressed the recombinant α-lactalbumin. With the use of 
microarray technology, they studied allergenic activity in patients with cow´s milk allergy. The 
prevalence was determined with IgE recognition for recombinant α-lactalbumin. Results from the 
study indicated that in 57.6% of the patients (n =38) with cow´s milk allergy recombinant α-
lactalbumin reacted with the patients' IgE antibodies. For native α-lactalbumin, the IgE reactivity 
was 75.8% (n =50).  
The different IgE reactivity is probably due to the folding patterns of recombinant and native α-
lactalbumin. Analysis revealed that native α-lactalbumin contained monomers and recombinant α-
lactalbumin contained monomers and oligomers which could influence the folding of the proteins 
(Hochwallner et al., 2010).

6.5.1 Issues to be considered 
Information relating to the GMM 
The genetic stability of the GMM was not investigated experimentally. However, the stability of the 
GMM was evaluated based on the knowledge, that the recipient strain has been widely used for 
industrial productions of especially amino acids for many years.  
Introducing recombinant genes on a plasmid has lower stability than inserted genes in chromosomal 
DNA. Additionally, the expression does not need to be induced, which increases the chance of the 
plasmid becoming unstable. The plasmid was active for the whole duration of the production, from 
when the strain was plated until the protein was recovered from the broth.  
Concerning safety towards humans the recipient strain is non-pathogenic and has QPS-status. The 
purpose of genetic modification with LaNA_pECCg1514 inserted into C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 
was to secrete α-lactalbumin. The plasmid already harbored antimicrobial resistance genes for 
selectivity during experiments and protein production. No other changes to the phenotypic 
characteristics were intended with this modification. The modification was previously confirmed by 
sequencing the plasmid.  
Based on the modification of the GMM, evidence of unintended modification resulting in toxic 
properties, production of new metabolites, or new allergenic properties was not found. However, 
the intended gene coding for bovine α-lactalbumin, makes the GMM able to produce an allergen.  
The modification of the plasmid and the genetic modification technique have been described in the 
hazard identification. The description was partially based on access to SnapGene and an oral 
explanation. Since the modification of the GMM was explained orally, some information could be 
lost or misunderstood.   
The C. glutamicum AA001 does not inherit the QPS status as it harbors the kanamycin resistance 
gene. The gene has a potential indirect risk for adverse effects on humans, as infections can become 
difficult to treat. The antimicrobial resistance could spread through DNA transfer to pathogenic 
bacteria.  
As described in the risk characterization of αs1-casein, the EFSA guideline recommends making at 
least three independent batches, analyzing in triplicates, and using pilot-scale production samples at 
the least. The uncertainties concerning batch independence and human error in a laboratory are 
similar.

Information relating to the GM product 
The secreted α-lactalbumin was confirmed in several ways. The harbored antimicrobial resistance 
made sure only the AA001 would be able to grow, and the attachment of his-tag made sure only the 
target protein was purified during affinity chromatography. The protein expression was confirmed 
by a western blot analysis, during the genetic modification. Additionally, the plasmid was verified 
through sequencing by an external company. During this risk assessment, SDS-PAGE was 
performed along with PCR with specific primers amplifying the target α-lactalbumin gene, and 
samples were visualized on electrophoresis gel. However, none of the SDS-PAGE experiments 
included a positive control to confirm the protein’s identity. 
During the SDS-PAGE analyses, two bands were visible in the final product, where one band was 
very likely α-lactalbumin degraded through enzymatic activity. The degraded protein was assumed 
not to constitute adverse health effects on humans. However, limited information about the 
degraded protein was obtained. It was confirmed that the smaller-sized protein was not a native C. 
glutamicum protein during the experiment with the recipient strain, which showed no bands on the 
SDS-PAGE gel.  
The recombinant α-lactalbumin is only intended for research purposes and was stored in a buffer 
containing 200 mM imidazole. The protein is functional, but further processing is necessary for a 
consumable formulation.  
No experiments investigated the composition and physical or technological properties. However, 
the strategy for the design of the plasmid was to express and secrete recombinant α-lactalbumin, 
similar to native bovine α-lactalbumin. The his-tag attachment is assumed to retain the nutritional 
value of the protein, but it might affect the structure and function of the protein. The his-tag 
attachment does not indicate any adverse effects for humans.

The final product belongs to Category 2 as defined in the EFSA guideline. As demonstrated in the

6.5.3 Conclusion 
The composition of the current product contains imidazole, a known chemical hazard that is unfit 
for consumption. Therefore, in the current state, the product composition would not be able to be 
authorized as a food ingredient and would not be placed on the market.  
The following risk estimations are based on an assumed product, where the process contaminants 
were removed, and the α-lactalbumin is used as a dry food ingredient in other products. 
The recombinant α-lactalbumin produced by C. glutamicum AA001 has demonstrated the absence 
of GMM and the concentration of recombinant DNA below an appropriate LOD. Therefore, the 
product would constitute a negligible risk of adverse effects on the environment with low 
uncertainty. The product placement on the market would be safe for the environment.  
As for the safety of human health, the product would also constitute a negligible risk of adverse 
effects with low uncertainty. The consumption of the product would be considered safe for the 
general population. However, there is a high risk of allergic reactions with low uncertainty in the 
population group with milk allergies. The allergen must be highlighted on the product’s labeling to 
avoid consumption by the allergic population.

7 Discussion  
7.1 Risk Assessment  
The EFSA guideline has a particular pathway for each Category, Category 1-4, that one should 
follow to create a risk assessment. However, this presented a challenge for students with no prior 
experience with this risk assessment type, since the requirements are tailored for teams within large 
companies. The company’s assessors can access resources of a bigger magnitude.  
The EFSA guideline is justifiably more demanding in case of potentially higher risk, such as the 
genetically modified DNA being on a plasmid and when antimicrobial genes are present. Even 
though this is the case for SB001 and AA001, this is due to them being not intended for commercial 
production. The research scale would also be unacceptable for market authorization, but this master 
thesis was intended as a learning process. 
Demonstration GMM absence  
The “Scientiﬁc Guidance for the submission of dossiers on Food Enzymes” was used for inspiration 
in the development of the experimental design (EFSA CEP Panel, 2021). Due to low product 
volume, less sample volume was used than the recommended amount. The setup used for the 
experiment included positive control with samples of product spiked with low counts of viable cells 
(e.g. 10-300 cells per plate). 
The background composition of αs1-casein or α-lactalbumin could inhibit cell growth, including 
cells in the spiked batches.  
Therefore, a positive control with a spiked growth medium was included. If the spiked samples 
would not grow and the positive control would, this control would eliminate the possibility of error 
due to process or material.  
Furthermore, a negative control was included to identify possible contamination that could cause 
false positive results.  
Demonstration of recombinant DNA absence 
The demonstration of DNA absence is dependent on a LOD. The DNA concentration must be 
demonstrated below an appropriate LOD. LOD is specific to the experiment and depends on the 
precision of the instruments and the method used to obtain data.  
The chapter “DNA from the production strain” in the “Scientiﬁc Guidance for the submission of 
dossiers on Food Enzymes” states several specific requirements, among them a recommendation for 
LOD of 10 ng/mL or lower.  
Aside from the controls and sensitivity tests included in the EFSA document, unpurified samples 
were also included. They were included to confirm the need for DNA purification. As no bands 
were visible in the unpurified samples, the PCR reaction was very likely inhibited by a component 
in the product, such as imidazole or the milk proteins. DNA purification was necessary to isolate the 
DNA, amplify it, and observe bands on the gel.  
==PAGE:79==
 
Master Thesis 
 
 
Spring 2024 
Page 79 of 100 
 
A contamination was observed during the detection of DNA in αs1-casein in some of the negative 
controls. Normally, a contaminated negative control means that results cannot be considered, as 
they can be false positive results. The contamination appeared in three experiment repetitions (one 
not included in the report). Steps were taken to avoid contamination.  
However, it was not possible to achieve this for the casein samples. In the end, the PCR tubes were 
suspected to be contaminated, as they were not autoclaved and often not used in a sterile 
environment.  
There was not enough time to repeat the αs1-casein experiment, however, the observed DNA 
presence in the four batches was determined as the correct result. The bands in the batch samples 
were much thicker than the contamination in the negative control. Even if a contamination was 
present, its contribution to the band’s thickness would be negligible. Additionally, while the 
contamination appeared random, the positive results for the batches were consistent.  
It was also not possible to estimate the LOD, however, the DNA concentration could be estimated 
through real-time PCR. 
Finally, the “Scientiﬁc Guidance for the submission of dossiers on Food Enzymes” recommends 
targeting the antimicrobial resistance gene during PCR amplification. It would then be possible to 
confirm its presence with experimental results, which is important for the risk assessment.  
The estimation of risk 
The lack of time and resources introduces several uncertainties concerning experiments that were 
not possible to carry out. These were included as uncertainties when assessing the risk.  
The hazard characterization section included no experimental work, even though it would be 
relevant. The information provided in this chapter was based solely on literature data.  
For the allergenicity part, the investigated information suggested that the allergenicity of the 
recombinant and native proteins were different. The degree of allergenicity is considered crucial for 
the final product, however it can be difficult. The allergenicity should be evaluated according to the 
“Scientific Opinion on the assessment of allergenicity of GM plants and microorganisms and 
derived food and feed” from 2021 by EFSA.  
7.2 Downstream processing and quality of the product 
Choice of strains 
The two strains used to produce αs1-casein and α-lactalbumin are gram-negative and gram-positive, 
respectively. The yield was very low for both proteins, and it needs to be improved significantly to 
be feasible for industry production.  
Based on the two performed risk assessments, it was found that the use of C. glutamicum as a 
recipient was advantageous. The secretion pathway means fewer unit operation steps in the 
downstream processing than intracellular expression. The fact that the cells do not need to be 
destroyed to release the protein means that DNA residue does not constitute a problem. 
Additionally, the QPS status means less information is needed for the risk assessment. 
==PAGE:80==
 
Master Thesis 
 
 
Spring 2024 
Page 80 of 100 
 
As mentioned in the theoretical background, the US-based company Perfect Day is leading in the 
area of precision fermentation. The company produces secreted, functional, and bioidentical whey 
proteins from recombinant fungi.  
Perfect Day chose a GRAS strain of filamentous fungus to ensure it met food safety requirements 
more easily. Since the company operates on multiple production sites globally and has different 
target markets, it was more economically feasible (Geistlinger et al., 2024). GRAS status, given by 
the FDA, is equivalent to the European QPS status.  
Degradation of α-lactalbumin 
It was confirmed through the recovery of proteins of C. glutamicum ATCC 13032, that the second 
band present on SDS-PAGE gels is not a native protein that is purified with the α-lactalbumin. 
Therefore, it is possibly degraded α-lactalbumin. Several factors could contribute to the extensive 
degradation.  
Firstly, the C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 has not been optimized for protein expression to the extent 
that E. coli BL21 (DE3) is through modifications. Although, ATCC 13032 has been used for many 
years for amino acid expression and has low protease activity.  
Secondly, the plasmid is not induced, which means that the protein was being expressed possibly 
already when it was inoculated. This would mean that some of the protein in the product could 
originate from as early as the overnight culture, which raises the duration of room temperature 
exposure and the potential for degradation.  
Lastly, the fermentation was long, and the protein expressed in the beginning had an increased 
potential to be degraded. Furthermore, the culture likely reached the stationary phase before the 
fermentation ended. Although, this could not be observed due to the gap between the OD600 
measurements. Gayen & Venkatesh (2008) study observed a stationary phase of C. glutamicum 
(CECT 79) in an LBG5 medium at 17 hours (Gayen & Venkatesh, 2008).  
The rapid growth phase is commonly used for protein expression, as the growth phase is likely to 
affect the level of protein expression. This means that the fermentation’s length contributes to the 
protein degradation, but potentially does not correspondingly increase the yield. 
DNA presence and removal 
The different DNA concentrations observed in the αs1-casein compared to the α-lactalbumin are 
very likely due to the location of the expressed protein. After homogenization and subsequent 
centrifugation, filtration through a 0.2 μm pores-sized filter was made. This could influence the 
permeation of plasmids compared to the chromosomal DNA.  
A study by Kong et al., (2010), investigated the filtration of plasmids (6 kb) and bacterial 
chromosomal DNA (72 kb) through a 0.22 μm pores sized PVDF (polyvinylidene difluoride) 
membranes, among others. The study collected data over time during filtration. The smaller-sized 
DNA had significantly higher volume filtered and higher filtration flux rates. The DNA 
transmission also decreased with the increase in the vector size (Kong et al., 2010).  
==PAGE:81==
 
Master Thesis

The complete genome of E. coli BL21 (DE3) is around 4,558 kb and it would most likely be 
retained by the filter when not fragmented (NIH, n.d.-b). The pET15alpha plasmid with only 6 kb 
was likely transferred through the membrane in high quantities.   
During the purification with affinity chromatography, it is likely that some of the plasmid DNA 
could bind to the nickel or other bound compounds and thereby be retained in the column through 
the flowthrough and the two washes performed. DNA binding to immobilized metal is supported by 
different scientific studies. Several possible mechanisms can be involved, such as interaction with 
purine bases or nickel complexes with guanine, but the main contributor is possibly the nickel 
binding with adenine bases. The imidazole was also described as being able to release the bounded 
DNA fragments, which explains why the recombinant DNA was present in the protein samples. 
(Nastasijevic et al., 2008).  
The concentration of the recombinant DNA must be lowered to an LOD of 10 ng/mL or lower. 
DNA purification could be achieved by a removal step/-s during downstream processing. However, 
most of these propositions are only feasible in a research setting. They are not feasible in 
commercial production due to the high costs.  
The enzyme DNase could be used either after the homogenization step or after the affinity 
chromatography. DNase cleaves DNA into 5'-phosphorylated di-, tri-, and oligonucleotide products 
(ThermoFisher Scientific, n.d.-b).   
Another method would be ion exchange chromatography with the use of sodium. DNA has a 
negative charge and can bind to cationic resin, while other compounds run through the column 
(Kristyanne et al., 2003). This principle could be used for milk protein purification in flow-through 
chromatography, where the contaminants bind to the column, and the proteins run through it during 
the flow-through step.  
On an industrial scale, flocculation would be a suitable option. This method is often included as a 
step to collect contaminants from intracellular products. Flocculation happens through a reduction 
of charges on biological particles, including DNA. The contaminants aggregate together and 
precipitate. They are then removed through centrifugation or filtration. The reduction in the 
negative charge is achieved by the addition of metallic salts or polyionic polymers (Harrison et al., 
2015).   
Upscaling and changes to the production process 
The production process of proteins in a laboratory differs from the production process in the 
industry. While the research focuses on whether it is possible to express a protein with acceptable 
yields, market entry requires a lot of considerations for the feasibility of producing a profitable and 
safe product.  
Some changes are intended from the start. The his-tag would be removed to make it even more 
similar to the native milk protein. The his-tag removal would also mean that affinity 
chromatography would no longer be used, and future upscaled downstream processing should also 
work on avoiding other chromatography methods, as they are very costly.

Further, the recombinant DNA would be integrated into the genomic DNA of the production strain. 
It would increase the genetic stability of the recombinant DNA and decrease the metabolic burden 
on the production cell. As discussed in the earlier sections, genomic DNA is larger than plasmid 
DNA. Therefore, it would be easier to separate it from the product. As an example, after 
homogenization, a flocculation step could be integrated, followed by a filtration step.  
The antimicrobial resistance genes would be removed from the commercial strains. This would 
decrease the risk of spreading antimicrobial resistance among microorganisms. The recombinant C. 
glutamicum would also be able to retain the QPS status. 
When it comes to the α-lactalbumin degradation, the proteases responsible for the degradation 
would also be removed through genetic modifications. The proteases would not be removed if they 
are essential to the cell.  
Due to future changes, the risk assessment done in this master thesis would not be relevant in 
regards to the genetic modifications, and the demonstration of GMM and recombinant DNA 
absence. A new risk assessment needs to be made but could reuse the knowledge about the 
recipients and some of the experimental design setup.  
7.3 EU approval 
It is possible that the milk proteins produced by precision fermentation could enter the European 
market in the future. Both milk proteins will be authorized as novel food under Regulation (EU) 
2015/2283. However, casein does contain recombinant DNA and would fall under Regulation (EC) 
1829/2003 in its current state. 
In novel food applications, the EFSA panels have already concluded several precision fermentation 
applicants as among others, as safe or safe under proposed conditions of use, such as 2′-
fucosyllactose from recombinant C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 (EFSA NDA Panel et al., 2022a), 
lacto‐N‐tetraose from recombinant E. coli BL21 (DE3) (EFSA NDA Panel et al., 2022c), and 3‐
fucosyllactose from recombinant E. coli BL21 (DE3) (EFSA NDA Panel et al., 2022b). 
The strict EU laws regarding genetically modified food reflect local consumer acceptance. A 
systematical review from 2024 on consumers’ perspective of novel food in the European Union 
shows that people are hesitant to consume novel food products such as insects and cultivated meat 
majorly due to disgust, fear, neophobia, and cultural norms across countries (Monaco et al., 2024).   
In 2019, the Danish Council of Ethics made a new statement in the debate on GMOs due to the 
evolution of many gene-modification aspects since the introduction of GMOs in Europe. Back then 
gene modification was believed an invasive technology that would alter nature in unknown ways 
together with potential risks arising in the long term. 
 
In 30 years, technology has improved and many years of scientific evidence for potential risks have 
been gained along with discovered potential benefits for society including the challenges associated 
with climate change.

The statement debates ethical concerns from different points of view. Opponents point of view 
claim that the changes done to nature are ethnically problematic supported by 59% of Europeans 
that consider GMOs unsafe to consume and 53% that consider GMOs to be harmful to the 
environment.  
 
Advocates for GMOs argue that it would be wrong not to benefit from the technology, as it can 
solve serious problems and so far, GMOs have not caused harm to the environment and humans. 
More arguments were put forward in the statement.    
 
Most of the Council members agreed that not all GMOs should be prohibited based on the used 
gene-modification method, but instead, they should be assessed by the organism’s traits and risks 
(The Danish Council on Ethics, 2019).  
 
A change in the EU's approach towards GMOs could help to catch up on the global GMO market 
and be able to support GMOs that contribute to food security. It would benefit both economically 
and sustainably. However, this can only be done through European citizens’ support. Just as with 
EU regulation, the population's opinion on GMOs is dated, and it would be beneficial to inform the 
general population through campaigns and school systems about the modern techniques of direct 
mutagenesis and the benefits of recombinant food.

8 Conclusion  
Proteins αs1-casein and α-lactalbumin produced by strain SB001 derived from E. coli BL21 (DE3) 
and strain AA001 derived from C. glutamicum ATCC 13032 were evaluated using “Guidance on 
the risk assessment of genetically modified microorganisms and their food and feed products” by 
EFSA. Furthermore, uncertainties were identified and used to evaluate the risks.  
The proteins were recovered in four batches through precision fermentation. The intracellular αs1-
casein had a mean of 2.9 mg protein per L of used fermentation broth and a size of around 32 kDa. 
The secreted α-lactalbumin had a mean of 9.8 mg protein per L of used fermentation broth and a 
size of around 14 kDa. The secreted protein was more easily recovered and produced more protein 
than the intracellular casein, however, the α-lactalbumin required longer fermentation and was 
partially degraded into smaller proteins.  
The absence of the GMM was demonstrated in both milk proteins. The absence of recombinant 
DNA was demonstrated in α-lactalbumin with an LOD of 0.1 ng DNA per mL product. The 
absence of recombinant DNA in αs1-casein could not be demonstrated and a LOD could not be 
established, as DNA was present in high concentrations. The DNA was released into the product 
stream during homogenization of SB001, while most DNA was contained in intact AA001 cells 
during secreted protein recovery.  
The α-lactalbumin is considered safe for consumption by the general population if the process 
contaminants are removed. Recombinant α-lactalbumin falls under the Novel Food Regulation for 
market authorization and therefore must follow general laws of labeling. Though it might be 
required to state any differences to the conventional α-lactalbumin. 
The α-casein must demonstrate recombinant DNA absence before it can be authorized using the 
same regulation. Currently, the product imposes a high risk of adverse effects on the environment 
due to the possible presence of antimicrobial resistance genes. There is a lot of uncertainty in the 
risk level due to the information the risk is based on. The recombinant DNA can be removed 
through additional downstream processing after homogenization, e.g. by enzymatic degradation or 
flocculation.  
The performed risk assessments were not sufficient for market authorizations, because of their 
shortcomings. Although, overall, the learning process of risk assessment for food produced by 
GMM was successful. 
9 Future aspects 
Future versions of the risk assessments should include experimental data of at least a pilot scale, 
three independent batches, and at least three technical replicates. Recombinant DNA presence 
should be investigated for antimicrobial resistance genes presence, concentration, fragmentation, 
and the quantity of different fragments. A feasible method for DNA removal on a larger production 
scale should also be investigated experimentally. Furthermore, when the protein is finalized, a more 
detailed investigation of its characteristics, expected consumption, and ADME should be included. 
A study of chromosomal DNA and plasmid DNA presence through downstream processes could 
investigate how recombinant DNA placement affects its removal. The effect of fermentation length 
on the degradation of α-lactalbumin and yields should also be investigated.
