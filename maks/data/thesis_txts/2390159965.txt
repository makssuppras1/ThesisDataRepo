Summary
The main focus is to study the process inside a mixing valve, which have been de-
veloped by Alfa Laval. Mixing valves comes in various varieties, depending on the
application. The aim of mixing valves is to mix two or more mediums together. This
dissertation will be based on two mediums which are sludge-water and polymer-water,
and how the mixing of these two takes place inside the valve. Thereafter improvement
suggestions within the given limitation are suggested and evaluated. The mixing was
found to be improved by making geometrical changes to the valve. The behaviour
of the polymer suggests less mixing for smoother geometry. Two different turbulent
models was also tested, and the results showed difference in the velocity profiles, while
other variables was approximately the same.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Alfa Laval is a Swedish based company, which orients towards a business to business
model. There are three different type of fluid handling methods which dominates
the current industrial market for Alfa Laval. The three methods are separation, mix-
ing and chemical reactions. The machinery available in their product portfolio is
mostly configured towards bigger plants. One type of plant is wastewater treatment
plants (WWTP), in which many different machines can be used. It is essential for
the facility to treat the wastewater efficiently, so that it will be financially and en-
vironmentally feasible. The end product of the wastewater treatment is technical
water and bio-waste. The waste in water is separated step wise, taking the bigger
unwanted particles first and then reducing the filtering process down to a membrane
level. After initial separation of large particles the wastewater will reach a decanter
centrifuge. The mixing valve, which this dissertation is about, is positioned right
before the decanter. The main purpose of the mixing valve is to mix polymer with
wastewater, thus making the separation process inside the decanter more efficient.
The polymer molecules have a sticky attribute, because of their electrical charge.
This means that waste particles will stick together with the polymer, thus gaining
more mass. This will benefit the separation process in the centrifuge, which will yield
a better end product. The mixing capacity and overall process will be studied during
this dissertation.
1.1
Mixing Valve
The mixing valve is often used in wastewater applications, to mix polymer and wastew-
ater together. The type of mixing which happens inside the valve is called hard mixing,
this is due to the low time factor the two mediums spend together inside the valve
itself. The geometry of the valve is designed in such a way that the cross sectional
area decreases upstream of the gate inside of the valve. This is illustrated on Figure
1.1. The narrowing of the geometry forces the two mediums together and ensures
optimal mixing conditions. As the mediums are forced together the sticky attributes
of the polymer ensures that waste particles in the wastewater are lumped together.
The sticky attribute of the polymer will contribute to the initial mixing of particles
in the wastewater. Therefore it is important to have the mixture in an evenly spread
continuum, so the chances for lumpiness are low. Water is added in the mixture to

Component 3 is a gate implemented to provoke resistance such that a higher mixing

through the valve. This gate is installed with an angle to make sure it blocks the
flow unless there is enough pressure too push the gate open and thus make a channel
for the flow. Component 3 is where this gate is mounted on a solid rod, which is
connected to a weight that corresponds to the pressure of release.

1.1.1
Sludge
The definition of sludge is the slurry byproduct of wastewater treatment. The com-
position and type of sludge depends on the source of waste. Most industrial plants
which utilize water, will have a corresponding output of wastewater leaving the plant.
These plants include the food processing plants, power plants, mines and quarries,
nuclear power plants and WWTP. The dry solids of the waste is referred to as cake,
and most commonly the cake is incinerated. This is due to majority of waste that
comes from these different applications consist of environmentally dangerous sub-
stances. Wastewater applications which involves sewage treatment, is the producer
of the most environmentally friendly waste. In the case of municipal and agricultural
segments, the byproduct is called biosludge as it mainly consist of organic waste. Dif-
ferent advantageous applications of biosludge includes: usage as fertilizer in cropland
to increase soil quality and productivity due to containing nutrients and other organic
matter[2], usage as landfill[3] as part or cover, and as auxiliary fuel [4].

1.1.2
Polymer
There are different type of polymers for each application, and each with different
areas of application. This is due to the chains of monomer that make up the polymer.
These chains vary in size and length, which is what attributes to the varied properties
of each polymer.
Applying certain sticky types of polymer to wastewater will help coagulate the solids
thus generating larger curds. There are three different types of polymerization pro-
cesses used for treating wastewater: Emulsion, Suspension and Solution. Emulsion
and suspension are both a type of radical polymerization, where the latter is more
relevant for this particular case. There exist two different forms of suspension poly-
merization. One is powder and the other is bead. In case of bead polymerization
the polymer is soluble in its monomer thus resulting in translucent bead. While pow-
der polymerization is not soluble the same way thus resulting in porous and chaotic
chains of beads. This leads to a cross-linked molecular structure which will function
as a bridge between the waste particles. Improving the solids settling time, waste
compaction as-well as reducing the biochemical oxygen demand and suspended solids.
The settling time and polymer dose are proportional to each other, Figure 1.3 illus-
trates the increase in dosing from left to right in each sample. The Emulsion polymer
will be mixed with water, without emulsifiers for this particular case. The polymer
mixture is immiscible, therefore it is necessary to maintain a steady pressure when
injecting it. Another reason for the higher pressure is to prevent backflow and sludge
water entering the polymer injection tubes. For emulsion polymer it is important that
there is a homogeneous spread of beads in the mixture, this is due to their charge.
When polymer and sludge get in contact with each other, they will equalize each oth-
ers charge and prevent water or other waste particles to penetrate the newly formed
curds [5].

2.1
Computation Fluid Dynamics
The behaviour of any fluid particle can expressed by Navier-Stokes equations. Navier-
Stokes is a system of partial differential equations (PDE). The Navier-Stokes equa-
tions can be difficult and non-intuitive which also means that the equations only have
analytical solutions for some special cases where a lot of simplifications can be made.
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) uses mathematical formulations such as the
finite volume method (FVM) to discretize the physics into arbitrary control volumes
and approximate a solution. Using CFD can be advantageous, especially in cases
where a physical setup for experiments hasn’t been made. One can test several de-
signs and numerically determine important coefficients. CFD is a strong tool which
besides numerically solving partial differential equations also can visualize the fluid
flow. Being able to see the flow and where the highest speed or pressure loss will take
place is a must for almost any design.

2.1.1.1
Finite volume method
FVM uses the shown conservation laws on volume integral form. The partial differ-
ential equation with the volume integrals are converted to surface integrals by the
use of the divergence theorem, where the fluxes are calculated at the surfaces of each
finite volume. Since the flux leaving one volume is the same amount entering the next
volume the method is conservative. This is also where one of the biggest advantages
of the FVM lies, since the method calculates the flux through surfaces it can be used
on unstructured mesh.

Wall functions
A significant assumption made throughout this report is that the walls in the valve are
assumed to be no-slip walls. This means that the velocity at the wall is equal to zero.
Because of this assumptions the velocity gradient near the walls will be extremely
high and thus requires a different approach when solving for the variables. When
modelling near wall regions it is common to divide the region into 3 layers. Closest to
the wall is the viscous (laminar) sublayer, then the logarithmic layer and finally the
turbulent layer. As the Reynolds number increases the laminar sublayer’s influence
decreases and does not need to be resolved. For higher values of Reynolds number a
so called wall function is used, which connect the turbulent layer with the wall. The
wall function approach is used for the simulations made in this project. The distance
from the wall to the first mesh node is designated y+ and is given by

K-ω:
This is a two equation model which solves kinetic turbulence energy and the
specific dissipation rate which determines the scale of turbulence [11]. Based on the
cell size, this model will automatically switch from a wall function to a low Reynolds
number formulation if the criteria are met. It is also more accurate in predicting the
transition phase and near wall boundary layers.
SST K-ω:
SST stands for shear stress transport. This model consists of two models,
K-ω and K-ϵ. This model utilizes both of the aforementioned models in regard to
where the boundaries are presented. Therefore near boundaries the model uses K-ω
and in free shear flow K-ϵ.
Implicit Unsteady:
There are different types of time solvers to choose from, the
implicit model has been chosen as it does not only take the last discretized node
into account. This means that by affecting one cell the reaction of the next cell can
go in any direction in the three dimensional system. while the time dependency is
parabolic, the effect is only seen at future cells. Therefore in each physical time-step
it involves some number of inner iterations to converge for that instant. These inner
iterations can be obtained by two methods, implicit- and explicit-spatial integration.
The user then defines the physical step size which will be used in the outer loop. The
integration system tries to match this by using pseudo-time steps which is obtained
from the predetermined Courant number [12].
Gradients:
Is used within the transport equation solution methodology which re-
constructs the field values at the cell faces. This also includes secondary gradient
for diffusion terms, which in continuation of this will have pressure-velocity coupling
in the segregated flow model. This algorithm is derived from the momentum and
continuity equations[12].

CHAPTER 4
Discussion
The discussion chapter will evaluate on all the assumptions made thus far, which have
formed the boundaries of all the simulations. The change within each simulation have
been kept to a minimum and the thought processes together with the affect will be
discussed.
4.1
Initial setup
The simulations conducted in this section were done with the turbulent model k-ω
and with a viscosity value of µ = 8.88 · 10−4 for both liquids.
The simulation results have been compared to the guide lines, which was the initial
boundary conditions. This have been the foundation for all the other simulations
conducted. The validation was done by comparing the values of Table 1.1 with the
results section 3.1.
Pressure
The pressure difference is an important factor when efficiency is discussed.
The mixing valve will perform within the set criteria, when both liquids have the same
attribute. Accepting the current design, it is therefore complimentary to look at the
improvement suggestions. The suggestion in which the value of pressure is lowest is
the 40◦model, where the deviation reaches 8%. As the gate is stationary while the
inlet angle changes the pressure would appropriately increase. The polymer inlets will
in majority of time have a higher pressure value as the cross sectional area is smaller
and is it not desired to have sludge mixture entering the inlets. It is noteworthy that
the pressure drop is calculated in regards to the sludge inlet and the outlet. The
reason why the 40◦angle improvement suggestion was chosen, was due to having
counter flow, which caused localized flow disturbance. The change from four to two
inlets also had an affect on the pressure, even when the area was kept the same.
The other models suggested almost the same value as the current design, making
the change obsolete. Another small part experiencing pressure difference is the flow
before the gate, this is caused by the geometrical change. The cross section contracts
from the bottom, thus forcing a pressure difference locally, as the velocity have to be
maintained.
==PAGE:47==
4.2 Final setup
37
Polymer
The spread of polymer inside the valve in this case, is rather fast and
covers majority of the flow. As both liquids have the same attributes as water, the
diffusivity of polymer in sludge is rather high. This should be taken into consideration
when analyzing the results. Therefore the trajectory is more relevant when looking
at these simulations. All the improvement suggestions have a disadvantage when it
comes to time, as the inlets have been moved further down stream. This is done to
increase the concentration of the polymer that passes the gate, to theoretically reach a
higher mixing through the gate opening. Figure B.1 and B.2 in Appendix B illustrates
this very well. This is due to the mass fraction of polymer inside the valve are not
proportionally equal to the mass fraction going through the gate opening. The sample
points take an average of the plane surface, therefore the velocity gradient helps spread
the polymer is the deciding factor. As the injection points move closer to the gate
opening, the amount of mass fraction of polymer inside the valve decreases, which
are described further in Appendix B. The volume stays the same but the polymer
gets injected further downstream, therefore a good portion of the valve will solely
consist of sludge water. The flow gradient forces polymer to have a specific profile
when passing through the gate opening. It requires a greater disturbance to have the
polymer spread more, which will yield better mixing.
General
For the applications in which both mediums have the same viscosity, the
valve can be substantially improved. When the mixture passes the gate, it will not
utilize the whole area of the outlet. Right after the gate the small pressure difference
will force the domain to reach a steady flow in which the only mixing factor will be
the time component. It is not desired to deplete the flows energy, but simply to use
it efficiently.
4.2
Final setup
All simulation conducted in this section will have µp = 1 Pa · s for the polymer
mixture. Furthermore a comparison between k-ω and k-ϵ turbulent models will be
discussed.
Pressure
The boundaries of the simulation stayed the same while the viscosity of
the polymer changed, therefore the pressure were increased. In regard to the different
models it can be seen that the difference in the results is almost negligible. This is
because both models use the same equations when away from the boundary layers.
The pressure for the current design and the 40◦is the same while the rectangular
model have an increased pressure. This is due to a combination of higher viscosity
and more inlet surface area. While the cross sectional area stays the same, the sur-
face area enfolding the polymer increases. Thus more energy is required in order to
maintain the same predetermined values.
==PAGE:48==
4.2 Final setup
38
The correlation between pressure and velocity stays the same, even when the ve-
locity is lower for the simulations with rectangular inlets. This is not necessarily an
undesired change, as with lower velocity through the gate, hints of vortex shedding
is seen. While the velocity is too high, the stream coming from the gate opening
shoots directly at the shell of the valve. Decreasing this value will cause the profile to
move periodically, as inlet pumps have a frequency. Being able to induce vortices will
improve the mixing, even after the gate opening. This can be beneficial, as the con-
sumption of polymer can be lowered, while maintaining same mixing ratio. Further
experimental data is required for this part of the study, before it can be concluded.
Polymer
The reason for rectangular inlets suggestion was to spread the polymer
right as it enters the valve. A common observation with circular inlets were that the
concentration of polymer would stay high in the middle of the profile, while the spread
would occur in the outer periphery. Having a more homogeneous profile of polymer
would increase the initial spread, but the concentration would still be suitable for
when passing through the gate. There should be balance between these two factors,
and by changing the inlet profile one of these criteria can be fore filled. The circular
inlet is the fastest to reach the transition point, which can be seen on Figure C.4
inside Appendix C. Both suggestions, rectangular and circular, share the same exact
boundaries. Therefore it is speculated that the disturbance in flow is higher for the
rectangular suggestion. This is a by product of higher pressure, which is illustrated
in Figure C.2 inside Appendix C, thus more energy to dissipate. The stream lines
for the polymer indicates that the polymer will follow the velocity profile all the way
to the gate opening, and only spread after the gate opening due to vortices. The top
polymer inlet have a homogeneous spread throughout the gate, but this is due to the
geometry, as there is an abrupt stop before the gate which forces the flow to change
trajectory.
The initial boundaries for the valve decides that the flow domain is completely filled
with sludge mixture before the polymer inlets start to inject the polymer. The rectan-
gular inlet shape compromises concentration for more surface area, therefore a higher
spread is reached. The development illustrated on Figure C.12 inside Appendix C,
showcases the lowest value for the rectangular suggestion until 0.75 seconds. The de-
velopment of the polymer after the gate opening can be different compared to reality.
The fluid which are underneath the gate do not get exposed to the polymer too much
in the simulations, as the run time of most simulations are below four physical sec-
onds. Table 3.2 illustrates this particular behaviour well, as the diffusivity numbers
are quite low for the outlet. This value is directly proportional with the viscosity,
which have a direct affect on the outcome. Based on the current simulations it is
expected that the diffusivity will increase by increasing the physical run time. This
is due to the developed vortices after the gate, which eventually will drag in more
polymer mass and thus increasing the mixing.

General
All conducted simulations in this dissertation, had defined boundaries.
The frame of reference were one specific application, in which the valve was per-
forming as intended but not as efficient. Due to limitation on polymer usage, other
solutions were suggested. The spread of polymer can not directly be linked to ef-
ficiency but it gives a good indication of the performance of the valve. The only
parameters which were changed was the geometry, in which the pressure followed.
The pressure difference inside have a direct impact on the end results, thus it makes
a dominant factor when working on solutions. It can be discussed if the cost in in-
creased pressure it worth the extra mixing coming from the rectangular inlet. The
dosing cost of the polymer increases the energy cost of the increased pressure, within
a small time frame[1].
The velocity profile before the gate can have more aberration to achieve a higher
homogeneous profile in regard to polymer. The lower part of the valve have a smooth
transition all the way to the gate opening, by having a more abrupt style of transition,
as seen on the upper part, the polymer will reach a better spread. The k-ϵ suggestions
a higher value for diffusivity even when the velocity profiles look more favourable for
the k-ω model which can be seen on Figure D.6 and D.7 in Appendix D. The same
tendency can be seen on Figure D.3 and D.4 in Appendix D, when using stream lines.
The behaviour of the fluid, in regard to irregularities and initial spread of polymer,
is the major component to higher diffusivity.
The initial point of interest in regard to diffusivity of polymer, lays before the gate
opening. It can be seen that for the current design on D.8(c) in Appendix D, that the
value and spread is lower when compared to the suggestion models. While after the
gate opening a larger area are covered in a high value of diffusivity. The maximum
diffusivity value are lower for the current design, while the area of high diffusivity is
higher than the other two counter parts. D.8(a) in Appendix D showcases an inter-
ruption after the gate opening. For the k-ϵ model the tendency do not follow. D.9(a)
illustrates a higher diffusivity factor after the gate opening and near the outlet. The
higher diffusivity value near the outlet is common for all the simulation. Both of
the turbulent models benefit the circular polymer inlet, but it shall be taken into
consideration as the inspections planes are all in the mid section of the valve. The
rectangular design utilized the spread of polymer better, thus maintaining sufficient
levels of diffusivity while offsetting the sampling plane. While the circular improve-
ment suggestion will yield less sufficient levels of diffusivity while offsetting the plane
towards the sides.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
The process inside the valve have been investigated, and the lead cause of polymer
spread within the given criteria have been found to be geometrical change. Different
turbulence models were also tested and the results were approximately the same. For
the current design of the valve and both liquids sharing same viscosity, the mixing
was suffering due to dilution. The four polymer injections are relatively too far from
the gate opening, therefore lower polymer concentration is present at the gate open-
ing. By moving the polymer injections closer to the gate, the polymer concentration
will increase and thus better mixing will be achieved.
Changing the polymer viscosity to 1 Pa·s did not affect the results in a great manner.
The pressure was regulated to maintain the boundary conditions. The inlet shape for
the polymer have an affect on the initial mixing. It can be concluded that rectangular
inlets are more beneficial than circular, when the injection points are closer to the
gate. It shall be noted that by changing the inlets to rectangular shape more energy
is required to maintain the same boundary conditions.
Another observation was that within certain velocity limits, hints of vortex shedding
was being developed. Therefore regulating the opening size can also yield beneficial
results. It is expected that after a sufficient angle of the gate opening concentration
shedding be lowered.
The bottom of the valve has a smooth transition all the way to the gate opening,
which is not beneficial for optimal mixing. Having a sudden geometrical change will
yield a better spread, as seen from the stream lines.

CHAPTER 6
Further Work
The simulations can be updated to be more dynamic in regards to the gate. Enabling
oscillations on the gate will greatly affect the results. Designing the gate in such a
manner that it will align with the frequency of the inlet flow, can be another field of
study.
The models used can be updated, as RANS is not that efficient when it comes to
separation and mixing. Further studies with multiphased interactions can be done to
understand the behaviour of polymer better on a molecular level, using either Eule-
rian or Lagragian[12].
Geometrical improvement can be made inside the valve, to induce more rotational
forces before the gate. One example can be adding a static mixer upstream the gate,
and study the behaviour of the polymer over the static mixer. It will also have an
affect of the outlet flow.
Another study can be conducted to understand the mixing after the valve, in which
the mixture will have time to develop fully.
