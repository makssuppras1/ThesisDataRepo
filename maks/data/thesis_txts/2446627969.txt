Improved Wind Turbine Load Validation using Detailed Turbulence Measurements
by Anaïs PAULIN
In this thesis work, different methods of wind ﬁeld generation are compared to improve the
loads comparison of the V52 wind turbine measurements and simulations made with the
aero-elastic code HAWC2. The following methods are studied: A method of constrained
turbulence box generation based on the KSEC model, and an unconstrained model where
both turbulence models, Mann and KSEC models are used. A preliminary work focus on the
selection of the measured data. Then, the validation of the simulations is done by compar-
ing electrical power, pitch and rotor speed signals, that use already established calibrations.
Constraining the synthetic wind ﬁeld to the measured values gives very close results to the
measure values for the wind speed. However, unconstrained wind is less predictable, lead-
ing to unexpected standard deviation or lack of information at high frequency depending
on the turbulence model. For loads and fatigue analyze, two tracks have been followed:
comparison of the turbulence models and constraining levels, and comparison to the mea-
surements. The ﬁrst one has been successful, strengths and weaknesses of each method
have been identiﬁed. The second one was less fruitful as the quality of the measurements
and gains are too unclear.

1 Introduction
1.1
Simulation of loads on a wind turbine with an aero-elastic code
Predicting the dynamic loads, the damages and the lifetime of a wind turbine has been a
concern since their introduction [10], as much for the researchers as for the industries in the
wind energy sector. Their anticipation is one of the ﬁrst step in the design of a turbine with
the avowed goal of optimizing the production and the efﬁciency of this kind of installation.
The rotor, composed of blades, is the source of the main wind loads applied on the turbine.
The loads are analyzed to design the rotor, but the ﬂuctuation of the loads has a huge impact
on the lifetime and the fatigue loads acting on the wind turbine. A wind turbine is meant
to be designed for a 20 years life time, and it is thus of the greatest importance to accurately
predict fatigue loads.
Aero-elastic codes are mathematical models used to predict the answer of a system subject
to unsteady ﬂow conditions. These are commonly used, and must be used for designing a
turbine and predict its lifetime and productivity under speciﬁc conditions. Usually the wind
turbines are divided into categories according to the IEC classiﬁcation [5], and turbulence
levels. If all the requirements are fulﬁlled, the IEC standard gives the necessary information
to predict the loads. In case of non fulﬁllment of the requirements, for instance when turbu-
lence is high or when the wind is affected by neighborhood, the usage of aero-elastics code
is more than recommended to simulate the real loads.
These models are validated through a long campaign of development and comparison with
real measurements, to be the most trustworthy possible. HAWC2 [7] (Horizontal Axis Wind
turbine simulation Code 2nd generation) is one of them, and claim to calculate wind turbine
response in time domain. Developed by the aero-elastic Design Research Program of DTU
Wind Energy at Risø, it is part of the commercially available codes from DTU Wind Energy
such as the software WAsP and BECAS. It has already been proven that HAWC2 provides
realistic simulations [12], but researchers continuously seek to improve its results and extend
its domain of application.
A wind turbine is subject to an unsteady inﬂow, depending on the wind speed, the wind
shear, and most of all, the turbulence intensity. Since the turbulence around the rotor has
a great impact on the response time of the turbine, it is important to precisely reproduce
the turbulence of a site in order to accurately simulate the operation of wind turbine. Until
now, only the main statistics of a given period (such as the mean wind speed or the shear of
the wind proﬁle) are used in the HAWC2 model to reproduce the loads on the wind turbine

according to real measurements. It uses the Mann model, as suggested in the IEC standard
[5]. This implies that although the simulation results are similar to the real loads on the wind
turbine, there are some misﬁts for short period of time or high frequency.
A wind ﬁeld is simulated to describe the inﬂow coming to the turbine: it is represented
by a turbulence box (see ﬁgure 1.1). Several wind models exist: the most common is the
Mann model [9], but it is not the only one. Each value of the box represents the value of the
turbulence of the wind at a certain position in space, and at a certain time. The turbulence
box is in 3D: two dimensions represent the positions in a plan, and the last one is the time.
It allows to describe the variation of the turbulence in space and time. The turbulence box
reproduces the turbulence of a plane in space y-z for a certain amount of time. The time
history of wind is not independent, so this dependence has to be taken into account for the
generation of the wind speed around the rotor at several points. It mainly depends on the
distance between the points of the turbulence box, in space and in time.

Motivation and objectives of the thesis
As part of my MSc Wind Energy, writing my Master Thesis at Risø DTU Wind energy is
a great opportunity to enjoy all the facilities provided by the campus and to be part of a
resourceful and ambitious wind energy research team. The intention of the subject is to
improve the simulation of loads carried out by the aero-elastic code HAWC2, using more
detailed wind measurements to better quantify turbulence.
Furthermore, there is still a small deviation between the simulated loads and the measure-
ments in the bending curves of 5% [12], and differences in the history at high frequency. One
possible solution would be to constrain the synthetic wind ﬁeld to the measured values, for
describing the inﬂow of the turbine.
Until now, the simulation of the loads under HAWC2 for actual wind measurements only
takes into account the basics statistics of the wind conditions to generate the turbulence box.
Even if the results are certainly similar to the measured loads and are trustful to predict
turbine lifetime, when the loads are considered regarding a short time, the simulation does
not fully match the measured data.
Constraining a turbulence box means to impose certain values according to actual measure-
ments and then calculate the rest of the values according to the required ones. It can be one
value for one position at a certain time, or at many positions for every time step, depend-
ing on the required degree of constraining. Therefore, the accuracy of the turbulence box
comparing the actual conditions is assumed to be improved.

Besides, some tools have been developed to constrain the generation of the turbulence box
according to time-series values. They allow ﬂexibility for the degree of constraining. The
turbulence box can be constrained at several heights, step time, and with measured values
coming from various measuring devices. Different methods exist, such as the Mann tech-
nique or the KSEC method. However, it has not been tested yet as an improvement of the
loads simulation. Combining all the possibilities from DTU facilities and installations will
hopefully lead to the conclusion or not of the relevancy of constraining the turbulence data
for loads simulation, and which model is the most appropriate to do so.
To achieve this objective, the ﬁrst step is to select the relevant time-series available from the
measurements by removing untrustworthy data and periods with no desired conditions of
working. Then, the different methods of turbulence box generation which are compared are:
• Unconstrained with KSEC model for the interpolation
• Unconstrained with Mann model for the interpolation
• Constrained at hub height with KSEC model
• Constrained at ﬁve heights (including hub height) with KSEC model
The simulations with the different methods are carried out for a large range of wind speed
and turbulence intensity to cover all the conditions of operation. HAWC2 is used, along
with the available tools wetb and PyConTurb.
Finally, the comparison of the methods, between each others, and to the measurements, will
hopefully help to answer the following concerns:
• What are the differences between Mann and KSEC models for the simulation of a wind
ﬁeld, and what are their advantages and disadvantages?
• What are the implications on the wind and the loads simulation by choosing one of
the two wind models?
• What are the effects of constraining the turbulence box on the wind generation? On
the loads and fatigue loads simulation?
• And so, can we improve the loads comparison by constraining the turbulence boxes?
If so, which channels are most affected?
Improving the design of turbines and controllers to further optimize energy production is of
the utmost importance for researchers. Research around the utility constraining falls within
that line of work as it may lead to better prediction of a wind turbine lifetime.

3.1
HAWC2 aeroelastic code
The aeroelastic code HAWC2 [4] has been developed since 2003 by DTU Wind Energy. It
calculates wind turbine response and its principle is based on multi-body dynamics, and
extended momentum theory to handle dynamics and deﬂections.
The turbine is divided into main bodies, mainly the tower and the blades, linked by con-
straints that allow only certain movements between bodies. A main body also consist of
several bodies with a node distribution, that permits to have data at particular points of the
system.
A lot of work have been realized to validate the code. It is now totally suitable and trustwor-
thy to simulate loads on a wind turbine, for instance. The structure and the commands of
the code are presented in the ofﬁcial user manual written by Torben Juul Larsen and Anders
M. Hansen [7].
As inputs, the code needs a main input ﬁle .htc where the model is deﬁned (that is to say
the bodies, constraints, boundaries, external conditions the system is subject to), as well as
the time simulation. The control is given in ddl ﬁles, and the necessary data are stored in
other ﬁles called by the htc ﬁle. The structure is illustrated in the ﬁgure 3.1.
3.2
V52 model and control
The V52 model has been developed through the years at DTU Wind Energy in collaboration
with VESTAS. The model has been calibrated according to diverse sources such as measure-
ments, and observations with recommendations are given in the article written by Hansen,
Rinker and Larsen[6]. Two controllers are coupled to the model, one from DTU [3] and the
other one from VESTAS (VTS).
The controllers have been compared and the model has been validated.

Coordinate systems
The different coordinates systems must be declared for the next sections because they are
different according to the source and the regarded system (the blade or the turbine itself).
In general, the meteorological system u, v and w is used, where u is the main direction of
the mean wind speed, v is horizontal and w is vertical upwards.
HAWC2 also has its own coordinate systems [7]: the global one, x, y and z that refers to
the meteorological one, where y is the default direction of the wind, the z-axis is pointing
vertical downwards and x is horizontal to the side. It also has local coordinate systems
deﬁned according to the body considered. For the blade structure, it is deﬁned with the z-
axis pointing from the blade root to the tip, the x-axis in the tangential direction of rotation
and the y direction is from the pressure side towards the section side of the blade proﬁles.
In the ﬁgure 3.2, the different coordinate systems are represented. XG, YG and ZG is linked
to the turbine, XB, YB and ZB to the blade, so it differs from the ﬁrst one by the yaw and
pitch angles, and the azimuth position of the blade.

For the declaration of the turbulence, there are two possibilities:

• If no binary ﬁles are speciﬁed, the turbulence box is directly generated by an intern

the turbulence parameters indicated with the command create_turb_parameters in
the sub command bloc mann. The parameters for the turbulence in the command asso-
ciated are calculated according to the time-series selected for the simulation. The mean
wind speed, turbulence intensity and shear are calculated through data extracted from
the time series. The seed turbulence number has also to be speciﬁed to this command,
such as the length scale of the turbulence.
3.5
Presentation of the different methods of generation of turbu-
lence boxes constrained by measurement
3.5.1
Different degrees of constraining
The objective of the report is to improve the simulation of the loads with new methods.
These new methods are based on different ways to generate the turbulence boxes.
Different techniques of constraining are tested in this report for the generation of the turbu-
lence boxes, used as inputs for the simulation. Two models of turbulence exist, the KSEC
and the Mann method. The Mann method is implemented within the HAWC2 code through
the intern generator whereas te KSEC model is the one implemented in the PyConTurb tool,
as presented in section 3.5.2.

the Spinner Lidar information. Using such data increases the level of constraining

3.5.2
Introduction to PyConTurb
When it is not done internally to HAWC2, the generation of the turbulence boxes is realized
with the open-source constrained turbulence generator called PyConTurb [11]. This method
of constraining by measured time-serie is based on the KSEC (Kaimal spectrum with Expo-
nential Coherence method) model technique, according to the wind turbine standard IEC
61400-1 Ed. 3. It is associated with the measured time series in the spatial correlation step.
This tool is implemented in Python and allows a high ﬂexibility in the constraining data. The
==PAGE:42==
22
Chapter 3. Simulation model with HAWC2 and PyConTurb
python package is available at the repository source https://gitlab.windenergy.dtu.dk/
rink/pyconturb.git.
The KSEC model allows to describe the turbulence in a wind model, such as the Mann model
used for the generation of the turbulence directly with HAWC2.
The Mann method is more complex to compute than the KSEC method and there is no tool
publicly available. The Mann method generates two ﬁelds: a random and unconstrained
Gaussian ﬁeld and a second one is overlaid to force certain random values to take the re-
quired values and this way constrain the synthetic ﬁeld. However it sometimes lead to
unrealistic speeds for two close positions, or to unrealistic variations of speed. This is one
of the reason why the open-source tool PyConTurb is incremented with the KSEC method.
But this last method also has issues, because it does not conserve the mass, contrary to the
Mann method. Also, the coherence is sometimes not conserved in the lateral and vertical
turbulence components.
The ﬁrst step for generating the turbulence box is to select a reference time-serie as explained
in the section 2.5. A data ﬁle that PyConTurb needs to generate the turbulence box is created,
from the measurement data ﬁle. The values of the measured turbulence are stored in this
ﬁle. The number of kept values depends on the level of constraining wanted. For one
position constrained, only the values in the three directions at this position will be kept, but
if the constraint is applied at different heights, the corresponding values are stored too. A
detailed example is given in the next section.
Then, from this ﬁle, the turbulence box is created using PyConTurb to interpolate the missing
points in the turbulence box and complete the ones known from the measurements. It is
saved as a HAWC2 binary ﬁle, so that it can be used as an input for the simulation with the
aeroelastic code.

5 Loads comparison
After checking the good agreement between the control channels for the simulation and the
measurements, now the comparison of the different methods of simulation can be done for
the dynamic response.
A wind turbine is normally designed to have a lifetime of at least 20 years, but premature
breakdowns can occur if fatigue and loads are not well anticipated. Anticipation can be
done with accurate simulations that prevent unexpected fatigue loads.
There are three main sources for the loads on a wind turbine [2]. The ﬁrst one is the gravita-
tional loading caused by the earth’s gravitation force, which is sinusoidal with a frequency
depending on the rotor’s rotation. The second one is inertial loading that occurs when the
rotor is accelerating or decelerating.
The last and less predictable loads source is the aerodynamic loading. It is caused by the
turbulent inﬂow coming through the rotor, varying in space and time.
Thus, the main loads are coming from the wind: in order to extract energy from the wind,
the speed of the wind has to be decreased using a force upwind direction. This total load,
called the thrust, has two components, normal to the inﬂow, and tangential, in the direction
of the rotation of the blades. To characterize these two forces, the ﬂapwise and edgewise
blade root moments are stated.
The ﬂapwise bending moment Mx tends to deﬂect the blade out of the rotor plan, and the
edgewise bending moment My comes from the tangential force distribution.
As previously stated, this load source is the less predictable, and the results from simulation
are very sensitive to it, because of a lack of accuracy in the wind ﬁeld simulation. Constrain-
ing the turbulence thus aim at improving the aerodynamic load simulation

6 Fatigue loads comparison
6.1
Fatigue loads
The loads on a wind turbine vary constantly with time and it is one of the reasons of a
possible breakdown, because of the fatigue damage. The best way to study the dynamic
response of the turbine is to look at the fatigue loads, because it is representative of the
variation of the loads on the system (including standard deviation and energy contribution
of natural frequencies), with the number of cycles during the lifetime. The inﬂuence of the
constraining method for simulation is more likely to be noticeable on these values.
Before going any further, it is worth pointing out that for the calculation of the fatigue loads
from 10min time-series, the 60 ﬁrst seconds are removed to avoid the transient period due to
the simulation calculation. Also, the shift due to the delay between the measurements and
the simulation, explained in the section 3.6.3 is taken into account, and the ﬁrst values of the
measurements (plus the 60 ﬁrst seconds to ﬁt with the simulation) are removed, according
to the mean wind speed of the history.
Some conclusions have already been drawn from the previous comparison. The wind speed
generated from the unconstrained KSEC model gives higher standard deviation, and higher
power density for high frequencies, so fatigue loads are expected to be higher than with the
other methods. The unconstrained Mann model is the opposite, and the constrained KSEC
model is placed between both. However the dispersion in the loads is more disparate for the
unconstrained Mann model (or unconstrained in general) than for the constrained model,
so no obvious assumption can be done between these two.
6.2
Rainﬂow counting and equivalent loads
To analyze the fatigue loads, the ﬁrst step is to describe the loads history with numbers of
cycles with the same stress range, that can be then presented into a histogram. The method
based on the rainﬂow counting is used, because it varies in an irregular way in time.
The rainﬂow method is named from an analogy of the rain falling on a roof. It consists in
imagining a rain ﬂow starting at each peak, and falling until it meets a peak which is more
positive, or it meets an other path of a rain ﬂow. The method is illustrated in the ﬁgure 6.1.
With this method, the number of cycles with the same stress range is counted for the history
considered. It describes the variations of the time-serie.

7 Final discussion and conclusions
for future work
7.1
Conclusion
HAWC2 aeroelastic code is already trustworthy in predicting the dynamic response of a
wind turbine subject to unsteady conditions, however the loads comparison between simu-
lation and measurements can still be improved.
The focus of this report has been given in suggesting advanced techniques for improving
the accuracy of the wind ﬁeld generated for inputs in the aeroelastic code. Two models
have been tested, the Mann model and the KSEC model, and two methods have been used
for the generation of the turbulence boxes: totally unconstrained based on the main statis-
tics of times-series extracted from measurements, and constrained by actual values of the
turbulence provided by meteorological devices. Indeed, Risø site is equipped with great in-
stallations that supply complete information about the meteorological conditions of the site
and operation of the V52 turbine erected at this same location.
A particular attention has been given to the selection of the data. The simulations are based
on 10min time-series from actual measurements. Filters for the selection were found by
looking at the conditions of the site, and limitations of the devices. This selection can be
used for other campaigns, it covers the entire range of wind speed and turbulence intensity
of the site, with enough samples to get trustful statistics.
With the same global wind statistics, the Mann and the KSEC models do not provide the
same wind ﬁeld. The KSEC, with the tool PyConTurb, does not conserve the standard devia-
tion, and the energy at high frequency is slightly higher than expected. It is probably related
to the non conservation of the mass with this model and the lack of coherence in the lateral
and vertical turbulence components. On the contrary, the Mann model, generated inter-
nally to HAWC2, gives the good standard deviation comparing the measurements and the
value indicated as inputs for the generation, however high frequencies do not contribute a
lot comparing to what it should be. But this model is more complex to compute, that is why
the constraining method, coded in PyConTurb tool, has been done with the KSEC model.
Constraining the turbulence box for the wind ﬁeld is done with the KSEC model as men-
tioned. The constrained values can be as much detailed as wanted. In this report, two cases
have been studied: a turbulence box constrained at hub height, and one constrained at ﬁve

heights. Both give similar results. The wind ﬁeld shows a very satisfactory agreement with
the actual wind ﬁeld for the 10min time-series, that should lead to accurate loads simula-
tions comparing the measurements.
However, the loads comparisons do not give as clear results as expected.
Preliminary work validated all the simulations by comparing the following channels: power,
rotor speed and pitch angle. The measurements agree with the simulations, even if through-

A calibration has to be suggested, this error leading to errors in the measurements of the

Then, unsteady states of the loads were compared, taking into account the pitch error to im-

simulated and measured loads, constraining the data does not help. The ﬂapwise moment
still presents 50kNm of difference between measurements and simulation for wind speed
lower than 10m/s. But for this kind of comparison, the non efﬁciency of changing model
and constraining data was expected. In this respect, the best track to follow is to correct
the calibration of the measured data, which is, in this report, not taking into account the
variation in time and with the temperature because of a lack of information.
Actually, the impact of the model choice or the fact of constraining can be seen looking
at the variations of the loads or the associated PSD. Once again, whereas all methods of
simulation agree on it, the measurements do not give the same natural frequencies for the
mode shapes. Furthermore, the energy contribution for the modes is not the same according
to the source of simulation. The KSEC model predicts more energy from mode shapes than
the Mann model, and even more when it is unconstrained. The difference increases with the
frequency, and modes with high frequency are almost non-existing according to the Mann
model. It is connected to the fact that the wind ﬁeld generated from the KSEC model has
more components in high frequencies, so it makes sense to have more excitation for the
modes at these frequencies for both ﬂapwise and edgewise.
In addition to these observations, the DEL moments have been calculated for all methods
of simulations. For ﬂapwise, both unconstrained methods give the highest values, and con-
strained method conjectures the smallest. The difference is quite important between the
methods (almost 50%), that inﬂuence a lot. The edgewise DEL is almost not impacted by the
method choice, even if unconstrained KSEC model is slightly higher.
Finally, the method of turbulence generation should be chosen wisely because the ﬂapwise
moment DEL is really impacted by this choice, and constrained method is closer to the
measurements from this point of view. Looking only at edgewise damage, the methods
does not really matter. However, at high frequency the results are not so clear and the
KSEC method, that the constraining generation uses, do not give enough information for
high frequencies. Some mode shapes can be missed, and in addition, the simulated natural
frequencies do not match the measured ones.

7.2
Recommendations for further research
Firstly it is important to notice that the simulations have been carried out on a relatively
small rotor, that is why no signiﬁcant difference has been noticed between level of con-
straining. It is one of the reasons among others that the Spinner Lidar were not used to
investigate the interest of constraining with so detailed data. The same study should be
performed on a bigger turbine, like a 10MW turbine for instance, to examine other levels of
constraining. However, for this study, the mesh reﬁnement should also be increased, so it
has to be realized that time computing will be important.
Only the west sector has been studied for validation and comparison of the simulations. The
study can be expanded to other wind direction, taking into account that now, the line from
the met mast to the wind turbine is not in the same direction than the wind. Moreover, the
neighborhood affects the wind turbulence so it can bring other differences comparing the
measured wind at the met mast and the actual wind at the wind turbine. In this case, the
Spinner Lidar would be more appropriated for simulation of the wind.
Nevertheless, for smaller turbines, the comparison of loads could also continue being im-
proved without constraining, but by investigating other parameters of the turbulence. For
example, the effect of the length scale or scaling on the unconstrained method were not
explored, and could be done. It has been seen in previous studies [12], that the difference
between simulations and measurements changes only by modifying meteorological length
scale and shear distortion parameter, so it could be enough to follow this track for small
turbines.
Lastly, measurements and simulation do not give the same natural frequencies for the loads,
whereas they should agree. Further research should be carried out in this trail, to conﬁrm
or not the validation of the measurements and/or the model. The measurements remain
uncertain, but it could also come from the simulations, that all agree, whatever is the method
of turbulence generation.

A Spinner Lidar installation
Different methods of constraining have been presented using the data provided by the met
mast. Recently a Spinner Lidar has been installed, in order to provide high accuracy in
the measurements of the wind and the turbulence around the rotor. Using these data can
improve the generation of the turbulence box with better constraints and in the meantime,
improve the simulation of the loads by the code HAWC2 because it offers new possibilities
for constraining the turbulence box, as it is more detailed.
A.1
Introduction to the Spinner Lidar
From the 23/04/2018 to the 30/04/2018, a Spinner Lidar was moutained on the nacelle of
the V52 wind turbine of the Risø campus.
The measurements have started since the 09/05/2018 and been calibrated.
The Spinner Lidar is a research Doppler wind Lidar. The principle is based on the Doppler
effect and the continuous wave of the laser system. It permits to scan the inﬂow of a turbine.
With a such installation, the wind speed can be measured with a high accuracy, close to
the blades, so it includes the turbulence occurred by the wake of the rotation of the blades.
Using only the measurements from the devices on the met mast doesn’t include the effect of
the rotor. Moreover, the wind speed is measured at a lot of positions, not only at a certain
height. and nevertheless, the measurements are done immediately on the nacelle, contrary
on the met mast. So the turbulence measured is the actual one that the turbine is subjected.
The Spinner Lidar generates a geometry of a rose, as illustrated in the ﬁgure A.1. One rose
is generated every two seconds, and it corresponds to 400 positions for each rose.
The system provides two ﬁles, called spin and spec files. The ﬁrst ﬁle contains informa-
tion about the scans, such as the time, the radial velocity, azimuthal position of the lidar, the
two components of the unit vector in the x-y plane, focus distance, or the lidar inclination.
The second ﬁle contains all 256-point noise radial velocity spectra within 1-min. These ﬁles
are processed to be user-friendly and the data are available in a SQL database.

A.4.1
By constraining with the mean wind speed
One possibility is to constrain every time step with the effective mean wind speed. It does
not differ a lot from unconstrained box that uses almost only the mean wind speed, but
here the mean wind speed changes at each time step, and is way more accurate than before
because of the accuracy of the device and its location directly on the nacelle.
A.4.2
By constraining with the radial velocity for each position of the grid
An other possibility is to constrain every positions of the pattern generated by the Spinner
Lidar. There is one average value for each position over 1 min.
The expectations are that if the shear of the wind speed is high, constraining over the space
seems more adapted, whereas if the turbulence intensity is high, constraining in the time
should give better results.

B Inﬂuence of the meshing
reﬁnement
Before the generation of the turbulence, while selecting the parameters for the constraining
part, the size and the number of points of the mesh have to be declared.
Limited by the time computing and computing memory, some choices have to be done.
During the entire process (reading the data base, generation of he turbulence box, simula-
tion with HAWC2, post process) the two steps the more time-consuming are the genera-
tion of the turbulence box and the simulation itself. The simulations are done with DTU
cluster, so many simulations (around 100) can be run in parallel, and one takes maximum
20min. However, the generation of the turbulence box requires the installation of several
tools (PyConTurb or wetb) that prevents the use of a cluster in a simple way. Even if there
is the possibility, in the code, of controlling the ratio between the time-computing and the
memory of the computer used, it is impossible to generate in a short amount of time a large
number of turbulence boxes. In this project, around 1000 time-series are considered for ini-
tialing the values of the turbulence boxes, and there are four different methods of generation
(depending on the degree of constraining). It is large amount of turbulence boxes to gen-
erate, so the reﬁnement of the mesh has to be chosen cleverly to avoid loosing time on the
computing for nothing.
In this appendix, the results from a turbulence box with a mesh of 5x5 and of 16x16 are
compared, in order to decide the reﬁnement of the mesh necessary.
