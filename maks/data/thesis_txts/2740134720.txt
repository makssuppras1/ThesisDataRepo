Abstract
NNE works primary on big pharmacy projects, where the complicity and precision are very high. Following NN’s
trajectory, the company strives towards digitization of data in all fields.
Commissioning is one of the final stages of a project, and at the same time is a very important one. Poor quality of
work will result in potential problems in operation, which can lead to stop of production, and substantial revenue
losses. Delays in commissioning activities can affect the time schedule, which will result in the project not being
delivered on time, and therefore delays in production.
The C&Q Management in NNE has established methods for commissioning, which are used successfully on projects.
After closer examination however, they seem to be inefficient in utilizing existing data, and in documenting activities
in an structured manner.
The purpose of this project, is to create an alternative method for commissioning, utilizing the functionality of BIM
software, used in PPV. This report describes the methodology of creating a digital tool for the C&Q department, and
analyzes the potential benefits of using it.
The principle can be broken down to three activities:
• Adding parameters to the 3D model in the design software.
• Representing activities digitally via workflows, task, and checklists.
• Coordination of activities, digitization of documentation, and data transfer from the managing software.
Two alternative solutions were developed partially, to demonstrate the functionality of the prototypes. Both solutions
were tested successfully in an experimental environment, and were approved by the C&Q Lead manager.
Solution A was favored, because of its flexibility, and organization of data. The prototype will be developed to a final
version, that can be implemented on future NNE projects.

This project proposes an alternative approach to the current commissioning methods used by NNE. If approved, this
solution can be implemented in M3 and other future projects. The Master thesis consist of a report, describing how
to construct a digital and the benefits of using it, and a prototype of the tool, including it’s two variations.
1.1
Guide to the report
The first chapter introduces the PPV project, which is taken as a case study. A short description of the project with
numbers for reference gives an idea about the size and complexity of it.
The second chapter identifies and analyses the problem, sets objectives, and describes the methods used for acquiring
information needed to solve the problem.

methodology. It details their specific applications and functionalities and helps understanding these tools and the
underlying principles of the proposed solution.
Chapter four defines the scope of work of C&Q department in NNE and describes the current method for commis-
sioning with examples. This establishes a reference framework for the solution’s objectives.

Chapter six is divided in two parts: the first describes with specifics how to implement the principles from previous
chapters to the specific case, and the second part presents the prototype, including the two solutions developed over
the semester.
Chapter seven analyses the results and compares the new solutions to each other and the current method used.
Limitations, possible future development, and use of the tool are discussed here.
Chapter eight provides a summary of the report, assessing whether the initial objectives have been met.

Purification Plant V (five), or for short PPV, is a project for Novo Nordisk (NN), with Novo Nordisk Engineering

The factory will purify the medicine OG217, also known as Yagovy. Initially, the facility was designed to produce

documentation. The complexity of the project requires expertise from many different stakeholders, which work must
be coordinate, according to their scope.

2.2
Commissioning
Commissioning is the process of verification, that a system is designed, manufactured, installed, tested, operated,
and maintained as intended. During those processes, manuals, certificates, models, reports, test results, approvals,
inspection plans, and time schedules are used for documentation.
The concept was first developed during the early industrial revolution, and consisted of basic verification of machin-
ery and equipment. With time and development, the processes evolved and the procedures became more structured.
In the late 20th Century, standards and guidelines were developed across the globe. Today, commissioning plays a
crucial role in the handover of a project.
Commissioning follows the same principles in general, but methods can vary, depending on the geographical loca-
tion, or functionality. American Society of Heating, Refrigerating and Air-Conditioning Engineers (ASHRAE) has
develop guidelines and standards for procedures (ASHRAE Guideline 0-2019, ANSI/ASHRAE/IES Standard 202-
2018), while the European Union (and Denmark) have developed their own (DS 62337, DS CEN TS 18036 2024,
DS3090). Relevant for this project will be the standard DS/EN 62337 Commissioning of electrical, instrumentation
and control systems in the process industry - Specific phases and milestones. It sets the framework for commissioning
of a production plant, according to Danish law. An illustration of the activities can be seen in Figure 2.
Figure 2: Commissioning phases - DS62337
A detailed description of all the procedures can be found in the Annexes of the standard.
The phases in commissioning can be summarized as the following:
• Completion of erection - Mechanical checks and test are performed. The contractor performs an inspection of

materials, components, and systems, where he verifies that mechanical installation is complete in accordance
with the general design requirements.
• Pecommissioning (Mechanical Completion) - various tests and checks are performed by the contractor’s and
owner’s representative on site, to verify the Mechanical Completion. Every part/section/facility of the plant
must be inspected individually before handing the project for commissioning. The contractor is responsible for
documentation.
• Commissioning - After Mechanical Completion, the commissioning phase begins. Procedures (activities) should
follow the order: the system is warmed up or cooled down, initial running using test media are performed,
operation is adjusted, the system is fed in, operation is demonstrated as stable, system is loaded up to maximum
capacity, final adjustments are made.
• Performance test and acceptance of the plant - when the initial operation test of the plant is completed, a perfor-
mance test must be carried out to demonstrate that the operates and performs according to the project specifi-
cations, standards, and manufacturer’s manuals. The contractor is responsible for planning of the test and the
procedure, test criteria and measurement instruments. Operating data from the test must be provided to the
owner to the contractor. Testing of samples should be carried out by the owner.
When all the phases are completed, the project can be handed over to the owner.
2.3
State of the art
Internet of Things (IoT)
The concept was first introduced by British scientist Kevin Ashton, who was working on the technology of sending
wireless signals to microchips in various physical devices (point 6. in Bibliography).
IoT refers to a network of devices, vehicles, appliances, and other physical objects that are embedded with sensors,
software, and network connectivity, allowing them to collect and share data (point 15. in Bibliography).
These devices communicate and interact with each other, allowing to be monitored and controlled remotely. The data
gathered from censors (thermometers, pressure sensors, sensors measuring flow rate, motion detectors, etc.) is sent
14
==PAGE:17==
2
INTRODUCTION
to the cloud via Wi-Fi, Bluethooth, or other methods, where it is assessed by a software. From there, action can be
performed automatically (for example if a room is heated above 26◦C the sensor will send an alert to the server, and
ventilation will turn automatically in that room), or manually (using the same example - when the user receives the
alert, he can choose to turn the ventilation on, or take no action). Devices can communicate with each other, or/and
with the cloud, depending on the application. Data can be shared with more than one party. Very often, an AI is
incorporated into the system.
Time and technology increases the use and applications of IoT, as they have proven beneficial in their implementation.
Most notable areas they are applied to are:
• Smart homes - sensors monitoring energy usage, quality of air, temperature, etc., allows for optimal control of
house appliances and increase the overall efficiency of them. It also allows disabled people to actively control
their environment without the need for help of others (point 16. in Bibliography).
• Health care - remote monitoring of patients and medical devices and equipment can save lives, save time, and
prevent further medical complications.
• Production and manufacturing - processes in facilities can be monitored remotely in real time. Using AI (with
historical and input data), IoT can predict the expected behaviour of the system, saving time and resources, and
making maintenance more efficient.
• Smart cities - waste management and city traffics can be improved by implementing the IoT, as the amount
of data that need to be assessed is enormous, and analyzing possible solution and choosing the best requires
sophisticated computing.
Digital Twins
The concept was first used by Dr. Michael Grieves in 2003 for manufacturing (point 5. in Bibliography). He intro-
duced the idea of a physical object being represented by a virtual model, which contains all the relevant information.
He also explained the method for information flow between the physical object and the virtual model.
The concept developes further the idea of IoT with more specific applications. After input parameters are defined,
real-time data is collected via IoT devices and uploaded into a model in the cloud. Various software, machine learn-
ing, and AI are used to analyze it and simulate scenarios to predict future outcomes.

The concept can be applied to single component, where for example a part of a machinery is represented virtually, or
a set of components grouped together, for example an entire machine. It can also be applied to entire systems, like a
production line composed of multiple components, or even whole processes like manufacturing.
Because of its multi-functionality, the technology is used widely in different sectors. Most notable are:
• Manufacturing - increases efficiency of processes and improves quality of products by finding the best solutions.
It improves maintenance and reduces the overall cost (point 7. in Bibliography).
• Health care - simulates possible outcomes and improves patients treatment.
• Energy - it finds the most optimal solution for operation, enhancing processes and improving efficiency of
power plants, or other systems, dependant on energy.
Overall, it improves performance by analysing data and simulating outcomes, saves money by improving processes,
and enhances decision making.
The information comprising the Digital Twin directly correlates to the complexity of the physical model. The more
complex models require high computing capabilities.

Goal and Scope
3.1
Problem analyses
The production of medicine requires high efficiency and precision of processes to ensure the quality of the product.
A small failure, or a mistake, can cause serious health hazards for the users and huge economical losses for the
manufacturer. NN’s control strategy (in production phase) is to implement real-time monitoring of production using
sensors, indicators, historical data, registered and analyzed on an IT infrastructure. The goal is to digitize the data
and processes, so AI can help improve production and automation.
The Construction phase for PPV is well documented digitally, and NN has set high requirements regarding the
level of detail of the digital models. All contractors must reach at least an LOD 300 from the American Institute of
Architects according to the ICT contract. They also must have a digital model of the system/s under their scope. All
equipment (mechanical, electrical, instrumental, etc.) must be designed in 3D.
All digital models are reviewed (typically in the same software), and if the information is correct, they are linked to
a common model. If issues are found in relation to other models - actions are assigned to the concerned person/con-
tractor. Dalux is used as a platform for management and documentation.
Commissioning is an important phase, because all the mechanical and IT systems must undergo it, before the project
is delivered to the client. Most activities related to it are registered on two CDE (common data environment) plat-
forms in a semi-digital way. No relation to digital models is established and no transfer of data is utilized. The use
of digital tools is very limited.
3.2
Problem definition
NN’s trajectory is towards digitization and has set high requirements for documentation, and for the level of detail
(information) of the 3D models. Currently the C&Q department does not make use of that existing information for
their Commissioning activities. Furthermore, it registers and manages its activities in an unstructured way, making
it difficult to coordinate with different disciplines and manage work.

GOAL AND SCOPE
The goal of this project is to create a digital tool (or a set of tools) for the C&Q department, using the software that
are already used on PPV.
3.3
Objectives
The objectives of this project are:
• Digitize information and documentation of activities, using well known software.
• To visualize the systems, that have to be commissioned.
• Relate 3D objects from models to tasks and checklists.
• Make use of existing meta data.
• Minimize the risk for error in documentation.
• Structure and sort/filter data.
3.4
Limitations
This project will only investigate solutions derived from the official software used in NNE projects, and most specif-
ically – on PPV and M3. Other alternative BIM software can possibly achieve the same results using the same princi-
ples, but will not be considered, as they will not be of interest for NNE.
Due to time limitations, only a prototype of the solution will be developed, to demonstrate that the principles work,
and the tool can be used as intended. To compare quantitative its efficiency with the current method used will not be
possible, as it is only a prototype, so a qualitative assessment from the C&Q manager will determine its value.
3.5
Methodology
Papers, standards, and articles on the topic of commissioning were investigated to gain an understanding of the
processes related to it.

AutoCAD Plant 3D
AutoCAD Plant 3D is a software, designed for modelling process plant factories. It is widely used in industries like

The software used in PPV for designing pipes, valves, instrumentation, machinery is AutoCAD Plant 3D from Au-

Users and user groups
All stakeholders in the project, are registered as a Users in Dalux. User groups consist of one, or more users, and
are used to manage permissions, access, and roles in the project. Example: Construction Management, Architects,
Building Utilities, Contractor A, Subcontractor A1, etc.

Together with User groups, they are used to manage the flow of information in the project. Work packages can
represent different activities (for example Supervision, Construction Management, Health and Safety, Core drilling,

the order of execution of an activity. Every step is associated with a Role. Individual Users or User Groups (or both)
can be added to the Roles, and permissions can be added/edited. Workflows can be reorganized in various ways.

Current principle of information flow
Before activities on site can take place, the system (subject to commissioning) must be designed. In mechanical
engineering, objects, units, and systems are represented by a technical drawing, called a Piping and Instrumenta-
tion Diagram (P&ID). Symbols, classifications, specifications, and standards can can vary, depending on the litera-
ture. Some important international standards are: ISO Standards - ISO 14617, ISO 10628-1- 2014, and ISA Standards -
ANSI/ISA-5.1-2009, ANSI/ISA-S5.5-1985, ISA (1983) S5.3, ANSI/ISA 5.7 . Figure 18 (from article Principles of P&ID
Development, Engineering journal) shows the stages of development of the design concept. The Block flow diagram
(BFD) describes the main idea with primary streams and unit operations. The Process flow diagram (PFD) develops
further the modules from BFD, adding tanks, pumps and instrumentation. The Piping and instrumentation diagram
(P&ID) adds further details and specifications, and finalizes the design of the system.
Figure 18: Principles of P and ID Development
When the detailed design is finalized, a final P&ID from the contractor is submitted to NNE and uploaded to Share-
point. Figure 19 shows the information flow and the data transfer principles, in terms of commissioning.

Utilizing BIM software
Even though the primary function of a BIM software is the design and management of construction, its principles
of work can be applied to other activities, like Commissioning. Taking advantage of already existing settings, work-
flows, structure, and information flow, opens the door to numerous possible implementations.
BIM software allows for quick and efficient sorting and editing of multiple objects at once. The main idea is to add
information to the model, which later can be updated almost instantly. The data then can be extracted and used for
commissioning activities with a few clicks of a mouse.
6.1
Adding data to the models
The amount of information in a BIM model very much depends on the of input from designers and the level details
they provide. The properties a BIM object (Element) has, are defined by its parameters. Schedules, checklist, and
tables can be created, and elements can be selected/grouped/sorted/filtered by their values, directly from those
lists, without having to locate each element in the model from a working view. Adding parameter to an object will
create a meta data related to this object. This will later be used to automatically fill in fields with values, and/or to
be grouped/filtered by a value.
Figure 21: Hierarchi of BIM objects

looked closer at.
There are five types of parameters classifications in Revit Autodesk:
• System parameters are built in the software and cannot be edited or delated. They can be tagged and scheduled.
• Project parameters can be assigned to all categories of elements in Revit.
• Family parameters created and edited inside the Family and apply to all Types from the same Family.
• Shared parameters are Family parameters, which can be shared among other Families and Projects, and also can

• Global parameters are used to control multiple parameters at once.

6.2
Assigning tasks to site activities
Managing information related to the construction phase is different than adding data to the models. Dalux (and other
BIM software) has developed several modules for managing activities on site.
The principle is that every activity can be represented in a digital way by a workflow. A workflow specifies the flow
of information (and action), and the users’ role in that workflow. Approvals and tasks are associated with workflows,

==PAGE:42==

and represent the content of the information. An example could be a request for information. Instead of writing an
email, specifying what information is needed, a task (HSE Punch from the HSE workflow) can be created by the
person requesting information, and the person receiving the task has to provide the information and send back the
task for approval (Figure 13).
Multiple activities can also be grouped into one workflow - for example Core Drilling in PPV (Figure 4), where each
step of the flow represents an independent activity. In this flow, both digital (creation of the 3D object in Revit and
task in Dalux, various verification) and physical activities are registered in the task as it switches Role from the flow.
Information about the task is automatically and instantly generated. Data, such as: Date of Creation, Created by,
Date of Modification, Modified by, Work Package, Workflow, Type of approval/task, Role, Responsible is always
displayed, but other relevant data can be generated, depending on the template settings.
Other activities can be represented by multiple workflows - for example filling out a checklist. Tasks (from different
workflows) can be associated with the same checklist (one workflow), and completing it therefore requires several
other activities (Figure 10).
This principle of representing activities digitally and generating data in the process, is not limited to construction. It
can be applied on other aspects of the project using the same methodology. Besides construction, it is currently used
by administration for permits, requests, contracts, etc., and by the economical department for documenting expenses,
approvals of budget, quantity lists, etc.

7.1
Methodology
The main idea is to utilize the already existing meta data from different BIM objects when creating tasks and checklists
in Dalux, and to reorganize the workflows for commissioning activities to increase efficiency in Management.
A diagram, illustrating the new workflow, can be seen in Figure 24. The process starts with adding data to the design
software. Then, commissioning activities will be represented digitally by assigning tasks and checklists to each one.
When creating the tasks and checklists, filters in Dalux can be created using one or more of the parameters set, in
order to isolate all the objects of interest. Specific tasks related to one or more points of the checklist can be created
and assigned to relevant user groups. There will be a possibility to attach documents, test results, and manuals to
them. The tasks can then be grouped and/or filtered by a location, deadline, work package, workflow, type, BIM
property, or other parametric value relevant for the specific purpose.

AutoCAD Plant 3D setup

Using one of the methods mentioned in Chapter 6.1, a number of parameters relevant to the PCA and CA are created

Some of them will be relevant only on a system level, while others will be relevant for the individual element as well.
Values of those parameters will be called out in Dalux and be displayed in checklists and task as meta data.
Starting with drawing the system with all its elements according to the P&ID, the first parameter should be P&ID .
As it is known from the beginning, the value can be defined before drawing. It should be implemented on a Model

defined value. It will be used for documentation and reference in commissioning activities in checklists and tasks.
A major component of a P&ID is one or more pipes grouped in a Line number, which sometimes require activities
performed on them separately. They are only relative to pipes and not valves, or other instrumentation, and therefore
the Line Number parameter should be applied on a Family level. The different set of elements from a P&ID should

8
Discussion
This project is not driven by the necessity to replace existing methods due to their ineffectiveness. Instead, it operates
on the premise that the tool will support the C&Q department in their activities, save time, and enhance overall
efficiency.
The two solutions were tested with a prototype. Both demonstrated that they function as intended, and can produce
the same results as the current method. They were developed partially and used in a test environment and not a real
project by different stakeholders. Because of that, there is no empiric data to compare, if the proposed new method
will save time, and improve management and documentation in general.
8.1
Advantages and disadvantages
The advantages of using the new method (Solution A or Solution B), compared to the current method for commis-
sioning, is the generating and utilization of data:
• From the design software - parametric values can be generated and used for input data for different activities
(tasks, checklists, schedules), or for visualizing objects and systems.
• From the managing software - structured data from tasks and checklist (and 3D objects related to them) can
provide a better overview of activities, which can improve planning and management.
The new method is still in a development stage and has not been used in a project. Implementing it will mean, that
training of personnel will be required. Like most new ideas, some will dislike it and would prefer using the current
methods, which they know very well.
The main concept in Solution A is to represent digitally all activities and combine them in one checklist, which
will contain all information related to the commissioning target in one place. Interdependent phases will be man-
aged directly from the checklist. There will be no need for referring to other activities and having multiple tasks
and checklists reviewed before filling in data. Information, regarding activities, will be updated and accessible for
whomever needs it. The checklist will be filled out by the C&Q Management and tasks will be assigned individually
to contractors. All activities will be registered and coordinated more efficiently.
60
==PAGE:63==
8
DISCUSSION
Having all information about a commissioning target in one place may be relevant for the final verification phase, but
for others, it may overwhelm. This is the case for activities, which are independent - for example pressure testing and
power testing of the system. Each phase has a manager, responsible for it, who coordinates and documents activities
on site. The results of the tests are irrelevant to each other.
In Solution B, consecutive activities, belonging the same phase, are grouped together in two separate flows - a HPC
(for PCA), and a pressure testing and flushing checklist (for CA). The two flows are independent, so the checklists
will contain information, relevant only to the phase.
All information related to the commissioning target will be stored in two separate checklist, which cannot be linked
together. For the final verification, both checklist must be reviewed.
A HPC is a one-way workflow, which cannot have branches, allowing only one option to choose, when forwarding
the checklist. The hold points of the checklist can only be edited one at a time, and have to be marked ready and
forwarded, before the next one can be edited. In theory, the completion of one activity is required for the start of the
next, but in real life those activities overlap some times.
The major differences between Solution A and Solution B are:
• Solution A contains all information about PCA and CA in one place, compared to Solution B, where the two
flows are independent and registered in two places.
• Solution A is more flexible and detailed in assigning activities, than Solution B.
8.2
Limitations
The digital tools were developed for products from Autodesk and Dalux. Other software on the marked may well
achieve the same results, and some could possibly do it more efficiently. Those alternatives have not been assessed,
as it would not be realistic to implement such a huge change as switching to another software. NNE has already
trained their employees to use them, and is in general satisfied with their service.
Only a prototype, demonstrating the functionality, was developed for this project. Tasks, and checklist have not been
fully designed.

DISCUSSION
This project is limited to commissioning of process systems. Commissioning of HVAC and electrical systems is not
explored.
Workflows (approvals, tasks, checklist, hold point checklist) from the Field module can relate to one object from the
Location module. In reality, a commissioning target consists of many 3D objects. A full list of them is not generated
automatically in the task (or checklist), and information about the objects is not available. A comparison (of quantity)
with other documents, or a cross-check reference is not possible. All objects, with the exception of the one related to
the task/checklist, will transfer no data with the task/checklist.
Checklist and hold point checklists cannot contain other checklists in them. Only tasks can be associated with, or
created in, a point from a checklist, establishing a link between the two. If checklists could also be associated to
points, one master checklist could serve as a platform for registration of the individual phases, with links between
them. This will give more flexibility in managing activities on site.
Checklist, and/or hold point checklists, cannot be linked together like tasks. A practical implementation could be to
link a two independent flows, both of which are required to complete another activity - like in Solution B.
Adding additional parameters in the (already detailed) 3D model imposes challenges on storage capacity and com-
puting power. Furthermore, if commissioning becomes more digital, additional data will be generated and worked
with. With the progress of the project, the level of detail increases, more activities are being registered, and the PPV
project in Dalux is experiencing lag some times. Even at this stage, without the implementations, the limits are being
tested.

C&Q management
I presented to Jorge and Dylan three times during the course of this project - one presentation about the concept,
and two presentations of the prototypes. The idea to combine all activities in one place was well welcomed, as they
are often struggling with scattered information, traceability, and linking interdependent activities together. After
introducing the prototype to them, both agreed, that the new method and the tool have potential applications, and
could immediately see benefits from using them. To name a few major ones - visualisation of systems, improvement

CONCLUSION
9
Conclusion
The purpose of this project, was to create a digital tool, or a set of tools, that can supplement the C&Q department in
their activities, and improve planning and management. The solution had to be based on software, already used in
PPV.
Research on the topic of commissioning was made by reading articles, standards, and guidelines.
Interviews were conducted with specialists from different fields, to gain input information, discuss functionality and
design, and evaluate decisions.
A prototype, with two alternatives, was developed in a testing environment to present the solutions.
Both solutions demonstrated successfully their functionality, and achieved the objectives set for the project in the
beginning.
The progress and success of the tool was assessed qualitatively, by the C&Q managers.
Both solutions were of interest, but Solution A was preferable.
The project will be developed further in the future.
