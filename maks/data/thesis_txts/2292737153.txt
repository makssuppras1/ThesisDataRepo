Abstract 
The yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae is well-studied eukaryotic organism and widely used as a 
cell factory for bioproduction of various products. In this context, vanillin is an important flavor 
compound in the food- and pharmaceutical industry, which is conventionally produced via 
chemical synthesis. Recently, a de novo heterologous biosynthesis pathway for vanillin-β-
glucoside production has been reconstructed in identical manners in two S. cerevisiae strains, 
CEN.PK and S288c, as a biosustainable alternative to the chemical synthesis, with dramatic 
differences in yields. The aim of this study was to investigate the differences in the genetic 
backgrounds between the two strains, to find the optimal genetic background for improved 
vanillin-β-glucoside production. This was done by crossing the two strains and 22 crosses were 
created. From these strain crosses, 88 heterozygote strains were obtained, and out of those 7 
strains were found to have improved vanillin-β-glucoside productions. In additional 
characterizations, 5 of these strains were confirmed to have better production capacities and 
higher yield of vanillin-β-glycoside, compared to the reference strains in the same setup. This 
study demonstrates that different yeast strains may be combined into new strains that have 
optimized genetic backgrounds and improved abilities. This is of great importance in 
development of future cell factories.

Aim of study 
Vanillin is an important flavoring component in a vast variety of products within the 
pharmaceutical- and food industry, as a synthetic substitute to natural vanilla from Vanilla 
planifolia. In recent years, work has been put into produce vanillin in a microbial cell factory in a 
bio-sustainable matter, as an alternative to the chemical vanillin synthesis, and for this a 
heterologous biosynthesis vanillin-β-glucoside pathway has been introduced in the yeast 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae [1].  
The aim of this master thesis is investigate the genetic background in two Saccharomyces 
cerevisiae yeast strains CEN.PK and S288c [2], to find the optimal genetic background for 
production of vanillin-β-glycoside. The two stains differ dramatic in yield as S288c showed a 10- 
fold higher vanillin-β-glucoside production compared to CEN.PK. In this study, strain S288c and 
CEN.PK were sexual crossed to obtain a considerably high number of heterozygote strains with 
new abilities. In a high-throughput growth setup all the new strains should be characterized as 
to find strains with improved ability for producing VG and a high cellular productivity. 
Furthermore, a number of strains that showed better yields of VG should be furtherly 
characterized in a larger controlled growth setup for a more precise determination of 
metabolite yield and growth. Additionally, an engineering strategy concerning correction of a 
point mutation in the cyr1 gene of the CEN.PK strain [3] should be applied, by replacing the 
gene with the version from S288c. This can reveal if some of the superior abilities of S288c are 
related to functions of CYR1.

1 Background 
1.1 Microbial cell factories 
Microorganisms have been used for centuries by mankind for a variety of purposes and as far 
back as 3000 BC Egyptians have been shown to have used yeast for the fermentation of 
alcoholic beverages and bread making [4]. Throughout modern history thousands of 
microorganisms have benefitted to the development of our modern society, not only as 
components in food and beverages, but more importantly through microbial fermentations. 
These fermentations have initiated production of various compounds, chemicals or enzymes for 
numerous applications. Since the industrialization, from the end of the 19th century, microbial 
fermentations have expanded towards large-scale productions. An example of this is the first 
industrial large-scale production of citric acid by filamentous fungi in the early 1920s. But 
perhaps even more important is the famous discovery of Penicillin, produced by the fungus 
Penicillin chrysogenum, by Alexander Fleming in 1928 and its mass-production in the 1940s 
during World War II [5]. These examples, along with many more, initiated the use of 
microorganisms for production of commercially relevant products and started a new discipline 
within microbiology - biochemical engineering - and laid down the foundation of the modern 
industrial biotechnology as it is seen today [6], [7]. In the industry, microorganisms have a long 
range of applications as for production of a variety of chemicals, proteins, fuels, small-molecule 
drugs, therapeutic proteins and for detoxification of pollutants [8]. A diverse number of 
organisms are used within industrial biotechnology including bacteria such as Escherichia coli, 
Bacillus subtilis, yeast fungi such as Saccharomyces cerevisiae, Pichia pastoris, filamentous fungi 
such as Aspergillus niger, Trichoderma reesei and Fumisarium venetatum and animal- or 
mammalian cells such as Drosophila melanogaster and Chinese hamster ovary (CHO) cells. 
Microorganisms are intensively studied, selected and optimized over time, so that their 
individual characteristics and metabolism fits the production of a desired product in native or 
heterologous organisms [9]. In the field of modern industrial biotechnology the yeast 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae plays a very important role as a versatile, robust and molecular easily 
manipulative cell factory for production of a large variety of compounds and for microbiological 
research purposes.   
1.2 Improving microbial cell factories 
Ever since production of compounds from microorganisms became industrialized, attempts 
were made to optimize and improve the organisms and the yield of their valued products, and 
to attempt production of new compounds in native or heterologous production organisms. 
Strain improvements are, thus, a natural consequence of optimization strategies for 
microorganisms and screening for desired improvements. Examples of strain improvement

strategies vary from high yield of biomass and compound, improved affinity towards a certain 
substrate to adaptation to specific growth conditions. The desire for improvements of 
microorganisms, like higher growth rates or higher productivity, has driven the engineering of 
certain industrial microorganisms to be intensively exploited in relevant environments for 
various applications [8]. The classical method of doing strain optimization is by random 
mutagenesis. Random mutagenesis proceeds by exposing a microorganism to UV radiation or 
chemical substances that facilitate DNA nucleotide damages, thereby creating mutations in the 
genome of the organism. In order to create a mutated strain with better abilities, several rounds 
of random mutagenesis procedures, followed by screening, needs to be performed for a high 
numbers of strains, for one strain with superior abilities may be obtained (Figure 1). Obvious 
limitations using this method are the laborious and time-intensive procedures involved, but also 
the fact that most randomly induced mutations are neutral towards an improved phenotype. 
Furthermore, with increasing time and mutagenesis induction, the complexity of the mutational 
pool increases and this can instead have a collectively negative effect. This can result in ‘sick’ 
strains that perform poorly in an industrial production environment [8]. Despite these problems, 
random mutagenesis has been widely used for many decades and contributed to the rapid 
improvement of many organisms important to the pharmaceutical- and biotechnological 
industry.

The introduction of recombinant DNA technology in the 1970s enabled more rational 
approaches in strain optimization and turned the focus towards heterologous gene cloning and 
transformation [10]. This meant a new way of improving cell factories with the use of genetic 
information, to insert heterologous genes from other organisms, and in simple ways obtain 
more accurate results when inserting genetic changes. As for today, random mutagenesis 
strategies are still used in microorganisms where cellular compounds are made up by multiple 
gene expressions, which manifest into complex phenotypes, and require strategies less 
dependent on rational gene designed strategies. The methods that are used allow desired ‘traits’ 
to be selected for in the microorganism, in controlled stages of cellular growth. This can let 
strains adapt to a changed environment and push the evolution and genetic shuffling in a 
desired direction [11]. In the end, a screening designed to identify superior strains from a 
number of parameters can be used for selection.                                
1.3 Metabolic engineering as a tool for cell factory optimization 
One major breakthrough in biochemical engineering came with the introduction of recombinant 
DNA technology. With the first integrated recombinant DNA molecule created in 1972, the 
biochemical- and pharmaceutical industry experienced a rapid development in strain 
engineering up through the 1970s, and this became the beginning of heterologous 
microbiological production [7], [10]. In the following decade biotech companies experimented 
with the new technology, and several novel products, made by recombinant DNA technologies, 
were introduced on the market. The term metabolic engineering was first proposed in two 
publications in 1991 [12], [13] and the concept can simply be formulated as “the improvement 
of cellular activities by manipulations of enzymatic, transport and regulatory functions of the cell 
with the use of recombinant DNA technology”[13]. The advantage of metabolic engineering is 
the possibility to construct microorganisms with a desired phenotype through the use of 
precisely targeted genetic engineering tools. Since the early 1990s, metabolic engineering has 
gradually been growing in application and today it is one of the most important strategies for 
the development and optimization of industrial microorganisms. It combines a rational 
approach with information from genomics, proteomics or metabolomics data, in order to design 
knockouts, gene insertions or genetic mutations at specific sites in the genome. A rational 
approach for optimization ensures perturbation or deletion at a precise targeted genomic site, 
without affecting other genes in the organism. Designing a proper optimization strategy for a 
preferred organism is the key step before metabolic engineering can be applied. First, genetic 
information on the organism is required in order to locate genes that cause unwanted 
phenotypes, and then design and formulate the optimization strategy for that cause. Once a 
strategy has been formulated the work on improving the organism can start in the laboratory 
using a relevant technique suitable for a particular case. The introduced genetic changes need

improvements. These steps can be continuously repeated in a cycle, known as the iterative cycle,

Several different techniques are used within metabolic engineering and all are dependent on

methods exist for a large variety of microorganism for many different purposes and the 
development is accelerating in advancement of techniques and tools for whole systems-level 
analysis, metabolic pathways construction and higher computer analytical power.  
1.4 Saccharomyces cerevisiae as a heterologous cell factory 
The yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae has been used by mankind since ancient time and has 
contributed to winemaking, baking and brewing, and is certainly the oldest cell factory used for 
industrial purposes. Today, S. cerevisiae is widely used for production of ethanol, proteins, bulk 
chemicals and enzymes in the pharmaceutical- and biotechnological industry, and is one of the 
most well-established eukaryotic model organisms in molecular cell biology [16].

S. cerevisiae is a round or ovoid formed fungus with a diameter of 5-10 μm and in nature it is 
mostly found on ripe fruits e.g. grapes. Genetically, it belongs to the Ascomycota phylum 
determined as fungi that form sexual spores structured in sacs called ‘ascus’. It is a non-motile 
organism that reproduces by spore formation. S. cerevisiae can grow and survive as two cell 
forms: haploid and diploid. The haploid cell reproduction is asexual and proceeds by a process 
called budding, in which a new cell is forming and growing on the ‘mother’ cell membrane until 
it matures and burst away. The process of budding is characteristic for yeast and each cell can 
repeat the process several times and both diploid and haploid cells are capable of dividing by 
budding. The sexual cell cycle starts when two haploid cells of opposite mating types are in close 
proximity allowing cell-to-cell communication via releases of different pheromones. This 
enables the cells to initiate and synchronize their individual cycles and to enter the meiotic 
division event, creating diploid cells of non-mating character. The formed diploid cell can 
propagate under normal growth conditions and form budding through vegetative growth, 
however, if limited growth nutrients only are available, the diploid cell will initiate meiosis and 
sporulation that gives rise to a tetrad sac of four haploid cells [17] (Figure 3).

S. cerevisiae is one of the most well-studied eukaryotic organisms and is for that matter widely 
used for research and industrially relevant purposes, due to its robustness and tolerance 
towards environmentally harsh conditions [18]. The high tolerance is applicable in industrial 
fermentations where harsh conditions are present, and together with its status as a GRAS 
organism (Generally Regarded as Safe) it shows application in the biotechnological industry as a

food- and biopharmaceutical production organism. A great variety of different compounds is 
produced in S. cerevisiae due to its robustness and versatility. In biological research, it has an 
important role and gives valuable insight into essential biological processes such as cell cycle 
regulation [19], cell aging [20] and protein transport systems [21]. Importantly, it has been 
applied as a model for studying human diseases, such as the effect of anticancer agents on 
cancer [22], diabetes [23] and other important syndromes [16]. Lastly, it was the first eukaryotic 
organism whose complete genome was sequenced and several databases contain detailed 
information on genomics, transcriptomics, proteomics and metabolomics data which prove 
valuable for doing genetic engineering [18], [24]. Several molecular techniques are available for 
S. cerevisiae for improvement of gene expression, flux optimization, higher protein secretion or 
complete genome-scale metabolic models for computational modeling of the entire 
metabolome. Despite the high power of metabolic engineering tools, several techniques based 
on random genetic approaches are still applied for genetic background optimization work in 
yeast. Used together with high-throughput screening this offers a cheap, easy and fast way of 
investigating genetic interactions and differences for a relatively large population of strains  [25], 
[26]. 
S. cerevisiae strains used in laboratories, involve strains specially developed for research 
matters and which are easily handled in the laboratory by common genetic techniques and 
molecular tools. An important yeast strain within this is S288c. Since its development in 1986 
[27], it has been one of the most used laboratory strain, mainly because of its minimal set of 
nutritional requirements for growth and a strictly controlled life cycle. Furthermore, as it was 
the first yeast strain and eukaryotic organism to be genome sequenced, and it established the 
use of metabolic engineering studies on yeast cell factories. Today, it is  important as a 
reference strain in sequencing studies for yeast modelling [28].  A second equally important S. 
cerevisiae strain is the family of CEN.PK strains, which was developed in 1990’s by crossing of 
different laboratory yeast strains, for obtaining strains with improved abilities for use within 
industrial environments [29]. The CEN.PK strain shows easy application in molecular genetics 
techniques, and has since become an important industrial production strain for studies in 
systems biology, and benefitted to production of pentose sugars [30], ethanol production [31] 
and fungal polyketides [32]. These two strains has been developed for similar purposes, but 
they differ remarkably in many polymorphic sites within their genomes [33]. E.g. a single point 
mutation in CEN.PK, in the cyr1 gene, results in altered function of the corresponding adenylate 
cyclase enzyme, which leads to changed regulation during cellular growth [3]. These genetic 
differences also results in large phenotypic differences, and heterologous production is likely to 
give significantly different yields [29]. From previous studies, CEN.PK is known to have a 
generally high productivity (protein turnover rate) and biomass yield [34], and as a consequence

1.5 Creation of heterozygote S. cerevisiae strains through tetrad-spore analysis 
As previously described, when two haploid yeast cells of opposite mating type fuse together via 
sexual cycle, they form a new diploid cell and undergo meiosis. This newly formed cell will then 
undergo meiosis again and sporulation and form tetrads of four haploid spores, when exposed 
to conditions of limited nutrients. The created spores are held together by a surrounding cell 
wall called an ‘ascus sac’ which can be opened by treating it with an enzyme that causes the sac 
to be digested and making the spores available. Using a microscope and specialized needle 
(micromanipulator), the spores can be separated and plated out as individual cells [37]. 
According to the Law of Mendelian inheritance for allelic crossover, the created spores are 
arranged in four different genotypes: two parental- and two recombinant genotypes. This 
method proves applicable for easy creation of new ‘hybrid’ strains from two S. cerevisiae yeast 
cells of different genetic backgrounds, and a large number diverse hybrid cells can be obtained, 
with possibly few having improved characteristics. It is a relatively simple method that gives rise 
to a high number of new strains with varying characteristics. Moreover, it can easily be applied 
in connection with a high-throughput screening procedure that can be applied for an entire 
population of hybrid strains to search for strains with the desired phenotypes. The method is 
based on a random microbiological approach where each strain is created from chromosomal 
crosses. No knowledge is given about the genetic background of the new strains and possibly 
unwanted genetic combinations can occur. An application of this method could be a crossing 
between yeast strains CEN.PK and S288c. The formed hybrids could possibly have preferred 
characteristics e.g. a high productivity within a highly stable and characterized genetic 
background. 
1.6 Metabolic engineering in S. cerevisiae correcting point-mutation in 
adenylate cyclase in CEN.PK  
In S. cerevisiae, glucose transport is tightly regulated and the cell is able to change its 
metabolism according to the growth conditions and changing environments. When cells are 
growing, glucose uptake rises and intracellular glucose levels increase. This stimulate a glucose 
signaling response activating a large number of enzymes that collectively reprograms the cell by 
metabolic, proliferative and transcriptional regulation, to change the state of the cell to manage 
the increase in chemical energy [38] (Figure 4). In S. cerevisiae, as in other eukaryotes, cAMP is 
an important regulator of intracellular pathways acting as a second messenger regulator for 
==PAGE:15==

Master thesis, Jesper Emil Nielsen

signal transduction. In response to increased glucose levels it has key role in signaling of cellular 
growth and activation of protein kinase A (PKA).  
cAMP is synthesized from ATP by the enzyme adenylate cyclase, encoded by the gene CYR1. A 
point-mutation in the 6081 bp cyr1 gene cluster is found in the S. cerevisiae strain CEN.PK giving 
rise to a mutation seen as a K1876M substitution in adenylate cyclase. The effect of this 
mutation is, that CEN.PK strains under normal growth experiments have a greatly reduced 
glucose- and acidification activity, which is known to be induced through cAMP signaling [3]. The 
observed effect gives a delay in the transition from respiratory- to fermentative growth during 
exponential growth phase, as a consequence of a reduced agonist stimulation of cAMP. 
Importantly, the catalytic activity of CYR1 is unaffected by the mutation but the GTP- stimulated 
activity is strongly reduced [3]. The K1876M mutation may be important for optimizing the 
growth phase of CEN.PK strains and improve its abilities as a cell factory. Due to this, a 
metabolic engineering strategy was implemented in this thesis project, which tried to reconvert 
the cyr1 mutation in CEN.PK by introducing the CYR1 region from strain S288c.

1.6.1 USER cloning and gene expression in S. cerevisiae 
Uracil-specific excision reagent (USER) cloning is an efficient and easy way for DNA fragments of 
interest can be stably integrated into yeast chromosomes at specific sites. It can combine 
several implementation strategies based on different PCR, restriction enzyme recognition sites, 
expression vectors and selection markers, dependent on the overall genetic engineering 
strategy or chromosomal integration site you want to alter. The basics of USER cloning is 
relatively simple: The desired DNA fragment you want to clone is amplified from its native 
organism by PCR, using primers that are specifically designed with single-stranded nucleotide 
overhang sequences containing uracil bases. The amplified PCR fragment contains only blunt 
ends after elongation with the forward- and reverse primers, but by treating the fragment with 
a USER enzyme mix, single-stranded nucleotide overhang sequences are generated due to 
specific recognition of uracil bases on only one of the DNA strands. By using a vector with 
complementary overhang sequences to the PCR fragments, it is possible to assemble the 
fragment into the vector by hybridization and the produced vector can then be transformed 
into competent E. coli cells without the use of ligation (Figure 5). Transformed E. coli cells can 
be distinguished on selective media, due to marker genes in the vector, and furtherly 
transformed into yeast cells [39]. This study uses a specific gene integration strategy previously 
described at our center [40]. The cloning procedure includes a URA3 marker from the yeast 
Kluyveromyces lactis, which is widely used as a selection marker in yeast, for obtaining yeast 
colonies that have the complete USER cloning fragments inserted. Yeast cells with 
transformation fragments inserted, can be positive selected when grow on a media without 
uracil, promoting growth of only cells with URA3 marker. Afterwards a counter-selection can 
proceed for ‘pop-out’ of URA3 marker in the confirmed transformed yeast cells. This is done, 
when ura- cells are selected by growing them on media containing 5’-fluoroorotic acid (5-FOA), 
which converts into a toxic compound by URA3 gene, killing cells still possessing the marker.

1.7 Vanillin-β-glycoside production in yeast 
In recent years, focus has been turned on oil, gas and biological resources, as the exceeding 
global demand forces an increased production that in many perspectives is not good for the 
environment. Industrial processes are dependent on these resources, for chemical synthesis 
reactions, to convert them into industrial biotechnological relevant products such as plastic, 
chemicals and food ingredients. This has given an interest in renewable products produced by 
microorganisms, and organic compounds that previously were chemically produced can now be 
produced by microorganisms. In this bio-sustainable field for heterologous production of 
scientifically relevant products, the yeast S. cerevisiae plays an important role as a key versatile 
cell factory for production of new compounds e.g. vanillin. Vanillin is organic phenolic aldehyde 
which is naturally found in the vanilla orchid Vanilla planifolia, and has a long range of 
applications as extract in foods and as pharmaceuticals and cosmetics. It is derived from cured 
seed pods and forms the basic flavor component in natural vanilla. Vanillin can be synthetically 
synthesized in an industrial manner from lignin or petrochemicals, which is today by far the 
most commonly used way of production [42]. The global market for vanillin exceeds 16,000 tons 
annually with a value of $650 million (in which only 0.25% of this comes from cured seeds from 
natural vanilla orchids) [43].

Through a S. cerevisiae yeast cell platform, a heterologous de novo biosynthesis pathway for 
vanillin production was attractively proposed by Hansen et al. [1] and later optimized through 
genetic engineering- and yeast integration platform studies [43], [44] (Figure 6). The advantage 
of the alternative de novo biosynthesis of vanillin over synthetically produced vanillin comes 
with a great economic opportunity. Synthetically produced vanillin are sold for approximately 
$15 pr. kg, while vanillin produced by microbial bioconversion of plant metabolites are being 
sold for $700 pr. kg [1]. Optimization of vanillin production in yeast cell factories has gained 
increasing interest and recently, in a study by T. Strucko et al. [45], production of vanillin-β-
glycoside (VG) was performed in two strains of S. cerevisiae, CEN.PK and S288c. The study by 
Strucko et al.[45], showed that the laboratory derived strain S288c had a higher vanillin-β-
glycoside (VG) yield than the industrial yeast strain CEN.PK, and suggests, that the individual 
genetic background of the strains may play a role in the outcome of the VG production. All 
together, the two yeast strains have each individually different capability as VG production 
strains which interestingly could be combined in one single yeast strain.

2 Materials and Methods 
2.1 Hybrid strain construction 
Strains used in this study were constructed from the sexual crossing of VG strains TS165 (CEN.PK) 
and TS138 (S288c), constructed from the two strain backgrounds, S288c (isogenic isolate X2180-
1A) and CEN.PK113-11C described by Strucko [2]. The genotype, mating-type and source of 
strains used in this study are given in Table 1 and 2. Mating of S. cerevisiae yeast strains was 
done as follows; CEN.PK and S288c are both resuspended in 200 μl 0.9% NaCl, and then 10 μl  is 
poured on a YPD-plate and grown at 30°C for 6 hours. Formed yeast zygotes are visualized 
under microscope, if presence is confirmed, cells are dissolved in 150 μl 0.9% NaCl and 10 μl is 
poured in the top of a YPD-dissection plate an let run down the middle. Using a 
micromanipulator [37] zygotes were picked and placed separately outside the middle line of 
cells and the plate was incubated for 3-4 days at 30°C. Cells were then transferred to SPO media, 
in order to make formed zygotes sporulate, for 4-6 days at 30°C. Formed tetrads were treated 
with glusolase enzyme for digestion of ‘ascus’ sacs and then manually dissected under 
microscope using a micromanipulator. In total 88 hybrid strains were obtained which served as 
foundation for research done in this study (list of strains can be seen in appendix I).

2.2 Construction of cyr1 CEN.PK strains 
For construction of CYR1 in CEN.PK strains, two different background strains, CEN.PK113-5D and 
TS090, were used. The gene insertion was carried out using the vector pCfB258 (pX-4-LoxP-
SpHIS5), previously described [40]. Source of genotype of strains are giving in Table 1 and 2. 
Overview of the transformation strategy used in this study is given in Figure 7.  
2.2.1 DNA cloning procedures 
For gene insertion in CEN.PK, a PCR amplification of the entire CYR1 gene from strain S288c was 
performed using appropriate primers with specially designed USER tails for assemble with 
vector, and amplified in PCR reactions with PfuX7 polymerase [47]. Secondly, two fragments, of 
an up or down part of the URA3 marker, was amplified in two PCR reactions with designed USER 
tails for ligation with the CYR1 fragments (all primers used for gene targeting experiments are 
listed in in Table 3). 
Confirmed PCR fragments were cut out of the gel with a scalpel under UV light. The gel pieces 
containing the DNA fragments were purified using the GFX™ PCR DNA and Gel Band Purification 
kit (GE Healthcare, UK). 
In order to clone the DNA fragments into E. coli cells, the purified fragments were assembled in 
reactions using the uracil-specific excision reagent (USER™) enzyme mix and procedure, as 
previously described [41]. The two ligated fragments were each assembled into the prepared 
linearized vector pCFB258 specially designed with matching USER tails designed for the purpose 
(for exact USER cloning protocol see appendix III). 
2.2.2 E. coli transformation 
For transformation, competent E.coli DH5α cells were mixed in tubes containing LB ampicillin 
medium and one of the two USER fragments. The tubes were heat shocked to induce the cells 
to take up the plasmids (protocol can be seen in appendix III). The content of tubes was poured 
on LB ampicillin plates and grown overnight at 37°C to recover transformants. Single colony 
transformants were re-cultured in 5 mL liquid LB ampicillin medium. 
For recovery and purification of plasmids the cultured E.coli cells were prepared using the 
GenElute™ plasmid miniprep kit (Sigma-Aldrich). 
Confirmation of correct vector assembly was done by restriction analysis using appropriate 
restriction enzymes, cutting at defined sites in the vector. The restriction enzyme strategy was 
designed in CLC Main Workbench (Qiagen) and the chosen enzymes were mixed with the 
purified plasmids and incubated at 37°C for 2 hours. The restriction analysis pattern was 
confirmed by gel electrophoresis (full restriction protocol, see appendix III).

2.2.3 Yeast transformation 
Yeast strains were inoculated in 3 mL YPD O/N at 30°C, and thereby re-cultured in 30 mL YPD for 
4 hours at 30°C. The culture was then centrifuged and re-suspended with water in several steps 
to obtain a clear pellet of cells. The concentrated cell-pellet was added to an Eppendorf tube 
and on top was added the purified plasmids- or gel fragments, and lastly a transformation mix 
composed of PEG 4500 50% w/v, 1M of lithium acetate and ssDNA, was added. The tube was 
mixed vigorously and incubated at 42°C for 40 min. to make yeast cells take up the DNA (full 
protocol, see appendix III). The tube was once more centrifuged and re-suspended with water 
and content was then poured out on a selective SC–ura plate and grown at 30°C for 3-4 days. 
Single colony transformants were counted and compared with a negative control. Purified single 
colonies were re-streaked on SC-ura and incubated at 30°C for 2-3 days, two times, to ensure 
correct transformed single colonies.

metabolites was used, and concentrations could then be determine by using the general 
formula:  Csample=(Areasample/AreaSTD)*CSTD  (for data see appendix II).

3.6 Shake flask cultivations 
3.6.1 Initial shake flasks experiment shows long lag phase for strains  
On the basis of the results from initial microtiter-plate growth experiments, 7 high VG yielding 
strains were selected for further growth analysis in a batch culture setup in shake flask 
cultivations. In the first of two shake flask experiments, 12 strains were chosen; 5 hybrid strains: 
2G, 4F, 5D, 10A and 11F. For reference the parental strains TS138 and TS165, were included. 
Hybrid strains were grown as duplicates, while parental strains were grown as single cultivations. 
Pre-inoculation was done in SC media and the cultivations were carried out for 70 hours.  
Wild-type yeast grown in batch culture displays a two state growth course, also seen in previous 
VG experiments [45], where growth is divided into two phases: An initial phase exponential 
growth phase were all the glucose is fermented (GF-phase), and a second growth phase where 
accumulated ethanol is respiro-fermented (EF-phase), also known as the Crabtree- effect [51]. 
In between the two phases is a diauxic shift at which the strains are adapting to the changed 
substrate growth conditions. On the basis of data from T. Strucko [45] growth samples were 
taken at exact time points at which the strains were expected to be in a certain growth phase, 
see Figure 12. Growth curves showed that all strains had a lag phase lasting for 10 hours and the 
exponential phase topped after 25 hours. The lengths of the GF phases were comparably the 
same for all strains, but the slope of the growth curves varied. At the end of the GF phase the 
growth of the cultures (OD600) had reached 8.0 for strain 4F and parental strains, and 5.0 and 7.0 
for strains 5D and 2G. Two strains, 11F and 10A, reached the highest growth after GF phase at 
8.5 and 8.9. Maximum growth was reached after approximately 58 hours for the three strains: 
5D, 10A and 11F. Strain 2G and 4F both had two growth peaks after approximately 58- and 70 
hours, with the latter being the maximum peak at 10.33 and 11.1 for 2G and 4F, respectively. 
Parental strains had a slope continuously increasing at 25 hours until 70 hours and reached an 
OD600 at 13.6 and 12.8 for strain TS138 and TS165, respectively. As the cultivation experiment

The yield of VG of the hybrid strains and their overall growth performances were evaluated to

4 Discussion 
Optimization of the de novo vanillin-β-glucoside pathway in Saccharomyces cerevisiae has 
previously been done by Strucko et al. in two different strain backgrounds CEN.PK and S288c 
[45]. In this study, the aim was to use the knowledge of the genetic differences between the 
two yeast VG strains to create new hybrid strains by sexual crossing with new growth abilities. 
In theory, some of the new strains would have a recombinant genetic background of both 
parent strains - both in terms of a high cellular productivity and a high yield of VG. During the 
strain crosses, random mutations could likely arise, which ideally, could lead to a new created 
strain with optimized abilities, such as superior productivity and VG yield. Additionally, a 
metabolic engineering strategy was attempted implemented in the CEN.PK parent strain, with 
the purpose of improving the growth, by changing a point-mutation in the CYR1 gene, that is 
not present in the S288c strain. The overall idea of this thesis was, by a random optimization 
approach, to obtain hybrid yeast strains, which showed optimized production capabilities and 
hopefully had improved yield of VG combined with a higher productivity, possibly performing 
better than the two parental strains. In addition, in order to investigate the big genetic 
differences between CEN.PK and S288c, the growth characteristics of VG parent strain CEN.PK 
could possibly be improved using a metabolic engineering strategy concerning the CYR1 gene. 
The first characterization of the hybrid strains showed, that about 8 % of the strains had a VG 
yield above that of the parents strains. Forty-five % of the strains actually had VG yields below 
the parental strains, while 23 % of the strains had a VG yield in between the parental strain yield 
levels (Figure 10). It is clear, that the sporulated diploid heterozygotes from the two VG strain 
references resulted in numerous variant segregations, and in the terms of VG yield, the spores 
of each tetrad could not be separated in distinctively dual genotype plateaus. This suggests, that 
the main genetic differences in VG yield between the parental strains are spread across large 
genomic areas, and must be due to large chromosomal differences in the respective genomes. 
This genetic difference is observed among the 20 middle VG yielding strains, which have a VG 
yield median below the high VG strains, but have a growth (OD600) median above the high VG 
yielding strains (Figure 10). The growth plateau of this group matches that of CEN.PK (TS165), 
and as CEN.PK normally has a higher productivity than S288c, this suggests, that most of the 
middle VG yielding strains share this genotype ability. As can be seen from the results obtained 
here, they were better VG producers than CEN.PK parent strain, laying in between the VG yields 
of the two parental strains. This shows a random distribution of properties that altogether 
confirms the large difference in tetrad segregation between the hybrid genomes. Genetic 
differences and relation to phenotypes between the two strains CEN.PK113-7D and S288c has 
previously been studied [29], [33] and have shown a 22,000 single nucleotide polymorphisms 
(SNPs) difference between these two strains, in which 13,000 SNPs are found within open 
==PAGE:39==
Optimizing the genetic background in yeast 
 
Master thesis, Jesper Emil Nielsen 
 
 38 
reading frames (ORFs) and 83 SNPs are directly absent in CEN.PK relative to S288c. Analysis of 
the SNP differences between the two strains showed, that most of the SNPs were located in 
genes involved in carboxylic acid, organic acid and carbohydrate metabolism, thus suggesting, 
that the two strains could differ in enzyme structures of centric cellular metabolisms. Lastly, 
numerous unstable repeats in genes involved in transcription regulation differed between the 
two strains and may be important in transcriptional control of larger regulatory networks 
governed by transcription factors. 
In an attempt to investigate the genotypic outcome of the hybrid crosses, and the connection to 
those having superior VG yield abilities, mating typing of the hybrid strains, was carried out. 
Mating type results from the experiment should be used to map each spore according to mating 
type and illustrate how they were distributed. Roughly 60% (52/88 strains) of the hybrid stains 
could be rightfully mating typed in the expected pattern of: 2 MATa to 2 MATα, while the rest 
failed to meet a conclusive result. Those spores failing to be MAT typed, where neither MATa 
nor MATα or gave false positives as being both MATa and MATα. Yeast zygotes undergoing 
meiotic segregation and sporulation can be aberrant from normal segregation, and gene 
conversion can switch the MAT type yielding e.g. 3 MATa; 1 MATα in about 1-1.8 % of tetrads 
segregations [52]. In that case, it cannot be ruled out that a tetrad has undergone a MAT 
switching during zygote formation, and this should be tested for the hybrids of this study. 
However, as the typing method used in this case has shown not to be completely reliable, a re-
typing of all the hybrids using a different method should be done to correctly confirm mating 
types of all created hybrid strains. In a different typing method, hybrid strains can be placed on 
a growth media in a determined distance from a streak of known MAT type (a and α) cells. 
Production of the right MAT factors, from the streak of cells, will cause hybrid strains of 
opposite mating type to change form into ‘shmoo’ conformation, which can be visualized in a 
microscope.       
The CEN.PK strain contains a point mutation in the CYR1 gene that may be of interest, when 
trying to increase the VG yield of the hybrid strains constructed in this study. From previous 
studies by other groups it is known, that the cyr1 gene in CEN.PK is different from yeast strain 
S288c, in terms of a single nucleotide substitution from adenine to thymine in bp position 5627 
encoding adenylate cyclase. Overall, this affects glucose-induced changes in the cell and delays 
the transition from gluconeogenic- to fermentative growth in response to glucose uptake levels 
[3].  
In this study, the S288c-CYR1 DNA fragment was obtained from gel purifications, and cloned 
together with a URA3 marker into a plasmid though E. coli transformations. Transformation into 
CEN.PK was performed using the plasmid, and pure DNA fragments, and single colonies on 
==PAGE:40==
Optimizing the genetic background in yeast 
 
Master thesis, Jesper Emil Nielsen 
 
 39 
selective plates were obtained. Due to lack of time, confirmation of rightly constructed yeast 
colonies was not done. Ideally, if positive yeast transformants had been grown, the effect of the 
implemented gene could have been investigated in simple shake flask- or batch cultivations 
(similar to the growth cultivations carried out for hybrid strains), and could have provided 
further insight into CEN.PK and its VG yield and cellular productivity. Strucko et al. [45] has 
shown that in batch cultivations, the higher VG yields in S288c strains compared to CEN.PK 
strains, is mainly created after the diauxic shift in the ER-phase. This points to the fermentative 
growth phase being more efficient in S288c, even though it has been reported that wild-type 
S288c strains has a growth deficiency during the EF-phase, that results in a significantly lower 
biomass yields compared to CEN.PK [33]. Still, in this study, CEN.PK could possibly be optimized 
by replacing the CYR1 gene with that from S288c, and possibly reduce the transition shift 
through the diauxic phase. As little is known about the precise effect of the naturally mutated 
cyr1 gene acting on components of the cAMP signaling pathway in CEN.PK strains [38], 
predicted effects, if any, presented here may not be observed or detected in simple cultivation 
experiments. 
The main focus on this thesis was put on investigating the growth characteristics, productivity 
and VG yield of the 7 hybrid strains, which, from the initial growth experiments, showed 
superior abilities in VG yields over the parental strains. The strains could therefore likely had 
gained optimized genetic backgrounds from the tetrad crosses. Five of the 7 high VG yielding 
strains were analyzed in shake flask cultivations, leading to a second cultivation experiment for 
3 strains that was furtherly carried out in an optimized setup. The cultivations showed that the 
hybrid strains had an unusual long lag phase of about 10 hours. In the first growth cultivation, 
pre-inoculation was done in SC medium, while SM medium was used for pre-inoculations in the 
second experiment, and this gave different nutritional contents and provided very different 
environments for the strains. This, however, did not seem to affect the length of the lag phase 
or growth of the cultures, and the lag phase was approximately the same in both experiments. 
The long lag phase is consistent to what is known from other S. cerevisiae strains [53] and 
relates to cells adapting their cellular metabolism to a changed environment. As the VG 
pathway recruits carbon from the shikimate pathway, a part of the aromatic amino acid 
biosynthesis, which is tightly regulated by 3-deoxy-D-arabinoheptulosonate 7-phosphate (DAHP) 
enzymes and initial pools of shikimic acid concentrations, a certain adaptation of cells to a new 
metabolism can therefore be speculated. Examining the growth data from both cultivation 
experiments shows, that all strains have similar GF-phases at approximately 15 hours (minus the 
10 hours lag phase), including both parental strains. The specific growth rates and the optical 
density measurements for the hybrid strains varied significantly, but for the second experiment 
growth rates and optical density measurements were fairly identical and they were likewise 
==PAGE:41==
Optimizing the genetic background in yeast 
 
Master thesis, Jesper Emil Nielsen 
 
 40 
more closely distributed. The growth cultivations were pilot experiments for the newly created 
hybrid strains, and behavior of their performances was unknown. The second shake flask 
cultivation is likely to show the more true growth for the hybrid strains, because of more strong 
statistical evidence and uniformly results. The unusual low growth rate of strain TS165 in the 
second cultivation (Figure 12) sticks out but is, however, not significant and may be explained by 
slightly variation within the strain. Thus, it is contradicting to previous studies, which have 
shown that CEN.PK has a high protein synthesis capability, as seen by a high biomass yield and 
maximal growth rate in batch and, therefore, should have a potentially high productivity from 
the start of the growth experiment [34], [54]. TS165 may be investigated in additional growth 
experiments before deviations from known existing growth behaviors can be suggested. The 
end stationary growth phase was not reached for any of the strains in the second cultivation, as 
after 86 hours OD600 was still increasing for all strains, and no stable growth plateau was 
observed. From the previous work on VG yield optimization by Strucko [45], VG strains were 
expected to reach stationary phase after approximately 50 hours, as no more carbon substrate 
should be available. There is no simple explanation to the divergent behavior of the strains in 
this experiment, but a possible explanation could likely be, that the aeration during the 
experiments was insufficient and that the strains did not take fully advantage of their 
respiratory energy ideal metabolism. 
Measuring the intermediate metabolites and final product, VG, from the shake flask cultivations, 
could reveal if any of the hybrid strains had a higher yield of VG compared to the parental 
strains. The HPLC data from the two experiments was comparably similar, and despite the 
missing data for the parental strains in the first experiment, the quantified concentration yields 
was fairly statistical evident and likely showed the true metabolites levels in this experiment 
setup. Of the 5 different hybrid strains tested, strain 4F was the best VG producer with a 
significant higher VG yield compared to the parental strains in the second experiment. The 
parental strains had similar VG levels although the intermediate metabolites levels varied 
between the two strains. The similar levels are not expected from the previous study [45], and 
suggest that this experiment setup was not optimized towards their cellular metabolisms as to 
identify the previously confirmed differences between these two strains.  
The study of Strucko et al. also showed that the higher VG yield of S288c strain came with a 
higher accumulated level of PAC, suggesting a higher carbon influx into the VG pathway. For the 
strains examined here, the final accumulated level of PAC was significantly low at the end of the 
cultivation in fact levels of PAC and PAL were actually decreasing from the end of the GF phase 
and through the ER phase. For 4F in particular, PAC showed the highest significant drop in yields 
from 326.2±66.5 mg/L to 153.9±41.7 mg/L. The high VG yields of this strain suggests an 
==PAGE:42==
Optimizing the genetic background in yeast 
 
Master thesis, Jesper Emil Nielsen 
 
 41 
improved VG pathway with a more efficient turnover rate of intermediate metabolites, and a 
higher carbon flux towards the end product, indicating that the VG pathway is more efficient 
during the ethanol respiration. Possible explanations to this could be that the enzyme in 
conversion of PAC in the pathway has a higher specific activity. Another possibility is the 
availability of co-factors in the VG pathway for the conversion of PAC to VG, which previously 
have been shown to be a limiting step. As SAM enzyme levels has not been measured before 
and copy number of genes in the pathway is not known, in either of the two parental strains, 
the genetic background of the hybrid strains may differ largely and one strain could possibly 
have an improved pathway with no previously observed characteristics. From the previous work 
on VG production by Strucko et al., parental strains were expected to increase in metabolites 
yields during the growth like VG increased as carbon is being channeled through the pathway. 
There is no simple explanation to why the parental strains behave differently in this experiment 
setup and the metabolites decreased during the ethanol respiration phase, in that sense results, 
presented here, must be treated with caution and more growth experiments needs to be 
carried out in order to give conclusive answers about the hybrid strains abilities.  
It cannot be ruled out that that any of the other non-measured intermediate metabolites in the 
pathway, e.g. VAC, correspondingly accumulate in higher levels, but this is unlikely since the 
pathway should has an even higher carbon flux to counterbalance for the high VG yield.  
In conclusion, this study obtained 7 new hybrid yeast strains that had better capacities for VG 
production than the parental strains. A number of these strains were successfully grown and 
characterized in additional growth setups which provided important information on growth 
performances. Furthermore, metabolite measurements showed that the new hybrid strains had 
higher VG yields compared to the parental strains, in this experiment. Therefore, crossing of 
yeast strains with different genetic backgrounds is an effective tool in creating superior VG 
production strains. Applied together with other genetic engineering tools e.g. genome 
sequencing, it can give better understanding of the genetic background and the cellular 
processes involved in VG production of S. cerevisiae.

5 Future perspectives 
In general, much work still needs to be put in characterization of the superiority of the hybrid 
strains both in terms of growth and yields. First, they should be examined in larger controlled 
growth setups, both as batch- and continuous cultures, and with several measurements of 
metabolites throughout the cultivations. This could give more information about growth 
characteristics, such as dilution rates and specific yields on biomass, which could determine if 
the observed characteristics are true, compared to the parent strains. Whole-genome 
sequencing should be done on some of 7 hybrid strains, and compared to the parent strains, to 
investigate the arrangement of genetic backgrounds, and to identify varying regions or SNPs 
that could explain the improved characteristics. Lastly, the CYR1 transformed CEN.PK strains 
should be confirmed and later examined in growth experiments, to determine if any effect 
could be observed. Hereafter, the original CYR1 gene should be transformed back into S288 
strain, to confirm the reliability of the effects. 
In a larger perspective, metabolic data could be obtained for the hybrid strains, for construction 
of computational metabolic models, with use of new relevant -omics data. Future work should 
focus on the genetic differences between the hybrid strains and the parental strains, and try to 
find the changes in genotypic- and phenotypic level, that causes the difference in cellular 
performances. As a computational tool, metabolic flux analysis could be used to model the 
carbon flux through the pathway and identify intermediate carbon accumulation and determine 
limiting enzymatic steps.

9.3 E. coli transformation protocol 
1. 100 μl DH5α cells are taken from -80°C stock and thaw on ice in the lab. 
2. For each transformation 50 µl of cells are added to an Eppendorf tube. 
3. Add 10 µl of USER cloning reaction to the Eppendorf tubes 
4. Keep on ice for 2 min. 
5. Incubate at 42°C for 50 sec. 
6. Put on ice for 5 min. 
7. Streak out the content of the tubes on LB ampicillin plates using a Drigalskispartel. 
8. Incubate plates O/N at 37°C.

9.4 Yeast transformation protocol 
The day before: 
  
Inoculate the strain in 3 mL YPD, o/n, 30°C. 
  
On the day itself: 
1. Inoculate 1 ml of your O/N culture into 30 ml YPD. 
2. Incubate the culture at 30°C on a shaker at 150 rpm for 4 hours 
3. Harvest the cells by centrifugation at 3000 g for 5 min. 
4. Pour off the medium and wash the cells in 25 ml of sterile water. Pellet the cells again by 
centrifugation as described above.  
5. Re-suspend the cells in 1 ml of sterile water and transfer them into a sterile 1.5 ml 
Eppendorf tube. 
6. Pellet cells by centrifugation (5000 rpm) for 30 s. Discard the supernatant. Add water to 
a final volume of 1.0 ml and vortex vigorously to re-suspend the cells.  
7. Repeat step 6.
