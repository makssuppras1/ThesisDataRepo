Abstract
The present report describes the development of an educational Playware system aimed at promoting
learning and physical movement for middle school students. Following a thorough examination of the recent
reform of Danish public schools and the main educational theories applied today, a short survey of some of
the robotic systems and games used as educational tools and the relating research is presented. The results of
survey are then used to assess the preliminary requirements of the system. The methodologies of Interaction
Design and Rapid Prototyping were used simultaneously during the project and resulted in the creation of
four functional prototypes, all of which were tested with either the students and/or the teacher at Vedbæk
Folkeskole (public school north of Copenhagen). Analysis of the ﬁeld test results of each prototype was
used to assess the requirements for the next prototype. After discussion of the cumulated results, the fourth
and ﬁnal prototype is assessed according to the requirements set up in the beginning, showing that most of
these are fulﬁlled with the exception of two low priority requirements. It is concluded that the system can be
used in an educational setting in this speciﬁc class given that a few minor improvements are made to the
reliability of the Human-Machine interaction and the creation of a medium, to be used by the teacher, to
alter the questions.

The educational system has changed a lot since going to school became obligatory for all in the 19th

Denmark) and the right to corporal punishment was abolished in 1967[7]. In our current era of globalization
and constant technological development, schools have slowly become places where children and teenagers
are no longer simply sent to be taught but also to slowly mature. Schools have gone from being about
exclusion, plainly a mechanism that sorted the “good” students from the “bad”, to being about inclusion, a

To recapitulate what was mentioned earlier, the main goals of this new reform were to ensure that the

The Royal Society for the encouragement of Arts, Manufactures and Commerce (RSA) is a British

with an intellectually challenging problem, are autonomy (the desire to be self-directed), mastery (the urge
to become better) and purpose (the desire for the things that you do to have some kind of higher purpose)[9].

De National Test
De Nationale Test are a tool for the running evaluation of primary school students. During their time in
primary school the students will undergo ten mandatory tests in seven subjects3, they are tested both on their

gender (Male/Female) or hair color (Brown, Blond, Black, etc.). When a variable can only assume exactly
two values, such as either "True" or "False", the variable is described as being binary or dichotomous. These
variables are for example used in the mathematical tests, in which there is only one correct answer and all
the other possibilities are false for any one question. The use of dichotomous variables makes it relatively
easy to analyse the results of a test and thus yield a student’s proﬁciency score.
The idea is then that the resulting scores can be used by the teacher to construct a more personalized
curriculum for each student. However this does however not necessarily mean that the students are going
to learn, the next step is to ﬁnd the manner in which the student learns most effectively, meaning choosing

2.2.2
Learning Styles and Feedback
The "learning styles" theory refers to the concept that a given learner’s characteristics inﬂuences which
mode of instruction or study is most effective for him/her[13][14]. The idea of learning styles is however
controversial, the number of different styles ranges from four to over 70 [13] and a study, designed to
determine whether the "learning styles" practice was supported by any scientiﬁc evidence concluding that as
of yet there exist no adequate empirical evidence that supports fully incorporating learning style differentiated
teaching into general educational practice[14]. However an investigation of the link between teacher and
student learning styles and the student’s achievement and attitude[15] found that a large divergence between
the teaching styles and the student’s learning styles results in lower student gain and less positive attitude
toward the subject. The idea about learners having certain traits (tied to sensory modalities) that allow
them to learn more effectively by actively employing strategies linked that their particular modality should
therefore not be completly dismissed. Furthermore the second study mentionned earlier ([14]) also concludes
that whilst no adequate evidence was found, due to humans’ innate ability to learn, it is especially important
to ensure that as many learning options as possible are available to the learner. The "modality styles"
theory suggests that there exists four learning styles relating to different sensory modalities; Visual/Verbal,
Visual/Non-verbal, Tactile/Kinaesthetic and Auditory/Verbal. These four types are respectively aimed at
learners who prefer written language, visual information (pictures/illustration), the hands-on approach or
spoken language[13]. Another important factor when considering education is the manner in which feedback
is given. The most utilized method is recast[16], this is an implicit corrective feedback method, in which the
content is repeated correctly. Whilst this method ensures that the interaction ﬂow is uninterrupted and it
avoids embarrassing the student, it does not enable the student to ponder about the correct answer and is
even likely to go unnoticed by the student. Feedback to any given exercise should be given so that while the
student is made aware of him/her having made a mistake, permits them to ﬁgure out the correct answer by
themselves.
2.2.3
Constructionism
Constructionism is a learning theory proposed by Seymour Papert [17], based on the idea that student-
centered discovery learning is the most effective learning method. In addition this theory also holds that
the fact of making physical, tangible objects in the real world is an important part of the learning process.
Constructionism is actually a combination of the knowledge theory of constructivism, proposed by Jean
Piaget and the theory of experiential learning. In layman’s term, the constructionism learning theory would
be "learning-by-doing".
2.2.4
Flow and the Zone of Proximal Development
"Flow" or "Optimal Experience" is a concept used in psychology developed by Mihaly Csikszentmihalyi
in 1990 [18] and it is used to describe a person’s mental state. Csikszentmihalyi deﬁnes it as:
”[...] ﬂow - the state in which people are so involved in an activity that nothing else seems to
matter; the experience itself is so enjoyable that people will do it even at great cost, for the sheer
sake of doing it.”
It is thus a subjective experience, that refers to a person’s state when feeling an intense and focused
concentration on a given task. As such when taken in an educational context, it is the desired mental of the
student, the moment in which the difﬁculty of the task is synchronized with the student’s proﬁciency level.

Education with Robotics
Especially in the realm of education, the lack of student motivation is a serious problem, a survey
conducted for the Danish newspaper Politiken in 2010 [21], showed that 46% of students in 8th and 9th grade
were already tired of school. Research has shown that the use of robots can greatly promote motivation and
can even be used as direct tool to direct the learning process or be used to lead to learning[22][23][24][25].
2.3.1
Playware
The term Playware was ﬁrst used by Henrik Hautop Lund and Carsten Jessen in the article Playware-
intelligent technology for children’s play[26], in which it was deﬁned as:
"[the] use of technology to create the kind of leisure activities we normally label play, i.e.
intelligent hardware and software that aims at producing play and playful experiences among
users and of which e.g. computer games are a sub-genre.”
In addition to "creating play" another goal is to use the state of playfulness to motivate. And while most
people will immediately think about children whenever the word play is mentioned, Playware is not solely
meant for children. As Gregory Bateson stated in 1972: "play is life itself"[27] and should therefore not

2.3.3
Research on Educational Robotics
As part of the background research for this project I have also examined some of the research done in the
realm of educational games and robotics.
A trend quickly appeared in the reviewed research, the large majority of the studies approached the
problem of education and motivation either by using social interactions with a "partner", be it computer-
or robot-based, or relying on the theory of learning-by-doing/building. A few others used exploration or
game-based learning as a medium to induce learning. Table 2.3 shows the approach investigated in the
different studies and the used medium and table 2.4 shows the educational goals or the subject intended to
be taught in each article.
Social Interaction
The experiments using the method of interacting socially with a robot were mainly done with humanoid
robots, such as NAO or iRobiQ. The nature of the Human-Robot Interaction (HRI) differed from study to
study: the robot was used as tutor/facilitator, guiding the user through a process, for instance showing how
to build a LEGO Minstorms NXT robot[30] or as a learning partner, with which the user could discuss
questions or had to teach the robot. However all of these studies showed that the use of a robot with which the
user can interact increases the user’s motivation to learn. A Japanese study[31] showed that in order to help
with actual learning performance of young children (aged 4-8), it is necessary for the robot companion to be
able to learn the correct answers through interaction with the user or already know the answers beforehand.
However the "all-knowing" robot does not motivate the user as well as the learning robot or even as well as a
"stupid" robot, incapable of learning. Instead of focusing on the robot’s proﬁciency level and it’s ability to
learn, a Korean study[22] compared three different learning methods that can be applied at home: the use
of an ordinary book with audio tape, web-based instructions and lastly with a home robot(iRobiQ). They
found that the robot aided e-learning produced the best performance in both concentration levels, learning
interest and academic achievement. Both of these studies were aimed at teaching English(second language)
to a "normal" user. The research focusing on teaching vital social skills to children affected by learning
disabilities such as ADHD or even autism[32][33][23][34] showed that in order to achieve motivation and
actual learning, the user must be able to developed an emotional connection to the robot, and this was best
achieved with a robot, or a computer game[32], that would learn at the same speed at the user. Furthermore
a study[34] promoted the importance of learning by exploration, saying that all learning that occurs in the
early ages of life is accomplished in this manner and thus there should be no reason that this could not be
achieved later in life, especially in the case of disabled/special needs children.

Learning-by-doing
The studies that focused on the constructionist approach to learning mainly used either the LEGO
Mindstorms NXT kit or chose to have the children build a robot "from scratch". In the case of the later one
study[35] even had the older users (18+) manufacture their own circuit board for their robot. The goal of that
project was to give children/teenagers, aged 13 to 18+, an insight into electrical design and engineering and
furthermore had the prerequisite that the children had prior knowledge about robotics. In contrast another
study [36] was aimed towards teaching about physics and mechanics. In the cases were the NXT kit was
used, in addition to teaching the children about mechanics or electrical components (sensors, motors, etc...),
learning to program the robot was also important. The students had to accomplish a given set of tasks by
building the right robot for the task and then program it. An analysis of the educational value of the "Robot
Soccer" competition [37], in which teams of children create mobile robots that can play football, concluded
that the use of robotics and programming in education shows actual promise, while they did/could not make
any decisive conclusions about its educational value in relation to school curriculum, they found that it
clearly taught the children about the importance of teamwork and tolerance when working in groups and
furthermore they observed that it was highly motivating for both genders. The positive effects of robotics on
children’s motivation is supported by the following other studies: [38], [23], [24] and [39].
The learning-by-doing approach does however not only entail the building of mobile robots and the
programming thereof, Seymour Papert[17] arguments for the beneﬁcial effects of sole programming on both
motivation and learning ability. The I-Blocks are modular units, which each their own sound or piece of
music, by attaching them to each other in different ways, the user can create his own music. A study[43]
found that the use of modular robotics and the act of building one’s own system promotes creativity.
Exploration And Game-Based Learning
Two of the articles[52][53] focused on the inherent nature of humans to explore. Two different systems
for smart-phone or PDA were created that through either having the users scan QR-codes or according to
their GPS-location, provided them with relevant information about the object or area they were looking at.
No conclusions on this method’s ability to teach were made, but results from questionnaires and interviews
showed a deﬁnite interest from the users. The game-based learning stems from the idea that learning best
occurs when one is having fun.
A Brazilian study[46] used the LEGO NXT to teach children about the rules of the road, by giving them
an immersive experience, where learning to program the robot was not in focus but ensuring that the robot
followed the rules was. This resulted in the children learning about the rules of the road but also learning
about programming and robotics, nearly without realizing it.
The most common game-based learning medium is computer games, the games mateMatris or Homicide
are example of this. The ﬁrst game is aimed at teaching algebra and the second is an interdisciplinary game
that combines several school-related subject. In mateMatris[45] two teams play against each other, they
must ﬁrst travel through a maze before being presented with a screen much like in the game "Tetris". Using
algebra the teams can create blocks for the opposing team, in order to make them lose. Homicide[47] is as
the name suggests a game in which the goal is to solve a murder, through scientiﬁc experiments and analysis
of clues. In both cases the results of the studies showed that the integration of school-related subjects in a
game increased the players motivation towards learning and their willingness to learn. A problem however
arose during the course of the Homicide-game, the teacher was often unable or unwilling to "play along".
Their focus was, understandably, that the students performed the experiments correctly and the fact that the
results were not exactly as expected was not as important. This creates a rift in the students’ immersive
experience and resulted in a immediate decrease in motivation and left them at a loss about what to do

2.4
Interaction Design
The creation of a successful product entails not only ensuring that the product, technologically, works
perfectly but also making sure that the target user is able and willing to use it. More concretely this means
bringing usability into the design process. Interaction design is the process used when designing products
aimed towards supporting people in their daily life activities, be it at work, at home or in school. In order
to do so one must examine the User Experience (UX), meaning the user’s behaviour, attitude and emotion
about the product, it entails both the user’s overall impression and the opinion about the smaller details of
the system[54].
This particular design process involves four basic activities:
1. Needs and Requirements: identify the needs and establish the requirements for the user experience.
2. Developing design: develop alternative designs that meet the set requirements.
3. Building prototypes: in order to ensure that the system requirements are met, prototypes should be
created.
4. Evaluation: evaluate the designs and the prototypes throughout the process.
It is important to mention that the interaction design process is non-linear, meaning that each of the four
activities are intended to inform each other and most importantly to be repeated.
2.4.1
Requirements and Design Principles
A requirement is a statement about an intended product, which describes what the product should able to
do and/or how it should do it. In the ﬁeld of interaction design, several types of requirements are identiﬁed:
Functional: describes the manner in which the product works and what it should be able to do.
Data: determines the amount, accuracy, persistence and value of the required data.
Environmental/context of use: refers to the circumstances in which the product is expected to oper-
ate. When describing the environmental requirements, four main environment aspects are con-
sidered:Physical, Social, Organizational and Technical.
User characteristics: the key attributes associated with the intended user group.
Usability: the ease with which the user can use the system.
When designing interactive product, the usability is especially important and is divided into the following
goals:
Effectiveness: how good is the product at doing what it is supposed to do?
==PAGE:40==
22
CHAPTER 2. BACKGROUND RESEARCH
Efﬁciency: does it manage to support the user in carrying out the task?
Safety: it is able to ensure that the user avoids coming into undesired situations through errors?
Utility: to what extent does the products provide the right kind of functionality?
Learnability: is it easy to learn how to use the system?
Memorability: how easy is it to remember how to use the system, once it has been learnt?
After having established the system’s requirements, the design process can commence. In order to ensure
that the design maintains the right course, certain principles have been established. These are meant to orient
the designer’s thought process and ensure that all aspects of the design will be examined. The most common
principles are:
Visibility: the more visible the product’s functions the greater the likeliness that they will be used and that
the user will know what to do.
Feedback: this principal is related to visibility, it describes the action of sending/showing information about
what action has been undertaken to the user.
Constraint refers to restricting the actions the user can take at a given moment, this principle can thusly be
linked with the safety requirement.
Consistency: meaning that interfaces should be designed in such a way that similar elements should be
used to perform similar tasks.
Affordances: are deﬁned as the attribute of an object that allows people to know how to use it. Thus when
the affordances of an object are perceptually obvious it is easy to know how it is meant to be interacted
with.
Setting up a set of requirements for an imagined future product can be difﬁcult, a helpful tool for
facilitating this process is storytelling, since it is an easy and intuitive approach for people to communicate
and discuss ideas. I will present two types of narrative techniques commonly used in interaction design:
scenarios and use cases. The former is an "informal narrative description"[54], it describes activities or
tasks performed by a human user and does not explicitly describe the use of a certain software or product
in the achievement of a chosen task. The level of detail involved in a given scenario, depends on where
in the development process, it is being used. As such the usability, UX goals and context may be more
dominant when discussing requirements than when a speciﬁc functions is examined. While scenarios focus
on the user’s task, the use cases concentrate on the user-system interaction, while still being from the user’s
perspective. Once a design has been developed, it can be tested through the use a prototype.
2.4.2
Prototyping
Prototype is deﬁned in the Oxford Dictionary as:
"A ﬁrst or preliminary version of a device from which other forms are developed.9"
Prototypes are a support during the development of a product, it can for example be used to test the
technical feasibility of an idea, to clarify vague requirements or to evaluate and validate the prototype. When
developing a product for a certain user group, a prototype is valuable because it allows for direct user testing,
9Source: http : //www.oxforddictionaries.com/definition/english/prototype

23
this is a valuable aspect since it is often said that "users can’t tell you what they want, but when they see
something and get to use it, they soon know what they don’t want"[54]. As such even though it is a limited
representation of the design, it allows the user to interact with it and to explore its suitability and thus allows
the developer to get useful feedback. Prototypes are divided two different types: low- and high-ﬁdelity
prototypes.
A low-ﬁdelity prototype does not necessarily resemble the ﬁnal product, it can for example be made from
materials very different from the ones intended to be used later. Because of the simple nature of this type
prototype, it is easy and cheap to produce and thus easy to modify, it is however only used for exploration
and are thus not ever intended to be kept or integrated in the ﬁnal product. Storyboards and index cards
are two examples of low-ﬁdelity prototypes. A storyboard is a series of sketches that show how a user
might interact with the system. Index cards can be used to simulate a working system, where an actual user
interacts with small pieces of cardboard.
A high-ﬁdelity prototype uses materials that are expected to be in the ﬁnal product and will much more
closely resemble the ﬁnished product. This often causes high-ﬁdelity prototypes to be more expensive and
much more difﬁcult to produce and therefore more difﬁcult to modify and any errors/bugs interrupts the
testing process. Furthermore other inherent problems with high-ﬁdelity prototypes include that reviewers
and testers tend to believe that the prototype is the actual ﬁnished product and will therefore often comment
on superﬁcial aspects or be unable to look past any ﬂaws or lacks the prototype may have. Where low-ﬁdelity
prototypes are appropriate for validating the content and overall structure, high-ﬁdelity prototypes are good
for testing ideas’ technical feasibility and performing ﬁnal user tests. Prototypes can be further categorized
as being evolutionary, experimental or build-and-replace prototypes. The ﬁrst term denotes prototypes that
are meant to "evolve" into a ﬁnal system, and thus are the ones allowing for user satisfaction and requirement
review, the experimental prototypes, as the name entails, are for exploring functions or components technical
feasibility. The last type, also known as "throw-away" prototypes, are used to explore the concept and the
product’s desired behaviour.
By their very deﬁnition, prototypes are representations of the ﬁnal product and certain compromises
must therefore be made. One of such compromises could be on the prototype’s functionality, commonly
functionality is divided into two categories: horizontal and vertical. The former aims at providing a wide
range of functions with little detail and the later few very detailed functions. Other compromises might
involve the number of users to be using the system simultaneously or a lack of structure in the code to be
implemented.
As mentioned earlier, interaction design is a non-linear process and an important aspect of the process is
iteration.
2.4.3
Evaluation
The evaluation process differs from prototype to prototype depending on which type has been produced.
In interaction design, evaluation is achieved through testing, of which there are three main approaches:
usability testing, ﬁeld studies and analytical testing. The goal of these approaches are however mainly to
evaluate the user-product interaction (that the user can use the product and that they like it) and are not
meant to be used to assess purely experimental prototypes. That is not saying that the test performed may
not highlight technological ﬂaws/problems.
Usability testing consists of testing the ease with which the user can use the product and their satisfaction.
Questionnaires and interviews are used to elicit the users’ opinions. The main characteristic of this approach
is that the test environment is thoroughly controlled by the evaluator and it relies on quantifying user’s
performance, through monitored tests. Field studies are however performed in the users’ natural environment
==PAGE:42==
24

and are aimed towards understanding how people naturally interact with the product. This kind of test
are helpful in establishing or revising product requirements and evaluating the overall product. Analytical
testing involves identifying usability problems without interacting with a user, through knowledge, guided
by heuristics of the typical user. This approach is however dangerous as ﬁndings may be erroneous due to
inaccurate or incomplete knowledge of the user.

2.5.1
Microcontrollers
A microcontroller (MCU) is a small computer on a single integrated circuit, which contains a processor
core, memory and programmable I/O peripherals. When creating prototypes for automated electronic
systems, the use of MCUs is suitable both because of their small size and the low price compared to a design
using a separate microprocessor, memory and I/O ports. Arduino is an open-source platform based on a
simple MCU board and a related development environment for writing software for the board. The Arduino
platform is especially well suited for prototyping, both because its ﬂexibility, it offers a large variety of
digital and analogue inputs, SPI and serial interfaces and the wide and digital and PWM outputs and because
of it being extremely accessible in the way of the large variety of sensors and switches that can be easily
connected and controlled. The advantages of using an Arduino board over a simple MCU board lies mainly
in the thereto related development environment, which allows the user to program the onboard MCU in
C rather than in a low-level language. The use of C makes the prototype easier to develop and maintain
and creates an abstraction from the hardware. These advantages are however also the main disadvantages
compared to the use of any low-level language, the hardware abstraction makes it hard to reduce the memory
footprint created by the software and also results in a slower execution. In the case of this project these
points are negligible compared to the difﬁcult task of creating a large program in a low-level language.
RFduino
The RFduino[56] is a miniaturized version of an Arduino. The system is composed of several shields,
such as a USB, RGB LED/button or a servo shield. The system is built in order to allow for simple plug-
and-play prototyping. The controller shield (RFduino DIP) also comes with a built-in transceiver, allowing
wireless connection to up to eight other devices, as well as Bluetooth Low Energy (BLE) module which
grants easy connection to another bluetooth device, such as most smartphones. The Arduino programming
environment can also be used to program the RFduino, which makes it easy and quick to create a working
software. These features makes it a good tool for the creation of a simple and rough ﬁrst prototype, which
can be used to test the main concept of the project.

Bus topology
The Bus network topology is the described as being the simplest of all topologies, in this
type all components (nodes) are connected to a single line (bus). When a signal is sent from a node (the
source)(see ﬁgure 2.11(a)), the message is sent to all other nodes on the bus but only the intended recipient
accepts the message, while the others discards it. The main advantage of this topology is the ease in setting it
up, however the data transmission is relatively slow and inefﬁcient since messages are sent to all nodes and
not only intended recipient. Furthermore this type of network makes fault detection difﬁcult at the individual
station difﬁcult, and the efﬁciency of the topology decreases as the number of devices increases.
Ring topology
In a ring topology, all nodes are connected to each-other to form a closed loop as in ﬁgure
2.11(b). The transfer of data ﬂows in a single direction, and the sending and receiving of messages is done
by the help of token passing. The token contains the information sent from the source and which node it
is intended to, the token is thus sent from node to node until it reaches the intended target, at that time the
token is cleared and then continues to the next node. The nodes with an empty are the only ones allowed
to send data. The advantages of this layout is that it is very organized and that the transfer of data is done
through a token being passed helps reduce the chance of collisions and allows for for very high speed trafﬁc
ﬂow in one direction. The single direction of the data ﬂow is also one of the main disadvantages of this
topology since it makes it slow, each data packet must pass through all nodes between the sources and the
destination as well as the fact that if a single node goes down, the entire network is affected.

Star topology
The star topology is one of the most widely used topologies, due to among others its good
performance and the isolation of devices. The star network consists of a single central hub (or Host) which
acts as a conduit for all transmitted messages. All others nodes (or Devices) are only connected to the Host,
the layout of such a network can be seen in ﬁgure 2.11(c). One of the main advantages of this layout is that
the failure of one device will not affect the rest of the system, since this single node is completely isolated
from the others. However this centralization of the communication around the Host is also one of the biggest
disadvantages of this network because of the high reliance on the central device (Host). This device is a
single point of failure, if the Host fails the entire system will fail in turn. Furthermore there is also a limited
capacity for connected nodes (in case of the RFduino a maximum of eight nodes) in contrast to the near
unlimited amount of nodes available in a bus or ring topology.
Mesh topology
The mesh topology is characterised by all nodes being connected to all other nodes as can
be seen in ﬁgure 2.11(d). Every node can not only send its own signals but can also relays data from other
nodes. This topology makes it possible to transmit data from different devices simultaneously, furthermore
expansion and modiﬁcation of the network can be done without disrupting other nodes. However this layout
increases the amount redundancy in the network and its set-up, maintenance and administration are very
complex.
Gazell Link Layer Protocol
As mentionned earlier the RFduino DIP shield is equipped with a transceiver module enabling wireless
communication with the Gazell Link Layer (GZLL) protocol[58].
GZLL is a communication protocol used for wireless links between a single Host and up to eight others
Devices in a star network topology. It is furthermore designed to minimize power consumption thus making
it especially suitable for power-sensitive applications. The main features of this protocol are:
• Star network topology with one Host and 8 Devices
• Bi-directional data transfer between Host and Devices
• Robustness to interference and good wireless coexistence with other wireless systems
• Packet acknowledgement and retransmission optimized to prevent data loss

Radio-Frequency Identiﬁcation
Radio-frequency identiﬁcation (RFID) is a method used for short-range wireless data transfer. All RFID
systems consist of two parts; a tag (or transponders) and a reader. Although there are many types of RFID
systems, they can be divided into two groups: passive or active[60]. The main difference between the two
being that the later uses active tags, which require a power source (be it either by being integrated into
a powered structure or by using a battery), whereas in passive systems, the tags do not require power to
function. Another advantage of the passive RFID is the near indeﬁnite operational life and small size of the
tags. These tags are made up of an antenna, a semi-conductor chip and some kind of encapsulation of these.
The reader is the component that allows communication with the tag, the tag’s antenna captures energy from
the reader and transmits the tag’s ID10. The power transfer between reader and tag is achieved by either
magnetic induction or electromagnetic wave capture. Both of these methods are able to transfer enough
power (between 10µW and 1mW) from the reader to the tag in order to sustain the operation of the chip in
the tag and thus the transmission of the tag’s ID. Thusly given tags with each their speciﬁc ID, the RFID
reader is able to access which tag it has powered.
Serial Communication
Serial communication is a method of transmitting data between two devices[61]. The data is sent
sequentially one bit at a time over a channel or bus. Traditional serial communication only enables data to
travel in a single direction at a time, however the Serial Peripheral Interface (SPI) protocol makes it possible
to operate in full duplex mode, meaning that the data can travel in both directions simultaneously.
Serial Peripheral Interface
The Serial Peripheral Interface is method used for synchronous serial communication at short distances.
It is most widely used to send data between an MCU and small peripherals (shift registers, sensors, SD
cards or another MCU). It differs from traditional serial ports, in that it is synchronous, meaning that it
utilizes separate lines for data and clock. This ensures that the sender and the receiver are in constant sync
(which is a common problem area when using serial ports). Another advantage is the simple wiring. An SPI
connection is always made between a single master device and one or several peripheral devices (slaves).
The connection is made up of four data lines of which the ﬁrst three are common to all attached devices:
MISO (Master in Slave out): data line that allows for data transfer from the slaves to the master device.
MOSI (Master out Slave in): line for sending data to the slave devices.
SCK (Serial Clock): this line holds the clock pulses generated by the master.
SS (Slave Select): the master utilizes this line to enable or disable the attached devices. When a slave’s SS
pin is set to low, it communicates with the master and when high it ignores any data it may receive.
Inter-Integrated Circuit
Inter-Integrated Circuit (I2C) Protocol is a communication protocol intended for short distance commu-
nication between multiple slaves and one or multiple masters. The main advantage of I2C compared to SPI
is that only two bus lines are required, in addition there are no strict baud rate requirements, the bus clock is
simply generated by the master.

the other students. This last aspect should also be taken into account when creating the game for the next
prototype, the students should be made to feel secure about giving an answer and reduce the risk of them
being patronized by the other students as much as possible.

6.2.4
Improving The Code
The time needed to send a message from the master device and receive an acknowledge from a single
slave device situated 1 meter away was examined. The result was that a single "ping-pong" cycle took over
300 ms, which considering that radio waves travel at the speed of light, showed that something was deﬁnitely
slowing down the communication Scrutinizing the code revealed several problems:
• The radio pipe was closed in order to:
– Update the LCD in the middle of the "ping-pong" process.
– Update several variables, like the question counters.
– Write status messages to the serial monitor.
• The Master and Slave could only receive messages when respectively in the "wait4TeamAns" and
"wait4ans" states.
These problems showed that the software indeed a problem source and was far from adequate to perform
effectively in context and would therefore have to be revised in the next prototype. To assess whether the soft-
ware was the only reason for the slow communication a new script, in which only the "send&acknowledge"
process was performed, was written3. Furthermore rather than using a while-loop to ensure that a message is
sent correctly, each message is only sent once and if an acknowledge is not received within 5 ms, the message
is sent again and so on. On the receiver part as soon as a message is received and a single response is sent.
150 send&acknowledge cycles were made, each cycle only being rated as ﬁnished when an acknowledge
from the Slave was received. This resulted in an average send&acknowledge time per single cycle of around
40 ms with 8 m between devices, which while a noticeable reduction, was still not sufﬁcient.
6.2.5
Interference
In the previous prototype the communication between modules was noticeably faster, the main difference
being that now the components were cramped into a small box. Furthermore by chance, the side of the box
unto which the RFID reader’s antenna was accidentally separated from the main body of the main and this
however resulted in a sudden decrease of send&acknowledge time. A second hypothesis was therefore made:
the antenna used by the RFID reader causes interference. Furthermore it was hypothesized that shielding the
antenna from the other components with a thin layer of tinfoil would reduce the interference without causing
radical impact on the reader’s operating range.

Discussion
In the following chapter the results obtained during the project are discussed and another possible test

• An important factor to be taken into account when assessing the overall successfulness of the prototype
are the biases in the answers given by the students and especially the teacher. These biases come from
the teacher having been part of the development of the system from the start and thus might feel a
natural attraction to the system. In order to assess the system correctly, it should be tested at several
schools with different teachers and different students.
• Furthermore, it is also found to be beneﬁcial to examine whether simply using the more rudimentary
games as proposed by the Ministry of Education is faster and more efﬁcient. The project "Sæt skolen i
bevægelse" suggests the following game, similar to the one having been implemented in this project:
"The students are divided into two teams. On their backs are attached
equations. The point of the game is to solve the other teams equation and
then to call-out the name and the solution of that students equation, and
then to take that person’s equation. When one loses his/her equation, the
teacher gives him/her a new one. The goal is to win as many equations as
possible."
The two games could be compared by having teachers preparing both the paper based game and the
game implemented in this project (implementing new equations) and examine the time and resources
needed in each case. The results could be used to assess the changes, if any, needed to be implemented
to the system, in order to ensure that the design is both easier to prepare for and less time consuming.
Furthermore, such changes could also be applied in order to ascertain that there actually is a need for
a teacher’s interface and the features that it should have.
• In retrospect the radio communication problems, experienced during the ﬁeld test of the third prototype,
could have been avoided, if thorough tests of all the components had been performed before the game
was created.

9.1
Development Summary
The original idea was conceptualized through a brainstorm with the help of my advisor Jari Due
Jessen, then further clariﬁed by visualizing how the system would/could be used. On this basis the initial
requirements for the system were decided upon. In order to establish the need for this kind of system and to
assess the particular user-characteristics of the intended users, students in 7th grade, the ﬁrst prototype was
created. This ﬁrst low-ﬁdelity prototype was evaluated through a ﬁeld study, while observing the satisfaction
and actions taken by the participating students. The ﬁeld test results supported the hypothesis that there is
indeed a need for the envisioned system, but modiﬁcations would have to be made to further motivate the
students and to incite them to move physically. It was furthermore also decided that in the context of this
project a smart-device application would not be implemented, seeing that it was assessed that the beneﬁts of
it did not outweigh the difﬁculties of implementing it. The second prototype was developed with this in mind.
The components needed to ensure that the system would be capable of "standing alone" and have the desired
features, such as user identiﬁcation or inter-module communication, were chosen. The resulting high-ﬁdelity
experimental prototype was thus used to assess the technical feasibility of creating the envisioned modules
and the mathematics-based game. It was evaluated through a meeting with one of the main shareholders: the
teacher. The response received during this meeting was that while the system indeed seemed to offer the right
functionalities, it would need to be further expanded to allow for the whole class (19 odd students) to play at
the same time and the components of each modules would need to be protected from the students. In order
to meet the requirements set forth by the teacher, a "relay-race"-type game was developed and protective
boxes were designed and 3D printed for the third prototype. The main objective of the third prototype was
to perform a successful playtest with the entire class. The prototype would be evaluated according to the
following design requirements: effectiveness, environment and usability. In other words how well does the
prototype perform when used in the "real" world and are the students able to easily understand/learn how it
is supposed to be used. The ﬁeld test was unfortunately a partial failure; the students were not able to ﬁnish
the game because of system failures, but the reason for these failures was identiﬁed: the inter-module radio
communication. Experimental tests performed ensuing this ﬁrst playtest revealed that the time needed for
a send&acknowledge cycle between two modules was greater than 100 ms. Two of the main causes for
this proved to be the software and interference from the RFID reader’s antenna. The ﬁrst playtest however

9.3
Conclusion
This present report has comprised a description of the development process of an educational Playware
system for use in the 7th grade class at Vedbæk Folkeskole. The motivation for this project and the
methodologies applied have been described in detail. The theories both in relevance to education and the
technological aspects, as well as the currently available educational robotic systems and the pertaining
research have been presented. After having set up the preliminary requirements for the system, a ﬁrst
low-ﬁdelity prototype has been developed, aimed at checking whether the assumptions made as to the need
of such a system were supported. The analysis of this prototype was then used to re-examine the requirements
for the second prototype. The objective of the second prototype functionality was to ensure that it was
technologically feasible to create the envisioned system and what functions would have to implemented
in order to create a useful system. After analysis of the performance of the prototype and discussion with
the class’ teacher, the third prototype was developed and a "relay-race" game, called "MATStafet" was
implemented and tested with the students. The problems revealed by the ﬁeld test were then ﬁxed in the
fourth prototype.
However, since the performance of the fourth prototype is not ﬂawless, with the main issue being that
while enabling the entire class to play at the same time and successfully promoting movement, the students
==PAGE:104==
86
CHAPTER 9. CONCLUSION & FUTURE WORK
spent much of the time waiting for their turn. A solution to this problem has, as yet, not been found, but
maybe increasing the number of modules or reducing the size of the teams might solve it. Furthermore, it
was unfortunately not possible to assess whether the students actually learned anything or if their proﬁciency
in solving equation was improved by using the system. This would require lengthy, rigorous testing with
more students and having a group of students not using the system as a control group. However this could
only be achieved when the system fulﬁls all the main requirements.
All in all a system incorporating both learning/practice and movement has successfully been developed.
The system did manage to challenge all the participating students, even though certain students did not feel
sufﬁciently challenged. The ﬁeld studies moreover showed that the usability of the system was sufﬁciently
good, such that both students and teacher were able to use it without extensive external assistance, and if the
teacher’s comments are to be believed the system does indeed seem to have potential to become integrated in
a school context.
9.4
Suggestions For Further Work
This section describes the modiﬁcations envisioned for the next prototype and possible future develop-
ments for the system.
9.4.1
The Future Fifth Prototype
In the next prototype the main feature to be implemented would be adaptivity in the equations, since
the test of the fourth prototype revealed that whilst the system makes it possible for the students to practice
solving simple equations, there was a signiﬁcant demand for harder equations. However the ﬁrst step
would be making the equation difﬁculty adapted to the team’s performance and to the individual student’s.
Further, in order to improve the overall usability both the physical design of the boxes and the RFID reader’s
reliability would be improved. The design would be altered to ensure that the students only try to scan their
RFID card directly in front of the reader and it will also be examined whether replacing the RFID module
would improve the interaction reliability. Moreover an overview over the problematic equations (for the
students) will me made accessible to the teacher, so the system becomes a true educational tool rather than
simply a game. The prototype would then have to ﬁeld tested to assess whether a sixth iteration is needed
before a ﬁnal prototype is made. In the ﬁnal prototype it is envisioned that the wiring will be replaced by a
custom-made PCB.
Furthermore the system should also be tested in other classes and schools over a longer period before it
can be truly be deemed to be useful in a school context.
9.4.2
Future Development
The long term vision for the system would entail that the following features or at least some of them be
added to the system:
• Teacher interface: an interface that allows the teacher to quickly and easily create new games.
Furthermore the games could be saved in a database allowing other teachers to use pre-existing games
rather than having to make their own.
• Wireless upload: ability to upload new questions/games to the devices wirelessly.
==PAGE:105==

• Modularity: instead of having a ﬁxed type of box, the system could be built by the teacher with
different modules with each their own functionality, as such if a screen was not required but a speaker
was, the teacher could simply remove the screen and add the speaker.
• Connection to tablet: in order to increase the number of possible games, it should be made possible to
connect a tablet to the modules. Thusly the screen and other features available on the tablet could be
used to make more complex games.
