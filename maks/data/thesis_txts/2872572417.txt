Abstract
This thesis presents the design, implementation, and testing of a wideband reconfigurable
antenna array for vehicular applications, operating in the 0.70‚Äď5.90 GHz range. The sys-
tem enables 360‚ó¶beam steering using a circular array of eight tapered slot antenna ele-
ments per polarization for both horizontal and vertical polarization to support 2√ó2 MIMO.
Azimuthal reconfigurability is achieved through PIN-diode-based switching, allowing one
or two elements to be active while the rest remain inactive. The two-active element config-
uration is used to increase low frequency gain rather than providing additional beams for
the same frequencies as the one-active configurations. The horizontal and vertical polar-
ized elements were individually optimized for gain, impedance matching, and beamwidth.
They were subsequently assembled together with the PIN diode feed networks into a pro-
totype. Simulated and measured results showed agreement for single elements, while
the assembled array demonstrated |S11| ‚Č§‚ąí8 dB and a typical gain of 7 dBi for 0.70‚Äď
4.50 GHz. The prototype validates the feasibility of wideband, reconfigurable vehicular
antennas with beam steering and highlights areas for future improvement in feed network
bandwidth, minimize main lobe variation over frequency, and improve overall compact-
ness.

Introduction
Antennas are fundamental components of modern wireless communication systems. They
enable the transmission and reception of electromagnetic waves, serving as the physical
interface between electronic devices and the wireless domain. By propagating through
open-air environments, antennas facilitate flexible and scalable communication without
the need for physical connections.
The growing demand for high data-rate wireless communication has driven the develop-
ment of technologies such as 4G Long Term Evolution (LTE) and 5G New Radio (NR),
both of which currently operate largely within the 0.70‚Äď5.90 GHz frequency range [1]. A
study by the Norwegian Defence Research Establishment (FFI) concludes that the pri-
mary limiting factor for the performance and range of 5G NR is the radiated power of
the user equipment (UE). The study recommends increasing radiated power using high-
power UE [2]. Directive antennas are not emphasized due to the challenges of maintaining
alignment while in motion and reorienting after base station handovers.
While the recommended omnidirectional antennas with increased transmitted power could
help address this limitation, this approach has significant drawbacks. Most of the the
radiated power will not propagate toward the its intended recipient, resulting in low ef-
ficiency, increased interference for nearby users, and greater exposure to signal inter-
ception. Comrod A/S seeks to overcome this challenge by developing a reconfigurable
vehicular antenna with 360‚ó¶beam steering. This design aims to combine the directional
gain benefits of a focused antenna with the flexibility of an omnidirectional one.
Most reconfigurable antennas with 360‚ó¶beam steering suitable for vehicular applications‚ÄĒ
whether developed in academia or commercially available‚ÄĒare based on some form of
circular array. Designs presented in academic literature are typically multiband and in-
tended for Direction Finding (DF) and Multiple Input Multiple Output (MIMO) communica-
tions, such as those in [3, 4]. In contrast, commercially available solutions, such as those
in [5, 6], often achieve excellent performance but are limited to single-band operation,
while also focusing on DF and MIMO applications.
There remains a gap between academic research and commercial products: namely,
the lack of a wideband, 360‚ó¶reconfigurable antenna operating across the 0.70‚Äď5.90 GHz
range that offers relatively high gain compared to omnidirectional antennas and is suited
for MIMO applications.
The purpose of this work is to design a 360‚ó¶reconfigurable antenna array tailored for ve-
hicular applications. The objective is to develop a circular, switch-based array using eight
tapered slot antenna (TSA) elements (per polarization) capable of operating from 0.70 to
5.90 GHz. The system must support dual linear polarization for 2√ó2 MIMO and config-
urable beamwidth through one- or two-active-element operation. The goal is to design
and assemble a prototype that demonstrates the viability of such an array ,that through
further development, could address the radiated power limitations of UE in commercial
use.
The thesis is organized as follows: Chapter 2 reviews current trends in vehicular wireless
communication and antenna technology. Chapter 3 defines the theoretical framework, de-
sign metrics, and simulation and measurement methods. Chapter 4 presents the design
and optimization of the individual antenna elements. Chapter 5 details the integration of

Vehicular Wireless Communication and
Antennas
Antennas are essential for wireless communication, as they enable the transmission and
reception of electromagnetic waves to convey information. Wireless communication is
especially critical for mobile platforms, where tethered connections are impractical. As
integral components of modern communication systems, antennas support technologies
ranging from compact, millimeter-scale Radio-Frequency Identification (RFID) chips to
large, satellite-based systems such as Global Navigation Satellite Systems (GNSS).
This chapter outlines the major domains of vehicular wireless communication namely
Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X), telemetry, and infotainment. In addition, it compares om-
nidirectional and directive antennas, highlighting the growing relevance of reconfigurable
arrays in enhancing link robustness and mitigating interference. The issue of mutual cou-
pling is discussed in the context of densely packed single-band vehicular antennas, moti-
vating the use of wideband solutions that reduce physical footprint while supporting mul-
tiple services. Circular arrays are introduced as a promising architecture for directional
coverage, and the TSA is presented as the preferred element for the array design pro-
posed in this thesis.
2.1
Wireless Vehicle Communication
Modern vehicles operate across multiple frequency bands, each covered by antennas
specifically tailored to its respective range. An emerging domain of wireless communica-
tion, known as Vehicle-to-Everything (V2X), leverages the increased throughput capacity
enabled by the rollout of 5G NR and the expansion of the Internet of Things (IoT). This do-
main requires dedicated antennas operating within Frequency Range 1 (FR1), spanning
0.45‚Äď6.00 GHz,1 and Frequency Range 2 (FR2), covering 24.25‚Äď52.60 GHz [8]. Along
with telemetry and infotainment, V2X constitutes one of the primary domains of wireless
communication relevant to vehicular systems, as illustrated in Figure 2.1.
V2X
V2X is an umbrella term in which the ‚ÄúX‚ÄĚ represents a multitude of subcategories. The
most central being Vehicle-to-Infrastructure (V2I), Vehicle-to-Vehicle (V2V), and Vehicle-
to-Network (V2N). The primary objective of V2X is to facilitate the exchange of information
between vehicles and their environment to enhance situational awareness and improve
safety. Currently, two main standards define V2X communication: cellular vehicular-to-
everything (C-V2X), developed by 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP), and dedi-
cated short-range communications (DSRC), developed by Institute of Electrical and Elec-
tronics Engineers (IEEE) 802.11p. Both operate primarily in a 5.9 GHz band, although
V2N typically utilizes existing FR1 cellular frequencies [9]. V2X facilitates direct commu-
nication links between actors offering low latency, as the data does not need to be relayed
through a central network. This low-latency communication is essential for real-time up-
dates and is a critical for applications such as vehicle platooning and autonomous driving.
Telemetry
The telemetry domain is primarily concerned with communicating the state and location
of the vehicle. This includes both cellular communication and position tracking via GNSS.

Omnidirectional and Directive Vehicular Antennas
Omnidirectional antennas, such as shark fin and monopole antennas, provide theoret-
ically uniform coverage around the vehicle. This is particularly useful in for vehicular
applications, where the direction of nearby transmitters and receivers changes frequently
as the vehicle moves. However, because omnidirectional antennas radiate energy in all
directions, including directions where communication is unnecessary, the transmissions
tend to be power-inefficient and more susceptible to interference and signal interception.
As a result, there is growing interest in beam-steering antennas for vehicular applica-
tions. Beam steering enables the antenna to reconfigure its radiation pattern toward the
intended transceiver, using array-based solutions such as electronically scanned phased
arrays or switched beam arrays. By focusing energy in a specific direction, these systems
can achieve significantly higher gain (typically 10‚Äď15 dBi), extended range, and improved
link robustness [6]. Examples of a monopole antenna and a circular array are shown in
Figure 2.4. A major advantage of beam-steering antennas is their ability to suppress radi-
ation in undesired directions. This directional control reduces interference and improves
communication security by limiting signal exposure to unintended receivers.
Implementing beam steering on moving platforms poses several technical challenges.

Antenna Coupling and the Case for Wideband Solutions
As vehicles incorporate more wireless functions such as cellular, GNSS, and satellite
communication, the number of onboard antennas continues to grow. Placing multiple
antennas on constrained surfaces like the roof or hood leads to reduced spacing between
them. When antennas are positioned close to one another, they begin to interact, resulting
in mutual coupling. This coupling can distort radiation patterns potentially introducing nulls
in the azimuth plane, defined as the horizontal plane relative to the vehicle. It can also
cause impedance mismatches, and reducing the antennas efficiency. These effects are
illustrated in the radiation patterns shown in Figure 2.5, taken from Comrod marketing
material, which compares the performance of four individual antennas on a vehicle with
that of a single wideband antenna designed to cover all channels.
To mitigate these issues, multiple frequency bands can be incorporated into a one wide-
band or multiband antenna capable of operating across a broad frequency range, such as
0.7 GHz to 5.9 GHz. A wideband antenna reduces the need for multiple physical elements,
thereby minimizing coupling effects and simplifying both mechanical and electrical inte-
gration.2 While wideband antennas typically trade some gain and impedance matching
in exchange for broader bandwidth, they can still outperform tightly spaced narrowband
antennas due to reduced mutual coupling.

2.3
The Tapered Slot Antenna
The array elements used in the design presented in this thesis are determined by a com-
bination of electrical, radiation, and mechanical requirements, as outlined in Chapter 1.

This chapter outlines the theoretical background, analytical expressions, and simulation

the antenna element and array design. Following this a theoretical expression for the the

3.1
Relevant Design Parameters
The three primary parameters defined in the design requirements‚ÄĒgain, half-power beamwidth
(HPBW), and scattering parameters (S-parameters), were central to the antenna design.
Each of these addresses a distinct aspect of antenna performance: field behavior and ra-
diation efficiency, spatial coverage, and impedance matching. This section outlines their
roles and how they were applied throughout the design.
Gain
Gain was chosen over realized gain as the primary field metric to decouple the effects
of return loss from the radiation characteristics. This separation allows the field behavior
of the antenna to be assessed independently of impedance matching. Realized gain
includes mismatch losses and would therefore complicate the analysis of the radiation
pattern. Notably, gain is considered from the port of each element and does not account
for attenuation or standing wave losses in the feed network. Realized gain is used when
comparing with measured gain for the prototype, as it accounts for mismatch losses
Half-Power Beamwidth (HPBW)
HPBW was closely monitored due to the 45‚ó¶-sector constraint imposed by the circular
array geometry. The HPBW is measured from the gain pattern.
S-Parameters
S-parameters (|S11| and |S21|) were used to assess the impedance matching, isolation
and insertion losses and of the antenna elements and array. Among these, |S11|, the re-
flection coefficient, was preferred over directly analyzing the complex input impedance.
This is especially relevant for the TSA, where the feed-point impedance is strongly influ-
enced by the microstrip feed balun rather than the slot itself. |S11| provides a measure
of how effectively the antenna is matched to the system impedance. This thesis includes
matching to both 50 ‚Ą¶and 75 ‚Ą¶, to avoid confusion the relevant matching is mention in
the caption of the |S11| plots.
|S21|, and more generally |Sij| for i Őł= j, was also considered to evaluate network isolation
between elements in array configurations and insertion loss through the feed network.
Lastly, |S21| was simulated between elements (excluding the feed network) to model inter-
element coupling, and also simulated for the standalone feed network to estimate inter-
element isolation.

PIN Diode
PIN diodes were used in the feed network to toggle array elements active and inactive.
They offer advantages over RF transistors, as their bias (control) current is routed through
the RF path rather than requiring a separate bias line, thereby saving space. The bias
circuitry can be integrated along the traces, where more physical area is available. The
compactness of the diodes is critical, as it determines the physical size of the star junction,
which must remain small (less than őĽ/4) to maintain proper matching at the junction [35].
The PIN diode is named after its structure, which consists of a p-type (P), intrinsic (I), and
n-type (N) semiconductor region arranged in sequence.

3.5
Configuration of Simulations
CST 2024 offers multiple solvers to choose from. For the simulation of the antenna ele-
ments, both time domain (TD) and frequency domain (FD) solvers were considered. The
TD is better suited for wide frequency ranges, as only one simulation is needed and the
frequency response can subsequently be obtained using a Fast Fourier Transform (FFT).
Since the frequency resolution does not complicate the simulation calculations, it is more
suitable for electrically large antennas [32]. However, resonating structures complicate
the transient response, as the signal will reverberate for a long time.
The FD solver, on the other hand, performs simulations at discrete frequency points with
interpolation in between. CST recommends using adaptive mesh refinement in FD, which
automatically adjusts the mesh across the frequency range [38]. As such, the FD solver
is typically preferred for narrowband and electrically small antennas [39].
Regarding simulation environment configuration, all simulations for elements and arrays
are conducted in with open boundaries and without any specific background, as the prox-
imity to other array elements are modeled by placing the elements into the simulation
model. However for future simulations of a near complete and satisfactory array a hori-
zontal conducting plane could be added under ((‚ąíy)-direction) the array to represent the
roof of a vehicle. Although the front side conducting layer that makes up the shape of the
TSA antenna is symmetric along its length, the feed balun is not. Therefore no symmetric
conditions are applied to the simulations.
An overview of the chosen simulation configurations is provided below to give context to
the results used throughout the design process.
Element Simulations
The antenna elements fall into a ambiguous state of being somewhat wideband and elec-
trically small at low frequencies, but becoming electrically large at high frequencies. This
makes it difficult to select a single optimal solver in advance. To address this, both solvers
were tested using an existing TSA design from the literature that has previously been both
simulated and measured. The design features a total width of 252.00 mm and a length of
278.20 mm, including oscillating edge structures (Appendix Figure A.2) [40]. It serves as
a reasonable proxy for the intended design, which is approximately 200 mm in width and
length and is likely to incorporate similar structural features.
Ref. [40] includes CST FD simulations and measurements of a fabricated prototype.
The design was recreated and simulated using both TD and FD solvers. Adaptive mesh
refinement was used in FD, while mesh refinement was manually applied in TD. The
resulting VSWR values are compared in Figure 3.6, along with the results reported in the
paper.
All data sets display the same general behavior: a steep drop in VSWR below 1 GHz,
followed by fluctuating values between 1:1 and 2:1 in the range of 1‚Äď6 GHz. Notable
differences include the paper‚Äôs near-perfect matching around 1 GHz, which is not repro-
duced in the recreated simulations, and discrepancies in ripple behavior. These ripples
are visible in both CST simulations but not in the measurement data. However, the mea-
surements appear to use a non-uniform and sparse frequency resolution, possibly as
coarse as 0.4 GHz, which could explain the absence of these features.
Both TD and FD simulations are reasonably consistent with the measured results in the
0.7‚Äď5.9 GHz range. The TD simulation exhibits ripples across the full bandwidth, likely
due to resonating edge structures that complicate the time response. These ripples are
significantly reduced in FD, making it a better approximation of the measured VSWR.

Outdoor far-field measurements pose several challenges that can affect both accuracy
and repeatability. The primary environmental factors include precipitation, wind, and elec-

Moisture on the antennas, such as from rain, introduces additional losses, particularly at
microwave frequencies. While these losses can theoretically be accounted for if the ref-
erence and Antenna Under Test (AUT) are measured under identical conditions, ensuring
such consistency is difficult. Unequal dynamic water distribution can lead to mismatches
in performance.
Wind may cause physical movement or misalignment of the antennas. While the impact
on gain is typically minor, beamwidth measurements can be affected.
Electromagnetic interference is another concern, as the measurement band (0.70‚Äď5.90 GHz)

Corrugated Edges
To improve gain and impedance matching for f < 2.0 GHz, Corrugated Edges (CEs)
were introduced to mitigate parasitic currents running along the outer TSA edges. At low
frequencies, surface currents travel along the tapered waveguide edges [28]. These par-
asitic currents can radiate out of phase or at undesired angles, causing pattern distortion.
The corrugated edges act as high-impedance őĽ
4 barriers, suppressing these currents [41,
28].
Resonances form when the slot length equals a quarter wavelength, creating an open-
circuit equivalent. These resonances increase gain at low frequencies. The magnitude of
the resonance increases with the number of CEs, and the bandwidth can be broadened
by varying their individual lengths of the edges.
A model with four edges per side is shown in Figure 4.8. The CE design was adapted from
ref. [40], which uses so-called oscillation circuits tuned for a similar frequency range. The
neck length and distance to the element‚Äôs front edge were optimized accordingly. While
ref. [40] used five edges per side, this was reduced to four to avoid proximity to the tapered
opening, which would have caused high-impedance areas. Both a three and a four edge
design were simulated and compared to the plain triangular element in Figure 4.5b.

Reconfigurable Feed Network
The feed network connects the array elements to the input, and manages which of the
elements are active and inactive. By controlling which elements are active, the feed net-
work realizes the beamforming dictated by the bias. The feed network should have one
SMA input port and eight outputs to the array elements. Furthermore it should be tuned
the specification of |S11| ‚Č§‚ąí8 dB from 0.70 to 5.90 GHZ. The resulting feed network
prototype is shown in Figure 5.6.
Network Configuration
Two Transmission Line (TL) circuits were proposed to manage the unique requirement
of having one or two elements active at any given time. The first configuration, shown
in Figure 5.7a, has a 75 ‚Ą¶load connected to the 50 ‚Ą¶input, resulting in a mismatch for
both the one-active and two-active cases (the latter having a characteristic impedance
of 37.5 ‚Ą¶). The second configuration, shown in Figure 5.7b, uses separately matched
paths of 50 ‚Ą¶and 100 ‚Ą¶for the one- and two-active-element configurations, respectively.
However, this complicates proper matching at the junction on the element side of the cir-
cuit. The additional arms would drastically increase the size of the junction, complicating
high-frequency matching as the physical length could approach or exceed őĽ/4.
This complication can be mitigated by splitting the junction into two or more smaller junc-
tions, an example of doing this is provided in Appendix Figure C.1. However, this ap-
proach is not feasible in the this design, as it would prevent the formation of certain two-
active-element beams. The 75 ‚Ą¶configuration was ultimately chosen, as it only requires
a 8 + 1-arm star junction, compared to the 16 + 1 arms needed for a configuration with
separate paths for each element. This simplifies star junction impedance matching.

capacitors are used to shunt any conducted or coupled AC noise to ground. A typical bias
circuit is shown schematically in Figure 3.5 and in the PCB layout in Figure 5.12a. The
circuit is designed and simulated for 10 V reverse bias and 50 mA forward bias operation.

Assembly
This section outlines the practical steps taken to assemble the array antenna prototype,
including substrate ordering, component mounting, and bias integration. Emphasis is
placed on ensuring reliable electrical connectivity and replicating the simulated configu-

ulated results. The procedures described here form the foundation for the subsequent
measurement results.
The assembly process for the H-pol and V-pol arrays is summarized in Figures 5.13
and 5.14, respectively. Divinycell PVC foam was used to enhance the mechanical rigidity
of the arrays while minimizing interference with electromagnetic wave propagation, owing
to its low dielectric constant and low loss characteristics.
When installing the PIN diodes, it was noted that the glass housing of the MA4SPS402
PIN diode was very fragile, and that the star junction was very cramped. Consequently
not all installed PIN diode function properly. Diode for element 8 in the H-Pol array and
didoes for element 4 and 5 were not functional (no DC current could run through in forward
bias).
For the MIMO configuration, the two arrays were assembled together with a spacing of
43 mm between the H-pol and V-pol elements. Both RF inputs were routed to the bottom
of the H-pol array using SUCOFORM 141 coaxial cables with N-type connectors.

Summary
This chapter detailed the design and physical realization of the antenna arrays. Building
on the individually optimized horizontally and vertically polarized elements, the focus was
placed on their arrangement in circular configurations.
For the H-pol array, close-packed elements were integrated into a 45‚ó¶-sector wedge. The
V-pol array was designed to maintain a broad beamwidth, using FR4 spacers to mitigate
unwanted reflections caused by the corner-like shape of the adjacent elements.
A reconfigurable feed network was developed to enable beam steering by switching be-
tween one-active and two-active element modes. This included PIN diode modeling, star
junction impedance matching, and the design of a bias circuit. The complete system was
then assembled into a prototype.
In the next chapter, the performance of the prototype is evaluated through measurements
and compared to simulated results, providing insight into the viability of the array design.

Future Work
Building on the results and limitations identified in the measurements, several aspects
of the array design can be further explored and improved. The following items outline
specific recommendations aimed at enhancing both the performance and practicality of
the antenna system. The recommendations are outlined into bullet point lists considering
the feed network and the array and elements.
‚ÄĘ Viability of the 50 ‚Ą¶to 75 ‚Ą¶-element Configuration: The viability of interfacing a
50 ‚Ą¶RF input with either a 75 ‚Ą¶antenna element or the parallel equivalent 37.5 ‚Ą¶
configuration should be critically evaluated. While the current design demonstrates
significantly improved low-frequency gain, this enhancement comes at the expense
of optimal impedance matching. Experience gained from the feed network design
and full-array simulations confirms that improved low-frequency performance can
be achieved compared to a standalone element. As an alternative, a dedicated
one-active-element array, fully normalized to a 50 ‚Ą¶system, should be considered.
However, the adoption of such a configuration may ultimately depend on the pref-
erences and requirements of potential end-users.
‚ÄĘ Make the Feed Network Test-Friendly: The current feed network design is only
configured to allow for |S11|-measurements. For prototyping purposes, DC block
capacitors should be added, as well as proper matching at the intended antenna
ports, enabling multi-port measurements.
‚ÄĘ Feed Network Bandwidth: The feed network performance above 4.5 GHz needs
to be improved. However, based on the current work and measurements, it is un-
clear what causes the poor performance above 4.5 GHz. Further efforts should be
directed at isolating and resolving the mismatch.
‚ÄĘ Increase Power Capability: The existing PIN diodes could be replaced with alter-
natives capable of handling higher power levels, as relevant radios for this antenna
typically deliver up to 25 W.
There are many steps left before this work is close to a commercial product. Steps like
integrating a radome and beam-steering logic are essential future tasks. However, more
immediate steps for improving the array are prioritized.

Summary
This chapter presents the measured impedance matching and gain pattern of the proto-
type, covering standalone elements and separate and MIMO array configurations. The
standalone horizontal and vertical elements were measured to be consistent with simula-
tions in both gain and impedance. The array measurements in two-active and one-active
modes largely demonstrated satisfactory impedance matching and gain magnitude in the
frequncy interval of 0.70‚Äď1.35 GHz and 1.35 4.50 GHz, respectively. At higher frequen-
cies mainlobe distortion and low radiation efficiency were observed, likely due to feed
network limitations and excitation of higher-order modes and parasitic effects of the inac-
tive elements that were not properly modeled in the array simulations. There was also
significant impedance mismatch (|S11| > ‚ąí8 dB) after 4.5 GHz that contributed to a dete-
riorated gain magnitude. It is likely caused by insufficient modeling of the simulated feed
network, that lead to improper impedance tuning.
Nonetheless, the array achieves competitive bandwidth and low frequency gain relative
to state-of-the-art designs, using a simpler analog reconfiguration approach based on PIN
diodes. Key limitations include performance above 4.5 GHz, inconsistent high frequency
mainlobe, physical size, and the need for improved array modeling and feed network
design, which are addressed in the proposed future work.

Conclusion
This thesis has presented the design and development of a wideband, reconfigurable an-
tenna array for vehicular applications, designed to support 360‚ó¶beam steering across
the 0.70‚Äď5.90 GHz range. The key design challenges being achieving wideband per-
formance, consistent gain pattern, and beam steering within the limited physical space
available on vehicles. These challenges were addressed by a design based on eight
TSA elements (per polarization) arranged in circular, switch-based arrays, offering both
horizontal (H-pol) and vertical linear polarizations (V-pol) to enable 2√ó2 MIMO operation.
Reconfigurable control is achieved through dynamic switching between one- and two-
active element configurations that collectively cover 360‚ó¶. A prototype was assembled
consisting of two eight-element circular arrays, one per polarization, along with integrated
networks for beam steering. While the standalone elements showed good agreement
with simulation results, the complete array failed to meet design specifications, particu-
larly due to degraded impedance matching and gain patterns at higher frequencies. Still,
array measurements showed |S11| ‚Č§‚ąí8 dB and a typical gain of 7 dBi at 0‚ó¶in the az-
imuthal plane for the 0.70‚Äď4.50 GHz range.
In Chapter 2 the communication domains relevant to vehicular applications were reviewed.
These systems predominantly operate within or adjacent to FR1 cellular bands. Om-
nidirectional and directive antennas were discussed as well as coupling challenges for
single-band antennas in close proximity and the need for multiband antennas.
Chapter 3 defined the theoretical framework for the design of the antenna system as well
as methods for simulation and measurements.
In Chapter 4, the individual H-pol and V-pol array elements were developed and optimized
for. The H-pol element was fitted within a 45‚ó¶sector using triangular shaping and cor-
rugated edges to improve low-frequency gain. The V-pol element was kept rectangular,
and has an air-filled taper that increase the beamwidth at high frequencies. Both elements
achieved good impedance matching and radiation performance across the 0.70‚Äď5.90 GHz
band.
Chapter 5 described the integration of the antenna elements into their respective circular
arrays. A reconfigurable feed network using PIN diode switching was implemented to
toggle between one- and two-active element configurations.
In Chapter 6, measurements of the array elements, separate and MIMO arrays were
compared to simulations. The individual elements matched simulations closely, but in
the full array configuration, some high-frequency performance deviations appeared.

figuration that balances bandwidth, gain, and beamwidth prior to further element-specific

The TSA element is fed by a balun that feeds the signal from a microstrip path on the
backside of the print to the waveguide in the frontal conducting layer. The signal is trans-

balun is typically either a quarter-wave stub or some form of a radial stub that provides
