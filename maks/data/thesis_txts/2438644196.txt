Abstract
Class-D ampliﬁers are often designed to be used with a ohmic load. Hence the dynamics of the
loudspeaker is completely disregarded. This can lead to unwanted oscillation or stability issues
once the loudspeaker is connected to the ampliﬁer because the load is now dynamic. To accommo-
date these problems the ampliﬁer needs to be designed conservative to still perform decent under
dynamic loads.
In recent work [18] it is proposed to intentionally make the output ﬁlter oscillatory. Doing
this will result in a degradation in the performance of conventional feedback loops, and another
control method must be used. In this thesis the possibilities of combining an LQR loop and an
PI-controller in an optimal manner to obtain high audio ﬁdelity is investigated. Linear models
are constructed for a class-D ampliﬁer which are combined with the electrical parameters of the
loudspeaker for the design of the controller.
Theoretical results suggest up to 4 times the improvement in noise and almost 3 times the bandwidth
by using the proposed controller compared to a conventional PI-controller. Furthermore, ampliﬁer
prototypes was constructed to validate the theoretical results. The measurement shows that the
proposed controller has signiﬁcant better tracking capabilities and a ﬂatter frequency response than
a conventional PI-controller. However, the signal-to-noise ratio turned out lower than the open-loop
ampliﬁer for both controllers, indicating that noise are picked up in the feedback path. All in all the
proposed controller showed theoretical signiﬁcant better performance, which was partly validated
in the measurements where it performed slightly better than the PI-controller overall.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Today class-D ampliﬁer are designed with the assumption that the output of the ampliﬁer will
be connected to a resistive load. This method is not ideal as the dynamics of the loudspeaker is
neglected during the design. Furthermore, loudspeakers are very nonlinear and can add signiﬁcant
amounts of distortion to the audio signal during the conversion from an electrical signal to a change
in air pressure, but will also affect the dynamics of the ampliﬁer.
The loudspeaker, when connected to a class-D ampliﬁer will affect the output ﬁlter of the ampliﬁer,
often in an unsatisfactory way. This can result in increased oscillations of the output voltage, a
non-ﬂat frequency response, or in worst case the system becomes unstable. One way to address this
problem is to design the control and output ﬁlter of the ampliﬁer sufﬁciently conservative to obtain
a robustness against the dynamic load. However, this will in turn limit the bandwidth and thereby
the tracking performance of the ampliﬁer, degrading the ampliﬁers ability to correct nonlinearities.
In recent work [18] there have been an interest in intentionally designing an oscillatory out-
put ﬁlter to improve the thermal properties of the ampliﬁer. Doing this, however, would lead to the
conventional control methods of using a single dynamic output controller to become insufﬁcient,
and an alternative control method is needed.
It has been shown in [8] that optimal control can be used to address the oscillations by reas-
signing the poles of the system to more favourable locations. However, the main focus of the work
was to restore the dynamic properties of the ampliﬁer, and only limited attention was given to the
actual audio ﬁdelity.
In this thesis the work made in [8] will be expanded to improve the audio ﬁdelity of the am-
pliﬁer.

Project Description
Based on previous works [7, 6, 8] I want to construct a switch-mode audio system with multi-
variate control. The loop will be a combination of a Linear Quadratic Regulator (LQR) loop and a
Proportional-Integral (PI) loop. The primary focus will be on purely analog control strategies and
ﬁnding the optimal combination of the LQR and PI loop. The purpose of the LQR loop will be
to precondition the linear dynamics of the ampliﬁer and loudspeaker such that the system is well
behaved before adding the PI-controller. The found controller will be compared to a typical control
conﬁguration for a class-D ampliﬁer. Lastly, the possibilities of using estimation techniques to
obtain the speaker behaviour are addressed. The focus in this thesis will be on audio quality. Hence
the attention will be directed to the ﬂatness of the frequency response, distortion and noise ﬁgures.
The thesis will only focus on loudspeakers in single resonance conﬁgurations, i.e. free-air and
closed-box conﬁgurations.

2.1
Class-D Ampliﬁer
A class-D ampliﬁer is a switch-mode audio ampliﬁer which differs signiﬁcantly from other
common ampliﬁer classes like the class-A and class-AB ampliﬁer. While class-A and class-AB
ampliﬁers work by directly amplifying the input signal, the class-D ampliﬁer works by converting
the input signal to a sequence of pulses before the ampliﬁcation takes place leading to a vast
improvement in the efﬁciency. In practice, efﬁciencies around 90% can be obtained while the
theoretical maximum for class-AB ampliﬁers is 78% [1, 33]. The greater efﬁciency compared
to class-AB ampliﬁers allows for smaller designs as heat sinks can be reduced or even removed
due to the lower losses. This has resulted in integrated ampliﬁers for low- and mid-level output
power applications [11, 10]. All this clearly illustrates that class-D ampliﬁers are very suitable
for compact audio systems and battery powered systems while still working well for high-power
systems.

2.1.1
Basis of Operation
Figure 2.1 shows the typical modulation and demodulation that takes place in a class-D
ampliﬁer.
Figure 2.1: Signal path in a class-D ampliﬁer
Here the audio signal is converted to a Pulse Width Modulated (PWM) signal by using a
modulator. Then the PWM signal is ampliﬁed in the power stage before it is ﬁnally demodulated at
the output ﬁlter. From this description it can be said that the class-D ampliﬁer consists of three
separate modules: The modulator, the power stage and the output ﬁlter, as depicted in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Basic block diagram of a class-D ampliﬁer
The basic conﬁguration shown in Figure 2.2 is rarely used in practice due to its lack of feedback.
The power stage is connected directly to the power supply to create a low impedance path to the
output. Thus, any noise, ripple or disturbance in the power supply will be audible on the output
degrading the sound quality of the ampliﬁer. To address this problem usually a control loop is
placed around the ampliﬁer going from the output voltage to the input. Further, sometimes the
power stage is built into the modulator to have the modulator compensating the disturbances from
the power supply [39, 38].
Although class-D ampliﬁers have a more convoluted signal path, due to the modulation, compared
to class-A and class-AB ampliﬁers, it is still able to obtain comparable low distortion measurements
with Total Harmonic Distortion plus Noise (THD+N) measures as low as 0.001% [29, 39].

2.1.2
Modulator
The modulator is the circuit in the ampliﬁer that handles the conversion from the continuous
audio signal to a level discrete pulse coded signal. Among the various pulse codes in existence, it is
typical to use PWM due to its simplicity and ease of implementing. The audio information is coded
into the PWM signal by comparing the audio signal to a properly scaled triangle waveform called
the carrier waveform. Whenever the audio signal is larger than the carrier a HIGH output will be
produced and when the audio signal is lower than the carrier signal a LOW output is produced. The
ratio of the HIGH time to the time of the period is called the duty cycle which is used to express
the value of the PWM modulation. Figure 2.3 shows the waveforms resulted from this behaviour.
Figure 2.3: The typical waveforms in a PWM conversion. The audio is compared to the carrier to
produce the PWM.
By coding the audio information using PWM, the amplitude of the audio becomes the duty
cycle of the PWM. Hence to obtain a good representation of the audio, the frequency of the carrier
needs to be signiﬁcantly higher than that of audio. Typical switch frequencies for the carrier lie
within 300 kHz to 2 MHz [11, 12, 9]. From an audio perspective, it is always preferable to use the
highest possible switching frequency as it allows for a higher sample rate and provides a larger
bandwidth to the system, beneﬁcial for control. However, a high switch frequency introduces
increased losses. Hence a compromise between losses and audio ﬁdelity needs to be made.
There exist multiple different modulator topologies which generates the PWM signal by different
means [39, 38]. The simplest one is the modulator on Figure 2.4.

This modulator is just a comparator connected to the input audio signal and an external triangle

the ﬁxed frequency of the carrier results in a build-up of energy at the harmonies of the switching

Another approach to designing a modulator is to design a self-oscillating modulator. The self-
oscillating modulator, at its core, works by using its output, ﬁltered, to generate the carrier waveform
used for the oscillation of the modulator [7]. The self-oscillating is then maintained by continuously
preventing the modulator from reaching a stable state. This can be realised either by introducing a
hysteresis window or a 180◦phase shift in the feedback [37, 39]. Due to the inherent feedback in
the self-oscillating design, these modulators have error correcting capabilities [37]. Further, the
feedback results in a variation of the switching frequency which vastly reduces the EMI problem
found with the ﬁxed frequency modulator [32].
For this thesis a hysteresis based self-oscillating modulator will be used. Its operation princi-
pals are covered in section 3.1.2.
2.1.3
Power Stage
Once the audio is coded into a PWM signal, the signal needs to be ampliﬁed to be able to
deliver enough power to drive a loudspeaker. This is achieved by using the PWM signal to drive
one or two pairs of transistors to get an operation equivalent to that of a synchronous buck converter
[19]. The power stage can be conﬁgured as either a half-bridge or a full-bridge as seen on Figure 2.5
The selection of the output conﬁguration is very application dependent. The loudspeaker needs a
symmetric voltage swing around zero volts. To realize this with a half-bridge conﬁguration either a
positive and negative supply voltage are needed or a large DC-blocking capacitor at the output to
prevent any DC from entering and damaging the loudspeaker. However, the half-bridge only needs
two switching devices compared to the four switching devices in the full-bridge conﬁguration.
Thus the total losses in the half-bridge conﬁguration are lower.

2.2
Electro-Acoustical Transducer
A loudspeaker, also known as an Electro-Acoustical Transducer, is a mechanism which can
convert an electrical audio signal into motion. For loudspeakers this motion is used to move the
surrounding air back and forth creating a change in air pressure which is perceived as sound. Figure
2.9 shows the most common type of loudspeaker, the moving-coil electrodynamic loudspeaker.
Figure 2.9: Illustration of a moving-coil electrodynamic loudspeaker. Taken from [45]
The electrodynamic loudspeaker consists of four major parts: 1. A permanent magnet surround-
ing the voice-coil, separated by a small air gap. 2. The voice-coil where the wire to the speaker is
wounded. 3. The suspension which holds the driver in place. The outer suspension surrounding the
diaphragm is called the surround, and the inner suspension is called the spider. 4. The diaphragm
which sometimes, as in Figure 2.9, has a dust cap in the middle.

Whenever a current is applied to the voice-coil, a magnetic ﬁeld is created. This ﬁeld reacts to
the static magnetic ﬁeld made by the permanent magnet hence generating a force [25]. The force is
generated perpendicular to the magnet ﬁeld lines in accordance with the Laplace force resulting
in a force in a single direction. Due to the large size of the permanent magnet, the force created
will be approximately proportional to the current passing through the voice-coil. The generated
force creates a movement of the diaphragm which moves the air, thus creating a sound pressure
difference.

2.2.2
Nonlinear Behaviours
To ﬁnalize the short introduction to loudspeakers some of the nonlinearities in the loudspeaker
will be mentioned brieﬂy. All the presented Thiele-Small parameters are in practice nonlinear with
Le, Bl and Cms being the worst affected. These parameters a highly sensitive to the position of
the diaphragm in the loudspeaker, but also varies with frequency and temperature among others.
Additionally, other nonlinear phenomena can occur at high amplitudes and high frequencies.
Nonlinear voice-coil inductance
When the loudspeaker’s diaphragm is moved back and forth the voice-coil is also moving inside
the magnetic ﬁeld of the permanent magnet. As mentioned earlier, the permanent magnet is made
of an iron alloy and henceforth have a high permeability. Thus, the permeability seen from the
voice-coil will change dependant on the position of the voice-coil. When the voice-coil is all the
way inside the magnetic system a high permeability is seen and the inductance will be larger. When
the voice-coil moves outwards, more of the coil becomes surrounded by air and the permeability
will fall, making the inductance small. Hence the voice-coil inductance will be asymmetric around
the center position of the loudspeaker.

Other nonlinearities
Besides the three above major nonlinearities of the loudspeaker there exist multiple other less
prominent sources. These will not be considered further in this thesis, but some are mentioned
below for completeness.
• Rocking Modes, Buzz and Rub: When the loudspeaker is excited with a large signal, the
movement of the diaphragm can become so large that the end of the voice-coil hits the
backplate of the magnetic system. Also, small geometrical errors in the design can cause the
diaphragm to move from side to side resulting in the voice-coil rubbing against the magnetic
system causing high degrees of distortion [4].
• Displacement varying mass: The more the diaphragm is extruded, the more the suspension
needs to move as well. This results in the suspension potentially adding to the mass of the
diaphragm, presenting a displacement varying Mms [3].
• Flux modulation: When a current is passed though a voice-coil, the voice-coil will generate
a magnetic ﬂux which will modulate the static magnetic ﬁeld of the permanent magnet. This
will cause a hysteresis loop, which causes distortion [15].
• Non-stiff diaphragm: Whenever a loudspeaker is played with a high enough frequency, the
diaphragm stops acting like one solid body. Instead, it starts to ﬂex, creating overtones to the
played frequency.
• Temperature variations: The resistance of the voice-coil, Re and the compliance Cms are
both fairly sensitive to changes in the temperature. The voice-coil will heat-up during use
due to the power dissipated in it. The compliance comes from the suspension which is made
of a material with viscoelastic properties. These properties, such as stiffness/elasticity can
quickly change with temperature because the material becomes softer or harder.
The above mentioned nonlinear effects makes it hard to make exact models of loudspeakers
without the demand of excessive computational power. Therefore this thesis will focus on the use
of linear models based on equation (2.6) to (2.10) as a computational light approximation for the
loudspeaker model. These models will be used during control design and a model with the three
major nonlinearities Le(x), Bl(x) and Cms(x) will be used for validation.

2.3
Noise and Distortion
When designing an ampliﬁer or a complete audio system, it is important to consider potential
sources of noise and distortion which exist within the audio domain (20Hz to 20kHz), to limit the
impact of these on the ﬁnal design. Distortion can come from both linear and nonlinear dynamics
and can inﬂuence the audio signal at different frequencies or amplitudes or only during transients.
Thus multiple metrics have been developed to try and quantize the different types of distortion
which can impact the sound quality of an audio ampliﬁer. Below some of the more used metrics
are listed. Most of these metrics will be used when evaluating the performance of the constructed
ampliﬁers later on in this thesis.
2.3.1
Amplitude and Phase Distortion
In general, when designing an audio ampliﬁer or loudspeaker, it is desired to make the system
as linear as possible to avoid distortion caused by nonlinearities which are difﬁcult to resolve.
However, there also exist linear distortion which needs to be considered although this is often far
easier to address than the nonlinear distortion. The linear distortion consists of Amplitude distortion
and Phase distortion. Amplitude distortion is caused by the frequency response not being ﬂat in the
audio domain. Hence some frequencies will have a larger or smaller ampliﬁcation which leads to
a distortion of the signal. Further, the amplitude distortion can also result in transient distortion
and a poor clip recovery if the distortion is caused by a resonance since hearable oscillations can
occur. Phase distortion is caused by a change in the phase in the audio domain, making different
frequencies in the audio becoming out of phase.
It is relatively easy to reduce the amplitude distortion in an ampliﬁer as the ampliﬁer can be
designed to have its cutoff frequencies above and below the audio domain efﬁciently making the
frequency response ﬂat. For loudspeakers, this is not as easy as its mechanical properties forms a
2nd order low pass ﬁlter with a resonance which tends to be in the audio domain resulting in an
ampliﬁcation of the frequencies around the resonance and an attenuation for frequencies above.
The same story holds true for the phase distortion where the ampliﬁer can be designed to stay
within a few degrees of phase shift in the audio domain. However, loudspeakers will have a more
substantial phase change due to the mechanical resonance. Fortunately, the human ear is mostly
insensitive to phase distortion reducing the severity of the problem.
2.3.2

type of distortion is called Harmonic distortion as the distortion presents itself as harmonics to the
fundamental frequency. The measure for this distortion is called Total Harmonic Distortion, and its

LQR in Switching Systems
Class-D ampliﬁers is in the family of switch-mode power converters (SMPC). Hence a Class-D
ampliﬁer is a switching system3. A switching system is a type of system that switches between
two or more different subsystem[27]. The switch is often caused by a state trajectory reaching
a switching surface from where the system falls into another subsystem. This is called state-
dependent switching, and it is the working principle for SMPC’s. For this kind of systems, linear
control is not necessarily applicable, and a switching rule which creates an underlying linear system
across all the subsystems must be constructed. Fortunately, it is known from the theory of sliding
mode control that this can be realized if a stable convex combination of the switching system can
be made [41, 27]:
A = αA1 + (1 −α)A2
α ∈(0, 1)
(2.27)
Here A1 is the system matrix for the ﬁrst subsystem and A2 for the second subsystem. By
switching between A1 and A2 adequately fast with the ratio of α the system will act as the linear
system A, and linear control like LQR and PID control can be applied. The switching strategy, in
this case, results in what is called a sliding-mode, as the trajectory of the two systems A1 and A2
will point to the switching surface, limiting the movement of the system. This further results in the
system becoming Global Uniform Exponential Stable. In practice, a sliding-mode behaviour will
result in inﬁnitely fast switching which will quickly cause wear to the system. To limit the switching
speed, a typical procedure is to add a hysteresis window. Figure 2.12 shows how the trajectories of
the two subsystems create a sliding-mode behaviour and bounces between the hysteresis bounds.

Tuning Methods
When selecting the parameters for the PID controller, one is often interested in obtaining good
performance, typically characterized by measures of rise time, settling time, overshoot etc., while
keeping the system robust. The robustness properties may be determined based on the phase
margin and gain margin of the system which provides an indication of how close the system is
from becoming unstable [20].
The controller gains of the PID controller is often found by using some simple design rules
like the famous Ziegler-Nichol tuning method [20] or the newer Internal Model Control (IMC) [42]
to get an initial controller design which can then be ﬁne tuned through empirical tuning. These
methods usually works well, but lacks inherent guarantees for robustness and is not optimal in any
sense.
Thus over the last decades, there has been a push for methods to design PID controllers which in
some sense are optimal, like the linear quadratic regulator. The equivalent to the LQR problem for
the PID controller is known as the Static Output Feedback problem [46]. Here a static feedback
gain matrix K needs to be found such that A −BKC is a stable system which has the poles
assigned to the best obtainable location. However, this problem has turned out to be a difﬁcult
problem to solve. In fact, the problem is NP-hard [14]. Hence in the lack of better, sub-optimal
designs have been developed for PID controller.
One method reduces the order of the plant down to a second order system from where the LQR
routine can be applied directly to ﬁnd the controller gains for the PID controller [34, 2]. This creates
a PID controller which is optimal in regards to the reduced plant and, hopefully, close to optimal
for the entire plant, depending on the size. Another approach is to use some performance measure
of the closed-loop response which is then minimized using numerical optimization methods [44].
The performance measures are designed to encapsulate all the before mentioned time-domain
characteristic of the closed loop response by comparing the response to an ideal response. The
three most used performance measures are the Integral Square Error (ISE), the Integral Absolute
Error (IAE) and the Integral Time Absolute Error (ITAE), all seen in equation (2.39).

A complete audio system consists of three major components: An audio source, an ampliﬁer

will upon receiving the signal actuate the diaphragm based on the current. For this thesis, the audio
source will be considered purely as a reference signal used to actuate the remaining audio system,
i.e. The ampliﬁer and loudspeaker. Thus, for the sake of simplicity it is assumed that the audio
source is ideal, meaning that no noise will be present.

Before the audio signal coming from the source can be used in the ampliﬁer, the signal needs
to be pre-conditioned. Hence, a preamp or input ﬁler is needed. The pre-conditioning primarily
consist of ensuring that the audio signal is biased to swing around the reference voltage of the

there is no guarantee that the provided audio signal will have the correct offset or even the same
ground potential. Thus, if no preamp is implemented problems like DC-currents in the loudspeaker

being that the compliance is constant in the tested range and therefore does not have the classical

3.3
Power Supply
The power supply is one of the major contributors to the noise in the system. Therefore it is of
interest to investigate the properties of the noise coming from the supply to determine how much it
impedes the SNR and how much the noise can be reduced by means of control.
A power supply can produce two signals which we will consider as noise entering the ampli-
ﬁer; actual noise and voltage ripple. The voltage ripple can come from the rectiﬁcation of the AC
power line, but for SMPS it will also emerge from the high frequency switching. The actual noise
will most likely be coloured since the power supply itself is a dynamical system, and some ﬁltering
takes place inside the supply. To model the dynamic behaviour of the supply a linear model must
be identiﬁed, but before this can be done measurements of the noise from the power supply must
be made.
3.3.1
Model Identiﬁcation
In practical applications, a class-D ampliﬁer will be powered by an SMPS to maintain a high
efﬁciency throughout the system. To try and recreate this set-up a 30V 3A laboratory SMPS9 is
used. Because the ripple is load dependant, the power supply was loaded, so it delivered 60 W
during the measurements. 60 W was selected as it approximately halfway to the maximum power
the ampliﬁer can deliver. The measurements were performed with the audio analyse because of its
low noise ﬂoor (4.35 µVrms), high resolution ADC (24-bit) and bandwidth up to 250 kHz. In total
four measurements with a duration of 0.2 s were made. Figure 3.18 shows a snippet of one of the
measurements (a), and the resulting Power Spectrum Density (PSD) (b).

To describe the system an AutoRecursive Moving Average (ARMA) model is used. This is
an external model with the form:
A(q−1)yt = C(q−1)et
(3.19)
where A and C are the parameter polynomials in the forward shift operator q−1, yt is the output
of the system, and et is an uncorrelated stochastic process entering the system. Thus the ARMA
model is a model suited for identiﬁcation of systems which is driven only by noise. The model is a
discrete-time polynomial model which makes it suitable for estimation as it can be used directly
with simple iterative minimization methods, i.e. Extended Least Squares and Maximum Likelihood
[36]. The identiﬁcation methods will not be discussed in this thesis, but the reader is referred to
[36] and [28] for an in-depth coverage of the subject.
When identifying a model the order of the ARMA model must be speciﬁed in advance. In
general, it is desirable to use a low order model to avoid overﬁtting. However, the model order still
needs to capture all the relevant dynamics. From the PSD we saw that the noise was attenuated
with 20 dB per decade. Hence models with orders going from 1 to 3 have been identiﬁed. Each
time the model order was increased it was veriﬁed that the new model performed better than the
lower order model through a statistical analysis. It was found that moving from a second order to

4.1
Cost Function
The controller presented in this thesis will be a two-part controller; An inner loop made with
a Linear Quadratic Regulator and an outer loop made with a PI-controller. The purpose of the
LQR is to shape the linear dynamics of the ampliﬁer to provide the PI-controller with the best
possible conditions to correct nonlinearities and improve tracking. However, to design the LQR, it
is necessary to understand how the LQR should shape the system such that a cost function can be
derived. In general three properties of the LQR are desired:
• Preservation of steady-state gain.
• Sufﬁcient damping of all complex pole pairs.
• Low control effort.
The open-loop gain of the ampliﬁer should remain the same after the LQR is implemented. This
prevents the PI-controller from wasting control effort on re-establishing the gain of the ampliﬁer.
Thus being able to use more effort on correcting nonlinearities. However, the maximum allowed
effort of the PI-controller is limited by the resonances in the system. As the controller gains of the

between the PI and the LQR. Therefore it is important that the LQR can use a limited amount of
effort such that more effort can be used by the PI-controller to correct the nonlinearities.

4.2
Modulator Integration
In the previous section, the requirements for the LQR to preserve the open-loop gain and damp
the system was addressed, while the control effort was not touched upon. From the condition in
equation (4.3), it is known that the control effort enforced by the LQR will go to zero as the system
reaches a steady-state. However, for an audio system, a steady-state is only rarely reached, and
this property of the realized LQR cannot be used for much. Also, as the damping of the system is
increased, increasingly more control effort is needed during the transients to drive the system to
the right trajectory. Hence there is seemingly no good way to deliver damping to the system while
keeping the control effort at a minimum with the LQR controller.
The problem with the control effort is that it is shared between the LQR and the PI-controller, such
that if one of the controllers uses up a lot of effort the other cannot. This is a limiting factor in all
control designs and places an upper bound on the achievable performance. However, if there are
some way to either increase the maximum available control effort or separate the control effort of
the two controllers, the achievable performance can be increased, and a better sound reproduction
by the audio system can be obtained.
4.2.1
Integration
A way to both increase and separate the available control effort of the two controllers could be
to integrate the LQR control loop into the AIM. The AIM has the important property of having its
carrier limited by the hysteresis window. This prevents the carrier from ever reaching the voltage
limit, because merely reaching the hysteresis window will cause the switch in the comparator and
the carrier will move the opposite direction. Therefore, if the LQR can be integrated into the AIM
the problem with clipping due to reaching the voltage limit will disappear, practically removing the
control effort limitation. The circuit in Figure 4.2 shows how the LQR can be integrated into the
AIM.

4.3
Loudspeaker Estimation
Since a linear quadratic regulator will be used for controlling the ampliﬁer, it is of interest
to investigate the possibilities of including estimates of the loudspeaker to expand and improve
the control. Estimating states is often a complex task and is most of the time done on a MCU or
DSP. This is convenient since most audio systems today have a small DSP inside. However, as
efﬁciency is paramount for a class-D ampliﬁer, the addition of the estimation must not lead to
a signiﬁcant increase in the losses. Moreover, the DSP’s inside the audio systems are often not
too powerful, so only a limited amount of computations can be made during a sample period. To
meet this requirement, a simple estimation method must be used. Thus a stationary predictive
Kalman ﬁlter as presented in section 2.4.2 will be used for the investigation since this is the most
straightforward linear optimal estimator.

observable. Observable means that the provided measurements to the estimator contain information

Sampling Limitations and Nonlinearities
It is unlikely that the used audio DSP in a consumer audio system will be able to sample
and process signals faster than 48 kHz. This sample frequency is sufﬁcient to represent audio
information, but it will not be enough to capture the dynamics of the output ﬁlter of the ampliﬁer
satisfactory. Thus an analog LQR would still be needed to correct the linear behaviour of the
ampliﬁer, and the estimations would more or less exclusively be used to address the dynamics
of the loudspeaker. Nonetheless, the dynamics of the ampliﬁer is still needed in the estimator,
although they will be of limited use.

both are highly nonlinear. One way to accommodate this problem to some degree is to manually

4.3.3
Usage
The primary reason for estimating the loudspeaker dynamics is to correct the nonlinearities
emerging from the loudspeaker. This could be achieved by using a PID-controller with the estimate
of the diaphragm position. However, as we just saw, not all the nonlinearities can be corrected

using a Kalman ﬁlter, which makes the method less appealing. Furthermore, a PID-controller
would also only have a limited effect on the nonlinearities. Because the mechanical resonance of
the loudspeaker is at a low frequency ( 100 Hz) it would not be possible to make a particularly
fast PID-controller and its tracking capabilities at frequencies just a few times larger than the
mechanical resonance frequency would be close to zero.
Another reason to use the estimation could be to reduce the resonance peak of the mechani-
cal resonance such that no oscillations would occur. This could have a positive impact on the
performance for large loudspeakers and subwoofers which can produce sound below a 100 Hz.
However, for smaller full-tone units the mechanical resonance can help to reproduce some of the
low frequency content which would have been attenuated if the mechanical resonance peak had
been reduced.
From the analysis and arguments presented above, the usage of a non-invasive linear estima-
tor, in this case, a stationary predictive Kalman ﬁlter, in an audio system to estimate the loudspeaker
behaviour is hard to justify for most applications. The relative improvement obtained from adding
an estimator with a controller does not add up with the signiﬁcant increase in complexity, cost and
losses this would cause. The only exception to this would be for use with a subwoofer. Here both
eliminating the mechanical resonance peak and using a PID-controller will make sense because of
the limited frequency range of the subwoofer.

5.1
Design Procedure
For both of the control conﬁgurations above the main objectives are to: Reject nonlinearities, re-
duce noise on the output, and ﬂatten the frequency response by eliminating resonances. Among the
three objectives, the PI-controller excels at reducing the effect of nonlinearities due to its integrator
term and its error based correction. Moreover, the PI-controller is also able to reduce noise, but it has
limited capabilities when it comes to eliminating resonances. However, this is where the LQR shine.
To have a streamlined design procedure for all the control designs, the PI-controller will be
designed by minimizing the ITAE performance measure. The reference step used with the ITAE is
the step of a 2nd order low pass ﬁlter with ζ = 0.707 and a cutoff frequency of up to the open-loop
cutoff frequency, e.g. fc = 60kHz. Figure 5.3 shows the reference step.

Controller 2 - LQR-PI-Controller
For the LQR-PI-controller the γ value of the LQR also needs to be determined. This could be
done by including it directly in the minimization process of the ITAE as an extra variable, however,
doing this would make the design a completely black-box, where no insight into the interaction
of the LQR and PI-controller would be revealed. Thus, to reveal the interaction of the LQR and
PI-controller the γ parameter is swept from 0.2 to 2 using 200 steps and a PI-controller is found for
each point. If the PI-controller should be found using a heuristic each time this process would take
hours 3. Therefore another faster approach has been developed.
For the ﬁrst iteration of the sweep, the PI-controller is found using a PSO like in section 5.2.
However, for all consecutive iterations, the PI-controller gains are found using Sequential Quadratic
Programming (SQP) initialized with the controller gains found in the previous iteration. This can
be done because of the relatively small change in the γ parameter for each iteration. The small
change in γ will result in a small change of the system and therefore also only a small change
in the PI-controller gains. Hence the controller gains can be found in the near neighbourhood
of the previously found controller. The SQP optimization routine is used because it can perform
extremely fast and reliable when it is initialized closed to the minimizer [47]. Lastly, as an extra
guard, the search space of the SQP is constrained by only allowing the controller gains to change a
few percents from the previously found gains4.
For the heuristic, the PSO is used again with the same conditions as was used in section 5.2.

CHAPTER 6
Measurements
In this chapter the measurement results made with the prototype ampliﬁers are pre-
sented and discussed. Moreover, the used measurement setup for the two loudspeakers
will be explained. Measurements showing THD+N, SNR and frequency responses
are presented and the two control conﬁgurations are compared to each other and a
open-loop ampliﬁer to assess the relative improvements obtain from using different
kind of control. Lastly, time-series data will be presented to show how the distortion
of the system impacts the signal ﬁdelity with the different conﬁgurations.
6.1
Measurement Setup
In total four different measurements will be made for each of the loudspeakers. The mea-
surements are: THD+N, SNR, frequency response, and time-series measurements. Of these four
measurements the SNR and time-series are made with the same setup for both loudspeakers, while
the THD+N and frequency response will be made on different setups. The SNR is measured at 1
kHz with a resistive load. A resistive load is used instead of a loudspeaker, to avoid accidentally
blowing up the loudspeaker due to the high power outputs needed for the measurements. The
time-series measurements are done for the voltage output (vspk). Hence it can be made by using a
function generator and an oscilloscope.
The measurement setups for the frequency response and the THD+N measurements for the full-tone
unit and the subwoofer is presented below.
6.1.1
Full-Tone Unit
The full-tone unit will be measured using a microphone in an anechoic chamber. This method
is used to capture the actual effect the controllers have on the produced audio. The loudspeaker will
be placed in a wall to separate the front and backside of the loudspeaker. This setup is called inﬁnite
bafﬂe and prevents interference from the sound waves produced on the backside of the loudspeaker
from inﬂuencing the measurements, resulting in measurement results which are more representative

for the dynamics of the loudspeaker. For the measurements the microphone a placed one meter
away from the loudspeaker. The close proximity of the microphone helps prevent distortion of the

chamber used is a small (5m x 5m) aged chamber, which limits its performance. However, the
chamber should be anechoic down to 200 Hz.
The loudspeaker will be connected to the prototype ampliﬁer which will also be placed in the

frequency of the laser is limited and measurements for frequencies larger than 500 Hz cannot be
trusted. This makes the laser ideal for the subwoofer which is designed to typically work below
250 Hz. The laser will be used for both the frequency response and the THD+N measurements.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
Four closed-loop full-bridge class-D audio ampliﬁers have been designed and tested. The
ampliﬁers are made to work with either a full-tone unit or a subwoofer, and the control loop
has been designed to the speciﬁc loudspeaker. Two types of controllers have been designed for
the ampliﬁer. The ﬁrst is a conventional PI-controller and the other is the proposed multivariate
controller, that combines an LQR control-loop and an PI-controller in an optimal manner. The
control designs are based on linear models derived for the ampliﬁer and the loudspeakers.
The theoretical results suggested that a signiﬁcant improve in both the distortion and noise could
be expected with the proposed design compared to the PI-controller. The proposed controller have
noise ﬁgures up to 4 times better than the PI-controller and 15 times better than open-loop and
almost 3 times the bandwidth of the PI-controller.
To validate the theory, the ampliﬁers were implemented on PCB’s and multiple measurements
quantifying the distortion, noise and frequency response were made to verify the controllers perfor-
mance and the models used during the design.
The measurements showed that the proposed controller performed as good and at some points up
to 6 times better than the PI-controller for the THD+N measurements, but 1dB worse on the SNR
measurements. Investigations into the matter suggested that the results were inﬂuenced by noise
coupling on the feedback path of the controllers, which degraded the results. This was further
veriﬁed by inspecting some time-series which clearly showed a superior tracking capability of the
proposed controller.
The frequency response measurements showed that both the controllers were able to suppress the
mechanical resonance of the loudspeaker although the controllable output was the output voltage
of the ampliﬁer. Also the limited bandwidth of the PI-controller resulted in an attenuation of the
higher frequencies in the audio domain, where the proposed controller design was able to truthfully
reproduce the audio.

While the prototype was not able to obtain the performance expected from the theory, it was
still superior to the PI-controller. The aim of this thesis was to show that a performance gain in
regard to audio ﬁdelity could be obtained by using a controller able to adjust the dynamics of the
ampliﬁer/loudspeaker system before implementing a PI-controller, which has been successfully
achieved.

CHAPTER 8
Future Work
During this thesis multiple new questions and ideas regarding the control of class-D ampliﬁer, or
a complete audio system, has risen from the work performed. Unfortunately, due to time limitations
and to constrain the thesis, the pursuit to answer these question was put on hold. However, looking
into these questions could lead to even better controllers, simpler implementations or a faster design
procedure.
Different Cost Function
It was seen in section 5.3 that the optimal solutions for the LQR-PI controllers often exists
outside the range where stability is guaranteed by the Ricatti equation. While this turned out
not to be a problem, it is not the best of practices. Therefore it could be relevant to look for
modiﬁcations to the current use of the cost function like investigating if adjusting the R1 and
N matrix instead of R2 would still cause the problem. Another possibility would be to look at
implementing augmentations to the state-space model prior to the control design to obtain an
optimal PI-controller directly using the LQR routine. Methods for some of these augmentations
was found in [43] a few month into the project work.
Integration of P-term
During the work with integrating the LQR into the AIM it was found that a similar trick could
be employed to a P-controller such that the same beneﬁts could be achieved. However, caution was
needed in not making the P-term too large as the slew rate is limited by the supply voltage of the
ampliﬁer. Because of this, and time limitations, it was decided to put the method on hold until a
sufﬁcient mathematical foundation has been derived. Nonetheless, this could be of great interest as
it could also be used with the PI-Controller to further increase the integrator gain by moving the
control energy of the P-controller into the modulator. Hence further improving the performance of
the LQR-PI controller conﬁguration.

CHAPTER 8. FUTURE WORK
Alternative Kalman Implementations
The stationary predictive Kalman ﬁlter turned out not to handle the nonlinearities caused by the
loudspeaker well, resulting in a reduction in the quality of the estimations. The bad handling of
nonlinearities is an inherent disadvantages for linear observers and to solve this pseudo-linear or
non-linear observers would need to be used. The observers could be an Extended Kalman Filter
(EKF) which is the nonlinear counterpart to the standard linear Kalman ﬁlter. However, the EKF
performs multiple updates each iteration and could be too computational heavy for the DSP’s
typically used in consumer audio devices. Another approach could be to use gain scheduling.
Here Kalman ﬁlters and controllers are designed for multiple linearization points and stored on
the DSP. Then each Kalman ﬁlter will only be used in a small neighbourhood of it linearization
point and switch to the next linearization point with the next Kalman ﬁlter when moving away
from the neighbourhood. This will not necessarily be much more computational expensive than the
stationary predictive Kalman ﬁlter, but will require storage for all the models.
However, none of these alternative approaches would solve the problem of not being able to
observe the force factor, but the force factor in the internal model would be improved to better
represent the reality.
Selection of Controlled Output
For this thesis it was decided to use the voltage across the loudspeaker as the controllable output
for the controller. The voltage was selected because it is the most common signal to control within
conventional switch-mode systems. Nonetheless it could be interesting to use the current through
the loudspeaker as the controllable output instead. The mechanical domain of the loudspeaker
is driven by the current, so by controlling the current a better access to these dynamics could be
achieved. This would mean that the controller would be able to perform better correction of the
nonlinearities due to the position of the diaphragm.
