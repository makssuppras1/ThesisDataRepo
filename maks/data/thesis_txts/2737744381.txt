Abstract 
This project investigated the synthesis and characterization of a compound consisting of the antiviral 
drug acyclovir, ACV, and the cleavable linker Val-Cit-PAB-OH, to be used as part of a prodrug for 
delivery over the blood-brain barrier, BBB.  
ACV and linker were joined via a carbonate, which necessitated protection of primary amines on both 
molecules. N-protecting ACV with Cbz gave no reaction, though protection with MMTr worked well, 
with a yield of 63%. Comparison of different Fmoc protection conditions on the linker amine found 
that the best yield in comparison to purity was obtained when protecting with Fmoc-OSu and 
purifying by crystallization, resulting in a yield of 57% and a mass purity of 94%. Synthesis with 
Fmoc-Cl resulted in a higher purity than Fmoc-OSu, but a significantly lower yield, 23%. 
To make a carbonate, para-nitrophenol carbonate, PNP, was used as an activation group. The group 
added easily onto ACV, producing a yield of 82%. In comparison, adding PNP to the linker was a 
slower reaction, with a lower conversion, and was found to be unstable to flash column 
chromatography in DCM and methanol. Adding PNP on ACV was judged the better synthetic route. 
Coupling of Fmoc-linker and PNP-ACV-MMTr was found to work best under basic conditions. 
Separation of product from the starting materials was problematic, with the best achieved mass purity 
being 81%, and the reaction yield being 41%. 
Fmoc deprotection to produce the final compound likewise showed separation difficulties from 
impurities in the previous step. Purification via HPLC was attempted, but found unsuitable due to the 
compound’s low solubility in HPLC solvents. Flash column chromatography worked, provided no 
impurities of the Fmoc protected linker remained. Linker-ACV-MMTr was produced in 35% yield 
and is ready for use in future steps of the prodrug synthesis. 
A test reaction of MMTr deprotection from ACV was carried out and showed full cleavage after 4 
hours.

1 Introduction 
Efficient delivery of pharmaceuticals across the blood-brain barrier, BBB, is an ongoing challenge 
in medicinal chemistry. [1], [2] One method of overcoming this barrier is the application of 
prodrugs, drugs with chemical modifications that are transformed into their active form under 
certain physiological or chemical conditions, such as during metabolism, at a specific pH, or in the 
presence of specific enzymes. [3], [4] 
This project explores the initial steps in creating BBB penetrating prodrugs of acyclovir, ACV, an 
antiviral drug used against herpes simplex infection, including brain infection from herpes simplex 
encephalitis. [5], [6] 
The proposed prodrugs consist of four key elements. Firstly, ACV itself, the active pharmaceutical 
ingredient to be delivered to the brain. Secondly, a BBB penetrating ligand to bring ACV across 
the BBB. Two such ligands are proposed – the cell-penetrating peptide Tat, and a newly synthesized 
aptamer made to target the transferrin receptor on the BBB. [7], [8] Third, a cleavable linker 
between ACV and the BBB penetrating group, to be removed and release ACV after crossing the 
BBB. Val-Cit-PAB-OH was used. [9], [10] And finally, a fluorescence tag, to make the prodrug 
visible in fluorescence imaging.  
The focus of this project is the synthesis and characterization of the ACV-linker compound, with 
the ligands and fluorescence tag to be added at a later date. Starting from ACV and Val-Cit-PAB-
OH, different synthetic routes, protection strategies, and reaction conditions were tested to optimize 
yields.

2 Scientific Background 
This section presents the most relevant background information for the project.  
 
2.1 The Blood-Brain Barrier 
2.1.1 Structure and Function 
The blood-brain barrier, BBB, refers to the layer of endothelial cells separating the bloodstream 
from the central nervous system, CNS. Its purpose is to regulate the exchange of substances 
between the bloodstream and the CNS. Blood vessels are very permeable to many chemicals, 
including ones that could potentially be damaging to the brain. The BBB protects the neural tissue 
of the brain from coming into contact with toxins from the bloodstream. While this barrier is 
essential for normal brain functioning, bypassing it poses a challenge for the delivery of drugs 
targeting the brain. [1], [2], [3], [4], [11], [12] 
 
The interface between the bloodstream and the CNS consists of three parts: 
The blood-brain barrier, BBB, which consists of endothelial cells with tight junctions lining the 
walls of the capillaries in the brain and spine. The BBB has a large surface area, between 12 and 
18 m2 for an adult human, and is the main exchange site between blood and CNS. 
The blood-cerebrospinal fluid barrier, BCSFB, which is formed of epithelial cells in the choroid 
plexus of the brain. The BCSFB separates the cerebrospinal fluid of the brain from the extracellular, 
or interstitial, fluid brain. 
The arachnoid epithelium lies beneath the dura, the outer membrane of the brain. It separates the 
surface of the brain from the rest of the body. This barrier layer is avascular, and has a relatively 
small surface area, which means that no significant exchange between blood and CNS happens 
over the arachnoid epithelium. [1] 
 
The BBB regulates brain intake of solutes in several ways. Firstly, as a physical barrier. The tight 
junctions between the endothelial cells in the BBB significantly reduce permeation of polar solutes 
through paracellular diffusion between the endothelial cells from the blood plasma to the brain 
==PAGE:10==
3 
 
extracellular fluid. Secondly, as a transport barrier, with transport mechanisms mediating solute 
flux over the barrier. And finally, as a metabolic barrier, with enzymes metabolizing molecules in 
transit. [1], [3] 
The BBB controls the ion content in the brain. Neurons in the CNS communicate via a mixture of 
chemical and electrical signals, and precise regulation of the microenvironment is needed for 
efficient signaling. [3], [11], [12] Cerebrospinal fluid, CSF, has lower concentrations of K+ and 
Ca2+, and higher concentrations of Mg2+ than is found in the bloodstream. The BBB also regulates 
pH and keeps out macromolecules. The protein and amino acid content in CSF is much lower than 
in plasma and has a totally different composition. [1], [3] 
 
Transport across the BBB happens in several ways: 
Since the barrier consists of plasma membranes, lipid soluble molecules, such as alcohols or 
anesthetics, may pass through via passive diffusion. Lipid solubility is correlated to rate of entry 
into the CNS. [1], [4], [12] Blood gasses like O2 and CO2 likewise cross the BBB via diffusion, 
moving along their concentration gradients. Oxygenation of the brain is dependent on blood flow, 
not on the BBB. [1], [4] 
Entry over BBB is restricted for molecules with high polar surface area (over 80 Å2), or ones that 
form 6 or more hydrogen bonds, since this increases the free energy requirement for going from 
aqueous phase to lipid membrane. Having many rotatable bonds or a mass over 450 Da also restricts 
BBB permeability. Most small molecule drugs, and all large molecule drugs, fall inside these 
characteristics, and are thus unable to pass the barrier. [1], [2], [3] 
Not all polar molecules need to be kept out of the brain. Some, such as glucose, are essential to 
brain function. Small polar nutrients can be transported over the BBB by solute carriers. This is 
called carrier-mediated transport. [1], [4] 
Macromolecules like peptides or proteins primarily cross the BBB via transcytosis. This can 
happen in two ways. Receptor-mediated transcytosis happens when a macromolecular ligand binds 
a receptor on the BBB cell surface, triggering endocytosis. The enclosed macromolecule is then 
routed across the cytoplasm and released inside the BBB via exocytosis. Adsorptive-mediated 
transcytosis can happen with positively charged molecules. Cationic interactions with the 
negatively charged cell surface triggers endocytosis. [1], [2], [4] 
==PAGE:11==
4 
 
Even if a molecule manages to cross the BBB, there is no guarantee of it staying inside the brain. 
Active transport proteins are used to pump compounds, including lipid soluble ones, out of the 
brain. This is primarily done by ATP-binding cassette transporters, ABC-transporters. Many drugs 
are substrates of ABC transporters. [1], [4], [13] 
 
2.1.2 Strategies for Delivering Molecules Through the BBB 
The BBB is a favored drug target for drug delivery to the brain. No brain cell is further than about 
25 μm from a capillary, so once the BBB is crossed, diffusion distances to neurons and glial cell 
bodies for solutes and drugs are short. Targeting a drug across the BBB leads to swift global 
delivery to all brain cells. There are many approaches to how to deliver pharmaceuticals through 
the BBB. [1], [3]  
 
The tight regulation of the BBB prevents most drugs from passing directly from the bloodstream 
to the brain. One strategy for increasing transport over the BBB is to make the molecule more 
lipophilic, to enable crossing via passive diffusion. If crossing from systemic circulation is possible, 
drugs can be administered via intravenous injection and still reach the brain. However, this strategy 
does not always work, since ABC transporters may efflux the drug after it crosses the BBB. [1], 
[14] Additionally, increasing lipophilicity may inhibit the drug’s biological effect, and increase 
retention time in non-target organs, which can lead to increased side effects. [3] 
A way to cross via lipophilicity without modifications to the drug is by use of liposomes. A 
liposome consists of a lipid bilayer with an aqueous compartment inside for carrying the drug. 
Liposomes have high biocompatibility and biodegradability and can be modified with targeting 
ligands. Micelles can carry drugs, proteins or genes. [3], [4] 
Another option is to bypass the issue entirely by direct injection into the CSF, or intracranial 
implantation of slow-release drug carriers. These methods are considered invasive and carry a risk 
of exposure or damage to the brain, which limits their long-term usefulness. [2], [3], [14] 
A more commonly used approach is ligand conjugation. This consists of conjugating a drug to a 
ligand with high specificity for a receptor on BBB endothelial cells. This brings the drug to the 
BBB, and helps to cross it, though a method of releasing the drug from the ligand is still necessary. 
==PAGE:12==
5 
 
Examples include conjugating to transferrin-, insulin-, LDL-, or folate receptors. [2], [3], [14] More 
on this in the next subsection.  
Another way to pass drugs through is by weakening the BBB. This can be done in several ways. 
One way is to administer agents that downregulate the expression of tight-junction proteins, which 
enables drugs to cross via paracellular pathways instead of transcellular ones. [3] Laser irradiation 
can also cause BBB structure breakdown. The effect peaks after 2 hours and persists for about 24 
hours. Breakdown from this method is highly localized to the targeted area of brain. The effect of 
laser irradiation can be difficult to control, as it is highly dependent on power and distance. 
Prolonged laser irradiation also causes temperature increases in the surrounding tissue. [3] While 
damaging the BBB integrity may allow a drug to cross over, it comes at the marked disadvantage 
of potentially also allowing undesired solutes to cross from systemic circulation into the brain. 
Some drugs can be delivered via intranasal administration. This bypasses BBB via nose-to-brain 
pathways. The route from nose to brain is relatively short, and delivery is fast. However, dosing is 
difficult, due to elimination and the possibility of entering systemic circulation. Also, this 
administration route may not be applicable in patients with colds or allergies. [3] 
 
2.1.2.1 Cell-penetrating peptides and Tat peptide 
Cell-penetrating peptides, CPP’s, is a group of peptides capable of crossing biological membranes 
in various ways. CPP’s are usually short peptides, about 5-30 amino acids, water soluble, and 
nontoxic.  [7], [8] CPP’s have the ability to cross cell membranes without causing lethal membrane 
damage. [15] Between that and their lack of toxicity, they make for a noninvasive and efficient 
approach to transportation over membranes. [7] 
CPP’s are useful in drug development, both as targeting molecules and as drug carriers. 
Conjugation of a drug to a CPP, either covalently or noncovalently, can be used for increasing drug 
delivery into tissues or cellular compartments that are otherwise challenging to reach. This is 
especially interesting for treatment of brain- or CNS-related diseases, where successful drug 
delivery requires crossing the BBB. CPP’s have been shown to successfully cross endothelial 
barriers, and can reach sites of pathology in brain. [7], [8]  
CPP’s can come from several sources, and their origins are usually characterized as protein derived, 
chimeric, or synthetic. Protein derived CPP’s consist of the minimum effective partial sequence of 
==PAGE:13==
6 
 
a larger translocation protein, also called protein transduction domains. Chimeric CPP’s consist of 
domains from several different sources, combined to make a new peptide with the desired 
properties. Synthetic CPP’s are usually modified versions of naturally occurring CPP’s. 
Modifications can be made to optimize factors like stability or uptake. [7] 
CPP structures are categorized into three groups. Cationic, amphipathic, and hydrophobic. [7], [15] 
Cationic CPP’s have high positive net charges at physiological pH. They are rich in positively 
charged residues, primarily Arg and Lys. [7], [15] The positively charged CPP’s interact with the 
negative charge of cell membranes, and cause uptake through endocytosis. Amphipathic CPP’s 
consist of a hydrophilic and a lipophilic domain for interaction with membranes. Hydrophobic 
CPP’s are rich in apolar residues, and have a low net charge. [7], [15], [16] 
CPP entry pathways include energy-independent direct diffusion across plasma membranes, and 
energy-dependent endocytosis. Direct diffusion mainly occurs at high peptide concentrations. 
Energy-independent pathways include transient pore formation, and membrane destabilization. 
CPPs mainly enter cells via endocytic pathways, but it is possible for the same peptide to utilize 
multiple different pathways. A major challenge in CPP design is to prevent the peptide, and any 
conjugated drugs, from being trapped in an endosome. Therapeutic efficiency requires endosomal 
escape, and crossing the BBB requires exocytosis on the other side of the BBB endothelial cells. 
[7], [8] 
 
While CPP’s hold great potential as drug delivery systems, some difficulties remain. In any kind 
of targeted delivery system, specificity to the target is essential. Making the peptide target-specific 
while still maintaining the penetration properties is a challenge. Toxicity must also be carefully 
examined. CPP’s may suffer from poor biocompatibility or bioavailability, which reduces the 
efficacy of delivery. CPP’s are also at risk of degradation by endosomes proteasomes. Metabolic 
stability and plasma half-life can be extended by incorporation of non-natural amino acids, or D 
amino acids. [7]

Tat peptide 
CPP research started in earnest in 1988 with the discovery of Tat peptide, a derivative of 
transcription-transactivating peptide found in HIV virus. [7] Transcription-transactivating peptide 
is an 86 amino acid CPP involved in replication of HIV virus. It has the ability to pass through 
plasma membranes into the host cell nucleus, and transactivates the HIV viral genome.  [3], [17], 
[18] 
In HIV patients, Tat is known to be involved in HIV-associated neurocognitive disorders. Tat 
reduces BBB integrity by lowering the expression of tight junction proteins and upregulating 
proapoptotic proteins. This lowered integrity leads to neurological symptoms. [18] 
For therapeutic cell-penetrating purposes, at truncated version of Tat which only contains the 
translocation domain is used. Residues 48-60 are often used, though some variation on the 
truncation is possible. [8], [7] [15]  
Tat is a cationic CPP, consisting primarily of charged residues. Tat uptake happens both via direct 
penetration and endocytosis. The uptake of Tat is endocytosis-driven at all concentrations, but 
starting from a certain threshold concentration, the peptide directly penetrates into the cell [15]. 
Tat peptide has a high unidirectional influx through BBB in vivo. [15] Unlike the full protein, 
truncated Tat is nontoxic to neuronal cells, which makes its ability to cross the BBB an advantage 
rather than a problem. Tat can and has been used as a targeting drug as well as a penetrating one, 
and has been shown to successfully deliver conjugated nanoparticles to the CNS without damaging 
the BBB’s integrity. [8]

2.1.2.2 Tf aptamer 
The Tf aptamer is an RNA aptamer designed for targeting the transferrin (Tf) receptor on the BBB.  
 
Aptamers are single-stranded oligonucleotides, (DNA, RNA), or short peptides. They fold to 
tertiary structures that can bind target molecules with high affinity and selectivity. This makes them 
useful targeting molecules. Aptamers have molecular recognition properties similar to antibodies, 
but have several advantages compared to antibodies, including ease of synthesis, smaller 
size/weight, lack of immunogenicity, and ease of modification. Aptamers can be used against many 
targets including small molecules, nucleic acids, proteins, bacteria and viruses, cell lines and even 
whole cells or tissues. [19], [20] 
Aptamers are selected by a universal process called “Systematic Evolution of Ligands by 
EXponential Enrichment” (SELEX). SELEX is an iterative process of binding, partitioning and 
amplification. The process starts by using a library of synthetic oligonucleotides of about 106 
sequences, each with a random region flanked by two known binding regions. The library is 
incubated with the target molecule, and possibly with a fluorescence- or radio tag for monitoring 
the binding. Partitioning separates aptamers that bound the target from ones that did not.  This can 
be done in several ways, including affinity chromatography, membrane filtration, and 
centrifugation. Target-bound oligos are eluted, and amplified by PCR for DNA, or RT-PCR for 
==PAGE:16==
9 
 
RNA. The amplified nucleotides are then used to make a new library. This is repeated for several 
cycles, with each cycle selecting and amplifying the sequences with the highest binding affinities. 
[19], [21] 
Cell-SELEX is done on live cells, and is useful for finding aptamers with binding affinity for targets 
on the cell surface. This has the advantage of examining binding in the cells’ native conformation.  
A challenge with cell-SELEX is targeting proteins that are less expressed on the cell surface. [19] 
In cross-over SELEX, both a purified protein and the cells expressing it are used as targets. The 
first few selection cycles are run on cells. The aptamer pool from the cells is then incubated with 
purified protein for the next selection cycles. Two such cycles have demonstrated an over 50-fold 
increase in binding affinity compared to the cell-SELEX. [19] 
 
The primary function of the transferrin protein is iron transport throughout the body. [22], [23] 
Transferrin receptors (TfR) are overexpressed on brain capillary endothelial cells (the BBB). As 
such, the receptor can be used as an anchor for targeting the BBB. Conjugating drugs directly to 
transferrin leads to competitive inhibition from endogenous transferrin. This reduces the likelihood 
of drug uptake, and interferes with the brain’s iron transport. Targeting the TfR instead of Tf itself 
gives the possibility of binding at an allosteric site of the receptor, so Tf binding is not interrupted. 
[16], [23], [24] 
When Tf binds to the TfR on the cell surface, both Tf and receptor enter the cell via endocytosis. 
Endosomes have a slightly acidic pH, which promotes release of iron from transferrin. The unbound 
iron can then cross the endosomal membrane and becomes available in the cytosol. Meanwhile, the 
iron-depleted Tf-TfR complex undergoes retro-endocytosis to return to the luminal membrane, 
where Tf is released from the receptor and returns to circulation. [22], [24] Binding to the Tf 
receptor via the Tf aptamer thus allows for drug transport into the BBB endosome. Drugs designed 
for endosomal cleavage can then be released into the cell. 
 
The Tf aptamer used in this project is new and unpublished. It consists of 26 RNA nucleotides, 
where all the uracil residues have been modified to have fluorine on the 2’ position. The fluorine 
modification makes the RNA more resistant to nuclease degradation. [23] In addition to the 
==PAGE:17==
10 
 
oligomer, a carboxy modifier is added on the 5’ end, to be used for drug conjugation. Structure of 
the aptamer and carboxy modifier can be seen on figure 3.

2.2 Cleavable Linkers 
Prodrugs are modified drugs, that are designed to change into their active form upon metabolism 
or reaction conditions at a specific target. Once the target conditions are met, the prodrug changes 
into the actual drug. 
When using ligand conjugation to cross the BBB, the next challenge is how to release the drug 
once in the desired location. One way of doing this is to make a prodrug via cleavable linkers. [3] 
Such a linker has two attachment points – one for the targeting ligand and one for the drug payload. 
Cleaving the linker results in release of the drug. [9] 
For ligand-linker-drug conjugates to be efficient, the linker should be stable in circulation, soluble 
in water, and able to efficiently release the drug payload. In circulation, carbonate bound linkers 
are more stable than ester bonds. The stability can be further improved by adding a para-
aminobenzyl (PAB) spacer. Carbonates show some selectivity for the acidic conditions in 
lysosomes. [9], [25] 
Cleavable linkers are divided into two subgroups, called chemically- and enzyme cleavable linkers. 
Chemically cleavable linkers are cleaved under specific chemical conditions. Examples include 
==PAGE:18==
11 
 
being acid cleavable at a specific pH, to cleave in the lysosomes’ pH of 5, but not in plasma’s pH 
7.4. Another example of this is cleavage via reduction, which is often seen in disulfides, which 
cleave in the presence of thiols. Enzyme cleavable linkers are cleaved by specific enzymes. 
Depending on the enzyme, they often have a higher specificity than the chemically cleavable 
linkers. [9] 
 
For efficient cleavage, linkers must be able to differentiate 
between circulation conditions and target cell conditions. One 
way to achieve this is by use of dipeptide linkers. These 
linkers are often cleavable by cathepsin B, a cysteine protease 
that is relatively exclusive to the lysosomal compartment in 
healthy mammals. Cathepsin B cleaves dipeptides from 
linkers at their C-terminus. Some general trends are seen in 
cleavage rates with cathepsin B. The first residue from the C-
terminus must be hydrophilic, and hydrolysis rate increases 
with basicity. Arginine can be used, but citrulline is often preferred due to ease of synthesis, despite 
the lesser basicity. The second residue must be hydrophobic. This enables cleavage and increases 
plasma stability. Phe, Val, or Ala is used. [9], [10] 
A spacer unit can be added after the dipeptide to prevent steric hindrance at the cleavage site. A 
useful one is a PAB-carbonate or carbamate. Val-Cit-PAB carbonates are relatively stable in 
plasma, while being readily cleaved by cathepsin. Upon cleavage of the peptide, the carbonate 
undergoes 1,6-elimination to release the payload, CO2, and aza-quinone methide, see Scheme 1. 
[9], [10] Val-Cit-PAB carbamates have been used in several clinically approved antibody-drug 
conjugates. [10]

2.3 Acyclovir 
Acyclovir (ACV) is an antiviral drug used against herpes simplex virus (HSV) types 1 and 2, as 
well as varicella-zoster virus. [13], [26], [27] These double-stranded DNA viruses are among the 
most common pathogens worldwide, and even almost 50 years after its discovery in 1977 and 
clinical approval in 1982, acyclovir is still held as the gold standard for antiherpetic drugs. [28], 
[29], [30] ACV is available in topical, oral, and intravenous formulations. [29] 
Acyclovir is a nucleoside analogue drug. It is distinguished from its parent nucleoside guanosine 
by having a 2-hydroxyethoxymethyl group side chain instead of a cyclic sugar residue, see Figure 
5.  
ACV has an anti-proliferative effect on HSV-1 and works as both an inhibitor and a substrate for 
HSV-1’s DNA polymerase. Its effectiveness and selectivity is caused by several factors. [26], [31] 
Firstly, HSV-1 infected cells have a higher uptake of ACV than healthy cells. Second, the active 
form of ACV is its triphosphate, ACV-TP. Phosphorylation of ACV into its monophosphate, ACV-
MP, in infected cells is catalyzed by thymidine kinase UL23 from HSV-1 virus, then 
phosphorylation into di- and triphosphates happen via host cell kinases. Almost no phosphorylation 
of ACV into ACV-MP takes place in uninfected cells. Finally, ACV-TP is both a substrate and a 
competitive inhibitor of HSV’s DNA polymerase, with ACV-TP being incorporated into viral DNA 
in place of deoxyguanosine triphosphate, dGTP. This leads to strand termination, since ACV does 
not have a 3’ position to continue the DNA elongation from. Cellular DNA polymerases have much 
lower affinity to ACV-TP than the HSV variety.[28], [29], [31], [32], [33] 
Between HSV-1 infected cells having a higher concentration of ACV-TP, viral thymidine kinase 
being necessary for initiating phosphorylation, and HSV-1’s DNA polymerase being more sensitive 
to ACV-TP than the host cell’s, there is a 3000-fold difference in the inhibitory dose for healthy 
cells and HSV-1 infected ones. [31]

Figure 5: Chemical structures of guanosine and acyclovir. 
 
In addition to the immediate disease symptoms, HSV virus is capable of lying latent in the sensory 
ganglia of the brain. [6], [28], [29], [32], [33] Since acyclovir and other related anti-herpetic drugs 
all target replication, they do not eradicate latent infection. [28], [29], [33] Reactivation of the virus 
can give recurring outbreaks and can cause herpes simplex encephalitis (HSE). Encephalitis is an 
inflammation of the brain tissue (called parenchyma). It is associated with neurologic dysfunction. 
Infection with HSV-1 virus is also the leading identified cause of encephalitis globally, and 
accounts for over a third of diagnosed cases in the US. [6], [29], [34] HSV-2 and varicella-zoster 
virus infections rarely cause encephalitis, but are leading causes of viral meningitis, an infection of 
the brain membranes rather than the brain itself. [6], [35] 
ACV therapy is standard practice in encephalitis. Intravenous ACV treatment is started when 
patient presents with encephalitis, and persists until HSV infection is disproven. Even with 
acyclovir therapy, mortality of HSE remains at 5-10%, and neurologic or cognitive deficits persist 
in 70% of survivors. [6], [13] 
 
Disadvantages of acyclovir include its poor oral bioavailability (10-30%), its limited water 
solubility, and its relatively short plasma half-life, only about 2-3 hours in healthy adults. [27], [28], 
[33] This leads to a need for rather high and frequent doses. Even though acyclovir is generally 
well tolerated, prolonged high doses can lead to neuro- and nephrotoxicity, though the damage is 
usually reversible. [34], [36] The neurotoxicity mainly comes from one of ACV’s metabolites, 9-
carboxymethoxymethylguanine. [37] Acyclovir-induced neuropsychiatric symptoms (AINS) can 
be difficult to estimate, since AINS symptoms are very similar to those of HSE. [13], [36], [37]

Several studies have been made into improving availability, solubility and stability via prodrugs or 
conjugation to polymers. There are several prodrugs of acyclovir on the market. [26], [27], [28],

2.4 Fluorescence Imaging 
Fluorescence is a form of radiative decay. It happens when a fluorophore enters an excited state via 
light absorption, falls to a lower energy state via nonradiative decay, and then returns to the ground 
state by emitting light. Fluorescence always has a lower energy, and therefore higher wavelength, 
than the absorbed light. [38], [39] 
 
Fluorescence imaging makes use of an external light source to excite fluorophores. The emitted 
fluorescence intensity is then measured and used to make an image. This can be done both in the 
plane (2D) or spatially (3D). This makes for a potentially powerful visualization tool on several 
counts. Firstly, fluorescence is highly sensitive. Imaging can be done at concentrations down to 
pico- or femto-molar. The very low doses needed to get high-contrast images makes for a low 
likelihood of toxicology issues in patients. It also reduces the cost. Second, fluorescence 
measurements are fast, and do not require post-processing. This makes it possible to do imaging in 
real-time. Imaging can be used on cell or tissue level. It is also possible to multiplex by using two 
or more different fluorophores. [40], [41] 
Imaging is typically done in the near-infrared (NIR) spectral range, between 650 and 1700nm. Few 
biomolecules have absorption or emission in the NIR area, so measuring in this range minimizes 
autofluorescence and light attenuation, and gives a better signal-to-background (S/B) ratio. [40], 
[42] Tissue transparency is highest in the near-infrared II, (NIR II), area, between 1000-1700nm 
where light can penetrate a few centimeters. [40], [41], [43] However, no NIR II dyes are approved 
for clinical use as of 2022. [40]  
A major challenge for fluorescence imaging is the limited penetration depth of light through tissue. 
Depending on wavelength, the penetration can be between µm to cm. Light attenuation from 
absorption and scattering in tissue gives approximately a 10-fold drop in light per cm tissue. [40], 
[44], [45] 
==PAGE:22==

Cyanine derivatives are the most used NIR I dyes in preclinical imaging. [40], [45] Cy7 is one of 
these dyes, so named for the total length of 7 carbons in its methine chain. The dye has its 
absorbance peak at 750nm, and emission peak at 773nm. [46] In addition to the main body of the 
dye molecule, the two nitrogen atoms are used for adding one or two linking moieties. Cy7 is 
commercially available with a number of different linkers. [47] Among these is the NHS ester, 
which is useful for labelling amine groups. Coupling an amine with an NHS ester forms an amide 
bond between the dye and the fluorescence labelled molecule.[40] Figure 6 shows the structure of

2.5 Protection Groups 
In synthetic chemistry, one often works with molecules with multiple possible reactive groups. This 
poses the issue of how to perform a reaction selectively, especially if reaction is desired on a less 
reactive moiety. One way of overcoming this challenge is by use of protection groups. [48], [49], 
[50] 
The core concept of a protection group is to block the protected moiety from reacting by turning it 
into another functional group. Once the main reaction is complete, the protection group can be 
cleaved. The most common protected groups in literature are ketones, aldehydes, amines, alcohols, 
carboxylic acids, and sulfides. [48] When working with multiple reaction sites that you want 
cleaved sequentially, several different protection groups with different cleavage conditions can be 
utilized. [50] In general, protection groups should only be used when necessary. Protection and 
deprotection adds two extra steps to the synthesis, which can be costly both in time and in yield.

2.6 Methodology 
This section outlines the theoretical background for the analytical methods used in the project.  
 
2.6.1 Chromatography 
Chromatography is a widely used separation technique, with many variations.  
At its core, chromatography works by having two phases – a solid stationary phase, and a liquid or 
gaseous mobile phase. The mobile phase runs through the stationary phase in a column, and 
different compounds will have a different degree of retention on the stationary phase. This leads to 
a separation in elution time from the column. [39] 
 
Liquid chromatography, LC, separates solutes via polarity. In normal phase LC, the column is 
packed with a stationary phase consisting of polar silica gel. The mobile phase is a liquid, and less 
polar than silica. A sample is added to the top of the column, and is flushed through with the mobile 
phase/eluent. This leads to the least polar compounds eluting first. The more polar a compound, 
the stronger the attraction to the stationary phase, and the longer the retention time on the column. 
The eluent is typically run on a gradient, getting more polar as time goes by in order to more quickly 
flush everything through the column. The exit of the column is connected to a detector, to determine 
when compounds elute. A common detection technique for liquid chromatography is to combine it 
with mass spectrometry, LC-MS. Optical sensors are also common, but are more limited in 
usefulness, since they only detect optically active compounds. [39] 
LC can also be run in reverse phase, where the mobile phase is more polar than the stationary one. 
In these columns, the most polar molecules elute first.  
 
High-performance liquid chromatography, HPLC, is a specialized form of LC. It works by the 
same principle of a stationary and a solid phase, separating by polarity. The main difference 
between LC and HPLC is in the particle size of the stationary phase. HPLC uses much smaller 
silica particles, which leads to a significant improvement in separation. However, the smaller the 
particle size, the more power is needed to move the mobile phase. In order to force eluent through 
the column, high pressure pumps are used. [39] 
==PAGE:29==
22 
 
 
2.6.2 Mass Spectrometry 
Mass spectrometry, MS, is a technique used for measuring the mass-to-charge (m/z) ratio of 
compounds. In MS, a sample is ionized, then sent flying through a mass analyzer, and onto a 
detector. MS is always done in a vacuum, to avoid collisions between ions and atmospheric air. 
[39], [56] 
MS falls into two overall categories – high- and low-resolution. The categorization is dependent 
on the mass accuracy, the smallest m/z difference that the machine can differentiate between. Low-
resolution machines have unit mass accuracy (±1 m/z differentiation) or worse. High-resolution 
MS has better than unit accuracy. [39], [56] 
Low-resolution MS typically uses a quadropole. A quadroplole consists of four poles, two with a 
positive charge, and two with a negative. Depending on the charge of the quadropole rods, some 
m/z ratios can be allowed to pass through its length in a stable trajectory, while others will be 
removed by hitting the sides or passing between the rods. The quadropole can be used either in full 
scan mode, measuring all ions in a sample, or in selective mode, examining specific m/z ratios only. 
Quadropoles work well between about 30-1000 m/z. [39] 
Two common high-resolution techniques are time-of-flight and orbitrap. In time-of-flight analysis, 
ions are set to flying in a long tube, and the time of flight is measured. When the initial kinetic 
energy and the flight time are both known, they can be used to calculate the mass. An orbitrap 
separates ions by oscillation in a magnetic field. m/z ratio can be determined from the frequency 
of oscillation. [39], [56] 
 
There are many methods of ionization in mass spectrometry. Two of these are electrospray 
ionization and MALDI.  
Electrospray ionization, ESI, is the most commonly used ionization method. Solvent is nebulized 
and exposed to an electric potential, which leaves ions in gas phase. ESI can be run both in positive 
and negative mode, creating ions of the corresponding charge. Ions are formed by uptake or release 
of protons to make [M+H] or [M-H] ions. [39], [56] 
==PAGE:30==
23 
 
MALDI is used on macromolecules, like proteins and nucleic acids. The sample is mixed with a 
matrix solution, and added onto a plate. The liquid is evaporated, leaving behind a thin layer of 
compound in the matrix. Ionization is done with a laser pulse. MALDI is a soft ionization 
technique, and rarely produces fragmentation. [39], [56] 
 
2.6.3 NMR 
Nuclear magnetic resonance, NMR is a powerful technique for structure determination.  
NMR is all about nuclear spin. In the presence of a powerful magnetic field, nuclear spin states are 
split. The spin up state is called α, spin down is called β. The frequency at which a nucleus resonates 
is directly proportional to the strength of the magnetic field. [38], [57], [58] 
A main property of NMR is chemical shift, δ. Electrons moving around an atom’s nucleus create a 
small magnetic field, which makes each nucleus experience a local magnetic field. Differences in 
electron density due to bonding leads to small changes in resonance frequency, called chemical 
shifts, typically measured in parts per million, ppm. [38], [57], [58] 
NMR can be used with a number of different magnetic nuclei, and to visualize correlations between 
several different ones. Below is a short overview of the ones relevant for this project. 
 
1H NMR is used with 1H nuclei. 1H NMR has a very strong signal compared to other nuclei because 
1H has a high natural abundance, and all hydrogens are visible in spectra. 1H spectra are also useful 
in determining the number of hydrogens via integration, and their peak multiplicity gives 
information about the protonation state of neighboring nuclei. However, hydrogen has a relatively 
narrow chemical shift range, usually within 0-12 ppm, which makes overlapping signals a common 
occurrence, especially in large molecules. [57] 
 
13C NMR uses 13C nuclei. Carbon has a much wider shift range than hydrogen, from 0-220 ppm, 
which makes peak overlap a rarity. However, 13C NMR does not give as much structural 
information as 1H does, since the spectrum shows no integration or J-coupling. The signal strength 
in 13C NMR is also lower than in 1H because of 13C’s lower natural abundance and magnetogyric 
==PAGE:31==
24 
 
ratio. As such some carbons, especially unprotonated ones, may not be visible in the spectrum. 
[38], [57], [58]  
 
2D NMR shows correlation between two nuclei. HSQC shows 1-bond H-C correlations, making 
it possible to match which hydrogen is attached to which carbon. H2BC shows 2-bond H-C 
correlations, provided both neighbors are protonated. HMBC primarily shows 3-bond H-C 
correlations, though 2- and 4-bond ones may also be weakly visible. Unlike HSQC and H2BC, 
HMBC can show correlation to unprotonated carbons. [57], [58]

The original project plan was to add the aptamer or peptide at the linker amine, and then deprotect 
the amino group on acyclovir. Since the coupling with aptamer or peptide at the linker never came 
to pass, the deprotection of MMTr from ACV-MMTr was done as an analogue experiment, to test 
that the deprotection reaction works. MMTr deprotection with TFA happened quickly. Full 
deprotection was observed on TLC and LC-MS after only 4 hours. The reaction had an impossibly 
high yield of 234%, which indicates large quantities of impurities. NMR of the compound shows 
several unidentified signals, not consistent with any used solvents or TFA. [60], [71]  
Tracking the deprotection reaction via LC-MS was not ideal, since acyclovir produces no signal in 
LC-MS, even when running the pure compound for reference. The lower detection limit on the LC-
MS is m/z=130, so ACV should have been visible. It was, however, possible to see the LC-MS 
peak from ACV-MMTr disappear. This was corroborated by TLC, where acyclovir’s spot was 
easily visible, and matched the pure reference. 
Despite the impurities, the reaction was proven to work, and to do so in a reasonable timeframe. In 
the future, when the deprotection is carried out on the full aptamer/peptide-linker-ACV-MMTr 
molecule, purification will likely be carried out via HPLC instead of flash chromatography, which 
should give a better separation of product and impurities than was found in the test reaction. The 
molecule will also be so large that mass determination will happen via MALDI rather than LC-MS.

3.10 Discussion of Previous Work 
Considering the drug’s age and unideal pharmacokinetics, it is hardly surprising that a number of 
prodrugs of ACV already exist. A clinically approved example is valacyclovir, the L-valine ester of 
ACV. The Val group increases the bioavailability of the drug through an increase in intestinal 
absorption, followed by ester hydrolysis in the small intestine. [28]  
Frich et al. 2019 worked on an ACV prodrug with a cleavable linker for prolonged release into 
systemic circulation. They used a self-immolative disulfide linker, which is stable in blood but not 
in the presence of thiols, such as glutathione, which is found in mammalian cells. Thus, linker 
cleavage happens shortly after cell entry. The linker’s other end was attached to an albumin-binding 
polymer to increase protraction. The prodrug was tested in vitro and in vivo, where subcutaneous 
administration was found to prevent disease progression in mice, proving systemic effect. [26] 
Though the purpose of the prodrug was systemic treatment rather than targeted, the overall 
approach was very similar to the one presented in this project, with a prodrug consisting of a 
polymer, a cleavable linker, and ACV. The early synthetic steps of MMTr protection and PNP 
activation of ACV to make a carbonate in this report were completed by use of the same procedures 
as in the article. [26]  
Kamel et al. 2009 worked on creating ACV-containing nanoparticles in order to increase residence 
time. [72] 
Li et al. 2008 worked on targeted ACV delivery to hepatoma cells using drug conjugation to a 
linker with terminal galactose or N-acetylgalactosamine moieties as targeting ligands towards the 
overexpressed galactose receptor on human hepatoma cell line hepG2. The goal was controlled 
prolonged release and liver targeting. The linker coupling was attempted both on the NH2 and OH 
groups on ACV, to make either ester or amide couplings. Drug release was found to be faster with 
esters than amides. Liver cell targeting worked in vitro, with linker length showed to affect the 
cellular uptake, with shorter linkers giving the best uptake. [27] While the target site is the liver 
rather than the brain, there is precedence for targeted drug conjugation of ACV.  
Pamornpathomkul et al. 2018 worked on increasing skin permeation via microneedle arrays. [73] 
In general, most of the work done on ACV has been focused on improving pharmacokinetics, and 
work on targeted delivery is either topical or hepatic. No studies were found that specifically 
targeted the brain.  
 
==PAGE:53==
46 
 
Using Tat peptide to cross the BBB has been done before, though not with ACV specifically. Erazo-
Oliveras et al. 2016 showed that dimerizing Tat causes leakage in late endosomes, which can 
increase endosomal escape of drug conjugates. [74] 
The BBB work with Tat generally focuses on delivery of nanoparticles or liposomes. [75], [76], 
[77] Fu et al. 2015 used Tat and a cleavable linker to make a tumor-targeted prodrug of paclitaxel 
liposomes. They used a disulfide linker, not unlike the one used by Frich et al. 2019. [26], [77] The 
approach of combining Tat, drug, and a cleavable linker has been done before, though with redox 
cleavable linkers rather than the enzymatically cleavable Val-Cit-PAB linker used in this project.  
Several studies have been made in using aptamers to cross the BBB. MacDonald et al. 2020 used 
a process very similar to the one proposed in this project. They used an aptamer targeting the 
transferrin receptor on the BBB to deliver the drug DOX over the BBB. They used two different 
DNA aptamers, and bound DOX directly onto the aptamer, without any linker. [78] While using 
the transferrin receptor as an aptamer target has been done before, the specific aptamer used in this 
project is new, and has not yet been tested. 
Successful coupling of aptamers to a cleavable linker has been used by Qi et al. 2022, who used a 
cathepsin cleavable linker along with a cancer cell targeting aptamer to deliver gemcitabine. [79] 
Though the cathepsin cleavable peptide linker was different than the one used in this project, it 
shows that the approach has been successfully attempted before.  
 
The project made progress on the synthesis of new prodrugs. Though the coupling to Tat and Tf 
aptamer have not been examined yet, the project has shown that the approach of creating Val-Cit-
PAB precursors conjugated to ACV is possible.

4 Future Work 
Even though the linker-ACV-MMTr molecule was produced, the yield and purity were not optimal. 
If there had been more time, it would have been interesting to do the Fmoc deprotection again and 
purify by flash chromatography without ever attempting to do HPLC. This would avoid any loss 
from filtering and then eluting from the filter. More care could also be taken in evaporating 
triethylamine after chromatography, to get a cleaner product and NMR spectrum. Since the 
observed impurities other than triethylamine were not identified, it cannot be determined if they 
came from reaction and workup, or from contamination. A repeat experiment could help clarify 
this. 
Impurities aside, the linker-ACV-MMTr molecule was produced. The next planned steps were to 
couple the linker to Tat peptide or the Tf aptamer to make two different ACV prodrugs for targeting 
the BBB. However, the aptamer synthesis did not succeed, and Tat peptide was synthesized but not 
purified in time for coupling. As such, these steps were not reached in this project.  
The Tf aptamer was to be made with an NHS ester attached on its 5’ end, which can be used for 
coupling with the amine of the linker to make an amide. [80] Coupling can be done by mixing 
aptamer and compound 7 at a pH of about 8.3-8.5. 0.1 M NaHCO3 or 0.1M phosphate buffer can 
be used. [81] 
The Tat peptide will need some modification before coupling. Figure 17 shows the structure of Tat 
as prepared for use in this project. On one end of the peptide is an alkyne click handle, and on the 
other is an amide, neither of which can be used directly with the amine on compound 7. A possible 
way to make the coupling would be to convert the amine on the linker into an azide, and use click 
chemistry to couple Tat and linker. [82] Alternatively, the Tat peptide could be modified to end in 
a carboxylic acid, and then coupled to the linker in the presence of DMAP and a coupling agent 
like DCC or HATU, to make an amide. [50], [83] Using this method would require N-protection of 
the lysine residues on Tat.

5 Conclusion 
Throughout the project, protection, deprotection, and coupling reactions were carried out to 
synthesize the linker-ACV-MMTr molecule (compound 7) from ACV (compound 1) and Val-Cit-
PAB-OH (compound 2).  
N-protecting ACV with Cbz-Cl and pyridine to create compound 8 was tested twice, and found to 
give no reaction. However, N-protecting ACV with MMTr-Cl under the same conditions to create 
compound 3 worked well, resulting in a high purity, and a yield of 63%, comparable to those found 
in literature.  
Adding PNP carbonate to the hydroxy group on 3 to make compound 4 was done successfully. It 
was found that washing with water to remove PNP also washed away product, and that removing 
the washing step from the procedure increased the yield markedly, from 46% to 82%. LC-MS of 4 
showed a mass peak not seen in NMR or TLC, suggesting some degradation happens under LC-
MS conditions.  
An alternative route of synthesis was tested by adding PNP carbonate to the hydroxy group of 2 to 
create compound 9. This was found to be a much slower reaction, with less conversion. 
Additionally, the product was found to be unstable to flash chromatography with DCM and 
methanol. Adding PNP to compound 3 was judged the better synthetic route. 
Fmoc protection of compound 2 was performed in several ways. The best yield in comparison to 
purity was found when the protection was done with Fmoc-OSu and purified by crystallization, 
resulting in a yield of 57% and a mass purity of 94%. Synthesis with Fmoc-Cl resulted in a higher 
purity than Fmoc-OSu, but a significantly lower yield, 23%. 
Coupling 4 and 5 to make compound 6 was tested with and without triethylamine and was found 
to happen significantly faster and with higher conversion under basic conditions. Compound 
purification was problematic, since it runs identically to compound 3, and close to compound 5. 
The impurity of compound 5 was found possible to separate by increasing column length. The 
highest yield observed was 41%, with a mass purity of 81%. 
Deprotection of compound 6 into the final compound 7 worked swiftly, but showed some 
purification difficulties. The compound was not suitable for purification via HPLC due to its low 
solubility in water and acetonitrile, though purification via flash chromatography was possible 
provided the impurity from compound 5 was removed. NMR analysis of the compound showed 
some impurities, chief among them being triethylamine, which was removed by repeated dilution 
==PAGE:58==
51 
 
and evaporation. Comparison of analytical HPLC chromatograms of compound 7 purified with 
HPLC or flash chromatography showed similar purity profiles except for the flash chromatography 
purified compound containing triethylamine. Structure was confirmed by 2D NMR, and molecular 
mass confirmed by LC-MS and MALDI. Yield was 35%. 
Deprotection of ACV-MMTr with TFA was done as a test reaction, analogue to how deprotection 
would have been carried out after conjugation of Tf or Tat. The reaction showed full conversion 
after 4 hours.  
In summation, the project completed all the necessary synthesis steps to prepare the linker-ACV-
MMtr molecule for conjugation to Tat peptide and Tf aptamer. Given that Tat and Tf were not 
available, this is as far as it was possible to go. When the peptide and aptamer become available, 
compound 7 is ready for conjugation, MMTr deprotection, and addition of Cy7 dye to create the 
planned prodrugs 12 and 15.

syntheses except Fmoc deprotection were carried out under argon atmosphere, and in oven-dried

Procedure from Frich et al. 2019. [26] 
ACV-MMTr (200 mg, 0.40 mmol, 1 eq.) was added to

saturated NaHCO3, and 1×20 mL brine. The organic phase was dried with Na2SO4, and solvent was 
evaporated. The crude product was purified by flash chromatography (DCM:MeOH, 100:0→96:4) to 
afford compound 4 as a yellow-white solid.
