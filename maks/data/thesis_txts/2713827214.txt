Abstract
The human proteome is enormous and despite the fact that it contains hundreds of thou-
sands of proteins, only a few groups of protein types can be targeted. For a protein to be
targeted, it must have an active site, an enzyme, or an allosteric site to which a smaller
molecule can bind. However, the majority of proteins are not built in this manner. The
problem of undruggable targets has been researched since 1980. Many different biologics
were expressed, however, the approach to solving the problem of unsolvable targets with
protein-loaded nanoparticles has yet to be thoroughly investigated yet. Lipid nanoparticles
have the ability to penetrate the lipid bilayer of cells and effectively transport the cargo they
carry into the cell. The drug carried via lipid nanoparticle is then engulfed into an endo-
some, which bursts upon the change of pH, resulting in drug release. The delivery of mRNA
with this drug delivery system has already become a gold standard in the pharmaceutical
industry. However, the delivery of protein still poses a significant challenge.
This research explores the possibility of encapsulating and releasing a model protein (Bovine
Serum Albumin) by lipid nanoparticles. The success of protein-loaded lipid nanoparticles is
assessed by size and PDI measurement via Dynamic Light Scattering (DLS). Moreover, the
surface charge of the protein-loaded particles is measured with the Zetasizer (Zeta Poten-
tial). Additionally, the protein encapsulation efficiency is calculated via Bicinchoninic Acid
Assay (BCA). Formulations containing different lipids (F.A-F.D), different lipid-to-protein
ratios (F1-F10) and formulated in different media (f1-f3 in MQ water, PBS, tris-buffer) are
formulated to assess the chemical influence of each of those variables on the success of formu-
lation of protein-loaded lipid nanoparticles. Furthermore, the purity of BSA used as a model
protein is tested to assess the effect of any impurities on the quality of the formulated lipid
nanoparticles. The smallest particles were formed in formulation with ionizable, packing,
and helper lipids, where the protein to lipid ratio is kept between 4:1 and 5:1.
Secondly, a part of the research is devoted to release testing in organic solvents, MQ water in
physiological temperature, and SDS. An attempt to release the protein from nanoparticles
formulated in MQ water is unsuccessful. However, it poses a question of the influence of
electrostatic interactions between the lipids or between protein and lipids. That leads to the
formulation of BSA-loaded lipid nanoparticles in PBS, which yields successful release results.
Even though the results presented in this paper are promising, further research concern-
ing the influence of impurities present in protein on loading dynamics and the condition of
the released protein should be done to assure the success of this research

Chapter 1
Scientific Background
1.1
Drug Delivery
The development of a new pharmaceutical drug is very costly and highly time-consuming.
What is worse, the vast majority of approximately 97,5% drug candidates do not manage to
complete the drug discovery stage and therefore do not progress to the pre-clinical develop-
ment stage [1]. Across all the drug discovery stages, most often, the reason for the failure of
the drug candidate is its poor solubility, penetration and pharmacokinetics of the drugs. In
these cases, drug delivery systems are created to improve pharmaceuticals’ chemical stability
and aqueous solubility, increase their pharmacological activity, and even reduce some of the
side effects.
Drug delivery systems introduce technologies that help to carry drugs into and through-
out the body. They describe how the drugs are ’packaged’ into, a carrier such as a micelle
or a nanoparticle. Those carriers can protect the active ingredient from being degraded
in the body while additionally enabling it to travel to its site of action. The field of drug
delivery has progressed substantially in the past few decades, and even greater innovations
are anticipated in the years to come. The development of biomedical engineering provided
2
==PAGE:15==
one of the essential contributions to the field, which allowed a better understanding of the
physiological barriers. This led to drug delivery becoming even more efficient. Moreover, it
also led to the discovery of new modes of drug delivery [2].
Nevertheless, with this progress, many disease treatments still have unacceptable side ef-
fects. They occur due to the interaction of the drug with healthy organs or tissues, which
limits the capacity to treat many diseases, such as infectious diseases, cancer, or neurode-
generative diseases. Continuing advances in this space will help to facilitate the targeted
delivery of drugs while also mitigating their side effects [3].
Drug delivery systems are designed to improve drug pharmacokinetics and general thera-
peutic properties. Thus, many drugs’ pharmacological properties could be improved with
the implementation of drug delivery systems. This area of science consists of a broad field
of research on the development of novel materials and carrier systems for more effective
therapeutic effects and delivery of drugs. Drug delivery is a commonly used method that
can assure a controlled, steady and targeted delivery. In the early days of medical drug
delivery systems, pharmaceuticals were administered through conventional methods such as
liquid solutions, lotions, creams, ointments, powders, injectables, pills, or immediate-release
capsules and tablets [2].
Nowadays, drug delivery systems include devices with tremendously improved therapeutic
potential, such as liposomes, micelles, transdermal patches, oral controlled release systems,
aerosols, fast dispersing dosage forms, taste-masking systems, and site-specific delivery sys-
tems.
This paper’s primary focus will be on Lipid Nanoparticles and maximising their
loading efficiency and controlled release [4].

1.2
Lipid Nanoparticles
In order to truly understand the structure and science behind Lipid Nanoparticles, it is
necessary first to understand liposomes and lipid nanoemulsions.
Figure 1.1: Structure of Lipid Nanoparticles compared with Liposomes and Lipid Nanoemulsions.
Liposomes consist of a shell formed by a bilayer of amphipathic phospholipids, which are
there to encapsulate the internal aqueous cavity. Liposome structure makes them highly
suitable pharmaceutical carriers because the hydrophilic drugs are packed inside the bilayer
while hydrophobic ones are packed within the bilayers. This way, loading drugs into lipo-
somes can significantly improve drugs’ solubility and stability. Moreover, liposomes find
additional use as signal enhancers/carriers in medical diagnostics, adjuvants in vaccination,
and solubilisers. Interestingly, their properties found early applications as penetration en-
hancers in cosmetics [5].
Lipid nanoemulsions (LNEs) consist of lipid droplets stabilised by surfactants. The sta-
4
==PAGE:17==
bilisation is necessary to prevent aggregation in an aqueous solution. One of the leading
medical uses of LNEs is the intravenous nutrition administration that consists primarily of
plant-based lipid droplets stabilised by phospholipids. Moreover, LNEs also serve as carriers
for poorly water-soluble substances such as anaesthetic drugs, cancer therapeutics and some
vitamins. Because of that, they have their applications in the pharma, cosmetics, and food
industry.
Lipid Nanoparticles (LNPs) resemble liposomes, with the main difference being that they
are developed to encapsulate RNA and DNA. They are the most prevalent non-viral oligonu-
cleotide delivery system, and their significance is shown especially by the recently developed
Pfizer and Moderna COVID-19 vaccines utilising LNPs as mRNA carriers [6]. Contrary
to liposomes, LNPs lack the outer structure composed of the phospholipid bilayer. Instead,
LNPs contain a complex micelle-like structure that consists of cationic lipids which surround
the genetic material, incorporating it inside the lipid structure. Furthermore, LNPs utilise
their surface charge to tune and improve the drug’s efficacy and therapeutic effect. An ex-
ample is PEGylation of surface lipids, which improves the stealth effect while preventing the
LNPs from phagocytosis via specific binding to target cells [5].
The most standardised version of LNPs is formulated by combining DSPC, cholesterol,
Polyethylene glycol (PEG), and genetic material encapsulated inside the particle. Nanopar-
ticles form spontaneously when the aqueous and organic solutions are mixed under reduced
pH conditions due to hydrophobic/hydrophilic interactions and the electrostatic interactions
between the positively charged lipid amine groups and the negatively charged phosphate
backbone of genetic material. These interactions result in the robust and quick formulation
of lipid particles [7].

1.3
Lipids Used for Lipid Nanoparticle Formulation
1.3.1
DOTAP
1,2-Bis(oleoyloxy)-3-(trimethylammonio)propane (DOTAP) is a cationic lipid that is a nat-
ural constituent of cell membranes. DOTAP has two 18-carbon long chains and contains
quaternary amines in its head groups. It also has a molecular weight of 663 g/mol and is
fully protonated at pH 7.4. DOTAP was first industrially synthesised in 1990.[8]
Figure 1.2: Chemical structure of DOTAP [9].
The introduction of ionisable cationic lipids, such as DOTAP, combined with the development
of an ethanol-loading procedure, has provided methods to achieve high loading efficiencies
for genetic drugs in small (<100 nm diameter) LNP systems with low surface charge, thus
overcoming many issues noted for complexes. Specifically, ionisable cationic lipids such as
DOTAP have an apparent pK (pKa) <7 and can be used to achieve efficient encapsulation
of negatively charged polymers into LNP at low pH, where the ionisable lipids are positively
charged. Originally scientists came up with a hypothesis that the hydrolysable ester bonds
could reduce cytotoxicity and make the lipids biodegradable. Using only DOTAP without
any additional helper or packing lipids proved to be inefficient in gene delivery. It is hypoth-
esised that it is due to a high surface charge and thus, the density of LNPs, which may cause
release problems. Thus, while formulating LNPs, it is crucial to consider using combining
DOTAP with a helper and stabilising lipids such as DOPE and cholesterol [10].

==PAGE:19==
1.3.2
DOPE
DOPE is a helper lipid with a molecular weight of 744 g/mol. Helper lipids with cone-
shaped geometry are used to promote endosomal release. Transfection activity is increased
when a neutral phospholipid such as DOPE is added to cationic lipids such as DOTAP
or DSPC. DOPE is known to exhibit structural transition depending on the change of pH
and takes the shape of spherical micelle above pH 9.0. DOPE is a specific form of phos-
phatidylethanolamine, a plasmid membrane phospholipid. [11],[12].
Figure 1.3: Chemical structure of DOPE[11].
Lipids with a cationic charge are desirable in the formulation of LNPs for gene delivery
because the positive charge of lipids interacts with the DNA backbone.
Moreover, the
addition of a class of neutral lipids, also so-called helper lipids, often improves the efficiency
of formulation and encapsulation. In liposomal formulations, DOPE also permits a tighter
packing of DNA helices. It is so because of the presence of the salt bridges between head
groups of cationic lipids (positive charge) and the phosphate groups of DOPE molecules
(negative charge). This allows the primary amine of DOPE to stabilise itself in the liposome
surface, which causes closer interactions with phosphate groups of DNA(negatively charged)
[10], [13].

1.3.3
DSPC
Distearoylphosphatidylcholine (DSPC) is a glycerophospholipid comprised of 2 acyl chains
and a polar head group with two saturated acyl chains. It has two 18-carbon long chains and
a molecular weight of 790 g/mol. Phosphatidylcholines usually possess 18-carbon chains and
their hydrogenation from the raw substrate such as soybean results in obtaining a. mixture
of 85% DSPC [14].
Figure 1.4: Chemical structure of DSPC [15]
DSPC is a phospholipid which is a natural constituent of cell membranes. It is commonly
used to prepare cationic liposomes.
Recently DSPC has found a large-scale use in the
production of lipid nanoparticles in mRNA vaccines, such as the Moderna and Pfizer COVID-
19 vaccines [6].

1.3.4
Cholesterol
Cholesterol contains 27 carbons and has a quite unique structure. Its molecular weight is
386.65 g/mol. Cholesterol has a hydrocarbon tail, a sterol nucleus with four hydrocarbon
rings in the centre, and a hydroxyl group. The central ring (sterol nucleus) is a standard
feature for all steroid hormones.[16]
Figure 1.5: Chemical structure of Cholesterol [16].
Cholesterol is often included in LNP formulation as a helper lipid because it improves intra-
cellular delivery and LNP stability in vivo. The lipids used for gene delivery typically have a
glycerol backbone, two fatty acid tails, and a cationic head group. However, the head group
is the variable that changes depending on the target cell type. Often, cholesterol is added
to increase the number of hydrophobic lipids in the formulation. The hydrophobic portion
of the lipid aids the self-assembly of liposomes. The morphologies of these structures can
vary. However, cholesterol is often chosen because of its biocompatibility and the stability it
conveys to lipid membranes. Using cholesterol also yields desirable transfection efficiencies
and relatively low cytotoxicities[10].

1.3.5
DOPE mPEG
1,2-Dioleoyl-snâglycero-3-phosphoethanolamine (DOPE)-polyethylene glycol methoxy (DOPE-
MPEG) is a linear PEGylated, unsaturated DOPE phospholipid. It has a molecular weight
of 2786.489 g/mol [11].
Figure 1.6: Chemical structure of DOPE mPEG 2000 [17].
DOPE mPEG is a useful self-assembling reagent used in the formulation of PEGylated LNPs.
PEG is used in drug delivery with the lipid bilayer to improve drug solubility. The PEGyla-
tion provides extended circulation and half-life, stealth properties, and reduced non-specific
protein binding of drug-carrying LNPs. Additionally, PEG can assure that the amine groups
conjugate targeting molecules, including antibodies, proteins, and peptides [18].
The inclusion of a PEGylated lipid can enhance LNP colloidal stability. PEGs have a wide
application in LNP delivery systems because they improve LNP circulation time in vivo.
However, they may inhibit the endosomal release and reduce uptake at the cellular level.
This issue can be managed by choosing a reversible PEGylation in reversible PEGylation,
where the PEG moiety is gradually released in the circulatory system [19].

1.4
Protein drugs
Nowadays, the market of protein-based pharmaceuticals is estimated to produce revenue of
39 billion euros per year. That means that proteins make up around 10% of the pharma-
ceutical market [20]. Moreover, this section of the pharmaceutical market is growing much
faster than the markets of small molecules. It is estimated to make up an even more sig-
nificant proportion of the market in the future. Over a previous couple of years, plenty of
therapeutic proteins were developed, tested and approved for clinical usage. Approximately
40% out of nearly 6000 pharmaceutical products currently in the clinical development stage
are protein-based biopharmaceuticals. Such dominance of proteins was established because
using proteins from a therapeutic perspective carries many advantages to the alternative
treatments [21].
Currently, more than 100 therapeutic proteins are approved for clinical use. Out of these
100, 29 are antibodies, 22 are enzymes, and the rest vary in structures and function. Based
on the pharmacological activity of these proteins, they can be divided into five main groups:
• replacing a deficient or abnormal protein
• augmenting an existing pathway
• providing a novel function or activity
• interfering with a molecule or organism
• delivering other compounds or proteins (cytotoxic drug or effector proteins)
Protein drugs can also be divided based on their molecular types. That includes antibody-
based drugs, anticoagulants, blood factors, engineered protein scaffolds, enzymes, growth
factors, hormones, interferons, interleukins, and thrombolytics. Lastly, three main groups
can be distinguished based on the protein drug molecular function mechanism. These groups
include specific noncovalent binders, proteins affecting covalent bonds, and others.
11
==PAGE:24==
The astonishing success of protein-based therapeutics was achieved mainly due to the appli-
cation of the key concepts and methodologies that dictate that the newly developed thera-
peutics should fulfil the three main requirements set by the FDA: safety, efficacy, and quality.
Fulfilling these requirements can become a limiting factor to the success of drug develop-
ment. However, it is also crucial for the success of any drug.
Proteins display high specificity as well as high potency, which makes them an exciting
drug candidate. That, combined with low intrinsic toxicity and high clearance from the
body, assures that the protein-based drugs are safer and generally have more prolonged
therapeutic effects. That shows a clear advantage of protein-based drugs compared to the
low molecular weight drugs, which often cause the production and accumulation of harm-
ful metabolites and frequent off-target effects [22]. However, despite the steadily increasing
impact of protein-based therapeutics, their instability, short half-life, and immunogenicity
are still considered factors which limit their safety and efficacy. Consequently, that often
prevents the formulation of successful protein-based drug candidates[23] [21], [20].
1.5
Protein drug delivery
Generally, oral delivery is the preferred route of drug administration because of its conve-
nience and high patient compliance. Thus, oral drug administration is believed to be the
simplest and easiest way of drug delivery. Despite many protein therapeutics being discov-
ered yearly, the need to deliver them orally remains unresolved issue. Protein drugs have
low bioavailability when administered orally due to natural barriers in the body. That in-
cludes the epithelial barrier, gastrointestinal proteases, and efflux pumps. Unfortunately,
upon administration, proteins are degraded via hydrolysis and specific enzymes (proteases)
in an acidic environment, such as the one in the stomach and the gastrointestinal tract.
12
==PAGE:25==
Moreover, human cells possess at least 569 proteases, out of which five main classes of pro-
teases can be distinguished (serine, cysteine, threonine, aspartic and metalloproteinases).
These proteases play essential roles in DNA replication, transcription, fertility, hemostasis,
inflammation, cell proliferation, stem cell mobilisation, apoptosis and many other cellular
and regulatory processes. Trypsin, carboxypeptidase and chymotrypsin are enzymes secreted
from the pancreas into the small intestine. These enzymes are responsible for around 20% of
the enzymatic degradation of delivered proteins [21]. Because of those problems, liposomes
and lipid nanoparticles could have great potential in oral drug delivery of proteins. The main
concern in using liposomes as a drug delivery system is the carriers’ stability under the GI
tract’s physiologic conditions. Moreover, an additional barrier that should be considered is
mucus, which can block the diffusion of liposomes to the epithelial layer. Despite this, orally
administered liposomes can demonstrate a high success rate in protein drug delivery.
Liposomes demonstrate great potential as a carrier system for systemically administered
protein therapeutics. Liposomes cause little to no allergic, toxic, antigenic, pyrogenic or
immune reactions if formulated correctly. Liposomes have also been successfully used in
in-vivo tests to cross the blood-brain barrier. In this case, liposomes were used to deliver
an active enzyme upon injection into the tail vein of a rat. The first-generation liposomes
did not contain helper lipids and were easily cleared from the bloodstream and accumulated
in Kupfer cells in the liver [20]. Following that, advances have been made to limit these
issues. Firstly, PEGylated liposomes have been synthesised. These liposomes noted a re-
duced aggregation while also achieving an increased circulation time. PEGylated liposomes
(SteathTM liposomes -Johnson Johnson) have been industrially used to deliver the anthra-
cycline chemotherapeutic called doxorubicin, and they were able to deliver preferentially
to the tumour site. This was likely achieved via enhanced permeability and retention of
liposomes, proving that this drug delivery system has potential and should be researched
further[21].

1.6
Expression of undruggable targets
The human proteome is very large. Even though it contains hundreds of thousands of pro-
teins, only several groups of protein types are targetable. For the protein to be targeted,
usually it should contain an active site, an enzyme, or an allosteric site, for a smaller molecule
to bind to [24].However, most of the proteins are not constructed in this way. Some of the
proteins are densely packed or have a very smooth structure, which means there is no space
on their surface to bind to, and thus such a protein becomes an undruggable target [25].
Researchers found that most proteins are inside the cell rather than expressed on the cell
membrane. It is so because most glycoproteins are expressed mainly for the reception. That
means they contain the binding site since part of their mechanism allows other molecules to
bind with them. However, the proteins expressed inside the cell are not built like this be-
cause they perform a very different function, which is part of a signalling cascade [26]. That
sparked a scientific discussion about undruggable targets and their treatment inside the cell.
The main ideas for solving the issue involved the delivery of biologics, such as antibodies or
hairpin oligonucleotide loops, or the application of different binding motifs [25].
Since 1980, researchers have been looking into solving the problem of undruggable targets.
Many different biologics were expressed, and two main approaches to solving the problem
emerged. The first approach involved the delivery of proteins (in this case, antibodies)[27].
This method proved unsuccessful due to the unfavourable properties of the protein. This
caused the scientist to lean toward exploring the second method, which included introducing
DNA and later RNA for treatment of the undruggable targets. This approach proved to be
highly effective and led to the creation of therapies based on intracellular delivery of genomic
material, also called gene therapy [28].

1.7
Research towards gene delivery via viral vectors
Since 2018 gene delivery has increasingly been approved as a successful method for alter-
nating protein expression from RNA (vaccines) or correcting genetic faults (gene therapy).
With the emerging popularity of gene therapy, scientists had to find an appropriate deliv-
ery method. This research was split into two main groups that initially worked with lipid
nanoparticles and viral delivery. Even though both these methods are currently approved
and widely used, they still carry advantages and disadvantages[29]..
There are five main viral vector classes that can be further categorised into two main groups.
The division is made according to whether the genome carried by the viral vector is then
integrated into host cellular chromatin or persists primarily in the cell nucleus in the form of
extrachromosomal episomes. The preference of viral vectors for clinical use relies on factors
such as the ease of production, efficiency of transgene expression, stability, toxicity, and
safety. Similarly, the types of vectors are represented by both RNA and DNA viruses with
either single-stranded or double-stranded genomes [30].
Viral vectors are generally considered safe because of the deletion of potentially harmful
areas of their genome, preventing replication of these areas. This system provides highly
efficient gene transduction and precise delivery of genes to target cells [30]. However, the
disadvantages of viral delivery include causing a strong immune response and the possibility
of leading to oncogene activation, or tumour suppressor gene inactivation.
One of the critical risks associated with the choice of viral vectors for drug delivery is viral
shedding. Shedding in gene therapy indicates the dissemination of the gene therapy product
through the excreta by the patient or subject. Despite the considerable number of clinical
gene therapy trials conducted worldwide, there is currently no overview of actual shedding
15
==PAGE:28==
data for different viral vector systems. Studies have shown that display vectors used in gene
therapy research can be excreted from the body for at least a week after injection. This
means that the shedding of the vector can be found in the blood, saliva, stool and urine
for up to a week after the injection. Thus, most health care facilities recommend standard
precautions to be taken by the patient between 14 and 30 days after administration of the
vectors [31], [32]. Moreover, another problem connected to the viral vector use for drug
delivery is that the viral capsid’s size limits its genetic payload.
However, novel research claims that adapting adenoviruses as gene delivery tools can de-
velop high-capacity adenoviral vectors. These vectors retain relevant features earlier than
previous viral vector generations. They are characterised by their excellent stability, remark-
able efficacy of in vivo transduction, and production at high titers. Additionally, the lack of
viral coding sequences in the genomes of the novel viral vectors causes an extension of the
cloning capacity up to 37 Kb while allowing a long-term episomal persistence of transgenes
in non-dividing cells [33].
Nevertheless, when viral vector systems are used in gene therapy experiments in a clini-
cal setting, these recommendations are adapted to do a comprehensive risk assessment and
for developing a mitigation plan. Since the biosafety levels differ depending on the used vi-
ral vector systems, the proper group classification had to be created. Therefore, during any
research and clinical studies, the Institutional Biosafety Committee has to be entrusted with
the responsibility of reviewing any risks associated with viral vector shedding and insertional
mutagenesis during the study [31].

1.8
Research towards lipid drug delivery systems
The introduction of Luxturna[34] therapy, consisting of a gene delivery via lipid nanopar-
ticles, initiated an even faster-growing interest in gene therapies and caused scientists to
reinvent the idea of lipid nanoparticles and their application in drug delivery. Shortly after
their discovery, lipid-based nanoparticles were used as model membrane systems. Scientists
quickly realised their potential in drug delivery and started using them as a small-scale
delivery system for low molecular weight drugs and therapeutic nucleic acids. They were
first used in the 1980s, as delivery vehicles for contrast agents and imaging agents for the
detection of infection sites or inflammation.
1.8.1
Mechanism of formation
Liposomes are formulated with phospholipids containing a hydrophilic head and hydropho-
bic tail. The hydrophilic region consists mainly of phosphoric acid bound to a water-soluble
molecule, whereas the hydrophobic region consists of two usually 14-24 carbon atom-long
fatty acid chains, with 0-6 double bonds in each of the chains. When the phospholipids are
dispersed in an aqueous medium, they form membrane sheets by aligning the polar head
group facing outside the aqueous region. At the same time, the fatty acid groups face each
other, by which they form spherical structures- the liposomes. Phospholipids are also one
of the main components of cell membranes. Thus, they possess excellent biocompatibility
as well as amphiphilic properties. The amphiphilicity causes the phospholipids to be capa-
ble of self-assembly and emulsification. Additionally, their wetting properties are crucial in
drug coating because they provide hydrophilicity to hydrophobic pharmaceuticals. When
phospholipids are introduced into an aqueous environment, they self-assemble into different
structures with specific properties at various conditions. One of those structures are lipo-
somes, which can be employed for drug delivery systems. Moreover, adding lipids with other
17
==PAGE:30==
specific properties can enhance the drug delivery properties of lipid nanoparticles [35], [36].
1.8.2
Characterisation
The vesicles obtained during the formulation of liposomes and LNPs differ in size and charge
they carry. When stored, vesicles aggregate and increase in size to reach a state that is ther-
modynamically favourable. The vesicles can also leak, leading to collapsing of the particle,
its fusion and breaking. Therefore, size distribution and morphology of the vesicles are the
two most important parameters to measure when assessing the stability of LNPs and lipo-
somes. Various techniques are employed to improve the morphology of the vesicles. Adding
cholesterol is one of the most popular techniques since it makes the lipid membrane stronger
by randomly incorporating cholesterol into the membrane. However, it is essential for the
cholesterol content not to exceed 50% of the entire lipid content. Additionally, the physical
stability of the LNPs and liposomes can be maintained by avoiding the excess unsaturation
in the phospholipids and by maintaining the pH conditions [35].
1.8.3
Chemical stability
Phospholipids are unsaturated fatty acids prone to oxidation and hydrolysis. This may alter
the stability of the drug product loaded into Lipid Nanoparticles. Other parameters that
play a significant role in maintaining a liposomal formulation are the pH, ionic strength and
solvent system [7]. Liposomes can be prevented from oxidative degradation with protection
from light, the addition of anti-oxidants or the removal of heavy metals by adding EDTA.
Moreover, hydrolysis of the ester bond at the C-4 position of the glycerol moiety of phos-
pholipids causes the formation of lysophosphatidylcholine (lysoPC). This can enhance the
permeability of the liposomal contents. Therefore, control of lysoPC limit within the drug
product of lysosomes is essential [35].

1.8.4
Gene delivery
The rationale behind using lipidic nanoparticles for DNA and RNA is because, unlike small
molecules, nucleic acids cannot diffuse through tissues, into the cells. Because nucleic acids
are not prone to such an uptake and are highly charged, they cannot cross the cell membrane
and get degraded by nucleases.
Lipidic nanoparticle mode of action in gene therapy is
straightforward when compared to the mode of action of viral vectors.
Figure 1.7: Examples of structure of mRNA, siRNA, DNA and Protein loaded into Liposomes and
Lipid Nanoparticles inspired by article "Lipid-based nanoparticles in the clinic and clinical trials"
by t.Thi, E.Suys et.al [37].
Viral delivery systems contain fusion proteins and generally have a much more sophisticated
mode of action but at the same time cause a lot harsher immunological reactions and can be
considered unsafe. Thus, lipidic nanoparticles can be considered safer to use. Additionally,
they are easy and safe to produce and can encapsulate large amounts of material.
To this day, lipid nanoparticle delivery systems continue to gain even more significant scien-
tific relevance. The perfect example is the worldwide approved application of lipidic nanocar-
riers in Moderna, and Pfizer COVID19 mRNA vaccines [6]. Moderna vaccine works because
it contains non-charged lipids. When the pH drops in the endosomal pathway, the lipid is
charged. This charge change causes the endosomal membrane disruption, resulting in leakage
and the release of mRNA to the cytosol [35].

1.9
Protein Loaded Lipid Nanoparticles
Even though the initial study on protein delivery for undruggable targets was unsuccessful,
it is still an exciting area for further research. Only one available treatment on the market
successfully employs lipid nanoparticles for protein delivery. This means that protein delivery
via lipid nanoparticles is still a vast area that should be explored and optimised. Proteins
are crucial regulators of signal transduction and physiological functions of cells. Protein
expression and binding abnormalities are associated with various diseases, including various
types of cancers. Protein therapy is necessary to either replace the malfunctioning proteins
inside cells or to modulate cell signalling. Furthermore, proteins are a highly diverse class
of biomacromolecule. They vary drastically in terms of size, surface charge, hydrophobicity
and hydrophilicity. All this makes proteins challenging in terms of their delivery. Moreover,
proteins are easily degraded inside the body by various proteases, which makes them quite
fragile cargo.
Thus, regarding therapeutic applications, proteins greatly depend on the
method, targeting capability, and efficiency of the drug delivery systems [38],[18].
1.9.1
Mode of action
Protein-loaded lipid nanoparticles consist of protein inside a hydrophobic lipid membrane,
due to which, lipid nanoparticles can easily be dissolved into the cell membranes, which are
also made from lipids. This way, lipid nanoparticles can both hydrophilic and hydrophobic
molecules into the cell. The delivery of the drug to the site of action bases on lipid bilayers
fusing together to enter the cell and then release the liposomal content[35]. Firstly, lipid
nanoparticles are absorbed into the cell via contact of the phospholipids of lipid nanoparticles
with the phospholipids of the cell membrane bilayer. Then, the adsorbed material is engulfed
and internalised into the lipid nanoparticle, forming a phospholipid bilayer around the drug.
Finally, depending on the decrease of the pH, the endosome structure is disrupted, causing
20
==PAGE:33==
the collapse of the phospholipid membrane and the release of the protein drug[36].

liposomal doxorubicin (PLD). It is a chemotherapy drug used in AIDS-related Kaposi’s
sarcoma, breast cancer, ovarian cancer, and other solid tumours. Interestingly, this drug is
one of the few examples of lipid drug delivery systems being used for protein delivery [39].

3.4
Stability and Release testing
Lipid nanoparticles are colloids composed of a lipid matrix encapsulated in an emulsifier
layer. They are being researched for the formulation of poorly water-soluble drugs and, due
to their small particle size, are particularly appealing as drug delivery systems. It is critical
to evaluate the drug release behaviour of these systems in order to assess their potential for
drug delivery applications.

==PAGE:41==
3.4.1
Organic Solvents
In order to assess the release of protein from LNPs, the particles were mixed with different
organic solvents to assess their stability and possible release. Organic solvents were used to
cause the LNPs to break and collapse, leading to leakage of encapsulated protein.
To achieve that, the experiment was designed where 10µll of f1 to f3 sample in MQ wa-
ter and PBS were mixed with 90µll of Ethanol, Methanol and Acetonitrile. The samples
were vortexed thoroughly, and their size was measured with DLS. Provided that the release
indeed happened, peaks at the size of 10-30nm were expected. That would mean that LNP
collapsed, and BSA was released. The results were compared against the control sample of
LNP without any organic solvents.
3.4.2
Temperature
LNPs are thermosensitive particles. Thus, incubating them at higher temperatures should
cause the collapse of the LNP structure, which should lead to the release of encapsulated
protein. The experiment checking the stability of LNPs in higher temperatures was designed.
10 µll of f1 to f3 samples in MQ water and PBS was incubated at 37°C for one hour. The
samples were mixed with 990 µll of MQ water, and their size was measured via DLS. Peaks
between 10 and 30 nm in size were anticipated, assuming that the discharge did indeed
take place. That would imply that BSA was released and LNP failed. The outcomes were
contrasted with the LNP sample used as a control, which was not incubated at a higher
temperature.
3.4.3
SDS
Similarly, to release tests with organic solvents, SDS was added in an attempt to disrupt
the lipid layer of LNPs and cause leakage of proteins from inside the particle. Interestingly,

==PAGE:42==
when working with SDS, the Critical Micelle Concentration was an additional parameter
that had to be considered.
The critical micelle concentration (CMC) in colloidal and surface chemistry is defined as
the concentration of surfactants above which micelles form, and all additional surfactants
added to the system will form micelles. The CMC of a surfactant is an important property.
The surface tension changes dramatically with surfactant concentration before reaching the
CMC. The surface tension remains relatively constant after reaching the CMC or changes
with a lower slope.
The CMC of dispersant in a given medium is affected by tempera-
ture, pressure, and (sometimes significantly) the presence and concentration of other surface
active substances and electrolytes. Micelles form only above the critical micelle temperature.
The CMC value of SDS equals 0.0083M. Thus appropriate calculations were made to ensure
that the amount of SDS added to each sample was slightly below the CMC value for SDS.
Samples f1 to f3 in MQ water and PBS were mixed with SDS, and the size of particles was
measured to check if protein release took place.

Methodology
4.1
Dynamic Light Scattering
Detection of light scattering from matter is a valuable technique with applications in nu-
merous scientific disciplines where specific properties of molecules can be studied depending
on the light source and detector. A sample is exposed to a monochromatic light wave in a
typical light-scattering experiment, and an appropriate detector detects the signal.
The foundation of Dynamic light scattering is the Brownian motion of scattered particles.
Particles move irregularly in all directions when dispersed in a solution. The fundamental
idea behind Brownian motion is that particles and solvent molecules constantly clash. Par-
ticle movement is brought about by the energy that is transmitted during these collisions.
Smaller particles are more affected since the energy transmission is more or less constant.
Smaller particles are therefore travelling more quickly than larger particles. By measuring
the speed of the particles, you can calculate the hydrodynamic diameter if you are aware of
all other factors that affect particle movement.
The Stokes-Einstein equation provides the relationship between the particle speed and size.
31
==PAGE:44==
The translational diffusion coefficient D determines the particle’s speed. Furthermore, as
both the temperature and the dispersant’s viscosity have a direct impact on particle mobil-
ity, they are both included in the equation. The movement of the particles must entirely be
dependent on Brownian motion in order to satisfy the Stokes-Einstein equation. Sedimenta-
tion eliminates random movement, which could produce unreliable results. As a result, the
beginning of sedimentation denotes the maximum size for DLS measurements. The signal-
to-noise ratio, on the other hand, establishes the lower size limit. Small particles do not
scatter light very much, which results in a weak measuring signal [45].
The measurement of the size of the formulated nanoparticles defines the success of their
self-assembly and loading. The size of the nanoparticles was measured via Dynamic Light
Scattering (Malvern Panalytical, DLS Zetasizer Nano series- Nano ZS). The test samples
were diluted by taking 10µl of the test sample and diluting it in 990 µl of MQ water. The
dilution was necessary to reach the detection limit of the Malvern Panalytical DLS apparatus.
The desired size of nanoparticles was in the range of 150 nm to 250 nm.
4.2
Zeta Potential
Electrokinetic potential in colloidal systems is referred to as zeta potential, or . From a the-
oretical perspective, Zeta potential is the electric potential in the double layer of an interface
between a dispersed particle or droplet and a point in the continuous phase away from the
interface. Zeta potential, then, is the difference in potential between the mobile dispersion
medium and the stationary layer of the dispersion medium that is associated with the dis-
persed particle. The medium’s pH is the main element that influences zeta potential. Ionic
strength, the concentration of any additives, and temperature are further considerations.
Zeta potential is significant because both the short- and long-term stability of emulsions can
be predicted by its value. Emulsions that have a high zeta potential, whether it be positive
32
==PAGE:45==
or negative, are electrically stabilised, whereas emulsions that have a low zeta potential tend
to coagulate or fluctuate, which could compromise their physical stability. In general, when
an emulsion’s zeta potential is strong, the repulsive forces outweigh the attractive forces,
creating a comparatively stable system [46]. The measurement of the charge of the formu-
lated nanoparticles defines the presence of charge that is affecting the lipid nanoparticles.
Ultimately the measurement of the charge of the formulation can help in the assessment
of the success of lipid nanoparticle self-assembly and give an overview of its stability. The
charge of the nanoparticles was measured via with Malvern Panalytical Zetasizer Nano ZS.
The test samples were diluted by taking 10µl of the test sample and diluting it in 990µl
of MQ water. The dilution was necessary to reach the detection limit of the Malvern Pana-
lytical DLS apparatus. Moreover, the Zeta potential of the lipid nanoparticles was expected
to be slightly positive.
4.3
Polydispersity Index
The polydispersity index (PDI) is a size-based measure of sample heterogeneity. Polydis-
persity can occur as a result of a sample’s size distribution or agglomeration or aggregation
during isolation or analysis. The PI can be determined using dynamic light scattering (DLS)
instruments or electron micrographs. International standards organisations (ISOs) have de-
termined that PI values of 0.05 are more common in monodisperse samples, whereas values
greater than 0.7 are more common in large samples [47].
The test samples were diluted by diluting 10l of the test sample in 990l of MQ water. The
dilution was required to reach the detection limit of the Malvern Panalytical DLS apparatus.
PDI was measured with Malvern Panalytical Zetasizer Nano ZS, alongside the size and zeta
potential measurements.

4.4
Bicinchoninic Acid Assay (BCA)
In order to assess the loading efficiency of Lipid Nanoparticles the BCA assay was performed.
Bicinchoninic acid assay (BCA assay) is used to determine the concentration of protein in a
solution. The working solution contains two stable reagents, which cause BCA to be com-
patible with many ionic and non-ionic detergents. The samples were treated according to
the PierceTM BCA Protein Assay Kit manual [48] provided by ThermoScientific.
In order to measure the loading efficiency, the protein-loaded lipid nanoparticles were spun
down at 13000RPM for 5 minutes. Following that, the supernatant was collected for further
BCA assay and the pellet was resuspended in MQ water or PBS, depending on the formu-
lation media of each of the samples. The supernatant was used for BCA assay in order to
measure the amount of protein that was not loaded onto the lipid nanoparticles, and ulti-
mately assess the amount of loaded protein. The control containing a stock of Bovine Serum
Albumin was treated in the same way as the test samples, that is spun down to assure the
protein sticking to the walls Eppendorf tubes was also taken into account when measuring
the protein loading.
Following, the samples were diluted 100 fold to make sure the samples are within the detec-
tion limit of the BCA assay. Next, the standards and working reagents were prepared. All
test samples and standards were loaded onto the microplate and incubated for 30 minutes
in 37°C. After the incubation, the plate was cooled and the absorbance at 562 nm was mea-
sured on a plate reader. The absorbance provided by the platereader allowed to prepare a
standard curve, according to the manual provided by ThrmoFisher [48]. the standard curve
allowed to calculate the amount of protein in the test samples, and compared against the
control containing protein stock, provided the loading efficiency of lipid nanoprticles.

4.5
SDS-PAGE
Polyacrylamide gels are created when acrylamide and bis-acrylamide (N,N’-methylenebisacrylamide)
react, resulting in a highly cross-linked gel matrix. The gel acts as a sieve, allowing proteins
to move in response to an electric field. Proteins have an overall positive or negative charge,
which allows them to move towards the isoelectric point, where they have no net charge. It
is possible to separate proteins based on size as they migrate towards the positive electrode
by denaturing them and giving them a uniform negative charge.
To perform SDS-PAGE on the test samples, the Leammli buffer (Sample Buffer Laemmli
Concentrate, product number S3401 from Stemcell Technologies) was added to each one. All
samples were then incubated with the Leammli buffer in a Benchmark Scientific H5000-HC
Multi Therm Shaking Vortexer at 70°C for 10 minutes with continuous stirring. The running
buffer is loaded (MOPS SDS Running Buffer 20x concentrate, prod. no. 11589156, from
Sigma Aldrich). Meanwhile, SDS-PAGE gel and apparatus were prepared.
Thermo Fisher Scientific NuPAGE 4 to 12%, Bis-Tris, 1.0-1.5 mm, Mini Protein Gels
(prod.no 10247002) were used to load the samples and ladder (PageRuler Prestained NIR
Protein Ladder, cat.no. 26635). Samples were initially run at 90 V until they escaped from
the well. The voltage was then increased to 125 V and held for 45 minutes. Following that,
the gel was stained with invitrogen’s Simply Blue Safestain (cat. no. LC6060) and visualised
with Li-cor Odessey Fc.

Chapter 6
Discussion
6.1
Choice between F.A., F.B, F.C and F.D
The results described in the previous section suggest that Cholesterol’s packing ability helps
to maintain less variable and smaller liposomal size. Data from the formulation F.A., where
DOPE-mPEG is not used, can be compared with data from F.C, where all lipids are in-
cluded until the ratio reaches 8:1. Looking at the data from F.B, where Cholesterol and
DOPE-mPEG are not used, shows non-linear size values. However, no significant difference
can be observed when comparing this data to the previously mentioned formulations.
What is more, the data from formulation F.D suggests that the presence of PEG with-
out the packing ability of Cholesterol results in micro-metric particles, which are too big
to be considered for any therapeutic purposes. The surface charge is common in all the
formulations. In this case, the surface charge was always negative regardless of the lipid
combination and ratio. The results do not match the expectations, because the lipids used
for manufacturing LNPs had a positive charge. Using cationic lipids to encapsulate an an-
ionic protein is easy due to the electrostatic interactions between the two charges.
59
==PAGE:72==
Lastly, the surface charge achieved is the one provided by the lipids, which camouflage
the protein and turn out to be exposed. The positively charged nanocarriers play a crucial
role in the interaction with the negatively charged cell walls to promote protein or genetic
material uptake. The fact that no positive charge was registered in DLS measurements,
may indicate that some free, non-encapsulated proteins provided this negative charge to the
analyzed sample.
Analysing data from figures 4.1, 4.2, and 4.3, presents changes that can be observed com-
pared to the original data. The most apparent differences between the samples were the
size and the surface charges. In each of the samples, the procedure of pellet resuspension in
MQ water caused the liposomes to agglomerate and stick together; therefore, the size values
were more significant than in the previous tests. Regarding surface charge, the presence of
the MQ water interacted with the electrostatics of DOTAP and caused the surface charge
to switch to positive values.
Having analysed all the points above, the choice of the formulation to continue with was
made. That decision was mainly based on the size and PDI results since all the samples had
comparable Zeta potential results. The decision was to continue with formulation F.C since
the results of this formulation were the most consistent. Compared with other formulations,
in F.C, most samples created particles within the desired size range and the desired PDI.
60
==PAGE:73==
6.2
Choice between different lipid to protein ratios (F1
to F10
The reformulation of F.C to assess the best lipid-to-protein ratio yielded results consistent
with the initial study. It was observed that lipid-to-protein ratio influences all the measured
parameters. The size measurements showed that the higher the lipid content, the bigger the
particles in the formulation tend to be. This shows that when the encapsulation capacity is
reached, the remaining lipids start to aggregate with each other or onto the newly formed
lipid particles, making them bigger. However, apart from F7 with lipid-to-protein ratio of
7:1, all the remaining formulations found themselves within the 150 nm to 300 nm size range.
The three formulations with the lowest sizes were F1, F2, and F3, which had the lipid to
protein ratio 4:1, 4,5:1, and 5:1 respectively.
The PDI measurements are directly comparable with the size results. The higher the average
size was, the higher the PDI got. Here, many samples fell outside the operational range of
0.1 to 0.3. High PDI values indicate that the distribution is broad or multimodal, in which
case the average size value reported alongside PDI becomes less relevant and a distribution
analysis is more representative. Hence, only samples with the smallest PDI values are rele-
vant.
Lastly, the loading efficiency results showed that the size and surface charge of the par-
ticles do not influence the loading capacity. The samples with the biggest size and PDI
values also had poor loading when compared to smaller particle samples. The loading effi-
ciency was expected to be around 10%, however, the encapsulation capacity exceeded these
expectations by reaching 30%.
61
==PAGE:74==
Having considered all relevant results it was decided that the best lipid-to-protein ratios
to be used in the following steps of the research will be 4:1, 4,5:1, and 5:1, because these
particles were big enough to be loaded with protein and the protein-loaded nanoparticle size
stayed within the range of 150 nm to 300 nm, while having the lowest PDI and the highest
protein loading.
6.3
Release of BSA from MQ LNPs
The release of protein from formulations prepared in MQ water was unsuccessful. The sam-
ples aggregated, forming big clumps of lipids. Control samples of BSA in the organic solvents
had sizes between 5-20 nm. Since no spikes at this range were present, it is certain that no
protein was released, as described in a Figure 8.8
It was hypothesised that this happened, because of the strong electrostatic forces between
lipids and lipids with protein. In order to mitigate this issue, three approaches emerged.
First, it included using a different helper lipid than the original formulation. DSPC was
chosen to replace DOPE, and a formulation with the same lipid-to-protein ratios as before
was performed. Second, it involved dissolving BSA in tris-buffer at the isoelectric point of
BSA. That would cause BSA to lose all charge it usually carries and cause the interactions
between lipid and protein to seize. The third approach was to add salt into the solution to
influence the interactions within the particle. For that purpose, PBS was used to dissolve
the BSA and later load it into the lipid nanoparticle. Out of these formulations, the most
successful one was later used to test release in organic solvents.
Lastly, the time stability testing proved that after four months of being kept at 4 °C, the lipid
nanoparticles tend to aggregate. Results of all samples, apart from one, which is believed to
have been a laboratory error, had bigger sizes than their initial results.
62
==PAGE:75==
6.4
Choice of the best alternative formulation
The alternative formulations’ manufacturing results were analysed and compared to the
initial formulation with MQ water to choose the formulation for further release testing. In-
terestingly, the results varied significantly between these formulations.
Size measurements showed that the PBS formulation was similar to the MQ formulation. All
three formulations (f1 to f3) had small sizes between 112 and 168 nm. DSPC and tris-buffer
formulations had each one sample with results within the expected range, making them less
successful. Similarly, the PDI results showed the superiority of the PBS formulation over
the DSPC and tris-buffer formulations, with none of the latter having its result within the
expected range.
The results of Zeta potential measurement revealed that both the DSPC and tris-buffer
formulations had a positive charge, whereas similarly to the MQ formulation, the PBS for-
mulation had a negative surface charge. This result in DSPC formulation confirmed the
hypothesis that the negative charge of MQ formulations was acquired due to the interac-
tions between MQ water and DOTAP. Similarly, the low pH provided by the tris-buffer
caused the lipids in the formulation to change their charge and become passively charged.
The last measured parameter was the encapsulation efficiency. Here the results were very
surprising. Even though DSPC and tris-buffer formulation were bigger and their PDI values
were outside of the acceptable range, the loading efficiency was higher or comparable to the
results of MQ loading efficiency. The least satisfying results were obtained by the PBS for-
mulation that reached the loading efficiency of 12 to 18%. However, considering the size and
PDI results, it was decided that even with the PBS formulation’s worst loading efficiency
results, it was still the best formulation to continue the release testing.
63
==PAGE:76==
6.5
Release of BSA from PBS LNPs
The results of release testing from PBS nanoparticles show that the addition of a charge from
salts present in BSA into the formulation was successful. The additional charge affected the
stability of LNPs, causing the lipids to have a less rigid structure. This meant that lipids
were able to collapse on themselves upon contact with organic solvents and, by doing so,
released the protein.
The size measurements show that LNPs size lowered upon contact with the organic sol-
vents. All samples from all formulations managed to have the nanoparticles collapse on
themselves and cause the release of BSA. Moreover, the PDI of each sample became larger,
which means that polydispersity was disturbed. Additionally, separate spikes at the size of
6 nm were present in the DLS output (Figure 8.10). Such presence was not noted in the
control samples, which proves that BSA was released from the LNPs.
64
==PAGE:77==
6.6
SDS-PAGE
After the unsuccessful attempts to release the protein from MQ water formulations, SDS-
PAGE was performed to attempt a disruption of lipid nanoparticles and release of the protein.
It was expected that none of the samples would manage to release the protein; hence, no
bands in test samples would be visible. Nevertheless, the results showed the presence of
protein in all test samples and their controls, which means the protein managed to escape
from their lipid nanoparticles. The test samples had a clear band at 66kDa, indicating the
presence of BSA.
However, all samples had additional bands at higher molecular weights, which indicated
either the presence of contaminants or the formation of oligomers of BSA. Sigma Life Sci-
ence, Merck claims that the purity of their product is at 96% [43]. Thus the possibility that
such a big contaminant would be present in the sample is unlikely. In addition, accord-
ing to the literature, BSA is prone to aggregation at various pH values. Furthermore, the
high molecular weight bands correspond to double or triple the size of BSA, which confirms
that most probably they are caused by BSA aggregation. Moreover, BSA control samples
also had additional bands at lower molecular weights. This indicated the presence of low
molecular weight contaminants such as sugars or peptides. It is possible that the lack of
those contaminants in the test samples was due to the centrifuging and resuspension that
accidentally helped to remove them. The second possibility is that dose contaminants got
trapped inside the lipid nanoparticles and did not manage to escape it, which caused them
to become invisible in the SDS-PAGE of test samples.
The results of SDS-PAGE caused the consideration of the need for further purification of BSA
before formulation. If the contaminants are present in BSA may influence the formulation
and encapsulation of BSA. Thus, a formulation with purified BSA was performed.
65
==PAGE:78==
6.7
Formulation with purified BSA
The last formulation prepared in this research contained purified BSA. The results of this
formulation were analysed and compared with the results of the MQ water formulation and
PBS formulation.
The size of the purified BSA formulation was within the acceptable range, yet f1 and f3
were bigger than their MQ and PBS equivalents. Nevertheless, the sizes of these formu-
lations were satisfactory. PDI, on the other hand, was above the limit range for all three
samples. High PDI values indicate that the distribution is broad, in which case the average
size values reported alongside PDI become less relevant, and a distribution analysis is more
representative.
The Zeta potential of purified BSA formulation was positive for all the tested samples.
This result was unexpected and may mean that some of the impurities were in an electro-
static interaction with some of the lipids, causing the particles to gain a negative surface
charge. The encapsulation efficiency results were average compared to the MQ and PBS
formulations. The loading efficiency values averaged at 25%, which is a greater result than
initially expected, but less compared with the MQ water formulations’ efficiencies.
Apart from a notable difference in surface charge, the other results do not point out a
clear advantage of purification of BSA before the lipid nanoparticle loading.

Chapter 7
Future Work
Unfortunately, the Master thesis research had a limited time frame, which meant that not
all of the experiments that could provide meaningful insight into the research could have
been carried out. These additional tests would have aided the research with additional data
to support the discussion and conclusions.
One of the prominent examples of such tests is the preparation of other formulations with
different lipids, media and proteins. These tests would help to find an even more efficient
formulation for protein delivery. Once the formulations are made and their sizes and loading
capacities are measured and analyzed. It would have been helpful to perform further release
testing in organic solvents and temperature or in cell culture. Moreover, a circular dichroism
should be performed to assess the state of the released protein, to make sure it did not get
damaged in encapsulation or release.
Lastly, it would have been beneficial to see the release results from the purified BSA formu-
lation described in this paper. Additionally, having the SDS-PAGE of purified BSA could be
compared with the initial SDS-PAGE results, and it could provide more in-depth information
about the influence of impurities present in BSA on the lipid nanoparticle formulation.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
Based on the results and discussion, it can be concluded that proteins can be successfully
encapsulated into and released from lipid nanoparticles. Secondly, the formulation of protein-
carrying lipid nanoparticles is the most successful when formulated with ionisable packing,
stabilising and helper lipids. Moreover, the electrostatic interactions dictate the best medium
for successfully formulating protein-loaded lipid nanoparticles. Furthermore, the release of
the protein from lipid nanoparticles is possible. However, it has to be researched further for
more productive results. Lastly, in case of a successful release, the state of the protein has
to be assessed in future work to ensure that protein was not damaged during encapsulation
or release.
