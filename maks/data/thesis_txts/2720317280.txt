Abstract 
New national requirements are forcing the Danish building industry into a more sustain-
able mindset. From January 2023, all new buildings must include a life-cycle assessment 
(LCA), and new buildings with a heated area above 1000 m2 must also stay within a 
specific CO2 limit. It is estimated that these requirements result in a drastic increase in 
the number of LCA calculations. More automated and digital solutions regarding LCA 
calculations on buildings are therefore needed to meet the future workload of LCA cal-
culations. Therefore, this thesis's research objective is to contribute to the development 
of digital LCA calculation tools by investigating the user acceptance of an LCA-workflow 
in an engineering consultancy (Sweco). The research objective was realized by first ex-
amining the user acceptance of LCAbyg using the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use 
of Technology (UTAUT), a technology acceptance model. Next, overall features and 
requirements for an optimal LCA workflow were mapped by using semi-structured inter-
views and literature. From the mapped features and requirements, the potential optimi-
zation areas in the LCA-workflow in Sweco (DIPP-workflow) were identified by conduct-
ing an online survey and using a point system. The results revealed that three out of 15 
hypotheses explaining the user acceptance of LCAbyg were significant. These results 
showed that social influence and usefulness have a positive effect on the behavioural 
intention to use LCAbyg, which also leads to actual use. From the list of 28 mapped 
features and requirements, seven of them were identified as potential optimization areas. 
Qualified recommendations for further development of the LCA workflow in Sweco 
(DIPP-workflow) have been presented based on the results. Additions and changes to 
the DIPP interface may reduce the amount of manual work and improve the structure 
of the life-cycle inventory (LCI). Using a future application programming interface (API) 
from LCAbyg may improve the automatization of the analysis and results, as manual 
imports and exports will be eliminated. The methodology for identifying potential opti-
mization areas is considered a solid tool optimization workflow, and a repetition of the 
investigation may be valuable in the future. 
Keywords: Technology Acceptance Model; UTAUT; user acceptance; BIM integration; 
LCA-workflow; Optimization; New CO2 requirements.

1  Introduction 
Denmark aims to be a green frontrunner in global climate action and inspire and encour-
age the rest of the world to follow their footprints. Denmark has set a national goal to 
reduce 70% of the country's emissions in 2030 compared to 1990 and to become climate 
neutral by 2050 (Klima- Energi og Forsyningsministeriet, 2020). Currently, the construc-
tion sector accounts for 37% of the global CO2 emissions (United Nations Environment 
Programme, 2021). As for Denmark, 30% of the CO2 emissions are associated with build-
ings in Denmark (Regeringens klimapartnerskaber, 2020), which demonstrates the im-
portance and potential to reduce the carbon footprint of the building and construction 
sector.  
Due to the CO2 reduction potential within the building and construction sector in Den-
mark, new national requirements regarding the sustainability of buildings have been in-
tegrated into the Danish building code by the beginning of 2023 (The Danish Housing 
and Planning Authority, 2021a). These new requirements ensure that all new buildings 
in Denmark will include a life-cycle assessment (LCA), which forces the industry to in-
tegrate sustainability into the building design. To force the industry into an even more 
sustainable mindset, the new requirements have also introduced CO2 limits, which will 
become stricter throughout the following years. It has been estimated that the newly 
introduced requirements will result in a drastic increase in the number of LCA calcula-
tions (The Danish Housing and Planning Authority, 2021b). It is therefore also believed 
that there is a desperate need for more automated and digital LCA workflows in order 
to meet the future workload. 
1.1  Problem definition 
Based on the introduction above, the research objective of this thesis is to contribute to 
developing digital and automated LCA workflows due to the new requirements intro-
duced. This is realized by investigating the user acceptance of an LCA-workflow in an 
engineering consultancy, through the following objectives:  
y 
Investigate the user acceptance of the most used LCA calculation tool in Denmark. 
y 
Map overall features and requirements of a general LCA workflow. 
y 
Identify potential optimization areas in the new LCA workflow in Sweco (DIPP-
workflow). 
y 
Use the knowledge obtained to provide qualified recommendations for further devel-
opment of the DIPP-workflow.

2.1  National regulations towards greener buildings 
On March 5, 2021, the Danish government has alongside with several other political 
parties come to an agreement on the National Strategy for Sustainable Construction 
(The Danish Housing and Planning Authority, 2021a). This agreement ensured that from 
the 1st of January 2023, requirements for the climate footprints of buildings were phased 
into the building regulations. This means that all new constructions must include a life-
cycle assessment (LCA) and document the climate impacts in kg CO2-eq/m2/year (The 
Danish Housing and Planning Authority, 2023, § 297). Other than the requirement for 
LCA, new CO2 limits have been introduced and will be regulated step-by-step throughout 
the years. Figure 2.1 shows the step-by-step phasing of the CO2 requirements.

The initial and current functioning regulation states that all new constructions with a 
heated floor area above 1000 m2 must not emit more than 12.0 kg CO2-eq/m2/year (The 
Danish Housing and Planning Authority, 2023, §298). Stage D in the life-cycle assessment 
(see section 2.2 for more about stages of LCA) shall not be included in the calculation 
when complying with the CO2 limit as it is beyond the system boundary. From 2025 the 
threshold limit value will be reduced to 10.5 kg CO2-eq/m2/year and will apply to all 
new constructions, not only buildings with a heated floor area above 1000 m2. In 2029 
the threshold limit will be reduced to 7.5 kg CO2-eq/m2/year for all new constructions. 
The threshold limits for the Voluntary CO2 standard are more ambitious and will, in 
2029, be at 5 kg CO2-eq/m2/year. However, these limits are up for revision as the building 
industry keeps generating new data and knowledge on which the new limits should be 
based. The limit values will be defined two years before they take effect (The Danish 
Housing and Planning Authority, 2021a). The initial threshold limits of the new require-
ments are based on a two-year test phase of the Voluntary Sustainability Class (see 2.1.1 
The Voluntary Sustainable Class below for elaboration). 
2.1.1  The Voluntary Sustainable Class 
On Maj 29, 2020, the former minister of Housing launched the Voluntary Sustainability 
Class (FBK1) to accelerate the green transformation (The Danish Housing and Planning 
Authority, 2020). From the announcement of FBK, all building owners were invited to 
take part in a two years testing phase, which should provide data and experience that 
could be used for political decisions regarding new regulations of the Danish building 
code. However, the testing phase has, in March 2022, been extended to the 1st of Novem-
ber 2023 (Videnscenter om Bygningers Klimapåvirkning (VCBK), 2022), which will give 
the industry more time to collect experience. FBK holds nine requirements for new con-
struction and renovation, which were more environmentally ambitious than the former 
building code (Danish Transport Construction and Housing Authority, 2020). Some of 
the requirements were introduced as entirely new requirements compared to the Danish 
building code, including initial and final life-cycle assessments. Especially with the re-
quirement of life-cycle assessments, FBK has been a significant contributor to the Na-
tional Strategy for Sustainable Constructions and the new legal regulations.

2.2  LCA calculations of buildings 
Life-cycle assessment (LCA) has become an essential part of the building industry. LCA 
has gained so much interest and importance that it is now a part of the building regula-
tion in Denmark. LCA describes the environmental impact of the entire life cycle of a 
product or service (for instance, a building). The method for life-cycle assessments of a 
building has been standardized through European and International Standards. DS/EN 
ISO 14040 (CEN, 2008) has been developed towards a general method for life-cycle as-
sessment. A more building-oriented standard DS/EN 15804 (CEN, 2012), is developed 
specifically for building materials and provides the basis of Environmental Product Dec-
larations (EPDs). DS/EN 15978 (CEN, 2011) is the standard for the life-cycle assessment 
of a building and defines the life-cycle stages, see Figure 2.2.  
 
Figure 2.2 The life cycle of a building according to DS/EN 15978 
The modules A1-A3 describe the product stage, which includes the emissions associated 
with the production of a material, in other words, "cradle-to-gate". The product stage 
involves extracting raw materials such as mining and farming followed by transporting 
the raw material to manufacture specific products. The phases A4-A5 describe the con-
struction stage, which includes all emissions associated with the transport of building 
materials as well as the construction work at the site, such as cranes, scaffolding, 
==PAGE:15==

2.3 Towards digitalization of sustainability 
 
14 
installations etc. B1-B7 covers the use stage, which includes the lifetime of the building 
from construction completion to deconstruction/demolishment. The system boundary of 
the use stage includes, among others, the energy and water use, maintenance including 
cleaning and replacement of building components such as windows, roofing felt, heating 
systems, paint etc. The modules C1-C4 cover the end-of-life stage, which covers the 
emissions of deconstruction/demolishing of the building if it is not intended to be used 
any further. This stage also includes all the processes needed for products to be discarded, 
reused, or recycled. The modules from A1-C4 describe the "cradle-to-grave" and cover 
the whole life cycle of a building. If materials or products are assessed to be recycled or 
reused, this benefit is declared in module D, which holds the net impacts from recycling 
or reuse. The net impacts are the potential benefits from recycling as the product stage 
(A1-A3) will be avoided.  
2.2.1  LCAbyg 
Currently, the most used LCA calculation tool in the building industry is LCAbyg, de-
veloped by BUILD for the Danish construction industry (The Danish Housing and Plan-
ning Authority, 2021b). LCAbyg calculates the environmental profile and resource con-
sumption of a building, given the correct information is provided. The information about 
the building parts, constructions, materials and the energy consumption of the building 
is manually entered through the user interface. Based on the provided information, 
LCAbyg automatically computes results in compliance with the standards, which are 
presented in tables, graphs and reports. A new version of LCAbyg has just been launched 
under the name LCAbyg 2023, which is adapted to the new requirements and therefore 
includes the same modules as in Figure 2.2 (BUILD, 2023). 
The workflow using LCAbyg is primarily a manual process, making it very time-consum-
ing and expensive to perform LCA calculations in any building design stage. Especially 
the early-stage design phase demands many calculations when trying out different solu-
tions, which is why improved usability and automatization of the LCA calculation work-
flow is needed. At this point, LCAbyg allows the use of the JSON format as a third-
party integration, which enhances the possibilities of automatization. However, this pos-
sibility requires more advanced programming skills and is therefore not an option for the 
average user. 
2.3  Towards digitalization of sustainability 
The National Strategy for Sustainable Construction (The Danish Housing and Planning 
Authority, 2021b) contains a wide range of initiatives that aim to form the future regu-
lation of sustainable construction. These initiatives are divided into five focus areas that 
cover the three dimensions of sustainability: environmental, economic and social sustain-
ability. One main ambition of the initiatives is to improve the area of digital workflows, 
which can make sustainable construction simpler and more efficient. With the new

requirement for LCA calculations for buildings (see section 2.1 for more), the number of 
LCA calculations is expected to increase drastically. In fact, it is currently estimated that 
the industry will face an additional cost of DKK 218 million due to the new requirements

LCA calculations, it is therefore important to digitalize and simplify the LCA workflows.

2.3.1  BIM integration 
Based on the literature presented in the previous sections, there is a drastic need for a 
more digitalized workflow for life-cycle assessment calculations. The manual workflow 
when using LCAbyg for LCA calculations is inadequate with the estimated future loads 
of these calculations.  
The building industry has already entered the digital age, as an increasing number of 
consultants and contractors are using building information modelling (BIM) as a main 
tool for designing and planning a construction project (The Danish Housing and Planning 
Authority, 2021b). BIM is an intelligent shared digital model that can provide a high 
level of information of all construction areas. This shared digital model allows all project 
stakeholders to work together efficiently, making BIM the main digital planning method 
in construction (Adepoju, 2022).  
The BIM model can include a wide range of properties, including data on quantities of 
building materials and building parts, which makes it obvious to use the BIM model as 
a base for more efficient LCA calculations, as the model already contains a lot of relevant 
information. The "only" thing to do here is to match and multiply the quantities of the 
materials with the respective environmental impacts as defined in DS/EN 15804 (CEN, 
2012). However, BIM integration of LCA comes with many barriers, which will be dis-
cussed in the following section. 
Disadvantages and barriers 
BIM integration of LCA is not as straight forwards as it sounds. A study (Tam et al., 
2021) identified three major barriers to BIM-based LCA from critical reviews of 71 pub-
lications about BIM-based LCA. These barriers include the lack of interoperability, the 
inefficiency of data input into LCA tools, and the complexity of extracting data in BIM-
based LCA implementation. The lack of interoperability is recognized as one of the main 
barriers due to the waste of time and effort for manually entry and transfer of data. 
==PAGE:17==
2 Background 
2.3 Towards digitalization of sustainability 
 
16 
Therefore, there is a need for more compatible solutions if BIM-based LCA should work 
successfully. Another study has identified and ranked 21 advantages and seven disad-
vantages of BIM-based LCAs through a mixed-method review (Seyis, 2020). Nr. 1 disad-
vantage in this study is supporting the statement above as it is lack of interoperability 
between LCA and BIM tools followed by nr. 2: lack of standardization of LCA procedures 
and nr. 3: lack of BIM data for whole LCA applications. Disadvantage nr. 3 is also backed 
up by another critical review of a BIM-based LCA method (Soust-Verdaguer et al., 2017), 
which explains that BIM models should include more information about material prop-
erties in order to improve the data exchange between BIM and LCA. This means that if 
the level of development (LOD) of the BIM model is low, then the LCA of the building 
will not represent the true environmental impact if the LCA is based on the BIM model 
alone. Various studies suggest a minimum of LOD300 or higher for the BIM model in 
order to base the LCA on the BIM model (Hollberg et al., 2020) (Durão et al., 2020).  
Advantages 
When the barriers of BIM integration of LCA are overcome, BIM-based LCA has a lot 
of potentials. As mentioned in the section above, the study (Seyis, 2020) has ranked 21 
advantages of BIM-based LCAs. The top five advantages have a significant level of ex-
tremely important and four of these advantages circle around ease of early-stage design 
and decision making. The other extremely important advantage is about optimizing the 
documentation process. The main contributors behind all these advantages of BIM-based 
LCAs are the effectiveness and reduced workload associated with the automatization of 
using BIM. This is only a reality when the barriers mentioned in the section above are 
overcome. 
2.3.2  Other LCA tools 
LCAbyg is currently the most used LCA-tool in Denmark (The Danish Housing and 
Planning Authority, 2021b); however, many other LCA-tools are available on the market. 
The tools are designed for different purposes, whereas some tools are better for design 
stages and others are better for complete and final life-cycle assessments. This section 
will try to give a short overview of some of the different building specific LCA-tools on 
the market. 
Tally Life Cycle Assessment (tallyLCA) 
TallyLCA is a Revit2 application that allows architects and engineers to use life-cycle 
assessments of building materials for a whole-building analysis as well as comparative 
analyses directly in the Revit environment (Building Transparency, 2022b). In the BIM 
environment, the user can define the scope or the functional unit of the LCA, which can 
be filtered by Revit categories, work sets, design options and more. Furthermore, the

2.4.2  Advantages and disadvantages with DIPP 
Advantages 
DIPP is developed to ease many workflows, such as LCA calculations within each project, 
which is why this platform has several advantages. First of all, DIPP allows BIM-inte-
gration, which is one of the main advantages and reasons for the development of DIPP. 
By working with a correctly structured DIPP Project linked to a BIM model, users can

place. Furthermore, another advantage is the JSON integration with LCAbyg. Many

2.5  Technology acceptance models and theories 
Since the 1970s, many technology acceptance models and theories have appeared with a 
common purpose; trying to understand usersݏ acceptance and behavioural intention to 
use new technology. When users are introduced to a new technology or system, many 
factors influence their choice of how and when they will use it (Rondan-Cataluña et al., 
2015). Therefore, when planning a new system or technology, information system prac-
titioners would like to be able to: Firstly, predict whether a new system will be acceptable 
to users. Secondly, diagnose the reasons why a planned system may not be fully accepta-
ble to users. Thirdly, to take corrective action to increase the system's acceptability to 
enhance the business impact resulting from the large investments in time and money 
associated with introducing new information technologies into organizations (Davis et 
al., 1989).  
The technology acceptance models and theories are helpful tools that help information 
system practitioners plan their systems or technologies in the best and cheapest way 
possible. A historical overview of the most popular models and theories is shown in Figure 
2.5. Only the most relevant models and theories will be elaborated in this study.

2.5.1  Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) 
In 1986 Fred D. Davis introduced an adaption of the Theory of Reasoned Action (TRA), 
which came to be well known as the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) (Davis, 1985). 
Later in 1989, Davis used TAM to explain computer usage behaviour using the first 
modified version of TAM, shown in Figure 2.6. TAM was specially tailored for modelling 
usersݏ acceptance of information systems or technologies with the key purpose of provid-
ing a basis for tracing the impact of external factors on internal beliefs, attitudes and 
intentions (Davis et al., 1989). 
 
Figure 2.6 Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) (Davis et al., 1989) 
TAM posits that two beliefs: Perceived Usefulness (the degree to which a person believes 
that using a particular system would enhance his or her job performance (Davis, 1989)) 
and Perceived Ease of Use (the degree to which a person believes that using a particular 
system would be free of effort (Davis, 1989)), are the primary factors for explaining 
==PAGE:24==

computer acceptance behaviour. This means for the Perceived Usefulness that people will 
simply not accept a new system if they do not find it useful. And as for the Perceived 
Ease of Use, people will simply not accept a system if it requires too much effort com-
pared to other systems. Based on numerous studies, TAM consistently accounts for about 
40% of the variance in usage intentions and behaviour (Venkatesh & Davis, 2000). 
2.5.2  Extensions of TAM 
About a decade after the introduction of TAM, many empirical tests had consistently 
proved Perceived Usefulness to be a strong determinant of usage intention (Venkatesh 
& Davis, 2000). However, Venkatesh and Davis believed that the determinants of Per-
ceived Usefulness had been overlooked and that a better understanding of these determi-
nants would help them design interventions that would increase usersݏ acceptance and 
usage of new systems (Venkatesh & Davis, 2000). The desire to understand the determi-
nants of this construct led to an extension of TAM, known as TAM2 (see Figure 2.7). 
 
Figure 2.7 TAM2 (Venkatesh & Davis, 2000) 
TAM2 uses TAM as the original starting point, whereas it incorporates additional theo-
retical constructs to explain the determinants of Perceived Usefulness. The added con-
structs include social influence processes (Subjective Norm, Voluntariness and Image) 
and cognitive instrumental processes (Job Relevance, Output Quality and Results De-
monstrability). TAM2 accounts for about 34% - 52% of the variance in usage intentions 
(Venkatesh & Davis, 2000).  
Alongside the development of TAM2, an interest in understanding the determinants of 
Perceived Ease of Use led to the development of a model of Determinants of Perceived

with TAM2 and resulted in an integrated technology acceptance model, TAM3 (see Fig-

& Bala, 2008).

2.5.3  Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) 
Concurrently with the increasing number of technology acceptance models and theories 
at the beginning of this millennium, researchers were confronted with a choice among 
many models and found that they needed to pick their ݒfavoured modelݓ and ignore the 
contributions from other alternative models (Venkatesh et al., 2003). This led to a review

of existing models in order to formulate a unified view of usersݏ acceptance, which resulted 
in the Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) (see Figure 2.9). 
 
Figure 2.9 Unified Theory of Acceptance and Use of Technology (UTAUT) (Venkatesh et al., 2003) 
UTAUT consist of four key determinants: Performance Expectancy (the degree to which 
an individual believes that using the system will help him or her to attain gains in job 
performance), Effort Expectancy (the degree of ease associated with the use of the sys-
tem), Social Influence (the degree to which an individual perceived that important others 
believe he or she should use the new system) and Facilitation Conditions (the degree to 
which an individual believes that an organizational and technical infrastructure exists to 
support use of the system) (Venkatesh et al., 2003). Performance Expectancy and Effort 
Expectancy correspond to Perceived Usefulness and Perceived Ease of Use from TAM, 
respectively. Furthermore, UTAUT includes four moderators: Gender, Age, Experience 
and Voluntariness of Use. UTAUT was able to account for 70% of the variance in usage 
intention (Venkatesh et al., 2003), which is higher than the previous mentioned models. 
UTAUT was originally developed to explain employee technology acceptance and use in 
an organizational context (Venkatesh et al., 2012). As the UTAUT needed attention in 
the consumer use context, a new model, UTAUT2, was developed and tailored to fit the 
consumer use context. UTAUT2 was able to account for 56% - 74% of the variance in 
usage intention (Venkatesh et al., 2012).

2.6.2  Evaluation of PLS-SEM results 
The evaluation of the PLS-SEM typically follows a two-step process, which is divided 
into the assessment of the measurement model (outer model) and the structural model 
(inner model). The first step is to examine the measuresݏ reliability and validity according 
to certain criteria. Then, if the measurement model is reliable and valid, step two can be 
initiated, which involves the assessment of the structural model. In this step, the hypoth-
eses are either accepted or rejected based on the statistical significance. In the following, 
the criteria for the evaluation of the measurement and structural model are described. 
Outer model (model of measurements) 
The first step in assessing the measurement model is to examine the loadings of the 
indicators (referred to as factor loadings). A factor loading above 0.708 is recommended, 
as it indicates that the construct explains more than 50% of the variance of the indicator 
(Hair et al., 2019). Thus, the indicatorݏs reliability is acceptable.  
The next step is to examine the internal consistency reliability using composite reliability. 
A composite reliability value between 0.70 ݋ 0.95 is recommended; however, values be-
tween 0.60 ݋ 0.70 is also acceptable (Hair et al., 2019). Higher reliability values simply 
indicate higher levels of reliability. Another measure of internal consistency reliability, 
which assumes similar thresholds, is the Cronbachݏs Alpha. However, the Cronbachݏs 
Alpha is a less precise measure of reliability as the indicators are unweighted, and the 
values are often more conservative than the composite reliability (Hair et al., 2019). 
However, the composite reliability may be too liberal, which is why an alternative meas-
ure for internal consistency reliability (പጲ) has been proposed by (Dijkstra & Henseler, 
2015). This measure can be a good compromise if only one of the other two measures 
accepts the reliability.  
The third step is to examine the convergent validity of each construct. The convergent 
validity is the extent to which an indicator in a particular measure converges to represent 
the underlying construct. For evaluating the convergent validity, the average variance

extracted (AVE) for all items of each construct is used. An acceptable AVE is above 
50% for each construct, which indicates that the construct explains at least 50% of the

The fourth step is to assess the discriminant validity, which is the degree to which one 
construct differs from all other constructs in the research model. The discriminant valid-

threshold means that discriminant validity is not present. Other measures are available 
for assessing the discriminant validity, including the Fornell-Larcker scale and cross-
loadings. However, the HTMT is a newer technique in PLS-SEM and is recommended

Structural model (inner model) 
The next step in evaluating the PLS-SEM results is to assess the structural model and 
discover the relationship among the constructs. The first step is to examine the coefficient 
of determination (੽ᇼ), which measures the variance explained in each of the endogenous 
constructs. The ੽ᇼ is thus a measure of the modelݏs explanatory power or, put in other 
words, the in-sample predictive power (Hair et al., 2019). ੽ᇼ ranges from 0 to 1, and

Bootstrapping (a re-sampling technique) is used to test the significance of the path coef-
ficient. The critical t-value for a two-tailed test is 1.96 at a significance level of 5% (Hair 
et al., 2011). This means that absolute t-values above 1.96 indicates that the path coef-
ficient is significant at a level of 5%.

given all registered users were active. In order to perform the PLS-SEM, a minimum 
sample size of 30 was needed based on the ten-times rule presented in section 2.6.1 as 
the inner model had three constructs directed at a particular inner construct. A sample 
size of ઒= 103 was collected through an online questionnaire in Google Forms, which 
circled around for approximately one month using a non-probability sampling technique. 
The questionnaire was distributed online through various platforms, including LinkedIn 
and a specific Facebook group for architectural engineers. Furthermore, the supervisor 
from DTU distributed the questionnaire among his students, and the supervisor from 
Sweco distributed the questionnaire among her employees. 
The questionnaire was divided into two parts; Part 1: demographic items and Part 2: 
UTAUT items. Part 1 was designed to get an understanding of the respondents, which 
contained questions about their profession, knowledge of LCA and years of working with 
LCA. The three moderators, Age (A), Gender (G) and Experience (E), were also placed 
in Part 1. Part 2 included the theoretical constructs and items from UTAUT and con-
sisted of the six constructs; Performance Expectancy (PE), Effort Expectancy (EE), So-
cial Influence (SI), Facilitating Conditions (FC), Behavioral Intention (BI) and Use Be-
havior (USE). Each construct contained three questions except USE, which only had one 
question. The items in the questionnaire were adapted from (Venkatesh et al., 2003) and 
then modified to fit the study. The questions regarding the constructs were rated on a 
7-point Likert scale ranging from 1 = ݒstrongly disagreeݓ to 7 = ݒstrongly agreeݓ. The 
remaining questions had individual scales and answer options. At the end of the ques-
tionnaire, it was possible to comment on likes and dislikes about LCAbyg to have quali-
tative data from this study. The complete questionnaire can be found in Appendix 6. 
3.1.3  Data analysis 
As mentioned earlier, structural equation modelling (SEM) is used to analyse the data 
when applying a technology acceptance model. To decide the right SEM approach for 
the data analysis, the rules of thumb presented in section 2.6 will be used. The goal of 
this study was not to test a theory but to apply the theory and identify the key "driver" 
constructs provided by the theory. Furthermore, the structural model was recursive 
(meaning no casual loops), and the sample size was small. Based on these arguments, the 
right approach for this study was to use PLS-SEM. The data analysis was done using 
SEMinR (version 2.3.1), as it is free at an unlimited sample size (smartPLS 4 used in the 
pilot study has a limit of 100 samples). SEMinR is an open-source software package 
developed for the R statistical environment. R version 4.2.1 was used. The workbook for

3.2  DIPP analysis 
As mentioned in the introduction of this chapter, the DIPP-workflow could not be eval-
uated using a technology acceptance model, introducing a new approach to analyse the 
DIPP-workflow. This approach includes qualitative input from semi-structured inter-
views with LCA experts from Sweco compared with the literature already presented. 
Potential optimization areas of the DIPP-workflow will afterwards be highlighted by 
using a questionnaire and a point system. 
3.2.1  Mapping features and requirements for optimal LCA workflow 
The first step in identifying potential optimization areas in the DIPP-workflow was to 
work out the requirements and features of an optimal LCA workflow in general. In order 
to organize and keep track of the requirements and features, they were mapped based on 
the workflow shown in Figure 3.2.

The features and requirements were mapped in the seven stages of the LCA-workflow 
(the dark grey boxes). "1. Use Case" describes what the LCA should include. In other 
words, the scope or functional unit will be defined in this stage. "2. Needs" describes what 
information is needed in order to conduct the LCA. This information can be provided by 
a BIM-model, schedules and other sources. "3. Model" is the input stage, which includes 
the modelling of the life-cycle inventory (LCI). "4. Analyse" describes the LCA analysis 
and calculation stage. "5. Guide" is the output stage and describes the presentation of 
the results. "6. Decide" describes the stage where the architect base a choice on the 
results, and "7. Change" describes the actual change that is made. A "General" step was 
added to capture overall requirements and features. 
The requirements and features of an optimal LCA workflow were collected from two 
different sources: Semi-structured interviews with LCA experts from Sweco and the lit-
erature presented in section 2.3 including the subsections. The two sources were selected 
to cover both a general view from the industry and a specific view from the actual users. 
These two approaches will be described shortly in the following sections. 
From LCA experts 
The requirements and features from the actual users were collected through semi-struc-
tured interviews with subject matter experts (SMEs) from Sweco. The semi-structured 
interviews were conducted to obtain qualified features and requirements directly from 
the LCA experts with hands-on experience. The use of semi-structured interviews was 
inspired by the study mentioned in section 2.3.1 that identified advantages and disad-
vantages associated with BIM-based LCA (Seyis, 2020). This study used semi-structured 
interviews to obtain expertsݏ opinions regarding the integrated use of BIM and LCAs 
and to collect data about the sector from professionals with hands-on experiences. The 
same objective applied to this study. A group of 8 LCA experts from Sweco was selected 
as SMEs and agreed to participate in the semi-structured interviews. They were individ-
ually asked what their wishes for an optimal LCA workflow, in general, would be in the 
different stages described in Figure 3.2. This resulted in a variety of features and require-
ments in each stage. The responses from all the participants were afterwards systemati-
cally categorized using inductive coding, a bottom-up approach. This approach was used 
to obtain a complete set of features and requirements from the actual users based on the 
semi-structured interviews where all perspectives were considered.  
From the literature 
Based on the literature presented in section 2.3 including the subsections, many features 
and requirements for LCA calculation workflows were mentioned. To capture the general 
view of features and requirements for LCA calculation workflows, these features and 
requirements were also considered. Using the framework mentioned above, features and 
requirements based on the literature were mapped in the stages described in Figure 3.2. 
The features and requirements were afterwards systematically categorized using deduc-
tive coding (a top-down approach) using the categories already generated from the semi-

structured interviews. When new categories presented themselves from the literature, 
they were marked as new features and requirements. The list generated from the litera-
ture represented the general view from the industry of an optimal LCA workflow. 
Final list 
Based on the two lists generated throughout the semi-structured interviews and the lit-
erature, triangulation was used to create a final list from the different sources. The new 
features and requirements from the literature were added to the list from the actual 
users. By doing this, features and requirements would be found to be important despite 
they were not highlighted by the actual users. This approach provided a thorough and 
unbiased reliable evaluation of features and requirements of a general optimal LCA work-
flow. To reduce the size of the final list, it was decided to include features and require-
ments that were categorized by at least two sources, including all participants from the 
semi-structured interviews and the literature. If a feature or requirement only was cate-
gorized by one source, it was not included in the final list. The final list included 28 
features and requirements (will also be referred to as ݒitemsݓ). 
3.2.2  Evaluate features and requirements in relation to DIPP-workflow 
The second step of identifying potential optimization areas in the DIPP-workflow was to 
evaluate and narrow down the items in the final list from a user-perspective context. To 
do this, two things for each item were rated by a selected group of Sweco employees. 
This group included LCA experts and a few architects with sufficient knowledge about 
LCA. The first area to be weighted by the LCA experts was the importance of the item 
when conducting LCA in general, which was rated on a 7-point Likert scale ranging from 
1 = ݒNot at all importantݓ to 7 = ݒVery importantݓ. The second area to be weighted 
was the satisfaction with the way the item is fulfilled by the current DIPP-workflow, 
which was rated on a 7-point Likert scale ranging from 1 = ݒStrongly disagreeݓ to 7 = 
ݒStrongly agreeݓ. This part could be skipped if the respondentݏs knowledge about DIPP 
was limited. A comment field was added for each item to collect potential comments, 
which may be valuable for Sweco and the discussion. All of this was done through an 
online survey in Google Forms, which was distributed to the relevant Sweco employees. 
A total of 15 employees answered the survey.

The points given for "importance" were in a range as follows: a mean rating of 1 (Not at 
all important) gave 0 points, and a mean rating of 7 (Very important) gave 6 points. 
The points given for "satisfaction" were the opposite of the importance and were in a 
range as follows: a mean rating of 1 (Strongly disagree) gave 6 points, and a mean rating 
of 7 (Strongly agree) gave 0 points. Interpolation was applied to allocate the points to 
the mean values within the range. The point system indicates that the higher the mean 
of the importance, the higher score and, thereby higher priority the feature/requirement 
will have. And as for the satisfaction, the lower the mean, the higher the score and 
thereby the higher priority the feature/requirement will have. By multiplying the scores 
for importance and satisfaction, a total score for each item was calculated.  
The total score explains the optimization potential for each feature and requirement in 
relation to the DIPP-workflow. For instance, if a feature shows to be very important but 
is already well integrated into the DIPP-workflow, the total score will be relatively low. 
Also, if a feature shows not to be important and people are not satisfied with the inte-
gration of this feature, the score will also be relatively low. However, if a feature shows 
to be important and not well integrated into the workflow, the score will be relatively 
high. The items with the highest scores will represent the features and requirements with 
the highest priority and biggest potential for optimizing the DIPP-workflow. It was de-
cided to highlight the items with scores above the third quartile. 
The three areas of the BIM ecosystem 
A digital workflow is more than just a tool, as it usually requires additional processes 
and information. (Gu et al., 2015) describes a BIM ecosystem and how three definitions 
in this ecosystem coevolve. The three definitions are: products, process and people (PPP). 
ݒProductsݓ refers to the technology in a BIM context and will be referred to as ݒToolݓ 
in this study. ݒProcessݓ refers to work practices and protocols and will also be referred 
to as ݒProcessݓ in this study. The last P, ݒPeopleݓ refers to roles and relationships in a 
BIM context and will in this study cover the part where information is needed and 
provided in the workflow and will therefore be referred to as ݒInformationݓ.  
To identify the potential optimization areas of DIPP, it was therefore relevant to consider 
distinguishing between Product, Tool and Information to see where the actual potential 
of the workflow lay. For example, does the real optimization lie in the digital tool, the 
workflow processes, the information provided, or a mix of all three? To do this, all 28 
features and requirements (items) were categorized into the three different BIM areas 
based on whether the feature or requirement was related to Tool, Process, Information 
or a mix. Then, the mean score of all items within each BIM area was analysed and 
evaluated using the same method described in the section above to identify the most 
critical area of the BIM ecosystem.

5  Discussion 
In this chapter, the results presented until this point will be discussed and will be divided 
into the two studies: UTAUT on LCAbyg and DIPP analysis. 
5.1  UTAUT on LCAbyg 
This section will discuss the results of the investigation of the user acceptance of LCAbyg 
using UTAUT. Firstly, the starting point and expectations of this research will be dis-
cussed, followed by a discussion of the respondents and the quality of the sample col-
lected. Next, the model's explanatory power will be discussed and compared to other 
studies leading to a discussion of the final results. Here, the meaning of the hypotheses 
will be explained, and the reasons for these will be discussed. Finally, recommendations 
for LCAbyg based on the results will be presented. 
5.1.1  Pioneering study 
Technology acceptance models have been widely used for evaluating user's acceptance of 
new technology, such as E-governance (Mohammad Ebrahimzadeh Sepasgozar et al., 
2020) and digital and e-learning (Bhattarai & Maharjan, 2020) (Masrom, 2007) (Liaw, 
2008). This thesis evaluates the user's acceptance and behavioural intention to use 
LCAbyg using UTAUT, a technology acceptance model. This study is believed to be a 
pioneer study as usersݏ acceptance of an LCA calculation tool has never been evaluated 
using a technology acceptance model. Therefore, it is not possible to compare the results 
to previous studies of this kind. However, it would have been interesting to see other 
studies evaluating users' acceptance of LCA calculation tools, as it would have provided 
initial insights into this study of what to expect from the results. Furthermore, these 
insights would have been helpful when deciding which technology acceptance model 
should be used and perhaps what areas that needed to be further investigated. 
5.1.2  What was expected from the results? 
As mentioned in the previous section, there are currently no other studies evaluating 
users' acceptance of LCA calculation tools. Therefore, no expected results could be stated 
from previous studies. However, the initial phase of this study raised some thoughts 
about what would be expected from the results. It can be roughly said that LCAbyg has 
monopoly on the Danish market when conducting LCA calculations of buildings, which 
means that people use it because it is the only option for most people in the Danish 
building industry. It was therefore expected that UTAUT would only explain a limited 
number of relationships in the use of LCAbyg. In other words, it was expected that only 
a limited number of the hypothesis would be confirmed. It was also dreaded that the 
research would not provide any useful results that could be used to evaluate DIPP.

5.1.3  Respondents 
The data analysed in this study were collected from an online questionnaire, which was 
answered by 103 respondents. Interestingly, 65% of the respondents are between 20 and 
29 years old, which is a relatively high representation of the younger generation. However, 
this was somehow expected, as sustainability in the building industry is an emerging 
field, which might affect students' interest. The increased interest in sustainability in the 
building industry can be deduced from the increasing number of DGNB certifications 
(Green Building Council Denmark). The level of LCA experience among the respondents 
can be argued to follow the age distribution, as 81% of the respondents have less than 
four years of experience. It is likely that younger people have fewer years of experience, 
which can explain these observations. However, this does, of course, not exclude cases 
with older people with only a few years of experience. 
5.1.4  The sample 
The profile of the respondents cannot be concluded 100% representative of the actual 
users of LCAbyg, as the sample size of ઒= 103 was below the estimated minimum sample 
size of 388 calculated by Slovin's formula (equation 2.1). The minimum sample size of 
388 was, however based on the total number of registered users of LCAbyg and not by 
the number of active users. Therefore, it is uncertain how well the collected sample rep-
resents the entire population of active users of LCAbyg. It is also worth mentioning that 
the sample of the survey is a result of the way the questionnaire was distributed. The 
fact that the questionnaire was distributed on a Facebook page for architectural engineers 
from DTU and in an LCA course at DTU means that many students or recently gradu-
ated have been exposed to this survey, which could have resulted in an overrepresentation 
of students in the sample collected. Furthermore, the questionnaire was distributed 
through LinkedIn to capture the professionals in the industry using LCAbyg. However, 
distributing through LinkedIn has a limited range of impressions based on the number 
and relevance of professional connections, reposts and sends. The supervisor from Sweco 
has a comprehensive network of LCA experts on LinkedIn, which might have contributed 
to many respondents. However, it is certain to say that not all active LCAbyg users were 
exposed to the questionnaire of this survey. Despite the uncertainties with the sample 
collection, the sample is still considered representative. 
5.1.5  The explanatory power 
UTAUT has previously been proven to explain about 70% of the variance in behavioural 
intention to use a technology (BI) and about 50% of the variance in technology use 
(USE) (Venkatesh et al., 2003). These values are relatively high compared to previous 
technology acceptance models, which is one of the reasons this model was used. Another 
study evaluating the acceptance of an E-Government Service using UTAUT was able to 
explain 55.4% of the variance in BI and 14.7% of the variance in USE (Wang et al., 
2006), which are a bit low compared to the other values. UTAUT was in this thesis able

to explain 66% of the variance in behavioural intention to use LCAbyg (BI) and 38% of 
the variance in use behaviour of LCAbyg (USE) using the ੽ᇼ value. The ੽ᇼ value of BI 
is really good, as it is almost as high as the value for the original UTAUT, and it is 
higher than the other considered technology acceptance models: TAM explains about

in section 2.5 about technology acceptance models). The explanatory power of the en-
dogenous constructs BI and USE are therefore very satisfied. 
5.1.6  Hypotheses explained 
The explanatory power is a result of the relevance of the path coefficients in the model, 
which have been analysed and tested during the hypothesis testing. Only three out of 15 
hypotheses were confirmed, as their path coefficients were significant at a level of 5%. It 
was expected that UTAUT would only explain a few relations in the acceptance of 
LCAbyg, which was confirmed by the results. However, the few confirmed hypotheses 
were proven to be quite important due to the relatively high explanatory power of the 
endogenous constructs. All the different hypotheses will be discussed in the following 
sections. 
H1 about Performance Expectancy (PE) 
The strongest significant key predictor of behavioural intention to use LCAbyg (BI) was 
calculated to be Performance Expectancy (PE) with a path coefficient of 0.398. The 
significant path coefficient from PE shows that the more useful LCAbyg is, the more 
people intend to use the program. This is not surprising as people usually strive for 
efficient and useful ways of doing their jobs.  
H2 about Effort Expectancy (EE) 
An interesting result from the hypothesis testing is that Effort Expectancy (EE) does 
not have a significant effect on BI. This means that people still intend to use LCAbyg 
whether it is easy to use or not. This can, however, also be recognized in other calculation 
and engineering programs, which can be very useful but not very intuitive and user-
friendly. The other UTAUT study about E-Government Service (Wang et al., 2006) also 
concludes that all paths except EE Æ BI (H2) were significant at a level of 5%, which 
correlates with the results from this study. However, other technology acceptance models 
(TAM, TAM2 and TAM3) posit that perceived ease of use (EE in UTAUT terms) has a 
significant positive effect on perceived usefulness (PE in UTAUT terms). In other words, 
the easier a system is to use, the more useful it will be. This relationship was, however, 
not examined by UTAUT but would be very relevant for explaining the real effect of the 
ease of use. Perhaps LCAbyg will be found more useful if it gets easier to use, which 
would lead to more intentions from people to use the program.

H3 about Social Influence (SI) 
The second strongest significant key predictor of BI was calculated to be Social Influence 
(SI) with a path coefficient of 0.363. The path coefficient shows that people have stronger 
intentions to use LCAbyg the more they are influenced by other sources. In other words, 
people are encouraged to use LCAbyg by sources with influence (could be a teacher, a 
manager or maybe even the government), which leads to a stronger intention to use the 
program. This result also fits quite well, as LCAbyg is the most used LCA calculation 
program in Denmark, among a few other possibilities (The Danish Housing and Planning 
Authority, 2021b).  
H4 about Facilitation Conditions (FC) 
Hypothesis H4, which states that Facilitating Conditions (FC) have a significant effect 
on Use Behaviour (USE) of LCAbyg, was rejected. This means that the existence of 
technical and organizational infrastructure to support the use of LCAbyg does not affect 
the actual use of LCAbyg. This can be explained by the fact that LCAbyg is a free 
software that requires no additional software or hardware to function. 
H5 about Behavioural Intention (BI) 
Until this point, it has been established that people intend to use LCAbyg more if they 
are encouraged to use the program and if it is useful. However, the intentions need to 
lead to actual use in order to affect the actual use of the program. Fortunately, hypothesis 
H5: "Behavioural Intention (BI) has a significant effect on Use Behaviour (USE) of 
LCAbyg", was confirmed during the hypothesis testing. This path coefficient was calcu-
lated to be 0.313, which explains that people with intentions to use LCAbyg most likely 
also will use the program. 
Moderators 
The rest of the hypotheses include the moderators: gender (G), age (A) and experience 
(E). None of these hypotheses were confirmed, as the path coefficients were not signifi-
cant at a level of 5%. This means that gender, age and experience do not moderate on 
any of the relationships and that the hypotheses above apply no matter who you are. 
5.1.7  Recommendations 
Based on the discussion above, suggestions for further development of LCAbyg can be 
presented. As for H3 about social influence (SI), it would promote the use of LCAbyg if 
the encouragement from influencing sources is increased. However, it would be difficult 
to improve the social influence, as LCAbyg is already well-recognized in the Danish 
building industry. A suggestion for improving social influence would be to include 
LCAbyg as a suggested calculation program in the Danish building code alongside the 
new LCA and CO2 limit requirements. This would be a strong influencing and recognized 
source, which, according to the results from this study, might lead to more intentions to

use LCAbyg. On the other hand, the social influence can also be endangered if other 
competitive LCA calculation tools present themselves. If this happens and the influencing 
sources promote the competitive tools - perhaps based on usefulness - the intention to

As for H1 about performance expectancy (PE), it will promote LCAbyg if the program

on the commented dislikes about LCAbyg, which are found in Table 4.6. The most sup-
ported statement of dislikes about LCAbyg is that it has a poor user interface, which 
refers to effort expectancy (EE) in the UTAUT theory. As discussed above in hypothesis 
H2 about effort expectancy, EE does not influence the user intentions (BI). Also, it is 
uncertain whether or not EE affects the performance expectancy (PE). Therefore, only 
if it can be proven that EE has a positive influence on PE, the usage of LCAbyg will be 
promoted if the user interface is improved. The second most supported statement of the 
dislikes about LCAbyg is that it lacks open-source options and BIM-integration. This is 
definitely an area of improvement to promote the usefulness of LCAbyg. As earlier men-
tioned, LCAbyg offers JSON-integration, which allows digital-strong companies to im-
plement smart solutions to make LCAbyg more useful. However, most users do not have 
these options, which might pressure LCAbyg to implement open-source and BIM-inte-
gration solutions. As a matter of fact, LCAbyg states on their website that they are 
currently working on BIM-integration using IFC so a solution might be on the way 
(BUILD, 2023). As for the open-source options, the people behind LCAbyg have re-
sponded that they are currently working on releasing a public API, hopefully by the end 
of February. This will open up for creativity and automatization of LCA-workflows in 
general, which will make LCAbyg more useful. The following two statements state that 
it is very time-consuming and requires too much manual work, which also refers to per-
formance expectancy. These statements call for automatization, which will speed up the 
process and reduce manual work, which will improve the performance expectancy.

5.2.2  Reliability of the results 
The results of this research were the outcome of the scope of this research, which was 
limited to the LCA experts within Sweco. Due to the limitation of the scope, relatively 
few respondents participated in the study, as there was a natural limit on how many 
LCA experts were employed at Sweco. A total of 15 employees participated in this re-
search, but it was far from all of them that had adequate knowledge to the DIPP work-
flow, as DIPP was not used by many employees of Sweco at the time of this research. 
However, given the circumstances of the few respondents, an evaluation of DIPP was 
still obtainable. This would not have been possible by using the ideal methodology for 
this purpose, namely using a technology acceptance model for the evaluation. 
The standard deviations for some of the requirements and features were quite large, 
which may be due to the few respondents. As explained in the methodology section, 
answering the questions about the satisfaction with the DIPP workflow was not manda-
tory if the respondent did not have adequate knowledge about DIPP. Therefore, the 
results from this were only based on very few responses ranging from 2 to 8. Furthermore, 
it is believed that these questions might also have been misunderstood, which might have 
led to even fewer responses. This belief is based on individual ratings followed by their 
individual comments (see Appendix 14 for comments). For instance, comments like "this 
feature does not exist within DIPPݓ (simplified) come with no rating for satisfaction. 
Ideally, for these cases, the ratings of the satisfaction should have been equal to 1 = 
"Strongly disagree" instead of neglected, meaning the feature does not exist within DIPP.

5.2.3  Potential optimization areas 
Based on semi-structured interviews and a review of the literature, a list of 28 features 
and requirements (will be referred to as "items") of an optimal LCA workflow was cre-
ated. All 28 items were rated based on importance and satisfaction, and a simple point 
system computed a total score for each item. The ones with a total score above the third 
quartile of 19.1 have been highlighted and will be discussed in this section. 
F) Operational energy information 
This item scored 31.2, which is the highest score of all 28 items. This high score is firstly 
due to its very high importance, as it is essential when conducting an LCA. Secondly, 
the high score is due to the very low rating of satisfaction, as DIPP, at this moment, 
does not handle the operational energy at all. The operational energy information is 
obtained from building envelope calculations (typically in BE18) and is entered manually 
in LCAbyg after importing the JSON-file exported from DIPP. The entering of the op-
erational energy information is, therefore, a manual step that needs to be done every 
time a change in the LCI in DIPP occurs. This item, therefore, has a lot of optimization 
potential. 
J) Easy structure and overview of LCI 
This item scored 19.4, which is just above the third quartile categorizing this item as one 
having a lot of optimization potential. Based on a few comments, it is difficult to get an 
overview of the LCI in DIPP, which might be the reason for the low satisfaction. The 
overview of the LCI is, at the moment, only possible to obtain in the LCAbyg-file after 
exporting from DIPP. The problem with this is the same as for the operational energy 
information: the flexibility is limited as the export, and import step is needed prior to 
getting the overview of the LCI. An easier structure and overview of the LCI in DIPP is 
therefore an important optimization potential.

This item scored 19.7, which is due to that is has the lowest satisfaction score of all items

DIPP, which will lead to unnecessary re-entry of information. This applies to properties

T) Easy location of errors/mistakes

explained by the fact that DIPP does not provide LCA results on its own, as the project

the processing of the results, which is done at the end of the LCA workflow.

5.2.4  Beneficial extension of LCAbyg? 
DIPP is, as explained in section 2.4 a platform where the composition of all building 
parts in a project are stored and described. DIPP itself does not make any life-cycle 
assessment calculations, as it uses the calculation program LCAbyg for this part instead. 
With DIPP, all the preparations prior to the LCA calculations are done outside of 
LCAbyg, which may classify DIPP as an extension of LCAbyg. This extension has been 
necessary for Sweco in order to optimize their LCA workflow due to the current issues 
with LCAbyg. In Table 4.6, the dislikes about LCAbyg are presented, which represents 
some of the main issues with LCAbyg. It is interesting to discuss whether DIPP, as a 
beneficial extension to LCAbyg, solves these main issues, which introduces the following 
discussion. 
The most supported issue of LCAbyg is that it has a poor user interface (g), which 
initially cannot be solved by DIPP. However, the preparation, including modelling of the 
LCI in DIPP prior to the calculations, corresponds to the modelling of the LCI in 
LCAbyg. With that said, the user interface of DIPP can be argued to solve the user 
interface of LCAbyg, as the work in DIPP replaces the work in LCAbyg. The item I 
(Simple and user-friendly workflow), from the DIPP analysis results, had a mean score 
in satisfaction of 3.17, which is rated just below somewhat satisfied, meaning that DIPP 
is not considered user-friendly and therefore does not solve the first issue. The second 
most supported issue is that LCAbyg lacks open-source and BIM integration (f). This is 
believed to have been solved by DIPP, as DIPP was created from the drastic need for 
BIM integration. The following two issues in line concern the large workload (b) associ-
ated with the amount of manual typing (a). It is also believed that DIPP has solved 
these issues, as automatization is a keyword when talking about DIPP. Quantities are 
automatically exported from Revit and imported into LCAbyg alongside the respective 
building parts, which reduces the amount of manual work drastically. The issues about

5.2.5  Recommendations 
Until this point, the potential optimization areas in the DIPP-workflow have been pre-
sented and discussed. This section will discuss recommendations and potential solutions 
to the potential optimization areas. A number of various LCA tools were presented in 
section 2.3.2 and it will be discussed whether these tools somehow include features that 
can contribute to the recommendations. 
F) Operational energy information 
As mentioned in the discussion, the operational energy information is being manually 
entered into LCAbyg after importing the DIPP project to LCAbyg. It is believed that 
this step can be eliminated and become a part of the imported project that is exported 
from DIPP, which will reduce re-entries when testing out different variations. The up-
coming documentation tool LCAcollect will, according to their webinar (Molio, 2022b), 
include a ݒProject Settingsݓ page where all necessary information about the building can 
be entered, including the building energy use. These fields correspond to the fields in 
LCAbyg that need to be filled out, which makes it suitable for the integration with json. 
If such a ݒProject Settingsݓ page is added to DIPP and the information is exported with 
the rest of the project, this area might fulfil its optimization potential. 
J) Easy structure and overview of LCI 
It has been briefly discussed that the overview of the life-cycle inventory is difficult to 
obtain in DIPP. The two BIM-based LCA tools, TallyLCA and One Click LCA, have 
their proposition of how an easier overview of the LCI can be achieved. TallyLCA struc-
tures the building components of the LCI in the same way as in Revit using the hierarchy 
order: 1. Model, 2. Category, 3. Family and 4. Material. Colours indicate the status of 
each material, which gives an easy overview of the entire LCI and what is missing. The 
quantities are, however, not a part of the overview, as they are stored within each ma-
terial. One Click LCA divides the building materials into categories and shows the unit 
in the overview. The quantities are, however, not shown in the overview. DIPP has been 
considered an extension of LCAbyg, making it obvious to structure the LCI according to 
the structure of LCAbyg. A structured LCI will improve the navigation within the LCI, 
and if a hierarchy structure based on the structure in LCAbyg is added to DIPP, this 
area might fulfil its optimization potential. 
L) Generic suggestions/assumptions 
There is a significant optimization potential within the manual re-entries of information 
related to properties such as lifetime, ratios, densities etc. TallyLCA has incorporated 
default values for properties such as lifetime and ratios when adding new materials and 
finishes such as paint. This will reduce the time when modelling a new building part.

T) Easy location of errors/mistakes 
In the earlier discussion, it was mentioned that this feature does not exist within the

The automated visual results are currently presented in Sweco's own acclaimed result 
sheet, which generates close to all necessary graphs and figures from the LCAbyg result 
file. As discussed, the LCA calculation needs to be run through LCAbyg, which makes 
these manual steps a barrier for full automatization. In order for the results to be gener-

that they hopefully will have their first public test ready by the end of February, meaning 
a solution is on the way. This direct link would also help realize item T and X, which 
are also placed after LCAbyg in the LCA workflow.  
X) Benchmark to other projects/thresholds 
As discussed, it is currently possible to compare a project to different benchmarks but is 
it not a part of DIPP. The comparison takes place in the processing of the results, which 
are exported from LCAbyg. This is yet still considered a part of the LCA workflow. In 
order to automate this process and include the feature within DIPP, the manual steps 
when exporting and importing from LCAbyg need to be eliminated, which might be 
possible by using an API. However, it was just mentioned above that such an API does 
not exist at the moment but might be a solution in the future.

A shared database including all variant studies conducted within Sweco is on the way, 
which will provide different alternatives for the design of the building. This will help the 
decision-makers make informed decisions regarding a sustainable design. However, in 
order to test out the variant studies in context with a given project, the alternatives still

6  Conclusions 
The purpose of this thesis was to investigate the user acceptance of an LCA-workflow in 
an engineering consultancy. This has been achieved by fulfilling the objectives of this 
thesis. This chapter includes the most central conclusions from this project, followed by 
the resulting limitations and future work. 
The user acceptance of the most used LCA calculation tool in Denmark, LCAbyg, has 
been investigated using the technology acceptance model UTAUT (Unified Theory of 
Acceptance and Use of Technology). Three relationships explaining the userݏs acceptance 
of LCAbyg were found to be significant. The first significant relationship was that Per-
formance Expectancy has a positive effect on Behavioural Intention (H1), meaning the 
more useful LCAbyg is, the more people intend to use the program. The second signifi-
cant relationship was that Social Influence has a positive effect on Behavioural Intention 
(H2), meaning the more people are encouraged to use LCAbyg, the more people intend 
to use it. The third significant relationship was that Behavioural Intention has a positive 
effect on Use Behaviour (H3), meaning if people intend to use LCAbyg, it will most likely 
lead to actual use of the program. According to the results, BUILD should focus on 
increasing the usefulness of LCAbyg and promoting the program in order to increase the 
chance of people using it. 
The overall features and requirements of a general LCA-workflow have been mapped as 
a part of this thesis. A total of 28 features and requirements were found and mapped 
into different stages of a general LCA-workflow. These features and requirements were 
further used for identifying potential optimization areas in the DIPP-workflow. 
The potential optimization areas in the new LCA workflow in Sweco (DIPP-workflow) 
have been identified. Seven out of the 28 features and requirements were highlighted as 
areas with optimization potential to the DIPP-workflow based on the user's point of view 
on importance and satisfaction. The seven potential optimization areas are as follows:  
F)  Include the operational energy information in the DIPP-workflow 
J)  Create an easy structure and overview of the LCI in DIPP 
L)  Include generic suggestions/assumptions in the modelling stage 
T)  Include error checks by comparing the LCA against benchmarks 
V)  Integrate simplified and automated visual results in DIPP 
X)  Benchmark results to other projects or thresholds 
Y)  Provide standard guidance/alternatives based on experience 
Finally, this thesis has presented qualified recommendations on how to realize the seven 
potential optimization areas in the DIPP-workflow. The potential optimization area F is 
recommended to be a part of DIPP as a ݒproject informationݓ page to reduce manual 
typing in LCAbyg after import of the DIPP project. Area J and L need attention within

6.1  Limitations 
The research of this thesis was affected by some limitations. In this section, these limi-
tations will be briefly presented.  
In the investigation of the user acceptance of LCAbyg using UTAUT, a large number of 
respondents was needed in order to obtain full representation of the intended population. 
However, as described in section 5.1.4 the sample size was relatively low compared to the 
minimum sample size estimated by Slovin's formula (equation 2.1). Furthermore, the 
distribution of the questionnaire used for collecting the sample was not completely ran-
dom, as it was distributed through the authorݏs and the supervisorsݏ networks. Therefore, 
the two mentioned limitations have affected the data used for the analysis in such a way 
that it may vary from the intended population, which is all actual users of LCAbyg. 
The evaluation of the user acceptance of LCAbyg in this study does not show the full 
picture of the influencing factors that affect the use of LCAbyg. UTAUT only has a 
limited number of constructs and relationships that can be tested. In section 5.1.6 it was 
mentioned that the relationship between Effort Expectancy (EE) and Performance Ex-
pectancy (PE) could be very interesting to investigate, as many other technology ac-
ceptance models (TAM, TAM2 and TAM3) explain this particular relationship. 
The study about the DIPP analysis also had some limitations, where the biggest limita-
tion will be the shortage of users of DIPP. At the time of this study, not many of the 
employees at Sweco had adequate knowledge to DIPP, which made the interpretation of 
this study a bit challenging. In section 5.2.2 it was mentioned that some of the questions 
in the survey were only answered by two respondents. It was also mentioned that the 
questions about DIPP might have been misinterpreted, which would also have led to 
fewer answers. The data obtained from the limited number of respondents resulted in 
uncertainties in the results, which is why a much larger group of respondents will be 
preferred.

6.2  Future work 
The experience gained from this master thesis, alongside the limitations above, leads to 
other relevant future work. In section 5.1.1 it was explained that the UTAUT on LCAbyg 
was a pioneering study, as the user acceptance has not yet been evaluated using a tech-
nology acceptance model. It will be relevant to conduct another evaluation of the user 
acceptance of LCAbyg using another technology acceptance model to test other con-
structs and relationships. It has been discussed in this thesis that the relationship be-
tween Effort Expectancy (EE) and Performance Expectancy (PE) could be very inter-
esting to investigate. The original technology acceptance model (TAM) tests whether or 
not user-friendliness has an effect on usefulness and might therefore be relevant. 
Another interesting study for future work will be to evaluate the user acceptance of 
another LCA tool. An examination of the user acceptance of another LCA tool will 
provide further knowledge and explanation of the user acceptance of LCA tools in gen-
eral. In addition, evaluating an LCA tool that does not have monopoly, might also pro-
vide more precise explanations of usage behaviour and acceptance. 
The DIPP analysis is considered to be a solid tool optimization workflow and is therefore 
believed to be relevant for future optimizations of the DIPP-workflow. It will be relevant 
to conduct the same study after further development of DIPP and, most importantly, 
when DIPP is used by significantly more people. 
Finally, if DIPP attracts enough users in the future, an evaluation of the user acceptance 
using a technology acceptance model will be ideal in order to understand the usersݏ ac-
ceptance and behavioural intention to use DIPP.

Appendix 1 
Pilot study: Acceptance of a scissor 
1. Introduction 
Prior to the main study, a pilot study was conducted to test out and get familiar with 
the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM), which is generally used for evaluating the 
user acceptance of a technology. The new LCA calculation workflow from Sweco (DIPP-
workflow), which are evaluated in this thesis, has a limited number of users (n = 12 - 
15). As mentioned earlier, performing structural equation modelling (SEM), which is 
needed to test the relationships between the constructs of the model, might require more 
samples than it is possible to collect. However, the pilot study will confront this state-
ment to explore the possibility with a very small sample size. The pilot study has been 
chosen to be about something very comprehensible to make it easier to interpret the 
results: Evaluating the acceptance of a scissor.  
2. Research framework 
For the pilot study, the Technology Acceptance Model (TAM) (Davis et al., 1989) will 
be used because of its position as the most used framework in explaining the adaption of 
technology (Lai, 2017). An extension of the original TAM was considered. However, a 
comparison study concluded that the new extended versions of TAM do not seem to offer 
a better explanation for the acceptance of new technologies than the original TAM (Ron-
dan-Cataluña et al., 2015). Furthermore, with a limited number of samples, a simpler 
model is recommended when performing structural equation modelling (Hair et al., 2014). 
A simpler model will also provide simpler results to interpret, which is preferable when 
getting familiar with a new methodology. In this study, six hypotheses were tested, which 
were supported by the literature: 
H1. 
Perceived Ease of Use (EOU) will have a significant influence on Perceived Usefulness 
(USEF). 
H2. 
Perceived Ease of Use (EOU) will have a significant influence on Attitude Towards Using 
(ATT). 
H3. 
Perceived Usefulness (USEF) will have a significant influence on Attitude Towards Using 
(ATT). 
H4. 
Perceived Usefulness (USEF) will have a significant influence on Behavioral Intention to 
Use (BI). 
H5. 
Attitude Towards Using (ATT) will have a significant influence on Behavioral Intention 
to Use (BI). 
The final conceptual model can be seen in Figure 8.1.

3. Data collection 
For the pilot study, a sample size of n=15 was chosen to reflect the expected users of the 
final product (DIPP). In order to perform the PLS-SEM, a minimum sample size of 20 
was needed according to the ten-times rule presented in section 2.6.1 as the inner model 
had two constructs (USEF and EOU) directed at a particular inner construct (ATT). 15 
samples were therefore not enough according to the rules of thumb. The participants 
were asked to perform a simple task followed by completing a questionnaire. The ques-
tionnaire consisted of 4 constructs; Perceived Usefulness (USEF), Perceived Ease of Use 
(EOU), Attitude Towards Using (ATT) and Behavioral Intention to Use (BI). The items 
in the questionnaire were adapted from (Davis, 1989) and a previous study (Masrom, 
2007) and afterwards modified to fit this study. The questions were rated on a 7-point 
Likert scale ranging from 1 = ݒstrongly disagreeݓ to 7 = ݒstrongly agreeݓ. The task 
description and the questions are placed in Appendix 2 and Appendix 3, respectively. 
4. Data analysis 
As mentioned earlier, structural equation modelling (SEM) is used to analyse the data 
when applying a technology acceptance model. To decide the right SEM approach for 
the data analysis, the rules of thumb presented in section 2.6 will be used. The goal of 
this study was not to test a theory but to apply the theory and identify the key "driver" 
constructs provided by the theory. Furthermore, the structural model was recursive 
(meaning no casual loops), and the sample size was small. Based on these arguments, the 
right approach for this study was to use PLS-SEM. Many different software can be used 
for PLS-SEM. In this study, SmartPLS 4 was used for the calculations as it is a free 
software using PLS-SEM with an intuitive user-friendly interface (Hair, 2022). The re-
sults were evaluated as described in section 2.6.2 Evaluation of PLS-SEM results. 
5. Results 
All results from the pilot study are placed in Appendix 4. An initial run was performed, 
and items with the lowest factor loadings were removed. For the final run, the measures'

6. Summary and baseline for the main study 
It was not possible to test the relations between the constructs that would explain the 
acceptance of a scissor. This was due to the low sample size, which led to invalid solu-

issue with invalid solutions might still occur when evaluating the DIPP-workflow due to 
the limited number of users.  
Based on the results from the pilot study, a new approach for the main study was pro-
posed. The proposal included a technical evaluation of usersݏ acceptance of LCAbyg using 
a technology acceptance model with a much larger sample size. The scope for this part 
of the study was therefore extended to outside of Sweco to collect more samples. This 
new approach did not evaluate the DIPP-workflow but the general LCA workflow used 
by the majority of the building industry in Denmark. The DIPP-workflow will instead 
be evaluated using a more qualitative approach, including semi-structured interviews and 
questionnaires.

footprint of the buildings. The most frequently used software for performing LCA cal-

  interaction_term(iv="PE", moderator = "G", method = two_stage),

  interaction_term(iv="FC", moderator = "A", method = two_stage), 
  interaction_term(iv="FC", moderator = "E", method = two_stage))

  paths(from = c("PE", "EE", "SI"), to = c("BI")),

  paths(from = c("A", "E"), to = c("USE")), 
  paths(from = c("FC*A", "FC*E"), to = c("USE")))

  data = Data, 
  measurement_model = mModel, 
  structural_model = sModel)

Appendix 14 Comments on DIPP workflow 
A) Defining goal and scope 
x 
Siger DIPP noget om, hvad der skal inkluderes i modellen? Hvis det gør, har jeg ikke opdaget den 
feature. 
x 
Jeg tror det kunne være behjælpeligt både for LCA specialister men også for de forskellige faglig-
heder tilknyttet projektet, hvis der lå et overblik over hvilke bygningsdele der skal inkluderes iht 
hver beregningsmetoder (DGNB, FBK, bygningsreglement etc.) 
x 
DIPP mangler forsat noget brugervenlighed og måske en visualisering af hvilke bygningsdele som 
skal inkluderes i en LCA. Dette kunne også viderebygges med en funktion der kan fremhæve hvilke 
konstruktioner, som er "DIPP'et", så man kan se hvilke materialer/bygningsdele man har tilføjet 
data for. 
B) 
Understanding the building context 
x 
Revit giver mulighed for at man kan fremhæve bygningsdele, dog kunne man måske godt i forlæn-
gelse af første spørgsmål, lave et bestemt view som viser alt der skal inkluderes i LCA'en. 
C) 
Time expectancy for the calculation 
x 
Det er vigtigt at kende omfanget af opgaven, men omfanget af opgaven afhænger vel ikke af DIPP? 
x 
Oftest er der tid til at løse opgaven, men nogle gange er tidsrammen sat så man ikke har mulighed 
for at dykke ned i sine resultater og tænke over optimeringsmuligheder - hvilket jo er en skam for 
den integrerede rådgivning. 
x 
Vigtigt at vide tidsestimering, DIPP kan sparer én meget tid ved udarbejdelse af LCA'er, specielt 
ved iterationer af LCA for projekter. 
D) BIM-model with high level of development (LOD) 
x 
Ikke vigtigt, hvis vi ikke benytter den til mængder, men ift. DIPP er det jo meget vigtigt 
x 
En model af høj kvalitet er vigtig ved alle LCA-beregninger, men essentiel hvis man skal bruge 
DIPP. Det er lidt to forskellige scenarier 
x 
God funktion at DIPP kan automatisere valg af data for byggevare. Men også en brugbar funktion 
at man kan koble det uafhængigt af BIM-modellens detaljeringsgrad. 
E) 
Detailed bill of quantities (BoQ) 
x 
Der er vel ikke behov for, at faglighederne udfylder et mængdeskema, hvis man bruger DIPP. 
Selvom det ville være rart med noget tilsvarende et mængdeskema, som man kunne anvende til 
dokumentation. 
x 
Der ligger ikke en tydelig og let tilgængelig mængdeoversigt i DIPP 
x 
DIPP kan kun levere et excelark med totale mængder, så ikke særlig gennemsigtigt. kan godt 
optimeres. 
F) 
Operational energy information 
x 
Energirammen er vigtig, men den er vel ikke tilknyttet DIPP 
x 
DIPP behandler ikke driften, da den baserer sig på materialer. Hvis der kunne indarbejdes et Revit 
Plugin som kunne foretage energirammer samtidigt, som kan benyttes i forlængelse af DIPP, vil 
det være et stærkt værktøj! 
G) Structured information and transparency 
x 
Jeg ved ikke, hvordan jeg skal lave en LCI, når jeg anvender DIPP. Kan man få DIPP til at spytte 
sådan et ark ud? Jeg skal bruge et sted til at skrive antagelselser, også dem der kører gennem 
DIPP. 
x 
Kommer gennemsigtigt ind i selve LCAbyg, da den samlede materialemængde gradueres ud på 
bygningsdele. Dog savnes der et overskueligt mængdeark.

H) Automatic import of quantities 
x 
Det gør det nemmere rent beregningsmæssigt, men også en nødvendighed at man kigger mængder 
og valgte datasæts igennem for at sikre at LCA'erne er repræsentante! 
I) 
Simple and user-friendly workflow 
x 
Hvis jeg skal lave LCIen manuelt, bliver det alligevel dobbeltarbejde, som ellers er det, vi prøver 
på at undgå med DIPP... 
J) 
Easy structure and overview of LCI 
x 
Vigtigt med god LCI ift. transperans. DIPP leverer kun opdelt i form af LCAbyg-filen, som opdeler 
det på gruppeniveau. Inddeling foregår dog manuelt i selve DIPP, så bygningsdele/konstruktioner 
bliver rigtigt segmenteret. 
K) Easy access to and import of EPD's 
x 
Indtastninger af EPD'er foretages ikke i DIPP. DIPP benytter en LCAbyg skabelon, hvor database 
udbygges, så EPD'er kan genbruges på tværs af projekter. Dog skal EPD'er som udgangspunkt 
manuelt indtastes i LCAbyg. 
L) 
Generic suggestions/assumptions 
x 
Funktioner med levetid er ikke funktionelt i DIPP. Det kan defineres i DIPP, men foregår pt. ikke 
automatisk. DIPP's database har densitet indskrevet for alle materialer, så mængder kan udregnes 
ud fra angivet dimensioner i BIM-model. 
M) Easy unit handling 
x 
Det vides hvilken enhed der benyttes, enheden er dog fastlagt på baggrund af datasæts (EPD'er) 
enhed. 
N) Own control of input 
x 
Jeg synes det er vigtigt, at man kan gennemskue hvordan processen er, altså hvordan ens input er 
blevet til det modtaget output. Det er selvfølgelig en fordel ift. tid, at have en automatisk proces 
(med den antagelse af det virker). 
x 
Alt valgt materiale (DIPP'et) kan gennemgås og skiftes datasæt, som vil tilpasse sig til de auto-
matisk beregnede fejl. Generelt vil DIPP's mængdeberegning mindske manuelle beregningsfejl, og 
gøre det konsekvent på tværs af projekter. 
O) 
Large material and component library 
x 
Der er altid variationer i byggeriet med opbygninger, valgt EPD'er osv. Derfor kan det være svært 
at have et fyldig bibliotek. Det er særlig min erfaring ved træbyggerier. 
x 
Database i DIPP kan altid udvides. Datagrundlag opbygges på baggrund af EPD'er benyttet i 
tidligere projekter. Største problem er vedligeholdelse og opdateringer af database. 
P) 
Easy matching BIM-materials with environmental database 
x 
DIPP kan godt optimere brugerflade og søgefunktioner. 
Q) 
Fast and easily accessible results 
x 
Der er ikke, så vidt vides, en real time komponent til LCAbyg i DIPP 
x 
Jeg har kun prøvet at bruge det overslag, der ligger direkte i Revit. Har DIPP et tilsvarende modul 
efterfølgende eller? Funktionen i Revit er ret fed. 
x 
Der kan godt arbejdes med databehandlingen og resultatfremvisningen. Funktion mest brugbar i 
designfase og for LCA-estimater i de tidlige faser. 
R) 
Hotspot analysis 
x 
Jeg plejer at bruge den i LCAbyg. Har DIPP noget tilsvarende? 
x 
God funktion at det man ser i view i Revit inkluderes i hotspot. Flere funktioner man kan benytte. 
Layout af fremvisning og enhed kan godt tilpasses. 
S) 
Easy comparative and parametric studies

x 
DIPP kan/gør det hurtigt at udskifte forskellige bygningsdele til variant undersøgelse. Det er nok 
mere LCAbyg der er en begrænsende faktor. 
T) 
Easy location of errors/mistakes 
x 
Mere "nice to have" end "need to have" 
x 
Det kan måske være en smart funktion, men man burde med en lille smule erfaring have en idé om 
ens resultat virker fornuftig. Så nok mere en "nice to have" funktion end en "need to have". 
x 
Jeg har ikke prøvet den funktion i DIPP. Men det lyder nyttigt! 
x 
Ikke indarbejdet i DIPP, kunne være en meget essentiel funktion, som vil minimere fejlagtige 
LCA'er. 
U) LCA in BIM-environment 
x 
Som jeg forstå DIPP skal man både bruge DIPP-manager og LCAbyg, altså to programer ud over 
Revit og der plug-in i Revit. 
x 
Funktionen i Revit er god til et hurtigt estimat, vi skal bare sørge for at brugerne (ARK?) er klar 
over, at det kun er et overslag. 
x 
Kræver dog at DIPP også er åbent, så ved ikke om det tæller. Men der kan opnås en hurtig LCA-
fremvisning i Revit, hvis der er benyttet materialer fra Default templates eller sweco templates. 
V) Simplified and automated visual results 
x 
Jeg ved ikke hvordan DIPP gør det. 
x 
Graffer kan godt opdateres visuelt, så en bedre formidling af resultater. 
W) Easy customize/filter graphs aimed towards receiver 
x 
Resultatarket er mega godt. Bliver det integreret i DIPP? 
x 
Kan ikke justeres i DIPP. Der kan dog laves et hurtigt LCAbyg udtræk og konvertering til Sweco 
resultatark, hvor der er langt flere grafer og skabeloner til præsentationer af resultater. Her er der 
også mulighed for at tilpasse ens resultater. 
X) Benchmark to other projects/thresholds 
x 
Ikke en funktion i DIPP pt. 
Y)  Standard guidance/alternatives based on experience 
x 
Nemmere at udføre variansstudier i LCAbyg. Dog kan variansstudier i DIPP/Revit vise direkte 
indvirkning for det specifikke projekt. 
Z) 
Automated documentation process 
x 
Der mangler en nem måde at lave LCIen på. Lige nu kommer jeg til at indtaste det manuelt, 
hvilket går lidt imod tanken med DIPP. 
x 
LCAbyg-fil er automatiseret, der mangler forsat mængdeskemaark. 
AA) Overview of information input 
x 
Fungerer i DIPP på samme måde som man ellers ville med dataudtræk fra BIM-modeller. På sigt 
kan værktøjet måske endda forbedre det, da det kan indarbejde mere data en de oprindelige Revit-
udtræk. 
BB) Standardization of LCA procedures 
x 
Kræver værktøjet bliver brugt i højere grad. Så med tiden vil det forhåbentligt komme.
