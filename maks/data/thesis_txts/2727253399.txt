Abstract
In the aim of Denmark reaching carbon neutrality by 2030, the modes of consumption of its citizens will
have to evolve, but ideally in a way that would not sacrifice healthy lifestyles. To determine which actions
Danish citizens will be able to take to drastically reduce their carbon footprint, an analysis of their consumption
patterns linked with a life cycle assessment of those patterns has to be realized. This is done by considering the
effects on both the human health and the ecosystem of the consumption of food and energy, the transport and
the background processes associated with accommodation and various spending. Average data derived from
statistics were used in this study, coupled with a more in-depth look at the distribution of heating consumption
on a residential level. This generated an LCA of the profile of an average Dane in the year 2021, coupled
with a distribution of individual impacts related to residential heating on a national scale. A carbon footprint
of 10.53 tons of CO2eq per capita was obtained for the year 2021, and further analysis showed that road and
rail travel, followed by food consumption and goods and services purchases were the main contributors in an
average Dane’s LCA, and that achieving behavioral improvements in all of these categories is highly feasible
and could be a first step to a more sustainable Denmark.

Introduction
As the threat posed to our planet by global warming
grows increasingly in our consumption-based soci-
ety, it becomes urgent to find ways to reduce our
environmental impact, without sacrificing healthy
lifestyles in the process. To find those compromises
between sustainability and health, a relationship be-
tween them needs to be established, as to find ways
for individuals to healthily adapt their lifestyles to the
needs of a more environmentally conscious society.
To do this, it is important to associate consumption
with the impacts over the entire life cycle.
The Danish National Inventory established by Aarhus
Universitet (Nielsen et al., n.d.) for the year 2022
gives the greenhouse gas emissions of Denmark on a
national scale, but such an inventory doesn’t display
any kind of statistical and individual data for Dan-
ish households and as such, it becomes necessary to
find data from statistics agencies or from any kind of
available surveys.
Aalborg University also established a literature re-
view of various ways that the input-output emis-
sions of Denmark have been established in 2014
(Schmidt, Højrup, & Muños, n.d.).
This study
gives an individual impact for Danish citizens of
15 tonsofCO2eq/person/year, based on an Input-
Output analysis on the whole of Denmark.
While
this number is very useful as a means for comparison,
it was decided in this study to focus on an individual
level, in order to highlight the specific consumption of
an average Danish citizen. While it is expected that
the results of this present work will be in the same
order of magnitude, they will probably be lower due
to the emphasis on personal consumption, which will
in turn be a bit more detailed. A study case (Kalbar,
Birkved, Kabins, & Nygaard, 2016) linking Personal
Metabolism (PM, as opposed to Urban Metabolism,
an assessment of the impacts on an individual level)
and LCA has been established in 2016, the first one
of its kind, using data provided by a survey con-
ducted on 1281 participants.
Due to the access to
actual personal data, this study case can present its
results in the form of a repartition, however, the
questions asked to the participants regarding food
and housing are pretty broad and not as precise as
data given by Statistics Denmark. Furthermore, we
have solid reasons to believe that the Covid crisis and
the increasingly felt global warming effects may have
modified the average Danish citizen’s behavior, and
as such, differences are expected between the results
of both studies.
In the frame of the project "Digitalized platform for
sustainable and healthy lifestyles", this work aims
to explore and identify how to use statistical infor-
mation on energy, housing, transportation, food and
goods consumption in Denmark to assess the life cycle
impacts and their main determinants in the Danish
population.
More specifically, this study aims to:
• Identify the available statistical data to charac-
terize the consumption patterns of Danish pop-
ulations.
• Combine these consumption data with life cycle
databases to determine average life cycle Im-
pacts in the Danish population.
• Identify crucial data that substantially affect in-
dividuals’ impacts in Denmark.
• Identify the key determinants in each consump-
tion domain that substantially affect individu-
als’ impacts in Denmark.
• Focus on the housing domain of consumption to
determine a distribution of impacts across the
entire building park in Denmark (more specifi-
cally in regard to heating).
• Discuss the possibility to evaluate impacts at
an individual level, as well as the missing data
that will have to be collected in the future.
During the execution of this study, several contacts
were initiated to obtain data in the near future: the
Danish Energy Agency, which has already established

the carbon footprint of an average Dane but would
also be interested in how it relates to health, DTU
Food, which is, at the time that this report is writ-
ten, finalizing its 2020-2021 food survey (and would
therefore be able to provide data from August 2023
onward) and Statistics Denmark, which sells the spe-
cific data (originating from surveys) they use to build

Due to the lack of precise individual data, a vast ma-
jority of the data used in this study are taken di-
rectly from the public website of Statistics Denmark
(Statistics Denmark main page, 2022). This under-
scores another constant challenge encountered dur-
ing the course of this study: the difficulty of get-
ting actual behaviors based on individual profiles of
Danes. As a result, the various categories of impacts
presented in this report aim more at identifying the
hotspots for the average consumption of a Dane than
at getting a distribution of impacts over the whole
Danish population (with the exception of the impacts
related to housing, for which data to create a repar-
tition was found and which will be further detailed
in this report). Furthermore, even with the reparti-
tion used in this report, it is impossible to connect
the sources of impacts together on an individual level
without access to CPRs.
The endgame goal of this study is to inform Danes of
their impact on the environment through their daily
life, how they can improve their carbon footprint, and
how to do so without sacrificing a healthy lifestyle.
The results of the study are intended for the Master’s
Thesis project supervisor, Olivier Jolliet, in order to
serve as a base for two PhD studies: on the one hand
"Key Determinants of Sustainable Lifestyles", which
will aim to use Machine Learning to identify key de-
terminants of sustainable lifestyles via the creation
of a digitalized platform, with data on food, house-
hold products and energy consumption for a cohort
of more than 10000 persons, and combine these with
life cycle databases.

Methods
Figure 1: Methodology to estimate individual environmental impacts of the Danes
The functional unit of this study is the average con-
sumption of a citizen of Denmark (although we get
a distribution of impacts for all the citizens of Den-
mark regarding housing, in the end, this distribution
is brought to an average value for the final results)
in the year 2021 (corresponding to one person equiv-
alent per year). This work will base its LCA on a
cradle-to-grave perspective, in an attributional mod-
eling framework, and will try to be as exhaustive as
possible regarding the different sources of impacts on
an individual level. The objectives of the calculations
in this study are:
• To characterize the composition of buildings
and dwellings in Denmark, as well as their heat-
ing consumption, using data from SparEnergi
and Statistics Denmark. This composition will
then be used to create a distribution of en-
ergy labels, heating types and energy consump-
tion. The final product of this objective will be
a characterization of building-associated end-
point impacts across all Danish households.
• To calculate the impacts of the various spend-
ing of a Danish household using the Exiobase 3
monetary to impact flows.
• To analyze the impacts of the average diet, as
well as the transport and energy consumption
in Denmark
• Ultimately, to calculate the average impacts of
2
==PAGE:14==
Technical University of Denmark
a Dane.
In the following sections, some of the emission do-
mains will be explored in more detail. The average
consumption of a Dane is indeed divided into sev-
eral consumption domains: change items with do-
mains
• Housing (emissions originating from the con-
struction of the building itself and emissions
linked to the heating of the building)
• Food
• Transport (road, rail and airplane)
• Spending in goods and services
• Electricity use
The individual data for the Food repartition was
to be provided by DTU Food, based on the 2013
National Food Survey, but this did not happen in
the time frame of this project.
Therefore, average
data in publications had to be used. Data regarding
transport were taken in "The Danish National Travel
Survey: Denmark 2021 Annual: Statistical Report"
(Christiansen & Baescu, 2021). Statistics Denmark’s
FU02 table (FU02: Household average consumption
by group of consumption and price unit, 2023) was
used to get the average spending of a Danish house-
hold, which was then converted into impacts with
the ExioPole database for products in 2021. Finally,
the domain of emissions for which the most detailed
data was obtained befalls the heating of the house, of
which the repartition is calculated and analyzed later
in this article (the impacts of the construction of the
building, spread over 50 years, were also included in
those calculations).
Calculations in this study have been performed by
combining Python (with the pandas library to handle
large databases and automatize impact calculation)
and Microsoft Excel. The software SimaPro (9.4.0.2)
was also used for some calculations.
2.1
Housing data
2.1.1
Buildings repartition
For this following section, it is important to make a
distinction between what we will call housing type
or residential building type, which corresponds
to the type of residence (Detached house, Terraced
house, multi-dwelling house...), and what we call
the housing profile, which is a residence with four
parameters associated to it: housing type, heating
source, energy label, and surface. Using data from
SparEnergi (Energimærkning af huse, 2022) (Find
energimærket på din bygning, 2021), it is possible
to categorize the housing types into four categories:
• Parcel houses, or detached houses
• Terraced, linked, and semi-detached houses
• Condominiums, which contain Multi-dwelling
houses, Student hostels, Residential buildings
for communities, and Other residential build-
ings
• Cottages for agriculture or Farmhouses, assim-
ilated with detached houses.
SparEnergi also provides data for six types of heating:
• Biofuel
• Oil
• Natural gas
• Coal and coke
• District heating
• Electric heating, heat pump, etc. . .
For each type of house and each type of heating,
SparEnergi provides a number of houses per energy
label (A to G), detailed in Appendix 1: Percentage
of energy label, per type of house, for each heating
type. Using those numbers, it is possible to establish
a repartition of residential buildings, per type, heat-
ing, and energy label.
Using the BYGB12 table from Statistics Denmark 2,
it is possible to set up a distribution of residential
buildings per surface.
3
==PAGE:15==
Technical University of Denmark
Figure 2: Repartition of houses per intervals of surfaces
Each of those broad intervals of surfaces is then sep-
arated into smaller intervals. This is done using the
Excel solver module, taking into account several pa-
rameters:
• An average surface value is calculated for each
housing type, to reach the average surface given
by Statistics Denmark.
• The cumulated amount of houses within smaller
intervals has to reach the number of houses in
the big intervals provided in table BYGB12 2.
• For each small interval of surfaces, an average
surface is obtained by optimizing the average
surface per person and per residential building
type. The goal being to reach the value given
by Statistics Denmark. The calculated surface
is then rounded up (round and precise values
were preferred) and assigned to the interval.
• There is a difference to be made between
dwelling and building. This project considers
buildings as the main unit to determine en-
ergy consumption, rather than dwellings, since
dwellings that belong to the same building will
have the same energy label.
As a result, ev-
ery type of residential building (except for de-
tached houses, for which one building is also one
dwelling) will be separated into several smaller
dwellings. This affects the distribution of the
labels that are used from the SparEnergi data
(see Appendix 1: Percentage of energy label,
per type of house, for each heating type), as
well as the distribution of the type of heating
used.
• Knowing that the data provided by Statistics
Denmark is for the buildings rather than the
dwellings, the minimum surface for detached
houses,
terraced,
semi-detached and linked
houses, as well as other residential buildings
is assumed to be 60 m2.
Likewise, the mini-
mum surface for multi-dwelling houses, student
hostels and residential buildings for communi-
ties is taken at 90 m2.
The detailed repartitions of houses per type of res-
idential building can all be found in Appendix 2:
Number of houses per interval of surfaces.
2.1.2
Occupant density
To calculate the impacts of the Danes on an indi-
vidual level, it is necessary to determine an average
number of occupants per building (which will vary ac-
cording to the type of house/residential building the
person lives in and the surface of the house/residen-
tial building). For this purpose, Statistics Denmark
has data on the average number of residents per type
of residential building, as well as the average surface
of dwelling that a resident lives in (this data is a na-
tional average). It then becomes necessary to have
the characterized data match the average statistics.
Using once again the Excel solver, it is possible to
obtain a distribution of occupants in each type of
house. The detail of this repartition is displayed in
Appendix 3: Number of occupants per interval of sur-
faces. Another thing worth noting is that the number
of residents in Residential buildings for communities
doesn’t match the number of residents provided by
Statistics Denmark. In an e-mail exchange, Statistics

The emissions linked to the construction of the build-

This document
presents results for the carbon footprint of 60 new

the assumption was taken to consider a lifespan of 50
years for those embedded emissions.
Figure 5: Values of global warming potential used for the
embedded emissions for each type of building
This document only gives the GWP100 of the embed-
ded emissions of the buildings, and to calculate the
rest of the impacts, it is necessary to make several
assumptions:
• The median values for the GWP of materi-
als are 7.4, 7.1 and 7.0 kgCO2eq/m2/year for
detached houses, terraced houses and apart-
ments/other buildings respectively. Those are
taken as the standard value of GWP100 used in
this study.
• The student hostels and Residential buildings
for communities are assimilated into apartment
buildings.
• The SimaPro process "Building, hostel PE|
building construction, hostel | Cut-off, U" is
used as an initial proxy to calculate the other
impacts.
A hostel was going to be similar
in structure to a house, as it displays simi-
lar facilities.
The building itself occupies a
plot of 550 m2, with a lifetime frame of 75
years. Using this, endpoint and midpoint im-
pacts to the square meter of this hostel were
calculated.
The hostel has a GWP100 of
12.7 kgCO2eq/m2/year, which is the closest
available process in SimaPro in terms of cli-
mate change.
Using the ratio between this
value and the ones given by Aarhus University
(Zimmermann et al., 2021), it is possible to get
a ratio for each type of building. This ratio can
then be applied to every endpoint impact to get
a value in all 27 indicators to the square meter
for each type of house. The final step to cal-
culate the embedded emissions was to multiply
the new ratios found for the 27 indicators by the
surface of the building. Using the distribution
of buildings with the repartition of occupants,
those impacts can be reduced to the individual
level.

With an input-output approach, the spending (in

This database gives Input-Output matrices that are

data used is the matrices from 2021 using products
rather than industries. To get the total input-output
emissions of the households in Denmark, the follow-

Dcons = S · L · Y + FY
(2.1)
Where S is the "Direct stressor/impact coefficients"
matrix, which gives the direct background impacts of
the products (sorted by country). Y is the final de-
mand. In the case of the present study, Y is modified
to only include the desired spending of a household

in the previous sections). FY represents the direct
impacts of the final use of the products.
L is the
Leontiev inverse, which modelizes the interactions be-
tween various industries and various products. L is
not given by Exiobase 3, so it needs to be calculated
(using Python) using the formula:
L = (I −A)−1
(2.2)
Where A is the matrix with the inter-industry coeffi-
cients
The FY matrix wasn’t considered in the calculations,
as most of the final demand’s direct emissions are
already accounted for in the previous sections.
The matrix that is useful to transform the monetary
ratios given by the FU02 table into ImpactWorld+
impacts is S · L, which when converted from mone-

The average carbon footprint of a Dane is assessed at
10.53 tons of CO2eq per year.
Road and rail travel rank high in every impact cat-
egory (and are always in the three most important
sources of emissions, with the exception of Land oc-
cupation, biodiversity).
It ranks worst category in
terms of impacts in 13 of the 27 indicators evaluated
in this study and is especially prevalent in the impacts
Water availability, human health (77.8% of the total
impact, which is a surprising result and induces doubt
regarding the reliability of this indicator), Freshwa-
ter ecotoxicity, long-term and short-term (74.1% and
62.9% of the total impact respectively), Human Toxi-
city, cancer, short-term (65% of the total impact) and
Ozone layer depletion (53.5% of the total impact).
Food consumption comes as the second biggest cause
of impact, being in the 3 most prominent factors in
23 of the 27 indicators). It especially dominates the
impacts Human toxicity cancer, long-term (68.4% of
the total impact), Freshwater eutrophication (52.6%
of the total impact), Ionizing radiation, freshwa-
ter ecosystem (55.5% of the total impact), Land
transformation, biodiversity (93.7% of the total im-
pact), Land occupation, biodiversity (60% of the to-
tal impact), Water availability, freshwater ecosystem
(85.4% of the total impact) and Water availability,
terrestrial ecosystem (93.4% of the total impact).
Goods and services consumption is the third most
important cause of impact.
The categories Ioniz-
ing radiation, human health, Ozone layer depletion,
Water availability, human health, Ionizing radiation,
ecosystem quality, Land transformation, biodiversity,
Water availability, freshwater ecosystem and Water
availability, terrestrial ecosystem are not given by
Exiobase 3, and therefore the emissions linked to the
goods and services are probably extremely under-
estimated.
The impacts of goods and services are
especially notable in the categories Human toxicity
non cancer, short and long term (63% and 89.4% of
the total impacts respectively), Particulate matter
formation (51.6% of the total impact) and Marine
eutrophication (44.4% of the total impact).
Due to the fact that air travel is averaged on the
whole country, its impact is lower than the other cat-
egories (it ranks the lowest in 17 of the 27 indicators).
Heating consumption for the house has a pretty con-
sistent share of around 7 to 15% of the total impact
for 12 of the 27 indicators, and electricity follows a
similar trend, with a share of the impact of around 4
to 7% in 11 indicators. Embedded emissions of the
houses have a share of the impact of around 4 to 9%
in the majority of indicators (13 out of 27).
When compared to the PM LCA study case (Kalbar
et al., 2016), which estimated the consumption-
based average carbon footprint of a Dane to 6.8
tCO2eq/capita/year (taking into account accommo-
dation, energy, road transport, air travel, and food),

3.2.1
Repartition of the kWh/m2
To verify the hypotheses made regarding the distri-
bution of buildings in Denmark, the kWh/m2 was
printed for each profile of house as a function of the
total surface of each profile of house. This led to a
modification of several of the hypotheses made re-
garding the repartition, most notably the Terraced
houses distribution (smaller houses attached together
and forming one building rather than one big house
assimilated into one big building):
Figure 8: Repartition of the kWh/m2 as a function of the
building’s total surface sorted by labels
Figure 9: Repartition of the kWh/m2 as a function of the
building’s total surface sorted by housing types
The circles on those graphs represent the number of
residents in each profile of house (the larger the circle,
the more people leave in this profile of house). The
label’s distribution (larger kWh/m2 for worst labels)
is consistent with the assumptions.
Figure 9 highlights the assumptions made about the
building’s surfaces: more detached houses at lower
surfaces of buildings and more multi-dwelling houses,
student hostels and residential buildings for commu-
nities at higher surfaces.
Terraced, semi-detached
and linked houses are pretty evenly spread through-
out the entire graph due to the assumption made that
those buildings would correspond to an amalgama-
tion of smaller houses. The spikes in the number of
people at around 1000, 2000 and 5000 m2 are a result
of the Excel solver generally allocating more residents
to the lower surfaces of the big intervals (see table
BYGB12 2) so as to get the correct average surface
for each category of residential buildings.

Overall district heating represents by far the best
compromise, as it is never the most harmful heat-
ing source in any of the indicators. This is due to the
decrease of fossil fuels as a vector to produce district
heating and the growth of renewables (an increase
from around 20% in 1990 to 63.8% in 2019 in renew-
ables, and a decrease from around 50% in 1990 to
8.6% in 2019 of coal used in combined heat and power
(CHP) plants (Johansen & Werner, 2022)). The ob-
jective set by the Danish government to decarbonize
Denmark by 2030 means that district heating will
grow even more in the next years, and that it will
integrate more and more renewable energies in the
CHP plants, most notably biofuel (which, as illus-
trated earlier in this section, is not ideal for several
indicators, despite in general performing better than
fossil fuels).
As a whole, multi-dwelling houses perform better
than detached houses, as they are spread across fewer
buildings and use more district heating in general,
making them an overall "healthier" option.

Areas for improvement
The calculations around housing are based on several
broad hypotheses:
• As evidenced by figure 2, only big surface inter-
vals are given for each type of house. The sub-
division of those intervals into smaller ones and
the subsequent number of houses per smaller
intervals is approximated.
• The same goes for the number of occupants per
house, a broad value was used per interval, but
it does not represent the diversity that can oc-
cur within one interval.
• For the different types of heating and the repar-

• Incidentally, one of the heating types displayed

Table 7 shows that on a distance that is less than 2
kilometers, taking the bike or the car does not make
any difference in the traveling time (for 0.5 km trips,
using the bike takes 1.1 minutes whereas using the
car takes 1 minute. For a distance of 2 kilometers,
using the bike takes around 4.4 minutes and using
the car takes around 4 minutes), so avoiding using
the car and using the bike instead would reduce the
emissions on this distance by 100% without much ef-
fort. According to the National Travel Survey, trips
of a distance of less than 2 kilometers represent 0.21
of the journeys made by an average Dane per day. An
average Dane has 1.28 journeys per day, so trips of
less than 2 kilometers contribute to 16% of the daily
trips of a Dane. Similarly, on trips of 2 to 4 kilome-
ters, the time to make the trip by bike is only slightly
longer than the time to make the trip by car (for 4 km
trips, using the bike takes around 8.5 minutes whereas
using the car takes around 7.8 minutes). Those trips
account for 17% of the daily trip of an average Dane.
It is then possible to calculate a maximum impact
and a minimum daily impact using the Danish Na-
tional Travel Survey (calculating the sum of impacts
of the maximum distance on every interval and the
sum of impacts of the minimum distance on every in-
terval, multiplying those impacts by the share of daily
journeys that each distance interval represents for an
average Dane). With those numbers, it is possible to
evaluate the reduction of impacts that daily changes
in transport could bring. Replacing the trips by car
and other vehicles (vans) with bike trips at intervals
<2 km and 2-4 km will only reduce the total emis-
sions for road and rail transports from 0.15 to 0.8%
(which is due to the emissions on the short distances
being low as a whole compared to the emissions on the
longer distances, mainly covered by cars). It is there-

public transport. Replacing cars with buses or trains

There are interesting leads for an improvement in the
impact linked to road and rail transport. The use of
bikes for short distances and of public transport for
longer trips allow for a drastic reduction of emissions
in the majority of endpoint indicators. Those results
need to be contrasted with several factors. Some peo-
ple might not have access to public transport and thus
will rely entirely on cars for their trips, and it must
also be stated that some residents of Denmark are
likely not in the physical capability of using a bike.
The results of this study must then clearly be com-
pleted with a socio-geographic study that would take
into account the availability of public transport in
specific areas, as well as the inclusion of disabilities
of any sort for some Danes to better understand how
to efficiently reduce the impacts caused by road and
rail transport.
3.4.2
Air transport
With the calculations based on Statistics Denmark,
for a population of 5 850 248.24 (an average of the
population of the four semesters of 2021 given by
Statistics Denmark), we get that each individual in
Denmark travels approximately 824.4 km per year by
plane, divided into 133.3 km of very short haul flights,
100.9 km of short-haul flights, 542.1 km of medium-
haul flights and 48.1 km of long-haul flights.
3.4.3
Areas for improvement
Data in the National Travel Survey (Christiansen &
Baescu, 2021) do not include trips by motorbike,
which is included in the Exiobase goods and service
calculations, but a more precise characterization of
the vehicles used in Denmark could be established.
The 2016 PM and LCA study case (Kalbar et al.,
2016) has access to individual data on the motorized
vehicles used by the respondents, the year of purchase
of the vehicles and the number of kilometers that they
used those vehicles for during the year of the survey,
which allows for a distribution of impacts to be estab-
lished regarding road transport. Trips by plane are
taken as direct trips, which can lead to the emissions
being underestimated if there is a correspondence on
the trip. The survey made in the article by Kalbar
et. al also asked its respondents where they went by
plane during the year it was made, which, while still
using the approximation of direct flight, may display
a split of impacts regarding air travel between the
wealthiest citizens of Denmark and the rest of the
country. This lead could be interesting to explore in
more detail in the future.

Areas for improvement
Despite the extremely detailed data provided by
Statistics Denmark for the average Danish household,
there is still a lot of imprecision regarding the exact
purchases of the households, as Statistics Denmark
groups them into big categories. As such, it is com-
plicated to assess which of those spending can be cut
and which are considered to be necessary and healthy.
Therefore it is difficult to assess clear actions that
would cut the emissions linked to those purchases.
Still, getting the data on the distribution of purchases
across Denmark (that Statistics Denmark can pro-
vide) would allow the identification of profiles related
to goods and service spending in Denmark. Another
uncertainty related to this specific part of the calcu-
lations relates to the direct emissions of some of the
spending accounted for in the FU02 table, mainly the
services. The direct emissions of those services (for
example the average electricity that a Dane would
consume in a hotel) have not been taken into account
in the present calculation. In the future, a more spe-
cific study allocating direct impacts to some services
will need to be conducted to get more precise results.

4
Conclusion
4.1
Achievements
This study calculated an average carbon footprint of
10.53 tons of CO2eq per capita for the year 2021, and
highlights that the three main contributors to the im-
pacts of an average Dane, in terms of consumption,
are food, road and rail transport, and background
processes associated with the purchases of goods and
services. Furthermore, there are clear ways that those
emissions can be reduced: for transport, using the bus
instead of the car for all daily trips can save up to 66%
of global warming emissions, and using the train up to
90%. Limiting the consumption of meat and choco-
late can also drastically reduce the emissions related
to food consumption (a plant-based diet would reduce
the carbon footprint of an average Danish citizen by
around 45%).
Combining those actions can, in an
ideal scenario, reduce the total carbon footprint of a
Dane by 32 to 42%. Other actions can be taken on
a national scale to reduce the impacts of accommo-
dation (further developing district heating based on
renewables and building more durable houses with an
A2020 energy label for example) In addition to those
results, this study developed a distribution of the res-
idential heating impacts on the whole of Denmark, on
an individual level. Graphs have been produced for
the 27 endpoint indicators of ImpactWorld+, display-
ing individual impacts as a function of the dwelling
surface per person, and sorted by energy label of the
residential building, its type of heating and its hous-
ing category. Those distributions also highlight how
many buildings are associated with a specific profile
of house (which is the combination of a type of res-
idential building, an energy label, a type of heating
and a surface), and how many residents live in those
buildings.
4.2
Limitations and possible improve-
ments
The main difficulty in this study was getting the char-
acterization of data for all the domains of consump-
tion in a Danish household. Getting most of this data
takes time, and issues regarding privacy are render-
ing the process of modeling the impacts of each in-
dividual in Denmark very complicated. A first dis-
tribution was created for the accommodation in the
country, but data regarding food was also supposed
to be included in this study but could not be obtained
on time. The likeliness of some emissions linked to
input-output being not accounted for in this study is
high, as the average carbon footprint of a Dane ob-
tained for 2021 was not as high as the value given by
Aalborg University (Schmidt et al., n.d.) based on a
national input-output study. Furthermore, bridging
data from different sources (Statistics Denmark, DTU
Food, The Danish National Travel Survey) might lead
to some differences in calculations from those sources
that may impact the results of this present study.
Obtaining data to establish "profiles" of Danes can
be done by getting the distribution used by DTU
Food for the 2020-2021 National Food Survey (which
will come out after the end of the present work), as
well as by getting Data from Statistics Denmark for
the FU02 table. Bygnings-og Boligregistret also pos-
sesses exact data for accommodation and domestic
energy consumption throughout the whole of Den-
mark, which would refine the calculations made in
this present report.
If all those organizations also
provide CPRs for their data of distribution, it can be
possible to characterize profiles, and therefore con-
sumption patterns that can be acted upon to change
Danes’ consumption habits to be more sustainable.
If data from DTU Food is obtained, a more in-depth
study can be made to link the exact nutritional val-
ues of each of the components of Danish diets and
sustainability, to find a compromise between health
and LCA.
