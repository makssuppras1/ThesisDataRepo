Abstract
Killer-cell immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIR) are important receptors for antigen recognition and
regulation of natural killer (NK) cells, enabling immune surveillance and elimination of abnormal
cells. Recently, the expression of KIR receptors has been described on a subset of CD8+ T cells,
while the implications of this receptors presence is yet to be fully understood. The presence of
these KIR+CD8+ T cells has been observed to contribute between 1% to 38% of the total T cell
population, while these numbers might change in a variety of autoimmune, infectious and cancer
diseases. These observations have sparked curiosity for their potential impact on CD8+ T cell
adaptive immunity against oncologic, autoimmune and infectious diseases. The effects of pMHC
interaction with KIR receptors on CD8+ T cells function are still being investigated, as this
interaction were found to have varying influenza over CD8+ T cell expression. Insight into KIR+CD8+
T cells might unveil potential methods for improving or blocking CD8+ T cell function, depending on
the disease in question.
In this study the characteristics of KIR-expressing CD8+ T cells are investigated through single cell
sorting of KIR+CD8+ T cells and subsequent expansion into a homogeneous population. This
approach enables a comprehensive study of the phenotype, functionality, differentiation patterns
and specificity of KIR+CD8+ T cells in vitro. The characteristics of the KIR+CD8+ T cells population
are investigated through a phenotyping assay utilizing different markers to determine deviations
from control clones. The functionality of activating CD8+ T cells co-expressing KIR are investigated
though a restimulation assay utilizing CD8+ T cells section of TNF-α and IFN-γ to assess KIR+CD8+
T cells ability to be activated and execute effector functions independently of their endogenous T
cell receptor. Lastly, the TCR of the KIR+CD8+ T cells was investigated through sanger sequencing
of the TCR α-chain and β-chain to obtain insights into the status of the TCR after expansion.
Through this examination, the KIR+CD8+ T cells was designated as a more terminally differentiated
and less proliferative subset compared to control cells. It was also found that the presence of a KIR
receptor on CD8+ T cells had an inhibiting effect on its proliferative capabilities. The KIR+CD8+ T
cell subset was not able to be activated and execute effector functions independently of their
endogenous T cell receptor, but sanger sequencing of the TCR was possible for most of the
selected clones. Only a few KIR+CD8+ T cell clones was obtained throughout the project, limiting
the accurately characterizing this subset of KIR expressing CD8+ T cells.

1.0 Introduction
1.1 Introduction to the immune system and T cells
The immune system is widely recognized as one of the most complex biological systems and is
essential for the protection of the body from foreign entities, cancer cells and pathogens. The
immune system can further be categorized into the innate and adaptive immune system, each
characterized by its own distinct functions [1].
The innate immune system serves as the primary defense against pathogens, utilizing physical,
chemical, and microbiological barriers to prevent their entry into the body. It relies on myeloid cells
and natural killer (NK) cells with hard-coded responses that recognize unique patterns exclusive to
these intruders. To guide and modulate the host immune response, the innate immune system
utilizes pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) expressed by antigen-presenting cells (APCs). These
receptors detect specific pathogen-associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) and facilitate pathogen
uptake for antigen presentation. In response to infections or tissue damage caused by pathogens,
the innate immune system also activates an inflammatory response [2]. Antigen presentation by
APCs is crucial for bridging the innate and adaptive immune system and initiate additional highly
specific immune reactions against pathogens [1].
The adaptive immune system serves as the secondary defense against pathogens, utilizing
antigen-specific receptors on T cells and B cells to enable precise effector responses. Upon
encountering their specific antigens, these specialized cells undergo proliferation to generate a
sufficient population to mount an effective response against invading pathogens [2]. The adaptive
immune system can further be categorized into cell-mediated and humoral immunity [1]. The
cell-mediated immune response act upon direct cell-contact with infected cells and relies on
antigen presentation by APCs through major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules. This
response is primarily driven by T cells, with antigen presentation on MHC class I for CD8+ T cells or
MHC class II for CD4+ T cells to initiate the adaptive immune response [2]. The humoral mediated
immune response involves B cells, which undergo proliferation and activation upon interaction with
CD4+ T helper cells and initiate differentiation into plasma cells. By secreting antibodies specific to
the antigen internalized through the B cell receptor (BCR), plasma cells can activate the
complement system and eliminate pathogens [2].
The adaptive immune system produces both effector cells and memory cells. Memory cells are
crucial for immunological memory, which is a hallmark of the adaptive immune response.
Immunological memory enables the immune system to mount a faster and more effective immune
response upon secondary exposure to the antigen [1].
1.1.1 T cells
T cells are lymphocytes derived from hematopoietic stem cells (HSCs) created in the bone marrow
[6]. The development of T cell commences with the differentiation of multipotent hematopoietic
stem cells that commit to the T cell lineage by giving rise to early T cell progenitors. These

progenitor cells migrate from the bone marrow to the thymus, where they mature into either naive
CD4+ T cells or naive CD8+ T cells [6]. Maturation of naive T cells involves T cell receptor (TCR)
gene rearrangement [7] to produce a wide repertoire for peptide recognition and positive/negative
selection in the thymus to induce central tolerance [8].
Maturation in the thymus is a unique process that enables T cells to effectively recognize a broad
variety of peptides while maintaining self-tolerance [9,11]. The TCR is composed of two chains,
either a TCR α- and β-locus or a TCR γ- and δ-locus, with the majority consisting of an α- and
β-locus. TCR gene rearrangement involves random recombination of gene segments encoding the
variable regions of the TCR α- and β- chains, with the aim of generating a diverse repertoire of
TCRs [7]. This process entails somatic rearrangement to create unique combinations of variable
(V), diversity (D), joining (J), and constant (C) gene segments within the coding region of the TCR.
A unique complementarity-determining region (CDR3) is created through joining of these regions
during the recombination process and this region contributes to the antigen-binding site of the TCR
[10]. This selection also includes TCRs that recognize self-antigens, necessitating the utilization of
positive and negative selection in the thymus to eliminate self-reactive T cells [7,8].
An essential component of the immune system is the ability to discriminate between self from
non-self to achieve tolerance. To achieve this balance, central- and peripheral tolerance [3,4] are
crucial components of the innate and adaptive immune system [1]. Central tolerance involves
positive and negative selection, occurring during lymphocyte development in primary lymphoid
organs, where any self-reactive lymphocytes are eliminated to avoid mounting an immune
response toward the host itself [3].
Positive and negative selection occurs in the thymus within the cortex and medulla, respectively
[11]. Positive selection is a process that is responsible for selecting T cells with TCRs constructed
from the previous TCR gene rearrangement that is capable of binding to the MHC [7]. T cells that
express both CD4 and CD8 co-receptors become designated as double positive thymocytes and
undergo lineage commitment for either CD4+ or CD8+ T cells. The subset of T cells that have a
TCR incapable of binding MHC class I or MHC class II undergoes apoptosis [8, 13].
Negative selection eliminates the positively selected T cells that express a strong self-reactive TCR
to uphold central tolerance and avoid autoimmune reactions [13]. A wide selection of medullary
thymic epithelial cells (mTECS) and dendritic cells present a repertoire of self-antigens to test the T
cells for self-reactive TCRs. A TCR that recognizes self-antigens presented by the self-MHC
molecules on the APCs receives apoptosis signals [8,13].
The activation of differentiation of naive T cells into effector cells are mediated by the recognition of
a cognate antigen by the TCR in conjugation with various co-stimulatory signals. Naive CD8+ T cell
recognizes peptides displayed by MHC class I and differentiate into effector cytotoxic T cells (CTL)
and memory CD8+ T cells upon receiving appropriate co-stimulatory signals. Contrarily, naive CD4+
T cells recognize peptides displayed on MHC class II and differentiate into multiple immune
cytokine secreting support subsets depending on the cytokine environment [14, 15]. The
recognition process is illustrated in figure 1.

1.1.2 T cell activation and differentiation
The activation of T cells is a complex process that can occur in a TCR-dependent or
TCR-independent manner. The TCR-dependent activation of T cells follows a three-signal model
[16, 17]. This process is illustrated in figure 2.

by the binding between the TCR on the T cell and the MHC I/II complex on the APC [16]. To
enhance the stability of binding between the TCR and the MHC complex, the co-receptor CD4 or
CD8 can induce phosphorylation of the CD3 domain of the TCR. This phosphorylation initiates a
series of intracellular signaling events where immunoreceptor tyrosine-based activation motifs
(ITAMs) within the CD3 proteins are phosphorylated by associated protein tyrosine kinases. These

The second signal involves interaction between co-stimulatory molecules CD80 (B7-1) and CD86

The third and final signal is mediated by cytokines released by APCs. Successful binding between

promote the differentiation of the T cell into various subsets, depending on the binding partners
involved in signal one and signal two [20].
If the T cell fails to receive any of the previously mentioned signals, activation-induced cell death
(AICD) can be triggered. AICD involves apoptosis of T cells stemming from activation through
TCR- or autonomous activation events. This regulatory process contributes to both central and
peripheral tolerance through induced expression of ligands such as Fas ligand (CD95) [21].

Proper activation of all three signals is crucial for initiating activation of several signaling pathways
and transcription factors that drive the gene transcription needed for the proliferation and
differentiation of effector cells in response to pathogens [17].
The different CD8+ T cells effector mechanisms can be classified as lytic (cytotoxicity) and non-lytic
(cytokine production). The cytotoxic capacity of CD8+ T cells depends on the content of their lytic
granules, which contains effector molecules such as granzymes and perforin. Cytokines secreted
by activated CD8+ T cells include IFN-γ, TNF-α and IL-2, important for promoting antiviral,
inflammatory responses, and T cell survival and proliferation, respectively. Figure 3 provide an
overview of the lytic, non-lytic and receptor mediated mechanisms [56].

T cells can undergo activation through alternative pathways known as TCR-independent activation,
which involves signals and mechanisms distinct from direct recognition of antigens by the TCR.
This mode of action often leads to a more limited and transient response compared to
TCR-dependent activation. In TCR-independent activation, T cells are capable of proliferating into
effector cells in the absence of antigen stimulation [22]. This alternative activation relies on
cytokine responses [23], toll-like receptor (TLR) signaling [24] or alternative receptor bindings [23],
which serve as a complementary or supporting pathway to enhance or suppress T cell responses
in different circumstances [22, 23, 24]

1.1.3 MHC class I recognition
The main function of the MHC is to generate MHC-peptide complexes (pMHC) using various
pathways, facilitating the interaction between antigens and TCRs. MHC class I genes encode
glycoproteins expressed on the surface of almost all nucleated cells, enabling them to present
peptide antigens to CD8+ T cells [25]. MHC genes exhibit high levels of polymorphism, and the
MHC class I molecules derived from these polymorphic genes have the capacity to bind and
present a diverse array of epitopes. These polymorphic residues are important in determining
which peptides are presented by specific MHC molecules, ensuring the hosts ability to mount an
effective immune response against a wide range of pathogens [25, 27].
The human MHC is called human leukocyte antigen complex (HLA) and encompasses distinct
gene designations for MHC class I and MHC class II [25]. MHC class I is primarily comprised of
HLA-A, -B, and -C genes, encoding an α-chain transmembrane polymorphic heavy chain and a
light chain known as β-2 microglobulin chain, which forms the MHC class I molecule [27]. The HLA
gene locus exhibits extensive genetic diversity from various mechanisms, including gene
duplications, rearrangements, recombinations, and point mutations. This diversity gives rise to a
multitude of HLA alleles within the human population, contributing to the specificity and
heterogeneity of immune responses [28].
The genes within the HLA class I and HLA class II regions of the MHC have a crucial role in the
adaptive immune system, providing the necessary flexibility to respond to highly variable antigenic
targets. In the context of MHC class I recognition, cells that are undergoing a viral infection or have
become cancerous often display abnormal properties and by mediating CD8+ T cell binding, the
cell will undergo apoptosis [26]. MHC class I molecules can also interact with receptors such as
killer immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIRs) found on natural killer cells and CD8+ T cells. The
precise mechanisms and implications of these interactions between MHC class I molecules and
KIR receptors are still under investigation. In the case of KIR receptors on NK cells, their
interaction with MCH class I molecules is considered an indicator of immune evasion by a
pathogen that has downregulated class I antigen expression [29].

1.2 Natural killer cells and killer immunoglobulin-like receptors
Natural killer cells belong to the innate lymphoid cell (ILC) family that exhibit both cytotoxicity and
cytokine-producing effector functions, performing a critical role in the innate immune system. NK
cells are comparable to cytotoxic CD8+ T cells in effector functions, but a distinctive characteristic
of NK cells is the ability to recognize and eliminate certain abnormal cells without the need for prior
priming. Targeted cells include virus-infected cells or tumor cells that express abnormalities
recognizable by NK cells. The primary role of NK cells is to provide immunosurveillance and they
are distributed widely in both lymphoid and non-lymphoid tissue. NK cells, unlike B cells or T cells,
do not express somatically rearranged antigen receptors, but rely on a stochastic combination of
activating and inhibitory receptors to maintain a balance between activation of effector functions
and suppression to maintain tolerance [32]. NK cells possess the ability to exert its effector function
directly
without
undergoing
positive
and
negative selection processes, necessitating tight
regulation of its function. The activity of NK cells is governed by a variety of cell surface activation
and inhibitory receptors that control their responsiveness and ensure appropriate target recognition
and elimination [29].

NK cells exhibit two primary modes of action: direct killing of the targeted cells and indirect effects
through cytokine production. Figure 4 illustrates a model depiction the classical pathways of NK
cell activation. Direct killing involves the release of cytotoxic granules containing perforin and
granzymes to induce apoptosis of the targeted cell. Indirect effects through cytokines such as
interferon-gamma (IFN-γ), enhance the overall immune response or facilitate the differentiation of
specific immune cells [30].
NK cell is activated when the NK receptors, such as TLRs, detect the presence of ligands on cells
that exhibit a form of abnormality in the form of stress-induced ligands or non-self ligands. NK cells
use inhibitory receptors such as KIRs to detect the absence of self-molecules on abnormal self
through a mechanism called “missing-self” [29, 31]. NK cells interact with MHC class I molecules,
which are consistently present on the surface of most healthy cells. These interactions are
facilitated through these inhibitory receptors, which enable NK cells to maintain self-tolerance while
also exerting toxicity towards stressed cells that have lost their MHC class I molecule [29].
The potent cytotoxic killing capabilities, coupled with their repertoire of activating and inhibitory
receptors,
have
positioned NK cells as a promising platform for cancer immunotherapy.
Manipulating the balance between activating and inhibitory signals in NK cells holds great potential
for enhancing their effector function in immunotherapy applications. Utilizing immune checkpoint
inhibition has the potential to alleviate the suppression of NK cell mediated cytotoxicity by blocking
inhibitory signaling through receptors such as PD-1, NKG2A, or KIR receptors. This approach aims
to enhance the activation of NK cells, redirecting their response towards cancer or tumor cells [32].
KIRs have been involved in autoimmune diseases and maintenance of immunological tolerance.
Variants of KIRs or specific KIR-HLA combinations can contribute to susceptibility or immunity to
autoimmune disorders. Depending on the specific autoimmune condition, modulating NK cells
through activation, suppression or manipulation with a specific KIR variant may achieve the
suppression or elimination of autoreactive T cells [33].
1.2.1 KIR receptor: Structure and function
KIR receptors are part of the immunoglobulin superfamily and are named based on the number of
domains they possess, along with the presence of a short or long cytoplasmic tail. KIR receptors
can consist of either two domains (2D) or three domains (3D) and the length of the cytoplasmic tail
determines the presence of ITIMs. Receptors with long cytoplasmic tails contain ITIMs, enabling
them to exert inhibitory functions. Receptors with cytoplasmic tails lack ITIMs and instead utilize
DAP-12 as a signaling mechanism to transduce activating signals to the cell. The nomenclature for
the KIR receptors is determined by the receptor which can contain either two (KIR2D) or three
(KIR3D) extracellular domains, while the cytoplasmic tail length and thus ITIM status is either long
(KIR2DL, KIR3DL) or short (KIR2DS, KIR3DS) [36].
The KIR receptor repertoire can be classified into activating KIRs (aKIRs) or inhibitory KIRs (iKIRs),
with a few variants that exhibit ambiguous characteristics falling between this division [37]. The
inhibitory function of iKIRs arises from the phosphorylation of the ITIMs domain upon ligand
binding, which recruits tyrosine phosphates that inhibit cell function.

aKIRs lack ITIMs in their cytoplasmic domain and instead contain a positively charged amino acid
residue, which allows them to engage the adapter protein DAP-12, leading to cytokine secretion
and cytotoxicity [36].
The KIR receptors exhibit specific binding to HLA-A, -B, and -C molecules, allowing recognition of
polymorphism within these class I molecules. Within the KIR repertoire, haplotypes have been
found to show HLA class I specificity [37]. The inhibitory KIR receptors KIR2DL1 recognizes HLA-C
alleles with lysine at amino acid position 80 (HLA-C2), while KIR2DL2/3 recognizes HLA-C alleles
with asparagine at amino acid position 80 (HLA-C1). The inhibitory KIR receptor KIR3DL1
recognizes HLA-A and HLA-B alleles with Bw4 motifs located at the 77-80 amino acid position.
Information regarding aKIRs is limited, but it has been demonstrated that aKIR2DS1 shares the
same ligand (C2) as the iKIR2DL1. However, the binding affinity for the activating aKIR receptor is
substantially lower compared to its inhibitory counterpart [37, 38]. A list of iKIRs and aKIRs with
their associated cellular ligands can be seen in figure 5.

The binding of a peptide to KIRs is structurally restricted to the peptide-binding region of HLA
molecules. There can be variation in the expression of KIRs on different NK cells within individuals
with respect to allelic variants. Research investigating the function of KIR haplotypes in various
contexts, such as autoimmune diseases, has revealed conflicted findings regarding HLA class I
restrictions and associated function [39, 40]. The functional interpretation of each KIR haplotype
appears to be context-dependent, as conflicting data from research teams have been reported
regarding the proposed functions of each KIR haplotype in different settings [39, 40, 41, 42].
The KIR gene family exhibits a high level of diversity, which is a result of variations in KIR gene
content. This diversity arises from differences in the presence or absence of specific KIR genes,
variations in KIR gene copy number, and allelic polymorphism. The KIR genes have been mapped
on human chromosome 19q13.4 and are arranged in a tightly organized head-to-tail orientation
[43]. Figure 6 provide an overview of the general gene organization of the KIR locus.
The KIR haplotypes can be categorized into two major regions based on the presence of a
recombination hotspot: the centromeric region (Cen) and the telomeric region (Tel). The
centromeric regions can further be divided into three different regions called Cen-A, Cen-B1, and
Cen-B2. Similarly, the telomeric region can be divided into two regions called Tel-A and Tel-B.
These regions constitute two main groups of KIR haplotypes, which have been termed haplotype A
and haplotype B [43]. Haplotype A has a fixed gene content of Cen-A and Tel-A regions, which
primarily include inhibitory KIR genes and a single activating KIR gene. Haplotype B can consist of
various combination of Cen-A/B and Tel-A/B regions, and it predominantly encompasses the
majority of activating KIR receptors [44, 45].

The KIR receptor has also been reported to be present on CD8+ T cells in humans, where the
KIR+CD8+ T cell expresses randomly selected KIRs on its surface with distinct specificities [35].
These KIR+CD8+ T cells have sparked interest in their potential in immunotherapy due to the
specificity of the KIR receptor compared to the stochastic combination of activating and inhibitory
receptors on NK cells. To unravel the potential of KIR+CD8+ T cells, research into the KIR receptor
with regards to its structure and function in cancer and autoimmune disease settings is being
performed to potentially engineer this subset of KIR+CD8+ T cells into an immunotherapeutic
setting [34, 35].
1.3 KIR expression on T cells
A subset of KIR+CD8+ T cell populations has recently been gaining attention due to their unique
combination of cytotoxic properties from CD8+ T cells and regulatory aspects associated with KIR
receptors [33]. This combination of innate and adaptive immune characteristics has prompted
investigations into their potential as a novel immunotherapeutic candidate. The functional
significance of these KIRs and the implications of possessing both KIR receptors and TCRs are
poorly understood and pending further investigation [51,54].
The KIR+CD8+ T cell population has been found to express both iKIRs and aKIRs [35]. In healthy
individuals, the KIR+CD8+ T cell population contributes between 1% to 38% of the total T cell
population, but these proportions may vary in different diseases such as HIV-1, HTLV-1,
COVID-19, cancer, and autoimmune diseases [35, 36, 47]. The KIR+CD8+ T cell repertoire has
been found to express different selected KIRs and exhibits distinct specificities compared to NK
cells, which express a stochastic combination of activating and inhibiting receptors [34]. Recent
research has revealed that KIR+CD8+ T cells may have a distinct role in the development or
outcome of autoimmunity, cancer, and other diseases [47, 54, 50, 51, 52].
The activation mechanisms of KIR+CD8+ T cells are still under investigation, as the presence of
both iKIRs and aKIRs raises the possibility of both activating and inhibiting the KIR+CD8+ T cell via
their respective ligands. It is hypothesized that there is a distinction between TCR-dependent and
TCR-independent activation of KIR+CD8+ T cells. Additionally, it is unclear whether KIR+CD8+ cells
can be activated by binding of the KIR receptor alone. As distinct KIRs have been found to
possess either activating or inhibiting effects, or both, these might both co-stimulate and or dampen
the TCR-mediated activation of the cell [22, 24].

The focus of these early studies was to elucidate the association between KIR+CD8+ T cells and
specific diseases, aiming to comprehend their role and contribution within distinct pathological
contexts. More recently, the focus on KIR+CD8+ T cells has shifted towards characterizing these
cells to uncover their potential in immunotherapy.
Björkström et al. [35] conducted a comprehensive analysis of KIR expression on CD8+ T cells to
gain further insights into their characteristics. The study revealed that the majority of the KIR+CD8+
T cells exhibited a terminally differentiated phenotype (CD45RA+CD57+CCR7−CD27−CD28−CD127−),
indicating their advanced maturation stage. Further examination of the KIR+CD8+ T cells
demonstrated a high degree of restriction in the KIR repertoire, with most cells expressing only a
single inhibitory KIR.
The study did not observe any significant differences in the expression
frequencies of KIR2DL1, KIR2DL3, and KIR3DL1 haplotypes, suggesting that KIR expression on
KIR+CD8+ T cells is independent and distinct from that of NK cells. Expression of activating KIRs
such as KIR2DS1 and KIR2DS4 was found commonly on CD8+ T cells and these aKIRs did not
co-express iKIRs to dampen their reactivity. Björkström et al. [35] proposed a hypothesis that
ligation of inhibitory KIRs by their cognate ligands negate proximal TCR-mediated signaling,
leading to impaired effector responses of CD8+ T cells. To investigate this hypothesis, Björkström
et al. assessed the functional consequences of specific KIR expression on CD8+ T cells by
evaluating their overall responsiveness to anti-CD3 stimulation. They observed that KIR+CD8+ T
cells expressing two inhibitory KIRs exhibited minimal responsiveness to stimulation [35].
Li et al. [47] conducted a study to investigate the role of KIR+CD8 T cells in autoimmune diseases,
including their potential relevance to COVID-19. They observed a higher frequency of KIR+CD8+ T
cells in both blood and tissue samples obtained from patients with multiple autoimmune diseases
compared to samples from healthy donors. A major discovery with implications for potential
immunotherapy applications was the positive correlation between the increase of KIR+CD8+ T cells
and disease activity in celiac disease (CeD). In the context of CeD, it was observed that KIR+CD8+
T cells were able to eliminate gliadin-specific pathogenic CD4+ T cells of CeD patients by utilizing
their cytotoxicity in a class I MHC-dependent manner. Li et al. [47] suggest that KIR+CD8+ T cell
could be classified as an important regulatory T cell subset in humans, as this subset of T cells is
induced as part of the response during an autoimmune reaction. They hypothesize that KIR+CD8 T
cells might exert their regulatory function through a negative feedback mechanism, specifically
suppressing self-reactive cells while preserving the normal immune response against pathogens
[47, 48]. Li et al. [47] further suggest that the recognition of pathogenic CD4+ T cells may require
engagement of other receptors, such as NK-cell activating receptors, in addition to TCR activation.
KIRs might also negatively regulate the suppressive activity of KIR+CD8+ T cells [47, 49].
Gimeno et al. [54] investigated the role of KIR+CD8+ T cells in cancer immune surveillance. They
analyzed the frequency of these cells at diagnosis and explored their relationship with overall
survival (OS) in cancer patients. The study revealed that KIR2DL1+CD8+ T cells had the ability to
expand in the presence of HLA-C2 ligands in patients that survived, but this expansion was not

These interactions had a profound impact on the cellular microenvironment and the capacity for
cancer immunosurveillance. This observation led to the discovery that KIR2DL1/S1+CD8+ T cells
exhibited a gene expression signature associated with an efficient tumor immunosurveillance
profile.
KIR2DL2/L3/S2+CD8+ T cells displayed a gene expression signature indicative of
suppressive anti-tumor responses. These findings suggest that KIR interactions with their ligands
impact CD8+ T cell expression profiles and are associated with different immune functions such as
tumor immunosurveillance or suppression of tumor development [54].
The presence of KIR+CD8+ T cell population has also been documented in investigations of various
diseases, including HIV-1 [52, 53], HTLV-1 [50, 52], coronavirus [51], and influenza [51]. Twigger et
al. [50] explored the role of KIR+CD8+ T cells in HTLV-1 infection. They discovered that KIR
genotype influences CTL efficiency by affecting HLA class I mediated HTLV-1 immunity. Certain
KIR haplotypes, such as KIR2DL2, enhance the effect of known protective or detrimental HLA
class I alleles in different subgroups of HTLV-1 patients. They observed that KIR+CD8+ T cells
could be detected in PBMCs from both HTLV-1+ and uninfected donors. KIR+CD8+ subpopulations
had higher frequencies of total KIR+CD8+ T cells in HTLV-1+ samples compared to healthy donors.
Stimulation with Tax peptides to KIR2DL2+CD8+ T cells revealed that this subset constituted only a
small proportion of the total IFN-γ producing Tax-specific CD8+ T cell population. It was also
observed that HTLV-1+ asymptomatic carriers had higher frequencies of IFN-γ producing
Tax-specific KIR2DL2+CD8+ T cells compared to symptomatic carriers, suggesting that a higher
number of KIR2DL2+CD8+ T cell might be advantages in preventing HTLV-1 progression [50].
While progress is being made in understanding KIR+CD8+ T cells, continued investigation into their
characterization and functional properties are crucial to unlock the potential of KIR+CD8+ T cells for
immunotherapeutic applications. Further studies examining the role of KIR+CD8+ T cells in various
contexts, including autoimmune and cancer settings, have the potential to yield breakthroughs and
identify novel therapeutic candidates. In order to develop effective therapeutic strategies, deeper
understanding of the interactions between KIR+CD8+ T cells and their target cells, their regulatory
mechanisms of immune responses and their impact on disease outcomes is essential.

2.0 Objectives
The role of killer immunoglobulin-like receptors (KIRs) in antigen recognition and the function of
natural killer (NK) cells has been well established. Recently, expression of KIRs on a subset of
CD8+ T cells has been described, but the exact function and characteristics of these cells are yet to
be discovered. These KIR+CD8+ T cells have been observed in varying numbers in healthy
individuals, while these numbers might change in a variety of autoimmune, infectious and cancer
diseases. The dissimilarity between healthy individuals and patients in terms of KIR+CD8+ T cell
representation has sparked curiosity regarding their potential impact on CD8+ T cell immunity
against oncologic, autoimmune or infectious diseases. The interactions between KIRs and CD8+ T
cells effect on CD8+ T cell expression are still poorly understood, as interactions between KIR
receptors and pMHC have varying influence over CD8+ T cell expression. Consequently, little is
known about the antigens recognized by these KIR+CD8+ T cells through their KIR receptor, as well
as the phenotype, functionality and therapeutic potential these cells may possess. New insight into
KIR+CD8+ T cells might unveil methods to improving or blocking CD8+ T cell function, thereby
providing valuable information for designing novel T cell mediated therapies.
The aim of this project is to characterize KIR+CD8+ T cells by generating T cell clones through the
expansion of single cell sorted KIR+CD8+ T cells. This approach will create a homogeneous cell
population, enabling a more comprehensive study of the phenotype, functionality, differentiation,
and specificity of KIR+CD8+ T cells under controlled conditions. The project aims to assess different
clones in order to gain a better understanding of dynamics, differences, and differentiation status
within the KIR+CD8+ T cell population. This will be achieved through the utilization of cell culturing
techniques, fluorescent activated cell sorting (FACS) and RNA-sequencing. Furthermore, the
project seeks to investigate whether these cells can be activated and execute effector functions
independently of their endogenous T cell receptor.
To accomplish this aim, a subset of milestones has been determined:
●
Establish and expand a homogeneous KIR+CD8+ T cell population.
●
Examine KIR+CD8+ T cell positive samples for signal- and surface markers, intracellular-
and intranuclear molecules, and transcription factors.
●
Determine whether these cells can be activated end execute effector functions independent
of their endogenous TCR.

3.0 Methods
This project is divided into two major parts. The first part involves a series of steps to establish and
expand a homogeneous population of IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cell and a homogeneous control population
of RL8/EM9 virus-specific CD8+ T cell. To achieve this, a combination of general cell culturing and
single cell sorting techniques has been employed. The process of establishing the homogeneous
populations begins with the construction of single pMHC tetramers. These tetramers are used to
identify KIR+CD8+ T cell within a PBMC sample. By utilizing FACS, the KIR+CD8+ T cells and
virus-specific CD8+ control T cells was single cell sorted into 96-well plates. The expansion of both
KIR+CD8+ T cells and virus specific CD8+ control T cells into a homogeneous population of
sufficient size for experimental use are thereafter achieved by series of feeding rounds.
The second part of the project involves phenotyping, restimulation, and sequencing of the
homogeneous KIR+CD8+ T cell clones for characterization of this cell population. Phenotyping of
the KIR+CD8+ T cell clones involves staining for several surface molecules and intracellular/nuclear
antigens. The restimulation assay is conducted to assess the KIR+CD8+ T cell clones ability to
generate activating cytokines to mediate effector functions. Lastly, the TCR α- and β-chain of the
KIR+CD8+ T cell clones was sequenced. Figure 7 provides an overview of the key components of
the project.

3.2.2 Thawing and freezing of cells
Thawing of PMBCs was performed by adding heated R10 medium (20°C) to the frozen vials
containing the PMBCs. The PMBCs were counted to ensure consistency with biobank cell count
information. Subsequently, the PBMCs were washed twice by centrifugation at 1500 rpm for 5min
at 4°C to remove any remaining DMSO present. The media was discarded, and the cell pellet was
resuspended in a given volume for the desired cell concentration in X-Vivo medium.
Cell freezing was carried out by resuspending the cells in freezing buffer (FBS/FCS with 10%
DMSO) to a desired cell concentration. The cell suspension was added to cryovials in the volume
of 1mL per vial. The cryovials are kept at -80°C for 24 hours in containers with gradual cooling and
transferred to liquid nitrogen freezers (-200°C) to preserve the cells for further studies.
3.3 Establishment of homogenous T cell population
The initial step in obtaining a homogeneous T cell population of the desired subpopulation involves
the precise identification and isolation of the target cells using FACS. To accomplish this, single
pMHC tetramers were generated, designed to specifically bind to the KIR receptor in the case of
KIR+CD8+ T cells, and to the TCR for virus-specific CD8+ T cell controls. Identification of the
targeted cell population was followed by preparation of the ideal growth environment for these
cells, followed by single cell sorting.

3.3.2 Feeder cells
Feeder cells were prepared to create an optimal environment for expansion of single cell sorting of
IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cells and RL8/EM9 virus-specific T cells controls. PBMCs from different healthy
donors were obtained from the SUND biobank and thawed according to section 3.2.2. The PBMCs
were prepared in different 15mL falcon tubes to prevent mixing of PBMCs before irradiation. The
number of PBMCs required for feeder cells was 1.5⋅106-2.0⋅106 cells per milliliter X-vivo medium.
Irradiation of the PBMCs was performed using the MKT-013 RS-2000 series irradiator at shell level
one. A radiation dose of 40Gy was administered over 8 minutes and 58 seconds to ensure the
disablement of the proliferation capabilities. The irradiated PBMCs were pooled together and
centrifuged with 1500rpm for 5 minutes at 4°C. The resulting cell pellet was resuspended in X-vivo
medium to achieve a final concentration of 1.5⋅106-2.0⋅106 cells per mililiter X-vivo medium. To
promote activation and proliferation of the cells being expanded, PHA (Phytohemagglutinin-L 500x
invitrogen Cat. 00-4977-93) was added to the feeder cell solution at a final concentration of
40μg/mL. In preparation for single cell sorting, 1.5⋅105-2.0⋅105 feeder cells were deposited into
each well of multiple 96-well plates with a total of 100μL feeder cell solution per well. The plates
were incubated at 37°C in 5% CO2 with 80% relative humidity until single cell sorting.
3.3.3 Tetramer staining
The constructed tetramers in section 3.3.1 were employed for staining of PBMCs during single cell
sorting experiments and for staining of KIR+CD8+ T cell or virus-specific CD8+ control cells during
expansion. Staining with tetramers, along with a selection of antibodies specific for relevant
markers, can be utilized FACS to determine the presence of the targeted T cell population.
The staining procedure begins with thawing and washing of the cells, as described in section 3.2.2.
A suitable number of cells, ranging from 10.000-1.000.000 cells in X-vivo medium per clone, was
transferred to a 96-well plate. The cells were prepared for staining by washing twice in FACS buffer
(5% FBS/FCS Gibco™Cat. 10500064 in PBS) by centrifugation with 1500rpm for 5 minutes at
4°C. The cell pellet was resuspended in FACS buffer and 1μL of IY9, RL8 or EM9 tetramer were
added to the respective expanding clones or PBMC sample. The plate was then incubated for 15
minutes at room temperature (20°C). An antibody master mix was prepared, containing all the
antibodies for markers of interest and dump channel antibodies. The full list of antibodies used in the
master mix can be seen in table 4. The master mix was added to each well containing a clone to be
stained, and the plate was incubated for 30 minutes at room temperature (20°C). The cells were
then washed twice with FACS buffer. The cells were filtered directly before data acquisition through
fluorescence activated cell sorting (FACS) on a BD FACSCanto™/BD FACSFortessa™for clone
verification or on BD FACSmelody™cell sorter for single cell sorting.

3.4 Cell expansion and feeding
The expansion of single cell sorted IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cells and the virus-specific CD8+ T cell controls
involves a three step program designed to create an optimal proliferation environment.
3.4.1 96-well, 24-well and T25 flask expansion
Cell expansion begins after the single cell sorting process described in section 3.3.4. The single
cell sorted IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cells and EM9/RL8 virus-specific CD8+ T cell controls was incubated at
37°C in 5% CO2 and 80% relative humidity for 3 days. On the third day, the clones were fed by
adding 100μL X-vivo and 0.1μL IL-2 (stock 200U/μL) to each well of the 96-well plate, resulting in a
final concentration of 200U/mL. The fed cells were then incubated at 37°C in 5% CO2 and 80%
relative humidity for 12-15 days.
During the incubation period, the single sorted IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cells and EM9/RL8 CD8+ T cell
controls were screened for growth on the bottom of the wells. Wells showing signs of growth, such
as cell cluster formations and color change in the X-vivo medium, was further examined under a
microscope. Wells demonstrating enhanced growth and proliferation clusters were considered as
positive wells and marked for further expansion.

The positive wells were then verified to contain Tet+CD8+ T cells for IY9, RL8 and EM9 clones

Expansion into larger format follows the same format with preparation of a new feeder cells

After 21 days of expansion in the 24-well format or T25 flask format, a new round of restimulation
was performed using feeder cells and PHA as described above. Expansion in T25 flask was
indefinitely until the end of the project.
Multiple clone verification was conducted during the expansion in 24-well format and T25 flask

and RL8 clones aimed to assess if the clone growing in each well exhibited homogeneity and
retained the Tet+CD8+ T cells.

frozen down at the end of the project, as described in 3.2.2, to secure future research using the

4.2 Distinguishing KIR+CD8+ T cell populations within PBMC sample
Cell staining with the constructed (CMV, C*02:02)-IY9 tetramer and CD8-BV480 antibody was
performed to identify KIR+CD8+ T cells within the general lymphocyte population of the PBMC
sample. In previous unpublished data from the Experimental & Translational Immunology (xTI)
group, this tetramer has been shown to bind to the subset of KIR expressing CD8+ T cells via their
KIR receptor rather than their TCR. The staining can be seen in figure 10A. In this healthy donor,
3.37% of CD8+ T cells bound the C*02:02-IY9 tetramer.
To confirm that binding of the C*02:02-IY9 tetramer to the CD8+ T cells was mediated by the KIR
rather than the TCR, a second experiment was conducted. The results of this experiment can be
seen in figure 10B. The experiment demonstrated that addition of panKIR antibodies prior to the
tetramer staining prevented the binding of the C*02:02-IY9 tetramer. Addition of the tetramer
without panKIR antibodies resulted in a detectable KIR+CD8+ T cell population. These findings
show that (CMV, C*02:02)-IY9 tetramers identify KIR+CD8+ T cells. Addition of panKIR antibodies
had no impact on the binding of (EBV, B*08:01) tetramer to the virus-specific CD8+ T cell
population, as this tetramer binds to the TCR instead of the KIR.

5.0 Discussion
The success rate for survival and expression of single sorted cells into a proliferating and viable
clone cell line from the single cell sorting experiments was low with a total of ten EM9 clones, eight
IY9 clones and one RL8 clone. Excluding the non proliferating and tetramer negative clones, the
survival rate was determined to be 2.78% for EM9 clones, 0.52% for IY9 clones and 1.39% for RL8
clones. This translated into a total of four EM9 clones, three IY9 clones and one RL8 clone that
were usable in the subsequent experiments. A caveat of this study was therefore the small number
of KIR+CD8+ T cell clones that could be analyzed. The limited number of clones may not adequate
represent the diversity and functional properties of the entire KIR+CD8+ subset. However, overall
CD8+ T cells that expressed KIRs were harder to amplify initially, but also stopped proliferating or
lost expression of KIR and CD8 to a much greater extent than their EM9/RL8 specific counterparts.
This reflect findings from Arlettaz et al. [58] which found that expression of inhibitory KIRs limit
CD8+ effector cells proliferative ability in vivo. This stands in contrast to Gimeno et al. [54] which
was able to expand CD8+ T cells co-expressing different KIR receptors (KIR2DL1, KIR2DL2/S2,
KIR2DL1/S1 and KIR2DL2/L3/S2).
Whether this is an intrinsic characteristic of KIR expressing
CD8+ T cells and if it resembles the situation in vivo, remains to be further investigated. The
obtained results from the phenotypic assay and the restimulation assay should therefore be viewed
with the notation that the results are represented by only three datapoints and might not be
representative for the whole KIR+CD8+ T cell subset but give a suggestion of its characteristics.
The multiple clone verifications performed throughout the expansion period resulted in unexpected
data. The index sort confirms that all of the IY9 clones originate from a single KIR+CD8+ T cell.
During the expansion, these single cell sorted clones developed in four distinct ways represented
by Tet+CD8+ T cells, Tet-CD8- T cells, Tet-CD8+ T cells and a mixture of Tet+CD8+ T cells and
Tet+CD8- T cells. Note that for IY9 clones, Tet positivity can be used as a correlate for KIR
expression. As the clones originate from a single cell confirmed to be a KIR+CD8+ T cells, it was
expected that the majority of the acquired clones would develop into a homogeneous Tet+CD8+ T
cell population. The limited and deteriorating capability of these cells to proliferate over time might
be explained by the presence and functionality of the KIR receptor on the CD8+ T cell. The exact
function of either activating or inhibiting KIR receptors on CD8+ T cells are still being investigated,
but research done by Arlettaz et al. [58]
found that KIR expression was shown to impact the
proliferative capacity and functionality in vitro.
The phenotyping assay performed in this project did provide conclusive evidence to why the IY9
KIR+CD8+ T cell clones proliferated efficiently at day 20, but exhibited limited proliferation at day
40. This was evident in the low expression of Ki67 and CD38 at day 40. A low expression of Ki67
and CD38 is evident of less proliferation and less activation, respectively. This support the live
proliferation data and might be one explanation to why the IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cells exhibited limited
proliferation and day 40 compared to EM9/RL8 CD8+ control clones, which expressed more Ki67
and CD38. A phenotyping assay utilizing antibodies specific for the inhibitory KIR receptors
(KIR2DL1/2/3/4/5 and KIR3DL1/2/3) and the activating KIR receptors (KIR2DS1/2/3/4/5 and
KIR3DS1) could be used to determine if the majority of the clones possesses an inhibitory or
activating KIR receptor.

Unfortunately, KIR antibodies are highly cross-reactive, and therefore difficult to use. Sequencing
of clone cells could therefore be more valuable approach. Arlettaz et al. [58] investigated the effect
of CD158b (KIR2DL2/DL3) and CD158j (KIR2DS2) presence on CD8+ T cells with regard to
proliferation capabilities. In the study, they characterized a subset of human peripheral T cells
expressing the inhibitory KIR CD158b to determine the modulation of antigen-driven T cell
expansion by an endogenous inhibitory KIR. They found that engagement of CD158b by MHC
ligands diminished early TCR signaling and activating-induced cell death (AICD), but the reduced
AICD did not rescue the cells for proliferation as T cell expansion in the presence of CD158b
triggering was impaired. Arlettaz et al. [58]
hypothesize that expression of inhibitory KIR on
effector CD8+ T cells may explain in part the poor replicative capacity of T cells at early stages of
proliferation.
The phenotyping assay revealed that most of the IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cell clones and EM9/RL8 CD8+ T
cell control clones were found to be EM cells. It was also observed that the IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cells
clones have a bigger subset of Temras compared to the EM9/RL8 CD8+ T cell control clones. This
indicates that the IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cell clones are more terminally differentiated cells compared to
EM9/RL8 T cell control clones. This finding is in agreement with Arlettaz et al. [58] that found that
CD158b expression was confined to a population of CD8+ TCRαβ+ effector T cells defined by a
CD45Ra+CCR7- phenotype with high constitutive expression of granzyme B1. Björkström et al. [35]
also found that the majority of the KIR-expressing subset being investigated was terminally
differentiated (CD45RA+CD57+CCR7-CD27-CD28-CD127-) CD8+ T cells. Choi et al [59] also made
the same observation in their study, where they examined the expression of differentiation markers
in KIR+CD8+ T cells and found that the frequency of CCR7-CD45RA+ effector memory T (Temre)
cells was significantly higher among KIR+CD8+ T cells compared to NKG2A+CD8+ T cells. It was
also observed that IY9 clones had more expression of Tbet, but the implications might contradict
with the expression of granzymeB and the performed functionality assay. The observed expression
of Tbet in IY9 clones might be high as a result of outliers in a small dataset. EM9/RL8 clones was
shown to have more expression of Eomes involved in maintenance of memory CD8+ T cells and
promotes their survival and long-term persistence. The CD38 marker was also found to be more
expressed in EM9/RL8 clones compared to IY9 clones, which shows EM9/RL8 clones are more
activated and might thus indicate that activation of the KIR+CD8+ T cells might be less efficient
compared to activation through the TCR. The status of the KIR being either an inhibiting or
activating receptor might influence the expression of CD38. It was also observed that EM9/RL8
clones expressed a higher amount of Ki67, a marker used to assess cell proliferation, compared to
IY9 clones. This correlates well with the expansion data that exhibited high proliferation for
EM9/RL8 clones and low proliferation for IY9 clones. All the clones exhibited high CD8+ effector
function detonated by high expression of GranB.
Notably, we observed a significant difference between EM9/RL8 clones and IY9 clones for both
PD1 and CD57 expression, with EM9/RL8 clones exhibiting a higher expression. The high
expression of both PD1 and CD57 simultaneously on EM9/RL8 clones suggest that these clones
are highly activated and proliferative, which is physiologically counteracted by expression of
inhibitory molecules.

Long-term expression of these markers might drive these cells towards exhaustion and terminal
differentiation, thereby limiting their proliferative potential. However, this is not surprising as we are
subjecting primary cells to continuous rounds of strong, artificial activation and high amounts of
cytokines to sustain proliferation. Contrary to common cell lines, these primary cells are not
immortalized and therefore mirror the physiological response to strong activating stimulus in vivo.
Unfortunately, the restimulation assay contains several technical errors as no CD8 antibody was
added to the positive controls in figure 19, F12 and H6 and no PMA/ionomycin was added to the
positive control for A5, F12 and H6 clones (appendix D figure D2). The reactivation assay still
demonstrated that the KIR+CD8+ T cells could not be activated and execute effector functions
independent of their endogenous T cell receptor. This could be explained if the majority of the
clones expresses an inhibitory KIR receptor, as activation of these cells with IY9 peptide might
inhibit the effector functions of these clones. Arlettaz et al. [58] utilized quantitative reverse
transcription (RT)-PCR to study the expression of IFN-γ, perforin and granzyme B1. They found
that CD8+CD157b+ (KIR2DL2/DL3) constitutively express granzyme B1, perforin and IFN-γ
compared to naive T cells. Choi et al. [59] on the other hand found that the frequency of IFN-γ
producing cells was lower among KIR+CD8+ T cells following anti-CD3 stimulation compared with
other CD8+ T cell populations. It is also possible that the stimulation with free peptide which
provokes a cytokine release in TCR-mediated responses in virus-specific CD8+ T cells is not an
adequate tool to stimulate activation of the CD8+ T cell via the KIR receptor. This remains to be
elucidated further.
In the TCR sanger sequencing analysis of the α- and β-chain segments from the different clones,
amplification of the β-chain segments yielded a large segment with good quality PCR data.
Amplification of the α- segment yielded poor quality PCR data with multiple mismatches between
variable segment and the constant segment. The poor quality α- segment PCR data might be
explained by the gel electrophoresis in figure 21. The majority of the TRAV primers chosen for the
sanger sequencing produced more then one PCR product, indicating a level of homology between
the primers. The TRAV and TRBV primers was chosen from the IMGT database without specific
primer design for each clone, so a level of cross reactivity between homologous primers could
produce more than one PCR product. The primers chosen for sanger sequencing were also
determined solely on the Cq-value of the primers, indication that the primers chosen might not be
the most optimal primers for amplification of the α- and β-chain segments, resulting in subpar
sanger sequencing data. Björkström et al. [35] performed a TCR β-chain analysis of a repertoire of
KIR+CD8+ T cells and found that KIR+CD8+ T cells have a narrow TCR β-chain expression. They
found that a larger fraction of terminally differentiated CD8+T cells expressed one prominent TCR
β-chain, whereas the TCR diversity was even more restricted within KIR+ terminally differentiated
CD8+ T cels. They also found that T cell clones with the same rearranged TCR can have distinct
KIRs and that the largest KIR-expressing CD8+ T cell subset investigated displayed the narrowest
TCR β-chain repertoire. Cells expressing two distinct KIRs either shared TCR β-chain or
constituted two distinct CD8+ T cell clones. A total of five clones was sequenced with three of them
being IY9 clones. The sequencing yielded a mixture of good quality PCR segments that could be
aligned, but many of the clones failed QV. There is not enough evidence to judge the repertoire of
the KIR+CD8+ T cells from the sanger sequencing.

6.0 Conclusion
The main aim of this project was to establish and expand a homogeneous KIR+CD8+ T cell
population for characterization of this cell population. This included establishment of clones
suitable for subsequent assay evaluation of phenotypic characteristics and effector functions.
The establishment of a homogeneous KIR+CD8+ T cell population was successful, but with overall
poor long term-survival. This conclusion is based on the low survival rate of 6.94%, 2.78% and
1.04% for the EM9, IY9 and RL8 clones respectively. Some of the expanded lost expression of KIR
and/or CD8. It was not possible to determine the reason for this diversity of proliferation patterns
stemming from a single cell sorted KIR+CD8+ T cell. The discoveries here suggest that the
presence of a KIR receptor on CD8+ T cells have a profound influence on proliferation patterns.
The characterization of the IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cell clones was based on a phenotypic assay and the
functionality was tested on a restimulation assay. Based on the phenotypic assay it is concluded
that the IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cells can be designated as a more terminally differentiated and less
proliferative subset compared to EM9/RL8 CD8+ control cells. The phenotypic assay also
concluded that the KIR+CD8+ T cell clones possessed potent cytotoxic effector functions, but
activation of the cell through the KIR receptor might be less efficient compared to activation
through the TCR.
Furthermore, the restimulation assay concluded that the acquired IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cell clones was
unable to be activated and execute effector functions independent of their endogenous T cell
receptor. As the phenotypic assay and restimulation assay exhibited mixed results, it would be
favorable to repeat these assays with additional IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cell clones, as the limited number
of clones may not adequately represent the diversity and functional properties of the entire subset.
It can also be concluded that all the clones posses enough RNA to perform sanger sequencing of
the TCR α- and β-chain segments. A good PCR product for the β-chain segments was observed in
most of the clones chosen for sequencing, whereas the quality of the α-chain segments varied
between the clones. It can also be concluded that the IMGT library of variable and constant
primers have the ability to amplify the TCR α- and β-chain segments without additional primer
design. Amplification of both the TCR α- and β-chain segments from clone G5, which was found to
be CD8 negative and KIR negative, suggest that even with no expression of CD8 or KIR, enough
RNA was present for amplification and subsequent sanger sequencing of the TCR α- and β-chain.

7.0 Future perspectives
The project described here is a pilot project focused on the feasibility of expanding KIR+CD8+ T
cells into a homogeneous population to enable a thorough investigation into the characteristics of
this subset of KIR+CD8+ T cells.
The future plan for is project includes improvements to clone acquisition and several assays to
further assess the characteristics and functionality of the KIR+CD8+ T cells.
To further characterize the KIR+CD8+ T cells, various sequencing strategies could be applied.
Single-cell analysis utilizing single-cell RNA sequencing data could be employed to investigate the
heterogeneity and subpopulations within KIR+CD8+ T cells directly ex vivo. Differential gene
expression analysis could also be used to compare the gene expression profiles of KIR+CD8+ T
cells with CD8+ control T cells to identify expressed genes associated with KIR expression.
As expanding sufficient IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cells necessary for a comprehensive characterization is
unfeasible, another approach utilizing CRISPR-Cas9 will be tested as an extension of this master
project. A major obstacle in acquiring IY9 KIR+CD8+ T cells stems from reduced proliferation during
expansion. This reduced proliferation is not observed in the EM9/RL8 CD8+ T cell control clones.
To circumvent the proliferation problem, we will utilize CRISPR-Cas9 to knock in different inhibiting
and activating KIR receptor genes into the EM9/RL8 CD8+ T cell control clones. A subsequent
proliferation experiment will then be performed to test if the EM9/RL8 CD8+ T cell control clones
expressing KIR after the CRISPR-Cas9 knock-in lose their ability to proliferate, and how the
functionality of these cells is impacted by KIR expression. As the antigen specificity of these cells is
known, restimulation experiments targeting the TCR vs the KIR using their respective ligands will
give further insights into functionality and characteristics of this subset.
