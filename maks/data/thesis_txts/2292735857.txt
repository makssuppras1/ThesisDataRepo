Chapter 1
Introduction
In this thesis we will take a look at the problems that are caused by the architecture of a system
that is designed to serve a single customer (single-tenant system). The overall goal of the thesis
is then to propose a set of guidelines that help developers to avoid these problems beforehand
and how to migrate these systems to multi-tenant systems.
Software as a Service (SaaS) systems are often developed to serve a single customer. This is
especially true when the initial idea of the system originates from a customer which will be
the only user of the system. Another big reason is a narrow delivery time window and limited
resources. For startup software companies, this is a common problem since startup companies
have very limited access to funds and manpower. Startup companies also typically do not have
much experience in creating a large scale system capable of serving many diﬀerent customers.
The problems resulting from this ad-hoc system design appear when other potential customers
begin to express their interest in the system. Since the system was designed for a single customer
the system will have to be re-launched for every new customer, which can be very time consuming.
Since the system was specially designed to serve the original customer many things that should
be ﬂexible and conﬁgurable are instead hard-coded and taylor made for the original customer.
The developers now also face a maintainability problem since they end up with many identical
systems, one for each new customers.
This maintainability problem further escalates when
customers begin to express their own ideas for additional functionalities for the system.
At
that point the previously identical systems begin to diﬀer quite drastically depending on which
functionalities each customer has. Now the developers not only have to maintain all the systems
but also have to know how each and every system works and operates.
In order to highlight these problems more clearly and put this into perspective, we will introduce
a system called ’The Operation Logger’ as a case study in this thesis. The Operation Logger is
a web application originally developed to serve a single customer. It was developed by a startup

company called SAReye. The system has, as of today, gained three additional customers since
its original launch for the ﬁrst customer in mid-2013. Due to the fact that The Operation Logger
is a single tenant, has quadrupled in count, and is serving four diﬀerent customers makes it an
ideal candidate as a case study system for this thesis.
In this thesis we will use The Operation Logger as a case study to analyze its problems. Based
on related works, which will be discussed in Chapt. 4, a set of solutions for these problems will
be presented. Afterwards the system will be redesigned and re-implemented with these solutions
in mind. Finally a set of guidelines will be created for companies developing a system of similar
nature as The Operation Logger. These guidelines will contain instruction on what to keep in
mind from the beginning when designing a new system. This will be useful for companies since
it helps them avoid these common design ﬂaws mentioned above.
1.1
SAReye and The Operation Logger
SAReye was founded in early 2013 as a software company. The original goal of the company was
to crate an image detection system that could detect lost people in pictures taken from the air.
ICE-SAR, an umbrella organization for all of Iceland’s search and rescue parties, approached
SAReye with an idea of a system called The Operation Logger. SAReye agreed to develop the
system since they would then be able to fund themselves and continue developing the original
image analysis idea.
After The Operation Logger was launched many other organizations started noticing the system’s
potentials and expressed their interests. Many came to SAReye and purchased access to the
system. There are, as of July 2015, four diﬀerent customers that have access to this system.
After SAReye sold access to The Operation Logger they decided to abandon their original idea
and focus solely on The Operation Logger. Problems began to arise when SAReye gained more
customers since the system was originally only developed for ICE-SAR, and was therefore very
custom designed for them.
The Operation Logger is the case study system we will be using in this thesis. We will analyze its
problems, discuss possible solutions to these problems and ﬁnally redesign the system with these
solutions in mind. Finally in this thesis we will present a set of guidelines for future developers
from the experience of redesigning the case study system.
1.2
Breakdown of the thesis
We will break this thesis down into several chapters. In Chapt. 2 we will take a look at the case
study system, The Operation Logger, in detail. We will discuss how it was created and who its

customers are. We will take take a thorough walkthrough of the system to show how it works,

In Chapt. 4 we will be discussing various theories and approaches that can be used to ﬁx the
problems mentioned in Chapt. 3. These theories address the problems that we discussed in
Chapt. 3 and provide methods to make the system more conﬁgurable and more ﬂexible. They
also discuss how the system can be designed to allow many tenants to use the same system while,
at the same time, satisfying all the need each tenant has. These theories were mostly found in
various journal articles but a few of them were found on the internet.
In Chapt. 5 we will be discussing how the Operation Logger was redesigned in order to allow
for a more conﬁgurability, ﬂexibility and multi-tenancy. There we will present arguments on
why a certain approach or method was used in order to solve a speciﬁc problem. We will also
be demonstrating the new system in order to show the better ﬂexibility and conﬁgurability that
resulted in the new implementation. We will also show the multi-tenancy capabilities of the new
system. These demonstration will provide a walkthrough of the newly designed multi-tenant
Operation Logger, much like we do with the original Operation Logger in Chapt. 2. There we
will also provide screenshots of the parts of the systems being discussed. We will then compare
the new system’s performance against the original Operation Logger performance. Afterwards,
we will present a categorization system of multi-tenant web-application systems created during
this thesis. This categorization discuss three diﬀerent levels of variation between tenants in a
multi-tenant web-application. The last section in Chapt. 5 we will present a set of guidelines
that were produced during the redesign of the new system. These guidelines can be used by
system developers to help them design the system correctly from the beginning and therefore
save them time and resources in the long run.
In the last chapter, Chapt. 6, we will discuss the conclusion from this thesis and what has been
learned during the whole process of the thesis. We will also discuss possible future work.

Chapter 2
System analysis
In this chapter we will take a look at the case study system used in this thesis, The Operation
Logger. As discussed brieﬂy in Chapt. 1 the Operation Logger has been re-launched 3 times in
addition to the original one developed for the ﬁrst customer. We will only discuss the original
Operation Logger developed for the ﬁrst customer in this chapter. In Chapt. 3 we will take a
look at the problems regarding the Operation Logger in more detail.
In Sect.
2.1 we will explain how the system was developed, it’s purpose and the customer
requirements it satisﬁes. In Sect. 2.2 we will analyze the system’s technical background. There
we will discuss the frameworks used to create the system, the database server, and the domain
model of the system in detail.
2.1
The Operation logger
The Operation Logger was created by a startup company called SAReye [1]. The system was
originally created for ICE-SAR [2], an umbrella organization for all search and rescue teams in
Iceland. ICE-SAR wanted a system that would serve as an operation management tool where
all information regarding an operation would be visible and well structured in order to keep
operation managers updated and give them a good overview. An example of an operation is
presented in the next paragraph. Operation managers log which personnel and groups are active
in an operation and who is doing what. The system provides a list of all incidents and other
relevant information in a given operation. This gives managers a good overview of the whole
operation.
An example of an operation is a tourist lost in the wilderness of Iceland. This is a relatively
common emergency and happens many times per year. When the lost tourist is reported to
the authorities ICE-SAR is immediately notiﬁed. The ﬁrst responders are sent on scene and
5
==PAGE:16==

6
operation managers are sent to the command center. The operation managers create an operation
in the system and enter which personnel are taking part in the operation and which groups they
belong to. During the lifetime of the operation the operation managers frequently manage groups
by assigning new personnel and assign new tasks to groups. A task is deﬁned as something that
needs to be completed within the operation and a group is deﬁned as a party of SAR personnel
and operation managers, where the users of the system can be assigned to a group. The system
also serves regular ground personnel that are situated on-site and are taking an active part in
the operation. The ground personnel can use the system to input relevant information into the
system, such as ﬁndings of clues, and update the status of their group’s tasks. The system is
accessible through a mobile phone or a tablet which allows regular ground personnel to upload
images taken on scene with their mobile phones. The system also provides a simple list of all
incidents, groups and tasks through mobile devices.
In Sect. 2.1.1 we will take a walkthrough of the whole system in order to demonstrate its features
and capabilities.
2.1.1
System walkthrough
This subsection will be split into further sections where every view that the user can access will
be discussed. A view is deﬁned as a visual representation of a part of The Operation Logger
system, or a layout. There are many diﬀerent views in The Operation Logger.
The ’Main view’, discussed in Sect. 2.1.1.1 is the primary view that the user sees when he
logs onto the system and contains a list of all operations in the system. The ’Operation view’,
Sect. 2.1.1.2, is the view the user sees when he access a particular operation through the ’Main
view’, and contains all the information about that particular operation. The ’Round view’, Sect.
2.1.1.3, is contains all of the information regarding the operation and is accessed through the
’operation view’. The ’Setup view’, discussed in Sect. 2.1.1.4, provides a view to edit all of the
operations properties. The ’Checklists view’, Sect. 2.1.1.5, provides a list of all of the checklists
that the users can use in the operation. The ’Screens view’, Sect. 2.1.1.6, provides a big list of
limited information regarding the operation. The ’Change log view’, in Sect. 2.1.1.7, contains
information about all of the changes made to the information regarding operations. ’Regions
view’, Sect. 2.1.1.8, is a tool designed to help the operation managers to organize the search
eﬀort.

Main view

2.1.1.8
Regions view
Next we’ll discuss the ﬁnal view available to the user for an operation, the Region view. This
view allows the users to organize the search eﬀort for the entire operation’s area. This feature
was created about one and a half year after the initial Operation Logger was created for ICE-
SAR when they approached SAReye, the developer of the Operation Logger, and requested this
new functionality.
In search and rescue mission ICE-SAR uses many statistical tools to help them with their search
and prioritize where to look. This is based on many diﬀerent factors. One factor is the last
known location of the person missing. From this factor, along with many more, the search and
rescue organization can create 25%, 50%, 75% and 95% area radii. The 25% radius indicates the
area where it is 25% likely to include the person missing within it, and the 50% radius indicates
the area where it is 50% likely to include the person missing, and so on. This regions view
provides a functionality to create these radii areas on a map. In Fig. 2.9 we can see these radii
on the map.
This functionality also allows operation managers to create search areas. These search areas can
then be assigned to a particular task in the Operation Logger. This allows operation managers
to better organize the search eﬀort in terms of ground coverage. They will have better overview
of the exact areas that have been covered by a search and rescue team. Fig. 2.10 shows a
screenshot of the system using these search areas.

Technical background
In this section we will discuss the system’s technical background. This includes the framework
and programming language, database server and domain model. We will brieﬂy highlight the

2.2.1
Framework
During the preliminary design phase of The Operation Logger it was clear to the developers that
the system should be a web based application accessible through an internet browser such as
Internet Explorer, Chrome, Firefox and so on. This made The Operation Logger cross-platform
and very accessible.
The system could be accessed through any major computer operating
system such as Windows, Mac OS, Linux and Solaris.
By using a particular frontend and
backend framework made it possible to easily design the system for mobile devices as well as
computers. It was therefore possible to access the system through all of the major mobile/tablet
operating system such as iOS, Android and Windows 8. By being able to access the system
through a web browser eliminated the need for system setup on individual platforms since most
have already access to the web.
In order to create both the backend and the frontend of the system we needed to use a couple
of frameworks that serve diﬀerent purposes. In the following subsections we will discuss each
framework that was used for the development of The Operation Logger.
2.2.1.1
Ruby on Rails
For the backend the developers chose to use Ruby on Rails (RoR) as the web application frame-
work. RoR is free and open-source and has a good documentation. The online support is very
good compared to other potential web application framework since it is widely used all around
the world and has a strong online community. RoR is very modular and due that fact it is
possible to install many plugins created by third party developers. These plugins can be used to
handle user authentication, connection to the database, javascript extensions and so on. Many

"Rails is a web application development framework written in the Ruby language. It is designed
to make programming web applications easier by making assumptions about what every developer

In the rest of this section we will discuss MVC, ORM and the schema migration framework in
more detail. We will provide visual explanation and discuss how each of these methods interacts
with Ruby on Rails.
MVC
Ruby on Rails is based on the Model-View-Controller (MVC) design pattern. MVC is a software
architectural pattern. Many diﬀerent web application frameworks use the MVC design pattern
as its architectural pattern such as Django, ASP.NET MVC and Ruby on Rails. Like the name
suggest MVC separates the business logic into three objects; a model, a view and a controller.
The model represents the applications data and is inﬂuenced by commands from the controller.
It does this by representing data from the database tables as a class in the system’s software,
that can then be used to access and update data in the database. The controller handles all
of the user inputs. We can think of a case when the user clicks a link on some page in the
system. On the server side the controller gets a command from the client, makes some database
commands by giving command to the model and gives a responce back to the client. The view
handles the visualizations of data for the user. It controls the layout of the page he sees and
how the page responds to user input commands. It does this by controlling the HTML pages,
Javascript and CSS. Fig. 2.11 shows the MVC design pattern visually.

Object-relational mapping
ORM (Object-relational mapping) is a technique for converting data from SQL data entities to
objects that can be understood by an object-oriented programming language. ORM is used to
avoid using direct SQL commands when accessing the database by making use of the object
orientation that Ruby oﬀers. Instead of thinking about objects like database tables we think of
them like classes in the object oriented nature of the programming language.
Info Novitas descibe ORM in the following way: "Object-relational mapping (ORM) in computer
software is a programming technique for converting data between incompatible data types in
object-oriented programming languages. This creates, in eﬀect, a “virtual object database” that
can be used within the programming language." (Info Novitas [5]).
By using ORM it is possible to avoid creating complex SQL scripts and commands altogether by
taking advantage of the object-relational mapping provided by ActiveRecord. ActiveRecord is
the object-relational mapping (ORM) framework used by Ruby on Rails. "Active Record is the
M in MVC - the model - which is the layer of the system responsible for representing business
data and logic. Active Record facilitates the creation and use of business objects whose data
requires persistent storage to a database. It is an implementation of the Active Record pattern
which itself is a description of an Object Relational Mapping system." (RailsGuides [6]).
Schema migration
Schema migrations allows the developers to avoid dealing with database structure directly.
Schema migrations do not deal with data itself stored in the database but does handle the
database structure. When changes have to be made to the database, by adding/removing/chang-
ing columns to database table or creating or deleting database tables, the developers can use a
schema migration framework to do it for them. The schema uses the ORM technique discussed
above to use take the advantage of the fact that Ruby is an object oriented programming lan-
guage. It therefore uses ruby scripts to manipulate database tables in the database. Schema

User
A user is someone who accesses the system for any reason, both to view operations along with
their relevant information and to create new operations. A user is an object in the system and,

for example create operations, view all operations with the exception of password protected
operation in which case he needs to know the password to that operation, view entities marked

super admin privileges can view all operations, both password protected and otherwise. He can
also delete or hide any operation from the front page. The user entity can be seen in the domain

The

An incident belongs to an operation. An incident is a detailed explanation of some occurrence

The text attribute in the incident entity is the description of what the incident represents. The

The ’timeline’ attribute indicates

the MVC logic framework, or a static database entry (an entry manually put in the database)

or in the database. They were hard coded by design since, like previously mentioned in the
beginning of this chapter, The Operation Logger was designed and made for a single customer
with no intention of ever serving another diﬀerent customer.

The biggest challenge regarding the domain model, and in turn the data architecture, is to
allow conﬁgurability and ﬂexibility for the individual customers. In Chapt. 3 we discussed the
problems of the architecture of the original Operation Logger in detail. In short, this architecture
does not allow for ﬂexibility or conﬁgurability. In order to derive at a domain model that allows
for a more ﬂexibility, conﬁgurability and allows many tenants to access the system we need to
have all the problems mentioned in Chapt. 3 in mind. In this chapter we will focus on strategies
and methods that can be used to solve the problems presented in Chapt. 3. We will present
these methods and strategies as proposals for the new design of the Operation Logger.
The chapter is split into sections where each section discusses possible solutions related to a
speciﬁc problem mentioned in Chapt. 3. In Sect. 4.1 we will discuss diﬀerent multi-tenant
architectures and how the data from individual tenants should be separated. In Sect. 4.2 we will
discuss how the new architecture might be designed to allow diﬀerent tenants to have diﬀerent
access to various features. In Sect. 4.3 we will discuss how the users and role management might
be designed to allow for a ﬂexible role deﬁnition for tenants and also how the roles might have
diﬀerent access to features. Finally, in Sect. 4.4, we discuss what approaches can be taken to
allow diﬀerent entities in the domain model to have ﬂexible attributes between tenants.

4.1.1
Multi-tenant framework
Gao Bo et al [15] describe their research in designing and building a cost-eﬀective, secure,
customizable, scalable and non-intrusive multi-tenant database which greatly accelerates the
migration and development of SaaS applications. They focus on database layer multi-tenancy
as they state that it is the most important and challenging part of a SaaS application. They
discuss ﬁve important aspects that need to be considered when designing a architecture for a
multi tenant system; Low cost, security isolation, ﬂexibility, scalability and availability.
Since SaaS applications become widespread mainly because of its low cost for customers, cost
is one of the most important factors of such systems. Multi-tenant systems must be able to
serve many tenants in the same system, so security isolation is extremely important. Security
isolation therefore refers to the mechanism that only allows users to access the data belonging
to the tenant to which that particular user is associated to. A user should not be able to access
data from other tenants that he is not associated to.
Since tenants usually have their own
requirements such as speciﬁc entity attributes and speciﬁc features, the system must support
tenants to customize the system according to their needs. In order for the system to be able to
support access to the system to new tenants all the time the system must be highly scalable and
available.
After they have analyzed the requirements of multi-tenant system they propose their framework
to help address these challenges. In Fig. 4.1 we can see a ﬁgure of their proposed framework.
This framework is very modular, that is it does not have a ﬁxed method of providing, for exam-
ple, security isolation or performance optimization. "The framework provide nearly transparent
programming interface as the traditional database and logical view for each tenant, so developers
can focus on the business logic development without caring about multi-tenant related details."
(Gao Bo et al [15], p. 325).

4.1.2
Data & Application layers
Kabbedijk Jaap et al [16] present a comparison of 12 Multi-Tenant Architecture (MTA) patterns
that supports architects in choosing the most suitable architectural pattern, using 17 assessment
criteria. These criteria are; Time Behavior, Resource Utilization, Throughput, Number of Ten-
ants, Number of End-Users, Availability, Recoverability, Conﬁdentiality, Integrity, Authenticity,
Maintainability, Portability, Deployment Time, Variability, Diverse SLA, Software Complexity,
and Monitoring. They discuss layers concerning the application related layers and data related
layers. The application layers indicate how many instances of the system are running and how
many tenants can be served on each instance. The data layers indicated how many databases
are running and how many tenants are using each database. The three layers for the application
related layers are:
1. AD - A Dedicated Application server is running for each tenant, and therefore each tenant
receives a dedicated application instance.
2. AS - A single Application Server is running for multiple tenants and each tenant receives
a dedicated application instance.
3. AI - A single application server is running for multiple tenants and a single Application
Instance is running for multiple tenants.
The four layers for the data related layers are:
==PAGE:64==
Chapter 4: Domain Model
54
1. DD - A Dedicated Database server is running for each tenant, and therefore each tenant
receives a dedicated database.
2. DS - A single Database Server is running for multiple tenants and each tenant receives a
dedicated database.
3. DB - A single DataBase server is running for multiple tenants, data from multiple tenants
is stored in a single database, but each tenant receives a dedicated set of tables.
4. DC - A single database server is running for multiple tenants, data from multiple tenants
is stored in a single database and a single set of tables, sharing the same Database schema.
From the options in the data layer and the application layer a collection of multi-tenant system
architecture can be constructed. Each one of these multi-tenant architectures is deﬁned as a tuple
of both one option from the data layer and one option from the application layer. Kabbedijk
Jaap et al [16] then present a multi-tenant architectural comparison matrix which compares all
12 possible combination from the datalayer and application layer. Fig. 4.2 show this comparison
matrix where 1.0, being the lowest, indicates a very negative eﬀect, by applying the pattern, on
the decision criterions, and 5.0, being the highest, indicates a very positive eﬀect.

Using this decision matrix Kabbedijk Jaap et al [16] compose ﬁve Rule of Thumbs (RT) which
are helpful in giving decision makers a quick overview of the most important consequences of a
multi-tenant assessment.
Rule number one states that decision makers should focus on the database dimensions ﬁrst. This
means that they should ﬁrst focus on the database related decisions ﬁrst before the application
related decisions. This is because "The eﬀect of diﬀerent MTAs on decision criteria is largest
on the database dimension. The MTA Comparison Matrix shows the eﬀect of database related
decisions is higher than application related decisions." (Kabbedijk Jaap et al [16], p. 206)
Rule number two states that sharing database tables (DC) enables serving of many tenants but
harms robustness. In other words, when designing a SaaS system which has limitied variables
related to what functionalities the tenants can have, sharing a database is generally a good
decision because it makes the product easier to maintain and monitor and reduces deployment
time.
Rule number three states that sharing application instances helps maintainability and perfor-
mance but harms variability. This makes sense since having only one instance of the system
simpliﬁes maintainance since developers only have to worry about one system.
Rule number four states that ease of implementing variablitiy diﬀers greatly from the multi-
tenant architectures. This means that if an AD architecture is chosen then variablitiy between
tenants is easily achieved.
Choosing a AI architecture causes large problems implementing
variability over all tenant instances.
Rule number ﬁve states that dedicated servers improve performance. This is because "When
choosing an MTA with dedicated servers (i.e.AD, DD) the time behavior, recoverability, vari-
ability and conﬁdentiality are expected to be good, and software complexity low." (Kabbedijk
Jaap et al [16], p. 208). However this also hampers scalability because since when the number
of tenants increases, it becomes harder to maintain all of the dedicated servers and hardware
cost will rise.
From these rules we can conclude that there are trade-oﬀs between performance, variability,
scalability and maintainability. Choosing a single instance for each tenant improves performance
and variability but reduces scalability and maintainability.
Choosing a shared instance for
all tenants greatly simpliﬁes maintainability and makes the system more scalable but reduces
variability and hinders performance somewhat.
Using this comparison matrix and the rules concluded from Kabbedijk Jaap et al [16] we know
the trade-oﬀs between the various quality attributes. We can therefore make an informed decision
on how to design a system’s architecture.

Function based architecture
This section focuses mainly on how a SaaS system can handle all the diﬀerent functionality
requirements from various tenants. These functionality requirements are diﬀerent depending on
the tenant but they can also change quite rapidly. In other words a tenant might request access
to new functionalities that he did not have access to earlier.
Wang Hua et al [17] discuss how they achieved multi-tenant feature access by using their feature
categorization method. This is duscussed in Sect. 4.2.1. Rico Ortega Antonia et al [18] discuss

a method provided by Niedriti Aivars et al [19] for adapting the user interface for the users of

4.2.1
Feature categorization
Wang Hua et al [17] discuss how achieving conﬁgurability in SaaS applications might be possible.
They ﬁrst discuss function conﬁgurability and afterwards discuss user interface conﬁgurability

When discussing the function conﬁgurability Wang Hua et al [17] ﬁrst identify atomic functions.
By this they mean that the whole application should be divided into principal and independent
atomic functions where all the atomic functions combined compose all of the functions (features

tionalities. We can see a possible design of the atomic function in Fig. 4.3. In that ﬁgure we
see how a function is conceptualized into an entity named ’Function’ in the domain model of
a system. That ’Function’ entity is then associated to many other ’Function’ entities since in
order to make that function available to the tenant, some other functions may be required to be
available to that tenant as well.

Feature access administration
Rico Ortega Antonio et al [18] talk about that in traditional multi-tenant applications diﬀerent
functionality requirement require diﬀerent applications, or diﬀerent instances. While some of the

only the ability to share instances among tenants, but also to allocate diﬀerent functionalities and
serve them selectively on demand depending on each tenant’s contract." (Rico Ortega Antonio
et al [18], p. 147). MT2 systems are split into two levels; Administrative level and an Instance
level. Fig. 4.5 shows an administrative and instance level of a MT2 system. Fig. 4.5 "illustrates
the general model of the MT2 architecture. The administrative level (now represented by the
MT2 MP) has to deal with a diﬀerent kind of multi-service subscription; as well as mono-target
terms, contract obviously need to specify what functionalities from the portfolio are to be served.
In turn, the instance tier will selectively deploy functionalities on demand according to tenant’s
subscription contract." (Rico Ortega Antonio et al [18], p. 152).

User & role management
This section focuses on user management between tenants. The tenants will all have their own
set of users so the architecture of a multi tenant system need to be able to cope with the fact
that users belong to certain tenants. The user roles will also be discussed in this section but
in Sect. 3.5 we discussed how the original operation logger designed for ICE-SAR dealt with
the user role management. The architecture that deals with the user and roles in the original
Operation Logger is very inﬂexible and unconﬁgureable since that system is single-tenant and
the roles are static and hard-coded.
In Yang Shin-Jer et al [20] they discuss the cloud service model, under a multi tenant architecture,
by using identity management and role based access control in order to propose and design a
role-based multi-tenancy access control (RB-MTAC). This access control identiﬁes the user and
determines it’s applicable roles. This is usefull since diﬀerent user for the same tenant can have
diﬀerent functionality privilages based on the user’s roles. The RB-MTAC also achieves data
isolation to improve the system’s processing performance and hardens the security of the system.
Fig. 4.7 shows the operation schematic of RB-MTAC. "[In Fig. 4.7] every tenant or user needs
to log in with an account number and password while the system authenticates the user account
through identity management. If the users are valid, the role assignment will capture the role
corresponding to the user from the RB-MTAC database and assign the role and access rights,
which belong to the user. The user can carry out activities, access functions and computing
resources in the system through RB-MTAC." (Yang Shin-Jer et al [20], p. 275)

4.4.9
EAV (Entity-Attribute-Value)
EAV is a data model sometimes known as ’vertical database model’ that allows entities to have
ﬂexible and conﬁgurable attributes. These attributes can be created and deﬁned at run time.
This allows entities for individual tenants to gain attributes when the tenant gains access to a
certain feature in the system. Being able to deﬁne attributes at run time also allows users of the
system to add more attributes to entities. This is useful since diﬀerent tenants of the system
(diﬀerent customers) will have diﬀerent functionality requirements and therefore the entities,
such as operation may have diﬀerent attributes for diﬀerent customers.

EAV is an acronym for Entity-Attribute-Values, which indicates how the database should be
structured.
The Entity represents the entity that is being extended by allowing it to have
ﬂexible attributes. The Attributes represents the ﬂexible attributes that the entity has. The
Value represents the actual value of that ﬂexible attribute for a particular entity. These three
concepts are separated from each other in the domain model. In Fig. 4.16 we can see an EV
designed database (on the right) compared to a conventional designed database (on the left).
Fig. 4.16 is taken from Löper Dortje et al [24] where they discuss the implementation of the
EAV database model in a healthcare information system.
Figure 4.16: EAV data model compared to a conventionally designed database model [24]
This healthcare information system that Löper Dortje et al [24] discuss should be able to handle
ﬂexible attributes. They discuss the implementation of the EAV model and discuss the advan-
tages and disadvantages of implemention the EAV database model in an information system.
Löper Dortje et al [24] provide a good comparison of the EAV model and a conventionally de-
signed database model. This comparison can be seen in Fig. 4.16. In their conclusion they
state that "This approach [EAV] oﬀers ﬂexibility concerning diﬀerent standard types and the
evolution in healthcare knowledge and processes. It also allows for highly sparse data to be stored
in a compact way." (Löper Dortje et al [24] p. 1). However, they point out a disadvantage of
using the EAV method, which is the performance problem at the query processing.
We implement the EAV data model by separating the attributes and values into diﬀerent indi-
vidual objects. For each entity we would therefore have an entity object (such as operation), an
attribute object (such as ’active’ boolean attribute used to specify weather or not the operation
is active or not), and a value object (such as ’true’ or false’). In order to allow diﬀerent customers
to have diﬀerent attributes associated to the entities the property objects would belong to the
organization object. Fig. 4.17 introduces the concept of the EAV implementation of the system.
For simplicity only the Operation entity is introduced in this ﬁgure where the entity is placed
on the left side, the attributes are on the right side and the attributes values are in the middle.

5.1.1.2
Data separation
In order to provide data conﬁdentiality between tenants a level of data separation must be
decided. The possibilites for data seperations were discussed in Sect. 4.1 in detail. PostgreSQL
was used in the design of the original Operation Logger with good success. During research for
potential solution methods for the data separation it was discovered that PostgreSQL provides
another level of data layer between data tables and databases, called schema. A schema allows
for the splitting of databases into identical fragments. Each schema is therefor identical in terms
of containing data tables.
As discussed on the PostgreSQL website: "There are several reasons why one might want to use
schemas: (1) To allow many users to use one database without interfering with each other, (2)
To organize database objects into logical groups to make them more manageable, (3) Third-party
applications can be put into separate schemas so they do not collide with the names of other
objects." (PotgreSQL [28]). In Fig. 5.1 we can see a visual description of the schema layer, and
as we can se they are between the database layer and the data table layer.
Figure 5.1: Visualisation of schema [29]
By splitting the database into schemas we only have one database server and one database. The
data between the tenants is however separated into schemas. We therefore have a combination
of both the DS method and the DB method mentioned in Sect. 4.1.2. The DS method speciﬁed
that a single Database Server is running for multiple tenants and each tenant receives a dedicated
database, whereas The DB method speciﬁed that a single database server is running for multiple
tenants, data from multiple tenants is stored in a single database, but each tenant receives a
dedicated set of tables.

In order to accomplish this data separation a special third-party library, or in Ruby terms a
Gem, called ’Apartment’ [30] was used. "Apartment provides tools to help you deal with multiple
tenants in your Rails application. If you need to have certain data sequestered based on account or
company, but still allow some data to exist in a common tenant, Apartment can help." (Inﬂuitive
Corporation [30]). This gem allows the system automatically create a new schema for every new
customer that subscribes to the operation logger. The gem also allows the system to easily switch
between schemas depending on how you access the system. A diﬀerent schema will therefor exist
for every single tenant but there will also be a ’public’ schema that is used for all the data that
may be shared across all the tenants.

the instances of The Operation Logger in order to maintain which tenant gets access to which
instance.

5.1.3
Conﬁgurable entity attributes
One element that diﬀers drastically between tenants are the entity attributes. This problem was
discussed in Sect. 3.4. The tenants may want to be able to assign some speciﬃc string, integer
or boolean attribute to an entity. They may however also want to assign a selectable attribute to
an entity. Selectable attributes are attributes that the user can select a value from a predeﬁned
list, instead of inputting the value himself. In order to allow the system to be able to have
conﬁgurable entity attributes and to allow the system to actually know which tenants have what
attributes associated to various entities we split the solution into two layers called ’Database
layer’ and ’Software layer’. We will discuss these two layers in the following subsections.

5.1.3.1
Database layer
This layer relates to what method is used to allow entities to have ﬂexible attributes between
tenants, and how that method works. In our case we used PostgreSQL database server but this
method could be used with most of the other widely used database servers. In order for the
system to allow entities to have diﬀerent attributes between tenant, we chose implement the EAV
method (see Sect. 4.4.9). The reason that method was chosen was because it has proven itself
in other systems if implemented properly. Another reason why the EAV method was chosen was
because there exists a ruby GEM (third party library) that handles all the EAV functionality.
The GEM that was used to implement EAV is called Hydra_attribute [31] and is an open source
ruby library. "Hydra_attribute is an implementation of EAV (Entity-Attribute-Value) pattern
for ActiveRecord models. It allows to create or remove attributes in runtime. Also each record
may have diﬀerent sets of attributes, for example: Product with ID 1 can have diﬀerent set of
attributes than Product with ID 2." (Kostiantyn Stepaniuk [31]).
In Fig. 5.4 we can see the database schema that is used by the Hydra_attribute GEM. In that
ﬁgure they take an example in order to show what the database looks like for every entity that is
able to have conﬁgurable entities. In Fig. 5.4 they take an entity called ’products’ as an example.
That entity has an integer attribute named ’id’ and two datetime attributes named ’created_at’
and ’updated_at’. It also has an integer attribute named ’hydra_set_id’ which links the entity
together with the ’hydra_sets’ and ’hydra_attribute_sets’. We will not be discussing these
entities since we do not use them in our system. As we can see the ’products’ entity is linked to six
other entities call ’hydra_string_products’, ’hydra_text_products’, ’hydra_integer_products’,
’hydra_ﬂoat_products’, ’hydra_boolean_products’, ’hydra_datetime_products’. These tables
contain all the ’product’ entity’s ﬂexible attributes along with their values. These six entities
have the excact same attribute apart from one, the ’value’ attritbue. All of the six entities have
a ’value’ attribute but what diﬀers is what type of attribute it is. In the ’hydra_ﬂoat_products’
entity the ’value’ attribute is a ﬂoat type attritbue, in ’hydra_boolean_products’ it is of type
boolean, in ’hydra_datetime_products’ it is of type datetime, and so on.
Hydra_attribute
gem therefor allows the conﬁgurable and ﬂexible entity attributes to be either a ﬂoat, boolean,
datetime, string, text and integer.
These are all of the attribute types that Ruby on Rails
supports. All of these six entities are also linked to another entity called ’hydra_attributes’.
This entity manages the attributes and decides for example what the default value of an attribute
should be, what is allowed and so on.
Since these six entities are all linked to only one entity, in our case an entity calles ’products’,
identical six tables exist for every entity in the system that should have conﬁgurable and ﬂexible
attritbues. We therefor need to decide which entities in the system should have ﬂexible attributes
and which entities should not.

selected from a list of possible values or if the user can write in his own value.
The ’Function’ entity represents all the diﬀerent features available to the tenants. The ’Property’
entity is placed in the public schema since these entities are deﬁned by the system developers
and cannot be created or deﬁned by the tenants.
5.1.4.3
Property Group
The ’Property Group’ entity exists to be able to group many ’Property’ entities together. All
of the ’Property’ entities that are assigned together to the same ’Property Group’ all belong to
the same ’Entity’ entity. The reason why we may need to group ’Property’ entities together is
to allow the user to understand many diﬀerent ’Property’ entities as the same multi-selectable
attribute. We discussed these multi-selectable attritbues in Sect. 3.4 where we provided a ﬁgure
to illustrate it (see Fig. 3.10). The ’Property Group’ entity only has two attributes; ’name’ and
’grouped’. The ’name’ attribute indicates the name of that ’Property Group’, and the ’grouped’
boolean attribute indicates whether or not the ’Property’ entities are grouped together to form
a multi-selectable attribute for the user.
5.1.4.4
Extra Property, Extra Property Group & Extra Property Option
The ’Extra Property Group’ was discussed in Sect. 5.1.3.2. There we discussed that this entity
could be used to create new entity attributes for individual tenants. However, these proper-
ties are also necessary to support diﬀerent feature requirements between tenants. Whenever a
’Function’ entity as assigned to a tenant, all of the ’Property Group’, ’Property’ and ’Property
Option’ get copied to the tenants in the form of an ’Extra Property’, ’Extra Property Group’,
or ’Extra Property Option’ entity. This is done to support conﬁgurability between attritbues
for the individual tenants having the same functions. This is also necessary since some of these
’Property’ entities can be selectable. If the properties are selectable the tenants have to be able
to create their own value options independent of other tenants.
In order to be able to tell the properties assigned to features that the tenants have access to and
the properties that the tenants have created themselves, a boolean attribute exists in the ’Extra
Property’ called ’extra_property’. This attribute is true if the tenants created the property
themselves and it is false if the property comes from some function that the tenant has gained
access to.

5.1.4.5
Function
The ’Function’ entity can have many ’Property’ entities and be associated to many ’Tenant’ enti-
ties. An example of a ’Function’ would be a one named ’Geolocation’. This function represents
a feature in the sytem that allows users to geo-tag operations. This function has two ’Prop-
erty’ entities called ’latitude’ of type ’double’ and ’longitude’ of type ’double’. These ’Property’
entities would then be linked to an ’Entity’ entity called ’Operation’. When this function is
associated to a tenant two ’Extra Property’ entities are created in the tenant’s schema with the
same attributes and connections as the two ’Property’ entities associated to that function. This
would therefor make the ’Operation’ entity for that particular tenant gain two more attributes
called ’latitude’ and ’longitude’, with the help of Hydra_attribute GEM that allows for a EAV
implementation of the database. The reason why these ’Extra Property’ entities are created are
so that if the ’Property’ is selectable the tenants can create their own individual value options.
Like the ’Function’ entities are associated to tenants to imply which tenants have access to those
features the ’Function’ entity is also associated to the ’FR Setting’ entity.
Apart from telling what attributes the various entities gain from the tenant being associated to
a ’Function’ entity, the ’Function’ entity is also used to decide whether or not a speciﬁc part of
the system should be rendered. It can also be used to decide if the tenant’s users have access
to a part of the system. The ’Function’ entity is placed in the public schema since these entites
are deﬁned by the system developers and cannot be created or deﬁned by the tenants.
5.1.4.6
FR Setting
The ’FR Setting’ entity links the ’Role’ entity (which is discussed in Sect. 5.1.2) and ’Functions’.
The ’F’ and ’R’ in ’FR’ stand for ’Function’ and ’Role’. This is so that the tenants can choose
which of their user roles can access which features that the tenant has access to. Not only can the
tenant use the ’FR Setting’ entity to specify which of its user roles can access the feature but he
can also specify how that speciﬁc role can interfere with that property by using the ’FR Setting’
entity’s ’create’, ’read’, ’update’, and ’delete’ attributes. This method is based on the CRUD
functions. Other two attributes that the ’FR Setting’ entity contains are called ’list’ and ’show’,
both of them boolean attributes. These attributes specify whether or not the connected ’Entity’
entity’s ’Property’ entities should be shown on the index list or the show page for that entity.
Lets say a ’Role’ entity is connected to another ’Entity’ through the ’FR Setting’, ’Function’ and
’Property Group’ entities and the ’FR Setting’ entity that connects them have the ’list’ boolean
attribute as false. When a user having that ’Role’ in question is viewing the list of those entities
that that speciﬁc ’Entity’ entity represents, then he would not see those attributes. The ’FR
Setting’ entity is placed in each of the tenants schema since they are created and deﬁned by the
tenants.

5.1.4.7
PGR Setting
The ’PGR Setting’ entity links the ’Role’ entity (which is discussed in Sect. 5.1.2) and the
’Extra Property Group’ entity, which is discussed in Sect. 5.1.4.4. The ’PG’ and ’R’ stand for
’Proeprty Group’ and ’Role’. The purpose for this entity is so that the tenants can not only
create their own attributes to an entity, but they can also choose which of its roles has what
CRUD privileges to that attribute. A tenant might, for example, create an attribute for some
speciﬁc entity that will contain some conﬁdential information. He might therefor only want some
administrative role to be able to read and write those information in the system.
5.1.5
Database population
In this section we discussed that the database is split into a public schema and many other
schemas belonging to each tenant of the system. The public schema handles everything that
is deﬁned by the system administrators. The public schema stores information about ’Entity’,
’Property’, ’Property Group’, ’User’, ’Tenant’ and ’Function’ entities. In order to populate the
database tables that stores the information about these entities we use a database seed ﬁle. This
database seed ﬁle is a ruby ﬁle situated in every Ruby on Rails project and is used to populate
a database by using a single command. This command runs this seed ﬁle.
In this seed ﬁle we deﬁne all the ’Function’, ’Entity’, ’Property Group’ and ’Property’ entities
that the system needs to be able to run. This is usefull since the system developpers need to
create new features to implement into the system. During the development of a new feature the
developpers deﬁne new entities, such as a ’Feature’ and/or a ’Entity’ entity, in the seed ﬁle and
run it. The seedﬁle would then create the appropriate entities in the public schema.

5.3
Performance test
Since we have successfully created a system architecture for The Operation Logger that allows
for a more ﬂexibility and conﬁgurability than the original Operation Logger we need to compare
the system performance of these two system. The new architecture is a lot more complicated
than the original one so one can assume that there will be some performance drawbacks. In order
to properly measure performance we will use a third party tool to measure the performance of
these two systems. The original operation logger is not comparable to the newly created system.
This is because the original Operation Logger is a lot more advanced in terms of features and
user interface. Due to project scaling for this thesis, the new Operation Logger system designed
and created in this thesis omitted most of the user interface and most of the features. With
the help of the version control used to store the systems code we could easily revert back to the
version when the multi-tenant features of the system were not implemented. Afterwards a few
entities were implemented in order to correspond properly to the multi-tenant system developed
in this thesis
We used a third party system called JMeter [32] which is an open source Java application
designed to test web applications. We used this program to simulate multiple users accessing
the system at the same time, and performing some predeﬁned actions. We used a server to
host both the multi-tenant and the single-tenant version of the Operation Logger designed in
this thesis. We then programmed JMeter to simulate multiple users trying to access the system
at the same time. All of these users performed the same routine. They ﬁrst access access one
speciﬁc operation, wait for 3-7 seconds (uniform random), then access the active round of that
operation, wait again for 3-7 seconds and then repeat. This routine was performed 15 times
for each users. We made JMeter perform this action for the multi-tenant and the single-tenant
system for 10, 20 and 40 users running at the same time.
In order to analyze how much each extra entity property had on the overall performance of
the multi-tenant system we also ran this test for the multi-tenant system with more attributes
associated the the ’Operation’ entity. We also performed the test on the multi-tenant system
with the operation containing many incidents, tasks and groups. This was done in order to
analyze how much inﬂuence it had on the system performance when the operation contained a
lot of data
In the following subsections we will discuss these individual tests, and then ﬁnally in the last
subsection we will discuss the results of this performance test.

5.4
Multi-tenant system categorization
During the development of the new Opertaion Logger we noticed how multi-tenant web applica-
tion systems can be split into several categories. These categories explain the levels of variation
of requirements between the tenants. Each category of a multi-tenant system therefore describe
how much the tenant requirements diﬀer from one another. There are a total of three categories
presented here. and in the following subsections we will discuss each of these categories in detail.
The ﬁrst category, discussed in Sect. 5.4.1, is called ’No functional variations’ and describes
multi-tenant systems that provides the same service to all of its tenants. The second category,
discussed in Sect. 5.4.2 is called ’Diﬀerent data collection’ and describes multi-tenant systems
where the system’s tenants can specify the data they want to collect in the system. The third
category, discussed in Sect. 5.4.3, is called ’Varying feature requirements’ and describes multi-
tenant systems that can provide features for the tenant depending on the tenant’s requirements.

This section will be split into further subsections. In Sect. 5.5.1 we will discuss guidelines on

the system developers need to have in mind when designing a system that is very likely to ever

5.5.1
Low or no multi-tenant likelihood
In the case where the system is considered unlikely, or will deﬁnately never, have to become
multi-tenant in the future there is one issue that the system developers should be aware of when
designing the system. This issue related to having everything in the system conﬁgurable. This
issue relates to the problem discussed in Sect. 3.1. There it was pointed out that the system
had many data objects hard coded as enumerated lists in the system’s code or as static database
entries.
Conﬁgurable data-objects
In order to avoid this from the beginning the system developers should design the system in
a way that all data-objects should be conﬁgurable by the customer. What this means is that
all possible attribute values that the user can select from should not be hard-coded into the
system but should exist as a conﬁgurable entity in the domain model. An attribute value is
a value for some entity attribute in the domain model. This means creating a special entity
for each selectable attribute in the system. If we take the Operation Logger as an example all
the enumerated lists in the system, discussed in Sect. 3.1.1, should be deﬁned by an entity in
the domain model. This allows the customer to create new possible attribute values and even
update existing ones.
The system should also provide a good method for the systems users to create and update these
possible attribute values. This can be done by creating an administer console in the system that
can be used by the administrators of the system.
Making these decisions and implementing this conﬁgurable method into the system costs very
little extra for the system developers during system design and will save considerable time and
resources later on in the system’s lifecycle. This is because system maintenance reduces for the
system developers since the customer himself can conﬁgure the systems.

5.5.2
Medium multi-tenant likelihood
In the case where the system is considered to be around 50% likely to ever needing to support
more than one tenant, we suggest that the developers should consider three things, in addition
to the issues mentioned in Sect. 5.5.1 Conﬁgurable data-objects, when designing the system.
In addition system developers should also consider three other issues which we will discuss in
more detail in this section: Coarse layout theme, System accessibility and Data seper-
ation. We do not suggest that the developers should consider how variable the system will be
between tenants. This is because designing a system with a lot of variance between tenants is
very expensive and will lengthen the development time a lot. Since delivery time is often very
important for system developers, and the system isn’t that likely to ever having to serve more
than the ﬁrst original tenant in this case, we will assume that the system being designed will
have very little variance between tenants.
It is not very time consuming to implement methods that react to these issues mentioned here
below and will allow the system to serve multiple tenants. The system developed with these
issues in mind will allow diﬀerent tenants to access the system, but the system will only be able
to provide the same service to all of its tenants. If the system developers concern themselves
with the issues mentioned here below they are more likely to save money in the long run if the
system later on becomes multi-tenant.
Coarse layout theme
First is that each tenant should consider is that each tenant will in almost all cases wanting
to have individual layout theme and logo. Each tenant should be able to customize the layout
theme and logo to his speciﬁc needs. This problem presented itself in the original Operation
Logger when the system’s second customer purchased access to it. Every time the Operation
Logger’s developers had to relaunch the system for every new customer the system’s theme color
and logo needed to be changed. It did not matter if the new customer did not have any additional
feature requirements, they always needed to have diﬀerent layout theme and logo.
In this thesis we did not discuss possible solutions to this problem. This was due to scaling
since we could not accomplish to ﬁnd solutions to every single problem identiﬁed in the original
Operation Logger. However, implementing a method to deal with the varying layout theme and
logo should not be a complex task.
System accessibility
The second issue that the system developers should consider is that every tenant needs a way to
access the system. The system need to be able to diﬀerentiate between tenant and so the access
method have to imply what tenant is being accessed. In the case of the Operation Logger we
decided to use subdomains to make a distinction between tenants. There would be a domain
name for the system preceeded by a subdomain to clarify what tenant was being accessed.
==PAGE:122==
Chapter 5: Results
112
The domain was, in the example for The Operation Logger, ’www.sardynamics.com’.
Each
tenant could then access the system by appending a subdomain before the actual domain name.
For example, a tenant named ’Landsbjörg’ would have a subdomain called ’landsbjorg’. And so,
Landsbjörg’s users could access the system by typing ’www.landsbjorg.sardynamics.com’ in their
internet browsers. The implementation of this method for The Operation Logger was discussed
in Sect. 5.1.1.1.
Data seperation
The third issue that developers should be aware of is data security. Data leak between tenants is
considered a very serious system security ﬂaw. Since the system is hosting many diﬀerent tenants,
each with its own set of data, this issue needs to be considered. Many possible approaches exist
to solve this problem. In Sect. 4.1.1 and 4.1.2 we discussed a few possible approaches to data
separations.
In the case of The Operation Logger a decision was made to split the database into schemas,
where each tenant would have its own individual schema. This schema contains all of the data
belonging to that tenant. All of the tenants’ schemas are identically structured and contain all
of the same entities. This is discussed in Sect. 5.1.1.2.
5.5.3
High multi-tenant likelihood
In the case where system is considered highly likely to ever needing to support more than one
tenant we will also need to consider on what level the system varies between tenants. This is the
category that our case study system for this thesis, The Operation Logger, falls into. In Sect.
5.4 we discussed categorization of multi-tenant systems. These categories discuss the diﬀerent
variations between tenants in a multi-tenant system. For each category that the system can fall
under we will discuss what the system developers should have in mind when developing a system
of that nature.
5.5.3.1
Low variety
Multi-tenant systems with low variety is categorized under Category 1 discussed in Sect. 5.4.1.
We deﬁne low variety between tenants as no diﬀerence of functional or non-functional require-
ments between tenants. However, the tenants will require some very basic conﬁgurability of the
coarse layout of the system
If the system developers assume that the system they are developing falls under Category 1 then
they need to have the following issues in mind: Conﬁgurable data-objects, Coarse layout
theme, System accessibility and Data seperation. All of these issues have been discussed
in the previous sections. By following these recommendations the system being developed will
==PAGE:123==
Chapter 5: Results
113
be capable of providing access to multiple tenants. However, the system would be identical
for every tenant apart from some coarse layout. This is not a problem since the system being
developed doesn’t need tenants to have diﬀerent requirements.
5.5.3.2
Medium variety
Multi-tenant systems with medium variety is categorized under Category 2 discussed in Sect.
5.4.2. We deﬁne medium variety in a multi-tenant system when the system’s tenants need to
use the system to collect additional data which the other tenants don’t require. If the system
developers assume that the system they are developing falls under Category 2 then they need
to have the following issues in mind: Conﬁgurable data-objects, Coarse layout theme,
System accessibility and Data seperation.
In addition to those four issues the system
developers also need to consider two other issues that will be discussed in more detail in this
section: Flexible entity attributes and Flexible role management.
Flexible entity attributes
The system needs to be able to handle diﬀerent data collection between tenants. This means
that entities in the domain model need to have conﬁugrable and ﬂexible attributes. The tenant
should be able to deﬁne their own attributes to entities in the domain model. There are a few
ways that this can be implemented. We discussed a few possible methods that can be used in
Sect. 4.4. In Sect. 5.1.3 we discussed what method we used and how we implemented this
into The Operation Logger. The way this was implemented in the Operation Logger was using
a method call Entity-Attribute-Value as well as creating a few entities in the overall system
domain model. This was discussed in Sect. 5.1.3.
Flexible role management
Since the system will be serving many diﬀerent users, and each user having a role assigned to
him, the system developers also need to take into consideration role privileges. This means that
the system has to be able to support conﬁgurable roles. The tenants have to be able to create
their own roles that they can then assign to users. This will allow tenants to specify which
roles has access to which entity attributes. In Sect. 5.1.2 we can see how the new multi-tenant
Operation Logger implemented role management.
5.5.3.3
High variety
Multi-tenant systems with high variety is categorized under Category 3 discussed in Sect. 5.4.3.
We deﬁne high variety in a multi-tenant system when the system’s individual tenants require
features that other tenants may not want.

If the system developers assume that there will be medium variety between the tenants of the sys-
tem then they need to have the following issues in mind: Conﬁgurable data-objects, Coarse
layout theme, System accessibility and Data separation, Flexible entity attributes
and Flexible role management. These issues have been discussed in the previous sections.
In addition to those six issues system developers also need to consider Varying feature access
between tenants, which will be discussed here.
Varying feature access between tenants
Since the system being developed needs to allow the tenants to have diﬀerent feature access
between them the system developers need to consider how this might be achieved. In this thesis

Chapter 6
Conclusion
In this thesis we examined problems of an existing web-application system. This system is called
The Operation Logger and was developed for one particular customer from whom the idea for
the system originated from. The problems discussed and addressed in this thesis arose when
the company that created The Operation Logger gained more customers. The system therefore
needed to serve more additional tenants, but since the system was only designed for a single
tenant the system was redeployed for every single customer. This created a big maintainability
issue due to the inﬂexibility- and unconﬁgurability problems in the original system.
The general idea behind this thesis was to research possible solutions to these problems and to
redesign and implement the system having these solutions in mind. In the meantime we took
what was learned from the whole process and presented a set of guidelines that could be used
by future system developers to avoid these problems in the initial phase of system design.
In order to give a good overview of the case system and what purpose it serves we provided
a detailed overview of the system. This overview included a walkthrough of the whole system
where we discussed what it had to oﬀer in terms of features. We then described the system’s
technical background. This included taking a look at the framework used, the system’s database
server and the system’s domain model.
Next we analyzed the system’s problems, which where six in total. One problem explained how
many parts of the system were hard-coded into the system speciﬁcally for the original tenant.
Three of these problems then explained the single tenant capabilities of the system and how the
system did not support diﬀerent requirements for diﬀerent tenants. All of these problems made
the system very unconﬁgurable and inﬂexible.
After all of the problems had been identiﬁed we went on to research possible methods that
could be used to solve these problems.
Many methods discussed how the system could be
designed to allow more conﬁgurability and ﬂexibility. Furthermore we focused on designing a
115

system that could serve multiple tenants. After a few methods had been introduced we went
on to redesigning and implementing the system from scratch. While doing this we proposed a
categorization system which categorizes multi-tenant systems in terms of the variation between
the diﬀerent tenants of the system. Finally we presented a set of guidelines that future system
developers, designing a multi-tenant web application, can use to avoid the design ﬂaws that
were made while developing the original Operation Logger. These guidelines point out what
developers should have in mind when developing a web-application. These guidelines take into
consideration the likelihood of the system becoming multi-tenant in the future, as well as the
degree of variation of requirements between the system’s tenants.
In order to evaluate the system that was redesigned in this thesis we performed a performance
test. This performance test compared the new system developed with an old architecture that
replicates the architecture used in the original, single-tenant, Operation Logger. This perfor-
mance test showed that the new system’s architecture had some considerable drawbacks. These
drawbacks can, however, be reduced to some degree by using many known techniques that re-
duces the load on the server from client requests. We also provided a walkthrough of the system
in order to demonstrate its capabilities.
The guidelines presented in this thesis were created from the process of redesigning and re-
implementing the case study system. If these guidelines would have been available to the de-
signers of the system from the beginning the system would not have suﬀered the problems that
causes systems to be inﬂexible and unconﬁgurable. We can therefore conclude that if system
developers designing a web-application use these guidelines the system will not suﬀer from these
problems. The developers will therefore save some considerable time and resources in the long
run.

Future Work
The problem presented in Sect. 3.6 needs to be addressed in future work. Research has to be
made on the possible methods that can be used to solve this problem. The ﬁndings in that
research can then be used to add to the guidelines presented in this thesis.
A conﬁguration console for the tenants in a multi-tenant system needs to be developed. This
conﬁguration console allows the tenants of the system to conﬁgure themselves of the entities
created in the domain model to allow for varying features and ﬂexible attributes.
Further research needs to be in order to create a method that allows diﬀerent tenants of a multi-
tenant system to have varying features. In this thesis we presented a couple of methods based
on related works that can be used to allow a system to have varying features. However, these
methods were not as helpful as, for example, the methods used from related works to allow
entities to have diﬀerent attributes, such as the EAV approach.
Future work can research how it might be possible to implement some sort of tenant-feature
access control that has a speciﬁc expiration date. When the expiration date has passed the
tenant no longer has access to that feature.
The guidelines created in this thesis gives the developers an advantage on what they should
really concern themselves within the initial stages of web application system development on an
abstract level. These guidelines could however be extended to further specify what exactly the
system developers should consider in diﬀerent scenarios.
These guidelines could also be extended to diﬀerent types of system than web-application sys-
tems.
In this thesis we did not dive into other types of systems, and therefore we cannot
determine the usefulness of these guidelines for other types of systems.

The Operation Logger
The system that is used in this thesis as a case study system is called The Operation Logger.
In this thesis we will analyze its problems, and later on come up with ideas on how the system
might be ﬁxed. From this process we will come up with guidelines that can be used by future

Customer
A customer is deﬁned as an organization who pays for access to The Operation Logger system.
This is for example ICE-SAR, Almannavarnir or Landsnet.
Tenant
A tenant represents a customer in the system. A tenant has access to The Operation Logger and
has its own data collection and its own set of users. The tenants can have diﬀerent requirements
from one another in terms of feature requirements and data collection requirements.
User
A user is someone who accesses the system, both to view operations along with it’s relevant
information and to create new operations. A user in the system can have diﬀerent role privileges,
which provide diﬀerent access to features in The Operation Logger. The users are associated to
a particular tenant.
Operation
The Operation Logger consists of many operations. An operation is created by a user to deal with
an crisis in an organization such as ICE-SAR when a tourist is reported lost in the wilderness.
An operation is the top hierarchical object in the system and contains many incidents, tasks,
==PAGE:129==

groups, etc. An operation also contains one or more rounds with always one round marked as
active. When the system is accessed a list appears containing all operations in the system in
chronological order.
Incident
An incident is deﬁned as a detailed explanation of some occurrence in an operation in The
Operation Logger system. This can range from personnel arriving at certain place, ﬁndings
of clues or people and so on. Incidents are created by the users of the system. They can be
geotagged by either specifying coordinates when they are created or by updating them later on.
Task
A task is deﬁned as an assignment that needs to be completed within an operation in The
Operation Logger system. All the tasks have priorities from F1 to F3, where F1 has the highest
priority. A task also have a status of either, to-do, doing or done. A task can be assigned to a
group or many groups.
Group
Groups are deﬁned as a party of SAR personnel and operation managers, where the users of the
system can be assigned to a group. Each user can only be assigned to one group in the operation
in The Operation Logger system. New groups are created in every operation and are not deﬁned
in the system as a whole. The groups have a status of either ‘free’, ‘busy’, ‘on hold’ or ‘ﬁnished’.
Round
A round belongs to an operation and is deﬁned as a phase in the operation in The Operation
Logger system.
Operations always has one active round.
Initially that round is the ‘First
responce’ phase which is the default initial phase, followed by ‘Round 1’, ‘Round 2’, ‘Round
3’ and so on. Every round has a changeable starting datetime stamp and a changeable ending
datetime stamp. The starting datetime indicates when that particular round was started and
the ending datetime indicates when that round was ﬁnished. Every task, incident and group
that gets created is associated to the active round.

View

layout. There are many diﬀerent views in The Operation Logger. When the user has logged
onto the system he is greeted with a particular view called the main view. When the clicks on
a particular operation he then sees the Operation view. These views are discussed in detail in
