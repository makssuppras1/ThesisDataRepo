ABSTRACT
This research project studied the response of the OC3 Hywind spar type ﬂoating platform
undergoing Vortex Induced Vibrations (VIV) in cross ﬂow direction. The project was
carried was carried out in two phases. The ﬁrst phase included an experiment that was
conducted in the Advanced Fluid Mechanics Laboratory at the Korean Advanced Institute
of Science and Technology on a scaled version of the spar. The model was manufactured
with a scaling ratio = 1 : 180 and was constrained to one degree of freedom to ensure the
response obtained was due to VIV only. The peak displacement amplitude ratio was found
to be Y/D = 0.9 with a lock-in range from a reduced velocity of Ur = 5.18 to Ur = 10.3.
The second phase involved simulating a VIV response by adopting a coupled structure
and wake oscillator model. The experimental and numerically simulated responses were
compared and and the analysis of the comparison showed the experimental lock-in regime
to be underestimated which was attributed to manufacturing limitations. Other experi-
mental challenges were also identiﬁed. The simulation was then performed using the real
scale ﬂoating spar properties with model parameters at the equivalent Reynold’s number.
The response obtained was compared with a similar experiment on a scaled model of the
ﬂoating spar that was recently conducted. A 50% reduction was observed in the predicted
lock-in range. The deviation was attributed to inabilities and challenges in maintaining
equivalent Reynold’s number for model scale tests. The study concluded with perform-
ing a simulation by incorporating wave conditions. A simple model was chosen for this
purpose and the simulation was performed using structure parameters of the conducted
experiment in this thesis. Although, the vortex force magniﬁcation response was found
to be inconclusive, the structural response was found to predict displacement ampliﬁca-
tion when resonance was observed. The response obtained from the PSD of the structural
response appeared to support the ﬁndings.

INTRODUCTION
With the advent of renewable sources of energy, wind power is playing an increasingly
larger and decisive role in the production of green energy. According to the Wind Vision
Detailed Roadmap Actions [1] report by US Department of Energy (2015), wind power
is expected to contribute 20% of the nation’s total energy by 2030 and is expected to
increase to 35% by 2050. European countries such as Denmark draw approximately 44%
[2] of the country’s total energy through wind and have even larger projections for the
future. Other countries including India and South Korea are also showing similar trends
to become growingly independent of conventional sources of energy and rely more on
renewable sources of energy such as wind [3] [4].
To expedite and accomplish these visions, much attention has been given to the extraction
of offshore wind energy, especially from ﬂoating wind turbines. Whilst onshore wind
turbines are abundant and widespread, ﬂoating wind turbines have recently been concep-
tualized in the late 1900’s and early 2000’s [5]. The rationale behind this technology is the
unbroken ﬂow and higher velocity of offshore wind allow for greater and efﬁcient energy
generation. It is a well-established scientiﬁc fact that the power generated is proportional
to the cube of the wind velocity[6].
Notwithstanding the aforementioned advantage(s), offshore regions pose a greater chal-
lenge for energy production. There has only been one ﬂoating wind farm installed, Hy-
wind Scotland, which was commissioned in 2017. It remains to be the only ﬂoating wind
farm installed until today. Deep offshore waters are often characterized by their extreme
and hazardous nature presenting a variety of instability conditions to the ﬂoating plat-
forms.
This study focuses speciﬁcally on the effects of one such instability called Vortex Induced
Vibration (VIV). VIV is a phenomenon that occurs when a ﬂuid ﬂows past an object
and results in an instability (vibration) due to pressure differences at the interface of the
ﬂuid and the object. These pressure differences are induced by vortices in the wake of
the object. The resulting instability is a transverse motion which is perpendicular to the
direction of ﬂow of the ﬂuid [7].
The dynamics of VIV is of particular importance where slender structures structures are
concerned such as deep sea risers, chimneys, high mast poles, suspended cables used on
bridges, which may be either rigid or elastic. The observation of vortex shedding was
found to occur as early as in the 1870’s [8] and has lead to wide-ranging research in
this ﬁeld. Thorough reviews on these aspects have been provided by Bearman [7] and
Parkinson [9]. Despite the extensive research that has been carried out in this ﬁeld, the
modelling of VIV is yet empirical and descriptive in nature [8].
In this thesis, the response of the OC3 Hywind ﬂoating spar type platform that is con-
strained to one degree of freedom and undergoing VIV is studied. The fundamentals and
basics of VIV along with the key characteristics that have been observed for this phe-
nomenon have been outlined in Section 2 . Correspondingly, the various VIV modelling

methods that have been explored along with the method adopted and applied in this thesis
has been outlined in Section 3. Section 4 describes the experiment that has been carried
out along with the analysis and interpretation of the results obtained.
In Section 5, a simulation using the adopted model for VIV is veriﬁed with the analyti-
cal solution as provided with the authors. Subsequently a further analysis is carried for
the deeper understanding of this modelling method. The generated simulation is then ap-
plied using model parameters from the conducted experiment. The results for both cases
are compared and any discrepancies arising thereof are subsequently explained. Further-
more, the model parameters are artiﬁcially varied to observe variation in responses and
understand the inﬂuence of those model parameters.
The numerical simulation is then carried out using the real scale OC3 Hywind parameters
and the responses are compared with data from similar previous experimental studies
which is outlined in Section 6. Any discrepancies arising in the results are explained. A
ﬁnal examination is carried out by incorporating wave conditions. Due to the complexities
involved in modelling of VIV, the simulation considering wave conditions was performed
on the scaled version of the spar as used in this experiment using a simplistic approach.
The conclusion follow subsequently in Section 7.

LITERATURE REVIEW ON VIV
2.1
VIV and Vortex Shedding Frequency
Vortex shedding is a phenomenon that occurs when an object is placed in the domain of
a moving ﬂuid. A system, as described in Section 3.1, is considered where a cylinder
is represented linear mass spring system placed in the domain of a moving ﬂuid and
constrained to one degree of freedom. As the ﬂuid ﬂows past the object, a periodic pattern
of alternating vortices are created in the wake on either side of the object. This alternating
pattern of vortices, known as the Karman vortex street [10], occurs as a result of separation
of the ﬂuid at the boundary layer. The periodic creation of vortices is known as vortex
shedding.
Extensive research has been carried out to explain the mechanism involved in vortex shed-
ding. Bearman [7] in his review states that the formation of a vortex-street wake is sig-
niﬁcantly dependent on the mutual interaction between the two separating shear layers in
the wake of the body. As the vortex continues to grow, it is fed by recirculation from its
connected shear layer, until it is strong enough to draw the opposing shear layer across
the near wake. The approach of oppositely signed vorticity, in sufﬁcient concentration
cuts off further supply of circulation to the growing vortex which is then shed and moves
downstream.
Figure 1: Filament-line sketch of the vortex formation region. Arrows showing reverse ﬂow (c) and
entrainment (a) and (b). (Figure taken from [11])
The entrained ﬂuid (a) is engulfed into the growing vortex while (b) ﬁnds its way into
the developing shear layer. The near wake region between the base of the body and the
growing vortex oscillates in size and some further ﬂuid (c) is temporarily entrained into
it. Entrained ﬂow (a) which contains some ﬂuid with oppositely signed vorticity to that
in the growing vortex is the largest of the three ﬂows. The size of the formation region
as described by Gerrard [11] is determined by the balance between entrainment into the
shear layer and the replenishing of the ﬂuid by the induced reversed ﬂow.

Bearman [7] in his review also states that at low Reynold’s number, when separation ﬁrst
occurs, the ﬂow around the bluff body remains stable, but as the Reynold’s number is in-
creased a critical value is reached beyond which instabilities develop. These instabilities
can lead to organized unsteady wake motion, disorganized motion, or a combination of
both. Vortex shedding and general wake turbulence induce ﬂuctuating pressures on the
surface of the generating bluff body, and if the body is ﬂexible, this can lead to oscilla-
tions. A comprehensive study on the dynamics of vortex shedding for different Reynold’s
number ﬂow regimes may be found in [12].
Oscillations excited by vortex shedding are usually in a direction normal to that of the
free stream ﬂow and the frequency of these ﬂuctuating pressures or forces is determined
by the vortex shedding frequency [7]. The vortex shedding frequency for a cylinder is in
turn related to a dimensionless parameter called the Strouhal number which is deﬁned as
follows.

study where the mass damping parameter was m∗ξ = 0.36. The compared response has
been illustrated in Figure 5.

branch region and upper branch to the lower branch.
Feng’s response of high mass damping was found to be hysteretic in nature and displayed
only two branches of response namely: the initial excitation regime, and the lower branch.

3
MODELLING OF VIV
As can be seen from the previous section, vortex induced vibration is a critical instability
that affects systems especially with low mass damped system. Furthermore, the effects of
VIV presented in the previous section have do not include the wake dynamics of the ﬂuid
ﬂow ﬁeld. A full and thorough analysis of VIV would require solving the Navier Stokes
equation with the moving structure as boundary conditions [27].
For such reason, several coupled wake and structure oscillator models were explored
which provided a useful alternative in helping understand such phenomena. The term
”coupled” implies that that the motion of the structure affects the wake dynamics and lift
forces. Conversely, the wake dynamics and lift forces affect the motion of the cylinder,
leading to a coupled system [14]. As seen in the previous section 2, the dimension-
less oscillating frequency f ∗presented itself depending on the mass and damping ratios.
Likewise, the extent of lock-in was also dependent on the mass ratio indicating a coupled
relation between the vortex and structure dynamics.
Coupled wake and structure oscillators however can be used to predict the response of
the ﬂuid-elastic system which may then be applied to the 3D version with all degrees of
freedom. Hence, these models are often referred to as phenomenological [14] [28]. In
the following section, the governing equations for the ﬂuid structure system are described
following which a brief overview of the nature and types coupling has been provided. In
anticipation of what follows in this thesis, it is critical to mention here that equations have
been formulated following the model of Fachinnetti et al. [28].

3.2
Overview of Coupled Wake And Structure Oscillators Models
The three basic types of couplings that have been explored are: displacement based cou-
pling, velocity based coupling, and acceleration based coupling [28]. The displacement
coupled model reads f = Ay, velocity coupled model reads f = A˙y and the acceleration
coupled model reads f = A¨y. The parameter A is a constant that has been mentioned in
the next section.
Krenk and Nielson [27] proposed a displacement coupling model ( f = Ay) for represent-
ing VIV which was based on the energy transfer or energy balance between the ﬂuid to
the structure oscillator. In this model, the authors assumed that the lift coefﬁcient q to be
associated with the transverse motion of a representative ﬂuid mass ma. The ﬂuid mo-
tion was modeled as an oscillator with variable displacement where self excitation was
represented by means of negative damping.
Several velocity based couplings (f = A˙y) have been previously proposed for which Gab-
bai and Benoraya [14] provided a comprehensive review overview. One of the explored
models was a van der Pol soft nonlinear oscillator where the lift force that was linearly
coupled to the cylinder velocity. However, the author states that model parameters used
in the equations were not related to any physical parameters of the system. The authors
provided a review on another velocity coupled model that had been explored by using
a modiﬁed van der Pol equation. The model parameters for the coupled equations were
dependent on experimental data and stated to be “an inherent ﬂaw” in the model. Landl
[29] also explored the velocity based coupled model by adding a non-linear aerodynamic
term of the 5th order to the wake oscillator.
Fachinnetti et al. [28] proposed a linear inertial or acceleration based coupling (f = A¨y)
of the structure on the ﬂuid. The authors considered a simple model where the ﬂuid
structure coupling terms on the right-hand side of (18) were limited to be linear functions

3.4
Summary of Salient Features of the Acceleration Coupling Model
The authors found the acceleration coupling model to successfully capture all the features
of VIV as have been described in section 2 which include: the oscillation amplitude and
lift force ampliﬁcation; the basic condition of resonance which is Ur = 1/St; the phase
shift between structure and wake during lock-in; the trend of peak amplitudes as a function
of SG numbers; and the unbounded lock-in for m∗< mc = 0.54.
At low SG numbers, the displacement and velocity couplings did not succeed in describ-
ing the main phenomena of VIV. Conversely, the acceleration coupling model at low SG
numbers accurately predicted the dimensionless oscillating frequency f ∗= ω/δ and was
found to be quantitatively consistent with experimental results. The remaining features
were found to be qualitatively consistent with experimental results.

5.1.5
Further Analysis of the Coupled Oscillators
An attempt is made here to explain the frequency response curve for the sake of clar-
ity. The frequency curve in Figure (16) is the key feature that predicts the lock-in phe-
nomenon. Equation (13) is recalled where the non-dimensionalization parameter is the
vortex shedding frequency Ωf . Prior to lock-in, the vortex shedding angular frequency
follows the Strouhal relationship and hence, the angular frequency response obtained is
f = Ωf leading to the common angular frequency ω = f/Ωf = Ωf /Ωf = 1.
In the lock-in regime, the vortex shedding angular frequency synchronizes with natural
angular frequency of the system. Hence, the frequency response f = Ωs = constant lead-
ing to ω = f/Ωf . Therefore, as Ωf increases with increasing velocity whereas f remains
constant, which leads to a decreasing ω. (It is recalled here the Ωs is constant since this
is a high mass ratio system).
43
==PAGE:52==
At lock-out, the vortex shedding angular frequency follows the Strouhal law again lead-
ing to ω = Ωf /Ωf = 1. The resulting dimensionless oscillation angular frequency f ∗=
f/Ωs = ωΩf /Ωs = ω/δ (equation (15) is recalled where δ = Ωs/Ωf ). A comparison of
the dimensional and angular oscillating frequency ω has been provided in Figure 40 to
provide an easier comparison similar to responses as shown in literature. (The initial rise
in ω is interpreted as an inherent property of this coupling model).

A further analysis was carried out for better understanding of this coupling model. For
simplicity, the damping and non-linear terms of equation (18) were neglected. The solu-
tion of the eigen-frequencies resulting from the coupled equations from equation (18) was
found to yield two responses, one which was the structure frequency and the other was
the vortex shedding frequency. Both frequencies were found to yield real and imaginary
components ωR and ωI respectively which is explained as follows.
In the lock-in regime, the real components of vortex shedding angular frequency Ωf and
structural angular frequency Ωs collapsed in to a singular value. An imaginary compo-
nent developed for both the oscillators in the corresponding region which is not an actual
oscillating frequency but relates to the amplitude exponent. A resulting ampliﬁcation in
the structural oscillation amplitude and wake variable amplitude was thus found in the
same region. The higher the amplitude exponent, the higher is structural displacement
amplitude and wake variable amplitude.
Prior to lock-in or at lock-out, the amplitude exponent dies out or does not exist as there

Comparison of Simulated Response with Previous Studies
A penultimate set of simulations was performed by considering structure parameters of

compared with the results as obtained by Carlson and Sadeghi [26]) and mentioned in
Section 2.5. (The comparison was made with Carlson and Sadeghi due to the increase in
mass ratio in the current experiment. This challenge was found to not arise for the authors
and hence their response was considered for a better comparison).
Concerning the model parameters, CL0 and CD are known to be a function of the Reynold’s
number and these parameters were found to have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the structure
response and lock-in regime. The Reynold’s number using equation (26) for the OC3
ﬂoating spar considering an average ﬂuid ﬂow velocity U = 0.2 m/s and diameter D =
9.4m was found to be Re = 1.8 × 106. Norberg [37] compiled a list of the reference
coefﬁcients of lift for high Reynold’s numbers up to Re < 106. Coefﬁcient’s of lift higher
than this value were not found and hence CL0 = 0.1 was used. The drag coefﬁcient CD =
0.6 was obtained from Figure 42 leading to γ = 0.3.
Concerning the structure parameters of the OC3 ﬂoating spar, the system mass including
the nacelle, hub, rotor and tower was calculated to be ms = 8.06×106 kgs. The submerged
depth of the ﬂoating spar was z = 120 m below sea level. The ﬂuid density ρ = 1000.
The resulting mass ratio for the ﬂoating spar was calculated to be µ = 1.55 such that
m∗= 0.97. The damping coefﬁcient provided was cs = 105.
The total restoring force was accounted for by considering two springs, each with a spring
stiffness of k = 41180 N/m attached at an angle of 30◦to the direction of motion such that
the total restoring force kTOT = 2×41180×cos(π/6) = 7.13×104 N/m. The natural fre-
quency of the system was thus calculated to be fs = 0.0105 [Hz] with a resulting damping
ratio of ξ = 0.046. The Strouhal number was chosen using the deﬁnition of the universal
Strouhal number St
′ = 0.16.

Sadeghi [26] as described in Section 2.5 was calculated to be Re ∼4000. (As a side
note, the diameter of spar in their experiment was D = 20mm whereas the breadth of
the water tunnel test section was B = 50cm. The resulting blockage ratio was calculated

Simulation Considering Wave Conditions
A ﬁnal simulation was carried out to observe the dynamical response considering wave
conditions. Incorporating the wave conditions presented a signiﬁcant challenge in pre-
dicting the dynamical response of the structure. Referring to Figure 15, the wave velocity
in reality is a function of U = f(z,t). This implied, at a given time t = t0, the wave speed
along the length of the spar z is not constant U = f(z,t0). This leads to a varying value of
fvs (equation 1) and therefore Ωf along the length of the spar.
The governing equations for the coupled wake and structure oscillators using equation
(18) would thus need to be solved in a sectional manner and subsequently forces be in-
tegrated over the length of the spar. This presented a huge computational task. Whilst
this was attempted, the interpretation and analysis of the results obtained were found to
be inconclusive.
Considering the above, some assumptions were made and a simpler model was approached.
The simulation was carried out using the scaled version of the spar with the model pa-
rameters used in this experiment for a time frame of 600 seconds. The irregular wave
conditions considered were such that they were constant over the length of the spar z,
however were allowed to vary in magnitude and direction in time.
The JONSWAP spectrum was used to simulate the wave conditions. The peak height
Hs = 0.35m and peak period TP = 10s for wave conditions were obtained by trial and error
such that resulting reduced velocity range spanned from Ur = 0 to Ur = 5 . (Generation
of irregular waves was part of one of the courses over two years ago. Hence it was based
on trial and error). The wave velocity occurring at the sea water level was selected from

CONCLUSION
An experimental study and numerical simulation has been carried out to examine VIV on
a scaled version of the OC3 Hywind spar type ﬂoating platform constrained to one degree
of freedom. The experimental study has been carried out in unidirectional uniform ﬂow
whereas the simulation was carried out for uniform ﬂow and wave conditions. The char-
acteristics for a low mass structures undergoing VIV which such as the different regimes
of responses and mode transition were observed. The results from the experimental study
yielded a peak displacement amplitude of yM = Ymax/D = 0.9 and were in accordance
with historical studies carried out in this ﬁeld. The lock-in regime was found to be from a
reduced velocity Ur = 5.18 to Ur = 10.03.
The numerical simulation on VIV was performed by adopting a coupled wake and struc-
ture oscillator in accordance with the proposed model of Facchinetti et al [28]. The simu-
lation was veriﬁed with the analytical solution and the responses obtained were found to
be comparable. The simulation was subsequently carried out by considering model pa-
rameters used in the current experiment and the responses obtained were compared with
experimental results. The comparison showed that the experimental lock-in regime was
found to be underestimated which was consequence of overestimation of the system mass
and was attributed to manufacturing limitations. Other experimental challenges such as
blockage effect were identiﬁed. The features of the coupling model were analyzed and the
model parameters were varied to understand the inﬂuence of these parameters in relation
to the displacement response and predicted lock-in regime.
A simulation was subsequently carried out using the OC3 Hywind system parameters
which are mentioned in Jonkman [32]. The simulation assumes unidirectional uniform
ﬂow and taking into account the appropriate reference conditions for CL0 and CD at the
Reynold’s number for the actual spar. The responses obtained were compared with a
recently conducted similar study by Carlson and Sadeghi [26]. The simulated structural
response was found to approximately 50% lower as was the lock-in regime. The deviation
in the responses were attributed to the Reynold’s number mismatch for model scale test
and real scale conditions. The maintenance of Reynold’s number similitude for model
scale testing therefore proves to be a signiﬁcant factor that needs to be incorporated for
such tests. The range of ﬂow velocities for structural excitation were found to be from
U = 0.49m/s to U = 0.89m/s.
A ﬁnal consideration was made for the response of VIV by incorporating wave conditions.
Due to the complexities and computational task involved for such conditions, a simple
model of wave conditions was adopted such that the wave velocity over the length of the
test cylinder is remained uniform, however, was allowed to vary in time, magnitude and
direction. The structure parameters were those for the cylinder used in this experiment.
Since a physical experiment with wave conditions could not be performed, the veriﬁcation
of the simulation is limited to analysis of the response obtained. Although, the vortex
force magniﬁcation response was found to be inconclusive, the structural response was
found to predict displacement ampliﬁcation when resonance was observed. The response
obtained from the PSD of the structural response appeared to support the ﬁndings.

x = x’;
