Summary
During the past decades microalgae is being widely exploited by industries due
to its potentiality to make use of solar energy and ﬁx CO2 to induce the growth
of biomass, thus producing carbohydrates , lipids and proteins in a much larger
scale compared to terrestrial plants [1, 2]. From the many valuable compounds
produced by microalgae such as proteins, lipids, pigments ; there is a huge
demand for the use of natural pigments obtained from microalgae by many
industries in order to use it as natural colourants in their products. In this
study the pink ﬂuorescent coloured pigment phycoerythrin is extracted from
Rhodomonas baltica. The method used in this experiment is adopted from the
article, A simple and fast method for extraction and quantiﬁcation of crypto-
phyte phycoerythrin [3]. Phycoerythrin was extracted from Freeze dried samples
and wet/ alive samples. The biomass of freeze dried samples were extracted by
two types of centrifugation, one - time centrifugation (denoted as Sample A)
and more than - one time centrifugation (denoted as Sample B). The freeze
dried samples were stored at two diﬀerent temperatures +4°C and -20°C for a
period of 36 days. The wet sample was harvested two times from the cultivation
photobioreactors.
The main aims of this study was to determine the eﬀect of number of centrifu-
gation and storage time for the extraction of phycoerythrin from freeze dried
Sample A and Sample B, the eﬀect of buﬀer volume and storage temperature for
the extraction of phycoerythrin from wet/ alive algal biomass and comparison
of the method I followed with 6 diﬀerent methods explained in the report for
the improvement of the method followed in this study.
Freeze dried sample A and B were treated with 3ml buﬀer solution stored at
+4°C decreased after 36 days of storage period.
But both sample A and B
stored at -20°C resulted with 0.012 mg PE/mg biomass after a storage period
of 36 days. The wet/ alive algal biomass when treated with 3ml and 5ml buﬀer
resulted with same PE value, 0.0076 mg PE/ml biomass.

Introduction
1.1
Introduction and Aims
Microalgae are eukaryotic, unicellular, photosynthetic and one of the oldest life
forms on earth. Prokaryotic cyanobacteria are also included under microalgae.
It is capable of producing chemical energy carriers such as carbohydrates, lipids
and proteins by utilization of sunlight, carbon sources such as sugar, CO2 and
minerals such as N, P and K. Since microalgae can be cultivated in large sun -
rich drylands or areas that are not capable of vegetation growth, it is regarded
as the most sustainable and promising biological resource for the production
of biomaterials, biochemical and biofuels [9, 10, 2]. The study of microalgae
dates back from the 1950s. During these years, there was constant modiﬁcation
of bioreactors used of for the cultivation of microalgae. Due to the alterations
and invention of eﬃcient bioreactors it was successful to cultivate four types of
microalgae in an industrial scale, which include Spirulina (cyanobacterium) ,
Chlorella (green algae) , Dunaliella spp and Haematococcus pluvilais. Chlorella
and Spirulina are used as nutraceuticals and as food additive whereas the strain
Dunaliella spp is used for the production of β carotene and Haematococcus
pluvialis for the extraction of astaxanthin.
There are many other strains of
microalgae which are capable of producing the above mentioned biological com-
pounds, but not at an industrial level scale of production. Some of the new
strains which are produced now in a large scale are Euglena and Porphyridium,

which are mainly used as food supplements and in cosmetics [10, 11].
Over the past decades there is an increasing demand of usage of colouring com-
pounds obtained from microalgae in food, cosmetics, detergent and molecular
genetics industries. Among which, the pigmentcobiliproteins (PBPs) is gaining
more demand- in industry [12]. PBPs are brilliantly coloured proteins found in
the chloroplasts of red algae and cyanobacteria. There are four types of PBPs,
phycoerythrins, phycocyanins, phycoerythrocyanin and allophycocyanins. Phy-
coerythrins are abundantly found in red algae and is a highly ﬂuorescent pink
colour pigment. This uniqueness of this ﬂorescent colour, renders it in high de-
mand in industry [13, 12, 14].It is widely used for the development of ﬂuorescent
probes, as a natural colorants in food, cosmetics and as a photo sensitizer in
tumour photodynamic therapies. The commercial price of PE depends on the
purity ratio (Amax / A280), and can cost up to 50 USD/mg provided that the
purity ratio of Amax / A280 is higher than 4.0 [15].
The rigidity of the cell wall is a bottleneck for the recovery of proteins from
microalgae. Initial cell disruption method allows the eﬃcient extraction of the
proteins. Bead milling, ultrasonication, microwave radiation, enzymatic treat-
ment, cell homogenizer and high pressure cell disruption are some of the dis-
ruption techniques employed for the extraction step process. Additionally, the
eﬃciency of these methods also dpends on the cell wall of the species [16].
In this study we have used Rhodomonas Baltica a crytomonad, which has no
deﬁnite cell wall, instead a fragile rectangular plates of periplast are found under
the plasma membrane of these cells. Often the cell disruption of PE involves
usage of unnecessary chemicals, and many types of equipment which are costly
and time consuming [3]. For this reason, this study elected to employ a method
which involves the use of few chemicals and laboratory equipment for the ex-
traction of PE from Cryptomonad.
Thus, the main aim of this study is to
compare and evaluate the PE content obtained from Rhodomonas baltica. This
was achieved by processing the obtained biomass, using two diﬀerent methods
of centrifugation. Firstly, the obtained samples were labelled Sample A and
Sample B. Secondly, the PE content from the live (wet biomass) was analysed.
Finally, the inﬂuence of storage time on both the freeze dried and wet sample
was also evaluated.

motile green algae. Astaxanthin is currently being produced on an industrial
scale, but the usage of solvents, treatments with enzymes and homogenization
under high pressure at cryogenic temperatures reduce the eﬃciency of extrac-
tion of Astaxanthin. To remedy this, a method was developed without the usage
of the homogenization process in an attempt to extract the astaxanthin in an

1.2
Theoretical background
1.2.1
Phycobiliproteins
Phycobiliproteins (PBPs) are water soluble, highly ﬂorescent disc - shaped pro-
teins found in cyanobacteria, rhodophytes , cryptomonads and cyanelles [2, 18].
It traps solar energy in the visible spectrum in the wavelength range between
495nm and 650nm and transports it to chlorophyll a in the photosynthetic sys-
tem [19]. Inside the cells PBPs are found as aggregates in protein microbodies
which are attached to thylakoids called phycobilisomes (PBSs). These protein
microbodies or structures called phycobilisomes (PBSs) help the phycobilipro-
teins (PBPs) to arrange in a geometrical pattern thus enabling maximum light
capture and energy transfer in the photosynthetic system [2, 20, 21] . On the
basis of the size and shape viewed in electron microscope, Elizebeth Gantt and
S.F .Conti ﬁrst coined the term phycobilisomes [22]. These PBPs are situated
at the outer region of the inner thylakoid membrane. Electron micrographs re-
vealed that these structures are aligned in a regular pattern in the thylakoid
membranes of cyanobacteria and red algae and have a size that is two to three
times larger than ribosomes [23]. Phycobilisomes have a hemidiscoidal shape.
There are three cylinders in the center of this hemidiscoidal phycobilisome and
that gives a triangular outline which is termed as a core. Six cylinders hav-
ing same diameter are located around the triangular outline. Theses cylinders
which are called peripheral rods are packed with disc-shaped proteins called
phycobiliproteins (PBPs) (ﬁgure 1.1) [24].

PBPs are made up of 2 non–identical polypeptide subunits, α and β in the ratio
of 1:1. In addition to these two polypeptides, there is the γ polypeptide found in
R-phycoerythrin and B-phycoerythrin. α β monomers are the building block of
PBPs and they are made of equal molar concentrations. They aggregate in the
form of trimer (α β)3 and hexamer (α β)6. Trimer (αβ)3 contains 3 identical αβ
monomers arranged in a 3-fold symmetry axis while hexamer (αβ)6 contains 2
trimers arranged in a face to face manner. Both trimer and hexamer discs have
a central cavity [2, 19, 25]. In addition to these proteins, PBPs also contains
linker polypeptides, which helps to stabilize the whole phycobilisome structure
by assembling or connecting the PBP discs (rods) to the PBP core. It connects
the rod and core by passing through the central cavity in the discs and is denoted
as follows:
Linker polypeptide = LXY
where,
• L – linker polypeptide,
• X – position of linker polypeptide in PBS. It can be at rod (R) or core
(C) or rod-core junction (RC) or core- membrane junction (CM),
• Y – Mass of the linker polypeptide in kDa.

These disc shaped proteins are covalently attached to open chain tetrapyrroles
called phycobilins which are also referred to as chromophores. Phycobilins or
chromophores are attached to phycobiliproteins (PBPs) via one or two cys-

each PBP. Additionally, PBPs found in Cryptomonads contain peculiar types
of chromophores. Thus, all these factors tend to classify PBPs according to the
diﬀerent numbers, combinations and positions of the chromophores [19, 25].
1.2.2
Types of Phycobiliproteins
As discussed before the rods of PBSs containing PBPs are comprised of 4 dif-
ferent types of PBPs in cyanobacteria and red algae. They are phycoerythrin,
phycoerythrocyanin and phycocyanin. All of these 3 PBPs exists as hexamers
(α β)6 . Meanwhile the core of PBS is composed of the PBP, allophycocyanin
which exists as trimer (α β)3 [26]. It can be diﬀerentiated as follows:
1. Allophycocyanin (APC) – Allophycocyanin is positioned at the core in
PBS. It exists in the form of trimer (α β)3 in combination with 6 PCB
(phycocyanobilin) chromophore. The absorption maxima is 652nm with
the highest peak at 625nm. It is bluish green in colour and has a molecular
weight of 100 kDa [2, 27, 13].
2. Phycocyanin (PC) – It can be found in the middle of rod in PBS. It
exists in the form of trimer (α β)3 and hexamer (α β)6 and is arranged

1.2.4
Rhodomonas baltica
Rhodomonas baltica also called Rhodomonas baltica Karsten, 1898, is a marine
microalgae. It belongs to the Phylum Cryptophyta, family Pyrenomonadaceae
under the genus Rhodomonas. It is geographically distributed in North and
Baltic seas. It lives between a temperature 5-10°C, in waters with salinity 10 –
15 PSS and within 0 – 10 m depth from the surface of the water [32].
1.2.5
Early methods for extraction of Phycoerythrin

Chapter 4
Conclusion
The PE values of Sample A, which was centrifuged one time, showed a slow
decrease of phycoerythrin value after 36 days of storage time at +4°C. After
a storage period of 8 days the value decreased from 0.016 to 0.014 mg PE/mg
biomass. Again after a storage period of 28 days the value decreased from 0.014
to 0.012 mg PE/mg biomass. The parallels showed close values for each analysis.
The same sample stored at -20°C when analyzed after 36 days of storage period
resulted with 0.012 mg PE/mg biomass as well.
In the case of the freeze dried Sample B, which was centrifuged several times,
stored at +4°C the PE value obtained after a storage period of 8 days decreased
from 0.026 to 0.019 mg PE/mg biomass. The PE value after the storage period
of 28 days was not able to analyze due to the unavailability of the sample. PE
value for the sample after a storage period of 36 days stored at -20°C was 0.012
mg PE/mg biomass.
Since there was unavailability of the sample stored at
+4°C on 36th day for the analysis, it is not possible to conclude that if there
is any signiﬁcance of the storage time. However the PE value is slightly higher
(0.02 mg PE/mg biomass) than Sample A which shows that there might be an
inﬂuence of several time centrifugation of the sample during harvest of the algal
biomass (before freeze dying). The PE value for the sample stored at -20°C
after a storage period of 36 day is 0.012 mg PE/mg biomass.
Although both the samples showed a similar result after 36 days of storage

period at-20°C, it cannot be concluded that whether the number of times of
centrifugation have any inﬂuence on the extraction of phycoerythrin from freeze

On the other hand, in the case of wet/alive sample, there was not much extrac-

When comparing to the method I used for extraction of phycoeythrin to the
diﬀerent methods discussed earlier, it seems that the method I followed is very
simple and easy to be done in a laboratory using basic equipment. But when
comparing in terms of puriﬁcation and diﬀerentiation between diﬀerent types of
PE, it is a weak method. To make this method a step better, addition of chemical
detergent such as Triton X-100 could be added along with the buﬀer while
extraction. This could increase the eﬃciency of cell disruption [8]. Analyzing
the ﬂuorescence is also an eﬃcient way of conﬁrming the PE content from the
microalgae because apart from the experiment described in the ﬁrst article [8],
the rest of the experiments have all done Fluorescence Spectrophotometry.
Although the various chromatography methods can be used to increase the yeild
I think it is very costly to be applied.
More study on the cell wall structure of Rhodomonas baltica, addition of the
few more steps from the opinion I have expressed above to the method I followed
in this experiment and using more parallels could hypothetically increase the
PE value from Rhodomonas baltica.
