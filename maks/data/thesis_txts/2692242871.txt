Abstract
This thesis project aims to explore how supplier collaboration can be beneficial in
production planning. In the past decades, companies have become more connected,
thanks to new technologies and globalisation; hence, there is a great opportunity to
succeed through collaboration. Two strategies to enhance cooperation in the supply
chain network are studied and presented, outlining the benefits and challenges of their
implementation. Both techniques aim to reduce the total cost of the supply chain
by applying a negotiation-based approach between a supplier and a producer. The
foundation of the analysis lies in the development of optimisation models, the out-
come of which provides the input necessary to reach a mutual agreement that benefits
both parties. First, a negotiation-based collaborative planning approach is performed.
Second, the project explores another solution, the negotiated-based Vendor Managed
Inventory (negotiation-based VMI). Both strategies are based on an iterative process
where the two parties collaborate and negotiate to achieve a favourable arrangement
for the entire network. Afterwards, an experimental analysis is carried out, challeng-
ing the original setup with different data and providing more insights to the analysis.
This study expands the existing literature about supplier collaboration in produc-
tion planning, creating a bridge between mathematical modelling and collaborative
practices in supply chain.
The results of this thesis prove that supplier collaboration improves the economic ben-
efit of the supply chain, allowing a reduction of 4.40% in the total cost of the network
with the negotiation-based collaborative planning and of 4.29% with the negotiation-
based VMI. Moreover, additional advantages of creating a good collaboration and
engagement in the supply chain are presented.

1.1
Background
A supply chain is a global network of organisations that cooperates to improve the flow
of material and information between suppliers and customers at the lowest cost and
highest speed (Manish & Proth, 2002). As a consequence, supply chain management
is a ”systematic, strategic coordination of the traditional business functions within a
particular organisation and across businesses within the supply chain for the purpose
of enhancing long term organisational performance and the overall supply chain”
(Olugu & Wong, 2009).
Today’s supply chains are more global, and they have more and more stakeholders
involved, creating a vast network. Furthermore, thanks to the new technologies and
globalisation, companies are more connected and do not operate alone. That is why
they can benefit from a certain degree of collaboration, increasing the supply chain
visibility and improving the relationship between the stakeholders. Indeed, Olugu
and Wong (2009) state that the implementation of the supply chain is a long-term
process that involves proper analysis and investigation of the relationship between
the manufacturer and the suppliers. The sharing of information leads to shared goals
and improved methods that allow cost reduction and increase the firms’ competi-
tive advantage. Collaboration is the new area of value creation as companies are
increasingly looking beyond their individual enterprises to find ways to increase sales
revenues and profit margins (Ireland & Crum, 2005). One of the main aims of this
thesis is to explore how collaboration can be implemented in a specific supply chain

network and the factors that can affect its effectiveness.
Collaborative planning approaches are conceptualised in this thesis as a mutual re-
lationship between supplier and producer that can improve the supply chain perfor-
mance. Supplier-buyer relationships have been the subject of many research studies
in the past, and increasingly in the past three decades, companies have been estab-
lished collaborations with other supply chain partners. After the remarkable example
of Wal-Mart’s collaboration with upstream suppliers such as P&G, supply chain col-
laboration has become one of the common norms for many companies (Ramanathas
& Gunasekaran, 2012). Specifically, this application is an example of the so-called
Vendor Managed Inventory (VMI). With this supply chain partnership, the vendor
decides on the appropriate inventory levels of each of the products and the right inven-
tory policies to maintain those levels. Subsequently, many other leading companies
in various industries, such as GlaxoSmithKline, Electrolux Italia, Nestle, Tesco, etc.,
implemented VMI (Sari, 2007). This initiative is one of the most widely discussed
partnership activities that encourage collaboration and information sharing among
trading partners.
The case study analysed in this thesis project has been inspired by the supply chain
behind an insulin pen manufactured by Novo Nordisk. Novo Nordisk is a Danish
global healthcare company founded in 1923. Its purpose is to drive change to defeat
diabetes and other serious chronic diseases such as obesity, rare blood and rare en-
docrine diseases. The company produces the insulin pen in its production sites, and
it requires different raw materials coming from various suppliers. This supply chain
inspired this research, creating the basis of the study and helping the formulation of
the models.
Many studies have been conducted on how collaboration between supply chain part-
ners can be done and its benefits and limitations. This thesis expands the existing
literature on supply chain collaboration, collaborative planning and negotiation-based
configurations. The thesis’s novelty lies in comparing different collaboration strate-
gies with mathematical models that support the cooperation between trading partners

1.2
Objectives and Research Questions
The main purpose of this thesis is to explore different strategies of supplier collab-
oration in production planning to understand which one would be the best to be
implemented in the studied supply chain.
Collaboration within the supply chain
network is becoming a trend of value creation, as today’s companies are becoming
more global and have many stakeholders to manage. Hence, this thesis analyses two
different supplier collaboration strategies with the scope of examining the potential
benefits and challenges. Both strategies have the same target and expand the ex-
isting literature on this topic, creating a bridge between mathematical models and
negotiation processes. Specifically, the thesis seeks to answer the following research
questions:
• Can supplier collaboration in production planning lower the total cost of the
supply chain, and to what extent?
• When is supplier collaboration the most effective?
• What are the main benefits and challenges of supplier collaboration?
• Which strategy works the best in the studied supply chain?

CHAPTER 2
Literature review
The literature review is divided into three sections. First, the literature on Collab-
orative Planning is introduced, and different methods to establish and successfully
develop a supplier-producer alliance are presented. Second, the literature on the im-
portance of manufacturing configurations and inventory management on production
are discussed. Lastly, the Vendor Managed Inventory (VMI) strategy is presented
along with the benefits and challenges associated with this methodology
2.1
Collaborative Planning
Companies increasingly realize that focusing only on competitive relationships with
others makes it diﬀicult to maintain the market share. As pointed out by Panahifar
et al. (2015), modern companies are facing multiple challenges such as globalisation,
supply chain risk management, rapid development in technology, demand uncertain-
ties and the necessity to have a punctual delivery. One way to face these issues is to
use collaborative approaches where the parties involved in the supply chain are willing
to share relevant information and practices creating an integrated system. Specifi-
cally, collaborative planning is defined as a joint decision-making process for aligning
plans of individual supply chain members with the aim of achieving coordination in
light of information asymmetry (Günther & Meyr, 2009).
Petersen et al. (2005) identified eight collaborative planning processes that are poten-
tially effective for supply chain integration:
1. Supplier scheduling: it controls the order release, and communicate priorities,
needs and quantities between producer and supplier;
2. Forecasting and inventory positioning: it ensures that required items are
available at the proper time;
==PAGE:17==
2.1 Collaborative Planning
7
3. Inventory visibility: it provides the ability to track where a specific item is
located
4. Capacity planning: it ensures that the supplier has the adequate capacity to
produce the required quantity at the required lead-time;
5. Post-supplier selection performance evaluation/feedback and confor-
mance: it ensures a joint understanding of the supplier’s performance;
6. Joint goal/target setting: it provides mutually acceptable performance tar-
gets;
7. Part/material standardization: it reduces the number of unique parts in
the inventory system.
One of the most critical pillars of collaboration between suppliers and producer is
the quality of the information shared (Petersen et al., 2005). Nonetheless, this is a
necessary but not suﬀicient condition for a successful relationship. Indeed, before
even start the collaboration, a good level of trust must be ensured to guarantee a fair
and honest partnership. Therefore, creating each of the eight aforementioned types
of collaborative planning requires time as trust needs to be built.
2.1.1
Hierarchical planning system
Focusing on a supply link within a supply network consisting of a producer and a
supplier, Schneeweiss and Zimmer (2004) identified different types of coordination
mechanisms that could lead to different results. It has been considered a hierarchical
situation where the producer is upper-level, and the supplier represents the lower level.
Therefore, the producer is never fully informed of the supplier’s capacity, and the sup-
plier is not wholly aware of the manufacturer’s forecasts and operations. Accordingly,
producer and supplier have their production models where they do not collaborate.
From this starting point, there are four ways of coordination and integration of the
two models. The pure top-down hierarchy assumes that the supplier model is not
taken into account by the producer. In contrast, the ideal model fully integrates
==PAGE:18==
2.1 Collaborative Planning
8
the supplier’s model in the producer’s one. This last case represents the optimal con-
dition where the parties are considered a single decision-maker, and hence, they do
not have information asymmetry. To achieve the optimal situation, the two objective
functions are summed to have the lowest costs for the parties. These two situations
represent the two extremes of the collaboration that may arise between the parties.
More moderate types of integration are the non-reactive and reactive anticipa-
tion where the producer takes into account important features of the supplier to a
certain degree. The result of this study shows that the reactive approach is nearly as
good as the ideal model, as can be seen in Figure 2.1.

2.1.2
Non-hierarchical planning system
While the hierarchical scheme assumes a single, centralized planning task for syn-
chronizing operations across the supply chain, Dudek and Stadler (2004) developed a
non-hierarchical approach that can be used to synchronize different operations. This
study reflects the idea that collaborative planning helps to achieve better results in
terms of the total cost of the supply chain. It consists of different iterations where
a supplier and a producer propose modifications to the supply plan, establishing a
negotiation-based process among the partners. First, one party creates a supply plan,
==PAGE:19==
2.1 Collaborative Planning
9
and the other evaluates it, calculating its own cost and considering the total cost of
the supply chain. Second, a different supply plan is proposed accordingly, introducing
some modifications to decrease its own cost. The partner then carries out the same
process of analyzing the offer and presenting changes. It is clear that these modifi-
cations can be allowed to a specific limit; otherwise, each party will go back to its
original proposal. The negotiation scheme does not affect the total volume provided
to the buyer, and it continues until a reduction in the total cost of the supply chain
is noticed.
This model only works if there is a good flow of information between the parties as
well as a cooperative team behaviour of the parties (Dudek & Stadler, 2004). Sharing
information is a basic form of supply chain coordination, and supply chain partners
could significantly benefit from thorough and eﬀicient communication (Ren et al.,
2016).
2.1.3
Information sharing
Information is a key supply chain driver as it enables supply chain visibility, allowing
managers to make effective decisions, improving the supply chain’s performance. The
information allows the other supply chain parties to work together with the goal
of creating an integrated, coordinated network. To support effective supply chain
decisions, information must be accurate, must be accessible in a timely manner, must
be of the right kind and must be shared. (Chopra & Meindl, 2016).
In today’s world, companies do not operate alone, they have a specific network, and
therefore they can benefit from a qualitative information sharing process across the
supply chain. Lotfi et al. (2013) investigated some of the benefits that sharing informa-
tion could bring to industries. For instance, if information sharing is used eﬀiciently,
the manufacturers may reduce the inventory costs by 5 to 35 per cent.
Further-
more, when information is available, the visibility increases, causing a reduction of
the uncertainties and improved flexibility and adaptation to changes. Moreover, good
communication between partners leads to earlier detection of problems. The benefits

2.2
Manufacturing strategy
Manufacturing is a crucial process within a company that has a direct impact on the
corporate strategy (Skinner, 1969). Equally important is to set an inventory policy
aligned with the production system that allows the organization to maximize its profit.
To formulate production planning models, these two aspects have to be considered.
2.2.1
Production configuration
There are two types of production strategy: pull and push. In the pull-type model, the
production occurs when a process upstream in the supply chain demands a product
from the previous process. Toyota firstly introduced this model to eliminate waste in
production (Hall & Hall, 1983). Therefore, the production volume in each process is
determined by the upcoming process, and variance in the demand directly impacts pro-
duction, resulting in very low inventories of raw materials and semi-finished/finished
products. This system is controlled utilizing the so-called Kanban cards. Significant
is the paper elaborated by Karmarkar (1986) about Kanban systems that states that
==PAGE:22==
2.2 Manufacturing strategy
12
the number of Kanban cards constrains the upper limit on Work-in-Process (WIP).
The importance of variability reduction in pull systems is highlighted by Schonberger
(2008), who states that changes may lead to increments in the amount of time spent
on set‐ups and changeovers. As a consequence, productivity is reduced, resulting in
higher costs, longer lead times and late deliveries. Contrarily, a push-based produc-
tion system is driven by a production plan based on demand forecasts and production
scheduling (Stevenson, 2007). Hence, the material flows across all processes and final
products are stored following the initial plan, incurring increasing warehousing and
investment costs.
Factors such as cost, flexibility, speed, quality or reliability are highly considered when
designing the production layout (Garcia-Diaz & Smith, 2014). There are several con-
figurations for the assembly and packaging processes depending on the prioritization
of the factors aforementioned. As defined by Pawlewski (2018) ”a layout is the ar-
rangement of machines and equipment within a workspace that considers the associated
products, processes, and logistics as well as their relationships”. To select the best
configuration, several methods have been developed. For instance, the systematic
methodology elaborated by Muther (1973) identifies and analyzes different arranging
configurations based on the flow of material and relationships between processes. By
doing so, the cost and handling of material are optimized (Patil & Kuber, 2014).
2.2.2
Inventory management
The purpose of the inventory is to guarantee that the product demand in the following
process in the supply chain can be satisfied. Traditionally, the main goal of inventory
models is to minimize the costs associated with holding inventory, such as inventory
storage, insurance, transport, obsolescence, wasting and spoilage costs (Grzegorz,
2008). Moreover, the Economic Order Quantity (EOQ) is a well-spread methodology
based on a formula that determines the most optimal quantity to order per purchased
component. This method assumes that the demand is constant, that there are no
discounts in the price associated with the order size, and that lead time is independent
to order size (Muller, 2019). Furthermore, mathematicians like Park (1987) or Sweiss
==PAGE:23==
2.2 Manufacturing strategy
13
(1982) have developed in their papers algorithms that resemble more faithfully a real-
life case. By doing so, they have analyzed different ways to refine this formula to
match real-world situations.
Finally, ensuring an adequate safety stock is another relevant topic to take into ac-
count by a company when defining its inventory strategy. The use of safety stocks
serves the purpose of absorbing fluctuations in the supply or the demand to prevent
stock-outs that can lead to disruption in the production and unsatisfied demands
(Lancioni & Howard, 1987). Historically, several methods have been studied to set
the appropriate safety stock level for each specific case. Remarkably, Graves and
Willems (2000) developed an algorithm to analyze the supply chain and set a safety
stock level accordingly. Furthermore, to decide the best way to tackle the replen-
ishment lead times within a multi-stage inventory system, Lee and Billington (1992)
and Ettl (2000) develop and simulate nonlinear mathematical models whose objective
function is to minimize inventory costs in the supply chain.
2.2.3
Backlogs management
Another domain that helps to coordinate and optimize the supply chain is the manage-
ment of the backlogs. In some industries, as the case at hand, backlogs are permitted
to decrease the total cost of the supply chain. However, backlogs imply the unful-
fillment of the demand. Anderson and Morrice (2002) reflect upon the management
of backlogs in a make-to-order two-stage serial supply chain by developing a queuing
model to adapt the capacity level according to the level of backlogs. In his paper,
Chauhan and Proth (2005) develop a model in a two-stage supply chain, similar to
the case at hand, where the backlog costs are split between supplier and producer.
In this model, the part that caused the backlogs is responsible for paying the cost.
Hence, if the supplier delivers the amount agreed, but the demand is higher than
expected, the producer pays these costs to the consumer. Contrarily, if the supplier
is not able to fully satisfy the order placed by the producer, the possible backlogs
caused will be paid by the supplier to the producer. It is important to note that
these costs are a numerical representation of the loss of reputation that both parts
==PAGE:24==
2.3 Vendor Managed Inventory
14
would suffer if backlogs occur. An important countermeasure to mitigate the adverse
effects of backlogs is sharing information across the supply chain. The paper elabo-
rated by Lau et al. (2004) relates to the benefits of this praxis in different aspects of
a three-stage supply chain. Nonetheless, this topic has been addressed previously in
section 2.1.3.
2.3
Vendor Managed Inventory
Inventory is vital in a company as it reduces the uncertainty of supply and demand and
takes advantage of economy of scale. In today’s supply chains, customer orders and
demand change frequently, and therefore, having high inventory increases flexibility.
However, if the demand decreases to a great extent, keeping high inventory could
lead to considerable losses (Ivanov et al., 2017). One way to overcome this issue is to
coordinate the supply chain, guaranteeing a certain degree of transparency of demand
and inventory.
Vendor Managed Inventory (VMI) is one strategy for supply chain coordination based
on good collaboration between supplier and producer. Precisely, with the VMI, the
vendor controls the inventory at the buyer side (Ivanov et al., 2017). Indeed, in most
of the cases, the manufacturer, sometimes also a reseller or distributor, makes the
main inventory replenishment decisions for the consuming organizations (Waller et
al., 2001). According to Marquès et al. (2016), on the one hand, the buyer’s target
is to ensure a higher consumer service level with low inventory costs. On the other
hand, the supplier’s target is to reduce production, inventory and transportation costs.
Lastly, some shared objectives include tightening the different flows, speeding up the
supply chain, and reducing the bullwhip effect.
The main differences from a traditional supplier-customer push relationship to a pull
relationship are:
• There is not a purchase order from the customer’s medium-term processes but
only short-term information about the consumption of the inventory;
==PAGE:25==
2.3 Vendor Managed Inventory
15
• The supplier’s Material Requirement Planning (MRP) function no longer issues
a work order, only a target level for the producer’s inventory.
Marquès et al. (2016) argues that three processes define the VMI:
1. Partnering Agreement: it sets the whole collaboration process integrating the
partner’s planning processes into a VMI replenishment planning process.
2. Logistical Agreement: it sets the parameters that regulate the management
of the articles such as minimum/maximum inventory level, minimum delivery
quantity or transport schedule. Both the actors transmit their constraints. On
the one hand, the consumer has to ensure a minimum consumer service level,
minimizing the inventory holding costs. On the other hand, the supplier has to
take into account the production lead time and transport. Hence, they have to
agree on objectives and constraints mutually.
3. Production and Dispatch process: it monitors short-term pull decisions such as
production dispatch and transport.

2.3.1
Benefits of VMI
According to Ivanov et al. (2017), Waller et al. (2001) and Marquès et al. (2016),
there are multiple advantages in the adoption of the VMI both for the supplier and
the buyer.
For the supplier:
• Early recognition of fluctuation of demand that implies mitigation of demand
uncertainty as well as higher flexibility;
• Optimization of production planning and consolidation of the demand from
different buyers allowing larger quantities at one time and lower set-up costs;
• It forces disciplines because it implies a good measurement of all the parameters
and great communication;
• Better planning and resource use via visibility that allows a more eﬀicient pro-
duction;
• Higher replenishment frequency with smaller replenishment quantities that al-
lows a great inventory cost saving;
• Increase of the percentage of full truckload shipments.
For the buyer:
• Increase of inventory availability and decrease of inventory cost as well as the
safety-stock because of the reduced uncertainty;
• Reduction of procurement activities as there is no need to place purchase orders
and the order-issue costs are transferred to the supplier;
• Fewer stock-out with higher inventory turnover;
• Lower operating, purchasing and administrative costs;

• Greater customer satisfaction and increased sales.
For the supply chain:
• Optimization of the inventory management and cost reduction because there is
only one decision-maker and this simplifies the process;
• Improvement of financial planning;
• Supports long-term collaboration as in order for the VMI to work, a good level
of trust must be ensured between the parties. A good partnership helps the
parties to have successful processes.
2.3.2
Limitation of VMI
Successful implementation of the VMI often depends on the IT equipment of the
parties.
Software systems are essential because they facilitate such discussion as
replenishment quantity and timing, safety stock levels and transportation routing
(Waller et al., 2001).
Hence, the VMI implies high costs of implementation and
investment in IT.
The benefits of the VMI are mostly for the end-users and the buyer, while the sup-
plier does most of the work. Thus, the trust in the supply chain should be high,
and the objectives for both parties should be stipulated in the beginning to prevent
misunderstanding and to allow a smooth implementation of the VMI (Ivanov et al.,
2017).
Lastly, according to Sari (2007), one of the main reasons for failed VMI is the sharing
of outdated or inaccurate sales and inventory data due to a lack of adequate informa-
tion technology and mutual trust. Furthermore, there may be an inaccurate demand
forecast because, with the VMI, retailers are excluded from the demand forecasting
process.

3.1
Methodological Approach
One of the aims of the thesis is to improve a two-stage serial supply chain by applying
collaborative planning methodologies. By doing so, a mathematical model for both
parts has been developed. Hence, both qualitative and quantitative data are needed
to address the research questions and shape the supply chain. Qualitative data has
been primarily collected, meaning that it has been directly obtained from the primary
sources through interviews. Differently, quantitative data has been inspired by Novo

used methodology for studying optimisation problems within Operations Research.
3.1.1
Data Collection
Different methods have been carried out to gather relevant data depending on whether
it was qualitative or quantitative input.
3.1.1.1
Qualitative method
Several individual interviews were conducted to gather qualitative data about the
following topics: different collaborative planning methods applied in real-case supply
chains, types of configurations of the supply chain in different industries, best prac-
tices for optimising networks, contractual bonds and limits between parties and pro-

3.1.2
Model formulation
A mathematical model is a simplified numerical representation of a complex real-
world situation.
It is crucial to fully understand the data obtained previously to
develop a model according to the needs and faithfully represent the case. Integer
Linear Programming is the methodology employed to study the case at hand. More
specifically, the so-called Aggregate Production Planning (APP) is used, which aims
at determining the company’s production, inventory and resource employment levels
over a finite time horizon in order to meet the forecasted product demand in a cost-
effective manner (Nam & Logendran, 1992).
The model consists of an objective
function, a set of input data, several variables and constraints.
In this case, the
objective function for both models is to minimise the cost. The data are the values
obtained by using quantitative methods.
Differently, the variables are the values
provided by the solver once the model has been simulated. The constraints restrict
the possible values of the variables.

3.1.3
Model computation
After the formulation of the models, it is necessary to run the models in a solver to
obtain the optimal solutions for the objective function. In order to compute these
models, a virtual platform equipped with an ILP solver is needed, where the model
is coded.
For this purpose, Atom is the platform utilised to code the model and
solve it. Conveniently, the computational language selected to transcript the model
in the solver is Julia. Finally, Gurobi is the solver responsible for determining the
optimal solution. This solver applies an algorithm based on the so-called branching
strategy. This methodology works based on a decision tree where the algorithm looks
for the best solution of the discrete variables while keeping track of the best feasible
computed. The following strategies are some of the most utilised to define the order
in which each node should be explored (Ole Kröger, 2018):
• Most infeasible: It detects the solution which is less likely to be the optimal
• Pseudo cost: First, it records how all the variables impact the objective func-
tion. Then, it gives priority to the ones that improve the most the objective
function.
• Strong: It tests all the possible branches and only considers the one with the
best results.
The output obtained in the solver is not the optimal solution but the best result
obtained within a tolerance gap of 0.00000001%. The smallness of this number enables
the assumption that the solution can be accepted as the best one.

Collaborative Planning
This chapter presents the negotiation of a supply plan between a producer and a
supplier.
First, the production models of the two parties are described.
Second,
the fully integrated model is identified and presented, visualising the optimal supply
plan for both actors in the supply chain. Third, the negotiation process is described

5.1
Background
One of the primary purposes of the thesis is to investigate possible collaborative strate-
gies that can be used in the supply chain to achieve better results. After exploring
the literature about this topic, an interesting approach has been discovered, explored
and studied. This technique is based on a negotiation-like process between a producer
and a supplier, where the supply plan is first created by the producer and successively
adapted through a negotiation between the planners. Eventually, the planners will
commit to a compromise solution that will improve the total cost of the supply chain
(Dudek & Stadler, 2004). It should be noted that the original supply plan proposal
cannot be entirely changed, but some constraints must be respected, as it is described
in the following sections. Furthermore, in all the models, the components’ cost has
not been considered a cost for the producer nor a revenue for the supplier, as it does
not affect the negotiation process. It is due to the fact that the demand needs to
be fulfilled, and the price of the components sold by the supplier is not negotiable.
Hence, this is a constant value that does not influence the whole process.
==PAGE:36==
5.2 Non-collaborative approach
26
The negotiation process is based on different iterations where the supply plans are
changed and negotiated. In Section 5.2, the producer model is described together
with the supplier model. In this case, it has not been considered any collaboration,
and therefore, the producer runs its model to determine the quantity to order to the
supplier. These two models are considered the basis for the whole process, and they
will be adapted but never wholly distorted. Successively, a model where supplier and
producer are fully integrated is described. Here, the objective function is the sum
of the objective functions of the two individual models. In this model, the supplier
and the producer are considered as a single decision-maker, and hence, it is an ideal
situation. Afterwards, the actual negotiation is described where the supplier, after
evaluating the producer’s proposal, is allowed to deviate from the order quantities
by a certain percentage to decrease its costs.
The producer will then assess the
new plan and negotiate it accordingly. The process continues until the two parties
find a reasonable agreement. Moreover, it is worth mentioning that penalty costs
do not apply to the supplier when delivering late or early as the goal of the model
is to enhance collaboration between parties. This chapter will explore this strategy,
analysing the results and consequently discussing the conclusions.

the producer represents the upper level, and the supplier represents the lower level,
the producer is not fully informed about the operational capacity of the supplier
(Schneeweiss & Zimmer, 2004). Having this in mind, the producer model is run first,
obtaining the supply plan without considering the supplier’s capacity. After that, this
supply plan is shared with the supplier, which will calculate its costs with the strict
requirement of delivering each month what the producer ordered. In doing so, any
kind of collaboration is excluded, and a hierarchical approach has been considered.

5.8
Conclusions
This chapter presented a negotiation-based collaborative planning that aims to reduce
the total cost of the supply chain and clarify one of the main research questions of
this thesis, which is to conclude how the supply network can be improved with the
use of collaboration practices between partners. By using this approach, producer
and supplier are willing to deviate a certain amount from their respective optimal
solution in pursuance of reaching a supply plan that allows both parties to reduce
the costs. As it can be seen in Figure 5.3, the fully integrated and non-collaborative
model set the upper and lower bounds, respectively, while the results obtained in the
iterations lie within these limits.
Figure 5.3: Distribution of costs - Negotiation-based collaborative planning.
Satisfactory, the best solution, namely Iteration No.3, has a cost only 1.55% higher
==PAGE:56==

than the fully integrated model, which represents the ideal solution. However, this
methodology has a major pitfall, namely that in the best solution, the producer faces
a higher cost compared to the non-collaborative approach, although the total cost
of the supply chain is improved. Hence, there is a need to find a good cost-sharing
technique to ensure that both parties have some savings if they choose to pursue a
collaboration. Thereby, it is required to negotiate a contractual solution to mitigate
this and share the savings between the two parties.
To solve this problem, one solution could be that the expenses for the additional back-
logs are paid by the supplier and not by the producer. This could be an appropriate
approach considering that having a larger number of backlogs allows the supplier to
have flexible deliveries and save on capacity expansion cost. In doing so, the supplier
would still have a significant saving, and the producer would not have to pay more
than in the non-collaborative approach. Indeed, it can be seen a reduction of 0.53%
due to a less usage of extra capacity in Iteration No.3 than the non-collaborative
approach. Thereby, this solution enhances the fact that collaboration within the sup-
ply network allows different parties to work together negotiating different proposals
and thus, working towards a common goal (Dudek & Stadler, 2004). To implement

CHAPTER 6
Negotiation-based VMI
In this chapter, the Vendor Managed Inventory (VMI) technique is explored and
applied to the case study.
First, the supplier and producer model are explained,
highlighting the differences from the previous setup.
Second, a negotiation-based
approach is presented, emphasising the need for a strong collaboration to achieve
better results. Finally, a summary of the main findings is developed.
6.1
Background
After having analysed the effects of the negotiation-based collaborative planning in
Chapter 5, it is shown how collaboration between parties can have enormous advan-
tages for the supply chain in terms of costs but also in terms of relationship. Indeed,
having a good exchange of information creates a safe environment and eﬀiciency in
coordinating actions (Lofti et al., 2013). To further enhance this concept and explore
different strategies, a new technique, Vendor Managed Inventory (VMI), has been
applied to this case study. Hence, this chapter will explore a standard use of the
VMI and a negotiation-based approach of it.
The VMI is a strategy of supply chain coordination, also known as continuous replen-
ishment or supplier managed inventory. The concept behind this strategy is that the
vendor decides on the appropriate inventory levels of the product in the producer’s
warehouse and the appropriate inventory policies to maintain those levels (Sari, 2007).
The producer aims to ensure a higher customer service level with lower inventory costs,
while the supplier’s target is to reduce production, inventory and transportation cost.
One of the key drivers for a successful VMI is trust and collaboration between the
actors as well as a good flow of information (Marquès et al., 2016).
The production planning models have been adapted to implement this strategy, and
some changes had to be made. Specifically, the two new models require that the
==PAGE:59==
6.2 Non-negotiated VMI
49
components’ warehouse at the producer’s site will be entirely managed by the sup-
plier, who will be responsible for deciding when and how much to deliver. To do that,
the producer shares its forecast for each component, allowing the supplier to know
exactly how many components the manufacturer is foreseeing to consume. This in-
creased transparency allows the supplier to early recognise fluctuations of the demand,
decreasing the uncertainty and increasing the flexibility (Waller et al., 2001). How-
ever, specific parameters are set to guarantee that the quantity shipped is kept inside
a certain range and ensure that the producer’s safety stock level is maintained. This
helps to secure continuous production with limited backlogs at the manufacturer’s
site.
The producer model is run first to determine the number of components needed to
run a smooth assembly process. Specifically, the model explained in Section 5.2.1 is
run with the assumption that the producer has unlimited components to use. This
forecast is then shared with the supplier that can determine the delivery quantities
and inventory level at the producer’s site accordingly. In doing so, the supplier is
not able to deviate from the forecast given by the manufacturer. This first configu-
ration will be called from hereon ”non-negotiated VMI” as the supplier is expected
to satisfy the producer’s forecast and plan the delivery and the inventory level at the
producer’s site accordingly. Afterwards, a negotiation-based approach (from hereon
called ”negotiation-based VMI”) is applied to find the best solution for the supply
chain. The concept behind this process is very similar to the one explained in Chapter
5. However, some differences are noted and shown.

any restrictions related to the components. This means that the producer will as-
sume that the component’s warehouse will always be filled with a suﬀicient amount
of products to satisfy its demand. Afterwards, the supplier’s model will be run. In

tested a situation where the producer’s cost is significantly higher than the supplier’s

the producer have been increased. Contrarily, these same costs have been decreased
for the supplier. Subsequently, the models presented in Chapter 5 and Chapter 6 are
run, and the outcome of this analysis can be seen in Figure 7.4.
Focusing on the upper part of the results, it is noticeable that the cost improvement
of the fully integrated versus the non-collaborative model is minuscule, around 0.3%.
Likewise, a similar result can be observed in comparing the non-negotiated VMI
and the fully integrated model.
Consequently, the improvement achieved after a
few iterations compared to the initial supply plan is almost insignificant. To be more
precise, the improvement after the best iteration is less than 0.01% for both strategies.
Hence, the most relevant insight that can be extracted from this analysis is that,
independently of the type of strategy, if the cost gap between the two parties is large,
it is not worthy for them to negotiate. In other words, when the bargaining power of
one of the parties is considerably big compared to the other, the non-collaborative plan
can hardly be improved, and the common agreement reached after the negotiation
process does not pay off the resources spent along the process.
Furthermore, it can be observed that every time that the supplier is proposing a new
solution, the cost of the producer is increasing, and the total cost is higher compared
to when the producer is the driver of the proposal. It is due to the significant influence
of the producer on the total cost of the supply chain, i.e., more than 90%. Therefore,
in a supply chain with this type of configuration, only the supply plans proposed by
the dominant player are able to improve the total cost.

CHAPTER 8
Discussion
This chapter discusses the main findings of the two analysis. The aim of the dis-
cussion is to summarize the results from the two analysis and relate them to the
objectives of the thesis as well as the research questions stated in the introduction.
Also, the limitations of the analysis are presented.
8.1
Main Findings of the Analysis
The main finding of this thesis is that supplier collaboration can be beneficial for
the supply chain network. In fact, by applying a negotiation-based approach to both
strategies, the total cost of the supply chain was reduced by more than 4%. However,
when looking at the single costs of the best solution after the negotiation process,
the producer has a higher cost than the non-collaboration setup.
Hence, it must
be considered a final negotiation between the parties that aims to share the savings.
This thesis proposed two different strategies of cost-sharing that allow supplier and
producer to benefit from the collaboration.
The experimental analysis conducted showed that, in this network, collaboration is
needed and effective when the demand is not flat. Indeed, it has been proved that
if the demand was stable, the supplier cost could not be lowered with the negotia-
tion process, and hence, the collaboration was not successful. Furthermore, if one of
the parties has a much greater cost than the other, the negotiation process is inef-
ficient. This result was the same for both strategies. Lastly, it has been seen that
the negotiation-based collaborative planning could lower the cost by 4.4%, resulting
in the most effective strategy. Furthermore, it is easier to implement as it does not
require investing in expensive technologies like the VMI, and it does not imply the
same level of trust and information-sharing.

8.2 Research Questions

8.2
Research Questions
• Can supplier collaboration in the production planning lower the total cost of
the supply chain, and to what extent?
The thesis has proved that supplier collaboration can significantly
reduce the total cost of the supply chain. The negotiation-based col-
laborative planning reduced the cost by 4.40%, while the negotiation-
based VMI lowered it by 4.29%.
• When is supplier collaboration the most effective?
As a result of the experimental analysis, it can be stated that sup-
plier collaboration is needed and more effective when the demand
fluctuates and the difference between producer and supplier cost is
not significantly high. Also, to apply both strategies, high flexibility
is needed.
• What are the main benefits and challenges of supplier collaboration?
The most relevant benefits identified are cost reduction and increased
supply chain visibility, while the main challenges are related to the
accuracy of the data, the risk of sharing sensitive information and
lastly, the decision linked to the cost-sharing of the savings.
• Which strategy works the best in the studied supply chain?
Negotiation-based collaborative planning should be pursued in this
specific network. It is more effective as it brings a higher cost reduc-
tion, and it is linked with fewer implementation challenges.
8.3
Limitation of the Analysis
The analysis conducted during this thesis has cast out positive and insightful results
worth considering for future strategical decision-making in the supply chain. Nonethe-
less, some limitations have hindered the study to some extent.
==PAGE:90==
8.3 Limitation of the Analysis
80
Firstly, the developed negotiation process implied that the new input needed to be
added manually every time an iteration was performed. This process has been re-
peated for each iteration of both strategies, resulting in a time-consuming, cumber-
some process that has lagged the development of the study. In the same line, a supply
chain with only a producer and a supplier has been considered. If more parties are
included in the analysis, it would require developing new models that would add extra
layers of complexity to the study, requiring more time to input and process the new
models.
Secondly, to apply these negotiation-based methodologies, it is required that the
producer is willing to have backlogs, although the amount of backlogs is limited.
However, industries such as pharmaceutical would not be suitable for this type of
collaboration as the allowance of backlogs is very limited. Thus, the study is not
suitable for every kind of supply chain.
Thirdly, a supplier collaboration approach relies on information sharing and trust
between the parties. The extent to which these are willing to share sensitive informa-
tion can limit the analysis and hinder the proper implementation of the methodology.
Moreover, the producer could be reluctant to negotiate as this approach implies an
increase in the backlogs and thereby, the brand image of the organization may be
damaged.
Lastly, another limitation faced while running the models has been the impossibility
of finding an optimal solution within an acceptable time range. Consequently, there is
a tolerance gap lower than 0.0000001% between the solution found and the potential
optimal solution.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
This chapter presents the conclusion of the entire thesis projects, based on the
analysis and results presented. Also, some recommendations for future research on
the topic are presented.
This thesis can be considered a success as it was able to answer the research questions
exhaustively. The analysis provided great insights into how a specific network can
benefit from a collaborative approach. The study expanded the existing knowledge
about collaboration between manufacturers and suppliers, creating a bridge between
mathematical models and negotiation processes.
Based on the findings of this thesis and the literature studied, the suggestion for this
specific supply chain network is to implement the negotiation-based collaborative
planning. Adopting this strategy ensures a more significant cost reduction, and it is
considered less challenging to implement as it requires fewer resources and less infor-
mation sharing. Furthermore, it is recommended to adopt the cost-sharing scheme
that allows both parties to have the same savings share, namely 4.40%. This is fun-
damental to ensure that both parties have an appropriate cost reduction compared
to not engaging in collaboration with the other actor.
Future work and research can be performed from this thesis project and the results
obtained in order to improve the methodology utilised and achieve better results.
Hence, some proposals for future work are shown below.
• Explore different programming techniques - As stated in Section 8.3, the
use of linear programming has limited the study to some extent. Hence, it could
be convenient to scout other methodologies that can allow the development of
a more dynamic model. In this way, the number of iterations and experiments
could be increased considerably, and therefore, a more robust analysis could be
==PAGE:92==

obtained. Additionally, some machine learning algorithms could be explored,
easing and optimising the negotiation process.
• Investigate different supplier collaboration strategies - Naturally, con-
sidering other collaboration systems can be undoubtedly insightful. By doing so,
areas that the methods analysed in this thesis partially cover could be further
considered. Moreover, these new scenarios could overcome the disadvantages
of the analysed one, like implementing a supplier collaboration in an industry
with limited or no backlogs allowed.
• Expand the size of the supply chain - The integration of more parties in
the supply chain, such as more suppliers, subsidiaries, or wholesalers, would
add another layer of complexity to the study that would arguably enrich the
analysis.
• Assess the implementation of the results in a real-life scenario - To
validate the analysis carried out in this thesis, the next step should be to analyse
the implementation of this negotiation-based approach in a real-life supply chain
that already makes use of VMI or collaborative planning. Moreover, it would
be relevant to study how to deploy from scratch one of these strategies in a
supply chain that is not using any of the configurations mentioned above.
