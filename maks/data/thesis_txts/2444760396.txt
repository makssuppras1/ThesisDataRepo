Abstract 
The design optimization of steel structures is usually carried out by choosing a profile that minimizes 
the amount of steel, under the boundaries of carrying mechanical loads and being compliant with 
maximum allowed displacements. The fire verifications are typically performed at a later design stage 
by applying the amount of insulation needed to keep the profile below its critical temperature during 
fire. 
This strategy keeps the steel costs to a minimum, but often results in relatively high costs for insulating 
the structure, which may nullify the scope of the original design optimization. A typical example is the 
use of I-profiles, which possess the highest elastic and plastic resistance modulus at 20°C, but the lowest 
in fire, due to the rapid heating caused by the high surface-to-volume ration. This thesis investigated 
possible beneficial effects of the consideration of fire verification in the early design stage on a steel car 
park.  
 
Car parks represent at present critical structures from the point of view of fire design, as indicated by 
several recent and past cases of major car park fires (the most recent and resounding being the car park 
fire in Liverpool, in 2008, where 1400 car were involved in the fire). Despite such events, steel car parks 
are often designed by assuming a local fire of 3-4 cars. This assumption allows sparing (and often 
completely avoiding) the fire insulation of the elements, thus making the steel car parks competitive in 
price with a concrete structure. If a larger design fire, involving more cars, are used the general fire 
safety level of the car park will be higher. This seems desirable, but it will add the necessity of fire 
insulation and thereby increase the price of the car park.  
 
For this reason, car parks are one of the structures that could mostly benefit from an early inclusion of 
fire consideration into the design procedure, which could allow it to keep being competitive on the 
price while also ensuring a higher fire safety level. 
In the work with this thesis the formulas and methods for designing steel profiles in both bending and 
axial loading was used to make an Excel sheet program that graphically show the potential 
optimization or saving options for an initially designed profile.  
 
A case study of a typical steel car park was initially designed using the current methods of a local fire 
with 3 cars thereby totally avoiding fire insulation. The design was then added the fire insulation 
needed to withstand the temperatures of a larger design fire following the parametric fire curve. This 
increased the price of the total price (including decking, facades, etc.) of the car park by 29% and 87% 
for the steel structure alone.  
The optimization program was then used for an optimization of the profiles and it was found that the 
price increase from the initial car park could be reduced to a 5% increase in the total price of the car 
park by and 16% for the steel structure alone. 
 
It has been concluded that considering the choice of profile type at an early stage may show that a 
saving option is possible but that this option could be very hard to find without the help of the 
developed Excel sheet program. Quantifying this by the use of the case study showed that a saving 
potential can be found that ensures the structure can be designed using a much larger design fire 
resulting in a building with a higher fire safety level at a minimal price increase.

1 
Introduction 
Optimizing material consumption in structural design has always been a key factor in engineering. 
From ancient times where the availability of materials where scarce and the shear transport to site 
could be limiting, to the present time where the cost and environmental impact of building materials 
heightens the importance of optimization in design.  
Using less materials means lower price, more easy building process, and less self-weight. This in 
turn means smaller foundation which again limits the total price of a structure.  
1.1 
Motivation 
The use of steel in residential structures have been expanding through the last decades. An article 
from the British national organization for the steel construction industry (BCSA) states the since 1980 
the market share for steel frames in multi-story buildings have increased from 33% to 66% in 2017. In 
the same time period the market share in the same areas for concrete has decreased from 49% to 20% 
(steelconstruction.info, 2018). This market is only a small area of the entire structural market, but it 
shows an increasing demand for steel structures.  
In Denmark the increased use of steel in residential buildings has also been acknowledged by 
contractor NCC in an article from 2016 in the Danish structural news site “BygTek” (Bygtek.dk, 
2016). Here the production director of the contractor states that the general inclination to concrete in 
residential structures has begun to lean towards the choice of steel as an alternative. It is mentioned 
that with steel many of the designs of modern architecture is possible. 
With the increased use of steel also comes an increased interest in the optimization of steel 
structures. Even though steel structures can accomplish architecture that concrete is not capable of, 
steel structures remain more expensive than using precast concrete elements.  
A main advantage of concrete over steel is the immediate resistance to fire. Normally concrete will 
need no or very little fire protection in order to sustain a satisfactory capacity in a fire situation for 
60 or 120 minutes (as normally demanded by the Danish code). Steel on the other hand has a much 
better strength to weight ratio which benefits on the constructability but is a clear disadvantage 
when it comes to fire resistance. Hence in most cases steel will need some sort of fire insulation to 
maintain an acceptable resistance during a fire.  
I the case of industrial buildings this may easily be achieved using a simple mineral wool insulation 
but in residential buildings the demands for architecture may dictate a much cleaner look. This is 
possible using more expensive intumescent paints that can be made with a surface that looks very 
similar to a normal topcoat paint.  
 
Price of steel structures 
When discussing optimization of steel structures, the main objective is to reduce the price. I order to 
do this a general understanding of the components of the price must be known. In Figure 1 an 
estimate of the composition of the price is shown. This will depend on the type of structure, the 
complexity etc. It can be seen that the raw material (steel) only makes up approximately 30-40% of 
the total price. This shows that in order to optimize the price, the whole process: raw materials 
fabrication, construction/erection, and fire protection must be considered. The fire protection can 
make up more or less of the total cost depending on the requirements.

1.2 
State of art and practice 
Optimizing steel structures is usually done by a simple choice of the profile which results in the least 
amount of steel still capable of carrying the applied load hence reducing the cost of steel. When the 
profile choice is made the fire protection is designed thereby ensuring the capacity in a fire situation.  
The knowledge of both steel design and fire protection design is rarely mastered by the same person 
and much less carried out be this same person. However, choosing profiles wisely by considering 
the fire protection in the profile optimization process could optimize the combined cost of steel and 
fire protection.  
In Denmark the design of steel structures according to the Eurocode is described in the book 
“Teknisk Ståbi” (Jensen, 2018) and “Stålkonstruktioner efter DS/EN 1993” (Bjarne Chr. Jensen, 2015). 
These are widely considered the “go-to” books for designing and optimizing steel. In the UK design 
is done using the “green books”. Neither of these guides implement any chapters on the design for 
fire insulation or even design when requirements for capacity in fire is required.  
This means that normally the design of a steel structure will be done by a structural engineer 
designing for the ultimate limit state (ULS), the serviceability limit state (SLS, deformation), and 
perhaps earthquake in the accidental limit state (ALS). From this the optimal profiles are chosen as 
the profiles suitable for the structure that has the least weight. For these chosen profiles the 
structural engineer will calculate the critical steel temperature which is the maximum allowable 
temperature at which the profile will have enough capacity in a load combination in ALS (fire).  
This design will be given to a contractor who will design the fire insulation from the known section 
factor of the profiles and the critical temperature given. In that way the total price for the steel and 
fire insulation is never considered in the design optimization process. 
 
Steel car parks 
Fire insulation is added where the structure needs to have a resistance to fire. This is the case in most 
residential buildings. The price of the fire insulation depends on the thickness requirement and the 
type of material used. The thickness relates to the properties of the profile, the critical temperature, 
the insulation properties, and the design fire. The type of insulation is determined mostly by the 
requirement for the general look of the structure. If the profiles are hidden in the final structure the 
insulation can be made with a type of mineral wool which is very cheap but does not look nice and 
is very vulnerable if left to wear and tear from the users of the building.  
If the structures are visible the insulation can be made from a hard board product encapsulating the 
profiles or a paint coat which insulates the profiles when exposed to fire. As it will be shown in this

A good example of a structure where the profiles are visible is a steel structure car park. In recent

The conventional way of making car park houses in Denmark have primarily been concrete

In Denmark the requirement for car parks resemble that of normal residential building requiring 
60min or 120min fire resistance to the standard fire curve. This can be achieved by fire insulating or 
by designing the steel for the critical temperature without insulation. 
This makes the car parks a good example where optimization is a necessity for being competitive 
and as such it will be used in this report as a case study to show the optimization methods 
suggested. 
 
Current European method for designing steel car parks  
In 1993 the “European convention for constructional steelwork” published a report called “Fire 
safety in open car parks” (ECCS, 2013). This document aimed to collect the different fire safety 
requirements for open car parks throughout Europe. Along with (Schleich, et al., 1999) this lays the 
foundation for the current method for steel based open car parks. 
The design fire described in these reports are based on a series of test with car fires made in different 
scenarios and with different types of cars. From the tests a standard car fire is deducted and used as 
the design fire. The assumption is that when a fire starts in a car it will spread to the neighboring 
cars in a set amount of time (12 min.). The fire will then spread from these cars to the next in another 
set time. This means that the fire will spread as a local fire as the first fire will burn out before the 
fire fills the room. The theory only applies for open car parks with an opening area in the façade of 
at least 30% and the use is illustrated in (Haremza, et al., 2013). 
 
Safety level of steel car parks 
The theory and fires that lay the foundation for the above described method has been challenged in  
recent years by a number of papers (Heijden & M & Loomans, 2013), (Márton, et al., 2016) and fires 
in car park houses described i.e. in (Sørensen, 2016). Most recently a fire in Liverpool where it 
seemed that a condition resembling a flashover fire was reached. After this fire the community of 
“Engineers Ireland” sent out an alert stating that “steps should be taken by fire engineers and structural 
engineers, as well as by owners and managers of similar facilities, to consider the potential impacts of fires in 
car park structures on both life and property” (Structural-Safety UK, 2018).  
The Eurocode, the national annex, and the Danish fire regulations together determines that any 
structures should comply to a given safety class (R30, R60, or R120) or equivalent safety. This means 
that the required safety is bound to the definition of the standard fire curve. For different other 
design fire types (e.g. parametric fire) an equivalent method is described but when specific fires are 
used the specification and verification of safety level is up to the designer. Whether the current 
method of local fires spreading from one car to the other lives up to the required safety is not 
covered in this thesis, but it is clear that using a more conservative fire (parametric fire) as design 
fire would yield a higher fire safety level and a fire safety level that is more easily documented to 
live up to the requirements of the Eurocode, national annex, and Danish fire regulations. 
1.3 
Objective 
With increased fire safety level by choosing a more conservative design fire it must also be expected 
that the price of the structure is increased. As described the competition in the building industry is 
hard and increasing the price dramatically would most likely make the solution not accepted even 
though a higher safety level is delivered. 
If, however the price increase could be minimized the gain of a higher fire safety level could turn the 
need into a gained value and hence make the solution acceptable. The higher fire safety level could 
even be a selling point when considering the amount of damage to the structure during a fire.  
The case of the car park is a good example of the general desire to minimize the cost of structures. A 
general desire which is often not achieved as the normal division of design is as described above:

The initial structural designer may not have the required knowledge of fire insulation design to

The aim of this thesis can hence be divided into four parts: 
 
•

•

• 
The third part is to describe if further savings can be achieved by using more advanced 
optimization methods that may not be as easy and fast to use.   
• 
The final aim is to show that using these methods the price increase of designing a steel car 
park house, using a safety level that is higher than what is done currently, can be 
minimized. 
 
1.4 
Method 
Firstly, the theory for designing steel structures are described and will cover different design fires 
and methods for designing steel beams and columns. The governing parameters for design will be 
shown so they can be used for the optimization process. As it is the desire with this thesis to 
described methods that can be generally understood and used the theory used is all based on the 
Eurocode. Furthermore, as this thesis is done in Denmark the Eurocode choices from the Danish 
national annex is used.  
With the knowledge of the beam and column design together with the design fires the possibilities 
of optimizations can be set up in both simplified and more advanced methods.  
Both the simple and more advanced optimization options will be implemented into an Excel 
spreadsheet program which will be able to show the potential saving options for a given profile 
choice. The program Excel is chosen as it is the desire to make the methods readily available and 
easy to implement in other uses by professional engineers which may not know programming code. 
To exemplify the use of the optimization and to show the potential in saving a case study of a car 
park will be used as described above. For the case study the input parameters as geometry and loads 
will be described, a base design will be done using no fire insulation and based on the local fire.  
The profiles chosen in this base design will then be added fire insulation in an amount that will 
withstand a fully spread design fire following the parametric fire of the Eurocode. This design will 
serve the basis of the desired safety level of the building. The base design (local fire) and the 
extended base design (parametric fire) will then be compared on price to show the price increase.  
The optimization methods using simple methods will then be applied and a profile and insulation 
choice will be made showing the saving potential of this method. Finally, the more advanced 
methods will be applied to show the final saving potential and thereby the necessary additional cost 
of going from the original safety level using the local fire and to a higher safety level using the 
parametric fire.

2 
Theory 
2.1 
General introduction 
The process of optimizing the choice of profile for a steel structure consists many parts. A profile 
must be chosen that comply with the following: 
 
• 
Shall have the structural bearing capacity to carry the load applied in all required situations 
– including fire situations.  
• 
Shall cost as little as possible. 
• 
May need to live up to certain architectural demands. 
 
In order to describe how these demands can be followed in the optimization process the following 
section deals with the governing theory for the design of steel structures. 
First the behavior of the material in fire is described. As composite cross sections will be used in the 
case study both the behavior of steel and concrete is described. 
Then the design method for beams and columns is described to show which parameters of the 
profile is important for the optimization.  
The process of finding the load situations or load combinations are then described. This gives the 
basis for the loads that the chosen profile needs to carry.  
Finally, the price of steel and fire insulation is described in order to find the profile with the least 
cost.  
2.2 
Design fires and steel temperatures 
The case study will be analyzed both for a local fire considering the fire in each car and a more 
conservative fire following the parametric fire method. In the following sections the theory for the 
two methods will be described. 
 
Local fire  
In 1993 the European convention for constructional steelwork (ECCS) published a report named 
“Fire safety in open car parks” (ECCS, 2013). This report presented a model for a burning var that 
was based on fire tests. Since then several other studies have been done on the heat release from a 
burning car which are all summed up in the report “Development of design rules for steel structures 
subjected to localized fires in closed car parks” (Schleich, et al., 1999) which was a joint European 
project. 
Based on the tests described herein a time dependent curve for heat release was found for both one 
and several cars.

When a beam deforms under a load it is known from beam theory that plain cross section remains

the height of the cross section and the outer point in the cross section reaches the used yield stress.

2.4 
Concrete thermal response 
As described above in this thesis a case study will be used to show use of the optimization. In the 
case study composite beam are used as structural beams. The composite beams consist of a steel 
beam with overlying deck of concrete which is fastened to the steel with cast-in studs. This allows 
the steel concrete and steel to form a composite cross section. As such the thermal heating of 
concrete must be described.  
In the Danish annex to the concrete Eurocode (DS/EN 1992-1-2 DK NA, 2011) in section 3.2.2.1 it is 
shown that the strength reduction factor of concrete under heating up to the temperature of 350°C is 
0.9 meaning that concrete under heating that has a lower temperature is not reduced in strength.  
The case study used in this thesis would, if it was designed for a standard fire, be required to 
maintain its structural integrity for 120min. According to table D.5 in (DS/EN 1994-1-2, 2007) the 
temperature of a 100mm concrete slab in a depth of 60mm after a standard fire of 120 min is 342°C.  
This means that if the compression zone in the composite beam has a height of less than 40mm (total 
thickness 100mm subtracted 60mm depth) a reduction factor for the concrete of 0.9 is conservative.  
It will be shown that the compression zone is no more than this and as so a conservative value for 
the reduction will have a neglectable influence on the choice of steel profile.

2.6 
Load combinations 
When designing structures, it is the intention to ensure that the structure will have sufficient 
capacity in any combination of actions possible. For each situation the necessary safety for loads are 
used as a factor on each load. This could be the highest wind pressure which according to the 
Eurocode should be taken as the wind pressure that occurs with a return period of 50 years (DS/EN 
1991-1-4, 2007). To account for this, the fluctuation of wind etc. the base value of the wind load is 
multiplied with a load combination factor. If wind is the dominant load this factor is 1.5. The same 
principle applies for every other load using different factors for natural loads, imposed loads, self-
weight etc.

the paint is applied it is a very thin layer, with very poor insulating properties. When the hardened

that the insulating properties is highly dependent on the current temperature and the speed at 
which the temperature rises as the expanding of the foam is not instant. Hence the design of 
intumescent paint is designed using tables from the supplier based on (extrapolated) test results in a  
standard fire.

Steel price 
According to (Molio, 2018) the price of a finished steel structure is between 28kr/kg and 32 kr/kg

normal paint, transportation and erection.

smaller profiles with many weld demanding attachments e.g. stairs and ladders. As such the linear 
approach of a single price per kg used in this thesis may be somewhat conservative. Furthermore,

3.5 
Reference design (Base design local fire) 
The reference design case is design done with the local fire as the design fire. No fire insulation is 
applied. The steel response is done using the 2% strain limit or “effective” yield.  
For the design case the amount and price of necessary steel is reported.  
 
Choice of profiles 
For the beams, which are all similar, a profile is chosen which has the lowest price even though the 
height will increase the total height of the structure (as a set free height is given).  
For columns a profile is chosen for the façade columns, and internal columns that is the same all the 
way from top to bottom. A smaller profile could have been chosen for the top profiles, but this 
would increase the complexity of the connections (as columns would not have the same profile 
height) and since the price for the columns make up a smaller part of the total steel price no 
optimization of this sort is made.  
 
Design fire 
Using the theory for the local fire explained in section 2.2.1 an incoming heat flux is found. Two 
scenarios (which are also described in (Schleich, et al., 1999)) are used (see figure 21).  
• 
Scenario 1 is one car starting the fire while it is park in a full car park. After 12 min the fire 
spreads to the two adjacent cars. This fire affects the beam above at 2.5m from the end of the 
beam as this is the center of the car. It does not have its center under the middle of the beam 
above. 
• 
Scenario 2 is one car starting the fire while in the driving lane. This fire is only one car, but it 
has its center below the center of the beam above which is where the beam will have the 
largest section forces.  
The beam will hence be designed for both scenarios while the columns and bracings will be 
designed for scenario 1 which is closer and has a larger heat release rate.

4 
Analysis 
4.1 
Desired safety level 
As described in the introduction the question of the safety level of the local fire method has been 
raised in recent time. It is stated that the safety level by using the local fire does not meet the 
requirements of the Eurocode. Instead it is suggested to use a parametric fire curve corresponding to 
the fire load and surrounding of the car park.  
The impact of this is threefold:  
Firstly, the fire will be a more global fire meaning that where the local fire assumed a lesser fire in 
the middle of the beam (1 car) and a larger fire at the end of the beam (3 cars) the parametric 
assumes a global fire affecting the entire car park at once. As so the beam will reach the same 
temperature at both the middle and the end of the beam. 
Secondly the parametric fire reaches a higher maximum temperature than the local fire.  
Thirdly as the fire affects the entire car park house at once it is no longer possible to allow using the 
2% strain “effective” yield limit as the larger strains affecting the entire house will compromise the 
structural integrity of the building. Hence the design fire is increased, and the yield limit is 
decreased to the 0.2% “design” yield limit. 
 
 
Base design – parametric fire 
The temperature of the design fire using the parametric fire is calculated using the theory presented 
in section 2.2.2. The following parameters are used for the input to the design fire: 
The fire load density related to the total surface area of the enclosure, 𝑞𝑡,𝑑, is set to 300 MJ/m2 as 
suggested in (Spearpoint, et al., 2015). This source states fire load from 250 MJ/m2 to 300 MJ/m2 and 
other sources may list values up to and beyond 330 MJ/m2. As this thesis is not directly aimed at 
finding the correct value for the fire load the value of 300 MJ/m2 is used. 
The opening factor is found to 0.04 and the b value for the surroundings are found to 1160 J/m2s½K 
These values are not easily determined for a car park which is much different from a normal room 
for which the theory for the parametric fire is meant. However, the found parametric fire is an 
acceptable estimate of the design fire. 
The design process uses the same methods, loads, and geometry as the base case – local fire only this 
design applies the use of intumescent paint to achieve the necessary capacity for the much hotter 
parametric fire. The beam design calculations can be seen in appendix A.9 the column design 
calculations can be seen in appendix A.10 and the bracing design in appendix A.11.The design yields

Influence of section class 
To further optimize the steel the effect of the section class of the profiles is included in the program.  
Section class is a definition for a cross section that defines the whether the profile is susceptible to 
local buckling. The definition is done different kinds of loading the section class of a profile can be 
different from bending to axial load. A Lower section means a more stable cross section that is not 
susceptible to local buckling. As local buckling can happen in cross sections with higher section 
classes a smaller section modulus is used as described in section 2.5.1. 
As an example, a HEA240 in bending is a section class 3 and the section modulus to use is the elastic 
section modulus. A CHS 323.9x10 and many other CHS types are on the other hand section class 1 
and the section modulus to use for bending is then the plastic section modulus which is 30% higher. 
This means that the choice of optimal section can be even better.  
The use of section class can be chosen in the program and the optimization plots then looks as 
shown below.

Discussion of case study results and comparison of methods  
Although the increase of 16% in price of the steel structure may seem a lot it must also be compared 
to the total value of the building where it is only a 5.2% increase and it must be seen in the light that 
the added safety level also yields added value and the process of adding safety could in this way 
maybe even be turned into a selling point:  
• 
An insurance company may see the benefit in having a structure that is more durable in fire 
and less likely to get permanent damage during a fire.  
• 
The downtime of the carpark after a fire could be minimized with less damage to the 
structure thereby minimizing the loss in parking fees and loss of market share. With an 
estimated turnover of more than 5 million kr each year for a car park the size of the case 
study this loss could be substantial.

Car parks in city centers may be very important to the infrastructure of the city and

The found profiles which make up the optimized solution may however be hard to implement.

and expensive to do than for simple H-profiles. How much this increase in cost is, is hard to 
determine. 
The different methods used clearly shows that there is a potential in including the fire insulation 
design at an early stage and comparing the methods used on the case study shows that optimizing 
the profile yields a saving of 38% from the insulated base design (parametric fire) to the design using 
optimization with reduction matrix. This can be seen in the figure below. 
 
 
Figure 40 Comparison of optimization methods  
The use of the Excel spreadsheet which graphically shows the saving options also shows its worth in 
the case study as the found profile types are not easily found without. This is especially shown in the 
difference in what is chosen for the composite beam. Both the RHS 500x200x12 and TPS 
300x200x12,5 each with different insulation thickness are valid choices as the utilization ratio is just 
below 100% but the price of the TPS is much less. This may not have been guessed without the 
graphical illustration of several choices.

Conclusion  
This thesis presents the importance of considering the choice of profile in an early stage of the design 
phase. This is mainly achieved by implementing optimization procedures into an easy to use excel 
sheet that graphically shows optimization options. The optimization and verification of structural 
elements are based on the formulas given by the various Eurocodes. The optimization possibilities 
are exemplified by the use of a case study that show the potential of methods. The following section 
will summarize the key section and describe suggestions for further work.   
5.1 
Highlights  
The presented theory for both design fires and structural design constitutes the basis of the design 
and optimization process. Combined with the knowledge of steel thermal response an analytical 
solution is found for a very simple profile that shows that an optimization in profile choice is indeed 
possible. Extending this by the use of numerical methods an Excel program has been made that can 
show the actual optimal profile choice for a given initial design.  
The results from this program shows that in many cases a benefit form considering the profile choice 
at an early stage can be found. The graphical representation in the program presents the results so 
the user can quickly get an overview of possible saving options.  
Quantifying this by the use of the case study shows that a saving potential can be found: The initial 
design can be optimized in such a way that when adding approximately 5% to total price of the 
building the structures can be designed using a much larger design fire resulting in a building with a 
higher fire safety level.  
 
Returning to the initial four points in the general aim of the thesis: 
  
• 
It has been shown that if the choice of profile is considered early in the design process a 
general saving can be achieved. 
• 
A simple implementation into an Excel program has been made. This program is very easy 
to use a yields a saving that is acceptable but is a conservative estimate of the full potential 
saving. 
• 
During the work with the thesis a more advanced option has been built on to the Excel 
program that enables the program to consider the correct critical temperature of every 
profile. This method however requires the user to have the program recalculate a steel 
material reduction matrix. Even though this calculation is done by the program it takes 
some time and the method is not as fast as the simpler method.  
• 
It has been shown that when using the above-mentioned methods and the implementation 
of these in the Excel program a large saving can be found in the case study car park.  
 
5.2 
Limitations and further studies 
Working with implementing the optimization process in a simple program calls for making choices 
in what to implement and the method. In many ways the program and the underlying data can be 
imroved, and the following section attempts to list some of these  
 
Intumescent paints for different design fires 
The tabular value for intumescent paint presented in appendix A.2 is only valid for the standard fire 
curve. A constant lambda value has been used in this thesis and in the appendix,  it is shown that 
this value can be assumed to yield safe results. However, the results also mean that an extra 
optimization can be found if a more precise determination can be done. This could be implemented 
by doing a more thorough study of the parameters of the intumescent paint that is safe to use also 
for a parametric or even other design fire.  
 
Different types of fire protection 
In the thesis much emphasis is put on the intumescent paint. The saving option for cheaper 
insulation is not as high as these are not as expensive. However as mentioned before the prices for 
==PAGE:61==

                            Page 59 
 
 
 
all the insulation types are varying and a further study could be used to show the benefits and price 
for more types of insulation. For example: does the findings in this thesis also apply for a high riser? 
 
Influence of connections on saving options 
It is stated in the thesis that the influence of connections to the saving result is not considered. 
Connections on different profile types may vary much in price. E.g. a connection on an RHS is more 
expensive than a simple connection as more welding is needed. The price for an RHS may be much 
cheaper than that of an IPE but what about the connection? Will the added price of the connection 
actually level out the potential saving on the profile.  
 
Implementation in more advanced programming. 
The optimization is, as of now, implemented in excel as it is an easily accessible program in which 
the program can be accessed and changed by almost everyone. As this is done with the time 
pressure that goes with a master thesis, the programming method may not be optimal an the 
calculational sheets may not be as easy to read as wanted. This could be changed by implementing 
the same methods into a more advanced programming language as e.g. python. This also gives the 
possibility to implement it in other programs as for example the “SteFi” program developed in 
(Andersen & Dyhr, 2018). This could also counter the fact that when using the reduction matrix 
option for considering the critical temperature the calculation of the matrix is very time consuming. 
This could much faster be done with another programming language. 
 
Implementation in BIM programs  
In order to make the method and program more accessible and wider used it could be implemented 
in a function for a BIM or CAD program used for structural design. This could be a program like 
“Revit” which is a very commonly used BIM program for residential building in Denmark. It could 
be even more useable to implement it as a function in “Tekla” which is also a CAD and BIM 
program used for drawing steel and concrete structures. As “Tekla” is developed for doing 
workshop drawings it is also used by contractors who could benefit from having an easy 
optimization tool available.

3. The utilization ratio of the initial profile in ULS. If the initial profile for some reason is not 
utilized to 100% this is considered so the steel can be fully utilized. 
4. A loading method of either “axial” (columns), “bending” (beams), or “composite” 
(composite beams) can be chosen. This decides which limiting parameter to be used. For 
columns it will be the area times the chi factor, for beam it is the section modulus etc. 
5. For columns the weak or strong can be chosen (for now only the weak axis is implemented 
in the program this means that all columns are designed for the minimum which for a 
column with the same critical length for both the Y and Z axis is correct. This is true in the 
case study used on this thesis).  
6. Insulation thickness of the initial designed profile. 
7. Price of steel including fabrication etc. 
8. Price of insulation. 
9. For a column the critical length is input here 
10. Decides if the member is affected by fire (and thereby also insulated) on 3 or 4 sides. This 
affects both section factor and amount of insulation (paint area). 
11. Further knowledge can be found if putting the exact  initial profile here (shown in plot, see 
figure A-14 for example). 
12. If the box is checked “yes” the changing of section factor is considered. This is fully 
allowable, so this should always be set to “yes”. 
13. If the box is checked “yes” the buckling curve will be determined according to the Eurocode 
for fire design for column. As such this should always be set to “yes”. 
14. If the box is checked “yes” the critical temperature is considered when optimizing. This 
should be done with some care as this will do a complete optimization but only if the correct 
input fire is calculated, see section 20. This is not necessary when this is set to “no”.
