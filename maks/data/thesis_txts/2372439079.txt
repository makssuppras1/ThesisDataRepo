Abstract
The aim of this project is to validate a TRNSYS model for a solar heat pump system, that is tested
in a laboratory test facility at the Department of Civil Engineering (DTU) and perform a data
analysis from observations done on the system.
The system is composed of a solar collector and a ground source heat pump, that supply heat to
a hot water storage tank, which in turn is used to tap heated water for both domestic hot water and
space heating. The thermal performance of the system has been measured through a monitoring
system. Measured thermal performance has been compared to thermal performance calculated with
a detailed simulation model for two short periods. A good match between measured and calculated
thermal performance was achieved for the system.
The yearly thermal performance of the system was calculated for a diﬀerent domestic hot water
and space heating demand proﬁle, than that being tested in the facility and the weather data is
based on data extracted from the DTU climate station for the year 2016.
The thermal performance for the tested system is lower than the thermal performances of the
most eﬃcient tested heat pump systems available. The reason is that the solar collector loop has a
relatively high ﬂowrate and therefore less high temperatures are achieved, leading to having a low
net coverage ratio and a larger dependency on the heat pump. The horizontal ground source heat
exchanger oﬀers a relatively low temperature, due to the depth factor and the particular period the
validation test is conducted on, that is; for the ﬁrst half of the year. The heat pump performance
is poor in itself. Heat losses reductions can also improve the overall performance.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction
The necessity of both Domestic Hot water and Space heating in households leads to one of the
largest energy demands in this sector. Stand-alone water heating systems are essential, especially
in locations where district heating is not available. This application has been fulﬁlled throughout
the years by various technologies, which are unsustainable or relatively ineﬃcient.
In order to
achieve a full-scale sustainable energy system, water heating boilers, which run on oil, gas or wood
pellets, need to be replaced by less carbon emitting technologies. The electrical water heaters are as
such clean energy technology as long as the electrical energy supplied is derived from clean sustain-
able energy sources. However, their Coeﬃcient of Performance is limited to a factor of 1, meaning
that the maximum thermal energy gained by heated water is at its best, the same magnitude of
the electrical energy supplied.
A better alternative to the latter, are Solar heat pump systems, which have been developed
to decrease electricity consumption required for the same application, whilst ensuring a reliable
source of heat. Solar water heaters have very minimal electricity consumption requirements, as this
is mostly related to water pumping work. However, this on its own is not a suﬃcient and reliable
source of heat, as it strictly depends on solar irradiance, which is intermittent and varies in mag-
nitude throughout the year. In order to avoid intermittent hot water production, an auxiliary heat
source is combined to the system through a ground source heat pump. This technology ensures a
better coeﬃcient of performance, whilst ensuring reliability and sustainability; in an energy system
where energy generation is derived from sustainable energy sources such as Wind Energy.
There are various conﬁgurations of this type of technology which are used in Denmark.[1] The
combination of a solar heat pump with a ground source heat exchanger is generally preferred, as
this oﬀers the possibility of having a higher Coeﬃcient of Performance for the system. A Solar
thermally assisted heat pump system oﬀer various advantages:
1. space heating and domestic hot water have a larger solar fraction
2. it is a good alternative to concepts using very large heat storages
3. Overall solar fraction can exceed 50%
4. increased yearly COP (above 5)
==PAGE:14==
5. Domestic hot water and space heating can be directly heated with solar energy if collector
temperature is suﬃcient and heating is eﬀective.
6. Low variation in ground temperature over many heating seasons
The system being tested in the pilot test facility at the Department of Civil Engineering, is com-
posed of a ground source heat pump and a solar collector, which are both connected to a heat
storage tank separately. This system also oﬀers the option of connecting the solar collector with
the ground source network, however this is not utilized yet.
Project Scope and Overview
In this project, initially the focus is on the analysis of the measured data, from both the ground
source heat exchanger and the solar heat pump system. Consequently, restructuring of simulation
model of the system in the simulation environment TRNSYS, together with the validation of the
model with the measurements is conducted.

The scope of this project is to execute a TRNSYS model of the solar heat pump system, which
is validated with the real system and successively run for an annual simulation. The model should
be designed with the following main components:
1. Heat Storage tank (tank in tank)
2. Solar collectors
3. Ground Source heat pump
The system is composed of a Solar collector and a heat pump system, which circulate heated
water to a hot water storage tank as required. The storage tank is a tank-in-tank type that allows
for domestic hot water to be stored separately within the same tank, supplying Domestic Hot water
through its respective outlet. A Space heating loop is also connected to the storage tank in order to
simulate the space heating of a house, through an external cooling system as shown in ﬁg.1.1. The
Solar collector primary loop is connected to two heat exchangers; one used for the tank heating,
whilst the other one is for ground heating. (second application is not currently connected). The
secondary loop from the solar collector is connected to the heat storage tank and the heated water
enters the tank through a stratifying pipe. This minimizes water at diﬀerent temperatures to mix.
The ground source heat pump modulates and supplies heated water at diﬀerent temperatures at
speciﬁc levels in the tank.
Test conditions: There is a Domestic Hot water tapping three times a day at 7:00, 12:00, 19:00.
Tappings takes place in three equal sizes energy amounts of 1.53 kWh, cooling water from 57°C to
20°C. It corresponds to a daily loss of 4.6 kWh. Space Heating water tapping is done four times
a day at 2:00, 6:00, 11:00, 18:00. Tappings takes place in approximate energy amounts of 6.04
kWh, 9.56 kWh, 13.73 kWh and 6.57 kWh,totaling to around 36kWh per day.The temperature at
the middle of the storage tank is kept at approx. 45°C. The space heating and domestic hot water
loops, return to the tank water which is cooled to 20 °C approximately, by means of a cooling system.
A period has been selected to validate the Trnsys model of solar heat pump system with. The
period includes the use of both solar and auxiliary heating (Heat Pump). The total period is from
10th April - 29th April 2017, divided into two periods. The period is selected according to the
following requirements:
1. There must be heating by both the solar collector and auxiliary heat pump
2. Tappings for both Domestic hot water and space heating are made everyday.
3. There must be varying sunshine throughout the whole period

Chapter 2
System Description
2.1
Heat Storage Tank
The demand of heated water varies throughout the day and the unpredictable timing and magnitude
of the heat gained through the solar collector, make it ideal to use a heat storage tank. The heat
storage is composed of a tank in tank conﬁguration, in order to store domestic hot water in the
smaller tank, which in turn is heated by the water inside the larger tank (DHW volume).
This system uses a low ﬂow mantle tank with higher stratiﬁcation possibilities, rather than a
high ﬂow system and a hot water tank with with a built-in spiral heat exchanger. The latter equal-
izes temperature diﬀerences in the hot water tank during operation periods of the solar collector.[1]
Low ﬂow systems with a highly thermal stratiﬁed hot water tank, such as a mantle tank, have
higher thermal performances than high ﬂow solar heating systems based on spiral tanks. Further-
more, there are no pipe connections in the top part of the tank. These pipe connections result
in signiﬁcant thermal bridges and thereby in a low thermal performance of the solar heat pump
system.[2]
As it was previously shown in the ﬁg.1.1, the heat storage tank has multiple connecting ports
for various functions in order to increase the eﬃciency of the whole system.[3] The following list
shows the loops connected to the tank for both extraction or supply into the heat storage tank.
1. Heat Extraction from the tank
a) Space Heating loop (SH water demanded by house)
b) Domestic Hot water loop (DHW demanded by house)
2. Heat supplied into the tank
a) Ground Source heat pump (Space heating volume)
b) Ground Source heat pump (Domestic Hot water heating volume)
c) Solar Collector secondary loop (from heat exchanger) (stratiﬁer)
The pipes within the heat storage tank are at diﬀerent heights and some are stratifying pipes
so as to minimize mixing of water at diﬀerent temperatures.[4] The exact lengths and conﬁguration
of the pipes is shown in ﬁg.2.1. Further height measurements can be found in table 5.1. The total
height of the tank is 1568mm, with a diameter of 800mm for the main tank and 450mm for the
tank in tank. The volume if domestic hot water tank is 175 litres, whilst the volume of the exterior
tank is 550 litres. (refer to Appendix A)

2.7
Solar Collector
The Solar collector is ﬂat plate type, which is composed of three parts connected together. With a
lower and upper horizontal pipes that connect eight vertical copper pipes per panel. This conﬁg-
uration should allow minimal ﬂow resistance and thus maintain an ideal low ﬂow-rate to obtain a
desired high temperature water supply as much as possible. The solar collector is run on a alcohol
glycol mixture. This primary loop is fed into a heat exchanger, which in turn heats the secondary
loop containing water that is pumped into the tank. The primary loop has two pumps in line
(TOP-525,WILO and SOLAR 25-120100, GRUNDFOS) The secondary loop contains one pump
(UPS 25-40 130, GRUNDFOS). The solar collector is also connected to a heat exchanger, which in
turn is connected to the ground source heat exchanger, that supplies the heat pump with heat. This
connection is available but is not currently used. The aim of this connection is to directly charge
the ground with any excess heat available from the storage in order to be used when necessary by
the heat pump.
2.8
Ground Source Heat Pump

which uses R134a as the refrigerant. The compressor in use is a single stage piston type compressor
(HGX 12P/110-4 S) with a power range of 3-10 kW and a frequency band between 30 - 70Hz. This
allows for modulation to occur thus allowing diﬀerent water temperature outputs as required by
the system.

2.10
Control System
Control sensors are all the ﬂow and temperature sensors connected to the control system in order
for the controller to have feedback data on the system. These are referred to in ﬁg. 2.7 as Temper-
ature sensors marked in black and ﬂow meters in orange. (See also Appendix C for full list.) The
control system monitors compares the temperature at T3 (DHW volume) and T6 (SH volume) to
the respective set temperatures. The DHW volume has the priority over the SH volume. Thus
if both volumes need to be heated simultaneously, the Heat Pump operates on the DHW heating
loop ﬁrstly.
For the heat pump to operate, Pump 5 (condensing loop) and pump 6 (evaporator loop to
ground source) must be in operation. When the DHW volume reaches the set temperature, the
motorized valve M6 returns to default position (SH mode). As for the Solar collector loop, which is
composed of a primary and secondary loop, T1 is the control temperature sensor that activates the
loops. T1 is located at the top of the solar collector, thus, when the temperature at T1 increases
more than T10 (bottom of the tank), Pump 1 and Pump 2 are started simultaneously in order to
allow the heat transfer to the tank. The heated water in the secondary loop enters the tank through
a stratiﬁer. The solar collector loop works independently from the heat pump, and introduces heat
in the tank whenever possible.
The Domestic Hot Water loop and the SH loop function independently from the system with
pre-scheduled draw oﬀs from both loops. The SH demand loop also contains a temperature sensor
in the simulated house, to signal the reduction in temperature below the set temperature, if the
system is intended to work this way. All temperatures and ﬂow rates from the control system are
recorded with a time step of two minutes.

sensors are insulated from outside, the heat losses from the heat pump contribute to a misreading

3.10
Modulating Heat Pump
The heat pump operates in a modulating manner and it operates for both Domestic Hot and Space
Heating water. From the data analysis it has been noted that the operation of the heat pump stands
mostly on the production of Domestic Hot water, with very short instances of Space Heating Water
operation. It is expected that the heat pump runs more on Space heating due to the considerate
demand for Space Heating water during the day. The Domestic Hot water operation of the heat
pump has a priority over the Space Heating operation. Reducing the operation of the heat pump
on domestic hot water operation and in turn running it on space heating, can increase the COP of
the system, since the heat pump can run on a lower temperature diﬀerence.
The question of why the heat pump is not running on Space Heating water mode more frequently
is tackled by investigating a number of queries that could explain the reason for such behaviour:
1. space heating loop draw oﬀfrom the tank could be aﬀecting the domestic hot water volume.
2. The operation of the heat pump to heat Domestic Hot water volume could be aﬀecting the

As part of the data analysis a comparison was made with the solar collector in operation and
without. From the data analysis it could be seen that the central part of the tank is being aﬀected
mainly by mixing due to the commonly used pipe, both as return and supply for DHW and SH
respectively, and less due to Space heating draw oﬀ. This clearly observed in ﬁg.3.15, where we
compare F3, F5, T29, T30 at around 40 minute time step. Thus, it is clear that at minute 40
when SH demand starts ( F5 increases), T29 remains constant initially, but a soon a the Heat
pump operation starts on SH volume heating mode, T29 decreases. This conﬁrms the signiﬁcant
inﬂuence of mixing eﬀect in the centre on the tank.
When heating for the DHW volume occurs, Space heating water is being extracted, rather than
the lower part of the DHW volume as a result of the single pipe conﬁguration. Thus, the SH volume
and the DHW volume were too close in the older version of the system.
Due to the mixing taking place in between volumes and the heat pump’s constant operation on
DHW volume heating, a system alteration is suggested whereby, a pipe is added, so as the return
for DHW volume heating and the supply of SH volume heating are separated, as shown in Fig.2.1
and Fig. 2.7. Additionally, T3 (control temperature sensor for DHW volume), is moved up by
100mm, to further separate the operation of the two volumes.
Another related issue is that of having the heat pump running on DHW for a the majority of
the time, as shown in Fig. 3.14, where F5 shows the ﬂow rate of the heat pump loop , and the
instances with a slight increase in ﬂow rate show the heat pump operation under SH heating. The
reallocation of the temperature sensor T3 is intended to solve this issue by have a less temperature
variation at T3 thus allowing SH mode to function more frequently.
3.11
Top of tank
Another observation made in data analysis is that of having T28 and T29 (top part of the tank),
with similar temperatures.(Fig.3.14) This should not be the case since the temperature sensors are
at diﬀerent height, thus diﬀerent temperature levels. T28 delays in both heating up and cooling
down, in relation to T29. It is important to note that T28 is nearly at the utmost top of the tank,
where Hot water is expelled into the tank from the heat pump. Therefore T28 should respond
quickly and with a higher temperature than the measurements show. It has been concluded that
T28 is aﬀected by an air gap at the top of the tank, thus explaining the delay in heating and cooling
and the reduced temperature when compared to the exit temperature of the heat pump DHW.
3.12
Solar collector
The solar collector data has also been analyzed. From the data measured in January an anomaly
is observed where T1 (solar collector output), reached temperatures over 100°C. T4 which is the
input temperature is also observed to increase in temperature drastically during the day. The solar
collector transfers heat to the main tank by means of two loops. The primary loop contains a glycol
mixture, which in turn heats the secondary loop ﬂuid (water) through a heat exchanger. The the
ﬂuid in the latter mention loops are circulated by means of pump 1 and pump 2 respectively. When
comparing the ﬂow rates of these loops, as shown in ﬁg. 3.16, pump 1 does not circulate water,
whilst pump two is operating. Clearly the temperature limit has been reached for pump 1 to start
as well (pump 1 and pump 2 should start together). Pump 1 eventually starts again till the solar
28
==PAGE:41==
collector cools down eventually and the pumps stop. After a period of time the limit temperature
is surpassed again and pump 2 starts without pump 1 again. Pump 1 is therefore not damaged
since it operates at times.
This suggested that the primary loop is restricted and water boiling was subsequently occurring
leading to more overheating. The ﬁltration system was check prior to this and it has been found
to be partially blocked due to particles accumulating on the ﬁlter itself. Following the cleaning of
the ﬁlter the system resumed normal operation.
However, the clogging of the ﬁltration system was derived from another cause. The reason the
ﬁlter was clogged was derived from deterioration of the glycol mixture. This was later conﬁrmed in
May 2017 when the solar collector was opened to determine the condition of the piping. And was
found to be restricted due to the sludge formed around the inner circumference of the pipe. For
this reason the primary loop is not included into the validated model.

Chapter 4
Simulation model
4.1
Introduction
The simulation model is completed in the simulation program TRNSYS. The model must reﬂect
the operation of the system in various conditions. Since the system was conﬁgured during March
and April, the ﬁnal model represents the ﬁnal version of the system in April 2017. Fig.4.1 shows the
system’s Trnsys model and its various components which will be described further in detail in this
chapter. The model simulates the main components similarly to the system, each with its adjusted
parameters to assimilate the components realistic behaviour in the model. The model is composed
of a tank in tank, which is connected to a heat pump through two pumps simulating the diﬀerent
operation of the heat pump (domestic hot water and space heating). The DHW tank inside the
heat storage is simulated as a heat exchanger. The heat pump is a water to water type and is
connected to a ground source heat exchanger, which is also set with the realistic parameters. The
solar collector is also connected to the tank through a heat exchanger, thus two pumps are included
in both the primary and secondary loop. Pump 1 simulates the two pumps that are installed on
the primary loop in the test facility. Input data ﬁles are introduced in the model in order to allow
for the model to calculate the transient operation at diﬀerent conditions inputted. Output ﬁles are
necessary to allow for the result to be stored.
4.2
Input and Output of Data
The input data required for this model includes ﬁve ﬁles, each with a set of data for the same
period of simulation to be run. The data included in the ﬁles is generally a one minute time steps,
similar to that of the measurements taken from the real system. The input data is gathered from
the measurements. These include:
1. Space Heating Demand
2. Domestic Hot water Demand
3. Weather Data
4. Indoor Temperature
5. Heat pump heating characteristics
31
==PAGE:44==
Space heating demand input ﬁle includes the ﬂowrate (F3) and the temperature of the water
returning in the tank from the house (T11). Similarly, the domestic hot water demand input ﬁle
includes the ﬂowrate of the DHW demand loop (F7) and the return temperature of the water
entering the tank from the DHW demand loop (TC). The input ﬂowrate units are adjusted from
litres per second to kilograms per second by means of a calculator as shown in ﬁg. 4.1. The indoor
temperature input ﬁle includes the input temperature where the tank is located. This is necessary
for the simulation model to calculate the heat losses from the tank. The weather input data ﬁle
includes the ambient (outdoor) air temperature and the irradiance level. This is essential to de-
termine the heat gained through the solar collector, and eventually transferred to the heat storage
tank. Finally the heat pump characteristic input ﬁle needs to reﬂect the actual heating load and
power consumption for various combinations of evaporator temperatures, condenser temperatures
and ﬂowrates the heat pump operates at.

4.3
Controls
The control system in the model plays a very important role on the outcome of the results and their
validity. Thus, it is important to ensure that all control signals within the model are reﬂecting the
operation of the actual system. The solar collector pumps are controlled through the component
Type 2b which compares the temperature T1 at the solar collector with the temperature at the
bottom of the tank T10. If the latter is lower a control signal activates Pump 1 and Pump 2.
As for the heat pump, since it is a modulating one, diﬀerent outputs are regularized to diﬀerent
volumes in the heat storage tank. Therefore, in the model, two pumps simulate the diﬀerent ﬂow
33
==PAGE:46==
rates into the tank according if its the DHW loop or the SH loop in operation. These two loops
are only operated separately, by means of the control check. A calculator is set to start Pump P5
(DHW) and similarly another one is set to start Pump P5 (SH). The temperature signals for the
DHW volume and the SH volume are linked to Start P5 (DHW) and two conditions are set for each
temperature in order to give a control signal to the heat pump and respective pump to initiate. In
addition, both signals also control the starting of pump 6 (Start P6). Pump 6 is the circulating
pump the the ground source heat exchanger, which is operated anytime the either P5 (DHW) OR
P5 (SH) are operated.

The heat pump used in the Trnsys model is a water to water heat pump (type 927). The heat
pump component is updated with all the parameters from the real conditions of the system. Such
parameters include; Rated Power, maximum Flowrate at the condensing and evaporative heat ex-
changers and other related parameters related to the operation characteristics of the heat pump as
seen from ﬁg.4.3.

4.5
Heat Storage tank
All the parameters of the heat storage tank are applied to the type 340 tank in order to simulate
the the dynamic heat transfer in and out of the tank appropriately.
Temperature Sensors
The Temperature sensors modelled in the heat storage tank are part of the monitoring system,
thus enabling for a comparison to be made between the measurements and the calculated water
temperatures in the model. Temperature sensors are distributed along the height of the tank and
include temperature sensor: T28, T29, T30, T31, T32. These are initially set to the speciﬁc height
within the tank, as in the real system. Fig.2.1.
Heat Storage Ports
This includes various doubleports which allow for separate loops connected tank to be to be simu-
lated. Doubleports are pairs of connections to the tank which are linked together for a particular
loop. These ports have speciﬁc heights as a factor of the total height of the tank to deﬁne their
positioning. These include doubleports for the following applications:
1. Solar collector loop whereby the input is deﬁned as a stratiﬁer (double port 1)
2. Domestic hot water volume heating loop (doubleport 2)
3. Space heating volume loop (doubleport 3)
4. Space heating demand loop (doubleport 4)
5. Domestic hot water demand loop (tank in tank modelled as a Heat Exchanger)
The tank is modelled as a tank in tank and is divided into 22 nodes that separate the whole
volume into segments with a common temperature for every speciﬁc node. This has an inﬂuence
on the accuracy of the model.

Heat pump performance characteristics
One of the input data ﬁles regards the heating performance characteristics of the heat pump, which
contains default performance data supplied by Trnsys. This data contains all the possible com-
binations of load and energy consumed by the heat pump at diﬀerent condensing and evaporating
temperatures and ﬂow rates. This ﬁle has been amended to match the performance data of the
heat pump as deﬁned by the manufacturer in order to make the model more accurate. Refer to
Appendix B for the heat pump performance data (manufacturer).
5.4
Validation period
The period before the 10th April 2017 has been stepped down from valid periods for comparison to
the measurements taken from the solar heat pump system, as a result of the system rebuild. A new
period is selected, that is, from the 10th April to the 19th April 2017. Initially the adjustments are
carried out to the model for the previous system in February 2017. May 2017 was not included in
the validation period due to complications with the solar collector as a result of blocked tubes and
due to the fact that other activities were carried out on the system, including the switching oﬀof
the cooling system that returns water to the space heating and domestic hot water demand loops
back into the tank. Due to continuous variation in the solar collector performance, as a result to
the blockage in the tubes; the primary loop of the solar collector loop is bypassed in the model and
the input data from the secondary loop is directly feed into the heat storage tank in the model.

5.5
Validation Results
The following results are a graphical representation of the ﬁnal results of the validated periods.
Each period is 10 days long and the ﬁrst and last days of each period are to be neglected to en-
sure stabilized results from the trnsys model (included in results). Figures of various processes of
the system are shown in this section, except for the primary Solar Collector loop, which was not
validated due to unreliable measured data available due to changes in the solar collector loop.
Figure 5.15 and 5.16 show the measured and calculated water temperatures at ﬁve diﬀerent
levels in the heat storage tank, for the whole duration of both periods respectively. The validated
temperature readings include those of temperature sensors T28, T29, T30, T31 and T32 (top to
bottom). The calculated results are consistent for both periods.
It is important to note that T29m (measured reading), has a large variation in temperature,
which does not fully match the calculated temperature (T29) at the same level in the tank. From
the data analysis carried out, it can be observed that T29m (orange graph line) exceeds the tem-
perature of T28m when the heat pump starts operating on DHW volume heating.
The eﬀect of this is due to two main reasons. The top part of the tank contains a relatively
small amount of air and thus, T28m cannot reach the highest temperature outputted in the top
part of the tank from the heat pump since the sensor T28 is in contact with the air volume. This
can be observed from the delay in heating and cooling of T28m. Another eﬀect seen by T29m is
that a certain degree of mixing is occurring due to the relative high ﬂow rate of the DHW volume
heating loop (Pump 5) and the positioning of the pipe opening at the top of the tank, leading to
a deﬂection in direction of the outgoing ﬂuid into the tank. This can be observed by the sudden
increase in temperature of T29m which is lower 309mm from the top of the tank.
This result does not aﬀect the validation results and thermal performance comparison since it
is a one sensor issue detecting localized micro mixed water temperatures. The DHW present in
the tank in tank has inertia to respond to such micro mixtures and no evident variation has been
observed in this regard.

of the solar collector as it heats the whole tank very eﬀectively through the warmer months of the
year. The use of a mantle tank with a stratiﬁer, rather than a spiral heat exchanger allows for
higher temperatures to be reached in the top layers without any mixture and heat dissipation in
the lower levels in the tank. Fig. 5.24 portrays the space heating demand through the ﬂow rate
indicated in blue. The water temperature at 12X is shown in red. 12x is situated at the Space
heating loop exit from the tank. Here we can see the variation in the warmer months with a much
higher temperature readily available. This is also counter acting since its the least period where
space heating is required. Fig. 5.25 shows the domestic hot water proﬁle used, with both ﬂowrate
and water temperature displayed in the ﬁgure.

are characterized by a SF of 1, meaning that the heat pump does not need to operate under these
demand proﬁles since all the thermal energy required is attained from the solar collector.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
6.1
Conclusions
This Thesis describes the data analysis conducted on the Solar heat pump system plant, which is
installed at the test facility in DTU Civil Engineering Department. Furthermore, it describes the
validation of the computer model of the same system. The TRNSYS simulation model validation
is conﬁrmed by showing the diﬀerence between measurements and calculations.
The solar heat pump system relies on the ground source heat pump for auxiliary heating, with
a ground source heat exchanger at 1 metre ground depth and a low ﬂow solar heating system
connected to a stratifying mantle tank with a tank in tank for domestic hot water storage.
Space heating and Domestic hot water are tapped from the heat storage tank at regular intervals.
The solar collector has been subject to high ﬂow operation due to blockages formed in the top central
part. This was a result of the degraded glycol mixture that adhered to the hot tube surfaces in low
ﬂow area where hot spots formed.
With the ground temperatures being 2°C lower due to the eﬀect of the ground source heat
exchanger, the overall eﬃciency is less than expected. A larger area distribution or further depth
are the factors that decrease this negative eﬀect.
The system was rebuild in March 2017, allowing for the installation of a new domestic hot water
volume return to the heat pump and an improved pipe and temperature sensor conﬁguration in
the tank that reduces mixture and improves eﬃciency.
The thermal performance of the system was calculated for a diﬀerent domestic hot water and
space heating demand than that being tested in the facility and the weather data is based on data
extracted from the DTU climate station for the year 2016.
A yearly simulation is complete with the validated Trnsys model. The simulation is made for a
diﬀerent domestic hot water and space heating demand than that being tested in the facility and
the weather data is based on data extracted from the DTU climate station for the year 2016.
There is a Domestic Hot water tapping three times a day at 7:00, 12:00, 19:00. Tappings takes
place in three equal sizes energy amounts of 1.53 kWh. It corresponds to a daily loss of 4.6 kWh.
Space Heating water tapping is done four times a day at 2:00, 6:00, 11:00, 18:00. Tappings takes
place in approximate energy amounts of 6.04 kWh, 9.56 kWh, 13.73 kWh and 6.57 kWh,totaling to
around 36kWh per day.The temperature at the middle of the storage tank is kept at approx. 45°C.
The space heating and domestic hot water return water to the tank is cooled to 20 °C approximately
by means of a cooling system.
The results gathered using the validated Trnsys model of the solar heat pump system show
that: The model’s overall global deviation of the period from the measurements to the calculations

is that of 1.5%, without including the solar collector primary loop; whilst the combined COP and
combined SCOP of the period are of 2.60 and 2.29 respectively.
Yearly simulation using the validated Trnsys model of the solar heat pump system show that:
From the annual simulation it is concluded that the system runs on an solar factor larger than 0.5
for 6 months of the year under space heating and domestic hot water proﬁle. However it runs on
a very low solar factor for the rest of the year.
The thermal performance for the tested system is lower than the thermal performances of the
most eﬃcient tested heat pump systems available.
The reason is that the solar collector loop has a relatively high ﬂowrate and therefore less high
temperatures are achieved, leading to having a low net coverage ratio and a larger dependency on
the heat pump. The horizontal ground source heat exchanger oﬀers a relatively low temperature
due to the depth factor and the period tested, that is; for the ﬁrst half of the year in comparison
to other systems. The heat pump performance is poor in itself. Heat losses reductions can also
improve the overall performance.

Future work
• Integrate the primary solar collector loop in the validated model.
• Choose validating period in colder months
• Integrate the solar to ground heat exchanger and analyse gathered data.
• Simulate diﬀerent control strategies by which heat is transferred to recharge the ground in
certain period of the year
• Compare system with other systems available when the solar collector loop is included. [7]
