Preface
This Master’s thesis ”Evaluating p-y curves for non-slender monopiles from cone penetration
tests” is conducted during the Spring of 2016 at the M.Sc. in Civil Engineering at Technical
University of Denmark (DTU) in cooperation with COWI A/S.
The thesis is divided in to two parts. The ﬁrst part reﬂect on modelling p-y curves. First a
literature study is made. Then the methodology explained. Hereafter the results are presented
and then compared. After, the same approach is done for the investigation of small strains.
The thesis sums up both parts in the end with a discussion and conclusion. The results from
both parts in this thesis is very site speciﬁc and should be tested and evaluated if the methods
were to be applied to another site.

Summary (English)
During the last Unitied Nations climate meeting an agreement was reached by the participat-
ing countries. That there is need for an increase in the sustainable way of producing energy,
to lower the green house eﬀect. By building oﬀshore wind turbines in large farms a great
amount of energy can be harvested. In a sustainable and environmental healthy process.
Oﬀshore wind turbines can be built on diﬀerent kinds of foundations depending on the condi-
tions of where the turbines are built. Most wind turbines today (medio 2016) are built upon
a monopile foundation. A monopile is classiﬁed as a non-slender pile with its low length to
diameter ratio. To calculate the lateral loads on monopiles, which is the most critical load
p-y curves can be established. The p-y curves are developed in the oil and gas sector where
they are used for slender piles. These does not consider small strains and the large diameter
eﬀects of the monopiles correctly. To establish p-y curves, parameters are gained from Cone
Penetration Test (CPT) at a speciﬁc site. Today the parameters obtained from CPTs are
used to evaluate relative density and friction angle.
In this thesis four diﬀerent methods
are investigated to evaluate p-y curves from cone resistance. In this way assumptions and
considerations is not needed for when calculating relative density or friction angle. The four
methods tested, are developed by Bouaﬁa (2014), Li et al. (2014), Suryasentana & Lehane
(2015) and Kim et al. (2015). These are compared in ultimate lateral resistance, friction angle
and diameter with the American Petroluem Institute (API), which is the method most used in
todays engineering practice. The API is evaluated in two diﬀerent ways. First Jamiolkowski
et al. (2003) which evaluates relative density and then friction angle. The second way is eval-
uated by Mayne (2007) which calculate friction angle directly. The methods tested showed an
increase in ultimate lateral resistance. Furthermore they showed a decrease in friction angle
and that the diameter of the monopile have an inﬂuence on the lateral soil resistance.
Small strains are also investigated as these are currently calculated in the same procedure for
slender piles as for non-slender monopiles. Four diﬀerent methods are tested and compared.
The methods tested are composed by Kallehave et al. (2012), Kirsch et al. (2014a), Sørensen
& Augustesen (2016) and Suryasentana & Lehane (2015).
The ﬁrst three mentioned are
based on Jamiolkowski et al. (2003) with the API (2007) formulation for p-y curves. The
methods modify the API formulation to achieve a higher initial stiﬀness modulus. The last
method establishes the p-y curves regarding to cone resistance were the authors state that
their method considers small strains on monopiles. The methods showed diﬀerent results.
Two of the methods showed equal or lower result compared with API. The last two showed
an increase with the factor of two compared with API. The software LPile was used to compare
the diﬀerent methods. It showed that the method of Suryasentana & Lehane (2015) obtained
the highest resistance, while Kirsch et al. (2014a) had lowest resistance of lateral load.

Introduction
This thesis is written to analyse diﬀerent methods able to establish p-y curves for monopiles
directly from the information obtained from CPT. An analyse of methods is also made on
how to modify small strain. As these are currently evaluated for slender piles.
1.1
Introduction
During the last United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), cli-
mate meeting in Paris (COP21) in December 2015. An agreement was reached by the partici-
pating countries to lower the emission of greenhouse gases and secure the rate of which the
temperature rises to below the rate of 2◦C. (COP21, 2015)
Wind energy is a green, sustainable process to harvest renewable energy and one of the most
eﬀective. Oﬀshore is currently a popular way to build wind farms as to onshore wind farms,
as oﬀshore wind farms can utilize the wind better and do not disturb neighbours around
them visually or with noise. The Danish Energy Agency (DEA) has conducted a research
on how oﬀshore wind farms aﬀect the nature around them such as ﬁsh, birds or mammals.
Their reports shows only a minor impact, with the most critical eﬀect of disturbing birds that
has to ﬁnd new hunting grounds (Energistyrelsen, 2016). The downsides with oﬀshore wind
farms is that they cost more to produce and the maintenance cost is higher than onshore
wind farms. There is set an objective by the European Wind Energy Association (EWEA).
That by 2030 around 320 GW will be harvested by wind farms. Of these 320 GW, 66 GW
are to made by oﬀshore wind farms. In total, this will mean that 24.4% of the energy needed
in Europe will be harvested by wind farms and will be a larger energy source than coal and
gas. In 2015 there was 12.6 GW harvested by oﬀshore wind farms. This means that there
needs to be built more oﬀshore wind farms and more eﬀective ones to reach the goal in 2030
of 66 GW. (Corbetta, 2015)
Of all the oﬀshore wind turbines built today, approximately 80% is built upon a monopile
foundation (Ho & Mbistrova, 2015). Monopile foundations is subject to larger horizontal
forces from wind and waves than vertical force acting on the windmill. Therefore, these forces
are most focused on when a monopile foundation is considered. The forces are considered
as static loading in this thesis as to cyclic, sustained or dynamic loading. To estimate the
horizontal forces and displacement of monopiles, p-y curves can be established. To estimate
the p-y curves, parameters such as relative density (Dr) and/or friction angle (φ) are used.
These can be calculated from a cone penetration test(CPT). The p-y curves is as a method
ﬁrst used in the oil and gas sector. But over the years, more often seen in the oﬀshore wind
turbine sector.

1.2
Initial problem
In this thesis, the diﬀerent methods on how to use the parameters gained from Cone Pene-
tration test (CPT) to establish p-y curves for non-slender monopile will be investigated and
compared. In total six methods are tested. The ﬁrst two methods are based on Jamiolkowski
et al. (2003) and Mayne (2007) together with API (2007) (American Petroleum Institute)
design regulations. These two are amongst the most common way to interpret CPT data and
to establish p-y curves. The other four methods used are recently develop by Bouaﬁa (2014),
Li et al. (2014), Suryasentana & Lehane (2015) and Kim et al. (2015). The methods are
develop and based on slender ﬂexible piles. As monopiles are considered as rigid non-slender
piles. The eﬀect of diameter of the monopile is examined as diﬀerent diameter piles are tested
under the same conditions.
Three CPT test are given by COWI A/S and consists mainly of sand. The CPT data is
collected in the North Sea which often consists of a thick layer of sand. In the North Sea,
large waves have compacted the sand to a high relative density, Dr. The sand is typical very
dense and reaches a relative density of 90% or higher. The data is interpret and used to
establish p-y curves with the six diﬀerent methods and then compared.
The p-y curves are also investigated on how small strains eﬀect non-slender monopiles as
these are currently approximated as if the monopiles were slender. The monopile is modelled
in a program called LPile where p-y curves can be used as input for soil layers. The method
which will be used for evaluating parameters is Jamiolkowski et al. (2003) with the API (2007)
formulation of p-y curves. The p-y formulation will be evaluated for a new initial stiﬀness
modulus by implementing three methods that modiﬁes initial stiﬀness modulus (Kallehave
et al. (2012), Kirsch et al. (2014a) and Sørensen & Augustesen (2016)) and one method
develop for p-y curves Suryasentana & Lehane (2015).

Monopiles
Monopiles is the foundation most often used when oﬀshore wind farms are constructed
(Doherty & Gavin, 2011). There are also other types of foundations e.g. jacket foundation,
gravitational foundation, bucket foundation, tripods and ﬂoating foundation. A few of these

Monopiles are used up to a water depth of 35 meters and penetrated 20-30 meter into the
soil. After 35 meters water depth, a jacket foundation is more eﬀective. Floating foundations
has been tested for larger water depth but there are still some questions remaining on how
safe they are. With larger water depths come an increase of the length of the cables needed
to connect the wind turbine with the power transmission station which is then connected to
land.

2.1. Monopiles
Chapter 2. Literature Study: CPT and p-y curves
Though with increasing water depth monopiles are given a larger diameter and a stiﬀer cross-
section. Today (medio 2016) a diameter of 4 to 6 meter is used for most monopiles. An increase
in depth will also result in a larger moment force on the foundation, as the distance increase
between turbine and seabed. The diameter of monopiles is increasing due to the desire to gain
larger eﬀect in GW from the turbines. The monopile consists of a open-ended steel pipe that
is driven into the seabed. On top of the steel pipe a transition piece is connected by a grouted
connection. The last piece to connect is the wind turbine itself. All connection are assumed
rigid which means that deformation in the connections are not considered (Klinkvort, 2012).
Typical, monopiles for oﬀshore windfarms have a slenderness ratio of L
D < 10. Where L is
the length of the monopile into the seabed and D is the diameter of the monopile (Sørensen
et al., 2009).
The resulting forces acting on a monopile foundation can be seen on Fig. 2.2, the horizontal
and moment forces are most critical in design of this type of foundation (Doherty & Gavin,
2011). The vertical load consists of the weight of the tower and turbine. The vertical load is
carried by the toe and the shaft resistance of the monopile. The horizontal and moment forces
is due to wind, current and waves acting on the wind turbine. These forces are transferred
to the soil next to it as the forces deﬂect the monopile in to the soil, causing the soil to be
pushed in the same direction as the forces. This means that the soil at the top is most critical,
given that it has less overburden soil.
Figure 2.2: Sketch of resulting forces acting on a monopile.
The forces in this thesis are focused on static loading. Static loading is a short term loading.
This type of loading is rarely the case for monopiles, though it gives a good estimate of the
p-y curves and a possibility to evaluate other loading types (Reese & Van Impe, 2001).

2.1.1
Failure of lateral loaded piles
A pile can reach failure in two modes. Failure of a pile can occur when the pile rotates as
one, this failure mode is called rigid. The failure is caused when the soil resistance is less
than the soil pressure along the pile. The second failure mode is called ﬂexible. This failure
will occur when the yield or ultimate resistance of the pile is less than the bending moment.
This failure mode will create a plastic hinge at the maximum moment (Broms, 1964). Failure
modes are illustrated in Fig. 2.3.

2.2
CPT
The CPT has been used for a long time to measure cone resistance, which is the total axial
force per unit cross sectional area of the cone tip. Today a drill rig connected to a computer can
measure cone resistance qc, sleeve friction fs and pore pressure u. The idea with preforming
CPT is to:(Lunne et al., 1997)
 Determine the structure of the soil layers and what type of soil is present at the location.
 Achieve geotechnical parameters.
 Use of the parameter for geotechnical designs.
There are currently many diﬀerent ways on how to interpret a CPT for axial bearing capacity
for axial loaded pile. These have been produced from diﬀerent test site around the world.
The most used methods are API, NGI, ICP, Fugro and UWA (API, 2007), (Clausen et al.,
2005), (Jardine et al., 2015), (Xu et al., 2008) and (Lehane et al., 2005).
2.2.1
CPT in sand
Sand contains diﬀerent kinds of rock minerals, e.g. quartz, feldspar, mica or calcite. Bolton
(1986) tested diﬀerent type of sands and found that sand which contains more feldspar has a
higher friction angle than sand containing mostly quartz (Bolton, 1986).
Sand can be cemented under high pressure if it contains carbonates, silica, hydrous silicates
or hydrous iron oxides. This gives a cohesion eﬀect between the grains. Which can be seen
on a CPT proﬁle by the rise in cone resistance. This phenomenon is often neglected when
interpreting CPT. Therefore, the data can be given a higher cone resistance than it truly has
and result in over-estimation of soil strength (Lunne et al., 1997).

Soils with a positive dilatancy has an increasing structure when it is endured with shear
strain, this decreases the pore water pressure. Soils inﬂuenced by a negative dilatancy has
decreasing structure, increasing the pore water pressure. The negative dilatancy occurs to
soils which has a low relative density and vice versa (Schmertmann, 1978). Sand with positive
dilation angle and high relative density should therefore have an increase in ultimate lateral
resistance (Sørensen et al., 2012a).
The Soil dilatancy angle can easily be calculated with the Eq. 2.3. Therefore, it will not be
calculated in the result section. As the Eq. 2.3 is only a good estimate. To determine the
dilation angle, a triaxial tests is required, which is expensive.
2.4
p-y curves
When modelling lateral loaded monopiles, the p-y method is the most commonly used in
engineering practice. p is the soil resistance force per unit pile length and y is the horizontal
displacement of the pile. The monopile is modelled as a beam with elastic foundation and
the soil surrounding is modelled as springs when the Winkler approach is applied (Sørensen
et al., 2012a). A schematic of the Winkler approach can be seen in Fig. 2.5.
Figure 2.5: Sketch of Winkler approach of a pile, after Sørensen et al. (2012a)
The Winkler approach considers the springs as individual springs and continuity of the soil
is therefore not considered by this approach. It means that the springs do not consider the
springs above or below it. Which gives the springs freedom to move independently and implies
that there is no friction between the soils.

Chapter 2. Literature Study: CPT and p-y curves
The p-y curves consists of to main parts the ultimate lateral resistance pu and the initial
stiﬀness modulus Epy. The maximum lateral resistance indicates that the soil has reach a
plastic behaviour. Implying that if shear strain increases there is no loss of shear strength.
There are a few methods when modelling p-y curves for lateral loaded piles. Four methods
where tested by (Murchison & O’Neill, 1984). Two of these are the most widely used today.
The ﬁrst is derived from Reese et al. (1974). The second method is derived from Murchison &
O’Neill (1984) in their report ”An Evaluation of p-y Relationships in Sands”. These methods
are used today by design codes such as Eurocode, API and DNV (Det Norske Veritas). There
is a ﬁnite element method (FEM) for calculting p-y curves. This method gives a good result
of deformations and resistance. The downside with this method, is that it consumes both
time, CPU and it also depends on the accuracy of the model (Sørensen et al., 2012a). The
FEM method will not be further described in this thesis.

The small strain for monopiles are investigated as the p-y formulation from API (2007) are

The small strain is today
considered a more important design criteria for monopiles than the ultimate lateral resistance.
Usually when designing oﬀshore windfarms the load is seen as i cyclic load. Though the initial
part of a p-y curve is similar for a static load and cyclic loads (Sørensen & Augustesen, 2016).
The small strain can be evaluated by calculating the true initial stiﬀness modulus is given by

ferance Oﬀshore Foundations a presentation given by Kirsch (Kirsch, 2014b). The article
and conference presentation suggest a modiﬁcation to the initial stiﬀness and ultimate lateral
resistance of the p-y curves formulated from the API (2007). The ultimate lateral resistance
is adjusted by reducing the friction angle. The initial stiﬀness is adjusted by calculating the
Young’s modulus of elasticity for the soil, Es and the Young’s modulus of the soil at low
strains, Esd. Es is evaluated by the stiﬀness modulus G which is gained from shear veloc-
ity of the soils (Vs) for shear-waves. This method lowers the ultimate lateral resistance as
the initial stiﬀness modulus is increased. As the other methods do not change the ultimate
lateral resistance, the ultimate lateral resistance will only be compared with the API (2007)
formulation.
The third method tested for evaluating initial stiﬀness modulus is evaluated by Sørensen &
Augustesen (2016). They established a 3D FEM model of a monopile and validated the FEM
model with laboratory test made in a pressure tank in Sørensen et al. (2012) and the oﬀshore
wind turbines at Horns Rev located west of Esbjerg, Denmark.
Sørensen & Augustesen
(2016) implies in their article that initial stiﬀness modulus depends on parameters such as

Discussion
In this section, both parts in this thesis are discussed. The ﬁrst part studied methods that
established p-y curves directly from CPTs using cone resistance. Instead of calculating relative
density and friction angle prior to establishing p-y curves. The second part studied methods
for modifying the initial stiﬀness of the method used by API. To clarify, these methods are
evaluated on CPTs gained from COWI. The result is not to be used generally, as they are
site speciﬁc. If used for another site new interpretations should be evaluated.
10.1
First part
The ﬁrst part of the thesis studied the four alternative methods A to D in chapter 3, for
establishing p-y curves. Comparing them with two of the most used methods in engineering
practice, the API and DNV. The methods were evaluated mainly on the parameter of cone
resistance. The methods A to D showed a higher ultimate lateral resistance, with a factor of
20 for the smallest diameter when compared with the methods where the friction angle was
calculated. The higher ultimate resistance could be caused by, that too many assumptions are
inﬂuencing the calculations of the most widely used methods by Jamiolkowski et al. (2003)
and Mayne (2007) with the API (2007) formulation of p-y curves. The back-calculation of
friction angle gained a unrealistic result, due to that they were at the lowest, approximatley
10◦lower than the friction angle calculated with Jamiolkowski et al. (2003). Some of the
results of the ultimate lateral resistance and friction angle, matched at some depth but not
consistent throughout the proﬁle.
By comparing the methods. The new methods suggest that the API method is conservative
in ultimate lateral resistance at shallow depths. They also suggest that in most cases, the
API method over-predict the friction angle. Resulting in a higher resistance towards lateral
load and a higher initial stiﬀness. Though these results are gained from the test site used by
this thesis does not necessarily propose that the same result would be gained at other test
sites. The inﬂuence of diameter was studied and showed that a smaller diameter has a larger
relative mass of soil it has to move. The 2m diameter pile showed a larger lateral resistance
than the 6m diameter pile.
The friction angles was back-calculated for methods A to D. Eq. 3.13 is solved for φ. This
equation is stated to be valid only for friction angles between 28◦< φ < 45◦. The calculated
friction angle was evaluated in a range of 27.62◦< φ < 49.86◦. It was estimated that the
diﬀerence in friction angle seen in the ranges does not aﬀect the result in a remarkable way.
Even though an increase in friction angle of 5◦for Method B could be seen as a great diﬀerence.
The Eq. 3.13 is not further studied for higher friction angles.

at other test sites.
Kirsch et al. (2014a) shows the lowest resistance towards lateral load (Fig. 10.1) and also
obtains a higher reference depth to where p = 0 (Fig. 10.3B). An increase of pile length is
needed to receive the same lateral load resistance as the method by Jamiolkowski et al. (2003)
with (API, 2007) formulation. Approximately an increase of 1.2m in embedded length was
calculated in LPile before the methods gave the same result in resistance towards lateral load.
The methods tested in both parts should be tested for other sites to see if the similar result
is gained. As they are very limited in this thesis to the test site gained from COWI. For
an appropriate investigation a full-scale test and then comparison of the methods. However,
full-scale tests are expensive. The methods could also be compared to a FEM monopile with
the parameters gained from this speciﬁc site, to observe which method obtains the closest
result.

11
Conclusion
This thesis tested alternative methods for establishing p-y cures directly from CPT, were
relative density or friction angle is not calculated as needed for the API formulation tradi-
tionally used when designing monopiles. Also, studied in this thesis, is the evaluation of small
strains as these are currently evaluated as for slender piles and typical the design criteria for
monopiles. The API formulation for p-y curves is the most used method in engineering prac-
tice today when designing monopiles. The API formulation is established in the oil and gas
sector, for piles with a diameter smaller than 2m. This thesis has evaluated and tested four
other methods capable of establishing p-y curves of monopiles directly from a CPT. Two of
the methods tested, directly stated that they could be used on non-slender monopiles. The
methods tested gave diﬀerent results due to that they are originally based on diﬀerent test
sites and methods. As the methods in this thesis were tested to one speciﬁc site received by
COWI. The result are very site speciﬁc and only validated for the given site.
11.1
First part
The Method A, by Bouaﬁa (2014) seemed to only work for the 2m diameter monopile as
the curves are too linear when a larger diameter is used with the same embedded length.
The method gave non-linear curves when length was set close to the methods ﬂexible/rigid
criteria. This could be interpreted as misleading, as the larger piles needed large increase in
embedded length for the method to model non-linear curves. The 6m monopile had to have
a length of 46m before it could give a reasonable result. The article of Method A also never
mentions that it is evaluated for monopiles. Only that it considers the cone resistance and
sand as the soil material. Still it gives an interesting aspect as API is evaluated in the same
conditions.
The Method B, by Li et al. (2014) was easy to work with as there were few parameters
needed for establishing p-y curves. However, this also makes the methods results uncertain.
The article does not describe how the ultimate lateral resistance is evaluated and therefore it
is discussed whether to use the same equations as the method it is based on or an equation
evaluated by Reese et al. (1974), as Method D also uses this equation.
Ultimate lateral
resistance was calculated with the method it is based on (Suryasentana & Lehane, 2014),
which gave a high ultimate lateral resistance. The method also evaluated the highest friction
angle for the top soil layers which indicates that the method received a high initial stiﬀness
for these layers.
Method C, by Suryasentana & Lehane (2015) gave both high ultimate lateral resistance and
a modiﬁcation to initial stiﬀness modulus. The shape of the p-y curves are debatable as they

The ﬁrst of the three methods is evaluated by Kallehave et al. (2012). It gives the highest
initial stiﬀness modulus of all the tested methods. The method considers the initial modulus
of sub-grade reaction of the API method, depth and diameter. The low references values used
in the equation could explain why this method returns the highest initial stiﬀness modulus.
The use of the sub-grade reaction modulus from API could be regarded as one this methods
downsides. This is due to that the friction angle is then used which is evaluated by steps of
considerations and assumptions.
The second method based on Kirsch et al. (2014a) and Kirsch (2014b). Approximately double
the value of sub-grade reaction modulus for the 6m diameter monopile. The method considers
Young’s modulus of elasticity for soil, diameter and friction angle as the initial modulus of
sub-grade reaction is based on this parameter. The same discussion on how much the friction
angle inﬂuences the result can also be discussed as in the previous method. This is the only
method where the friction angle is reduced. This is done by taking the inﬂuence diameter in
to consideration. This works well for the 6m diameter pile but it does not change for the 2m
diameter pile, which is also when pile could be considered as a slender pile. The idea with
reducing the friction angle is to lower the ultimate resistance and increase the initial stiﬀness
modulus.
The third method by Sørensen & Augustesen (2016) gives a low increase in initial stiﬀness
modulus. The method considers depth, diameter and Young’s modulus of elasticity for soil.
The method involves a lot of constants. Some of these constants are ﬁtted to a test result
at a speciﬁc test site. Which make this method possibly to only work at the test site used
in the article. By resembling this methods with the other methods. It does not consider
the sub-grade reaction modulus. Therefore, the friction angle does not have inﬂuence on this
parameter. Which could be evaluated as one of the positive aspect for this method as relative
density and friction angle are not evaluated from Jamiolkowski et al. (2003).
The last method tested for this part is the method evaluated by Suryasentana & Lehane
(2015). This method is also used in the ﬁrst part and referred to as Method C. As written
in the result section only the ﬁrst part is considered as the small strains are investigated.
The method gives a rather low initial stiﬀness. When comparing Suryasentana & Lehane
(2015) with the original method from Suryasentana & Lehane (2014) there can be observed
a small increase of initial stiﬀness in the beginning of the p-y curves, though the increase
was expected to be higher. The method considers many parameters which is seen as positive.
The low initial stiﬀness modulus could be caused by the shear modulus calculated. The shear
modulus is evaluated from Mayne (2007) and a equation given for all type of soils. If the
equation for evaluating shear modulus for sand would have been used instead.
