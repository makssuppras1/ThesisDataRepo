Abstract
Patients with hematological malignancies (HM), such as chronic lymphocytic leukemia
(CLL), are prioritized for COVIDôÙ19 vaccination due to their increased risk of severe
complications and higher mortality from SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 infection. Understanding T cellôÙ
mediated immunity in this vulnerable population is essential, particularly in the context
of hybrid immunity, which arises from the combination of natural infection and vaccinaôÙ
tion.
This study assessed whether SARSôÙCoVôÙ2ôÙspecific CD8+ T cells exhibit crossôÙreactivity
with related viral antigens, potentially influencing the breadth and durability of the imôÙ
mune response. Furthermore, SARSôÙCoVôÙ2ôÙspecific immune responses elicited by the
BNT162b2 mRNA COVIDôÙ19 vaccine were evaluated in CLL patients with different exôÙ
posure backgrounds, including those who remained uninfected and those who experiôÙ
enced breakthrough infections postôÙvaccination.
PeptideôÙMHC tetramers targeting specific immunogenic SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 epitopes were
used to characterized crossôÙreactive CD8+ T cell responses in healthy postôÙpandemic
donors. Minimal crossôÙreactivity of SARSôÙCoVôÙ2ôÙspecific CD8+ T cells was observed,
indicating limited immunity to SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 from exposure to HHVôÙ2. Additionally, imôÙ
munodominant SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 epitopes were analyzed to characterize CD8+ T cell reôÙ
sponses in HM patients. Findings demonstrate that hybrid immunity enhances CD8+ T
cell responses, leading to increased activation and functional capacity. Notably, spikeôÙ
specific CD8+ T cell responses were higher in infected HM patients compared to uninôÙ
fected patients, suggesting that hybrid immunity strengths T cellôÙmediated protection.
Furthermore, variable expression of killer cell immunoglobulinôÙlike receptors (KIRs) was
observed, which could indicate that KIR expression may influence the cytotoxic funcôÙ
tion of CD8+ T cells in response to SARSôÙCoVôÙ2.
In conclusion, this study highlights the critical role of hybrid immunity in enhancing CD8+
T cell responses in CLL patients and provides insights into the limited crossôÙreactivity
of SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 specific T cells. These findings have important implications for optimizôÙ
ing vaccination strategies and improving protective immunity in immunocompromised
individuals.

Introduction
The COVIDôÙ19 pandemic, caused by the severe acute respiratory syndrome coronôÙ
avirus 2 (SARSôÙCoVôÙ2), has had a significant global impact resulting in over 776.8
million cases and more than 7 million deaths worldwide [1]. Although COVIDôÙ19 is no
longer classified as a public health emergency, SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 persist and the potential
for emerging variants remains a concern [2]. While most infected individuals experience
mild to moderate symptoms, immunocompromised populations, such as patients with
hematological cancer are at substantially higher risk of severe disease and increased
mortality [3].
Patients with hematological malignancies (HM) are more susceptible to infections and
face higher risk of severe COVIDôÙ19 outcomes due to immune deficiencies associated
with their disease and immunosuppressive treatments. Although vaccination offers proôÙ
tection, their immunocompromised immune system often results in reduced vaccine
efficacy [4, 5]. This concern is heightened by the exclusion of HM patients from main
clinical trials of COVIDôÙ19 vaccines, leaving uncertainties regarding vaccine efficacy,
immune response durability and protection levels in this vulnerable population [6].
TôÙcell mediated immunity has been proposed to be of high importance in disease conôÙ
trol, with robust CD8+ T cell responses correlating with improved disease outcomes
and longôÙterm immunological memory [7]. However, knowledge regarding the cellular
immune response to SARSôÙCoVôÙ2, particular in HM patients, is still limited [8].
The aim of this project is to investigate the T cell specific response to SARSôÙCoVôÙ2
hybrid immunity in hematological cancer patients by functional and phenotypic assessôÙ
ment of CD8+ T cells. Assessing this response is essential for effective vaccine recomôÙ
mendations and could potentially reduce infection and disease severity. The following
sections provide background for the experiments conducted in this project. They deôÙ
scribe development, activation and effector functions of cytotoxic T cells and examine
the impact of CD8+ T cells on immunocompromised outcomes of COVIDôÙ19.
1.1
The Immune System
The immune systemãs primary function is to protect the body from harmful pathogens,
including viral and microbial infections, cancer cells and toxins. The immune system
can be divided into two lines of defenses: innate immunity and adaptive immunity [9].
The innate immune system is the first line of defense against foreign pathogens and
relies on pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) [10]. These receptors are expressed
on innate immune cells, such as macrophages, neutrophils and dendritic cells (DCs)
1
==PAGE:9==
and recognize pathogen associated molecular patterns (PAMPs) produced by many miôÙ
croorganisms [11]. The binding of PAMPs by PRRs triggers internalization and phagoôÙ
cytosis by macrophages, monocytes and DCs, as well as apoptosis by natural killer
(NK) cells [10]. Additionally, the innate immune system recruits other immune cells
to the site of infection by producing inflammatory mediators typically cytokines and
chemokines [9]. Antigens from foreign pathogens are presented on the cell surface
of antigenôÙpresenting cells (APCs) such as DCs, which helps to activate the adaptive
immune response [10].
Unlike the innate immune response, the adaptive immune response develops over
longer time. However, it can eliminate pathogens more effectively, because of its tarôÙ
geted antigen recognition through highly specific receptors [11]. Additionally, the adapôÙ
tive immune system possesses immunological memory, enabling it to respond more
quickly to subsequent exposure with the same foreign pathogen [10]. The adaptive
immune system is mainly mediated by B cells and T cells that provide a humoral and
cellôÙmediated response, respectively [9]. B cells recognize antigens through B cell reôÙ
ceptors (BCRs) expressed on their surface. Upon encountering an antigen, B cells
can become activated and differentiate into plasma cells. These plasma cells secrete
antibodies, which can bind to pathogens and facilitate neutralization through various
effector functions [10].
T cells recognize foreign antigens through their T cell receptors (TCRs), which, opôÙ
posite to the BCR, specifically recognize short peptide fragments of antigens. These
peptide fragments are presented by major histocompatibility complex (MHC) molecules
on the surface of APCs. In humans, the MHC is also referred to as the human leukoôÙ
cyte antigen (HLA) complex [11]. The antigen presentation process stimulates T cells
to differentiate into either cytotoxic T cells (CD8+ T cells), which recognize antigens
presented on MHC class I molecules, or T helper cells (CD4+ T cells), which recognize
antigens presented on MHC class II molecules. CD8+ T cells are primarily involved in
eliminating virusôÙinfected or cancerous cells by inducing apoptosis in the target cells.
In contrast, CD4+ T cells play an important role in mediating the immune response by
releasing cytokines that direct the activity of other immune cells [9].
This work primarily focuses on CD8+ T cells.
1.1.1
Antigen processing and presentation by MHC class I
MHC class I molecules are expressed on most nucleated cells and present peptide
fragments derived from cytosolic proteins. For MHC class I molecules to present self
and nonôÙself peptides to CD8+ T cells, the peptides must first be processed through the
ubiquitinôÙproteasome pathway. In this process, proteins are tagged for degradation by
ubiquitylation. These tagged proteins are delivered to the proteasome, where the polyuôÙ
biquintin chain is removed, and the proteins are cleaved into peptides ranging from 2ôÙ25

amino acids (aa), which are released into the cytosol (see Figure 1.1). Subsequently,
peptides are transported from the cytosol into the endoplasmatic reticulum (ER) via the
transporter associated with antigen processing (TAP). This transporter consists of two
subunits, TAP1 and TAP2, which most efficiently transport peptides of 8ôÙ12 aa in length.
While longer peptides can also be transported, their efficiency is significantly reduced.
These longer peptides may be further processed in the ER or transported back to the
cytosol for additional processing. Once inside the ER, peptides are loaded onto MHC
class I molecules with the help of the peptideôÙloading complex. After successful bindôÙ
ing, the peptideôÙMHC class I complexes (pMHC) travel to the cell surface via the Golgi
apparatus. At the cell surface, these complexes present antigens to the cognate TCR
on CD8+ T cells, initiating an immune response. Peptides derived from selfôÙproteins
are normally ignored by CD8+ T cells under normal conditions, preventing an immune
attack against healthy cells [12, 13]. In addition, certain subsets of DCs can present
extracellular proteins via MHC class I molecules called crossôÙpresentation. This proôÙ
cess is essential for priming CD8+ T cells and initiating a cytotoxic T cell response [11].
The ability of the immune system to recognize a wide range of pathogens relies on the
genetic diversity of MHC molecules. These molecules are highly polymorphic, meaning
that each type of MHC molecule exists in many different variants. In humans, the clasôÙ
sical HLA molecules are divided into two main classes: HLA class I (including HLAôÙA,
HLAôÙB and HLAôÙC) and HLA class II (including HLAôÙDR, HLAôÙDP and HLAôÙDQ). Each
individual inherits one HLA haplotype from each parent, resulting in a total of six MHC
class I alleles and six MHC class II alleles [11, 14].

1.1.2
T cells
T cells are essential for combating viral infections, targeting intracellular pathogens and
contributing to longôÙterm immunity. They progress through distinct stages of developôÙ
ment, activation, effector function and memory formation [15].
T cell development
T cells derive from multipotent hematopoietic stem cells in the bone marrow. However,
the T cell progenitors migrate from the bone marrow through the bloodstream to the
thymus, where they undergo maturation. When the progenitor cells first enter the thyôÙ
mus, they do not express surface molecules typical of mature T cells and their receptor
genes are not rearranged. These cells are referred to as ãdouble negativeã thymocytes,
as they lack both CD4 and CD8 expression [11]. The double negative thymocytes can

The diversity of the TCR is based on three complementary determining regions (CDRs)
located in the variable regions of each TCR chain. CDR1 and CDR2 primarily interact
with the MHC molecule, while the hypervariable CDR3 region mainly interacts with the
bound peptide [17]. The CDR3 region undergoes conformational changes to facilitate
binding to the pMHC complex, playing a key role in determining the antigen specificity
of the TCR [16]. Due to the flexibility of the hypervariable CDR3 region, a single TCR
can adopt multiple conformations to accommodate variations in peptide sequences.
This, referred to as crossôÙreactivity, enables a single TCR to recognize a wide range
of pMHC complexes. It has been estimated that an individual TCR can recognize beôÙ
tween 10,000 ã 10,000,000 different MHCôÙassociated epitopes, significantly enhancing
the immune systemãs ability to respond to diverse antigens [18, 17]. However, once a
TCR binds to a specific pMHC complex, the likelihood of it binding to another random
pMHC complex reduces significantly. Thus, TCR recognition of pMHC complexes is
both crossôÙreactive and highly specific, enabling T cells to recognize previously unenôÙ
countered pathogens while maintaining specificity for a limited set of epitopes. This
dual capability allows the immune system to mount a rapid and targeted response [18].
After TCR recombination, T cell development in the thymus proceeds with positive
selection, where only T cells that can interact with selfôÙMHC molecules are selected.
Positive selection is mediated by cortical thymic epithelial cells (cTECs), which present
various peptides on MHC class I and II molecules. DP cells whose TCR fail to bind
to selfôÙpeptides die by neglect, while those with TCRs that binds weakly to the pMHC
complex survive and mature. At this point, the cells commit to a lineage, differentiating
into either CD4+ or CD8+ T cells [11, 19].
Following positive selection, the T cells proceed to negative selection, where medullary

molecules. T cells that recognize these selfôÙderived peptides with high affinity are elimôÙ

T cell activation
Following infection or vaccination, APC such as DCs, uptake pathogenôÙderived antiôÙ
gens and migrate from the site of infection to secondary lymphoid organs. There, they
present these antigens on their MHC molecules to the T cell. The interaction between
the T cell and the APC is crucial for the initiation of T cellôÙmediated immunity, a process
facilitated by three distinct signals (Figure 1.2). The first signal occurs when the TCR
recognizes a specific peptide presented by MHC on the surface of an APC. In CD8+
T cells, this interaction is stabilized by the CD8 coôÙreceptor binding to the MHC class
I molecule [11]. Additionally, the CD3 complex, which is present on all T cells, assoôÙ

second signal is a coôÙstimulatory interaction between the CD28 receptor on the T cell
and its ligands, B7.1 (CD80) and B7.2 (CD86) on the APC. The signal is essential for
promoting T cell division and differentiation. Lastly, a third signal involves cytokines
such as ILôÙ2, IFNôÙöÝ/öý and IFNôÙö°, which can be secreted by the APC or the surrounding
environment. These cytokines drive the differentiation of the activated T cell into speôÙ

is upregulated and helps regulate T cell retention within lymphoid tissue [21]. ContinôÙ

effector functions and the expression of inhibitory molecules such as PDôÙ1 [22].
Activated CD8+ T cells can kill their target by different mechanisms resulting in apoptoôÙ
sis of the target cell. One of the primary mechanisms by which CD8+ T cells eliminate
infected cells is through the release of cytotoxic granules containing cytotoxic effector
proteins such as perforin and granzyme. Perforin facilitates the formation of pores in
the membrane of the target cell which causes direct damage to the cell but also allows
granzyme to enter. Once granzyme is delivered to the cytosol of the target cell it inôÙ
duces apoptosis [11]. During viral infection, CD8+ T cells also secrete proôÙinflammatory
cytokines such as TNFôÙöÝ/öý and IFNôÙö° [15]. IFNôÙö° acts by inhibiting viral replication and
enhances MHC class I expression which increases the likelihood of recognizing inôÙ

target cell surface, initiating signaling pathways that lead to cell death [11]. The majority
of effector CD8+ T cells undergo apoptosis once the infection is cleared [15].

T cell memory
After clearance of infection, only a small fraction of effector CD8+ T cells differentiate
into memory cells, which can rapidly proliferate upon reôÙexposure to the antigen, ensurôÙ
ing a robust immune response [23]. Memory T cells are typically categorized into three
subsets based on their phenotypic surface markers: central memory T cells (TCM),
effector memory T cells (TEM) and terminally differentiated effector memory cells reôÙ
expressing CD45Ra (TEMRA). TCM cells express the chemokine receptor CCR7 enôÙ
abling their migration to secondary lymphoid tissues, where they can proliferate and
differentiate upon reôÙexposure. In contrast, TEM cells lack CCR7 allowing them to miôÙ
grate to peripheral tissues. These cells respond rapidly to infections by maturing into
effector T cells and exerting their effector functions [24, 11]. TEMRA cells, which also
lack CCR7, reôÙexpress the leukocyte common antigen isoform CD45RA, a marker typôÙ
ically associated with naû₤ve T cells [25]. TEMRA cells have been shown to be more
cytotoxic and exhausted than TCM and TEM cells [26].
1.1.3
KIRs and their role in immune regulation
Killer IimmunoglobulinôÙlike receptors (KIRs) are a family of receptors mainly expressed
on NK cells, but they are also found on some subsets of CD8+ T cells [27]. KIRs play a
critical role in regulating immune responses by interacting with MHC class I molecules
on the surface of target cells. Depending on the specific receptor engaged, this interacôÙ
tion can either activate or inhibit NK cell functions [28]. On CD8+ T cells, inhibitory KIRs
have been shown to modulate TCR signaling and suppress CD8+ T cell responses,
7
==PAGE:15==
whereas activating KIRs can enhance T cell responses [27].
The nomenclature of KIRs is based on their structural and functional properties. The
KIRs are classified according to the number of extracellular domains (2D or 3D) and the
length of their cytoplasmic tail (L for long and S for short). The length of the cytoplasôÙ
mic tail also reveals the protein function, where KIRs with a long cytoplasmic tail deliver
inhibitory signals through immunoreceptor tyrosineôÙbased inhibitory motifs (ITIMs). In
contrast, KIRs with short cytoplasmic tails associate with the signaling adapter protein
containing immunoreceptor tyrosineôÙbased activation motifs (ITAMs), mediating activaôÙ
tion signals [29].

SARSôÙCoVôÙ2
SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 belongs to the Coronaviridae family, a group of viruses commonly found
in both animals and humans.
Structurally, SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 shares significant similariôÙ
ties with other coronaviruses such as severe acute respiratory syndrome coronavirus
(SARSôÙCoV) and Middle East respiratory syndrome coronavirus (MERSôÙCoV). SARSôÙ
CoVôÙ2 primarily infects the upper respiratory tract, causing symptoms that vary from
mild, common coldôÙlike symptoms to severe illness, potentially leading to acute respiôÙ
ratory distress syndrome (ARDS) [30].
SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 is an enveloped virus with a diameter of approximately 60 to 140 nm.
The viral membrane is covered by the spike (S) glycoprotein, which enables the virus
to enter the host cell. The S protein consists of two functional subunits: S1 subunit
containing the receptor binding domain (RBD) responsible for binding cell surface reôÙ
ceptors on the host and S2 subunit, which facilitates fusion between the viral and host
cell membranes [31]. The RBD domain of SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 binds specifically to the anôÙ
giotensin converting enzyme 2 (ACE2), which is mainly present in the lung, intestine,
heart and kidney [32]. The viral envelope consists of a lipid bilayer containing the memôÙ
brane (M) glycoprotein and the envelope (E) protein [30]. During infection, both the M
and E proteins are required for the assembly and release of virions. However, the M
protein is the most abundant structural protein during infection and plays a crucial role
in the viral replication cycle as the primary driver of virus assembly [33, 34]. Within the
viral membrane, the singleôÙstranded RNA (ssRNA) genome is organized in a helical
manner associated with the nucleocapsid (N) protein [31]. The main role of the N proôÙ
tein is to encapsulate the viral RNA genome. However, it has also been shown to be
important for initiating replication and transcription [35].
The genome of SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 is a positiveôÙsense ssRNA genome of approximately
30,000 bases with a 5ã terminal cap and a 3ã polyôÙA tail (Figure 1.3). The genome
includes fourteen open reading frames (ORF) that encode sixteen nonôÙstructural proôÙ
teins (nsp1ôÙ16), four structural proteins (S, M, N and E) and a number of accessory
proteins [35].

1.2.1
Immune response to SARSôÙCoVôÙ2
The innate immune response against SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 is mediated via alveolar macrophages
and DCs [37]. These cells express PRR such as RIGôÙI, MDAôÙ5, TLR7 and TLR8 which
detect viral RNA and initiate a signaling cascade. This cascade triggers the release of
proinflammatory cytokines and type I IFNs. These IFNs exert various antiviral effects,
including blocking viral entry into the cells, degrading viral genetic material, enhancing
antigen presentation via MHC class I, inhibiting protein synthesis, inducing apoptosis
in infected cells and activating macrophages, NK cells and T cells. In most COVIDôÙ19
patients, recruited immune cells clear the virus in the lungs, terminating the immune reôÙ
sponse and leading to recovery. However, in some patients, the virus evades the innate
immune response, causing a delayed type I IFN response and excessive recruitment of
proinflammatory cells, such as neutrophils and macrophages. This can lead to severe
disease including pneumonia, ARDS and septic shock [31].
The adaptive response to SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 involves both humoral and cellular components.
Neutralizing antibodies play a crucial role in preventing viral entry into host cells, while
memory B cells provide ongoing protection by preventing reinfection. SARSôÙCoVôÙ2
specific CD8+ T cells are essential for eliminating infected cells by producing cytotoxic
cytokines such as IFNôÙö° and TNFôÙöÝ. CD4+ T cells are pivotal in orchestrating the adapôÙ
tive immune response. They promote B cell maturation and antibody production while
also enhancing the activation of CD8+ T cells [38, 31]. Dysregulation of T cell responses
may contribute to disease severity. Consequently, severe COVIDôÙ19 is more common
in older or immunocompromised individuals [31]. Additionally, subsets of both CD4+
and CD8+ T cells differentiate into memory cells. These can circulate throughout the
body and respond rapidly to secondary antigen exposures, facilitating virus clearance
and ensuring longôÙlasting immunity [39].
1.2.2
CrossôÙreactivity
PreôÙexisting CD8+ T cells capable of recognizing SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 have been identified in
unexposed individuals, indicating that T cells which were initially primed against other
pathogens are able to crossôÙrecognize SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 antigens [40]. Such crossôÙreactive
immune responses may impact disease outcomes of COVIDôÙ19 by influencing suscepôÙ
tibility to infection and disease severity [41].
Most studies have focused on crossôÙreactive T cells primed by other human coronôÙ
aviruses due to high genomic sequence homology. However, crossôÙreactive cytomegalovirus
(CMV)ôÙ and EpsteinôÙBarr virus (EBV)ôÙspecific CD8+ T cells have been detected in unôÙ
exposed donors, suggesting that previous exposure to CMV or EBV might affect the
immune response to SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 [40, 42]. Understanding crossôÙreactive immune reôÙ
sponses to SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 may help understand the heterogeneity of COVIDôÙ19 clinical
outcomes and identify conserved epitopes which could aid in the development of vacôÙ

1.2.3
SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 vaccines
The development of vaccines against SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 has been crucial in controlling COVIDôÙ
19, reducing disease severity and lowering mortality rates [43]. Vaccines are designed
to mimic natural infection which stimulates the immune system to generate protective
response [44]. Most vaccine candidates target the S protein due to its ability to inôÙ
duce both neutralizing antibodies and T cell responses [43] However, the emergence
of SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 variants with mutations in the S protein has challenged vaccine efficacy.
In particular, the Omicron variant, characterized by numerous unique mutations in the
S protein, has been shown to evade neutralizing antibody response, correlating with
an increased capacity for reinfection and reduced vaccine efficacy. This underscores
the importance of optimizing vaccine formulations and booster strategies to maintain
protection against emerging variants [45, 46].
In addition, hybrid immunity, developed from a combination of vaccination and natuôÙ
ral infection, has been shown to provide broader and more durable protection against
SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 and its variants.
Studies have shown that breakthrough infections in
vaccinated individuals enhance spikeôÙspecific T cell responses and generates a diôÙ
verse repertoire of T cells capable of recognizing various SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 variants [47,
48]. Understanding the durability and breadth of hybrid immunity is crucial for vaccinaôÙ
tion strategies against new variants, as it can guide the design of vaccines that elicit
longôÙlasting and crossôÙreactive immune responses.
1.3
Hematological malignancies
Blood cancers such as chronic lymphocytic leukemia (CLL) significantly impact the imôÙ
mune system, primarily due to its origin in immune cells and the effects of treatment
modalities. This malignancy originate in the bone marrow and disrupt the normal proôÙ
duction and function of blood cells. This leads to compromised immune function and
increased susceptibility to infection [49].
CLL is the most common form of leukemia in adults [50]. It is characterized by the acôÙ
cumulation of malignant B cells in the peripheral blood, bone marrow and lymph nodes
[51]. These malignant B cells exhibit phenotypic markers commonly associated with
regulatory B cells, including the surface expression of CD5, CD27 and CD24. RegulaôÙ
tory B cells contribute to immune suppression primarily through the secretion of ILôÙ10,
which negatively affects immune cell responses. This immune dysregulation impairs
adaptive immunity, leading to reduced T cell activity and decreased survival and funcôÙ
tions of plasma cells, resulting in low serum antibody levels [52]. CLL also causes
innate immune deficiencies, including impairment of the complement system, which reôÙ
duces the ability to opsonize bacterial pathogens. Additionally, functional defects are
observed in neutrophils, monocytes, DCs and NK cells [50]. These immune dysfuncôÙ
tions contribute to an increased incidence of secondary malignancies and infections,
which represent the primary causes of morbidity and mortality in CLL patients [52].

1.3.1
COVIDôÙ19 in hematological cancer patients
Patients with hematological malignancies (HMs) are more susceptible to severe COVIDôÙ
19 due to immune deficiencies associated with their disease and the immunosuppresôÙ
sive effects of their treatments [5]. Consequently, these patients face higher rates of
hospitalization, prolonged viral shedding and an increased risk of mortality [53, 8]. Their
compromised immunity limits their ability to mount robust immune responses to SARSôÙ
CoVôÙ2, highlighting the critical importance of effective vaccination strategies in this popôÙ
ulation [54].
Cancer patients, including those with HM, have been prioritized for COVIDôÙ19 vaccinaôÙ
tion globally, receiving both primary and booster doses due to their higher risk of disôÙ
ease. However, as these patients were excluded from main clinical trials, uncertainties
remain regarding the vaccine efficacy, the strength of humoral and cellular responses
and the potential risk of vaccineôÙrelated adverse events [6]. While mRNA vaccines
have been pivotal in controlling the spread and severity of COVIDôÙ19, their efficacy
is reduced in HM patients [43, 8]. These patients exhibit weaker immune responses
to vaccination, characterized by lower seroconversion rates and impaired T cell activôÙ
ity, emphasizing the need for further research to optimize vaccination protocols [54,
8]. Additionally, a study indicate that HM patients undergoing active treatment, exhibit
diminished antibody responses to vaccination [54]. To address these challenges, vacôÙ
cination strategies should be refined, potentially incorporating more frequent booster
doses and heterologous vaccine regimens to enhance immune protection in this imôÙ
munocompromised group [55].

Conclusion
This study aimed to investigate the functional and phenotypic characteristics of hybrid
immunity to SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 in individuals with hematological malignancies. The findings
highlight the limited crossôÙreactivity between vaccineôÙ and infectionôÙspecific CD8+ T
cells, as well as between SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 and HHVôÙ2, suggesting minimal crossôÙprotection
from prior exposures. Additionally, some HM patients exhibited minimal spike crossôÙ
reactivity between SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 and the Omicron variant, indicating potential immune
recognition across variants, though at low frequencies.
In uninfected HM patients, vaccination with the BNT162b2 mRNA vaccine elicited variôÙ
able CD8+ T cell responses, with one patient displaying a predominance of naive T
cells, while another exhibited a more differentiated phenotype. Importantly, hybrid imôÙ
munity in HM patients, was associated with enhanced SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 specific CD8+ T
cell responses, suggesting that exposure to infection contributes to a broader and more
functional immune response. The increased frequency of spikeôÙspecific CD8+ T cells
in infected HM patients compared to uninfected patients underscores the role of hybrid
immunity in strengthening TôÙcell mediated protection.
Functional assessment of SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 specific CD8+ T cells revealed heterogeneity in
cytokine secretion, with some patients demonstrating limited functional activity despite
high antigenôÙspecific T cell frequencies. This may be attributed to T cell exhaustion, as
evidenced by increased PDôÙ1 expression in some individuals. The detection of funcôÙ
tionally active, longôÙlasting memory CD8+ T cells up to 24 months postôÙvaccination
suggests that booster doses play a crucial role in maintaining cellular immunity in imôÙ
munocompromised individuals. Additionally, this study highlights the role of KIRs in
modulating CD8+ T cell responses. The presence of inhibitory KIRs on SARSôÙCoVôÙ2
specific CD8+ T cells may suppress immune activation. However, further assessments
incorporating blockade strategies are needed to investigate the role of KIRs in immune
responses in HM patients.
Overall, these findings emphasize the importance of hybrid immunity in shaping roôÙ
bust and functional SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 specific CD8+ T cell responses in HM patients. While
vaccination induces detectable immune responses, prior infection appears to enhance
the frequency of CD8+ T cells. These observations have important implications for opôÙ
timizing vaccination strategies for immunocompromised individuals to ensure durable
protection against SARSôÙCoVôÙ2 and its emerging variants. However, given the small
patient cohort, future research involving larger cohorts with comprehensive analysis of
samples across all time points is necessary to better understand the phenotype and
functionality of SARSôÙCoVôÙ2ôÙspecific T cell responses HM patients.

Future perspectives
While this study provides important insights into the role of hybrid immunity in HM paôÙ
tients, several aspects could be further explored. Expanding the cohort size would
improve the reliability of the findings and allow for a more comprehensive understandôÙ
ing of CD8+ T cell responses in both infected and uninfected HM patients. A larger
cohort would also enable more robust statistical analyses.
Additionally, to confirm crossôÙreactivity findings in healthy individuals, singleôÙcell RNA
sequencing could be used for a detailed analysis of individual cells. This approach
would help identify crossôÙreactive clonotypes by capturing variations in gene expresôÙ
sion and TCR specificity.
Furthermore, future studies could use the enzymeôÙlinked immunospot (ELISpot) assay
as a highly sensitive method for assessing T cell function. ELISpot allows for measureôÙ
ment of cytokine secretion at the singleôÙcell level, providing a more precise assessment
for quantifying low frequency immune responses. This technique could help determine
the effectiveness of CD8+ T cell responses and their role in protective immunity.
