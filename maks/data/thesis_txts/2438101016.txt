Abstract
Tilting-pad journal bearings (TPJB) are commonly used by the industry in turbomachinery
due to their excellent stability properties. The main drawback is the lack of damping at high
rotational speeds, which can be overcome by using a squeeze-ﬁlm damper attached to their
housing (passive approach) or active radial oil injection (mechatronic approach). TPJB with
active oil injection (ATPJB) uses high-pressure oil to adjust the rotor position and increase
damping.
Modelling these controllable bearings is not a trivial task from the viewpoint
of ﬂuid dynamics and strong ﬂuid ﬁlm forces couple the lateral movements of the rotor to
the bearing housing and foundation. In this framework the entire rotor-bearing-foundation
system must be considered and the coupled dynamics modelled. In this paper the complex
coupling between rotor, bearing, and foundation dynamics is described by a multi-physics
model instead of approaching the foundation dynamics via equivalent impedance.
Individual components of a test-rig composed of a ﬂexible rotor, ATPJB, pedestals, and
ﬂexible foundation are discretised using FE methods in MatLab and ANSYS, based on the
geometrical and material properties of each component. By implementing solid 3D models
in ANSYS and using Craig-Bampton reduction, the accuracy and eﬃciency of the model is
evaluated.
Experimental modal analysis (EMA) is used to measure the natural frequencies, damping
ratios and mode shapes of the coupled rotor-bearing-foundation system. The results are
used to validate the mathematical multi-physics model.
The theoretical and experimental results ﬁt with a high degree of accuracy, the ﬁrst 7 the-
oretical and experimental natural frequencies and mode shapes deviate less than 8%. More
tuning and optimisation of the model is recommended, particularly the coupling between
ATPJB and other components. Nevertheless the current model provides useful insights to
how these rotor-bearing-foundation systems can be described and is a step closer towards
regulating the ATPJB using model-based control design.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The concept of rotation is one of the most important elements in the development of human
civilization. It is prevalent at all levels of technology from wheels that grind corn into ﬂour
to turbines that propel airplanes around the world. It is a key concept for manufacturing,
transportation, energy production and everyday life. From a mechanical standpoint rota-
tion is one of the most eﬃcient ways to generate and transfer energy and it is therefore not
surprising to see it utilized so widely in both industry and everyday life. The continued de-
velopment of rotating machinery is therefore essential for pushing technological boundaries
and keeping up with the needs of society.
However, these rotating machines and components have their limitations. One of the main
problems when operating and maintaining such machinery is vibration. Everything vibrates
in some way or another and in some cases this is favorable. Music for instance relies en-
tirely on some manner of vibrating object to produce pleasant sounds. In most industrial
applications however, vibrations are a problem that have to be dealt with. The most famous
examples of this is the spectacular collapse of the Tacoma Narrows bridge in 1940 which
truly showed the dangers of uncontrolled vibrations.
For rotating machinery there are generally two ways to treat the issue. Clever design and
construction can change the inherent dynamics of a component so the resonant frequencies
lie outside the operational range. The second way is to choose the right bearings for the
rotating parts. Diﬀerent bearings have diﬀerent dynamic properties and choosing the right
bearing can have a huge inﬂuence on the overall dynamics of the machine. In turbo machin-
ery tilting-pad journal bearings (TPJB) have become widely used for their ability to decouple
the forces acting on the journal and negating oil induced instabilities. The main drawback is
that very little damping is provided by these bearings and it is therefore usually not possible
to cross the ﬁrst critical speed. This problem can either be overcome by using a squeeze-ﬁlm
damper (passive approach) or active radial oil injection (mechatronic approach), of which
the latter is the focus of this project.
Active tilting-pad journal bearings (ATPJB) combines the beneﬁts of dynamic lubrication,
from TPJBs, with static lubrication by injecting oil radially at high pressure[1]. By using
control theory to adjust the injection pressure, the position of the rotor can be controlled
4
==PAGE:16==
which in turn changes the system dynamics. Eﬀectively this means that damping can be
increased at critical speeds, expanding the operational range of the machine. Once devel-
oped, these hybrid bearings can greatly beneﬁt the industry and improve manufacturing
capabilities of both goods and energy.
At the Technical University of Denmark (DTU) a test-rig has been set up to develop and
test ATPJBs. It consists of a rotor supported by a ball bearing and ATPJB, fastened to a
cast iron T-slot plate. By attaching discs to the rotor it is possible to simulate the conditions
of driving a turbine or compressor. The purpose of the test-rig is to design a controller that
can successfully adjust the oil-injection pressure of the ATPJB to dampen the vibrations of
the rotor and drive it at higher velocities than previously possible. There are two ways of
designing the controller, it can either use experimental data deﬁning the dynamics of the
test-rig or it can be model-based. The model-based controller relies on a mathematical model
that calculates the dynamics of the test-rig and will therefore be limited by the accuracy of
the model. Modelling the test-rig has proven to be particularly diﬃcult as the vibrations
of the rotor, bearing, and foundation are strongly coupled through the ﬂuid ﬁlm forces in
the bearing. Therefore the entire rotor-bearing-foundation system must considered in the
modelling. Previous projects have linked rotor-bearing and rotor-foundation models of the
test-rig which substantially improved the accuracy when compared to experimental data.
Even so, the models were still too unreliable and more reﬁnement of the ﬁnite-element (FE)
modelling is necessary for the model to be useful. In this project a multi-physics model is
developed further to describe the rotor-bearing-foundation system dynamics instead of using
an equivalent impedance based approach to the foundation.
1.1
Project Statement
The overall goal of the project is to model the rotor-bearing-foundation system and validate
it with experimental results from the test-rig using experimental modal analysis (EMA).
This is done by modelling the most important components of the test-rig in ANSYS and
MatLab using FE methods. Each component is discretised based the geometry and material
properties and the full system is coupled in MatLab.
To prepare the model for use in
a controller for the ATPJB it is then reduced using the Craig-Bampton (CB) method of
reduction. Once the model has been built it is validated in three steps using EMA. First the
test-rig dynamics are measured with impact testing in ’free-free’ conditions by suspending it
in the air from a crane. These results are used to validate the model without the inﬂuence of
damping from the damping mat. The impact test is then repeated with the test-rig resting
on the damping mat, which is used to estimate the damping from the mat. Finally the
test-rig dynamics are measured while the rotor is being driven to simulate realistic operating
conditions of the system.
Aside from improving the accuracy of the model as much as
possible the minimum number of degrees of freedom (DOFs) required in the model should
also be investigated. The steps of the project can summarised as follows.
• Validate the ANSYS foundation model (solid elements) using experimental results.
• Couple the ANSYS foundation model with rotor, bearing, stiﬀeners, and pedestals in
5
==PAGE:17==
MatLab.
• Measure the dynamics of the test-rig under ’free-free’ conditions by suspending it in
the air.
• Measure the dynamics of the test-rig when resting on the damping mat.
• Measure the dynamics of the test-rig when resting on the damping mat with the rotor
running.
• Based on the ﬁrst two experiments, determine the damping contributed by the damping
mat.
• Validate the rotor-bearing-foundation model by comparing the model results to the
measurements of each experiment.

Chapter 2
ATPJB Test Rig
The test-rig was designed to test ATPJBs under realistic circumstances that could be found
in industrial compressors. It has undergone numerous modiﬁcations since it was originally
built to improve testing capabilities and control of the ATPJB. For instance, the foundation
was placed on a mat to dampen vibrations and stiﬀeners were added to increase the pedestal
stiﬀness. Figure 2.1 shows the test-rig as it was setup during this project. Call-outs show
the important components of the test-rig that are relevant to understanding and modelling
it. These components are all ﬁxed on the foundation using ’T’ shaped slots in it.
Figure 2.1: Photo of the full test-rig with call-outs to important components.
At the left end (drive end) the drive motor is located. It is coupled to a drive coupling
9
==PAGE:21==
using a V-belt so it can drive the rotor. The drive coupling is connected to the rotor via a
ﬂexible coupling that uses a special joint so only rotation can be transmitted. It decouples
the dynamics of the drive motor from the rotor while maintaining the ability to transmit
rotation. The ball bearing which supports the left end of the rotor is housed in the blue
pedestal, located right after the coupling point between rotor and ﬂexible coupling. Three
sensor pedestals are mounted on the test-rig, two of them between the ball bearing and
magnetic bearing, and one of them at the far right end. These can be used to measure the
horizontal and vertical displacements of the rotor but were not used in this project. Because
their size and mass is very small relative to the foundation and other components, these
pedestals are neglected in the mathematical model. After the second sensor pedestal a mag-
netic bearing is located which can be used to excite the rotor without being in contact with
it. Because this pedestal is not in direct contact with the rotor, it is questionable whether or
not it should be included in the model despite its much larger mass. Directly to the right of
the magnetic bearing the rotor is supported by the ATPJB, housed in a large grey pedestal.
This bearing provides both hydrodynamic lubrication by injecting oil using a low-pressure
pump and hydrostatic lubrication by using a high-pressure pump. Both of these pumps are
located on the ﬂoor next to the foundation. The two diagonally mounted valves supply the
high-pressure oil and the vertically mounted valve provides the low-pressure oil. Finally at
the right end of the rotor discs can be mounted on it to simulate the rotor driving a machine.
For the setup in this project a single 80mm thick disc is mounted as shown on the ﬁgure.
The most important components when modelling the test-rig dynamics are the ones relating
the foundation to the rotor. They consist of the rotor, the discs that can be mounted on it,
the ATPJB, the pedestals coupling the rotor and foundation, the stiﬀeners, the foundation,
and the damping mat. These components are all described in detail in the following section
to understand how they can be simpliﬁed and modelled.

Active Tilting-Pad Journal Bearing
The ATPJB is the most important component of the test rig as its development is the whole

The pedestal itself is made of aluminium and the housing is made of steel, giving it a total
weight of roughly 70kg. Inside the bearing housing are four tilting-pads located at diagonal
angles. They are designed in such a way that they can pivot slightly about the point they
are attached, giving the eﬀect of decoupling the journal forces and countering oil induced
instabilities.

==PAGE:24==
Figure 2.5: CAD model of the ATPJB pedestal and interior geometry, illustrating the high

The functionality of the bearing can be divided into two parts, hydrodynamic lubrication and
hydrostatic lubrication. The hydrodynamic lubrication is achieved by ﬁlling up the bearing
with oil using a low-pressure pump. The oil is fed from the pump into the bearing through
the vertical valve at the top of the pedestal, shown on Figure 2.5. From here the oil enters
the bearing cavity through four nozzles placed at 90 degree angles within the bearing, ﬁlling
it up. One of these nozzles are highlighted on the interior cross-section of the ﬁgure. As the
oil passes through the bearing it runs out at the bottom where it is collected and sent back
to the pump to be reused. When the rotor is in operation the interaction between the rotor,
oil and bearing creates the hydrodynamic eﬀect.
Hydrostatic lubrication is achieved by injection oil radially at high pressure using a high-
pressure pump. The oil is fed into the bearing using the two servo valves placed at the top
diagonals of the bearing as seen on the ﬁgure. Through internal piping the servo valves are
connected to two pads at opposite diagonals. Oil can thereby be injected directly at the
rotor at high pressure, changing its position within the bearing to add damping. The ﬂow
direction can be reversed in each nozzle if necessary. One of the high-pressure valves and
pad inlets are also shown on Figure 2.5.

Mathematical Modelling
Once the most important components of the test-rig, in terms of its dynamics, have been
established the mathematical model can be constructed. A CAD model of the test-rig with
these components is shown on Figure 3.1. These components are highlighted and the ﬁgure
shows how and where diﬀerent components in the model can be coupled. For instance, the
left end of the rotor should be coupled with the top of the ball bearing pedestal and stiﬀeners.
Comparing this ﬁgure to Figure 2.1 it is obvious that a lot of smaller components have been
excluded, of which the most signiﬁcant are the drive motor and the magnetic bearing. While
these two components are not directly coupled to the rotor their mass might be signiﬁcant
enough to aﬀect the coupled vibrations of the system. They therefore are not discretised and
modelled but should be kept in mind as including their mass in the system might increase
accuracy.
Figure 3.1: CAD model of the components included in the mathematical model.
Each component in the ﬁgure is simpliﬁed and discretised using FE methods based on its
16
==PAGE:28==
geometry and material properties. In other words, each component is modelled individually
and then coupled with the other components.
A summary of the element type used to
discretise each element is given below.
• Rotor - 2 node beam elements
• Disc - 1 node lumped mass
• Pedestals - 2 node beam elements
• Stiﬀeners - 3 node truss elements
• ATPJB - 2 node special bearing element
• Foundation - 20 node brick elements (ANSYS)

3.1
Rotor
The rotor can be described as a long asymmetric beam with varying thicknesses.
It is
therefore convenient to model it using beam elements with a round cross-section and Euler-
Bernoulli beam theory. The theory presented for the rotor model and disc model in the next
section is primarily based on [7]
As there are practically no axial loads on the rotor it can be assumed that it will not deform
in the axial direction. In addition, as the rotor is made of steel it can also be assumed
that the rotor will not be subjected to torsional deformation. Thereby two DOFs can be
eliminated, axial and torsional deformation. It is also reasonable to assume that the lateral
deformations of the rotor will be very small as it is very stiﬀ, meaning that any angles of
rotation will also be small. This assumption is useful in the derivations as it simpliﬁes some
of the equations. It should also be noted that the rotor has been manufactured without
any signiﬁcant unbalances, therefore it can be assumed that it has no unbalances. These
assumptions are summarised below.
• The rotor is ﬂexible
• The rotor is not subjected to axial and torsional deformation
• Only small angles of rotation
• Shear forces are neglected (Euler-Bernoulli)
• The rotor has no unbalances

3.3
Pedestals
The rotor is supported by the ball bearing pedestal at drive end and the ATPJB pedestal at
non-drive end, both of which are modelled using beam elements. The theory presented for
the pedestal model is primarily based on [3]. The following assumptions are made for the
pedestals.
• The pedestals are ﬂexible
• Shear forces are neglected
• The bottom node can only deform in the DOFs of the foundation
• The top node of the ball bearing pedestal can only deform in the DOFs of the rotor
• The top node of the ATPJB pedestal can only deform in the DOFs of the ATPJB pads
23
==PAGE:35==
Both pedestals are modelled using rectangular 12-node beam elements which are connected
to the foundation at the ﬁrst node. At the second node the ball bearing pedestal is directly
connected to the rotor, because it is assumed the ball bearing is completely stiﬀ, while the
ATPJB pedestal is connected to the ATPJB pads. Each node has 6 DOFs which are trans-
lations in the xyz-directions and rotations about the xyz-axes. Because it is assumed that
the pedestals will deform in the DOFs of the components they are attached to, some of these
DOFs will be locked when building the FE model.
A sketch of how the pedestals are discretised as a 12-node beam element is shown on Figure
3.6, illustrating the 6 DOFs on the ﬁrst node. The geometry is highly simpliﬁed and is given
by the width, height and length w × h × l which has to be tuned to approximate the shape
of each pedestal. It should also be noted that the x-axis of the coordinate system goes in
the direction of the pedestal length.

3.5
Active Tilting-Pad Journal Bearing
The ATPJB is the central component of the test-rig as the whole purpose of the rig is to test
and develop ATPJBs. The combination of hydrostatic and hydrodynamic lubrication inside
the bearing is what creates the highly complex coupling between rotor and foundation that
necessitates a physics based rotor-bearing-foundation model. The model that determines
the dynamic coeﬃcients of the bearing was developed in previous projects and will only
brieﬂy be discussed for the reader to understand how it works and interacts with the other
components. First a summary of how hydrodynamic and hydrostatic lubrication works is
given to better understand the bearing. This summary is primarily based on [8].
29

3.5.1
Hydrodynamic Lubrication
Hydrodynamically lubricated bearings generate a load-carrying force through the relative
motion of two surfaces separated by a ﬂuid ﬁlm (hence dynamic). This describes many dif-
ferent types of bearings but in this section only hydrodynamic lubrication of journal bearings
is covered, as these most closely resemble the ATPJB. Journal bearings are used to support
rotors by carrying radial loads and can in their simplest form be described as a cylindrical
housing wrapped around a part of the rotor (also called the journal). The rest of the bearing
is ﬁlled with a suitable lubricant, in this case oil. These types of bearings have very little
friction and are generally reliable and cheap to use. As these bearings are self-acting as long
as lubrication is supplied they can be deﬁned as passive bearings.
When the rotor is not in use it will rest at the bottom of the bearing due to gravity, as shown
on the left of Figure 3.9 (note that the clearance is greatly exaggerated). Once the rotor
starts rotating it will drag oil along its surface due to friction, forcing it into the converging
gap between itself and the housing. Assuming a no-slip condition the oil at the bearing
surface will move at u = Ωr and decrease to u = 0 towards housing wall. Because the
ﬂuid ﬁlm thickness decreases towards the bottom of the bearing, oil is being forced into
a decreasing area. As oil is incompressible this will create pressure locally and generate a
radial lifting force pushing the rotor and housing wall apart, allowing the oil to move through.
In other words, the ﬂuid ﬁlm thickness gradient combined with the rotation of the rotor is
what creates the hydrodynamic eﬀect keeps and the rotor levitating within the bearing. This
means that the rotor will normally not run concentric, as shown on the right of Figure 3.9.
The displacement from the bearing centre is called the eccentricity. When in operation, the
rotor will self adjust and reach an equilibrium position within the bearing where the load is
balanced by the pressure generation. The load carrying capacity of the bearing depends on
many factors, including the eccentricity, rotational velocity, lubricant viscosity and bearing
dimensions.
Figure 3.9: Hydrodynamic principle of journal bearings.
There are a few important limitations of journal bearings that should be kept in mind.
Firstly, during start-up there is little to no ﬂuid ﬁlm between the rotor and housing, as
was shown on Figure 3.9. This means the two surfaces will slide against each other and
30
==PAGE:42==
cause wear due to friction, until the rotor reaches enough speed to separate them. Similarly,
there is a chance of the ﬂuid ﬁlm breaking down when changing direction or stopping, which
can severely aﬀect the lifetime of the bearing.
Secondly, depending on the load it may
not be possible to generate enough hydrodynamic pressure to maintain the ﬂuid ﬁlm at
low rotational velocities. Finally, If the rotor reaches the exact centre of the housing and
runs concentrically with it, there will be no ﬂuid ﬁlm thickness gradient and therefore no
hydrodynamic pressure. This can result in the rotor becoming unstable which causes serious
problems and should therefore always be avoided. One way this can happen if the rotor
reaches a high enough angular velocity.
3.5.2
Hydrostatic Lubrication
While hydrodynamic bearings rely on relative motion of diﬀerent surfaces to maintain a ﬂuid
ﬁlm and separate them, hydrostatic lubrication requires no motion and maintains the ﬂuid
ﬁlm by an external pressure source (hence static). The main characteristics of these bearings
are low friction, high-load carrying capacity which is independent of the rotor velocity, and
that they are generally more complicated than hydrodynamically lubricated bearings. As
these bearings rely on an external pressure source they can be deﬁned as active bearings.
Because of this, hydrostatic lubrication is typically used when the load requirements are
extremely large combined with low speeds, for instance in telescopes and radar systems.
An example of they work is shown on Figure 3.10. On the left a vertical rotor is resting on
an axial hydrostatic thrust bearing. When the oil is not pressurised, the rotor will rest on
the bearing and there is no ﬂuid ﬁlm. Once the oil is pressurised it will ﬂow between the
rotor and bearing, separating them and generating a ﬂuid ﬁlm of thickness h0. The thickness
of the ﬂuid ﬁlm depends on a number of factors, among others load wz and the pressure. In
this speciﬁc example the rotor has a recess for the oil to ﬂow into although many diﬀerent
conﬁgurations exist.

3.6
Foundation
The dynamics of the foundation are extremely important to model correctly because of the
coupling with the rotor and bearing. A project was therefore dedicated to thoroughly inves-
tigating the original plate element based foundation model to determine how accurate it was
[6]. It was not possible to dismantle the test-rig and carry out experimental measurements of
the isolated foundation, but a diﬀerent research group with a similar T-slot plate did exper-
imental modal analysis to ﬁnd the natural frequencies and mode shapes of their foundation
[10]. The results showed that a plate element model gave a very poor representation of the
actual foundation dynamics and the model has to be reconsidered. The end result was a 3D
model in ANSYS using 20-node brick elements that gave extremely accurate results. The
model was then extracted from ANSYS and imported into MatLab to be coupled with the
rest of the test-rig components.

3.7
Model Reduction
While the ANSYS model is very accurate and eﬃcient it is too big and cumbersome to
couple and solve directly with the rest of the model. A way to get around this is to reduce
the model using Component Mode Synthethis (CMS). CMS techniques are used to divide
complicated structures into a number of substructures that are reduced-order models. These
are then assembled into a reduced-order model of the full structure. If applied correctly
these reduced order models are very quick to solve without losing much accuracy. One of
the main beneﬁts of using these techniques is that complicated and time consuming models
can be divided and resolved individually, making much better use of parallel processing.
Another reason these techniques are widely used in industry is because the reduced models
can be shared without exposing sensitive information. In this way part of a structure can be
and modeled externally and then the reduced model can be sent to test how the structure
performs without giving away the design. More information on CMS reduction can be found
in [13].
(a) CMS reduction procedure.
(b) ESA rocket.
Figure 3.23: Substructuring illustrations. (a) 3 step CMS reduction procedure. (b) Practical
example of substructuring application from ESA.
The CMS reduction procedure is illustrated in three steps on Figure 3.23a. Sketch a) shows a
simple FE model of a beam that is simply supported on the left side and a force is applied on
the right side. The nodes highlighted in red mark the places where the model is subdivided
and are called boundary nodes {b}. The remaining nodes are called interior nodes {i}. On
sketch b) the model has then been divided into two separate FE models with a set of interior
nodes and the boundary nodes chosen from a). Then on sketch c) each model is reduced,
46
==PAGE:58==
removing all the interior nodes and leaving only the boundary nodes behind. Each reduced
order model can then be solved individually and then be re-coupled to obtain the reduced
solution of the full model. Figure 3.23b shows a practical example of how the FE model of
a rocket can be subdivided to make it more eﬃcient to solve.
The main reason for implementing reduction methods in this project is to reduced the AN-
SYS model to a more manageable size. While the ANSYS solver is able to solve the model
relatively quickly, it needs to be coupled with the other components and solved using Mat-
Lab. For the purpose of eventually designing a controller based on this model it would take
too long solve the system with the full foundation model, but with the CMS reduction this
time can be cut signiﬁcantly without losing signiﬁcant accuracy.
In mathematical terms the model reduction is accomplished by creating a reduction basis
and projecting it onto the full system, reducing its size. Consider a system of ﬁnite element
equations (3.58) with matrices of size n × n. Note that while this system is undamped CMS
methods can also be used for systems with damping.
[M] {¨q(t)} + [K] {q(t)} = {f(t)}
(3.58)
Assume a reduction basis [R] of dimensions n × m where m ≪n is available and that
{q(t)} = [R] {q(t)}r
(3.59)
Where r denotes a reduced size. By then left multiplying with the transpose of the reduction
basis [R]T and substituting {q(t)} with equation (3.59) the reduced system is obtained (3.61).
This is also known as a Galerkin projection.
[R]T [M] [R] {¨q(t)}r + [R]T [K] [R] {q(t)}r = [R]T {f(t)}
(3.60)
⇒[M]r {¨q(t)}r + [K]r {q(t)}r = {f(t)}r
(3.61)
These reduced system matrices are of size m × m and therefore of a much smaller size
than the full system. Going back to Figure 3.23a, this reduction can be performed on both
substructures to provide two super-elements as shown. Each of these can then be considered
a standard ﬁnite element and assembled as normal and give the full CMS reduced system.
[M]CMS {¨q(t)}CMS + [K]CMS {q(t)}CMS = {f(t)}CMS
(3.62)
The trick when using CMS methods is to determine suitable reduction matrices for the
system, so that when it is reduced and solved it will provide the same dynamics as the
full system with reasonable accuracy. Note that the only requirement given to the reduction
matrix so far is that it must be a basis, meaning the columns should be linearly independent.
There are many CMS reduction techniques but for project only the CB reduction method is
used.

This chapter is therefore

The program can be divided up into four main sections, shown on Figure 4.1. First the AN-

Chapter 5
Experimental Modal Analysis - Theory
The purpose of the mathematical model is to be an accurate representation of the test-rig.
A quick way to evaluate the quality of a model is to check if its behaviour intuitively seems
physical. This is a useful guideline when building and testing a model but to truly know
if it is useful more qualitative methods are necessary. This is usually done by conducting
experiments to measure the physical properties the model is trying to replicate. If the dis-
crepancy between model and measurement is suﬃciently small the model is validated, if not
the diﬀerences should be examined to ﬁnd any potential errors. Note that it is possible for
there to be errors in the measurements as well as the model and both should be examined
before modifying the model.
In this case experimental modal analysis was used to measure the dynamics of the test-rig
under the same conditions used in the model. More speciﬁcally impact testing was used as
it requires very little equipment and setup. As the name implies the structure is exciting via
an impact with this method, usually by striking it with an impact hammer. The burst of
energy from the impact excites a large amount of modes in the form of accelerations which
are measured by an accelerometer.
The experiment was conducted three times under diﬀerent circumstances in order to verify
the model at diﬀerent stages. For the ﬁrst experiment the entire test-rig was suspended in
the air using a crane, ensuring ’free-free’ boundary conditions and eliminating the damp-
ing mat from the measurements. In the second experiment the test rig was resting on the
damping mat, thereby adding damping to the system. Finally measurements were supposed
to be taken with the rotor in operation to validate the model under operating conditions.
Each set of measurements was analysed using signal processing and modal analysis to ﬁnd
the natural frequencies, mode shapes, and modal parameters of the test-rig under the given
conditions.
This section covers the basic theory of how impact testing works, how to use signal processing
to analyse the measurements, and how to extract the dynamic and modal parameters from
the processed data with modal analysis.

==PAGE:70==
5.1
Impact Testing
Impact testing is a form of experimental modal analysis where the structure is struck by
an impact hammer, receiving a short burst of energy that excites a large range of modes
simultaneously. This is normally accomplished by striking it at a chosen impact point with
a special impact hammer that can vary in size from a small hammer used with one hand
to a sledgehammer used with both hands. The response of the structure is measured by
an accelerometer which is ﬁxed at a chosen response point. To measure the response of a
structure at multiple points one can either move the impact point or the response point,
depending on which is more convenient. By having multiple measurement points the mode
shapes of a structure can be mapped. An example of a impact testing setup is shown on
Figure 5.1 where a beam is suspended midair to achieve ’free-free’ boundary conditions. The
blue dots are measurement points and the hammer and accelerometer indicate impact and
response points for a single measurement. The signals from both the impact hammer and
accelerometer are ampliﬁed and then converted to digital signals in a data acquisition unit
(DAQ) before being stored on a computer for processing. If the structure is linear it does
not matter whether the hammer or accelerometer is moved between measurements as they
will be reciprocal. It may however be a good idea to test this by swapping the locations of
the impact hammer and accelerometer to check if the response is the same. The technique
generally works well for light structures because it is very easy to excite its modes whereas
for heavier structures problems can arise with the signal to noise ratio.
Figure 5.1: Simple example of impact testing setup with a beam suspended in the air.
Ideally the impact would have an inﬁnitesimally short duration and inﬁnite amplitude, which
corresponds to exciting the structure at all frequencies.
In reality this is not physically
possible and in order to achieve good results it is therefore important consider the shape
of the impulse and its frequency content. Because the force is an impulse the amplitude
of the applied energy is a function of the impact hammers momentum. In other words the
amount of energy used to excite the structure depends on the mass of the hammer and how
fast it is moving. While it is diﬃcult to consistently control the speed of the hammer, the
mass can be changed to tune the energy input to the speciﬁc structure. For instance, a very
light structure might only require little energy to be excited without causing an overload
of the output signal. In this case a very light hammer should be used. For a very heavy
59
==PAGE:71==
structure a heavier hammer should be used, either by attaching more weight to it or using
a bigger hammer (a "sledgehammmer" type impact hammer for instance). The amplitude
of the force impulse on Figure 5.2 is mainly determined by the mass of the impact hammer
and to an extent the velocity.
Figure 5.2: Example of measured force impulse and related power spectrum for diﬀerent
types of impact hammer tips (ﬁgure from [14]).
The width of the impulse depends on how long the impact hammer is in contact with the
structure. This can be controlled by the choosing an appropriate tip, a harder and more stiﬀ
tip will give a very narrow and high impulse whereas a softer tip gives a wider and lower
impulse. The eﬀect can be better understood by examining the power spectrum shown on
the right plot on Figure 5.2.
The frequency content of the impulse is a function of the
tip stiﬀness, meaning that with a softer tip more of the energy is distributed in the lower
frequency range, with only limited energy for the higher frequency. This can be useful if one
is only interested in exciting modes with a low natural frequency. As the tip gets harder the
input energy gets more evenly distributed, increasing the amount of higher modes excited by
the impact. It is therefore important to choose a tip with an appropriate stiﬀness to excite
the desired range of modes. Note that while a hard tip excites both low and high modes it
can still be desirable to use a softer tip if only the lower modes are necessary, as they will
have more energy and the response will be clearer. A suggested rule of thumb is to ensure
that the force spectrum amplitude has decreased no more than 10 or 20 dB at the highest
frequency of interest [14].

v(t).
The input signal comes from exciting the structure by striking it with an impact
hammer and is the force measured by a force transducer in the hammer. The impact causes
the structure to accelerate in the form of transient vibrations which is then measured by
the accelerometer as the output signal. These signals are both measured in time and are
normally measured as a voltage which is determined by the settings of the equipment used.
They can be converted to other physical units if desired but in this case it is not necessary.

5.3
Windowing
There are two problems associated with signal processing when doing impact testing, noise
and leakage. An example of an force impulse and transient response with a signiﬁcant amount
of noise is shown on Figure 5.4 to illustrate these concepts. The noise could for instance be
some unrelated electric current in the equipment or vibrations from external sources. Mea-
surements with a long recording time can result in a large amount of noise, especially if the
measured signal is very short. For instance, the force pulse is generally very short as seen
on the ﬁgure, and once it dies out background noise is dominating the signal. If the time
record after the impulse has died out is very long the noise will add up and contaminate
the measurement when calculating the spectrum. One should be especially aware of this for
heavily damped structures where both the impulse and transient response are very short.
Figure 5.4: Example of a force impulse and transient response.
Leakage occurs when the measured signal is not periodic (the discrete Fourier Transform
(5.3) assumes the signal to be periodic). If the signal has not completely died out in the
time interval it will have diﬀerent values at the start and end and there will be a jump in
amplitude. As a result new frequency content may be added to the signal that was not
originally present. This is mostly a problem for structures with very little damping where
the transient response is very long and might be cut oﬀif the time record is too short. The
example on Figure 5.4 will have some amount of leakage as the response has not completely
died out in the time record shown and there is a clear diﬀerence between the beginning and
end of the signal. In digital signal processing this eﬀect can not be entirely avoided as the
discretisation of a signal will always cause some discontinuities at both ends but the eﬀects
can be mitigated.
64
==PAGE:76==
Figure 5.5: Examples of window functions.
Both of these problems are solved by using windowing. Windows are often used in digital
signal processing to modify measured signals and getting rid of undesirable characteristics.
The most commonly used windows in impact testing are the force window and exponential
window but many others exist, such as the Hanning window. Examples of these three win-
dows are shown on Figure 5.5. There are both advantages and drawbacks of using windowing
which determine what applications diﬀerent windows are suitable for. The exponential win-
dow is often used for the response of an impact test as it matches the transient nature of
it very well. If a structure is lightly damped the response is forced to decay completely
within the time record, greatly reducing leakage. It also adds artiﬁcial damping to the sys-
tem although this can be compensated for. An example of this is shown on the ﬁrst plot
of Figure 5.6 where an exponential window is applied to acceleration measurement shown
earlier. Notice how the windowed signal is being attenuated compared to the base signal,
adding damping but reducing leakage. For heavily damped structures all the noise after the
signal has decayed is attenuated, reducing the overall noise of the measurement.
Figure 5.6: Example of applying an exponential window and force window to an acceleration
and force measurement.
As the name implies, force windows are commonly used on the force impulse [14]. The force
window starts out at maximum amplitude and then after a certain point drops rapidly to
zero. This works particularly well as it preserves the very short force pulse and then atten-
uates noise for the rest of the record. An example of this is shown on the second plot of
65
==PAGE:77==
Figure 5.6. There is no added damping in this case as the pulse itself is not attenuated.
It is generally beneﬁcial to use these windows when doing impact testing, with one exception.
If there is a periodic noise in the measured signal this will be ’smeared’ across spectrum,
contaminating other frequencies. If the signal is not windowed the noise will appear as a
sharp peak in the spectrum which can manually be excluded from further post-processing.
An example of such a periodic signal could be the 50 Hz DC current from the electrical grid,
which may appear in the measurements if any equipment is connected to it. The Hanning
window also has many applications and can be used successfully for the response signal but
is generally not used for the force impulse. If applied incorrectly it will attenuate the entire
impulse and only preserve the noise that comes after.

Experimental Modal Analysis - Results
In this project three impact tests were planned to determine the dynamic properties of the
test-rig under diﬀerent conditions. For the ﬁrst experiment pseudo ’free-free’ boundary con-
ditions were applied to the test-rig by lifting it into the air, removing the eﬀects of the
damping mat. It was then lowered back onto the damping mat for the second experiment,
referred to as the ’damping mat’ experiment. As the rotor was not being driven for these
tests the results could be used to validate the model without gyroscopic eﬀects and infer
the eﬀects of the damping mat. Finally the third test was supposed to be done with the
test-rig on the mat and the rotor being driven at 1000 RPM, referred to as the ’1000 RPM’
experiment. Due to time restrictions this experiment was cancelled and data was instead
provided by a previous project [15]. With these results the model could be validated under
more realistic circumstances by including gyroscopic eﬀects.
The equipment, set-up and data processing used in these tests is described in this section
so the experiments and results could be replicated if desired.
The data acquisition and
processing is all based on the theory from Chapter 5.
The results themselves are then
presented, interpreted and discussed.

6.2
Set-Up
To ensure that results of the three experiments are as useful and comparable as possible
the test procedure should be deﬁned in such a way that it can be used for each individual
test. Using the same procedure reduces the amount of variables that aﬀect the experiment
outcome. For instance, by using the same measurement points for each test the probability
that the diﬀerences are due to the change in conditions and not the procedure itself. Once
all the equipment has been set up, the procedure used for all three experiments can be
summarised as follows.
1. Divide the foundation and rotor into a number of measurement points that will provide
suﬃcient spatial resolution.
2. Choose a measurement point to measure the response and attach the accelerometer
here.
3. Strike one of the remaining measurement points 30 times and record the force impulse
and response.
4. Check the FRF and coherence of the measurement, if acceptable save the measurement.
5. Repeat step 3 and 4 until all remaining measurement points have been struck.
Aside from ﬁnding the natural frequencies, identifying the mode shapes is very important
when comparing the measurements to the model results. Otherwise it is not possible to
know which natural frequencies should be compared and it will be diﬃcult to interpret the
74
==PAGE:86==
discrepancies.
For a high spatial resolution the foundation was therefore divided into a
4 × 15 grid and the rotor was divided into 7 points, as shown on Figure 6.4. The blue dots
indicate foundation impact points, green dots indicate rotor impact points and the red dot
indicate the response point. These were all marked on the test-rig using chalk and pens so
they were clearly visible during the experiments. Some foundation impact points coincide
with components on the test rig, in these cases the impact point was taken on the given
component as close to the intended point as possible. It was assumed that the components
were ﬁxed rigidly to the test rig and therefore the response should be almost identical to
the response if the foundation had been struck. It should also be noted that the foundation
was only struck in the vertical direction, resulting in 60 measurements on the foundation.
Due to the geometry of the test rig the horizontal foundation vibrations are assumed to be
insigniﬁcant.
Figure 6.4: Sketch of all measurement points on the test rig.
The rotor measurement points were taken where the rotor surface was clearly accessible.
There is one point at the left end where the rotor connects to the drive coupling, 4 points
between the ball bearing and magnetic bearing, 1 point between the magnetic bearing and
TPJB, and ﬁnally one point at the far right end. Each of these points should be struck ver-
tically and horizontally as the rotor vibrations in both dimensions are important, adding 14
more measurements to the experiment. Despite only having 7 points the rotor mode shapes
should be easily to identify, as in the given frequency range only the ﬁrst and maybe second
bending modes ara expected. With a total of 74 measurement points (60 on the foundation,
14 on the rotor) a FRF matrix of dimensions 74 × 74 can be constructed. However, as
discussed in section 5.4 only one row or column is necessary to get the displacements of all
measurement points. For this particular setup it was very convenient to move the impact
point and therefore the accelerometer was kept in one location for all measurements, which
corresponds to ﬁlling out the ﬁrst column of the FRF matrix. From these 74 measurements
the mode shapes of the system can be determined.

6.3
Data Processing
The post-processing of all measurements was done in MatLab where the functions pwelch()
and cpsd() were used to compute the power spectral density and cross-spectral densities
from the input and output signals. Using equation (5.14) the transfer functions H1(ω) and
H2(ω) were then calculated. These functions are very useful as they carry out the discrete
Fourier transformation (5.3) using very eﬃcient FFT algorithms (Fast Fourier Transforma-
tion), divides the signal into a number of blocks that are then averaged together and applies a
Hanning window to reduce noise and leakage. They also have the option of ’zero-padding’ the
measurements which increases the frequency resolution of the FRF. This essentially means
zeroes are added on both sides of the signal, which increases the time length but does not
add any information.
In section 5.3 it was stated that force and exponential windows are generally used to process
the signals in impact testing. However, during the data processing the best results were
actually achieved when the cpsd() and pwelch functions used the default Hanning window.
It is not entirely clear how these functions work but due to the quality of the results the
Hanning window was left in. One possible explanation could be that periodic noise in the
signals caused problems when using the other windows.
Figure 6.5: Transient response and force pulse of a single impact.
By measuring the same point multiple times more blocks will be used in the average and
improve the quality of the FRF. Figure 6.5 shows the transient response from a single
measurement, which dies out completely in less than a second. This means that the impacts
can be repeated in relatively quick succession and 30 impacts per measurement was therefore
chosen to ensure good quality FRFs. An example of a full measurement with 30 impacts is
shown on Figure 6.6.
76
==PAGE:88==
Figure 6.6: Acceleration and force measurement of a single point.
A FRF calculated from the 30 averaged impacts on Figure 6.6 is shown on Figure 6.7.
This particular measurement is from the ’free-free’ experiment. A good measurement will
generally have a coherence close to 1 for most of the frequency range, as is the case for
this speciﬁc measurement. In the low frequency range there is a noticeable dip in coherence
which is due to low damping in the system and noise in the experiment. This is the case
for all measurements taken in the project, meaning the set-up and equipment does not work
well for ﬁnding natural frequencies below approximately 10 Hz. Fortunately, the smallest
expected natural frequency lies above at least 20 Hz and this should not be a problem.
There is also a noticeable drop of coherence for the ﬁrst two modes which only occurs for
the ’free-free’ measurements.
Because the test-rig is suspended in the air there is very
little damping for these modes in particular, causing the response to increase dramatically
compared to the input force. The measurement is therefore not invalid, despite these drops in
coherence. At higher frequencies there are two additional signiﬁcant drops in coherence which
are caused by antiresonances. This occurs because H1(ω) is generally better at approximating
the true transfer function at antiresonances than H2(ω). As the goal is to determine the
natural frequencies this is therefore not a problem. In fact, because of the large response
at resonances the signal to noise ratio will be smaller for H2(ω) and it will give a better
approximation of the true transfer function. H2(ω) is therefore be used when doing modal
analysis of impact testing.

The FRF itself is shown on the second plot of the ﬁgure and includes both transfer functions.
To carry out the modal analysis described in section 5.4 points are then manually picked
around each resonance peak to ﬁt the 1-DOF system. This could be done once for every
resonance, but in order to improve the statistical certainty that the natural frequencies and
damping ratios are determined correctly it is instead done for ﬁve diﬀerent FRFs. Since
many more than ﬁve FRFs are available (one per measured point) the ones with the clearest
response for the peak of interest are used. By using zero-padding when calculating the FRF
the frequency resolution was high enough to pick 5 points around each peak within 3 dB of
the peak value as shown on Figure 6.8.
Figure 6.8: Example of picking points around a resonance peak.
Once this procedure has been repeated for all the resonance peaks seen in the FRFs, the
undamped natural frequencies and damping ratios of the test-rig under the given conditions
are calculated. The mode shapes are then found by evaluating the amplitude of the response
at every measurement point for the undamped natural frequencies of the system. The sign
of the amplitude is determined by identifying the slope of the phase at the given frequency
and measurement point. This process is automated in MatLab but in some cases had to be
manually if the phase plot was not smooth at a given frequency.

Having established the theory behind the mathematical models and gathered qualitative

to document the improvements made to the model from its original conception, the best
achievable results from previous versions of the model are included and discussed in this

which still need improvement or change.

the experiment conditions of section 6.

are other unknown geometric features which are not accounted for in the model. Given the
high ﬁdelity of the ANSYS model it seems unlikely that the issue is due to the meshing.

The Young’s modulus of the stiﬀeners and pedestals, Es, Ep, are even more problematic.
These have been reduced by a factor of 104 in order to get good results. When testing the
model it was observed that modes would be ’split’ at nodes where the pedestals and stiﬀeners
were coupled, illustrated on Figure 7.1. Only reducing the Young’s modulus signiﬁcantly
appeared ﬁx this. This could be because all of the pedestal and stiﬀener stiﬀness is condensed
into 2 or 3 nodes, while the foundation consists of thousands of nodes. There is therefore
a spike in stiﬀness between the two which might be causing some form of locking issue.
Another explanation could be that the foundation is reduced while the other components
are not, which is causing some sort of coupling problem. In either case, these values are not
physical and needs to be ﬁxed before the model can be used in a controller. One major issue
caused by the low Young’s modulus is that the rotor becomes decoupled from the foundation,
which can be seen from the plots later in this chapter. The reason the model can still be
very accurate is because the natural frequencies in the range of interest are dominated by the
foundation. The mode shapes of the foundation model on its own are given in Appendix A.2
and are close to the measured mode shapes without accounting for any other components.

A secondary objective of the project was to determine the smallest possible size of the model
that would still give reasonable results. This is done by reducing the number of DOFs in the

stiﬀeners, pedestals and drive motor is not changed but could potentially further reduce the

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The work documented in this report was done over a period of 5 months in the spring of
2018. Over the course of the project an extensive physics based mathematical model of a ac-
tive tilting-pad journal bearing (ATPJB) test-rig at DTU was developed using ﬁnite-element
(FE) methods in MatLab and ANSYS. The work is a continuation of previous projects which
laid the groundwork for the work documented here and the end result was a MatLab program
that can be used to model the test-rig. The focus of the project was to accurately model the
dynamics of the entire rotor-bearing-foundation system while maintaining a physics based
approach to the idealisation of the physical structure. This model was then compared to
the measured dynamics of the test-rig, which were determined through experimental modal
analysis under diﬀerent conditions.
The motivation of this project was to enable the development of model-based controllers for
the ATPJB, which could not be done without accounting for the coupled dynamics of the
entire rotor-bearing-foundation system. To accomplish this, goals set by the project state-
ment are summarised as follows. 1) Validate the ANSYS dynamic model of the foundation
using experimental measurements. 2) Couple the ANSYS model with the rotor, bearings and
other components in MatLab. 3) Measure the dynamics of the test rig using experimental
modal analysis under diﬀerent conditions. 4) Validate the model for each set of conditions
and minimize the number of model degrees of freedom (DOFs).
Based on a brief-analysis of the test-rig the most critical components to include in the model
were determined as the rotor, disc, pedestals, stiﬀeners, foundation, damping mat, and AT-
PJB. These were chosen as they most directly couple the dynamics of the rotor, bearing and
foundation. Due to the massive size of the foundation, other components were assumed to
have very little inﬂuence on the system. By stating assumptions and simpliﬁcations, each
component was then discretised using diﬀerent FE methods and the dynamic matrices were
given. Some of these derivations were done in a previous project [3] and were left out of this
report. The rotor and disc models were validated individually through available experimen-
tal data, with the ﬁrst 3 natural frequencies being within 5% of measured values. Despite
being validated by analytical results it was found that the plate elements previously used
for the foundation were unsuitable and instead a full 3D model was developed in ANSYS
using 20-node brick elements. Simpler 8-node elements were also tested but through a con-
100
==PAGE:112==
vergence study they were found to be unreliable. By using experimental data from a similar
T-slot plate the ANSYS dynamic foundation model was validated, with the ﬁrst 12 natural
frequencies being within 5% of measured values. Due to the massive size of the ANSYS
model Craig-Bampton reduction was used to reduce the number of DOFs from hundreds of
thousands to close to a hundred.
To verify the mathematical model experimental modal analysis (EMA) was then performed
to measure the test-rig dynamics. This was done with impact testing by using an impact
hammer and accelerometer. By assuming the structure was linear, this was done by at-
taching the accelerometer to a single point on the test-rig and striking it with a hammer
in a grid covering the entire structure. For the ﬁrst experiment with ’free-free’ boundary
conditions the vertical response was measured at 30 on the foundation and 7 points on the
rotor. For the second experiment with the damping mat the procedure had been reﬁned and
60 points were measured on the foundation and both the horizontal and vertical response
was measured at 7 points on the rotor. As there was not time to do the experiment with
the rotor in operation, results from a previous project was used where the rotor was driven
at 1000 RPM [15]. Signal processing was used to compute the frequency response function
(FRF) for each measured point and modal analysis was then used to determine the mode
shapes, natural frequencies and damping ratios from the peaks. The ﬁrst seven mode shapes
were found to be diﬀerent combination of bending and twisting modes, corresponding to a
plate with ’free-free’ conditions, even in the case with the damping mat. The higher modes
were slightly more diﬃcult to identify as the response was more unclear. As found in previ-
ous projects the measurements showed that the dynamics of the rotor and foundation were
clearly coupled.
Finally the natural frequencies and damping ratios of the model were compared to the
measured ones.
To match the conditions of the ’free-free’ experiment the damping mat
parameters were set to zero and for the damping mat experiment they were manually adjusted
until the results matched the measurements.
Because the ATPJB model does not work
with zero angular velocity, it was set to ω0 = 100RPM for the ﬁrst two experiments as the
eﬀects of hydrodynamic lubrication should be negligible at low velocities. The most accurate
predictions were achieved by signiﬁcantly reducing the stiﬀness of the foundation, stiﬀeners,
and pedestals. With these parameters the ﬁrst seven natural frequencies were found to be
within 8% of the measured ones in all three cases (with exception of one mode), suggesting
a very good quality of the model. It was also found the solution time of the model could be
cut in half by reducing the number of DOFs and modes included in the foundation reduction
without signiﬁcant changes to the natural frequencies. The smallest size tested had 192 DOs.
This can likely be optimised further by looking more into what DOFs can be eliminated and
potentially reducing other components of the model. However, the pedestal and stiﬀener
stiﬀness were reduced by a factor of 104 in order to achieve the best results, which is not
physically plausible. It appeared that with a normal stiﬀness the stiﬀeners and pedestals
were causing some kind of ’locking’ problem in foundation, splitting the mode shapes. On
the other hand, when the stiﬀness was reduced the rotor was decoupled from the foundation
and does not interact properly with the foundation dynamics. It can therefore be concluded
that the implementation of the 3D foundation model and coupling of rotor, bearing, and
101
==PAGE:113==
foundation has signiﬁcantly improved the overall accuracy of the model but the coupling
with the pedestals and stiﬀeners need more investigation.
8.1
Future Work
The implementation of the 3D foundation model has signiﬁcantly improved the accuracy of
the model to a degree where it is almost ready for use in model-based controllers. However,
as the rotor displacements are essential for the controller the issue with the pedestals must
be resolved. It is likely also possible to further reduce the solution time by looking further
into the use of reduction methods. The following two sections are therefore suggestions for
any future work that could be done to improve the model so it can be used for model-based
control.
Pedestal and Stiﬀener Coupling
The low stiﬀness of pedestals and stiﬀeners is a signiﬁcant issue as it decouples the rotor from
the foundation. The reason why this is necessary for the model to work could be because of
the high stiﬀness condensed in the pedestal and stiﬀener nodes. A way of avoiding this might
therefore be to change the discretisation of the them to more closely match the discretisation
of the foundation. Alternatively it might be simpler to model them in 3D and directly add
them to the foundation model.
This also serves the secondary purpose of reducing the
number of coupling DOFs necessary in the Craig-Bampton reduction.
Model Size
Another important area to investigate is the overall model size. Once the coupling issues
with pedestals and stiﬀeners have been solved a more comprehensive study of how many
DOFs are necessary should be done. One way to do this is to simply reducing the number
of coupling points and modes in the foundation reduction and checking the changes to the
model results. A more interesting approach however would be to also reduce the rotor model
as it is currently at 92 DOFs. One could couple the rotor, pedestals, stiﬀeners and ATPJB
and reduce it as one whole model. In this way it would only need the DOFs where the
pedestals and stiﬀeners connect to the foundation. This might also resolve the issue of the
pedestal and stiﬀener couplings as the reduced models might work better when coupled.
