Abstract 
Nano-scaled drug delivery systems (DDSs) have transformed pharmaceuticals development 
by introducing high modifiability to improve efficacy and safety, especially regarding target-
specific nanoparticles. Manufacturing processes of multicomponent nanoparticle DDDs are 
exhausting and suffer from low reproducibility and poor clinical translation. Self-assembling 
encapsulin nanoparticles can address these shortcomings with streamlined production of 
structurally uniform target-specific nanoparticles. This study aimed to redesign Q. thermotol-
erans (Qt) encapsulin nanoparticles to incorporate single-chain fragment variable (scFv) anti-
bodies and heavy chain variable domain (HV) antibodies, collectively referred to as Qt-scFv, 
using enzymatic and recombinant techniques. A sortase-based ligation system was used to 
enzymatically modify Qt protomers to yield a Qt/SrtA-scFv platform. De novo protein struc-
ture predictions were made with AlphaFold2 to assess stability and interspacing peptide link-
ers for design of recombinant Qt-scFv platforms: Qt/Eloop-scFv and Qt/Eloop-HV. Genes rel-
evant for Qt-scFv production were synthesized in pEtDuet-1 vectors and protein expression 
were conducted in E. coli BL21 followed by purification with immobilized metal-affinity chro-
matography. All Qt-scFv platforms showed binding functionality to cellular targets confirmed 
by fluorescence-activated cell sorting analyses. CD3- and CD28-targeting Qt/SrtA-scFv and 
Qt/Eloop-scFv elicited activation of Jurkat NFAT Luciferase Reporter cells suggesting T-cell 
activation properties, while CD3-targeting Qt/Eloop-HV inhibited activation suggesting T-cell 
deactivation properties. No significant change in de/activation functionality was observed for 
Qt-scFv stored at room temperature for up to 10 days suggesting high functional and struc-
tural stability of the designed platforms. The study demonstrated streamlined production of 
functional target-specific encapsulin nanoparticles with prospective applications in biomedical 
research and target-specific DDSs development.

1  Introduction 
Nano-scaled drug delivery systems (DDSs) have proved useful tools in biomedical research 
and pharmaceutical development. Clinical development of nanomedicines has experienced 
great progress since its infancy in the early nineties [1]. This is evidenced by the vast number 
of approved therapies over the past three decades [2], [3]. Most of these therapies employ 
biocompatible lipid, polymeric, or inorganic materials to deliver a therapeutic cargo in nano-
particulate vehicles like liposomes, PEGylated drug conjugates, and metal-containing poly-
mers, respectively. 
 
Nanoparticle formulations offer a range of therapeutic advantages compared to traditional 
pharmaceutical modalities. It expands the leeway of available therapeutics by enhancing the 
delivery of frangible cargo, like nucleic acid-based therapeutics [4]. Encapsulation of volatile 
drugs, like cytokines and chemotherapeutics, improves safety by minimizing systemic expo-
sure, which reduces off-target effects and unintended immune responses. It allows pharma-
cokinetic alterations, like prolonged drug retention and controlled drug release, to improve 
therapeutic efficacy [5]. Notable regarding this study, nanoparticle DDSs exhibit excellent en-
gineerability, enabling the development of customized target-oriented platforms [6]. 
 
Nevertheless, manufacturing of pharmaceutical formulations incorporating nanoparticle plat-
forms, like lipids, polymerics, and inorganics, have production-related issues that hamper 
their applicability. The nanoparticle platforms are synthesized ex vivo and sequential to the 
production of therapeutic cargo, and additional production and coupling with targeting entities 
is required to yield target-specific DDSs [7]. The nanoparticle platforms are multicomponent 
systems that often entail batch-to-batch variations and poor clinical translation [8]. This de-
mands exhausting manufacturing and characterization processes and increases expenses 
as more instrumentation and materials are required. 
 
Self-assembling protein-based nanoparticles, like prokaryotic nanocompartments and virus-
like particles, are promising alternatives to multicomponent nanoparticle platforms [9]. Re-
combinant or post-translational modification of self-assembling protein nanoparticles and tar-
geting molecules enables the in vivo production of protein-based target-specific nanoparti-
cles [10]. The highly organized supramolecular architecture and well-defined uniform sizes of 
self-assembling protein nanoparticles can potentially increase reproducibility. Furthermore, 
their proteinaceous nature suggests ease of manufacturing and scale-up compared to multi-
component nanoparticles, which might aid in clinical translation.

Encapsulin Nanoparticles 
Encapsulin (Enc) represents a newly established class of prokaryotic protein nanocompart-
ments that self-assemble from multiple copies of a protomer into homomultimeric capsid-like 
nanocages [11]. First reported as a high-molecular weight protein aggregate with bacterio-
static properties from B. linens in 1994 along with later homologs reported in several other 
bacterial strains during the mid-1990s, these proteins were collectively mis-annotated as bac-
teriocins [12]–[14]. This annotation was later abandoned as studies were unable to replicate 
suggested antimicrobial and proteolytic properties [15], [16]. Structural experiments con-
ducted in the mid-2000s have established that these proteins, which are now broadly recog-
nized as encapsulins, are hollow icosahedral nanoparticles and serve solely as structural ele-
ments [17], [18]. 
 
Enc protomers is comprised of three domains; namely an axial domain (A-domain), a periph-
eral domain (P-domain), and an elongated loop (E-loop) [11]. The A-domain is composed of 
three α-helices that are spatially arranged to form a tetrahedral cone structure. Upon nano-
particle assembly, Enc A-domains are organized into fivefold symmetry pentamers and six-
fold symmetry hexamers, collectively termed capsomers. The P-domain is composed of a β-
sheet of three β-strands and two α-helices, namely the N-terminal helix and spine helix. Upon 
nanoparticle assembly, capsomers are assembled organizing P-domains adjacently at two-
fold symmetries. Each outer β-strand of the P-domain β-sheet is elongated away from both 
the A- and P-domain to form the E-loop (see Figure 1.a-b).  
 
The molecular conformation of Enc protomers is structurally homologous to the gp5 major 
capsid protein of Escherichia virus HK97, and the Enc nanoparticle shell assembles similarly 
to the icosahedral arrangement of the Escherichia virus HK97 capsid head [19] (see Figure 
1.c). The size and complexity of Encs are therefore characterized similarly to virus capsids 
by a triangulation (T) number corresponding to the number of protomers per asymmetric unit 
forming the threefold symmetry icosahedral faces [20]. To date, structural experiments have 
established the biomolecular architecture of three different Enc sizes [11]. T1 Encs from e.g., 
T. maritima [18], consist of 60 protomers that assembled into 12 pentamer nanoparticles with 
an outer diameter of 20-24 nm. T3 Encs, like those from M. xanthus [21], consist of 180 pro-
tomers orchestrated into 12 pentamer and 20 hexamer nanoparticles with an outer diameter 
of 30-32 nm. The largest known Enc from Q. thermotolerans has a T4 architecture and com-
prises 240 protomers assembled into 12 pentamer and 30 hexamer nanoparticles with an 
outer diameter of 42 nm [22] (see Figure 1.d).

Encs have the eponymous ability to encapsulate cargo proteins within their nanoparticle cav-
ity. The encapsulation is directed by a targeting peptide (TP) located terminally on the cargo 
protein [23]. Large scale metagenomic analyses, substantiated by structural experiments, 
have confirmed that Encs naturally encapsulate different enzymes and serve as organelle-
like biocatalytic entities in both bacterial and archaeal species [24]. These pseudo-organelles 
are involved in numerous metabolic processes like anaerobic ammonium oxidation, iron se-
questration, nitrosative and oxidative stress resistance, and sulfur metabolism.

Engineered Encapsulin Nanoparticles 
The inherent self-organizing property of Encs to form symmetric and uniform icosahedral na-
noparticles make them a robust platform for development of nano-scaled DDSs [11]. Enc na-
noparticles have high pH-, proteolytic and thermotic tolerance [25]. The N-terminus of Enc 
protomers is located on the inner surface, while the C-terminus is exposed from the outer 
surface of the shell following nanoparticle assembly [18], [22]. This makes Enc nanoparticles 
amenable to genetic and post-translational modifications, and therefore a modular and versa-
tile platform for engineering customized, nano-scaled drug carriers with various cargo-loading 
and surface functionalities [26]. Encouraged by these developability features, studies with 
Enc nanoparticle engineering for various biomedical applications have been published during 
the past decade. 
 
Moon et al. (2014) have reported an engineered T. maritima (Tm) Enc nanoparticle platform 
with a hepatocellular carcinoma (HCC)-targeting peptide (SP94) genetically inserted between 
residue 138 and 139 [27]. With additional surface-tethering of a chemotherapeutic prodrug, 
Aldoxorubicin, they have demonstrated successful targeting and killing of HepG2 HCC cells 
in vitro. These findings have highlighted the potential use of engineered Enc nanoparticles 
for target-specific anti-cancer nanomedicine. The group have likewise reported a similar Tm 
Enc platform with genetic insertion of an Fc-binding peptide and surface conjugation of fluo-
rescein-5-maleimide [28]. The fluorescent nanoparticles selectively bound to, and accumu-
lated on, the cytosol of squamous cell carcinoma (SCC-7) in vitro demonstrating the potential 
use of Enc nanoparticles in cancer diagnostic applications. However, due to their small size, 
targeting peptides suffer from low binding efficiency and specificity as well as low in vivo sta-
bility, which questions their applicability in target-specific DDSs [29]. 
 
Other studies have demonstrated the prospective application of Enc nanoparticles in rational 
nano-vaccine design. Choi et al. (2016) have reported a Tm Enc nanoparticle platform with 
genetically inserted OT-1 antigenic peptide at the N-terminus, C-terminus, and between resi-
due 42 and 43 (E-loop) [30]. With effective delivery to dendritic cells followed by antigen-spe-
cific cytotoxicity and tumor suppression in B16-OVA melanoma tumor-bearing mice, these 
findings have showed the potential for application of Enc nanoparticles in cancer vaccine de-
velopment. Lagoutte et al. (2018) have reported an engineered Tm Enc nanoparticle with 
Matrix protein 2 ectodomain (M2e)-peptide from Influenza A virus inserted between residue 
138 and 139 [31]. Nanoparticle immunization of Balb/c mice elicited an M2e-specific antibody 
response highlighting the potential for using Enc nanoparticles in nano-vaccines against viral 
diseases.

Sophisticated techniques involving SpyCatcher/SpyTag bioconjugation have also been uti-
lized for engineering functional Enc nanoparticle systems. SpyCatcher proteins can be irre-
versibly conjugated to SpyTag-displaying proteins by formation of an iso-peptide covalent 
bond [32]. In two comprehensive studies, by Bae et al. (2018) and by Choi et al. (2021), 
modified Tm Enc platforms were produced with genetic insertion of a SpyTag peptide be-
tween residue 138 and 139 and at the Enc C-terminus, respectively [33], [34]. Subsequent 
addition of various SpyCatcher fusion proteins have demonstrated versatile surface function-
alities like dimerizing ligands, fluorescent proteins, and targeting ligands for nanostructure 
formation, immobilization, cell-labeling, and cell-targeting. The two studies have generated 
both single and dual functional nanoparticles showcasing multifaceted engineerability of re-
combinant Enc nanoparticles for multiplexed biosensing, labeling, and targeting applications. 
While SpyCatcher/SpyTag exhibits a highly modular conjugation system, a drawback con-
cerns the in vitro mix-and-match procedure to produce these functional Enc nanoparticles. It 
requires concurrent production of both Enc-SpyTag and SpyCatcher fusion proteins followed 
by in vitro assembling to obtain a final target-specific product. Hence, it has a manufacturing 
process similar to or even longer than multicomponent nanoparticle platforms mentioned ear-
lier.  
 
Experimental Rationale 
This study has investigated the production of target-specific Enc nanoparticles by incorporating 
single-chain fragment variable (scFv) antibody molecules to the C-termini of Q. thermotolerans 
(Qt) Enc protomers using both an in vivo sortase A (SrtA)-based ligation system and genetic ma-
nipulation. The study sought to elucidate the producibility of scFv-displaying Qt nanoparticles (Qt-
scFv) in E. coli, which only requires a single production procedure to obtain target-specific Enc 
nanoparticles. Furthermore, the study sought to assess the feasibility of introducing antibody 
functionalities to Qt nanoparticles to develop target-specific Enc nanoparticles, thereby challeng-
ing the current modality of producing cell-targeting Enc nanoparticles. To date, streamlined pro-
duction of such scFv-displaying target-specific Enc nanoparticles has not been reported. 
 
SrtA transpeptidase is naturally present on the cell wall of gram-positive bacteria and facilitates 
the maturation of the outer peptidoglycan layer by catalyzing the cross-linking of peptidoglycans 
[35]. The transpeptidase reaction is directed by a SrtA cleavage motif (LPXTG), where SrtA 
cleaves between threonine and glycine followed by ligation of a second protein displaying a ter-
minal poly-glycine motif. SrtA therefore presents a promising protein engineering tool for site-di-
rected protein ligation [36].

Preliminary investigations, not included in this study, into the production of Qt-scFv nanoparticles 
by direct genetic fusion resulted in inclusion body formation due to aggregation of Qt-scFv fusion 
protomers. This aggregation was most likely caused by erroneous folding of the Qt-scFv during 
translation, which abrogated the post-translational nanoparticles self-assembly. Direct genetic fu-
sion was therefore deemed unsuitable for proper expression of C-terminal scFv-displaying Qt-
scFv nanoparticles. To circumvent this issue, a SrtA-based protein ligation system comprising a 
S. aureus-derived SrtA transpeptidase [37] and a Tobacco Etch Virus protease (Tev) [38] was 
suggested for conjugating scFv antibodies to Qt protomers. It was hypothesized that insertion of 
a C-terminal ‘LPETG’ SrtA recognition site on Qt protomers, and an N-terminal ‘ENLYFQG’ Tev 
recognition site on the scFv, would enable the formation of Qt-scFv. Here, Tev would cleave be-
tween glutamine and glycine exposing a poly-G motif on the scFv, and SrtA would cleave be-
tween threonine and glycine on the Qt protomer and subsequently conjugate the N-terminal scFv 
poly-G motif to yield Qt-scFv. Helalat et al. (2024) have reported a similar protein ligation system 
employing a SrtA-Tev fusion protein capable of efficiently conjugating a fluorescent protein (GFP) 
to both the interior and exterior surface of Qt nanoparticles without abrogating the nanoparticle 
self-assembly [39]. 
 
This study comprised the production and analysis of three different Qt-scFv platforms: 
• 
The first platform, Qt/SrtA-scFv, consisted of Qt nanoparticles where a full scFv mole-
cule with an N-terminal peptide linker was conjugated to the C-terminus of Qt pro-
tomers using SrtA-mediate protein ligation. This platform was employed for produc-
tion of construct C1 to C6.  
• 
A second platform, Qt/Eloop-scFv, consisted of Qt nanoparticles with a full scFv mol-
ecule with terminal peptide linkers genetically inserted between residue 58 and 69 in 
the E-loop of Qt protomers. This platform was employed to produce construct EscFv. 
• 
And a third platform, Qt/Eloop-HV, consisted of Qt nanoparticles with a heavy chain 
variable domain (HV) molecule with terminal peptide linkers genetically inserted be-
tween residue 58 and 69 in the E-loop of Qt protomers. This platform was employed 
to produce construct EHV.

2  Materials & Methods 
The scFv molecules used in this study were derived from commercial and patented antibody 
sequences (see Appendix A). ScFv molecules were chosen for targeting CD3 TCR co-re-
ceptor and CD28 co-stimulatory receptor on T-cells and CD19 B-lymphocyte antigen on B-
cells [40]–[42]. 
 
De Novo Predictions of Qt/Eloop-scFv and Qt/Eloop-HV Platforms 
To determine the optimal length of the peptide linker for connecting Qt protomer and scFv 
molecule in the Qt/Eloop-scFv and Qt/Eloop-HV platforms, ColabFold v1.1.5 (AlphaFold2) 
was utilized to generate 3D protein structure models [43]. The two platforms were generated 
with multiples of GGGGS (1xG4S, 2xG4S, 3xG4S, 4xG4S) peptide linkers. For each run, 
‘num_relax’ was set to ‘5’ and ‘relax_max_iterations’ was set to ‘2000’ to relax the top five 
ranked structures with Amber force fields. The ‘template_mode’ was set to ‘pdb100’ for better 
sequence alignment, and remaining settings were set as default. Adjusted settings were 
done to yield more confident 3D models. For each run, the average of the local confidence 
score (pLDDT) multiplied by the template modeling score (pTM) of the five top ranked struc-
tures (x̄ (pLDDT x pTM)) was calculated for model comparison (see supplementary A). 
 
Qt-scFv Plasmids Design and Preparation 
To generate plasmids necessary for production of Qt/SrtA-scFv constructs (C1-C6), genes 
for SrtA, Tev, Qt, and an scFv gene (C1-C6) were each cloned into a pETDuet™-1 vector. As 
cargo protein, an enhanced green fluorescent protein (EGFP) was included for nanoparticle 
detection. To generate plasmids necessary for production of the Qt/Eloop-scFv construct, 
EscFv, and Qt/Eloop-HV constructs, EHV, genes for each construct together with a superfold 
green fluorescent protein (sfGFP) gene were cloned into pETDuet™-1 vector, respectively. 
As negative control, a Qt gene and an EGFP gene were cloned into a pETDuet™-1 vector 
(see Figure 2 and Table 1). All plasmids were synthesized by GenScript Biotech©. 
 
Plasmids were transformed into E. coli BL21(DE3) competent cells by chemical transfor-
mation. 70 µL of competent cells were thawed on ice, mixed with 2.5 µL plasmids dissolved 
in nuclease-free water [Thermo Scientific™], and incubated for 30 min. on ice. Transfor-
mation was performed by a 42°C heat shock for 90 sec. followed by immediate incubation for 
2 min. on ice. Transformed cells were mixed with 500 µL lysogeny broth (LB; Lennox [Milli-
pore®]) medium and incubated for 50 min. at 37°C with continuous shaking (200 RPM). After

Positive transformants were inoculated in 5 mL LB (0.1 mg/mL ampicillin) starter culture and 
incubated at 37°C (200 RPM) O/N. Starter cultures were expanded in LB medium (0.1 
mg/mL ampicillin) and grown at 37°C (200 RPM) to an OD600 of 0.5 after which the

temperature was lowered to 30°C  and cultures were induced with Isopropyl β-D-1-thiogalac-
topyranoside (IPTG) to a final concentration of 1 mM. Cells were harvested by centrifugation 
(6000 RCF, 15 min., 4°C). Pellets were resuspended in Bacterial Protein Extraction Reagent

changed to PBS and concentrations were adjusted using Amicon® Ultra 100K MWCO cen-

tometry using a NanoDrop [Thermo Scientific™].

[BioXCell©] at 4°C O/N. Conjugated positive control samples were purified by size-exclusion

changed to PBS and concentrations were adjusted using Amicon® Ultra 100 K MWCO cen-
trifugal filters [Millipore®].

SDS-PAGE 
Nanoparticle assembly and sample purity were inspected by SDS-PAGE. 15 μL sample were 
mixed with 5 μL LDS Sample Buffer Non-Reducing 4X [Thermo Scientific™] and incubated 
for 4 min. at 95°C. 15 μL of denatured sample were loaded onto an Any kD™ Mini-PRO-
TEAN® TGX™ Precast Protein Gel, 15-well, 15 µL [Bio-Rad©]. 2 µL PageRuler™ Plus Pres-
tained Protein Ladder [Thermo Scientific™] was used for kDa measurement. Samples were 
separated by 135 V in Tris/Glycine/SDS Buffer [Bio-Rad©] for ~50 min. After separation, the 
gel stained with Coomassie Brilliant Blue R-250 staining solution [Bio-Rad©] for 50 min. fol-
lowed by destaining for 40 min. with destaining solution (50% methanol [Sigman-Aldrich®, 
40% Milli-Q® water, 10% acetic acid [Sigma-Aldrich®]). Destained gel was stored in deion-
ized water O/N. 
 
Western Blotting 
Purified samples of construct C1 to C6 were separated by SDS-PAGE and subsequently 
blotted onto a Immobilon® - P Transfer Membrane (0.45µm polyvinylidene fluoride) [Milli-
pore®] using a transfer buffer (12.5 mM Tris-HCl [Sigma-Aldrich®], 96 mM Glycine [Sigma-

3  Results & Discussion 
De Novo Predictions of Qt/Eloop-scFv and Qt/Eloop-HV Platforms 
A preliminary study highlighted that the use of direct genetic fusion to modify the structure of 
Qt protomers should be conducted with caution due to the risk of improper protein folding 
(see Experimental Rationale). 3D models for EscFv and EHV, representing the Qt/Eloop-
scFv and Qt/Eloop-HV, respectively, were generated with each of the peptide linkers: 1xG4S, 
2xG4S, 3xG4S, 4xG4S. The average pLDDTxpTM-score suggested the most confident pro-
tein structures constituted EscFv with a 2xG4S linker, and EHV with an 1xG4S linker (see 
Figure 3 and supplementary A). 
 
Figure 3. Structural overview of construct EHV (top) and construct EscFv (bottom) with peptide linkers of variable length (1 x 
GGGGS, 2 x GGGGS, 3 x GGGGS, 4 x GGGGS). The scFv (green) was inserted between residue 58 and 69 in the E-loop of Qt 
protomers (blue) with a C-terminal GTGGGGSCGHHHHHH 6xHisTag for purification (red). Structures were made with Al-
phaFold2. Illustrations were made with PyMol Molecular Graphics System. 
 
Structure predictions were performed to qualitatively validate the molecular orientation of the 
scFv-fragment relative to the Qt protomer and quantitatively validate the stability of Qt/scFv 
structures as function of peptide linker length. Evidenced from the predicted 3D models and 
substantiated by additional confidence data (see supplementary A), it was challenging to 
identify the most suitable length of the peptide linkers. The structural predictions of these 
peptide linkers had significantly higher uncertainty compared to the Qt and scFv parts. This 
uncertainty was expected as the peptide linkers constitute the most unnatural region of the 
Qt-scFv and PDB structures for homology modeling of such peptide linkers are sparse.

A pLDDT score above 60 usually corresponds to a correct backbone arrangement [44], and 
a pTM score above 0.5 means the overall predicted complex fold might be similar to the true 
structure [45]. While 2xG4S peptide linkers for EscFv and 1xG4S peptide linkers for EHV 
generated the most confident structure predictions, predicted structures of both EscFv and 
EHV with 3xG4S peptide linkers were considered of high confidence. 
 
It was therefore decided to genetically insert a 15 aa GGGGSGGGGSGGGGS (3xG4S) pep-
tide linker on each end of the scFv molecule for both Qt/Eloop-scFv and Qt/Eloop-HV. These 
peptide linkers were expected to facilitate scFv binding functionality by conferring adequate 
spatial distancing to the Qt protomer and necessary flexibility for correct lateral surface dis-
play and orientation of the scFv paratope. Suggested by the confidence data of the predicted 
3D models, these peptide linkers were expected to reduce the impact on Qt protomer folding 
and likely ensure proper nanoparticle assembly. Of note, based on all conducted de novo 
protein structure predictions, it cannot be ruled out that genetic insertion with other shorter 
peptide linkers, like 2xG4S for Qt/Eloop-scFv or 1xG4S and 2xG4S for Qt/Eloop-HV, might 
result in correct expression and nanoparticle assembly (see supplementary A). For platform 
Qt/SrtA-scFv (C1-C6), a 20 aa GGGGSGGGGSGGGGS-GGGGS (4xG4S) peptide linker 
was included on the N-terminus of the scFv molecule, flanking the Tev recognition site up-
stream. 
 
Expression and Purification of Qt-scFv Nanoparticles 
Purification of Qt-scFv nanoparticles was initially conducted with B-PER™ Reagent for lysis 
of IPTG-induced cells prior to IMAC using Ni-NTA resin. However, after multiple rounds of 
purification with adjustments to incubation times and reagent volumes, all purified samples 
suffered from low yield and contamination. Inspection of samples with fluorescence micros-
copy clarified that the samples were indeed not contaminated, but rather contained unlysed 
E. coli cells from the IPTG-induced cell cultures. This became evident as the bacteria resid-
ing in the purified samples shined with a bright green color upon illumination with 395 nm 
light indicating that they contained high quantities of GFP. Consequently, Qt-scFv nanoparti-
cle purification with B-PER™ Reagent was discontinued. As substitution, probe sonication 
was employed followed by centrifugation of the lysate and sterile filtration of the supernatant 
to ensure adequate lysis of IPTG-induced cells and minimize risk of transferring unlysed cells 
to subsequent purification steps (see Materials and Methods).

formants with IPTG at 30°C resulted in successful expression of Qt-scFv constructs. A

Initial FACS analyses of Qt-scFv samples elicited no observable cellular binding functionality. 
The cytoplasm of E. coli is known to be highly reductive, which inhibits the formation of disul-
fide bond formation of proteins [48]. The scFv molecules utilized for Qt-scFv production pos-
sess disulfide bonds situated between the beta-sheet bi-layer of each variable domain [49]. It 
was hypothesized that a post-purification treatment of the Qt-scFv samples with oxidized L-
glutathione could reconstitute the inherent disulfide bonds in the scFv molecules. The effect 
of oxidized L-glutathione treatment was confirmed by successful cellular binding functionality 
in subsequent FACS analyses (see In vitro Qt-scFv Cellular Binding Assay in this section) 
and complemented by SDS-PAGE analysis of EscFv and EHV, where the samples were 
compared with and without treatment with 50% dithiothreitol (DTT) (see Figure 4.b).

The explanation for the noticeable variability in cellular binding functionality between the dif-
ferent Qt-scFv samples is likely multifactorial. For the Qt/SrtA-scFv constructs, C1-C6, the 
binding functionality may significantly depend on the efficiency of the in vivo SrtA-based liga-
tion system. As earlier mentioned, during nanoparticle expression in E. coli, the rate of SrtA 
ligation activity and Tev protease activity likely determine the number of scFv molecules dis-
played on the nanoparticle surface. The probability of Qt-scFv cellular binding is likely corre-
lated with the valence of the Qt-scFv i.e., the number of scFv molecules displayed on the na-
noparticle surface. This expected correlation becomes evident when comparing the binding 
functionalities of the C1 sample and the EscFv sample. The Qt/SrtA-scFv construct C1 and 
the Qt/Eloop-scFv construct EscFv incorporate identical scFv molecules (see Appendix A). 
The EscFv nanoparticles have the maximum valence possible for these Qt-scFv nanoparti-
cles because all 240 Qt protomers contain a genetically inserted scFv molecule in their back-
bone, which may explain the more than 5-fold higher binding of the EscFv sample compared 
to the C1 sample. 
 
A second and significant factor can be attributed to the scFv molecules utilized for Qt-scFv 
production. Evidenced from the results of the FACS analyses, the C2 sample elicited binding 
functionality comparable to the negative control. The scFv molecule incorporated in construct 
C2 was extracted from the commercial bispecific anti-CD3xanti-CD20 antibody Epcoritamab 
(Genmab/Abbvie), which has previously been documented to elicit CD4+ and CD8+ T-cell ac-
tivation, conferred by its CD3 binding functionality, with EC50 values in the picomolar range 
[50]. The lack of binding functionality of the C2 sample can potentially be a result of improper 
folding of this scFv molecule when implemented in Qt-scFv nanoparticles. Also, as men-
tioned earlier, the length of the peptide linker (4xG4S for Qt/SrtA-scFv constructs) is ex-
pected to influence the orientation and accessibility of the scFv molecule. The differences in 
binding functionality between the Qt-scFv samples may be attributed to this as well. Although 
binding optimization of scFv molecules is out of the scope of this study, these results high-
light the importance of examining scFv molecules and peptide linkers when developing scFv-
displaying target-specific Enc nanoparticles. 
 
A third factor concerns the detection of cellular bound Qt-scFv nanoparticles using FACS. At-
tributed to the minuscule dimensions of the Qt-scFv nanoparticles and the suspected variabil-
ity of GFP encapsulation, it is difficult to determine the stochiometric coefficient of bound Qt-
scFv nanoparticles per cell required to yield a positive detection signal on FACS. The Qt-
scFv nanoparticles are expected to have <100 nm dimensions, which may entail that cellular 
bound Qt-scFv nanoparticles pass the FACS detector without eliciting a positive signal.

4  Conclusion 
This study has established the successful design and production of target-specific Enc nano-
particles by redesigning Qt protomers to incorporate scFv antibody functionalities. Three con-
ceptually different Qt-scFv platforms, differentiated by mode of modification and scFv molec-
ular fragment, were designed and produced, and subsequent cellular binding and activation 
functionalities were examined. An in vivo SrtA-mediated protein ligation system was em-
ployed to seamlessly introduce scFv molecules to the C-terminal of Qt protomers following 
co-expression in E. coli constituting a Qt/SrtA-scFv platform. In total, six different scFv mole-
cules, derived from commercial and patent antibodies, were each C-terminally conjugated to 
Qt protomers interspaced by a 4xG4S peptide linker constituting Qt-scFv construct C1 to C6. 
Successful expression at 30°C induction temperature and purification using an established 
Ni-NTA-based IMAC procedure were confirmed by western blotting. A full anti-CD3 scFv mol-
ecule identical to C1 and a HV domain of C1 scFv were genetically inserted in the E-loop of 
Qt protomers between residue 58 and 69 constituting a Qt/Eloop-scFv platform and a 
Qt/Eloop-HV platform, respectively. Based on de novo protein structure predictions, terminal 
3xG4S peptide linkers were inserted on the scFv molecule to interspace the scFv part and 
the Qt protomer part. Expression and purification were conducted similar to Qt/SrtA-scFv 
constructs. FACS analyses confirmed that all Qt-scFv constructs, except construct C2, pos-
sess scFv-mediated binding functionality to respective target cells. Anti-CD3 and anti-CD28 
Qt-scFv constructs, except EHV, elicited T-cell activation functionality confirmed by increased 
luciferase-mediated bioluminescence after incubation with Jurkat NFAT Luciferase reporter 
cells with activation observed for sample stored at RT for up to 10 days. 
 
The study has demonstrated a streamlined production of target-specific nanoparticles to ac-
commodate for the low reproducibility and clinical translation hurdles of multicomponent na-
noparticles DDDs. The positive binding and activation functionality of Qt-scFv nanoparticles 
can foster future applications within biomedical research and development. However, many 
characteristics are yet to be resolved, especially regarding biocompatibility and target deliver-
ability, before scFv-displaying Enc nanoparticles can be considered a novel DDS candidate.

5  Future Perspectives 
The presented study has solely investigated the production feasibility and binding functional-
ity of scFv-displaying Qt Enc nanoparticles. While this has generated valuable insight into the 
developability of target-specific Enc nanoparticles, more studies are warranted to further 
evaluate whether the presented concepts are suitable DDS candidates. 
 
Structural characterizations including transmission electron microscopy (TEM), nanoparticle 
tracking analysis (NTA), and dynamic light scattering (DLS) will provide both qualitative and 
quantitative measurements of nanoparticle morphology, size distribution, and zeta potential. 
This will generate data on production consistency and nanoparticle uniformity. Furthermore, 
zeta potential will give preliminary insight into the nanoparticle deliverability. A tentative 
agreement with Michigan State University on conducting cryogenic electron microscopy 
(cryo-EM) analyses will provide complete 3D molecular structures of the presented Qt-scFv 
platforms. 
 
A stability assay was included in this study to elucidate the stability of Qt-scFv by activation 
of T-cells as function of time and temperature. Additional forced degradation assays examin-
ing the structural stability at different pH and temperature conditions would explicate nano-
particle durability and stability in e.g., tumor microenvironments. 
 
Successive in vitro functional assays by co-culturing of effector cells, target cell, and Qt-scFv 
nanoparticles followed by monitoring using IncuCyte® or flow cytometry-based methods can 
establish Qt-scFv efficacy prior to in vivo experiments, and ultimately; in vivo studies will pro-
vide the necessary pharmacokinetic and pharmacodynamic data to pre-clinically validate the 
biocompatibility of Qt-scFv nanoparticles as a novel DDS candidate. 
 
Concurrent to the presented study, investigations with Qt-scFv nanoparticles displaying anti-
carcinoembryonic antigen (anti-CEA) scFv antibodies were conducted along with investiga-
tions with a bispecific T-cell engager platform conceived from a combination of the presented 
Qt/SrtA-scFv and Qt/Eloop-HV platform. These investigations are on-going and therefore not 
included in this study.
