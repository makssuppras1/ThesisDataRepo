1 Abstract 
The following report concerns on the continued development of the electrolyte CPA model 
developed by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [1] and further developed by Schlaikjer et al. [2]. 
The report contains a literature study, where the eCPA model is compared to similar equations of 
state for electrolytes, developed by other research groups. It was found that the eCPA generally has 
a wider application range than other models, but that the model needs further validation to fully 
prove its capabilities.  
This report further validates parameters estimated in both publications [1], [2] as well as 
investigating parameterization of the ion size parameter of the model. 
The validation of the model found that both developed parameter sets performed reasonably well at 
mixed salt osmotic coefficients except at very high temperatures. The relative average deviation 
was found to be 6.35 % for a mixture of NaCl and KCl at 298 K and 5.17 % at 318 K for the 
parameter set by Schlaikjer et al. [2], while the relative average deviation was found to be 6.26 % 
for at 298 K and 5.25 % at 318 K for the parameter set by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [1]. For a 
NaCl/CaCl2 mixture the relative average deviation was found to be 0.72 %, 2.39 %, 1.84 %, and 
19.67 % for 382 K, 384 K, 413 K, and 445 K respectively for the parameter set by Maribo-
Mogensen et al. [1]. 
Both parameter sets performed well for most solubilties, but the parameter set by Schlaikjer et al. 
[2] was notably better at higher molalities at extreme temperatures. Both parametersets had 
problems predicting the mixed salt solubility at other temperatures than 298 K when the mixture 
contained nitrate salts. 
By parameterization of the ionic diameter the relative average deviation could be improved. A 
systematic one by one fitting of the ion sizes proved only mildly successful. A simultaneous fitting 
of the ion size reduced the relative average deviation of NaCl to 1.93 %  and 1.62 %, for NaBr to 
2.81 % and 3.78% , for KCl to 2.81 % and 1.75 %, and for KBr to 2.66 % and 0.57 % for activity 
coefficients and osmotic coefficients respectively. The average of the relative average deviation of 
NaCl, NaBr, KCl and KBr was found to be 2.55 % for activity coefficients and 1.93 % for osmotic 
coefficients. 
Graphical validation gave, that the results were however notably worse for solubilities, where both 
the parameter set by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [1] and Schlaikjer et al. [2] followed the trend better 
than the parameter set presented in this work which had trouble at high temperatures.

6.1 Scope of this project 
This project contains the following: 
 A literature study of different attempts at creating an electrolyte EoS, and a discussion of 
applicabilities and limits of each relevant model. 
 Model validation is done of the two parameter sets developed by Maribo-Mogensen et al. 
[1] and Schlaikjer et al. Model validation is done for multisalt systems at different 
temperatures for solubilities and osmotic coefficients. 
 The possibility of changing the experimental ion diameters in the model with fitted ion 
diameters is investigated. 
The work is primarily done in MATLAB. Original code Maribo-Mogensen/Schlaikjer was modified 
for the purposes of this report. 
6.2 Background and industrial need 
Electrolytes are widely used in the chemical, biochemical, pharmaceutical, waste water treatment, 
and petroleum industries for enhanced oil recovery, and an accurate modeling of the phase behavior 
is important [3]–[5].  
Today activity coefficient models such as the OLI’s mixed solvent electrolyte model (OLI-mse), 
elec-Non-Random Two-Liquid model (elec-NRTL), and the Extended UNIQUAC are the most 
commonly used for modelling electrolytes in industry. This is a heterogeneous approach, the so 
called   –   approach where separate models are used for gas and liquid phases.  
However as the activity coefficient models are correlative, and usually require a large amount of 
data for adjustable parameters, which may end up having little to do with the physics involved, 
there is an increasing demand for the creation of an electrolyte equation of state (EoS). One of the

great disadvantages of activity coefficient models is that they cannot predict the density. This could 
however be described by an EoS. Another disadvantage is that the activity coefficient models rarely 
capture the pressure dependence of the activity coefficient (although minimal for liquid phase, it 
does have an effect) [6]. An electrolyte EoS is typically described as a summation of contributions 
to the residual Helmholtz energy by the physical and electrostatic forces. 
An electrolyte EoS would introduce a homogeneous approach, the  -   approach for determining 
the fugacity in each phase, rather than the activity coefficient for the liquid phase. The disadvantage 
is that the classical mixing rules for the cubic EoS are not always enough for describing the non-
ideal electrolyte mixtures, and as such binary interaction parameters may be needed [7]. Like with 
Gibbs Excess models the chemical potential of a species can be calculated from the residual 
Helmholtz energy by taking the partial derivative with respect to the molar amount of the species in 
question.

While several electrolyte Equations of State have been developed, none has shown to be fully 
accurate for a wide application range yet, so that it can replace activity coefficient models in 
industry. A full model should preferably model solid-liquid equilibrium, liquid-liquid equilibrium 
and vapor-liquid equilbrium (SLE, LLE, VLE), as well as properties (solubilities, activity 
coefficients, fugacities, densities, vapor pressures) accurately for a number of components and 
solvents, both in single salt/solvent systems and in multiple salt/solvent systems for a wide range of 
temperatures, concentrations and pressures. Several articles present good results using different 
EoS’s, but usually for a very limited application range.

7 Literature study  
The following literature study provides an overview over important aspects in the development of 
an electrolyte EoS, the state of the current project and projects by other research groups involving 
the development of similar models. For a full in depth literature study on the current state of 
electrolyte models, the study done in the thesis by Maribo-Mogensen [4] or similar is 
recommended. 
7.1 Current state of electrolyte EoS models 
The modeling of electrolytes is quite chaotic with a great deal of models available, each including a 
select number of terms. Each research group has their own approach as to which parameters to 
estimate, which phenomena to describe, and how to describe these phenomena. 
There are generally 3 levels of complexity of electrolyte models: non-primitive models, primitive 
models and restrictive primitive models. Primitive models treat the solvent implicitly as a 
continuum characterized by a static permittivity, and it is the model which is most widely used [4]. 
Some choose to simplify the physics even further with a restrictive primitive model where each ion

is modeled as having the same size [8]. Non primitive models have an explicit term for the solvent 
in all contributions to the Helmholtz energy. 
The physical representation can be done in a number of ways. Earlier exploits had simple physical 
representations with SRK or PR combined with an electrolyte term. Newer models are typically a 
version of SAFT or CPA for representing the physical as well as associating terms accounting for 
hydrogen bonding in water. 
The electrostatic ion-ion interactions are modeled with either the full Debye-Hückel (DH) or the 
Mean spherical approximation (MSA). MSA seems to be most popular choice, as this is a newer 
model. Also it seems that there is some confusion between the full DH and the Debye-Hückel 
limiting law, with the full model being forgotten by some [6], [9]. Regardless it was found, that 
either method had similar performances [4], [5], [10]. 
The addition of other contributions to the Helmholtz energy varies by author. A Born term for 
accounting for ion-dipole interactions and excess Helmholtz energy of going from one solvent to 
another is included in some models. However several groups choose not to include a Born term in 
their model [3], [8]. The consensus is among other things that the Born term does not contribute to 
the chemical potential. This however is not true, if the dependence on the concentration of the static 
permittivity is taken into account in which case the partial derivative becomes significant [7], [11], 
[12]. The contribution of the Born term is however mostly keeping the ions in the liquid phase, 
which some groups instead choose to do as a separate command to limit computation (though this 
may be a viable method, it is an unphysical way of doing it). 
Electrolytes are a complex subject to model completely, and some of the phenomena few describe 
are association between ions or between ion-dipole. Both forces are described in the model 
presented by Rozmus et al. [11]. For systems with weak electrolytes (such as some acids), full 
dissociation may not occur. And ions may be present as ion pairs (but still dissolved). Some ions 
may even be associated while still separated by a solvent layer. The association between ions is 
typically not modeled, and is not needed for strong electrolytes. For weaker electrolytes Held et al. 
[13] models the association as dispersive forces using a binary interaction coefficient, kij, while 
Rozmus et al. models the ion association explicitly with Wertheim theory [11]. 
Ion dipole association is the bonding of solvent molecules to ions. This typically is seen as solvation 
of ions or hydration if the solvent is water. The amount of solvent molecules associated to an ion is 
called the ion’s coordination number. This number varies greatly between sources, and some take it 
as zero (no association) or as high as 12 [11]. The typical coordination number is around 6-8 [11], 
[12]. The solvated ion radius is an important parameter in the Born term, and it varies on the solvent 
[14]. 
It should be noted that the physical terms treat the solvent explicitly as single molecules(Lewis 
Randall framework), while the primitive electrostatic models of DH, MSA and Born treat the 
solvent implicitly, as a continuous medium characterized with a static permittivity, with only ions 
modeled as single molecules (McMillian Mayer framework). It was noted that inaccuracies for

7.1.1 Debye-Hückel and Mean spherical approximation 
For describing long range ion-ion interactions, mainly two models are used: The Debye-Hückel 
law, and the Mean Spherical Approximation method. The two theories have been investigated and 
found to give similar results [4], [5] , [10], however the MSA is still much more popular as a 
choice.  
The main difference between the two theories is the approximation of the ion. The Debye-Hückel 
theory treats the ion as a point charge with a minimum distance to other ions being the ion diameter. 
The MSA treats the ion as a hard sphere with a charge. Either theory is a good approximation for 
simple ions but as the ion grows in size, the theories are less accurate. Both theories treat the ions as 
perfect spheres, but ions such as SO4
- are obviously not perfect spheres and the charge is neither 
centered nor uniformly distributed.

7.2 Parameter estimation 
While the choice of parameters may differ between models, the parameter estimation method is 
usually done as a regression to experimental data. Even though the models are desired predictive, 
parameter estimation must be done by empirically fitting parameters, as is done for the simple 
activity coefficient models. Several articles [7], [11], [17] present their way of doing it as a 
minimization of an objective function. The function usually takes on a form similar to this:

Where X is some variable, and “i” represents the overall variable choice with    being the chosen 
weight associated with this variable. “j” is an individual point of variable i. The objective function 
is minimized with Levenberg-Marquardt, Nelder-Mead or a similar algorithm.  
There usually is a tradeoff between simplicity and accuracy. It is desired to have a model as 
predictive as possible. However if the accuracy is low, the predictivity is usually compromised in 
favor of more parameters. The introduction of binary parameters between even ions may be 
unavoidable, but the more parameters fitted to the system the less predictive the model becomes. 
There is a choice between salt specific and ion specific parameters when developing a model. 
Usually Ion specific is the popular choice, as it allows for easy expansion of the model. Salt specific 
parameters tend to be easier to fit for single salt solutions, but it poses a problem for systems with 
many ions/salts [4].  
The choice of which parameters to fit to data also varies greatly. Some groups [9], [11], [18], [19] 
choose to use the experimental values for ion diameters determined by Pauling [20]. These are 
values based on measurements of salt crystal radii. A comparison of different values of the hard 
sphere ionic diameters is shown in Figure 2 and Figure 3, and a comparison of the solvated ion 
diameter is seen in Figure 4 and Figure 5. Both hard sphere and solvated ion diameter varies greatly 
between sources, and the correct physical values are not known. Usually the experimental values by 
Pauling tend to give a worse fit, but calculated ion diameters may end up deviating too much from 
the physics making it difficult to expand the model to more ions/salts [17]. When fitting the ionic 
diameter to experimental data, the best choice is probably keeping in mind, the diameters by 
Pauling or similar experimental results, so a fitted diameter still has a physical meaning.

While there is a choice on which parameters to be estimated, there is also a great difference to 
which data are used for parameter fitting. Data comes in the form of SLE, LLE, and VLE data, salt 
activity, water activity, density data, and thermal data types. Different groups use different data for 
parameter estimation, and several present only results of one or few of these data. A full model 
should preferably be able to correlate/predict all these data. The choice of which experimental data 
to include is discussed in the thesis of Maribo-Mogensen [4]. While a lot of data is available, some 
data, like osmotic coefficients, are typically scarce at temperatures or pressures other than 298 K 
and 1 atm., especially for other solvents than water that may also be deemed important, such as 
alcohols, and for multisalt data.  
7.3 Selected work on electrolyte EoS models 
Several groups have developed equations of states for the modeling of electrolyte systems. Some of 
the earlier models are all the way back from 1981 such as by Planche et al. [22]. A lot of work has 
been done since, but while some groups show great development, no one has yet presented a fully 
physical model, that provides decent results for all applications. Most groups tend to focus their 
efforts on modeling activity coefficients and osmotic coefficients, giving little concern to 
solubilities. Here selected work on some of the more developed models will be presented, to give an 
overview over the different approaches and results.  
Technical University of Dortmund 
 The ePC-SAFT model was presented by Cameretti et al. in 2005 [3]. It showed decent 
results but a limited application range. The model uses the Debye Hückel theory as one of 
the only groups for electrostatic interactions but uses no Born term. The presented results 
are good but are limited to densities and vapor pressures over a temperature and pressure 
range.  
 The model was further developed and presented by Held et al. in 2008 [23]. This introduced 
a greater application range with 115 salts. The results are quite good, with temperature 
dependencies for densities and vapor pressures. Activity coefficients are also presented but 
only at 298 K. There is still no SLE presented. 
 In 2012 further results and parameters for the model were presented by Held et al. [14]. Data

8 The electrolyte-CPA  
The electrolyte CPA model developed is presented in the thesis of Maribo-Mogensen in 2014 [4] 
and by Maribo-Mogensen et al. in 2015 [1]. While other earlier exploits are great each has their 
own limitation. The work of Maribo-Mogensen presents a systematic approach of creating a model 
with a wide application, that depicts the physics correctly rather than using empirical correlations. 
From a thorough literature study by Maribo-Mogensen, the following conclusions and decisions 
were made: 
 Correct representation of the static permittivity is very important for the primitive 
electrostatic terms, and a physical model is needed rather than the previous empirical 
models. 
 The physical forces should be modeled with a CPA EoS. While SAFT is also viable. CPA 
presents a simpler form with equally good results. 
 The electrostatic forces should be modeled by DH. MSA provides an equally good 
representation, but the DH equation is simpler, limiting computation at no cost of accuracy. 
 A Born term should be included to represent the energy of solvation in a physically correct 
manner. 
 Additional terms such as explicit terms for ion-ion association and ion-dipole association are 
not implemented at first as this would increase the complexity drastically. Instead such 
terms may be added in future development if the model proves unsatisfactory.

These models are fine for correlating the static permittivity, but they are empirical and do not 
represent the physical behavior, and as such a new model is needed if the system is expanded to 
other solvents and conditions. It may also pose a problem when calculating the thermodynamic 
properties from the partial derivatives of the Helmholtz energy [12], which limits extrapolation. 
Maribo-Mogensen et al. [12], [29] has done a great research on how different phenomena rather 
than variables affect the static permittivity. It is discussed that the method used for measurement of 
static permittivity itself introduces a systematic error. An error which has largely been ignored by 
researchers, but must be accounted for in a physical model. A model which accurately describes the 
physics must take into account the phenomena happening in the solution. When ions are present, the 
solvation happening around each ion will reduce the static permittivity, and as such an association 
term must be included in a full physical model. The static permittivity reduces with increasing 
temperature and increases with pressure. 
 4 models are tested numerically by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [12]. 2 empirical models and 2 
physical models with and without an association term. The model for the static permittivity with the

Association term 
The association term accounts for association between molecules. This is usually hydrogen bonding 
between solvent molecules.  
Ion-ion association and ion-dipole association may also happen, however most models neglect this 
term as it is quite complex. Ion-ion association is in the form of ion pairing which are non-charged 
particles dissolved. Ions can also be paired while still separated by a solvent layer. Ion-ion pairing 
usually does not happen for salts in water, but it happens in solvents with a lower static permittivity 
such as alcohols, or with weaker electrolytes, such as acids in water 
Ion-dipole association is usually in the form of Ion solvation, or ion hydration in water, where the 
charged ion will be surrounded by solvent molecules. This is accounted for implicitly in the Born 
term. 
Some groups try circumventing the ion-ion association with empirical binary dispersive interactive 
coefficients, such as done by Held et al. [13].

8.2 Limits to the application 
While the model has shown a wide application range (see Table 3), there are still limits to the 
model, which need further work. 
 The densities are not predicted correctly without the use of a Peneloux volume. While the 
Peneloux volume allows for accurate correlations, it is another parameter, which needs 
fitting. 
 LLE is modeled suboptimal. This is partly due to not fully dissociation electrolytes in 
solvents with lower static permittivity. This could possibly be fixed by including an ion-ion 
association term.  
 The apparent molal heat capacity is not predicted accurately  
 Using the salt specific parameters multisalt modeling can only be done if there is a common 
ion. As such one cannot model the system if there are both multiple cations AND multiple 
anions. 
While the model shows impressive results, it is only validated for a limited number of systems, and 
only few multisalt systems. To fully claim the validity of the model, additional data validation is 
needed

9 Validation of model for multisalt systems 
The electrolyte CPA has been validated against a variety of data for several electrolyte systems, 
however only for a limited number of multisalt systems. Here the model will be validated for 
additional multisalt systems at varying temperatures.  
For validation of the model parameters experimental data is needed. Since the model is set up in a 
specific way using salt specific parameters, the model can only be validated for systems with a 
common ion. The procedure takes the relation between salt and ion parameters (equation (23)), and 
calculates the ion parameters setting the contribution to the dispersive forces,     , for the common 
ion to zero. 
Two parameter sets are of interest for validation, the one developed by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [1] 
and the one developed by Schlaikjer et al. [2].The experimental data are collected from the DTU 
CERE database [33]. Multisalt data for osmotic coefficients are very limited, especially at 
temperatures other than 298 K, being mostly present for the systems of NaCl /KCl and NaCl/CaCl2. 
The parameters introduced by Schlaikjer et al. [2], does not include parameters for CaCl2, so 
validation of osmotic coefficients of these parameters are only done for the NaCl /KCl system.  
Validation will be done only for osmotic coefficients and for solubilities as these properties have 
easily available experimental multisalt data over a range of temperatures. Solubility data are plenty 
and a selected number of systems will be investigated. The systems are chosen, so as to have a 
system with a common anion, and a system with a common cation. Nitrate systems are considered 
as well as these are typically problematic as discussed in the section 7.3.1. 
The figures are made with the engine developed by Maribo-Mogensen. An example of a solubility 
calculation is seen in Appendix 1, and an example of an osmotic coefficient calculation is seen in 
Appendix 2. 
The mixed salt data are not used in the regression of parameters, and as such all results are 
predictive.

10.2 Fitting ions systematically 
It is noted by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [10] that DH and MSA have similar performance if the ion 
diameter is a fitted parameter, which it currently is not in the eCPA. As such it is desired to see if a 
fitting of the ion size could increase the accuracy. Simultaneous fitting of ion size parameters would 
be optimal, but requires extensive programming and computation time. Instead as a first attempt, a 
systematic fitting of ion diameters is done one salt at a time. A similar method was originally used 
by Maribo-Mogensen [4], for fitting of the interaction parameters systematically as described in 
section 8.1.5. First the ion sizes are fitted to NaCl and then all other ions are fitted to salts with 
fixed Na+ or Cl- ions. NaCl is used as a first fit due to well documented behavior and extensive 
amounts of data, as seen in Table 7. Ion sizes will be fitted simultaneously with the salt specific 
energy interaction parameters, while the Born radius will be kept as originally fitted by Maribo-
Mogensen et al. [1].  
As the ion diameters are ion specific parameters, while at present the energy interaction parameter 
is salt specific, fitting the ion diameters for single salts will result in different ion diameters for the 
same ions but for different salts. This is of course not feasible, and as such a fitting of ion sizes will 
have to be done systematically by first fitting for 1 salt and then keeping these values fixed. This 
will result in ion sizes for salts, not containing a Na+ or Cl- ion being purely predictive, and a 
thorough validation of the new parameters will be needed.  
Fitting of the ion size parameters is done in MATLAB (See script in  Appendix 3). First the 
reference state, ΔUref is fitted at 298 K and then Tα is fitted. Following this all parameters 
including ion sizes and all interaction parameters are fitted simultaneously. As such 5 parameters 
are fitted simultaneously for NaCl, 4 ions are fitted simultaneously for other salts containing, either, 
Na+ or Cl-, while 3 parameters are fitted simultaneously for the rest of the salts. While energy 
interaction parameters were fitted unconstrained the ion sizes will have to be constrained, as an 
unconstrained fit may result in unphysical values such as an ion size of zero Ångström. As there are 
many more data points at low molalities, the scarce data at higher molalities will again be weighted 
higher in a minimization term. The MATLAB function FMINSEARCH is used initially for 
unconstrained fit of interaction parameters while the function FMINCON and LSQNONLIN is used 
for a constrained fit of the ion sizes. 
The choice of boundary constraints for the fitting of ion diameters can vary greatly. As evidenced 
by Figure 2 and Figure 3 in section 8.1.5, the choice of ion sizes can sometimes vary more than 50 
% between sources. It was however found that even a size change of ± 25 % of the Marcus 
diameters, while giving lower average deviations would cause the trends of the data to clearly not 
be followed by the model for some of the salts. While the fit is reasonable for 25 % change of the 
NaCl ion sizes, it is found that fitting at a 25 % range puts the Cl- and Na+ ion to approximately 
==PAGE:45==

3-Jun-16 
By Søren Mortensen 
 
Page 45 of 131 
 
same size, which is not physically reasonable. Fitting at 10 % is better and is the choice for a 
systematic fitting of ion parameters. All available single salt data is used for fitting rather than 
having a maximum molality of ionic strength. This may compromise accuracy at low molalities, but 
after testing it was found to generally not have a big impact even with weighting molalities>4, 100 
times as much. 
The exact ion sizes may be unknown, but it is obvious, that larger atoms will also have larger ions 
and, that anions will be larger than cations. From the experimental Marcus diameters the order of 
ion sizes will be as follows: Mg2+<Na+<K+<NO3-<Cl-<Br-<SO42-. While this trend seems 
somewhat reasonable, it could be argued that Mg2+ and Na+ ions should be approximately same 
size, and though the nitrate ion is found to be smaller than the chloride ion, it may very well be 
larger.

10.3 Simultaneous fitting of ion sizes 
A simultaneous fitting of ion sizes is desired, as this would theoretically find the overall best 
solution, while it may give slightly worse results for some individual salts. The overall challenge is 
however setting up the script so it can easily be expanded to any number of ions. A generic script 
for fitting of any number of ions is seen in Appendix 5. 
As there is not an equal amount of data for each salt, a weighting is done for each data point in the 
objective function. The weight for a given salt, "i", is calculated as seen in equation (41). 
This ensures that each individual data point may be weighted differently between salts, but overall 
the salts will be weighted equally. Interaction parameters will remain salt specific, as further 
investigation to the temperature dependency of these is needed, however the script is easily 
converted to ion specific interaction parameters if needed. 
The iterations become increasingly slow the more ions/salts are included in the fitting. Since the 
number of salts is equal to anions times cations the amount of data is increased polynomially and as 
such, the time complexity of the system is polynomial as well. 3 anions and 3 cations have almost 
5000 data points, which have to be solved for, at each iteration. An illustration of the increase in 
iteration time is shown in Figure 14.

relative average deviation is calculated. This will give an overview of the optimal ion sizes for each 
salt. Note that each ion size is not fully validated, and as such this is not a complete sensitivity 
analysis.

10.6 Conclusion on fitting of ion sizes 
It was found that the Pauling ion sizes gave a significantly worse fit than the Marcus ion sizes, and 
that the true ion sizes should indeed be in the proximity of the Marcus diameters. 
There was a great improvement in relative average deviations by introducing the ion diameter as a 
fitted parameter. The great improvement was also reflected in the trends which were notably better 
at most temperatures compared to the parameter sets by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [1] and Schlaikjer 
et al. [2]. The results for solubilities were however worse even compared to the parameter set by 
Maribo-Mogensen et al. [1], and including the solubilities in simultaneous parameter fitting would 
be an obvious next step, giving hope that a parameterization of the ion diameter could combine the 
accuracy for osmotic and activity coefficients by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [1] and the accurate 
depiction of solubilities by Schlaikjer et al. [2]. The fitting to solubility data is more difficult, as it 
requires knowledge of the standard state properties of the solid phases as well. As some of the 
hydrated salts do not have readily available experimental data, the standard state properties of these 
phases will have to be fitted as well. The fitting of ion sizes would make sense alongside ion 
specific interaction parameters. 
The fit was very sensitive to changes in ion diameters. The sensitivity analysis showed that no 
single solution can be found that satisfies every plot perfectly, and that several local minima exist. 
As such the best solution should be found by iteration using simulated annealing, for the best 
possible global minimum. 
11 Discussion 
Currently it seems that the eCPA measures up well against similar models, but one should keep in 
mind the different scope of some of these models. It seems the eCPA is more focused on solubilities 
than any other models. While the solubility plots are impressive, the solubilities for hydrated salts 
can more easily be impressive for any model, when the standard state properties are fitted 
alongside. Generally however, the eCPA has a wider application range, with VLE, SLE, LLE and 
heat capacities as well. 
As discussed by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [1] the choice between DH or MSA for the modeling of 
electrostatic forces becomes less important when fitting the ion sizes alongside. As such one could

argue that fitting the ion diameters would be important, to avoid any inaccuracies. It seems the 
correlative trend of the activity and osmotic coefficients are generally improved, using fitted ion 
diameters as well, though the accuracy of solubility predictions is slightly decreased. 
By introducing the hard sphere ion diameter, the model now becomes quite heavy in parameters. By 
including density data in the fitting one might eliminate the Peneloux volume parameter, and thus 
not increase the amount of parameters. Alternatively, another correlation between co-volume and 
ion diameter could be considered. As mentioned in section 8.1.2 the correlation is not fully agreed 
upon, and another factor, may change the correlation.  
Generally it would be wise to keep the fitting of ions as a possibility, and consider it alongside 
implementation of ion specific interaction parameters, and if the number of ions in the fitting 
increases. Ion specific interaction parameters would be a great step in improving the applicability of 
the model to model multisalt systems containing both different cations and anions, and fitting the 
ion sizes alongside would be a likely optimal extension. If the ion sizes are to be fitted beyond the 
boundaries of 10 percent used in this work, it may be necessary to include additional hard 
constraints, to dictate the order of ion sizes. 
It may be considered to implement multisalt data in the fitting of parameters. The model was not 
able to fully predict multisalt solutions. Especially when nitrate salts is introduced; the trend of one 
salt, increasing the solubility of another salt was not captured by the model. Fitting to multisalt data 
would however further reduce the predictivity of the model. 
It may be that further optimization is not possible through parameterization of the model, and 
instead a more complex model is needed. Ion-ion association could be introduced or more complex 
models for temperature dependencies or even concentration dependencies. It is notorious for this 
model (as well as similar models) to have trouble with correlation/prediction of osmotic coefficients 
at very high temperatures. 
12 Conclusion 
From the literature study it was found, that the eCPA by Maribo-Mogensen et al. and Schlaikjer et 
al. [2] has a wide application range and generally performs very well compared to other models. But 
for some applications some of the other models still have an impressive range, in which the eCPA 
still needs further development. These areas include number of salts, weak electrolytes. And 
extreme conditions, (Very high pressures and temperatures). 
From the validation of the model it was found that the new parameters presented by Schlaikjer et al. 
[2] had a greater performance at elevated or decreased temperatures for predicting multisalt 
solubilities, but still has limits at conditions with both high concentrations and high/low 
temperatures, specifically for nitrate systems.  
The relative average deviation was found to be 6.35 for a mixture of NaCl and KCl at 298 K and 
5.17 at 318 K for the parameter set by Schlaikjer et al. [2]. The relative average deviation was found 
to be 6.26 for at 298 K and 5.25 at 318 K for the parameter set by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [1].

Further validation is needed to give a complete overview of the capabilities of the model, but a full 
validation will require additional experimental data, which is scarce especially for multisalt osmotic 
coefficient data at elevated temperatures.  
The fitting of the ion sizes showed a new aspect of the model which may be relevant. As concluded 
by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [10] the DH and MSA performs equally well if the ion size is included 
as a fitted parameter, which is not implemented until this project. The simultaneous fitting of ion 
sizes is extremely relevant especially for future implementations of ion specific interaction 
parameters. Results were good and the relative average deviations were improved to  
1.93 for activity coefficients and 1.62 for osmotic coefficients for NaCl 
2.81 for activity coefficients and 3.78 for osmotic coefficients for NaBr 
2.81 for activity coefficients and 1.75 for osmotic coefficients for KCl 
2.66 for activity coefficients and 0.57for osmotic coefficients for KBr 
The solubility trends were however less accurate with these new parameters and it is suggested that 
ion size fitting should be implemented alongside a fit to solubilities as well. 
12.1 Further work 
 The fitting of ion sizes should be included in a fit to solubility data as well, and alongside 
ion specific interaction parameters. 
 Validation of ion modified ion sizes for prediction (with data not used for fitting) as well as 
for multisalt data and other solvents. 
 Addition of data to the fitting pool could improve accuracies for these. Multisalt data would 
be a possibility as well as density data to eliminate the Peneloux volume parameter.  
 Besides parameterization the model could be further developed with expressions describing 
ion-ion interaction parameters for weak electrolytes. 
 The trend of osmotic coefficients at high molality/temperatures is not modeled accurately, 
with the current parameterization. To capture the trend accurately a new temperature 
dependency may need to be introduced somewhere in the expression.

– Osmotic coefficient calculations, script 
An example of osmotic coefficient calculations for NaCl/KCl system 
function script() 
%Calculations of osmotic coefficients of a 3 ion system with: 
% NaCl   (salt id=310) 
% KCl    (salt id=323) 
close all 
clc 
clear all 
  
%Salt id's in database are defined 
sid1=310; 
sid2=323; 
type=1; 
[sm1 sm2 exp st]=osmoticmix(sid1, sid2, type); 
  
%For some of the data the salts have been mixed up. This is corrected 
sm1s=sm2(55:58); 
sm2(55:58)=sm1(55:58); 
sm1(55:58)=sm1s; 
sm1s=sm2(1); 
sm2(1)=sm1(1); 
sm1(1)=sm1s; 
sm1s=sm2; 
sm2=sm1; 
sm1=sm1s; 
  
%Finding unique temperatures 
uniT=unique(st); 
%Unique temperatures 
% 298.1500 
% 318.1500 
  
%If type is 0. it is calculated as osmotic coefficient by: 
%exp=-N_water/(NU*N_salt)*.log(exp). 
if type==0 
    for i=1:length(exp) 
        exp(i)=transpose(-(1000/18)/(2*(sm1(i)+sm2(i)))).*(log(exp(i))); 
    end 
end 
  
%Sorting in temperatures 
mm1=NaN(length(sm1),length(uniT)); 
mm2=NaN(length(sm1),length(uniT)); 
exp2=NaN(length(sm1),length(uniT)); 
for j=1:length(sm1) 
    for i=1:length(uniT) 
        if st(j)==uniT(i) 
            mm1(j,i)=sm1(j); 
            mm2(j,i)=sm2(j); 
            exp2(j,i)=exp(j); 
        end 
    end 
end

16.3 Minimization function 
 
function r = 
SaltObjectiveFunctionsigma0(Uref,alpha,Talpha,sigma1,sigma2,vstoic,Ifac,Gammax,T
gam,mgam,gamma,Tphi,mphi,phi,charge1,charge2,ghyd1,ghyd2,bmix1,bmix2) 
%SolubilityIndex is a function (T,P,m) that returns the solubility index with 
the smallest value 
  
Tref = 298.15; 
mollim=1000; 
U0 = Uref+alpha-alpha*(1-Tref/Talpha)^2; 
Ut = -2*alpha/Talpha; 
Utt = alpha/Talpha^2; 
  
b01=(sigma1*10^-8)^3*2/3*6.022141*10^23*pi; 
b02=(sigma2*10^-8)^3*2/3*6.022141*10^23*pi; 
  
%INSERT ALL THINGS AS INPUTS 
  
SETNRTL(1,1,2,U0,U0,Ut,Ut,Utt,Utt,0,0); 
SETNRTL(2,1,3,U0,U0,Ut,Ut,Utt,Utt,0,0); 
SETCPA(1,14.515,1017.34,0.67359);%Water 
SETCPA(2,b01,1,0);% 
SETCPA(3,b02,1,0);% 
SETION(2,charge1,sigma1,ghyd1,bmix1*1e-6); 
SETION(3,charge2,sigma2,ghyd2,bmix2*1e-6); 
SETUP_THERMO() 
  
T = [Tgam;Tphi]; 
mm = [mgam;mphi]; 
gam = [gamma;0*mphi]; 
Typ = [ones(size(Tgam));2*ones(size(Tphi))]; 
t = unique([Tgam;Tphi]); 
r = 0*T; 
for i=1:length(t) 
    P = max(101325,WaterVaporPressure(t(i)-273.15)); 
    idx1 = T == t(i) & Typ == 1 & Ifac*mm < mollim & gam<Gammax & mm>0; 
    idx = Tgam == t(i) & Ifac*mgam < mollim & gamma<Gammax & mgam>0; 
    m = mgam(idx); 
    if(~isempty(m)) 
        w = 1e3/18.015./(1e3/18.015+vstoic*m); 
        [AW,PHI,GAMMA,VAPP] = ACTIVITY(t(i),P,m); 
        weight=1; 
        if m>4 
            weight=1;

        end 
        r(idx1) = (w.*GAMMA-gamma(idx))./(gamma(idx))*weight; %.*m.^3 
    end 
    idx1 = T == t(i) & Typ == 2 & Ifac*mm<mollim & mm>0; 
    idx = Tphi == t(i) & Ifac*mphi<mollim & mphi>0; 
    m = mphi(idx); 
    if(~isempty(m)) 
        w = 1e3/18.015./(1e3/18.015+vstoic*m); 
        [AW,PHI,GAMMA,VAPP] = ACTIVITY(t(i),P,m); 
        weight=1; 
        if m>4 
            weight=100; 
        end 
        r(idx1) = (PHI-phi(idx))./(phi(idx))*weight; %.*m.^3 
  
    end 
end 
  
  
r = real(r); 
end

function

    NrNRTL=i+NrNRTL; 
end 
global ThermoIsInit 
if(nargin<19) 
    ions = false;

    if(ions) 
        INIT_THERMO(1+Nrions,0,NrNRTL,0,0,Nrions)

        INIT_THERMO(1+Nrions,0,NrNRTL,0,0,Nrsalts) 
        ThermoIsInit=true; 
    end 
end

SETCRIT(1,647.13,220.55e5,0.3449);%Water 
SETCPA(1,14.515,1017.34,0.67359);%Water 
SETPOLAR(1,1.8546,1.613342581,0); 
count=0;
