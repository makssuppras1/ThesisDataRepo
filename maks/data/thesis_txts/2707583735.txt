In this thesis, a tool was developed to help enable informed decision-making when evalu-
ating indoor climate renovations. Based on internationally recognized methods, the tool
simulates the indoor environment and energy use before and after a renovation.
The
annual temperature and CO2 distributions of the simulated oﬀice environment are esti-
mated and used to predict the effect of the indoor environment on performance and short
term sick-leave. The wider benefits of the investment are estimated by calculating the
net present value (NPV) of the investment based on the change in productivity. The tool
enables the evaluation of a range of different renovation scenarios and supports decisions
on building upgrades.
The economic benefits of installing mechanical ventilation were
found to far outweigh the costs of installing and running the ventilation system.

In developed countries, people spend 80% to 90% of their lives indoors [1]. It is estimated
that employed people spend anything from 20 to 60 hours per week at their workplace
[2]. The pollution indoors can often be 2–100 times higher than outdoors. Humans are
exposed to indoor pollutants for significantly longer durations than outdoor pollutants
[2].
Indoor environmental quality has been shown to affect the health, productivity, and
well-being of its occupants. Poor indoor air quality is the consequence of elevated levels
of air pollutants emitted from building materials, furnishings, and pollution from human
metabolism. Lack of control over these exposures through ventilation can therefore have
detrimental effects on occupant comfort, health, and performance. [3].
The association between the indoor environment and the performance of oﬀice work has
strong economic implications. Studies show that the salaries of oﬀice workers greatly
exceed the cost of running a building in developed countries [4]. Therefore, even a small
increase in productivity from an improved indoor climate can result in a substantial
economic benefit.
When renovating buildings the primary driver is often to reduce energy costs and green-
house gas emissions through higher energy eﬀiciency.
The costs of a renovation are
routinely estimated through consultancy and the direct benefits of higher energy eﬀi-
ciency are easily reported.
The wider benefits of improved indoor climate are generally not considered in conven-
tional economic calculations on building design and operation [4].
One explanation is the mistaken assumption that we do not have suﬀicient knowledge to
quantify the secondary benefits of an appropriate indoor environment. Credibility and
measurability are therefore part of the challenge. Even though studies and organizations
have suggested methods of evaluating the advantages of an improved indoor environment,
the industry has not come to a wide agreement on how to evaluate the benefits.
There is a need for a shared framework on how to include the indoor environment in
evaluations of the total economy of buildings [5].
The objective of this thesis was to develop and implement a tool to estimate the economic
benefits of indoor climate renovations and their effects on oﬀice productivity. This can
be used to include the wider benefits of renovating buildings alongside the commonly
evaluated energy eﬀiciency.

When quantifying the socio-economic consequences of energy and indoor climate reno-
vations in oﬀices, one of the first steps is to establish how the indoor climate affects its
occupants. This was done through a literature study, where relevant articles were read
to get as much information on the topic as possible. The literature study was approached
with a coherent strategy and careful planning.
It is important to consider the main study objective. For our study objective, we chose
to define it as: To research the impact of the indoor environment in oﬀices on the health
and performance of oﬀice workers.
Defining what kind of literary material that is relevant for the study can help add a
sense of direction to the literary research. The main kind of literary material used for
this project were: books, scientific articles, literature reviews, and standards. Another
kind of material revisited in this project was a variety of different programming forums
and educational videos.
The main search engines used to locate literature in the field of engineering include DTU
Findit, Google Scholar, Web of Science, and PubMed.
Several key search terms were defined to make it easier to discover relevant literature.
Some of the key search terms used for this thesis were: health, productivity, performance,
indoor climate, air quality, thermal environment, oﬀices, particulate matter, building-
related symptoms, sick building syndrome. Some of these key search terms discovered
many articles, while other terms did not have as many associations. As an example,
we found a lot of literature linking health, productivity, and performance in oﬀices with
air quality and the thermal environment but not suﬀicient literature linking it with
particulate matter. A flowchart of the literature research process is shown in Figure 2.1
By going through the bibliographies of the identified literature, we discovered even more
studies and articles within the scope of the project. Using appropriate search methods
can help uncover a lot of relevant material. Early in the research process, the main
search methods used were random search and citation pearl search. Though, as the
scope of the project was narrowed down cited reference search and blocks search were
the most commonly used methods.
The different phases of the literature research are circular steps that once they are
completed, are re-evaluated and re-examined as the circle starts again until the scope of
the project is clear and confined to the correct literary area. The literature was screened
based on title, abstract, and conclusions. An overview of the processes involved with
the literature research study is presented in Figure 2.2.

The terms total value and total economy (Danish: ”totalværdi” & ”totaløkonomi”) are
closely related and yet have considerable differences. The following section describes the
differences between the two terms, as well as the challenges and benefits.

7RWDO HFRQRP\
Total economy has long been diﬀicult to incorporate into cost-benefit analyses, at least
if the wider benefits of indoor climate should be accounted for in the building’s financial
reports. Construction, maintenance, and operation costs are commonly considered in
newer construction projects, whereas indoor climate has proven diﬀicult to incorporate
in the financial estimation of buildings [59].
Total economy takes outset in a holistic view of the economy to describe the economic
value of e.g. a building project within a longsighted perspective.
Total economy is an evaluation of the total economical situation, where construction,
material life cycle, and expenses for e.g. supply, maintenance, and operation are ac-
counted for. By investing in quality early in the project life cycle, the life span of the
project increases, while operation and maintenance costs decrease which improves the
total economy [59].
Total economic evaluations can be done by transforming all expenses over the project
life cycle into a Net Present Value (NPV). The year, where the NPV equals 0 is the
payback time, which marks the time frame in which the Return of Investment (ROI) is
0 [59].
Total economic considerations have been required by government-funded projects that
cost above 5 Mio. DKK in Denmark since 1998, however, there is still found to be a lack
of focus on the subject [59].

7RWDO YDOXH
The term total value expands the total economic field to include the social and environ-
mental aspects of a building. Total value, therefore, consists of the three elements in the
traditional sustainability definition; environmental, social, and economic.
Total value includes wider benefits such as increased well-fare, comfort, aesthetics, work-
ing conditions, etc. Total value weighs a holistic perspective on a building and considers
the occupants and the local environment to help enable informed decision-making [59].

Total value and total economy consider more aspects than what is usually used in tradi-
tional building projects. The largest expenses of a building are rarely connected to the
construction or running of the building, but the activities happening inside the building
[59].

or rents. Productivity increases of 1% correspond to reduced sick leave of two days per
year, reduced breaks from work or increased time at work of 5 minutes per day, or a 1%
increase in the effectiveness of physical and mental work [58].

If the financial benefits of incorporating total value in building projects are as great as
described in Section 3.1.3, one might be inclined to ask, why it is still not found to be
common practice within the building industry [59].
One of the main challenges is, that the benefits of reduced running costs often do not
benefit the decision-makers, who determine the size of the investment. The reason for

this is among other things that the current Danish tender format does not allow the
contractors to incorporate both the construction phase and operational phase simulta-
neously. Another example of when this issue can occur is when a municipality with
limited funds for the construction of a building does not reap the economic benefits of
the running phase as the building is allotted to another party. In these cases, there is

The total value concept also has the challenge, that the wider benefits often can be
diﬀicult to assign a monetary value. Especially because they are often based on subjective

Many studies have tried to evaluate the wider benefits, however, actual calculation tools
and methods that quantify the economic consequences of total value are rarely used to
a large extent. Therefore, the total value is often overlooked in the financial equation,
although studies have shown that the economic benefits of indoor climate renovations
often overshadow the expenses [59].
This study aims to help incorporate the total value of oﬀice buildings into the financial
estimation of building projects.
Through a presentation of studies investigating the
impact of the indoor environment on productivity in oﬀices, the importance of total
value will be solidified, and by the development of a total value estimation tool to help
make informed decisions when quantifying the costs of indoor climate renovations.

It is important to distinguish between individual performance and productivity [41].
Mainly unsubstantiated associations between the indoor environment and productivity
exist, whereas hard scientific data is sparse. One of the main reasons for the lack of
data within the field is that productivity as a concept is multi-dimensional and can be
diﬀicult to measure [52].
Productivity is a complex concept, however, it is in principle a ratio of output relative to
input. Thus, improving productivity can be achieved by either increasing the output or
decreasing the input. Increasing the output can be achieved by increasing the quantity
and/or quality of the delivered product/service [61].
Individual performance metrics such as speed or errors are in many ways unrelated to
the monetary value of the work performed. However, these performance metrics can
affect the quality and quantity of the product/service and therefore directly influences
the general oﬀice productivity. Productivity is also affected by factors influencing the
input such as absenteeism, health costs, and the capital and running costs of a building,
none of which affect performance [41].
Often the term productivity is used instead of performance. In this report, the term

performance will generally be used, as productivity describes a wider economic term that
combines a volume measure of the output in relation to the input and is, therefore, less

It is believed that individual performance is likely to have a major effect on productivity,
as wage costs per unit floor area are usually many times greater than the cost per unit
floor area of providing and running the building [41]. Woods claimed that any envi-
ronmental condition that decreases individual performance (either quantity or quality),
increases absenteeism, or reduces turnover, is more expensive for organizations than the
costs of improving the indoor environment [62].

Individual performance is affected by a multitude of different factors such as working
environment, ability to perform a job, and personal motivation [39]. The work environ-
ment comprises indoor climate (temperature, air quality, noise, lighting, etc.), facilities,

The indoor environment in oﬀice buildings affects the health, comfort, and productivity
of its occupants. It is estimated that indoor environmental quality is more important
for the performance of oﬀice workers than job satisfaction and job stress [53].
The following section will describe the main associations between the indoor environment
in oﬀice buildings and work performance, health, and short-term sick leave.

Indoor temperature is one of the fundamental characteristics of the indoor environment
[31]. The temperature influences several occupant responses such as thermal comfort,
perceived air quality, BRS, and work performance. It is believed that the thermal envi-
ronment can affect the work performance of oﬀice-related tasks by at least six different
mechanisms: (I) thermal discomfort distracts attention, (II) warmth lowers arousal (the
state of activation of an individual), exacerbates building-related symptoms, and has
a negative effect on cognition, (III) cold conditions decrease finger temperatures and
thus have a negative effect on manual dexterity, (IV) rapid temperature swings have the
same effects on oﬀice work as slightly raised room temperatures, while slow tempera-
ture swings merely cause discomfort, (V) vertical thermal gradients reduce perceived air
quality or lead to a reduction in room temperature that then causes complaints of cold
at floor level, (VI) raised temperatures can result in increased CO2 concentration in the
blood, which may cause headaches [32][25].
In a study by Wyon (1996) thermal conditions within the thermal comfort zone have
been found to affect the performance of oﬀice related tasks by 5-15% [41]. However, the
study also postulated that thermal conditions that provide optimal comfort conditions
may not provide optimal performance conditions.
In a study by Pepler and Warner (1968) a group of young American subjects performed

Niemelä et al. (2002) compared the productivity in two call centers before and after

and after the intervention was 25.1 °C and 22.6 °C respectively [52].
Lan et al. (2011) investigated the effects of thermal discomfort on health and perfor-
mance in an oﬀice environment, in an attempt to describe the physiological mechanisms
involved. 12 subjects performed neurobehavioral tests and tasks typical of oﬀice work

subjects felt warm, they assessed the air quality to be worse, reported increased intensity
of many building-related symptoms, expressed more negative mood, were less willing to
exert effort, and task performance decreased. The results suggest that the negative ef-
fects on health and performance that occur when people feel thermally warm at raised
temperatures are caused by physiological mechanisms [53].
In another study by Lan et al. (2010) the effects of thermal discomfort on the perfor-

neurobehavioural tests examining different component skills, and addition and typing

caused by elevated air temperature had a negative effect on performance.

It is not only a build-up of pollutants that influences how the air quality is perceived.
Studies show that humidity and temperature of the inhaled air influence the perceived

sensation of the entire body is kept neutral [36][37].

The exact mechanisms by which indoor air quality affects performance are not fully
understood.
It is not unreasonable to suggest that the perception of the indoor air
quality is linked to performance, since people who feel unwell and have building-related
symptoms such as headaches and diﬀiculty in thinking clearly will not work to optimal
ability [32].
Nunes et al. [38] found support for this effect through a field investigation in an oﬀice
building, which demonstrated that oﬀice workers who had reported acute health symp-
toms that day performed significantly worse on diagnostic psychological tests that were
administered intermittently throughout the working day by a computer [32].
Studies of adult subjects performing simulated oﬀice work [11] showed that increased air
pollution caused by gaseous emissions from typical building materials, furnishing, and
oﬀice equipment caused subjects to exhale less carbon dioxide. This could either be a
consequence of reduced metabolic rate due to reduced motivation to perform work in
polluted air or a consequence of physiological changes leading to ineﬀicient gas exchange
in the lungs when polluted air is inhaled, resulting in a higher concentration of carbon
dioxide in the blood. The latter mechanism is known to cause headaches and diﬀiculty
in thinking clearly [32].

cognitive tests, as well as typing and addition tasks to simulate typical oﬀice work during
4.5-hour exposures. The indoor air quality caused 70% to be dissatisfied with the air
quality when the used carpet was present and 25% when it was absent. The presence

Carbon dioxide (CO2) and bioeffluents are both bi-products of the human metabolism,
where human bioeffluents cover a large range of pollutants emitted by humans. Studies
show that human bioeffluents are an important component of indoor pollution sources
[46].
In indoor environments, the concentration of CO2 depends mainly on human
occupancy [19]. The indoor CO2 concentrations are typically below 2000-2500 ppm but
have been shown to get as high as 4000-5000 ppm, which is considerably higher than the
outdoor CO2 concentration which is currently approximately 400 ppm [19]. The indoor
CO2 concentration is a good indicator for the effectiveness of ventilation and elevated
concentration levels have been associated with adverse health effects [46].
It has long been assumed that exposure to CO2 itself did not cause the adverse health
effects, but rather functioned as an indication of high levels of bioeffluent pollution,
which cause the discomfort. However, studies on the health effects of pure CO2 have
shown mixed results. Exposure to CO2 concentrations of 5000-15000 ppm has shown
to increase respiratory minute ventilation rate, end-tidal CO2 (ETCO2), lowered pH in
blood as well as increased headache [19]. CO2 concentrations higher than 5000 ppm are,
however, unlikely to be found in oﬀice environments [19].

A few recent studies have also examined the effects of pure CO2 at levels typically oc-
curring in indoor environments. Kajtár and Herczeg (2012) exposed subjects to CO2
concentrations from 600 ppm to 3000 ppm for 2-3 hours and studied the effect on hu-
man well-being and oﬀice work intensity. They showed that subjects exposed to 3000
ppm evaluated the air quality as less acceptable, more unpleasant, and became more
exhausted. The mental performance was lower when the CO2 levels increased. Subjects
also reported fatigue and a decrease in well-being and the ability to concentrate when
exposed to 3000 ppm or higher for 2-3 hours [33].
Satish et al.
(2012) exposed 22 subjects to CO2 concentrations of 600, 1000, and
2500 ppm in three 2.5 hours intervals. Under each condition, participants completed a
computer-based test of decision-making performance as well as questionnaires on health
symptoms and perceived air quality. The studies showed that decision-making perfor-
mance was significantly reduced when CO2 concentrations were increased to 1000 and
2500 ppm [34]. A similar pattern was found in the study by Allen et al. (2016), where
subjects performed a similar test to Satish et al. (2012). The study found that decision-
making performance decreased when CO2 levels were 945 and 1400 ppm compared to
CO2 levels of 550 ppm [29].
The studies performed by Kajtár and Herczeg (2012), Satish et al. (2012), and Allen et
al. (2016) suggest that CO2 should be considered as a pollutant in itself and not just
as an indicator of human bioeffluents. However, these results stand in contrast to other
studies on the effects of CO2 on the indoor environment. Zhang et al. (2016) studied the
effect of exposure to CO2 and bioeffluents on perceived air quality, self-assessed acute
health symptoms, and cognitive performance. Here 25 subjects were exposed to five
different indoor environments for 255 minutes to each condition. In three of the five
exposures, the outdoor air supply rate was high enough to remove bioeffluents resulting
in a CO2 concentration of 500 ppm. Chemically pure CO2 was added to this reference
condition to create exposures of 1000 and 3000 ppm CO2. In the two other scenarios, the
ventilation rate was lowered so that the bioeffluent CO2 reached 1000 and 3000 ppm. The
study found, that no significant effects on perceived air quality, acute health symptoms,
or cognitive performance tests were observed during exposure to pure CO2. However,
exposure to 3000 ppm bioeffluent CO2 increased the intensity of reported headache,
fatigue, sleepiness, and diﬀiculty in thinking clearly. It also resulted in a reduced speed
of addition, response time in a redirection task, and the number of correct links made
in a cue-utilization test. This study suggests that moderate levels of bioeffluents, but
not pure CO2, will result in adverse health effects on occupants during typical indoor
exposures. Other published toxicological studies agree that only CO2 concentrations
that highly exceed levels typically found indoors can produce harmful effects. Law et
al. (2010) found that only concentrations of 10000 ppm or higher can cause toxic effects
during short exposure [44].

Poor indoor climate has been shown to have adverse effects on health, building-related
symptoms (BRS), and sick leave. Many studies have focused on workers’ cognitive or
physical performance, whereas potential productivity gains and savings in health care
costs from reductions in adverse health effects have received much less attention despite
the high costs of adverse health effects [58].

Potential health and productivity benefits of an improved indoor environmental quality
are generally not considered in traditional cost-benefit evaluations, where the main focus
is on the cost of the initial investment as well as maintenance and energy costs [54].
To help enable informed decision-making when quantifying the costs of indoor climate
renovations in oﬀices, a tool has been developed. While elaborate programs that can
simulate a detailed indoor environment and energy use already exist, they often require
experience and specialized knowledge. Therefore, one of the main objectives of the tool
was to have low entry requirements and simplified inputs.
This chapter will explore how the tool was set up and used, as well as present some
of the calculation methods and assumptions that provide the foundation.
First, the
models used to predict the indoor environment are presented. Then the classification
of the indoor environment is described, and finally how these classifications are used to
estimate economic effects of indoor environmental exposures.


The tool is a software that performs indoor climate simulations on a building based on
a series of input parameters that describe the building envelope properties, ventilation
principle, occupant activity, etc. The input parameters are used to simulate the indoor
climate by inserting them into a variety of different models described in further detail
later in this chapter.
The software has been programmed in Java. So far there is no graphical user interface
(GUI), however, the long-term goal is to implement a GUI to provide a better user
experience and make the tool more accessible. A simplified unified modeling language
(UML) diagram has been developed and is presented in Figure 4.1. The UML diagram
illustrates the interactions between some of the constructed Java classes, which contains
the models developed to simulate the indoor climate.

The main objective of the tool is to incorporate a holistic approach to the economic
equation when doing indoor climate renovations in oﬀice environments by including
total value into the financial decision-making. This is achieved by implementing asso-
ciations between indoor environmental quality and productivity of oﬀice-related tasks
into the tool. Furthermore, the tool applies scientifically recognized calculation meth-
ods and international standards, as well as several existing and peer-reviewed models
for infiltration, one-sided natural ventilation, cross-flow natural ventilation, mechani-
cal ventilation, and energy performance that provide the foundation of the simulations
carried out by the tool. Some variables and input parameters need national adaptation.

The tool takes outset in a baseline level that may reflect a building’s state before ren-
ovation. The calculation flow starts with the definition of an existing building (defined
by location, geometry, air leakage, heat transfer properties, windows, ventilation, cool-
ing, and heating set-point temperatures). By first assessing the current energy use and
indoor environment of a building, the effect of renovation measures such as mechanical
ventilation, cooling, or envelope properties can be estimated. The tool simulates the
thermal indoor environment in hourly time steps and indoor air quality is then calcu-
lated in smaller time steps (default set at 5 minutes). Since hourly time steps do not
allow prediction of the dilution of the indoor air caused by short-term window open-
ing events, calculation of natural ventilation is subsequently carried out with a higher
temporal resolution.
The distribution of the output variables, such as indoor CO2 concentration, indoor
temperature, air humidity, and ventilation rate, is first established for a reference case
and the calculation is then repeated for the renovated building. These variables can
be used to evaluate indoor environmental quality. This is aligned with the assessment
approach recommended in DS/EN 16798–1 [66].
The improvement of indoor climate can then be estimated in monetary value by the
implementation of the relationships between indoor climate quality and occupant pro-
ductivity as well as other oﬀice-related parameters such as annual average salary, working
hours, etc. The relationships between indoor environmental quality and productivity are
described in Section 4.13 and 4.12.

Calculations of the indoor temperature and heating and cooling loads are done according
to the simple hourly method suggested in ISO 13790 [67]. The method has the following
input requirements: Hourly external air temperature, hourly incident solar radiation,
solar height and azimuth, and day of the week. If relevant, the following can be included
as well: local or meteorological wind speed, wind direction, albedo, and relative humidity
4OQURGP 9 2_TMH_K GTJ -KUXM . =GGMK
==PAGE:36==
).'6:+8  3+:.5*9

of external air.
The tool assumes the building as a single zone. According to ISO 13790, the building
can be treated as a single zone if:
• Temperature set-points differ by no more than 4 °C between spaces in the zone;
• Spaces are serviced by the same heating and cooling system if any;
• At least 80 % of the floor area of spaces are serviced by the same ventilation system,
if a system is present, and;
• The amount of ventilation in each space differs by no more than a factor of 4 within
80 % of the occupied space.
To perform the calculation, the tool requires information about the geometry of the
building, heat transfer properties of building components, heat capacity, thermal mass,
infiltration, ventilation, and outdoor conditions.
The calculation is performed for each hour by first simulating the conditions in the
building without heating or cooling. The simulation is then repeated with a fixed cooling
or heating effect of 10 W/m2. By linear inter- or extrapolation, the tool calculates the
required energy for heating or cooling to maintain a given set-point in the calculation
time step.
To simplify the required user input, the tool assumes that the total translucent surface
is distributed equally among all four building facades. Furthermore, it is assumed that
the building facades are oriented toward each cardinal direction. The solar heat gain
is distributed equally on all facades, making the solar load in a zone independent of
orientation. The solar load considers the transmittance of the glass and, if present, solar
shading.
If a ventilation system is defined the heating and cooling of supply air is included in the
simulated total energy need of the building.

When quantifying the productivity loss (either in work performance or absence) the
current situation is compared to the optimum case scenario, where no work hours are
lost due to the indoor environment. The annual productivity loss due to the indoor
environment is the decrease in effective work hours as a result of the effect of room
temperature on performance, and the effect of CO2 on performance and absence.
However, the total sum of all productivity losses cannot be summed without considering
variable dependencies. The following approach is suggested by Rehva Guidebook No. 6:
”The effect of ventilation on sick leave is independent of the effect on performance, the
effects being additive. [...] The effect of temperature is independent of ventilation but
often the control of temperature also affects ventilation rates. As the combined effect of
ventilation and temperature is not known the following is proposed: The magnitude of
the combined effect is at least the effect of the greater of the single parameters
and not more than the sum of the independent parameters.” [39]
Thus, when calculating the accumulated effects on performance an interval is gained,
where the minimum performance loss is the largest of either:
Pmin = Ploss,absence + Ploss,CO2
or
Pmin = Ploss,absence + Ploss,tOp
where
Ploss, absence is the performance loss due to increased absence [%];
Ploss, CO2 is the performance loss due to CO2-concentrations [%], and;
Ploss, tOp is the performance loss due to operative temperature [%].
And the maximum performance loss is:
Pmax = Ploss,absence + Ploss,CO2 + Ploss,tOp

In this study, the economic benefits of indoor climate renovations in oﬀices were ex-
amined. This was done through a literature study where the relationship between the
indoor environment and oﬀice productivity was investigated. These relationships were
then integrated into a tool that estimates the economic benefits of indoor climate and
energy renovations in oﬀices. In this chapter, the assumptions made when developing
the tool are discussed, as well as the results and overall capability of the developed tool.
In Section 5.3 the impact of the relationships between the indoor environment and
productivity was investigated. Quantifying the impact of the indoor environment on
oﬀice productivity can be a diﬀicult task. This relationship depends on many factors
such as social environment, personal characteristics, workspace layout, etc. The effect
of specific environmental factors such as temperature or ventilation rate can only be
studied, if the remaining factors stay constant.
Performance measurements are typically carried out as either laboratory studies or field
studies. Laboratory studies provide improved control over the indoor environment but
the change in scenery and workspace could affect the performance of an individual test
subject.
Field studies are performed in actual buildings with employees in their usual working
environment, which means that they perform their normal work during experiments.
This is an advantage over laboratory studies, especially as the results obtained can
be easily transferred into performance/productivity metrics that are relevant for oﬀice
work. This procedure is more diﬀicult in laboratory experiments, where subjects usually
perform a range of tests and tasks that approximate real oﬀice work but often does not
translate easily into actual oﬀice work. Common examples of this are proof-reading and
text typing. Consequently, the essential step would be to identify critical tasks for oﬀice
work, which may not be easy due to the variety of tasks performed, and then to make
an estimate of the proportion of total work time, for which they are significant. If, for
example, text typing only is performed for 10% of a typical oﬀice day, a 20% decrease
of typing performance would only affect the total work productivity by 2%.
Field studies have the challenge that it is more diﬀicult to control exposures in com-
parison to laboratory experiments. Many variables can change and eventually affect the
performance of oﬀice work. The resulting effect on the performance can therefore be a
combined effect of changes in many individual factors.
It is suggested that the results obtained in laboratory experiments using paid subjects,
simulated work, and limited exposure times should be validated in field intervention
experiments, in which the performance of real work is monitored over time in actual
oﬀices. Thus, obtaining the benefits of the two different test environments. However,
4OQURGP 9 2_TMH_K GTJ -KUXM . =GGMK
==PAGE:64==
).'6:+8  */9);99/54

due to the complex nature of performance, it will perhaps never be possible to gain
an accurate estimation of oﬀice performance. Therefore, all studies relating the indoor
environment with productivity have a degree of uncertainty.
Consequently, the impact of the relationships between indoor environment and produc-
tivity on the estimated NPV was investigated in Section 5.3. The results of this study
concluded that increasing/decreasing these relationships by 10% could affect the esti-
mated NPV by up to 28%. To increase accuracy when estimating the total value of
indoor climate renovations, it is important to study the relationship between productiv-
ity and the indoor environment further.
The case study validation of the tool’s ability to perform indoor climate simulations for
different ventilation principles, described in Section 5.1, suggested that the tool simu-
lated indoor climate was comparable to IDA ICE. Although, there were variations be-
tween the simulations performed by the two tools (the tool appeared to simulate warmer
indoor temperatures and lower CO2 concentrations), the magnitude of these variations
were deemed negligible and similar to variations observed between other established
simulation tools [82].
The tool’s ability to perform simulations with more complex geometry was investigated
by simulating an L-shaped building. The results of this validation suggested that the
geometry of the building does not affect the simulation significantly. The same tendencies
were observed as in the validation of the simple geometry, where the tool simulated
warmer conditions and lower CO2 concentrations than IDA ICE. However, a validation
of the tool’s ability to handle even more complex geometry and window placement should
be carried out until it can be concluded if the precision of the simulation is critically
affected by building geometry.
The tool uses the simplified hourly method presented in ISO 13790 [67] to calculate the
energy use and temperatures in the building. IDA ICE provides more detailed options for
input parameters than the simple hourly method does. This can lead to inconsistencies
between simulations performed by the two tools if one does not consider the additional
input options in IDA ICE. However, if basic geometry, physical parameters, and input
options on user behavior, temperature control, and environmental conditions are similar,
the results will be in agreement [67]. This is of course only correct, provided that the
algorithms of each tool are based on similar assumptions. Uncertainty between the two
tools increase as more parameters are introduced, such as interactions between multiple

solar shading by external obstacles [67]. This is not only true for the specific issue of
validations between the simple hourly method and IDA ICE but valid for all comparisons
between calculation methods.

The window opening schedule presented in Section 4.10 is used to simulate natural

assumption is to be justified, there would need to be someone responsible for coordinating
the opening of windows during each hour. Similar to how there is a teacher present in

be justified by a deliberate focus on window opening.
An alternative to this would be to model the window opening behavior stochastically,
where the starting time and window opening duration is modeled as probabilistic distri-
butions. Discrepancies between simulated and actual behavior can lead to a large offset
between simulation results and actual energy use [73].
Implementation of a stochastically modeled window opening behavior may provide more
accurate simulations that better capture the randomness and unpredictability of occu-
pant behavior but it would also increase the complexity of the model significantly, which
contradicts one of the main objectives of the developed tool which is to have simplified
inputs.
The occupancy schedule described in Section 4.7 includes five weeks of holiday in addi-
tion to the national Danish holidays. In the simulations, these five weeks are distributed
into three holidays and are taken in unison by the entire oﬀice staff. In reality, the
way these five weeks are planned varies between companies and industries. For instance,
Danish production plants have a coordinated summer holiday where all manufacturing is
stopped, whereas other lines of work might prioritize staying open for business through-
out the entire year. Most commonly, the holidays are scheduled individually between
each employee and their employer. This could have been simulated by assuming a lower
concurrent occupancy during the popular holiday seasons.
The benefits found in the cost/benefit case study outweighed the costs by a factor of 21
to 30, which resulted in a return on investment in less than a year. Economic benefits
from improving indoor climate may not always be of this magnitude as the indoor climate
conditions in the example building were very poor before the renovation.
The economic calculations currently only use the salary of the employees. Thus, it is
assumed that increased performance always leads to an increase in productivity by either
increasing the billable services or by decreasing the number of employees. However, this
does not take into account the contribution margin and net profit that the building
owner has to uphold.

In Section 3.1.4 the challenges of incorporating total value in building projects were
presented. One of the main challenges was that the wider benefits often can be diﬀicult
to assign a monetary value. Especially because they are often based on subjective judg-
ment. The lack of actual calculation tools and methods that quantifies the economic
consequences of total value makes the topic overlooked in many building projects. The
tool developed in this thesis aims to help incorporate the total value of oﬀice buildings
into the financial estimation of building projects.
In this chapter, many of the tool’s assumptions, uncertainties, and inaccuracies have
been discussed. The overall ability of the tool to perform indoor climate simulations
and estimate the NPV has some variations. However, these variations should be offset
by the significant difference between energy and staff expenditures in oﬀices.

the inaccuracy in estimated NPV from simulation imprecision should not affect the

In this thesis, a tool that quantifies the economic consequences of indoor climate and en-
ergy renovations in oﬀices was developed. The wider benefits of improved indoor climate
are generally not considered in conventional economic calculations on building design
and operation. The economic incentives for investments in buildings and in building op-
erations that improve worker productivity are unquestionable. Worker salaries exceeded
building energy and maintenance costs by a factor of approximately 100. Thus, there
are many benefits to gain by considering total value.
The main objective of the tool was to incorporate a holistic approach to the economic
equation when doing indoor climate renovations in oﬀice environments by including total
value into the financial decision-making. This was achieved by implementing associations
between indoor environment and performance adopted from existing literature.
The tool is based on internationally recognized methods for simulating the indoor envi-
ronment and building ventilation. The tool estimates the annual temperature and CO2
distributions of the simulated oﬀice environment. The associations established between
the indoor environment and productivity were then used to predict the effect of the sim-
ulated indoor environment on performance and short term sick-leave before and after a
building renovation. The wider benefits of the investment are estimated by calculating
the NPV of the investment based on the change in productivity. The tool enables the
evaluation of a range of different renovation scenarios and supports decisions on building
upgrades.
The precision of the tool’s indoor environment simulations was compared to IDA ICE
through a case study validation. The results of the validation showed that the indoor
environment simulations performed by the tool matched the simulations performed by
IDA ICE regardless of the applied ventilation principle or the complexity of the geometry.
The robustness of the tool’s economic output was estimated by increasing and decreasing
the relationships between the indoor environment and productivity by 10%. The average
difference between the estimated NPV for the three scenarios was 7% and the maximum
difference in NPV was 28%.
The profitability of an indoor climate renovation in an example oﬀice building was
assessed using the tool. A simulation of the indoor environment before and after the
installation of mechanical ventilation was performed. The economic benefits of installing
mechanical ventilation were found to outweigh the costs of installing and running the
ventilation system by a factor of 21 to 30 for the reference case scenario.

Personal development and optimization of the human body have been explored by soci-
ety for a long time. Currently, there seems to be an increased focus on the optimization
of human performance. At the 2021 Venice architecture biennial titled ”How will we live
together”, the future of the human body was explored. This unfolded in a subsection
called ”Designing for New Bodies”, where new perceptions of the human body that os-
cillated between cyberpunk and cyborg were experimented with, re-codifying the human
body as a mutant micro-cell ecosystem. The exhibition presented tools and gadgets that
merged with the human body to optimize performance, such as gadgets waking you up at
the perfect time of your sleep cycle, or others measuring blood sugar to tell you when and
how much to eat. In 2019 the world’s first sub-2-hour marathon was run by the Kenyan
athlete Eliud Kipchoge. The sub-2-hour marathon had long been deemed an impossible
feat. However, through careful planning and optimization of performance by the INEOS
team, Kipchoge managed to run a marathon at the record time of 1:59:40. Imagine if
even a fraction of the attentiveness given to breaking this record would be applied to
the conditions of oﬀice environments. Fisk (2000a) studied the effects of indoor envi-
ronmental quality on health and productivity in American oﬀices. The study estimated
that annually $60 billion were lost due to decreased performance from building-related
symptoms, $70 billion were lost due to short term sick-leave, and $20 - $160 billion were
lost due to decreased performance from temperature and lighting [84]. The potential for
economic benefits on a global scale is assumed to be much higher, and increased focus on
the indoor environment would improve the health and well-being of millions of people
worldwide.
The authors of this thesis hope that the future will bring increased attention to the indoor
environment and that the developed tool will support the inclusion of the wider benefits
of improved indoor climate when evaluating building projects to help build better in the
future.
