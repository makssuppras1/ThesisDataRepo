Abstract
The use of chemical inhibitors is a common and eﬃcient way of mitigating sweet corrosion
of production tubing in the oil & gas industry. This thesis has investigated a commercially
available corrosion inhibitor used in the North Sea.
The inhibitor’s working mechanism, stability and inﬂuence of concentration was studied
using electrochemical techniques such as linear polarisation resistance (LPR), electrochem-
ical impedance spectroscopy (EIS) along with scanning electron microscopy (SEM). The
selected inhibitor was found to be functioning by a ﬁlm forming process with precipitation
of corrosion product in ﬁlm imperfections. At low temperatures, ambient pressure, and
when using concentrations between 50 ppm to 300 ppm inhibitor, the eﬀective corrosion
rate was always below 0.06 mm y−1. The study revealed that the surface condition of the
metal has a considerable inﬂuence on the eﬃciency of the inhibitor and that the inhibitor
ﬁlm deteriorates over time
Water injection used for secondary production of hydrocarbons introduces a large amount
of Mg2+ and Ca2+ ion in the production well. The inﬂuence of these ions on the working
mechanism and performance of the same commercial corrosion inhibitor was investigated.
It was found that the introduction of cations did not aﬀect the eﬀective corrosion rate,
but that they approximately halved the incubation time of the inhibitor’s ﬁlm formation
due to suspected complexing between cations and inhibitor molecules.

Introduction
1.1
Corrosion and its mitigation in the oil and gas
industry
Production of hydrocarbons in the North Sea has been going on since the early ’70s. As in
many other oﬀshore structures, the task of mitigating corrosion in the production piping
is one of the many challenges related to production of oil and gas. Every year the industry
pays hundreds of millions of dollars in repairs and maintenance of production equipment [2].
The oﬀshore environment related to oil and gas production is very harsh and several factors
such as temperature, ﬂow rate, pH, pressure, salinity, oxygen concentration, sulphide
producing bacteria, structural issues related to fatigue, brine chemistry ect. inﬂuences the
corrosion. These factors can cause corrosion types such as sweet corrosion (CO2 corro-
sion), sour corrosion (H2S corrosion), galvanic corrosion, crevice corrosion, microbiological
induced corrosion and stress corrosion cracking [3]. Among these CO2 is considered a
signiﬁcant corrosion agents in hydrocarbon production systems [4] [5].
When producing oil and gas much water is inevitably also produced. The water will either
come from the formation or injection of seawater used for secondary production to uphold
the pressure in the reservoir, as shown in Fig. 1.1.1. Seawater production increases in
cases were there is a direct connection between injectors and the producers, as seawater
then easier can reach the production well [6]. CO2 from the reservoir, which is a natural
constituent from the decomposition of organic material, will mix in the saline seawater,
and all together increase the corrosion activity in the well. Classically the industry has
diﬀerent ways of protecting ferrous production equipment from sweet corrosion - one of
these being corrosion inhibitors [3] [7]. ”A corrosion inhibitor is a substance which, when
added to an environment in a small concentration, eﬀectively reduces the corrosion rate of
a metal exposed to that environment” [8].

secondary production.
When producing oil, there are typically several diﬀerent ways of adding the inhibitor to
the inside of the pipes. A prevalent method is by a capillary tube running on the outside
of the production pipe and adding the inhibitor into the production pipe as illustrated
in Fig. 1.1.2 [2]. This method is considered to be one of the most practical and eﬃcient,
as the inhibitor can be introduced in the system continuously [9]. Alternatively, batch
treatments, where the inhibitor is pumped down the production pipe with regular intervals,
can be used. A third option is squeeze treatments, where the inhibitor is pumped into the
formation and introduced with the production water [2]. The use of corrosion inhibitors is
generally only recommended in re-circulation systems as the consumption expenses would
be rather high in a once-through ﬂow system [10].
Another constituent in the injected water is magnesium and calcium ions [11]. Scaling
products (e.g. CaCO3 or MgCO3) formed from these cation in the CO2 rich environment
will narrow and decrease the performance of the pipes [12]. While corrosion and scale
products can be protective under certain conditions [13], they also reduce the ﬂow in the
pipe over time. The scales are removed either mechanically or chemically during shutdowns
in order to cope with narrowing of the pipes [14]. While this will clear out the pipe, it
also exposes a clean metal surface to a very corrosive environment and over extended
periods, this procedure can be harmful to the pipe and increase maintenance cost and
environmental risks in case of leakage. Both corrosion and scale inhibitor can be added
in the well to mitigate degradation of the pipe. The quality of corrosion protection has
though been reported by the industry in the North Sea to be varying with the currently
used inhibitors. At the moment, there exist limited studies on corrosion inhibitors under
the North Sea conditions and whether foreign ions will degraded their performance. In
continuation hereof, this thesis will try to clarify some of these matters of dispute regarding
the use of corrosion inhibitors.

There are many terms and aspects related to oﬀshore corrosion in the oil and gas industry.

2.1.1
CO2 corrosion
CO2 corrosion, which is also more commonly known as sweet corrosion, is one of the
many challenges related to the production of hydrocarbons. CO2 is a natural presence
in the formation of the reservoir, and to some extend the water used for production of
hydrocarbons. Dry CO2 gas is in itself not corrosive, but when it dissolves in water, it
hydrates and forms carbonic acid (H2CO3) [15]. Carbonic acid lowers the pH value of
the brine and supplies cathodic species in the form of H+ ions which supports the anodic
dissolution of iron from the pipe surface. CO2 corrosion is not a new phenomenon, but
a continuous challenge in production facilities. There exist several ways of coping with
sweet corrosion, but due to changing chemistry and integrity in the well it can be a very
challenging task to stabilise the water chemistry [16] [17].

2.1.2
Corrosion inhibitors
Due to its cost-eﬀectiveness, carbon steel is the most used material for hydrocarbon
production pipes [29]. Carbon steel does not have high corrosion resistance, and many
diﬀerent measures can, therefore, be implemented industrially in order to mitigate corrosion
Page 5 of 75
==PAGE:19==
Corrosion in oﬀshore oil and gas production
in piping material - one of these being corrosion inhibitors [30]. The use of inhibitors is a
common and cost-eﬀective way of mitigating corrosion of production piping material in
the oil and gas industry. There are several ways of classifying inhibitors; for this thesis,
they will be grouped according to their functionality [31]:
Anodic inhibitors shift the anodic potential and forces the surface towards the passive
region. The inhibitor promotes the formation of an oxide layer on the metal surface
which passivates the metal [32]. These inhibitors are sometimes called passivating
because of their mechanism. They can be fragile in environments containing high
amounts of chloride as the passivation process under these conditions can promote
pitting an accelerate corrosion [28].
Cathodic inhibitors can be a substance that precipitates on cathodic sites and thereby
increases the surface’s impedance [28]. Alternatively, they can act as environmental
scavengers that slow down the cathodic reaction in the electrolyte. The inhibitor
will then scout the aqueous solution for corrosive elements (e.g. oxygen) and react
with these to immunise them by complexing [32].
Mixed inhibitors or organic inhibitors aﬀect the entire surface of the sample usually by
forming a hydrophobic ﬁlm. This ﬁlm can have both anodic and cathodic properties
- hence the name mixed [28]. This ﬁlm can vary in eﬃciency depending on the
inhibitor’s molecular structure, aﬃnity to the metal surface and rate of the surface
kinetics. The diﬀusion of reactant to the surface will follow the laws of adsorption
why temperature and pressure will be signiﬁcant for the quality of the adsorption
[33] [28]. How the inhibitor will adsorb to the surface is based on both the ionic
charge of the inhibitor and the charge at the surface. If the surface has a positive
charge, it will be more prone to react with anions and vice versa. If the organic
inhibitor is based on either amines or sulfonates (anions), the inhibitor molecules
can bond chemically with the surface and make up a ﬁlm providing a barrier towards
the dissolution of metal. This process is also referred to as chemisorption and can
also be temperature activated to some extend [34]. The protectiveness of these ﬁlms
can be very dependent both on the concentration of inhibitor, surface coverage and
on pH value of the electrolyte [28].
Volatile inhibitors are often also called vapour phase inhibitors as they are often used
on enclosed environments. They get transported with a stream, and preferentially
neutralises corrosive acidic substances by shifting the pH value away from the acidic
regime [28]. These types of inhibitor are mostly used in gaseous systems.
In general, the eﬀectiveness of corrosion inhibitors can be aﬀected by many diﬀerent sources
such as pH, concentration, the presence of other species in the electrolyte, formation of
secondary inhibitors, surface coverage, the condition of the metal surface, functional
groups of the inhibitor and the type of metal applied to ect. [28]. The interaction can
be very complex, and it is not always possible to assign a single signiﬁcant factor to the
working mechanism of the inhibitor and how well it inhibits corrosion. The electrochemical
behaviour of the metal will often reveal the working mechanism and characteristics of an
inhibitor. Positive displacement of the corrosion potential will indicate that the inhibitor
mainly retards the anodic process. Contrary, a decrease in the corrosion potential indicates
cathodic control [28]. If the surface coverage of the inhibitor is low, then the retardation
will occur preferentially on anodic sites, e.g. by precipitates. At higher surface coverage

lower the cathodic reaction [28].

2.1.3
Water chemistry related to ions
As mentioned there are many diﬀerent contributors to increased corrosion in the hydrocar-
bon industry. Among these, ions from injected seawater are suspected to inﬂuence sweet
corrosion. There are currently limited studies on the inﬂuence of calcium and magnesium
ions on the performance of corrosion inhibitors used for corrosion protection of oil and
gas production piping. Production of oil and gas involves the production of a signiﬁcant
amount of water. The most signiﬁcant constituents of ions in this water, besides Na+
and Cl–, is Ca2+ and Mg2+ ions [11]. Under the right conditions, these ions can form
precipitates such as magnesite (MgCO3) and calcite (CaCO3) on the inside of production
piping [11]. As mentioned above can be many other attending ions contributing to the
scaling, and the brine chemistry can often be very complex.
As previously mentioned the precipitation of FeCO3 on mild steel during CO2 corrosion
can be protective of the steel. Both Mg2+ and Ca2+ are capable of replacing iron in FeCO3
[38] [39] [40], but the crystal structure CaCO3 and MgCO3 is isomorphous where as FeCO3
have a hexagonal crystal structure, and their concurrent precipitation can change the
mechanism of the formed corrosion product layer [41] [42]. Despite this potential inﬂuence
of Mg2+ and Ca2+ ions on the corrosion resistance of mild steel there are some ambiguity
in the literature towards the actual protectiveness of these ions and their inﬂuence on
corrosion inhibitors:
Early studies by Uhlig et al. [43] have shown that the addition of Ca2+ ions in combination
with the corrosion inhibitor polyphosphate did form calcium polyphosphates, which created
a diﬀusion barrier that increased the cathodic polarisation and improved the inhibition
eﬃciency of the phosphates. It was also observed that addition of calcium ions lowered the
critical amount of oxygen in the solution from 1 mL L−1 to 0.15 mL L−1 with the addition
of 60 ppm Ca2+ [43]. However, the oxygen level was determined as the predominant
corrosion parameter. The study further showed a signiﬁcant correlation between the
corrosion mechanism and the content of chloride in the solution.
Iovchev [44] found that the presence of Ca2+ ions reduced the inhibiting eﬀect of an
aluminium-thiourea (Al-thi) inhibitor on mild steel, while Mg2+ ions did not have any
apparent eﬀect on the inhibitor’s eﬃciency. These ﬁndings were made based on a test
period of 196 hours in a brine. Addition of divalent cations happened along with addition
of SO 2–
4
ions (sulfate), which also could also contribute to the corrosion rate. However,
the authors did not correlate these parameters.
Despite similarities of the divalent cations, Hau et al. [45] reported that increasing the
concentrations of Mg2+ ions from 1000 ppm to 10000 ppm resulted in higher general
corrosion. The same increase in Ca2+ ions decreased the general corrosion rate. The
experiments were based on 6-hour experimental monitoring before protective corrosion
products had precipitated. Subsequently, the formation of corrosion product reduced
the corrosion rate approximately 80% independently of the ion type and concentration
[45]. The experiments were carried out up to 96 hours duration and were all performed
without the use of corrosion inhibitors. Therefore corrosion was free to occur along with
precipitating corrosion products. The same study also observed that the presence of di-
valent cations seemed to promote the formation of pits in the early hours of the experiment.

Recent studies by Rublova et al. [46] have shown that the presence of Zn2+ and Ca2+ ions
increased the inhibitory properties of polyhexamethyleneguanidine (PCG) by increasing
the surface adsorption. The presence of divalent cations showed to change the structure in
the macromolecules of the inhibitor due to complexing with the ions. The eﬀect of the
inhibitor was increased by 1.5 times by blocking of the electrode surface [46]. According
to the author, the complexing with Zn2+ and Ca2+ ions changed the hydrophilic-lipophilic
balance of the PCG inhibitor, which led to a shift in free energy of the molecules and
increasing their ability to react with the steel surfaces. The ﬁndings by the various authors
mentioned above indicate that even with the presence of a corrosion inhibitor, Ca2+ or
Mg2+ ions does not have a conclusive impact on corrosion of mild steel and that there
might also be other inﬂuencing factors.

3.1.6.3
Evolution of methodology
Based on experience, 1 hour was chosen for the system to stabilise before EIS a potentio-
dynamic polarisation was carried out. After running several iterations, with the procedure
described for the 2-hour concentration study above, the majority of the measurements
indicated large variation. The scatter was believed to be caused by the presence of the
inhibitor, as this was the only foreign substance in the brine. Reviewing the laboratory
protocol, experimental data and literature concerning the formation of corrosion inhibitor
ﬁlms lead to the modiﬁcations of the sample preparations described in Sec. 3.1.2. Further-
more, it was suggested that this sequencing time was not suﬃcient to reﬂect the changes
happening at the working electrode. It was suspected that the inhibitor did not have
suﬃcient time to stabilise over the 2-hour period of the experiment. Long stabilisation
time would especially be the case for a chemisorption based inhibitor, where the incubation
can be long depending on the chemical reaction rate. Based on this a 24-hour loop was
introduced where OCP, LPR and EIS were measured every hour. It was subsequently
observed that especially the sequencing time had a positive inﬂuence on the obtained
results.

3.2.3.1
Interpretation of EIS measurements
Due to the ambiguity of EIS measurements, this section contains a brief review of literature
on how to correlate experimental observation in EIS with physical attributes. The examples
above showed a Nyquist diagram containing only a single semicircle. In the case of more
complex systems with a paint or a coating, more semicircles may appear, since this will
lead to a second time constant [58] [59] [60] .
Feliu et al. [58] investigated the inﬂuence on the Nyquist diagram in the case of a paint
ﬁlm on steel. The study showed that when two semicircles appeared the high-frequency
response is usually attributed to the paint ﬁlm. The length of the high-frequency arc is the
sum of the ionic resistance of the paint ﬁlm and the polarisation resistance attributed to
the corrosion reaction happening under the ﬁlm. Therefore, the paint resistance towards
corrosion cannot be solely be determined from the radius of the high-frequency arc. The
second semicircle was related to pores in the coating exceeding a certain critical size that
resulted distinct second time constant [58]. These defects form local corrosion cells which
can be observed in the Nyquist diagram. Results obtained from coated metals can also
contain large amounts of scattering due to the random nature of ﬁlm imperfections and
breakdowns. Surface condition and local eﬀects can aﬀect the result a great deal [58].
Page 22 of 75
==PAGE:36==
Methods of analysis - theory and application
When interpreting the shape of a Nyquist diagram, Thompson and Campbell [59], showed
that two semicircles correlate to defects in the coating and that a partially resolve high-
frequency response was related to coating defects. The characteristics of the imperfections
could then be related to the low-frequency response. Furthermore, they showed that
increasing the coating thickness resulted an increased pore resistance and a larger diameter
of the high-frequency response. They suggested that pores in the ﬁlm ﬁlling with corrosion
product, also referred to as pore plugging, induced an increase of the pore resistance.
Thompson and Campbell [59] also stated that the natural randomness of these defects and
their appearance could lead to uncertainties for exact interpretation if impedance results.
Similar observation using EIS was made by Tan et al. [60] who studied imidazoline
as an inhibitor in CO2 corrosion. The authors reported that there was an increase in
the diameter of semicircles plotted in a Nyquist diagram over 42 hours, suggesting that
the inhibitor gradually changes the protection and kinetics at the surface of the sample.
Furthermore, they observed large phase shifts at high frequencies which suggests that the
interface structure of the inhibitor is complicated. The authors reported that a continuous
evolution in the charge transfer resistance might be caused by the formation of a thin ﬁlm
created by a chemisorption reaction happening at the surface [60]. The chemisorption
is diﬀerent from adsorption by having a layer build up over time via chemical reaction
products at the metal surface. Breakdowns or imperfections in the thin ﬁlm layer may be
ﬁlled with corrosion product, as described by other authors [59], by pore plugging, and
will act protectively. A characteristic feature, found by the authors, about chemisorption
was that the surface coverage and electrochemical kinetics were independent of inhibitor
concentration, as the protection happened via a chemical reaction and less via the laws of
adsorption [60].
3.2.4
Light optical microscopy (LOM)
In classical Light Optical Microscopy (LOM) a beam of white light is shined on to and
reﬂected oﬀa specimen surface. Through a series of optical and focal lenses, the magniﬁ-
cation and clarity can be adjusted to inspect the surface properties, e.g. microstructure of
steels.
3.2.5
Scanning electron microscopy (SEM) and Energy disper-
sive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS)
Studying corrosion mechanisms and surface chemistry desires a larger magniﬁcation
and depth of ﬁeld. Scanning Electron Microscopy (SEM) and Energy Dispersive x-ray
Spectroscopy (EDS) allows obtaining this. SEM works by emitting a focused electron beam
on to the specimen surface. The beam will normally have an energy between 5 ke V to
30 ke V and a probe diameter around 10 nm [61]. When the beam reaches the surface of the
specimen, electrons will penetrate the material and create elastic and inelastic interactions
with the surface and bulk material. This region is called the interaction volume, which is
a pear-shaped volume with diﬀerent penetrations depths as shown in Fig. 3.2.5. Moving
the beam in a raster-like fashion over the surface will result in energetic information
from the individual interaction volume. Low energy electrons, which interact with the
surface in-elastically, will generate secondary electrons (SE). These have a very little escape
depth and will be suitable for topography studies. Higher energy electrons, which interact
Page 23 of 75
==PAGE:37==
Methods of analysis - theory and application
elastically at a larger escape depth, will generate backscattered electrons (BSE), which
will be more sensitive to atomic number and diﬀerent phases [61]. In order to conduct
elemental analysis, many SEM machine has an energy dispersive x-ray spectroscopy (EDS)
detector. This method will emit a high energy electron beam on to the surface of the
specimen, exiting surface atoms and promote the release of characteristic x-rays [61]. These
characteristic x-rays will have speciﬁc energy levels for every element, which will allow for
either a mapping over an area or a point analysis. The method will not reveal the phase
or compound composition, but will only show which elements are present and at what
weight percentage.

3.2.5.1
Use of SEM and EDS for investigating corrosion inhibitors
Nam et al. [49] have shown that the combination of electrochemical monitoring such
as potentiodynamic polarisation and EIS in combination with SEM imaging can be a
powerful tool to study the mechanism of corrosion inhibitors. The study used SEM
images to compare the physical inﬂuence of corrosion inhibitor to investigate the how
corrosion product formed on the surface of the sample. At an inhibitor concentration of
approximately 300 ppm, the presence of inhibitor resulted in a signiﬁcant reduction of

==PAGE:38==

precipitating corrosion product [49]. The authors described a light etching of the surface
along with a thin chemical coating/layer, caused by the inhibitor. It was determined,
using x-ray photoelectron spectroscopy (XPS), that the ﬁlm contained Pr(4 OHCin)3
which originated from the inhibitor used in the study. Comparing EDS images with and
without inhibitor it was observed that corrosion product, was enriched with Pr and C,
only precipitated locally on active reaction sites. The active sites could, for instance, be
pores in the ﬁlm, defects or other imperfections on the steel surface. These precipitates
were likely to be induced by the inhibitor and could be a part of the protective mechanism
along with the thin chemical ﬁlm [49]. The same study examined the ﬁlm formation
of the inhibitor with EIS and observed inductive loops at low frequencies indicating an
intermediate layer on the steel surfaces.

5.1
Analysis of inhibitor mechanism
The ﬁrst aim of the project was to get an understanding of the inhibitor’s working mech-
anism. Use of PDP in Fig. 4.1.2 showed that the inhibitor is organic (mixed) with an
anodic mechanism. The presence of corrosion inhibitor increased the rest potential, and
decreased the anodic corrosion current with increasing concentration of inhibitor. Contrary,
the cathodic part of the curve was constant, though diminished compared to no inhibitor,
for all concentrations of inhibitor. It is likely that the inhibitor forms a thin protective
ﬁlm on the surface of the steel, that prevents the anodic dissolution of iron, hence the
diminished anodic current density. Since the inhibitor has an amine base, it is likely
that the ﬁlm has a mechanism as displayed in Fig. 2.1.1 with an amine head that reacts
with the steel surface, and a hydrophobic tail that makes up the ﬁlming layer. Even
though the exact composition of the inhibitor is unknown, this working mechanism is plau-
sible and commonly found in corrosion inhibitors as described by other authors [35] [36] [37].
Investigations with EIS supported the hypothesis of a ﬁlm forming inhibitor. By looking
at the Nyquist diagrams measured over time in Fig. 4.1.4a and 4.1.7a, it was found
that a mid-range frequency response occurred in the form of a semicircle that increased
in radius over time. An increasing mid-range frequency response correlates to a layer
development, as described in Sec. 3.2.3.1. That the radius of the semicircle is changing
over the 24 hours indicates that a chemisorption process is happening at the surface.
Dependant on the speciﬁc reaction speed, chemisorption can be a slowly evolving process
and is not necessarily detectable right away. Diﬀusion of reactants to the surface is
dependant on temperature, agitation and concentration and will hence be decisive for
how fast the process can occur. With the given temperature of 40 ◦C, is expected that
the process will evolve over several hours. Furthermore, a low-frequency response was
observed after several hours, as seen in Fig. 4.1.4a and 4.1.7a, which could be related to
imperfections in the ﬁlm, as described in Sec. 3.2.3.1, forming local corrosion cells. The
presence of imperfections was supported by the partially resolved mid-range frequency
response that indicates imperfection in the barrier as stated by Thompson and Campbell
[59]. Finally, a presumption of pore plugging was supported by SEM images that showed

==PAGE:68==
Analysis of inhibitor mechanism
local precipitations of corrosion product. Corrosion product will under normal conditions
not precipitate instantly, and can be related to the low-frequency response that did not
appear until several hours into the experiments. With a higher concentration of inhibitor,
the chemisorbed ﬁlm on the surface has a larger density, thickness or better coverage.
This is evident when comparing the low-frequency response in Fig. 4.1.4a and 4.1.7a, re-
lated to ﬁlm imperfections, which appears much later for the high concentration of inhibitor.
The frequency responses were modelled by ﬁtting an equivalent electric system to the
experimental results as shown in Fig. 4.1.10 and 4.1.11. The pore resistance Rpr in
Fig. 4.1.10a and 4.1.10b, which increased with time, can be correlated to decreasing ﬁlm
imperfections. The standard deviations from the modelling of the 24-hour concentration
study also indicate surface imperfections and incomplete ﬁlm formation as it decreased
with the ﬁlm stabilisation. Especially the pore resistance was observed to have a stan-
dard deviation that decreased as the ﬁlm stabilised. Higher pore resistance for higher
concentrations of inhibitor correlates with the theory of higher concentrations creating a
more dense ﬁlm with fewer imperfections. Furthermore, the pore resistance will increase
as pore plugging occurs, and the surface becomes better sealed. The increase in pore
resistance also correlates with the mid-range frequency response increasing with time
as observed in Fig. 4.1.4a and 4.1.7a. Fig. 4.1.10c and 4.1.10d shows that the ﬁlm
capacitance Cfc was around 0.05 µF for all concentrations and remained almost constant
through all experiments. Such a low capacitance suggests a very thin layer - possibly only
a few molecule layers thick. It was observed that the ﬁlm capacitance was independent of
inhibitor concentration. A higher concentration of inhibitor does not create a thicker but
only a more dense ﬁlm. The double layer capacitance Cdl in Fig. 4.1.11a and 4.1.11b was
observed to increase faster for lower concentrations of inhibitor. This increase might be
caused by the lower concentration of inhibitor not blocking the diﬀusion of ions to the
surface as well as the higher concentration of inhibitor. This points towards a charge being
build up in front of the inhibitor ﬁlm. The charge transfer resistance Rct in Fig. 4.1.11c
and 4.1.11d increased with time, as would be expected when the protective ﬁlm stabilises
and pores become ﬁlled by corrosion product. The charge transfer resistance of the 300
ppm inhibitor samples was on average 34% larger than the 100 ppm inhibitor samples.
This diﬀerence could correspond to fewer imperfections, and larger precipitates of corrosion
product observed when comparing Fig. 4.1.5 and 4.1.8, as a consequence of better surface
coverage when using higher concentrations of inhibitor. These mechanisms are consistent
with observations made by other authors [49] [58] [59] [60]. Here a similar mechanism of
a ﬁlm formation and pore plugging was observed for a variety of diﬀerent organic inhibitors.
When the inhibitor’s performance was monitored with LPR, up to 10 hours of incubation
time, before the inhibitor had reached a steady corrosion rate, was observed. This incuba-
tion time was evident for both low and high concentrations of inhibitor as seen from Fig.
4.1.3. The incubation time supports the theory of the inhibitor bonding with the metal
by chemisorption, as this process is reaction rate dependent. As described in Sec. 2.1.2
chemisorption follows the laws of adsorption which are dependent on the Gibbs free energy
and thus, increases with the temperature. As stated in Sec. 2.1.2 the chemisorption itself
can also be temperature activated and at 40 ◦C it can be suspected that the chemisorption
is relatively low, resulting in a longer reaction and incubation time. It is possible that
incubation time could decrease by increasing the temperature due to higher kinetics of the
reaction.

Inhibitor eﬃciency and experimental conditions
The early hours of the process showed much scattering in the results. Furthermore, there
were observations of inductive loops, which especially manifested itself in the 2-hour
concentration study shown in Fig. 4.1.1, where there was no tendency or signiﬁcant
diﬀerence of the inhibitor’s presence. There is no clear physical behaviour associated with
the inductive loop as it is a mathematical artefact, but can be associated with insuﬃcient
or uneven surface adhesion or formation of an intermediate layer as stated by other authors
[49]. Thus, the early hours of the experiments did not reveal a distinct mechanism of the
inhibitor.
5.1.1
Use of equivalent circuit models
An equivalent electrical system was ﬁtted to the experimental data, in order to assess
the system’s mechanism. The model with two time constants gave a good indication of
which parameters were characteristic for the inhibitor. When using equivalent electrical
systems, several systems might be able to ﬁt the same experimental data. One example
could be the use of constant phase elements such as Warburg elements that can have
properties of a capacitor and a resistor at the same time. If included in the Randle’s
system shown in Fig. 3.2.2a, it could improve the ﬁt of the simpler system, but the
mixed behaviour of the Warburg element complicates the physical interpretation. It
is also possible that a more complex system has a better ﬁt. In order to know if the
chosen model is the optimal ﬁt of the inhibitor’s mechanism, experiments with diﬀerent
exposure conditions should be carried out. It is possible that the behaviour of the inhibitor
could change with temperature or interaction with other production chemicals. From the
preliminary studies made in this thesis, the two time constant system was deemed to be
a plausible build up, based on physical interpretation and work done by other authors
[58] [59]. In some of the experiments, it was observed that a high-frequency response
occurred, which increased in phase shift along with the primary layer of the inhibitor.
The response was disregarded due to its inconsistency, but it is possible that there is an
intermediate layer that the given experimental conditions were not capable of fully promote.
5.2
Inhibitor eﬃciency and experimental conditions
The second aim of the project was to investigate the inﬂuence of inhibitor concentration
and long term performance. From the graphs in Fig. 4.1.3 and the numbers in Tab.
4.1.2 it is evident that the eﬀective corrosion rate is independent of the concentration of
inhibitor. In average the corrosion rates of the diﬀerent concentrations were all below
0.06 mm y−1 and had an average value of CR = 0.03 ± 0.02 mm y−1 after a sequencing
time of 24 hours. The diﬀerences between the individual concentrations of inhibitor are
deemed so small that they are insigniﬁcant. EIS showed distinct diﬀerences between the
concentrations, especially for the pore resistance, build up of double layer capacitance and
charge transfer resistance. These diﬀerences do not appear to have a critical inﬂuence on
the corrosion rate in the measured time frame, as all concentrations of inhibitor yielded
an inhibitor eﬃciency of η > 98%. Textbook values for good inhibitors are normally
between 90-95%, as stated in Sec. 2.1.2, so these levels are extraordinary high. There is an
important remark concerning the inhibitor eﬃciency: The experimental conditions were
Page 56 of 75
==PAGE:70==
Inhibitor eﬃciency and experimental conditions
very favourable for the inhibitor to function well, which might be a contributing factor to
the high eﬃciency. The samples were polished to expose the inhibitor to a clean metal
surface, and the inhibitor was added to the brine from the beginning of the experiment.
Furthermore, the experiments were executed at low temperature and ambient pressure in
order to have an isolated introductory study, where the working mechanism of the inhibitor
could be studied. These conditions would minimise aggressive corrosion and formations of
corrosion product. In oil wells using water injection, the temperatures can reach up to
80 ◦C and the pressure hundreds of bars depending on exploration depth. Both of which
would increase the corrosion rate and challenge the inhibitor. The experiments neither
included oxygen, H2S, high ﬂow rates or other production chemicals, which also would
increase corrosion. The brine chemistry was kept simple, and the high eﬃciency might
therefore not be indicative of a real-life scenario.
A good example of one of the many conditions that can diminish the eﬃciency of the
inhibitor was the 100-hour study for ongoing corrosion shown in Sec. 4.1.3.3. Looking at
Fig. 4.1.16b and applying Eq. (2.1.8), using the corrosion rate just before the inhibitor was
added to the brine and the corrosion rate after 52 hours of settling with the inhibitor; the
inhibitor eﬃciency was approximately η = 40%, compared to the η > 98% observed with
the polished samples. Despite the favourable conditions described above, the inhibitor’s
eﬃciency was diminished signiﬁcantly by the surface condition of the sample. Therefore,
when estimating a real-life production scenario, it is important to have this perspective
in mind. The production environment will be much more aggressive towards corrosion,
and the surface condition will most likely not be as good as the samples prepared in this
study. The SEM images in Fig. 4.1.18 and Appx. C.2.1 showed that addition of corrosion
inhibitor to ongoing corrosion also produced a diﬀerent morphology of the corrosion prod-
uct compared to corrosion product formed under purely free corrosion. Getting optimal
protection of the steel will be a very complex task as there will be many environmental
inﬂuences along with other production chemicals that might contradict the eﬃciency of the
inhibitor. When comparing the study on ongoing corrosion with the experiments, where
the inhibitor was in the brine from the beginning, it is evident that even small diﬀerences
in the procedure have a large impact on the inhibitor eﬃciency.
The 24-hour concentration study showed that after 10 hours the corrosion rate stabilised
for the remaining time of the experiment as shown in Fig. 4.1.3. When prolonging the
sequencing time in the 100-hour concentration study, using a batch treatment of 100 ppm
inhibitor, there was a deterioration of the main inhibitor ﬁlm over time as evident from Fig.
4.1.12 and 4.1.13. Even though the gradient was low, the corrosion rate increased with
time. As evident from the Nyquist diagrams in Fig. 4.1.13a, there were at all times a mid-
and low-range frequency response, which, as earlier stated, represents the chemical ﬁlm
and its imperfections respectively. The low-frequency response related to imperfections
did not seem to change signiﬁcantly in size during the measured period, but the mid-range
frequency response did decrease in radius indicating ﬁlm break down. Simulations of the
ﬁlm break down showed that the pore resistance in Fig. 4.1.15c decreased approximately
35% over the 100 hours. A decrease in the pore resistance could indicate a ﬁlm breakdown
or an increase in the number of pores in the ﬁlm. Simulations of the charge transfer
resistance in Fig. 4.1.15e, which can be related to imperfections and the low-frequency
response mentioned above, did almost not change during the experiment. This observation
could point towards that when the rate of the ﬁlm breakdown is low; the local corrosion

cells ﬁll with corrosion product in the same rate as new ones are created resulting in a
steady response. Furthermore, it indicates that once formed, the precipitations do not
break down. At some point there might be a turnover point where the ﬁlm breakdown
reaches a critical point and no longer can provide suﬃcient protection. Similar, the ﬁlm
capacitance in Fig. 4.1.15b did almost not change during 100 hours indicating that the ﬁlm
thickness does not change signiﬁcantly over 100 hours. Furthermore, the surface appeared
slightly more corroded after 100 hours in the brine, as seen in Fig. 4.1.14, compared to the
24-hour experiments with the same concentration of inhibitor shown in Fig. 4.1.5. This
behaviour could be related to the longer submersion time. The modelling of the double
layer capacitance in Fig. 4.1.15d showed a slight increase over 100 hours, which could be
explained by the layer breakdown. As the layer deteriorates more ions are allowed to be
transferred to the surface, and the capacitance of the double layer increases. Furthermore,
this experiment did not show the high-frequency response in the Bode diagram in Fig.
4.1.13b, but had the same corrosion rate and additional electrochemical features as ob-
served when it was present. Therefore it is unsure if and how the high-frequency response
is related to the corrosion mechanism of the system or working mechanism of the inhibitor.
The rate of the deterioration might change with how aggressive the environment is, but it
is important to note that the protectiveness of the inhibitor is not constant with time, as
the 24-hour concentration study indicated. Therefore, a continuous supply of inhibitor
is necessary in order to sustain the eﬀect of the inhibitor. How the deterioration rate of
the ﬁlm is dependant on inhibitor concentration and experimental conditions cannot be
determined from this study. It would require a separate investigation.
5.3
Inﬂuence of cations
The third aim of the project was to investigate how Mg2+ and Ca2+ ions inﬂuenced the
working mechanism of the inhibitor. When adding either Mg2+ or Ca2+ ions to the brine,
there were no observations of signiﬁcant changes in the working mechanism or protective-
ness of the inhibitor. The Nyquist and Bode diagrams in Fig. 4.2.2 and 4.2.5 show similar
circuit components as the brine without additional cations. Comparing to the simple brine
in Fig. 4.1.4 and 4.1.7 there was still a low- and mid-range frequency response where
300 ppm inhibitor resulted in the largest radius of the mid-range frequency response in
the Nyquist diagrams. The main ﬁnding from the ion contamination experiments was a
decrease in the incubation time of the inhibitor. The incubation time was roughly halved
for the investigated concentrations of cations and inhibitor as shown in Fig. 4.2.1 and 4.2.4.
As described in Sec. 2.1.3 it is possible that the presence of foreign cations can improve the
performance of a corrosion inhibitor by complexing with the inhibitor molecules, change
the charge of the double layer at the electrode surface and lead to a faster chemisorption
process. Since magnesium and calcium have a similar chemical structure, it is likely that
the same kind of complexing happens for both Mg2+ and Ca2+. Similar behaviour was
observed by Rublova et al. [46], who also reported that the presence of cations increased
the inhibiting eﬀect of PCG, by increasing the surface adsorption of the inhibitor molecules.
The beneﬁcial inﬂuence of cations on corrosion inhibitor has also been observed with other
inhibitors by other authors [43] [44].

Future work
Noteworthy, the bode diagrams for Mg2+ and Ca2+ ions in Fig. 4.2.2b and 4.2.5b revealed
a distinct high-frequency phase shift that was not observed in the simple brine. This
capacitive behaviour could be related to the suspected complexing happening at the
surface, as described above. It is possible that with additional ions the thickness of
the double layer near the surface changes and increases capacitive behaviour. However,
the high-frequency response did not have a magnitude detectable on the scale of the
Nyquist diagrams. The more stable chemisorption could also explain the fewer and larger
precipitations of corrosion product observed in Fig. 4.2.3c and 4.2.6d. The stabilisation of
the pH value as observed both in Tab.4.2.2 and 4.2.4 could also relate to the presumed
complexing in the double layer between the inhibitor and the cations. Both LPR, EIS and
SEM showed that the concentration of ions did not have any inﬂuence on the corrosion rate
or the eﬀectiveness of the inhibitor. Generally, both pore resistance and charge transfer
resistance increased for the experiment with additional cations independently on the ion
concentration as shown in Fig. 4.2.8 and 4.2.9. An explanation for this could be that when
the concentrations of oxidising ions in the solution changes, the charge transfer resistance
increases as the altered charge in the double layer inhibits the release of electrons to the
solution. It is also important to notice that a considerable amount of NaCl was added
to the brine in some experiments, in order to keep the chloride content constant, which
thereby would increase the amount of both Na+ and Cl– ions in the solution. The Na+
ions could also inﬂuence the mechanisms mentioned above. As stated in Sec. 2.1.2 the
presence of cations in the double layer can alter the kinetics of the surface and lower
the corrosion rate. Despite any combinations of cations and inhibitor or observations of
higher resistance, the eﬀective corrosion rate only diminished by 0.01 mm y−1 on average,
compared to a brine without Mg2+ and Ca2+. Common for both Mg2+ and Ca2+ ions,
were that the inhibitor eﬃciency remained at η > 98%.
5.4
Future work
In order to optimised the use inhibitor and how concentration will aﬀect the longtime
performance, several topics could be elaborated to get a complete understanding of the
inhibitor’s working mechanism. It should be noted that all the obtained results are a
function of the chosen process parameters and that observations of contradictory tendencies
could appear with diﬀerent conditions. Based on the discussion of the ﬁndings described
above, and to support the results obtained in this thesis the following future work is
recommended:
Experimental conditions. The ﬁndings from the experiments were all carried out at low
temperature and ambient pressure. In order to simulate a real-life scenario, the exper-
iments should be repeated under elevated temperature or pressure. High-temperature
experiments should be carried out in order to determine if the temperature will
have an eﬀect on the performance of the inhibitor’s working mechanism, or if the
temperature will make a more distinct diﬀerence between high and low concentrations
of inhibitor. Higher corrosion rates are expected at elevated temperatures, but the
elevated temperature could also increase the chemisorption rate of the inhibitor, and
result in a decreased incubation time. The brine chemistry could also be changed to
represent the chemistry found in the production well by adding other production

Conclusion
chemicals or other environmental substances, that could inﬂuence how the inhibitor
might interact with the steel, or how corrosive the environment would be.
Surface condition. The experimental work has indicated that there is a signiﬁcant
reduction in inhibitor eﬃciency dependant on how the surface condition of the steel
is. In the case of addition of inhibitor to a sample that had corroded freely for
48 hours, the inhibitor eﬃciency was reduced to η = 40%, compared to η > 98%
when adding the inhibitor form the beginning to a polished sample. This study only
covered the eﬀect at low concentrations of inhibitor. The drop in eﬃciency should
be compared with high concentrations of inhibitor as well. Furthermore, it should be
investigated how diﬀerent surface conditions aﬀect the eﬃciency of the inhibitor, and
what conditions are realistic to obtain in a production scenario, in order to optimise
the production process.
Boundary limit of inhibitor. The given condition used for the experimental work
showed that over 24 hours there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in corrosion rate when
using 50-300 ppm inhibitor. The lower limit of the inhibitor should be determined,
in order to sustain a low corrosion rate.
Deterioration of ﬁlm. It has been found that there is a deterioration of the inhibitor
ﬁlm over time. This experiment was carried out with 100 ppm inhibitor. It is
recommended to investigate if the same rate of deterioration is obtained with a
higher concentration of inhibitor or under more aggressive environmental conditions.
It would be important for optimisation to determine if higher concentrations of
inhibitor produce a more robust ﬁlm.
Diﬀerent steel or inhibitor. It is known that the microstructures can have an impact
on the corrosion resistance of steel. It should be determined if the used inhibitor can
provide the same level of protection, independent of microstructure and chemical
composition of the steel. Furthermore, it is recommended to investigate if the
independence, when adding cations, is similar for other corrosion inhibitors that for
instance do not contain scale inhibiting compounds.
5.5
Conclusion
This thesis has investigated the working mechanism and performance of a commercially
available corrosion inhibitor used in the oil and gas industry in the North Sea. The
inhibitor has been investigated using various electrochemical techniques (PDP, LPR and
EIS) along with microscopy (SEM). The main conclusions are:
• It has been found that the inhibitor is an organic compound with an anodic working
mechanism. The inhibitor works by forming a thin chemical ﬁlm that inhibits the
anodic dissolution of iron. The inhibitor produces a primary ﬁlm, build up by
chemisorption of inhibitor molecules, with high ohmic resistance. Imperfections
and pores in the ﬁlm are ﬁlled locally by pore plugging, where corrosion product
precipitates and seals anodic active sites.
• It was found that the protectiveness of the inhibitor is independent of concentrations
of inhibitor between 50-300ppm under the given experimental condition.
Page 60 of 75
==PAGE:74==
Conclusion
• Over 24 hours the inhibitor showed an incubation time of up to 10 hours before the
ﬁlming layer has stabilised to its optimal protection level.
• During 100-hour experiments it was found, that the there is a deterioration of the
chemical ﬁlm. Thus, it is necessary to continuously supply inhibitor to the steel in
order to sustain the protective properties of the ﬁlm.
• It was found that the surface condition of the steel has a very high inﬂuence on the
eﬃciency of the inhibitor, as surfaces that were not polished signiﬁcantly reduced
the inhibitor eﬃciency.
• Addition of Mg2+ and Ca2+ ions showed a reduction of the incubation time from
up to 10 hours to approximately 5 hours. It is suggested that a change in charge
of the electrochemical double layer is promoting complexing between the inhibitor
molecules and the steel surface and improves chemisorption.
An essential remark about these ﬁndings is that all conclusions are based on speciﬁc
environmental conditions, which need to be expanded and compared, in order to get a full
understanding of the working mechanism and optimisation potential of the inhibitor.
