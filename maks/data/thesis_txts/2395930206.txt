Abstract 
The reaction of window glazing is of great importance to the structural fire design of buildings. The reason is 
the glazing’s ability to fallout, which changes its function from enclosure to a ventilation opening. Such an 
opening will completely change the enclosure fire dynamics and, thus, the heat exposure of the structural 
elements. At present, the possibility of having no glazing fallout, which leads to a longer fire and thereby 
higher heating, is not considered by resistance classes defined in regulations. This may lead to unsafe design 
of structural elements. Therefore, it is of great importance to better understand the process of glass breakage 
and reduce the uncertainty related to the amount of ventilation in post-flashover fires. 
Previous research (Pagni & Joshi, 1994 (II)) has shown that the reaction of multilayer pane windows is highly 
dependent on the pane closest to the fire. Cracking of all panes exposed to fire, which can lead to fallout, is 
initiated at the edge of the pane due to microfractures (from cutting), that weaken the tensional strength 
(Haldimann, et al., 2008). It is known (Pagni & Joshi, 1991), that the tensional stress at the edge is caused by 
the temperature difference between expanding heated central glass and the cold rim glass, which is shaded 
from exposure by the frame. From this knowledge, a fracture criterion was derived. 
However, the direct influence of the shading width on the cracking was only sparsely investigated (Wang, et 
al., 2013) and never with the influence of different heating rates. This thesis aims to address this problem by 
investigating the influence of shading width on a single layer glass pane exposed to different levels of heating. 
The work consisted of an experimental investigation of 400 ∙400 ∙4 mm float glass panes. The specimens 
were set up in a window frame replica and irradiated with a gas driven burner until cracking was observed. 
The burner was installed to deliver three different levels of heating, namely 5, 10, and 15 kW/m2, decided 
based on state of the art. The shading width was varied in four steps; 10, 15, 20, and 25 mm, according to 
state of practice.  
The results show that the shading width influences the time till cracking when the glazing is exposed to a low 
heating rate. However, heating rates above 10 kW/m2 will generate the temperature difference, needed to 
exceed the fracture stress, in such a pace that the shading width loses its significance. The time for cracking 
to occur for heating rates higher than 15 kw/m2 will be less than a minute, consequently it was deduced that 
cracking of single glass panes will occur, at latest, just after flashover, no matter the shading width. 
The shading width also influence the temperature difference needed for cracking. The influence is 
immediately seen on the fracture stress, as expected from theory. The experimentally achieved temperature 
difference at fracture is around 55 oC, which is very close to the theoretical calculated value of 57.5 oC (based 
on the fracture criterion). The average stress value is 36.2 MPa, and results suggests the use of either 32 MPa 
or 40 MPa as fracture criterion, according to the design situation. 
The experiments did not highlight any influence of the investigated parameters on the fallout of the broken 
parts of the pane. Moreover, the fallout is expected to be highly influenced by the room pressure aspect that 
was not considered in this study. Accordingly, a conservative assumption is to assume no glass fallout for 
structural fire design and the experimental maximum of 85 % glazing fallout for other situations. This is 
consistent with ISO room experimental work conducted by Shields et al. (2002).

1 Introduction 
Designing or redesigning a building, while ensuring the desired fire safety level for structures, requires a great 
understanding of numerous fire related parameters. A main parameter, for structural elements, is the heat 
exposure, because material strength properties deteriorate when heated. However, most structural 
elements either have a low thermal conductivity or are protected from the heat exposure, e.g. by gypsum 
cladding. Therefore, not only is the heat exposure very important during a fire, but also the progression of 
the fire over time has a great influence on the structural fire design, as it takes time for heat to conduct into 
the elements. 
The fire enclosure’s gas temperature, Tg, and peak time, tmax, can be estimated through a parametric fire 
curve (DS/EN 1991-1-2 DK NA, 2007):

The equation includes the fire load, q, which is a measure of the amount of fuel in the compartment, the 
thermal inertia, b, which is a measure of how much heat is absorbed by the compartment enclosure, and the 
opening factor, O, which is a measure of the compartment ventilation.  
Typical compartments (e.g. dwellings and offices) have a fire load of q=200 MJ/m2 and a thermal inertia 
varying from b=1160 J/(m2∙K∙s½) in old buildings to 500-600 J/(m2∙K∙s½) in modern buildings with low-energy 
requirements. The opening factor is dependent on the extent of openings in the enclosure, which is primarily 
controlled by windows. Changing the opening factor highly influence the outcome of a fire’s gas temperature. 
This is evident from the plot on Figure 1.1, where fire load and thermal inertia are kept constant to the value 
of 200 MJ/m2 and 1160 J/(m2∙K∙s½), respectively. The plot visualizes that the consequence of low ventilation 
is that the fire duration highly increases compared to the decrease in temperature. This yields a longer 
conduction period and results in a higher structural element peak temperature, which can lead to failure. 
The increase in peak temperature for structural elements is as example determined (Bengtsson, 2015) and 
visualized, in the plot, for a HE550B steel beam insulated by intumescent paint. As of this reason, correct 
assumptions on the ventilation are paramount for a safe and reliable structural fire design.

As mentioned, it is primarily windows that contribute to an increase in a fire’s ventilation conditions. This is 
because window glazing can crack and possibly fall to the ground, when exposed to fire conditions, and 
thereby leave a ventilation opening behind.  
At the time fire science developed, single-glazing windows were used in buildings. These, were assumed to 
fail before flashover and therefore considered as openings in structural fire design. Accordingly, the 
possibility of having a longer fire and higher heating of structural elements is not considered by resistance 
classes defined in current regulations. However, today newer multi-layer windows are very common. Not 
only in new buildings but also in older buildings, which have been renovated due to compliance with low-
energy requirements. Multi-layer windows are known to react differently compared to single layer 
constellations, because panes in succession to the first one will have a different heat impact (Cuzzillo & Pagni, 
1998; Wang, et al., 2017a). The assumption of glass breakage before flashover, or even later in the fire 
scenario, could therefore be highly inaccurate (Hassani, et al., 1997b). Thus, current design practices could 
lead to potentially unsafe design. Therefore, it is of great importance to understand the process of glazing-
breakage better and reduce the uncertainty related to the amount of ventilation in post-flashover fires. To 
do so, state of the art and state of practice for windows were investigated, with the aim of defining the final 
objective of this project. 
1.1 State of the art  
This section summarizes relevant prior research (in chronological order), which investigate glazing in 
combination with fire and at this point constitutes state of the art. The individual summaries were kept short 
and with focus on key findings that I found interesting and relevant for the greater understanding of the 
present project.  
The very first glazing related fire investigation was a small Harvard project created by two students (Barth & 
Sung, 1977). They irradiated 6” · 7” (152 · 178 mm) glazing, with a ¼” (6.35 mm) shading frame, by a radiant 
heat source. This single study stood alone for more than a decade until Emmons (1986) published an article 
before his retirement where he stressed its subjects need for development, due to its high impact on 
==PAGE:13==
Page 3 of 95 
 
compartment fire dynamics. Based on the Harvard study, he suggested that glazing failure was due to the 
poor conduction properties of glass, which leaves the shaded edge cool and in tension due to the thermal 
expansion of the heated central part. This is still the most accredited explanation nowadays.  
After the problem had been established, Keski-Rahkonen (1988) conducted an in-depth investigation. His 
theoretical study established a deterministic criterion, under which the brittle pane breaks, based on the 
temperature difference between irradiated and shaded pane and the glazing’s tensional strength 
𝜎𝐹= ∆𝑇 𝛽 𝐸 
 
The paper includes thoughts on a probabilistic approach, since glass strength depends on the cutting 
technology and micro-damages at the edge, in addition to the fire load. A 1D model for temperature fields 
was developed for thin panes exposed to radiation, from where a thermal stress field model was suggested. 
This study concluded that the maximum tensile stress occurs at the location with the lowest temperature. 
However, that the stress field does not extend far from the cool area, and the rest of the pane remains 
practically tension-free. Furthermore, he established a rough value for the maximum allowable temperature 
difference, based on the breakage criterion, equal to 80 𝑜𝐶 (based on glass properties 𝜎𝑚𝑎𝑥= 50 𝑀𝑃𝑎, 𝛽=
8 ∙10−6𝐾−1, 𝐸= 80 𝐺𝑃𝐴). The theoretical findings by Keski-Rahkonen were supported only by qualitative 
investigations and the theoretical analysis was limited by three main simplifications: 1. no temperature 
gradient across thickness (thermally thin material), 2. the shaded edge glass is kept at initial temperature at 
all time, 3. the radiative cooling of both sides of the glass is linearized and identical (Keski-Rahkonen, 1988).  
The next obvious step to take was to perform an experimental investigation of window glass breakage by fire 
(Skelly, et al., 1991). The authors studied breaking of single layer window glass (2.4·280·500 mm) in a small 
fire compartment, with and without an 25 mm wide aluminium frame. The compartment was heated by 
burning liquid hexane in four different pan sizes, which ensured that the pane was completely immersed in 
the hot gas layer within 10 seconds.  
 
The results of the edge-protected tests provided an average temperature difference in the pane, between 
the hot centre and cold rim, of 90 𝑜𝐶 (standard deviation of 18). All but one of the edge-protected panes 
cracked and at least partial window fallout was observed. The fact that one pane withstood the test can be 
explained by the probability of edge damages that Keski-Rahkonen (1988) mentioned. Furthermore, the pane 
had a max measured temperature difference of 81𝑜𝐶, which apparently was insufficient to induce cracking. 
In general, the results show that, the larger the fire, the faster the crack initiation. However, there was no 
systematic correlation between the temperature difference and the pool size or time to breakage. The edge-
unprotected tests were only tested for the two largest pan sizes and they all lead to crack initiation within 
65-200 seconds, at a glass temperature in the range 184 −218𝑜𝐶.  
 
To assess the validity of the established breakage criterion (Keski-Rahkonen, 1988), Skelly et al. (1991) 
investigated the experiment specific glass specimen properties. This led to a max allowable temperature 
gradient equal to 70 𝑜𝐶 (based on glass properties 𝜎𝑚𝑎𝑥= 47 𝑀𝑃𝑎, 𝛽= 9.5 ∙10−6𝐾−1, 𝐸= 70 𝐺𝑃𝐴). The 
temperature disagreement was explained by the fact that the pane thermocouple (TC) was directly heated 
by radiation, hence overestimating the temperature difference. The study also presents the cracking patterns 
and could conclude that the edge-protected had multiple bifurcations, while the edge-unprotected panes 
had less cracking and that the cracks rarely extended more than 7-10 cm from the initiation point. This led to 
a higher risk of fallout in the case of edge-protection due to uniting cracks.  
To reach a more exact level of understanding, Pagni and Joshi (1991) worked theoretically to circumvent the 
simplifications introduced by Keski-Rahkonen (1988). They derived a temperature history calculation 
procedure (based on energy equations) to investigate temperature history of the exposed pane. They 
==PAGE:14==
Page 4 of 95 
 
deduced a method for one-dimensional heating over the pane thickness and expanded it too two-
dimensional heating, to study temperature isocurves behind the shading. The latter generated some very 
visual figures of temperature isocurves behind the shading, which establishes that temperature increase 
seldom reach further than two times the pane thickness into the shaded area. They also worked on stress 
field determination similarly to Keski-Rahkonen and proved that it is the pane thickness-averaged 
temperature which is needed for the given breakage criterion. 
 
On top of the above study, the authors published two other studies, one theoretical and one experimental. 
The theoretical part (Pagni & Joshi, 1994 (I)) presented an investigation of the suggested temperature 
calculation procedure and utilized the derived calculation procedure to study included parameters like decay 
length, biot number, and irradiation level on the glass temperature variation. Interesting plots of the 
temperature difference across the pane are depicted, showing high difference between the two surfaces.  
In the experimental part (Pagni & Joshi, 1994 (II)) a total of 59 glass specimen (small “beams” of 178·25.4·2.5 
mm) were tested on a four-point-bending apparatus, to arrive at a tensile stress input for the cracking 
determination. The tests demonstrated breaking stresses from 36.5 to 128 MPa, which the authors fitted in 
a three-parameter Weibull distribution (with 𝑚= 1.21, 𝜎𝑂= 32.9 𝑀𝑃𝑎, 𝜎𝑢= 35.8 𝑀𝑃𝑎). The three-
parameter distribution resembles the data very well as seen in reference Fig. 5 (Pagni & Joshi, 1994 (II)). It 
was finally concluded, on the basis on the distribution, that a value of 40 MPa would be a conservative 
estimate for calculations. 
During the following years (1994-1997) Hassani, Shields, and Silcock conducted a series of experiments with 
glazing exposed to enclosure fires. Their goal was to evaluate the validity of the mathematical models derived 
by Keski-Rahkonen (1988) and Pagni & Joshi (1991). They performed their tests primarily in half scale 
concrete fire compartments (1.7 m high, 1.5 m wide, and 1.6 m deep) heated by wood cribs. The window 
design was changed alongside the tests, but the glazing was at all time supported on the entire bottom edge.
 
 
They tested larger windows (905 · 1615 mm) of different design, both single and two layers of glass. These 
were subjected to two-zone fire environments from which it was evident that the first cracks always would 
initiate at the top part immersed in the hot gas layer. Furthermore, they saw that crack bifurcations were 
contained at the upper heated part of the glass where tension was obtained. They utilized strain gauges to 
understand the stress fields in the pane and concluded that the non-uniform heating (two-zone fire) would 
result in tension at the top and compression at the bottom. Glass fracture morphology was used to validate 
the strain gauge results (Hassani, et al., 1994).  
 
Thermal fracture and stress in windows were investigated in more depth by Hassani et al. (1994b). This led 
to the conclusion that the assumption of uniform heating on exposed glazing can only be valid for very fast 
fires, where the entire pane is immersed in the hot gas layer. Moreover, the assumption of instantaneous 
glass failure after cracking is grossly invalid and multi-pane windows could retain their integrity way longer 
than what is expected from single layer units. The latter statement is justified by the fact that the outer pane 
reaction is highly dependent on the inner panes response to the fire exposure. 
 
Furthermore, single and double-glazing units were tested with wood crib fires placed centrally and in the 
corner of the compartment. The tests suggest that convective heat transfer is dominant and leads to cracking 
at a glazing temperature difference of 150 −190 𝑜𝐶, unless the fire is close and has good ventilation 
conditions. Strain measurements showed stress values of 24-56 MPa for smaller windows (6 · 787 · 463 mm) 
and 16-24 MPa for larger windows (6 · 787 · 965 mm), i.e. a trend that suggest that smaller windows are 
harder to break. The first crack was seen to occur in the single panes after approximately 300 s and twice the 
time had to pass before the second crack initiated. Nevertheless, the panes showed little sign of immediate 
fallout. Only one large specimen failed after some twenty minutes.

Hassani et al. (1994) conducted experiments on window specimen in half scale room fires, constructed and 
instrumented to ISO-standard. They conducted experiments with both single and double-glazing units 
exposed to centre and corner room fires. The term bowing is introduced to cover possible movements in the 
thickness direction induced be thermal gradient in this direction. The authors deduce that the average bulk 
temperature of the glass is to be used when applying the theoretical breakage criterion. 
 
For single layer windows breakage stress was recorded yielding results from 8.4 to 16.1 MPa.  
From just before the millennium and up to today multiple additional experimental studies was performed. 
The overall trends from the studies were the same as already defined i.e. that windows with multiple panes 
would withstand longer in a fire (Shields, et al., 2005). It was described that this among others were because 
the first layer of glazing works as a radiant filter, which only lets through electromagnetic waves (i.e. 
radiational heat) that will also pass through subsequent layers (Pagni, 2003). An overview of all prior 
experimental studies was combined in Table 1. The table grants a clear overview of important factors such 
as: glazing size, shading width, heating source.  
Amongst these studies is the work by Wang et al. (2013 to 2017). They have primary been investigating 
different installation forms and glazing types (Wang, et al., 2015) in order to understand the behaviour of 
glazing used for curtain walls. Among the tests is one study of the influence of shading widths between 10 to 
50 mm in 10 mm intervals (Wang, et al., 2013). The study found that the influence from the shading width 
was little influenced by the heating rate. Furthermore, that the shading width made the time to crack 
initiation go down from 10 to 20 mm and then gradually up towards a peak at 50 mm.

1.1.1 Deductions from the state of the art 
Based on the conducted literature review, it is clear that any glazing fallout, which will introduce ventilation, 
is initiated by cracking. Cracking always origins at the edge of the glazing and according to the breakage 
criterion it occurs due to a critical temperature difference. The temperature difference will occur during a 
fire because the window frame shades the outer rim of the pane, which keeps this part cooler than the 
exposed part. This phenomenon introduces tensional stress at the rim because the shaded glazing is 
expanding less than the exposed part of the glazing. 
Apart from the temperature difference does the cracking criterion (Keski-Rahkonen, 1988) include the glass’ 
breakage stress, its linear expansion, and its modulus of elasticity. However, ordinary window glazing (soda 
lime silicate product) is fabricated with consistent material properties and the most interesting factor to 
study is therefore the temperature difference. The temperature difference at cracking measured in prior 
experiments is greatly varying, from around 40-190 oC, and it clearly seems dependent on the experimental 
setup parameters such as frame width, glass thickness, heating rate, and measuring equipment.  
To understand the cracking behaviour for newer window installation forms, and thereby possible fallout, as 
suggested in the introduction; it is evident from prior studies that it is still relevant to study the behaviour of 
single layer glazing. This is true, because studies of multi-layer glazing units show that the first layer of glazing 
is determining for the outcome of the remaining layers. The first layer of glazing works as a radiant filter, 
which only lets through electromagnetic waves (i.e. radiational heat) that will also pass through subsequent 
layers. Thus, before investigating multi-layer glazing units it is essential to completely understand the 
mechanisms leading to cracking for single layer glazing. 
==PAGE:17==
Page 7 of 95 
 
It was deduced, based on the combined state of the art, that the most apparent missing research to 
investigate was the shading width’s direct influence on the cracking. This parameter was only sparsely 
investigated and only with a single heating rate. However, it is evident that the heating rate of glazing is 
crucial for the time to cracking. Therefore, it was decided to investigate the shading width influence on 
cracking under the influence on multiple levels of heating rate.  
For prior experiments the shading width have been variated from 6.35-50 mm. However, none of the 
researchers mentioned which shading width was common in real windows. This will be investigated in the 
next section. Multiple studies have specified that a heating rate less than 5 kW/m2 will not be sufficient to 
cause cracking and further that 18 kW/m2 will cause cracking in less than a minute. Thus, heating rate levels 
in between appears to be interesting to study.

1.3 Project objective and method 
Following the introduction to the problem and the investigations of state of the art and state of practice; the 
objective for this thesis is to investigate how a window frame shading width will influence the glazing reaction 
to different heat exposure levels.   
It was deduced that the investigation should be in the form of an experimental study. The experiments must 
be conducted with the most common window installation form i.e. a completely rim covering frame. The 
shading width of this frame should be varied in four steps: 10, 15, 20, and 25 mm, according to state of 
practice. On the other hand, state of the art showed that the glazing should be exposed to heat exposure

2.1.1 General heat transfer 
An objects temperature can vary in a fire due to the three means of heat transfer: conduction, convection, 
and radiation. These are described below in brief, mainly based on Wickström’s (2016) book “Temperature 
Calculation in Fire Safety Engineering”. The three modes of heat transfer are all based on the definition of 
temperature – “heat is transferred from places with higher temperatures to places with lower temperature, 
i.e. the temperature difference is the driving force of the heat transfer” (Wickström, 2016). 
Conduction, can be described as contact heat transfer and it works by transferring energy on a molecular 
level. The transfer is conducted during collision of molecules, energy will leave the high energetic molecules 
and be obtained by the low energetic molecules. Temperature raise will enhance the molecules energy level 
and heat will thereby move from high temperature molecules to low temperature molecules. This process 
does not only take place in solids but also in fluids, even though, the main mode of transfer in fluids typically 
is convection. 
Convection, can be described by motion of matter. Thus, convection works by movement of molecules within 
fluids, such as hot smoke moving towards the sky from a bonfire. However, standing close to a bonfire also 
makes you warm, i.e. not all heat is transferred away through direct contact (conduction) or leaving towards 
the sky (convection). The heat that you feel is primary coming from the last mode of heat transfer – radiation.

Radiation, is based on electromagnetic waves, which scatters in all directions from the hot medium. It differs 
from the two former modes by the fact that it requires no matter or medium to transfer through. Another 
difference, is that conductive and convective heat transfer depends on temperature difference to, 
approximately, the first power. Whereas, heat transfer by radiation depends on the temperature difference, 
between the emitting and receiving objects, to the fourth power. Therefore, radiation often dominates 
conduction and convection at high temperatures, as typically seen in fires. 
To successfully calculate heat transfer from a real fire to an object, it is essential to understand the boundary 
condition of the heat towards the object. This is due to the complex interference between the different 
modes of heat transfer. However, the experimental work of the present thesis is performed with a radiative 
heat source, in the form of a panel heated by gas combustion. Thus, heat transfer is dominated by radiation 
and no boundary conditions are required to describe the complex interference between the different modes 
of heat transfer. Heat transfer by radiation will be described in more depth in the next section.

2.2.1 Fracture mechanics of glass 
Fracture of glass is very different from that of most other materials in the building sector. Most materials will 
deform plastically under stress levels close to their breakage stress, but glass is a brittle material and has 
basically no room for plastic flow. The reason behind this is the amorphous molecular structure, which consist 
of an irregular network of silicon and oxygen atoms mixed with alkaline parts. This type of structural order 
of atoms has no slip planes or dislocations, which could accommodate the plastic flow before fracture. 
Consequently, glass behave as a perfectly elastic material at normal temperatures (Figure 2.4) and will fail 
without any warning when exposed to a critical stress level (Haldimann, et al., 2008).  
The brittle nature of glass makes it vulnerable to stress concentrations, which is typically seen around edge 
flaws in glazing. The flaws occur when cutting or accidentally damaging the edge and is generally accepted

It has been established (Hassani, et al., 1994b) that the above described thermal induced stress will lead to

adjacent to the frame. The tension, respectively compression, will act parallel to the edge. However, the

the corners is highly reduced and therefore cracking is likely to initiate at the central parts of the edge. The 
described stress field has more recently been supported by FEM simulation (Wang, et al., 2011). 
2.2.3 Fracture morphology  
Materials with a brittle nature like glass will leave a distinct fracture pattern when breaking in tension. 
Through studying the fracture pattern at the spot of crack initiation much basic information can be derived. 
Among these is the fracture stress, which I will report along the remaining results for this thesis work. The 
theory described in this section is based on the work of Krohn and Hasselman (1971), Mecholsky, et al. (1974), 
and Abdel-Latif, et al. (1977).  
A typical fracture pattern in glass is divided in three regions: mirror, mist, and hackle. The three regions are 
clearly seen on Figure 2.6 (left), which also includes a fracture pattern from one of the conducted pilot tests 
(right). The fracture always originates in a flaw at the glass edge and flaws in the glass will in general affect 
how the crack will advance throughout the pane. The mirror is the first region surrounding the initiating edge 
flaw with a smooth and reflecting surface. The propagation of the crack is accelerating within this region 
towards its critical velocity. When approaching the critical velocity, the surface starts to get more and more 
rough, which is indicated first by the mist and then by the hackle region. The fracture pattern gives a clear 
indication of where the cracking initially emerged, for instance at which pane surface the cracking initiated, 
which is important when understanding the experimental results.

3.2 Heating apparatus (H-TRIS) 
The goal of this thesis is to test glass specimen introduced to a uniform imposed heat flux. This is possible to 
achieve by utilizing the Heat Transfer Rate Inducing System (H-TRIS) at DTU fire lab. 
The H-TRIS at DTU fire lab consists of a gas driven radiating panel (Figure 3.2 - A), which is placed on a rail 
system (Figure 3.2 - B). The radiating panel, also described as the burner, is an industry purpose burner 
produced by “aem BRÜLEUR”; it measures 500 · 500 mm. The burner is supplied with nature gas from the 
supply system at the lab (Figure 3.2 - C) and supplied with air from a fan (Figure 3.2 - D). The fan is installed 
to deliver sufficient air for the gas combustion.  
 
Figure 3.2 - H-TRIS at DTU fire lab. A - burner, B - rail system, C - gas intake, D - fan, E - control box. 
The H-TRIS can deliver a big variety of incident heat flux at the specimen surface, ranging from zero to over 
one hundred and fifty kW/m2. The potential delivery is highly dependent on distance (i.e. view factor) from 
the burner to the specimen, as seen on Figure 3.3 (left). The distance can be varied easily by manoeuvring 
the H-TRIS back and forth on the dedicated rail system. However, the heat flux is also dependent on the 
intensity of the burner, which is controlled by the operator. The variation can be done manually through a 
small panel on the control box (Figure 3.2 - E) from 0 to 100 %, but it is also possible to pre-program it to 
create a time dependent heat flux output. The variation in burner intensity is performed by electronically 
variating gas intake and fan strength (min/max is based on air intake for sustained combustion, together with 
fan model and gas pressure).  
The variation possibilities can be seen at Figure 3.3, which clearly show the high impact of the distance (left) 
compared to the impact of changing burner intensity (right). Both variation possibilities were utilized during 
this work to reach the sought imposed heat flux. 
==PAGE:32==
Page 22 of 95 
 
 
Figure 3.3 - Variation of heat flux from H-TRIS, dependence on distance (left chart) and burner intensity (right chart) 
The H-TRIS was developed and implemented by Cristián Maluk, who studied self-consolidating concrete in 
highly optimized slender structural elements (Maluk, 2014). The H-TRIS aim at achieving “high repeatability, 
rationally quantified thermal boundary conditions, and good statistical confidence, all at low economic and 
temporal cost” (Maluk, 2014). Instead of introducing thermal exposure as temperature (furnace system), this 
system introduces a time-history of incident heat flux.  
3.2.1 Burner shield  
According to the pilot experimental work (see section 4.1.3), the burner need to warm-up for 150 seconds 
before it can deliver a constant heat flux output. Consequently, a shield was introduced at the start of each 
test, which blocked any pre-warm-up irradiation towards the glass specimen. The shield consisted of a fire-
resistant board (22 mm thick “FireFree 850”) fastened to an aluminium rod skeleton with wheels.  
3.3 Specimen setup 
The following section covers how the glass specimen is mounted in front of the H-TRIS, how the 
thermocouples (TCs) are attached to the specimen, and how the window frame replica was constructed. 
3.3.1 Glass pane mount 
The glass pane was supported on three sides: bottom, front, and back. All its weight transfers through two 
50 mm long supporting blocks placed at the bottom side, while it at the front- and backside was restrained 
by a window frame replica (described in the next section). The frontside will for the duration of this thesis 
refer to the side facing the H-TRIS, respectively the backside facing ambient. 
The load bearing supports at the bottom was designed based on the installation method used in Denmark 
(Glasindustrien & Teknologisk Institut, 2008). This type of installation form is common around the world, 
although it does vary slightly from country to country, e.g. from Denmark to Australia (Efficient IGU PTY LTD, 
NA). The two, bottom edge supporting blocks, must have a minimum distance to the edge of 50 mm, which 
was used for the entire set of experiments. Likewise, the installation method prescribes a minimum of 12 
mm edge cover, together with an edge clearance of 10 mm. 
All the glass specimens were investigated both before and after breakage to ensure that the edge contained 
no flaws. When flaws were present the specific specimen was discarded. During this process under the 
==PAGE:33==
Page 23 of 95 
 
preliminary experimental work, it was found that the glaziers cutting procedure had created a difference 
between front- and backside edge. Thus, one edge contains microfractures to a much higher extend than the 
other, see Figure 3.4. Therefore, all panes were mounted with the same surface facing the H-TRIS – to ensure 
that this would not affect the results, since it was established that microfractures highly impact glass 
strength.  
No prior research has established whether the cracks typically initiate at the front or backside, which this 
thesis will establish through the theory of fraction mirror. The fraction mirrors from the pilot tests (section 
4.1.2) showed a tendency of cracking to initiate at the edge with microfractures. Based on this knowledge I 
decided to mount the panes with the microfractures towards the backside in order to reach the most 
withstanding specimen. A longer time to initial cracking will be conservative for structural elements, as it will 
postpone the windows from functioning as vents.

3.3.2 Window frame replica  
The window frame replica was constructed by fire resistant boards (22 mm thick “FireFree 850”) on top of a 
supporting aluminium rod skeleton. The aluminium rods matched the ones of the H-TRIS frame, hence my 
frame was easily attached hereto. The frame front- and backside can be seen on Figure 3.5.  
 
The frontside was divided into five board pieces, one on each side and a lintel to shade the top of the pane. 
The boards could be shifted into the correct position regarding the desired shading width. In addition, the 
four larger ones were shading the aluminium skeleton from direct heating.  
 
The backside frame was constructed in one piece, here a new frame was created for each shading width to 
accommodate the changing parameter. The backside frame is supported at the bottom by the aluminium 
skeleton and is fastened on the sides by movable sliders. The sliders were combined with wedges that was 
introduced under light pressure to keep the frame in place.  
Both front- and backside frame was equipped with 10 mm thick compressible ceramic wool insulation. The 
insulation was installed to act as the elastic joint that typical can be found between the hard frame material 
and the glazing in actual windows. It ensures that the pane has the possibility to expand though still 
supported, which was observed during the pilot experiments to have a big influence on the potential fallout. 
Furthermore, it insulates from any heat conduction through the front frame, just like an actual elastic joint 
would do. The front frame is in addition protected from radiational heat up by a 25 mm wide aluminium tape 
strip.  
I expect that this frame installation form includes variations of up to 1 mm on the shading width.

3.3.3 Thermometer information 
The temperature measurement equipment, attachment, and reliability were among the most important 
parameters when conducting the experiments. The equipment chosen were K-type thermocouples (TCs) 
which is an easy an inexpensive sensor that has a measuring point at the end of a wire (referred to as the 
“tip”). The wire contains two different conductors and when the tip is heated the thermoelectric effect will 
ensure that a voltage is produced over the circuit. This voltage is measured through an acquisition system, 
where the free end of the TC is inserted. The utilized TCs were produced by Omega and had a diameter of 
0.25 mm and a cover made of glass braid insulation. K-type TCs are produced with a wire base of nickel with 
conductors of respectively chromium and aluminium. They have a working range of -270 °C to 1260 °C and a 
standard accuracy of ± 2.2 oC or ± 0.75%. Appendix “A – Setup manual” describes the data collecting process 
in more detail, together with thoughts and considerations on usage of the TCs. 
There is no such thing as a guidance for TC attachment to glass and former experimental work did not provide 
clear indications on how to implement the measuring equipment. Skelly (1991) used “cement-on” TCs, but 
concluded that directly irradiated TCs were heated more than the pane due to direct radiation. Harada (2000) 
used infrared thermometers, but did not state consideration on usability. Finally, Wang et al. (Wang, et al., 
2015; Wang, et al., 2017b) applied sheet TCs to the glass panes using high-temperature resistant adhesive 
and stated that it gives a more precise measurement, because the aluminium surface of the sheet will reflect 
radiational heat. Accordingly, I decided to run pilot experiments on how to apply the TCs for the present 
thesis. The pilot experiments are described in section 4.1 and it was through these tests that the attachment 
method described below was developed. 
The setup includes ten TCs (see Figure 3.6 (left)), which are numbered from 1 to 10. Number 1-9 is attached 
to the backside of the glass, while uneven numbers are placed at the exposed glass and even numbers are 
placed at the frame shaded glass. Number 10 is placed at the frontside close to the centre.  
 
==PAGE:35==
Page 25 of 95 
 
All the TCs have direct contact between the glass surface and the TC tip without the use of adhesive. The 
contact is ensured by the tension in the TC cable, which makes the cables act like a spring. The “spring” is 
flanked by a retaining hole in the back frame and the glass surface. Figure 3.6 (right) presents how this was 
managed.  
 
 
Figure 3.6 – Right – TC placement for experiments; Left – TC wire organisation (seen from the back) 
It was evident from the pilot experiments that the radiation transmitted through the glazing (theory section 
2.2.4) would influence the exposed backside TCs (uneven TC numbers). Consequently, these are shielded 
from transmitted radiation by a piece of aluminium tape (10 mm in diameter), which is placed on the 
frontside pane surface. After placing the TCs the shielding aluminium tape was placed, and it was insured 
that the tip of the TCs was centred according to the tape.   
The TCs located behind the frame was placed on the glazing surface through a 5 mm hole drilled in the frame. 
These TCs were placed as close to the edge as possible in order to measure the coldest part of the glass. 
The TC on the frontside (TC number 10) was placed with its tip pointing directly into the glazing surface. I 
knew that TC 10 would deliver falsely high temperature due to the direct heating, but it was established to 
obtain data that possibly could be compared to prior research that was measuring in this manner.

4.1.1 Edge-unprotected glazing – reaction to irradiation 
Skelly et al. (1991) established that edge-unprotected glazing would start cracking at surface temperatures 
around 197 oC, which was much later than similar edge-protected glazing, at 123 oC. Furthermore, they 
established that the cracking patterns of edge-unprotected glazing would have less bifurcations. All other 
relevant experimental and theoretical research, described in section 1.1, have no descriptions on such 
cracking; or, these sources simply neglect the possibility because theory describes that cracking is mainly 
caused by the temperature difference, introduced by the shading frame. Nonetheless, it was important for 
this thesis work to establish whether cracking could occur in edge-unprotected specimens, under the 
introduced thermal loads. 
Edge-unprotected samples, of this thesis work, were exposed to temperatures above 200 oC, approaching 
250 oC (see Figure 4.2). Here, cracking was expected but nothing happened; the experiments were 
terminated due to risk of overheating the surrounding setup. The key difference between Skelly et al. (1991) 
and this thesis tests was the heating rate, which for Skelly et al. (1991) was up to 2,3 oC/s and for this thesis’s 
tests closer to 0.5 oC/s. I therefore suggest that the cracking of Skelly et al.’s tests is due to the temperature 
gradient in the direction of the glazing thickness. This gradient and these temperatures will not occur during 
this parametric study and it will, as such, not be relevant to conduct comparing tests for edge-unprotected 
specimen.

4.1.3 Burner shield time 
It was established, based on tests of the heat flux sensor setup, that the H-TRIS burner did not deliver 
maximum heat flux impact immediately at ignition, but had to heat up. The same trend was seen for Mowrer 
(1998) and Harada et al.’s (2000) experimental setup. Nevertheless, it was my desire, for the final 
experiments, to irradiate the specimen with a close to constant heat flux. Therefore, a burner shield was 
introduced to block all radiation to the specimen, before the H-TRIS burner could deliver an almost constant 
output. 
The required shielding time was established through a set of tests with different burner intensity and distance 
to the measuring heat flux sensor. Thereby, to establish whether the time to constant output could be 
sensitive to H-TRIS installation. Moreover, it was established whether the heat flux sensor itself was 
vulnerable to the heat up, by introducing the shield at burner ignition and removing it after 100 seconds. The 
resulting data is plotted in Figure 4.4.

4.1.4 Heat flux sensor sensitivity  
As expressed in section 1.2 the specimen in this work will be exposed to radiation between 5 and 15 kW/m2, 
in three steps. The level of radiation will be measured by a Hukseflux heat flux sensor, as described, which is 
produced with a certain optimal working range. Within the given range, the sensor is reported to be most 
stable, but since these tests are conducted with a quite narrow heat flux range, it is perhaps accurate enough 
only to apply a single sensor type. Thus, it was tested whether it is possible to utilize only a 20 kW/m2 sensor, 
since the maximal level of irradiation is approximately 15 kW/m2, instead of swapping sensor for the low heat 
flux experiments (5 kW/m2). 
To do so, two sensors, with optimal sensitivity at respectively 5 and 20 kW/m2, were placed next to each 
other in a test setup and irradiated with the H-TRIS. The H-TRIS was installed at a distance of 75 cm and with 
a burner intensity of 15 %, which results in an approximate heat flux of 5 kW/m2.  The test was conducted 
twice with the same result, which can be seen on Figure 4.5. A quite comparable measurement was obtained 
with a better accuracy within the first 300 seconds of the test. According to section 4.1.2 this timeframe is 
sufficient for glazing cracking to occur. Based on this consideration, I assumed that the 20 kW/m2 sensor 
could be used with sufficient accuracy throughout all future tests.

4.2.1 Thermocouple attachment and placement 
This section describes how the thermocouple attachment method were derived. The process started by 
studying past experimental research, which all utilized different attachment methods, but had the K-type TC 
in common. Skelly (1991) applied glued “cement-on” TCs for glass temperature measurements, but 
concluded that the radiation from the fire made this attachment method overestimating the temperature. 
Mowrer (1998) applied his TCs to the backside glass surface, but did not specify how. However, he described 
that his frame shaded TCs were inserted through in drilled hole in the frame, with its measuring point pressed 
against the surface. Harada (2000) applied TCs at the edges shielded by aluminium tube, but with no 
recording of how it was kept at the surface by adhesive or other means. In the numerous studies conducted 
in China and Hong Kong (e.g. (Wang, et al., 2014)) was the measuring conducted by sheet TCs (25 · 15 mm) 
made of aluminium alloy with a high heat conductivity of 226 W/(m·K); these were attached to the glass using 
high-temperature resistant adhesive. Furthermore, the sheets are described as light reflective, which was 
deemed to yield the most accurate results, since the radiative heat up, identified as a problem by Skelly 
(1991), could be avoided.  
Based on the above, my preliminary idea was to attach TCs with thermally high conductive adhesive. The 
study hereof showed that the use of adhesive brought multiple problems along; it was expensive, had long 
==PAGE:44==
Page 34 of 95 
 
delivery time, and often had very long curing time or had to cure with elevated temperatures (acc-silicones, 
2017; mgchemicals, 2017). Altogether, this would slow down each experiment and lead to risk of delay. 
Hence, a set of preliminary investigations was conducted with a glass purpose silicone based adhesive, 
bought in a local builder’s shop. The idea was to understand how well the use of adhesive worked, before 
ordering the expensive final product. The test setup was comparable to the final one, but the specimen was 
edge-unprotected only supported between two screws at the top. The TC layout is presented on Figure 4.6 
(left). I knew that the builder’s shop adhesive had a lower thermal conductivity than the expensive product, 
but its influence on the temperature delay was believed to be small, since the TC itself would be in direct 
contact with the glass. The results for the silicone based adhesive gave clear smooth curves, but high 
temperature variations of around 50 oC, between front and back (see TC2+3 vs TC6+7 on Figure 4.7 (left)). 
Nevertheless, the experiments established that the silicone would withstand sufficiently high temperature.  
Based on the theory for the transmittance and reflection properties of glass, described in section 2.2.4, I 
expected that the TCs would heat up more in comparison to the glass; since, the TC wire will not transmit 
any of the received radiation. Therefore, a test was conducted to understand the influence of blocking direct 
radiative heating to the TCs. Thus, TC1 and TC4 were shielded from radiation using aluminium tape. The 
aluminium tape drastically changed the temperature results, which can be seen in the difference between 
shielded and unshielded TCs (TC3+4) on Figure 4.7 (left). The temperature differs approximately 50 oC (from 
150 oC to 200 oC), the two TCs were not more than 10 oC apart in the first test. An important observation was 
that the unshielded TCs quickly dropped to the temperature of their counterparts after the burner shut down. 
I recognise this as a phenomenon showing the actual glass temperature without any radiative influence, it 
was seen throughout all tests. Based on the above, it was deemed essential to utilize TC radiative shielding 
for all forthcoming experiments.

4.2.3 Reliability of developed thermocouple attachment method 
It is essential that the above described TC attachment not only works well for the testing, but also delivers 
reliable data. To understand whether this is true or not two tests was conducted with unshaded backside TCs 
applied next to shaded backside TCs (as seen at Figure 4.13). The flaw in the measurements by the unshaded 
TCs is that a fraction of the radiation will transmit through the glass but not through the tip of the TC; 
therefore, the reading will be higher than the actual glass surface temperature. To eliminate this error a large 
screen was used to block all radiation during the tests for 10 seconds, in intervals of one minute. The 
expectation was that this would make the unshaded TC temperature drop to that of the specimen level, 
which then should be the same as the shielded TC, any difference would then give an impression of the 
installation forms reliability.

4.2.4 Variation of temperature across the glazing 
As briefly mentioned in the past two sections, the pilot tests imply that there is a temperature gradient across 
the glazing. The gradient is mainly encountered across the height of the pane but changes over the width of 
the pane was also studied.   
A gradient across the height of the pane could possibly cause the upper part to heat up faster followed by a 
larger expansion and thereby stress, which in turn would cause cracking to initiate more often from the upper 
part of the pane. The tendency of higher heat in the top of the pane is also seen in former research (Wang, 
et al., 2017c). Any gradient across the width of the pane can be important to understand when studying the 
final test results. None of the variations has, to my knowledge, ever been described nor measured, but seems 
to me like an important indication for the total overview of the resulting cracking mechanism, furthermore, 
it can lead to an understanding of the general accuracy of the measurements. 
Previous pilot test gave the impression that the main temperature difference was related to a convective 
stream of heat towards the upper part of the specimen, therefore I expect that TCs at the same height would 
result in a reasonably similar result. During these tests I became aware that the convective part is not only 
happening at the specimen setup, but also leads to a temperature gradient across the height of the burner 
itself resulting in a temperature difference of around 50 oC from bottom to top of the burner. The convective 
heating at the burner side will ensure that a higher heat flux will be released from the top of the burner, 
which due to the view factor would have a higher impact on the top of the specimen. 
Figure 4.13 indicate the location of TCs for this test (as for the previous), which were mounted with an 
internal distance so small that the difference between the measuring points was kept at a minimum without 
jeopardizing the independence as described in the previous section. The temperature variation analysis in 
this section is only based on the shaded TCs (TC1, 4, 7, 10, 13, and 16). The tests were conducted with an 
approximate heat flux of 9 kW/m2. 
A total of three tests were conducted, all of which gave comparable result. The results with the highest 
difference compared to the expected is given in Figure 4.14 (right). The left-hand side TCs (TC 10, 13, 16) gave 
a very accurate picture on how the temperature was expected to grow with height, while the right-hand side 
TCs gave a completely different picture, which could not be explained. The right-hand side additionally gave 
significantly lower temperature values. No real similarity was seen between the TCs placed at the same height 
(i.e. the sets TC 1+10, TC 4+13, and TC 7+16), the best match being the middle TC set (TC 4+13) with a variation 
of only approximately 7 degrees. 
The first of the consecutive three runs granted the closest to expected temperature values, both regarding 
increase with height and similarity in sets with a maximal difference (TC 7+16) of 7 degrees; the two tests 
only difference was that the second test was started at a glass temperature of approximately 32.5 degree. 
This means that the cooling was not completed, which is taken as the main reason for the large deviation in 
test 02 and must be taken very serious when conducting the final tests. This finding was backed up by the 
third consecutive run. 
Utilizing test 01 as most accurate leaves me with the understanding that higher temperatures at the top of 
the specimen generally is correct and that this is due to convective heating. The temperature variation with 
height is approximately 10 oC, but it is not possible to establish how much of this variation is due to increase 
inside the glass or increase due to convective heat up of the TCs. Furthermore, it was established that the 
temperature variation across the width of the pane is up to 7 degrees in the top and bottom while it at the 
centre is less, i.e. a certain uncertainty in temperature measurement can be seen when measuring at specific

these two tests are likewise not considered throughout the results presented in this chapter. Moreover, a

5.3 The shading width influence on time to cracking 
On Figure 5.4 the shading width influence on time to first crack initiation is plotted in a graph for each level 
of heat flux. The graph clearly indicates that a higher imposed heat flux (and thereby higher heating rate) 
results in an earlier crack occurrence. This trend was expected, since the temperature difference drives the 
stress accumulation as described in section 2.2.2.  
 
Figure 5.4 - Time to first crack (incl. standard deviation) vs Shading width 
The time to crack initiation is observed to decrease with increasing shading width, for low heating rates (5 
kW/m2), although large standard deviation is seen. The same decrease is visible from 10 to 15 mm of shading 
in case of medium heating. However, for shading widths higher than 15 mm and for all shading widths in case 
of high heating, the time to crack initiation stays almost constant. A similar increment of the cracking time 
with increasing shading width was also observed during the experiments conducted by Wang, et al. (2013) 
for a similarly low heating build-up. This trend is not found for the high heating, for which the shading width 
has no noticeable influence.  
 
I believe that the big difference in time with heating rate is caused by the poor conductivity properties of 
glass. Thus, a low heating rate leaves time for the glass to conduct enough heat to the edge to delay the 
maximum allowed temperature difference and thereby the stress. On the other hand, a high heating does 
not give enough time for the heat to travel to the edge. This is supported by the theoretical background in 
chapter 2.1 and more importantly by the temperature measurement behind the frame, which is plotted 
against time on Figure 5.5. In the figure, it is clearly seen that for the lowest heating rate the temperature at 
the edge increases with time. The same trend can be seen for the few points with a longer time to cracking 
for medium heating. However, I believe that the small spread in data for high heating is caused by variating 
frame width. The temperature will be discussed more in the next sections.

rates lower than 10 kW/m2. However, for higher heating rates, the time to crack initiation is not influenced

5.4 Local temperature at crack initiation  
As expressed during the general remarks there seem to be a moderate variation in the local temperature 
measurements. A trend which is not seen at the global averaged temperature values. The original idea was 
to utilize the local temperature data as the more exact estimate of the temperature, but if the thermocouple 
placement has too big an influence this won’t be reasonable. 
Pilot experimental work (section 4.2.4) revealed that a temperature variation of 10 oC with pane height 
(increasing upwards) and 7 oC with pane width could be expected. However, this was a variation at a single 
timestep, therefore even higher variation could be expected when collecting data at the time of cracking 
from multiple tests. The final results presented in Table 10 support the observations seen during the pilot 
experiments, i.e. that temperature variation is observed across the pane. Furthermore, the new data 
consolidate the idea that measured temperature variation is not a genuine expression for points with higher 
glass temperature, but rather the expression of convective heating influence on the thermocouple itself. This 
is supported by sets of experiments with the same conditions (e.g. fracture stress) and time to crack initiation, 
but different crack location and thereby temperature (such as M15.1-2, M20.2-3, and H15.2-3).

As stated in section 4.2.3 (Reliability of developed thermocouple attachment method), I did not expect 
variations in the temperature measurements between the three levels of heating rate. If anything, I expected 
higher temperature differences for the low heating rate, since the longer duration for the critical temperature 
difference to occur would ensure the glass enough time to transport heat to the back of the thermocouple 
installation. However, I see now that I overlooked that all the pilot tests were conducted with approximately 
medium heating rate (9 kW/m2). Hence, the pilot experiments did not consider that the distance to the 
burner would vary from test to test and possibly change the readings. My theory is that the increase of 
temperature variation with the heating rate is caused primarily by moving the burner closer to the specimen. 
The reason is the temperature measurement method where a small aluminium shield is placed on the 
frontside of the pane in front of the thermocouple and when the burner gets closer it simultaneously 
increases its view factor directly to the tip of the thermocouple but also to the glass behind the aluminium 
patch. Therefore, the heat does not have to travel as far to reach the tip (see sketch Figure 5.8), and the 
shielded area is reduced when moving the burner closer to the specimen – first 5.1 % and then additionally 
6.7 %. 
Considering this theory to be valid, I have compiled two graphs where I have contracted the global 
temperature variation (see Figure 5.9), which I consider to be presenting a more correct temperature pattern.

5.6 Fallout and cracking pattern 
This section will present and discuss the results for the amount of fallout and cracking patterns (presented in 
Appendix D) for each test. It should be noted that cracking patterns and fallout are only truly valid for 
windows fully flooded with radiation, as it is the case in the experimental setup. This could be for windows 
engulfed in the smoke layer or located in front of a large radiative source that will flood the entire pane with 
radiation (e.g. a large piece of furniture or a wild fire). This is an essential remark here where internal stresses 
in the pane have a big influence on the results, while the time, temperature difference, and fracture stress 
that have already been discussed are more dependent on local conditions. 
The data show that the amount of fallout could have a small link to the shading width. Thus, Figure 5.11 (left) 
shows that there in general was seen more fallout for larger shading widths (all data is presented in scatter 
plots, because of the large spread in results). Further, Figure 5.11 (right) shows that a slow heating will 
possibly result in less probability for high fallout. However, it does not seem to have a link to the fracture 
stress (see Figure 5.12(left)), nor where on the edge the initial crack occurred (Figure 5.12 (right)), nor on 
which side the crack occurred. All in all, more data should be collected to find a statistical trend.

The combined average fallout is 16.7 % with a standard deviation of 24.4 %. This seems like a good indication 
for low fallout values. However, there is a very large variation among the tests, which as stated above can 
only vaguely be related to any specific parameters recorded here. Therefore, it is hard to assess when a fire 
exposure will lead to a high or low percentage of fallout. Consequently, this assessment should be related to 
what is more conservative for the individual design situation. The only true indication from this thesis’ tests 
is that fallout very rarely will exceed 80 % of the glazing area. Because, even though large pieces would fall, 
edge pieces would stay in the frame. Therefore, I suggest an upper limit for percentage of fallout for single 
layer windows under the same conditions could be taken as 85 %. For structural calculations where a lower 
opening factor will be more conservative it seems from this study that a much lower percentage of fallout 
should be used. This could be as low as 0 % or just a few percent. These values are consistent with the values 
obtained by Shields, et al. (2002) for single layer glass in a full-scale ISO room enclosure, here also different 
positions and sizes were tested. 
I expect that the fallout seen for my experiments will be an upper limit for amount of fallout. The reason is 
that my panes are clamped in between the frames and their ceramic wool insulation. This means that the 
pane can easily slip out from its clamped seat while, on the other hand, actual window panes would be kept 
in place by the elastic joint between pane and frame, together with the seal and spacer for insulated glass 
units. Furthermore, if this thesis’ tests were conducted on insulated glass units I believe that less fallout 
would be seen; since, some parts of the fractured glass would lean up against the subsequent pane rather 
than fall towards the fire. However, in that case also pressure build up in the gas filled cavity between the 
panes should be considered. As pressure builds up the inner pane will deflect towards the fire (Mumba, 2017) 
and thereby remove the possibility for the glass to lean inwards. Thus, for multilayer glass units I would expect 
a smaller overall upper limit area of fallout than the already mentioned 85 % and an even higher risk to see 
no fallout, which often is critical for structural elements. 
If the glass specimen was laminated with a low energy surface, the cracking should start earlier, since the 
coating will cause the window to absorb more radiation and therefore heat up faster. This is the reason that 
my tests, with non-laminated glazing, should crack after a longer time and therefore be safer in the case of a 
long fire duration impacting the structural elements. On the other hand, the coating might also have other 
==PAGE:68==
Page 58 of 95 
 
effects on the glass specimen and thereby generate a pane that stays put for a longer duration and has a 
smaller probability for fallout. This should be considered more closely in future research.  
Comparing the understanding obtained from the results of this thesis to that documented as state of practice 
for fallout (see section 1.2.1) – it is understood that this state of practice can be a decent assumption for 
situations where more ventilation is critical. However, the state of practice would result a high opening factor 
and therefore an unsafe structural fire design.  
The crack bifurcation did at all times run along the edges, except for the flawed sample H10.1, which had 
crack spread over the whole pane. A typical sample of the bifurcation pattern, taken from Appendix D, can

The results point towards the fact that a small trend of increasing shading width leading to increasing fallout

6 Conclusion 
A series of experiments were conducted to investigate the influence of a typical window frame’s shading 
width on the breakage of window glazing during different levels of fire exposure. Relevant parameters were 
identified by study of previous literature as 10, 15, 20, and 25 mm shading width, exposed to heat flux levels 
of 5, 10, and 15 kW/m2. Thereafter, I conducted several pilot experiments aimed at defining how to install 
and setup the specimens and the measurement equipment. 
The results clearly indicated that the shading width do influence the breakage of glazing. It was deduced that 
the shading width has an influence on both time to crack initiation, temperature difference between exposed 
and shaded glazing at cracking, and fracture stress. However, no clear indications of trends were found for 
the percentage of fallout. 
It is evident that for low heating rates (5 kW/m2) an increase in shading width will result in a decrease in time 
to crack initiation. For heating rates above 10 kW/m2, this trend was not found, and the shading width is seen 
to have no significant influence. The variation is caused by the timeframe to reach the temperature 
difference, which is required for cracking to occur. Thus, a longer time results in higher heat conduction to 
the back of the shading, which will postpone the critical temperature difference. If the shading width is 
smaller, the heat penetration is lower. 
 
In addition, the experiments made it clear that fracture stress is directly related to the temperature 
difference (as also expressed by theory). Furthermore, an increase in shading width will result in an increase 
in required critical temperature difference. However, this increase did not seem to follow a specific trend. It 
can be argued that the increase is likely due to be caused by the widened cool strip of glass, which is 
considered to equilibrate a higher tensional force. Nevertheless, the variations in temperature and fracture 
stress are relatively small. Thus, they will only have an influence for pre- and not post-flashover fires, where 
the heating rate will be of a significant magnitude.  
Finally, it was concluded that shading width had no clear influence on the amount of fallout or the fracture 
pattern of the glazing. The amount of fallout was highly random in nature, ranging from 0 to 81.6 %. However, 
the fracture patterns were similar throughout the tests with cracking initiating at the centre of an edge and 
traveling along all four edges. 
6.1 Future research 
The experimental research presented in this thesis gave the indication that the shading width, of typical 
window frames, influences how the window glazing will react to a fire exposure. However, additional 
research should be conducted to validate the described trends. In particular, it is suggested to conduct 
experiments with a medium-low heat flux (maybe 7.5 kW/m2), to see if the data will fit in between the low 
and medium heating rate applied by this thesis. Furthermore, tests with higher heating (perhaps 20 kW/m2) 
should be conducted to see if there truly is no effect on glazing breakage, related to shading width, for higher 
levels of heating rate. 
Besides the suggested future research mentioned above, which is directly linked to this thesis’ research, I 
also came across the following points of interest to investigate further.  
==PAGE:70==
Page 60 of 95 
 
Temperature measuring method  
 
It would be practical to investigate to a greater extend how glass temperature could be measured with higher 
accuracy – especially in situations with radiative influx. As discussed, radiative influx will influence standard 
wire TCs more than the transparent glass. Thus, one method for acquiring reliable data was developed during 
this thesis work, but the method is not perfect. I suggest utilizing sheet TCs in the future. However, two things 
are essential: 1. The sheet must be highly reflective to avoid radiative influence, 2. the geometry of the sheet 
should be minimal. I suggest a rather narrow strip in something like a donut shape. The special geometry 
must ensure that no glass underneath the sheet will vary too much in temperature, due to the shading from 
the equipment, compared to the remaining glass.  
Glass coatings influence on fallout 
 
For newer window installation types the glazing is often coated with a film that change the absorptivity. Such 
a coating will change the heat up of the glazing and thereby its time to failure. However, the film could also 
change the fallout of the pane e.g. if it restraints the broken glass. This should be investigated in the future. 
Fake muntins   
This thesis and prior experimental and theoretical research have primary been working with window panes 
framed on all four sides – i.e. without additional shading. However, in order to save money, new windows 
are often produced with a single large pane, which is aesthetically divided in smaller parts by fake muntins 
(see Figure 6.1). The fake muntins will shade central parts of the pane and result in internal stresses and it is 
for now unclear how this will affect the windows reaction to a fire.

panes with sizes of 400 · 400 mm. This trend was also observed by Mowrer (1998) and, if true, it could be 
used as input for determining whether a fire could break out of a window in one fire section and break in 
through a window in another, i.e. spread to otherwise undamaged locations.

8.1 A – Manual for the setup of glass breakage experiment 
This Appendix introduces thoughts and reflections that were put into the experimental setup, which were 
considered important to pass on. None of the paragraphs can stand alone but will act as an extension to the 
information given, in the main report (see chapter 3).  
8.1.1 H-TRIS 
The Heat Transfer Rate Inducing System (H-TRIS), owned by the fire lab at DTU, was built at the lab in the end 
of 2014. The main part of the H-TRIS is an industrial burner delivered with all parts for installation and control 
from “aem gas burners”, see Figure 8.1 (point 2.) The burner’s heat flux output, to a stationary test specimen, 
(point 1) can be controlled in two ways: first by moving the burner back and forth on an installed rail system 
(point 5) which adjusts the view factor to the specimen, second by a “WEST” P6100 regulator (point 4) that 
regulates the percentage of gas and air supply (point 3). 
The H-TRIS can deliver a huge amount of heat through its nature gas driven burner, therefore caution is 
essential when working with the machine. Thus, the H-TRIS was never turned on outside the large hood area 
and back coverage was always installed to avoid heating undesirable objects. Furthermore, DTU’s 
experimentally lab regulatory was always followed, which amongst others means that a second person 
always had to be close when working in the lab. 
DTU fire lab holds a checklist for proper use of the H-TRIS. The checklist was at all time followed during the 
experimental work.

8.1.2 Test frame  
A special test frame was built for this thesis work, see Figure 8.1 point 1. I constructed the setup by the same 
squared aluminium profiles that had been used for the H-TRIS, which resulted in a stable easy adjustable 
setup. The frame was padded with adjustable plates, which were mounted by bolts (13 mm) with a backend 
that could be inserted into the aluminium frame and twisted into a locking position. The plates were “FireFree 
Scandiboard 850” which is capable of withstanding 1000 oC and is declared to result in no toxic degasification.  
8.1.3 Glass installation and clean-up 
Before each test, all four sides of the glass specimen were marked with a pen to show the boundaries for the 
respective experimental frame width. Thereafter, the glass specimen was mounted by sliding it, from the 
top, in between the front- and back frame. The sliding technique was possible by taking aside the wedges 
and movable sliders that kept the frame in place and then rotate the top of the frame away from the setup. 
The process is displayed on Figure 8.2. After the glass was in place, I ensured that the front-frame correctly 
shaded the specimen according to the pen markings. Any corrections were made by adjusting the ceramic 
wool (with tweezers).  
 
Figure 8.2 - Installation of a new glass specimen 
After each experiment, all glass was removed from the frame by opening the wedges and rotating the sliders 
away. At all time it was ensured that no glass was left in the ceramic wool or at the supporting blocks at the 
bottom, which could possibly introduce local stress.

had also been used by earlier studies (Wang, et al., 2017a).

8.1.5 Thermocouples 
The thermocouple data is collected through a separate module (cable-box) for the acquisitions system. The 
software (BenchLink 3) lets the user choose the specific type of thermocouple (type J, K, E, T, N, B, R, or S) 
and then directly delivers the temperature (transformed from volt).  
It can be an advantage to be aware of discontinues thermocouple readings during each test run, since the 
thermocouples could move or have a bad connection. Such an error can be detected early by studying the 
live data on the PC, which is enough to stop the ongoing test and start over with just minor corrections, rather 
than a completely new setup.

8.1.7 Video equipment 
The base for video recording of the experiment lies with understanding the cracking propagation and time 
to first crack initiation. The camera will make it possible to determine where the cracking originates, and 
further to assess glass fallout and if fallout lead to subsequent events (such as further fallout or equipment 
damage). The time to initial cracking could be monitored through the timeframe of the video (time from 
burner shield removal to initial cracking). If not available, a stopwatch would be needed, which would lead 
to extra work and risk of errors. 
8.1.8 Infrared camera 
When using the infrared camera, special consideration should be taken of the cameras boundaries and how 
the photographed specimen will affect the outcome of the reading. When pointing the camera towards a 
material with a high reflectivity (like glass) a lot of the radiation captured within the lens will be of 
surrounding objects that is mirrored by the glass surface; thus, not representing the actual glass temperature. 
Furthermore, glass lets through some electromagnetic waves; therefore, the infrared camera should not be 
pointed in the direction of the burner when studying the glass. 
For this thesis, I was very careful about taking the pictures in such a direction that no reflective surfaces 
would send out higher temperatures than the glass surface itself. For instance, when studying the figures in 
the report it can be seen that the glass surface temperature is approximately 75 oC, and that the angle of 
photographing is so that the reflected material is either lab flooring or towards the surrounding room, which 
neither would have temperatures higher than this.

8.2 B – Experimental checklist 
To start up an experimental test run the checklist below should be followed. The procedures are divided into 
groups to create a better overview. 
Specimen 
1. Check specimen edge for undesirable flaws (discard if any) and mark shading width with a pen 
2. Secure specimen between front- and back frame (surface with microcracks towards ambient) 
3. Place correctly on supporting blocks (50 mm from each end) 
4. Establish correct frame width all around the glass edge according to pen markings (depending on 
test) 
5. Place thermocouples (TCs) cantered behind the aluminium tape 
6. Place edge protected TCs close to the specimen edge 
7. Ensure that TC wire tension continuously ensures the TC tip to glass bond 
8. Place shield carefully between burner and specimen  
Computer and acquisition 
1. Start PC 
2. Start acquisition machine 
3. Open video monitoring software and ensure connection 
4. Open acquisition software (BenchLink 3) and ensure that all connected inputs are loaded correctly 
(TCs are set to K-type and heat flux sensor has correct gain function) 
5. Ensure that data is saved in test specific folder 
6. Test run the acquisition software to ensure correct saving and downloaded initials 
Machine 
1. Start the large hood that the setup is placed underneath  
2. Ensure that the heat flux sensor is flush with the test specimen and connected to the pump 
3. Start pump and ensure flow 
H-TRIS 
1. Follow DTU fire labs given start-up procedures for the H-TRIS 
2. Ensure that distance to specimen is accurate 
3. Ensure that burner intensity is installed to test specified percentage  
(H-TRIS installation: 25 % - 75 cm; 100 % - 56 cm; 100 % - 42 cm) 
Start test 
1. Start H-TRIS (it takes a few seconds before the burner is ignited) 
2. Start video monitoring 
3. Start data logging at the point of burner ignition (BenchLink 3) 
4. Remove shield carefully after 150 seconds, which is counted in the logging software (remember 
personal equipment) 
 
 
==PAGE:80==
Page 70 of 95 
 
 
Terminate test 
As soon as initial cracking occurs, perform the following steps 
1. Shut down H-TRIS 
2. Take picture of cracking pattern from the frontside, so the entire specimen is visible (place camera 
lens flush to the burner, make sure that all cracks are visible) 
3. Finish photographing the entire specimen from the backside (roll up the hood wall) 
4. Sketch the crack bifurcation from the backside (register fallout and distance from edge to crack) 
5. Stop data logging approximately one minute after H-TRIS shut down and save data 
6. Subsequently terminate, and save video monitoring  
7. Make sure that saved data is in correct folder  
8. Mark and save fraction mirror for later analysis  
9. Deposit the rest of the glass

8.5 E – Second link between combined temperature variation and stress data 
If the two data points were not excluded for the stress analysis in section 1.1.4 – the picture would change 
into what is seen on Figure 8.7. Here the stress does not vary accordingly but seems rather constant across 
the shading widths. 
If so, the fracture stress can be considered as constant for all the different shading widths. This idea is 
supported by the large standard deviation at each stage of shading width. In this case, the temperature 
increase with shading width should be linked to something different than fracture stress. The only variable 
left is then the shading width. My theory is that due to the increase in width the strip of glass can equilibrate 
more tension and thereby a higher temperature differential, though still break at approximately the same 
fracture stress. My thoughts of an influence of the shading width is consistent with the work of Pagni and 
Joshi (1991), who introduced a geometric factor close to unity (see section 2.2.2). Nevertheless, a 5 mm wider 
strip (from e.g. 15 to 20 mm) combined with a constant stress would result in a stress relaxation of 
approximately 9.3 MPa.
