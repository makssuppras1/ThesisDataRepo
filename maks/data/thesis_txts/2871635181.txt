Abstract
This thesis primarily investigates forward error correction (FEC) techniques for
satellite communication systems, with a particular focus on Low­Density Parity­
Check (LDPC) codes defined in the DVB­S2 standard. It begins by introducing the
fundamental theory of linear block codes and the evolution toward LDPC codes
with sparse graphical structures. A range of LDPC decoding algorithms is an­
alyzed, including the Sum­Product Algorithm (SPA), Min­Sum algorithm and its
variants, as well as layered scheduling strategies, with a special emphasis on
their applicability to high­throughput scenarios. A custom LDPC decoder was im­
plemented and evaluated against MATLAB’s built­in decoding function, validating
both its accuracy and computational efficiency.
Extensive throughput and early stop simulations were conducted for LDPC­based
decoding over LEO satellite downlinks. To address the rapid signal­to­noise ratio
(SNR) variation inherent in low Earth orbit (LEO) satellite communication, this the­
sis proposes an Adaptive Coding and Modulation (ACM) scheme. The scheme
is optimized using spectral efficiency–based selection methods to reduce sys­
tem complexity while maintaining high throughput. Simulation results under a
simplified LEO channel model demonstrate that the optimized 6­modcod scheme
reduces decoding iterations and frame error rates significantly, while preserving
over 94% of the total system throughput.
Moreover, this work proposes an early termination strategy based on the distribu­
tion of log­likelihood ratio (LLR) values during decoding. As an alternative mech­
anism to conventional parity­check–based stopping, a logistic regression–based
machine learning model is introduced to support adaptive early stopping deci­
sions.
Keywords: LDPC coding, DVB­S2 standard, Sum­Product Algorithm, early ter­
mination strategy, adaptive coding and modulation (ACM), LEO satellite commu­
nication

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Transmitting signals from space to the ground over hundreds of kilometers has
always been a highly challenging task. Nowadays, with DVB­S2 (Digital Video
Broadcasting – Satellite – Second Generation), a satellite digital broadcasting
transmission standard developed by the DVB Project (Digital Video Broadcasting
Project) and released in 2005, it is possible to transmit HD/Ultra HD video content
at rates of several hundred Mbps. After nearly 20 years of development, DVB­S2
has become the mainstream standard for satellite broadcasting and television,
professional satellite communications, military, and emergency communications.
The DVB­S2 standard was originally designed for GEO (Geostationary Earth Or­
bit) satellites, and currently, GEO satellites continue to serve as the primary plat­
forms for the broad deployment of DVB­S2/S2X technologies. However, the geo­
stationary orbit is a limited resource, and its orbital altitude of approximately 35,786
kilometers results in latency exceeding 100 ms, as well as higher susceptibility to
weather­related interference, such as rain attenuation. To provide better quality
of service and higher data rates (e.g., for Internet access), some Low Earth Or­
bit (LEO) satellite constellations have also adopted elements of the DVB­S2/S2X
standard.
Unlike GEO satellites that maintain a relatively fixed position with respect to the
Earth’s surface and require minimal ground antenna tracking, LEO constellations
are characterized by the high velocity of their satellites and orbital altitudes typi­
cally ranging from 500 to 2,000 kilometers. This leads to frequent handovers and
dynamic variations in antenna pointing angles. Consequently, LEO communica­
tion systems must support rapid handovers, timely link recovery, and optimized
routing capabilities.
To meet these demands, DVB­S2 systems employ Adaptive Coding and Modu­
lation (ACM) and Variable Coding and Modulation (VCM) techniques. The DVB­
S2 standard offers 28 combinations of modulation schemes and FEC code rates
for standard­length frames (64,800 bits). The core idea of ACM/VCM is to dy­
namically adjust these coding rates and modulation schemes based on real­time
parameter feedback, estimations, or lookup­table methods, with the goal of max­
imizing system capacity and fully utilizing every possible link condition.
In recent years, ACM techniques have become a hot topic in research. Numerous
studies have focused on improving throughput performance using reinforcement
learning methods. In [1], neural networks and Q­learning­based AMC techniques
were respectively applied to enable fast and accurate real­time scheme selection
based on SNR feedback. In [2], Q­learning was used to realize dynamic scheme
adaptation in response to time­varying satellite downlink channel models.
However, real­time feedback is not always available in practical engineering sce­
narios. Some simpler and energy­efficient systems do not communicate frequently

with satellites and often have hardware limitations that prevent them from perform­
ing complex machine learning computations and large­scale decoding operations.
Based on the above background, the main focus of this project is to develop a
fast, reliable, and energy­efficient method for DVB­S2 communication between
LEO satellites and ground stations. The proposed method selects a modulation
and FEC scheme based on the time­dependent characteristics of the LEO satel­
lite downlink channel and can perform adaptive coding and modulation with little
or no reliance on real­time feedback. Furthermore, the method is designed to
save computational resources through an early­stopping strategy, which halts the
LDPC decoding process before completing the full number of iterations theoreti­
cally required for decoding every frame. Frames with higher error counts, which
may result from randomness, are instead corrected by the BCH error correction
code embedded in the DVB­S2 system.
This thesis is structured as follows: Chapter 2 introduces the theoretical foun­
dation of LDPC coding and decoding algorithms, along with a detailed review of
the DVB­S2 standard. Chapter 3 presents the proposed methods, an early termi­
nation strategy based on LLR distribution, and an optimized MODCOD selection
algorithm tailored for LEO satellite channels. Chapter 4 evaluates the proposed
strategies through simulations based on a simplified orbital model, analyzing de­
coding performance, throughput, and modulation switching performance. Finally,
Chapter 5 concludes the thesis and discusses directions for future research, such
as applying machine learning tools and more realistic SNR estimation models.

Research background
2.1
LDPC code and relevant decode algorithms
LDPC(Low Density Parity Check) code is a type of linear block code originally pro­
posed by Gallager[3] in his doctoral thesis in the 1960s. LDPC code has demon­
strated outstanding error correction performance very close to the theoretical max­
imum, known as the Shannon limit[4]. The property of LDPC codes that allows
their computations to be efficiently implemented in parallel structure has signifi­
cantly broadened their applicability, leading them to become the channel coding
standard in a wide range of advanced communication systems.
Since the 1990s, following the emergence of Turbo codes, LDPC has attracted
significant attention from both academia and industry. Classical LDPC codes ex­
hibit excellent performance for long code blocks and offer relatively low decod­
ing complexity, break performance records of approaching Shannon limit several
times.
As a result, LDPC was first adopted in the digital television standard (DVB­S2),
and later became an optional technology in the WiMAX (IEEE 802.16, 2004) stan­
dard and wireless LANs (WLAN), including high­throughput Wi­Fi based on IEEE
802.11n (2008). After years of research and development, thanks to its outstand­
ing capabilities, LDPC was finally included in October 2016 as the channel coding
solution for enhanced Mobile Broadband (eMBB) services in the 5G NR standard,
in the form of QC­LDPC (Quasi­Cyclic LDPC).
The matrix structure of LDPC codes inherently introduces interleaving proper­
ties, and their use of iterative decoding yields a dramatic performance improve­
ment over traditional linear block codes. Because LDPC codes are fundamentally
based on the original linear block code principles, they rest on a powerful math­
ematical foundation, effectively synthesizing graph theory, combinatorics, proba­
bility theory, matrix theory, algebra, geometry, algebraic number theory, and even
Riemannian geometry. The most prominent feature of LDPC codes is that their
parity check matrix is sparse matrix. Before discussing the parity­check matrix of
LDPC codes, first we have a review how a basic linear block code works.
2.1.1

2.1.2
LDPC code
The linear block codes introduced in the previous section typically involve all or
most bits in each parity­check equation. While this provides promising error cor­
rection performance, it also makes such codes difficult to scale to complex sys­
tems with large data flow.
Low­Density Parity­Check (LDPC) codes[3] were proposed to solve this problem.
LDPC codes are a class of linear block codes characterized by a key feature: their
parity­check matrix H is sparse, meaning that most of its elements are zero, with
only a small number of non­zero entries. This sparse structure significantly re­
duces the complexity of message passing during decoding, enabling efficient soft­
decision decoding using iterative algorithms such as Min­sum or the sum­product
algorithm (SPA). Therefore, LDPC codes can be regarded as a new generation
of coding schemes developed within the linear block code framework, enhanced
through sparsity and graph­based design principles.
In 1981, Tanner[6] developed the idea of using graphical models to represent
codewords, mapping the LDPC parity check matrix onto a bipartite graph known
as a Tanner graph. This approach provides an intuitive way to illustrate how the
LDPC parity­check matrix operates. For example, the parity check matrix H of

2.1.3
LDPC decode methods
The earliest LDPC decoders use only binary decisions. In Gallager A[3], each vari­
able node flips its bit if the number of unsatisfied parity­checks exceeds a fixed
threshold. Gallager B[3] refines this by using two thresholds: one for flipping and
a higher one for retaining the current bit, reducing oscillations and improving con­
vergence. These bit­flipping schemes boast very low complexity since operations
are simple counts and comparisons. But this scheme achieve modest error rate
performance, making them suitable for low power or high throughput scenarios
where soft information is too costly.
In recent years with the vast development of digital and computer technology, the
cost of large scale computation keeps dropping. Make it possible to investigate
and apply soft decision decoding algorithms. Soft decision algorithms exploit the
analog reliability of downlink, delivering substantial performance gains at the cost
of higher computational load.
SPA algorithm

algorithm. It implements belief propagation on the Tanner graph, iteratively ex­

graph, using local computations to estimate the posterior log­likelihood ratio (LLR)
of each bit. At each check nodes, messages are combined via the “box­plus” op­
eration; at each variable nodes, they are summed with the channel log likelihood
ratio.

2.1.4
Scheduling Strategies
Another crucial aspect aside from the choice of decoding algorithm in LDPC de­
coding is how to arrange the large number of parallel node update operations. One
of the most commonly used schemes is the flooding schedule, which updates all
variable­to­check and check­to­variable messages in parallel during each itera­
tion.
In a flooding schedule, each decoding iteration consists of two full phases:
• Variable node update: Every variable node computes outgoing messages to
all neighboring check nodes based on the most recent incoming messages
and its channel information.
8
FEC Coding for Satellite Communication System
==PAGE:17==
• Check node update: Then, each check node simultaneously updates mes­
sages to its connected variable nodes using the updated variable node mes­
sages.
This schedule ensures full parallelism and is straightforward to implement in soft­
ware and hardware. However, it suffers from slower convergence compared to
more refined scheduling techniques, as newly updated information from one part
of the graph cannot influence other parts until the next iteration. Despite this,
flooding remains a standard baseline due to its simplicity and ease of implemen­
tation in vectorized or parallel hardware architectures.
To improve the trade off between decoding speed and hardware efficiency, a class
of parallel LDPC decoding algorithms known as layered scheduling has been pro­
posed. The basic idea is to divide the set of parity check equations (i.e., the rows
of the parity­check matrix) into disjoint groups, or say layers. Each layer is de­
coded sequentially, but messages within each layer are updated using standard
algorithms such as SPA or its low complexity approximations like MSA and its
variants.
Compared to flooding schedules, layered decoding reorganizes the message pass­
ing sequence such that variable node messages updated within a layer are im­
mediately propagated to subsequent check node updates in the same iteration.
With an appropriate choice of layer ordering, this approach typically leads to faster
convergence, often reducing the required number of global iterations by up to half.
A particularly efficient implementation of layered decoding arises in the case of
quasi­cyclic LDPC (QC­LDPC)[7] codes, which are widely adopted in communi­
cation standards such as Wi­Fi (IEEE 802.11n/ac/ax), WiMAX, and 5G NR. In
QC­LDPC codes. As an important subclass of structured LDPC codes, address
practical limitations of randomly constructed LDPC matrices by organizing the
parity­check matrix into a grid of equal­sized square blocks. Each block is ei­
ther a cyclically shifted identity matrix or an all­zero matrix. This regular structure
facilitates memory­friendly storage and addressing, significantly reducing both
encoding and decoding complexity. In particular, QC­LDPC codes with repeat­
accumulate structures can support fast encoding with linear complexity.
In QC­LDPC implementations, the large parity­check matrix is typically derived by
lifting a small base matrix through circulant permutation, where the lifting factor Z
determines the block size. This leads to a natural partitioning of the matrix into
Z submatrices, allowing decoding to proceed in a layer­by­layer fashion. Each
layer corresponds to a set of check nodes connected to specific variable nodes
in a highly structured pattern. The official DVB­S2 specification[10] explicitly de­
fines the LDPC code structure as “structured parity­check matrices with circulant
permutation blocks,” generating the parity matrix in a similar way.
Formally, in each layer ℓ, only check nodes corresponding to that layer are acti­

2.2
DVB­S2 standard
DVB­S2[10] (Digital Video Broadcasting Satellite Second Generation) was offi­
cially introduced by the European Telecommunications Standards Institute (ETSI)
in 2005 as the successor to the DVB­S system. It represents a significant evo­
lution in satellite communication, transitioning from basic digital broadcasting to
a more efficient and robust transmission framework. The standard incorporates
advanced techniques in modulation, channel coding, framing structure, and link
adaptation, making it suitable for diverse applications such as television broad­
casting, satellite broadband, and professional data services.
Compared to DVB­S, DVB­S2 introduces a wider range of modulation schemes
including QPSK, 8PSK, 16APSK, and 32APSK, paired with flexible forward error
correction (FEC) coding rates ranging from 1/4 to 9/10 as shown in fig. 2.3. Under
the standard frame length of 64,800 bits, a total of 28 modulation and coding
(MODCOD) combinations are defined, enabling the system to adapt to a wide
range of link qualities. The FEC scheme use low density parity check (LDPC)
codes as inner codes, combined with outer BCH (Bose Chaudhuri Hocquenghem)
codes, ensures excellent error performance approaching the Shannon limit.
Figure 2.3: System configurations and application areas[10]
To further enhance adaptability in dynamic channel environments, DVB­S2 sup­
ports both Variable Coding and Modulation (VCM) and Adaptive Coding and Mod­
ulation (ACM). ACM, in particular, enables real­time adjustment of the modulation
and coding parameters based on feedback from the receiver about the instanta­
neous channel quality (e.g., SNR). This mechanism significantly improves spectral
efficiency and system throughput, and particularly effective in scenarios where
multiple users share satellite resources or where ground station link conditions
vary frequently.
DVB­S2 is commonly deployed in Ku­band (12–18 GHz) and Ka­band (26–40
GHz) satellite links. The Ka­band, which offers wider bandwidth, has become

BCH code
In digital communications, BCH codes form a classic family of cyclic codes, mean­

its generator polynomial g(x), which serves as the divisor during encoding and
determines the block length, dimension, and error­correction capability. A simple

3.2
Early stop strategy design
When performing LDPC decoding, the BER (bit error rate) of each received frame
varies even under the same SNR conditions due to the randomness of channel
interference. As a result, the number of iterations required to decode each frame
is always changing. A common approach is to set a maximum number of itera­
tions and in the DVB­S2 system this maxiter is typically set to 50, which basically
ensures that decoding failures do not occur within the operational range of each
modcod. Another approach is to verify decoding success by multiplying the LLR
hard decision outputs with the parity­check matrix H. If the result is a zero matrix, it
indicates there is no error patterns remaining, and the iteration decoding process
can be terminated early.
fig. 3.2 illustrate the average number of iterations required to complete decoding
under QPSK with code rate 1/4 and 32APSK with code rate 9/10 (max iteration set
at 100). It can be clearly observed that as the SNR approaches the limit of error
correction, the curve converges increasingly faster. This phenomenon is more
evident with higher­order modulation and higher code rates. Therefore, imple­
menting early stopping in higher SNR regions can significantly enhance decoding
speed and reduce computation time.

3.3
Adaptive modcod scheme design and
optimization
DVB­S2 standard provides a wide range of combination of modulations and code
rates(fig. 2.3), making it possible to transmit data under different channel quality.
Due to the orbital characteristics of LEO, the SNR fluctuates rapidly during a single
flyby. Therefore, developing adaptive schemes to maximize transmission speed
and quality has become a hot research topic.
With the development of computer science, use machine learning tools for design­
ing ACM scheme is a popular approach. Zhou et al. [1] use deep neural network
to approximate Q­learning, enabling accurate selection of the optimal modcod
based on a stochastic SNR model. A further advancement is made in the work
by Pasquevich et al. [2], where the Markov Decision Process (MDP) is integrated
alongside Q­learning. This allows the model to take the distance from the satellite
to the ground station as a time­dependent function d(t) into account, effectively
turning the system into a memory­aware model that can more comprehensively
handle the entire flyby communication window.
However, it is important to note that in practical applications, the ’optimal’ scheme
FEC Coding for Satellite Communication System
25
==PAGE:34==
obtained through methods like those mentioned above is often not directly usable.
One major reason is the requirement in limiting the system complexity: frequent
switching between modcods can lead to repeated decoder resets. Furthermore,
if the SNR thresholds of two modcods are too close, channel fluctuations or esti­
mation errors can trigger excessive or even incorrect switching. This is also one
of the reasons why adaptive schemes benefit from memory: rapidly switching be­
tween multiple modcods over a short time span is difficult to implement and leads
to lower efficiency. The fig. 3.6 below illustrates the throughput performance of
modcods from DVB­S2 standard (with code length of 64,800 and maximum of
50 decoding iterations) within the SNR range used in this work (and all relevant
DVB­S2 modcods in fig. A.1 appendix).

In this project, the satellite operates in the S and X bands (below 12 GHz), which
differ from the more widely studied Ka band (around 30 GHz) in recent research.
Due to the lower frequency, the impact of weather conditions or rain attenuation
is relatively minor, and path loss is dominated by free space propagation[15].
Another important source of interference is multipath propagation. However, con­
sidering that the receiving environments for such systems are generally unob­
structed, the received signal is primarily composed of a strong line­of­sight (LOS)
component, with only a limited number of multipath components likely to be gen­
erated near the receiver. Moreover, due to the relatively narrow bandwidth, these
multipath components are difficult to resolve. Based on these considerations, it
is reasonable to simulate the system using a simplified ideal circular orbit model
with zero inclination with path loss modeled solely as a function of distance.

to investigate the soft information characteristics at the bit level in a forward er­

distribution of log­likelihood ratios (LLRs) after transmission over a noisy channel.
As shown in table 4.4 QPSK was employed as the modulation scheme, and a
range of LDPC code rates including 1/2, 3/5, 2/3, 3/4, and 4/5 was selected for
evaluation.

In the simulation, a pre­trained machine learning model is employed to assess
whether the LDPC decoder has likely converged, based on the distribution of log­
likelihood ratio (LLR) values derived from soft demodulation. For each decoding
instance, a normalized histogram of the LLR values is computed and used to
construct a feature vector comprising 20 bin probabilities along with the corre­
sponding signal­to­noise ratio (SNR) and the current iteration count. This feature
vector is passed to the trained model, which outputs a convergence probability. If

==PAGE:46==
the predicted probability exceeds a threshold of 0.5, decoding is assumed to have
converged, and the process can be halted early. To evaluate the model’s reliability
and efficiency, the final decoding outcome—either using soft­decision decoding or
conventional hard­decision decoding at the same iteration—is compared against
the original transmitted bits to determine the bit error rate (BER).
This convergence aware approach allows decoding to be dynamically terminated
before reaching the maximum iteration count, potentially reducing computational
load without sacrificing decoding performance. The simulation is conducted across
a range of SNR values and code rates (1/2 to 4/5) of QPSK, and the process is re­
peated over multiple frames to statistically validate the model’s predictive behavior
and its impact on decoding throughput and accuracy.
Regrettably, the model’s parameter tuning is still in progress, and the performance
remains unstable. The full integration will be addressed in future work.

Conclusion and future works
5.1
Conclusion
This thesis focuses on forward error correction (FEC) techniques for satellite com­
munication systems, with particular emphasis on the decoding algorithms of Low­
Density Parity­Check (LDPC) codes defined in the DVB­S2 standard and the de­
sign of adaptive coding and modulation (ACM) schemes. Chapter 2 provides a
comprehensive review of the theoretical foundations and development of LDPC
codes, followed by a comparative analysis of mainstream decoding methods,
including the Sum­Product Algorithm (SPA), Min­Sum Algorithm (MSA) and its
variants, as well as layered scheduling techniques. Experimental validation and
benchmarking were also conducted using MATLAB’s built­in toolbox functions,
which clarified the underlying encoding structures and calling mechanisms. Sub­
sequent chapters present extensive simulations based on real SNR variation mod­
els to evaluate LDPC decoding performance, early stopping strategies, and ACM
scheme optimization.
In Chapter 3, the research explores three core technical paths in depth: the design
and evaluation of an LDPC decoder, the development of early stopping strategies,
and the construction and optimization of an adaptive modcod scheme. For LDPC
decoding, the SPA was selected as the foundational structure, and a custom de­
coder was implemented using sparse matrix representations. Special attention
was paid to the localized message exchange between variable and check nodes.
The decoder was step­by­step compared to MATLAB’s ldpcDecode function. Al­
though the logical flow and message updates are consistent, the MATLAB imple­
mentation showed superior speed, and was thus adopted for large­scale simula­
tions.
Regarding early stopping strategies, a standard BCH based method was first im­
plemented and reduce the average iterations from 11.56 to 9.04 as shown in ta­
ble 4.3. The convergence behavior under different SNR levels, modulation types,
and code rates was analyzed. Inspired by the concept of Sign­Changing Rate
(SCR) proposed in the literature, the distribution of LLR values across iterations
was tracked and modeled. It was observed that as SNR increases, LLR values
tend to concentrate toward the edges, motivating the use of statistical learning for
classification. A logistic regression model was ultimately constructed, taking as
input the LLR histogram, SNR, and iteration index, and outputting the probability
of convergence, forming a data­driven alternative early stopping mechanism.
In the design of the ACM scheme, a lookup­table­based strategy was first con­
structed based on simulated throughput curves. The issue of high error rates near
ambiguous switching zones was then analyzed, and a spectral­efficiency­based
filtering method from the literature was used for pruning. A brute­force search was
employed to select the optimal subset of modcods and fine­tune switching thresh­
olds, ultimately forming a compact scheme with only six modcods that balances
FEC Coding for Satellite Communication System
39
==PAGE:48==
performance and complexity. Notably, the optimized 6 modcod scheme achieves
an average throughput of 1.74, which is over 94% of the full modcod configuration,
while reducing the average decoding iteration count from 18.28 to 11.56. In addi­
tion, the probability redistribution rule for modcods with minimal average spectral
contribution was modified, making the scheme better suited to real SNR variation
patterns and avoiding the excessive bias toward lower modcod levels observed
in conventional approaches.
This chapter not only establishes the methodological foundation for the simula­
tions and experiments, but also demonstrates a gradual transition from standard
mechanisms to data­driven optimization and structured improvement. It highlights
the integration between theoretical concepts and practical engineering implemen­
tation.
And the key contribution made in this project is: Based on SPA, a custom LDPC
soft­decision decoder was implemented and its performance was benchmarked
against MATLAB’s built­in decoder, confirming its accuracy and structural sound­
ness. The joint decoding structure of LDPC and BCH codes under the DVB­S2
standard was analyzed, and the error correction capabilities of different modcod
settings were evaluated. The maximum iteration­based early stopping strategy
was investigated via simulation. A novel modcod scheme was proposed that re­
duces the number of active modcods to improve system stability and lower com­
plexity. The proposed scheme mitigated high BER issues during modcod tran­
sitions and achieved 94% of the original throughput while significantly reducing
decoding iterations and frame errors. Additionally, a logistic regression–based
early stop strategy using LLR histograms was developed and implemented, offer­
ing a lightweight and scalable alternative to conventional stopping criteria in LDPC
decoding.
5.2
Difficulty and future works
This study faces several key challenges. On one hand, most existing research
efforts have focused on higher frequency bands (such as the Ka­band) and their
associated channel interference models. In contrast, relatively few works have
investigated the joint optimization of FEC and ACM schemes for LEO satellite
communication systems operating in the S/X bands. On the other hand, accurate
modeling of the instantaneous signal­to­noise ratio (SNR) in LEO satellite links is
difficult due to the lack of standardized reference data, making it challenging to
tailor decoder behavior and modcod switching strategies to real world conditions.
Furthermore, maintaining a balance between system complexity and decoding
precision remains a significant engineering difficulty.
Future research may proceed along the following directions:
• Channel modeling enhancement: Extend the current channel model to in­
corporate commonly observed interference effects such as multipath prop­
agation, shadow fading, and rain attenuation, thereby improving the realism
of the simulation. Additionally, collect real­world SNR variation data from
satellite links to enable empirical evaluation and training of adaptive mod­
els.
40

• Machine learning integration: On the basis of advanced channel models,
introduce more sophisticated machine learning methods such as deep neu­
ral networks and sequential models. By leveraging LLR distribution fea­
tures and historical decoding information, it becomes possible to predict
satellite orbital dynamics, transmission power variations, and channel condi­
tion trends—ultimately enabling the construction of a more generalized and
adaptive ACM control system.
• Improved early stopping techniques: Conduct in­depth investigations into
the role of soft­decision outputs and high­order statistical features in early
termination mechanisms, with the goal of improving robustness and accu­
racy in low­SNR regions.
