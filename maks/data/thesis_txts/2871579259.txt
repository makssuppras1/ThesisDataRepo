Abstract
This work investigates the application of Vision Language Models for detecting physi-
cal presentation attacks and digital morphing attacks in biometric systems. Focusing
on open-source models under 8 billion parameters, the first systematic framework for
quantitative evaluation of VLMs in security-critical scenarios through in-context learning
techniques is established. The experiments demonstrate that VLMs achieve competitive
performance in presentation attack detection task, outperforming some of the traditional
CNNs without resource exhaustive training, and imply insightful trajectory for differential
morphing attack detection challenge. The results validate VLMs as promising tools for
improving generalization in attack detection.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Facial recognition has become one of the most common methods of identification in mod-
ern society, with authentication processes playing a crucial role in numerous daily activ-
ities. The critical nature of these processes, especially in security and access control,
drives researchers to continuously improve the reliability and security of such systems.
Recent research in biometric systems has made significant strides in addressing fraud-
ulent practices. However, as detection methods improve, attack techniques become in-
creasingly sophisticated. Attacks on face recognition systems can be broadly divided into
physical and digital approaches. In the physical realm, presentation attacks are the most
prevalent, utilizing various presentation attack instruments (PAIs) to deceive recognition
systems. Digital attacks have become equally concerning, with advanced tools like deep-
fakes making it easier to create highly convincing fake images.
This study focuses on Presentation Attack Detection (PAD) and Morphing Attack Detec-
tion (MAD), the latter being a digital technique that could potentially allow multiple individ-
uals to use a single passport [1].
Traditionally, deep learning models have been the primary defense against such attacks.
While these models perform exceptionally well in scenarios for which they have been
trained for, they often struggle to adapt to different types of attacks or varying environ-
mental conditions [2]. Current detection systems require substantial amounts of training
data to achieve reliable performance, yet biometric data collection faces significant chal-
lenges including privacy concerns and the logistical difficulties of capturing diverse attack
scenarios under controlled conditions [3, 4]. This limitation poses a significant challenge in
real-world applications, especially when the nature of an attack is unknown. The process
of developing specialized models is time-consuming, expensive, and requires extensive
data collection.
To overcome the above limitations, the emergence of vision language models (VLMs)
offers a promising alternative. These models, trained on vast datasets, can handle com-
plex questions and adapt to various scenarios [5], suggesting potential for addressing
generalization challenges in biometric attack detection. While preliminary investigations
have explored VLMs for PAD [6, 7] through qualitative assessments, these studies lacked
rigorous quantitative evaluation using standardized biometric metrics and were limited to
specific attack scenarios. Notably, no existing research has investigated VLM capabilities
for MAD tasks.
Following study bridges these critical gaps by conducting the first comprehensive quan-
titative analysis of open-source VLMs under 8 billion parameters for both physical (pre-
sentation) and digital (morphing) attack detection. By adapting models with contextual
knowledge injected only during inference process, in-context learning (ICL) is utilized, in
particular different inference techniques:
• Zero-shot learning, which involves evaluating the model’s ability to correctly clas-
sify attacks without any prior examples, relying solely on its pre-existing knowledge
[8],

attack type, testing its ability to generalize from minimal data,
• Few-shot learning extending previous concept by providing the model with limited

By comparing these approaches, the aim is to show the possibility of further improving

1.2
Research questions
Based on the motivation for the project, the following research questions have been for-
mulated:
• RQ1: How effective are SOTA VLMs in detecting digital (morphing) and physical
(presentation) attacks?
• RQ2: Can inference techniques mitigate the high costs associated with finetuning
models for PAD and MAD?
• RQ3: Are VLMs a viable solution for improving the generalization capabilities of
models in cross-domain attack scenarios?
• RQ4: How is the trade off between efficiency and detection performance of VLMs
on PAD and MAD?

Fundamentals
2.1
Biometrics
National and international organizations have long relied on biometrics, the unique phys-
ical or behavioral traits of individuals, such as fingerprints, iris patterns, facial features,
or voice, for critical legal and corporate operations. Recent advancements in technol-
ogy, along with decreasing implementation costs, have expanded the use of biometrics
to address a broader range of applications. Today, identification through biometrics is an
integral part of daily life, with notable examples including:
• Security and access control - Fingerprint and face recognition is commonly used
to authenticate devices like laptops or smartphones.
• Law enforcement - Criminal identification relies on global biometric databases to
match suspects with recorded data.
• Border control - Passports equipped with biometric data and automated border
gates facilitate swift and accurate identity verification for international travelers.
• Financial and governmental sectors - Access to resources is granted based on
successful authorization of individuals based on their biometrics.
2.1.1
Biometric Systems
Digital frameworks employing biometric characteristic for the identification or verification
of individual, known as biometric systems, are composed of four fundamental modules:
sensor, feature extractor, matcher, and decision rule [10]. The sensor module captures
the raw biometric input, such as a fingerprint or facial image, and converts it into a digital
format for further processing. This data is then passed to the feature extraction module,
where essential features are identified and isolated from the raw input, creating a biometric
template while filtering out noise. The matcher module uses this template to compare the
captured data against stored records in the system’s database. By applying matching
algorithms, it generates a match score that represents the similarity between the input
and stored templates. Finally, the decision module evaluates this match score against a
predefined security threshold. Based on the comparison, it determines whether to accept
or reject the identity of the individual. The overview of biometric system pipeline can be
found in Fig 2.1.
Figure 2.1: General framework of biometric systems.

Based on application context, a biometric system can work in two distinct modes: verifi-
cation and identification [11]. In biometric verification, the system performs a one-to-one
comparison, where the biometric input is matched against a single stored reference tem-
plate to confirm a claimed identity. This mode is commonly used in scenarios such as
unlocking smartphones or accessing secure systems, where the user explicitly identifies
themselves before authentication. In contrast, biometric identification involves a one-to-
many comparison, where the biometric input is matched against all stored templates in a
database to determine an individual’s identity without prior claims. Identification can be
further divide into two distinct approaches: closed-set and open-set. The system functions
under the assupmtion that the person’s biometric information is already in the database
when using closed-set identification. The objective is to precisely identify the input data
inside a specified dataset by matching it with one of the known entries. On the other
hand, open-set identification faces a more challenging problem since the system must
account for the possibility that the user’s biometric data may not be in the database. In
order to properly identify the subject, it must first determine whether they are included in
the dataset and if not, it must conclude that they are unknown.
2.1.2
Vulnerabilities
Unlike traditional authentication systems, biometric systems eliminate the need for indi-
viduals to remember passwords or carry physical tokens like ID cards or tags. While this
reduces the risk of repudiation attacks, biometric systems can still be vulnerable to various
forms of manipulation and deception.
According to [12], biometric systems are vulnerable to attacks at eight critical points, which
can be broadly categorized into direct and indirect attacks. Direct attacks refer only to
sensor attacks, that does not require any expert knowledge and involve presenting fake
biometric traits (e.g., synthetic fingerprints or facial images) to the sensor to bypass au-
thentication. These attacks exploit the sensor’s inability to distinguish between real and
artificial traits.
Indirect attacks, on the other hand, target the system’s internal components and require
knowledge of its operation. These include intercepting communication channels to replay
or tamper with biometric data, compromising feature extraction and matcher modules, al-
tering or stealing templates from the database, and overriding decisions during transmis-
sion between components. While direct attacks exploit physical vulnerabilities, indirect
attacks challenge the digital and logical security of the system, making comprehensive
protection a critical aspect of biometric system design.
Attacks requiring minimal technical knowledge of the system often target the sensor, ex-
ploiting its vulnerabilities through simple methods such as using a printed face mask or
synthetic fingerprint. Similarly, threats arising from the manipulation of the communication
channel between the sensor and the feature extraction module present significant risks.
Commonly referred to in the literature as replay attacks, these breaches involve intercept-
ing transmitted authentication or access control data and reusing it to gain unauthorized
access or produce an unintended outcome. For instance, an adversary might intercept a
facial recognition system’s transmission of raw biometric data and replay it to the feature
extraction module to bypass the authentication process.
2.1.3
Presentation Attacks
Given the vulnerabilities outlined earlier, it becomes essential to focus on specific types
of attacks that pose significant threats to biometric systems. Among these, attacks on
face recognition systems are particularly concerning due to the widespread use of facial
biometrics in both physical and digital security applications. Presentation attack instru-

ments include tools or materials designed to replicate or imitate a legitimate user’s facial
features. Common examples are photographs, video replays, 3D-printed masks, or sili-
cone molds. An attacker might use a high-quality printout of a facial image or a carefully
crafted mask to bypass the system’s authentication process.
2.1.4
Morphing Attacks
Morphing attacks have been identified as one of the most critical threats to biometric sys-
tems by the National Institute of Standards and Technology (NIST) [13]. These attacks
exploit the vulnerabilities in biometric enrollment and verification processes, particularly
in systems that rely on facial recognition. By creating a morphed image that blends the
features of multiple individuals, attackers can successfully deceive systems into recogniz-
ing multiple people as a single identity. This poses a significant security risk in sensitive
domains like border control, where accurate identity verification is crucial.
One scenario where morphing attacks pose a significant threat is in the context of border
control. Although the European Union has established guidelines requiring live capture
during the enrollment process [14], many European countries still allow individuals apply-
ing for passports to submit a pre-captured single photograph instead. If this photograph
is a morphed image, created by combining the facial features of two or more individuals,
it becomes the reference image stored in the passport database. During border control, a
live facial capture of the traveler, bonafide assumed, is compared to the reference image in
the passport. The morphed reference image may match both individuals who contributed
to the morph, potentially allowing unauthorized persons to cross borders undetected.
The detection and mitigation of morphing attacks are addressed through two main tasks:
Single and Differential Morphing Attack Detection.
Single Morphing Attack Detection (SMAD)
This approach is applied during the enrollment phase, where individuals submit pho-
tographs for official documents such as passports. The system analyzes the submitted
image to determine whether it has been artificially morphed. SMAD plays a vital role in
ensuring that compromised biometric templates do not enter the system, thereby safe-
guarding the integrity of the enrollment process.
Differential Morphing Attack Detection (DMAD)
This task is concerned with detecting morphing attacks at the time of identity verification,
such as during border control. Here, the live capture of the traveler at the gate serves
as a trusted comparison image. By comparing this live capture to the reference image
stored in the system, DMAD aims to identify discrepancies indicative of morphing. [15]
2.2
Vision Language Models
Vision language models are advanced multimodal AI systems that integrate visual and tex-
tual modalities. By combining a large language model (LLM) with a vision encoder, VLMs
enable an LLM to interpret visual data, such as images and videos, alongside textual in-
formation. This fusion allows them to process diverse inputs and provide sophisticated
textual interpretations and analyses. They are pretrained on extensive datasets compris-
ing image-text pairs, enabling them to perform a wide range of tasks, from visual question
answering to summarization and document parsing, without requiring additional training
for every new application [16, 17]. Due to their design, VLMs are able to understand and
execute instructions provided in natural language. This aspect makes them suitable to
tackle not only traditional computer vision problems, but also generative tasks in which
they can act autonomously [18].

The impact of VLMs becomes apparent when comparing them with prior SOTA mod-
els used for computer vision, which were mostly built on Convolutional Neural Networks
(CNN) [19]. Traditional CNN-based models were designed for narrowly defined tasks,
such as distinguishing between specific object classes [20] or performing optical character
recognition [16]. Expanding these models’ capabilities required collecting large, labeled
datasets and retraining, a process that is both resource-intensive and time-consuming.
Additionally, these models lacked the ability to understand natural language, limiting their
flexibility. In contrast, VLMs combine the foundational strengths of vision encoders with
LLMs, achieving strong zero-shot performance across various tasks. They eliminate the
need for task-specific retraining by leveraging natural language prompts, enabling users
to adapt them to a wide range of scenarios effortlessly.
2.2.1
Architecture
VLMs consist of three core components:
• Vision Encoder - Model, commonly built on transformer architectures, trained on
large-scale datasets containing both images and text. Through the use of atten-
tion mechanisms, these models effectively learn to correlate visual inputs with their
corresponding textual representations [21].
• Projector - A set of layers that maps the vision encoder outputs into a shared space,
often as tokens representing visual information, which further enables effective com-
parison and alignment of modalities [22].
• Text Decoder - Typically implemented as a large language model, component de-
signed to map input embeddings (both textual and visual) into meaningful sequences
of words or tokens in natural language [22].
This modular architecture allows for the integration of different encoders and decoders,
resulting in numerous VLM variants tailored to specific tasks or performance goals. A
schematic representation of VLM with it’s building blocks is illustrated in Figure 2.2.

2.2.2
Pretraining
Due to the multimodal nature of VLMs, their training follows a multistage process, begin-
ning with pretraining to align the core components: the vision encoder, the projector, and
the text decoder. Throughout their development, various techniques have been employed
to optimize this alignment. These methods can be broadly categorized into four principal
paradigms [23]:
6

==PAGE:14==
• Contrastive Learning – A widely adopted approach that trains models to differenti-
ate between semantically related and unrelated image-text pairs, thereby reinforcing
cross-modal alignment.
• Masked Modeling – This technique involves reconstructing missing elements, such
as masked image patches or omitted words in a caption, using available contextual
information. It enhances the model’s ability to infer and complete missing details.
• Mapping Pretrained Backbones – Instead of training both components from scratch,
this method aligns a pretrained image encoder with a pretrained language model,
significantly reducing computational overhead.
• Generative VLMs – These models extend beyond representation learning by ac-
tively generating images or textual descriptions. While they exhibit strong generative
capabilities, they are also computationally demanding to train.
It is crucial to highlight that those methods are not mutually exclusive. Many SOTA models
utilize multiple techniques at once in order to achieve highest performance across varius
vision-language tasks.
2.2.3
Finetuning
Following pretraining, VLMs often undergo a finetuning phase to better adapt to specific
downstream tasks. While zero-shot inference leverages the pretrained representations
without modification, finetuning techniques aim to bridge distributional and objective gaps
between pretraining and real-world applications. These gaps arise due to differences in
image and text distributions across datasets and the varying task-specific objectives en-
countered in downstream applications. Several finetuning strategies have been explored
to mitigate these challenges, which can be broadly classified into three categories [24]:
• Prompt Tuning –- Inspired by prompt-based learning in natural language process-
ing, this approach optimizes learnable text or visual prompts to adapt VLMs without
modifying their core parameters [25]. Methods such as text prompt tuning generate
task-specific prompts, while visual prompt tuning adjusts image inputs for improved
task alignment. A hybrid approach, text-visual prompt tuning, jointly optimizes both
modalities for enhanced transferability [26].
• Feature Adaptation –- Instead of modifying input representations, feature adapta-
tion methods introduce lightweight trainable modules that adjust intermediate image
and text features [27, 28]. This approach enables efficient adaptation while keeping
the majority of the model frozen, reducing computational costs.
• Others - Some methods perform full or partial finetuning of model parameters, mod-
ifying either the entire model [29] or selected layers [30]. Alternative techniques
leverage the cross-attention mechanisms enhancing the interaction between vision
and language modalities to improve task-specific performance [31, 32].
Prompt tuning and feature adaptation are particularly attractive due to their parameter ef-
ficiency, requiring minimal modifications while preserving the original VLM architecture.
Although most finetuning methods rely on supervised learning with labeled samples, re-
cent research explores unsupervised approaches that leverage self-training and pseudo-
labeling techniques for scalable adaptation. These strategies collectively enhance VLMs’
ability to generalize across diverse vision-language tasks while minimizing computational
overhead.

2.2.4
In-context learning
While finetuning updates model parameters for task adaptation, an alternative approach
known as in-context learning allows models to generalize to new tasks without requir-
ing parameter updates. Instead, these models leverage contextual examples within their
input to infer task-specific patterns dynamically [33, 34].
According to [35], in-context learning can be defined as follows:
Consider a query input x and a set of candidate answers Y = {y1, y2, . . . , ym}. A pre-
trained language model M predicts the most likely answer ˆy by selecting the candidate
with the highest conditional probability, given a demonstration set C. The demonstration
set C consists of an optional task instruction I and k examples {(x1, y1), . . . , (xk, yk)},
which are formatted to showcase the task. Formally, the likelihood of a candidate answer
yj is determined by a scoring function fM:
P(yj | x) = fM(yj, C, x).
The predicted answer ˆy is then computed as:
ˆy = argmaxyj∈Y P(yj | x).
Demonstration examples within C can either follow a task-specific format where all ex-
amples belong to the same task or a cross-task format where examples include their
respective instructions. The latter enables the model to generalize across diverse tasks
[36].
The core idea of ICL lies in learning through analogy. A typical ICL workflow involves
presenting a model with a few demonstration examples formatted as natural language
templates, followed by a query input. By analyzing the contextual examples, the model
identifies implicit patterns and applies them to generate predictions for the query [37].
This approach eliminates the need for costly retraining, offering a flexible and efficient
mechanism for adapting pretrained models to new objectives. The overview of how the
process of ICL looks like can be found on Fig. 2.3.
Figure 2.3: Example of in-context learning pipeline.
ICL offers several advantages. The use of natural language templates allows practition-
ers to convey task-specific knowledge directly through the input context, making it easier

to refine model behavior without retraining. Additionally, this paradigm mirrors human
decision-making, where reasoning often involves learning from observations and analo-
gies rather than explicit instruction [38, 39]. These features make ICL particularly effec-
tive for scaling models across diverse real-world applications with minimal computational
overhead.
Despite its promise, ICL presents challenges, including sensitivity to prompt design, ex-
ample selection, and order. These factors significantly impact performance, and the un-
derlying mechanisms of ICL remain an active area of research [40].
2.2.5
Improvements to in-context learning
Addressing the challenge of enhancing ICL involves exploring strategies that improve
its adaptability and accuracy across various tasks, all without the need for expensive
finetuning. The studies can be disected into two categories, basing on their focus: cross-
task trasnferability and few-shot and zero-shot approaches.
Cross-task transfer
Recent investigations highlight that ICL can achieve superior cross-task generalization
without requiring model finetuning. In-context knowledge editing (IKE) proposed in [41],
is an approach that efficiently updates factual knowledge in LLMs, with the example of
GPT-J. This method minimizes adverse effects like over-editing or forgetting previously
learned information. Similarly, Fei et al. [42] tackled the issue of label bias in ICL by
developing a bias calibration strategy, which enhances accuracy across different tasks
by adjusting for predicted biases. Meanwhile, Song et al. [43] proposed In-Context Di-
rect Preference Optimization (ICDPO), leveraging insights from more advanced models
to refine alignment, all while avoiding parameter updates. These methodologies collec-
tively contribute to a deeper understanding of how ICL can be tailored for more effective
knowledge transfer across varied applications.
Zero-shot and few-shot learning
Advancements in ICL research have yielded novel methods to improve both few-shot and
zero-shot performance. Self-ICL, as outlined by [44], enables models to generate their
own demonstrations for zero-shot tasks, achieving competitive results even without ex-
ternal examples. Similarly, Z-ICL, introduced by [45], constructs pseudo-demonstrations
from raw textual data, significantly narrowing the gap between zero-shot and few-shot
learning outcomes. Another promising approach is kNN Prompting, described by [46],
which extends the scalability of context-based learning while eliminating the need for cal-
ibration, making it effective across diverse datasets and model sizes. Additionally, [47]
investigated the use of diverse demonstration strategies for document information extrac-
tion, enhancing LLMs’ abilities to generalize to both familiar and novel document types.
Collectively, these innovations underscore the potential of ICL to address a wide range of
tasks effectively and flexibly.
2.3
Metrics
To assess the performance of systems in PAD and MAD several evaluation metrics align-
ing with ISO/IEC 30107-3 for Biometric PAD [48] has been employed, in order to quantify
the effectiveness in distinguishing bonafide inputs from spoofs and morphs.
2.3.1
Attack Presentation Classification Error Rate
The APCER measures the proportion of attack presentations (i.e., spoofed or manipu-
lated samples) that are incorrectly classified as bonafide. This is particularly crucial when
dealing with various types of presentation attacks, as it helps assess how well the system
can resist spoofing attempts and prevent false acceptance of unauthorized presentations.

Detection Error Tradeoff Curve
In real-world applications, reducing APCER often leads to an increase in BPCER and
vice versa. The Detection Error Tradeoff (DET) curve [49] is a widely used graphical tool
for evaluating the performance of biometric systems. It provides insight into the tradeoff
between different types of classification errors on a logarithmic scale.
Unlike the ROC curve, which plots the true positive rate against the false positive rate, the
DET curve is particularly useful in biometric security applications because it emphasizes
error tradeoffs. The DET curve typically has a convex shape, reflecting the fact that im-
proving one type of error (e.g., reducing false acceptance of attacks) often comes at the
expense of increasing the other (e.g., rejecting more genuine samples).

MAD tasks, a high AUC is crucial, as it reflects the model’s ability to detect attacks across
different threshold settings, ensuring robustness to variations in attack techniques and
environmental conditions.

Background
3.1
Presentation Attack Detection
Presentation attacks are commonly divided into three main categories: paper masks, 3D
masks and video projection (also known as replay attacks). Many researchers tried to
further divided the groups into sub-categories. Zhang et al. [51] proposed that that paper
mask attacks can be distinguished into cut-paper and wrapped paper instances. Others
focused on the source device for the replay attacks pointing out the importance of video
resolution [52] and the device type [53].
Due to the vast number of factors coming into play in the process of generation of such
attacks, the diversity in the detection was also immense. The systems working to counter
the problem are well studied in the literature, with two most commonly named groups –
hand-crafted features-based and CNN-based approaches. For the detailed guide on the
taxonomy of attacks the reader is addressed to take a look into the survey conducted by
Ramachandra and Busch in [54], further extended by Sharma and Selwal in [55]. Both
studies, apart from extensive analysis of the attacks, provides a comprehensive guide
into the detection techniques.
The expansion of studies in deep learning yielded many prospective models. Diverse
learning strategies, such as meta-learning [56], disentangled learning [57], or few-shot
learning [58] has been thoroughly examined. While a lot of studies focused on the ex-
plainability of decisions the systems are taking (known in literature as X-PAD) [59], the
generalization of problem domain still remained an issue.
Recently, the focus shifted towards harnessing the power of Visual Transformers (ViTs)
for the task. Srivastan et al. proposed in [60] a multimodal contrastive learning strategy
to address the problem of generalization. They showed that the initialization of the model
with multimodal pre-trained weights improved the generalization for the presentation at-
tack detection. The study showed a great potential for addressing the task of PAD by the
fusion of vision and language approaches, outperforming it’s counterparts trained only on
the image data.
Parallel innovations like Low-Rank Adaptation (LoRA) [61] layers offer complementary
benefits. The LoRA approach inserts trainable rank-decomposition matrices into frozen
pretrained networks, enabling task adaptation while preserving original model knowledge
- a strategy proven effective across multiple domains [62, 63]. A recent study by [4] lever-
ages LoRA for PAD with outstanding results. Their framework, FoundPAD, demonstrates
strong cross-domain generalization capabilities in data scarce scenarios, including the
use of synthetic samples.
Given the encouraging outcomes from using ViTs for this task, VLMs, with their adaptive
capabilities, are expected to produce similarly promising results.
3.2
Morphing Attack Detection
A comprehensive overview of techniques for both generating and preventing morphing
attacks was presented by Venkatash et al. [64], which covered various approaches to
addressing the issue in 2D facial images, both for SMAD and DMAD.

3.2.1
Differential Morphing Attack Detection
According to the taxonomy from [64], the first category of methods for DMAD problems
involves feature difference-based approaches, which compare feature vectors from both
images. This method subtracts features from the suspected morph image and a live
image captured in a trusted environment, classifying the differences to detect morphing
attacks. Various techniques, including texture, 3D gradient, landmark points, and deep
features, have been studied [65, 66, 67, 68, 69], with deep CNN features showing the best
performance [68]. Most studies focus on digital images, though recent work has improved
results using a print and scan dataset [70, 68].
The second category, demorphing techniques, was introduced in [71]. These techniques
aim to reverse the morphing process to uncover the original images. Initially designed
for landmark-based morph generation, recent advancements have primarily utilized deep
CNNs [72, 73]. While effective in controlled environments, all methods struggle with real-
world conditions like variations in pose and lighting, experienced in border control scenar-
ios.
To address this problem, Ramachandra et al. [74] proposed an approach using multi-
spectral imaging. By extracting seven different spectral bands from images captured with
multispectral cameras, they were able to enhance morphing attack detection. Consider-
ing the complexity of capturing reference images, the investigation’s limitation to a single
light source setting, and the narrow range of morph generation techniques explored, there
remains significant room for improvements.
3.2.2
Single Morphing Attack Detection
Using the taxonomy from [64], the following approaches have been studied for the SMAD
scenarios:
• Texture Feature-Based: These methods, including local binary patterns, local phase
quantization features and binarized statistical image features, show good perfor-
mance on digital SMAD but struggle with generalization across different image qual-
ities and print-scan processes [75, 76].
• Quality-Based: Techniques analyzing image degradation, such as corner and edge
distortion or reflection analysis, perform well on digital data but have limited effec-
tiveness on print-scan data and lack generalization studies [75, 77].
• Residual Noise-Based: These methods, which detect pixel discontinuities by an-
alyzing noise patterns, generalize well across digital datasets but have not been
evaluated on print-scan datasets [78, 79].
• Deep Learning-Based: Pretrained CNNs outperform hand-crafted methods but
have limited generalization across different print-scan datasets. Multiple different
SOTA models, including eg. VGG19 and AlexNet, has been explored in [80, 81].
• Hybrid: Combining multiple feature extractors and classifiers improves performance
but increases computational cost and lacks established generalization for various
print-scan processes [76, 82].
While each method has shown varying degrees of success, their generalization to different
image qualities and print-scan processes remained a key challenge.
As an extension to the two-dimensional techniques, Singh and Ramachandra in [83] pro-
posed methods utilizing clouds of 3D points for attack generation. The study highlighted

4.1
Overall architecture
A modular architecture was adopted to enable controlled experimentation and facilitate
adaptation to diverse configurations. The pipeline was segmented into four distinct mod-

4.2
Data Loader
The data loader was a critical component of the experimental architecture, designed to
handle multiple datasets with varying structures. Its primary role was to prepare and
provide images for the model, either as single images or paired images for the DMAD
scenario. The data loader served three key functionalities:
1. Setup - Function responsible for loading the configuration of each dataset, accom-
modating their varying structures. While some datasets consisted of different pro-
tocols, division sets or differentiated data per PAI, the data needed to be loaded in
accordance with experiments’ requirements.
2. Paths Loading - A mechanism that gathered all images used for evaluation, cat-
egorizing them as either bonafide or attack samples. The resulting list of image
paths was then stored in a structured JSON file for subsequent access by the mod-
els. This approach was adopted because of some models requiring specific loading
strategies or incorporated tailored preprocessing steps. When necessary, these
adjustments were implemented within dedicated model handlers.
3. Reference Paths Loading - Another mechanism that operated similarly to the path
loader but included crucial adjustments. To mitigate bias and ensure robustness,
reference images were randomized for each experiment run. An initial subset of
size N, where N represented the maximum number of inference shots tested in an
experiment, was selected from the full dataset. Subsequently, for each run inside of
an experiment, subsets of images were incrementally chosen from this initial sub-
set. As the number of shots increased, additional images were incorporated while
reusing images from lower-shot inferences. In addition to the image paths, metadata
indicating whether each reference image was bonafide or an attack was included

in the reference paths file. This information was subsequently used to structure
reference answers during model inference.
4.3
Prompt Object
The experimental design extended beyond basic prompt definitions to incorporate spe-
cialized fields enabling controlled interactions with evaluated models and their outputs.
The implemented parameters include:
• Evaluation Prompt (hereafter ”prompt”) - Core instruction accompanying test im-
ages during assessment.
• Reference Prompt - Instruction used during inference phases, intentionally decou-
pled from evaluation prompts to enable the possibility of inferring ground truth label
(that the image is either bonafide or attack), while requesting for the confidence rate
during evaluation.
• Reference Real Answer - Predefined output inferred to the model, when processing
authentic samples with reference prompts.
• Reference Attack Answer - Predefined output inferred to the model, when pro-
cessing attack samples with reference prompts.
• Confidence Flag - Binary parameter (True/False) determining whether models are
explicitly instructed to output confidence estimates or not. In latter case, two more
fields were required: Expected Real Answer and Expected Attack Answer.
• Expected Real Answer (optional) - Response pattern sought for in model’s output
for assessment of bonafide samples.
• Expected Attack Answer (optional) - Response pattern sought for in model’s output
for assessment of attack samples.
• Initialization Prompt (optional, used only in preliminary experiments) - An instruc-
tion preceding core tasks - unused in final experiments. While prior studies [7] sug-
gest that introducing the model to the role it is supposed to take on, preliminary trials
showed no measurable accuracy gains in our configuration.
4.4
Model Handler
The model handler component provided a standardized interface for interacting with pre-
trained vision language models. Given that pretraining and finetuning models were out-
side the scope of this research, publicly available pretrained models were utilized. Weights
and parameters corresponding to particular models were obtained from HuggingFaceHub
[106]. To accommodate the diversity of model architectures, including variations in pro-
cessors and tokenizers, the model handler exposed three primary functions:
1. Setup - Utility responsible for loading the pretrained weights specific to each model,
along with the appropriate processor and tokenizer. This step ensured that each
model was correctly initialized for subsequent tasks.
2. Query Construction - Each handler exposed this function due to the variability in
data input formats across different VLMs. It accepted two file paths—one for the
reference images used in a particular run and another for the evaluation images—
as well as the reference and evaluation prompts. This function then formatted the
input data according to the specific requirements of each model. The construction
involved three components:
Efficient Vision Language Models for Digital and Physical Attack Detection

==PAGE:29==
• Introduction Query (optional, used only in preliminary experiments) - An in-
troductory statement could be included to specify the desired role or behavior
of the model.
• Reference Queries - In this step, all reference images were formatted and
combined with the reference prompt and reference answers to provide in-context
knowledge to the model.
• Evaluation Query - For each evaluation image, the preceding reference mes-
sages were merged with an evaluation query containing the evaluation prompt
alongside the image that was to be assessed.
This approach allowed the architecture to enforce required components, facilitated
adjustments for few-shot inference using reference messages, and enabled testing
of the impact of introductory instructions.
3. Evaluation - for each evaluated image, the model handler saved the path to the
image along with the raw output generated by the model. In cases where the model
output was in a tokenized format, a detokenization step was performed to convert the
output into natural language, removing any artifacts such as whitespace or image
tokens.
This modular and adaptable design of the model handler enabled systematic experimen-
tation across a range of VLMs, ensuring that each model received the appropriate inputs
and produced outputs that could be consistently processed and analyzed.
4.5
Results Extractor
Following model inference, the raw output data was processed by the results extractor.
Two primary types of experiments were distinguished, each requiring a specific extraction
methodology. The first type (associated with positive value of confidence flag in the prompt
object) focused on extracting the confidence rate associated with the model’s decision.
In these experiments, prompts were designed to elicit a direct confidence score from
the model. The results extractor then parsed the raw output string, seeking a value in
a predefined format (a ratio between 0 and 1). Recognizing the potential for extraction
failures, an additional metric, failed-to-acquire (FTA), was reported to indicate instances
where the model output did not conform to the expected format.
The second type of experiment involved binary classification tasks (when confidence flag
set to False), where models were tasked with categorizing images into one of two classes.
In these cases, the results extractor searched for specific class labels (corresponding to
expected real answer and expected bonafide answer) within the model’s response.
Furthermore, experiments varied based on the dataset format. For the MAD task, datasets
contained only one image per subject. Conversely, PAD datasets provided multiple frames
from each video recording of a subject. This allowed for investigating the possibility of
calculating an average confidence rate for a subject based on multiple frames presented
to the model. The number of frames used was a configurable parameter for the entire
pipeline, ensuring consistency across all experiments within a given run.

Experimental Setup
The general goal of the experiments was to demonstrate the capabilities of in-context
learning with VLMs of parameters size up to 8 billion for PAD, SMAD and DMAD. All of

5.1
Model Selection
As detailed in Chapter 2, the Hugging Face [100] platform facilitated model choice and
setup. Our selection criteria prioritized open-source efficient models. In comparison to
SOTA models like GPT-4o and Gemini using over 200 billion parameter architectures, this
work focuses on practical efficiency – testing models within 8 billion parameters. Due to
the compact, 20 times smaller size, those models are suitable for real-world applications.
Due to the absence of standardized benchmarks specifically for physical or digital attack
detection, the Hugging Face Open Leaderboard [99] has been leveraged alongside rele-
vant computer vision benchmarks to guide our model selection. Based on these criteria,
three model families have been identified: Qwen2-VL, MiniCPM-V and MiniVPM-o.
Qwen2-VL is a SOTA multimodal language model developed by Alibaba Cloud’s Qwen
team [107, 108], part of the Qwen2 series. The model’s architecture is built on a dense
transformer with 7 billion parameters. According to multiple visual-task specific bench-
marks [109, 110, 111], the model excels in understanding visual elements at comparable
level to GPT-4o-mini [112]. This was the main reason behind choosing it for the exper-
iment, as for attack detections, the models need to not only recognize faces but also
interpret associated information like presentation attack instruments or contextual clues.
The experimental framework incorporated two vision language models from OpenBMB
[113], an organization specializing in developing computationally efficient architectures
that maintain competitive performance despite reduced parameter counts.
MiniCPM-V 2.6 represents a SOTA omnimodal architecture featuring 8 billion parameters
with integrated multimodal encoders and decoders trained through end-to-end optimiza-
tion [114]. This model demonstrates exceptional processing efficiency through its token
compression mechanism - analysis of 1.8 megapixel images requires only 640 tokens,
representing a 75% reduction compared to GPT-4o’s tokenization approach [115].
Second model provided by OpenBMB, MiniCPM-o 2.6 was selected for comprehensive
evaluation based on its superior performance in preliminary experiments. The model
achieves an OpenCompass score of 70.2 compared to MiniCPM-V 2.6’s 65.2 [116], demon-
strating enhanced visual reasoning capabilities despite sharing core architectural princi-
ples. While incorporating additional features for real-time speech interaction and multi-
modal streaming (capabilities outside this study’s scope), its improved accuracy on vision
tasks justified inclusion in the experimental protocol.

5.2
Design of PAD experiments
5.2.1
Data description
The data loading process varied depending on whether the data was used for reference
during inference or for evaluation. Evaluation was conducted on CASIA-FASD [51], MSU-
MFSD [86], OuluNPU [85] and Replay-Attack [87]. To promote generalization, the evalu-
ation did not differentiate between presentation attack instruments [51, 86, 85] or capture
devices [87, 86, 85].
5.2.2
Inference process
The SiW-Mv2 [88] database served as the source for all reference images used during
inference. This dataset distinguishes between PAIs, specifically replay and paper attacks,
in addition to providing bonafide examples. To maximize generalization, each inference
shot included one bonafide sample and one sample from each presentation attack instru-
ment type. Similar to the evaluation image selection process, the specific frame chosen
was selected at random. The video from which the image was extracted was also random-
ized within a closed set; once a video was selected for a reference image, it was excluded
from subsequent selections to avoid repetition. To maintain reference stability, especially
considering that some references might be more informative than others, new references
were incrementally added during each experiment run with increasing inference shots,
rather than randomly re-sampling the entire reference set. This process is illustrated in
Figure 5.1, while examples of used images located in the last column of Figure 3.1.

The concluding experimental phase evaluated single-image morphing detection. Here,

each evaluated step only one image was presented to the model (in comparison to two
in DMAD case). Alongside DET curves for both benchmarked dataset, HTER, AUC, RT

DET curve analysis for FRGCv2 and FERET (Figure 6.7 and Figure 6.8) revealed neg-

best results in zero-shot configurations for both datasets (FERET: 47.95% HTER, 52.67%
AUC; FRGCv2: 44.09% HTER, 55.57% AUC) – metrics approximating random choice
(50% AUC). The absence of progressive improvement with additional examples under-
scores the fundamental challenge of single-image morph detection, where models lack
comparative reference points despite contextual demonstrations of bonafide and morphed
samples.
Models followed instructions better in SMAD tests than in DMAD trials, with failed-to-
acquire rates dropping to almost no errors. This implies that asking models to compare
two images in DMAD created extra challenges that didn’t exist when analyzing single

Discussion
This research establishes the first quantitative evaluation framework for VLMs in Pre-
sentation Attack Detection, while simultaneously representing the initial investigation of
their applicability to Morphing Attack Detection - never seen before in the literature. The
demonstrated detection capabilities across both domains suggest meaningful potential
for developing novel anti-spoofing paradigms through VLM integration, marking a critical
methodological expansion in biometric security research.
7.1
Presentation Attack Detection
The experimental evaluation demonstrated that VLMs exhibit potential for PAD, a do-
main traditionally reliant on specialized deep learning architectures. As the first system-
atic assessment of VLMs in this context, this study confirmed their feasibility for zero-
and few-shot detection, with select models surpassing conventional SOTA solutions on
benchmarks such as CASIA-FASD and MSU-MFSD.
A key observation across three of four datasets was the consistent correlation between
the number of contextual examples and detection accuracy. Progressive error reductions
(averaging 11-30% in HTER improvements from 0-shot to 9-shot configurations) highlight
the adaptability of VLMs in few-shot settings. Notably, this performance was achieved with
significant efficiency: inference times remained under 2 seconds per sample, contrasting
sharply with the extended finetuning periods required by traditional methods.
Although all models exhibited improvements through the in-context learning in the ex-
periments, methodological constraints, such as the use of fixed prompts not optimized
for individual model architectures, limited performance uniformity across models. The
results underscore that minimal conversational few-shot adaptation enables VLMs to ap-
proximate dedicated PAD systems without architectural modifications.
The experimental methodology’s use of multiframe sampling from individual videos re-
veals a critical insight: enhanced performance may be achievable through increased
frame diversity. This finding suggests VLMs could yield improved detection accuracy
when processing richer input representations.
Used method of adaptation of scoring
mechanism to binary classification implies broader applicability potential.
The experiments also identified critical limitations - performance degradation on low-
quality data (Replay-Attack best results for VLMs: 52.24% AUC for VLMs vs. 89.63%
for SOTA methods) underscores VLMs’ sensitivity to capture conditions. This empha-
sizes the need for hybrid methodologies combining VLMs’ rapid adaptability with tailored
data processing steps, to achieve the highest generalization capabilities.
7.2
Morphing Attack Detection
The experimental framework for differential morphing attack detection required fundamen-
tal methodological adaptation, as multiframe binary classification scoring method proved
incompatible with single-image morph datasets. The detection accuracy is expected to
increase, if the experiments were run on datasets containing videos, rather than single
frames of subjects. Despite this constraint, the core performance trends observed in PAD
persisted, suggesting feasibility of use of VLMs for differential morphing tasks.

A critical challenge emerged from dataset constraints and the absence of standardized
reference images for genuine and morphing trials. Experimental iterations combining mor-
phed and bonafide samples revealed issues potentially attributable to quality disparities
between FRGCv2’s bonafide, morph and bonafide probe image sets. This observation
aligns with previous findings regarding VLM sensitivity to input consistency, where het-
erogeneous capture conditions degrade comparative analysis capabilities (as for PAD
experiment with Replay-Attack dataset).
While the engineered prompts generally enabled effective confidence score extraction
across architectures (FTA metrics close to 0 for all Qwen2-VL runs), certain models ex-
hibited limitations with concise instructional formats. This variability underscores the ar-
chitectural dependence of VLM prompt sensitivity.
Analyzing image pairs sequentially rather than isolated samples proved methodologically
advantageous, particularly where prompt clarity significantly impacts operational reliabil-
ity. Treating the DMAD presentation as a process of two images rather than focusing
on each one separately proved to be a viable solution for VLMs. The other approach
enforced by the task design was faced during SMAD experiments. In those runs, the
models exhibited no progressive learning effects across all configurations. Performance
metrics remained statistically equivalent between maximal (9-shot) and baseline (0-shot)
conditions, indicating that the contextual information quantity and the instruction design
remained a challenge that the VLMs were not able to overcome.

Conclusion
This foundational investigation represents the first systematic evaluation of VLMs for
joint detection of physical (presentation attacks) and digital (morphing attacks) biomet-
ric threats. While preliminary in scope, the results validate the feasibility of this method.
Moreover, the scope of the study covered additional critical evaluation dimension, the
efficiency, complied with by measured models’ size constraints.
The study prioritized open-source, computationally efficient VLMs to align with practi-
cal purpose. Though proprietary models of OpenAI or Google initially dominated first
positions in leaderboards, the emergence of high-performance open architectures like
DeepSeek R1 [126] during the research timeline underscores rapid advancements in ac-
cessible VLM capabilities.
In light of the preceding methodological considerations and experimental outcomes, the
research questions established at the beginning of this study are evaluated:
• RQ1: How effective are SOTA VLMs in detecting digital (morphing) and physical
(presentation) attacks?
The research quantitatively proves applicability of VLMs to presentation and mor-
phing detection. Tested models: Qwen2-VL, MiniCPM-V 2.6 and MiniCPM-o 2.6
achieved for PAD SOTA performance with peak AUC/HTER values reaching to
86.22%/18.44%, and relatively substantial 66.53%/33.47% for MAD evaluation.
• RQ2: Can inference techniques mitigate the high costs associated with finetuning
models for PAD and MAD?
Yes - The demonstrated positive correlation between few-shot examples and detec-
tion accuracy confirms in-context learning’s efficacy for PAD and MAD. Additionally,
measured average response times are incomparable to the time taken for finetun-
ing sophisticated deep learning models, achieving comparable results for certain
benchmarks.
• RQ3: Are VLMs a viable solution for improving the generalization capabilities of
models in cross-domain attack scenarios?
Yes - The design of the study did not differentiate per morphing type or presen-
tation attack instrument. Performance parity between seen/unseen data variations
demonstrates VLMs’ generalization advantages.
• RQ4: How is the trade off between efficiency and detection performance of VLMs
on PAD and MAD?
Results confirm resource-efficient VLM deployment is achievable without sacrific-
ing critical functionality. All models operated with hardware constraints, producing
energy consumption footprint inferior to proprietary large language models or con-
ventional deep learning pipelines - surpassing selected ones on PAD benchmarks
by up to 16.44% lesser HTER and 14.28% higher AUC. On top of that, the average
response time in most of the experiments did not exceed 2 seconds.

Limitations and Future Work
The study’s exploration of various dimensions in the problem domain revealed several
methodological limitations rooted in technical and architectural constraints.
During preliminary phases, trials of extended reasoning prompts (inspired by [7]), which
required models to justify detection decisions, proved incompatible with the lightweight
VLMs under evaluation. This incompatibility stemmed from the significant scale disparity
between the models tested, which were two orders of magnitude larger in this thesis. That
highlighted the critical role of model size in complex reasoning tasks.
Further operational challenges emerged during implementation. Idefics3 [118] exhibited
limitations in terms of number of shots for efficient in-context learning, while represen-
tatives from InternVL [119] model family introduced resource consuming preprocessing
overhead during batch image analysis. Additionally, parametric instability was observed
in certain models, such as Ovis2 [117], which suffered performance degradation with in-
creasing learning samples count.
Future research should prioritize scaling evaluations to larger architectures, with the ad-
vancement in the field of open-source solutions, to clarify whether current limitations re-
flect task complexity or model size dependencies. Parallel efforts could focus on prompt
engineering tailored to individual model architectures, as the quantitative analysis of SMAD
remained still an unsolved challenge.
By addressing scale limitations, optimizing architectural compatibility, and refining task
methodologies, subsequent work could unlock the full potential of VLMs in unified anti-
spoofing systems.
