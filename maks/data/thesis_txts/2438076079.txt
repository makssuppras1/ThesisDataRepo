Abstract 
 
The current antibiotic crisis has introduced the need for new drugs, which has initiated the search for novel 
chemistry from natural resources including the marine system. The marine environment is marked by its 
most abundant carbon sources chitin and mannose, which stem from exoskeletons of crustaceans and algae 
cell walls, respectively. Within the marine system microorganisms make up a significant amount of biomass 
with up to 109 microbes per milliliter of water. One of the more abundant genera in marine systems is 
Pseudoalteromonas, which is often associated to higher organisms. Pigmented species of this genus have 
shown high bioactivity in previous studies, and our strain P. rubra S4059 revealed a total of 28 biosynthetic 
gene clusters (BGCs) when analyzed using antiSMASH. These BGCs could hold novel chemistry and are 
thus of pharmaceutical relevance. However, eliciting the BGCs is not always possible under common 
laboratory conditions. Different approaches have been established in order to active silent BGCs and 
increase the production amount of active BGCs. Natural approaches utilize the manipulation of 
environmental factors such as addition of hormones or antibiotics, manipulation of the growth medium, co-
culturing of two or more species, and manipulation of carbon sources.  
In this study the focus lies on three carbon sources – chitin, its monomer N-acetylglucosamine (NAG), and 
mannose. Using reverse-transcriptase qPCR the effect of these carbon sources on the expression of selected 
BGCs of P. rubra S4059 was analyzed. While a total of 13 complete BGCs were found and primers were 
designed for each, the final number of investigated BGCs was reduced to 4 due to time limitations. Within 
these 4 BGCs however, significant differences were observed in relative quantification in NAG compared 
to mannose. Decreased expression under the influence of NAG was observed in all selected BGCs, with 
the most significant decrease shown for the BGC likely encoding for the pigment indigoidine. Lowered 
expression was also observed for most BGCs under the influence of chitin, however the changes were not 
significant in 3 out of 4 BGCs.  
Overall, we hypothesize that the influence of nitrogen in the chitin and NAG molecules plays a key role in 
the regulation of the expression of the 4 selected BGCs, with a more detailed analysis of the BGC revealing 
transcription regulatory elements, which can be influenced by the presence of nitrogen. Hence, the influence 
of carbon sources on the expression of BGCs in P. rubra S4059 is dependent on the composition of the 
carbon source.

1. Introduction 
1.1 The antibiotic crisis and its implications on research and drug discovery 
The antibiotic crisis is one of the most pressing health issues today. A study from 2014 showed that 
methicillin resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA) killed more patients in America than HIV/AIDS, 
Parkinson’s disease, emphysema, and homicide combined (Golkar et al., 2014). With that in mind, MRSA 
is only one species within the pool of antibiotic-resistant bacteria. The World Health Organization (WHO) 
has issued a priority pathogen list of a total of 12 pathogens for research and development of new antibiotics. 
This includes Acinetobacter baumannii, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, and Enterobacteriaceae as critical 
pathogen priority tiers; Enterococcus faecium, Staphylococcus aureus, Heliobacter pylori, Campylobacter, 
Salmonella, Neisseria gonorrhoeae, and their variants as high pathogen priority tiers; and three more 
species as medium pathogen priority tiers (Tacconelli et al., 2017). Moreover, estimations reveal that by 
2050 an  additional 10 million people worldwide will have died of diseases caused by antimicrobial resistant 
microorganisms per year (O’Neill, 2014). Despite that knowledge, antibiotics are still often misused and 
wrongly prescribed in up to 30 % of the cases in the US (Shallcross et al., 2017; Tamma & Cosgrove, 
2016).   
The discovery of penicillin in 1928 by Alexander Fleming saved millions of lives (Fleming, 1929; Gaynes, 
2017), and this discovery opened the door to the isolation of numerous antibiotic compounds from different 
organisms and environments with a strong focus on the microbially derived antibiotics (Fenical, 1997; Kong 
et al., 2010; Stal & Cretoiu, 2016). The antibiotic era (from around 1940 to 1980) introduced a high amount 
of different antibiotic classes and also a strong increase in the use of antibiotics (Clatworthy et al., 2007). 
Since 1940 the amount of antibiotics used in the treatment of patients has amounted to well over 1 million 
tons (Wise, 2002), with a total of  34.000 tons sold in 2016 in America alone (Luepke et al., 2017).  That 
number does not include the amount of antibiotics used in agriculture and aquaculture, which make up 
almost 80 % of the entire annual antibiotic use in the United States in 2014 with 13.540.000 kg and 150.000 
kg, respectively (Figure 1). Humans use around 19 % of all antibiotics, and the remaining 1 % of antibiotics 
is utilized for crops and pets (US Food and Drug Administration, 2015).

On the other side, the amount of new antibiotics released to the market has decreased, with only two 
specialized antibiotic drugs approved in 2017 (US Food and Drug Administration, 2018). The issue of

sulfonamides as the first recorded resistance to an antibiotic class in 1941. The number of drug classes 
released to the market has decreased dramatically since the 1970s, with only two new classes released 
between 2000 and 2005. Resistance to these classes emerged within two to three years after their release 
(Das et al., 2017). New antimicrobial compounds are thus needed to counter the continuing spread of 
resistant microorganisms (Michael et al., 2014). 
Search of these new antimicrobial compounds has lead back to natural resources. Soil and aquatic 
environments have been of major interest due to the large variety of bacteria and fungi found within. As 
terrestrial environments are more easily accessible, many recently studied and synthesized products come 
from soil dwelling organisms (Bueno Pérez et al., 2014). However, a recent study showed that the marine 
environment hosts more living (micro-)organisms than any other environment (Bosi et al., 2017).

 1.2 Synthesis of microbial secondary metabolites 
Biological and biotechnological potential can be roughly defined as the ability of living organisms to 
produce industrially and pharmaceutically relevant products (Egan et al., 2008; Stal et al., 2016) and is not 
limited to primary metabolites, but correlates strongly with secondary metabolites (Egan et al., 2008; 
Sponga et al., 1999). Secondary metabolites are products, which are not needed for the basic life functions 
of the organism, and only fulfill secondary survival functions including defense mechanisms, transporter 
functions for metals, agents for symbiosis, sexual hormones, and differentiation effectors (Demain & Fang, 
2000). The biological and biotechnological potential of these secondary metabolites is often considered to 
be of high value, as they can function as antibiotic, antimicrobial, antifungal, antiparasitic, and anticancer 
agents among others (Demain, 1999). While their chemical properties are well known, their functional role 
for the producing organism is often poorly understood (Traxler & Kolter, 2015). Understanding the 
ecological role of the compounds and why they are produced, could help in their elicitation under laboratory 
conditions and lead to new culturing approaches.  
Microbial secondary metabolites are encoded in so called biosynthetic gene clusters (BGCs): sets of co-
localized genes encoding the enzymes for a natural product pathway (Cimermancic et al., 2014). Within 
these BGCs core enzymes, enzymes responsible for the synthesis of specialized monomers such as 
Malonyl-Coenzyme A, transporters, regulatory elements, and host resistance genes can be found (Walsh & 
Fischbach, 2010). The most common classes of secondary metabolites are polyketides (PKS), non-
ribosomal peptides (NRPS), ribosomally synthesized and post-translationally modified peptides (RiPPs), 
and terpenes (Adamek et al., 2017).  
Polyketides (PK) are molecules made up of many smaller units forming a carbon-based skeleton, which is 
synthesized using Aldol- and Claisen-condensations. Modification through various enzymes results in 
different functional groups and thus diversification of the PKs. The sizes can vary from as low as 6 – 8 
carbons (e.g. triacetic acid lactone and 6‐methylsalicylic acid) to up to 164 carbons (maitotoxin) 
(Weissman, 2009). Examples of PKs include macrolides, tetracyclines, polyethers, and ansamycins.  
PKs are made up of domains organized into modules. PKS modules are composed of three core domains: 
an acyl-transferase (AT) and an acyl carrier protein (ACP), a ketosynthase (KS), and a thioesterase (TE). 
The AT is the binding and activating domain for the substrate, which is then transferred onto the ACP. 
There the KS catalyzes the condensation and decarboxylation of the substrate and the elongating polyketide

chain, until the extension is terminated by the TE. Modifications are carried out by additional domains 
including but not limited to ketoreductases (KR), dehydratases (DH), and enoyl reductases (ER) (Khosla et

unit. Three different types of PKSs are known: type I PKS, type II PKS, and type III PKS (Figure 3).   
 
 
Type I PKS contain several modules located on one or more proteins, with elongation occurring 
successively or iteratively. They are characterized by multiple active sites within each polyketide, however, 
differences in assembly machinery can be found between fungi and bacteria (Weissman, 2009). Fungi have 
been shown to contain three different categories of type I iterative PKS: non-reducing (NR), partially 
reducing (PR) and highly reducing (HR) (Cox, 2007). They can be differentiated in their number of KR, 
DH, and methylases (MT), and correspondingly form simpler or more complex structures (Weissman,

2009). On the other hand, many bacteria have been found to primarily utilize type I modular PKS for 
secondary metabolite production, with fewer cases of type I iterative PKS (Chen & Du, 2016). However, 
one example of the iterative PKS can be found within marine bacteria, which have been found to utilize 
type I iterative PKS to build long-chain polyunsaturated fatty acids (PUFAs). These are hypothesized to 
help them adapt to challenging environments (Metz et al., 2001; Napier, 2002). These PUFA synthesizing 
type I PKS are characterized by the presence of up to nine tandem ACP domains (Jiang et al., 2008). The 
other category of successive elongation is rather straight forward with one function per enzyme, which 
resulted in the term of “molecular assembly lines”  (Weissman, 2009). This assembly line synthesis allows 
for bioinformatics based structure prediction of the resulting compound, as the synthesis reaction can be 
closely followed and regenerated using trained algorithms (Cheng et al., 2009).   
 Type II PKS differ in that they, rather than the modular structure, have genes encoding for mono-functional 
catalytic domains and only operate iteratively. Their “minimal PKS” consisting of two KS subunits (α and 
β) and an ACP, initiates the chain elongation. Modifying enzymes such as KR, cyclases (CYC), and 
aromatases (AROM) direct the folding of the chain, with remodeling of the structures through oxygenases, 
and decorating of chains via glycosyl-, N-, O- and C-methyltransferases (reviewed by Rix et al., 2002).  
Type III PKS operate in an iterative fashion using a single KS-like active site. Repetitive condensation 
reactions typically yield mono- or bi-cyclic aromatic compounds, which can vary through the choice of 
starter units and cyclization mechanism (Austin et al., 2008; Austin & Noel, 2003).  
While the modes of synthesis differ, the products are highly similar due to the basic building blocks which 
are used. Generally they contain aromatic rings, a high amount of reactive oxygen end-groups, 
methylations, and chain structures. But just looking at a molecule will not tell which type of PKS was used 
for the biosynthesis, since the only predictable compound structures are those derived from a type I modular 
PKS (Adamek et al., 2017).   
Another important factor to consider is the common occurrence of hybrid PKS systems, which complicate 
the structure prediction. However, the knowledge of the importance of PKs remains and is shown in the 
sales of polyketide derived drugs, which results in an annual revenue of up to US $20 billion (Weissman, 
2009). 
Non-ribosomal peptides (NRPs) are molecules made up of the 20 proteinogenic amino acids as well as a 
large number of nonproteinogenic amino acids (Marahiel & Essen, 2009). Product types of NRPS include 
antibiotics, antibiotic precursors, cytostatics, immunosuppressants, siderophores, pigments, toxins, nitrogen 
storage polymers, and Phytotoxins (Schwarzer et al., 2003).  NRPS modules are similar to PKS modules in 
that they have three main domains: the adenylation domain (A) with a peptidyl carrier protein (PCP), the

condensation domain (C), and a thioesterase domain (TE). The functions of the domains are homologous 
to the ones of AT, ACP, KS, and TE respectively, however, the substrate differs.  
The A domain in NRPSs selects and activates amino acids as starter and extender units (Challis & Naismith, 
2004; Strieker et al., 2010), which are then bound to the PCP. The condensation domain then catalyzes the 
peptide bond formation between the two PCP-bound intermediates. At the end of the assembly line a TE is 
found, which releases the finished product from the NRPS. Additional enzymes for epimerization (E), N-
methylation (M), N-formylation (F), or heterocyclization (Cy) can tailor the product using serine and 
threonine side chains of the peptide backbone (Walsh, 2008). Most of the NRPS found have been identified 
as type I NRPS, however, a study by Du & Shen (1999) revealed the possibility of a type II NRPS system 
lacking the A domain (Figure 4). Both types of NRPS work in an iterative fashion.  
 
PKS and NRPS types can often be identified due to their structural properties, as they are made up of 
defined domains, which contain highly conserved motifs within key domains (Khosla et al., 2014; Weber 
et al., 2009). As these motifs differ for the different domains, identification is not infallible. As an example, 
the C domain in the NRPS systems contains the conserved motif HHxxxDG, and the cyclization domain 
retains the DxxxxD motif (Keating et al., 2002). In the PKS system one example is the DSxxxxxW motif 
in the ACP domain of type I PKS (Haines et al., 2013), and GxGxxGxxxA in the KR domain (Bachmann 
& Ravel, 2009).

1.3 Genome mining and the elucidation of hitherto unknown secondary metabolites 
Genome mining refers to the use of genome analysis to gather information on an organism (Anand & 
Mohanty, 2011). Various “omics” techniques are associated to the term including genomics, 
transcriptomics, proteomics, and metabolomics. These techniques have become indispensable in the search 
for novel chemistry, biological potential, bioactivity, secondary metabolites, and in the model construction 
for in silico work (Loureiro et al., 2018; Ziemert et al., 2016).  
The modular biosynthesis of PKs and NRPs is quite well preserved across genera and phyla (Gram, 2015). 
Therefore it is possible to mine (search) the genomes for sequences indicative of these biosynthetic gene 
clusters. Algorithms for the detection of BGCs have been build and incorporated in freely available online 
tools such as antiSMASH (Blin et al., 2017; Medema et al., 2011) and ClusterFinder (Cimermancic et al., 
2014). Based on a sequence alignment, the algorithms employ profile Hidden Markov Models (pHMMs) 
for the identification of BGCs (Medema et al., 2011). 
With the first full genome sequencing of the two well studied bacteria Streptomyces coelicolor (Bentley et 
al., 2002) and Streptomyces avermitilis (Ikeda et al., 2003), scientist first realized that Streptomyces genomes 
contain about 30 BGCs on average. However, at that time only 2 to 3 BGCs had been chemically identified 
(Ziemert et al., 2016). Since the rapid development of sequencing techniques and genome mining, more species 
have been found to contain a high amount of BGCs. However, the biosynthetic potential of bacteria is often not 
fully realized under laboratory conditions and orphan BGCs and silent BGCs are often overlooked (Chiang et al., 
2011). Orphan BGCs are clusters, for which the corresponding natural product metabolite is yet to be 
identified. Silent or cryptic clusters are BGCs, in which the genes are not expressed or only expressed at 
levels below detection limit (Chiang et al., 2011; Rutledge & Challis, 2015; Scherlach & Hertweck, 2009). The 
products of these silent and orphan BGCs are of special interest, as they often encode secondary metabolites, 
which can contain novel chemistry (Ren et al., 2017).  
For the elucidation of orphan BGCs, one needs to identify the products and link them to their respective 
orphan pathways. Several compounds have been identified by gene deletion or inactivation studies and 
comparative metabolic profiling (Gross, 2007; Zerikly & Challis, 2009). Another approach is 
genomisotopic labelling via isotope-labeled precursors (Gross et al., 2007). For this approach chemical 
analysis in the form of NMR analysis is required, and while the advantage of this method is not having to 
do genetic manipulations, the drawback is the requirement of adequate expression from the start (Chiang et 
al., 2011). However, often the products are present in very low amounts and thus the application of this 
method is not always possible (Chiang et al., 2011).

While the identification of BGCs is a relatively simple task by now, the elucidation and identification of the 
product remains a challenge. Researchers have come up with various strategies to induce and activate them 
(Figure 5). Elucidation of these silent BGCs and the increase in production of active BGCs have been a research 
topics in many studies, some of which will be discussed further in the following text. While some strategies focus 
on a synthetic approach (Ren et al., 2017), other strategies focus on a more natural approach for the elucidation 
of BGCs by adding chemical signals of different kinds.  
 
 
The synthetic approach primarily targets active and non-orphaned BGCs with limited expression. It utilizes 
cloning of the clusters into a heterologous expression host, often in combination with modifications to regulatory 
proteins (Ross et al., 2015).  While Ross et al. (2015) managed to heterologously produce several 
alterochromide molecules from Pseudoalteromonas piscicida JCM 20779 in E. coli, the production in E. 
coli was 60-fold lower than in P. piscicida when using the native promoter. Further modification of the 
promoter and growth medium resulted in a 20-fold increase to previously established levels in E. coli, yet 
the production of alterochromide molecules remained higher in the native producer. A study by Peirú et al.

The natural approach on the other hand proposes strategies such as changing the accessible cultivation parameters 
including temperature, carbon source, media composition, aeration, pH, co-culture approaches, and the addition 
of cell components or lysates (Abdelmohsen et al., 2015; Bode et al., 2002; Ruiz et al., 2010). 
Temperature has been shown to play a part in influencing the gene expression in several studies, both on its own 
and in combination with other environmental factors. A study by Han et al. (2004) found that the genes for 
pathogenicity in Yersinia pestis were mostly upregulated upon a temperature change from 26 °C to 37 °C. This 
phenomenon of gene expression changes in response to temperature changes is not limited to bacteria, but has 
also been found in fungi. A study on Aspergillus flavus showed an 2-fold increase in Aflatoxin B1 production, 
when the temperature was raised from 20 °C to 25 °C in combination with an increase in water activity (Abdel-
Hadi et al., 2012).  
Other environmental factors which can be influenced include the media composition, which can be manipulated 
by the carbon source, pH, and addition of cell components (hormones, antibiotics, signal molecules). A study on 
the fungus Glomerella acutata revealed visual color changes in cultures grown in different complex liquid 
media (Czapek dox: faint yellow aggregates, 2% malt extract: dark purple – black aggregates, potato 
dextrose: red aggregates, YPSS: black aggregates, YESD: dark yellow, and PYG broths: yellow) as well as 
on solid media (VanderMolen et al., 2013). Pigmentation is the product of the secondary metabolism (Narsing 
Rao et al., 2017), and thus the visual color change indicates a difference in secondary metabolite expression. The 
same study investigated the production of Aurofusarin by Fusicolla sp., which increased significantly when 
grown on grits, oatmeal, and rice compared to wheat germ, 2% malt extract broth, or potato dextrose.  
A study by Giubergia et al. (2017)  investigated the effect of chitin as carbon source on the gene expression in 
Vibrio coralliilyticus S2052 and Photobacterium galatheae S2753. Here, a 34-fold upregulation of genes 
of the secondary metabolism was observed. When looking into pharmaceutically relevant secondary 
metabolites, the two antibiotics andrimid and holomycin were of special interest. Production of both of 
these antibiotics, as well as the majority of the other BGCs, was found to be upregulated when grown on 
chitin versus glucose. One of her previous studies restored bioactivity in Vibrionaceae strains and even led 
to a 8-fold increase in the number of bioactive strains when grown on mannose and chitin (Giubergia et al., 
2016).

The pH has been found to influence the production of secondary metabolites in fungi and plants especially. 
Studies by Puder et al. (2000; 2001) investigated the effect of the pH during harvesting of Streptomyces sp. 
on the production of rubromycins. When the pH was slightly acidic, the harvest of rubromycins was 
successful. However, at a pH of 7.3, the extracted compounds were 3-ringed compounds. At pH 8.2 the 
isolated molecules were streptazoline and streptazone A and B, with no detection of rubromycins. At pH 
5.7 a 4-fold increase in production of one of the rubromycins was observed, whereas at pH 6.5 four new 
metabolites were discovered (Puder et al., 2000; 2001).  
Outside medium composition, aeration (measured in volume of oxygen per volume of liquid medium per minute, 
v v-1 min-1 or vvm) is also an important factor in fermentation technologies. An example for this is a study by 
Pfefferle et al. (2000), where Streptosporangium sp. NN22303 was cultured with different aeration (1 vvm to 7 
vvm). The concentration of the product ε-rhodomycione was observed to be highest at 4 vvm with around 35 % 
and then next highest at 2 vvm with 17 %. In another study by Cho et al. (2002), cell morphology and pigment 
production of Paecilomyces sinclairii were influenced by aeration changes from 1.5 vvm to 3.5 vvm. At 3.5 
vvm aeration the cells were highly vacuolated with higher pigment production and lower cell mass. The authors 
noted the highly different metabolism of vacuolated cells, which is hypothesized to be involved in the production 
of secondary metabolites.   
Co-culturing, another natural approach, is generally defined as the culturing of two or more different species with 
some degree of contact between them (Goers et al., 2014). Many ways of co-culturing have been developed with 
differences in the amount of contact (direct vs indirect contact, biofilm), species (interspecies, intraspecies, 
interdomain), and type of elicitation (biological, chemical, molecular) (Abdelmohsen et al., 2015). The exact 
mechanism of interaction in co-culture systems is little understood, but hypotheses include the influence of 
physical cell-cell interactions, production of small molecules and enzymes, and horizontal gene transfer as 
relevant mechanisms (Marmann et al., 2014). Actinomycetes have been the target of many biological co-culture 
studies with different species including other actinomycetes, other bacteria, and even fungi. A study by Dashti et 
al. (2014) co-cultivated two different sponge-associated strains of actinomycetes (Actinokineospora sp. EG49 and 
Nocardiopsis sp. RV163) in a single flask, resulting in the production of three new compounds not synthesized in 
monoculture. One of the compounds turned out to be bioactive against the Actinokineospora sp. EG49. Another 
study investigated the interactions of Streptomyces coelicolor and its predator strain Myxococcus xanthus, which 
triggered the production of the antibiotic actinorhodin as well as mycelia production (Pérez et al., 2011).    
These examples show the strong influence environmental factors have on secondary metabolite production. The 
reasoning behind all these natural approaches is to simulate the natural environmental conditions with niche 
specific parameters (temperature, carbon source, pH, etc.), competition (co-culture, biofilms), and other 
interactions as closely as possible. Natural approaches furthermore give some limited insight into the ecological

role of these secondary metabolites, which is generally accepted to be the provision of a biological advantage for 
the producer in the environment (Chiang et al., 2011).  In this research, the focus is on the natural approaches, 
specifically carbon manipulation using marine carbon sources.  
1.4 Marine microorganisms and their bioactive potential 
The marine environment covers around 70 % of this planet and is largely unexplored in diversity and 
chemical novelty (Bosi et al., 2017; Kong et al., 2010; Penesyan et al., 2010). More than 90 % of the marine 
biomass is made up of microorganisms (Delong, 2007), with the estimated global oceanic microflora 
comprising 3.6 x 1029 bacterial cells (Bowler et al., 2009; Karl, 2007). A single milliliter of sea water 
contains cell counts of 106 to 109 bacterial cells (Fenical & Jensen, 2006), with high diversity and high 
biological potential due to the extreme environmental conditions and niche adaptation (Jimeno et al., 2004).   
With the presence of up to 20.000 microbial species in a single liter of seawater (Grosberg et al., 2012), 
determining which organism to target for which bioactive compound can be a major challenge, as different 
microorganisms produce different compounds not all of which are relevant. Several parameters need to be 
considered, including abundance of the microorganism, variety of isolates per source, and production of 
pharmaceutically relevant bioactive compounds (Sponga et al., 1999). Further complicating the issue, is 
the culturablilty of the microorganisms, with 99 % of marine microorganisms considered uncultivable 
(Epstein, 2013). Concerning the abundance, a recent study by Haggerty & Dinsdale (2017) gave insights 
into the predominant families of bacteria in the marine environment over a global scale (Figure 6). 
Correlations between the research by Haggerty and Dinsdale (2017) and research by Amaral-Zettler et al. 
(2010), who analyzed the relative abundance of the 20 most abundant bacterial tags, are found. The 11 most 
dominant families include SAR 11 cluster, Prochlorococcaceae, Flavobacteriaceae, Rhodobacteraceae, 
Altermonadaceae, Pseudomonadaceae, and Burkholderiaceae, which are also represented in the top 20 most 
abundant bacteria tags. The majority of bacterial tags found by Amaral-Zettler et al. (2010) belong to the 
phylum of Proteobacteria, with the classes of α-Proteobacteria and γ-Proteobacteria with the highest 
representation.  
Studies on surface-associated microbes have shown a high potential for the production of bioactive 
compounds, as the organisms are under constant competition for space and nutrients.  The communities are 
often in symbiosis with their host: protecting them from fouling, providing hard to obtain nutrients, and 
synthesizing bioactive compounds of use for both host and symbiont (Egan et al., 2008; Martin et al., 2015).

However, especially with such associations and bacterial communities, it is hard to determine the orginial 
producer of relevant compounds. Sponges, whose biomass is made up of up to 60 % bacteria (Webster & 
Taylor, 2012), were long wrongfully thought of as producers of many marine metabolites, which were 
actually of microbial origin (König et al., 2006; Schmidt, 2005).  While this poses a problem to overcome, 
it is also an opportunity, as the microbes are easier to culture and upscale in production than sponges, and 
provide a sustainable source for the compound of interest (Penesyan et al., 2010; Satheesh et al., 2016). 
One of the advantages is the high potentcy of the marine derived compounds, which could be linked to the 
need to overcome the dilution in seawater (Haefner, 2003; Zhang et al., 2005). Furthermore it has been 
noted, that some of the secondary metabolites not only contain novel chemistry, but also novel mechanisms 
of action (Montaser et al., 2011). One example of such novel mechanisms is the blocking of N-type voltage-
sensitive calcium channels by the FDA approved drug Prialt® (Miljanich, 2004) from the marine cone snail 
Conus magnus, which is primarily found in the tropical Pacific and Indian Oceans, where it occupies its 
own specialized niche (Kumar et al., 2015).

Since 1917, the term ‘ecological niche’ has changed its meaning several times. Originally it was described 
as environments subject to certain conditions, which are inhabitated by organisms adapted to these 
conditions (Grinnell, 1917). Later niches were defined as the interrelationship of species with all abiotic 
and biotic factors influencing this habitat, and lastly as the role of the organism in the habitat (Elton, 1927; 
Hutchinson, 1957). Nowadays, scientists are interested in niches due to the adaptations of organisms and 
thus specialized compounds. Notable niches include hydrothermal vents with their extreme temperatures 
and gas emission (Thornburg et al., 2010); surfaces of algae and plastics with high competition due to 
biofilm formation; and nutritient limited niches including the deep sea (Egan et al., 2008; Husson et al., 
2017; Jimeno et al., 2004; Martin et al., 2015).  
Nutrient limitation refers mainly to nitrogen, phosphorus, and carbon as effectors on the biosynthesis of 
secondary metabolites (Abadie et al., 2015; De Morais et al., 2015; Ribalet et al., 2007; Ruiz et al., 2010). 
The carbon sources are especially interesting, as carbon regulation has been noted in Gram-positive 
organisms, including species of Streptomyces, Corynebacterium, Lactobacillus, Bacillus; and Gram-
negative organisms such as Pseudomonas, Escherichia, Erwinia, and Serratia species (Ruiz et al., 2010). 
Carbon regulation referes to the influence of carbon sources on regulation of BGCs due to carbon inducible 
regulators. Regulation of secondary metabolite biosynthesis by carbon can occurr through several ways. 
The preferred carbon source can prevent transcriptional activation or repress the BGC (Uguru et al., 2005). 
Mannose for example has been reported to interfere with mannosidostreptomycinase in the soil dwelling 
bacteria Streptomyces fradiae and S. griseus, and thus repress the biosynthesis of secondary metabolites 
(Demain & Inamine, 1970).  
In the marine environment, carbon sources are available in many forms. Some are readily accessible, 
including the small molecules glucose, mannose, and N-acetylglucosamine (NAG) (Chen et al., 2010). 
However, the vast majority of the marine carbon sources are derived from complex crystalline structures 
(Tang et al., 2015). The polymer chitin for example is found in the exoskeleton of crustaceans, in the cell 
walls of fungi, in fish scales, and in the pens of squid (Gooday, 1990; Ifuku, 2014; Tang et al., 2015). It is 
the second most abundant natural polysaccharide on earth after cellulose (Cheung et al., 2015). In order for 
the bacteria to utilize this carbon source, it must first be degraded into smaller subunits. This degradation 
of performed by chitinolytic bacteria, which secrete chitinases to the extracellular space, and break down 
chitin into the monomer NAG (Gooday, 1990).  
Macro- and microalgae also constitute highly abundant polymers to the marine environment. The building 
blocks of algae are relatively limited, but they can be linked together in almost infinite ways (Davis et al., 
2003). Mannose is such a building block synthesized by many algae (Aluwihare et al., 2002; Popper et al., 
2011). It is furthermore the most abundant neutral sugar of exopolysaccharides (EPS) in the marine

environment and has been found in many cold adapted Pseudoalteromonas strains, including P. arctica 
KOPRI 21653 (Kim & Yim, 2007) and P. haloplanktis TAC 125 (Corsaro et al., 2004). Many 
Pseudoalteromonas species have been found in association with higher organisms (e.g. brown algae), and 
have accordingly formed their own niches with appropriate adaptations, making it an interesting genus to 
investigate in regards to bioactivity ( Holmström & Kjelleberg, 1999; Ivanova et al., 2002). 
1.5 The species Pseudoalteromonas rubra  
One of the most frequently isolated marine bacterial genera is Pseudoalteromonas (Ivanova et al., 2004). 
The genus is a subgroup of the Gram-negative γ-Proteobacteria, and comprises more than 50 species 
separated into pigmented and non-pigmented species (Wang et al., 2015). Pigmented species are commonly 
displaying a higher degree of bioactivity than the non-pigmented species (Holmström et al., 1996). 
Pseudoalteromonas belong to the family of Alteromonadaceae, and can be found in coastal, open, and deep-
sea waters of the Atlantic and Pacific (Brian-Jaisson et al., 2014; Giudice et al., 2012; Haggerty & Dinsdale, 
2017). They are often found in association with algae, invertebrates, fish, or other higher organisms 
(Holmström et al., 2002), but have also been noted to be free swimming in the marine waters. Some of the 
pigmented strains are characterized by a high amount of operons (up to around 20 operons) for the 
biosynthesis of secondary metabolites (Bosi et al., 2017), and are thus of special interest in our quest to find 
novel antibiotics. Common bioactivities expressed by species of the genus Pseudoalteromonas include 
antibiotic, anti-inflammatory, antifungal, antifouling, toxins/antitoxins, and antitumor functionalities 
(Holmström & Kjelleberg, 1999; Isnansetyo & Kamei, 2003).  
Pseudoalteromonas rubra ATCC 29573 ud168329 has one of the highest numbers of operons with 18 
clusters, the majority thereof being NRPS and NRPS-PKS hybrids (Figure 7). Species of 
Pseudoalteromonas have been found attached to surfaces of seagrasses, seaweeds, and algae (Egan et al., 
2008; Martin et al., 2015; Satheesh et al., 2016). Surface associated bacteria are considered to have a high 
biological potential due to the space and nutrition limitations connected to biofilms (Bonar et al., 1986; 
Egan et al., 2008; Penesyan et al., 2010).

P. rubra is a red-pigmented species of the genus Pseudoalteromonas, which produces the antibiotics 
prodigiosin and cycloprodigiosin (Fehér et al., 2008). Prodigiosin is an antibiotic compound, which induces 
autolysis in bacterial pathogens and the producer under certain conditions (Danevcic et al., 2016). A draft 
genome analysis of our strain P. rubra S4059 using antiSMASH revealed 28 BGCs, many of which had no 
similarity to known compounds. The potential of finding novel chemistry within these BGCs is considered 
as high, due to the lacking similarity to known clusters. While the production of the pigment prodigiosin 
can be determined visually, the presence and active biosynthesis of other secondary metabolites has not 
been evaluated in detail. In this study we investigated the expression of BGCs quantitatively using reverse-
transcriptase quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR).  
1.6 Reverse Transcriptase quantitative Polymerase-Chain-Reaction (RT-qPCR)  
The analysis of gene expression is an important part of understanding microorganisms. For the elucidation 
of gene expression levels, one often used method is reverse-transcriptase quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR). 
RT-qPCR is a highly sensitive, cost-effective quantification method for gene expression, which allows for 
the quantification of specific amplicons via fluorescence (Udvardi et al., 2008).

RT-qPCR can be a one- or two-step process (Figure 8). As this study used the two-step process, this section 
will focus on this process. Before performing a RT-qPCR one needs to obtain pure RNA of the sample to 
be analyzed. This RNA is then reverse transcribed into complimentary DNA (cDNA) in the first step of the 
two-step reaction, in order to obtain a more stable library of transcribed sequences. During the second step, 
target specific primers are added to the cDNA and the amplification is quantified using a fluorescent dye 
such as SYBR® Green I, which binds to double-stranded DNA molecules. SYBR® Green has an excitation 
maximum at 494 nm and an emission maximum at 521 nm. 
 
 
When performing a RT-qPCR several factors can influence the outcome of the gene expression study. It is 
a multistep process with several prerequisites including a robust reverse transcriptase resulting in a high 
cDNA yield with good quality, gene specific primer sets for the genes of interest, and good reference genes 
(Udvardi et al., 2008). However, the advantages one gains using this process are the relatively low-cost, 
high precision quantification, high sensitivity, and reproducibility of this method (Metcalf et al., 2010). 
Furthermore many samples can be tested at once using 96-well plates (Rocha et al., 2015).  
The output data generated based on the fluorescent signaling are threshold cycle values (CT), which are 
shown in an amplification curve. A melting curve depicts the change in fluorescence when the DNA is 
melted and thus not binding the signal anymore. Plate sample values give the exact CT value and standard

curves can be used for data analysis (Dorak, 2006). When analyzing the data one can use two different 
approaches: absolute quantification or relative quantification. Absolute quantification determines the actual 
copy number of a target. Relative quantification compares the expression between different conditions (e.g. 
treated versus untreated) (Bonab et al., 2015). 
After the publishing of the Minimal Information required for Quantitative Real-Time PCR Experiments 
(MIQE) guidelines (Bustin et al., 2009), the requirements for publishing RT-qPCR studies were revisited. 
One consideration strongly insisted upon is the choice of appropriate reference genes. This is of high 
importance, as they are required to normalize the gene expression of the genes of interest (Pinto et al., 
2012). Housekeeping genes are preferred, as they are continuously expressed at relatively constant levels, 
and thus allow for normalization of the data. A recent study by Rocha et al. (2015) looked into the most 
commonly used reference genes for the evaluation of bacterial gene expression. Overall, the 16S rRNA 
gene was the most often used, however it was validated in only 23.5 % of the studies. Other genes included 
the DNA gyrase subunits A (gyrA) and B (gyrB), which were used in 11 and 6 studies and validated in  
54.5 % and 50 % of the studies, respectively. The most validated studies utilized the Rho termination factor 
(rho) with 3 of 5 validated studies, glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate dehydrogenase (gap) with 4 of 5 validated 
studies, and protein translocase subunit SecA (secA) with 2 of 2 validated studies (reviewed by Rocha et 
al., 2015).  
Previous studies looking at the best reference genes for carbon source dependent expression studies in 
different bacteria revealed that even within housekeeping genes, differences in CT values can be found in 
expression due to varying carbon sources (da Silva et al., 2016; Galisa et al., 2012; Pessoa et al., 2016). As 
a general rule, the most stable housekeeping genes were chosen as references. The study by da Silva et al. 
(2016) found that for Burkholderia tropica, the most stable housekeeping genes were gyrB, rpoD, and lpxC 
when comparing between sucrose, glucose, mannitol, fructose, aconitic acid, and sugarcane juice. Within 
gyrB, rpoD, and lpxC, CT differences of around 5, 5, and 3 cycles were found respectively. Within 
Herbaspirillum seropedicae, the most stable reference genes were glyA, groEL, recA, rpoA, and rpoB with 
a difference in CT of 2, 3, 3, 3, and 3 cycles respectively, when comparing between glucose, citric acid, 
mannitol, malic acid, and root extract (Pessoa et al., 2016). The study by Galisa et al. (2012) focusing on 
Gluconacetobacter diazotrophicus compared the expression of housekeeping genes between glycerol, 
glucose, mannitol, and sucrose supplemented cultures. They found the most stable expression within the 
genes for rho, 23S rRNA, rpoA, and rpoD. CT values differed in a range of 2-3 cycles for each of these 
genes (Galisa et al., 2012).   
For this study, rho and gyrB were chosen as reference genes. Rho has been previously used in a gene 
expression study on the marine flavobacterium Zobellia galactanivorans (Thomas et al., 2011). GyrB has 
==PAGE:26==

not been used for the studies of marine bacteria, however it has been used in plant associated bacteria 
(Galisa et al., 2012) and gyrA, which is the second subunit, has been used in marine bacteria studies (Brudal 
et al., 2013).  Lastly, both gyrB and rho have been used as reference genes for the assessment of gene 
expression under different carbon sources before in the study by Galisa et al. (2012), where rho was shown 
to be very stable in expression.   
Primer design is an important part of RT-qPCR, since it determines and affects the specificity of primer 
binding to the wanted amplicon, and efficiency of the amplification during the qPCR reaction. In a perfect 
reaction the amplification efficiency is a 2-fold increase with each cycle (Dorak, 2006). Several parameters 
are to be kept in mind when designing the primers, including the primer length, melting temperature TM  
(50 % of primers bound to the amplicon), GC content, and amplicon length (Technologies, 2010). Generally 
the primers should be long enough to be highly specific within a length range of 18 - 24 nucleotides (nt) 
and an absolute maximum of 30 nt. The longer the primer, the longer it will take to hybridize with the 
target, extend, and remove after amplification (Technologies, 2010; Thornton & Basu, 2011). With these 
limitations, one should furthermore make sure to have as few mismatches as possible, so binding is specific, 
strong, and efficient. The amplicon should preferably be less than 150 nt with similar length between 
targets, in order to equalize the SYBR® Green fluorescence intensity. However, in extreme cases up to 200 
nt are still acceptable, but the qPCR efficiency will likely be reduced a little (Thornton & Basu, 2011). The 
GC content should be within a range of 40-60 % (Technologies, 2010), as the optimal melting temperature 
is around 63 – 64 °C with preferably no more than 1 °C difference within a primer set. The TM can however 
be within a range of 59 – 68 °C in order to avoid secondary structures (Thornton & Basu, 2011). Other key 
factors for a successful PCR are no primer dimer formation, few secondary structures in the amplicon, and 
no self-complementarity within primers. Using online tools such as Primer3 (Rozen & Skaletsky, 1998; 
Untergasser et al., 2012), has significantly simplified primer design, however manual revision is crucial to 
make sure the primers bind only to the sequence they are intended to bind to and result in only one amplicon. 
Other online tools such as Beacon Designer™ Free Edition analyze primer dimer formation and self-
ligation, and mFold from IDT (Owczarzy et al., 2008) gives insight into the secondary structures within the 
amplicon and an estimation of the TM of these structures.

1.7 Purpose of the study 
The purpose of this study is to investigate the effect of carbon sources on the expression of biosynthetic 
gene clusters in P. rubra S4059, with the long term goal of discovering novel antibiotics from the many 
BGCs for which compounds are not known. Cultures of P. rubra S4059 will be grown in media 
supplemented with three different carbon sources: mannose, colloidal chitin, and NAG. The transcriptional 
level of BGCs in each culture will be investigated using RT-qPCR. Since the genome of P. rubra S4059 is 
a draft genome, only full length BGCs will be considered, as some BGCs could be located on more than 
one contig. Primers will be designed for the core genes within the individual BGCs. We will determine if 
these BGCs are indeed silent, and if so, if these clusters can be activated or induced when cultures of P. 
rubra S4059 are supplemented with carbon sources simulating the natural marine environment.

2. Materials and Methods 
The study aim is to determine the effect of carbon sources on the expression of biosynthetic gene clusters 
in P. rubra S4059. For this, an analysis of the draft genome was performed, and primers were designed for 
selected BGCs. The genomic DNA (gDNA) from P. rubra S4059 was extracted, and a growth curve for P. 
rubra S4059 in media supplemented with colloidal chitin was performed. A primer optimization using the 
genomic DNA determined the best annealing temperature and PCR run conditions. RNA was extracted 
from P. rubra S4059 and reverse transcribed into complementary DNA (cDNA). The cDNA was used to 
determine the expression of BGCs using RT-qPCR by utilizing SYBR® Green and measuring the 
fluorescence emission.  
2.1 Bacterial strains 
The strain P. rubra S4059 was isolated during the Galathea 3 expedition (Gram et al., 2010). Pure freeze 
stock cultures of P. rubra S4059 were stored at -80 °C.    
2.2 Culture conditions 
P. rubra S4059 was streaked on Marine Agar (MA, Difco 212185) plates and incubated at 25 °C for 2 days. 
Single colonies were inoculated in marine minimal media (MMM, Östling et al., 1991) supplemented with 
0.2 % mannose (Sigma 63580), 0.2 % N-acetylglucosamine (NAG, Sigma A3286), or 0.2 % colloidal chitin 
and grown overnight  at 25 °C, 200 rpm shaking. Colloidal chitin was prepared from shrimp shell chitin 
(Sigma C7170) according to the protocol by Giubergia et al. (2016) with hydrolysis overnight.   
2.3 Genomic DNA Extraction from P. rubra S4059 
gDNA was extracted from P. rubra S4059 using the QIAGEN Genomic-tip 20/G kit (Cat No. / ID: 10223). 
Two mL of P. rubra S4059 overnight culture were pelleted and the supernatant was discarded. Instructions 
of the QIAGEN Bacterial cells protocol (steps 1-7) and the QIAGEN Genomic-tip protocol (step 1-4, 5B, 
and 6B) were followed, and gDNA was eluted in 50 µl of MilliQ water. The gDNA was dissolved at 5 °C 
overnight, and the gDNA concentration was measured using the Denovix spectro-photometer (DeNovix 
DS-11, DNA setting).

using the antiSMASH algorithm version 4.0 (Blin et al., 2017) and ClusterFinder (Cimermancic et al., 
2014). AntiSMASH detected 28 BGCs, which were analyzed for completeness within one contig (no cut 
off at the end or beginning), and ClusterFinder provided the BGC for prodigiosin, the red pigment produced 
by P. rubra S4059. A total of 13 clusters were found to be complete. In addition, two housekeeping genes,

2.6 Primer optimization 
Primer optimization was carried out on the extracted gDNA from P. rubra S4059 cultures supplemented 
with mannose. The PCR reaction contained a final volume of 25 µl (Blue TEMPase Hot Start 2X Master 
Mix K (VWR, ID: 5200600-1250), ddH2O, 0.02 mM forward primer, 0.02 mM reverse primer, ~50 ng 
gDNA). For the negative control, ddH2O was used instead of gDNA. The run conditions were optimized to 
the following thermocycling parameters: initial denaturation at 95 °C for 15 min; followed by 40 cycles of 
30 sec at 95 °C, 10 sec at 61 °C, and 30 sec at 72 °C; and a final elongation at 72 °C for 5 min. 
PCR products were verified using gel electrophoresis. Four µl of the PCR product were mixed with 1 µl of 
GelRed and run on a 3 % agarose gel at 140 V for 50 min. For the determination of the correct size, 3 µl of 
100 bp plus ladder (O’Gene Ladder 100 bp plus, ThermoScientific) were loaded onto the gel. After the gel 
was run, it was scanned using the BioRad Gel DocTM XR+ and Image LabTM software using the GelRed 
Protocol setting.    
2.7 RNA extraction 
For the RNA extraction from P. rubra S4059, cultures of P. rubra S4059 were grown in MMM 
supplemented with 0.2 % of mannose, NAG, or colloidal chitin to a cell density of about 108 CFU/mL. 
CFU counts, dilution series, and purity streaks were performed. At 108 CFU/mL, 500 µl of culture 
supplemented with mannose and chitin, and 2x250 µl of culture supplemented with NAG were added to 2 
volumes of RNAprotect Bacterial Reagent. The RNA was then extracted from the cells using the 
“Mechanical Disruption of Bacteria protocol” (Protocol 3, RNAprotect Bacteria Reagent Handbook 
01/2015). This step was followed by the protocol for the “Purification of Total RNA from Bacterial Lysate 
Using the RNeasy Mini Kit” (Protocol 7, RNAprotect Bacteria Reagent Handbook, 01/2015) with on 
column digestion using DNase I (Appendix B). The RNA was eluted in 50 µl of RNase free water. As a 
last step a second DNase treatment was applied using the TURBO DNA-freeTM kit (Invitrogen, AM1907). 
RNA was measured using the Denovix spectro-photometer. The RNA was diluted to ~100 ng/µl and stored 
at -80 °C. RNA was checked for purity by running it in a PCR reaction using the primers for the reference 
gene Rho along with a positive gDNA control and a no template control. The PCR product was run on a

3. Results 
The aim of this study was to determine the effect of the 3 different carbon sources mannose, NAG, and 
colloidal chitin on the expression of biosynthetic gene clusters in Pseudoalteromonas rubra S4059.  
3.1 AntiSMASH reveals 28 biosynthetic gene clusters in P. rubra S4059 
The draft genome of P. rubra S4059 was searched for BGCs using the online tool antiSMASH in two runs 
– with and without ClusterFinder. Without the ClusterFinder algorithm, 28 BGCs were identified. With the 
addition of the ClusterFinder algorithm, the total number was elevated to 47 BGCs, though these included 
saccharides and fatty acids. Most of the BGCs did not have similarity to known clusters. The BGC for the 
red pigment prodigiosin, produced by P. rubra S4059 was only found using the ClusterFinder algorithm. 
A second pigment – indigoidine – was found by antiSMASH. Most BGCs encoded for NRPS and PKS. 
The 28 BGCs found by antiSMASH furthermore included two bacteriocins, and several BGCs with 
products which fit in none of the categories, labeled as “Other” type.  
After identifying and eliminating the incomplete clusters (cut off at the beginning or end), 13 BGCs 
remained. For the RT-qPCR two reference genes were chosen from the house keeping genes – Transcription 
termination factor Rho, and DNA gyrase subunit B. Thereupon a total of 15 primer sets were designed.  
3.2 Primer design for 13 biosynthetic gene clusters and two reference genes 
Primers were designed for 13 BGCs and two reference genes. Due to time limitations, the amount of BGCs 
investigated using RT-qPCR was reduced to four BGCs. These four clusters were found in contig_3 (Other), 
contig_12 (PKS:NRPS hybrid, prodigiosin), contig_32-1 (NRPS), and contig_32-2 (Other, Indigoidine). 
They were chosen based upon a comparison of all primer sets run in a PCR on cDNA from P. rubra S4059 
grown on the different carbon sources (more in section 3.6).  In this section, the focus is laid on these four 
BGCs and the two reference genes, the other 9 BGCs are described in the appendix (Table 7).  
3.2.1 Contig_3: Other  
Contig_3 contains the BGC for a non-classified product type (Other). The BGC contained two core genes, 
9 additional biosynthetic genes, and 6 regulatory genes (Figure 9).

Contig_12 contains the BGC for prodigiosin, the red pigment produced by P. rubra S4059. The BGC was 
only found when applying the ClusterFinder algorithm. It contains five core genes, two transporter genes 
and five complete additional biosynthetic genes (Figure 10). 
 
 
No regulatory genes were identified by the algorithm. The core genes are annotated as a putative acyl carrier 
protein, an 8-amino-7-oxononanoate synthase, an AMP-dependent synthetase and ligase, a beta-ketoacyl 
synthase, and an alkaline serine protease in that order. The transporter genes are annotated as drug resistance 
transporter EmrB/QacA and homoserine/threonine efflux protein, respectively. Seven genes could not be 
annotated by antiSMASH/ClusterFinder. The annotations of the draft genome again differed to those given 
by antiSMASH/ClusterFinder. The core genes corresponded to hypothetical protein, 2-amino-3-keto-

protease. The transporters correspond to a multidrug resistance transporter and a putative threonine efflux 
pump. The primer set was designed for the FIG01134662 hypothetical protein core gene, after a BLAST 
analysis revealed it to be a 3-ketoacyl-ACP synthetase in another P. rubra strain.

3.3 Pseudoalteromonas rubra S4059 growth curves at 25 °C 
A growth curve for P. rubra S4059 at 25 °C was established in MMM supplemented with 0.2 % chitin. In 
a previous study, the growth of P. rubra S4059 was investigated in MMM supplemented with 0.2 % NAG 
or 0.2 % mannose. Here a comparison between the different carbon sources in regard to their Log(CFU)/ml 
and Log(OD600) is shown  (Figure 14).  
 
The biological duplicate cultures were inoculated to a cell density of around 103 CFU/ml, with samples 
taken every three hours after a starting interval of 12 hours. The last sample was taken on the third day after 
72 h incubation. The OD600 was taken starting after 12 h incubation. The maximum cell density was reached 
after 24 h with around 8.5 Log(CFU)/ml for mannose and NAG supplemented cultures, and after 27 h with 
8.0 Log(CFU)/ml for cultures supplemented with chitin. A clear difference is seen in the OD600 
measurement, where the culture for chitin had an OD600 maximum of 0.8 Log(OD600), whereas the mannose 
and NAG supplemented cultures reached an OD600 maximum of around 4.5 to 5 Log(OD600). However, the 
pigmentation might have interfered with accurate determination of the OD600, with chitin particles further 
obscuring the path of the light and thus influencing the OD600.

The stationary phase started after 24 h for mannose and NAG and 27 h for chitin supplemented cultures 
respectively. No significant decrease was noted after three days. The generation times were 1.422 h,  
1.435 h, and 1.555 h for mannose, NAG, and chitin supplemented cultures, respectively. The generation 
time (g) was calculated using the formula g = t/n, where t is the time at which the maximum cell density is 
reached, and n can be calculated from the bacterial start concentration (N0) and end concentration (N, here

4. Discussion 
The present study investigated the effect on expression of biosynthetic gene clusters in P. rubra S4059 
when growth media was supplemented with three different carbon sources (chitin, mannose, and NAG) 
using RT-qPCR with gyrB and rho as reference genes. Growth in media containing mannose seems to 
induce expression of the selected BGCs. The growth of P. rubra S4059 in NAG resulted in a significantly 
lower expression of the selected BGCs.  
4.1 Composition matters - Differences in carbon sources with nitrogen as a key factor in 
regulation 
Carbon regulation has been found in many Gram-positive and Gram-negative microorganisms, including 
species of the order Actinomycetales, Bacilliales, and Lactobacilliales (Ruiz et al., 2010). Carbon regulation 
generally depends on the rapid utilization of a preferred carbon source for growth, while a second carbon 
source would result in the production of secondary metabolites. Linking to this, it has been found that lower 
growth rates are associated with secondary metabolism (Demain, 1989).  In this study, growth of P. rubra 
S4059 and expression of its BGCs under different carbon sources were investigated. The carbon sources 
were the monomers mannose and NAG, and the polymer chitin. While they were not investigated in 
combination, there were some differences seen between the growth rates already. The supplementation of 
chitin to the medium resulted in a growth rate of 1.55 h compared to mannose and NAG, which were very 
close in regard to their growth rates with around 1.43 h. This is a 1.08-fold change in growth rate, which 
may be explained by the structural differences. Chitin is a polymer, which needs to be broken down into 
smaller subunits before it can be taken up by the microorganisms (Gooday, 1990). Chitinases process the 
break-down of the polymer to oligomers, and the production and secretion of these requires time. The 
synthesis of chitinases in Streptomyces and Legionella sp. is based on a two-component regulatory system 
(Cianciotto, 2009; Tsujibo et al., 1999), meaning an extracellular signal needs to be received and processed 
before synthesis is initiated. Originally the two-component regulatory system was characterized for nitrogen 
regulation among other (Sanders et al., 1992), which hints at the presence of chitin (containing nitrogen) 
being the inducer for the synthesis of chitinases. Pseudoalteromonas sp. have been shown to secrete chiA, 
while other chitinases accumulate within the cell (Paulsen et al., 2016). However, the exact mechanism of 
chitinases secretion in Pseudoalteromonas is yet to be revealed. NAG, on the other hand is a readily 
accessible and easy to process sugar, being taken up by the organism at a similar rate as mannose. Hence 
the similarity in growth rate between the two carbon sources.  
Another factor, which needs to be considered in regard to the slower growth rate in medium supplemented 
with chitin, is the attachment of the microorganism to the polymer (Yu et al., 1991). For the species Vibrio 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
 
41 
 
furnissi, the attachment to chitin occurs via highly sugar-specific cell surface lectins (Yu et al., 1991). 
Similarly a previous study showed that the attachment of V. parahaemolyticus to monosaccharides such as 
mannose required mannose-specific lectin (Barondes & Mirelman, 1986). Thus, P. rubra S4059 should 
also express lectins specific for chitin, mannose, and NAG. The substrate-specific synthesis and binding of 
lectins would require time and thus cause a delay in growth in all carbon sources. One hypothesis about the 
decrease of growth rate in correlation to lectin, is that attachment to larger molecules such as the polymer 
chitin would potentially take longer or require more lectins, than attachment to smaller molecules. A 
genome analysis in regards to lectin biosynthetic genes could potentially reveal more information about 
relevant carbon sources to supplement to the growth medium, as lectins are often carbon source specific 
and could thus indicate preferences of growth substrates (Kletter et al., 2013a; Kletter et al., 2013b).  
When looking at the RT-qPCR results a significant difference in relative expression of the four analyzed 
genes in the selected BGCs can be observed for three of the BGCs. Mannose was regarded as the calibrator 
(standard) and thus NAG and chitin were compared to it. The strongest difference in expression was seen 
within the chosen gene in contig_32-2, which contains the BGC for indigoidine. In NAG a 0.06-fold 
decrease compared to mannose was observed in regard to both GyrB and Rho. Chitin showed a 0.75- and 
0.57-fold decrease compared to mannose for GyrB and Rho, respectively. Both fold-changes in contig_32-
2 were significant with p-values of 4.88-4 and 6.12-12 for chitin and NAG, respectively. The other significant 
differences in expression were found for the BGC in contig_3 and contig_12 under growth with NAG. For 
contig_3 a significant increase in expression (p = 0.022) was noted when grown in NAG supplemented 
medium. Contig_12 on the other hand showed a significant decrease in expression (p = 0.020). Overall, it 
does seem like the presence of nitrogen in the carbon source plays an important role in the regulation of 
expression of BGCs in P. rubra S4059, with the presence of additional nitrogen resulting in decrease rather 
than increase for the analyzed clusters. Thus, one potentially interesting idea to follow up on as an 
explanation for this phenomenon, is nitrogen catabolite regulation. Here, rather than carbon, nitrogen is the 
detrimental factor for the regulation of the expression of BGCs. Affected proteins include proteases, nitrate 
reductases, amidases, histidases, and ureases (Drew & Demain, 1977), some of which were found when 
analyzing the draft genome. In a previous study by Giubergia et al. (2016) chitin and mannose re-induced 
bioactivity in different Vibrionaceae strains. It was furthermore found that only 4 strains inhibited V. 
anguillarum when grown on glucose, 6 strains when grown on mannose, and 11 strains when grown on 
chitin (Giubergia et al., 2016). Another study on V. coralliilyticus S2052 showed that chitin induced the 
production of the antibiotic andrimid, with general upregulation of expression of BGCs (Wietz et al., 2011). 
While chitin resulted in higher expression of BGCs in those two studies, in this study on the other hand 
chitin resulted in lower expression compared to mannose.  
==PAGE:49==
 
 
 
42 
 
The BGCs of the four investigated contigs contained a two-component regulatory system as well as 
transcriptional regulators of the AraC, LysR, LuxR, and MarR families. A study investigating the 
transcriptional profile of nitrogen fixation in an Azoarcus sp., found that AraC, LysR, and LuxR were all 
affected in expression by nitrogen (Sarkar & Reinhold-Hurek, 2014). It is furthermore well known, that the 
two-component regulatory systems are made up of histidine kinases and phosphor-acceptors (Mascher et 
al., 2006), the first of which is affected by nitrogen, as shown in studies on Bacillus subtilis (Atkinson et 
al., 1990; Fouet et al., 1990). Contig_3 contains transcriptional regulators of the AraC, LuxR, and LysR 
families, contig_32-1 contains a two-component regulatory system, and contig_32-2 contains a two-
component regulatory system as well as transcriptional regulators of the AraC and MarR families. All three 
BGCs are thus likely to be affected by nitrogen, however no clear pattern is recognizable in the obtained 
results. While contig_32-2 shows a very significant decrease in expression, contig_32-1, which also 
contains a two-component regulatory system shows no significant results at all, and contig_3 which has the 
AraC regulator in common, only has a significant decrease when the culture is grown with NAG. Hence, 
the two-compound regulatory system in contig_32-1 and contig_32-2 is unlikely to be affected by nitrogen. 
AraC is likely partially affected by high amounts of nitrogen, as in contig_3, the NAG supplemented culture 
is the one to show significant change in expression. NAG is easier to take up and thus more likely to result 
in a higher amount of nitrogen in the cell. However, it is also possible that the LysR or LuxR regulator are 
affected. More research is needed to determine which regulator is affected in this contig. The regulator, 
which seems to be the most affected by nitrogen is likely to be MarR, which is the only regulator contig_32-
2 alone contains. The fold-change within contig_32-2 in NAG is a decrease of around 0.06-fold, which is 
a 10-fold lower value than the fold-change for all other contigs under the same condition.   
For contig_12, there were no regulatory genes annotated within the cluster, which could be affected by the 
addition of nitrogen. In S. marcescens however, the regulatory genes for the production of prodigiosin are 
pigS and pigP (Gristwood et al., 2011). The expression of the pig operon is in turn regulated by a smalR 
quorum sensing locus, which in part controls the transcription of three genes including a response regulator 
of a two-component signaling system (Fineran et al., 2005). While the exact point of interaction of nitrogen 
with the regulatory system is undetermined, influence of nitrogen on prodigiosin has been shown in my 
previous study. During my previous study P. rubra S4059 was grown in media supplemented with mannose 
or NAG and evaluated for bioactivity via clearing zones in an inhibition assay. When comparing the 
coloration of cultures supplemented with mannose or NAG after 12 h of incubation, clear differences were 
seen. While the mannose cultures were bright pink in color, the NAG supplemented cultures were more 
orange-red in color. When ammonium was added to the mannose supplemented culture to balance the 
nitrogen amount, the same color change was observed. This change in pigmentation indicated some 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
 
43 
 
interference of nitrogen with the BGC of prodigiosin, as pigments are secondary metabolites. Furthermore, 
when the cultures were streaked on MA, the colonies displayed the typical dark red coloration.  
When comparing NAG to chitin and linking their growth rate and structures to expression, the nitrogen 
dependent expression hypothesis seems likely. If the uptake rate is drastically different between these 
carbon sources, the amount of nitrogen influencing the system would differ as well. Chitin, which needs to 
be broken down and processed, might not be taken up in the same amount as NAG, which is easily 
accessible. Thus the concentration of nitrogen in the bacterial system should be higher in NAG 
supplemented cultures, as larger amounts can be taken up. The results of the relative expression 
correspondingly show NAG decreasing expression more significantly than to chitin.  
4.2 Analyzing gene functionality in biosynthetic gene clusters for potential target 
identification  
In this study a total of 28 BGCs with 13 complete BGCs were found for P. rubra S4059. This is a rather 
high number of BGCs, with other chitin utilizing microorganisms such as Vibrio coralliilyticus S2052 and 
Photobacterium galatheae S2753 isolated during the same cruise having 7 and 13 BGCs, respectively 
(Giubergia et al., 2017).  
Biosynthetic gene clusters contain several genes with different functions. These include genes encoding for 
transporters, regulatory genes, and biosynthetic genes among others. Bioinformatics-based tools such as 
antiSMASH give good overviews as to which functional enzymes are included. Using these functions, 
predictions about the BGC product can be made.  
Transcriptional regulators are important to note, as they can give insight into potential elucidation factors 
for BGCs. The necessary addition of specific carbon sources, co-factors, or other environmental cues can 
often be deducted from analysis of these regulatory genes. In this study, four regulatory gene families were 
found in the four studied BGCs – AraC transcriptional regulator, LuxR transcriptional regulator, LysR 
transcriptional regulator, and MarR transcriptional regulator. They are transcriptional regulators with DNA-
binding domains and represent 6 % of the regulatory genes (Gallegos et al., 1997).  Additionally a few two 
component system response regulators, histidine kinase sensors and response regulators, CheY-like 
receiver and HD-GYP domains, and CheV chemotaxis regulators were found.  
Transporters can give hints as to what kind of compound is produced, as they often encode for antibiotic 
resistance mechanisms such as efflux pumps (Alanjary et al., 2017). They could also give insight into the 
potential extraction methods, as these transporters export the compound to the extracellular space (Webber 
& Piddock, 2003). The transporters found in this study included drug resistance transporter EmrB/QacA, 
homoserine/threonine efflux pumps, acriflavine resistance proteins, membrane fusion proteins of RND 
==PAGE:51==
 
 
 
44 
 
family multidrug efflux pump, Co/Zn/Cd efflux system membrane fusion proteins, MATE efflux family 
proteins, and ABC transporter ATP-binding proteins.  
 
Contig_3 contains regulatory genes of the AraC-, LuxR-, and LysR-family. Members of the AraC 
transcriptional regulator family have previously been found to regulate genes of diverse functionalities, 
including carbon metabolism, stress response, and even virulence (Gallegos et al., 1997). These regulators 
are regulated by carbon sources such as arabinose, thus the addition of arabinose to the culture could result 
in an increased expression. It could furthermore be interesting to investigate this BGC in co-culture with 
another organism to induce LuxR, which plays a role in quorum sensing. Functions of these genes range 
from encoding virulence factors and biosynthesis of antibiotics, over motility, modulation, plasmid transfer 
and bioluminescence, up to biofilm formation (reviewed by Chen & Xie, 2011). In this study a maximum 
cell density of 108 CFU/mL was reached, which should already induce the LuxR family transcriptional 
regulators to a certain degree (Fuqua et al., 1994). Another option to induce this regulatory gene is the 
addition of autoinducers such as N-(3-oxohexanoyl) homoserine lactone, N-butyryl-L-homoserine lactone, 
and N-(3-hyrdobutanoyl) homserine lactone (Cao & Meighen, 1989; Eberhard et al., 1981; Pearson et al., 
1994). A comparison between a high density culture and a low density culture could give insight into the 
importance of this regulator. Activation of the LysR transcriptional regulator, which is regulating genes 
responsible for virulence, cell division, quorum sensing, nitrogen fixation, stress responses, toxin 
production, attachment and secretion, metabolism, quorum sensing, and motility, could be achieved by the 
addition of diaminopimelate or other co-factors (reviewed by Maddocks & Oyston, 2008). Contig_3 is 
interesting to investigate further, as it is annotated as “Other” by antiSMASH, and could potentially hold 
novel chemistry. For the isolation of the compound produced from the BGC in contig_3, one could analyze 
the cell lysate, as the BGC does not include any transporters, leading to the hypothesis, that the compound 
is intracellular. On the other hand, the lack of transporters could indicate that the product is not an antibiotic, 
as commonly the antibiotic resistance mechanism is encoded within the same BGC (Alanjary et al., 2017). 
 
Contig_12 on the other hand, which contains the BGC for prodigiosin, has no regulatory genes, but contains 
two transporter genes - drug resistance transporter EmrB/QacA, and a homoserine/threonine efflux pump. 
With the EmrB/QacA drug resistance transporter, resistance to tetracyclins and methylenomycin is 
mediated (Rouch et al., 1990). Prodigiosin however, which is made up of a pyrrolyl pyrromethene skeleton, 
would likely not be affected by this transporter as the structure shares no similarity to tetracyclins or 
methylenomycin. Prodigiosin is made up of several amino acids including proline serine, and methionine, 
and could thus potentially be affected by the homoserine/threonine efflux pump. The homoserine/threonine 
efflux pump belongs to the lysine exporter superfamily of transmembrane proteins, which focus on the 
==PAGE:52==
 
 
 
45 
 
excretion of amino acids, lipids, and heavy metal ions (Tsu & Saier, 2015). Transporters hint at bioactivity, 
and Prodigiosin has been found to be a bioactive compound, which works as an antibiotic against 
Staphylococcus aureus, S. saprophyticus, Aeromonas hydrophila, Proteus mirabilis, and Klebsiella 
pneumonia. It has furthermore been found effective as an antimalarial and anticancerous agent (Darshan & 
Manonmani, 2015).  
 
Contig_32-1 contains the BGC for a NRPS type compound. The BGC includes a CheY-like receiver 
domain with HD-GYP domain. CheY-like receiver domains (REC) are often found in combination with 
histidine kinases and other enzymatic domains, protein- or ligand-binding domains, or RNA-binding 
domains (Galperin, 2006). It is suggested that REC domains are involved in the bacterial transcription 
regulation, enzymatic activity, and protein-protein interactions in response to environmental stressors.  The 
transporters in the cluster include an acriflavine and RND family multidrug efflux pump, along with a heavy 
metal efflux system, and a MATE efflux family protein. The RND pumps are primarily found in Gram-
negative bacteria. They are believed to protect the bacteria against hydrophobic inhibitors (Ma et al., 1993). 
The efflux system affects many of the currently used antibiotics as well as disinfectants, dyes, detergents, 
and solvents (Zgurskaya & Nikaido, 1999). The pump works by proton-motive force exchanging an H+ ion 
for one molecule of the compound (Paulsen, 2003).  The MATE efflux pump family is dominant in Gram-
positive bacteria and utilizes two transport driving forces: sodium ion gradient driven transport and proton-
motive force. The best known MATE efflux pump is NorM from the Gram-negative bacterium Vibrio 
parahaemolyticus, which gives resistance against norfloxacin, ethidium bromide, some aminoglycosides 
(Kuroda & Tsuchiya, 2009). Other substrates, which are targeted, are fluoroquinolones, DNA-intercalating 
dyes, and detergents (Piddock, 2006). The produced compound of contig_32-1 could thus be of interest, as 
these transporters all hint at a resistance mechanism. However, the predicted structure is made up of the 
two amino acids valine and alanine, and these are not primarily targeted by any of these transporters. Still, 
the extraction of this compound should focus on the medium, as the compound would likely be excreted in 
some way.  
Contig_32-2 is contains the BGC for an “Other” type compound, which has 40 % similarity to the bioactive 
pigment indigoidine, and contains both regulatory and transport related genes. Thus it should be possible 
to induce it through one of the regulatory mechanisms, with the transporters hinting at bioactivity. The 
regulatory genes include an AraC transcriptional regulator, which could be induced by the addition of 
arabinose to the medium. Furthermore, one MarR transcriptional regulator family regulator was found, 
which regulates genes involved in stress responses, virulence, and degradation or export of harmful 
chemicals including phenolic compounds, antibiotics, and detergents. MarR proteins respond to small 
molecule ligands and environmental stressors, which induce a conformational change resulting in the 
==PAGE:53==
 
 
 
46 
 
activation of transcription (Grove, 2013).  The transporters include efflux pumps from the RND family and 
the ABC family. ABC transporters are found in both Gram-positive and Gram-negative bacteria and utilize 
ATP to facilitate the transport of cytotoxic compounds across the membranes (Dawson & Locher, 2006; 
Lankat-Buttgereit & Tampé, 1999). Both these families focus on the export of drugs through proton-motive 
force and ATP hydrolysis, respectively. Thus the addition of H+ or ATP to the medium could result in an 
increased export (Paulsen, 2003). If the compound produced by this BGC is indeed indigoidine, the 
presence of the transporters makes sense, as indigoidine has been shown to inhibit other microorganisms, 
including V. fischeri, V. anguillarum, and Ruegeria lacuscaerulensis (Cude et al., 2012). The pigment has 
furthermore been found to support bacteria of the Roseobacter clade in their attachment to higher organisms 
and in biofilm formation (Cude et al., 2012). Biofilms have been shown to be inducers of the secondary 
metabolism, forming a specialized and highly competitive niche (Sengupta et al., 2013). Hence, the 
introduction of P. rubra S4059 into a co-culture system could lead to interesting results. 
   
Conclusively, when looking at the antiSMASH cluster composition, analyses of transporters and regulatory 
genes could help on the path to elucidation and identification of secondary metabolites. It could furthermore 
help in the identification of potentially interesting targets.   
4.3 Are silent gene clusters really silent? 
Since the early 2000s, the elicitation of silent gene clusters has been an intensely studied topic in 
microbiology and ecology. With the current standard of laboratory equipment and chemical analysis, it is 
not surprising, that many new compounds have been identified (Mubarak et al., 2017). However, the current 
detection limit of compound in the  range of  ng/L might still not be low enough and miss out on poorly 
expressed compounds (Hu et al., 2018; Wang et al., 2018; Zhu et al., 2018). With that in mind, a recent 
study suggests that silent gene clusters may not be quite as silent as previously thought, based on 
transcriptomics analysis in combination with LC-MS (Amos et al., 2017). In this study, the primer sets for 
13 BGCs were designed and then run on cDNA, which had been synthesized from RNA extracted from P. 
rubra S4059 cultures supplemented with mannose, chitin, or NAG. The PCR products were run on a 3 % 
agarose gel and stained with GelRed, with bands appearing for every sample (primer on cDNA) of each 
carbon source. While differences in the strength of the bands were found, the results in this study do seem 
to support the study by Amos et al (2017) in saying that silence is potentially less common than thought. 
However, with the lack of the absolute quantification of the molecules of DNA for the individual genes in 
this study, it is impossible to say anything about the amount of template and thus detection limits for 
compounds produced by this strain. The absolute quantification of the reference genes showed a maximum 
of 0.02 ng template, with most samples containing less than 0.01 ng of template. Thus the pg/L range would 
==PAGE:54==
 
 
 
47 
 
be required for a more appropriate quantification of low concentration compounds. While methods such as 
RT-qPCR are highly sensitive and allow for absolute and relative quantification, it doesn’t support the 
identification of the compounds. LC-MS on the other hand, which has been used in several studies including 
Amos et al. (2017) and Bibi et al. (2017), is able to detect many different compounds on a ng/L scale and 
elucidate structures. The combination of LC-MS and transcriptomic data is a powerful coupling, being able 
to reveal potential silencing mechanisms, as shown in the study by Amos et al. (2017), where they found 
mobile genetic elements, upstream regulatory elements, and partial expression as the causes of BGC 
silencing. An investigation of the P. rubra S4059 draft genome in regard to these factors could give further 
insights into the BGC regulation and expression.

5. Conclusion and perspectives 
Expression of BGCs and identification of novel chemistry of pharmaceutical relevance has been a hot topic 
in the past years, with special focus on the elucidation of silent BGCs. The aim of the study was to 
investigate the effect of carbon sources on the expression of BGCs in P. rubra S4059 using RT-qPCR. Of 
the 28 predicted BGCs, primer sets were designed for 13 complete BGCs of different types including the 
two pigments prodigiosin and indigoidine.   
When analyzing the PCR products of the designed primers run on cDNA, bands were seen for all genes in 
question. This raises the question if silent BGCs are silent by default, or if other regulatory mechanisms 
outside the BGC play a bigger role than previously thought. With recent studies starting to identify mobile 
genetic elements, regulatory elements outside the BGC, and partial expression of the BGCs as the causes 
of silence, it is clear that more research is needed to settle this debate.  
In this study, three carbon sources were compared in regard to their effect on the expression of BGCs, with 
growth of P. rubra S4059 on the nitrogen containing carbon sources chitin and NAG resulting in a 
decreased expression of the BGCs compared to mannose. While the absolute quantification could only be 
completed for the reference genes, the relative quantification was able to give insights into the expression 
of the 4 chosen BGCs in regard to the carbon sources. Here significant differences were seen between 
mannose and NAG, with less significant results between mannose and chitin. Overall, it seemed that the 
nitrogen in NAG and chitin was interfering with the expression of BGCs, resulting in the highest expression 
being found in the mannose samples. While nitrogen availability is an environmental factor, it wasn’t the 
original starting point for this study. It does however show that composition does matter and that it 
influences expression levels. When investigating the regulatory genes of the BGCs, many regulatory genes 
were found, which can be influenced by the amount of nitrogen in the system.  
On a similar note, analysis of BGCs can give insight into the relevance of the product in that the presence 
of transport related genes could hint at bioactivity. Specifically the efflux pumps and secretory pathway 
related genes should be taken into consideration, as naturally produced antibiotics can generally be seen as 
small, secreted molecules with  a bioactive profile (Baquero et al., 2008; Martínez, 2008). Both pigments 
investigated in this study – prodigiosin and indigoidine – had several transport related genes in their BGC 
and are both known to exhibit antibiotic properties.   
Due to the time limitations, only 4 BGCs were investigated, leaving 9 more to look into. One which could 
be especially interesting is the BGC in contig_9-2 (NRPS), as the bands of the PCR products of the primers 
on cDNA showed strong differences, with a strong band for mannose, a faint band for NAG, and almost no 
band for chitin. Another argument for investigating this cluster is that antiSMASH was able to predict a 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
 
49 
 
structure for the product and the BGC contains transporter as well as regulatory genes, which could help in 
overexpression and identification of the product.  
Future research could also investigate the expression of BGCs in systems containing two carbon sources 
e.g. mannose and NAG, as this would be the next step towards natural environments with complex mixtures 
of substrates (Kovarova-Kovar & Egli, 1998). Other carbon sources (e.g. arabinose), which could act as 
inducers, could be tested individually and in mixed-carbon source systems as well. All in all, there is a lot 
more research to be done on this highly interesting strain.
