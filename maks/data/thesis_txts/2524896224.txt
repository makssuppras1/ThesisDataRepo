Abstract
Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) is the leading cause of death in developed countries and con-
stitutes a major social and economic burden. The underlying mechanism for CAD involves
atherosclerotic plaque build-up in the arteries supplying the cardiac muscle with blood. The
plaques cause narrowing of the vessel lumen which restricts the blood ow to the heart and can
lead to a range of diseases including heart attack.
The management of CAD patients include assessment of the coronary arteries for establishment
of a risk prole to determine the course of action for the patient. The assessment includes
determining the presence and degree of stenosis (lumen narrowing) as well as the plaque type.
In a clinical setting, visual inspection and quantication of medical images is currently the
standard procedure for assessing the coronary arteries.
This is a time-consuming task and
suers from large interobserver variability.
This project utilizes deep learning techniques for automated assessment of the coronary arteries
from Computed Tomography Angiography (CTA) data. The proposed method makes use of
a Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) for segmentation of the coronary artery lumen. The
obtained segmentations are propagated across slices to maintain spatial consistency, while uti-
lizing the strengths of 2D processing. The proposed model obtains a performance comparable
to that of the expert's, and the network is capable of correctly excluding plaque from the lumen
segmentation. The segmentation framework is also applied in a multitask setting for simultane-
ous lumen segmentation, plaque detection and quantication as well as stenosis detection and
quantication. The proposed pipeline does not obtain results comparable to the expert's for
the plaque and stenosis tasks, but has a substantially higher training eciency compared to
the corresponding single-task models.
To provide an automated assessment of the stenosis, it is proposed to utilize the promising
segmentation results directly for stenosis quantication. The obtained segmentations can be
compared to the estimated healthy lumen diameter to determine the degree of stenosis. The
development of this system can potentially aid clinicians, limit the resources spend on this task
and avoid the interobserver variability.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) is the most common type of Cardiovascular disease and is the
leading cause of death in developed countries. In addition to having an extremely high mortality,
CAD can also lead to signicant disability and reduced quality of life, making the disease a
major social and economic burden [1]. The underlying mechanism for CAD involves vascular
injury with plaque build-up in the coronary arteries, called atherosclerosis.
Atherosclerotic
plaque lead to narrowing of the vessel lumen (called stenosis) which restricts the bloodow to
the myocardium (cardiac muscle).
In order to determine how to manage patients with CAD, the presence and degree of stenosis
as well as the plaque type must be determined. This will enable establishment of a risk prole
to determine the course of action for the patient. In a clinical setting, visual inspection and
quantication is currently the standard procedure when assessing coronary arteries. This task
is very time-consuming for the medical sta and suers from large interobserver variability
[2, 3].
Computed Tomography Angiography (CTA) is gaining popularity as a non-invasive
technique for providing high-resolution 3D images of the coronary arteries.
This modality
enables assessment of the presence, extent and type of atherosclerotic plaques [4].
In medical imaging, semantic segmentation is the task of assigning a label to each pixel or
voxel, often corresponding to dierent tissue classes, organs, etc. This area has been studied
extensively as it holds great potential for automated processing of the large amounts of data
provided by medical imaging modalities.
Deep Learning (DL) has become an increasingly
popular method in the medical image analysis eld, and provides state-of-the-art performance
in many biomedical image segmentation tasks [5]. Automated segmentation of the Coronary
Artery (CA) lumen as well as assessment of presence and quantication of stenosis and plaque
would greatly benet the medical sta. It can reduce the amount of time and resources spent
on this task along with avoiding interobserver variability. This task has not yet been solved,
and it is therefore highly desirable to develop an automated procedure for reliable assessment
of the coronary arteries to detect or rule out CAD.
The purpose of this project is to use deep learning techniques to automatically assess CTA data
as a step towards aiding clinicians in detecting or ruling out CAD. In order to be utilized in
a clinical setting, the nal assessment should comprise of reliable and accurate detection and
quantication of CA stenosis and plaque as well as visualization of the CA lumen. A 2D Con-
volutional Neural Network (CNN) is utilized to obtain an accurate and reliable segmentation
of the CA lumen. Segmentation of the vessel lumen is of special interest, because the lumen
represents the part of the blood vessel which is perfused, and presence of stenosis or plaque elim-
inates perfusion. The lumen volume will therefore be reduced if plaque or stenosis is present.
Obtaining an accurate segmentation of the lumen may therefore provide information about

the presence and extent of stenosis and plaque. Recent methods for obtaining spatial consis-
tency between the 2D segmentations will be applied and the resulting segmentations evaluated.
The segmentation framework is utilized in a multitask neural network for simultaneous lumen
segmentation, plaque detection and quantication as well as stenosis detection and grading. Fi-

single-task networks, to assess whether multitask learning enabled a more robust analysis of

1.1
Related work
For automated assessment of CA stenoses and plaque lesions, dierent approaches can be uti-
lized. Since these lesions reduce the CA lumen (the perfused area), a commonly used method
involves obtaining a segmentation of the lumen and utilizing this segmentation to predict the
location and extent of stenoses. State-of-the-art methods for CA segmentation are presented
below.
1.1.1
Segmentation
Currently, the majority of commercially available software packages for CA lumen segmentation
require signicant manual interaction, especially in presence of imaging artifacts or atheroscle-
rotic plaques [2]. Many attempts have been made toward automatic vessel segmentation, how-
ever the majority of the proposed methods focus on healthy vessels and fail to segment the
lumen in presence of atherosclerotic plaques [6]. Although various studies have dealt with this
task, the resulting lumen boundary delineation is still not suitable for clinical use [7, 8].
CA lumen segmentation from CTA data was one of the objectives for the participants in the
Rotterdam Stenosis Detection challenge [9]. The best segmentation performance among the
participants in 2012 was obtained by Mohr et al. [10], who utilized a level-set approach based
on the results of a calcium segmentation and tissue classication step. They achieved a mean
Dice score of 0.715. A model-guided segmentation approach based on Markov Random Fields
was utilized in [6], applying priors to ensure the tubular structure of the lumen was kept. This
method holds the best performance with a mean Dice coecient of 0.755. The same authors
also applied a learning-based boundary detector together with a calcium exclusion step and
surface optimization, achieving a mean Dice of 0.735 in [11].

2.1.2
Coronary artery disease
Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) is the leading cause of death and disability in developed coun-

leading to insucient blood supply to the cardiomyocytes. This can cause a range of diseases

2.1.2.1
Atherosclerosis
Atherosclerosis is the most common cause of CAD. It is a result of accumulation of fatty
materials and formation of plaque, which leads to narrowing and hardening of the arteries.
The narrowing of a vessel is also referred to as stenosis. Atherosclerosis is present to some
degree in all teens and adults and progresses with age. It is a complex disorder, but major risk
factors include age, male gender, family history, smoking, obesity and hypertension [21]. The
atherosclerotic lesions can be divided into three categories:
• Fatty streaks are thin, yellow lines running along major arteries and consist of smooth
muscle cells lled with cholesterol and macrophages (a type of white blood cell). The
streaks do not produce symptoms but can progress into the more advanced stages.
• Fibrous plaques are characterized by accumulation of fat, proliferation of smooth muscle
cells, scar tissue formation and calcication. The lesions are covered by a brous cap. As

plaque is ruptured. This causes bleeding (hemorrhage), ulceration and further scar tissue

The plaques are composed of a mixture of fat, cholesterol, smooth muscle cells, macrophages,

Projection radiography
Projection Radiography encompass the modalities generating medical images using X-rays.
The following sections provide an introduction to conventional X-ray images and Computed
Tomography (CT).
2.2.1
Conventional X-ray images
In X-ray imaging, an X-ray beam is transmitted through the patient undergoing examination.
The X-ray beam is continuously attenuated as it propagates through the tissues of the body,
due to scattering and absorption. The result of an X-ray scan is a two-dimensional image of the
attenuated intensities, and can be viewed as a projection of the three-dimensional body-area
onto the two-dimensional image. There are many advantages of this image modality, including
low radiation exposure, low cost and very good contrast and spatial resolution. A considerable
disadvantage is that it does not provide 3D information since there is no depth resolution. This
means that the image is a result of superimposed tissues and it might therefore not capture all
the relevant information [24].
A conventional projection radiography system consists of an X-ray tube which generates a beam
of X-rays to be transmitted through the patient. The X-rays are ltered to prevent low-energy
photons from entering the body. These would be fully absorbed in the tissues and therefore
only contribute to radiation dose and not to the image itself.
A collimator (often two are
placed consecutively) is used to restrict the beam to avoid both imaging artifacts and radiation
exposure at areas of the patient that do not need to be imaged. A detector plate captures
the rays after they have passed through the body, and the attenuated intensities are used
to construct the X-ray image. The amount of attenuation depends on the linear attenuation
coecients of the tissues that the rays pass through. Since scattered X-rays create noise in the
image, a scatter-reducing grid is placed immediately before the detectors, removing photons
which are not travelling in a straight line between the X-ray tube and detectors [24].
2.2.2

CT is an imaging procedure where X-rays are used to generate detailed images of the inside
of the body. As mentioned above, conventional X-ray represents a projection of a 3D object
onto a 2D image, and thus suers from artifacts from overlay of multiple tissues. The great
advantage of CT is that the modality eliminates these artifacts. This is done by recording
multiple 2D projections of the 3D area of interest and doing so from many dierent angles, so
that each projection contains dierent information. This is done by moving the X-ray tube and

2.3
Deep learning methods
Deep Learning (DL) is a machine learning eld in which algorithms called neural networks learn
from large amounts of data. The neural networks are inspired by the structure and function
of neuronal structures in the brain and are organized into hierarchical layers, so the output of
each layer is given as input to the next layer. The DL models then attempt to approximate
a function which can map the input x to a category y (e.g.
does the input image show a
heart or a kidney). Provided with a large set of input and output pairs, the DL algorithm
attempts to minimize the dierence between its prediction and the expected output. This will
essentially enable the algorithm to learn the relationship between input and output, so it is able
to generalize to new samples.
By utilizing so-called deep neural networks with many layers, the model can represent func-
tions of increasing complexity. This ability has allowed DL methods to outperform traditional
machine learning algorithms in multiple domains such as speech recognition and image classi-
cation. Such tasks often have high-dimensional data, which makes the learning exceedingly
dicult. For traditional machine learning approaches, this often requires an expert to identify
features, in order to reduce the dimensionality of the data. They may also require incorpo-
ration of prior knowledge in order to generalize well [26]. DL methods do not rely on feature
engineering or prior knowledge. However, the high performance of these algorithms do require
very large amounts of data and high computational power.

The feed-forward neural network is the most basic DL model, where information is only prop-
agated forward through the layers of the network. The feed-forward neural network consists of
an input layer, one or more hidden layers and an output layer, with each layer being composed
of so-called nodes. The input signal is then propagated from the input layer through the hidden
layers and to the output layer where the nal prediction is provided as output. When each node
in one layer is connected to every node in the subsequent layer, the layers are referred to as Fully
Connected (FC) layers. For an extensive review of DL and the mathematical concepts behind
these methods, the reader is referred to [26, 27]. A special kind of feed-forward neural network
is the Convolutional Neural Network (CNN), which is designed for processing of images.
2.3.1
Convolutional neural networks
Semantic segmentation is the task of assigning a label to each pixel or voxel in the image data,
for example with the purpose of segmenting dierent tissue classes, organs, etc. In medical
imaging, this task has been studied extensively, as manual inspection of such vast amounts of
data is both tedious and time-demanding. The Convolutional Neural Network (CNN) is a type
of neural network which employs convolutions for handling images, and is capable of performing
semantic segmentation. The following section focuses on the considerations related to network
choices. It is assumed that the reader has a basic knowledge of key concepts and operations
such as convolution, pooling, backpropagation, optimization, overtting etc.
Otherwise, an
extensive review can be found in [26, 27].
The CNN architecture is usually the preferred choice in image analysis tasks, because it is
specialized for handling image data. As opposed to networks with only FC layers, the CNN
takes into account the spatial information of the image. The network functions as an auto-
matic feature extractor, and produces a set of feature maps for each convolutional layer. The
features learned in the rst, shallow layers are usually simple shapes (e.g. edges, blobs), while
the features of the deeper layers are more abstract and can be used for more complex repre-
sentations. The properties of CNNs also allow feature detection regardless of the position of
the feature, meaning the network is invariant to translation of objects within the image. The
CNN also requires fewer parameters than networks composed of FC layers, which makes them
much more ecient. The increased eciency allows for faster training of the networks and
enables construction of very deep networks, which have proven ecient for handling images
[27]. The CNN can be used for both image classication tasks (utilizing a nal FC layer for
mapping into a given number of classes) and for semantic segmentation (typically as a fully
convolutional network), and is thus an obvious choice for both of these tasks. The U-net is a
CNN architecture which was developed for semantic segmentation of biomedical images with
great success [13]. The network architecture is shown in Fig. 2.6.
The U-net is composed of an encoder and a decoder. The encoder consists of repeated blocks of
convolutional layers (extracting feature maps) and max pooling operations, which downsample
the feature maps. By repeating these operations, the input image is reduced to a large set of
low-dimensional feature maps. After each convolutional layer, a rectied linear unit (ReLU)
activation function is applied to ensure a non-linear transformation. The ReLU function returns
an output x if x is positive, and 0 otherwise. This activation function is the most commonly
used in modern neural networks [26], and has the advantage (compared to other activation
functions) that it does not saturate when the input becomes large.

2.3.2
Batch normalization
When training a neural network, the gradient indicates how each parameter should be updated,
under the assumption that the other layers do not change. However, in reality all layers are
==PAGE:33==
2.3 Deep learning methods
15
updated simultaneously. This can lead to unexpected and undesirable behaviour, because the
input to each layer is aected by the parameters of all preceding layers, which means that small
changes may amplify through the network. This complicates training of deep neural networks.
More specically, training requires careful parameter initialization, use of lower learning rate
and increased training time [26, 28].
Batch normalization is a reparametrization technique which essentially normalizes the output
of the layer across each batch of images, before the signal is propagated to the next layer. This
stabilizes learning by preventing exploding or vanishing gradients, avoiding saturation of the
activation functions and ensuring robustness towards hyperparameter settings [28]. Santurkar
et al. [29] further argue that batch normalization has a smoothing eect on the underlying
optimization problem, which implies more predictive gradients, leading to more eective op-
timization. The eect of batch normalization allows for use of a higher learning rate, faster
convergence and ensures robustness towards parameter initialization. In addition to this, batch
normalization can also reduce overtting and work as a regularizer, improving generalization
abilities [19, 29].
2.3.3
Multitask learning
Multitask learning is a method in which multiple tasks are learned simultaneously. The intuition
behind a multitask neural network is that the additional information from training related tasks
may boost the performance of the original task. As the signals related to a task will always
contain some noise, the aim is to focus on the relevant features to learn a good representation
of the task (ignoring the task-related noise).
Having additional tasks (with dierent noise
patterns) will eectively allow the network to better distinguish irrelevant and noisy features
from the relevant features, thus improving the ability to generalize. Therefore, the multitask
learning will also act as a regularizer, reducing the risk of overtting [16].
During training, the parameters of the layers related to one task should change according to
how the network performs on this task, but be unaected of how the network performs on other
tasks. The parameters of the shared layers should be updated according to all tasks. This can
be achieved in two ways:
1. In alternate learning, a loss function and an optimizer is dened for each task.
The
training is then performed by alternately calling each optimizer, which will result in only
the parameters related to that optimizer being updated. This approach is usually applied
when there are dierent datasets for dierent tasks. A disadvantage of alternate training
is that learning can become biased towards one task. This will be the case when the
datasets are of dierent sizes: the shared layers will be more tuned to the task with the
larger dataset. Even in the case where the datasets are of equal size, a bias will still exist
towards the nal task which was trained on, as pointed out by [30].
2. Joint training is applied when a single dataset has multiple labels. The losses from each
task are combined and fed to a single optimizer, so the tasks are trained at the same time.
Bias towards a specic task is thus avoided in joint training.

Quality assessment of references
An important limitation of the SD dataset is the inconsistencies for the two provided references

the QCA algorithm may result in incorrect scores [4]. These factors complicate the task, as the
neural network may be trained using an incorrect label from the CTA reference, and therefore
does not learn a correct data representation.
Another limitation of the SD dataset is the quality of the provided lumen contours. As these
are used directly to generate reference labelmaps for the dataset to train the neural network,
poor quality of the lumen contours will result in poor training. Examples of this limitation is
illustrated in Fig. 3.4.
It should be noted that the quality of the CTA image data itself has not been evaluated, as this
would require the expertise of a clinician.

This chapter contains descriptions of the methods which have been applied in this project.
The rst section covers preprocessing of the data.
Then, the extraction of cross-sectional
images of the Coronary Arteries (CAs) using Multiplanar Reconstruction (MPR) is presented.
This is followed by a description of how labels were generated for both datasets. The utilized

the evaluation metrics used for assessing the network performances are described with the

4.1
Preprocessing
The applied preprocessing steps are described in the following.
4.1.1
Coordinate conversion
The provided data is a 3D volume capturing the heart and parts of the surrounding structures.
The area covered by the data volume varies from patient to patient, but is approximately 15-
20 cm in the x and y direction (constituting the axial plane) and approximately 10-15 cm in
the z-direction. The Computed Tomography Angiography (CTA) data was provided as either
DICOM or raw les for the SD and CLE dataset, respectively. Both datasets were preprocessed
by converting centerline positions and lumen contour positions from world coordinates to voxel
coordinates. For this, the provided spacings or voxel sizes (the distance between voxels along
each axis) were used, and the scanner origin was taken into account.
4.1.2
Isotropic resampling
For all scans in both datasets, the voxels were anisotropic (i.e. did not have the same size in
all three dimensions). The mean voxel sizes were [0.32, 0.32, 0.40] mm3 and [0.38, 0.38, 0.37]
mm3 for the x, y, z axes, respectively for the CLE and SD dataset. However, the dierence for
each scan was found to be large with ratios between x,y resolution (which are always the same)
and z resolution down to 0.58. Furthermore, as both datasets arise from dierent scanners
and vendors, the variations in voxel size is also large across scans. For the SD dataset it was
found that while for some scans, the resolution in the x and y direction was smaller than
the resolution in the z direction (as is normally the case), in other cases the reverse was true
==PAGE:44==

with better z-direction resolution than x,y.
This results in one scan having voxel sizes of
[0.2891, 0.2891, 0.4000] and another (in the same dataset) of [0.4290, 0.4290, 0.2500].
The anisotropic voxels become problematic when performing MPR (described in detail below),
as it involves extracting a plane across all three dimensions and structures will look dierent
when rotated in a volume with anisotropic voxels. This also means that features learned by
the neural network (lines, shapes, structures) will look dierent from patient to patient. For
these reasons, it was decided to resample the data to make it isotropic. In order to utilize
both datasets together, they were resampled to have the same voxel size of 0.25. The data was
resampled using linear interpolation.
4.2
Multiplanar reconstruction
The data volume covers a much larger area than is needed, as only the coronary arteries
are of interest. To reduce the amount of data and make the processing less computationally
expensive, it is of interest to extract a small volume around each CA. This can be achieved
with Multiplanar Reconstruction (MPR), which is a visualization technique used in the clinic
for diagnosis of CAD and procedure planning, among other things [25].
The purpose of performing MPR is to reconstruct the medical images in arbitrary planes in
the data volume, creating a stack of 2D images centered around the arteries.
Each image
represents a cross-section of the coronary artery (i.e. each plane lies orthogonal to the direction
of the blood vessel). This will extract a small subset of the entire volume, only containing the
structures of interest. Furthermore, MPR provides better visualization of the artery lumen and
allows identication of non-calcied plaque [2]. The provided centerline coordinates from the
CLE dataset contain information about where in the data volume the CAs are located, and
were used as a starting point for the MPR algorithm. For the SD dataset, the Center of Mass
(CM) from the provided lumen contours were used instead of centerline points.
At each of the centered points, MPR was used to reconstruct a plane through the cross-section
of the vessel. The normal to each of these planes was dened as the vector pointing towards the
following centerline point (or CM). This is an approximation which may have the following lim-
itations: because of the curvature of the vessel, if the sampling points are not located very close,
the direction of the plane might be shifted, resulting in a skewed cross-section. Furthermore, if
the points are not placed exactly at the center of the vessels (e.g. by inaccurate annotations),
the points might zigzag, which will also lead to a skewed cross-section. To compensate for this,
the centerline points or CMs were smoothed by tting a spline to the 3D points [35]. This was
done by tting a 1D spline in each dimension (x,y,z) and combining the results. From the tted
spline, a set of equidistant points were then sampled, from which the MPR planes could be
extracted. An example of the smoothing eect of tting a spline and sampling equidistantly is
shown in Fig. 4.1. With this correction and from visual inspection of the result, it was assessed
that satisfying cross-sections of the vessels would be obtained with this assumption.
To generate the 2D images, a meshgrid of a specied size was created. For both datasets, a grid
with dimensions 208 × 208 was dened, covering an area of 15 × 15 mm. This area was chosen
to be large enough to contain the CAs when they are largest (originating from the aorta) while
still being small enough to capture details when the arteries are smallest and to avoid including

contour be denoted d1 and d2, respectively. Likewise, let the scaled distance from the closest
contour to the sampled point be t1, where the total distance between the two closest contours

distance to the center of the MPR image. For each MPR image, the classication labels were
then a number indicating the plaque type (4 classes) and a number indicating stenosis grade (5
classes).
4.4
Network architectures
The following section contains a description of the dierent network architectures which were
used.
4.4.1
U-net for semantic segmentation
A U-net with an architecture similar to [13] was implemented in TensorFlow [36] with the pur-
pose of performing semantic segmentation of the CA lumen. The u-shaped structure allows the
input image to be reduced to a large set of low-dimensional feature maps, which are then ex-
panded into a binary label image of the resulting segmentation [37]. The proposed architecture
is seen in Fig. 4.11
The network consists of a contracting path and an expansive path, as described in Sect. 2.3.1.
The proposed network utilizes padding with each convolution operation, such that the dimen-
sions of the feature maps is only reduced with the max pooling operations. This eliminates the
need for giving a much larger input image to account for the loss of border pixels during convo-
lutions (as was done in [13]) and reduces the required computational power. Another dierence
from the original U-net is the use of batch normalization. As described in Sect. 2.3.1, batch
normalization allows for use of higher learning rate, faster convergence and robustness towards
parameter initialization. Therefore, batch normalization was utilized after each convolutional
layer.
Dropout was applied as a regularization method to prevent overtting of the model to the
training data.
In classical/standard dropout, a randomly selected fraction of (non-output)
nodes is removed in each training iteration.
This essentially corresponds to training many
dierent networks and averaging the eects of these. Assuming that these dierent models
overt to the training data in dierent ways, the eect of dropout will reduce overtting.
Another advantage of dropout is that the individual nodes of the network cannot rely on the
presence of other nodes, and therefore must be able to perform well regardless of which other
nodes are present. As a consequence, the nodes learn more robust features, which are useful in
many dierent contexts (i.e. with many subsets of units) [26, 27]. However, standard dropout
may not be the best choice when dealing with CNNs, as argued by the authors of [38]. Because
images have a high spatial correlation, the units of the feature map will also be correlated.

Dropping out random units therefore has little or no eect, as the information is essentially

where entire feature maps are dropped instead of individual pixels within the feature maps.
By dropping out the entire feature map, the issue with adjacent pixels is eliminated, as these
are all zeroed out. For this reason, spatial dropout was chosen over standard dropout. This
method helped achieve independence between feature maps, which is in line with the original
purpose of dropout. Spatial dropout was utilized after each block of convolutions.

The SD dataset contains labels for classication of plaque type and stenosis grade, as described

labels for all three tasks for each input image, making joint training possible, and it was therefore
chosen over alternate training. The proposed architecture is inspired by [15, 17], both of whom

4.5
Training and testing strategy
The following contains a description of the methods used for training and evaluating the models.
4.5.1
Normalization of images
The regular 2D U-net processes each slice individually, so each input image was normalized to
have values between −1 and 1. This ensured that each image had the largest possible range
of values. For the contextual U-net taking the adjacent image into account, this normalization
choice was not considered appropriate, as the image intensities might vary signicantly between
images. Instead the normalization was performed over an entire vessel or segment. This way,
correlation between consecutive MPR images was ensured.
4.5.2
Randomization
Training was done in minibatches of size 8, meaning that at each iteration the network receives
and predicts on 8 images before weights are updated. This allows for faster convergence and
more robust training, compared to updating weights after each input image. In order to avoid
overtting, the training data was completely randomized.
This was achieved by randomly
selecting 8 images from the entire training set for each minibatch iteration. All training images
were trained on exactly once during each epoch.
For the contextual U-net, it would be most intuitive to process each segment sequentially, as
the predictions of the current image depend on the prediction on the image directly above.
This ensures that for each epoch, the most recent prediction is always given as input (except
for the rst image in each segment, which will always receive a null mask). However, the data
would no longer be completely randomized, and the model would be at risk of overtting to each
segment being processed. This reduces the models' ability to generalize, as it is overtted to the
last segment it is trained on. Therefore, it was decided to maintain complete randomization,
so at each iteration, the minibatch is composed of randomly drawn images from the entire
training set. The consequence of this is that the contextual input might contain the predicted
segmentation from the previous epoch, instead of the predictions from the current epoch. This
is illustrated in Fig. 4.15.
The dashed box is the image being processed and the blue boxes indicate images which have
already been seen by the network for the current epoch e, and have therefore resulted in an
updated contextual input which can be fed to the slice immediately below. For example, Si−2
has been processed so Mi−2 has been updated and is given as input when Si−1 is processed.
However, since Si−1 has not yet been processed, the contextual input Mi−1 given to Si contains
the predictions from the previous epoch e −1.
An alternative to updating the contextual input during an epoch would be to store all the
predictions generated during the epoch, and then updating them all at the end of the epoch.
This would ensure that at all iterations, the received contextual input is from the previous
epoch (as opposed to some receiving the current predictions and some receiving the previous
predictions).
However, it was assessed that this would not inuence the nal performance

For the multitask neural network, the goal is to optimize on all three tasks simultaneously using
joint training, as described in Sect. 2.3.3. To achieve this, the three cross-entropy losses must be
combined to a single loss, to be optimized. The losses could simply be added together. However,
if the magnitude of one of the losses is much smaller than the others, this task will essentially be
ignored. This was observed to be the case, with the magnitude of the segmentation loss being
much smaller than the magnitude of the losses for the classication tasks. To compensate for

this, an additional hyperparameter, λ, was introduced, scaling the segmentation loss to be in
the same order of magnitude as the classication losses. The two classication tasks had losses
of similar size, and were therefore not scaled individually.
The Adam algorithm was used for optimization, with a learning rate of 0.0001. Adam diers
from standard stochastic gradient descent in that it maintains a learning rate for each network
parameter (as opposed to one learning rate for all parameters) and adapts them throughout
training. This improves performance on problems with sparse gradients (e.g. computer vi-
sion problems). This algorithm is widely used and performs well on many problems, and was
therefore utilized [40]. Due to the large size of the input images, the minibatch size was kept
relatively low, as mentioned above. As a result, the network parameters are updated based on
a small number of images at each iteration. The network is then at risk of learning to model
the details and noise in the images, rather than being able to generalize. To avoid this, a high
momentum (β1 = 0.9 and β2 = 0.999) was used with the Adam optimizer, ensuring that the
update at each iteration was determined by a large number of previously seen images.
4.5.4
Class imbalance for the segmentation task
For the segmentation task, there is a signicant class imbalance problem, meaning there is a
much larger number of pixels belonging to the background class than there are pixels belonging
to the lumen class. This is mainly due to the CA vessels being large at the proximal end (closest
to the aorta) from where they narrow and become increasingly smaller. The extracted MPR
images, however, all cover the same area, and must be large enough to capture the entire cross-
section throughout the full length of the vessel. This results in an increasing class imbalance in
the MPR images as the vessel becomes smaller.
The consequence of the class imbalance is that the neural network can classify all pixels as
background and still obtain a very small cross-entropy loss. To solve this issue, weight maps
giving higher weights to the minority class were pre-computed from the reference segmentation.
The weight maps were constructed by computing the ratio Rlumen between the number of pixels
belonging to the lumen class and the total number of pixels in the image. The class weights are
then [Rlumen, 1 −Rlumen] for the background class and lumen class, respectively. This gives a
proportionally smaller weight to the background class, and a correspondingly larger weight to
the lumen class. A weight map was then constructed, with the background class-weight at the
pixels corresponding to background, and the lumen class-weight at the pixels corresponding to
lumen. The weight maps then scales the cross-entropy loss from Eq. 4.2, resulting in a higher
loss if lumen pixels are misclassied, and a lower loss if background pixels are misclassied.
4.5.5
Data split
The available data is split into three groups: training data, validation data and test data. The
training data is used for training the model and given as input together with the labels, to allow
the model to learn from the annotated examples. The validation data can be used to monitor the
training. The network generates predictions on these examples, but is not allowed to train on
them (i.e. update model parameters). The validation set is used to tune the hyperparameters of
the model and, most importantly, to perform early stopping as a regularization technique. Early

stopping is used to stop training when the validation loss begins to increase, as it is a sign that
the model is overtting to the training data (so it becomes increasingly better at predicting the
training examples, but loses the ability to generalize to new examples). Because the validation
set is used to make model choices, a separate test set consisting solely of previously unseen
examples was used for assessing the performance of the nal model.
The data split can be performed on image-level, segment-level or patient-level. On image level, a
random subset of images would be selected for the training, validation and test set, respectively.
However, adjacent images must be assumed to have a very high correlation, which results in
an articially high performance of the network, as the network is almost always guaranteed
to have seen images very similar to the test images. Another approach would be to select a
subset of vessel segments, so that all images within each segment are part of the same data
set. This reduces the correlation between training and test set, but there would most likely still
be a correlation between segments from the same CTA scan, again yielding an articially high
performance. For this reason, the data split was based on patients. The model was trained on
one subset of patients, validated on another subset, and nally evaluated with a test set from a
third subset of patients. This was assessed to be the most realistic approach, as this resembles
a clinical setting; the model would be trained on a set of annotated CTA scans and used to
assess the results of new patient scans.

4.6.1

such that it matches the class of the reference labelmap. The performance of the segmentation
model has been quantied with the Dice coecient which will be described briey in this section.
The Dice coecient is a similarity measure for two samples (here the output segmentation and
the reference) and measures the spatial overlap between the two samples. It is given as the size
of the intersection of the two sets divided by the sum of the number of elements in each set. It
can be written as:

The Dice coecient has a value between 0 and 1, where 0 corresponds to no overlap and 1
corresponds to perfect overlap. The Dice coecient has been used extensively for evaluation
of semantic segmentation tasks [41]. Furthermore, it has been utilized in the majority of the
articles presented in Sect. 1.1, enabling comparison with the proposed method.

Chapter 6
Discussion
This chapter will cover the following topics: rst, some limitations of the provided data and
labelmap generation methods are discussed. This is followed by an evaluation of the segmen-
tation results for both datasets. Then, the results of the multitask learning application are
discussed and assessed. Finally, some suggestions for future work are presented.
6.1
Data and labelmap limitations
Several obvious data and method limitations exist for this project. This includes mismatches
between Quantitative Coronary Angiography (QCA) and Computed Tomography Angiography
(CTA) reference les (human error) and image modality limitations, such as the blooming
artifact causing enlargement of the appearance of calcications. Another signicant limitation
was mentioned in Chap. 3 and deals with the small size of the CAs relative to the voxel size. This
is a signicant limitation of both datasets, and prolonging the CTA scanning time should be
considered in order to obtain smaller voxel size (i.e. better image resolution). Other limitations
of the provided data and preprocessing methods are discussed in the following.
6.1.1
Evaluation of lumen contours and labelmaps
The utilization of the lumen contour Center of Mass (CM) as the CA centerline point was
assumed to be unproblematic, as it was expected that the location of the CMs would coincide
with the centerline points.
This assumption was made based on the annotation procedure
described in [4], where all observers annotated the lumen contours based on the centerline
(dened as the line passing through the CM of the lumen). However, Fig. 5.1 shows a large
deviation between the two lines, especially at the location of 'Plaque 1'.
The deviation is
likely caused by poor annotations (human error) and possibly arises from the fact that the
lumen contours were mainly evaluated in the longitudinal direction, making it hard to detect
centerline/CM mismatches in cross-sections.
Regardless of the cause of this inconsistency, it indicates that the quality of the provided lumen
contours is less than optimal. This was further illustrated in Fig. 3.4 in Sect. 3.2, where examples
of contours of poor quality were provided. In these cases, the resulting labelmap may reect
the lumen very poorly. This might be the case in Fig. 5.14a, where a calcied plaque is located
towards the right side of the contrast enhanced tissue at the center of the image. This is seen
as a small area of the tissue having a higher intensity than the rest of the vessel. The labelmap
indicates the location of the vessel lumen (i.e. the perfused area), and should therefore not
==PAGE:94==
76
Discussion
include the plaque. However, the labelmap appears to be shifted towards the plaque, so that it
is located at the center of the contrast enhanced tissue, instead of towards the left. This could
be caused by poor quality of the annotations.
Another possible explanation of the displaced labelmap is that it arises from tting a spline
to the CMs. In the presence of plaque, the lumen contour CMs will be shifted away from the
plaque in the cross-section. However, the smoothing eect of the spline may move the CM back
towards the plaque, resulting in an inaccurate labelmap position. The application of the spline
t would then be a trade-o between risking obtaining skewed cross-sectional MPR images
(see Sect. 4.1) and obtaining displaced labelmaps. The actual cause of this potential labelmap
displacement and the possible solution is left to be investigated in future work.
To further assess the labelmaps, Fig. 5.2 shows the surface of the generated label volume for a
full segment, without and with smoothing. Note that while Fig. 5.2a has the tubular structure
of a vessel, the surface is rough and uneven, which is not a common characteristic of a vessel.
This could be caused by poor annotations, or by limitations of the method used to generate
the labelmaps. As indicated in Fig. 4.8 in Sect. 4.3.2, the combination of the two projected
labelmaps may induce some irregularities in the boundary delineation. The smoothed surface
in Fig. 5.2b still contains irregularities, but is generally in much better correspondence with
how a vessel should appear. However, the unsmoothed label volume in Fig. 5.2a was utilized.
Better segmentation results may have been obtained if the smoothing step had been utilized,
especially for the network taking spatial information into account. Nonetheless, smoothing the
surface would also come with a risk of smoothing out e.g. small stenoses or plaques, making
them even harder to detect. As detection of these structures is the main purpose of this project,
it was decided not to smooth the label volume prior to training.
From this section it is apparent that the provided data comes with signicant limitations, espe-
cially from human error in the annotation procedure. Furthermore, the process of interpolating
the annotated lumen contours to obtain a label volume is time-demanding and the utilized
methods come with other limitations. It is therefore assumed that the nal performance of the
model will be aected by these limitations, and that better results might have been obtained if
the provided reference had been more carefully reviewed and annotated as a 3D volume.
6.2
Evaluation of CLE data segmentation
As described in Sect. 4.1, the dataset annotations only contained the lumen radii, preventing
the application of this dataset for detailed lumen segmentation. The purpose of this dataset was
thus to use it for pre-training before segmentation of the SD dataset. Pre-training a network for
segmentation was found to increase performance in a related CA lumen segmentation task [8].
The results presented in this section therefore mainly reects whether the proposed network is
able to segment the coronary artery lumen, so the dataset can be used for providing additional
information.
The mean Dice score on the test set was 0.833 for the regular U-net and 0.837 for the contextual
U-net (Table 5.1). The dierence in performance between the two networks is small, and it was
therefore assessed that adding the contextual information as input did not aid the segmentation
task in this case. For this reason, the results are presented from the regular U-net, as this

network required far less training. The results in Fig. 5.5 indicate that the network performs
well in images with large lumen areas with high contrast, but struggles to detect the lumen
when the vessels are small and the image has low contrast. This is in correspondence with
human performance, as these vessels are also the easiest/hardest to assess visually. In addition
to this, the poor performance on small vessels could also be related to data limitations; as
described in Chap. 3, the centerlines were interpolated if the artery was indistinguishable e.g.
in case of occlusions or artifacts. This means that the artery may only be partially visible in
some images, which would reduce the artery lumen and could explain why the network performs
poorly on images with small lumen areas.
Fig. 5.6 and Fig. 5.7 present examples of special interest. It should be noted that the examples
have not been reviewed by a clinician, and therefore represent the author's assessment of the
data. The example in Fig. 5.6a illustrates the limitation of only having a radius as input to
generate labelmaps.
When assessing the MPR image visually, the lumen shape appears to
be more spade-shaped than circular. The form of the predicted segmentation (green and blue)
captures this shape better than the labelmap (green and purple), but the predicted segmentation
is penalized as it diverges from the reference. This will clearly have a negative eect on the
network performance. The example in Fig. 5.6b could contain a calcied plaque towards the
left of the vessel. The network has detected this as a part of the lumen (most likely due to the
bright intensity), which is not desired. However, as the reference labelmaps do not contain the
sucient amount of detail to indicate location of such plaques, the network has not been able
to learn this, which again underlines the data limitation.
The examples in Fig. 5.7 emphasize the complications when multiple vessels are located in a
single image.
From a performance point of view, it is desirable that the network does not
detect the additional vessels, as the majority of these are not annotated. However, intuitively
the network should be able to detect all vessels in an image, as this would provide the most
medically correct predictions. This again underlines the limitation of the reference. Only a
subset of vessels has been annotated, and un-annotated branches extending from these vessels
might appear in the images. To further complicate the task, an image may actually contain
annotations of two vessels, as in Fig. 5.7c, either if the same vessel has a lot of curvature and
therefore appears in the image at two locations, or if two of the annotated vessels branch o
from each other. The SD dataset contains the same limitations in that only a subset of vessels
is annotated in this dataset as well.
The obtained results show the limitation of the provided data, but overall segmentation of
the lumen on the CLE dataset achieved good results, indicating that this dataset may aid the
segmentation task on the SD dataset. In the following section, the results of performing the
segmentation on the SD dataset are discussed.

data combinations, and is a clear sign of overtting. Had the training been allowed to continue,
the validation loss would keep increasing and the validation performance (Dice, accuracy) would
eventually begin to decrease, while the training performance would keep increasing. The result
would be a model overtted to the training data, unable to generalize well to unseen examples.
Dierent regularization techniques could have been applied to prevent overtting, but were
left for future work (elaborated in Sect. 6.5) because overtting did not occur until late in the
training phase, after the model appeared to be converged. Therefore, it was assessed that it
would be sucient to introduce early stopping for regularization. The network was allowed to
train until signicant overtting was present and afterwards the training and validation curves
were inspected. It was then assessed when the model was converged (before overtting became
signicant) and the model at this point in the training phase was used to make the predictions
presented in this section. All the networks presented in Table 5.1 were trained following this

This is in correspondence with the labelmap and indicates that the network does not just make
the predictions based on image intensity, but has learned other, more complicated features to
identify the lumen.

6.3.1
Inuence of lumen size
Fig. 5.5 and Fig. 5.10 emphasize the large variations of lumen size in the MPR images. For the
SD dataset, the maximum fraction of lumen pixels to the total number of pixels in an MPR
image from the test set was found to be 0.076, less than 8%. In addition to this, in 93% of
the test images the lumen area constitutes less than 2% of the image. However, it was assessed
that the extracted area could not be made signicantly smaller, as the network would then lack
context of the surrounding tissue in images with large vessels. As indicated in the examples
in Fig. 5.11, the model appears to overestimate the lumen area when it is small, which was
observed to be a general tendency. This is seen in Fig. 5.13 showing that the mean performance
is substantially lower for the samples with the smallest lumen areas. There are 133 samples in
the smallest bin (lumen area between 0.1 and 0.5%), obtaining a mean Dice score of 0.51. The
potential causes for this performance decrement are discussed in the following.
The poor performance of these images could be caused by erroneous annotations/labelmaps.
It may therefore be the case that the network has actually segmented a more correct lumen.
This is emphasized by the fact that there is only mild and no stenosis present in the examples
in Fig. 5.11c and Fig. 5.11d, respectively, which could otherwise have explained the small size
and unusual shape of the labelmap.
Another explanation is the lack of detail in the lumen and around the vessel boundary. For
small vessels, the down-sampling of the image feature maps as they are propagated through
the encoder of the U-net may result in the lumen area being represented by just a few pixels at
the bottom of the network, and may therefore (even with the additional information from the
skip connections) not contain sucient information for correctly segmenting this area. This is
illustrated in Fig. 5.12 showing the result of down-sampling the two images in Fig. 5.11 by a
factor 16 (corresponding to the max pooling layers of the U-net). It is seen that the resolution
in these images is extremely poor, and the lumen is only represented by a single pixel. This
emphasizes the limited amount of detail at the bottom of the U-net.
Finally, the poor performance could be a reection of the limitations of the performance metric,
as the Dice coecient is sensitive to errors if the segmented object is small [41]. As an example,
if the image to be segmented has only one pixel belonging to the lumen class (with the remaining
43.263 pixels belong to the background class) and the model predicts that pixel and one other
pixel as being lumen, the resulting Dice score is 0.67. In this case, relatively trivial mistakes
might dominate the average score for the samples with small lumen area. The limitations of
the Dice coecient are discussed in more detail in Sect. 6.5.
Regardless of the cause, the images with small lumen fractions will have a negative eect on
the nal performance, as they constitute 12% of the entire test set. This issue could be handled
by letting the MPR images vary in regards to the area they cover. The images could be kept at
the same dimensions (i.e. 208 × 208) but cover a smaller area as the arteries become smaller.
The implementation of a dynamic extraction of MPR images poses a challenge in the form of
how to estimate the size of the area to extract (i.e. estimate the diameter of the vessels), and
was beyond the scope of this project.

6.3.2
Performance in examples with plaque and stenosis
The network performance in images with plaque and stenosis is of particular importance, as
this determines whether the segmentation is suitable for identifying plaques/stenoses, either
in the form of aiding the multitask neural network (the focus of this project), or as a stand-
alone method (the preferred method of the challenge participants). For this procedure, the
healthy lumen diameter is estimated and used to determine presence and extent of stenosis.
Note that the plaque type cannot be determined with this approach. Again, it is emphasized
that the examples have not been reviewed by a clinician, and therefore represent the author's
assessment of the data. In the examples in Fig. 5.14, the network generally classies the plaque
pixels as lumen instead of background, thereby overestimating the lumen size. This is the case
in Fig. 5.14c where the network provides a circular segmentation. However, it should be noted
that determining the location of the plaque in this image has not been possible for the author.
This may be due to the fact that the CTA reference indicates that it is a soft plaque. Only
two soft plaques were present in the test set, and the network did not perform well on either.
The network's inability to segment images with this plaque type is in good correspondence with
the literature, stating that current methods for detection of non-calcied plaques rely heavily
on extensive manual interaction (see Sect. 1.1). A dierent explanation can be found in the
gure caption, as the QCA reference has provided this segment with a score of 0, indicating
that no stenosis is present in the segment. It may therefore be the case that neither stenosis
nor plaque is present in this image. This illustrates the inconsistencies between the CTA and
QCA references.
Fig. 5.15 illustrates examples where the network correctly excludes the plaques from the lumen
segmentation. These examples include both mixed and calcied plaque. The ability to leave
out these areas with high intensity emphasize that the network has learned to distinguish the

inconsistency between references in Fig. 5.15a.

As the segmentation network proved capable of

6.3.3
Comparison of regular and contextual U-net
The regular U-net architecture has a big drawback: it does not maintain 3D-consistency, as it
processes each cross-sectional slice individually. This is a huge limitation, especially because
the ability to process 3D information is an important aspect of human skill within this area.
A Recurrent Neural Network (RNN) could achieve this, but may not be optimal for data with
large slice thickness, as only adjacent slices have signicant correlation and the RNN considers
all slices at once [12]. As described in Chap. 3, this was the case the for some of the CTA scans.
Therefore, the contextual U-net maintaining spatial consistency only by considering the slice
immediately above it was chosen.
To further investigate the dierences in the results of the two networks, Fig. 5.18 and Fig. 5.19
enable comparison of the obtained predictions on the same segment on a volume-based and
image-based level. In Fig. 5.18 it is seen that the contextual U-net gives a more smooth lumen
volume compared to the regular U-net. This could be the reason for the increased performance,
and reects that spatial consistency has been obtained by letting the model view the previous
image and segmentation. This is further emphasized by the odd-looking pouches of the volume
in Fig. 5.18b. From Fig. 5.19 an image-based comparison of the two network architectures is
enabled. The rst image in the segment is shown in the top row, and it is seen that the regular
U-net outperforms the contextual U-net in this image. This is probably due to the fact that
the contextual U-net does not receive contextual input for this image, as it is the rst in the
segment.
In some cases it appears that the spatial consistency is maintained too well, as discussed for
Fig. 5.16 above, where the lumen area is overestimated at a plaque location. Another (possibly
related) limitation is the risk of propagating error through the vessel. If the network overesti-
mates the lumen at one image, there is a risk that this overestimation is propagated to the fol-
lowing image (because the network receives the previous image and segmentation) and that this
is propagated to the next image, etc. This could be the case e.g. at plaque/stenosis locations.
Furthermore, the potential error propagation is dependent on the order of which the images are
processed. As the processing is performed randomly, the potential error propagation may de-
pend on which image is processed rst, and whether the network overestimates/underestimates
this image.
Regardless of this potential issue, the contextual U-net performed better than the regular U-net,
supporting the notion that spatial consistency should provide better results. For the segment
in Fig. 5.18, the regular U-net obtained an average Dice score of 0.772 and the contextual U-net
thus outperforms this method by a large margin with a score of 0.849.

6.4.1
Overtting
From the training and validation curves in Fig. 5.20 it is apparent that the network is overtting
to the training data; the training losses are decreasing for all three tasks, but the validation
losses for the classication tasks are not decreasing and appear to be constant throughout
training. Furthermore, the training accuracies for these tasks increase fast and are close to
100%, where the validation accuracies uctuate around the same value throughout training.
Dierent measures were taken to eliminate overtting for the classication tasks. Application
of additional dropout and higher dropout rate did not reduce the overtting or improve results.
From the architecture in Fig. 4.14 it is seen that at the bottom layer, 13×13×1024 parameters
are connected to the FC layers with 1024 hidden units. Since each of these parameters are
connected to all the units in the FC layers, this results in 13 × 13 × 1024 × 1024 = 177, 209, 344
parameters for each of the classication tasks. This vast amount of parameters was assessed
to potentially be the cause of the overtting; the model can t the training data extremely
well with so many parameters, without capturing the underlying correlation between input
and output. For this reason, an alternative network architecture was attempted, in which a
global average pooling step replaced the rst FC layer. This alternative network architecture
is illustrated in Fig. C.1 in the Appendix. This approach was utilized for multitask learning in
[19], where global average pooling outperformed the use of FC layers. The utilization of global
average pooling resulted in the 13×13×1024 parameters being mapped to only 1024 parameters,
representing the global average of each feature map. The output of the global average pooling
was then mapped directly to the four or ve classes, respectively. From Table 5.5 it is apparent
that the reduction in parameters did not result in signicant improvement of the results, and
from the corresponding training and validation curves, it was concluded that it did not eliminate
overtting. Due to time constraints, it was not possible to eliminate the overtting. Obviously,
this had a negative impact on the performance, and should be kept in mind in the following
section where the results are evaluated.

Class imbalance
Both classication tasks have highly imbalanced datasets, as seen in Table 5.6 and Table 5.7,
with the vast majority of the data samples belonging to the healthy class. For both tasks, 81%
of the samples belong to this class. Furthermore, it should be noted that these tables represent
the number of images with the given class, meaning that the number of stenoses and plaque
lesions is much smaller. For example, there are 28 samples with the soft plaque class, however
these constitute only two soft plaque lesions in the test set. In addition to this, the test set does

not contain any stenoses classed as severe or occluded, meaning only three of the ve classes
are actually represented.
The class imbalance can be problematic when training the neural network, as a relatively small
loss can be achieved by classifying all samples as healthy. This could be the reason why it
appears that all classes in both plaque and stenosis classication are confused with the healthy
class. To compensate for this, class weights were computed and provided to the loss function,
similar to the weighting of the segmentation loss, as described in Sect. 4.5.4. The weights were
designed to give a higher loss if the non-healthy classes were misclassied. However, this did
not improve neither training nor performance of the network, and was therefore not utilized
in the nal network. Another possible approach for solving the class imbalance problem is to
sample the mini-batches so the network receives an equal class distribution at each iteration.
This would either imply over-sampling the under-represented classes (i.e. repeating the non-
healthy samples) or under-sampling the over-represented class (excluding some of the healthy
samples). Since the data amount is already very limited, over-sampling would probably be the
best approach, but was left for future work.

To evaluate whether the combination of all three tasks for multitask learning had a benecial
eect on the performance, the result from Table 5.5 and the corresponding single-task network
results are presented in Table 5.8. In addition to applying the single-task network for each of
the three tasks, a multitask network with only the classication tasks was also tested, as these
two tasks are assumed to be correlated.
For the multitask network combining only the classication tasks (Pl. + St.), the obtained
accuracies are numerically better than the accuracies for the single-task networks. However, the
increase is slight (0.3% for plaque type and 1.3% for stenosis grade) and may not be statistically
signicant. For the multitask network combining all three tasks, the stenosis grade accuracy is
the same, the plaque type accuracy is slightly lower and the segmentation Dice score is slightly
higher (0.771 vs. 0.777) compared to the single-task networks. Again, while the segmentation
Dice score may be numerically better, it is probably not statistically signicant. The increase in
performance compared to the contextual U-net results in Table 5.1 could therefore be random
or the result of adding skip connections in the encoder for the multitask network.
The slight or absent performance gain for the multitask network combining all three tasks could
be explained by the tasks being unrelated, so the entire intuition behind the multitask learning
is invalid. However, as the plaque type and stenosis grade are assumed to be related because
plaque is always present if a stenosis is detected [2], this seems unlikely.
Another possible
explanation is that the overtting caused the network to learn a bad data representation,
making it impossible for the tasks to learn from each other. Therefore, it can not be ruled out
that it could potentially aid the training more convincingly if overtting was eliminated. In
addition to this, it should be noted that the performance gain reported in the related work (see

training instance.

6.5
Future work
Some possibilities for future work have already been mentioned in this chapter. In the following
section these are briey summarized and additional suggestions are discussed. The suggestions
are ordered by priority.
1. Due to time constraints, it was not possible to take more action against the overtting of
the multitask network. This aects the performance of the classication tasks negatively,
and more measures should be taken to avoid the overtting. The most obvious addition
would be to reduce the complexity of the model (thereby reducing the number of param-
eters), to obtain more data or to utilize data augmentation. In data augmentation, minor
alterations are made to the existing data samples to make the data appear more diverse.
Increasing the amount of data (even articially) is extremely eective against overtting
as the ratio of parameters to data samples is decreased. Meaningful data augmentation
methods for the CA images could include rotation and ipping of the segments.
2. The proposed multitask network architecture required a label to be provided for all the
tasks for each training input. This signicantly reduced the available data, as segmenta-
tion references were only available for a small subset of the vessel segments. To utilize the
additional data with only classication labels, the procedure from [15] could be adopted,
in which the loss for a given task only contributes if the corresponding label is present.
This would increase the amount of available data signicantly, and possibly eliminate the
overtting.
3. As discussed in Sect. 6.3.1, the lumen size appears to aect the network performance,
probably due to the limited amount of detail in the image. A very valuable addition
would be extraction of MPR images of dynamic sizes, to compensate for the decreasing
vessel size of the CAs.
4. As discussed in Sect. 6.4.2.1, the class imbalance could be handled by sampling the mini-
batches so an equal distribution of classes was received at each iteration. Furthermore,
the number of classes could be reduced to binary or three-class problems without loosing
much information.
5. In the multitask neural network plaque and stenosis classication are treated separately,
so the network may detect a plaque without detecting stenosis. This is impossible in real
life [2], and is a limitations of the network model. This should be compensated for by
modifying the network architecture to eliminate this possibility or by adjusting the loss
function to have a high penalty in this case.
6. Post-processing could be applied to the network output.
For the classication tasks,
single instances of e.g. plaque should be eliminated when found in a sequence of images
otherwise classied as healthy. Likewise, if a sequence of images classied as one type
of stenosis contains a single image classied as another type of stenosis, this could be
changed to correspond to the majority type.

7. As described in Sect. 4.5, the multitask loss was found as a summation of the three
individual losses with a hyperparameter, λ, scaling the segmentation loss to be in the
same order of magnitude as the classication losses. A highly relevant addition would
be scaling the loss according to 'how wrong' the classication is. Intuitively, predictions
which are further from the reference should be penalized more than close calls. As an
example, for the stenosis grade classication there are ve classes, each representing an
interval of percentage stenosis degree. This means that 69% stenosis gives the class label
'moderate', while 70% stenosis results in the class label 'severe'. If the network is only 1%
o in its prediction, it can therefore result in a misclassication and a high loss. Similarly,
if the network classies an image as being healthy when it is 100% occluded, it will result
in the same loss as in the previous case. The loss should therefore be modied according
to how severe the misclassication is.
8. As discussed in Sect. 6.1.1, some labelmaps appeared to be slightly displaced. The cause
of this displacement and potential solution should be identied, as having labelmaps of
high quality is essential for the success of the application.
9. As mentioned in Sect. 6.3.1, the Dice coecient is sensitive to errors if the segmented
object is small. In addition to this, in the case where the overlap is zero, the Dice score
does not consider how far apart the segmentations are. As an example, if the object to
be segmented is a straight line, the Dice score would provide the same value regardless
of whether the predicted line was located just above the true line (but with no overlap)
or far from it. In such cases a distance metric may be a more suitable choice [41]. The
Hausdor distance is an example of a distance metric, dened as the maximum distance
of a set to the nearest point in the other set [45]. This metric would be more robust to
segmentation of small objects. In addition to this, it is well-suited when the boundary
delineation (contour) is of importance, such as in this case, where the segmented lumen
can potentially be used to assess presence of stenosis/plaque. The Hausdor distance
could therefore have been added to aid in the evaluation of the performance of the model.

Conclusions
Computed Tomography Angiography (CTA) is a non-invasive image modality capable of pro-
viding high-resolution 3D images of the coronary arteries. The current standard procedure for
assessing this data to determine or rule out Coronary Artery Disease (CAD) is visual inspec-
tion which is time-consuming and suers from large interobserver variability. Therefore, the
aim of this project has been to use deep learning techniques for development of a reliable and
accurate system for automatic assessment of CTA data. This is thought to be a step towards
an automated framework for aiding clinicians in the assessment of presence of CAD.
During this project, two datasets from the Rotterdam Coronary Artery framework have been
utilized. Both datasets were prepared for the deep learning pipeline by extracting MPR cross-
sectional images of the coronary arteries from the data volume.
Furthermore, reference la-
belmaps were computed from the provided radii for the CLE dataset and from lumen contours
for the SD dataset. A pipeline for segmentation of the coronary arteries with a U-net archi-
tecture was established and expanded to a contextual U-net maintaining spatial consistency
between the generated segmentation masks. The contextual U-net resulted in a mean Dice
score of 0.80, and proved capable of correctly excluding calcied and mixed plaque from the
lumen segmentation. This has lead to the conclusion that a successful segmentation framework
was developed.
The segmentation pipeline was utilized in a multitask neural network for simultaneous lumen
segmentation, plaque detection and quantication as well as stenosis detection and quanti-
cation. The multitask network achieved a segmentation Dice score of 0.78, plaque type clas-
sication accuracy of 80% and stenosis grade classication accuracy of 80%.
However, the
performance beneted from the large fraction of healthy samples in the test set, and the model
struggled to correctly classify samples with presence of plaque and stenosis. The network was
found to overt to the training data, possibly due to the high model complexity or the very
sparse amount of training data. For this reason, higher performances may have been obtained
if the overtting had been eliminated. The multitask network was found to achieve compa-
rable performance to the corresponding single-task networks, and had a much higher training
eciency compared to training the single-task networks in cascade.
The obtained results on the plaque and stenosis detection and quantication tasks were not
comparable to the observer performance. Therefore, improvements are needed for these tasks
before the system can be applied in the clinic. However, the promising segmentation results
could instead be utilized with post-processing methods for estimating the healthy lumen radius,
and used for detection and quantication of stenosis.
