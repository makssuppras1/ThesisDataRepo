Enhancing detection limit of fluorescence spectroscopy by use of optical nanostructures 
Abstract  
This project aims at improving the detection limit of fluorescence spectroscopy in bio detection. 
Nano photonics is applied to realize the aim. A concept known as bound states in continuum 
(BIC) is especially exploited. This project elaborates how BIC can improve light-matter interac-
tion and redirect light emission into the wanted direction. The band structures of nanostructures 
are calculated, and BICs are marked on different bands. The relation as well as difference be-
tween BIC resonance and nanostructure reflection spectrum are analyzed. Based on the theory, 
various nanostructures supporting BIC are designed and simulated to compare their properties. 
One specific type of nanostructures supporting different orders or types of BICs is also studied. 
Designs based on real life bio dyes and available templates were done and compared to each 
other. Different types of dye (Cy5 and EvaGreen) are talked about. How they fit BIC based bio-
detection is stressed. 1-D structures and 2-D structures supporting BIC are designed sepa-
rately. Criteria by theory are proposed to judge whether a structure supporting BIC is good for 
fluorescence bio-detection. The criteria are applied to find the advantages and drawbacks of 1-
D and 2-D structures. For example, 1-D structures can concentrate light energy in the wanted 
area up to more than 65%, while 2-D structures can enhance emission in 2 times. The criteria 
are also used to optimize nanostructures. Robustness of different structures is tested for fabri-
cation. Fabrication of nanostructures was also discussed. Characterization was done on the 
fabricated samples to show how the nanostructures supporting BIC enhance light-matter inter-
action and redirect light.

Introduction 
Many different types of sensing methods are developed to detect bio targets. They include 
acoustic sensing such as QCM (quartz crystal microbalance) and Surface acoustic wave; Elec-
trochemical sensing such as Impedimetric and Amperometric; Optical sensing such as fluores-
cence test and SERS (surface enhanced Raman spectroscopy) [1]. Among the bio sensing 
methods, optical sensing, especially fluorescence sensing, has its inherent advantages since it 
destroys biomaterials in a mild way [2]. Optical biosensors also provide an option for compact, 
fast and precise sensing [3]. They can be applied to the detection of a wide range of biomateri-
als. For example, blood, microbe, virus, gene, and protein. They can hence be used to detect 
many diseases like flu, diabetes, and acute myocardial infarction (AMI) [4]. 
 
In fluorescent tests, the antigen carrying bio information combines with the antibody, and 
changes the optical properties of the optical tester, which can be detected by the fluorescent 
test. An illustration graph is shown on Fig.1.1 below, while a schematic diagram of fluorescent 
biosensing is attached as Fig.1.2. 
 
Figure 1.1: An illustration of biosensing [5] 
 
Figure 1.2: Fluorescent detection set-up. Metal enhancement is applied to this set up; hence a prism is 
used for phase-matching of plasmon on the surface of metallic layer [6]

In the past years, a lot of progress has been made in fluorescent biosensing. However, some 
drawbacks are still constraining the development and application of fluorescent biosensing. 
Among the limitations, the efficiency of light interaction with activated antibody, and the com-
plexity of detecting set up are obvious.  
 
Unsatisfying efficiency of light-matter interaction in fluorescent biosensing results in higher fluo-
rescent detection level, more energy consumption to require enough signal intensity, and higher 
risk of bio-sample destroy under intensive incident light. Fluorescent detection level is critical to 
the early detection of many diseases like cancer. It usually takes years for a cluster of cancer 
cells to grow into a tumor in the size of a bean, while it can only take several months for a bean-
sized tumor to grow into the size of an egg. The earlier the detection of a cancer is, the less cost 
and pain will be there to cure the cancer, and the higher the possibilities of success in curing will 
exist. However, at the early stage of cancer, the concentration of cancer-related antigen is very 
low. That requires extremely low level of fluorescent bio detection. 
 
Meanwhile, complex set up for fluorescent detection is another problem. The emission of fluo-
rescent light is usually omnidirectional which makes it necessary to use light collection focusing 
optics and makes the loss of light energy in the light-collecting process unavoidable. The loss in 
omnidirectional light collection comes in two sources, which is shown on Fig.1.3. First, the limi-
tation in focusing optic aperture. Second, the interior loss in the focus optics. The purchase and 
alignment of light- collecting focusing optics both pull up the cost. To get rid of the focusing op-
tics and the focusing related loss, highly directed fluorescent light is preferred. 
 
Fig 1.3: An illustration of the losses with focusing optics. 
 
BIC provides a good solution to these two drawbacks. BIC can enhance the light-matter interac-
tion, making fluorescence more efficient. This will lower fluorescent detection level, protect the 
sample with lower intensity of light incidence, and save energy. BIC can also improve the di-
rectivity of fluorescent emissions. This will enable fluorescent bio detection without focusing op-
tics. Hence, convenient, low loss, low-cost bio detection is also achieved.

There are defections in the material and fabrication of nano structures. Also, the dimensions of

with such periodic structures in real space, studying the interaction between light and matter in 
the retrospective domain (k space) can make the study easier [11]. Because in retrospective do-
main, light’s behavior in each Brillouin zone is the same.

is periodic in x direction, while infinity in y direction. So, the y component of k vector of light in 1-

On the k – ω diagram, one specific mode supported by the periodic structures propagating in 
one specific direction occupies one dot, according to its own direction of propagation (real part 
of k), loss (imaginary part of k), and frequency (ω). By varying the propagation direction (real 
part of k) of this specific mode, its frequency (ω) varies as a function of k. This forms a band on 
the diagram. Different modes propagating in the same direction usually occupy different points. 
So, they form different bands. But sometimes they share the same point on the diagram when 
the two modes have the same propagation properties. That is the crossing of two bands. But 
that doesn’t have to mean the two modes are the same at that band-crossing point. Because 
they are orthogonal (by mode definition) and hence cannot couple with each other. The horizon-
tal gap between different bands is bandgap, meaning no matter how the k vector varies, no 
mode can be supported in such frequency. Band, band crossing, and bandgap are marked in

2.2 BIC 
 
2.1.1 Definition and types of BIC 
 
BIC is the abbreviation of bound states in continuum [16]. An example of BIC is shown in Fig. 
2.8.  
 
2.1.1.1 
Definition of BIC 
 
BIC has two aspects.  
 
First, it is a bond state. That means BIC cannot be coupled with light propagating in the free 
space. This makes such a type of electromagnetic state unable to absorb light from free space 
or emit light into free space. So, BIC has an infinite Q factor, and real k vector.  
 
Second, it is in the continuum. This means BIC is above the line of light (1-D case), or within the 
light cone (2-D case) on k-ω diagram. Guided modes are also lossless, cannot directly be cou-
pled with light in the free space, but they are not BIC, because they are out of continuum. In de-
tail, the normal guided modes are under the line of light (1-D case), or outside of the light cone 
(2-D case). For example, all the modes shown in Fig. 2.7 are guided modes. They are not in the 
continuum. Hence, they are not BIC. On the contrary, BIC can only take place in the dark region 
on Fig. 2.7 which is inside the light cone. 
 
A combination of the two aspects makes BIC unique and flexible for many applications.

2.1.1.2 
Types of BIC 
 
There are two types of BIC. One is called symmetry protected BIC, while the other one is acci-
dental BIC.  
 
Symmetry protected BIC originates from the symmetry of periodic nanostructures. That 
nanostructure symmetry gives the supported BIC mode specific symmetry. Such symmetry of 
BIC mode disables the BIC mode from coupling with light in the free space, although the BIC 
mode is in the continuum region (above the line of light or within the light cone). Hence, all sym-
metry protected BICs are at Γ point in k-ω space which corresponds to the symmetry axis of pe-
riodic structures in real space. The BIC shown on Fig. 2.8 is a symmetry-protected BIC. 
 
Accidental BIC is also in the continuum region but not able to couple with light in free space. 
However, that disability of coupling isn’t because of the symmetry, but for other reasons. (Like a 
stand wave, it cannot couple to wave in the free space, but it’s not for symmetrical reason.) 
Their far electromagnetic field is eliminated due to the interaction between Electrical-dipole, 
Magnetic-dipole, Electrical-quadrupole, Magnetic-quadrupole.  
 
The accidental BIC is acquired by carefully tuning the parameters of nanostructures and the di-
rection of light mode. That is the origin of its name. An accidental BIC doesn’t have to be at Γ 
point in k-ω space, and usually they are not at that place. An accidental BIC is being shown on 
Fig. 2.9.

Sometimes, the two types of BIC are combined as merged BIC by tuning the parameters to uti-

2.1.2 Selection of BIC 
 
The two types of BIC have different properties. 
 
Symmetry protected BIC is sensitive to direction, their Q factor sharply drops when this kind of 
BIC tilts from Γ point. That is because of symmetry breaking while tilting. This is preferable in 
the project’s directional application.  
Also, this type of BIC is insensitive to wavelength variation and fabrication imperfections. That is 
due to their origin, symmetrical incompatibility. BIC exists where the symmetry of device re-
mains. That is also preferred in this project application, meaning less control over fabrication 
and light wavelength is required.  
In addition, this kind of BIC needs large numbers of period due to symmetry requirements, 
which is naturally met, because bio chips are usually very large.  
 
Accidental BIC is relatively insensitive to direction. This does not make it good for the directional 
application in this project.  
This type of BIC is sensitive to wavelength variation and fabrication imperfections. Due to this 
type of BIC’s origin, such variation can easily break the balance between Electrical-dipole, Mag-
netic-dipole, Electrical-quadrupole, Magnetic-quadrupole, and hence break the BIC. That is a 
bad thing for this project application, meaning extreme care will be taken for fabrication and inci-
dent wavelength.  
To support this type of BIC, large numbers of nanostructure periods are not needed. Only tens 
of period per side is completely enough [20]. However, since fabricated bio chips are very large, 
containing thousands of periods, this property is neither an advantage nor a disadvantage for 
this application. 
 
Based on the properties of the two types of BIC, symmetry protected BIC is chosen for this ap-
plication.

2.1.3 BIC and quasi-BIC 
When some of the parameters, like k vector of light, wavelength, or nano structures, deviate 
from the BIC supporting conditions, BIC becomes quasi-BIC. Then, the infinite Q factor of BIC 
becomes the finite Q factor of quasi-BIC. By carefully tuning the quasi-BIC, quasi-BIC with 
strong abilities in tuning the fluorescence can be achieved [21], [22]. 
 
Especially, only ideal perfect periodic structures can support BIC. Finite periods of structures, 
imperfection in shape, surface, and material of structures will all change BIC to quasi-BIC. 
 
2.1.4 How quasi-BIC improves fluorescent bio detection 
 
Quasi-BIC improves fluorescent bio detection in two ways. First, quasi-BIC enhances the light 
matter interaction and enhances the emission light intensity. Second, quasi-BIC enables highly 
directional fluorescent emission. The two advantages can be explained using coupled mode 
theory. 
 
Light energy from the incident light is coupled to localized modes around the nano structures, 
including some quasi-BIC modes. Then, it is the localized modes around the nanostructures 
that interact with fluorescent particles. They give electromagnetic energy to the fluorescent par-
ticles and extract energy from fluorescent particles’ fluorescent emission. Finally, these localized 
modes are coupled to light in the free space, which causes emission. The whole process is 
shown on Fig. 2.10 (left). Among all the localized modes, if there is one or more quasi-BIC 
modes, the quasi-BIC modes will attract most incident light energy, induce most interactions 
with fluorescent particles, and gives out most energy to the emission light. That is shown on Fig. 
2.10 (right). 
 
Fig. 2.10 Mode coupling process. Left: Incident light couples with localized modes, localized modes ex-
change energy with fluorescent particles, localized modes couple to emission light. Right: Quasi-BICs (if 
exist) dominates the process.

2.1.4.3 
Criteria for good BIC in fluorescent bio detection 
 
Based on physics, several criteria are proposed to judge whether a BIC mode is good for this 
application. 
 
First, the concentration of light energy in liquid. That’s because no fluorescent antibody can be 
put inside the solid dielectric material. If a BIC mode separates its energy mainly in solid dielec-
tric material, the overlapping integral term in equation (2.9) will be very low, limiting the en-
hancement. 
 
Second, the sensitivity to k vector variation. If a BIC mode maintains a lossless mode with large 
range of k vector, emission with high directivity will be disabled. 
 
Third, the effective depth of mode. According to equation (2.9), a smaller effective depth (deff) of 
BIC mode is preferred for a larger enhancement. That requires the mode electrical field separa-
tion to be closer to the interfaces of nanostructures. 
 
Fourth, if several BICs with different sensitivities of k vector clusters, the most k-sensitive BIC is 
preferred to have the best abilities of extraction enhancements. If that is not the case, electro-
magnetic energy will be extracted by the BIC which is less sensitive to k vector, and highly di-
rectional emission will be disabled. (The fourth criteria will be further illustrated in section 3. 2).

Another advanced function is Prediction. To illustrate this function, another photonic crystal 
(shown in Fig. 3.7) was simulated with/without predictor.

predictor, COMSOL won’t mix up two different bands even when they cross each other. This 
good case is shown on Fig. 3. 8 (right). The mode profiles of the two modes at their crossing are 
shown on Fig. 3. 9.

Hypothesis  
 
To explain the deviation of reflection peak or other BIC-induced reflection phenomenon wave-
length from BIC resonance, a hypothesis was proposed. 
 
First, if there is not any other mode at the BIC’s k - ω position, the reflection peak should be at 
the BIC’s resonance when the incident light shares the same angle as BIC’s k direction. Be-
cause the incident light cannot be coupled to BIC. 
 
However, the possible existence of some highly lossy modes at the BIC’s k_real - ω position 
cannot be eliminated. That is not the fault of eigenmode finder in COMSOL. The problem was 
believed to take place in the eigenmode finding process. Either finding mode near a specific ω 
shift or finding mode in a rectangular region abandon such highly loss modes.  
 
==PAGE:40==
 
DTU ELECTRO 
 
 
Enhancing detection limit of fluorescence spectroscopy by use of optical nanostructures 
For the first mode-searching method, it calculates the absolute direction between the set shift 
and the position of found mode in (ω_real, ω_imaginary) space. Such highly lossy modes have 
very large imaginary part of ω and are not “close” to the set shift. Hence, they are abandoned.  
 
For the second mode-searching method, the largest imaginary part of modes to be found are 
manually set. To effectively eliminate the unphysical modes, such settings cannot be too large. 
So, the highly lossy modes are also filtered out. 
 
The conclusion of this hypothesis is that there are some highly lossy modes at the similar k_real 
– ω position as the BIC. But such highly lossy modes cannot be found with mode searching 
methods at hand. Although BIC rejects the energy of incident light, such unfound highly lossy 
modes transport light energy to the other side of the PhC sneakily. This hypothesized process is 
shown on Fig. 3. 22. 
 
If this hypothesis is the reality, the E–field separation in the reflection simulation shall be sum of 
a quasi-BIC and unfound highly lossy modes. That coincides with the reflective simulation re-
sults where the E-field separation differs a lot from BIC (Fig. 3.19, Fig. 3.21).

“Double Period”  
 
In the previous simulations, the period of grating was fixed. A manual variation of bar distances 
while remaining the average bar distance and overall bar separation was proposed. In this way, 
the loss of Quasi-BIC can be controlled manually and precisely without adding undesired mate-
rial loss. To meet the two requirements, a way to vary the separation of bars was proposed. 
First, the distance between the first and second bar was set slightly larger than the original de-
sign, while the distance between the second and third bar was reduced for the same amount. 
Then, this was repeated for the third to fifth bars, and so on. An illustration is shown on Fig. 3. 
28. 
 
 
Fig. 3. 28 Periodically tuning the separation of bars while maintaining the average distance between bars. 
 
To simulate this periodically tuned grating, the model was re-built. Two adjacent former periods 
were put together as one new period. Then, each new period is the same. The percentage 
broadening / reduction for the adjacent two former periods was set to be the tuning parameter. 
(X% in Fig. 3. 28) First, X was set to be 0, which means the new structure is the same as be-
fore. The simulation results should be the same as before. This simulation was done, and the 
results were the same as before. That means the model was verified. The model set-up and 
verification were shown on Fig. 3. 29.

For EvaGreen, the desired pump WL is 500 nm, while the preferred fluorescent emission WL is 
526 nm. 526 nm corresponds to 570.34 THz. Unfortunately, the available templates and SiN 
layer range made it especially hard to design for this high frequency. 
 
For Cy5, when pumping at the absorption peak, the fluorescent emission spectrum overlaps 
much with the same dye’s absorption spectrum. That gave much challenge to the selection of 
extraction wavelength. If Quasi-BIC extracts the fluorescent light energy at emission peak of 
Cy5, it will be absorbed again and emit on longer wavelengths. (This second round of absorp-
tion doesn’t happen at the absorption peak. It absorbs at longer wavelengths, lower frequen-
cies. So, the emission of this second-round absorption will also be at lower frequencies, longer 
wavelengths than the emission of pump absorption). That is not preferable. So, it would be bet-
ter to extract fluorescent light energy at wavelengths farther from the absorption peak.  
 
To achieve a balance between avoiding absorption peak and relative emission intensity, 680 nm 
was selected as the Quasi-BIC extraction wavelength for Cy5. (Shown on Fig. 4. 1 (Upper) in 
the dashed line). At this point, the absorption of Cy5 is only 17% its maximum, which is low 
enough. The emission of Cy5 is around 80% of its maximum, which is still relatively high.

Fabrication 
Deep-UV lithography was applied to the fabrication of the designed PhC structures. That is fast, 
cheap, reliable and compatible with large number of productions. However, deep-UV lithogra-
phy is restricted to the template. That was why the designs in this article were made based on 
the available templates. The process of deep-UV lithography will be introduced in this section. 
(Shown on Fig. 5. 1 [29].) 
 
 
Fig. 5. 1 Deep-UV lithography process [29]. 
 
First, a layer bottom antireflective coating (BARC) was done on the bare substrate. This layer 
eliminated unwanted interference in the photoresist layer. Such interference would leave un-
wanted patterns after progressing and destroy the patterns of the fabricated chips [29]. After-
wards, a layer of photoresist spun on the BARC coating evenly. Even iridescence confirmed the 
photoresist layer thickness was similar everywhere. 
 
Afterwards, coherent deep ultraviolet light was applied to the photoresist through the template. 
This process transferred the pattern of the template onto the photoresist layer. The UV devel-
oped areas had different properties from the undeveloped areas. One kind of area would be 
preserved after the photoresist removal process decided by the type of photoresist (positive or 
negative). By this step, the pattern on the template had been transferred to the photoresist layer 
successfully. 
 
After fabricating patterned photoresist using deep-UV lithography, silicon nitride structures with 
the same pattern were constructed from the patterned photoresist. The process is shown on 
Fig. 5. 2 [29]. 
 
==PAGE:63==
 
DTU ELECTRO 
 
 
 
Fig. 5. 2 Constructure of the PhC from the patterned photoresist [29]. 
 
In this part of fabrication, O2 plasma was first applied to the chip to remove the BARC layer in 
the areas without photoresist protection. Then, silicon nitride was deposited in the valleys. After 
the removal of BARC and the remaining photoresist, the SiN PhC was fabricated.

Conclusion 
In this article, how BIC modes enhance the fluorescent emission intensity and directivity was 
elaborated. Higher fluorescent emission intensity after the two rounds of enhancements corre-
spond to lower detection limit. Highly directional emission will get rid of the focusing optics on 
detection. Criteria for good BIC mode in bio detection usage were proposed. 
 
The simulation process using COMSOL was talked about in detail, including the model set-up 
and advanced function in COMSOL. 1-D PhC and 2-D PhC were simulated and compared with 
references to verify the self-built model. Phenomenon in simulation, like the inter-connection 
and divergence between BIC resonance and BIC-induced reflection peak was analyzed. The 
influence of polarization, nanostructure sizes and shapes on BIC modes were also talked about. 
 
Several designs including 1-D PhC and 2-D PhC based on dye, available templates and fabrica-
tion abilities were made systematically and compared to each other. Characterization on the 
fabricated device verified the existence of BIC modes supported by the devices. Fabrication 
was also briefly sketched in this article.

Future Work 
In the next step, more templates need to be purchased to fabricate PhCs with lower pitch and 
smaller bars (1-D) or larger holes (2-D). In that way, the same order of BICs with higher fre-
quencies will be supported. They can be used to enhance the fluorescent emission of the better 
dye, EvaGreen. 
 
With more flexibility on pump wavelengths and pump angle, other orders of BIC or low-loss 
mode supported by the same structures can be utilized for excitation enhancement. With both 
excitation enhancement and extraction enhancement, the total enhancement on fluorescent 
emission will be higher. The detection limit will be pushed lower. 
 
In addition, more advanced 2-D structures will be tested. The hole will be changed to other 
shapes. The lattice will be changed to an equilateral triangle and hexagon from their original 
shape, square, in this article. SiN layer with serum-filled holes will be changed to columns in se-
rum. The changes will bring more potential for BIC modes with better properties.
