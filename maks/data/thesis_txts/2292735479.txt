Abstract
The vibration damping eﬃciency of the passive Tuned Vibration Absorber (TVA)
is characterized as high, when its undamped natural frequency is accurately tuned
to that of the hosting structure, but reduced when mistuned. A TVA capable of
varying its undamped natural frequency with reference to time, is used to suppress
the primary and superharmonic resonant responses of a linear and weakly nonlinear
structure. It is shown that the TVA with frequency sweep eﬀectively can outper-
form the passive TVA, in speciﬁc frequency bands of external harmonic excitation of
the structure. This eﬃciency is obtained for appropriately chosen frequency sweep
parameters, when a mistuning of both the sweep frequency and passive TVA of
6.8%, is used as a case study. Frequency response curves are used to study the
eﬀects of the frequency sweep, with illustrative examples to provide insight into the
dynamical response of the systems, when suppressing resonant vibrations.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The passive Tuned Vibration Absorber (TVA) is commonly used to attenuate un-
wanted vibration of engineering structures and machinery. Optimal performance of the
TVA is achieved through tuning its undamped natural frequency, to that of the hosting
structure (Ormondroyd and Den Hartog, 1928). Mistuning and hence a decrease in the
performance of the TVA may, however, occur if the natural frequency of the hosting struc-
ture is altered, as the eﬀect of changes in environmental or operational conditions. The
Swept Frequency TVA is in this work studied as a robust vibration damping alternative
to the passive TVA.
1.1
Tuned Vibration Absorbers
The Millennium footbridge in London UK is an example of a vibration problem of an
engineered structure, that appeared to exist after completion of the construction of it.
The bridge underwent unexpected lateral vibration on the opening day, stemming from
the pedestrian-induced excitation of it. The frequency of excitation was in the design
phase of the bridge not expected to coincide, with its lateral natural frequency (Dallard
et al., 2001). The bridge was closed two days after the opening, taking almost 1.5 years
before adding 37 linear viscous dampers and 50 TVAs to the initial bridge structure, had
solved the problem (Newland, 2015). Figure 1.1 depicts the underside of the deck of the
Millennium bridge before and after installation of the TVAs.
Vibration problems like the Millennium footbridge are often complex and require ad-
vanced engineering tools and skills to solve.
There is two approaches to enhance the
dynamical performance of such structures, termed modal modiﬁcation and modal addi-
tion (Agnes, 1997). Modal modiﬁcation is based on modifying the modal properties of
the structure, that is distributions of mass inertia, stiﬀness and damping, in order to alter
its natural frequency and vibrational mode, to avoid unwanted excitation of this.
Modal addition is based on attaching an absorber mass to the structure, in order to
get this to undergo large motion while attenuating the motion of the critical components
of the structure, this through transferring the vibrational energy from the structure to the
absorber. While modal modiﬁcation in many cases may turn out not being feasible, or
even impossible, the concept of modal addition is applied when using TVAs for vibration
damping. Figure 1.2(a) depicts an example of modal addition in form of the passive TVA:
attachment of Stockbridge dampers to overhead power lines.

1.1.1
The passive Tuned Vibration Absorber
As previously mentioned the passive TVA is eﬀective by means of vibration damp-
ing, when its undamped natural frequency is tuned to that of the hosting structure. It
may be used for two vibration damping purposes (Bonello, 2011): 1) for attenuating the
amplitude of a speciﬁc vibrational mode of the hosting structure, across a broad band of
excitation frequencies, 2) for attenuating the amplitude of vibration at a speciﬁc frequency
of excitation of the structure. The former purpose is fulﬁlled through designing the TVA

undamped TVA is eﬀective in a narrow band of excitation frequencies, centered about its

of a dampening eﬀect, that mistuning of a TVA may cause an actual ampliﬁcation of the

Other examples of environmental and operational conditions that may cause an alter-

It was found that traﬃc loading and temperature are the most important parameters to
measure, for predicting changes in the natural frequency of a suspension bridge (Laory
et al., 2014).
Another phenomenon that gives rise to a decrease in the performance of the passive
TVA, is when the structure is subjected to random excitation, causing it to vibrate in
multiple modes. Examples of this is the overhead power lines suspended in free weather
conditions, buildings subjected to seismic loading or even earthquakes, wheeled railway
transport wagons, automobile riding on rough roads and aerospace structures during
launch. To overcome this limitation of the passive TVA, a range of active TVA systems
(ATVA) has been invented, and emphasis on the study of the performance of such in-
creased. Section 1.1.2 provides a brief insight into the concept of ATVA systems and how
the Swept Frequency TVA diﬀers from these.
1.1.2
Active Tuned Vibration Absorbers
The numerous concepts of designing ATVA systems are neither studied nor presented
in this work, although Section 1.2 provides the reader with an insight into a handful of
studies on such systems. A short introduction to the basic terms used in control theory
is, however, included in this section. This in order to explain the main diﬀerence between
the vibration control method of the Swept Frequency TVA and the ATVAs.
Feedback systems
The terms feedback, open and closed loop systems are in this section brieﬂy introduced
and explained (Astrom and Murray, 2008), and illustrated with relevance to vibration
absorber systems, Figure 1.3.
Feedback refers to dynamical systems consisting of multiple components that are con-
nected, such that their dynamical responses inﬂuence on each other. An example of this
is the system that consists of a structure connected to an absorber mass, through stiﬀness
and damper elements.
Closed loop (Figure 1.3 (a)) refers to systems in which the components are connected
to form a closed cycle. The aim of this is to control the response of the main component,
i.e. the structure. This is done by using a measured output (y) of the main component
with a reference signal, to adjust the control component, i.e. the TVA. This enhances
the dynamical behavior of the main component through the controlled input u into it.
ATVAs are closed loop systems.
Open loop (Figure 1.3 (b)) refers to systems in which the connection between the
components does not form a closed cycle. A measured output signal is thus not fed back
into the control component, by which it follows that it does not correct for errors or
compensate for changes in external disturbances in real time. An input r is here used

1.1.3
Swept Frequency Tuned Vibration Absorbers
The term sweeping TVA (Gardonio and Zilletti, 2013, 2014a) does in this work refer
to a vibration absorber whose stiﬀness property is periodically varied within given ranges,
with reference to time. A blind sweep of its undamped natural frequency is thus intro-
duced. When mistuned the performance of the sweeping TVA is enhanced, in comparison
with that of a mistuned passive TVA. This was shown through numerical simulations of
multiple sweeping TVAs attached to a plate structure, outperforming mistuned passive
TVAs in controlling multiple resonant responses of the plate, subjected to broadband ran-
dom excitation (Gardonio and Zilletti, 2014a). It was experimentally validated as well,
considering a semi-active TVA cyclically controlling the ﬂexural responses of a cylindrical
structure (Gardonio and Zilletti, 2014b).
The uniform variation of the undamped natural frequency of the Swept Frequency
TVA, is in this work realized with a periodically varying absorber stiﬀness, following a
sinusoidal law with reference to time. Section 1.2 provides the reader with a brief insight
into the body of literature on both the passive TVAs and ATVAs.
1.2
Literature survey
Most teaching material and books on fundamental vibration theory (Angeles, 2011;
Inman, 2009) includes a section on the basic concepts of the linear passive TVA, while
the theory behind the nonlinear autoparametric vibration absorber (Thomsen, 2003; Vyas
and Bajaj, 2001) is included in works covering advanced topics in vibration.
==PAGE:15==
1.3. MOTIVATION OF PRESENT WORK
Page 5
A number of works on methods in passive vibration damping (Mead, 1998) and ap-
plication of dynamic vibration absorbers (Korenev and Reznikov, 1993; Snowdon, 1959)
exist, covering methods in vibration damping of diﬀerent types of structures subjected
to various types of excitation. The work of Hunt (1979) includes examples of structural
applications of TVAs with illustrative machine drawings.
Theoretical and experimental studies on the performance of various ATVA designs and
concepts with feedback control exist (Bonello, 2011), this including semi-passive vibration
damping using a statistical approach to the feedback signal (Guyomar et al., 2007). Vi-
bration damping using a so-called state-switched system has been studied (Cunefare et al.,
2000), this in form of a vibration absorber capable of changing its stiﬀness or damping
property instantaneously, as the sign of velocity of the structure switches.
Soto-Brito and Ruiz (1999) found that an increase in the nonlinearity of a structure,
results in a decrease in the damping performance of the passive TVA attached to it,
while it was shown that a passive TVA is eﬀective in suppressing both the primary and
superharmonic responses of a weakly nonlinear structure (Ji and Zhang, 2010a,b). The
latter was experimentally conﬁrmed through investigating the super- and subharmonic
responses of a nonlinear beam, when suppressed by an attached TVA (Bonsel et al.,
2004). It was also found that an ATVA is necessary, to eﬀectively suppress the responses
of a nonlinear structure subjected to multi-harmonic excitations (Beltran-Carbajal and
Silva-Navarro, 2014). The works of Gardonio and Zilletti (2013, 2014a,b) was the only
discovered through the literature survey studying the performance of sweep frequency
TVAs.
1.3
Motivation of present work
ATVA systems are not widely used in industrial applications due to their related
cost, since the design and implementation of such often includes costly and complicated
software, signal equipment and sensors. The tuning procedure of such systems is complex,
and their robustness and reliability in harsh environmental conditions is poor (Régis,
2010). This makes the passive TVA an appealing lower cost alternative to these, when
excitation and tuning conditions makes its application feasible.
The motivation of this work is ﬁrstly to study the performance of the Swept Frequency
TVA, as a lower cost and less complicated alternative to the ATVA system. When external
excitation and tuning conditions results in a decreased performance of the passive TVA,
the Swept Frequency TVA is studied as a robust and reliable solution, that can eﬀectively
operate and outperform the passive TVA. Figure 1.4 plots system cost versus application
complexity of the passive, Swept Frequency and ATVA systems.
It was found that the performance of sweep frequency TVAs is robust in comparison
with passive TVAs, when introducing a mistuning of both (Gardonio and Zilletti, 2014a).
Reductions of 4 dB to 6 dB of amplitudes in speciﬁc frequency bands was obtained, when
the structure natural frequencies was unchanged, while the sweep frequency TVAs still
produced a 4.6 dB to 5.3 dB control eﬀect of amplitudes when a mistuning of 7-40% was
introduced. Unlike these numerical simulation studies of multiple sweep frequency TVAs
attached to a continuous plate structure, this work is based on studying the damping
performance of a single sweep frequency absorber, attached to a linear or weakly nonlinear
Single Degree-of-Freedom (SDOF) structure. This is chosen to simplify mathematical
modeling of the systems, as it is believed to provide a clearer insight into the optimization

1.4
Outline of the thesis
The ﬁrst part of this work (Chapter 2 and 3) is devoted to the mathematical modeling
and analysis of the passive and Swept Frequency TVA attached to linear and nonlinear
structures.
Chapter 2 Includes mechanical modeling of the passive and Swept Frequency TVA at-
tached to linear and nonlinear structures, with related derivations of equations of
motion. The inﬂuence of design parameters of the TVA on the dynamical response
of the systems are illustrated, and performance measures and optimization criteria
for the damping eﬃciency of the TVA are introduced.
Chapter 3 Presents the details of the perturbation analysis employed, to obtain approx-
imate solutions of responses of the Swept Frequency TVA systems, when subjected
to external harmonic excitation. The perturbation analysis includes two analysis
cases for the combinations of external and parametric resonant excitations, for the
system with a linear structure. For the system with a weakly nonlinear structure,
analysis cases of the primary and superharmonic responses are carried out.
The second part of the thesis (Chapter 4 and 5) is devoted to the methods in numerical
simulation of the TVA models, and presentation of the results obtained throughout the
work.
Chapter 4 Introduces the methods of simulating the models with both harmonic and
random excitations as inputs, this including calculation of frequency response- and
transfer functions.
Chapter 5 Provides the reader with discussions of the sum of results obtained through
the mathematical analysis and numerical simulations, this with a focus on assessing
the eﬃciency, advantages and disadvantages of the Swept Frequency TVA, in con-
trast with the passive TVA. This includes validation of the mathematical analysis
against numerical simulations and vice versa.
Chapter 6 Finally, on basis of the conclusions on the completed study, recommenda-
tions for extended studies and design proposals for the Swept Frequency TVA are
presented.

2.4.4
H∞optimization of the TVA
The H∞criterion is a classical optimization problem when studying the performance of
TVAs, when the primary mass is subjected to external harmonic excitation (Ormondroyd
and Den Hartog, 1928). Its objective is to minimize the stationary amplitude at resonance
frequencies of the frequency response (Régis, 2010).
This problem may be approached through the now-called theory of ﬁxed-points of
the frequency response curves (Korenev and Reznikov, 1993; Mead, 1998) for the passive
TVA. Both approximate solutions of the H∞problem and later on exact solutions (Asami
et al., 2002) has been proposed, the latter presenting analytical solutions of the problem
with a damped primary system (ζ ̸= 0).
While the assumption of an undamped primary system in most cases remain too
restrictive, when studying the damping performance of a speciﬁc TVA conﬁguration,
other studies are focused on methods for solving the much more complex H∞problem
with a damped primary system. These includes frequency locus, perturbation and series
solution approaches (Régis, 2010).
The performance of the Swept Frequency TVA is in this work assessed by the H∞crite-
rion through numerical simulations of EOM and related frequency responses, as presented
in Section 5.2.

The H2 optimization problem is based on minimizing the total vibrational energy of
the primary mass response, when subjected to random excitation across a broad frequency

2.5
Concluding remarks
Three sets of non-dimensional coupled EOM has been derived on basis of two-DOF
mechanical models of the TVA systems. These are the passive TVA attached to a linear
structure, and the Swept Frequency TVA attached to linear and nonlinear structures.
Absorber tuning δ, damping ratio ζa and mass ratio α are introduced as the design
parameters for the absorber systems, with an addition of sweep amplitude q and sweep
frequency ˜ω for the Swept Frequency TVA.
Parametric excitation of the systems is introduced by the implementation of frequency
sweep, this in form of periodically varying absorber stiﬀness, and the mass ratio α deter-
mines the magnitude of coupling between the sets of EOM.
Deﬁnitions of eﬀectiveness, bandwidth, optimization criteria, energy and power ﬂow
measures are introduced, on basis of which the performance of the Swept Frequency TVA
is studied. Chapter 3 is devoted to the perturbation analysis of the mathematical models.

3.4
Concluding remarks
Approximate solutions of stationary responses has been derived on basis of four anal-
ysis cases, two for the linear structure and two for the nonlinear structure. Equations
governing stationary amplitudes a and b was derived, these without the inclusion of fre-
quency sweep parameters q and ˜ω, showing no eﬀect of absorber frequency sweep to order
ε0, due to the assumed smallness of terms proportional to q. If hard parametric excitation
was assumed, that is sweep amplitude parameter q to order ε0, solutions of corresponding
zero-order problems would not exist.
For the combination of external- and parametric resonance, equations governing time-
invariant stationary amplitudes a and b could not be derived, as a consequence of the
combination of excitations. The eﬀect of absorber frequency sweep is shown to inﬂuence
stationary amplitudes of the absorber mass to order ε1, while the inﬂuence is to order ε2
on the response of the primary mass.

5.2.3
Eﬀectiveness Frequency Responses
The performance of the Swept Frequency TVA has so far been studied on basis of
investigating reduction or ampliﬁcation of resonant peaks of frequency responses. These
may, however, be shifted to other frequencies of external excitation under the inﬂuence of
the frequency sweep, when comparing to the passive TVA. The eﬀectiveness (Hunt, 1979)
of the Swept Frequency TVA is assessed by studying the vibrational amplitude of the
primary mass with frequency sweep to that without, at the initial frequency of external
excitation.
This might be useful in industrial applications of the Swept Frequency TVA, through
designing it with appropriately chosen parameters of frequency sweep, when the frequency
of external harmonic excitation is known to lie within a speciﬁc bands.
On basis of the frequency responses shown in previous sections, eﬀectiveness curves
are in this section presented with related characteristic time records, providing an insight
into the dynamical response of the systems at excitation frequencies of special interest.

linear structure, for four values of frequency sweep ˜ω, with sweep amplitude q = 0.3.

for ˜ω = 0.2 where η = 2.3 at ˜Ω= 1.026. These are the resonance frequencies of the system
with passive TVA. The high eﬀectiveness at external excitation of these may occur, as
a consequence of sweeping the natural frequency of the absorber, canceling the eﬀect of
harmonic forcing to excite eﬀectively at resonance.
The frequency band within which the Swept Frequency TVA is eﬀective is widest
for ˜ω = 2.2, and narrowband for ˜ω = 0.2. A value of frequency sweep ˜ω resulting in an
attenuation of amplitude throughout the frequency response is not observed. Negligible
eﬀects are observed for ˜ω = 5.2, while ˜ω = 2.2 provides high ineﬀectiveness at ˜Ω= 0.925

5.3
Performance of the Swept Frequency TVA: Ran-
dom excitations
The H2 optimization criterion of a TVA is based on studying the dynamical perfor-
mance of it, that is the dampening eﬀect on the hosting structure, during a long period
of oscillation when subjected to random external excitation. The displacement response
and random force input in time domain are converted to frequency domain using the Fast
Fourier Transform, and the H2 FRF is calculated as the magnitude of the ratio of output
to input throughout the frequency band of random excitation.
H2 FRFs and calculations of corresponding H2–norms for the linear and nonlinear
structures subjected to random excitation, are in this section presented as the result of
numerical simulations.

5.4
Performance of the Swept Frequency TVA: En-
ergy and power ﬂow
The case studies of primary resonant linear and nonlinear responses, with the nonlinear
superharmonic response of the Swept Frequency TVA, are in this section approached with
an investigation of energy and power ﬂow calculations of the systems.
Based on the equations derived in Section 2.4.3 for measures of instantaneous energy
and power ﬂow in TVA systems (Anton, 1984; Yang et al., 2015), the performance of the
Swept Frequency TVA is quantitatively contrasted to the passive TVA by means of power
absorption ratio and kinetic energy of the primary mass.
5.4.1
Linear structure, primary resonant response
Figure 5.26 plots the variations of time-averaged energy and power ﬂow for the system
with a linear structure, with reference to excitation frequency ˜Ωin the primary resonant
range.
Frequency responses of power of external excitation ¯Pext(˜Ω) (a), absorption power
¯Pa(˜Ω) (b) and dissipation power ¯PD(˜Ω) (c) are qualitatively alike the corresponding
frequency response of stationary amplitude, Figure 5.6. The input power from external
excitation is in general reduced at the frequencies of peaks of the response for the passive
TVA, except for ˜ω = 5.2 that produces negligible eﬀects.
A non-smooth behavior of
¯Pext(˜Ω) is observed in the frequency range 0.85 to 0.88 except for ˜ω = 1.2, which may
occur as an eﬀect of the dynamical interactions between the external and parametric
excitations. The increase in values of absorption and dissipation power towards resonance
peaks makes sense, since large system response inherently may result in large deﬂection
of damper elements.
The power input to frequency sweep ¯Psw(˜Ω) (d) is presented as the magnitude of the
time-averaged value of this, during the stationary response. Subplot (d) is thus a measure
of the frequency sweep power, rather than a computation of at which frequencies there will
exist a net output of energy through the stiﬀness sweep mechanism. Studies on designing
vibration absorber systems (Hassaan, 2014) and dual-mass systems in general (Tang and
Zuo, 2011) for energy harvesting exist, these based on derivation of analytical criteria
for the concept in time-invariant systems. The qualitative trend of ¯Psw(˜Ω) is similar to
the curves previously discussed, with large ﬂuctuations and highest amplitudes for the
near-resonant parametric sweep frequency ˜ω = 2.2, beyond the 0.85 to 0.91 frequency
band.
The curves of power absorption ratio Ra(˜Ω) (f) ﬂuctuates in the frequency range 0.85
to 0.88, as a consequence of the previously discussed non-smooth trend of ¯Pext(˜Ω) in this
band, beyond which the near-resonant parametric value ˜ω = 2.2 produces an increase in
Ra compared to that without sweep. Sweep frequency ˜ω = 0.2 produces a decrease in RA
in the frequency range beyond ˜Ω= 0.95, in comparison to that of the passive TVA.
The kinetic energy of primary mass KM is reduced by 12 dB at the 1st resonance peak
with ˜ω = 1.2, and at the 2nd peak with ˜ω = 0.2 and ˜ω = 2.2. Similar plots of energy
and power ﬂow with q = 1 and q = 2 are found in Appendix A.5, Figure A.26 and A.25,
respectively. These shown similar qualitative trends as the above-stated, with decreased
values of the measures.

Concluding remarks
A validation of approximate solutions of stationary responses, as obtained through
the perturbation analysis, against numerical simulation of EOM for the TVA systems,
was presented as the ﬁrst part of the results. Good agreement between numerical sim-
ulations and perturbation analysis was obtained, for the system parameters chosen to
investigate the primary resonant response performance of the Swept Frequency TVA. A
poor agreement was obtained for the case study of the nonlinear superharmonic response.
The frequency sweep was shown not to be eﬀective for any of the discrete combinations
==PAGE:80==
Page 70
CHAPTER 5.
RESULTS
of values of q and ˜ω, throughout the entire frequency responses investigated, although
reductions of either the 1st or 2nd amplitude peak was obtained for speciﬁc combinations
of values of the sweep parameters.
The H2 optimization criterion was imposed in order to study the performance of the
Swept Frequency TVA, when the structure is subjected to random excitation, showing
negligible to negative eﬀects of sweeping the absorber frequency.
Energy and power ﬂow calculations of time-averaged quantities showed that the power
absorption ratio is increased, under the inﬂuence of near-resonant parametric excitation
˜ω = 2.2. The kinetic energy of primary mass was eﬀectively reduced at the resonance
frequency of the system with passive TVA, for appropriately chosen sweep parameters.
This especially in the case study of the nonlinear superharmonic response.

Chapter 6
Conclusions
The vibration damping eﬃciency of the passive TVA is characterized as high, when
its undamped natural frequency is accurately tuned to that of the hosting structure, but
reduced when mistuned. In this context, the objective of this work was to contrast the
performance of the Swept Frequency TVA to the passive TVA. This was done on basis
of a case study, in which a mistuning of both the Swept Frequency and passive TVA of
6.8% was introduced.
The ﬁrst part of the study consisted of carrying out mathematical two-DOF models
of the passive and Swept Frequency TVA systems (Chapter 2). In order to study the
suppression of both linear and nonlinear resonant responses, a cubically nonlinear system
was modeled in addition to the linear. The simpliﬁcation in form of modeling the systems
with two DOFs was chosen, with the aim of providing a clear insight into the optimization
problem of the Swept Frequency TVA.
The mathematical analysis of the models was carried out employing a perturbation
method (Chapter 3). This was chosen in order to obtain approximate solutions of station-
ary responses of the systems, and insight into how the frequency sweep would inﬂuence on
these. It was shown that under the assumptions of smallness of relevant system parame-
ters, the eﬀects of sweeping the absorber frequency on stationary amplitudes, is weak. As
a consequence of this, analytical expressions for stationary amplitudes, with the inclusion
of frequency sweep parameters, was not obtained.
Numerical simulation of the mathematical models (Chapter 4) provided a basis for
validating the results of the perturbation analysis.
Good agreement in three of four
analysis cases, between numerical simulation and perturbation analysis, was obtained.
The numerical simulation methods enabled a parameter study of the frequency sweep as
well, when the input to the systems in form of external excitation was both harmonic and
random.
Quantitative measures for investigating the performance of the Swept Frequency TVA
by means of eﬀectiveness, bandwidth, H2 and H∞optimization, absorption ratio and
kinetic energy, was obtained through the numerical simulations (Chapter 5). The study
of these showed that the Swept Frequency TVA eﬀectively can outperform the passive
TVA, in speciﬁc frequency bands of external harmonic excitation. This eﬃciency was
obtained for appropriately chosen frequency sweep parameters. The eﬀect of frequency
sweep was shown to be negligible or even negative by imposing the H2 optimization
criterion.

Future work
In order to provide more insight into the advantages and challenges which will relate
to an industrial application of the Swept Frequency TVA, further theoretical and experi-
mental investigation is recommended. Other mathematical methods may, in comparison
with that employed in this study, provide diﬀerent or even better insight into the eﬀects
of the frequency sweep. Investigations on basis of a two-DOF experimental setup, could
verify some observations in this work.
Two proposals for how Brüel & Kjær can design the frequency sweep mechanism, for
an industrial application of the Swept Frequency TVA, are found in Appendix C, Figure

function yp = ODEfunNonLinearSweptRandom(tau,y,ft,x_LPﬁltered,ODEparameters)
% ODEparameters = system parameters
% Interpolate the data set x_LPﬁltered at time points tau,
% ft = time span for low−pass ﬁltered input x_LPﬁltered
f = interp1(ft,x_LPﬁltered,tau);
% System eqs:
yp = zeros(4,1);
yp(1) = y(2);
yp(2) = −2∗ODEparameters.zeta∗y(2)...
+ 2∗ODEparameters.alpha∗(1+ODEparameters.delta)...
∗ODEparameters.zetaa∗y(4)...
−y(1) −ODEparameters.gamma∗y(1)^3 ...
+ ODEparameters.alpha∗(1+ODEparameters.delta)^2 ...
∗(1+ODEparameters.q∗cos(ODEparameters.OmegaTS∗tau))∗y(3)...
+ f;
yp(3) = y(4);
yp(4) = 2∗ODEparameters.alpha∗ODEparameters.zeta∗y(2)...
−2∗(1+ODEparameters.delta)∗(1+ODEparameters.alpha^2)...
∗ODEparameters.zetaa∗y(4)...
+ ODEparameters.alpha∗y(1) + ODEparameters.alpha...
∗ODEparameters.gamma∗y(1)^3 ...
−(1+ODEparameters.alpha^2)∗(1+ODEparameters.delta)^2 ...
∗(1+ODEparameters.q∗cos(ODEparameters.OmegaTS∗tau))∗y(3)...
−ODEparameters.alpha∗f;
end

FFT section
MATLAB code for calculating and plotting FFTs. Most parts of the code was provided
by Jon Juel Thomsen and modiﬁed by the author.
%%% FFT section by Jon Juel Thomsen, spring 2014 %%%
function [xFFT, nFFT] = FFT(y, m, per, tau)
% Input variables from output y of ODE45 solver
% m = length of arrays, per = post transient point, tau = time array
x = y(round(m∗per):m,1);
xa = y(round(m∗per):m,3);
t0 = tau(round(m∗per));
N = length(x);
tf = tau(m);
T = tf −t0; % Total sampling time
dt = T / (N−1); % Sampling interval (= sampling period)
fs = 1/dt; % Sampling frequency (= sampling rate)
df = 1/T; % Frequency resolution
fNyq = 1/2∗fs; % Nyquist frequency = Half the sampling frequency
dBmax = 120;
for j=1:N
wHann(j)=1/2∗(1−cos((2∗pi∗(j−1))/(N−1))); % Hann window
wRect(j)=1; % Rectangular window (= No window)
end
window = wHann;
x = x’.∗window;
xa = xa’.∗window;
nFFT = N/2 + 1;
xFFT = ﬀt(x);
xaFFT = ﬀt(xa);
dBnorm = dBmax −max([20∗log10(max(abs(xFFT))) 20∗log10(max(abs(xaFFT)))]);
xFFTdB = 20∗log10(abs(xFFT)) + dBnorm;
xaFFTdB = 20∗log10(abs(xaFFT)) + dBnorm;
fFFT = 0 : df : (nFFT−1)∗df;
ﬁgure()
% Primary mass FFT
subplot(1,2,1)
plot(fFFT,xFFTdB(1:length(fFFT)),’−k’);
axis([0 fNyq 0 1.1∗dBmax]); % Only display up to fNyq
% Absorber mass FFT
subplot(1,2,2)
plot(fFFT,xaFFTdB(1:length(fFFT)),’−k’);
axis([0 fNyq 0 1.1∗dBmax]); % Only display up to fNyq
end

%%% Jon Juel Thomsen / April 4, 2006 %%%
function [x_LPﬁltered,ft,p] = Filter_Lowpass(t0, tf, RES)
%%% Signal sampling parameters %%%
ft = t0:(1/RES):tf; % Time array deﬁned in time span t0 to tf with
% resolution RES
N = length(ft); % Number of sampling points
T = tf−t0; % Total sampling time
dt = T/(N−1); % Sampling interval
fs = 1/dt; % Sampling frequency
fNyq = 1/2∗fs; % Nyquist frequency = Half the sampling frequency
df = 1/T; % Frequency resolution
% Generate random force vector with amplitude p in [a;b]
a = −0.0145;
b = 0.0145;
p = (b−a).∗rand(N,1) + a;
% LP−ﬁlter time series
% NOTE: LP−ﬁltering may aﬀect transients of the signal to be ﬁltered.
% Adjust the below three ﬁlter parameters to suit your needs (see the
% help−ﬁle for cheby2for for explanations of ﬁlter parameters).
CutoﬀFrequency = 1.2;
FilterOrder = 6;
% Stop band attenuation in dB down from the peak passband value
% Larger values => wider transition widths (shallower rolloﬀ)
StopBandAttenuation = 20;
[ﬁlterCoeﬀ1, ﬁlterCoeﬀ2] = cheby2(FilterOrder, StopBandAttenuation,...
CutoﬀFrequency/fNyq);
x_LPﬁltered = ﬁlter(ﬁlterCoeﬀ1, ﬁlterCoeﬀ2, p);
end
