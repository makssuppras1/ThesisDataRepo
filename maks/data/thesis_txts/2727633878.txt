Abstract
Development of cancer immunotherapies over the past several decades have made significant
advancements in the treatment of non­small cell lung cancer. However, the treatment options
for patients with metastatic disease are still limited, with poor long term survival. This raises a
significant concern as 50% of patients have metastatic disease at the time of diagnosis.
To address this challenge, a novel combination of stereotactic ablative radiotherapy with tumor­
targeting L19­IL2, named ImmunoSABR, has been proposed as a promising candidate. Al­
though several studies have investigated this combination therapy, the precise immunomech­
anisms by which it facilitates tumor clearance are still not fully characterized. Therefore, this
thesis aims to elucidate these immunomechanisms in order to obtain a better understanding of
the combination therapy and to provide mechanistic insights into the relationship between the
immune system and cancer.
To achieve this aim, the immunomechanisms of the combination therapy are first investigated in
two murine preclinical models using the CT26 and C51 colon carcinoma cell lines. The results
from the two mouse models indicate two distinct innate anti­tumor immune responses which
involve the myeloid cell and NK­cell compartments.
Next, a similar immunophenotyping study is performed on samples from a human phase I
trial. The analysis shows heterogeneity among patients, suggesting patient­specific responses.
Moreover, a rare population of CD4+ TEMRA cells, uniquely associated with treatment­responsive
patients are found. Phenotypically, these cells represent a highly differentiated and cytotoxic
subset of cells, although their exact role in anti­tumor immunity is unclear.
The analysis of preclinical and phase I trial data opens the door to the investigation of data
from a phase II trial, which is currently ongoing. Therefore, in anticipation of the phase II trial
samples, three antibody panels for use in an immunophenotyping analysis are proposed.
Finally, to complement the immunophenotyping analysis, an investigation into the impacts of
different radiation types on tumor mutational burden is performed. Although this investigation
is preliminary, findings suggest a potential correlation between different radiation types, their
alterations to the tumor mutational burden, and the generation of tumor neoantigens.
The conclusions drawn in this thesis contribute to the understanding of the immunomechanisms
involved in the ImmunoSABR combination therapy and sets the stage for future investigations
in human patients, paving the way for improved treatment strategies in metastatic non­small
cell lung cancer.

Resume
Udviklingen af kræftimmunterapier har i løbet af de seneste årtier gjort betydelige fremskridt i
behandlingen af ikke­småcellet lungekræft. Imidlertid er behandlingsmulighederne for patienter
med metastatisk sygdom stadig begrænsede med dårlig langtidsoverlevelse. Dette er særligt
en udfordring, da 50% af patienterne har metastatisk sygdom på diagnosetidspunktet.
En ny kombination af stereotaktisk ablativ strålebehandling med tumor­specifikt L19­IL2, såkaldt
ImmunoSABR, er blevet foreslået som en lovende kandidat til at klare disse udfordringer. Selvom
adskillige undersøgelser har undersøgt denne kombinationsterapi, er de præcise immunmekanis­
mer, hvorved den faciliterer bekæmpelse af tumore, stadig ikke fuldt karakteriseret. Derfor har
dette speciale til formål at undersøge disse immunmekanismer for at opnå en bedre forståelse
af kombinationsterapien og for at give mekanistisk indsigt i forholdet mellem immunsystemet
og kræft.
For at nå dette mål undersøges immunmekanismerne af kombinationsterapien først i to præk­
liniske musemodeller ved brug af CT26 og C51 coloncarcinomcellelinjerne. Resultaterne fra de
to musemodeller indikerer to særskilte anti­tumor immunrespons, som involverer myeloidceller
og NK­celler.
En lignende immunfænotypningsundersøgelse er lavet på prøver fra et klinisk fase I­studie.
Analysen viser heterogenitet blandt patienter, hvilket tyder på patientspecifikke immunresponser.
Desuden findes en sjælden population af CD4+ TEMRA­celler, som er unikt forbundet med be­
handlingsresponsive patienter. Fænotypen på disse celler repræsenterer en differentieret og
cytotoksisk gruppe af celler, selvom deres nøjagtige rolle i anti­tumor immunitet er uklar.
Analysen af prækliniske og fase I forsøgsdata åbner døren til undersøgelse af data fra et igang­
vaerende fase II­studie. Derfor foreslås der tre antistofpaneler til brug i en immunfænotypeanal­
yse på fase II­prøverne.
Til sidst, for at komplimentere immunfænotypingsanalysen, udføres der en undersøgelse af
virkningerne af forskellige strålingstyper på tumormutationsbyrden. Selvom denne undersøgelse
fortsat er undervejs, tyder resultaterne på en potentiel sammenhæng mellem forskellige strålingstyper,
deres effekt på tumormutationsbyrden og dannelsen af tumor neoantigener.
Konklusionerne i dette speciale bidrager til forståelsen af de immunmekanismer, der er in­
volveret i ImmunoSABR­kombinationsterapien og ligger grundarbejdet for fremtidige under­
søgelser i patienter, hvilket åbner op for forbedrede behandlingsstrategier ved metastatisk ikke­
småcellet lungekræft.

Introduction
Lung cancer is one of the most commonly diagnosed types of cancer and accounts for an esti­
mated 1.6 million deaths each year, with this number expected to double by 2040 [1]. Among the
various subtypes of lung cancer, non­small cell lung cancer (NSCLC) is the most prevalent, ac­
counting for around 85% of diagnoses [2], [3]. Notably, half of NSCLC patients have metastatic
disease at the time of diagnosis [4]. While significant progress has been made in the treat­
ment of NSCLC, particularly with the emergence of more effective therapies such as immune
checkpoint inhibitors (ICI) ­ which have now become the standard of care (SOC) ­ patients with
metastatic disease typically do not experience long­term benefits from these treatments. The
limited clinical benefits in this context can be attributed to various resistance mechanisms that
hinder the induced immune response, such as inadequate antigen presentation, low neoantigen
load, and insensitivity of immune cells [5]. Although there are ongoing developments in the field
of ICI therapies aimed at addressing these resistance mechanisms, their impact in the context
of NSCLC remains limited. There is therefore a great need for new therapies against NSCLC
that can provide long term benefit for patients suffering from metastatic disease.
Recently, a novel combination of radiotherapy (RT) and the L19­IL2 immunocytokine, has shown
promising results in both pre­clinical, and phase I clinical trials (NCT02086721). In fact, a phase
II clinical trial (NCT03705403) is currently underway, with the aims of further investigating the
safety, efficacy, and optimal dosing in a larger cohort of NSCLC patients. The combination
therapy coined ”ImmunoSABR”, uses stereotactic ablative radiotherapy (SABR) to irradiate the
primary tumor, followed by intravenous (IV) administration of L19­IL2. This L19­IL2 immuno­
cytokine is a fusion product of human recombinant IL2 bound to L19, a short chain fragment
variable (scFv) of an antibody which binds to the B­fibronectin isoform, ectodomain­B (ED­
B). Due to ED­Bs involvement in angiogenesis, it is found upregulated in solid tumors, which
allows for increased intratumoral IL2 concentrations, without increased toxicity [6]. The com­
bined effects of RT and L19­IL2 have shown strong anti­tumor effects in both murine models
from preclinical trials and in human patients enrolled in the phase I trial [7], [8]. However, the
underlying immunomechanisms of the treatment, which contribute to the positive clinical out­
comes are yet to be fully understood. Therefore, the aim of this master’s thesis is to investigate

Background
Given the substantial emphasis on immunophenotyping in this thesis, the subsequent sections
will primarily focus on introducing the cellular components of the immune system, in addition to
highlighting their role in homeostasis and in cancer, with focus on their phenotype and functional
states.
2.1
The Immune System
The immune system is a highly sophisticated network of cells, tissues, and organs that work
together to defend the body against harmful invaders such as viruses, parasites, and bacteria.
The immune system is comprised of both physical barriers to infection (such as the mucosa
and skin) as well as two distinct branches of cellular immunity: the innate and adaptive immune
responses. A key underlining principle across both branches is their ability to tolerate the body’s
own, non­harmful cells and tissue (i.e., the ability to recognize self) [9].
2.1.1
Innate Immunity
The innate immune response is comprised of both cellular and molecular components which
contribute to a fast­reacting, first line of defense once a pathogen has entered the body. The
soluble molecular components (e.g., proteins, antibodies, and peptides) comprise the comple­
ment system, which aids in pathogen clearance through mechanisms such as opsonization (i.e.,
marking cells and pathogens for elimination), phagocyte recruitment, and direct antimicrobial
activity [9]. In conjunction with the complement system, the cellular components innate immu­
nity synergistically facilitate pathogen clearance. The major players within the innate immune
system are phagocytes (e.g., macrophages and monocytes), granulocytes (e.g., neutrophils
and basophils), dendritic cells (DCs), and natural killer (NK) cells. Phagocytes work with the
complement system to phagocytose pathogens and deploy antimicrobial proteins to facilitate
effective pathogen clearance. Granulocytes have direct cytotoxic capabilities, characterized by
cytoplasmic granules which carry cytolytic components that facilitate cell killing. Dendritic cells
are professional antigen­presenting cells (APCs), which initiate the adaptive immune response
and contribute directly to pathogen clearance via mechanisms such as micropinocytosis and
receptor­mediated endocytosis [10], [11]. A more in depth overview of the myeloid cells in­
volved in the innate immune response can be found in Section 2.3. Although innate immune
cells do not develop antigen­specific receptors, unlike their counterparts in adaptive immunity,
they still require the capacity to differentiate harmful pathogens from self. Innate immune cells
accomplish this through the use of pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) such as toll­like re­
ceptors (TLRs), NOD­like receptors (NLRs), RIG­1­like receptors (RLRs), and C­type lectin
receptors (CLRs) [12]. These PRRs recognize specific molecular motifs that are unique to non­
self pathogens, known as pattern associated molecular patterns or PAMPs [13]. In addition to
PAMPs, PRRs are also able to recognize danger signals such as specific proteins, molecular
components, and other metabolites which result from destructive events to self, such cell death
and tissue damage. These molecules are known as danger associated molecular patterns or
DAMPs [14]. The final subset of innate immune cells are the NK­cells which, through antibody
recognition in a process known as antibody­dependent cellular cytotoxicity (ADCC), eliminate
infected host cells. NK cells are characterized by their expression of CD49b+ and NKp46+ in
mice, and CD56+ in humans. They can also be further divided up into immature and mature
NK cells, indicated by CD11b expression in mice, and CD16 expression in humans. Function­
ally, mature NK cells are more cytotoxic, able to rapidly release cytotoxic granules, but secrete
lower levels of immunomodulatory cytokines. Whereas, the opposite is true for immature NK
cells [11], [15], [16].

2.1.2
Adaptive Immunity
In cases where the induction of innate immunity is insufficient in clearing a pathogen, the adap­
tive immune system is engaged. Adaptive immunity is dependent on antigen recognition, and
the cellular components of it achieve this through specialized antigen receptors which need
time to recognize the pathogen in question. This delay is also the reason why adaptive im­
munity takes longer to respond to pathogens. However, it has the advantage of being more
accurate, more effective, and can provide long term immunological memory [11]. The major
cellular components of adaptive immunity are T­cells and B­cells.
Figure 2.1: Visual overview of cytokine signaling and effects on T­cell functional fate. Fig­
ure is made using assets from Biorender.
T­cells (CD3+) are a subset of lymphocytes which express a specialized antigen receptor, known
as the T­cell receptor or TCR. Each TCR has a specificity towards a unique peptide fragment
of an antigen (Ag). The specificity of this receptor controls the T­cells’ function, which varies
depending on whether the T­cell is a CD4+ helper T­cell (Th), a CD4+ regulatory T­cell (Treg), or
a CD8+ cytotoxic T­cell (CTL) [9]. Regardless of the subtype, a T­cell becomes activated when
it encounters an APC, which presents the antigenic peptide on either a MHC/HLA­I molecule
(for CD8+) or MHC/HLA­II molecule (for CD4+). In addition to this, co­stimulatory and cytokine
signaling are provided to the T­cell to ensure proper function (see Section 2.2.2 for more). Once
a T­cell is activated, it travels to the site(s) of inflammation and exerts its respective effector
function once they encounter cells expressing the antigen in question. For what concerns Th
cells, the precise effector function is determined based on the cytokine signaling that the cell
receives during activation (see Figure 2.1 for details). Although, generally speaking, all Th cells
aid the coordination and function of other immune cells to facilitate pathogen clearance [17].
Tregs on the other hand, induce an immunomodulatory effect when activated. Their functions
play a significant role in maintaining immune tolerance and preventing an excessive immune
response. There are many mechanisms by which Tregs achieve this, including the production
of suppressive cytokines such as IL­10, TGF­β, and IL­35 [18]. Furthermore, upregulation of
ICOS in Tregs is associated with improved proliferation, survival, and function; in part due to the
induction of IL­10 production by ICOS [19]. Lastly, activation of CD8+ T­cells results in killing of
the infected/problematic cell. The elimination of target cells is facilitated by release of cytotoxic
proteins and enzymes, including perforin and granzyme­B (GZMB) [9].

In addition to T­cells, the other major subset of adaptive immune cells are the B­cells (CD19+).
These cells are able to recognize antigens through their own specialized receptor, known as
the B­cell receptor (BCR) [20]. Upon antigen recognition by the BCR, a multi­step activation
pathway is initiated. However, in the context of this thesis, the B­cells activation process is
not central, and will not be fully explained. Instead, it is important to note that B­cell activation
can generate several different types of B­cells. The first are extrafollicular plasmablasts, which
are short­term plasma cells that secrete IgM antibodies with low affinity to the antigen. These
cells have the advantage of being rapidly generated, serving as the initial response against
an antigen [21], [22]. In addition to this, long­term plasma B­cells, which secrete IgA, IgG, or
IgE antibodies with high affinity for the antigen, are also generated. The difference in affinity
and time taken to generate these cells is due to the multiple rounds of affinity selection where
somatic hypermutation and class switching allow for the selection of antibodies with the highest
possible affinity to the antigen. Furthermore, memory B­cells which can generate plasma B­
cells upon a re­challenge are also generated [23], [24]. The high affinity antibodies produced
by these cells can bind to the target antigen and facilitate clearance of pathogens and infected
cells through either neutralization, opsonization, complement activation, or ADCC [25].
2.2
T-Cells
2.2.1
Development
T­cells, or thymus­dependent lymphocytes, are derived from common lymphoid progenitors
(CLPs), that migrate from the bone marrow into the thymus. Since these CLPs can also give
rise to other immune cells, the thymic CLPs must first commit to their T­cell lineage. This com­
mitment occurs via Notch signaling, which induces transcription factors needed for T­cell de­
velopment, such as T­cell factor 1 (TCF1) [9]. An overview of further T­cell development can
be seen in Figure 2.2. These early thymic progenitors are referred to as double negative (DN)
cells, due their lack of both CD4 and CD8 expression [26]. Moreover, these cells begin as DN1
cells and transition through three subsequent DN­stages, ending with DN4. Throughout this
development, the differentiation between αβ­ and γδ T­cells occurs. Where the latter repre­
sents a more unconventional subset of T­cells, found in non­lymphoid tissue, which can exert
pro­inflammatory and cytotoxic effects independently of the aforementioned TCR­MHC inter­
action [27]. More specifically, this selection occurs between the DN2 and DN3 stages, where
TCR gene segment rearrangement occurs. Next, once the cells have transitioned to DN3 cells
­ having rearranged their TCR­β chain to form the pre­TCR complex, rescuing the cells from
apoptosis ­ the transition to DN4 occurs. This DN4 stage is actually transient, where the loss
of CD44 and CD25 expression leads to up­regulation of both CD4 and CD8, giving rise to the
double positive (DP) cells [26].

2.2.2
Activation
Following the journey of the naive T­cell, its expression of CCR7/CD62L allows it to home to­
wards secondary lymphoid organs (SLOs) [31]. It is here where activation of naive T­cells pri­
marily occur. Activation is crucial in inducing proliferation and differentiation into effector cells.
The activation of a T­cell is dependent on antigen presentation by an APC, which provides the
T­cell with three important signals, as illustrated in Figure 2.3.

The first signal is provided through the interaction between the peptide­MHC complex and the
TCR. The second signal is CD28 mediated co­stimulation, through its interaction with CD80/CD86
expressed on APCs. This signal is crucial in regulating autoimmunity, and the lack of signal
2 leads to an anergic T­cell response. The third, and final signal is through cytokines which
decides the effector function of the naive cell (into Th1, Th2, or Th17), ensures its survival,
and maintenance into memory cells [32]. Upon activation, naive T­cells also upregulate CD27,
another co­stimulatory molecule which ­ upon interaction with its ligand, CD70 expressed on
activated lymphocytes and matured DCs ­ aids in the survival of activated T­cells throughout
multiple rounds of proliferation [33]. In addition, CXCR3 expression is also rapidly induced on
activated naive T­cells. The CXCR3 chemokine binds to three receptors: CXCL9, CXCL10, and
CXCL11; which aid in trafficking T­cells to sites of inflammation [34].

memory subsets known as memory T­cells. At homeostasis, these cells are in a quiescent state,
indicated by low levels of Ki67 [35]. Moreover, these T­cells are characterized by their persis­
tence even without the presence of an antigen, as well as the improved time and effectiveness

is instead indicative of aging and repeated antigen stimulation which results in DNA damage,
with reduced proliferation and activation, while maintaining the cell’s pro­inflammatory charac­
teristics. As a result, T­cells in this state are characterized by decreased expression of CD28
and CD27, and the upregulation of CD57 [47].

2.4
Immune System and Cancer - The Tumor Micro Environment
Fundamentally, cancer is a disease caused by malignant cells, which are genetically unstable.
Due to their genetic instability, they are a constant source of altered self­antigens, which due to
their mutations, are no longer considered self by the host immune system. This important dis­
tinction should, theoretically, lead to the effective clearance of the altered, malignant cells by the
host immune system through the clearance mechanisms described previously. However, ma­
lignant cells initially employ several mechanisms by which they escape immune surveillance,
usually in the alteration of their antigenicity and immunogenicity [72], [73]. Once tumors de­
velop, a generally immunosuppressive environment, known as the tumor micro environment
(TME), is created. Within this environment, a functioning immune response should theoretically
consist of several key steps such as proper lymphocyte infiltration, recognition of cancer cells
by T­cells, cancer cell killing, and release of cancer antigens ­ which lead to effective tumor
clearance. However, the TME exerts various mechanisms which inhibit or severely reduce the
effectiveness of these crucial steps [74]. One such mechanism is through the expression of im­
mune checkpoint molecules, which can directly block T­cell function and lead to exhaustion, as
explained in Section 2.2.4). Another prominent mechanism is through the induction of immuno­
suppresive Tregs. As mentioned previously, Tregs can exert a suppressive effect on CD4+ and
CD8+ T­cells, usually to the benefit of the host organism by preventing autoimmune responses
and excessive tissue damage. However, this mechanism is instead hijacked in the TME to pre­
vent potent anti­tumor responses. In addition to their usual immunoregulatory functions, Tregs
in the TME also utilize mechanisms that work synergistically with the TME’s intrinsic features.
For example, Tregs in the tumor overexpress immune checkpoint molecules such as LAG3 and
TIGIT, further enhancing their immmunosuppressive potential. Moreover, the expression of the
immune checkpoint molecule CTLA­4 can also inhibit DC­mediated T­cell activation. Other,
non­specific effects such as the secretion of CD39 also contribute to the positive feedback loop
that augments suppression in the TME [43], [75].
Moreover, the TME also has profound effects on myeloid cell development and drive the differ­
entiation of immunosuppressive subsets such as tumor associated macrophages (TAMs) and
a broader category of cells know as myeloid­derived suppressor cells (MDSCs). With respect
to TAMs, the TME’s intrinsic characteristics such as hypoxia and the abundance of free adeno­
sine, in combination with the recruitment of suppressive immune cells such as Tregs polarize
macrophage differentiation toward TAMs instead of conventional MØs [48]. These cells con­
tribute to the suppressive TME non­specifically, through the secretion of factors such as IL­10
and TGF­β, in addition to the expression of checkpoint molecules (e.g. PD­L1) that can inhibit
proper T­cell activation [76]. In addition to this, TAMs also contribute to tumor growth by secret­
ing soluble factors which aid angiogenesis and facilitates metastasis [77], [78]. MDSCs on the
other hand, refers to a broader category of cells that are actually made up of several major pop­
ulations, such as monocytic MDSCs (M­MDSCs) and PMN­MDSCs. Where the former share
cellular markers similar to classical monocytes. However, instead of having pro­inflammatory
functions, they instead exert immunosuppressive effects primarily against CD8+ T­cells. The
most prominent being the secretion of ARG1 (which metabolizes L­Arg, a necessary component
of TCR signaling) and iNOS (which generates nitric oxide which regulates T­cell function and
has a pro­apoptotic role) [79], [80]. PMN­MDSCs also primarily target CD8+ T­cells. However,
this is achieved mainly through the production ROS, which have been shown to regulate T­cell

between M­MDSCs and PMN­MDSCs, the latter represent a larger majority in circulation, while
the former show greater suppressive capabilities per cell [84].

2.5
The ImmunoSABR Combination Therapy
As mentioned previously, the main focus of this thesis is on the ImmunoSABR combination
therapy which constitutes two distinct therapies: stereotactic ablative radiotherapy (SABR) and
the L19­IL2 immunocytokine. The following sections will provide background on each of the
monotherapies and highlight the expected therapeutic effect in the treatment of NSCLC.
2.5.1
Stereotactic Ablative Radiotherapy (SABR)
Stereotactic ablative radiotherapy (SABR) is a form of external beam radiotherapy (EBRT),
which is unique in its ability to deliver high doses of radiation (often >5Gy) to a highly specific
area with sharp dose fall­off, minimizing its effects on surrounding tissue [85]. It has been shown
to be effective in the treatment of solid tumors, while limiting toxicity, which helps to highlight
some of its benefits over traditional EBRT methods [86]. Furthermore, the effectiveness of
these traditional EBRT methods in the treatment of solid tumors are usually governed by the
4 R’s of radiotherapy. Those being: repair, redistribution, reoxygination, and repopulation [87].
However, by examining the intrinsic properties of SABR, it is clear to see that these requirements
contribute very little to its success, as all tumor cells are simply eliminated. For example, the
first R, repair, refers to challenges associated with sub­lethal doses of radiation which can occur
in traditional EBRT. In such cases, the non­lethal damage induced by the radiation can allow
for DNA repair and subsequent tumor resurgence. However, SABR delivers multiple fractions
of high dose radiation within a short time period. This makes DNA­repair in tumor cells an
impossibility, or at the very least, occur very sparsely [88]. This can partially be attributed to the
a greater induction of necroptosis, compared to apoptosis [89].
Instead, other radiobiological factors are believed to play a crucial role in its effectiveness ­
in addition to its ability to ablate tumor cells. For example, the high dose radiation used in
SABR has been shown to induce vascular injuries [88]. These injuries have been linked to
an enhance tumor clearance due to preventing angiogenesis and starving tumors of their vital
blood supply. The vascular damage also induces thrombosis and ischemia which can lead to
necrosis [90]. In addition, radiotherapy has also been associated with its capacity to stimulate
an immune response against both the primary irradiated tumor as well as non­irradiated tumors
(the latter is referred to as an abscopal effect). The severe damage to tumor tissue, resulting
in necroptosis, releases both tumor­associated antigens (TAAs) and DAMPs which can aid in
generating an immune response. This is further enhanced by either direct or indirect induction of
inflammatory cytokines such as type­I interferons and TNF­α [85], [88]. Therefore, the potential
synergistic effect of combining SABR with immunotherapy could offer an improved approach
for treating cancer.
2.5.2
The L19-IL2 Immunocytokine
L19­IL2 is a fusion product, consisting of human recombinant IL2 and the scFv L19. Where
the L19 fragment is specific towards the B­fibronectin isoform, ectodomain­B (ED­B). ED­B is
expressed by endothelial cells, myofibroblasts, and cancer cells which make up solid tumors.
In normal, healthy tissue, ED­B expression is tightly regulated and rarely expressed, with the
exception of embryonic tissue and the uterus/ovaries. On the contrary, ED­B is heavily ex­
pressed in solid tumors of many cancers, including NSCLC, in both the primary tumor and sites
of metastasis [91]. More specifically, ED­B tends to either be expressed around proangiogenic
12
Investigating the Immunomechanisms of the Combination of Radiotherapy with
Immunocytokine Therapy: Insights from Preclinical Studies and Phase I Clinical Trials
==PAGE:25==
vasculature or between stromal and perivascular tissue. Importantly, normal tissue which di­
rectly surround tumor tissue usually express low levels of ED­B [92]. This highly restrictive
expression of ED­B, in the context of L19­IL2, allows for highly specific delivery and accumula­
tion of IL2, limiting systemic exposure.
IL2 itself is an immunomodulatory cytokine, which has a potent effect on T­cell growth and pro­
liferation. Upon it’s discovery, it was aptly named the T­cell growth factor (TCGF). Moreover,
IL2 also plays a crucial role in both CD4+ and CD8+ T­cell function. In CD4+ T­cells, it has a
role in fate determination, as it is able to drive either a Th1 or Th2 response, by manipulating
the expression of key receptors and transcriptions factors [93]. In CD8+ T­cells, IL2 is able to
enhance their cytolytic activity. This is achieved through inducing expression of cytokines such
as IFN­γ, which stimulate the production of cytotoxic molecules like granzyme­B [94]. Due to
its intrinsic ability to promote a potent T­cell response, it has seen use in the treatment of malig­
nant melanomas and renal cell carcinoma. However, the systemic administration of IL2, either
through continuous IV infusion or a bolus injection, has been shown to cause several toxicities
[95]. The most infamous of which is capillary leak syndrome. This phenomenon is a result
of a complex mechanism involving various components including pro­inflammatory cytokines,
complement activation, and neutrophils. Regardless, the result is reduced vascular resistance
and increased capillary permeability which displaces fluid out into the extravascular space. The
significant displacement of fluid into the extravascular space can cause to hypovolemia, leading
to decreased blood flow to vital organs, which can result in organ failure (often affecting multiple
organs at once) [96], [97]. Therefore, specific delivery of IL2, through its coupling with L19 can
be used to circumvent the associated toxicities, by increasing intratumoral IL2 concentration
and limiting systemic exposure. This targeted approach not only mitigates toxic effects but can
also optimize the potent anti­tumor properties of IL2 for maximum therapeutic benefit.

the effectiveness of SABR, to work in conjunction with L19­IL2 to effectively clear solid tumors.

3.1
In-Vivo Mouse Experiments
The in­vivo mouse experiments were performed by collaborators at the University of Maastricht,
part of the ImmunoSABR consortium. The experiments were approved by the Animal Ethical
committee at Maastricht and adhere to guidelines for animal welfare. The experimental timeline
and overview of the murine cohort for the CT26 and C51 studies can be found in Figure 4.1 and
Figure 4.9, respectively.
CT26 Study
BALB/c mice were injected with 1.5x106 CT26 colon carcinoma cells (day ­5). Inoculated mice
were then randomized into four treatment groups: PBS only, RT only, L19­IL2 only, or RT+L19­
IL2. The RT only and RT+L19­IL2 groups had their primary tumors irradiated on day 0. The
L19­IL2 only and RT+L19­IL2 received intravenous L19­IL2 on days 1, 3, and 5. Mice in all
treatment groups were sacrificed starting on day 6, with excision of both the tumor and spleen
(see Supp. Fig. S.1 for sample overview). Tumors were measured using vernier calipers, where
tumor volume was calculated using the formula for ellipsoid volume (π/6·l·w·h). Measurements
were made until 4xTSV, the primary endpoint of the study, which was the time taken for the tumor
volume to reach four times that of its start volume.
C51 Study
BALB/c mice were injected with 1.5x106 C51 colon carcinoma cells (day ­10). Inoculated mice
were then randomized into four treatment groups: PBS only, RT only, L19­IL2 only, or RT+L19­
IL2. The RT only and RT+L19­IL2 groups had their primary tumors irradiated on day 0. The
L19­IL2 only and RT+L19­IL2 received intravenous L19­IL2 on days 1 and 3. Mice in all treat­
ment groups were sacrificed on day 6, with excision of both the tumor and spleen. No tumor
measurements were made during the C51 study.

were resuspended in 250µL of FACS buffer, with the addition of one drop of ArC™negative
beads.

3.5
DNA & RNA Extraction
For the extraction of DNA and RNA from murine tumor tissue, Qiagen’s AllPrep DNA/RNA Mini
kit was used. All centrifugation steps are performed for 15 seconds at 9000 x g, unless otherwise
stated.
Sample Preparation
Each snap­frozen murine tissue sections, embedded in OCT were placed into a 1.5mL Eppen­
dorf tube. Then 650µL of RLT Buffer Plus (with BME) was added to each tube, ad vortexed
for 3 seconds. One metal bead was added to the mixture. The sample was homogenized us­
ing the TissueLyser with for 2 minutes at 30 cycles per second. The homogenized lysate was
transferred to an AllPrep DNA spin column and centrifuged for 30 seconds at 9000 x g. The
flowthrough is used for subsequent RNA extraction steps. The column was transferred to a new
2mL collection tube and stored at 4°C until the ”DNA Extraction” steps.
RNA Extraction
First, 600µL of 70% ethanol was added to the flowthrough obtained from the sample preparation
phase. The solution was mixed, transferred to a RNeasy spin column. After centrifuging, the
flowthrough was discarded. 700µL of Buffer RW1 was added to the column and centrifuged,
and the flowthrough was discarded. Then, 500µL of Buffer RPE was added to the column and
centrifuged, where the flowthrough was discarded. This step was then repeated once more,
however centrifuging for 2 minutes at 9000 x g instead. The column was placed in a new 2mL
collection tube, and was centrifuged for 1 minute at 9000 x g was to dry the column. The column
was then transferred to a new 1.5mL collection tube, and 30µL of RNase­free water was added
to the column membrane. After waiting for 1 minute, the column was centrifuged for 1 minute
at 9000 x g to elute the RNA. To perform quality control (QC), 2µL of the elute was transferred
to a 0.2mL PCR tube, leaving 28µL of extracted RNA which was stored in the freezer (­20°C).
DNA Extraction
500µL of Buffer AW1 was added to the DNA spin column from the preparation phase, which was
then centrifuged. The flowthrough was discarded, but the collection tube was retained. Then,
500µL of Buffer AW2 was added to the column and centrifuged for 2 minutes at 9000 x g. The

3.6
DNA & RNA QC
Nanodrop
Quality control of both nucleic acid quantity and purity was performed using the Nanodrop®
spectrophotometer (Thermo Fisher Scientific). 1µL of nucleic acid sample was pipetted onto
the Nanodrop® platform and measurements of UV absorption at 260/280nm and 260/230nm
were recorded. The concentration is calculated using the absorption at 260nm, using the Beer­
Lambert law. Purity was assessed based on ratios between the absorption at 260nm/280nm
and at 260nm/230nm.
Qubit
Quality control of nucleic acid quantity was performed using the Qubit™fluorometer (Invitrogen).
Qubit™reagent is diluted 1:200 in Qubit™buffer to obtain the working solution. The standards
are prepared by adding 190µL of the working solution to 10 µL of dsDNA or RNA standards.
The DNA and RNA samples are prepared by adding 199µL of the working solution to 1µL of the
sample. The machine is then calibrated using the prepared standards. Thereafter, the samples
are measured to obtain the concentration.

4.3
Summary of Mouse Model Results
The immunophenotyping analyses conducted in two distinct mouse models were aimed at elu­
cidating the immunomechanisms in response to the combination of RT + L19­IL2 therapies.
The analyses revealed insights into the dynamics of the immune cell populations and their phe­
notypic characteristics. Notably, both studies demonstrated distinct alterations in immune cell
composition and activation profiles in response to the combination therapy. However, the large
amount of information obtained in this analysis complicates the understanding of the intricate
immunological processes at play. Therefore, narrowing down the analysis to the results that
actually shed light on the immunomechanisms underlying the therapy is necessary. This is
especially important in order to gain insights into how the therapy achieves its positive clinical
outcomes. This section aims to do so, by summarizing the observed changes in the immune
cell compartments in both the CT26 and C51 mouse models.
Figure 4.17 A presents a visual overview and summary of the general immune mechanisms un­
derlying the combination therapy, with a specific focus on observations derived from the CT26
mouse model. Analysis of the overall immune cell compartment reveals no changes within the
tumor or spleen which may indicate the mechanisms by which the combination therapy facil­
itates tumor clearance. However, within the myeloid cell compartment, a significant increase
in tumor infiltration by myeloid cells is observed. Further examination within this compartment
reveals that the increased tumor infiltration primarily stems from a significant rise in neutrophils
(PMNs). Additionally, a reduction is observed in the frequency of the overall TAM population,
in addition to a decline in the proportion of functional cells (characterized by CD80/CD86 and
CD38/CD39 expression). Conversely, no significant changes are noted in the myeloid cell com­
partment within the spleen. Regarding the T­cell compartment, no indicative changes in the fre­
quencies of CD4+ or CD8+ memory subsets are observed within the tumor. However, further
examination of the T­cell compartment reveals an increase in central memory Tex cells in the
tumor and an increase in CXCR3+ CD8+ TEMs in the spleen.
Figure 4.17 B presents a visual summary of the general immune mechanisms underlying the
combination therapy, and in particular, the results seen in the C51 mouse model. Exploration
of the major immune subsets reveals a rise in NK cell infiltration into the tumor, as well as an
increase in the mature fraction. In the spleen, a significant increase in the frequency of NK cells
is also observed, accompanied by an elevated proportion of mature cells as well. Within the in­
tratumoral myeloid cell compartment, a notable decrease is observed in the frequency of TAMs.
This coincides with a shift toward the pro­inflammatory subsets, namely PMNs, monocytes, and
MØs. However, an increase in monocytes and MØs expressing Egr2 is also observed. In the
spleen, no notable changes in the composition of the myeloid compartment is observed. Re­
garding the T­cell compartment, no improvement in tumor infiltration is observed. In addition,
investigations into both the tumoral and splenic compartments using a combination of manual
gating and clustering methods reveal no indicative trends of any coherent immunomechanisms
at play. The only consistent observations are non significant changes in the frequency of intra­
tumoral TEMs and CD4+ TCMs in the spleen.
As highlighted here, the outcomes of the murine preclinical studies exhibit heterogeneous re­
sults, indicating a diverse range of responses across the two mouse models. To further in­
vestigate this intriguing observation and determine its relevance in humans, a Phase I trial is
initiated. The exploratory outcomes of which, aim to investigate whether this trend persists in
human subjects. The Phase I study assesses the safety, tolerability, and initial efficacy of the
treatment in a small group of human patients, providing valuable insights into its potential for
further clinical development.

patient­specific for both responders and non­responders. However, a distinct cluster unique to
the responders is consistently observed across both patients (Fig. 4.20 B).

derived from the UMAP analysis (Fig. 4.20 C). The identified clusters are subsequently char­

analysis, the previously identified cluster exclusive to patients who respond to the combination

Phase II Antibody Panel
To further provide critical insights into the safety, efficacy, and optimal dosing in a larger cohort
of patients, a phase II trial of the combination therapy is underway to further bridge the gap
between preclinical studies and the subsequent phases of clinical development. Therefore, the
phase II clinical data is a crucial aspect in understanding the immunomechanisms in humans,
given the larger cohort, especially when combined with the results from the preclinical and phase
I studies. However, due to time constraints and challenges with the availability of samples, the
analysis of phase II patient samples was not possible in the thesis’s time­frame. Instead, the
antibody panels that are designed to be used in this analysis are presented, as they are serve
as an important insight to the analytical strategy of future investigations.
4.5.1
Proposed T-cell panel design
For the investigation of the T­cell compartment, two T­cell panels are proposed as part of this
analysis (Table 4.1 & 4.2). The decision to include a second T­cell panel was made to allow for
a more thorough analysis of T­cell function, to account for any effects related to Tregs and their
function, and to allow for potential interplay between the T­cell and myeloid cell panels. As a
result, Panel 1 has a focus on the functional subsets of cytotoxic and helper T­cells. Whereas
Panel 2 has a focus on exhaustion of T­cells and the function of Tregs. The proposed panel
design for the first T­cell panel can be seen in Table 4.1, with the proposed gating strategy to
be used with the panel, presented in Figure 4.23. As illustrated in Table 4.1, Panel 1 is built on
the T­cell panel from the phase I trial, with some changes. Notably, with regards to lineage, the
inclusion of both CD28 alongside CD27 allows for the differentiation between TEM and Teff cells
given the downregulation of both co­stimulatory molecules in the latter (Fig. 4.23). In addition,
TCRγδ was moved to Panel 2. Otherwise, only changes to the fluorochromes assigned to each
marker are made.
The proposed panel design for the second T­cell panel can be seen in Table 4.2, with the pro­
posed gating strategy to be used with the panel, presented in Figure 4.24. As illustrated here,
with regards to lineage, this panel will allow for the identification of γδ T­cells, CD4+ and CD8+
T­cells (including the Naive, TEM, TCM, TEMRA subsets), and Tregs. Moreover, the inclusion
of ICOS will also allow for a better understanding of the functional capacities of the identified
Tregs (see Section 2.1.2 for details). In addition, as eluded to previously, two markers are added
in an effort to improve the synergy between the T­cell and myeloid cell panels. The first addition
is the checkpoint molecule TIGIT (see Section 2.2.4 for details). The second, is OX40 (CD134),
a member of the TNF­receptor family. OX40 is found expressed primarily on activated CD4+
and CD8+ T­cells, and is cited as being a potent simulator of the immune response in several
immune diseases, including cancer [98]. Moreover, the ligands for these receptors ­ CD155
and CD252 (OX40L), respectively ­ can be expressed by a variety of myeloid cells and are thus
included in the myeloid cell panel (Table 4.3).
4.5.2
Proposed myeloid cell panel design
The proposed panel design for the myeloid cell panel can be seen in Table 4.3, with the proposed
gating strategy to be used with the panel, presented in Figure 4.25. Similar to T­cell Panel 1, this
panel is also built upon its phase I equivalent, with some additions. Namely, the lineage gating
has been altered slightly to now allow for the identification of early­stage MDSCs or E­MDSCs
(Fig. 4.25). In addition, CD57 is also added to the panel in order to examine its expression on
NK cells. Lastly, as stated previously, CD155 and CD252 (OX40L) are included in an effort to
better understand the interplay between T­cells in myeloid cells.

5
Discussion
Advancements in the understanding of the radiobiological effects of administering radiotherapy,
in particular SABR, has opened the possibilities for exploiting these immunostimulatory effects
through combination with various immunotherapies. In particular, ImmunoSABR’s combination
of tumor­targeting IL2 with SABR, has shown positive outcomes in pre­clinical trials and a safe
profile in a phase I trial. This thesis aims to uncover the immune­mediated mechanisms of tumor
clearance through immunophenotyping and other analyses which lay the groundwork for future
research. For what concerns the immunophenotyping analyses the investigation presents itself
as a black box scenario. Here, the input to the system (e.g., treatment, tumor cell line, etc.)
and the output (clinical outcomes such as PFS, cure, tumor growth delay, etc.) are known,
while the internal working of the systems which convert these ”inputs” to the observed ”outputs”
are unknown or poorly characterized. Therefore, the discussions held in Sections 5.1 & 5.2
will primarily pertain to uncovering the internal workings of these ”black boxes”, in an effort to
elucidate the possible immunomechanisms of the treatment.
5.1
Pre-Clinical Mouse Models
5.1.1
CT26 Mouse Model
Information on the outcomes of the mice at the time of sacrifice, show a significant delay in tumor
progression in mice receiving the combination therapy (n=8), when compared to the monother­
apy groups and the tumor controls (Supp. Fig. S.2). These outcomes indicate that the combina­
tion therapy could induce a potent anti­tumor immune response, leading to tumor growth delay.
Furthermore, from the results obtained in the immunophenotyping study of the CT26 model, it
is hypothesized that this anti­tumor effect is likely mediated by dynamics within the myeloid cell
compartment. The significant increase in tumor infiltration by neutrophils support this hypothe­
sis, with an approximate doubling of their presence when compared to the tumor control. This
increase is significant given their crucial role in anti­tumor immunity, where they can facilitate
direct tumor cell cytotoxicity and stimulation of adaptive immunity, including T­cells, by promot­
ing a pro­inflammatory environment [99]. Moreover, this shift towards a more pro­inflammatory
environment within the tumor is also indicated by the decrease in the frequency of immuno­
suppressive TAMs. Importantly, these TAMs are also seen to be less functionally active, an
aspect of which, is associated with positive clinical outcomes in several cancer models and has
recently become a target for novel cancer therapies [100].
Interestingly, it is also observed that changes in the T­cell compartment are relatively insignifi­
cant and do not suggest a coherent mechanism through which these cells could contribute to
tumor clearance. In fact, one of the only observations which was consistent between manual
gating and clustering is the increased frequency of CXCR3+ TEMs in the spleen. The lack of a
potent T­cell response against the irradiated tumor is actually something that has been reported
in a previous study of RT + L19­IL2 using mice bearing C51 tumors. In this study, the deple­
tion of CD4+ or CD8+ T­cells on day 5 after RT did not result in a significant difference in the
outcome of the mice. They instead suspect that the anti­tumoral activity against the irradiated
tumor was completed by day 5. It is however important to mentioned that a T­cell response was
involved in a curative abscopal effect on non­irradiated secondary tumors [6]. Although a poten­
tial parallel could be drawn, the mice bearing the CT26 tumors do not see a curative effect, and
so the lack of a T­cell response cannot be attributed to the lack of a need for one. Intriguingly,
another study of the combination therapy concluded that the observed curative effect on mice
bearing C51 tumors is dependent on CD8+ T­cells [7]. Therefore, the lack of a consistent and
potent T­cell response across different studies is not completely new, with contradicting results

being reported by our collaborators even when using the same model and treatment schedule.
These differences have been, and can be, attributed to several factors including the relative
expression of ED­B and the initial immune cell composition prior to starting treatment. As the
study carried out by Zegers et al. illustrates, the reduced level of ED­B expression found in LLC
tumors when compared to C51 tumors leads to a decline from a curative effect to just tumor
growth delay. In addition, an increased baseline frequency of CD8+ T cells was also seen in the
C51 tumor, when compared to the LLC tumor. Even though both models exhibited a significant
increase after treatment, only the C51 saw effective tumor clearance [7]. Therefore, although
no direct explanation is provided as to why a potent T­cell response is not seen, the diversity in
the observations seen here and in previous studies are not completely unexpected.
For what concerns the observed increase in CXCR3+ CD8+ TEMs, it is peculiar that this obser­
vation is not supported by other consistent changes in the T­cell compartment. For instance,
a corresponding increase in intratumoral TEMs would have indicated at least a migration of
cells which might contribute to an anti­tumor effect, where in fact the opposite is observed.
Furthermore, the observations made within the T­cell compartment using manual gating and
clustering were often inconsistent, leading us to believe that a potential T­cell response is not
at play. Moreover, plausible explanations for these mixed observation are further complicated
by the heterogeneity in the times at which these mice were sacrificed, which range from 6 to
26 days after RT. Since the mice sacrificed after day 6 had their tumors and spleen excised
on dates corresponding to 4xTSV, it could potentially explain the increased observation of a
memory subset. Since that the 4xTSV was significantly increased in the combination therapy
group; thereby prolonging their time until sacrifice as well. However, further studies into the
dynamics of the immune cell compartment over time is needed in order to properly understand
the changes that are occurring. Nevertheless, the results from the CT26 mouse model indicate
that the combination of RT + L19­IL2 is able to induce a potent early stage anti­tumor response,
primarily driven by neutrophils.
5.1.2
C51 Mouse Model
To further understand these intricate immune cell dynamics, the effects of the combination ther­
apy was also assessed in a C51 mouse model (as outlined in 3.1. Although similar data regard­
ing 4xTSV were not made available for the C51 study, it is known that curative effects were seen
in the mice receiving the combination therapy. Using this information, in conjunction with the
results obtained in immunophenotyping analysis, it is hypothesized that tumor clearance in this
model is facilitated by dynamics within both the myeloid and NK cell compartment. With respect
to the myeloid compartment, a shift away from the immunosuppressive TAMs and towards the
pro­inflammatory subsets (PMNs and monocytes/MØs) is observed. However, in the case of
the C51 model, this shift is driven by both the influx of monocytes/MØs and neutrophils (PMNs).
Where, the distinction between monocytes/MØs and TAMs are made in order to distinguish
phenotypically suppressive cells from those that are mostly pro­inflammatory. Interestingly, the
augmented populations of the monocytes/MØs subsets are shown to have a larger fraction of
cells that express Egr2, a marker associated with immunosuppression on MØs [101]. How­
ever, a clear majority of cells are still phenotypically pro­inflammatory, as only 10% of cells
express Egr2. In fact, the increase in Egr2 expression could potentially be explained by the
stress and pro­inflammatory environment that is created by both the combination therapy’s di­
rect physiological effects and the influx of pro­inflammatory cells, since such factors are known
to induce the upregulation of early response genes like Egr2 [102], [103]. Taking all these fac­
tors into account, including the reported positive clinical outcomes in these mice, it is likely that
the monocytes/MØs population predominantly enact pro­inflammatory effects within the tumor,
even in light of increased Egr2 expression.
In addition to the myeloid cells, changes in the NK cell compartment are also observed, both in

the tumor and spleen. In mice receiving the combination therapy, tumor infiltration by NK cells
almost doubles when compared to the tumor control. In the spleen however, the combination
therapy performs similarly to the tumor control, where an increase is seen only when compared
to the monotherapy groups. The observed influx of NK cells in the tumor is hypothesized to play
a significant role in the anti­tumor immunity, given their ability to effectively kill cancerous cells.
Their capacity to aid in anti­tumor immunity is also highlighted by the increased proportions
of mature NK cells, indicated by CD11b expression. As mentioned previously, these cells are
characterized by their improved capacity for direct cytotoxic effects, which could contribute to
the anti­tumor effect seen in these mice. This NK­dependent anti­tumor immunity is further
supported by a previous study of the combination therapy in a murine F9 teratocarcinoma model,
which also cites a lack of a cytotoxic T­cell response. However, it is important to highlight that
the F9 tumor model lacks MHC­I expression and is therefore known to predominantly generate
a NK cell response when treated with IL2 based therapies [104].
Similar to what was seen in the CT26 mouse model, the observed dynamics of and within the
T­cell compartment does not indicate a clear mechanism by which this subset of lymphocytes
contributes to the anti­tumor effect. As previously stated, another study on the combination
therapy by Rekers et al. note the lack of a dependence on T­cells in the clearance of the irra­
diated tumor, due a potent early stage response [6]. The hypothesis regarding the observed
innate immunity­driven effect in the CT26 mouse model did not hold up, as it only led to tumor
growth delay. However, in this model, it is hypothesized that the previously reported early stage
response could be attributed to the potent innate immune response consisting of proinflamma­
tory monocytes/MØs and cytotoxic NK cells. However, confidently drawing such a conclusion
across two parallel studies is difficult, and further research into the exact functional capacities of
these innate cells is needed. With that said, it does seem clear that an innate immunity driven
response is likely behind the anti­tumor effects that are seen in this mouse model. In particular,
with pro­inflammatory monocytes/MØs and NK cells playing a central role.
5.1.3
Comparing the Mouse Models
It is clear that when comparing the observations made in two the mouse models, some hetero­
geneity is observed in their anti­tumor responses. Where the CT26 model indicates a primarily
myeloid cell driven response, the C51 model indicates a significant contribution by NK cells as
well. As mentioned before, diversity in the observations made when studying RT + L19­IL2 is not
uncommon, and factors such as ED­B expression and starting immune cell composition have
been noted as potential explanations [6]. In fact, the difference in NK cell contribution could be
explained by the variations in the level of ED­B expression within the C51 and CT26 tumors.
The relatively high level of ED­B expression in the C51 tumor could have led to increased in­
tratumoral IL2 concentrations. This could in turn contribute to a more effective induction of NK
cells, since IL2 is known to induce proliferation and enhance cytotoxicity [105]. However, the
quantification of the relative expression levels of ED­B within both the CT26 and C51 tumors
is needed. Furthermore, information on the immune cell composition at the start of treatment
could also prove useful in accounting for some of the variation in the resulting immune cell re­
sponse. Regardless, the heterogeneity between mouse models is a crucial observation which
may foreshadow a significant variation in the induced anti­tumor immunity in humans, given the
vast heterogeneity between individuals.

therapy, prior to starting treatment, as this timepoint had the highest number of samples avail­
able. This analysis revealed that patients who responded to therapy do in fact have a unique
population of CD4+ TEMRA cells which express both granzyme B (GZMB) and CD57. Their
phenotype indicates a highly differentiated and activated subset, with a potential for cytotoxicity.
Very little is reported in literature surrounding this phenotype; however, one study on HIV found
that the activity of CD57+ cytolytic (GZMB+) CD4+ T­cells, in patients with undetectable viral
load may play a role in controlling HIV infection, together with CD8+ T­cells [106]. Moreover, in
vitro studies on these CD57+ cytolytic CD4+ T­cells indicate their capacity for antigen specific
cytotoxic effects. Interestingly, researchers also found that proliferation of these cells required
IL­2 [106]. Thus, these cells could potentially play a role in the maintenance of NSCLC as well.
However, to verify the significance of this finding, a concrete understanding of their role and
functional capacities within the context of NSCLC is required.
5.3
Phase II Panel Design
The phase II trial of ImmunoSABR presents a great opportunity to better understand the im­
munomechanisms of the treatment within humans, and to build upon the findings made during
the phase I trial. Although analysis of patient samples was not possible during this thesis, some
groundwork has been laid for future research. Namely, three antibody panels intended for
use in the immunophenotyping analysis are proposed: two T­cell panels and one myeloid cell
panel. As highlighted previously, the first T­cell panels builds upon its phase I counterpart, now
allowing for the identification of effector T­cells (Teff) and analysis of their functional and activa­
tion/exhaustion states. The second T­cell panel is introduced to allow for a more comprehensive
investigation into Tregs and their function (through the addition of ICOS), in addition to the in­
teractions between T­cells and myeloid cells. This is achieved through the addition of TIGIT
and OX40. TIGIT, as described previously, is an immune checkpoint molecule that has been
found expressed on T­cells in peripheral blood of NSCLC patients [107], [108]. OX40 (CD134),
is a receptor expressed primarily on CD4+ and CD8+ T­cells, and is a part of the TNFR­family.
The receptor has been shown to play a crucial role in promoting the proliferation and survival
of these cells. Both in vitro and in vivo studies indicate that OX40 signaling has a role in the
later stages of the T­cell life cycle, maintaining both survival during their effector­phase, and
late proliferation [98]. Moreover, it has also shown to have a blocking effect on the activity of
nTregs as well as the generation of iTregs [109]. Therefore, its inclusion on this T­cell panel
might also allow for correlating its expression with the observations made in the frequency and
functionality of Tregs. Importantly, OX40 (CD134) has been found expressed on T­cells in the
peripheral blood of NSCLC patients [110], [111].
As eluded to previously, these additions to the T­cell panel are intended to play synergistically
with the inclusion of their ligands ­ CD155 and OX40L (CD252), respectively ­ on the myeloid
panel. Both ligands have been found expressed on several myeloid cell subsets in human blood,
however expression dynamics and significance in the context of NSCLC is not well defined
[112]–[115]. Moreover, the myeloid cell panel for phase II is built upon the panel from phase
I. In addition to the two aforementioned ligands, the addition of CD15 and a corresponding
adjustment to the gating strategy now allows for the identification of the more recently identified
early stage MDSCs (E­MDSCs), which constitute a more immature subpopualtion of MDSCs
[116]. Since their function with respect to other MDSC subsets are not yet well defined, the
investigation into their roles within the context of NSCLC would be an interesting avenue to
pursue.

Lastly, CD57 is also added to the panel to further investigate the NK cell population in periph­

reported induction by RT + L19­IL2, and our results from the C51 model. More specifically, it
allows for an investigation into their maturation, since CD57 expression on NK cells is linked
to senescence, increased cytotoxicity, and a reduction in their proliferative capacity [117]. Al­
though the proposed panel provides the foundation for a future immunophenotyping analysis,
wet­lab work, such as antibody titration and confirming subset detection in peripheral blood, is
required to further develop and optimize this panel prior to use in analysis of the PBMC samples
from the phase II study.
5.4
TMB Analysis
Due to the highly significant role of radiotherapy (RT) and its radiobiological properties in the
effective combination with L19­IL2, a mouse study investigating the impacts of various radiation
types on the TMB of mice inoculated with the C51 colon carcinoma cell line was performed.
Murine tumor sections from the study were then treated to extract both DNA and RNA. Once
the samples were received by Novogene, their own quality control report revealed that the RNA
samples did not meet the required standards for sequencing. As a consequence, only WXS
sequencing data from the extracted DNA, could be utilized for this analysis.
The analysis of non­synonymous mutational counts would suggests that proton radiation, when
used alongside L19­IL2, may provide some advantages. The decrease in the observed mu­
tational counts can possibly be attributed to the successful generation of immunogenic tumor
neoantigens resulting from radiotherapy­induced mutations. These mutation­induced antigens
are assumed to be eliminated by an immune response against them and, consequently, the
reduced count of detectable mutations at the time of analysis could signify their effective clear­
ance. However, data on the clinical outcomes of these mice indicate that those treated with
C12 radiotherapy exhibit the highest PFS. Although it is important to underline that neoantigen
expression does not guarantee effective tumor clearance, and that other factors are also in play.
Moreover, the data presented in this thesis are preliminary results, and further analysis is cur­
rently underway, uncovering more regarding these effects. With that said, it could have been
interesting to combine these results with the RNA sequencing (RNAseq) data. The RNAseq
data could compliment the WXS data to better understand the mutational landscape on a tran­
scriptional level, giving us a more accurate representation of what is actually being expressed
on the cell surface.
These results may indicate that by substituting electron radiation with alternative radiation types
such as proton or C12, the synergistic effects of the combination therapy can be further en­
hanced, leading to an improved anti­tumor effect. However, the further translation of this effect
to the clinic remains to be seen, as the relationship between high TMB and improved clinical
outcome is still a contested topic. However, correlation between the two have been found in
several settings including ICI therapy, although the consideration of other co­factors is neces­
sary [118]. Further investigation would also provide useful in the understanding of C12 and
proton radiotherapy as a whole, given their more recent beginnings. Thus, the ongoing investi­
gation of radiotherapy both in the context of its radiobiological and its physiological effect could
aid in the further development of the ImmunoSABR combination therapy.

6
Conclusion and Future Perspectives
The advancements in cancer immunotherapy have made significant contributions to the treat­
ment of NSCLC. However, the challenges in treating patients with metastatic disease continue
to limit their long­term survival. To address these challenges, a novel combination therapy com­
prising SABR and L19­IL2 has been previously proposed as a promising candidate. The primary
objective of this thesis was to investigate the immunomechanisms underlying this combination
therapy, aiming to enhance the understanding of how it achieves positive clinical outcomes.
This is achieved through four distinct analyses. Firstly, an immunophenotyping analysis is con­
ducted on murine preclinical data. This is followed by a similar analysis on human phase I
trial data. Subsequently, antibody panels to facilitate immunophenotyping in a phase II trial are
proposed. Lastly, a murine study is conducted to compare the effects of different RT types on
TMB.
The initial investigations conducted in two mouse models as part of preclinical trials, reveals the
involvement of two distinct innate immune responses. In the CT26 model, a potentially PMN­
driven myeloid cell response is hypothesized to be involved in anti­tumor immunity. However,
only tumor growth delay is observed and the analysis is limited by the presence of several
experimental variables, such as an inconsistent sacrifice time. This prevents drawing definitive
conclusions from the CT26 model results. On the other hand, the C51 model indicates possible
involvement of NK cells, alongside myeloid cells in mediating the observed curative effects.
The differences in the specific immune responses between the two models also indicate that a
certain level of heterogeneity should be expected in humans.
Analyzing samples from the phase I clinical trial presented several challenges, relating to sample
quality and availability. Nevertheless, a comparison between responders and non­responders
suggested the involvement of a rare CD57+, GZMB+, CD4+ TEMRA subset in the anti­tumor
immunity of treatment­responsive patients. However, the knowledge about this subset is still
limited, especially in the context of cancer. Therefore, no definitive conclusions can be drawn
regarding these cells’ involvement in positive clinical outcomes.
Based on these studies, three flow cytometry panels have been proposed for future use in the
analysis of an ongoing phase II clinical trial. These proposed panels will serve as valuable tools
for delving deeper into the immunomechanisms underlying the treatment. Specifically, they will
enable a comprehensive investigation of T­cell, myeloid cell, and NK­cell populations, shedding
light on their interactions and their respective roles in promoting or suppressing tumor immunity.
In addition to studying the immunomechanisms of the treatment, its impact on TMB was ex­
plored. Although the results are only preliminary, they suggest a potential correlation between
different types of radiation, their effects on TMB, and the possible generation of immunogenic
neoantigens.
However, it is crucial to acknowledge that the outcomes achieved in the different partial aims of
this thesis should not be considered definitive and further studies are, thus, needed. To confirm
the role of the observed myeloid cell and NK­cell populations in the anti­tumor immunity seen in
mice, a more comprehensive and focused investigation is necessary. This investigation should
involve the analysis of the cells’ functional capacity with respect to anti­tumor immunity.
Moreover, for what concerns the immunomechanisms in humans, an investigation into the func­
tion of the identified CD4+ TEMRA subset is need. The analysis would allow a better under­
standing of the subset´s role in anti­tumor immunity and whether it can serve as a prognostic
biomarker for positive clinical outcome.

Furthermore, considering the limitations of the analyzed phase I trial data, it is evident that the
phase II trial will play a crucial role in understanding the treatment’s effects. This analysis will
also shed light on the significance of the rare T­cell subset found in responders for clinical out­
come. In this context, the proposed antibody panels are expected to be fundamental. However,
before they can be used for the analysis of phase II data, further optimization, such as antibody
titration, is necessary.
Lastly, a more in­depth analysis of TMB data obtained from WXS, coupled with RNAseq data, is
essential. This would help to shed light onto the transcriptional dynamics of induced mutations
and the potential generation of tumor neoantigens, which could aid in effective tumor clearance.
In conclusion, this thesis presents non­definitive, yet interesting findings on the immunomech­
anisms of ImmunoSABR. These serve as ground work for exciting opportunities for further re­
search and development, to enhance the understanding of cancer immunotherapy. In turn, this
opens the door for potential advancements in the treatment of NSCLC, benefiting millions of
patients with metastatic diseases.
