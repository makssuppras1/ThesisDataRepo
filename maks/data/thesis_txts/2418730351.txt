Porcine Checkpoint Inhibition: Binding of Therapeutic Antibodies to
Porcine PD1, PDL1, and CTLA4
by Alexander John Christoﬀersen
s123154
Checkpoint inhibition is a form of immunotherapy against cancer which has seen success
in treating a broad range of cancers. Animal models provide a platform to further study
checkpoint inhibition, and as pigs have a comparatively high level of homology to
humans they are a good choice of model. Before in vivo experimentation, tools to
investigate porcine immune regulation need to be developed. Receptors involved in
immune checkpoints such as PD1, PDL1, and CTLA4 play an integral role in both the
regulation of the immune system and cancer development and are targets of clinically
approved checkpoint inhibitors. In this project, mice are DNA immunized to produce
antibodies against poPD1, poPD-L1, poCTLA4, and huCTLA4; therapeutic antibodies
Nivolumab (anti-huPD1) and Ipilimumab (anti-huCTLA4) are acquired. Six proteins,
human and porcine versions of PD1, PD-L1, and CTLA4, are expressed on the surface of
porcine kidney (PK15) cells. These expressed proteins are used to assess cross reactivity
and binding of all antibodies. It is determined that the therapeutic antibody paratopes
cannot be used in pigs due to lack of reactivity. DNA immunization resulted in
generation of antibodies speciﬁc for porcine PD-L1; anti-CTLA4 antibodies reacted to
both porcine and human versions of the protein. Little to no reactivity was seen in the
antibodies generated by mice immunized with porcine PD1.

1 Introduction
In the recent decade immune checkpoint inhibition has shown great promise in the treat-
ment of a wide range of cancers. Some checkpoint inhibitors have already been approved
for therapeutic use, with many more in clinical trials [73]. Cancer is not in itself a single
disease, but an umbrella term used for a collection of diseases that are characterized by
uncontrolled proliferation of cells which can metastasize to other parts of the body. This
uncontrolled proliferation is the result of the accumulation of mutations, making cancer a
genetic disease [29]. It is the second leading cause of death worldwide, responsible for 8.8
million deaths in 2015. Approximately 14 million new cases were diagnosed in 2012, and
this number is expected to rise 70% in the next two decades [19]. As such, there is a large
incentive to identify and implement eﬀective cancer therapies and interventions.
Cancer is recognized as a complex disease, and eﬀorts to organize knowledge pertaining
to it are ongoing [75]. Despite the complexities of cancer classiﬁcation, progress has been
made in deﬁning the core characteristics of cancer. While not deﬁnitive, Hanahan and
Weinberg’s ‘The Hallmarks of Cancer’ (2000) put forth six hallmarks relating to tumor
pathogenesis that have been widely accepted. These are: sustaining proliferative signaling,
evading growth suppressors, resisting cell death, enabling replicative immortality, induc-
ing angiogenesis, and activating invasion and metastasis [26]. In the 2011 update, two
emerging hallmarks - deregulation of cell metabolism and immunoevasion - were outlined.
Genetic instability, inﬂammation, and the tumor microenvironment were also recognized
as key players in a nascent malignancies acquisition of cancer attributes[27]. The recogni-
tion of immunoevasion as a key factor in cancer development, along with an understanding
of the immune system, has led to the development of immunotherapies such as checkpoint
inhibition. If better therapies are to be developed, greater understanding of the immune
system is needed.
Animal models have been invaluable in both research and drug development [22].
As
the scientiﬁc community gathers data from them and thereby learns how to manipulate
various systems in human biology, they also have learned the limitations inherent in making
inferences about human systems using data from systems which diﬀer to varying degrees
from human [46].
As the systems investigated rise in complexity, the model in which
investigation takes place is of more import if only because more homology results in less
variables to extrapolate. Therefore, homology is an important consideration when choosing
an animal model.
With the annotation of the porcine immunome, pigs have been identiﬁed as possessing a
high level of homology to humans [16]. Therefore, pigs are a good candidate for a model

of research in checkpoint inhibition therapy, so basic comparisons need to be drawn and

This project aims to generate data pertaining to porcine checkpoint inhibition in order to

2 Background
2.1
Cancer immunity and current immunotherapies
An understanding of how the immune system responds to cancer is needed to contextualize
the relevance of checkpoint inhibition in cancer treatment. A 2013 review by Chen and
Mellman on the ’Cancer-Immunity Cycle’ provides a good overview of the key steps in a
functioning immune response against a tumor [14]. These steps are:
1. Due to the inherent genetic instability of cancer, neoantigens are created in the
process of oncogenesis and released upon cancer cell death and processed by dendritic
cells (DC).
2. Neoepitopes are presented T cells via MHC class II and I on DCs
3. Priming and activation of eﬀector T cells against epitopes which have mutated
enough to be seen as foreign or against which central tolerance is incomplete
4. Activated eﬀector T cells traﬃc to the tumor site
5. T cells inﬁltrate the tumor site
6. Neoepitopes presented by MHC class I are recognized by the T cell receptors speciﬁc
for them; T cells bind to cancer cells through this interaction.
7. Activation of eﬀector T cells results in the killing of cancer cells and the release of
additional tumor-related antigens
This cyclic process should in principle lead to the ampliﬁcation and broadening of T cell
responses against the targeted tumor as further cancer-related antigens are released, but
in cancer patients this process performs sub-optimally.
As can be seen in Figure 2.1,
which shows known regulatory elements in the cancer-immunity cycle, there are many
opportunities for either up or downregulation of the immune response in each step of the
cycle. This level of regulation is not surprising, as the natural immune response against
cancer toes the line between self-tolerance and autoimmunity[14].

While useful, this overview of regulatory elements cannot be considered complete. Though
many of the major players in regards to cells and regulatory elements have been identi-
ﬁed, the ﬁner regulatory mechanisms arising from the interactions between these players
in long-term inﬂammatory response are under investigation [36, 41, 51]. The goal of im-
munotherapy is to use an understanding of these mechanisms to correct changes in the
cancer-immunity cycle and restore proper functioning.
This project examines a type of therapeutic immunotherapy, so regulatory mechanisms
which are targets in immunotherapy are of interest. Table 2.1, adapted from Farkona et al.
(2016) provides an overview of these targets [18]. Cytokine immunotherapy showed early
promise, but has only seen limited success when used alone in immunotherapy [79]. Cell
based therapy relies on the identiﬁcation of cancer epitopes; these are susceptible to change
due to cancers genetic instability, reducing treatment longevity. Another consequence of
this instability is the lack of consistent epitopes for a wide range of cancers [35, 82].
Checkpoint receptors have been identiﬁed as key regulators of cytotoxic T cell response,
which makes the reversible blockade of these proteins a desirable target for immunotherapy
[80].

Chapter 2. Background
2.1.1
Immune checkpoint receptors
Several mechanisms, known as immune checkpoints, are involved in regulation of the
response of a T cell to its antigen. As the name suggests, immune checkpoint receptors
are proteins on the cell surface which are involved in regulating T cell activation. They
are found on the surface of both antigen presenting cells and T cells. Three checkpoint
receptors are targets for clinically approved receptor blocking therapy: programmed cell
death 1 (PD-1) and its ligand PDL-1, and cytotoxic T lymphocyte associated protein-4
(CTLA4) (Figure 2.2).
Figure 2.2: Immune checkpoint receptors and their ligands, along with the relevant
checkpoint inhibitors [17].
The checkpoint receptors (PD1, PD-L1, and CTLA4) which have been targeted in check-
point inhibition are those which provide inhibitory signals when bound to their ligand
[52]. The propagation of the signal generated when these inhibitory receptors are bound
is complex and results in altered metabolic programs, diﬀerentiation proﬁles, and func-
tional properties of T cells. However, the outcome of this signalling is the downregulation
of T-cell activity and both cell-mediated and cytokine based immune suppression [10, 12].
2.1.2
Therapeutic Antibodies
Small molecules can be checkpoint inhibitors, but the majority of known checkpoint in-
hibitors are antibody based [74]. Thus, how a therapeutic monoclonal is developed must
be reviewed. During the development of B cells, negative selection occurs to eliminate any
B cells which are reactive against self-antigens [62]. This serves to prevent autoimmunity,
and also presents a challenge when developing checkpoint inhibitors.
As consistent in
silico prediction of B cell epitopes given any antigen is yet to be accomplished, animals
must be used to ﬁnd eﬀective checkpoint inibitor epitopes [57]. However, due to varying

2.2
Animal models
To be able to both generate information regarding the interplay of cancer and the immune
system and to test therapeutic compounds, in vivo systems that can be compared to
human physiology, i.e.
animal models, are needed.
They have been valuable tools in
gaining insight into the genetic basis of cancer and its progression, and in testing cancer
therapeutics for their safety and toxicity before reaching human clinical trials. In essence,
animal models are critically needed in order to faithfully imitate human malignant diseases
which in return can establish basic knowledge in prevention of cancer in humans [83].
By knowing how two system are diﬀerent and the degree of that diﬀerence, information
about the ﬁrst system can be inferred from manipulations of the second. However, the
degree of diﬀerence is important in regards to the accuracy of the inferences made. Rodent
cancer models are frequently used and are acknowledged as traditional animal models for
preclinical studies in general of cancer. Despite the advantages of working with a well
known model, their success rate in translation to clinical cancer trials is quite low. In
addition, studies of therapeutic cancer drugs have shown that mice have a higher tolerance
to drugs compared to humans [13].
In biomedical research, pigs are in many cases the favorable alternative over other non-
rodent models, as they provide a high level of homology at the physiological level. They
also show promise in modeling genetic disorder [34]. In recent years, pigs have garnered
interest as a model in studying tumor oncology in part because they are a cost eﬀective
large animal but also because they are fairly similar in pathophysiology, metabolism,
genetics, epigenetics, and pathology [1]. An annotation of pig genes involved in immune
function found that humans and pigs share 42 genes that are not found in mice, mice and
humans share 14 genes that are not found in pigs, and mice and pigs share 7 genes not
found in humans. This suggests that pigs hold potential as a large animal model in cancer
immunology[16].
2.3
Heterologous expression
The transcription and translation of heterologous DNA is an integral part of modern
biotechnology in both industry and academia. When inserted into a known host organism
through the use of a vector, recombinant DNA can be expressed in a controlled manner
which allows for investigation or optimization of desirable traits. Cells of all types, from
prokaryotes to higher eukaryotes, have been used as expression hosts - the choice of host
is dependent upon the intended application of the expressed protein[59].

2.3.1
Expression hosts
In the context of this thesis, two diﬀerent types of hosts are needed. PD1, PDL1, and
CTLA4 are membrane-bound proteins, and the binding of therapeutic monoclonal anti-
bodies is investigated. Proper transport and folding are therefore important, making a
mammalian expression host desirable [3, 33]. However, as non-nucleic DNA is not repli-

Prokaryotic host
Escherichia coli is possibly the most well known organisms used in biotechnology [7].
As such, protocols for all aspects of this organism relating to biotechnology have been
developed and optimized[50]. Commercial strains which have been optimized and prepared
for transformation are readily available[31]. Therefore, E. coli is the organism of choice
for the prokaryotic expression host used in this project.
Mammalian host
To maximize the similarity between pigs and the cell line used in this thesis, a porcine
kidney cell line is used to express the investigated immune checkpoint receptors. The cell
line used in this project is porcine kidney-15 (PK15), which is a stable cell line generated
in 1961 by Frank Ruddle [61].
2.3.2
Vector for expression
A vector of some kind must be used to deliver DNA to the host organism. Two types of
vectors are prevalent: those that provide plasmid based expression or those that result in
expression through genomic integration. As genomic integration of heterologous DNA is
not used in this thesis, focus will lie on plasmid based expression vectors. A plasmid is a
double-stranded circular piece of DNA which replicates independently of the host’s chro-
mosomal DNA. To enable this, they contain an origin of replication which also determines
the number of copies present in the host Additionally, they usually contain a gene which
provide an evolutionary advantage to the host [2].
To be used as an heterologous expression vector, several additions to a natural plasmid
are needed:
• A selectable marker, often antibiotic resistance.
This enables selection for hosts
containing the plasmid.
• A cloning site for the insertion of heterologous DNA. When ﬂanked by multiple
restriction sites, it is called a Multiple Cloning Site (MCS).
• An insert consisting of the gene which is to be expressed.
==PAGE:23==

• A promoter region upstream of the cloning site and terminator downstream of the
cloning site for expression of the insert. The type of promoter used is dependent on
the species of host in which expression will occur.
Due to diﬀerences in promoter speciﬁcity, a vector can be designed which segregates gene
translation to the relevant hosts.
This allows for the use of antibiotic resistance as a
selectable marker under bacterial growth, and expression of the gene of interest in the
ﬁnal host. For expression in mammalian cells, a cytomegalovirus (CMV) promoter along
with a kozak sequence to ensure promotion and translation start is commonly used[81, 37]

2.3.3
Transformation and transfection
Regardless of host, delivery of the vector into the cell is a requirement for heterologous
expression to occur. Taking host organism into account, there is a diﬀerence in terminol-
ogy, though some of it may be due to semantics [72]. In this report, transformation will
be used to refer to the introduction of a vector into a prokaryotic host and transfection
to refer to the same in a eukaryotic host. Despite diﬀerences in terminology, methods of
facilitating gene delivery into hosts in which horizontal gene transfer does not occur nat-
urally can be generalized into two types: biophysical or chemical [84, 35, 32]. This thesis
utilizes electroporation, CaCl2 transformation, and cationic lipid mediated transformation
(lipofection).
Electroporation
Electroporation is the use of controlled direct current electrical pulses to induce a tem-
porary breakdown of the cell membrane. The resulting pore formation allows DNA to be
introduced into the cell; pore closure can take anywhere from milliseconds to minutes [11].
Electroporation can be utilized in both prokaryotic and eukaryotic cells. Additionally,
it has also seen use in animals as a protein delivery tool to facilitate the production of
antibodies [28, 9].
CaCl2 transformation
In 1970, Mandel and Higa discovered that incubation of E. coli with CaCl2 and bacte-
riophage lambda DNA on ice resulted in cellular transformation [44]. This method of
transformation was later improved upon with the addition of heat shock and recovery
steps. The mechanism behind heat-pulse facilitation of DNA uptake was investigated by
Panja et. al in 2006, who suggested that the cell membrane becomes depolarized with
an increase in temperature (heat shock), resulting in increased membrane permeability.
Membrane re-polarization occurs with the return to 0◦C. Today, this is the most widely
used method of E. coli transformation [84].

2.4. Flow cytometry

Lipofection
Lipofection is one of the most commonly used method of gene delivery in mammalian
cells [35, 32]. This transfection method utilizes cationic lipids, which contain a positively
charged head group attached to hydrocarbon chains. The positively charged group inter-
acts with the nucleic acid backbone to form a complex. This complex is then able to build
a liposomal structure with a positive surface charge that is capable of interacting with cell
membranes and eventually inducing the uptake of DNA [32].
2.4
Flow cytometry
Flow cytometry is used in this project to analyze transfected PK15 cells. This analytical
tool gathers information about individual particles through their interaction with light,
usually diﬀerent wavelengths of lasers. Interaction is measured at the interrogation point,
where particles pass between the light beam and the relevant detector, of which there are
three types. A simpliﬁed view of the optics set up can be seen in Figure 2.4. Before par-
ticles reach the interrogation point they pass through the ﬂuidics system, which separates
the sample into individual particles via pressure and a narrow aperture.
Figure 2.4: Simpliﬁed overview of typical ﬂow cytometry optics set up [78]
The ﬁrst detector is located across from the light source and sits behind a blocking ﬁlter
which removes direct light. This allows it to measure light which is scattered as a particle
passes through the beam; the degree of scatter yields information about the size of the
particle passing the detector, known as forward scatter (FSC)[47]. The second detector
is situated orthogonal to the path of the light source, and gathers light which has been
refracted by internal components of the cell. This provides a parameter pertaining to the
granularity of particles passing through the light, known as side scatter (SSC). Often, the

light used to measure these parameters is of short wavelength (blue/violet), as particles
smaller than the wavelength used to analyze them causes inconsistent refraction [78].
The last type of detector used in ﬂow cytometry is used to detect ﬂuorescence.
The
amount of detectors determines how many diﬀerent ﬂuorochromes are able to be used in
a given ﬂow cytometer, and as such the number varies from machine to machine. When
light of a certain wavelength hits a ﬂuorochrome, it is excited and emits light of a speciﬁc
wavelength. This emission does not change in wavelength, which means that emissions
from multiple ﬂuorochromes can be separated and sent to diﬀerent detectors by passing
them through a system of optical ﬁlters and mirrors (Figure 2.5).

2.5. Research strategy

2.4.1
Gating strategy
After data from samples has been collected, it must be analyzed. This is done by removing
unwanted cell populations using comparisons between FSC, SSC, and ﬂuorescence in a step
by step process known as gating. Examples of these steps can be seen in Section 3.8.
The ﬁrst step in gating is the removal of dead cells from the pool of particles. This is done
by gating out cells which have been stained with a live/dead stain and thus ﬂuoresce at
the wavelength dictated by the ﬂuorochrome in that stain. Next, as clumps or doublets of
cells can make it through the ﬂuidics system, single cells are isolated to ensure consistency
in ﬂuorochrome signal. This is done by comparing the forward scatter height (FSC-H) to
the forward scatter area (FSC-A) to provide information in regards to the size of particles
passing the detector. Clumps or doublets take up a larger area than singlets and as such
will lie further along the FSC-A axis [42].
A comparison between side scatter area (SSC-A) and FSC-A is then used. This comparison
is typically used to diﬀerentiate between cell types in heterogeneous populations as both
parameters vary between cell types. It can also be used to remove any non-cell debris
from the remaining cell population as debris will have both a small area and a low level
of granularity. The resulting population can be considered free of debris and consisting of
only viable single cells. The signal from bound ﬂuorochrome can then be identiﬁed by the
appearance of a population producing higher levels of signal in the ﬂuorochrome channel
than the non-stained population [42].
2.5
Research strategy
To build the toolbox for studying immune therapy in pigs, antibodies which are speciﬁc for
porcine immune checkpoint receptors are needed. The only practical method of producing
antibodies with unknown paratopes is to get an animal to do it for you by immuniza-
tion. Kerstin et al. (2012) utilized DNA immunization of mice to facilitate generation of
antibodies speciﬁc for a protein in the porcine immune system [43]. Therapeutic human
checkpoint inhibitors will also be incorporated into this study. The rationale behind the
utilization of human checkpoint inhibitors in this project is the prospect of being able
to capitalize on their availability, speciﬁcity and reliability [56]. Cross-species ractivity
to porcine immune checkpoint receptors would drastically shorten the time needed to
generate prociniﬁed antibodies by providing a known paratope to work with.
To asses the binding of the various antibodies to porcine and human immune checkpoint
receptors, expression of the checkpoint receptors on a cell surface is needed along with a
way to determine if antibody binding occurs. K¨aser et al. (2015) performed an experiment
with similar requirements. They utilized transient transfection of a constructed plasmid
followed by staining of cells with an antibody bound ﬂuorochrome. The stained cells were
then then analyzed via ﬂow cytometry[38]. As these are fairly standard techniques, this
methodology will be utilized in this project. Regarding the choice of insert sequence for
the expression vector, surface expression is desirable in order to simulate in vivo binding

3.4
PK15 cultivation, maintenance, and cryopreservation
Porcine Kidney-15 (PK15) cell cultivation was started from a seed ﬂask. All work was
done in a laminar air ﬂow (LAF) bench and with sterile gloves to maintain a sterile
working environment. Cells were cultivated in a in sterile 75cm2 cell culture ﬂasks [64].
Culture media consisted of 30mL Gibco RPMI 1640 with GLUTAMAX supplement from
Thermo Fisher containing 5% Fetal Bovine Serum (FBS) [65][63]. Before use, FBS was
heat inactivated by heating to 56◦C for 30 minutes. Incubation was carried out at 37◦C,
5% CO2, and 80% relative humidity. Once at least 90% conﬂuency was reached, cells were
passaged into new ﬂasks to maintain growth. To accomplish this, 0.25% trypsin-EDTA
was used to dissociate cells from the ﬂask wall and allow for suspension [71].
After suspension, the amount of cells present in a ﬂask was determined using a disposable
hemocytometer. The formula used to determine total cell count is as follows:
Total cells = avg cell count · dilution factor · 104 · suspension volume
It should be noted that the third term, 104, is the conversion factor for the hemocytometer
used. Once the cell count was determined, the suspended cells could either be passaged
to a new ﬂask for further culturing or prepared for cryopreservation. Cryopreservation
consisted of freezing two million cells in 1mL of media consisting of 10% DMSO, 40%
serum, and 50% RPMI. Full protocols for both maintenance and cryopreservation of PK15
cells can be found in Appendix A.2.3 and A.2.4.

5 Discussion
5.1
Plasmids
Direct conﬁrmation of plasmid ampliﬁcation success via restriction digest was achieved for
plasmids 1-4, as can be seen in Section 4.1. It should be noted that there is a slight dis-
crepancy in the restriction digest of plasmid 3 provided by Genscript. Documents received
along with the plasmids listed SmaI as the second enzyme used in digestion. However, the
same documents downloaded online listed PstI as the second enzyme. Thankfully, there
was not an observable diﬀerence in band sizes between either Genscripts digests or the
one performed in-house, which is likely due to nature of the MCS - multiple restriction
sites in such close proximity that the diﬀerence in fragment size was not visible.
Though restriction digests were not carried out for plasmid ﬁve and six, successful am-
pliﬁcation of huPD1 and huPDL1 could be indirectly conﬁrmed through the binding of
Nivolumab. As Nivolumab did not bind to untransfected PK15 cells, any binding which
occurred must be due to the expression of huPD1. In the antibody cross reactivity test
(Section 4.3, approximately 19% of the gated population transfected with huPD1 reacted
with Nivolumab, and appoximately 10% of the gated population transfected with huPDL1
were bound by Nivolumab. Information in literature regarding non-speciﬁc binding of
Nivolumab was lacking.
Nonetheless, a decrease of ﬁfty percent in the population of
Nivolumab bound cells is signiﬁcant, and suggests that human PD-L1 is expressed on the
surface of cells transfected with plasmid six.
Due to the quality control documentation provided by Genscript in the ﬁrst order batch
(plasmids 1-4), the consistency of fragment size generated in restriction digestion of picked
colonies, and the binding of Nivolumab, expression of huPD1 and huPD-L1 in strains ﬁve
and six is highly likely. Loss of insert is possible with an improperly designed or ligated
plasmid, but this was avoided by procuring plasmids from a company specializing in
heterologous expression vectors[23]. Additionally, plasmid loss is minimal in E. coli and
is further minimized via the use of ampicillin in growth media for negative selection and
growth from glycerol stocks made from O/N growth of picked colonies [4, 15]. Though
the likelihood of error is low, the presence of the correct inserts in plasmids ﬁve and six
should still be conﬁrmed in future work, either by restriction digest or sequencing.
A plasmid lacking an insert was ordered and ampliﬁed, but not transfected and analyzed;
the realization of a lack of a control for the eﬀect of plasmid presence on transfection and
ﬂow cytometry occurred late in the experimental process.

5.2
Cell staining and Flow cytometry
The protocol for staining of PK15 cells in preparation for ﬂow cytometry protocol went
through several trials and improvements in the course of this project, and can still be
optimized further to generate the cleanest possible data. One improvement, which is not
presented in the results, is the need to make fresh dilutions of the therapeutic antibodies
used to stain each immune checkpoint protein. Binding capability was lost after dilution in
FACS buﬀer and even overnight storage at -20◦C. The exact formulation of the antibody
solution is not known, but the presence of a stabilizer which became ineﬀective when
diluted is possible. An injection label for Nivolumab from the FDA speciﬁes that after
preparation the product should not be at room temperature for more than four hours and
in the refrigerator for more than twenty-four, nor should it be frozen [77].
Additionally, it was found that the trypsination performed before cryopreservation was
very eﬀective at removing dead cells from the culture; to complement this, viable PK15
are apparently quite robust even when cryopreserved and thawed. It was often challenging
to identify the population of dead cells during data analysis due to its small size. Addition
of ethanol to a small subset of cells, followed by washing as outlined in the PK15 staining
protocol, would increase this population and make the non-viable cell population easier
to identify during the gating process.
5.2.1
Fluorochromes
Achieving proper signal from ﬂuorochromes was also a challenge. The ﬁrst ﬂow cytometry
runs yielded far too much background signal from PE to make any quantitative decisions
based on the data produced. Thankfully, there was a large enough diﬀerence between
transfection methods that this was not needed 4.2.1. While the background was never
completely eliminated, it was observed that the levels of this background had two vari-
ables: the voltage of the laser used to excite PE, and the concentration of the therapeutic
antibody used to stain cells.
After the ﬁrst transfection test, the voltage of the laser was lowered which worked to lower
the the background signal in untransfected PK15 cells (Figure 3.6a). A secondary antibody
control which lacked the biotinylated secondary antibody was also added to further assist
in identifying the level of nonspeciﬁc binding occuring. However, smear still persisted
to a large degree in cells stained with primary antibody. As can be seen in Figures 4.9
and 4.10, there was a general trend of higher antibody dilutions yielding a more clearly
deﬁned positive population. However, even at the lowest dilutions, background did not
disappear.
One possible reason for this is that variable numbers of plasmid could be
transfected [45]. The presence of more than one plasmid would result in increased levels
of checkpoint receptor expression, which would then be reﬂected in ﬂow cytometry as a
higher ﬂuorochrome signal in that particle [60].
AF488 is a relatively weak ﬂuorochrome - signal separation between the bound and non-
bound population was quite poor [6]. This increases the degree of error present in gating

for bound cells, but more importantly it is not possible to determine if the same smear
present in the PE stainings is present in the AF488 stainings. To investigate this, one
could use a biotinylated secondary antibody and PE streptavadin to stain cells bound to
serum based antibodies. Standardizing cell staining in this way would also allow for more
accurate comparison of binding across mouse and therapeutic antibodies.
5.3
Transfection
Two methods of PK15 transfection were utilized in this project: electroporation and lipo-
fection. Electroporation had an expected transfection rate of ﬁfty percent yet yielded no
transfectants [11]. The expected transfection rate of lipofection was approximately ninety
percent [68], yet yielded an observed transfection rate of ten to ﬁfteen percent. This sug-
gests that the membrane of the PK15 cell is more robust than other mammalian cell lines
often worked with in biotechnology.
This hypothesis is supported by the high surviv-
ability of PK15 cells through cryopreservation, as mentioned in Section 5.2. Information
regarding transfection of PK15 cells was lacking, but Jizong et al. (2014) compared the
transfection PK15 cells to dulac cells, a subtype of the PK15 cell line. They noted that
the original strain was less receptive to transfection[39].
Electroporation most likely simply did not suﬃciently disrupt the membrane to allow for
the entry of plasmid into the cytoplasm.
Membrane disruption could be increased by
increasing the number of pulses administered. An increase in voltage could also be consid-
ered [11]. One thing that must be considered regarding this method is that electroporation
of cells from a culture ﬂask in cuvettes was very unwieldy. The suggested number of cells
per cuvette is two million, and a fully conﬂuent ﬂask can contain up to 30 million cells.
Up to ﬁfteen cuvettes need to be processed to transfect the same amount of cells via lipo-
fection. If electroporation is to be investigated further, it is strongly recommended that
either smaller culture volumes are used or that cells are grown in a way that allows for
the use of a plate electroporator.
As mentioned above, the transfection rate of lipofectamine was not as high as desired.
Jizong et al. (2014) found that a 3:1 ratio yielded the highest transformation eﬃciency.
However, when generating large quantities of transiently transfected cells this high ratio of
Lipofectamine:DNA is not very economically feasible in the long run. However, the level
of DNA in the transfection ratio should be investigated to determine if transfection of
multiple plasmid copies is the culprit behind the smear seen in PE stained ﬂow cytometry
results.
DNA immunization without adjuvant was successful in groups two through four, which
produced antibodies against poPD-L1, poCTLA4, and huCTLA4. The ﬁrst mouse group,
immunized against poPD1, showed a very slight increase in AF488 signal after the sec-
ond immunization. Unfortunately, the results from the third immunization are not yet
available.

Chapter 5. Discussion
5.3.1
Analysis of transfection and antibody binding
The ﬁrst transfection test (Figure 4.6 shows what seems to be several distinct cell popu-
lations. As these do not appear in later results, it must be assumed that inexperience in
protocol execution of trypsination, cryopreservation, and cell staining led to the irregular-
ity seen.
It has been stated that transfection eﬃciency should be directly correlated to the level of
signal generated by bound ﬂuorochrome. This holds true, as the use of an intracellular
marker protein such as green ﬂuorescent protein or a labeled nucleotide is standard practice
for the evaluation of transfection via ﬂow cytometry [30]. This project did not incorpo-
rate an intracellular ﬂuorescent marker, and as such used antibody binding to provide
this correlation. The measurement of transfection eﬃciency via the binding of secondary
antibody/ﬂuorochrome has been used to successfully evaluate transfection eﬃciency [54].
Though transfection eﬃciency does correlate to ﬂuorochrome signal, there is variation in
observed transfection eﬃciency. In the titration of therapeutic antibodies (Table 4.1), the
observed transfection eﬃciency for Ipilimumab and Nivolumab falls ﬁve and three point
four percent over the twofold serial dilution from 0.5 to 0.008µg per sample. This likely
means that there is not enough antibody in solution to bind all cells; a compromise had
to be struck to achieve signal clarity. While there is a correlation of tranfection eﬃciency
to ﬂuorochrome signal, the concentration of antibody does have an eﬀect on the eﬃciency
measured. With this in mind, ﬂuorochrome can be used as a general guide for transfection
eﬃciency.
5.4
Antibody cross reactivity
Of the two therapeutic and three serum-based antibodies tested, three showed cross reac-
tivity. Perhaps the most surprising of these was Nivolumab, which reacted to both huPD1
an huPD-L1 (Figure 4.14). As no literature pertaining to oﬀ-label binding of this anti-
body could be found, laboratory practice must be called into question. Laboratory error
causing the transfection of the wrong plasmid is challenging to rule out decisively but is
unlikely, as cells were transfected and cryopreserved in the same batch. No deviations in
protocol were noted during these procedures. Cross contamination of plasmid stocks could
have occurred, but a much lower observed transfection eﬃciency than that seen would be
observed.
Of interest is the observed transfection eﬃciency, which is almost 19% for huPD1 trans-
fected cells. This is higher than the observed transfection eﬃciency seen in the highest
titer of the Nivolumab titration (15%, Figure 4.9). The shape of the positive populations
are also similar, with no well deﬁned positive population. It is possible that a concentra-
tion of 1mg/mL was used to stain this assay, resulting in the use of 50µg Nivolumab per
sample.

No intra-species cross reactivity was seen in the therapeutic antibodies. However both
serum pools three and four (poCTLA4 and huCTLA4) showed cross-species reactivity.
This indicates that many of the mice in these groups did not generate antibodies with
very speciﬁc epitopes against these proteins. The low levels of reactivity in mouse pools
for huPD1 and poCTLA4 could be due to a higher degree of homology between these
proteins and their murine counterparts, resulting in the selection of weaker epitopes to
maintain immune tolerance [8]. The only mouse serum group which can be compared
to the binding of a therapeutic antibody is group three and Ipilimumab, which both are
speciﬁc for huCTLA4. Neither reacted to any other protein apart from their target, and
both had somewhat similar levels of binding (Table 4.3).
5.5
Future work
Though transfection was successful, Lipofectamine was a rather expensive reagent to use
when transfecting large quantities of cells. More investigation needs to be done in order
to determine the best transfection method for PK15 cells if work is to continue. The ratio
of Lipofectamine:DNA was optimized, but the amount of DNA used in transfection did
not deviate from that recommended in the manufacturers protocol. This could have an
impact on the consistency of checkpoint receptor expression levels in the transfected cells.
Electroporation should also be attempted again, as the observed transfection eﬃciency of
Lipofectamine was under 50%, electroporation could yield comparable results much more
cheaply.
Steps towards optimal protocols are not the only ones that can be taken. B cells from
the DNA immunized mice can be isolated and fused to an immortal cell line to create
hybridomas, eventually allowing for the production of monoclonal antibodies. Once this
has been accomplished, analysis of the binding strength and speciﬁcity of the individual
antibodies can be undertaken. In this, theraputic antibodies can be used as a benchmark
to ensure that binding kinetics in potential porcine checkpoint inhibitors are similar to
the binding kinetics in therapeutic checkpoint inhibitors.
The most important aspect of checkpoint inhibitors is that they bind their targets in a
way that prevents ligand binding. This must be reﬂected by any antibodies that are to be
used to study checkpoint inhibition in pigs. To that end, once monoclonal B cell cultures
have been established, their antibodies can also be tested in a bioassay for antagonistic
binding.
Data from these experiments would theoretically allow for the identiﬁcation of porcine
checkpoint inhibitors which are similar to clinically used human checkpoint inhibitors.
Once these are found, they must be sequenced and ’porciniﬁed’ by introducing the hy-
pervariable regions of the mouse antibody onto a porcine antibody. This will generate
an antibody which has the same speciﬁcity as the one generated in mice, albeit markedly
less immunogenic; once this is accomplished the antibody can be used in a pig model as a
checkpoint inhibitor.

6 Conclusion
Loosely stated, the goal of this project was to investigate therapeutic checkpoint inhibition
in sus scrofa. As porcine checkpoint inhibition is not an area which has seen focus in
research, investigation of porcine checkpoint receptors was undertaken. To this end, a
toolbox of protocols was gathered to enable the expression of both human and porcine
checkpoint receptors on the surface of a porcine kidney cell line, and to evaluate the
binding of antibodies to the expressed receptors. The concentrations of Nivolumab and
Ipilimumab which provided the clearest signal was determined. An established method
of DNA immunization in mice was then used to generate polyclonal checkpoint inhibitors
against porcine PD1, PD-L1 and CTLA4 and human CTLA4.
Investigation of therapeutic antibody binding and cross reactivity against porcine and hu-
man PD1, PD-L1, and CTLA4 revealed that due to the lack of cross-species reactivity,
therapeutic human antibody epitopes are not usable in the investigation of porcine check-
point inhibition. However, the same binding assessment using the antibodies produced
by immunized mice showed that anti-porcine checkpoint receptor speciﬁc antibodies are
able to be produced via DNA immunization. As the antibody pools tested consisted of
multiple mice worth of polyclonal antibodies which had only gone through two rounds of
immunization, binding comparison to therapeutic antibodies was challenging to carry out.
In the future, the mouse generated antibodies should be fused to an immortal cell line to
allow the production of monoclonal porcine anti-checkpoint receptor antibodies. In all,
this project has provided the practical basis for generating porcine checkpoint inhibitors,
which would allow for future investigation of checkpoint inhibition in a pig model.

procedure 
The instructions provided below are for general use. Specific instructions for 
particular applications such as Zero Blunt® PCR Cloning are provided in the 
manual for that kit.  
1. Centrifuge the vial(s) containing the ligation reaction(s) briefly and place on 
ice. 
2. Thaw, on ice, one 50 μL vial of One Shot® cells for each 
ligation/transformation. 
3. Pipet 1–5 μL of each ligation reaction directly into the vial of competent cells 
and mix by tapping gently. Do not mix by pipetting up and down. The 
remaining ligation mixture(s) can be stored at −20°C. 
4. Incubate the vial(s) on ice for 30 minutes. 
5. Incubate for exactly 30 seconds in the 42°C water bath. Do not mix or shake. 
6. Remove vial(s) from the 42°C bath and place them on ice. 
7. Add 250 μL of pre-warmed S.O.C medium to each vial. S.O.C is a rich 
medium; sterile technique must be practiced to avoid contamination. 
8. Place the vial(s) in a microcentrifuge rack on its side and secure with tape to 
avoid loss of the vial(s). Shake the vial(s) at 37°C for exactly 1 hour at 225 rpm 
in a shaking incubator. 
9. Spread 20–200 μL from each transformation vial on separate, labeled LB agar 
plates. The remaining transformation mix may be stored at 4°C and plated out 
the next day, if desired. 
10. Invert the plate(s) and incubate at 37°C overnight. 
11. Select colonies and analyze by plasmid isolation, PCR, or sequencing.

This protocol is designed for purification of up to 20 µg of high-copy plasmid DNA from
1–5 ml overnight cultures of E. coli in LB medium. For purification of low-copy plasmids
and cosmids, large plasmids (>10 kb), and DNA prepared using other methods, refer
to the recommendations on pages 35–36.
Please read “Important Notes” on pages 13–18 before starting.
Note: All protocol steps should be carried out at room temperature (15–25°C).
Procedure
1.
Resuspend pelleted bacterial cells in 250 µl Buffer P1 and transfer to a micro-
centrifuge tube.
Ensure that RNase A has been added to Buffer P1. No cell clumps should be visible
after resuspension of the pellet.
If LyseBlue reagent has been added to Buffer P1, vigorously shake the buffer
bottle to ensure LyseBlue particles are completely dissolved. The bacteria should
be resuspended completely by vortexing or pipetting up and down until no cell
clumps remain.
2.
Add 250 µl Buffer P2 and mix thoroughly by inverting the tube 4–6 times.
Mix gently by inverting the tube. Do not vortex, as this will result in shearing of
genomic DNA. If necessary, continue inverting the tube until the solution becomes
viscous and slightly clear. Do not allow the lysis reaction to proceed for more than
5 min.
If LyseBlue has been added to Buffer P1 the cell suspension will turn blue after addi-
tion of Buffer P2. Mixing should result in a homogeneously colored suspension. If
the suspension contains localized colorless regions or if brownish cell clumps are
still visible, continue mixing the solution until a homogeneously colored suspension
is achieved.
3.
Add 350 µl Buffer N3 and mix immediately and thoroughly by inverting the tube
4–6 times.
To avoid localized precipitation, mix the solution thoroughly, immediately after
addition of Buffer N3. Large culture volumes (e.g. ≥5 ml) may require inverting up

Suspending cells for counting 
1. Before starting work, heat reagents to 37C via water bath or incubator. 
2. Remove cells from incubator and pour out media.
