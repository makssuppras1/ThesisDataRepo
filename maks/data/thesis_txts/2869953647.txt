Abstract
The development of Dark Field X-ray Microscopy (DFXM) allows the dynamic observation of
defects in the microstructures of metals in a bulk sample. The motivation behind this work is
to quantify the defects into dislocation cells in 2D and 3D to describe the mechanics behind the
deformation of the metals.
In order to qualify the dislocation cells, the fitting of the experimental data is analyzed, before
the existing model for dislocation cells in 2D is evaluated and improved using newly developed
quantitative validation metrics. The 2D results are then used to develop and evaluate 3D dislocation
cell models.
The analysis led to question the quality of the only available 3D dataset observing such behavior
and made it difficult to evaluate 3D models. In 2D, using an established dataset, it was possible
to improve the existing model and highlight its limitations, while adjusting model parameters and
inputs.
In order to achieve 3D modeling of dislocation cells and provide high-quality material science re-
sults, a dataset that more clearly observes the dislocation cells is essential. When this data becomes
available, the better understanding behind the modeling decision for dislocation cells in 2D and
3D achieved by this thesis will provide valuable insights for further research.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The use of hard polycrystalline materials such as metals, ceramics and many semiconductors is
essential to many industries. The use cases range from renewable energy, aerospace, to the chip
industry and many more. Many properties, in particular the mechanical ones, are governed by the
local structure inside the crystals (grains). This socio-economic impact is the driving factor for
extensive studies of the microstructure within polycrystals[1].
However, despite extensive research, the mechanisms behind the type of microstructure that forms
as a function of material and processing remain unclear from first principles in metals. This gap
hinders the realization of fully computational materials science in this field [2].
Two main challenges underlie this limitation. First, when deforming metals trillions of atomic scale
defects are formed, known as dislocations [3, 4]. These can see each other and form cells within the
grains. Hence, the microstructure is organized hierarchically in dislocations, cells and grains. The
computational demands of modeling such a massive amount of dislocations are immense. While
the best models available today: discrete [5, 6] and continuum [7] dislocation dynamics models
(DDD and CDD) provide insight, their complexity limits them to small volumes and low strain
levels (0.5%-1%). Secondly, the traditionally used experimental method, transmission electron
microscopy (TEM) [8, 9, 10, 11, 12, 13] can provide high resolution images but is limited to
samples with a thickness less than 1 µm, that often do not reflect bulk behavior. In particular it is
challenging to perform dynamical studies with electron microscopy.
To address these challenges, synchrotron-based methods have been developed over the last two
decades. Techniques such as 3DXRD (3D X-ray Diffraction) [14] provide bulk analysis at the
grain level, allowing the determination of average grain orientations across thousands of grains.
However, due to problems such as detector resolution and diffraction spot overlap, 3DXRD cannot
resolve orientation differences within a single grain on a small enough scale. This can be achieved
by Dark Field X-Ray Microscopy [15, 16, 17, 18], which enables the study of the orientation
variations (caused by the dislocations) within and surrounding the individual cells in a single grain
in bulk samples through an innovative use of microscopy [1].
From a data analysis point of view the analysis of the DFXM data proceeds by a segmentation
based on a stack of 2D images. The dislocation cells then appear as areas of similar orientation.
Initial progress has been made on 2D data, corresponding to a layer in the sample [1]. What we
wanted to do in this project is to identify dislocation cells in higher dimensions - in 3D and 4D.
The forth dimension is here the external strain, which can also be thought of as time if the sample
is elongated macroscopically at a constant rate. If successful, the ultimate aim is from such 4D
analysis to identify events in space and time where a new dislocation cell originates/nucleates or
disappear/annihilates and then classify these events. This will help to gain further insight into how
the microstructure forms under stress.
As the first experiments on this subject were carried out in early 2024, there are still many un-
knowns, especially on the mathematical modeling and data processing side. Before the start of this
work, it was not possible to process the large amount of 2D DFXM data in less than 1 hour, not to
mention the 3D and 4D data.
This is the motivation behind this thesis, to go through the data thoroughly, to evaluate and speed
up the current processes, to develop new methods and ideas to model the behavior of dislocation
cells, and to question the current processes used from a non-material science view.
1
==PAGE:11==
The developed methods can eventually be used to gain material science insights and to build bet-
ter computer models of the behavior of such metals, and ultimately make better materials for the
industry of tomorrow.
1.2
Objectives and Key Research Questions
The aim of this work was to further develop segmentation models for dislocation cells in single
crystal metals in order to advance the research started in [1]. The ultimate goal when starting
this project was to model 4D cells. As there were only results and data analyzed for 2D in the
beginning, a pipeline needed to be built, including validation methods and modeling techniques on
how to extend the results to models of higher dimensions.
In order to model 4D dislocation cells, the process started with analyzing the data processing. This
was to be followed by modeling dislocation cells with the available dataset in 2D, then in 3D,
and finally in 4D. During this process, it became clear that the available data did not allow the
identification of 4D dislocation cells.
This led to a change in the objective of the thesis, shifting the focus to the development and analysis
of 2D and 3D models to provide a better general understanding of modeling dislocation cells. This
ensures that when better data becomes available, 4D modeling will have a more sophisticated
framework and modeling concepts that are easy to justify in the material science community.
Our main research questions will be the following:
1. Is the current processing of DFXM data in 2D valid, and can more of the data be leveraged
to improve the resulting maps of dislocation cells?
2. How can the 2D model used in [1] be validated? Are there improvements to be made to the
model? Can the model results be used as a baseline for higher dimensions?
3. Can 3D dislocation cells be modeled and the results validated?
1.3
Structure
Since there are two different types of models (2D and 3D), where the results build on each other, it
was decided to split them into separate parts. Each chapter includes its own methodology, results,
and discussion, so that the ideas derived in the 3D chapter can already be motivated by the 2D
results.
The structure of the thesis is as follows:
In Chapter 2, the DFXM method is examined in detail before modeling dislocation cells. The
chapter explores the type of data collected by the method, its pre-processing, and how the data is
fitted.
Chapter 3 first introduces different segmentation approaches. This includes general segmentation
methods and a look at what the material science community has been using. In a second step, more
general technical concepts are presented.
Chapter 4 focuses on the 2D model introduced in [1]. We introduce validation metrics and consider
the sensitivity of model quality to model changes.
Chapter 5 focuses on the process of building a model to identify dislocation cells in 3D. The
chapter starts with extending the 2D model, followed by introducing new ideas and concepts for
segmenting dislocation cells in 3D
In Chapter 6, the combined results of the work are discussed, limitations are addressed, and future
work is presented, with a focus on how the results can be used to model 4D dislocation cells.

2
Experimental Considerations
This chapter provides an overview of the technical setup and scanning procedure of the experiment,
the data processing and data fitting. The data fitting is of particular interest as part of this work is
to determine if it is possible to fit the data in a more efficient way for modelling dislocations cells.
2.1
DFXM
DFXM is an X-ray-based imaging technique that facilitates the observation of the microstructure
and stress on the 100 nm to 1 µm scale within a crystal (either a single crystal or a grain in a poly-
crystal) especially in bulk samples. It does so by monitoring variations in the diffracted signal. The
creation of the method and its underlying principles is complex and a full explanation is not within
the scope of this thesis. For details see [15]. However, it is essential to understand the fundamental
principles of the method, particularly the way in which the underlying data is collected.
DFXM is utilized to capture magnified real space images of the microstructure within an illumi-
nated slice in the sample. Its employment of an objective in the near field facilitates this process,
thereby differentiating it from methodologies such as 3DXRD [14], which relies on diffraction
spots in the reciprocal space to reconstruct the structure of a polycrystalline material. The use of a
microscope principle enables DFXM to provide direct insight into the structure of a single crystal
without the need for tomographic reconstruction.
Figure 2.1: DFXM Setup Basics [1]
The experimental setup is shown in Figure 2.1. At a synchrotron beamline, a monochromatic line
beam is directed at a single-crystal aluminum sample, illuminating a slice within the bulk. The
sample is initially positioned so that the Laue condition [15] is met, allowing the diffraction beam
to be registered on a 2D detector after passing trough the microscope. The microscope enables the
real space visualization of the orientations of the crystal lattice structure in dark-field conditions,
addressing the diffraction spot overlap problem in 3DXRD [19] and allowing bulk analysis of the
crystal lattice at the single-crystal scale.
4
==PAGE:14==
Note that the effective pixel size of the pixel is asymmetric. This is because the detector is not
oriented at a 90 degree angle to the diffracted beam, which stretches the effective pixel size in one
direction of the sample. With this approach, an experiment can be conducted already, by orienting
the sample in its unstrained state to meet the Laue condition and registering the diffraction beam
on the detector. Subsequently, the sample is strained, and the process is repeated at the original
orientation. While this method may be suitable for certain experiments, it is insufficient for study-
ing orientation changes within the crystal structure. This limitation arises because maintaining the
original orientation of the sample after deformation causes parts of the crystal to deviate from the
Laue condition due to structural changes under strain
Since it is not possible to change the wavelength of the beam in a fast and robust way, to monitor
orientations away from the undeformed sample, the sample is tilted in the x and y directions, in
the coordinate system aligned with the original orientation of the sample. These changes in tilt
are realized by the goniometer’s motors, at the angles χ and ϕ. The motors of the goniometer are
extremely accurate (up to 0.001◦[20]) and are one of the enablers that make this imaging technique
possible. For the experiments investigated in this thesis, the goniometer with the sample on top is
scanned in both the χ and ϕ angles in a total ranges of ±1◦around its original position. For each
setting of (χ, ϕ) we have a different contrast, in other words we can observe different orientations
of the crystal.
Figure 2.2: Goniometer Movements
So instead of taking the diffraction pattern at one angular orientation, a series of such images of
the diffraction pattern is taken for different angular combinations. This can be over 1000 different
angular combinations. This drives up the memory requirements for both data storage and process-
ing.
To obtain a volumetric image of the sample, the sample is moved in the z direction so that the line
5
==PAGE:15==
beam illuminates another plane. Later, the images can be combined to form a volume.
Note that tilting the sample introduces a systematic error in the z direction. This error increases
towards the edges of the images where the range of z is largest. For analysis purposes, this error
is negligible, but for analysis of the data, it is advantageous to concentrate on the central regions
of the image.
The procedure for tilting the sample and obtaining diffraction patterns is illustrated in Figure 2.2.
Each point represents an angular pair for which an image is registered on the detector. Note that
the image at each position is integrated over the movements of the ϕ angle, as represented by the
blue box in the figure. The goniometer moves in lines: first, different ϕ values are registered at a
static χ angle.
The red lines in the figure represent the motor movements where the detector is not active. When
a line is completed, the process starts again with the next line. This line-by-line movement is
deliberately structured due to hardware constraints. The χ motor is slower and less expensive than
the ϕ motor, so minimizing the χ motor movements allows faster image acquisition.
Even with this optimization, a single (χ, ϕ) scan for a single slice can take up to ten minutes to
acquire. For volumes collected over several z positions and several external strain steps - 4D data
- experiments can take several hours. For this reason, sample straining is performed in discrete
steps. The procedure is too slow for a continuous process. More details on the discrete straining
procedure can be found in [1].
Having obtained the stack of diffraction images for different orientation pairs, within the current
analysis chain these images are processed and fitted back into a single image for a slice. We will
look at this in more detail in subsection 2.2.2.
2.2
Data
In this section, a more detailed examination of the data from individual experiments is presented.
The discussion begins by introducing the datasets, followed by an overview of the preprocessing
steps, and concludes with an analysis of the current data fitting process.
This thesis analyzes four datasets to allow for comparison of modeling results both within and
between datasets. Three of the datasets were acquired through DFXM experiments conducted at
Beamline ID06-HXM at the European Synchrotron Radiation Facility (ESRF). One dataset was
synthetically created. The four dataset are:
Baseline Dataset
The dataset used in the paper [1] is referred to as ”baseline data” because it has been accepted by
the material science community as a standard reference for further research. The data was acquired
from a single crystal sample of 99.9999% pure aluminum, with angular positions of (37×26) in the
ranges χ ∈[−0.5◦, 1.4◦] and ϕ ∈[−7.1◦, 9.3◦]. It consists of a 2D slice extracted from the bulk of
the sample. Measurements were taken for multiple strain steps ranging between ϵ = 0%, ..., 4.3%.
The strain step sizes were selected to identify the point at which dislocation cells form.
The raw data for a single strain step has dimensions [962, 1569, 1829], representing 962 images
with a pixel size of 1569 × 1829. The effective cell size is 656 nm along the x-dimension and 202
nm along the y-dimension. The processed and fitted results are presented in Figure 2.7.

This dataset is a 4D dataset obtained with the DFXM setup. ”4D” refers to 3D volumes recorded

Synthetic 3D Dataset
This dataset has been created to provide a less noisy dataset that provides a second option to the
volumetric dataset for modeling dislocation cells in 3D. It has been developed by Adam Cretton
and added to the DARLING library [21].
To create a synthetic 3D dataset, we construct a 5D set of raw data in which we construct a volume
in which each voxel contains 225 angle values corresponding to different angle pairs recorded at
a resolution of 15×15. This results in the final 5D data structure of the form [15, 15, 200, 200, 50].
Here the first two dimensions represent angular coordinates and the last three correspond to spatial
coordinates. The data set is designed to satisfy the following characteristics:
1. The 225 angular values in each voxel follow a Gaussian distribution.
2. Throughout the volume, distinguishable cells are constructed where voxel intensities fol-
low the mean of the angular Gaussian distributions. The distribution of cell sizes follows a
lognormal distribution.
To achieve these properties, we generate 3D Gaussian blobs within the volume. A 3D Gaussian
blob is a structure in a 3D volume where the intensities around the centre of the blob follow the
properties of a Gaussian distribution for each axis direction. The definition is derived from the
same principles as a 3D Gaussian filter, whose weights represent a 3D Gaussian blob. [22]. and
for each blob a corresponding 2D Gaussian distribution for the angles. A 3D Gaussian blob is a
When generating the means of the distributions, we need to make sure that they are distinct from
each other and separated by a certain minimum distance. This ensures that neighboring blobs are
not only spatially distinct, but also have sufficiently different angular Gaussian distributions.
7
==PAGE:17==
This process results in X distinct blobs, each with a unique angular distribution characterised by
two Gaussian components. The means of these angular distributions are well separated, preventing
too much similarity between connected blobs.
Once the blobs are generated, we iterate through them and assign angular values to each voxel by
multiplying the angular Gaussian distribution by the intensity of the corresponding 3D Gaussian
blob. These values are then added to the volume. While each blob contributes independently, slight
overlap between neighboring blobs is possible, so we always add the values to the volume.
To ensure a lognormal distribution of cell sizes, we sample the variance of each 3D Gaussian blob
from a lognormal distribution. Since the variance directly influences the spatial extent of each cell,
this approach naturally enforces a lognormal distribution of cell sizes across the dataset.
This is a relatively simple approach to modeling the data, as the assumption that the cells follow
slightly overlapping 3D Gaussian blobs big. As it stands, it may be too easy to develop models
based on this synthetic data. This is because the data we derive from the DFXM experiments is not
yet well understood, and the simplistic modeling of the synthetic data means that the models de-
veloped on this dataset do not take these unknown factors into account. Results from the synthetic
data should therefore be treated with caution.

2.2.1
Preprocessing
To process the raw experimental data, it must first be cleaned of white noise and setup-related
errors. The preprocessing is carried out in four steps:
A. Background Subtraction
Background subtraction removes the constant background present in the dataset. This is achieved
by subtracting the median values, calculated on a per-pixel basis, across the stack of 962 images.
B. Hot Pixel Removal
Hot pixel removal targets outliers in the image data, often caused by cosmic rays hitting the de-
tector. These pixels, characterized by unusually high intensity, are identified using the following
approach. Since high intensity pixels can also represent diffraction peaks of the crystal structure,
we need to distinguish them from hot pixels. To do this, we set a threshold derived from the vari-
ance of the local median differences to the original pixel values. If a pixel exceeds this threshold,
it is replaced by the local median around the hot pixel.
C. Tresholding
The purpose is to set a lower boundary for the intensity values of the raw data to get rid of white
noise, it can differ between experiments, and is set at the intensity level of 12 for the volumetric
data.
D. Shift Correction
Due to the experimental setup and mechanical movement of the goniometer, images recorded on
the detector exhibit multiple shifts within the x-direction, leading to overlaps. These shifts are
corrected through image registration.
In Figure A.1, the raw data for a single angular position (χ = 0.3, ϕ = 7.9901) is visualized,
illustrating the different preprocessing steps.

2.2.2
Fitting of the Data
After preprocessing the raw data, the next step involves fitting the stack of (χ, ϕ) images acquired
at various goniometer positions, into a single image representation. Below, we review the methods
implemented in the darfix [23] software currently used at the beamline, optimize their application,
and propose alternative fitting techniques.
The initial data-fitting methods share a fundamental principle: processing the image stack by iden-
tifying the angular pair (χ, ϕ) that represents the optimal diffraction condition for each pixel. This
optimal angle pair is approximated based on the assumption that the intensities around the true
diffraction angle follow a Gaussian distribution. This assumption is validated by visualizations of
the raw data, most notably in the χ angle projection plots shown in Figure A.2.
The optimal angle pair is determined as the Center of Mass (COM) of the Gaussian distribution,
providing a practical solution to account for the experimental challenge of achieving the exact
diffraction condition, even with high angular resolution. The concept is illustrated in Figure 2.3,
using a simplified fitting example.
Figure 2.3: The plot shows the fitting procedure of the raw DFXM data using 1D moment fitting,
which is described in more detail later in the section. In Raw Data Images we show how each
pair of angles from the goniometer movements has a corresponding 2D image. The fitting of the
image stack is done on a pixel-by-pixel basis, as indicated by the red framed pixel. In 1 D Moment
Fitting for χ we show how the fitting is done for one pixel and one angle in the resulting fitted
image. This process has to be repeated for the second angle. In COM Image we show the fitted
COM for a region of interest of a slice of the volumetric dataset. The colors correspond to the
COM angle pair of (χ, ϕ) in each pixel.
The process of fitting raw data to an image of angle pairs presents two major challenges. First,
Gaussian fitting is computationally expensive. It is an optimization problem, and for a single slice
it can requires optimizing millions of functions. Second, some pixels have more than one peak
9
==PAGE:19==
in the orientation angles, a phenomenon known as peak broadening. This occurs when multiple
orientations are present within the same pixel, and is of particular interest in this study. Peak
broadening often correlates with the boundaries of dislocation cells, as shown in [1]. An example
of peak broadening is shown in Figure 2.4. We can see that there are two visible peaks in the data
in the projection plots on the left.
Two solutions are proposed to address the computational cost of Gaussian fitting. First, approxi-
mating the behavior of the 2D Gaussian simplifies the optimization process. Second, using paral-
lelization with frameworks such as PyTorch or Numba significantly accelerates the computation.
These approaches are discussed further in this chapter.
In regards to the peak broadening, even when multiple peaks are present in one pixel, in the darfix
program only a single Gaussian is fitted, with the COM representing an averaged orientation be-
tween the peaks. This approach ensures smooth transitions in the data, which is essential for im-
age analysis, as noisy transitions can interfere with downstream processes. In addition, the feature
vector of the image is expanded by incorporating the full width half maximum (FWHM) of the
Gaussian distribution in both the χ and ϕ directions. The FWHM is directly related to the variance
of the Gaussian, with FWHM ≈2.355 · σ.
Figure 2.4: Raw Pixel Data in Pixel (832,1023) in the 4 slice of Volumetric data for strain ϵ = 6.5%
At the end of all Gaussian methods, the fitted data containing the COM and FWHM features are
smoothed with a median filter in darfix. The kernel size of the median filter is set to 3. This further
ensures a smooth transition of the data in real space for later filtering operations. To further explore
the implications of different Gaussian fitting approaches, both computational aspects and raw data
are analyzed in greater detail:
2D Gaussian Fitting
This method involves fitting a 2D Gaussian function using the darfix [23] library which fits the
2D gaussian using the curvefit [24] function, which formulates the problem as an optimization
task. The ”LM” solver is used within the curvefit method. However, fitting 2D Gaussians is
computationally expensive; processing a single slice of data in the manner described takes about
8 hours in darfix. This makes it impractical to apply this method to the entire 4D dataset.
Since the fitting process is performed pixel by pixel for all Gaussian fitting methods (Figure 2.3),
parallelization offers a solution to reduce the computation time. We decided to implement a Py-
10
==PAGE:20==
Torch-based approach to 2D Gaussian fitting on a GPU. Parallelization on the CPU would have
been valid as we could have used the curvefit function, but did not seem as interesting since we
would have been limited by the amount of kernel that could run in parallel.
In the absence of a GPU-compatible implementation of curvefit, backpropagation with the Adam
optimizer [25] was used to fit 2D Gaussians on the GPU. This approach successfully enabled
parallel optimization and allowed up to 20,000 curve fits to be run simultaneously. The problem
with this GPU-based approach was that memory limitations and optimizer inefficiency posed chal-
lenges. With a maximum GPU capacity of 80 GB per node at DTU, the batch size was limited to
20,000 fits. With a batch taking between 1 and 5 minutes to compute, the total time per slice on the
volumetric dataset would still be 3-4 hours, which is still too slow. Even if we were to use multiple
nodes, which would increase the computational complexity, achieving ultra-fast computation on
the DTU cluster using GPUs is challenging because with the memory we could only increase the
computations linearly and we would reach the limits of the DTU GPUs.
At this stage, work on this topic was discontinued due to its high computational demands and
limited scope within the project. As a next step, we would try to parallelize on the CPU, where the
curvefit makes the optimization faster per operation, although the parallelization will be much less
due to the more limited number of CPU cores. If that does not work, an implementation of curvefit
that runs directly on the GPU would be an interesting project, but again was out of scope.
1D Gaussian Fitting
The 1D Gaussian fitting is a faster alternative. For both angles, the data is mapped onto the corre-
sponding angular coordinate (χ or ϕ), creating a 1D representation along each angular axis. The
result of the projection, is illustrated in Figure 2.4 for the χ angle, enables the fitting of two 1D
Gaussians. The results for both angles are then combined to determine the COM and FWHM in
the 2D space.
Fitting two 1D Gaussians is computationally faster than fitting one 2D Gaussian because the feature
space of the two 2D Gaussians to solve for does not grow linearly and leads to a more complex
optimization problem.
1D Moment Fitting
This approach follows the same principles of projecting the data into one dimension for both angles.
However, in this method, the Gaussian behavior is approximated by calculating the moments of
the data. This significantly increases the speed of fitting but makes the process more sensitive to
outliers.

The Gaussian fitting process converts the raw data into an [4, width, height] image for each slice,
where the four features are COMχ, COMϕ, FWHMχ, and FWHMϕ. This applies to 2D Gaus-
sian fitting, 1D Gaussian fitting, and 1D Moment fitting.
The subsequent methods take a different approach, reducing the feature dimension at each pixel us-
ing a purely data-driven strategy instead of relying on the Gaussian assumption. The premise is that
by using a higher-dimensional feature space, it may be possible to achieve a more representative
characterization of the data.
PCA
PCA projects data into a lower dimensional space by identifying directions of high variance in the
original feature space. [26] A major problem with applying PCA to our feature space is that the
calculation is not pixel-wise, but requires the entire dataset. The currently implemented version in
darfix is therefore impractical due to memory and time constraints.
A possible workaround would be to compute the principal components on a subset of the image
and then apply the dimensionality reduction to the rest of the image. We have implemented this,
but it still proved too slow. As it stands, PCA cannot be used effectively on our dataset to fit the
whole image. What might work is a super-small sub-region of the images. We did not explore this
further, though.

Autoencoder
What has not been implemented in darfix is an autoencoder to generate a representation of the raw
data. This approach is computationally interesting, as training such a network is initially expensive,
but once trained, it could be reused for new datasets, drastically reducing computational effort. The
concept of a potential architecture is outlined inFigure 2.6.
Figure 2.6: Architecture Considerations for an autoencoder network for DFXM data
Two options are proposed for inputting pixel data into an encoder. The first option involves stack-
ing the values of a pixel into a vector and adding a positional encoding of the pixel position to
indicate where the pixel is located in the image. This approach is problematic as it loses spatial
information about the angular relationships.
To overcome this, the second option proposes to use an input image where the angular values are
spatially ordered, allowing a Convolutional neural network (CNN) to extract local angular infor-
mation. The positional encoding was added to the representation learned by the CNN. Applying
positional encoding after the first CNN may seem counterintuitive, as we initially stated that spatial
information is lost. However, we cannot have a convolutional layer at this stage, as the network
operates on single pixel inputs.
An encoder-decoder network is then used to reconstruct the original input, with the latent space
providing an adapted representation of the data for further analysis. The use of convolutional
autoencoders is quite common, and the concept is further explained in [27]. What is more specific
is the approach of having an image within an image, for which we did not find specific further
research on autoencoder.
The first results of the network were inconclusive, as we were not able to reduce the training loss
of the model. The sparsity of the pixel data seems to be an issue that was not sufficiently addressed
in the model architecture and could be a reason for this result. Furthermore, training deep learning
models is inherently complex and requires extensive engineering, which was beyond the scope of
this work.

To summarize the fitting methods, Gaussian fitting, motivated by the physical nature of the data,
proved to be the most effective. Although the computational cost is high, the use of appropriate
computational tools allows for parallelization, which significantly reduces the computational time.
The approaches of finding feature representations using PCA or an autoencoder were not feasible
for the scope of this work. This does not mean that we could never extract a meaningful data
representation using these methods, but rather the choice to use a Gaussian fit, which has already
shown high quality results in [1]. Therefore, further time spent on these alternative methods did
not seem justified.
For this work and the volumetric dataset, the data were fitted using the 1D moments method.
However, the small step and baseline data sets were fit using the 2D Gaussian approach. Given
that the small step and volumetric datasets were acquired during the same beamtime and their fitted
results are comparable, the validity of the moments approximation is further supported.

darfix libary
All data preprocessing and fitting described can be executed using the darfix library [23]. The pri-
mary function of the darfix library is to facilitate the processing of raw experimental data through
a user interface provided by Orange [28]. However, the user interface currently lacks a bulk pro-

dataset consists of over 70 slices. Each slice can take up to 30 minutes to process, even with the
parallelization of the moments fitting.
This limitation necessitated the use of the darfix package API to automate processing tasks previ-
ously performed in the user interface. Additionally, modifications to the darfix code were required
to enable parallelization of the moments calculation.
Using the API without the user interface is challenging, as some functionalities are directly tied
to the front-end logic. Consequently, custom functionalities had to be implemented to save fitted
files for bulk processing.
Further development on the darfix software is ongoing. While working on this thesis, another
software package DARLING [21] was established, which solves the problem of having a front-end
application and enables easier modifications to the software.

2.2.4
Conclusion
To conclude this chapter, we can say that the processing and Gaussian fitting method from the
original paper [1] are valid but computationally restrictive. Accelerating these processes is possible
but requires significant optimization and knowledge of the darfix library [23]. Efforts are being
made to make such optimizations easier to implement with the new library DARLING [21].
Alternative fitting models than Gaussian exist, but are poorly suited to our data and introduce addi-
tional computational challenges. While better feature representations are theoretically achievable,
Gaussian fitting remains the most effective and practical approach.
The volumetric data is of notably lower quality than the baseline data, as confirmed by material
scientists. The discrepancies in data quality are attributed to experimental factors rather than dif-
ferences in data processing methods. This lower data quality, significantly hinders the validation
of 3D and 4D dislocation cell models, as there is only this one dataset in existence.

Theoretical Modeling Framework
In this chapter, we will present an overview of the state-of-the-art segmentation approaches and
technical concepts that are important for this thesis. For segmentation, we will provide a section
on general state-of-the-art methods and a section on the current state-of-the-art methods used in
the field of crystallography related to DFXM.
3.1
Segmentation Overview
Segmentation is the process of dividing data into distinct regions or segments, each segment typ-
ically corresponding to different objects, structures or features [29]. In most cases, the data are
images or volumes, but it can also be applied to higher dimensional tensors, such as an evolving
volume over time. The aim of segmentation is to simplify the representation of the underlying
data and make it easier to analyze. In the case of this work, it is the segmentation of a crystal into
dislocation cells.
With the recent innovation in deep learning, segmentation methods should be divided into two
groups: Traditional and non-traditional (deep learning) approaches.
Non-traditional deep learning approaches to segmentation have seen significant success since the
introduction of U-Net and its variants [30], particularly in fields such as medical imaging. These
architectures are widely used for both 2D and 3D segmentation, and in some cases have been
extended to 4D applications [31].
While these models achieve state-of-the-art results, they rely heavily on labeled datasets for train-
ing or fine-tuning. This dependence is a major challenge when labeled data is not available. In
such cases, supervised learning approaches become impractical, limiting the applicability of deep
learning.
For traditional segmentation techniques, there are a variety of methods, each suitable for different
applications. The categorization of these methods varies in the literature, with different classifica-
tions found in [29] and [32]. One of the most basic and widely used approaches is thresholding,
which segments an image, volume or higher-dimensional data into a binary representation. The
threshold can be determined manually or automatically, with Otsu’s method [33] being one of the
most recognized techniques for automatic threshold selection. This approach can be extended to
multi-class segmentation by applying multiple thresholds within the data [34].
Beyond thresholding, traditional segmentation techniques can be grouped into three broad cate-
gories: edge-based, region-based and energy-based methods in [29]. Edge-based segmentation
relies on the detection of intensity transitions using gradient operators such as Sobel and Gaussian
derivative filters subsection 3.2.2, or more advanced algorithms such as Canny edge detection [29].
While edge detection is effective for describing boundaries, it is often insufficient for direct object
segmentation. Instead, it is often combined with other techniques that use the detected edges to
refine the segmentation results. [29].
Region-based segmentation methods focus on grouping data elements into regions based on sim-
ilarity criteria. These elements can be pixels, voxels or higher dimensional structures, depending
on the specific segmentation method. These include techniques such as region growing or cluster-
ing. Examples include watershed segmentation, which grows regions from seed markers [35], and
clustering methods such as or Gaussian Mixture Model[26].
Energy-based methods approach segmentation as an optimization problem, where an energy or
cost function is minimized to achieve the desired segmentation. Graph-based approaches, such as
graph cuts [36], belong to this category. These methods are flexible trough the definition of the
17
==PAGE:27==
loss function and can produce great segementation results, but are computationally expensive due
to their use of these optimization techniques. In addition, defining an appropriate energy function
can be non-trivial.
Another class of energy-based methods are active contour models, where an initial contour/struc-
tures is placed around a target object and is iteratively adjusted to fit the object’s boundaries. The
optimization process balances smoothness constraints with data constraints. A well-known exam-
ple is the snake model [37].
The methods discussed here represent only a subset of traditional segmentation techniques. Many
other approaches exist, often designed to address specific segmentation challenges. They can com-
bine several techniques and incorporate pre- and post-processing steps, such as morphological op-
erations or smoothing filters, to improve segmentation accuracy[29].
One of the key advantages of traditional segmentation methods is their ability to be applied to un-
labeled data and their interpretability, which can be lost with deep learning approaches. There is
also more control with traditional methods over the behavior of the segmentation. For example,
watershed segmentation inherently produces continuous object boundaries, whereas thresholding
does not enforce such constraints. Energy-based methods allow explicit regulation of boundary
smoothness, object size, or other structural properties[36], providing flexibility in fine-tuning seg-
mentation output.
3.1.1
Research context specific Segmentation methods
The segmentation methods discussed so far are approaches that can be used in a variety of con-
texts. This section focuses on segmentation techniques specifically used in materials science, more
specifically for the segmentation of grains and dislocation cells.
In this section, we will almost exclusively consider methods used in Electron Backscatter Diffrac-
tion analysis.
Data processing in EBSD experiments is often performed using the EBSD specific library MTEX,
which provides functionality for grain segmentation and local grain deformation analysis [38].
Grain segmentation in MTEX is typically based on the misorientation angle between adjacent mea-
surements and is implemented for 2D and 3D. Local grain deformation is often quantified using
the Kernel Average Misorientation filter [39].
Grain segmentation in the MTEX library follows a thresholding approach where measurements
are segmented based on a misorientation angle threshold (KAM). The misorientation in the EBSD
context is defined by calculating the angular difference between two orientation vectors [40]. In-
dividual grains are identified using connected component analysis or similar methods. There are
more advanced approaches to grain segmentation [41], which differ mainly in how they deter-
mine segmentation thresholds rather than in the overall methodology. As the data in DFXM are
fundamentally different from EBSD, these methods cannot be applied directly.
For dislocation cell analysis, methods for identifying dislocation cell boundaries in EBSD were
investigated without the use of MTEX [42]. Rather than explicitly segmenting dislocation cells
into distinct regions, this method focuses on calculating the ratio of cell walls to inner cell area.
To segment dislocation cells in DFXM the research consists of the model introduced in [1].

Watershed
Watershed segmentation interprets single-channel images as topographic surfaces, where inten-
sity values represent elevation. The algorithm simulates a flooding process, where valleys (local
minima) fill first, and segmentation boundaries form when flooded regions merge [45]. To pre-

the expansion, ensuring that only meaningful structures are segmented.

2D Dislocation Cell Analysis
4.1
Introduction
The ultimate goal is to model dislocation cells in higher dimensions beyond 2D. In the scope
of this thesis it is the modeling of dislocation cells in 3D. A critical step is to understand and
analyze existing 2D modeling approaches. Focusing on 2D is important because it provides a
potential foundation for constructing 3D models, either by stitching together results or by using
its fundamental ideas with techniques better suited for 3D. In addition, 2D modeling currently
provides the only accredited results for dislocation cells in bulk samples, making it the only reliable
benchmark for measuring model quality and therefore essential for validation in higher dimensions.
This chapter investigates the following key research questions:
• What is the current approach to 2D dislocation cell modeling and how does it compare to
the current state-of-the-art techniques in the material science field?
• What validation metrics can be derived from the baseline results?
• Is the current modeling choice robust, and can it serve as a reliable baseline for developing
validation metrics in higher dimensions?
• How can the current 2D model be improved?
To address these questions, a methodology for experiments and validation techniques is presented,
followed by a presentation of the results. The results are discussed to answer the research questions
and provide a basis for extending cell modeling into higher dimensions.

4.3
Methodology
The methodology we introduce for this thesis consists of two parts. First, a quantitative approach

4.3.1
Validation Methods
The baseline model was validated using an ”eye-test” method and insights from material science.
This qualitative approach can be misleading, particularly when exploring different modeling tech-
niques. Therefore, a quantitative assessment is essential.
The fundamental concept of the validation process is that we need to establish the validation on
the results obtained by [1] and the baseline data,as there are no other proven results. Consequently,
when defining validation methods, the benchmark values used for comparison must originate from
this baseline approach.
To enable a structured evaluation, different criteria are introduced and defined, serving as the basis
for subsequent comparisons. For the evaluation in this 2D chapter, the full crystal detected in the
DFXM method will be the basis for all the evaluations. We will start by describing the metrics
used in [1]:
• Cell Size Distribution
The cell size distribution in [1] follows a lognormal distribution with a mean cell size of 4.65 nm2.
It is visualized in Figure 4.3. The lognormality was established using the Kolmogorov-Smirnov
test.
To compare models based on this measure, two approaches can be used: First assessing the
similarity between model-generated distributions, which can be evaluated using Kernel Density
Estimation [49], or second verifying whether both distributions follow a lognormal distribution
and assessing the quality of their fit.
When testing for lognormality, we report the p-value of the Kolmogorov-Smirnov test. If the
p-value is in the 0.05 range, meaning it is not significantly lower, we will still consider the
distribution to be lognormal. However, if the p-value is significantly lower, we will treat it
differently and analyze the distribution using violin plots of the Probability Density Function,
estimated via KDE, not assuming lognormality.
Figure 4.3: Dislocation Cell Analysis measurements as in [1]
• Misorientation Angle Distribution
The misorientation angle quantifies the difference in average orientation between neighboring
dislocation cells. It is calculated as follows: For each defined 2D cell we calculate the average
orientation across the COM. We then determine all possible connections of the cells by perform-
ing a morphological dilation for each cell and determining overlapping cells. We then take the
set of connected cells and calculate the difference in average orientation for each connection. Its
distribution of the baseline model is shown in Figure 4.3 and follows a χ-distribution. The use
23
==PAGE:33==
of misorientation angle as a validation metric follows the same principle as the cell size distri-
bution. If the distribution is confirmed to follow a χ-distribution, we compare the models based
on this fit. However, if the assumption of a χ-distribution is rejected, we will instead compare
the probability density functions (PDFs) using Kernel Density Estimation (KDE).
The original measurements were designed to provide insight for material science applications.
Which makes using them as standalone validation metrics problematic. If the goal was simply to
match the observed distribution, this could be achieved by randomly segmenting the image without
ensuring meaningful dislocation cell structures. This highlights the need for additional validation
metrics that assess the performance of individual cells rather than just the overall distribution.
Despite this limitation, distribution analysis remains relevant. As shown in [1], dislocation cells
follow specific statistical distributions. If a model fails to reproduce these distributions or fits them
at a different scale, while other validation metrics support the model quality, it can provide valuable
insight into its dislocation cell behavior.
To provide a more robust validation framework, the following sections introduce additional metrics
that evaluate the performance of individual cells.
• Cell Density

The Smallest Bounding Box Dimension Ratio is introduced as a validation measure to capture

The final measure is the Average of the Five Largest Menger Curvature Values, which quantifies

computed by approximating the cell using its convex hull and calculating the Menger curvature
for every set of three consecutive points along its boundary. The points used in this calculation
are highlighted in red in Figure 4.4.

may overlook fine local details, it is beneficial as the focus is on global curvature rather than
localized variations. Averaging only the five highest curvature values captures broader curvature
features while preventing excessive smoothing from the numerous convex hull points. As ex-

This validation measure is based on the features inside each cell, specifically the COM and

i. Compute the cell mean.
ii. Calculate the absolute difference between the cell mean and all cell pixel values
and normalize by the cell size (Feature loss of a single cell)
(b) Compute the average feature loss across all cells.

The feature loss per pixel is normalized by cell size to account for the correlation between cell
size and feature loss. However, this normalization may not fully remove the correlation, which

filters are applied to the recorded crystal orientations, DFXM captures both orientation and FWHM
data, allowing for a more detailed characterization of dislocation structures. Given its correlation
with dislocation cell boundaries, the inclusion of FWHM in the model is well justified.

The KAM filter has historically been used to identify grain misorientations and define grain bound-

Despite its historic use, the KAM filter has certain limitations. Due to constraints in pixel length

alternative is the Gaussian derivative filter subsection 3.2.2. This filter is separable, significantly
improving computational efficiency. Furthermore, it supports pixel weighting, allowing for better
handling of asymmetry compared to the KAM filter.
To assess whether the Gaussian derivative filter can replicate or improve the results of the KAM
filter, its performance is evaluated based on the validation metrics and computational efficiency to

4.5
Discussion
• Historical Approaches
When looking at the results of the historical approaches, we need to discuss the modifications made
to the original model from the MTEX library and the multiscale thresholding approach.
In the basic grain segmentation approach in [38] using MTEX, no morphological operations are
originally applied to the thresholded KAM. This is also true for the multiscale Otsu approach.
Further processing of the thresholded images is necessary because performing a connected com-
ponents analysis on the thresholded image itself does not yield any identifiable cells for either
approach. Therefore, morphological operations are introduced to denoise the thresholded image
and produce clearer structures.
We test for different morphological operations and structuring elements. However, a more thor-
ough investigation of the effects of different morphological processing methods and structuring
elements is not included in the results, as the efforts do not lead to qualitative results.
The results we present show the best effort using a morphological approach, but the identified
components still do not correspond in any meaningful way to the regions in the MOSA, which can
be confirmed by comparing the plot with the colored connected components.
The inability to effectively separate dislocation cells by thresholding is primarily due to the nature
of the data. For grain segmentation within EBSD, angular thresholds are typically set at around
10 degrees [38], whereas DFXM data require much lower thresholds. The baseline model uses a
threshold of around 0.3 degrees, making conventional thresholding methods ineffective for dislo-
cation cell segmentation.
The limitations of these conventional approaches led to the development of the presented baseline
model section 4.2. This model works differently by over-thresholding cell walls and then refining
the threshold using skeletonization, resulting in cell walls of uniform size.
• Evaluation of Model Adaptions
The thought process behind investigating the baseline model was to validate potential new mod-
eling concepts with a proven dataset and model, but also to further investigate the performance of
the model and improve it if possible, as the model validation in [1] is based on a slightly biased
material science view, which consists mostly of visual evaluation.
We will discuss the results of the different variations of the model in the order in which they appear
in the results:
Correlation between kernel size and cell size
The results observed for different sizes of the KAM filter are in line with expectations. Larger
kernels reduce the sensitivity to noise and capture larger scale differences, leading to an increase
in cell size. In addition, the observed positive correlation between cell size and feature loss is con-
sistent with expectations. Meaning that even with cell size normalization the feature loss increases
for bigger cells.
When it comes to model selection, it is not so clear which kernel produced the ”better” model.
For each kernel size, the cell size distribution is lognormal and the misalignment distribution is
a χ distribution. Also, we cannot argue for one model over the other with the feature error as it
behaves as expected, increasing for larger cells and decreasing for smaller cells. We will return to
this model selection problem later.
Usage of asymmetric filter and incorporating FWHM
The use of an asymmetric filter is a logical approach as it helps to mitigate directional bias in cell
modeling. Given this advantage, asymmetric filtering should always be considered when analysing
35
==PAGE:45==
DFXM data. Therefore, the question is not whether we should use it, but which asymmetric kernel
size reproduces the result of the baseline data.
The results show that the asymmetric KAM filter with a smaller kernel size (3, 9) reproduces the
result of the previously used (5, 5) kernel. The asymmetric (5, 15) kernel results in larger cell
sizes.
Looking at the inclusion of the FWHM feature in the kernel, we could argue that the result doesn’t
change much and it just increases the computational complexity. But what if the two features are
not as perfectly correlated as they are in the baseline data, then withholding the information may
lead to a false result. Given this, it is important to include both COM and FWHM in the filter, as
this increases its robustness to noise.
Performance Trade-off between the KAM Filter and Gaussian Derivative Filter
We investigated the Gaussian Derivative Filter as an alternative to the KAM filter, as we wanted to
challenge the material science research standard with a well-established image analysis approach.
While the GDF significantly reduces computation time, its performance differs from that of the
KAM filter.
For the symmetric GDF with σ = (1, 1) and σ = (2, 2), the smaller kernel (σ = 1) introduced
noise, reducing the average cell size and increasing the density, contrary to the expected smoothing
effect of the filter. With σ = 2, the results were more in line with the KAM filter, maintaining rea-
sonable p-values for cell size and misorientation distributions. However, the feature loss increased
for smaller cells, suggesting that the GDF performs worse in modeling dislocation cells.
An asymmetric GDF setup with σ = (1, 3.23) and σ = (1.5, 4.8) showed similar trends. Even with
comparable pixel kernel sizes, the GDF produced smaller cells with greater feature loss compared
to the KAM. These results suggest that although GDF can approximate the results of the KAM
filter, it introduces artifacts due to a more noisy filter.
This is the main drawback of the filter, and probably the reason for the poor performance: The
orientation changes in the image occur on such a small scale that we need such a small filter size
that we lose the smoothing benefits of GDF.
To summarize: The main advantage of GDF is its computational efficiency, especially for high-
dimensional datasets where KAM filtering becomes infeasible. The problem is that the kernel
size for a GDF must be so large that it introduces noise by including multiple cells in its kernel
region. While the GDF is useful for iterative modeling due to its speed, the KAM filter remains
the preferred choice for final segmentation, ensuring superior structure preservation and feature
integrity.
Local Normalization
The differences in the results where local normalization is applied are small, but the model with
normalization performs slightly worse. Nevertheless, it may be interesting to consider this ap-
proach for other data, as more localized patterns may appear in higher strain experiments, such as
the volumetric data. What we also have to consider is that the 70% threshold takes away the effect
of the local normalization as it is very robust in identifying walls at different scales. If we change
this approach, we may see a greater benefit from the localized normalization approach.
Adapted Model
Finally, we propose an adapted model using an asymmetric Gaussian kernel with σ = (1, 3.23),
incorporating both the FWHM and COM features. The Gaussian derivative filter was chosen for its
computational efficiency, while the asymmetric filter and additional features improve the robust-
ness of the model. The selection of the kernel size discussed earlier, is set at the level corresponding
36
==PAGE:46==
to the baseline model result. As it is not clear from the quantitative results, the best option is still
to follow the visual judgment and the proven baseline results.
• Effects of Changing the Dataset
When comparing the baseline and adapted models on both datasets, it is evident that the adapted
model performs worse on the baseline data, even with an asymmetric filter and the addition of
the second moment, which should make the model superior to the baseline. As previously ana-
lyzed, this worse performance can be attributed to the GDF, which tends to introduce worse model
performance.
Interestingly, this effect is less pronounced in the volumetric dataset, where the feature loss of the
adapted model is more comparable to that of the baseline model. There could be several explana-
tions for this. One possibility is that the GDF performs better in the presence of noise, effectively
filtering out some of the additional variability in the volumetric data. A more plausible explanation
is that the higher feature loss in the baseline model is due to the relatively uncorrelated COM and
FWHM in the volumetric dataset. As the baseline model only applies the KAM filter to the COM,
it is likely to have a proportionally higher feature error as it is evaluated on both COM and FWHM.
Including FWHM in the KAM filter could improve model performance, further supporting the use
of the KAM filter.
The cell shape measures for the different models and datasets remain almost constant across the
different experiments. This is because we do not change the skeletonization of the model, which
is the part of the model that defines the final shape of the cells. In the next chapter, we will move
away from skeletonization and it will be interesting to see how the shape measures compare to the
baseline model.
The distributions on the volumetric data are interesting. This is due to the fact that the volumetric
data is of rather poor quality and modeling the dislocation cells is not as trivial as with the baseline
data, especially with the low correlation of COM and FWHM. Therefore, it is interesting that the
baseline model follows the expected χ distribution parameters on the volumetric dataset, especially
as the FWHM was not included in the filter.
This is a key finding as it shows that the volumetric data set follows the principles established in
[1], despite the noisy behavior of the data.
4.6
Conclusion
In this chapter we have investigated and evaluated the 2D baseline model introduced in [1]. The
research aimed to answer the following questions:
What is the current approach to 2D dislocation cell modeling and how does it compare to the
current state-of-the-art techniques in the material science field?
The model currently used for dislocation cell segmentation of DFXM data is the 2D baseline model.
It was developed to overcome the limitations of historical thresholding-based methods, which fail
to capture dislocation structures for our data. Instead of relying only on a KAM filter threshold, the
baseline model incorporates morphological operations and skeletonization to open up well-defined
dislocation cells.
What validation metrics can be derived from the baseline results?
Model validation is a challenge due to the lack of ground truth. We explored several validation
metrics: The most effective was a combination of feature loss and average cell size. When the
metric did not follow the expected positive correlation—where larger cells exhibited greater feature
loss—it provided valuable insights into model performance. The shape-based metrics on the other
hand proved less useful as they stayed constant throughout the model variations. These metrics
37
==PAGE:47==
are expected to become more relevant when alternative skeletonization methods are introduced in
the 3D chapter.
Is the current modeling choice robust and can it serve as a reliable baseline for developing
validation metrics in higher dimensions?
The 2D baseline model demonstrates robustness on a global scale, maintaining stable cell size
and misorientation distributions across different parameter settings. What is not clear is the local
robustness of the cell structures. As different parameter settings lead to quantitatively good results
on a global scale, there are multiple modeling options to choose from. The problem is that those
different modeling choices can affect the local position of cell boundaries, which can be seen in
the MOSA plots of Figure 4.8. Using the global results to validate models in higher dimensions is
valid as we have seen that the model retains global statistics through parameter changes. We need
to be careful with local cell definitions as they are sensitive to the model parameters.
How can the 2D baseline model be improved?
The baseline model has been improved in three main ways. First, FWHM was added to make
the model more robust when the COM doesn’t match perfectly. Second, asymmetric kernels were
introduced to better handle pixel asymmetry. Thirdly, alternative filtering methods were tested.
While the KAM filter gives better segmentation, the Gaussian derivative filter is computationally
more efficient and works better for iterative modeling.
Final Statement on 2D Cell Modeling and Future Work
Despite the challenges introduced by the inability to directly threshold the filter results and the lack
of ground truth, the results of the 2D analysis provide a good basis for the development of a 3D
model, which will be explored in the next chapter.
What we have not changed in this chapter is the skeletonization of the 2D model, this seems wrong
as it has a huge influence on the model. By using skeletonization instead of direct thresholding
of the cell boundaries, we are ignoring the direct feature information in the definition of the cell
boundaries. This is the trade-off between modeling smooth, continuous cells vs. the data fidelity
of the cell boundaries. It seems logical to explore whether we can increase the importance of the
feature information while still preserving the cell structures. As the aim of this work is to model
higher dimensional dislocation cells, we will explore this topic further in the next chapter, where
we will develop methods to incorporate the cell features more closely.

5
3D Dislocation Cell Analysis
5.1
Introduction
Having validated the 2D model, this chapter considers the modeling of dislocation cells in 3D.
This is of particular interest as it has never been done before in the field of DFXM. For this reason,
creativity was required to find a solution to the problem by trying concepts from the 2D models
and using 3D modeling approaches from other domains. While being creative, it was important but
challenging to maintain a quantitative evaluation scheme and focus on a few specific approaches
rather than trying every 3D segmentation approach. The focus of this chapter is on the following
research questions:
• Can we segment the volume into 3D dislocation cells?
• Which segmentation models are most suitable, and what criteria guided their selection?
• What are the challenges when switching from 2D to 3D dislocation cell modeling and how
are they addressed?
• How can we validate the model in the absence of ground truth data?
To address these questions, we first discuss the selection of fitting 3D segmentation models, fol-
lowed by a methodology for experimental validation of chosen segmentation methods. The results
are then analyzed to answer the research questions and propose future directions.
5.2
Model Selection for 3D Segmentation
Before explaining the methodology for testing 3D models, we first establish which segmentation
models to investigate. Implementing every possible approach is not feasible due to time constraints,
so careful pre-selection is necessary.
It is worthwhile to investigate whether the baseline 2D model can be extended to 3D, as it has
already shown proven results. If successful, this approach would provide a robust foundation for
3D segmentation. However, additional segmentation models must be considered to address the
challenges unique to volumetric data, for this, we discuss in a more data-specific approach the
state-of-the-art segmentation presented in section 3.1.
Segmentation Objective
The primary goal of the model is to segment the volume into distinct regions separated by cell
walls, making this a binary segmentation task (walls vs. no walls). The main challenge is ensuring
continuous and well-defined cell walls throughout the volume.
In 2D, a tresholded binary image is ’opened’ using skeletonization to perform binary segmenta-
tion. This process allowed thin, mostly continuous cell walls to improve segmentation quality over
basic tresholding. In 3D, a similar cell-opening approach needs to be adapted, taking into account
challenges such as data noise, cell wall continuity and cell initialization.
Constraints and Challenges
Several constraints and challenges influenced the model selection.
The absence of ground truth data prevents the use of supervised deep-learning segmentation meth-
ods. Manual annotation of 3D volumes proved infeasible due to ambiguities in defining precise
cell walls and the risk of introducing subjective bias. Attempts to enhance feature representation
using an autoencoder (Figure 2.2.2) did not yield satisfactory results on creating a better feature
representation.
39
==PAGE:49==
The characteristics of volumetric data further complicate segmentation. The dataset is highly noisy,
making direct thresholding unreliable. Since direct thresholding has already been shown to be
ineffective in 2D, it is unlikely to be a viable option in 3D. Additionally, ensuring continuous and
well-defined cell walls across slices is particularly challenging only using a tresholding approach.
Given these constraints, we focus on traditional segmentation methods, specifically marker-based
approaches that provide greater control over cell boundaries. Marker based approaches are such
methods, that initialize a region to be segmented.
Rationale for Marker-Based Approaches
As stated an ideal segmentation approach would avoid explicitly defining N cell markers and di-
rectly binary segment cell walls. However, given the high noise levels and structural complexity of
the dataset, such marker-free methods are unlikely to succeed. Instead, we focus on marker-based
segmentation strategies, which provide better-defined cell boundaries and more stable segmenta-
tion results.
Region-Based and Energy-Based Methods
Within marker-based segmentation techniques, we look at two categories: Region-based methods
and energy-based methods.
Region-Based Methods:
The watershed method segments the 3D volume based on markers (local minima or predefined),
ensuring that all regions are assigned to a specific label. This method creates continuous cell
boundaries and segments the complete volume (subsection 3.2.3). Its ability to preserve structural
consistency makes it a good choice for dislocation cell segmentation.
GMM offer a probabilistic approach to clustering by iteratively optimizing a mixture of Gaus-
sian functions. In volume segmentation with initial markers, GMM can be applied by initializing
Gaussian components, where the number of mixture components corresponds to the number of
markers. While the clustering process itself lacks spatial constraints, these can be incorporated
to improve segmentation quality. The method is particularly appealing because it allows mark-
ers to evolve dynamically through an optimization process, providing greater flexibility compared
to rigid marker-based approaches. However, this flexibility comes at the cost of increased com-
putational complexity, especially with a higher number of markers. It also does not guarantee
well-defined, smooth segmentation regions, requiring post-processing with methods such as K-
nearest neighbor. Choosing the right amount of smoothing can be a challenge - over smoothing
can be a problem.
Energy-Based Methods:
Graph cut segmentation was considered for its ability to optimize segmentation boundaries using
energy minimization. However, extending graph cut segmentation to non-binary classification,
which would be necessary to incorporate more than 2 cell markers, introduces significant compu-
tational overhead and implementation complexity [55]. Additionally, defining a suitable energy
function that accurately represents the volumetric data characteristics is non-trivial. Due to these
limitations, graph cut segmentation was not selected to further investigate.
Selected Models
Based on this analysis, we have selected marker-based watershed segmentation and GMM as the
primary models for investigation. These methods offer the best balance of accuracy, robustness,
and feasibility given the constraints of the dataset.

5.3.1
Data, Filters, and Validation
In this section and point one in the methodology, we look at the differences in data, validation
methods and filters that need to be accounted for when looking at 3D data.
Data
The volumetric dataset exhibits significantly different characteristics compared to the 2D baseline
dataset. Specifically, the correlation between COM boundaries and regions of high FWHM is not
well-defined. Additionally, regions with similar orientations (COM) often exhibit a consistently
high FWHM, which is an indicator for noisy data making segmentation challenging.
Another major limitation is the uniqueness of the dataset, which limits validation. To address this,
we use the synthetic dataset introduced in section 2.2, which allows model evaluation when the
volumetric dataset does not provide adequate data. It should be noted that the synthetic dataset is
problematic as it oversimplifies the segmentation problem and is therefore only used for qualitative
evaluations in this chapter.
Volume Registration
During data acquisition of the volumetric dataset, individual slices shift along the z-axis, leading
to misalignment in the volumetric dataset. Although we cannot provide the original video of the
41
==PAGE:51==
shift in this thesis, sequential images confirm that the misalignment is essentially a rigid translation
( A displacement in the x and y directions) [56].
To correct this, an intensity-based registration approach is applied using the OpenCV library [57].
The registration process aligns each slice to a reference region in the center slice of the volume.
The reference region itself is located in the center, as the noise is smallest at that location by the
experimental setup of DFXM. Since the shift originates from the raw data, all features experience
the same displacement. Therefore, aligning one feature is sufficient to correct the entire image, as
no relative shifts exist between individual features.
Filter Considerations
The asymmetric filtering approach used in 2D must be adapted for 3D segmentation, particularly
regarding padding effects. In 2D, padding effects were negligible due to the large pixel size in
both the x- and y-directions. However, in 3D, the z-dimension is significantly smaller (11 pix-
els), making padding effects more pronounced. Improper padding can introduce artifacts at image
boundaries, disrupting the segmentation process.
To mitigate this issue, a mirror/reflection padding is implemented for the KAM filter [58]. While
this method introduces some bias at the edges of the volume, it reduces noise compared to constant-
value padding. The Gaussian Derivative Filter, on the other hand, presents additional challenges
due to its larger filter size in pixels. As a result, its suitability for 3D segmentation is questionable.
For the KAM filter, an asymmetric filter size of (3,9,3) is chosen, reflecting the voxel aspect ratio
and minimizing padding effects in the z-dimension. The filter size is chosen somewhat ad hoc but
in alignment with the 2D results.
Validation Strategy
To evaluate segmentation performance in 3D, the validation metrics introduced in ?? are adapted.
Since 3D segmentation lacks direct ground truth comparisons, two distinct evaluation approaches
are used:
1. Slice-wise Evaluation as 2D Cells
2. Direct 3D Cell Evaluation
For slice-wise evaluation, the segmented 3D volume is analyzed by extracting slices along the z-
axis, focusing on the three central slices. This produces a set of 2D dislocation cells, which are
evaluated using the same metrics established in the 2D segmentation approach (subsection 4.3.1).
By comparing these results to the 2D model, the consistency of the 3D segmentation can be as-
sessed against an existing baseline.
The calculation of validation metrics differs slightly between 2D and 3D. Since full crystal seg-
mentation is computationally expensive, the volume for 3D segmentation is limited to a subset of
the volume for the volumetric dataset. The Region of Interest is situated in the center part of the
image and has the size (150, 200, 11).
Additionally, for cell size and misorientation distributions, multiple statistical distributions are
considered, including log-normal, chi, Gaussian, Weibull, and Gamma distributions, following the
methodology from [1].
For direct 3D cell evaluation, the goal is to compare different 3D segmentation models on the
statistics of full 3D dislocation cells.
Not all validation metrics from the 2D model can be directly applied in 3D. Due to computational
constraints and a lack of robust 3D implementations, metrics, such as Perimeter Ratio, Circularity,
and Minimal Bounding Box Ratio and Curvature are not evaluated for now in 3D.
For visual inspection, only slice-wise representations of the cells can be presented. We provide
the MOSA with overlaid cell boundaries, as has been done in the 2D section, it is necessary to
42
==PAGE:52==
validate whether the cells remain connected across the volume. To achieve this, we provide slices
that display the class assignments of the cells, allowing for a more detailed assessment of their
continuity.
5.3.2
Identifying Cell Markers
In Step 2 of the methodology, cell markers are identified using a minimum filter applied to the
filtered output of KAM, detecting local minima. Since the filter highlights cell boundaries, the
minimum filter is expected to locate values within the cells. This approach differs from the per-
centage thresholding used in the 2D baseline model, which selects entire inner-cell regions rather
than discrete center points.
An important aspect of the minimum filter is that it introduces a form of local standardization due to
its fixed size. As observed in chapter 4, local standardization has minimal impact on performance,
making this localized identification a viable alternative to the global percentage thresholding ap-
proach.
The filter size is a crucial parameter, as it directly influences the number of initialized cell markers.
It must be chosen carefully—small sizes risk detecting multiple minima within a single cell, while
large sizes may skip cells entirely. To account for structural asymmetry, an anisotropic filter with
a 1 : 3 : 1 ratio is used and scaled for larger filter sizes.
For the z-axis, padding remains a challenge, particularly because the filter sizes are significantly
larger than those used in the KAM filter. We will use ”mirror” padding again. This can lead
to a larger number of markers on the outside of the volume. This is something that needs to be
considered when looking at the results.

Let’s explain in more detail how we use the Gaussian Mixture Model and K-Nearest Neighbor for
the segmentation of dislocation cells.
Clustering with GMM
The GMM is a probabilistic clustering method that models data as a mixture of multiple Gaussian
distributions, each characterized by a mean and variance. The objective of GMM is to estimate the
parameters of these distributions to fit a Gaussian mixture function to the dataset. More technical
details on GMM can be found in [26].
In our case, the input to the clustering algorithm consists of n-dimensional feature vectors, where
each voxel is characterized by a set of features. To initialize the GMM, we define the means
of the Gaussian components based on the local minima extracted from the filtered KAM image.
The feature vector at these voxels corresponds to the Gaussian means. The variance is initialized
uniformly for all Gaussian components.
Since GMM alone does not incorporate spatial relationships between voxels, we introduce po-
sitional encoding as an additional feature and a weighting term to control the influence of the
encoding. The final feature vector for each voxel consists of:
• Positional encoding (spatial coordinates)
• KAM intensity values
• Center of mass (COM) values
• Full width at half maximum (FWHM) values
The exact calculation of the feature vectors for each voxel is detailed in subsection A.4.1. The
implementation used to fit the GMM can be found in [60].
Post-Processing with K-Nearest Neighbors (KNN)
Following Gaussian Mixture Model (GMM) segmentation, clustering can be noisy with unsmooth
cluster boundaries. To mitigate these artifacts, we use a K-Nearest Neighbours (KNN) method,
which we have implemented ourselves in Python. The neighborhood size is specified in three
dimensions, so that, for example, a filter size of (1, 3, 1) considers neighbors within one pixel
along x and z, and three pixels along y. This refines the segmentation boundaries and prevents
small outlier clusters from being misidentified as cells.

5.5
Discussion
5.5.1
2D Model Extensions
Neither of the two approaches for mapping overlapping cells provides a high-quality 3D segmen-
tation of the volume. This is primarily due to the limited number of connected cells and the short
extent of their connectivity. The main reasons for this is the local sensitivity of the 2D models
section 4.6 and the noise in the volumetric data. As a result, cells in individual 2D slices differ too
much from one another, preventing the identification of sufficient overlapping structures.
Even with improved data, the overlaying method will be challenging, particularly in ensuring
smooth boundaries for the overlaid cells. If the overlap threshold is set at 65%, cells may ex-
hibit significant jumps between slices, leading to non-smooth structures. Conversely, increasing
the threshold makes it even more difficult to map connected cells.
Complete 3D Extension
The results of the 3D skeletonization approach also fail to produce meaningful cell structures.
Instead of producing a valid segmentation, the model generates random lines and small bubble-
like artifacts.
To understand this failure, we must consider that skeletonization in this model is an approximation
of the medial axis. The issue could arise from one of two factors: (1) the dataset does not contain
53
==PAGE:63==
well-separated structures that can be meaningfully represented as the medial axis of 3D cell walls,
or (2) the skeletonization approximation itself is inadequate.
Given the high level of noise in the dataset, the first explanation seems more likely. One possible
solution would be to use larger morphological operators during the opening of the thresholded
image to reduce noise and obtain a cleaner volume for skeletonization. However, further tests
showed that does not change the outcome.
The poor performance of these 2D model extensions motivated the development of alternative
methods, which we discuss next.
5.5.2
Watershed Approach
Watershed segmentation on the volumetric dataset does not achieve meaningful segmentation of
dislocation cells. While the method successfully segments the volume into 3D cell structures and
allows the calculation of validation metrics such as smoothness measures, and feature loss, the
results are unreliable due to the high noise level in the dataset and the weak correlation between
COM and FWHM. Consequently, the segmentation does not provide valuable insights for materials
science applications.
Nevertheless, the results still provide information about the method itself, and the analysis on the
synthetic dataset helps to assess whether the approach could be effective on less noisy data.
First, the sensitivity of the method to the minimum filter size and the position of local minima
markers needs to be discussed. Increasing the filter size results in larger average cell sizes, while
decreasing it results in smaller cells, making the choice of filter size critical. The positions of
the markers and the distance between them form the basis of the distance transform that guides
the segmentation in the watershed approach. This makes the method dependent on good marker
placement. The use of the distance transform has the disadvantage of not using the actual voxel
feature information. This limitation motivates the GMM-based approach, which integrates voxel
features directly into the segmentation process.
We considered an idea where we deliberately initialize too many minima markers to then reconnect
neighbouring small watershed regions of similar orientation. We introduced a graph model with
connected watershed cells, where we could treshold cells together based on different character-
istics of the cells. This proved challenging as the resulting cell structures lacked convexity, and
introducing a criterion to enforce this was non-trivial.
Watershed segmented cells have sharp edges rather than smooth boundaries, which should be re-
flected in higher curvature values. However, the curvature validation metric does not capture this,
suggesting a possible problem with the curvature measure that requires further investigation.
On the synthetic dataset, the watershed segmentation visually approximates the 3D cells well, but
lacks smooth cell boundaries as previously discussed. In addition, the segmentation produces black
regions at the cell boundaries where voxels remain unassigned to any cell. However, this problem
can be solved by programmatically assigning these voxels to neighboring cells and is not a major
concern.
To summarize: The watershed approach fails on the volumetric dataset due to high noise and weak
feature correlations, but performs well on the synthetic dataset. The method is highly dependent
on the initial cell markers and their distance transform. It can be a valid tool for approximating
cells in a larger global context, when the specific cell shape doesn’t need to be extremely accurate
and we are trying to retrieve global statistics. For local dislocation cell information it may be too
inaccurate.
54
==PAGE:64==
5.5.3
GMM-KNN Approach
The GMM-KNN approach does not produce a meaningful segmentation of dislocation cells in the
volumetric dataset. Unlike the watershed-based method, the GMM-based approach can’t generate
spatially connected structures across the volume. This problem is mainly due to the high level of
noise in the volumetric data. As the GMM approach incorporates feature values directly into the
clustering, the method is more sensitive to noise, resulting in poor segmentation.
When the GMM-derived cluster regions are examined, large background clusters appear in dif-
ferent slices rather than forming smaller, localized cells around the initial markers. This behavior
seemed to indicate that the positional encoding was not working as expected. To address this, the
positional features were given more weight in the clustering process. This adjustment, though, had
little effect in reducing the background clustering problem.
To enforce connectivity across slices, a large KNN neighborhood was used, which successfully
connected cell structures across the volume. However, this came at the cost of losing the correlation
between the clusters and the underlying feature values. The dynamic adjustment of cluster centers
(initialized with cell markers) - one of the key advantages of GMM - ultimately contributed to the
failure of the method, as the feature information in the volumetric data was not meaningful enough
to guide the segmentation effectively and lead to random cluster centers
To test whether this feature-based method was viable for less noisy data, it was applied to the
synthetic dataset. The results were significantly better than for the volumetric dataset, with well-
segmented cells and smoother boundaries compared to the watershed-based approach. Some arti-
facts were observed, such as randomly smaller cells within other cells, but overall the segmentation
quality was high. Looking at the model steps, GMM alone provides a strong initial segmentation.
The role of KNN is to act as a noise filter and create convex cells, removing very small random
regions and eliminating narrow elongated structures.
However, the application of KNN introduces trade-offs. It removes the direct feature-based seg-
mentation structure and, if not carefully tuned, can lead to over-smoothing, as seen in the volu-
metric dataset. Therefore, the filter size must be carefully chosen to balance noise reduction and
boundary preservation.
Computational cost is another factor of the approach. In its current implementation, GMM seg-
mentation on the synthetic dataset took 15 minutes. The impact of large cluster counts in a larger
region of interest will increase the computational cost of the GMM approach even more, as it in-
creases the number of parameters to be optimized. Therefore, the GMM-KNN approach seems to
be best suited for smaller regions.
To summarize the GMM-KNN results:
For the volumetric dataset, the method fails to produce a connected, well-defined segmentation. In
contrast, the synthetic dataset successfully generates smooth cell boundaries with minimal error.
While the inclusion of voxel features allows for a more adaptive segmentation, it also increases the
sensitivity to noise. Given its uncertain performance on larger regions, the approach is best suited
for smaller areas, where it allows for smoother segmentation of local structures.
5.6
Conclusion
This chapter investigated the segmentation of 3D dislocation cells using watershed-based segmen-
tation and GMM-KNN clustering, while also evaluating extensions to the 2D baseline model. The
research aimed to answer the following questions
Which segmentation models are most suitable and what criteria guided their selection?
Due to the high noise levels and the lack of ground truth labels, traditional thresholding, and deep
learning methods are unsuitable. Instead, marker-based segmentation approaches were chosen.
55
==PAGE:65==
Watershed segmentation was chosen for its ability to produce continuous segmentations using a
distance transform on the marker positions, while GMM-KNN was chosen for its potential to dy-
namically optimize cluster centers based on the voxel features. The results suggest that Watershed
is more robust to noise and provides a good approximation suitable for large-scale evaluation. The
GMM-KNN approach is better suited for segmenting smaller sub-regions where accurate smooth
cell boundaries are required and computational complexity is less of an issue.
How can we validate the model in the absence of ground truth data?
In the absence of ground truth, validation relied on quantitative metrics and qualitative compar-
isons. Three main validation strategies were used:
1. Slice-wise evaluation: By extracting 2D slices from the 3D volume and comparing them
with known 2D segmentation results, segmentation quality could be assessed.
2. Direct 3D cell evaluation: Metrics such as feature loss, smoothness measures, cell size- and
misorientation distributions provided insight into segmentation consistency and structural
properties between different 3D models.
3. Visual Qualitative Evaluation: Qualitative methods are still needed as quantitative metrics
can be misleading, as in the 3D watershed evaluation.
Although these methods allowed relative comparisons between datasets, it was difficult to eval-
uate models on the noisy volumetric data. To overcome this limitation, a synthetic dataset was
introduced to allow qualitative validation under controlled conditions.
Can we segment the volume into 3D dislocation cells?
The results show that segmentation of 3D dislocation cells is technically possible, but the result
might not be of great quality due to the limitations of the volumetric dataset. The watershed ap-
proach produces spatially connected structures but is highly dependent on marker placement and
distance transform of marker distances. The problem is that the watersheded cells lack visual align-
ment with cell structures.
The GMM-KNN method, on the other hand, fails to produce continuous cell boundaries in the
volumetric dataset because feature-based clustering is highly sensitive to noise.
Both methods perform significantly better on the synthetic dataset, suggesting that the volumetric
dataset is the main obstacle to successful 3D segmentation.
What are the challenges when switching from 2D to 3D dislocation cell modeling, and how
are they addressed?
The transition from 2D to 3D segmentation presents several challenges:
1. Ensuring connectivity between slices: 2D segmentation methods can be applied slice-wise,
but without additional constraints, cells may not form continuous 3D structures. Watershed
overcomes this by using a 3D distance transform, while GMM-KNN struggles with spatial
consistency, resulting in disconnected clusters.
2. High sensitivity to noise: The high level of noise in the volumetric dataset disrupts the con-
sistency of segmentation across slices. Watershed partially mitigates this by enforcing con-
tinuity through the distance transform, but GMM-KNN amplifies the noise by clustering
directly on voxel features.
To address these challenges, marker-based initialization was introduced to enforce structural con-
sistency, which worked well in the controlled synthetic environment but failed on the volumetric
dataset. The setting of the markers is crucial to the methods presented and requires further inves-
tigation to ensure that it enables high-quality modeling.

6
Discussion and Future Work
In each of the three chapters: Experimental Considerations, 2D Dislocation Cell Analysis and 3D
Dislocation Cell Analysis we have discussed the implications of the results individually. In this
final discussion, we will not discuss these individual results again, but try to connect them in terms
of dislocation cell modeling.
This will include a discussion followed by a presentation of the limitations and concludes with
future work in which we will also consider how we can model 4D dislocation cells.
The discussion is divided into 3 parts:
Importance of the underlying data for dislocation cell models in 2D and 3D
In the Experimental Considerations chapter we discussed the fitting of the raw DFXM data and an-
alyzed different model fitting methods. The already established Gaussian fitting approach proved
to be the best, giving a high-quality fit to the data with the computationally efficient ”1D Moment”
fitting method.
The quality of the fitted data was important for 2D and 3D modeling, with the volumetric dataset
proving difficult to model as regions of high FWHM were not inherently correlated with the bound-
aries of the COM values. This led to further investigation of data fitting during this thesis and the
eventual creation of the synthetic dataset, as we were unable to fit the volumetric dataset in a way
that allowed proper modeling of dislocation cells, especially in 3D.
Establishing a quantitative evaluation scheme to justify model changes
Throughout the work on this thesis, we did not shy away from developing new ideas and challeng-
ing existing legacy concepts for modeling dislocation cells. To quantitatively evaluate these new
concepts, we established several validation methods that were applicable to 2D and could also be
extended to 3D.
In particular, a combination of mean feature loss and average cell size allowed us to make quantita-
tive assessments on different models. This is a huge addition to the previous qualitative ”eye-tests”,
as it adds another layer of model evaluation. It needs to be said, that it can’t fully replace the qual-
itative validation though, as sometimes, like we could see with filter sizes in 2D models, we still
need to make qualitative judgments on what model is best.
Using the new validation methods, the KAM filter was analyzed and found to be slightly better than
a Gaussian derivative filter in 2D validation, even though the filter is computationally expensive.
In addition, asymmetric filters using both the FWHM and COM features were added to the filter
calculation to improve model performance.
For further research, we can use the established modifications to the 2D and 3D models, but also
the validation method itself, which can be used for further projects and with more data we will be
able to judge the model performance even better.
Balancing structural continuity and data fidelity
With the introduction of the new baseline model for 2D dislocation cell modeling, the direct thresh-
olding approach for defining cell boundaries historically used in EBSD modeling was replaced by
a skeletonization approach that opened cell boundaries from an over-threshed image.
This resulted in a trade-off that favored the smooth, well-separated cells introduced by skeletoniza-
tion, but lost direct pixel feature information (data fidelity) in the process of modeling cell walls.
This trade-off was necessary because direct thresholding focused too much on the data and did not
57
==PAGE:67==
result in identifiable dislocation cells.
The trade-off was also observed in the 3D modeling of dislocation cells, where the watershed ap-
proach, like skeletonization, leads to well-segmented cells, whereas segmentation only indirectly
uses direct voxel information. With the GMM-KNN approach, we tried to incorporate the fea-
ture information of the voxels to a greater extent, which was done by clustering with the Gaussian
mixture model. However, to obtain a connected smooth cell, we had to add a knn filter, which
compromised some of the data fidelity gained. It must be said that finding the right balance be-
tween well-defined cells and keeping cell boundaries true to the data is challenging, especially with
noisy datasets such as the volumetric dataset.
6.1
Limitations
This thesis has identified some limitations. We would like to highlight the two that are proving
to be the most influential and have the greatest impact moving the research forward. The first is
the availability and reliance on a single dataset beyond two dimensions (volumetric dataset). The
second is the novelty of the research topic, which meant that there were no complete, out-of-the-
box solutions to the modeling and data fitting problem, making the research rather dynamic.
Regarding the first limitation. In this thesis, the volumetric dataset proved to be the bottleneck
when it came to developing and evaluating models in 3D and 4D. As the dataset did not represent
the dislocation cells clearly enough, the models developed for 3D dislocation cells lack validation
on a dataset that can capture the dislocation cells in a proper way. Even with the introduction of the
synthetic dataset, we cannot be sure of our validation as the synthetic dataset is a highly simplified
model for describing dislocation cell behavior.
This does not mean that the modeling results are not relevant. With new data, the models developed
are ready to be used and evaluated. Also, the questionable data quality of the volumetric dataset led
to the questioning of the qualitative validation by ”eye-test”, which was not completely sufficient
and led to the development of quantitative validation methods.
The limitations imposed by the novelty of the research led to a large scope of the thesis, which had
to be adapted as the work progressed.
This resulted in a collection of findings, some of which require further investigation, rather than
a single, unambiguous result. This should not be seen as a disadvantage, as this first mover pain
opens the doors for further research, which can be more narrowly defined with a deeper under-
standing of the modeling problem, and will hopefully lead to the clear identification of dislocation
cells in higher dimensions.
6.2
Future Work
For future work, a new and better data set with clearer identifiable cell boundaries is essential for
modeling the dislocation cells in 3D and later 4D. Efforts to obtain such a dataset have already
been made by Adam Cretton and initial looks at the data appear promising.
With this new dataset we could run and validate our 3D models to evaluate their ability to segment
the data into dislocation cells. For this evaluation we would use both the established quantitative
methods and some qualitative ”eye-tests”.
In terms of future technical work, there are many things that could be investigated further. We
have already touched on some of this in previous chapters, like speeding up the data fitting of 2D
Gaussian fitting or analyzing the influence of the skeletonization method for 2D models.
What I will present here are the things that I think can have the biggest impact on fitting dislocation
cells in 3D and subsequently 4D and would be my next step moving forward.
After further testing of the existing models on the new dataset, I suggest to implement a multi-
class graph-cut approach for 3D segmentation of dislocation cells. This method seems promising,
58
==PAGE:68==
particularly in terms of balancing structural continuity of cell boundaries with data fidelity in seg-
mentation. There is no clear evidence or explanation confirming its effectiveness in our context,
making its performance uncertain. Despite the implementation effort required, its potential exten-
sion to 4D makes it a worthwhile direction to pursue.
Since the ultimate goal is to identify 4D dislocation cells, we will outline what such an effort entails,
including additional considerations for modeling in 4D and the suggestion of some modeling ideas.
6.2.1
Identifying 4D Dislocation cells
Future work in 4D will be presented in two parts: General considerations and modeling ideas based
on the results and knowledge gained from the analysis of the 2D and 3D chapters.
4D Considerations
When extending the KAM filter or GDF to 4D, determining the filter size for the fourth dimen-
sion becomes non-trivial for the strain/time dimension. If changes in the dislocation cell structure
evolve gradually, a larger filter dimension is appropriate; if the cell changes rapidly, meaningful
feature extraction may not be achievable even with a small filter size. In addition, we will expe-
rience similar problems with padding effects in the fourth dimension (strain/time) as in the third,
due to the small size of the dimension (7 in the volumetric dataset).
Computational constraints become even more important in 4D as shown in Figure 4.12. Full vol-
ume modeling is impractical and modeling should focus on reduced regions of interest. The size
must be carefully chosen to extract statistically meaningful results on the 4D cells.
Validation methods need to be adapted in 4D. As in 3D, extracting and evaluating 2D slices against
baseline segmentation results remains a viable approach in 4D. Other metrics such as smoothness
need to be re-evaluated due to the higher dimensionality of the cells.
Modeling Ideas
Extending marker-based segmentation methods seems to be the most promising approach for 4D,
rather than trying to connect 3D segmentations over an additional dimension, which already proved
challenging in 3D.
A first step in 4D segmentation could therefore be the GMM-KNN or Watershed approach. For
both, the extension to 4D needs to be carefully considered. This is because we need to incorporate
the 4th dimension into the models. This is not trivial as the domain of the fourth dimension is no
longer spatial.
The placement of the markers remains crucial and is even more challenging in 4D. As in 3D, seg-
mentation results were highly sensitive to the initial selection of markers. Refinement of automated
methods for selecting markers based on local feature variations will be necessary to achieve stable
results in 4D.
Although GMM-KNN and watershed provide a starting point, graph-cut segmentation could be a
valuable alternative if initial 4D clustering results remain unstable. This method, if proven suc-
cessful in 3D, may provide a better way to balance structural continuity and cell wall smoothness
with data fidelity.
The transition to 4D requires a balance between computational feasibility, segmentation accuracy
and data fidelity. Lessons learned from 3D segmentation provide a foundation, but further refine-
ment is needed to develop models for higher dimensional dislocation cells. It is hoped that the new
dataset will provide a clearer cell structure, as it is important to note that even with the best model,
the data is the critical factor in modeling cells.

Conclusion
With this work we have contributed to the development of dislocation cell modeling of DFXM
data. We have challenged established concepts and developed new ideas that can be used for
further research to understand the mechanics of dislocation cells.
To conclude this thesis, we will answer the research questions established in the introduction:
Is the current processing of DFXM data in 2D valid, and can more of the data be leveraged
to improve the resulting maps of dislocation cells?
In the Experimental Setup chapter we analyzed the data fitting and processing in great detail. We
can conclude that the processing and Gaussian fitting used in the paper [1] are valid for processing
the DFXM data, but computationally restrictive. Acceleration of these processes is possible, but
requires significant optimization and knowledge of either the darfix [23] or DARLING library. [21].
Alternative non-Gaussian fitting models exist, but are poorly suited to our data and introduce ad-
ditional computational challenges. While better feature representations seem possible, Gaussian
fitting remains the most effective and practical approach.
How can the 2D model used in [1] be validated? Are there improvements to be made to the
model? Can the model results be used as a baseline for higher dimensions?
To validate the quality of the 2D baseline model, we conducted multiple experiments with vary-
ing parameter settings. To systematically compare these results, we introduced validation metrics
that provide a quantitative assessment of model performance. These metrics complement the qual-
itative ”eye-test,” which remains essential due to the lack of available ground truth in the data,
requiring domain expertise in the material science field for effective validation.
We tested several ideas to improve the baseline model. The following three stood out. First,
the FWHM feature was added to make the model more robust. Second, asymmetric kernels were
introduced to better handle pixel asymmetry. And thirdly, alternative filtering methods were tested.
While the KAM filter provides better segmentation results, the Gaussian derivative filter is more
computationally efficient and works better for iterative modeling.
The 2D baseline model is essential for validation, as it provided the only scientifically validated re-
sults. The results provide baseline values for the introduced quantitative validation metrics, which
can be used for model validation in higher dimension, by breaking down higher-dimensional cells
into 2D and comparing them to the baseline data.
Can 3D dislocation cells be modeled and the results validated?
The research for 3D dislocation cell models was limited by the volumetric dataset which made
it difficult to identify and validate such models. Nevertheless, we developed two models that
successfully segment dislocation cells on the synthetic dataset. It needs to be determined how the
models perform on different data, as the validation on the synthetic dataset is problematic, because
the dataset might not describe the behavior of the dislocation cells realistically enough.
While we cannot definitively answer the research question yet, we have established a modeling
framework and introduced quantitative validation methods. These provide a foundation for future
validation and modeling once more representative experimental data becomes available.
