Abstract
A full­scale experimental campaign was conducted on a commercial 75 kW / 500 kWh iron
flow battery installed at the Brande Hydrogen test site in Denmark. The resulting data set,
covering 0–100% state­of­charge and ±100% rated power, structured into deep­cycle,
mid­window, and dynamic­tracking series, supplies a time­aligned basis for parameter
identification and validation.
Using these measurements, a hybrid battery model was developed in three layers. A
segmented AC/DC efficiency map captures steady losses as a function of power and
SoC; a second­order autoregressive­exogenous (ARX) kernel reproduces millisecond­
scale voltage dynamics; and a data­driven residual corrector removes systematic bias
across the operating envelope. Calibrated and verified using the testing data, the model
predicts AC power with a lowest root­mean­square error of 1.6 kW (R2 = 0.999).
The validated model was embedded in a micro­grid simulation that combines down­scaled
2020 Danish wind generation with a matching local load. A proportional­integral controller
(Kp = 0.225, Ki = 2.1 × 10−5 s−1) drives the battery to offset net fluctuations. Results
show a 96% reduction in short­term power variance and a 75% cut in ramp rate at the point
of common coupling, while keeping the battery’s state­of­charge within 5–95%. Capacity­
sizing analysis indicates that 42284 kWh is sufficient to avoid saturation under the same
wind­load scenario.
The work thus delivers an experimentally anchored model and control framework that
demonstrates the technical viability of iron­flow batteries for firming variable renewables
in Denmark’s grids.

Introduction
This chapter is grounded in the macro backdrop of the global energy transition and sys­
tematically constructs a framework for the development of sustainable power systems.
Based on an in­depth analysis of the current energy landscape, this chapter first argues for
the inevitability and urgency of deep decarbonisation of power systems. Then it focuses
on analysing the technical challenges posed by integrating high proportions of variable
renewable energy into the grid. Based on this analysis, this chapter establishes the criti­
cal role of energy storage technology, particularly long­duration energy storage systems,
in ensuring grid stability and introduces Iron Flow Batteries (IFBs) as a cutting­edge tech­
nical solution for addressing long­duration energy storage needs, detailing their unique
technical advantages compared to traditional energy storage technologies. Finally, this
chapter clearly defines the technical approach and paper structure of this study, laying a
systematic theoretical foundation for subsequent chapters and establishing clear research
boundaries.
1.1
Global Renewable Energy Transition
Over the past two decades, the global energy system has been undergoing a profound
paradigm shift. This structural transformation is driven by three key factors: the urgent
need to address climate change, strategic considerations aimed at reducing reliance on
fossil fuels, and the inherent requirement to establish a sustainable energy security sys­
tem. Against this backdrop, renewable energy sources such as wind power, solar energy,
and hydropower have evolved from supplementary energy sources to become the core
pillars of modern energy systems, thanks to significantly improved technical and economic
viability, as well as continued policy support [1]. As shown in Figure 1.1, the total installed
renewable capacity worldwide has reached more than 3000 GW in 2023.
Figure 1.1: Installed Renewable Capacity Worldwide by Technology (GW)[2]
Denmark, as a pioneer in the global energy transition, offers a valuable model for its
1
==PAGE:14==
development trajectory. By systematically advancing renewable energy grid integration
technology, optimising grid architecture design, and improving market operation mech­
anisms, the country has successfully established a power supply system dominated by
wind energy, with wind power accounting for over 50% of total power generation, as shown
in Figure 1.2. This practice not only validates the technical feasibility of high­proportion
renewable energy power systems but also provides a referenceable institutional inno­
vation model for the global energy transition. Denmark’s experience demonstrates that
achieving deep decarbonisation of the energy system requires the synergistic evolution
of technological innovation, institutional design, and market mechanisms.[3]
Figure 1.2: Electricity generation sources, Denmark, 2023[4]
As the penetration rate of variable renewable energy (VRE) continues to rise in power sys­
tems, the reliability and flexibility of system operations are facing unprecedented technical
challenges. Specifically, the inherent intermittent nature of wind and solar power gener­
ation, coupled with their strong dependence on meteorological conditions, often leads to
significant mismatches between power generation output and load demand across tem­
poral and spatial dimensions. This, in turn, triggers a series of critical issues￿including
constraints on renewable energy integration, deterioration of system frequency stability,
and reduced overall operational efficiency [5].
To address these technical bottlenecks, long­duration energy storage (LDES) technology
is emerging as a critical enabler for high­penetration renewable energy power systems.
By enabling energy decoupling between the generation side and the consumption side
across time scales ranging from hours to days or even longer, this technology significantly
enhances the power system’s dynamic regulation capabilities and operational flexibility,
providing an effective solution to the technical challenges posed by integration of renew­
able energy and the grid [6].
1.2
Energy Storage in Modern Power Systems
Energy Storage Systems (ESS) serve as a critical enabling technology for modern power
systems, playing a pivotal role in enhancing system reliability, operational efficiency, and
resilience to disturbances. Existing research indicates that ESS primarily contributes to
power system operations through four core functions: 1) optimising energy allocation
across temporal and spatial dimensions; 2) providing rapid frequency response services;
3) possessing black start capability; 4) serving as an emergency backup power source to
ensure power supply continuity. As the penetration rate of renewable energy continues
2
==PAGE:15==
to rise, the strategic value of ESS in maintaining the dynamic stability of power systems
is increasingly evident [7].
As shown in Figure 1.3, current mainstream energy storage technologies exhibit signif­
icant differences in power­duration characteristics. Among these, short­duration energy
storage technologies represented by lithium­ion batteries dominate the current energy
storage market due to their high energy density, modular advantages, and continuously
improving economic viability. However, this technology is limited by its typical discharge
duration of 2–4 hours. When demand exceeds the 4–6 hour threshold, its levelised cost
of storage (LCOS) exhibits an exponential growth trend [8], and this technical and eco­
nomic characteristic severely restricts its application in long­term system regulation and
seasonal energy storage scenarios. In contrast, long­duration energy storage technolo­
gies, including flow batteries, pumped hydro storage, and compressed air energy storage,
demonstrate unique advantages due to their scalable capacity and sustained discharge
capability. Among these technologies, IFBs are considered one of the most promising
technical routes due to their power­energy decoupling design, ultra­long cycle life, and
inherent safety characteristics [9].
Figure 1.3: Comparison of existing energy storage technologies[10]
The Danish government’s commitment to transitioning the nation’s energy infrastructure
towards an entirely fossil fuel­free power system by 2030 highlights the imperative for in­
tegrating advanced long­duration energy storage technologies.[11] Projects such as Fer­
roBAT, which is exploring commercialised iron flow battery solutions, are highly aligned
with Denmark’s national energy vision and are expected to be an important technology
underpinning the future power system.[12]
1.3
The Need for Long­Duration Energy Storage
The inherent intermittency and variability of renewable energy generation pose significant
technical challenges for achieving a fully decarbonised power system. Existing research
indicates that while short­term energy storage, such as lithium­ion batteries and flexible
power generation technologies such as gas­fired peaking units can effectively mitigate
hourly to daily supply­demand fluctuations, long­term supply­demand imbalances, for ex­
ample prolonged periods of low wind speeds or seasonal solar shortages, require long­
term energy storage technologies with the ability to transfer energy across multiple time
3
==PAGE:16==
scales[13].
This challenge is particularly pronounced in Nordic countries, such as Denmark. Statistics
show that wind power accounts for over 50% of Denmark’s annual electricity generation,
but the country frequently experiences ‘wind lulls’ lasting over 72 hours [14]. In such
extreme scenarios, traditional energy storage systems, typically with discharge durations
of less than 10 hours, struggle to maintain system stability, necessitating solutions with
weekly­scale storage capabilities. Without the necessary technological support, this leads
to a double dilemma: on one hand, it results in the forced curtailment of large amounts of
clean energy. The research shows that Denmark’s wind power curtailment rate reached
3.5% in 2021 [15]. On the other hand, fossil fuel backup units must still be retained to
ensure power supply reliability. This structural contradiction significantly hinders progress
toward achieving carbon neutrality goals.
As Figure 1.4 illustrates, LDES systems utilise energy transfer mechanisms across time
dimensions to store electricity during periods of excess renewable energy generation and
release it in a controlled manner during periods of insufficient generation. This energy
storage capability yields multiple system benefits: first, it smooths load curves through
time­domain power regulation; second, it significantly reduces renewable energy curtail­
ment rates; and finally, it effectively replaces the peak­shaving function of traditional fossil
fuel backup units [13]. From a technical and functional perspective, LDES primarily plays
three key roles in power systems: achieving cross­seasonal energy balance, alleviating
congestion in transmission and distribution networks, and providing reliable power support
for off­grid and weak grid areas.
Figure 1.4: LDES applications graphic[16]
As the proportion of weather­sensitive power sources in the power system structure con­
tinues to increase, developing long­duration energy storage technologies with commer­
cial viability, high safety, and scalable capacity has become a core requirement for the
energy transition. Against this backdrop of technological evolution, novel energy storage
systems, such as iron flow batteries, are poised to seize significant development oppor­
tunities. The current technological innovation environment not only creates conditions
4
==PAGE:17==
for performance optimisation of traditional energy storage solutions but also provides in­
dustrialisation cultivation space for emerging energy storage technologies with disruptive
potential.
1.4
The Role of Iron Flow Batteries
IFBs are a new type of redox flow battery that stores and releases energy through the re­
versible electrochemical reactions of iron­based electrolytes. Unlike traditional batteries,
flow batteries feature a unique structural design that separates the external electrolyte
storage tank from the stack, enabling decoupled optimisation of system energy capacity
and power density [17].
The modular architecture of this technology demonstrates significant advantages in long­
duration energy storage applications: first, in terms of technical and economic viability, the
independent scalability of system capacity and power significantly reduces the marginal
cost of large­scale energy storage; Second, in terms of operational safety, IFBs operate
at ambient temperatures, and their electrolyte materials are abundant and environmen­
tally friendly. More importantly, the system maintains excellent cycle stability under deep
charge­discharge conditions, with a degradation rate significantly lower than that of tra­
ditional battery systems. These technical features make IFBs particularly suitable for
grid­scale energy storage and applications requiring long­term operation.[18]
Additionally, compared to vanadium­based or lithium­based energy storage systems, iron­
based materials exhibit significant resource sustainability characteristics due to their abun­
dant reserves, which are 5.6% of the Earth’s crust, low cost, and environmental compat­
ibility. The price of iron is only one­twentieth of vanadium [19]. This material property
gives iron­based flow batteries a unique competitive edge in developing sustainable, low­
cost, large­scale energy storage systems, particularly suitable for Nordic countries like
Denmark that have set a target of 100% renewable energy generation by 2050 [20].
Currently, international research initiatives such as the FerroBAT project, supported by
the European Union (EU) innovation programme, are actively advancing the commercial­
isation of this technology. These projects are targeting three key technical challenges:
1) enhancing energy density to over 40 Wh/L through the development of new chelating
agents; 2) reducing system costs to below $100/kWh via modular design; and 3) vali­
dating long­term cycling performance through MW­scale demonstration plants. Empirical
studies indicate that integrating such systems into existing power grids not only verifies
their technical feasibility but also provides critical data support for assessing the economic
viability of their large­scale application..[12]
1.5
Thesis Objectives and Structure
This study focuses on the integration of IFBs systems into high­proportion renewable
energy power systems, with a particular emphasis on assessing their technical feasibility
as LDES solutions. Based on real­world data from a commercialised 75 kW/500 kWh
system, the study constructed a dynamic battery model and conducted multi­scenario
validation to evaluate the technical performance of IFBs systematically.
The research objectives encompass the following dimensions:
1. Clarifying the strategic positioning of LDES systems in the context of global energy
transition;
2. Analysing the technical principles, competitive advantages, and current state of in­
dustrialisation of IFBs;
5
==PAGE:18==
3. Establishing a dynamic simulation model of IFB based on multi­condition experi­
mental data;
4. Validating the model’s predictive capability for Alternating Current (AC) power dy­
namic characteristics using an independent dataset;
5. Evaluating the power regulation effectiveness of IFB in simplified grids and the effi­
cacy of Proportional­Integral (PI) control strategies;
6. Conducting capacity optimisation design to ensure reliable system operation under
power fluctuations.
The paper adopts a progressive structural organisation:
• Chapter 2 systematically reviews the current status of energy storage technology
development, with a focus on the technical principles of IFBs and the progress of
the FerroBAT project;
• Chapter 3 details the experimental platform construction, data collection, and pre­
processing methods;
• Chapter 4 comprehensively explains the hybrid modelling method, incorporating ef­
ficiency maps, autoregressive with exogenous inputs (ARX) structures, and residual
corrections;
• Chapter 5 quantitatively evaluates model accuracy through independent validation;
• Chapter 6 conducts grid integration simulations to analyse control strategies and
capacity configurations;
• Chapter 7 summarises research findings, discusses technical limitations, and out­
lines future directions.
This research method, which progresses from theoretical foundations to empirical stud­
ies and engineering applications, provides a systematic methodological framework for
assessing the applicability of IFB in new power systems.

2
Theoretical Background
This chapter provides a comprehensive review of various energy storage technologies,
categorising them based on their operational principles and applications within modern
power systems. A particular emphasis is placed on LDES solutions, highlighting their in­
creasing relevance in grids with high penetrations of renewable energy. Subsequently,
the chapter delves into the fundamental working principles of IFBs, detailing their electro­
chemical reactions, unique advantages, and current commercialisation efforts, including
an overview of the FerroBAT project.
2.1
Overview of Energy Storage Technologies
Energy storage technology serves as a critical enabling element for modern power sys­
tems and plays an indispensable role in the transition to renewable energy. In response
to the inherent intermittent and non­dispatchable characteristics of renewable energy
sources such as wind and solar power, energy storage systems provide a core techni­
cal solution for ensuring grid stability through their ability to balance supply and demand
across multiple time scales from second­level responses to seasonal regulation.
From a technical principle perspective, mainstream energy storage technologies can be
categorised into four major systems: 1) electrochemical storage (represented by vari­
ous types of batteries); 2) mechanical storage (including pumped­storage hydroelectricity
and flywheel energy storage); 3) thermal storage, such as molten salt thermal storage
and phase­change ice storage; 4) chemical energy storage, including hydrogen storage,
power­to­gas, etc. [21] [8]. Among these, electrochemical ESS have demonstrated the
most significant technological development trends due to their excellent scalability, mod­
ular characteristics, and continuously improving economic viability.
Batteries are highly versatile. Both utility­scale and behind­the­meter battery storage can
provide a wide range of services to electricity systems, as shown in Figure 2.1. In addition
to energy shifting, which helps balance electricity supply and demand, utility­scale battery
storage can contribute to maintaining grid stability and ensuring the security of supply
by providing ancillary services such as inertia, voltage control, frequency regulation, grid
forming, and delivering fast­starting reserves. It can also supply capacity to ensure system
adequacy and help manage congestion in electricity networks. Behind­the­meter batteries
can provide backup power and help consumers lower their electricity bills by allowing
them to increase the consumption of self­generated electricity, take advantage of variable
electricity tariffs or reduce peak electricity consumption from the grid. If aggregated into
virtual power plants (VPPS), they can also provide many of the same services as larger
scale utility­scale systems [22].
In the field of electrochemical energy storage, lithium­ion batteries (LIBs) have become
the dominant solution for short­term energy storage, due to their high round­trip (RT) ef­
ficiency, which is approximately 90%, compact system design, and mature supply chain
advantages. However, this technology still faces significant technical challenges in terms
of cost structure optimisation, inherent safety improvements, and cycle life under deep
charge­discharge conditions [23]. In contrast, alternative electrochemical systems, such
as sodium­sulfur batteries, zinc­air batteries, and vanadium redox flow batteries (LIBs),
demonstrate differentiated competitive advantages in terms of cost­effectiveness, safety
performance, and system scalability [24]. In particular, flow battery technology, with its

unique power­energy decoupling characteristics, demonstrates outstanding technical ap­
plicability in applications requiring prolonged continuous discharge.
The current analysis indicates that for the specific application scenario of LDES that re­
quire discharging for 8–100 hours or more, several emerging technology systems have
demonstrated significant potential to overcome the limitations of lithium­ion battery tech­
nology. These include pumped hydro energy storage (PHES), compressed air energy
storage (CAES), hydrogen­based energy storage systems, and IFBs, which is the focus
of this study [25] [26].
These technological solutions exhibit differentiated characteristics across various dimen­
sions, including technical maturity, cost, environmental friendliness, and grid compatibility.
While PHES possesses the advantage of commercial maturity, it is constrained by spe­
cific geographical conditions. Hydrogen systems can achieve seasonal energy storage
but face bottlenecks in energy conversion efficiency. In contrast, iron­based flow battery
technology achieves an optimal balance between system scalability, inherent safety, and
lifecycle costs [27]. The research findings indicate that while lithium­ion batteries will con­
tinue to dominate in mobile energy storage and short­term frequency regulation applica­
tions, in power systems with high renewable energy integration, such as Denmark’s grid,
LDES technologies represented by iron flow batteries will emerge as a strategic techni­
cal choice to ensure system resilience, enhance operational efficiency, and achieve deep
decarbonisation goals [28].
2.2
Iron Flow Battery Technology
2.2.1
Electrochemical Reactions
IFBs are one kind of redox flow battery (RFB), storing energy in liquid electrolytes contain­
ing redox­active species. These electrolytes are stored in external tanks and circulated
through electrochemical cells during charge and discharge cycles [9].
The electrochemistry of IFBs typically involves two redox couples of iron: Fe2+/Fe3+ on
the Cathode, and Fe2+/Fe (solid) on the Anode. As Figure 2.2 shows, the reactions are
reversible and proceed as follows[29]:

Figure 2.2: Iron Flow Battery Electrochemical Reactions[30]
During charging, Fe2+ ions in the catholyte are oxidised to Fe3+, while Fe2+ in the anolyte
is reduced to solid iron, which is deposited on the electrode surface. During discharging,
these reactions reverse: solid iron is oxidised back to Fe2+ and Fe3+ is reduced to Fe2+,
releasing electrical energy.
The standard electrode potentials for the Fe3+/Fe2+ and Fe2+/Fe couples are approxi­
mately +0.77 V and –0.44 V vs. SHE, respectively. This gives a theoretical cell voltage
of approximately 1.21 V, which is comparable to that of vanadium flow batteries and suf­
ficient for practical applications [29].
Although the single­cell voltage of 1.21 V may appear modest compared to lithium­ion
cells, which typically range from 3.6 to 4.2 V, flow batteries are generally assembled
into large stacks containing tens or hundreds of cells in series. It is straightforward to
scale the output voltage to match system­level requirements, such as 48 V, 400 V, or
even megawatt­scale Direct Current (DC) bus voltages in grid­connected storage sys­
tems[29][9].
2.2.2
Stack and System Architecture
The architecture of an IFBs system is designed to separate the functions of energy stor­
age and power delivery physically. This modularity is a defining feature of all redox flow
batteries, and is achieved by decoupling energy capacity from power capacity.
As Figure 2.3 shows, the following components are typically incorporated within an IFB
system:
9
==PAGE:22==
Figure 2.3: Architecture of an Iron Flow Battery System[31]
1. Electrolyte Tanks: Two external tanks are employed for the storage of the anolyte and
catholyte solutions. The volume of these systems directly corresponds to the total energy
capacity of the system, which is measured in kWh or MWh.
2. Pump and Flow System: Electrolytes are circulated through the cell stack using pumps,
ensuring consistent reaction rates and uniform ion distribution.
3. The core unit, in which oxidation and reduction reactions occur, consists of multiple
stacked cells. Each cell contains two electrodes separated by an ion­selective membrane.
4. Power Electronics and Control Unit: The system incorporates DC­DC converters, bat­
tery management systems (BMS), sensors and real­time controllers. The purpose of this
combination of components is to ensure safe and efficient operation.
The modular cell stack is typically constructed from repeating units that incorporate graphite
felt electrodes, ion­exchange membranes, such as Nafion or more economical alterna­
tives, and flow channels. The number of cells in a stack is indicative of the operating
voltage range, which is typically 10–100V for lab­scale systems and hundreds of volts for
commercial units.
In IFBs systems, the flow characteristics of the electrolyte and the flow field configuration
have a decisive impact on overall performance. Research indicates that an optimised flow
field design can achieve uniform distribution of the electrolyte, which not only effectively
reduces system operating pressure drop and electrolyte concentration gradients but also
avoids critical issues such as local drying of the proton exchange membrane or excessive
saturation of electrode pores. Currently, multi­physics field coupling modelling based on
computational fluid dynamics (CFD) has become the primary research method for opti­
mising flow field parameters, as it can accurately simulate the coupled processes of mass
transfer and electrochemical reactions in complex flow channel structures [33].

Compared to traditional flow battery systems, such as vanadium flow batteries, IFBs ex­
hibit unique electrochemical behaviour characteristics, including the deposition and disso­
lution reaction of metallic iron on the anode side. This reaction mechanism requires pre­
cise control of operational parameters: first, the optimal selection of current density can
effectively suppress dendrite growth; second, strict control of electrolyte pH can prevent
electrode passivation; additionally, dynamic regulation of flow rate ensures the stability
of the reaction interface [19]. The synergistic optimisation of these parameters is a key
factor in ensuring the long­term cycling performance of IFBs.
From a systems engineering perspective, IFB’s modular design offers significant advan­
tages for commercial applications. Specifically, this technology decouples power units
from energy units, enabling flexible system capacity configuration: energy capacity can
be linearly scaled by increasing tank volume. In contrast, power output is proportional
to the active area of the stack. This unique scalability enables the IFB system to adapt
to diverse application scenarios from grid­scale large­scale energy storage to microgrid
applications in remote areas, without altering the core electrochemical system. More im­
portantly, this design philosophy significantly reduces marginal costs during large­scale
deployment, providing a structural foundation for enhancing the economic viability of IFB.
2.2.3
Advantages and Limitations
IFBs have emerged as one of the most promising technical routes in the field of LDES
due to their unique technical, economic, and environmental synergistic advantages. Com­
pared to traditional electrochemical energy storage systems, IFBs address the three core
challenges posed by integrating high proportions of renewable energy into the grid through
their distinctive technical characteristics, including power­energy decoupling design, in­
herent safety, and excellent cycle stability [19]. These challenges include energy transfer
across time scales, enhancing grid flexibility, and meeting environmental sustainability
requirements throughout the entire lifecycle.
A notable benefit of IFBs is their decoupled power and energy design. System integrators
can achieve economic optimisation in the design of integrated frequency control systems
by independently sizing tanks for energy capacity and stacks for power output. This ap­
proach enables the system integrators to tailor IFBs to meet diverse use cases, ranging
11
==PAGE:24==
from diurnal balancing to multi­day backup. Moreover, the utilisation of non­toxic, earth­
abundant iron salts leads to a substantial reduction in material costs and environmental
concerns, thereby distinguishing them from vanadium and lithium­based systems. [34]
From an operational perspective, IFB batteries exhibit extremely high tolerance to deep
charge­discharge cycles, with minimal capacity degradation, making them particularly
suitable for applications that require frequent cycling. The currently considered water
electrolyte formulation offers inherent safety advantages, including low flammability and
thermal stability. In terms of lifespan, iron flow batteries have a longer life, spanning 20
years, compared to the 7­ to 10­year life of a heavily cycled Li­ion battery, thereby achiev­
ing a lower LCOS over the system’s lifecycle [35].
However, there are also several technical and economic challenges to IFB. The most
significant among these challenges is the relatively low energy density, which results in
larger system footprints. Furthermore, it is crucial to exercise meticulous system engi­
neering and electrolyte management to prevent the occurrence of electrochemical side
reactions, such as hydrogen evolution at the negative electrode, and mitigate the risk of
iron dendrite formation [36].

Experimental Data and Platform
This chapter introduces the experimental platform and dataset that form the empirical
foundation of the modelling and simulation work in this thesis. The data were obtained
from a commercial iron flow battery system deployed and operated at the Brande Hy­
drogen Test Site in Denmark, as part of the FerroBAT project. While the project team
established the test platform and procedures, this study utilises the logged data to ana­
lyze energy conversion efficiency, operational behaviour, and dynamic response under
various test conditions. A detailed description of the testbed, instrumentation, operating
protocols, and data characteristics is provided to ensure transparency and reproducibility
of the subsequent modelling process.
3.1
Test Platform and Setup
As shown in Figure 3.1, all experiments were executed at the Brande Hydrogen test site
in Western Jutland, Denmark. The core asset is an ESS Inc. 40­ft containerised Energy
Warehouse iron­flow battery, rated at 75 kW / 500 kWh with a 400 V AC interface. Elec­
trolyte tanks, cell stacks, power electronics and thermal subsystems are integrated into a
single cabinet, enabling stand­alone operation and data logging.
To mitigate catastrophic spill risk, the unit is situated on a 12 m³ containment basin; a
replacement container was recommissioned in autumn 2024 following transport damage
to the initial delivery. Control and logging rely on the vendor HMI over a Modbus­TCP
bus. Standard channels are sampled every 1 second, while dynamic campaigns retain
the same interface but increase the sampling rate of key electrical quantities to 100 ms.
The logged variables comprise DC power, AC power, system power, auxiliary power, SoC,
tank temperature, container temperature, Power Electronics Cabinet (PEC) temperature
and other auxiliary metrics, a total of eleven key channels that support later efficiency,
thermal and dynamic modelling. During fast­transient tests, electrical feeds are mirrored
to Elspec power­quality meters on both AC and DC buses, ensuring synchronous RMS
and harmonic data with a 100 ms resolution. Safety procedures prohibit grid connection
below 40 °C electrolyte temperature. If required, an external 5 kW heater brings the
system to the operating range, and the associated energy draw is fully logged for later
balance calculations.

3.2
Experimental Conditions and Test Matrix
To furnish the modelling work with a data set that spans the entire operating envelope of
the 75 kW / 500 kWh iron flow battery, two complementary suites of tests were devised
15
==PAGE:28==
and executed at the Brande Hydrogen site. The first suite addresses steady­state cy­
cling behaviour under controlled charge and discharge regimes, while the second probes
the system’s ability to track rapid power commands representative of grid­service duty.
Both suites employ the instrumentation architecture described in Section 3.1, retaining
the Modbus­TCP backbone and recording electrical signals to Elspec meters; routine
channels are sampled at 1 s, whereas fast transients were recorded at 100 ms for full
waveform reconstruction. Eleven principal variables—including DC and AC power, total
system power, auxiliary demand, SoC, and six temperature points are logged continu­
ously. Every file is time­stamped in UTC with SHA­256 checksums to ensure traceability.
The steady­state suite consists of three cycling families, designed to expose efficiency
and capacity variations across distinct SoC windows. Full­range cycles begin at 0% SoC,
charge to 100%, and return to full discharge at rated power, modelling deep­storage duty
and delivering baseline energy conversion metrics. Medium­window cycles confine op­
eration to 20 – 80% SoC, charging at half­rated power and discharging at either rated
power or half­rated power. Each profile was launched only after the electrolyte has sta­
bilised above 50 °C; inter­cycle rest periods of 30 min dissipate residual heat. If SoC
trespass 5% or 98%, or electrolyte temperature exceeds 65 °C, the supervisory controller
halts the test and enforces a controlled standby routine.
Dynamic trials were organised around two scripted power­reference patterns that comply
with the Danish grid code TF 3.3.1 requirements for storage resources. In the one­minute
staircase sequence, the set­point steped every 60 s through +90, +75, +50, +25, +5,
−5, −25, −50, −75 and −90 kW at three 80%, 50%, 20% SoC status, thereby subject­
ing the inverter and stack to large bidirectional excursions and providing high­resolution
data for latency analysis. The five­second burst sequence utilises the same SoC levels
but alternates every 5 seconds among ±75, 50, 25, and 5 kW values. Values below 5
kW are avoided because the battery control logic enters standby below that threshold.
Both patterns were executed with 100 ms logging of RMS and harmonic content on AC
and DC buses, and each setpoint transition was flagged in the event channel to facilitate
subsequent correlation with response metrics.
Collectively, the cycling and dynamic programmes yield a cohesive matrix of operating
points that captures efficiency, auxiliary demand, thermal drift, latency and stability over
the full SoC spectrum, under both slow and fast power variations. The resulting data sets
underpin the model design workflow in Chapter 4 and constitute the empirical foundation
for the control­oriented analyses presented later in this thesis.

3.4
Operational Characteristics and Issue Analysis
3.4.1
Optimal Power Profiles and Thermodynamic Considerations
Among the tested cycling strategies, the profile involving charging at half the rated power
and discharging at rated power resulted in the most favourable performance in terms of
round­trip efficiency and delivered energy capacity. This observation is thermodynami­
cally consistent with the redox behaviour of the all iron system: the charging half­reaction
is exothermic, whereas the discharge half­reaction is nearly thermally neutral.
18
==PAGE:31==
As such, applying half­rated power during charging helps mitigate excessive thermal
buildup and maintains the electrolyte near its optimal reaction temperature, which is above
50◦C. Conversely, discharge at half­rated power leads to marginally lower efficiency, likely
due to insufficient heat retention in the electrolyte, which causes a deviation from the op­
timal temperature range. Charging at rated power or a too low power level will lead to
suboptimal performance due to overheating and underheating, respectively.
3.4.2
Efficiency Deviations from Nominal Specifications
For the P/2 charge and P discharge profile within 20–80% SoC window, the maximum
usable energy output was measured at 414 kWh, considerably below the nominal 500
kWh value provided by the manufacturer. The maximum system­level round­trip efficiency
was 42.3%, which is significantly lower than the expected nominal range (typically above
70%).
A breakdown of the energy losses across the system revealed the following average con­
tributions:
• 9.7% losses from DC­AC conversion;
• 14.5% from auxiliary consumption;
• 33.5% electrochemical losses.
While inverter losses remained relatively stable across all tested cycles, stack and aux­
iliary losses varied substantially depending on the cycling parameters. RT DC efficiency
ranged from 59% to 62%, and auxiliary losses spanned from 14% up to 27%, particularly
during low power or idle phases.
3.4.3
Power Delivery Limitations at Low SoC
In the profile extending across the full SoC range, the system exhibited an even lower
overall efficiency of 34.35%. This was primarily due to its inability to sustain rated output
power below approximately 33% SoC. In particular, when SoC dropped below 10%, the
system output fell rapidly to below 20 kW, and the auxiliary power demand remained
relatively high, increasing the proportional losses.
For reference:
• SoC decreased from 6.5% to 3.5% over a span of approximately 2.5 hours;
• From 3.5% to 1.8%, the system took an additional 9 hours, indicating extremely low
discharge rates.
This suggests that low SoC operation should be avoided in energy dispatch planning
unless absolutely necessary.
In summary, the operational analysis highlights several critical aspects of the IFBs that di­
rectly influence its practical deployment and modelling accuracy. Optimal RT efficiency is
achieved under moderate charging and rated­power discharging within the 20–80% SoC
range, while performance degrades significantly near the SoC limits. Auxiliary power con­
sumption introduces non­negligible energy losses, particularly under low­power or SoC
conditions. These findings inform the model architecture and assumptions introduced in
the next chapter, particularly concerning efficiency segmentation and SoC constraints.

4
Battery modelling
4.1
Overview
This chapter establishes a dynamic modelling framework grounded in experimental data.
The model is designed to capture both long­term energy behaviour and short­term power
response, while maintaining interpretability and computational efficiency. Instead of re­
lying on purely electrochemical principles or black­box machine learning, the approach
combines a segmented steady­state efficiency model with a data­driven dynamic predic­
tion structure based on ARX modelling.
The modelling process begins with the definition of key input and output variables￿includ­
ing power setpoints, AC/DC power, auxiliary consumption, SoC, and their interrelations
under charging and discharging modes. A segmented energy efficiency model is then
constructed, assigning distinct charge and discharge efficiencies to discrete SoC inter­
vals. These segment values are optimised through a simulation­in­the­loop (SITL) pro­
cess that compares simulated and measured SoC trajectories. This enables the model to
accurately reflect the SoC­dependent energy conversion behaviour, including asymmetric
losses and nonlinear efficiency trends observed at both low and high SoC levels.
To simulate system response to real­time power commands, an ARX model is trained on
experimental data. It uses historical power and SoC data, along with their nonlinear inter­
actions, to predict AC power output. The model is embedded in a closed­loop framework,
where predicted AC power is converted to DC power via the efficiency model, used to up­
date SoC, and then fed back into the next prediction step. This loop ensures consistency
between the power and energy domains, allowing the model to operate autonomously
without external SoC input.
Recognising the limitations of linear ARX structures in capturing certain nonlinear tran­
sients, a residual correction mechanism is introduced. By learning systematic discrepan­
cies between ARX predictions and real measurements, this component provides refined
corrections based on recent input­output history. The final model thus achieves a balance
between accuracy, physical realism, and structural clarity.
The remainder of this chapter details the implementation of each modelling layer. Section
4.2 defines the model structure and signal flow; Section 4.3 presents the development and
optimisation of the segmented efficiency map; and Section 4.4 describes the ARX model,
residual correction design, and the integration of all components into a feedback­coupled
simulation framework.
4.2
System Architecture and Input­Output Definition
The IFBs under investigation is equipped with power electronics and internal sensors to
deliver AC power according to externally provided power setpoints. Internally, the system
consists of a DC energy storage stack, DC/AC inverters, and auxiliary systems for fluid
circulation, temperature management, and safety. The model’s objective is to delineate
the relationship between the power command (setpoint) and the observable outputs of
the system, particularly AC power, system power, and SoC.

4.3.1
Rationale and Methodology
The development of a segmented efficiency model has enabled the characterisation of
the steady­state energy behaviour of battery systems. The efficiency model under dis­
cussion is predicated on efficiency calculations and supports the observation of internal
DC power and auxiliary loads corresponding to the observed AC power output. In con­
trast to the use of a single fixed efficiency, this model employs empirical efficiency curves
derived from experimental data across multiple SoC intervals. These intervals reflect the
nonlinear characteristics of internal electrochemical behaviour, particularly those related
to iron redox reactions and thermal side effects.
This approach provides a more accurate approximation of actual system performance, es­
pecially in conditions where electrolyte imbalance or bypass reactions cause deviations
from ideal behaviour. The efficiency model captures the following characteristics: The
charging efficiency is high at low SOC, while a substantial decline in charging efficiency
is observed from a high SoC to a point approaching 100% SOC. Stable discharge effi­
ciency is maintained at elevated levels of SOC, while a decline in efficiency is observed
at reduced levels of SOC.

4.4
Dynamic ARX Modelling Based on Experimental Data
To further capture the time­dependent behaviour of the IFBs, a dynamic ARX model
was constructed [37]. While the static segmented efficiency model accounts for SoC­
dependent round­trip efficiency, it cannot predict transient system response under fluc­
tuating power commands. Therefore, a second­order ARX structure was adopted and
embedded within the battery loop to simulate the AC power trajectory and its feedback on
SoC evolution.
As shown in Figure 4.1, the simulation loop enables autonomous prediction of system dy­
namics by feeding SoC evolution back into the ARX predictor. This hybrid framework of­
fers dual benefits: it captures historical temporal dynamics and integrates SoC­dependent
nonlinear constraints, forming a coherent dynamic model.
4.4.1
ARX Model Structure and Simulation Framework Design
The goal of this section is to describe how the ARX model structure is developed, how
its parameters are identified, and how it is integrated into a closed­loop simulation frame­
work that reflects real­time battery behaviour based solely on control inputs and battery
parameters.
The AC power output at time t, denoted as PAC(t), is modelled as a linear function of pre­
vious AC power values, input power setpoints, and battery SoC variables. The functional
form adopted in this work is given by:
PAC(t) = a1PAC(t−1) + a2PAC(t−2) + b1u(t−1) + b2u2(t−1)
+ c1SoC(t−1) + c2SoC2(t−1) + d1SoC(t−2) + d2SoC2(t−2) + e
(4.4)
Here, coefficients ai, bi, ci, di and the bias term e are estimated from experimental data us­
ing multivariate linear regression. This structure incorporates memory through lag terms
and nonlinear behaviour through squared terms while remaining linear­in­parameters, en­
abling high interpretability and computational efficiency for control­oriented applications.
Unlike black­box approaches such as neural networks, the ARX model retains a clear
physical meaning for each input term. In this framework, the AC power output depends
directly on recent system states and inputs, making it suitable for simulation­driven control
design and validation.

Two­Pass Training Procedure
To train the ARX model, a two­step process is used:
1. Step 1: A dummy ARX model with zero­initialised weights is used to generate an
initial SoC trajectory from input setpoints and known initial SoC.
2. Step 2: A multivariate linear regression is applied using the measured PAC(t) as
the output and a feature matrix composed of lagged values of PAC, u, and simulated
SoC as inputs.
This ensures that the learned model parameters are entirely consistent with the simulated
battery state evolution and not reliant on external SoC measurements.
Modularity and Automation
The model is implemented in Python as a reusable class. It reads experimental datasets
at selected SoC levels, for example 20%, 50%, 80%, performs SoC reconstruction, ex­
tracts features, fits the ARX coefficients, and validates model performance PAC plots. This
modular structure allows:
• Easy retraining on new datasets or SoC regimes;
• Integration with higher­level dispatch models and controllers;
• Flexible simulation of transient system behaviour under control.
The ARX­based closed­loop model achieves a balance between simplicity, interpretabil­
ity, and predictive power. Its integration with SoC­segmented efficiency maps and self­
updating energy logic enables robust forecasting of battery behaviour, laying a foundation
for hybrid modelling and real­time deployment in grid­integrated storage systems.

QQ Plot Analysis of ARX Model Residuals
The QQ plot of the residuals from the main ARX model illustrates the extent to which

nonlinear relationships within the system have not been fully captured by the model.
• At 20% SoC, the residual distribution exhibits a pronounced ‘S’ shape, with data
points deviating more significantly from the theoretical straight line in the negative
value region. This may indicate that the model systematically overestimates power
output at low SoC conditions or has limitations in handling negative deviations. Such
deviations typically stem from nonlinear enhancements in battery electrochemical
behaviour or significant changes in internal impedance at low SoC, which suggests
that the linear ARX model does not adequately characterise.
• At 50% SoC, the ‘S’­shaped feature in the QQ plot becomes more pronounced,
with discernible curvature at both ends of the distribution. This curvature indicates
that the kurtosis of the residual distribution differs from that of a normal distribution,
meaning the frequency of extreme errors does not align with the predictions of a nor­
mal distribution. This further indicates that the residuals are not purely Gaussian and
may contain systematic patterns or more complex nonlinear dynamics that cannot
be fully captured by a linear ARX model, even when including a squared term. For
example, within the moderate SoC range, battery charge and discharge efficiency
may be subtly influenced by the complex interactions of various electrochemical and
control factors.
• At 80% SoC, although the centre of the residual distribution appears closer to zero
and data points are relatively closer to the theoretical line, its tails still show de­
viations from the normal distribution. This indicates that even at high SoC condi­
tions, the frequency of extreme prediction errors remains higher than expected from
a purely random normal distribution, suggesting the possibility for improvement in
modelling some fine dynamic characteristics when the battery is near full charge.

• PAC_sim(t −1): The simulated AC power output from the main ARX model at the
previous time step; this variable captures the current prediction trend of the main

• Pset(t −1): The power setpoint at the previous time step; this input is used to reflect

• SoCsim(t −2): The simulated SoC from two steps prior; this lagged term is intro­

the residuals.

designed to weigh and combine these input features concisely and efficiently to predict

Residual(t) = k1PAC_sim(t −1) + k2Pset(t −1) + k3SoCsim(t −1) + k4SoCsim(t −2) + k0
Where ki represents the model coefficients and k0 is the intercept term. These parameters

minimise the MSE between the actual residuals and the predicted residuals. Similar to
the training strategy for the main ARX model, the parameter optimisation for the residual
model is performed independently for each SoC level. This enables the residual model to
more accurately capture error characteristics specific to certain SoC conditions, thereby
laying the foundation for improving the performance of the final model.
The final predicted AC power (PAC_final) is then obtained by superimposing the prediction
from the main ARX model with the prediction from the residual model. This superposition
method directly corrects the original prediction errors, thereby enhancing overall predic­

PAC_final(t) = PAC_sim(t) + Residual(t)

The paramount objective of the IFBs within this grid application framework is to signif­

Pbatt,setpoint = −Pnet
(6.1)
This formulation intrinsically ensures that the battery actively counteracts the fluctuations
observed in Pnet, with the overarching aim of achieving a stable and balanced net power
exchange with the main grid infrastructure.
6.1.3
PI Controller for Power Tracking
The precise tracking of the calculated Pbatt, set point is crucial for achieving effective grid
stabilisation. Given the inherent dynamic characteristics and non­linearities intrinsic to
battery systems, a rudimentary open­loop control mechanism would prove insufficient.
Consequently, a PI controller was introduced to minimise the observed error between the
desired battery power setpoint and the actual simulated battery power output.
Principle of PI Control
A PI controller constitutes a fundamental feedback control loop mechanism extensively
employed within industrial control systems. It computes an error value, which is defined
as the algebraic difference between a measured process variable, in this context, which
are the actual battery power, Pbatt, sim) and a predetermined desired setpoint (Pbatt, setpoint.
The controller endeavours to minimise this error by systematically adjusting the control
output (Pbatt, cmd).

==PAGE:61==
The Proportional term in the controller’s algorithm directly accounts for the immediate
error, while the Integral term systematically addresses the cumulative effect of past errors
over time. The operational output of a PI controller is mathematically expressed as [39]:
u(t) = Kp · e(t) + Ki ·

(6.2)
Where:
• u(t) represents the controller’s output, which corresponds to the command signal
transmitted to the battery, designated as Pbatt,cmd.
• e(t) denotes the error, precisely defined as:
e(t) = Pbatt,setpoint(t) −Pbatt,sim(t)
(6.3)
• Kp signifies the proportional gain. A higher value for Kp correlates with a more vig­
orous response to the current error, potentially leading to an accelerated response
time but concurrently increasing the propensity for oscillations if its magnitude is
excessive.
• Ki refers to the integral gain. A greater value for Ki implies a more decisive cor­
rective action against accumulated errors, which is beneficial for the elimination of
steady­state errors. However, an excessively large Ki can also contribute to system
instability or pronounced overshoot.
PI Controller Implementation in Simulation
Within the simulation program, the PI controller operates iteratively within each discrete
time step. The instantaneous error is computed at every step, and the resultant propor­
tional and integral terms contribute synergistically to the battery control command signal,
which is subsequently fed as an input to the battery model. This battery model then com­
putationally predicts the AC power output, designated as the simulated battery power.
Subsequently, this simulated battery power value is meticulously fed back to the PI con­
troller in the ensuing time step to facilitate the calculation of the new error. This inherent
closed­loop feedback mechanism thus empowers the battery to dynamically adjust its
power output, ensuring precise tracking of the desired setpoint.
The precise implementation of this mechanism within the simulation loop is delineated as
follows:
1. Calculate the error: The current net power minus the previous simulated battery
power output. The current net power serves as the setpoint for the battery, and
the previous simulated battery power output represents the actual output from the
preceding time step.
2. Update the integral term: The integral error term is incremented by the product of
the current error and the time step.
3. Compute the command: The control command is derived from the product of the
proportional gain and the error, summed with the product of the integral gain and
the integral error term.
4. Constrain the command: The computed command is limited within the physical
power bounds of the battery.
49
==PAGE:62==
5. Transmit the command: The constrained command is transmitted to the battery
model to obtain the simulated battery power output.
6. Update the SOC: The new battery energy level is computed based on the power
exchange and efficiency model.
PI controller plays a crucial role in system power tracking control, ensuring that the output
of energy storage systems accurately matches grid stabilisation requirements by eliminat­
ing power deviations in real­time. Without this control strategy, the battery power output
will deviate significantly, resulting in continuous fluctuations in grid­connected power and
severely affecting system regulation.

PI Controller Parameter Tuning
The optimisation of the PI controller parameters constitutes a fundamental prerequisite
for achieving robust and accurate power tracking while concurrently preventing system
instability. A purpose­built tuning program has been devised to automatically determine
the optimal values for these gains by minimising the RMSE between the battery power
setpoint and the simulated battery power output.
6.2.1
Optimisation Methodology
The tuning program employs a global optimisation algorithm for the exhaustive search
of optimal proportional and integral gain values. This particular method is preferentially
selected over conventional local optimisers, such as Nelder-Mead, owing to its inherent
robustness in identifying global minima within potentially complex and non­convex search
spaces.
The objective function for this optimisation process entails executing a complete simu­
lation of the grid application for a given set of PI parameters, subsequently yielding the
RMSE of the power tracking performance.
The search bounds allocated for the parameters have been chosen using the following
method: the proportional gain is permitted a broader range from 0 to 2.0, whereas the
integral gain is confined to a smaller interval from 0 to 0.01. This constraint imposed
on the integral gain is critically essential for preventing system instability, mainly when
operating with a comparatively large simulation time step, as an unduly high integral gain
can predictably lead to pronounced overshooting and oscillatory behaviour.

Battery Capacity Requirement Analysis
The precise calibration of a battery system’s energy capacity is a critical technical pa­
rameter for ensuring the continuous and stable operation of grid­scale energy storage.
Based on the initial capacity parameters determined in Chapter 4, validation must be
conducted through charge­discharge cycle tests spanning extended time scales, with test

6.3.1
Methodology for Capacity Calculation
The capacity calculation program performs a simulation where the battery’s energy state,
measured in kilowatt­hours (kWh), is tracked in response to the net power demand. This
approach, distinct from directly simulating SoC limits, allows for the identification of the
full cumulative energy throughput required by the given power profile. This is achieved
without being limited by the battery’s physical capacity or operational boundaries during
the tracking process.
The key steps in this calculation process are outlined below:
1. Run Energy Tracking Simulation: The battery model is simulated over the entire
dataset. During this process, the cumulative energy charged or discharged from
the battery is tracked. This effectively measures the relative change in the battery’s
energy state as it responds to the power profile, independent of its initial capacity
setting.
2. Determine Required Usable Capacity: From the simulated energy state, the min­
imum and maximum relative energy levels reached are recorded. The difference
between these two values represents the total usable capacity that the battery must
be able to cycle through. This is the energy swing required by the power profile.
3. Relate Usable Capacity to Total Nominal Capacity: The usable capacity deter­
mined in the previous step must fit within the battery’s operational SoC window. For
example, to operate between 5% and 95% SoC, the usable range is 90% of the
battery’s total nominal capacity.
4. Calculate Estimated Total Nominal Capacity: The estimated total nominal bat­
tery capacity is then calculated by dividing the required usable capacity by the op­
erational SoC window to get the theoretical 100% capacity needed.
This capacity optimisation method uses a systematic design process to ensure that the
energy storage system has sufficient energy throughput capacity to effectively smooth
out power fluctuations in the grid while avoiding unsafe conditions such as overcharg­
ing or over­discharging of batteries, thereby ensuring long­term operational reliability and
maintaining optimal performance.

Conclusion
This chapter successfully integrates the dynamic model of iron­based flow batteries into
simplified grid scenarios. By using proportionally adjusted wind power and load mea­

of key technical aspects in grid applications, including a battery setpoint algorithm based
on power balance and a PI control strategy to ensure precise power tracking.

with the synergistic effect of proportional and integral gains enabling precise tracking of
fluctuating power commands. The power tracking RMSE was controlled at a low level of
1.29 kW. This high­precision control provides a critical assurance for stable grid operation.
Capacity analysis results indicate that a 42.3 MWh energy storage capacity configuration
can ensure that the SoC remains within the safe operating range of 5%–95%. This finding
highlights the deeper technical and economic significance of energy capacity parameters
compared to power ratings in energy storage system design.
The simulation results demonstrate the IFBs system’s outstanding grid regulation capabil­
ity. Compared to the original net power, the grid­connected power after battery regulation
shows significantly reduced fluctuation amplitude. Meanwhile, the SoC curve remains
stably within the safe operating range, verifying long­term operational reliability. Through
grid­level simulation, this study fully validates the effectiveness of the IFBs model and
its control strategy, confirming the technology’s important application value in promoting
renewable energy integration and enhancing grid stability.

Future Work and Conclusion
This chapter serves as the summary and outlook section of the entire paper, aiming to
summarise the research findings and outline future research directions systematically.
Through the progressive research conducted in the preceding chapters, this study has
successfully established a dynamic modelling theoretical framework for IFBs and vali­
dated their power smoothing effects in simplified grid scenarios. The research results
indicate that the developed battery model can accurately characterise the dynamic re­
sponse characteristics of IFBs, with prediction accuracy and engineering applicability both
meeting the expected objectives.
Although this study has built the theoretical foundation for the dynamics of IFBs systems
and their application in renewable energy grid integration, there is still room for further
exploration in terms of improving model accuracy, adapting to complex operating condi­
tions, and engineering applications. Based on the limitations of the current research, this
chapter will propose targeted future research directions focused on key technical bottle­
necks to facilitate the transition of IFBs technology from theoretical models to engineering
practice. Finally, the innovative achievements and academic contributions of this study
will be comprehensively summarised, and the completion of research objectives will be
objectively evaluated.
7.1
Model Refinement and Enhancement
The current ARX and residual models demonstrate commendable predictive accuracy,
particularly within typical operational ranges. However, these models can still be opti­
mised for enhanced fidelity across all operating conditions.
7.1.1
Advanced Dynamic Modelling
While the second­order ARX model effectively captures a substantial portion of dynamic
behaviours, a meticulous analysis of residuals, particularly at extreme SoC levels, such
as 20% and above 80%, indicates the persistence of systematic errors. Subsequent re­
search could investigate more sophisticated non­linear modelling techniques or higher­
order ARX structures to characterise these challenging operational regions better. Ap­
proaches such as Neural Networks (NNs), Support Vector Machines (SVMs), or hybrid
models that judiciously combine physical principles with data­driven components warrant
investigation. Such advancements would potentially yield improved accuracy in scenarios
involving rapid power changes near SoC limits, which are frequently influenced by highly
non­linear electrochemical phenomena.
7.1.2
Integration of Thermal Dynamics
The present model primarily concentrates on electrochemical and power dynamics. Nev­
ertheless, as previously documented in Chapter 3, thermal effects exert a significant influ­
ence on IFBs efficiency and performance, particularly during periods of high­power opera­
tion or at low SoC. Future research endeavours should, therefore, prioritise the integration
of a detailed thermal model into the existing framework. This undertaking would neces­
sitate the accurate modelling of heat generation within the stack, the characterisation of
heat dissipation mechanisms, and the precise quantification of electrolyte temperature’s
impact on reaction kinetics and overall efficiency. Such a comprehensive enhancement
would facilitate more accurate predictions of long­duration performance and concurrently
provide invaluable insights for the design of robust thermal management systems, which
are indispensable for successful real­world deployment.
57
==PAGE:70==
7.1.3
Enhanced Lifetime and Degradation Modelling
The IFBs dynamic model established in the current study fully considers the short­term op­
erational characteristics of the system, but does not yet incorporate the key mechanisms
that characterise long­term performance degradation. Future research could focus on de­
veloping refined sub­models to describe capacity decay and efficiency degradation. By
establishing quantitative relationships between their evolutionary patterns and key oper­
ational parameters such as cumulative charge and discharge cycles, depth of discharge,
and maintenance intervals, precise predictions of battery performance throughout its en­
tire lifecycle can be achieved. This comprehensive model framework, incorporating age­
ing mechanisms, not only enhances the accuracy of long­term economic assessments but
also provides a theoretical basis for developing optimal maintenance strategies, thereby
significantly enhancing the practical guidance value of IFBs models in engineering appli­
cations.
7.2
Advanced Control Strategies
The present work employs a PI controller for power tracking, which demonstrably exhibits
robust performance. Nevertheless, exploring more advanced control strategies could po­
tentially unlock additional performance benefits and enhance operational flexibility.
7.2.1
Model Predictive Control
Given the successful development of a validated dynamic model, the implementation of
Model Predictive Control (MPC) warrants thorough investigation. MPC possesses the
inherent capability to explicitly manage system constraints, including power limits, SoC
limits, ramp rates, and to optimally determine control actions over a defined future hori­
zon, taking into account predicted renewable generation and anticipated load demand.
This proactive approach to control could potentially facilitate more intelligent and antici­
patory battery dispatch, leading to discernible improvements in grid stability, reductions in
operational costs, and an enhanced utilisation of the battery’s full operational capabilities
when compared with conventional reactive PI control methodologies.
7.2.2
Integration with Grid­Level Optimisation
The simplified power grid model used in this study has validated the basic regulatory
functions of IFBs; however, it cannot fully reflect the complexity of actual power systems.
Subsequent research should focus on deeply integrating the IFBs dynamic model with
a multi­node power grid simulation platform, addressing the following key issues: first,
interface modules should be developed to connect with grid­level optimisation tools such
as optimal power flow algorithms and economic dispatch strategies, achieving seamless
integration between battery models and grid operation control; Second, practical opera­
tional factors such as network topology constraints, line transmission capacity limitations,
and real­time electricity price signals must be systematically considered. This integrated
modelling approach not only enables more accurate assessment of the technical and
economic value of IFBs in complex grid environments but also provides a scientific basis
for formulating large­scale deployment plans, including optimal siting and capacity deter­
mination strategies, as well as collaborative operation rules and other critical decision­
support measures.

fundamentally contingent upon their demonstrated scalability and compelling economic
competitiveness.

7.5
Conclusion of the Thesis
This thesis conducts a systematic study on the integrated application of IFBs in mod­
ern power systems, with a focus on their value in supporting the grid integration of high
proportions of renewable energy. The core innovation of this research lies in the establish­
ment and validation of a high­precision IFBs dynamic simulation model, which success­
fully achieves accurate characterisation of the system’s steady­state energy conversion
and transient dynamic response.
Through a multi­dimensional research approach, this study achieved the objectives. First,
the hybrid dynamic model, constructed based on experimental data, underwent rigor­
ous validation. This model innovatively integrates a segmented charge­discharge effi­
ciency characterisation method with ARX­based system identification technology, intro­
ducing a residual correction mechanism that demonstrates excellent predictive perfor­
mance across the 20%­80% SoC range. Quantitative validation results show that the
model maintains a RMSE of approximately 1.29 kW in power tracking tasks, with a co­
efficient of determination (R2) for AC power prediction generally exceeding 0.99. These
metrics fully validate the model’s ability to capture complex electrochemical dynamics and
accurately assess its engineering applicability.
This thesis systematically evaluates the technical effectiveness of validated IFBs in mit­
igating fluctuations in renewable energy output by integrating IFBs dynamic models into
a simplified grid. Simulation results analysis shows that IFBs systems have significant
grid regulation capabilities. The standard deviation of grid­connected power is reduced
by 96.6%, and the maximum power variation rate is reduced by 75%, effectively improv­
ing the dynamic stability of grid operation. Capacity optimisation studies indicate that a
storage capacity of 42.3 MWh is required to maintain continuous system operation within
the safe SoC range. This research highlights that, in the design of grid­scale energy
storage systems, determining energy capacity parameters holds essential technical and
economic value, providing important engineering guidance for planning and deploying
future large­scale energy storage systems.
This thesis established a comprehensive research framework and methodology for IFBs,
spanning from dynamic modelling to grid applications. The dynamic battery model inte­
grates physical principles with data­driven advantages, and its findings have confirmed
the engineering applicability of IFBs as long­duration energy storage solutions from mul­
tiple perspectives, particularly demonstrating outstanding performance in enhancing grid
stability and promoting the integration of high proportions of renewable energy. By deeply
revealing the dynamic response mechanisms of IFBs, this study provides critical technical
support for the development of new power systems, offering significant theoretical guid­
ance and practical engineering implications for advancing the global transition toward a
low­carbon energy structure.
