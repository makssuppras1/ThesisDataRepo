Abstract
This thesis investigates the spectral element method (SEM) for modeling transient,
incompressible Navier-Stokes (NS) equations in two dimensions, highlighting its high
accuracy and potential for eﬀiciency. SEM is a formulation of the finite element
method (FEM) that combines high-degree polynomials as basis functions, utilizing
FEM’s adaptability for complex geometries and the spectral methods’ high conver-
gence rates.
Systematic discretization of NS equations demonstrates SEM’s potential for expo-
nential accuracy, verified by example problems, ensuring accessibility for developing
SEM programs.
A direct numerical solver is employed throughout the study to
achieve precise solutions.
Performance assessment focuses on physical accuracy, robustness, and adaptability
for topology optimization, employing the vortex shedding behind a cylinder example:
(a) Velocity field
(b) Pressure field
The scalability of the Spectral Element Method (SEM) in parallel computing is ev-
ident, showcasing its potential for eﬀiciently solving large-scale problems. However,
significant challenges persist, particularly concerning the computational intensity
observed at high element orders and maintaining stability.
At higher Reynolds
numbers, such as Re = 500, critical limitations arise that necessitate optimizing
memory usage and implementing matrix-free operations to enhance performance
and manage resources more effectively.
In addressing stability issues, particularly when employing the immersed boundary
method, we propose various parmeter filter shapes. This approach aims to mitigate
numerical instabilities and improve the robustness of the solutions.
In conclusion, SEM demonstrates considerable promise as a scalable and effective
computational method for CFD and topology optimization.

1.1
Motivation
Flow at high speeds or turbulent flows are inherently transient, meaning the flow
is unsteady in time, observable in phenomena ranging from fuel injection and aero-
dynamics to simple duct flows.
Decades of research have aimed to develop the
most accurate approximations of turbulent flow. In computational fluid dynamics
(CFD), the most common solution schemes are the Reynolds-Averaged Navier-Stokes
(RANS) Solver and Large Eddy Simulation (LES). RANS computes the laminar flow
as an average of the field and approximates the turbulent flow with a myriad of dif-
ferent models, κ −ε, κ −ω and so on. LES neglects smaller scales of turbulence,
by assuming that the primary flow is dominated by large vortexes/eddies. Conse-
quently, neither approach fully resolves all details of the flow fields. Our goal is to
provide exact solutions for transient turbulent flows using Direct Numerical Sim-
ulation (DNS), which aims to resolve every scale of the flow. An example of the
possibilities for simulation with DNS can be seen in figure Figures 1.1a and 1.1b

State of the Art
1.2
State of the Art
This section presents the state of the art within spectral elements applied to model
fluid problems.
1.2.1
Development of Spectral Element Methods
The initial development of the spectral element method was undertaken by Young
in 1981 [5], who was the first to suggest lagrangian polynomials as shape functions
on Gauss Lobatto Legendre(GLL) mesh grids. However, it is Anthony Patera’s 1984
paper [6] that is often credited as foundational to the spectral element method, which
builds on the formulations by Orzag [7], who initially proposed using Fourier expan-
sions to represent complex geometries in partial differential equations. Subsequent
refinements and contributions to these methods have been made by researchers such
as Lee-Wing [8], Rønquist [9], Couzy [10], and Fischer [11]. Notably, Fischer played
a pivotal role in the development of the NEK5000 open-source code [12], which is
considered a state-of-the-art research tool in the high-order spectral element method
for incompressible fluid dynamics. NEK5000 is particularly recognized for its large-
scale computations and its eﬀicient parallelized framework for directly solving the
incompressible Navier-Stokes equations.
The mathematical derivations underpinning the use of the Spectral Element Method
for the Navier-Stokes equations in this thesis mainly built upon the works of Røn-
quist [9] and Deville and Fischer et al. [13].
1.2.2
Solution stability
When computing fluid flow using Navier-Stokes equations, we typically employ it-
erative methods, which are step-by-step procedures to find solutions. Stability in
this context means that if these steps are not executed carefully, the solution might
exhibit unwanted and non-physical behaviors; in other words, the solution becomes
unstable. This instability is largely due to the nonlinear and time-dependent char-
acteristics of the Navier-Stokes equations, which are sensitive to small changes in
the steps taken during the computation.
For this reason, considerable effort has been dedicated to ensuring stability.
When computing flow problems using the standard Galerkin method, ie the method
we are going to continue within this thesis, Franca [14], suggests that the instabilities
stem from node-to-node oscillations in the velocity field and Tezduyar [15], suggest
that the instability stems from using the same functions to approximate both the
velocity and pressure, creating spurious pressure modes.
Maday and Patera [16] initially advocated for a staggered grid approach, the PN ×
PN−2 grid, to circumvent spurious pressure modes, occurring from using the same
functions to approximate the velocity and pressure fields cf.
Tezduyar [15].
As
illustrated in Figure 1.3, in the PN × PN−2 grid, velocity is represented on a grid
with elements of order N, while the pressure field is resolved on a grid with elements
of order N−2. For the pressure grid, regular Gauss–Legendre (GL) points, instead of
GLL points, are used as nodes (degrees of freedom and integration points), leading to
a discontinuous pressure field. This approach only addresses instabilities arising from
the use of same order velocity and pressure fields. To tackle instabilities caused by

The PN × PN−2 configuration, while widely used, has inherent drawbacks, partic-

exploration of a PN × PN method, which is considered ideal for achieving higher
accuracy, especially in turbulence simulation. Deville et al. [13] and Fischer and

enhances time-stepping schemes and incorporates sophisticated pressure boundary
conditions. This approach facilitates the adoption of a PN × PN grid, significantly
improving turbulence resolution.
The Karniadakis scheme has been successfully
implemented in advanced computational platforms such as Nek5000 [12] and Neko
[20], demonstrating its eﬀicacy and robustness in practical applications. These im-
plementations highlight the scheme’s capability to handle complex fluid dynamics
scenarios with enhanced accuracy and stability.
Rong and Xu [21] propose that instability in the PN × PN system arises from the
accumulation of high-frequency pressure modes. They argue that stability can be
achieved by eliminating the last two pressure modes, thereby rendering the sys-
tem stable at the PN × PN level. Malm et al. [22] identify that the instability in
the solutions originates from improper integration of the elements. They propose
that stability can be regained through over-integration. They also note that over-
integration might add on 20% extra computation time. Philsu et al. [23] advocate

1.3
Topology optimization using spectral
elements
Topology optimization in mechanics is a method designed to optimize the placement
of material within a structure by modifying its topology. This innovative approach,
first introduced by Bendsøe [24], has been extensively refined and applied to various
fields beyond its initial structural mechanics applications.
Sigmund significantly
advanced this method, an example of these advancements is his seminal work on
compliant mechanisms [25].
The adaptation of topology optimization to fluid mechanics has been further devel-
oped by researchers such as Borrvall and Petersson [26], Alexandersen [27], Kreissl
et al. [28], Andreasen [29], and Nobis et al. [30]. Notably, Nobis et al. have incorpo-
rated high-order spectral elements into topology optimization. Using the Neko code
[20], they performed topology optimization on transient fluid dynamics problems,
employing the Brinkmann term as a design parameter through the immersed bound-
ary method. This method simulates a solid structure within the fluid by applying
a friction term (the Brinkmann term, cf. Angot et al. [31]) to alter fluid veloc-
ity within the domain (The immersed boundary method cf. [32]), setting it apart
from traditional density-based topology optimization where the design parameters
typically affect the entire element. An example of the traditional method used to
resolve a circular structure can be seen in Figure 1.4.

geometry is a circle, marked with the black line. The computed geometry is seen with the
red squares, symbolizing elements.

1.3.
Topology optimization using spectral elements
One challenge in using the spectral element method for topology optimization is
related to the characteristics of Lagrangian polynomials. These polynomials can
attain values below zero, potentially leading to non-physical negative values and, in
practice, removing energy from the system. A proposed solution is to use Bernstein
polynomials as the basis, as suggested by Lambe [33]. While Bernstein polynomials
maintain many spectral properties similar to those of Lagrangian polynomials, they
lose some advantageous properties. Properties which we will discuss later.
Another approach to using high-order elements for topology optimization involves
moving away from traditional density-based methods.
Specifically, the level-set
method, as compared to density-based methods by Andreasen et al. [34], modifies
the boundary directly. Although not as widely used or developed as density-based
methods, the level-set method has limitations in design possibilities. Unlike topol-
ogy optimization, which can theoretically create new holes independently and indef-
initely, the level-set method cannot generate new holes in a structure unless they
are already predefined by the user.
This thesis will explore the specific application of the Brinkmann term within the
immersed boundary method in fluid dynamics, employing density optimization tech-
niques and Lagrangian polynomials as shape functions. While topology optimiza-
tion presents a wide array of challenges, this work will concentrate on this particular
implementation of the design parameter, recognizing but not delving into other com-
plexities.

2
Fundamentals of the Spectral
Element Method
This chapter lays the groundwork by clarifying the fundamentals that distinguish
SEMs, which employ high-order elements, from traditional FEMs, which typically
employ linear or quadratic elements. We will get around the differences with regard
to the following key aspects:
• Strategies for mesh refinement
• The use of high-order Lagrange polynomials as shape functions
• The selection of Gauss-Lobatto-Legendre (GLL) nodes
• The application of GLL quadrature
We want to clarify that the term Spectral Element Method might suggest a method-
ology distinct from FEM. However, SEM should be viewed as an extension of conven-
tional FEM and could also be termed ’high-order FEM.’ The term ’spectral’ comes
from the incorporation of spectral methods principles, utilizing a sum of certain
”basis functions” that yield spectral (exponential) convergence of error terms.
2.1
Mesh Refinement
A fundamental difference between the SEM and the FEM lies in their approaches
to increasing geometric resolution as depicted in Figure 1.2. FEM typically employs
h-refinement, which increases the number of elements in the mesh.
In contrast,
SEM uses p-refinement, enhancing resolution by raising the polynomial order of the
elements’ shape functions as detailed in Section 2.2.
The increase in polynomial order results in a greater number of internal nodes within
each element. It is advantageous to distribute these internal nodes using a Gauss-
Lobatto-Legendre (GLL) distribution rather than spacing them equidistantly, as
discussed in Section 2.3. This results in the unevenly spaced nodes observed in the
SEM mesh refinement in Figure 1.2.
2.2

In both SEM and FEM, Lagrange basis polynomials serve as shape functions (nodal
basis) to construct solutions over elemental grids, enabling the accurate approxima-
tion of functions within these domains. Whereas FEM typically employs lower-order
polynomials—linear for linear elements and quadratic for quadratic elements—SEM
extends this to higher orders, generally ranging from 3 to 15. Figure 2.1 illustrates
Lagrange basis polynomials ℓi for different polynomial orders N.

3.1.2
Runge and/or Gibbs Phenomena
When utilizing Lagrange polynomials for numerical approximation, oscillations of-
ten occur in the resulting values. These oscillations, known as Gibbs Phenomena
[40, 41] and Runge Phenomena [42], can lead to unphysical behaviors in the com-
puted solutions. In the context of optimization, such artifacts may be exploited
by optimization algorithms, leading to suboptimal or misleading results. Therefore,
it is crucial to implement strategies that mitigate these phenomena to ensure the
reliability and accuracy of the optimization process.
While Gibbs phenomena traditionally refer to oscillations in the Fourier series and
Runge phenomena pertain to issues in polynomial interpolation, the literature often
uses these terms interchangeably due to their similarities. Although the term ”Runge
phenomena” traditionally describes the oscillations that occur at edge nodes when
interpolating equidistant nodes, in this thesis, we will use it more broadly to refer
to all oscillations that occur when using Lagrangian polynomials for interpolation.

Diagonal Mass Matrix
In SEM, the mass matrix B acquires a diagonal form, as discussed in Section 2.4,
and depicted in Figure 3.9. This characteristic results from the use of Lagrange
polynomials evaluated at GLL points. The cardinal property of Lagrange polyno-
mials, detailed in Equation (2.2.2), ensures that each polynomial equals 1 at its
corresponding GLL point and 0 at all other GLL points, leading to a diagonal mass
matrix.

3.3.4
Steady Stokes Solver
In modern computing, the backslash operator from MATLAB serves as a powerful
tool for solving modestly sized systems like those encountered in this work, as for-
mulated in Equation (3.3.15), which is a saddle point problem.
However, when using purely velocity boundary conditions and forcing, this method
can yield a pressure solution with an arbitrary offset from the analytical solution.
In contrast, employing the Uzawa algorithm, detailed in Appendix D.1, eliminates
this pressure offset, aligning the solution closely with the analytical one. For small
steady, linear problems, the computational time difference between the Uzawa algo-
rithm and the backslash operator is negligible. Yet, in the realm of transient prob-
lems, the cumulative time difference becomes significant. Thus, while the Uzawa
algorithm is adequate for steady and linear convergence analysis, it is highly recom-
mended to employ a direct solver and establish a reference pressure node or value
for greater accuracy.

Convection Operator
With a Stokes-equations solver established in the previous section we move on to a
crucial part of the incompressible Navier-Stokes equation, the convective operator
which is temporally non-linear, since it depends on the velocity.
Therefore the primary goal of this section is to develop an accurate spatial dis-
cretization for the convection operator C(u), which will be utilized to represent the
convective (nonlinear) term in the incompressible Navier-Stokes equations. Rønquist
provides a comprehensive derivation of the convection operator in [9], specifically
in section 2.5 (pages 47-69). Given the limited scope of this report, we will not
delve into the details of this derivation as thoroughly as we have for other matrix
operators. Instead, our focus will be on the implementation.
In appendix B.6, the effectiveness of the Spectral Element Method (SEM) in ap-
proximating the spectrum of the one-dimensional convection operator is analyzed.
Rønquist [9] notes that the comparison of the spectral approximations between SEM
and the Linear Finite Element Method (FEM) underscores SEM’s accuracy, with
about two-thirds of the spectrum being precisely represented by the spectral ele-
ment approximation. This observation underscores the benefits0 of SEM in cap-
turing essential features of the spectral distribution, which is crucial for accurate
modeling. However, it is also noted that SEM can introduce higher frequency eigen-
values, potentially leading to spurious modes. These aspects are further detailed in
Equation (B.6.3) and section 4.3, where the implications of these higher frequency
components on system behavior and model convergence are discussed.

3.5.5
Discussion of other solvers
The explicit FD1/AB3 scheme derived earlier represents just one of many possible
solvers.
While it effectively utilizes the diagonal mass matrix, other algorithms
might offer distinct advantages and should be considered as well.
Iterative solvers
In solid mechanics, when solving nonlinear material problems, iterative solvers such
as the Newton-Raphson method are commonly employed. This approach ensures
force equilibrium during displacement steps by maintaining the correct slope of the
stiffness matrix, a technique known as the tangent stiffness method [45]. Similarly,
this concept could be beneficially applied in the time-stepping algorithms used in
fluid dynamics, specifically to ensure equilibrium when solving C(u) or to aim at
reducing the residual. Implementing such a method could potentially allow for the
use of larger time-steps. However, assembling the matrix C(u) is computationally
expensive, which might make this approach prohibitively costly. Consequently, this
method is not commonly recommended, as noted in the comprehensive text by
Deville et al. [13], specifically section 6.4 and 6.5.

parts of the flow equations. This technique, known as the Operator-Integration-
Factor Splitting method (OIFS), addresses the equation:

• Pros:
– Better Scalability: SEM has greater potential for parallelization, as

At N = 15, the oscillations are nearly entirely eradicated, as observed in Figure 4.21,

lations can occur when spatial gradients are high and are not adequately resolved
by elemental polynomials. As shown in Section 3.1.2, oscillations in interpolated

for the internal nodes to effectively resolve, which is not the case here since the
parameters over the entire domain are constant.
It may simply be the natural instabilities commonly known to occur in the standard
Galerkin formulation of the Navier-Stokes equations, as mentioned by Franca [14],
that cause the nodal oscillations.
In general, oscillations occur whenever there are abrupt changes in the fields—
such as parameters, pressure, or velocity—that exceed what can be resolved by the
elemental polynomials. This issue also manifests when initiating flow fields from
zero, where the domain experiences a significant wave, or within boundary layers
where zero velocity is enforced. Specifically, in high-velocity scenarios, the velocity
profile sharpens, increasing the likelihood of encountering these phenomena if the
field is not properly resolved.
To reduce oscillations, we propose two strategies: 1. If oscillations are observed in
the nodal values in parts of the flow with high spatial gradients, one should increase
mesh refinement. 2. If oscillations are noted near parameter changes, such as with
a Brinkmann term, then smoothing or decreasing the parameter change may be
beneficial.

Nonetheless, it is crucial to maintain the Brinkmann term at a suﬀiciently high level

to establish a clear relationship between the Brinkmann term, velocity, and viscosity

h-refinement
Though SEM theoretically can increase accuracy solely by p-refinement, it has been
observed that such is not always adequate when using the PN×PN−2 setup, where the
pressure is discontinuous, so to resolve more of the pressure domain we will need to
perform h-refinement as also discussed and highlighted in the previous Section 4.3.5.
h-refinement can also facilitate a shorter transition length for parameter filters, as
the Runge phenomenon associated with parameter interpolation is confined to the el-
ement where the parameter changes, as discussed in Section 3.1.2. This relationship
between filter length and h-refinement deserves further practical research to build
upon the findings from Section 3.1.2. We will further discuss filter considerations in
the subsequent section.
Filtering
To prevent solutions from blowing up, we can employ various strategies, such as
filtering approaches. Two filtering methods exist: filtering the entire flow field and
filtering only specific parameters. Our initial efforts with parameter filtering are
documented in Section 3.1.3 and demonstrated in Figure 4.7b, where parameter
filter methods contributed to solution stability.
We have implemented only the linear parameter filter as suggested in Section 3.1.3.
Investigating alternative filters, such as the Sigmoid filter with an increased transi-
tion length, could be worthwhile. A thorough analysis to determine the optimal type
of filter and transition length that effectively reduces penetration length and mini-
mizes oscillations is needed. Additionally, exploring other parameter filters within
the realm of topology optimization—often referred to as density filters—could be
beneficial.

Towards sensitivity
To optimize problems like the Navier-Stokes (NS) equations using the Spectral El-
ement Method (SEM), it is crucial to compute the gradient of the objective using
eﬀicient methods like the adjoint method. While it is possible to employ a method
such as finite differences, the computational expense becomes prohibitively high
with the number of design variables typically involved.
In their exploration of adjoint sensitivity, Andreasen and Jespersen [38] implemented
discretized temporal sensitivity, employing methodologies similar to those described
by Kreissl [28] and Kristiansen and Aage [58]. However, this implementation has
proven to be challenging. As a result, it may be beneficial to consider the adoption
of continuous temporal sensitivity, as outlined by Kang et al. [55] and successfully
implemented in Andreasen’s work on microfluidic particle manipulators [59].
Both approaches involve solving boundary value problems that require integration
from the endpoint back to the start, which can significantly increase memory us-
age, since all the time-steps has to be retained. One way to mitigate this issue is
through the use of a check-pointing method, which allows for partially calculating
the temporal contributions to the sensitivity in steps.

Enhancing Computational Eﬀiciency
Assessing the computational eﬀiciency of SEM requires analysis of its application
on large-scale problems optimized for high-performance computing (HPC) environ-

Topology Optimization
Continued advancements in topology optimization for fluid dynamics, particularly
using SEM, are essential. We propose the following research initiatives:
• Optimization of α: Future research should focus on identifying the optimal
ranges for the inverse permeability factor α (Section 5.2.2). α significantly
influences the stability and smoothness of solutions; therefore, determining its
eﬀicient range is critical for effective topology optimization.
• Development of Advanced Parameter Filters: There is a pressing need

instabilities associated with the Navier-Stokes equations and the polynomial
characteristics of Lagrangian polynomials.

• Alternative Design Parameters: The current use of the Brinkmann term
allows for pressure diffusion through nodes where it is applied, which is subop-

that prevent pressure diffusion could lead to more effective control of fluid
flow and enhanced optimization results.

A.2.3

While GL quadrature is commonly used in QFEM and LFEM, we have chosen to
use GLL quadrature for these methods as it allows for mass diagonalization at the
same accuracy and number of quadrature points.
In LFEM, although GL quadrature can integrate bi-linear shape functions with only
1 quadrature point, a 2x2 quadrature rule is typically used to avoid hourglass modes,
which are non-physical deformation modes causing numerical instabilities. Using
GLL quadrature with a 2x2 rule also avoids these modes and provides the added
benefit of mass diagonalization, where the mass matrix is diagonalized, improving
computational eﬀiciency.

meshes. Further analysis is required to determine if the pseudo Pn × Pn configura-
tion maintains stability in later stages or if it falls short of the advantages offered
by a genuine Pn × Pn setup, which would utilize a single grid.

Conclussion
We successfully implemented both the finite ele-
ment method and the spectral element method to
address the 1-dimensional wave propagation prob-
lem depicted in Figure 2.1, achieving results in line
with Dahl’s benchmarks [4]. In our study, we em-
ployed two distinct time integration schemes for
these formulations: the implicit Newmark scheme
and the explicit backward difference scheme. The
backward difference scheme proved faster yet con-
ditionally stable, in contrast to the slower but un-
conditionally stable Newmark scheme.
For design optimization, we defined the objective
function 𝜙(𝜇) =
∫︀𝑇

mize displacement at the target node. While the
sensitivities of this objective function were com-
puted following Kristiansen and Aage’s guidelines
[7], discrepancies were noted during the finite dif-
ference check, indicating inaccuracies in our com-
putation.
The exact source of these errors re-
mained unidentified. Despite this, the computed
sensitivities, albeit imperfect, were still effective in
optimizing the structure under the objective func-
tion, yielding satisfactory outcomes.

Fourier transformation
Fourier transformation using Matlabs fft()
function [amplitude, frequency] = fourierTransform(force, time)
% fourierTransform: Compute the amplitude spectrum of a signal using the Fourier transform.
%
% Input:
%
force: Input signal (time-domain).
%
time: Time vector corresponding to the signal.
%
% Output:
%
amplitude: Amplitude spectrum of the input signal.
%
frequency: Frequency vector corresponding to the amplitude spectrum.
% Compute the Fourier transform of the input signal
fft result = fft(force) / length(time);
% Calculate the sampling frequency
sampling frequency = 1 / (time(2) - time(1));
% Number of samples in the signal
num samples = numel(force);
% Generate the frequency vector
frequency = (0:num samples - 1) * (sampling frequency / num samples);
% Keep only the positive frequencies (up to Nyquist frequency)
frequency = frequency(1:floor(end / 2));
% Keep only the corresponding part of the amplitude spectrum
amplitude = abs(fft result(1:floor(end / 2), :));
end
