Abstract
Within the scope of developing an autotuning framework for controllers aimed at industrial
applications, this thesis focuses on the study of a robust nonlinear controller: the Super
Twisting Sliding Mode Controller (STSMC). Since finite time stability tuning strategies
are already available, the behaviour of the closed loop STSMC has been studied under the
assumptions of the controller gains being undertuned (the condition for finite time stability
are not met).
The main results of the thesis are the definition of requirements for the guarantee of ulti-
mate uniform boundedness of system trajectories under the presence of different external
perturbations, the formulation of conservative relation between controller’s gains and the
found bounds and the development of two separate tuning techniques for the undertuned
STSMC.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Over the years the industrial world has manifested growing interest towards improving
the reliability and robustness of production lines. In fact these aspects have proven to be
fundamental to achieve better quality of the end products and enhance production vol-
umes. Therefore, robustness towards the presence of external disturbances has become an
extremely important aspect when designing production floors.
Another goal for the industrial plants of the future is to become truly autonomous and
this involves the ability of self commissioning. The end goal of this innovation process
is to create entire production lines able to calibrate and tune without the intervention of
human operators, while guaranteeing predefined accuracy and performance according to
predetermined specifications.
These new horizons are possible because more and more data is being acquired on pro-
duction sites everyday, which can be leveraged to improve current processes and develop
new control strategies: allowing the development of a self monitoring and self adjusting
production environment, capable of a more efficient allocation of resources and robust be-
haviour under the presence of uncertainties and external perturbations.
Since any process needing to guarantee accurate and reliable performance presents feed-
back control loops, any request for enhancing robustness, self commissioning plants and
better utilisation of data within production translates directly to the world of control sys-
tems and this leads to the scope of this thesis.
1.2
Scope
Supported by the motivation of improving the current production environment, the main
scope of this project regards the study of advanced control techniques and more specifically
the world of nonlinear control.
The relevance of nonlinear control comes from the observation that every system presents
a degree of nonlinearity. In fact one of the first step of classical (linear) control design
is to linearise the system’s dynamics around the operating point, which determines the
controller being formulated around an approximation of the model. This method is often
sufficient to guarantee good results, but if either the system is very nonlinear or higher
performance is needed, it becomes necessary to resort to other control strategies, such as
nonlinear control.
Another reason that supports the design of a nonlinear control algorithm is the growth and
diffusion of data driven modelling methods. In fact, by leveraging the growing availability

of data, new techniques have been developed to create nonlinear models which can capture
the complex nonlinear dynamics of real systems. These innovative approaches merged with
classical modeling techniques based on physical principles, can be used for the construction
of nonlinear models that are extremely accurate.
Within nonlinear controllers, a specific class has shown great success in guaranteeing robust
performance: Sliding Mode Controllers (SMC). These controllers are, in fact, inherently
capable of dealing with external disturbances and perturbations. These are all reasons why
it’s relevant to define tuning rules and strategies for such family of controllers.
1.3
Main goal
Since now it’s clear why the study of nonlinear control theory is relevant and SMC have
been mentioned, in this section is going to be addressed the main topic of this project.
This thesis will focus on a specific type of nonlinear controller within the Sliding Mode
Control family: the Super Twisting Sliding Mode Controller (STSMC).
The reasons behind the choice of this controller are its properties: it’s inherently robust
in respect to external disturbances, it’s not as affected by chatter as the first order SMC
and its tuning complexity is not as high as other nonlinear controllers, since the entire
controller’s tuning relies on the definition of two real parameters for the gains.
While the low number of parameters to be defined for a STSMC it’s a strong selling point
of the controller, the tuning strategies available are often very demanding in terms of the
magnitude of the gains. This has to do with the fact that the controller’s tuning strategies
are mostly aimed at guaranteeing finite time stability, which requires very high gains in
the presence of rapidly varying disturbances.
The aim of this thesis is to address what happens when the gain selection doesn’t satisfy
the finite time stability conditions, with the goal of finding what are the requirements for
a close loop STSMC to guarantee boundedness. Once the system requirements for bound-
edness are found, the final step is to use the description of the bounds to define a specific
strategy to choose the gains for the undertuned STSMC, creating a tuning strategy capable
of guaranteeing a prescribed accuracy while requiring small gains.
1.4
State of the art
Variable structure controllers [1] have experienced growing popularity in the last decades
with great attention being directed toward sliding mode controllers [2]. The main reason
for this growth in interest has to do with the resilience they display towards bounded
external perturbations and with the property of guaranteeing finite time stability, which
can’t be guaranteed using classical controllers (such as PID). Such characteristics have led
to the use of sliding mode controllers in different areas, from the control of manipulator
dynamics [3] and quadrotors [4] to applications such as power converters [5] and nonlinear
chemical processes [6].
Sliding mode controllers have been, also, utilised for fault diagnosis [7] and fault control
[8], as well as for the design of observers [9], since the estimates that they provide converge
exactly to the true value in finite time.
The high performance of sliding mode controllers comes at the cost of requiring (theo-
retically) infinitely fast switching of the control input, which it’s not implementable in
real world applications. This phenomenon of limited switching frequency determines the
appearance of chatter both on the input and output signal [10]. Chatter is extremely un-
desirable: it causes wear of the actuators and introduces loss of performance (in respect
to the theoretical behaviour that the close loop system should have). In order to mitigate
2

==PAGE:11==
the impact of chatter, a series of strategies have been developed involving observers [11],
filtering [12] or the substitution of the sign function with a smoother function [13] [14] [15].
An alternative approach involves the use of higher order sliding mode controllers which are
less affected by chatter [16].
The Super Twisting Sliding Mode Controller (STSMC) presented in [17] was shown to
be less prone to chatter [18] then the regular SMC and only requires the tuning of two
gains, maintaining a limited complexity in comparison to other higher order sliding mode
controllers.
In light of the better performance provided by the STSMC, multiple tuning strategies have
been presented: [19] developed strict Lyapunov functions and introduced a tuning strategy
that guarantees finite time stability and [20] presented a necessary and sufficient analytic
condition that the gains need to satisfy in order to guarantee finite time stability, extending
the previous results in [21].
A common aspect of all the tuning strategies is the requirement of both gains to be larger
than the largest derivative of the disturbance: this results in very high values for the gains
[22]. One way to avoid the definition of large gains for the STSMC is to design adaptive
strategies: the gains are designed to increase or decrease over time based on the magnitude
of the disturbance. Multiple methods have been developed using this principle: in [23] was
proposed an adaptive approach capable of handling an uncertain drift, [24] presented a dual
layer adaptive approach for the STSMC where both gains adapt over time, meanwhile [25]
used barrier functions (an overview of which is found in [26]) in order to determine an
adaptive strategy for the gains.
While adaptive strategies reduce the requirements on the gains for low values of the dis-
turbances, they still require high gains in order to guarantee finite time stability as soon
as the disturbance grows. An alternative approach is the application of undertuned gains
[22] [27]: the idea behind undertuning strategies is to accept a lower performance of the
system (no finite time stability) in order to obtain reasonable tuning parameters. This
is often a desirable choice since finite time stability requires too high gains for real world
scenarios. It, also, should be noticed how the guarantee for prescribed accuracy bounds
(boundedness of system trajectories) is often sufficient for industrial applications, since it
still guarantees performance within a certain accuracy.

2.1
Definition of perturbations and model inaccuracies
A nonlinear and nonautonomous system can be described using the following state space
formulation [28]:
˙x = f(x, t, u)
(2.1)
where x is the vector of the state variables, t is time and u is the external control input.
In order to control such system a model of its dynamical behaviour is constructed with the
following expression:
˙x = ˆf(x, t, u)
(2.2)
The model ˆf(x, t, u) can be obtained, as mentioned before, using data driven techniques
(classical estimation techniques or machine learning based methods for example) or using
physics principles and known physical laws.
Regardless of how the modeling of the system takes place, the existence of external distur-
bances, parameter variations and other hard to predict phenomena (system degradation
over time, components aging, wear ...) determines a difference between the model and the
real system. On top of this external factors, it’s normal practice to model the system’s
behaviour within a specific operational range and therefore some dynamics are commonly
left out in order to reduce the complexity of the representation of the system and simplify
the design of a controller.
While these modeling decisions guarantee a more concise description of the system and
reduce complexity of the design of the feedback loop, they introduce an error, which needs
to be taken into account.
Due to existence of model inaccuracies and external perturbations, the equation f(x, t, u)−
ˆf(x, t, u) will almost never be identically zero. This discrepancy between the modelled de-
scription and the real world system leads to the necessity of designing robust controllers.
Within the scope of nonlinear controller design Sliding Mode Controllers have proven to
be suitable options and are often used in conjunction with sliding surfaces.

surface in order to control systems with multiple states.

The dynamics of a sliding surface have also to be specifically design in order to guarantee

the surface its dynamical behaviour is entirely dictated by how the sliding surface has been
designed.
For this reason the definition of a sliding surface is part of the design of the control loop,
since its definition will determine the convergence rate to the origin and the trajectory of
the states of the system, once it’s on the surface and follows the sliding regime.
Once a sliding surface has been designed, its attractiveness and invariant set property can
be guaranteed using Sliding Mode Controllers, since they can assure finite time convergence
of the sliding variable to the sliding surface.

The reasoning behind the definition of M comes from the observation that in multiple real
world scenarios the disturbance ρ2(x, t) is characterized by having a maximum value L2
which is significantly higher than its average value M. A real world example of an appli-
cation and a disturbance where this assumption is valid is presented in [22] (Permanent
Magnet Synchronous Motor subject to the presence of viscous and Coulomb friction).
The practical meaning of the property in 2.17 is that the average of the disturbance ρ2(x, t)
over a sliding time window of dimension ∆is inferior to a specific value M ≤L2 from a
certain time instant onward.

5.3
Summary
In this section has been proven the necessity to satisfy finite time stability requirements
in respect to the "average" component of ρ2(x, t) when the assumption of periodicity is
not guaranteed, taking in consideration the result presented in [20]. Then the edge case
Φ(k1, k21, M) = 1 was analyzed and the system’s trajectories have been proven to be
unbounded when P(t) = Pmaxsgn(x).
Therefore, given a specific M, if k1, k21 satisfy Φ(k1, k21, M) < 1 then the system is proven
to be globally uniformly ultimately bounded.

While progress has been achieved by proving the boundedness of the close loo STSMC and

Chapter 8
Conclusion
This thesis contributed to the development of autotuning strategies for the Super Twisting
Sliding Mode Controller by investigating how the behaviour of the system is affected by
undertuning. The main result of the thesis is the definition of the requirements that are
necessary in order to guarantee uniform ultimate global boundedness under a specific class
of external perturbations.
Equations for the description of closed bounds for the trajectories of the closed loop system
have been found, whenever boundedness could be proved, and based on these findings two
separate tuning techniques have been described.
The validity of the results have been
shown through simulations of different types of disturbances.
The discovery of the bounds as well as the definition of tuning strategies represents a step
forward toward the realization of an automated tuning framework for the Super Twisting
Sliding Mode Controller.
In conclusion, the thesis has thoroughly assessed the behaviour of the undertuned STSMC,
providing insights on the design of its gains, showing that, if accurately designed, a STSMC
is still capable of guaranteeing a predetermined accuracy under the effect of bounded
perturbations.
