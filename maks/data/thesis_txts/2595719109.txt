Abstract
Oﬀshore wind farms perform better than onshore wind farms by providing higher power
generation per amount of capacity. Thus, their scientiﬁc interest is increasing exponentially
for the last two decades. However, their substructures foundations are continuously subject to
all kind of waves. The breaking waves have the highest hydrodynamic loads compared to the
other waves and thus a higher impact on the material’s ultimate loads aspect. Consequently,
the development of numerical models that reproduce the waves, including the breaking ones, is
one of the lately main focuses of researchers, in order to predict and evaluate their generated
loads on the wind turbines foundations.
This master thesis investigates the reliability of the potential fully-nonlinear solver Ocean-
Wave3D (19) upon the fast reproduction of experimental kinematics and loads for both long-
crested (2D) and short-crested (3D) waves, for diﬀerent sea states. For the 2D and 3D cases,
the analysis is carried out through two main parts: the ﬁrst one is a fast numerical model
constructed to reproduce the wave surface elevation and the wave kinematics. During this
process, a breaking wave ﬁlter is applied to simulate the breaking wave height and occurrence
in time and space. The simulations are later on compared to the experiments in order to check
the validity of the numerical reproduction. The second part focuses on wave loads based on
two fast numerical models: the Rainey(6) model which aims on computing the non-breaking
non-linear waves, and the Pressure Impulse (PIMP)(3) model which is applied on the breaking
waves after identifying them thanks to the ﬁrst part.
Finally, the analytical models limits and sensitivity to diﬀerent parameters were investigated
in order to certify their reliability, and suggestions for improvements are made. In the 2D case,
the reproduction of the wave kinematics and loads turned out to be accurate and reliable,
with slight errors in some aspects in comparison with the experiments. For the 3D case, the
simulation results are reliable but still can be improved to match better the experiments.

State of the art
1.1
Oﬀshore wind farms statistics
In the last two decades, increasing interest is brought to oﬀshore wind farms as they perform better
than onshore wind farms by providing higher power generation per amount of capacity, all thanks to
their geographical locations and surrounding micro-climate. Only in Europe, the cumulative oﬀshore
wind farms capacity is increasing exponentially as shown in Fig. 1.1, and most of them are located
in the North Sea (Fig. 1.2) where the water depth ranges from 20m to 40m. As oﬀshore wind farms
has gained an important place among sustainable energy sources for the future, they attract more
scientists and research groups and it becomes important to optimize their design with the objectives
of high power production and increased longevity with minimum costs.

1.2
Oﬀshore wind turbine foundations and challenges
Depending on the sea states and the water depth, diﬀerent substructures foundations can be used
to set the oﬀshores base in the sea bed as it is schematically described in Fig. 1.3. Those foundations
are continuously subject to loads from regular, non-linear and breaking waves. The breaking waves
have the highest hydrodynamic loads compared to other waves and thus a higher impact on the
material’s ultimate loads aspect. Therefore, focus has increased in the past few years into modelling
numerically the breaking waves in order to predict and evaluate their generated loads on the wind
turbines foundations. The modeling study is of great importance to characterize the materials fatigue
and avoid overestimating their lifetime (21).

1.3
Project scope and objectives
This master thesis is performed in the framework of the The DeRisk project (9) conducted by the
DTU Wind Energy department. The aim of this project is to develop improved models and methods
3
==PAGE:8==
for accurate determination of wave loads on the foundations of bottom-ﬁxed oﬀshore wind turbines
through ﬁve main tasks :
• WP1: Wave Models
• WP2: Wave Physics
• WP3: Validated Load Models
• WP4: Wind turbine structures
• WP5: De-Risked design
The present Master’s thesis contributes to WP1 and WP3 task which focus on improving the numerical
description of waves and on improving the description of load models.
Breaking waves are a viscous phenomenon. In order to accurately predict the ﬂow and the load
on structures, fully-resolved Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) methods are widely used. In the
The Wave Loads project (9), experimental breaking waves have been re-simulated using OpenFOAM,
achieving a very good ﬁdelity. However, CFD methods which are very time consuming. For instance,
in Terp-Paulsen et al. (18) around 7 · 105 elements in the mesh are used in this case. Assuming just
5 minutes of computation with a time step of 0.1 seconds, the high number of equations generated by
the mesh are to be solved 3 · 103 times. Since each element gives information on the water particle
position in space (x, y, z), its velocity (vx, vy, vz), and the local loads (Fx, Fy), and knowing that a real
number takes 4bytes in RAM, the memory needed for the computation is approximately around 67.2Gb.
Besides, considering that each real number (4bytes) needs 10−4 seconds to be computed and saved in
disk, this leads to approximately 19 days of computation. This master thesis project’s objective is to
develop fast and accurate computational methods to evaluate wave loads, including breaking waves,
on oﬀshore wind turbines foundations while avoiding CFD methods.
This thesis addresses the reproduction of the experimental kinematics and loads for both long-
crested (2D) and short-crested (3D) waves. For both 2D and 3D waves, we divided the analysis in two
parts. In the ﬁrst part, we construct a numerical model that accurately reproduces the wave surface
elevation and the wave kinematics.
In this process, we payed particular attention in reproducing
the breaking wave occurrence in the sea state, both for what concerns time and location. As the
employed ﬂow model is irrotational (7), it cannot reproduce the wave breaking directly, as it is a
viscous phenomenon. Hence, a breaking ﬁlter is present in the code, which smooths the wave surface
when a particular condition is met, subtracting energy and preventing the simulation from becoming
unstable. This ﬁlter is based on the theory developed by Longuet-Higgins et al. (15) which describes
the acceleration associated with the limiting shape of a regular wave. In this condition, the downward
particle acceleration at the surface crest is a half of the gravity g. Following the theory, a wave is
considered as breaking when its downward particle acceleration is larger than a fraction β of the
gravity (g), with β = 0.5 for regular waves. However, there is no consensus on which value is the best
for irregular sea states, and multiple studies have presented diﬀerent values (4). Dawson (23) suggests
a values of β = 0.335 for irregular waves, Longuet-Higgins (16) suggest values around 0.25 while (20)
suggest β = 0.3 for 2D waves and β = 0.5 for 3D waves.
In this work, in order to further investigate the best value of β that enables to numerically char-
acterize the breaking wave behavior, a set of 6 experiments from the Wave Loads campaign was
re-simulated with diﬀerent β values. In the experimental setup, a high resolution camera was used to
record videos of the sea states in the wave tank. Thanks to MATLAB’s video processing capabilities,
the time when the breaking waves occur could be precisely identiﬁed. Consequently, the numerical
results with diﬀerent β are compared to the experimental sea states to ﬁnd the β that matches best the
experimental results. These studies allowed us to investigate the behavior of β against water depths
and the signiﬁcant values of the wave’s height and period (Hs, Tp). The model is also compared to
other existing models in order to check its validity, such as the probability of breaking in a sea state
introduced by Dawson’s team (23).
4
==PAGE:9==
The second part of the project holds the study of the wave loads. For non-breaking waves, the
Rainey (6) model is used to compute the loads of non-linear waves.
For the breaking waves, the
Pressure Impulse (PIMP) theory developed by A.Ghadirian and H.Bredmose (3) is applied. After
identiﬁcation of the breaking waves, their pressure impulses are computed via the PIMP model and
added to the quasi-static Rainey loads.
Finally, a conclusion will be presented on the reliability of OW3D reproducing the sea states
kinematics and wave loads, and on the reliability of the PIMP model.

Chapter 2
Time appearance of breaking waves (2D)
The aim of this chapter is to compare the time when breaking waves occur between a numerical
model and experiments performed in DHI Denmark. In order to achieve it, the numerical model and
the experimental setup will be presented introduced ﬁrst, then a video processing will be assessed in
details to show how the breaking waves time occurrence are extracted from the experiments. Finally,
the results of comparison between the numerical model and the experiments will be studied in depth
to conclude on the validity of the simulation.
2.1
Breaking wave types
A regular wave breaks when its steepness overcomes a certain threshold. The limiting steepness changes
with the depth, and local conditions as the seabed slope can enhance the tendency of waves to break
Fig. 2.1i. The seabed’s slope slows down the motion of the particles at the sea bottom and increases
the wave’s steepness until the crest reaches the breaking limit, where it releases its energy and creates
foam afterwards. The breaking waves can be classiﬁed into three types, depending on the sea state
and the slope Fig. 2.1ii:
• Spilling breakers: These waves happen mostly where the sea bed is almost ﬂat (gentle slope).
The wave will steepen slowly until the crest becomes unstable and collapses gradually, generating
a white cap of foam at the front. The wave energy is slowly dissipated in those conditions.
• Plunging breakers: These waves happen where there is a sudden change in slope at the sea
bed (steep slope). The wave becomes very steep, almost vertical at the crest, then rolls and
collapses, releasing suddenly a lot of energy comparing to the spilling breaking waves.
• Surging breakers: These waves happen in the same conditions as Plunging breakers. They
are long wave period with low amplitude that builds up at the front, generating a small amount
a foam comparing to Spilling ones. Their energy can be reﬂected to the bottom back creating
stationary waves.

2.3
Numerical modeling of breaking waves
2.3.1
OceanWave3D fully-nonlinear potential ﬂow solver
OceanWave3D (OW3D) (19) is an open source model developed at DTU, at the Department of Applied
Mathematics and Computer Science. This model is a fast and robust potential ﬂow solver of the fully
nonlinear and dispersive free surface equations, in marine areas. OW3D can achieve numerous tasks
such as the high-order ﬁnite diﬀerence resolution of the Laplace equation, the nonlinear wave-structure
interactions and large scale simulations.
The two-dimensional (2D) nonlinear kinematics, including the surface elevation η and the down-
ward acceleration dw
dt , are computed using OW3D(19). The simulations are run at model scale, in a
computational domain with the same slope of the experimental one as displayed in the upper panel
Fig. 2.3. The monopile is located at X = 14.19m (blue line) surrounded by 5 wave gauges (red dashed
lines) as shown in the bottom panel, which is a zoomed view of the upper panel around the monopile
location. The waves are generated in a relaxation zone at the deep end of the domain X = 0m, and
run up the 1:25 slope where the waves and the kinematics are sampled. At the shallow end of the
domain X = 30m, a numerical beach absorbs the waves and avoids reﬂections.

2.3.2
Breaking ﬁlter
As introduced in the previous chapter, OceanWave3D’s breaking ﬁlter is based on Longuet-Higgins’
(15) theory. A wave is considered as breaking if its absolute vertical acceleration at the crest is higher
than β times the gravitational acceleration g,
dw
dt < −βg
(2.1)
where dw
dt is the particle acceleration at the crest (material derivative of the vertical velocity w), g
is the gravitational acceleration and β is a dimensionless parameter to be determined in the range of
[0,1]. Physically, β represents how much a wave is close to free-fall condition. For example, if β is
close to 1, then a wave is certainly breaking, as the particles are in free fall. Practically, waves break
also for lower values of the β parameter. The value of β is not only conditioned by the seabed’s slope
but also the sea states and the wave type. Therefore, the goal of this chapter is to compare the time
appearance of breaking waves between the model with diﬀerent β and the experiments, for diﬀerent
sea states, in order to decide which β best ﬁts each condition.
In the simulation, at each time t of the surface elevation η, the waves are separated from trough
to trough, then their acceleration at the crest is computed and compared to βg. When the Eq. 2.1
is valid, the amplitude of η is lowered down to match the curly shape of a real breaking wave. The
Fig. 2.4 gives a schematic description of these numerical steps. At the time t = t0, the surface elevation
of a wave is captured from trough to trough (blue ﬁlled line). At x = x0 where the crest is located, the
downward acceleration is computed and the Eq. 2.1 is checked. When Eq. 2.1 is true, the amplitude
of the wave is lowered down to simulate the change of the shape due to breaking (blue dashed lines).

2.4.1
Reading a video in Matlab
For speed and performance purposes, it is important to control and optimize how the frames are read
and processed in MATLAB. Thus, the following three methods are possible:
• The use of "read" function, that takes a video and an index of a frame as inputs. The index is
the position of the frame in the video. For example, if the video is 60FPS (Frames Per Second),
and the frame wanted is at the time 2 minutes (120 seconds), then the index of the frame is:
120·60FPS+1 = 7201. The ﬁrst frame is indexed as 1. The "read" function is not recommended
by MATLAB for reasons explained later.
• The use of "readFrame" function, that takes only a video as input. The time step deﬁned by
this function is ∆t =
2
FPS and it cannot be changed. Therefore, skipping frames is not possible
without changing the video’s parameters. For example, if the user wishes to read only the frames
each ∆t = 0.1s, the function cannot do it, unless the user changes the videos starting time in
loop which is achieved by the "CurrentTime" parameter. The "readFrame" function is the most
recommended by MATLAB users.
• The use of "readFrame" in a function that changes the "CurrentTime" parameter, to have full
control of the video frames. We name this new created funcion "frameread", and it takes the
same inputs as the "read" function. Its code is as follow:

2.4.2
Statistical processing
The "Statistical processing" technique combines the RGB color plan ﬁltering methods and statis-
tical studies. Two images were taken from the video of the sea state as basis, one with a breaking wave
and the other one without, as displayed in Fig. 2.7. Since there are waves that are hard to decide if
they are breaking or not, 4 diﬀerent statistics with 4 validation criteria will be assessed. Each time the
frame fulﬁls one of these criteria, it will be given a positive score of 1, and 0 otherwise. The maximum
score is therefore 4 (surely breaking) and the minimum is 0 (surely non-breaking).

does not (bottom).

The breaking waves can be recognised by the foam produced at the top, that are white and brighter
comparing to the still water or regular waves. The glare must be ﬁltered as much as possible, because
its high brightness can perturb the detection of breaking waves which can lead to the wrong statement

By assessing the breaking wave types, it has been observed that most of the breaking waves with a
downward acceleration between 0.2g and 0.4g are Spilling breaking waves. The Plunging ones can be

downward acceleration exceeds 0.4g, while the other breaking waves are recognised to be Spilling ones
with a downward acceleration of 0.3g . This can be explained by the diﬀerence in which the breaking
waves dissipate energy. This is investigated in detail in the numerical simulations by Iafrati (2). For
small amplitude breaking waves, the celerity of the crest is close to the celerity of the wave, and thus,
thanks to surface tension loads at the top, the formation of the overturning shape is prevented, which
leads to the creation of Spilling breaking waves. For large amplitude breaking waves, the crest celerity
is very high compared to the celerity of the wave, which leads to the overturning shape, and therefore to
water particles with a downward acceleration close to gravity g. Those are Plunging breaking waves. A
β ≤0.2 is not representative for breaking waves, since numerous non breaking waves have a downward
acceleration around 0.2g. To conclude with, β = 0.3 is the best ﬁt to reproduce deep water sea states,
where Spilling breaking waves are dominant. Otherwise, β can be higher depending on the sea state.
Regarding the experimental breaking events that did not match the model, most of them are errors
from the video processing program, that can be improved using machine learning or deep learning.
Besides, for the sea states number 5 and 6, there are some non-breaking steep and/or fast waves
having a downward acceleration higher than 0.3g in the numerical model. And as stated before, the
Spilling breaking waves are seen for 0.2g < dw/dt < 0.4g. Since the optimal β for these sea states
is greater than 0.5, then some Spilling breaking waves are not considered. In the case of Sea State

Chapter 3
Reproduction of the wave loads (2D)
The aim of this chapter is to reproduce numerically the loads of the waves. To do so, two numerical
models were based on. One is the Rainey (6) model, which is an extension of the Morison’s equations
(11), and the other one is the pressure impulse model, suggested by Amin.G and Henrik.B (3). The
Rainey model is used to reproduce only the quasi-static part of the wave loads, and doesn’t take
slamming events into consideration. The pressure impulse model computes a numerical added load
which a breaking wave would have as an impact on the monopile. More details about the pressure
impulse will be given in section 3.2.
This chapter contains two parts. The ﬁrst one will introduce the Rainey model, and it will be
compared with the experimental wave loads of the same sea states as before, ﬁltered from breaking
events. The second part will introduce the pressure impulse, its numerical model and the comparison
between the simulation and the experiment.

The

Due to time restrictions, only

Consequently, a new breaking wave detection method is used based on the
pressure measurement signal, and will be presented in detail in the next sub-section.
4.2.1
Breaking waves time appearance detection
Experimental breaking events detection
Only results from Test N°17 will be presented in this sub-section as an example as the same
methods will be applied for the rest of the tests. In order to ﬁnd the breaking waves (BW) happening
times in the experiment without relying on the video processing, a new method based on the pressure
sensor measurements signal is used. The pressure sensor selected is P2, since it gives the most reliable
observations to capture the breaking waves. Consequently, when a breaking event happens, the slope
of P2 changes drastically. Therefore, by computing the gradient of P2 in time, the output signal ∇P2
will give peaks where there might be a breaking event.
Fig. 4.3 displays ∇P2 against time (black line). A threshold of 7 · 104Pa/s has been chosen (green
dashed line) to ﬁlter out the interesting peaks (indicated by circles). Each peak with an amplitude
higher than the threshold is checked in the video by eye, and given a score from 0 to 4 about weather
the wave is breaking or not (color table). The scores are as following: (0)Not breaking, (1)Undecided
but more likely not breaking, (2)Undecided, (3)Undecided but more likely breaking and (4)Breaking.
Note that most of the waves captured by this method are breaking (score=4). The undecided events
(score<4) generate a peak in the ∇P2 signal with an amplitude close to the threshold. Hence, this
method seems eﬃcient in capturing breaking waves from the measurements, and will be compared to
the (∆) and (∇) methods introduced before in Subsection 2.4.3.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
In this project, we reproduced numerically the experimental loads on monopiles from two diﬀerent
measurement campaigns from the the DeRisk and the Waveloads projects, performed at DHI. First,
we reproduced the wave kinematics via the potential non-linear wave solver OceanWave3D (19). Both
two-dimensional (2D) and three-dimensional (3D) sea states were simulated, corresponding to storms
with diﬀerent return times. The breaking waves were taken into consideration in the simulation by
applying a breaking wave ﬁlter model based on a dimensionless parameter β. Secondly, quasi-static
loads were computed via the Rainey force model and breaking loads were added via the Pressure
Impulse model.
In the 2D cases, the recomputed kinematics is accurate as the Peak Exceedance Probability (PEP),
the power spectrum (PSD) and the surface elevation in time match well most of the experiments, with
the exception of the sea states with a lot of breaking waves, where there is a diﬀerence in the tail of the
PEP (large waves). In these sea states, the agreement seems to depend on the type of the breaking waves
happening in the sea states. When spilling breakers are dominant, the simulation underestimates their
heights, which can be improved by revising the breaking wave ﬁlter model. When plunging breakers
are dominant, the simulation seems to overestimate their height. We believe that part of the issue
comes from the wave gauge measurement instrument, which underestimates the wave height when
overturning waves are measured. This issue might be improved by using another eﬃcient wave gauge
that captures the surface elevation with its detailed shapes. Regarding the 3D cases, the numerical
results are also promising. Indeed, the PEP and the PSD comparison between the simulations and the
experiments match well. However, it seems that the simulations are overestimating the spread in the
directional spectrum, which has consequences on locating the breaking waves in time and space, and
on the accuracy of the transverse loads. This may be improved by decreasing the directional spread
error between the simulation and the experiment, as it is an important parameter for the 3D waves.
These assessments lead us also to conclude that β = 0.3 is a good guess for most 2D cases, especially
in deep water, and around 0.5 in 3D cases. However, it is less clear what breaking factor should be
used as the water depth becomes smaller.
As for the wave loads, assessing the reliability of reproducing numerically the wave loads, including
the ones from the breaking events, was another challenging task of this thesis.
Two models were
considered based on the numerical surface elevation.
The ﬁrst one is the Rainey (6) model that
computes the non-linear non-breaking wave loads. The second one is the Pressure Impulse (3) (PIMP)
model that computes the impulse load of a breaking wave. The simulated wave loads are in great
agreements with the experimental ones, as their values are close in time and statistically. Indeed,
regarding the 2D cases, the Rainey (6) model reconstructs accurately the quasi-static loads in terms
of the force time series, the PEP and the PSD, and The PIMP theory is positive when it comes
to reconstruct the slamming loads. For the 3D cases, the quasi-static loads in the maind direction
(x-direction) are reliable, but the y-direction forces should be revised.
For future investigations it is recommended to:
• Improve the breaking wave ﬁlter to get their a match between the wave heights with the experi-
ment.
• Find a relationship between β and the wave parameters.
• Investigate more the 3D waves and their loads, including the PIMP.
• Investigate the behavior of the β parameter in the 3D cases depending on the water depth.
59
==PAGE:64==
Finally, this master thesis contributed in validating the reliability of the potential wave solver
OceanWave3D in reproducing the sea states and their loads, including the breaking events in no time
comparing to the CFD methods. Indeed, the simulations with the breaking waves take around 3 to
4 hours, while the CFD methods take days to compute. Also, the prject contributes on the behavior
of the parameter β in diﬀerent sea states and wave types. Therefore, OceanWave3D and the PIMP
models together will contribute to the oﬀshore wind energy research.
