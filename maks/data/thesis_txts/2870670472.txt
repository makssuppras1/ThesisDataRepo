Abstract
This thesis examines the utilization of deep learning, particularly sequence­to­sequence
(Seq2Seq) neural networks derived from Natural Language Processing (NLP), to auto­
mate the classification of multiplicity in one­dimensional Nuclear Magnetic Resonance
(1D NMR) spectroscopy. The conventional manual annotation of NMR spectra is arduous,
subjective, and susceptible to inaccuracies, particularly when addressing intricate spec­
trum data with overlapping peaks. This study systematically assesses various Seq2Seq
models to tackle these challenges, integrating enhancements such as attention mecha­
nisms, multi­head attention, embedding layers, gradient clipping, focal loss, and Condi­
tional Random Fields (CRF) across synthetic and real­world datasets.
The efficacy and generalizability of the suggested models are evaluated by meticulous
experiments, including accuracy, loss metrics, and bootstrap confidence intervals across
diverse training­testing configurations utilizing synthetic and real spectra. Results indicate
that models trained solely on synthetic data have constraints in accurately representing
the complexity and variability present in actual spectrum datasets. In contrast, models
utilizing focus loss and multi­head attention mechanisms regularly demonstrate enhanced
classification accuracy and robustness, underscoring the effectiveness of these methods
in addressing class imbalance and sequential dependencies in NMR spectrum data.
The research highlights the essential significance of thorough preprocessing, meticulous
normalization, and the application of synthetic data to enhance constrained real datasets.
The results offer significant insights for improving automated spectrum annotation, mini­
mizing operator bias, and augmenting the reliability and efficiency of molecular structure
determination by NMR spectroscopy.

Introduction
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is a powerful and versatile tool for
quantitative molecular analysis, long established as a core method in both chemistry and
biology [1]. Recent technological developments have significantly expanded its applica­
tions, particularly in metabolite detection, health diagnostics, and food analysis.
In metabolomics, NMR enables non­invasive and highly reproducible profiling of a wide
range of metabolites present in biofluids and tissues. Techniques such as 1H­NMR spec­
troscopy, supported by statistical approaches like principal component analysis (PCA)
and partial least squares (PLS), are employed to quantify metabolites and map metabolic
pathways using isotope­labeled compounds like 13C and 15N [2, 3].
In the context of health diagnostics, NMR­based metabolomics has been extensively ap­
plied to discover biomarkers associated with diseases such as cancer, cardiovascular
and metabolic disorders, as well as to study the effects of environmental exposures. Un­
targeted profiling approaches—coupled with chemometric methods such as orthogonal
partial least squares­discriminant analysis (OPLS­DA) and PLS­DA—allow effective dis­
ease classification and insight into underlying biochemical changes [4, 5, 6].
Similarly, in food science, NMR plays a key role in verifying product authenticity, tracing
geographic origin, assessing quality, and identifying bioactive compounds. By capturing
the metabolic fingerprint of food items such as milk and honey, researchers can distinguish
subtle differences and detect adulteration through spectral analysis combined with pattern
recognition tools [7, 8, 9].
NMR offers comprehensive molecular insights by examining the magnetic characteristics
of atomic nuclei within a sample. This method is non­destructive and highly reproducible,
setting it apart from destructive analytical techniques like mass spectrometry (MS), gas
chromatography (GC), and liquid chromatography (LC), which typically require extensive
sample preparation, chemical derivatization, or eventual sample degradation. These prop­
erties make NMR particularly suitable for large­scale and repeated studies [10].
When analyzing NMR data, classical machine learning and statistical methods such as
Partial Least Squares Discriminant Analysis (PLS­DA) and Analysis of Variance (ANOVA)
are commonly used to associate spectral data with experimental outcomes. For exam­
ple, in plant metabolomics, PLS­DA has been employed to distinguish species based on
their metabolic profiles obtained from 1H­NMR spectroscopy. The technique successfully
identified key metabolites — such as glucose, lactic acid, and arginine — that differentiate
among species [11]. Similarly, univariate ANOVA has been applied in food metabolomics
to analyze metabolic differences in gluteal muscle tissues from various meat sources (cow,
goat, donkey, and chicken), revealing significant variation in metabolites like lactate, cre­
atine, and choline. This demonstrates ANOVA’s utility in biomarker identification for meat
authentication [12].
Regardless of whether multivariate (PLS­DA) or univariate (ANOVA) analysis is used, the
process of annotation is essential prior to interpreting NMR spectra. Annotation translates
spectral features — such as peak positions (frequency or ppm values), integration, and
amplitude ratios—into chemical insights by classifying multiplicity patterns and assigning
them to molecular structures [13]. Traditionally, this annotation is carried out manually,
which becomes highly challenging when dealing with complex spectral data containing
1
==PAGE:8==
hundreds of overlapping peaks. This issue is particularly common in the analysis of chem­
ically diverse mixtures, including biofluids, cell extracts, or fermentation products.
The complexity of manual annotation emphasizes a clear opportunity for automation. Au­
tomated annotation can significantly reduce the time required and minimize operator bias
in extracting meaningful chemical information. However, full automation is limited by the
necessity to recognize multiplicity relationships among peaks — information often em­
bedded within molecular databases or established through manual assignments. While
peak frequencies and amplitudes can be extracted through deconvolution techniques (see
Section 1.4.2), accurately classifying multiplicity from these parameters remains difficult
in intricate spectral environments.
Therefore, the goal of this thesis is to investigate the feasibility of automating multiplicity
classification based on extracted amplitude and frequency relationships. This will involve
applying novel deep learning techniques to enhance the interpretation of complex NMR
spectral data.
1.1
Thesis overview
The first chapter of this thesis starts by presenting an overview of NMR spectroscopy ap­
plications, followed by a short comparison to other methods utilized in analytical chemistry.
The second part of Chapter 1 introduces current methods being applied for automating
the analysis of NMR data. Finally, the last part introduces how deep learning algorithms
traditionally applied in Natural language processing may be utilized in a novel fashion for
the classification of NMR multiplicity.
1.2
Applications of NMR Spectroscopy
High­resolution Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) involves placing a sample in a strong
magnetic field and applying radiofrequency pulses to excite specific nuclei—typically hy­
drogen or carbon — into resonance. The resulting spectra yield finely resolved signals,
each corresponding to nuclei in distinct chemical environments [14, 15].
High­resolution NMR is a cornerstone in metabolomics research, particularly for the iden­
tification and quantification of metabolites in complex biological mixtures such as biofluids,
cell extracts, or plant tissues [13, 16]. Among its unique advantages, NMR is intrinsically
quantitative and capable of delivering structural insights that allow for the unambiguous
identification of unknown compounds. It can analyze minimally processed samples, in­
cluding intact biological matrices like serum or tissue extracts, without chemical alteration.
For example, 1H­NMR metabolite profiling of biofluids can reveal biomarkers indicative of
disease or nutritional status. Compounds such as lactate, choline­containing molecules,
creatinine, glucose, branched­chain amino acids, citrate, and acetate are frequently mon­
itored to track metabolic alterations. For instance, elevated lactate levels may signal hy­
poxia or impaired oxidative metabolism; high choline levels may reflect cancer or altered
membrane turnover; and increased creatinine or glucose may indicate renal dysfunction
or metabolic imbalance.
The standard analytical workflow includes spectrum pre­processing, normalization, and
multivariate statistical analyses — such as Partial Least Squares Discriminant Analysis
(PLS­DA)—to distinguish metabolic profiles associated with healthy versus pathological
conditions. Additionally, NMR’s ability to detect isotopically labeled molecules (e.g., 13C,
15N) enables detailed studies of metabolic pathways and fluxes that are difficult to trace
using other techniques [17].
2
==PAGE:9==
Clinically, NMR’s high reproducibility across instruments makes it ideal for large­scale and
longitudinal studies. This is exemplified by its integration into FDA­approved lipoprotein
profiling assays that assess cardiovascular risk from a single serum sample, as well as
its emerging role in early disease detection and personalized medicine [18].
In the domain of food science, NMR’s capability to generate unique molecular fingerprints
is transforming food authenticity testing. It allows for the simultaneous quantification of
various components, detection of adulterants, and verification of product integrity with min­
imal sample preparation. For instance, in honey analysis, NMR can concurrently quan­
tify sugars, amino acids, and other constituents to determine botanical origin and identify
adulteration with foreign syrups. Unlike targeted chemical tests that screen for single adul­
terants, NMR’s holistic profiling approach captures a broad spectrum of variables, making
fraudulent modifications more difficult to conceal [19].
The use of NMR in food quality monitoring is rapidly expanding with the introduction of
automated systems like the Bruker FoodScreener™[20]. These platforms can screen
beverages, wine, dairy, and honey in under 20 minutes with minimal sample prepara­
tion. Their high reproducibility ensures lab­independent, standardized results that meet
stringent regulatory standards. Notably, the European Union has adopted NMR as an
official method for verifying the authenticity of wine and honey, relying on consistent and
quantitative profiles supported by comprehensive reference databases [20].
1.3
Comparative Advantages of NMR Spectroscopy
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) spectroscopy is distinguished by its exceptional ca­
pacity for highly quantitative, reproducible, and non­destructive molecular analysis. Un­
like Mass Spectrometry (MS), NMR provides accurate quantification without requiring
compound­specific calibration or extensive sample preparation, making it particularly ad­
vantageous for analyzing complex biological mixtures and preserving valuable clinical
samples. Moreover, NMR is unbiased toward specific molecular classes, effectively de­
tecting both polar and non­ionizable metabolites that MS may miss. Its ability to deliver
detailed structural information — via chemical shifts, J­couplings, and multidimensional
experiments — also facilitates the identification of novel compounds beyond the capabili­
ties of MS, which often relies on spectral libraries [17].
In contrast, MS offers superior automation, supported by tools such as molecular network­
ing on the GNPS platform, which organizes MS/MS spectra based on similarity for efficient
annotation and visualization of chemical space [21]. It further benefits from extensive
open­access spectral databases like METLIN and MassBank, enabling high­throughput
and community­driven analysis [22]. However, MS is limited to inferring hypothetical struc­
tures from mass and fragmentation patterns, lacks full structural resolution, and cannot
detect non­ionizable compounds regardless of their abundance [23].
Compared to Near­Infrared (NIR) spectroscopy, which is rapid and economical yet of­
fers restricted chemical specificity, NMR delivers unparalleled molecular resolution, en­
abling the precise identification and quantification of individual molecular species. NIR
necessitates comprehensive calibration against established reference methods and pre­
dominantly yields bulk property data instead of intricate molecular­level information [24].
Recent improvements in NMR technology, including elevated magnetic field strengths (up
to GHz­class magnets), cryogenic probes, hyperpolarization techniques, and advanced
automation systems, have markedly improved its sensitivity, resolution, and throughput.
These advancements have broadened NMR’s utility, enhancing its competitiveness with
MS for trace analysis and its value for high­throughput investigations [25]. Thus, NMR has
3
==PAGE:10==
established itself as the favored method for comprehensive metabolite profiling, accurate
structural determination, and reliable quantitative assessment across various applications
in research, clinical diagnostics, and industrial quality control.
1.4
Automation in NMR
Recent advancements in automated tools and methodologies for NMR data analysis have
significantly enhanced both the efficiency and accuracy of complex spectrum interpreta­
tion. Traditionally, manual tasks such as peak picking and assignment are now aided
by advanced software employing computational algorithms, Bayesian inference, and ma­
chine learning. For instance, tools like PINE and FLYA can automatically assign peaks to
specific residues or nuclei in proteins by integrating data from various multidimensional
experiments in a probabilistic manner [26]. However, their performance remains highly
dependent on the quality and completeness of the input data; missing or ambiguous peaks
can lead to erroneous results or require manual verification, thus limiting full automation
[26].
Comprehensive platforms such as UNIO further optimize the entire analysis pipeline —
from spectral preprocessing to final structure determination — and have demonstrated
robust performance even with complex biomolecules [27]. Despite its capabilities, UNIO
has not seen widespread adoption due to its dependence on specific acquisition protocols
like APSY­NMR, limited flexibility, steep learning curve, and the need for expert oversight
[28].
In the domain of small­molecule analysis, automated methods utilize predictive algorithms
combined with chemical databases. These approaches leverage predicted chemical shifts,
coupling constants, and chemically­informed heuristics to achieve accurate atom­to­peak
assignments often reaching correct assignment rates of 80% or higher [29].
Bayesian­based systems, such as BAYESIL and BATMAN, have shown utility in metabo­
lomics and mixture analysis. They apply probabilistic models to resolve and quantify
metabolites in complex 1D NMR spectra, handling complications like peak overlap and
chemical shift variation [30]. Yet, these systems face major challenges BATMAN, for
example, requires intensive computation due to its use of Markov Chain Monte Carlo
(MCMC) sampling, which slows down analysis considerably [31]. Furthermore, both tools
demand high­resolution 1H­NMR data — typically at 600 MHz — with minimal baseline
noise and precise phasing, and are less robust against peak shifts and poor water sup­
pression [32, 33].
In parallel, machine learning techniques like Probabilistic Latent Semantic Analysis
(PLSA) have been applied to NMR by treating spectral data analogously to text — where
peaks are akin to words and spectra to documents. This approach identifies latent ”topics”
for use in clustering, compound classification, and dereplication [34]. Such topic modeling
is especially valuable in natural product research and drug discovery, where it enables
rapid and automated screening of large spectral libraries [35]. Nevertheless, the success
of these methods is hindered by limitations in spectral database quality. Identification
accuracy declines when unknown compounds fall outside existing reference data [36, 37].
Additionally, noise or suboptimal preprocessing may lead to false associations.
Spectral fingerprinting offers another means of annotation, relying on each molecule’s
unique pattern of chemical shifts, integration, multiplicity, and coupling constants [38, 39].
However, overlapping peaks, complex multiplets, second­order coupling, and baseline
drifts make manual fingerprint interpretation highly challenging. To address this, auto­
mated alternatives have been developed. For example, Global Spectral Deconvolution
4
==PAGE:11==
(GSD) enhances peak detection and multiplicity classification in 1D NMR [40], while ho­
modecoupling and J­resolved techniques reduce spectral complexity, though often at the
cost of longer acquisition times and reduced sensitivity [41].
Despite these advances, a fundamental limitation of NMR persists: its dependence on
spectral databases for compound identification. This reliance hampers the discovery of
novel or poorly characterized molecules. Consequently, future progress demands either
substantial expansion of spectral databases or the development of alternative methodolo­
gies not reliant on such references.
This study pursues the latter, focusing on extracting structural information directly from
raw spectral features such as frequency, amplitude, and multiplicity. By doing so, it aims
to enable compound identification independent of pre­existing spectral databases.
1.4.1
Database independent annotation
As opposed to the database­driven methods highlighted in the previous section. The
opportunity for automatic structural assignment through annotation seems an attractive
opportunity, with the current quality of NMR databases. Therefore, this section gives an
overview of annotation practices.
Automating the annotation of 1D NMR spectra requires a thorough grasp of three essential
characteristics: annotation frequency, amplitude, and multiplicity. The term frequency
denotes the rate at which annotations occur across chemical shifts, which plays a crucial
role in determining the resolution and accuracy of multiplicity assignments. Increased
annotation frequency can yield more comprehensive insights, yet it also amplifies the
demanding aspects of the annotation process and raises the likelihood of human error
[39].
The amplitude, indicative of the intensity of NMR signals, plays a vital role in differenti­
ating significant spectral peaks from baseline noise. The variability in amplitude presents
challenges for annotation, as subtle peaks may frequently be misinterpreted or missed, es­
pecially in noisy or overlapping spectral areas. Manual annotators face the ongoing chal­
lenge of distinguishing between significant peaks that indicate chemical environments and
minor peaks that arise from noise or artifacts, which complicates the process of achieving
accurate and consistent annotations. [42, 43]
Multiplicity, a critical characteristic resulting from spin­spin interactions, significantly in­
fluences the annotation of NMR signals. An NMR­active nucleus generates multiplets
— series of closely spaced peaks—when spin­coupled to adjacent nuclei. Multiplets are
categorized by the number of observed lines (frequencies/peaks): one line constitutes a
singlet, two lines form a doublet, three lines create a triplet, four lines comprise a quartet,
and so forth [44]. For nuclei with spin 1
2 (e.g., 1H, 13C, 19F, 31P), multiplicity adheres to the
well­known n+1 rule, where a signal split by n equivalent adjacent spins produces n + 1
peaks. The intensity ratios in these multiplets follow Pascal’s triangle for first­order cou­
pling (e.g., 1 : 1 for doublets, 1 : 2 : 1 for triplets, and 1 : 3 : 3 : 1 for quartets). This pattern
consistently assists chemists in deducing molecular connectivity [45]. However, in practi­
cal scenarios, overlapping peaks, intricate splitting patterns (multiplets within multiplets),
and second­order coupling effects significantly complicate manual annotations. When a
nucleus is coupled to several sets of non­equivalent nuclei, complex splitting emerges, re­
sulting in compound multiplets, such as doublets of doublets (dd). Furthermore, coupling
constants (J), representing the frequency spacing in Hz between multiplet lines, remain
independent of spectrometer field strength and are essential for accurate analysis. The
intricacies inherent in multiplicity assignment demand considerable expertise, substantial
5
==PAGE:12==
manual effort, and meticulous analysis, which often leads to errors and inconsistencies in
manual annotations [39, 38, 45].
The frequency (chemical shift), multiplicity (splitting pattern), and amplitude (integration)
collectively yield a comprehensive response that enables the deduction of molecular struc­
ture by associating unique signals with distinct groups within a molecule.
For example, in ethyl acetate (CH3COOCH2CH3), the methyl group attached to the car­
bonyl (COCH3) resonates at around 2.0 ppm as a singlet because it has no adjacent
protons; the methylene group (OCH2) appears at about 4.1 ppm as a quartet (coupled
to the three protons of the terminal methyl group); and the terminal methyl group (CH3)
shows up near 1.2 ppm as a triplet (coupled with the two protons of the methylene group).
Figure 1.1: Proton NMR Spectrum of Ethyl Acetate
The integration of these signals in a 3:2:3 ratio confirms the number of protons in each
fragment, and together these parameters allow us to deduce the complete molecular struc­
ture. Hence, a molecular structure can in principle be deduced solely from ppm (frequency
normalized across different spectrometers), amplitude ratio, and multiplicity.
1.4.2
Deconvolution
Spectra obtained from 1D Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) can vary significantly in
complexity, ranging from simple spectra with only a few distinct peaks to highly intricate
ones containing numerous overlapping signals. Thus, extracting amplitude ratios, fre­
quency values, and multiplicity is a tedious and time­consuming task when faced with
complex spectral datasets with overlapping signals. To automate and increase the quality
of the extraction process, deconvolution is a critical method employed to extract individual
peaks or signals from these overlapping regions in spectra [46, 47]. In metabolomics, the
necessity for deconvolution arises primarily from the high spectral complexity and exten­
sive data volume. Biological fluids and tissues generate spectra containing hundreds of
metabolite signals, often leading to significant peak overlap due to limited spectral reso­
lution. Consequently, traditional one­dimensional NMR typically struggles to accurately
resolve all metabolite peaks, complicating both the identification and quantification pro­
cesses [48].
Deconvolution directly addresses this challenge by separating overlapping signals, facili­
tating accurate assignment and quantification of metabolites [49]. Various methodologies
6
==PAGE:13==
are available for spectral deconvolution, with conventional peak­fitting approaches com­
monly representing spectra as combinations of analytical line shapes, such as Lorentzian,
Gaussian, or pseudo­Voigt functions. Software tools like Mnova GSD and rNMRfit utilize
these models to refine peak parameters systematically, although these approaches typi­
cally require careful optimization and parameter adjustment [50].
One of the key benefits of deconvolution is the substantial improvement in spectral reso­
lution. It allows for clearer visualization of subtle spectral features and more precise mea­
surements of coupling constants, which may otherwise remain obscured due to broaden­
ing effects. This is particularly beneficial for analyzing complex mixtures found in metabolomics
or reaction mixtures, significantly enhancing the interpretability of densely populated spec­
tra. By sharpening the spectral peaks, deconvolution also highlights minor frequency
differences and subtle splitting patterns, crucial for detailed structural characterization
without resorting to higher magnetic fields or more intricate experimental setups [51].
However, it is important to consider that the effectiveness of deconvolution techniques
relies heavily on the validity of underlying assumptions. If these assumptions are incor­
rect or unmet, artifacts may arise, potentially leading to erroneous results. Additionally,
accuracy in the known splitting values is critical; incorrect values can negatively impact
the simplification process, causing further inaccuracies. Tools such as the NMR­Onion
frameworks seek to mitigate these issues by offering model diagnostics, as well as post­
hoc evaluation of peak position of highly overlapping peaks [50]. However, even with the
evaluation of peaks, NMR­Onion, nor any other tool, produces an automated assignment
of multiplicity for the extracted peaks. Thus, automated annotation is not carried out, and
Chemists must identify and consistently assign multiplicities in complex scenarios with
peaks numbering in the hundreds. Manual annotation is characterized by subjectivity,
significant labor demands, and a propensity for inaccuracies, particularly when applied
to large datasets. Automating the annotation process is advantageous; however, exist­
ing automated methods face considerable challenges, including managing spectral noise,
detecting subtle amplitude variations, accurately interpreting intricate spectral fingerprints,
and reliably establishing precise annotation frequencies [39, 42].
Natural Language Processing (NLP) encompasses methodologies designed to analyze
and interpret human language through algorithmic approaches.
At first glance, NLP
methodologies might seem unrelated to numerical or spectral data analysis; however,
a critical commonality lies in their sequential nature.
In recent years, sequence tag­
ging has become a potent technique in natural language processing (NLP), supporting
tasks such as part­of­speech tagging, named entity recognition, and semantic role la­
beling. Sequence tagging models allocate categorical labels to each element inside a
sequence, similar to tagging words in a phrase, by capturing both local and global rela­
tionships. Cutting­edge methodologies such as Bidirectional LSTM­CRF and transformer­
based frameworks (e.g., BERT) have exhibited remarkable efficacy by simulating token
interactions and integrating contextual information. Models that incorporate output tag
dependencies using tag­level LSTMs or CRFs surpass conventional sequence models
by adeptly capturing structural links among labels.[52] Moreover, in specialized domains
such as war incident classification and biomedical text mining, BERT­based models have
outperformed conventional neural networks owing to their capacity to generalize across
intricate, real­world data. [53, 54] Specifically, an NMR spectrum can be perceived as
a sequence of data points or spectral peaks arranged along a chemical shift axis, anal­
ogous to words arranged in a sentence. Connections can be drawn between words in
NLP and spectral sequences in NMR, adapting sequence tagging to classify multiplicity
patterns in deconvoluted NMR spectra. Similar to how each word in a sentence can be
7
==PAGE:14==
designated with its grammatical function, each spectral peak can be classified according
to its multiplicity (e.g., singlet, doublet, triplet). This conceptual framework facilitates the
application of NLP­inspired neural architectures to automate the classification of intricate
spectrum datasets, providing an innovative and scalable resolution to a key difficulty in
NMR annotation.
This project leverages this similarity by separating spectral data into individual sequences
per spectrum and applying sequence­to­sequence (seq2seq) neural network architec­
tures.
Seq2seq architectures, which effectively handle sequential inputs and outputs,
can thereby model spectral patterns for subsequent tasks such as molecule classification.
This approach mimics NLP sequence tagging by interpreting distinct spectral features
or peak patterns as sequential ”entities” or ”tags.” Consequently, applying NLP­inspired
seq2seq methodologies significantly advances automated interpretation and classification
of spectral data, enabling more efficient, consistent, and scalable molecule classification
[55, 56, 57, 58, 59].

Theoretical Background
This chapter establishes a theoretical framework for NMR data processing and advanced
sequence­to­sequence (Seq2Seq) architectures. This discussion initiates with a compari­
son between traditional deep neural networks and Seq2Seq models, which are specifically
structured to manage variable­length input and output sequences via an encoder­decoder
framework. The following sections systematically present architectural improvements, in­
cluding bidirectional LSTMs, attention mechanisms (both simple and multi­head), and
embedding layers, each targeting particular shortcomings of the fundamental model. Ad­
ditional enhancements encompass methods such as gradient clipping and focal loss to
enhance training stability and mitigate class imbalance, alongside the incorporation of
Conditional Random Fields (CRF) for structured prediction. It ends with evaluation met­
rics as it ties all the enhancements together, providing a systematic way to measure their
impact on performance and ensuring the overall framework is effective and robust.
2.1
NMR data processing
After acquiring NMR data, the next step in the analysis process is to convert the raw data
into spectra. To acquire data via NMR spectroscopy, the Free Induction Decay (FID) is
recorded at first, which is a direct time­domain signal caused by nuclear spins pausing
right after an RF pulse. The FID contains all of the material’s chemical and structural
data. It is considered the most important dataset (a ”primordial repository” of NMR in­
formation) from which spectra are made [60]. To achieve an interpretable outcome, the
time­domain free induction decay (FID) is transformed into a frequency­domain nuclear
magnetic resonance (NMR) spectrum by Fourier transformation [61]. Before executing
the Fourier Transform, several time­domain processing techniques may be employed on
the Free Induction Decay, such as applying an apodization window or incorporating zero­
filling, to refine signal decay, improve resolution, and mitigate artifacts in the resultant
spectrum [62]. Nonetheless, these processing techniques may generate artifacts. Uti­
lizing a quickly decaying exponential window function helps attenuate noise in the latter
segments of the FID, although it may also reduce weaker signal components, so obliter­
ating narrow or low­intensity peaks and resulting in information loss [63] [64] [65]. The
trade­off between noise suppression and peak preservation underscores the necessity of
meticulous optimization of FID processing in NMR data analysis.

2.2
Sequence to Sequence Model
Conventional Deep Neural Networks (DNNs) are robust machine learning models that ex­
cel in challenging tasks such as speech recognition and visual object identification. How­
9
==PAGE:16==
ever, they are applicable solely to extensive labeled datasets where inputs and goals
may be appropriately represented as vectors of fixed dimensionality. They are not ap­
plicable for mapping sequences to sequences (e.g., machine translation) [66]. Seq2Seq
(Sequence­to­Sequence) models are a category of neural networks, specifically an ad­
vanced architecture of Recurrent Neural Networks, intended to convert one data sequence
into another. It can manage input and output sequences of diverse lengths, rendering it
especially efficient for jobs involving structured sequential data. The architecture employs
an encoder­decoder framework, wherein the encoder transforms the input into a context
vector, and the decoder produces the associated output sequence. Seq2Seq models
are extensively utilised in applications including machine translation, text summarisation,
speech recognition, chatbots, picture and video captioning, time series forecasting, and
code creation [67].
2.3
Classic Seq2Seq Architecture (Model 1)
A conventional Seq2Seq model comprises an encoder­decoder architecture, whereby
the encoder condenses the input sequence into a fixed­length representation, and the de­

As sequences may vary in length (50–5000), during batching, sequences of unequal
length are padded with zeros (features) and −1 (labels) to the length of the longest se­
quence in each batch. Sequence lengths are tracked separately and used by PyTorch’s
pack_padded_sequence utility so that padded timesteps are ignored by the encoder­decoder
LSTM. In the loss computation (masked cross­entropy or focal loss), padding positions
(target = −1) are excluded via the ignore_index parameter, ensuring that gradient up­
dates only reflect valid timesteps.
Two distinct data sources are utilized to train the model and assess its generalizability. All
synthetic examples drawn from the “long” and “short” sequence files are combined into a
single PyTorch dataset, then randomly partitioned into training (75%) and validation (25%)
subsets. This stratified split ensures that both long and short synthetic sequences appear
in each fold, providing diverse examples during learning.
In contrast, the training pipeline completely excludes the real­world spectra. After group­
ing by set_num, normalizing amplitudes, and encoding class labels exactly as for the
synthetic data, these sequences populate an independent test loader (batch size = 64)
without any further splitting. Consequently, model performance on real data reflects true
out­of­sample accuracy on real measurements, untainted by synthetic training data.

Results
This section provides a thorough assessment of seven variants of sequence­to­sequence
models discussed in Chapter 2. The assessment framework is organized into four unique
experimental scenarios, each aimed at evaluating model robustness, generalization, and
sensitivity to variations in data origin and distributional shifts.
The initial scenario assesses each model by training and testing them solely on real­world
data, thereby setting a benchmark for performance in genuine conditions. The second
scenario reflects this methodology using synthetic data, facilitating controlled benchmark­
ing in idealized and less noisy environments. The third scenario examines the concept
of generalization through the training of models using synthetic data, followed by their
evaluation on real­world data. The fourth aspect alters this configuration, assessing the
adaptability of models trained on authentic data when applied to synthetic domains. Pre­
senting results across these distinct contexts allows for a comprehensive comparison of
model behavior in environments with differing complexity and domain characteristics.
The evaluation of performance is conducted through established metrics, including test
accuracy, average training and test loss, as well as bootstrap­based test accuracy ac­
companied by 95% confidence intervals. This focus on both point estimates and statis­
tical variability provides a trustworthy perspective on predictive performance and model
consistency.
The results indicate significant differences in model performance across various scenar­
ios. The use of synthetic datasets typically resulted in enhanced training stability and in­
creased accuracy, particularly when applied for both training and testing purposes. On the
other hand, models that were trained using synthetic data faced difficulties in fully general­
izing to real­world data, underscoring the challenges associated with domain adaptation.
Model 6 consistently demonstrated superior performance across all variants in both real
and synthetic domains, achieving the highest test accuracy and the lowest loss values in
the majority of settings. The findings suggest that Model 6 provides the most favorable
balance among complexity, predictive accuracy, and reliability across various data types.

5
Discussion
Our study demonstrated significant strengths, particularly the effectiveness of sequence­
to­sequence (Seq2Seq) neural networks enhanced by multi­head attention mechanisms
and focal loss in classifying multiplicity in 1D NMR spectroscopy. Models employing multi­
head attention consistently outperformed simpler models, showcasing improved robust­
ness across both synthetic and real datasets. The incorporation of focal loss effectively
addressed class imbalance, leading to noticeably better performance on minority classes
such as quintets and sextets compared to traditional loss functions.
However, a major limitation was observed in the transition from synthetic to real­world data.
Despite achieving high performance on synthetic datasets, our models encountered dif­
ficulties generalizing to real NMR spectra due to intrinsic variability, baseline drifts, and
noise that were not adequately represented in synthetic data. For instance, performance
dropped by 16.4 percentage points when transitioning from synthetic to real­world test
data. Models predominantly trained on synthetic data demonstrate diminished effective­
ness when faced with real spectra that have baseline changes, noise distortions, and rare
or intricate multiplet patterns.
To address these challenges, a detailed distribution shift investigation is recommended.
Specifically, analyzing and quantifying differences in the distribution of both input fea­
tures (amplitudes, frequencies, and coupling distances) and response labels (multiplic­
ity classes) between synthetic and real datasets would be beneficial. Employing statisti­
cal techniques such as the Kolmogorov­Smirnov test, Wasserstein distance, or Kullback­
Leibler divergence could quantify these distributional discrepancies and identify specific
features or labels most affected by shifts.
Furthermore, examining discrepancies in the conditional distributions of features based
on response labels may yield more profound insights. This could involve examining the
consistency of amplitude and frequency distributions for each multiplet class across syn­
thetic and real datasets. Additionally, applying techniques such as feature importance
analysis or dimensionality reduction methods (e.g., PCA or t­SNE) could further clarify
which aspects contribute most significantly to the observed domain shift.
Besides, concentrated initiatives aimed at tackling class imbalance via oversampling tech­
niques, sophisticated augmentation approaches, or customized loss functions could likely
improve outcomes for underrepresented multiplet types.
Addressing class imbalance
through intentional oversampling or the application of specialized loss functions, like fo­
cal loss, has the potential to greatly enhance performance, particularly for rare multiplet
classes. Furthermore, employing stratified splitting in the division of training and testing
data guarantees that every class, including those that are less common, is represented
in proportion, which facilitates a more reliable assessment of performance and training
outcomes.
Future studies may greatly benefit from the incorporation of generative modeling approaches,
such as Autoencoders or Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), to align discrepancies be­
tween simulated and real data distributions. Moreover, domain adaptation techniques
and transfer learning could enhance model efficacy during transitions from synthetic to
real datasets. One practical implementation of transfer learning would involve initially pre­
training the Seq2Seq model on a large synthetic dataset where labels are abundant and
controlled. Once pretraining stabilizes, the model could be fine­tuned using a smaller,
31
==PAGE:38==
curated set of real spectra. Specifically, the encoder and attention components could be
frozen or partially unfrozen to retain learned general representations, while the decoder is
adapted using real data with adjusted learning rates to allow for gradual domain­specific
calibration.
Our method heavily relies on deconvolution to extract amplitude and frequency informa­
tion from overlapping NMR signals. Deconvolution inherently assumes idealized condi­
tions such as symmetrical peak shapes, consistent baseline behavior, and limited noise.
These assumptions often do not hold true in real­world data, leading to inaccuracies in
peak extraction, amplitude estimation, and frequency assignments, which subsequently
affect the downstream classification performance. Limitations also arise from difficulties
in resolving closely overlapping signals, peak distortion due to baseline fluctuations, and
sensitivity to noise, which frequently compromise the accuracy of extracted features and,
consequently, classification results.
Furthermore, inaccuracies in estimating coupling constants due to noisy or overlapping
peaks pose a significant risk, potentially propagating errors through subsequent analyses.
Thus, reliance on conventional deconvolution techniques introduces a notable vulnerabil­
ity in the preprocessing pipeline.
The scarcity of complex multiplets in real datasets obstructs the establishment of resilient
decision boundaries, leading to reduced accuracy for these infrequent classes.
This
scarcity primarily arises from limitations within current NMR databases, including incom­
plete molecular coverage, inconsistent annotation standards, and insufficiently annotated
rare spectral cases. These database­related issues restrict the availability and reliability
of high­quality data required for effective model training and validation. Real­world NMR
databases frequently exhibit insufficient molecular coverage, inconsistent annotations,
and unidentified artifacts, requiring thorough preprocessing, curation, and standardiza­
tion for effective data­driven analysis. To address these issues, adopting an initiative sim­
ilar to the Global Natural Products Social (GNPS) molecular networking platform, which
significantly enhances mass spectrometry classification through comprehensive molecu­
lar networks and community­driven database curation, could substantially improve NMR
spectral annotation and classification.
Enhanced preprocessing techniques, such as refined baseline correction, adaptive noise
reduction, and peak identification algorithms, are expected to increase the reliability of
spectral analysis. However, these techniques are most useful when real spectra are in­
tended for manual inspection or integration into traditional pipelines. In the context of train­
ing deep learning models, it may be more beneficial to incorporate these distortions into
the synthetic data generation process rather than eliminate them from real data. Overcor­
recting distortions in real spectra can introduce artificial artifacts or erase subtle but mean­
ingful variations, potentially reducing the realism and diversity needed for robust model
training. Therefore, embedding baseline shifts, noise patterns, and peak asymmetries
into synthetic data can help models generalize better without introducing preprocessing­
induced biases.
Comparing our findings to the 1H spectra presented in Fischetti et al.[39], our approach im­
proves the handling of sequential dependencies through the integration of advanced atten­
tion mechanisms and embedding layers, representing a refinement of existing methodolo­
gies. Fischetti et al. employed a hybrid convolutional­LSTM network trained exclusively
on synthetic spectra to classify signal regions into multiplet classes. Their model achieved
high performance (e.g., >95% accuracy on synthetic data) but did not explicitly address
class imbalance or rare multiplet prediction. In contrast, our study incorporates focal loss,
32
==PAGE:39==
leading to precision on rare classes, and applies cross­domain testing to evaluate gener­
alization.
In the domain of compound identification, Wei et al[82]. proposed a pseudo­Siamese CNN
model that reached 99.8% accuracy on augmented test data and 91.7% on experimental
mixtures. However, their focus was on determining the presence of known compounds
rather than classifying individual multiplet types. Our work differs fundamentally by classi­
fying multiplet structures directly and working with embedding­enhanced attention models.
Leveraging transformer­based models, known for handling complex sequential relation­
ships, also presents promising avenues to further advance the automated interpretation of
NMR spectra. Our current architecture, which integrates multi­head attention, a key com­
ponent of transformer models, already demonstrates improvements in classifying over­
lapping and complex multiplets compared to simpler architectures. These gains suggest
that attention mechanisms contribute significantly to the model’s ability to manage long­
range dependencies across the spectral domain. While our implementation stops short of
using a full transformer framework, future studies could explore encoder­only or encoder­
decoder transformer variants to further enhance performance, especially in the context of
noisy or signal­dense regions. Future work should explore alternative or complementary
preprocessing approaches such as advanced noise modeling, improved baseline correc­
tion algorithms, and integration of generative models like Variational Autoencoders (VAEs)
to produce more realistic synthetic datasets.
Additionally, integrating data from complementary analytical methods, such as 2D NMR
or mass spectrometry­inspired basis functions, could significantly mitigate the depen­
dence on extensive spectral databases.
This would allow for the extraction of richer
structural information, enhancing annotation accuracy without exhaustive database ref­
erencing. Leveraging transformer­based models, known for handling complex sequential
relationships, also presents promising avenues to further advance the automated inter­
pretation of NMR spectra.

Conclusion
This thesis examined the viability of utilizing advanced deep learning NLP techniques,
particularly Seq2Seq models with attention mechanisms, embeddings, gradient clipping,
focal loss, and Conditional Random Fields (CRF), for the automatic classification of multi­
plicity in 1D NMR spectroscopy. The findings indicate that these models, especially those
utilizing multi­head attention and embeddings, proficiently manage sequential dependen­
cies in spectral data, exhibiting significant accuracy in both synthetic and actual datasets.
Models trained on synthetic data faced difficulties in generalizing to real­world data due
to intrinsic complexities and noise. However, the integration of focal loss and CRF lay­
ers significantly enhanced the model’s performance in practical applications, suggesting
progress toward dependable automated spectral analysis.
The potential applications of this work are vast. Improving domain adaptation using trans­
fer learning and generative modeling methods, like Variational Autoencoders (VAEs), may
effectively reconcile disparities between synthetic and real data distributions. Employing
sophisticated preprocessing techniques for enhanced baseline correction and noise miti­
gation, alongside specific data augmentation tactics, may further elevate model precision.
Furthermore, investigating transformer­based models, recognized for their exceptional ca­
pacity to manage long­range sequential interactions, may yield enhanced resilience and
efficiency for intricate spectral studies. This research establishes a robust basis for fu­
ture advancements in fully automated, precise, and scalable NMR spectral interpretation,
enabling activities such as molecular classification, structural elucidation, and biomarker
discovery.
