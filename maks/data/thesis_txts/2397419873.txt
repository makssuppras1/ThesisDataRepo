Abstract
Technology has evolved immensely during the past decade, bringing scalable cloud ser-
vices capable of handling thousands of users at a low cost. As most architectural software
paradigms are incapable of harnessing these powerful multi-core, in-memory, clustered
environments, a paradigm shift presented by the concept of reactive systems is proposed.
The paradigm of reactive systems based on the actor model enable engineers to build scal-
able, concurrent, distributed and message-driven applications capable of exploiting the
provided resources. Favoring distributed over non-distributed architectures introduces
crucial challenges in terms of correctness, as classic approaches based on centralized
database transactions (ACID) can not be relied on. This thesis focuses on the task of
guaranteeing responsiveness and correctness in reactive systems. Multiple concepts and
principles including terminology related to distributed systems, message driven architec-
tures, responsiveness, correctness, the actor model and associated actor frameworks must
be comprehended in order to complete this task. A case study (payroll system) is created
with the purpose of illustrating how a reactive system is built using the concept of actors.
Responsiveness is guaranteed by finding the optimal granularity of thoroughly designed
actors. Furthermore, it is essential that the message flow is optimized using applicable
respond patterns, proper message sizes and the concept of routers. Dedicated database ac-
tors must be implemented in order to throttle access to databases and to avoid concurrency
issues. Correctness is guaranteed by defining a set of invariants. These invariants are sat-
isfied by long lived transactions realized by the concept of sagas and by the properties
of actors processing messages sequentially although they communicate asynchronously.
Building reactive systems is hard, which is the reason for producing a guide to reactive
development based on best practice and findings gathered throughout the project.

1
Introduction
The growing demand for fast real-time data-driven applications has led to responsiveness,
elasticity and resiliency as primary business system requirements. Applications must be
structured carefully to meet these requirements. We are facing issues in terms of scaling
of today’s software. Architectural software paradigms of the past may not scale to meet
the requirements of today, and definitely not the future. At the same time we see that
technologies available for building the applications evolve at a rapid pace. We are able
to utilize clusters of processor cores on individual servers and clusters of servers as sin-
gle applications. Memory and disk space prices have declined and network speeds have
increased. Science has evolved, but most architectural software paradigms do not take ad-
vantage of the power of multicore, in-memory, clustered environments. To cope with this
challenge, we propose the paradigm of reactive systems [18] founded on the actor model
[8], enabling engineers to build scalable concurrent, distributed and message-driven ap-
plications capable of fully exploiting the provided resources.
1.1
Reactive systems
Reactive systems are defined by a set of principles, a way of thinking about system archi-
tectures in distributed environments, where concepts, design patterns and development
techniques are parts of a larger whole. Reactive systems use responsive, resilient, elastic
and message driven software architectures - as defined by the principles of the Reactive
Manifesto [18] - principles which meet the demands of today, and take advantage of the
maturity of present technology. Selling the benefits of reactive systems is fairly straight
==PAGE:24==
forward, but the ultimate challenge is to innovate the traditional approach to system design
and head for a true reactive direction. It is easy to convince an organization to buy into a
concept. It is a completely different story when it comes to changing culture, ultimately
adapting a paradigm shift in system design. Why would we take an interest in challenging
the way we have built software systems for years? An answer to the question could be
another question: What kind of system benefits are we trying to provide to our users?
It is fair to say that we want our systems to be responsive to user interactions, be able
to handle failures and remain available during outages of system components, perform
under varying load conditions, and finally be able to send, receive and route messages in
varying network conditions. These properties have become fundamental necessities, but
are usually dealt with late in the development process. They are not essential properties of
classical system design. They are on the other hand the very core of the traits in reactive
systems.
1.2
Project goals
This project will do a case study by designing a reactive, distributed, message driven
software system based on the principles defined by the Reactive Manifesto [18], and im-
plemented using a framework based on the actor model [8] in order to address the above-
mentioned challenges, which many software paradigms fail to overcome. This way, we
try to investigate to what extend it is possible guaranteeing responsiveness and correct-
ness in a reactive system and how it is achieved. The system intends to automate the
process of running payrolls, easing tasks of calculating tax, pension and salary. System
requirements and the surrounding domain are described in details in Chapter 3 on page 43.
In order to achieve responsiveness, the system must react to varying loads. This requires
an actor design which scales dynamically. To avoid bottlenecks and to gain performance,
we will find the optimal granularity and number of actors. Data consistency is hard to up-
hold when scaling is enforced. Therefore, it requires identification of possible execution
scenarios, and creation of strategies of how to handle them in order to gain correctness.

ambition is to design a system being able of handling excessive user loads (being respon-
sive), and do heavy computational work in terms of payroll calculation. Most importantly,
this must be done with correctness in mind. It is essential that data does not become incon-

2.1.1
Concurrency vs. parallelism
Concurrency and parallelism are very similar concepts, but differs on some essential
points. Concurrency means that multiple tasks make progress at the same time, but might
not be executing simultaneously. Therefore, tasks are sliced into smaller parts and ex-
ecuted alternately, making progress on each task in a gradual manner, as illustrated in

2.1.2
Race condition
Race condition occur when multiple threads can access shared data and try to modify
the data at the same time. Since the scheduling of threads is managed by the operating
system, it is not possible to know when and which thread are accessing the shared data at
a given time. Multiple threads modifying shared data concurrently can cause corrupted
states because the threads working on the data can be replaced at any time, which means
another thread can have changed the data when the first thread continues its work.
2.1.3
Synchronous vs. asynchronous
A method call is considered synchronous if the calling thread cannot proceed until the
method returns or throws an exception. In other words, the thread is unable to make
progress on other tasks since it has to wait for the synchronous call to return.
An asynchronous call, on the other hand, allows the calling thread to proceed working on
something else while waiting for the asynchronous call. When it returns, the thread can
continue working on the associated task.

2.1.4
Blocking vs. non-blocking
Blocking and non-blocking relates to the synchronous and asynchronous methods, since
blocking methods execute synchronously and non-blocking methods execute asynchronously.
Blocking occurs when a method call prevents a thread from further execution by retaining
it while waiting for the method to return. A non-blocking method, on the other hand, will
release the thread and thereby allow it to do other tasks, making better utilization of the
CPU.
The blocking issue
Figure 2.3 illustrates the blocking issue. For simplicity, consider a single-core processor
having to execute two threads. Both threads want to run simultaneously, but since the
CPU can only handle one at a time, thread 1 is chosen to run first. Thread 1 has to do
some I/O work by invoking a blocking method to a database, illustrated by the grey box.
While thread 1 waits for the blocking call to return, the CPU is wasted doing nothing,
while in fact thread 2 could have run, illustrated by the stribed box. Instead thread 2 gets
to run after thread 1 is complete, increasing the total execution time. If the processor had
released thread 1, it would have been available to run thread 2 while thread 1 was waiting
for a response from the database, consequently reducing the total execution time.

2.2
Message driven architectures
A message DrivenArchitecture defines a structured application consisting of autonomous
components which communicate via messages. Message driven architectures are com-
mon in distributed systems as components are deployed on different servers which have
to interact with each other. A message contains relevant data in relation to its purpose,
and should be as simple and specialized as possible. Message driven architectures guar-
antee messages to be sent at least once, which means that the message handler must cope
with messages potentially being received twice. No matter how many times a message is
processed, the outcome must be the same, idempotency.

2.2.1
Message driven architectures vs. event driven architectures
Message driven architectures are often referred to as Event DrivenArchitectures (EDA’s),
but they differentiate, as illustrated in Fig. 2.4. A message is a piece of data which is
sent to a specific destination aka recipient, whereas an event is a signal emitted from a
component upon reaching a given state [18]. In message driven architectures, addressable
recipients react to incoming messages, otherwise stay idle. In EDA’s, a set of of listeners
aka event handlers are connected to one or more event sources in such a way that they
are invoked when a given event is emitted. EDA’s focus on addressable event sources,
while message driven architectures focus on addressable recipients. Resilience is rather
hard to achieve in EDA’s, which is why the reactive manifesto favors message driven
architectures over EDA’s. Messages in message driven architectures can be interpreted
in many ways, being the reason for the need to identify the different kinds.

Kinds of messages
Each type of message must show intent, indicating the reason of the sender to commu-
nicate with the receiver [11]. Actors interact using three kinds of messages as illustrated

perform a given kind of action, it will do so by sending a command message. If the re-

was performed, it will do so by sending an event message. Finally, if a large body of data
has been requested, it will be fulfilled by a document message.

2.3
Reactive systems
The concept of reactive systems is not new, and can be traced back to the 70’s and 80’s.
As opposed to systems implementing typical input-output relations, a reactive system im-
plements interactive behaviour with its environment [7]. A reactive system cannot be
specified in isolation from its environment: a computation in a reactive system is usually
non-terminating, and concurrent reactions cannot simply be handled as a progression of
input-output computations. The philosophy behind reactive systems was ahead its time
(different focus), extremely limited by contemporary hardware, but in a true cyclical man-
ner, it has now become relevant because of technology evolution. Due to different inter-
pretations of the concept, it is important to stress that this thesis refers to reactive systems
as being the principles, defined by the reactive manifesto [18].
2.3.1
The reactive manifesto
The reactive systems concept was given an awakening when the reactive manifesto was
published in 2013. Originated by Jonas Bonér [17], and enhanced/reviewed by Erik Mei-
jer, Martin Odersky, GregYoung, Martin Thompson, Roland Kuhn, James Ward and Guil-
laume Bort to version 2, released and tagged in 2014. The motivation behind the mani-
festo is the evolution in technology, consisting of cloud-based, multi-core, runtime envi-
ronments, but also on the growing needs for systems providing low latency, high through-
put/availability, being able to scale almost linearly. Circumstances and traits which de-
mand applications to be written in a fundamentally different way than most programmers
are used to. This has led to a burst of different techniques, tools and frameworks, ad-
dressing these new requirements. Some are old and proven, but to a large extent forgot-
ten techniques, while other are unusual and innovative. Regardless, most of them have a
common theme, as they facilitate applications to:
• React to events/messages: Enables the capability to react to load, failure and users
• React to load: Scalability should be prioritized over single-user performance
• React to failure: Create software being able to recover at all levels
• React to users: Combine the above traits in order to provide an interactive user experience
Table 2.1: A common theme: Reaction
Interestingly, these techniques and tools have risen across communities, platforms and
industries. Although diversity is a positive thing, it has led to communication challenges
and missed opportunities for collaboration and integration. The reactive manifesto ar-

The lack of a common vocabulary has made it difficult to bridge between the different
communities, inspire each other, and ensure consistency between techniques and tools.
The reactive manifesto wanted to come up with a name for these kind of applications,
similar to concepts like REST and NoSQL, and to create a natural vocabulary to de-
scribe them, including technical aspects as well as business values. The reason being, the
need to bring communities together, and make vendors and developers understand each
other. Before the reactive manifesto, the typical way of expressing such systems would
be with a blend of technical and business-related buzzwords, for instance: loosely cou-
pled, asynchronous, non-blocking, highly-available, concurrent, distributed, low latency,
high throughput, push instead of pull. Although most words are comprehended by peo-
ple in the software business, they do necessarily not help communication. The reactive
manifesto specifies the most important ones in a common vocabulary.

2.3.2.1
Responsive
Responsiveness means being able to respond to incoming request in a proper manner, and
to detect problems quickly in order to deal with them effectively. Responsive systems
are built upon a foundation based on rapid and consistent response times, leading to ele-
vated user confidence and inspires to additional interaction. Responsiveness brings speed
under varying conditions, like third party outages or peaking system loads, but depends
on resilience and scalability . A message-driven architecture provides the ground for a
responsive system.
2.3.2.2
Resilient
Resiliency is all about being responsive under failure, an essential system property, which
needs to be designed for, as opposed to something that is added retrospectively. One im-
portant trait which resiliency brings, is the ability to fully recover from failure, to self heal.
This depends on component isolation and failure containment, in order to prevent failures
spreading to adjoining components, and thereby avoid fatal, intensified failure scenarios.
The key to achieve self-healing systems is to let failures be: contained, wrapped as mes-
sages and sent to relevant components, that act as supervisors, and handled in a safe
context externally from the failed component. The message driven architecture enables
this quality, avoiding strongly coupled, frail, deeply nested synchronous call chains. By
decoupling failure management from the call chain, the client is liberated from the re-
sponsibility of handling server related failures.
2.3.2.3
Elastic
Elasticity denotes being responsive under load, indicating that the system throughput dy-
namically scales up or down, as well as in and out, when resources are added or removed.
Elasticity is crucial in order to meet varying demands, and is an important element re-
quired to utilize cloud computing, which brings resource- and cost-efficiency pay-per-
use plans in transparent environments. This trait requires designs with no central bottle
necks or contention points, represented by shared or replicated components, which handle
distributed input. The facilitator for this is the concept of location transparency, where
physical locations of components are abstracted away, so no matter where they reside, the
communication method stays the same, attainable by messaging. This facilitates a general
programming abstraction with the same semantics beyond all dimensions, scaling from
virtual CPU cores to clusters/data centres.

2.3.2.4
Message driven
The most important principle of the reactive manifesto is message driven, relying on asyn-
chronous message-passing to create a boundary between system components, leading to
loose coupling, isolation and location transparency. This enables concurrency for dis-
tribution and mobility. This boundary also facilitates the means of delegating failures as
messages. Message-passing empowers load management, flow control and elasticity by
monitoring message queues and implementing back-pressure when needed. This kind of
non-blocking communication allows components to only absorb resources while being
active, that being, having messages to process. Location transparency makes it possible
for monitoring components to use identical semantics across clusters as well as within a
single machine. Message-passing comes with gains in the shape of abstraction and de-
sign simplicity, but introduces loss of control, which requires dealing with the reality and
constraints of distributed systems, including partial errors, failure detection, dropped/du-
plicated messages and eventual consistency among others. These challenges needs to be
dealt with, instead of masking them behind an abstraction.

2.4
Responsiveness and correctness
This section briefly introduces the essential concepts of responsiveness and correctness,
as we in Chapter 4 on page 81 and Chapter 5 on page 89, respectively, show how they
are achieved in the payroll system. Both concepts are essential in order to succeed in
enterprise software development, but not trivial to master.
2.4.1
Responsiveness
A responsive system is fast and efficient, but it is not all about speed. The key to respon-
siveness is the ability to respond to incoming requests within a short period of time, even
though the system is under high load. The most important factors are that all resources
of the system should be optimally harnessed, and that the system is still able to respond
with statuses on the running tasks.
Responsiveness is not to be confused with performance, as responsiveness can be very
poor, even on fast a fast machine, depending on the design. A responsive system brings
user feedback at all time, rather than performance hidden behind long delays.
2.4.2
Correctness
In theoretical computer science, correctness of a program is achieved when the program
is correct with respect to a given specification. Partial-correctness is achieved when the
program returns a correct result, whereas total-correctness requires that the program ter-
minates. Correctness depends on data consistency. Non-distributed systems usually rely
on database transactions in order to achieve this consistency. Correctness in distributed
systems is harder to achieve. The larger amount of subsystems, the higher the probabil-
ity, that at any given time, one or more of those subsystems are experiencing some sort
of error or failure. Distributed systems are difficult to develop for two reasons: partial
failures and asynchrony. Partial failure is the concept of components failing, leading to
incomplete data or results. Asyncrony is based on non-determinism of timing and order-
ing within the system. Distributed systems bring challenges as data and resources are
accessed concurrently. Sagas [6] and invariants are introduced in order to achieve cor-
rectness.
2.4.2.1
Invariants
Invariants are conditions which can be relied upon during a computational flow. They are
also seen as logical assertions, held to be true throughout the time of execution. Proof of
correctness heavily rely on invariants.

Sagas
The concept of sagas was originally defined by Hector Garcia-Molina and Kenneth Salem
in the paper, Sagas [6]. The term, saga, relates to a mechanism managing long-running
business processes. The paper recognizes that business processes are usually defined by
multiple steps, each of which involves a transaction or a request, and that overall consis-
tency is achieved by combining these individual steps into one big distributed transaction,
based on the classic ACID model with two-phase-commit. However, distributed transac-
tions impact the overall performance and concurrency, as locks are held for the period of
the distributed transaction. Sagas act as controllers facilitating rules for routing jobs to
collaborating subsystems, and allowing these subsystems to backtrack and take corrective
action in case of failures. A saga breaks down the distributed transaction into a sequence
of small steps, referred to as a long lived transaction. Steps can be performed in atomic
transactions, but the the overall consistency is controlled by the saga, as illustrated in

2.5
The actor model
To model a reactive system we introduce The Actor Model, a mathematical theory, that
treats actors as universal primitives of digital computation, motivated by physical laws
[8]. The Actor Model has become relevant as it links conceptually to the reactive Man-
ifesto, and is defined by principles which can be implemented in such a way that re-
sources can be exploited, meaning that the computing abilities are used to their fullest.
The actor model can be used as a framework for theoretical understanding of concurrency,
and works as a basis for multiple software languages and frameworks. The actor model
is built on non-determinism, assuming asynchronous communication. Non-determinism
may sound unacceptable, but it enables concurrency, and together with the idea of reli-
able addresses to stateful isolated processes, it allows actors to be decoupled in space and
time, entailing distribution and mobility. The actor model is defined by a list of traits,
shown in Table 2.2.
• Actors rather than objects
• Asynchronous messages replace method calls
• Communication using immutable messages
• Actors have unique addresses
• Messages are kept in mailboxes and processed sequentially by actors
• Actors create other actors, and when they do, they receive back the new actor addresses
• Asynchronous and non-blocking
• Each actor has its own process, being a lightweight user-space thread, not an OS process
• Each actor has mutable state,
• Actor state is kept internal, no shared memory →no locking required + race conditions are reduced
• Work proceeds concurrently
• Failures are isolated in components
Table 2.2: Traits of the actor model
2.5.1
History
The actor model was defined by computer scientist Carl Hewitt in 1973 (Artificial In-
telligence A Universal Modular ACTOR Formalism for Artificial Intelligence) [8], and
refined by computer scientist Gul Agha in 1986, A Model of Concurrent Computation in
Distributed Systems [1]. The first real implementation came with the introduction of the
Erlang language in 1986, developed by JoeArmstrong, applied by tele company Ericsson
in The Open Telecom Platform project, 1996. In the aftermath of the cloud computing,
AKKA, an actor framework based on Java, developed by Jonas Bonér, was released, 2009.
Finally, in 2016, Proto.Actor, a light-weight, cross platform, actor framework running in
.NET, GO, Java and Kotlin, developed by Roger Johansson, saw the light of day.

2.5.2
A higher level of abstraction
Actor systems are relatable to the physical world. System designs are simplified, as we are
able to produce system components which interact very much like we humans do. Using
this intuitive and simple approach allows for designing and implementing elegant, effi-
cient applications and components, that know how self-heal when failures occur. Building
actor based systems has a dramatic influence on the overall software engineering process.
Designs and implementations based on actors empowers software architects to focus more
on the core functionality, and focus less on technical details required to construct and cul-
tivate distributed systems. Systems built upon high levels of concurrency are typically
associated with a lot of low-level plumbing, and very technical programming techniques,
burdensome to master. All these technicalities tend to move the attention away from im-
portant business use cases. Actor systems are built with a higher level of abstraction, as
concurrency is dealt with and built into the actor model [10].

2.5.3
Actors
An actor is a primitive unit, and can literally be any kind of a computational entity: a
human being, a role, an object etc. Actors do not differentiate very much from objects
as known from object oriented programming, and yet they do. Objects receive mes-
sages (method calls) and act upon them, computing something related to the message
(the method call). Actors receive dedicated asynchronous messages, do work, and reply
with another message, indicating that jobs are done. Actors are lightweight, scalable and
extremely fast. They have dedicated processes (not to be mistaken with OS processes),
non-shared internal states, and dedicated mail boxes, from which they process incoming
messages sequentially. Actors react, rather than act. If actors were to be given a more
descriptive dimension, they can be compared to narrow application services, domain en-
tities or aggregates. These terms are taken from Domain Driven Design (DDD) [5], in
which actors fit very well.

2.5.3.1
What do actors do?
The behavior of actors is defined by three axioms, listed in Table 2.3. Actors interact
with each other, the same way as humans, by exchanging messages, and like in the real
world, they do not stall, waiting for responses. An actor may expect a response, but
it does not block everything out in the period of waiting. By decoupling the sending
actor from the message it sends, the actor model enables asynchronous communication
and control structures of passing messages. Messages can be compared to conversations,
text-messages, phone calls, letters, emails, pictures, body language etc. Actors react to a
set of messages in different ways, depending on a given behaviour (state). This is also
comparable to human behavior. In a state of happiness, a compliment can be responded
to with a smile, but in a state of insecurity, it can be responded to with sarcasm. Messages
are queued upon arrival at the destination, in a mail box, and processed sequentially in a
FIFO (First In First Out) manner. In response to a message, an actor:
• Send - Actors send or forward messages to other actors
• Create - Actors can create other actors
• Become - Actors mutate state (in order to handle the next message)

overall responsibility. Thus, leaving a part of the responsibility to a child. Actors created

Supervisors and workers
Another way of thinking about the parent/child relationship, is through a supervisor/worker
relationship. Like in the real world, bringing a child into the world comes with a respon-

of their workers. If a worker experience complications, it notifies the supervisor about
the failure using a message, and finally suspends itself. The supervisor then decides how
to deal with the problem, and how to get the worker back into a healthy state. The sus-
pension can potentially influence the work of other workers, so many things may be taken
into consideration. Several strategies for rectifying suspended workers are discussed in
Section 2.6.2.4 on page 37. In the nature of the actor model, supervisors are able to send
asynchronously messages to each of their workers concurrently and independently.

2.5.7
System scaling
One of the strongest characteristics of actor systems, is the ability to scale, in order to
meet varying demands, and stay responsive. It all comes down to this. The paradigm
shift, the hard work, the challenging design requirements, they all lead to easily scal-
able systems. Scaling is either done vertically (up), or horizontally (out). Regardless the
chosen direction, the actor model is great at taking advantage of the added resources [3].
2.5.7.1
Scaling up
Scaling up means increasing the overall power of the single server environment which
the actor system is running in. This is done by adding more CPU’s or cores, as well
as increasing the memory. The purpose of this is to enable the system to work faster
than before. The actor model automatically distributes load across threads, cores and
CPU’s. This technique is a simple solution, and it does not require any kind of software
architecture reconstruction, but is not cost-efficient and limited by expensive hardware.
Balancing dispatchers
Balancing dispatchers are used to exploit the serving environment to its full potential,
making sure that a set of actors do not plunder up the resources, ending up starving the
others, but also to ensure resources to be harnessed optimally, as illustrated in Fig. 2.21.
Dispatchers are responsible for selecting actors, and assign them to the CPU. Dispatchers
are assisted by thread pools, and are easily shared by different types of actors. The dis-
patcher picks an actor and assigns it to a thread from the pool. After processing a given
number of messages, the actor is relinquished from the thread.

Scaling out
Scaling out means spreading the application over multiple environments. This is done

With the advent of virtual machines hosted in cloud based infrastructure, it is possible to
distribute work and responsibility across many smaller servers, achieving the same effect
as with vertical scaling, but potentially at a fraction of the the price. Horizontal scaling
is not limited, as opposed to vertical scaling, but there are trade offs, as the amount of
communication is increased.
Routers
To accomplish a horizontally scaled system, we make use of the concept of router ac-
tors (part of most actor frameworks). Routers know where to route messages, and as the
overall system is divided into smaller sub systems, it is essential that messages are routed
to the proper sub systems. The system as a whole may reflect a company, where the
sub systems represent the company departments (A,B and C), as illustrated in Fig. 2.22.

to actor C. This is done using consistent hashing, which is a distributed hashing scheme
capable of routing messages based on some value in the message. Since it uses a hash
function, a message will always be routed to the same destination if the value stays the
same. Messages are always meant to be handled by a specific sub systems, and consistent
hashing ensures that the message is routed to the same sub system every time by hashing
on the company department. A sub system can be based on a cluster, discussed in Sec-
tion 2.5.7.4 on the facing page, of actors (server nodes), which makes them able to scale
further individually. The consistent hashing algorithm can be replaced by a Round-robin

2.5.7.3
Remote actors and location transparency
Actor systems are based on actors, but actors vary in size and may be located in different
places. Technically, we differentiate between local and remote actors. Remote actors are
deployed some place else. Those places could be on another system hosted on the same
node, or on a completely different node, which is usually the case. Remote actors com-
municate the same way as local actors, but messages are being sent on the wire, so they
need to be serialized for transport. A protocol for sending and receiving the same type
of message, is required. Actor systems like AKKA [13] use platform specific serializers,
whereas Proto.Actor [37] uses Google proto buffers [25], which are cross platform. It
does not matter whether an actor is located locally or remotely, because the actor model
is built upon location transparency. It might matter for performance though. Location
transparency breaks down physical barriers, as small actors running locally can be re-
placed by large actors running remotely in a clustered formation changing configurations
rather than code.

2.5.9
Persistent actors
Persistent actors save history of events, events being actions performed, messages re-
ceived etc. Because of the decoupled and asynchronous nature, the actor model relies on
recoverable state, especially when essential computational scenarios are interrupted by
unforeseen failures. A persistent actor acts like other actors but persists the list of events
happened over time (event sourcing). In case of a crash, the actor is restarted and re-
covered to its current state by replaying events, illustrated in Fig. 2.26 on the following
page. Events are unique to the given actor and stored in a database journal. This feature
is applicable for sagas, discussed in Section 2.4.2.2 on page 14 and depends on actors
being deterministic. Persistent actors are different from CRUD actors. Persistent actors
hold states of event streams. CRUD actors hold states of entities or aggregates. However,
CRUD actors can also be persistent.
2.5.10
Event sourcing and snapshotting
Persistent actors utilize event sourcing or event sourcing paired with snapshotting. Event
sourcing becomes problematic in those actors which have complex lifetimes caused by
large amounts of events. Resuming is expensive in case of failure, as all events have
to be recovered. To overcome this struggle, the concept of snapshotting is employed.
Snapshotting is the processes of persisting entire states contrary to persisting the changes
of states, event sourcing. Figure 2.26 on the following page shows how an actor persists
one snapshot and subsequently persists three events before crashing. Upon resume, the
most recent snapshot is applied, succeeded by three events. This way, an actor can be
resumed with one snapshot and a few number of events rather than thousands.

2.6
Actor technologies
Creating and managing concurrent and distributed applications has shown to be very chal-
lenging, and the need for a higher level of abstraction to get away from threads and locks
has only become bigger. This has led to several frameworks and programming languages
being developed over the years to deal with concurrent and distributed programming.
2.6.1
Preceding languages and frameworks
The following sections mention some of the popular actor technologies, starting with one
of the earliest actor programming languages up until present frameworks.
2.6.1.1
Erlang
Erlang, one of the earliest languages designed to handle concurrency in software appli-
cations, was developed in the 80’s to be used in highly concurrent telecommunication
systems [4]. The language has been maintained and improved over the decades and is
still used in today’s smartphones and by companies like Facebook and WhatsApp [23].
Since then, a lot of new frameworks have seen the light of the day of which some of the
popular are Orleans [32] and Akka [13].
2.6.1.2
Orleans
Orleans [32], which is created by Microsoft and running on the .NET platform, is an actor
model framework used extensively in Azure and by an increasing number of companies.
Orleans was the first to introduce the concept of virtual actors, which removes the concern
of distributed resource management. The term virtual means that an actor always exists,
virtually. Therefore, virtual actors cannot explicitly be created or destroyed, but instead
its lifecycle is automatically managed by the framework. This means the physical location
of the actors is abstracted away from the application code, which greatly simplifies the
development.
2.6.1.3
Akka
Another popular framework for building concurrent and distributed applications with the
actor model is Akka [13], which is used by large companies such as Intel, PayPal and
Amazon. Akka offers a lot of extensions which makes it easy to plug in new features
to the framework as well as to integrate it to third-party libraries. Unlike Orleans, Akka
does not support virtual actors, but instead uses non-virtual actors like Erlang, where
the lifecycle and physical placement of actors are managed by the developer. Akka was
CONCEPTS AND PRINCIPLES
31
==PAGE:54==
originally created by Jonas Bonér to run on a JVM with language support for Java and
Scala, but was later ported to run on the .NET platform and was named Akka.NET.
Both Orleans and Akka have their benefits and drawbacks. In particular, one comes with
virtual actors and the other without. There is no support for both approaches in the two
frameworks. Furthermore, picking one of the two locks the application into a specific
platform, which is best to avoid in this ever changing world of technology. This leads to
a yet another actor model framework called Proto.Actor [37].
2.6.2
The Proto.Actor framework
Proto.Actor [37] is a cross-platform actor model framework running on .NET Core [30].
The framework is asynchronous and distributed by default, and provides a high level
of abstraction for concurrent programming, omitting the developer from having to deal
with explicit locking and thread management. Proto.Actor was created by the original
creator of Akka.NET [13], Roger Johansson, who believed Akka.NET was faced with
too many design challenges, and therefore wanted to do a more lightweight actor model
framework. Proto.Actor is built on top of proven standards, letting the framework focus
on what the actor model is about: concurrency and distributed programming. For this
reason, existing building blocks are used for many of the secondary aspects such as for
serialization, streams and network communication, taking advantage of proven stability
and performance. The vision of Proto.Actor is to keep the framework simple and build on
standards, which gives highly understandable, maintainable and extendable code. Using
open-source standards allows it to communicate between different platforms, making the
framework platform independent and thus giving it a huge advantage compared to earlier
actor model frameworks. This means that Proto actors are not limited to only commu-
nicate to other Proto actors, but can communicate to any actors on any system. This is
for instance not the case for Akka actors, who are only able to communicate with other
Akka actors. Finally, Proto.Actor provides support for both virtual actors and non-virtual
actors, which brings more flexibility than other actor model frameworks. Having both ap-
proaches available enables the developer to freely choose what suits the application best,
or even use both, under a common framework. This is certainly an advantage because
it eliminates the decision on whether to go with one instead of the other when deciding
on which framework to use. Due to the aforementioned reasons we believe Proto.Actor
is most suitable choice for our payroll system. A more detailed walkthrough, and how
the concrete usage of Proto.Actor comply with the conceptual aspects, is explained in the
following sections.

2.6.2.4
Supervision
Proto.Actor uses the parent/child relationship between actors to manage failure handling.
The relations form a natural hierarchy as previously illustrated in Fig. 2.28 on page 33.
CONCEPTS AND PRINCIPLES
35
==PAGE:58==
In a supervision context, the figure can be interpreted as a supervision hierarchy, where a
parent acts as supervisor for all its children, meaning that it is responsible for monitoring
and handling failures that occur in its children.
Directives
When an actor fails, i.e. by throwing an exception, it gets suspended along with all its
children, which means that it will not process any messages, making the actor unavailable.
When that happens, the actor escalates the failure to its supervisor who then decides how
to handle the failing child. Escalation refers to the supervision hierarchy and means that
the supervisor gets notified of the failing child. When a failure is escalated to a supervisor,
it is the supervisor’s job to translate the failure into an appropriate directive. A directive
is an action the supervisor can apply on the failing child in order to bring the system back
to a non-failing state. Proto.Actor provides four directives to perform on a failing child,
listed in Table 2.4. A supervisor can choose any of the directives depending on the type
of failure, however the default directive in Proto.Actor is to restart the child.
• Resume: the child actor is resumed, keeping its internal state
• Restart: the child actor is stopped and then recreated, resetting its internal state
• Stop: the child actor is stopped
• Escalate: the failure is escalated to the next supervisor in the hierarchy, thereby failing itself
Table 2.4: Supervision directives
Resuming an actor means starting it with the same state it had when the failure occurred.
This directive is also applied to all of the failing actor’s children, recursively, bringing all
suspended actors back to process messages without losing the internal states. Restarting
an actor is equivalent to when it is first started. It will be stopped, i.e. cleared from
memory, and then recreated, resetting it to its starting state. Again, this directive is applied
recursively to all its children. Stopping an actor will simply stop it and all its children,
recursively. Escalate will send the failure upwards in the supervision hierarchy. Since a
supervisor is also a child of another supervisor higher up the hierarchy, it can choose to
fail itself, consequently escalating the failure further up. All directives except stop will
keep the messages in the mail box.
When escalating a failure, the chain of supervisors stops at some point. As explained
in Section 2.6.2.1 on page 33, all custom actors will become children of the User actor,
who therefore is the top level supervisor and hence the last actor receiving a failure upon
36

==PAGE:59==
escalating it. User and System are supervised by Root, who is the grand-parent of all
actors. Since Root does not have a parent, it does not have a supervisor. Therefore, it will
be terminated upon any type of exception.

in case of a failure.
• One-for-one: the directive is only applied to the failing child
• All-for-one: if one child fails, the directive is applied to all children

3.1.2
Product perspective and functions
Customers sign up to the payroll system, and periodically maintain static master data and
more dynamic payroll data related to their employees. Payrolls in draft mode are ap-
proved and then scheduled for nightly runs. Using the payroll system, customers are able
to outsource a painful process, with a predictable cost. The Payroll system automates the
tasks of running payrolls meaning calculating tax, pension, expenses, deductions based
on gross income, resulting in net income. Finally, it handles payroll results by transfer-
ring salary and tax payouts, generating digital payslips, as well as reporting statistically
data, required by the government. Customers interact with the system using a simple user
interface, and then let the system take over the repeated and time consuming work.
3.1.2.1
User characteristics
Users of the payroll system usually have financial authority. They are employed as man-
agers, accountants or office assistants etc, either responsible for their own business, or
have been given the task of overseeing salary payouts for a given business.

Domain model
The domain model aims to provide a conceptual overview of the entities comprising the
payroll system. The glossary in Appendix A.11 on page 143 gives a short description of
the entities, however in order to facilitate a better understanding, the following sections
provide a more detailed description of the domain and entities. As for a concrete example

3.1.6.1
Domain areas
Figure 3.5 on the next page shows the domain model and the associations between the
entities. The entities are grouped into three areas named MasterData, PayrollData and
PayrollCalculation, illustrated by the dotted boxes, each representing a different concep-
tual aspect of the domain.
MasterData
MasterData consists of all data belonging to employers and employees, including infor-
mation about salary, tax and pension for each employee. This data is static in the sense
that it does not have to change between different payroll runs, but can simply be reused.
In other words, it represents a specific state of the business data used in the payroll cal-
culation, and this state is used until changed. Situations where this data might change is
when an address of an employer needs to be updated, or when an employee gets a pay
raise.
PayrollData
PayrollData comprises the data belonging to a specific payroll. A payroll represents a
pay period within a company, and contains all salary relevant information for that spe-
cific period, typically lasting a month. In relation to MasterData, this data is considered
dynamic because it constantly accumulates over time within a given pay period, and a
new payroll is created when a new period begins. This means a payroll is completely
independent from the previous payroll and cannot be used between different runs.
PayrollCalculation
PayrollCalculation consists of the results generated by running a payroll, i.e. calculating
salary, tax, deductions etc. for each employee. This data is calculated by using data from
both MasterData and PayrollData, and represents the final payslips for the employees,
containing pay, tax amount, pension etc. for the given period. This data is final and
cannot be changed afterwards, as it defines a truth and acts as documentation for previous
payroll calculations.

3.1.6.2
Domain entities
The following is a detailed description of the entities and the associations among them.
The entities are listed in a top-down order, starting with MasterData, then PayrollData
and finally PayrollCalculation.
Employer
An employer represents a business with its associated employees, and contains relevant
information of the business such as name, category, address and e-mail. An employer
is also considered the customer of the system, because the business represented as the
employer pays to use the system to manage its salaries. The employer is created when a
business signs up as a customer.
Employee
An employee is a person working for an employer, and contains personal information of
the employee such as name, address, social security number etc. An employee can only
work for one employer, but an employer can have many employees. The employer is
responsible for creating and managing its employees, e.g. when a new person is hired,
the employer creates the new employee in the system with the related information.
Configuration
A configuration represents a state defining the pension, taxcard and salary that is active
and used at a given payroll run. Although an employee can be associated to multiple
configurations, it is only associated to one current, which is the currently active config-
uration, illustrated with the qualified association in Fig. 3.5 on page 50. If either one of
the pension, taxcard and salary changes, the current configuration becomes part of the
configuration history, and a new current configuration is created based on the previous
one and the associated change. This provides history of previous configurations, bringing
traceability into the system by being able to trace back in time which configuration was
active at a given payroll run.
Pension
A pension is one of the three elements constituting a configuration, and consists of a
provider, a percentage rate and whether it is active. When a payroll is run, the pension
is used to calculate how much of the salary should be paid in pension to the associated
employee.

==PAGE:74==
TaxCard
A tax card is also part of the configuration and defines the deduction amount and tax
percentage rate when calculating the salary.
Salary
Salary is the last element in the configuration and represents either a monthly or hourly
salary, thus it cannot represent both at the same time. Together with the tax card and
payroll data, it determines the net income for a given employee when running the payroll.
Payroll
A payroll defines a pay period for an associated employer, and contains all related infor-
mation in regard to salary for that specific period. The payroll is associated to multiple
payslips, each representing an employee in the business. Furthermore, it holds relevant
information such as start- and end date for the pay period, a run date, information relating
the payroll to an employer, and a state. A new payroll is always created after a payroll
is approved, making the approved payroll ready to be processed. During approval of a
payroll, all the payslips are associated to the current configuration of the respective em-
ployee, ensuring that modifications in the configuration afterwards will not change the
outcome of the approved payroll. A payroll can either be in state ”Draft”, ”Approved” or
”Processed”.
Payslip
A payslip is associated with an employee and belongs to a specific payroll. It acts as a
container for all the salary specific entries belonging to the employee during the period
defined by the payroll. When a payroll is created, a payslip for each employee is cre-
ated, initially with no entries. However during the pay period, entries are added to the
payslip, which together with the configuration of the employee is used to calculate the
final payslip.
PayslipEntry
A payslip entry is a salary posting representing either an expense, deduction, income
or hourly income. A payslip entry contains the type, category, date added and amount.
During a pay period, entries are added to the payslip associated with the employee, in
order to keep track of how the salary should be calculated in the end of the period. If the
salary of an employee is a fixed amount, an entry is added immediately after the payroll
is created, however if an employee is paid by the hour, the time registrations are steady
accumulated to the payslip during the pay period.

Expense
An expense is added to the payslip if an employee buys something the business needs to
pay for. An expense therefore represents an amount the employee needs to have reim-
bursed.
Income
An income represents a fixed amount of salary, usually measured on a monthly basis. It
contains the number of hours the employee works in the period defined by the payroll.
When a payroll is created, an income entry is immediately added to the payslip in order
to prepare the payroll for the next run.
HourlyIncome
Hourly income represents a time registration for an employee paid by the hour. Besides
containing number of hours, it also contains amount per hour. This entry is added contin-
uously to the payslip when an employee registers working hours.
Deduction
A deduction represents an item an employee buys through the salary and can either be
before or after taxes, depending on whether the item is taxable or not.
PayrollResult
A payroll result represents the result of a payroll run and is associated to the payroll that
was basis for the run. It contains much of the same information as the payroll such as the
period, run date and employer information. The payroll result is associated to multiple
payslip results, each representing an employee.
PayslipResult
A payslip result represents the results of the payroll run for a specific employee. It acts as
a container for all the payslip entry results generated during the calculation, and contains
the net income and other basic information of the employee, such as name, address and
social security number.
PayslipEntryResult
A payslip entry result arises when combining data from MasterData and PayrollData dur-
ing the calculation. A payslip entry result represents different postings such as amount of
tax to pay, net income and tax deduction, which together comprises the final results on
the payslip.

3.2
Design
Based on the requirements specification, a design is accomplished, expressing the system
from top to bottom, starting with general and preliminary thoughts about software archi-
tecture and software design. Further on, it describes static aspects of the system, like the
overall structure, components, actor hierarchies, database design as well as more dynamic
behavior . This design section will focus on a few, yet essential parts of the system, given
that a complete and thorough description will be overwhelming, adding very little extra
value.
3.2.1
Software architecture
A good software architecture, being a structure of structures [2], is essential to secure a
transparent and maintainable implementation. For several decades, the n-tier architecture
has been the favoured approach, and a text-book example of how to structure applications
in tightly coupled presentation, logic and data tiers, with dependencies to infrastructural
elements. We discard the classic n-tier architecture, and replace it with the onion archi-
tecture, as illustrated in Fig. 3.6 on the next page.
3.2.1.1
The onion architecture
Jeffrey Palermo introduced the onion architecture [36] in 2008 as an alternative to the
classic tiered architectures. The onion architecture favours the domain, and focuses on
controlling coupling. Onion layers can only depend on more internal layers. Coupling is
toward the center.
Layers
The onion is built upon a core, consisting of application services, domain services and
the domain model layers. The core is application-driven with no respect to infrastructure.
At the very center, we find the domain model, being a set of essential business entities
and rules. The domain model also holds repository interfaces defining the functionality
in relation to data manipulation. More externally, we find the domain services layer. Do-
main services have little yet important responsibility. They can be seen as helpers and
communication mediators between domain entities. Most externally in the core we find
the application services layer. Application services act as an application API providing
the use case functionality, hence the near edge location. Outside the core, we find a layer
split into groups of UI, Infrastructure, API and Tests. Groups are not required, they can
be added if necessary, and can easily depend on each other, as they are located in the
same layer. The UI and API groups are system interfaces which act as the boundary to
54

==PAGE:77==
users. They simply present the use case functionality provided by the core, in a proper
way. The Tests group consists of tests belonging to code in all layers. The last group
is the Infrastructure. Infrastructure is considered technology specific, and is seen as a
concrete implementation of the abstractions defined in the core e.g. a SMTP mail ser-
vice, a SQL repository or a NLog logging service. Infrastructural implementations are
loosely coupled, and thereby easily replaced by other technologies, if required. Finally,
most externally we find the dependency resolution layer, providing an abstraction for in-
stantiating interfaces to specific infrastructural implementations at run time. The onion
architecture relies on dependency injection to achieve a completely decoupled nature.

3.2.2
Software design
Software should be closely linked to the business environment, and not built upon ran-
dom definitions. That is why Domain Driven Design is proposed as a tool to deliver a
maintainable product, closely related with the domain.
3.2.2.1
Domain Driven Design
Initially introduced by Eric Evans in 2004 [5], Domain Driven Design (DDD) concen-
trates on the domain by including domain experts in the development process, using an
CASE STUDY: PAYROLL SYSTEM
55
==PAGE:78==
ubiquitous language. DDD is founded on models, being abstraction and aspects of the
business domain, and bounded context, being groups of related and scoped domain en-
tities. Although DDD provides a lot of process practice, we mostly adopt the software
practice which is founded on the following building blocks. Entities are domain objects
defined by identity rather than attributes. Value objects are objects with no real iden-
tity. Aggregates are collections of entities, having one single entity as the aggregate root.
Aggregate roots guarantee consistency of changes within the aggregate by prohibiting
external objects from having relation to its members. Repositories are storage contain-
ers responsible for saving data. Factories handles the responsibility of creating instances
in order to control implementations. Domain Driven Design and the Onion architecture
are very compatible, as they focus on the domain and the essential business use cases,
avoiding tightly coupled structures. The concepts are to be used in all actor systems.

UI
The user interface (UI) provides the space where users interact with the system. To ac-

3.2.3.2
API
The application programming interface (API) acts as the facade and gatekeeper to the
internal system. To follow accepted norms, the API is to be implemented as a REST API,
offering an external interface based on the HTTP protocol, and an internal actor provider
is to be used for communicating with the rest actor based subsystems. The stateless nature
of the REST API is consumable by the web application, but also able to provide services
to other types of UI’s e.g. smartphone apps and desktop applications. To protect the
system, the API has to ensure that authentication and authorization is addressed.
3.2.3.3
MasterData
The MasterData subsystem is intended to provide fast access to employer related data.
It is to be implemented as an actor system with all accessible data available in-memory,
backed by a dedicated database. All employers are to be represented by actors in a big
hierarchy behind the root actor, MasterData.
3.2.3.4
PayrollData
The PayrollData subsystem is designed, and has similar intended use, as MasterData, but
in the scope of payrolls. All non-processed payrolls are accessible available in-memory,
backed by a dedicated database
3.2.3.5
PayrollCalculation
The PayrollCalculation subsystem is reckoned to be the very core of the payroll system,
and intended as a engine. When activated, it runs payrolls, based on data fetched from
MasterData and PayrollData, culminated in payroll results. Payroll results are persisted
and dispatced into money transfers, statistics reporting and payslip posting.

payslips and payslip entries, in order to provide a more detailed payroll insight. The
wrapped payroll result is finally returned to the requesting actor.

Payroll runs are done in batches at night, triggered by a run payroll on all employers
command. When receiving the command, the PayrollCalculationActor creates a

payrolls being in draft mode, a PayrollRunSagaActor is created. The PayrollRu-

a payroll preview. A PayrollRunActor is then created, and delegated the payroll run
task. The returned payroll result is finally saved in the database using a PayrollCalculati-

same way as in the other subsystems, with a router, distributing load using the Round-
Robin algorithm. All the PayrollRunSagaActors are started immediately leading to

Message flow
A message is often sent through different actors before reaching its final destination. The
chain of actors involved relates closely to the actor hierarchy explained in Section 3.2.5
on page 61, where a message typically is sent downward. However, in some cases, e.g.
when replying to a message, it is sent directly to the receiving actor. In relation to this, the
address of the requesting actor is always kept in the messages, allowing an actor always
be able to reply the originally requesting actor.
Figure 3.15 shows an example of the message flow when requesting an employer from
the API. The API initiates the request by sending a GetEmployerCommandMessage
to MasterDataRouterActor, which is the root actor and entry point of the Master-

for maintaining the EmployerActor that represents the specific employer requested by
the API. In case the data is not in memory, the EmployerActor sends the message to
the MasterDataDatabaseRouterActor, which forwards the message to the database
actor. After retrieving the employer data, the database actor replies the EmployerActor
with a document message, which is then sent back to the API. In case the employer al-
ready exists in memory, the EmployerActor omits involving the database actors, but
instead replies the requesting actor immediately.

Behaviors
When receiving a message, an actor may have to behave in a certain way depending
on some state. Figure 3.16 shows such an example of a saga actor representing a state
machine responsible for creating a new payroll for a single employer. Since this process
is asynchronous and requires external communication with other actors, we want to avoid
wrong messages interrupting the ongoing process in the actor. For this reason, the process
is divided into states representing each step, shown in Fig. 3.16. When the actor is created,
it enters the initial WaitingForCommand state, in which the actor will only react to a start
command message specifying an employer id for which the payroll should be created.
Upon receiving this command, the actor sends a message to MasterData asking for the
employer required for creating the payroll. For this reason, the actor changes its state to
WaitingForData, restricting the actor to only react on a message containing the employer
data, and therefore discarding any other received messages. If for some reason the saga
actor receives a second start command message while waiting for the employer data, the
state prevents a possibly duplicate process from being started.

4
Responsiveness
This chapter outlines the various approaches used in relation to achieving responsiveness
in the payroll system. First, it describes the design of the actors and how the granularity
of actors helps achieving responsiveness and scalability. Secondly, it explains how the
message flow in the system has been optimized to process large amount of messages while
still being responsive. Lastly, it describes how database communication is an important
concern in achieving responsiveness.
4.1
Actor designs
The actors in the payroll system are designed with responsiveness and scalability in mind.
This means the granularity of actors are considered carefully in order to accommodate
responsiveness, as well as each actor is designed to process messages in a fast manner in
order to keep an actor responsive at all times.
4.1.1
Granularity of actors
When specifying the granularity of actors, there are at least two perspectives to consider.
First of all, it relates to the domain model, as it should be decided on which level the
domain entities are represented as actors. In other words, this means to what extent the
domain is decomposed into specific actors. Secondly, it relates to technology specific
actors, e.g. database actors, as an optimal number of such actors should be used in order
to achieve responsiveness.
==PAGE:104==
4.1.1.1
Domain actors
The granularity of domain actors is closely related to the design, as the design dictates
which domain entities an actor represents. Since the payroll system contains employers,
employees, configurations etc., we could have an actor representing each type of entity.
However, having too fine grained actors leads to a lot of message overhead and also
involves a lot of data aggregation, e.g. when requesting the data of all employees of a
specific employer. Therefore, a boundary should be established for when an entity is
represented as an actor and when it is part of an actor representing an aggregation of
domain entities. The area of responsibility should not be too small nor to big. It is a
balance between number of actors, message overhead and separation of concern.
When using domain driven design, it is natural to implement actors as aggregates. In
fact, actors comply well with domain aggregates due to aggregates representing a group
of related entities with no overlap. Since actors also do not overlap, an actor can own
the specific entity data without any other actors knowing about it, and at the same time it
provides a natural separation, imitating the real world, where e.g. an employer is a self-
managed organization running concurrently with other employers. Therefore, we have
designed the domain actors to represent the top-level domain aggregates, responsible for
maintaining all related entities. These entities are employer, payroll and payroll result.
This allows us to group and manage all data related to a specific top-level entity in one
single actor. That said, it would also be possible to add an extra level of actors by having
employees, payslips and payslip results represented as actors. However, it would also add
an extra level of message overhead, and since the requirements for this system specifies
a maximum number of 100 employees for each employer, it is manageable to maintain
within a single actor. Having thousands of employees per employer might have required
to also implement employees and payslips as actors.
The granularity in relation to domain actors contributes to responsiveness by establishing
a logical boundary between independent business processes. By having this boundary
at the level of the domain aggregates, an actor has an adequate amount of responsibility
while still being able to be responsive. This means the work required for a single employer
or payroll can easily be handled by an actor. Another way the granularity helps achieving
responsiveness is that it allows for scaling out on several virtual machines, explained
further in Section 4.1.2 on the next page and Section 7.5.7 on page 116.

4.1.1.2
Technology specific actors
The granularity of the technology specific actors, i.e. actors not represented in the domain
model, such as the database actor, is not a matter of specifying a boundary in relation to
some predefined entities, but rather the actual number of actors used. While the number
of domain actors is equal to the number of domain entities, e.g. the number of employers,
the number of the technology specific actors is based on experiments on what provides
the best responsiveness and overall performance. Section 6.1.1 on page 101 analyses the
optimal number of database actors in the payroll system.
4.1.2
Scalability of actors
The scalability of actors is related to the granularity, as the granularity determines to what
extent actors can be spread out on different machines. It is important to have scalability
in mind when designing actors if more than one machine is to be used to run the system.
Since we have designed actors to represent the top-level domain aggregates, we are able
to distribute groups of employers and payrolls on different machines. This means we
cannot have a single employer located on more than one machine, which would require
the granularity to be more fine grained, e.g. by having employees represented as actors.
However, since a single virtual machine easily can handle several thousands employers,
demonstrated in Chapter 6 on page 101, this is not a problem.
4.1.3

processing messages, e.g. by making a database request, as it leaves the actor unavailable
to process other messages while waiting for a response. Figure 4.1 illustrates this by
showing an actor and its mail box, where messages are enqueued at the top and dequeued
from the bottom. Two messages are waiting to be processed while Message 1 is currently

Message 1 requires a blocking operation, such as a database call, which makes the ac-
tor unresponsive while waiting for the database. This should be avoided and instead a
message should be processed as fast as possible in order to maintain responsiveness. To
accommodate this, we delegate long running operations to dedicated worker actors. This
way, the worker actor will do the operation while the other actor continues processing
messages. It does not matter that the worker actor blocks, because its only job is to run
the long running operation, after which it will terminate. In some cases, however, it is
necessary to wait for an operation to finish. E.g. when updating an employer, it should
not be possible to retrieve the employer data before the update is finished. Thus in this
case, we want the messages to be queued in the mailbox and processed subsequently, in
order to avoid an inconsistent state.
4.1.4
Keep data in-memory
To optimize responsiveness, it provides a great improvement to store data in-memory,
since a lot of database calls can be avoided. This concept complies well with actors,
because each employer and payroll are represented as distinct actors, and since the data is
not shared, it is natural to store the related data within the specific actors. However, not
all data is applicable to store in-memory. For instance, the payroll system does not keep
payroll results in-memory, partly due to its size but also because the data is not requested
as often as employers and payrolls. Also, it is important to be aware of the size of the
data and how much memory is available to avoid memory related issues. The payroll
system uses roughly 5GB of RAM when having 20.000 employers and payrolls loaded
into memory.
4.2
Message flow
An important part of achieving responsiveness is to keep the message flow fast and to
avoid bottlenecks. For this reason, we have used different techniques in regard to opti-
mizing the message flow.
4.2.1
Routers
We use routers to distribute messages between actors, thereby spreading the load to sev-
eral actors of the same type. The router actor is stateless and its single purpose is to route
messages to a routee, for which reason it is important to ensure that a router actor does
not do anything else than routing messages. The payroll system uses routers in the entry
point of MasterData and PayrollData, and when sending messages to the database actors.
The entry point routers use consistent hashing in order to guarantee that a message related

to a specific actor always is routed correctly. In relation to this, it is important to use an
effective hashing function to optimize the message throughput and thereby preventing the
router from becoming a bottleneck.
4.2.2
Respond patterns
When a message reaches its final destination through the chain of actors responsible for
delivering the message, a response needs to be sent back. There are mainly two ways of
doing this, illustrated in Fig. 4.2, which are either to send the message back through the
chain of involved actors, or to send it directly to the actor who originally made the request.
Both approaches work, however it is important to avoid unnecessary communication that
can slow down a message, in order to maintain responsiveness. If a message was sent back
and ended up in the router, the router would suddenly have to handle a larger amount of
messages. Therefore, it is better to send the response directly to the requesting actor.
The payroll system uses this pattern in all communication. It requires, however, that an
identifier of the requesting actor is always associated to a message, for which reason we
always store the PID of the originally requesting actor in the messages.

4.2.3
Message size
When sending messages, a question quickly arises on whether to send many small mes-
sages, or to bulk the data and send one big message. This typically depends on the use
case, however as a general rule, sending many small messages achieves better responsive-
ness. This is due to the fact that the receiving end can start processing the messages while
the sending end is still sending messages. Having large messages might also be slow to
process, which causes the actor to block and thereby unable to process other messages.
Therefore, throughout the implementation we have minimized the message size where
possible in order to optimize responsiveness.

4.3
Database usage
Using a database is a standard requirement in most applications, however in doing so of-
ten involves a lot of network traffic as well as read/write operations to the disk, which can
lead to poor responsiveness and performance if not used carefully. Furthermore, tech-
nology specific factors such as using an object-relational mapping framework also has
a big impact. The following outlines some of the approaches used in order to optimize
responsiveness when interacting with the database.
4.3.1
Database actors
In order accommodate high database usage, we have introduced database actors, which
purpose is to manage all communication with the database.
4.3.1.1
Throttling database access
Having many actors accessing the database at the same time can overload the database
and cause slow response times. For instance, when executing a payroll run containing
thousands of payrolls, potentially thousands of actors would try to access the database at
the same time to store the payroll result. For this reason, we control the number of con-
current connections to the database by having dedicated database actors responsible for
performing database operations, as illustrated in Fig. 4.3. Instead of actors accessing the
database directly, they send messages representing the required operation to the database
actors through the router, which will be queued in the mail box and processed sequen-
tially within the individual database actors. By controlling the number of database actors,
we can avoid too many concurrent connections, and using a round robin router enables
the requests to be distributed evenly among the database actors. In regard to this, Chap-
ter 6 on page 101 analyses the optimal number of database actors. When using dedicated
database actors, it is important to note that unhandled messages will be lost if a database
actor shuts down. Thus, it requires some sort of mechanism to handle this, e.g. by using
persistent queues or simply resend the messages to the database actor.

4.3.1.2
Bulk insertion
While throttling limits the amount of concurrent connections, bulk insertion reduces the
amount of requests sent to the database. This is done by bulking, i.e. accumulating the
data of multiple insert operations and store it in one transaction. While running a payroll,
we have a lot of insert operations when storing the payroll results, and it would be a
time consuming process to store each payroll result individually. For this reason, we
bulk multiple payroll results in order to improve responsiveness. Chapter 6 on page 101
analyses the optimal number of bulked payrolls.
4.3.2
Object-relational mapping
Choosing the right ORM is essential in achieving responsiveness and performance. This
choice heavily depends on the amount of data required to be stored and updated, as most
ORMs are sufficient to use for at small amount of data. However, not all ORMs are
designed to deal with insertion of hundred of thousands of rows in an efficient way. For
instance, when executing a payroll run containing just 1000 payrolls with 100 employees
each will produce ≈400.000 rows of data. Using an ORM such as Entity Framework
will cause the payroll run to be incredible slow. This is due to the fact that rather than
focusing on efficiency, Entity Framework is designed as a very high level framework
with the aim of omitting the developer from having to deal with any SQL, though this
might cause some operations to be very inefficient, especially if there is a great amount
of data. For the purpose of the payroll system, this is untenable and a more low level
ORM is required. Therefore, we use Dapper, which is much more low level and allows
the developer to control the SQL queries and thereby providing the means for controlling
the efficiency of the queries. Applying Dapper instead of Entity Framework provides a
huge improvement in terms of responsiveness and performance.
4.3.3
Database server
The aforementioned approaches all helps achieving responsiveness in relation to using a
database. However, in the end it all comes down to the server on which the database is
hosted. We experienced that hosting the databases with the required specifications in the
cloud (Azure) was too expensive, and therefore instead acquired a personal laptop to be
used as database server, simulating a cloud setup with several databases on one server.
Nevertheless, this should not be a problem for a company to afford. We recommend to
use a database server for each database, thereby guaranteeing that the databases does not
effect one another.

Correctness
Correctness is challenging in reactive systems due to the concurrent nature, but it must be
defined and fulfilled. In the payroll system, the most essential elements are payroll runs,
as they deal with money. It is crucial that payroll results are never lost or modified regard-
less of the outcome of payroll runs, and that the system is always recoverable to a known
state, which it can proceed from after a failure. This chapter shows how correctness is
achieved using the various concepts described in Chapter 2 on page 5 - summarized in
Section 5.2.6 on page 99. First, we describe four invariants acting as the base for the cor-
rectness specification. Then we outline the process of a payroll run including successful
as well as failure scenarios, and how we deal with them by executing long lived transac-
tions using sagas. Finally, we describe how payroll runs are designed to terminate and
how changes of master and payroll data do not effect payroll runs.
5.1
Invariants
Table 5.1 shows the four overall invariants which have to be satisfied at all time in order
to achieve correctness. Correctness is still achieved even though the system breaks down,
1. A given payroll result either resides in a saga or is persisted in the database
2. Modification of master data or payroll data has no impact on ongoing payroll runs
3. Master data and payroll data are traceable from any payroll result
4. Approved payrolls are linked to specific, non-changeable employee configurations
Table 5.1: Invariants

as payroll runs can be resumed or restarted depending on the progress. If the payroll run
succeeds, one or more payroll results are found in the database. The second invariant
ensures that the system stays responsive (allows modification of master data and payroll
data during payroll runs) and that these modifications do not interfere with ongoing pay-
roll runs. The third invariant ensures that data used to run the payroll is always traceable
and unique. The fourth invariant ensures that no newer configuration is used in a recovery.

for a payroll run of 20.000 payrolls (≈8 million rows), a bulking strategy has been im-
plemented in order to persist the massive amount of data within a short period of time.
During the payroll run, several things can crash or fail to respond. Thus, it is crucial that
the individual payroll runs can be recovered, if failed. In order to grasp all aspects of

5.2.3.1
State machines
State machines create the means to structure flows of activities, and provide the ability to

5.2.4
Termination
Timeouts are used to detect absent message responses in all phases of the payroll run.
Rather than communicating backwards and forwards, we let non-responding payroll runs
fail gracefully. This leads to a partly successful scenario which can be recovered at a
convenient time. The timeouts ensure that the payroll run is terminated.
5.2.5
User input during payroll runs
During running payrolls, the system must stay responsive and receptive for user input,
but at the same time should no modification of the basis for the ongoing process alter the
outcome. This is dealt with differently in MasterData and PayrollData.
5.2.5.1
Master data
At the time a customer modifies a Pension, TaxCard or a Salary associated with an
employee, a copy of the Configuration and its sub classes is created. This leads to
a complete history of configuration states, which is very useful for traceability. When
the PayrollRunSagaActor requests the Employer document, it will receive the most
recent Configuration as part of it. If a customer chooses to modify a given Salary
while the payroll run is active, it will lead to a new state of the Configuration. The
Configuration used in the payroll run is older, but represents the exact state present at
the time of the run. This solution is realized without using database locks.
5.2.5.2
Payroll data
Payroll runs are based on approved payrolls. At the time a Payroll is approved, a new
Payroll is created for the upcoming period. The previous Payroll is marked as ap-
proved and can not be modified. If a customer modifies the payroll data while the payroll
run is active, it will be on upcoming period and not the one used in the payroll run.
5.2.6
Achievement of correctness
Correctness is achieved as the four invariants are satisfied, and because of the fact that
actors process messages sequentially and that they are deterministic. The first invari-
ant is satisfied, as PayrollResults can always be found in either a saga or in the
database. The reason for this is the nature of persistent actors and long lived transac-
tions. The second invariant is satisfied, as employee Configurations evolve states,
and that approved payrolls leads to new editable payrolls. The third invariant is satisfied,
as Configurations and Payrolls are never removed from the database, and that the
PayrollResults have conceptual references to them. The fourth invariant is satisfied,
as configurations linked to approved payrolls are never modified.

Guidelines for reactive
development
Many things have been accomplished throughout the course of this project. Insight into
actor systems has been gained and many obstacles have been overcome. As the project
progressed, we found that reactive development is a non-trivial task and calls for mod-
eration, training and understanding in order to succeed. We have gathered a substantial
amount of information about potential pitfalls, experiences, pieces of advice and best-
practice gained from all the struggles we been through, in what we call guidelines for
reactive development. The guidelines are grouped into four categories: Paradigm shift
adjustment, actor design, messages, system monitoring and technology.
7.1
Paradigm shift adjustment
First of all, reactive development represents a paradigm shift in the way we reason about,
and how we create software systems. For most software engineers, it requires a com-
pletely different way of thinking, but most importantly it depends on agreement in design
and implementation. Non-deterministic asynchronous communication is without a doubt
the most challenging hurdle caused by the loosely coupled and distributed composition.
It has to be embraced. Possible non-deterministic flows of computation require immense
amounts of analysis, as all kinds of failure scenarios have to be identified and dealt with
individually. This time-consuming consequence stands in opposition to sequential ap-
proaches which are based on more centralized failure handling schemes. Reactive sys-
==PAGE:132==
tems require thorough design and heavily rely on software architects to ensure optimal
message flows. Software engineers must commit to perfect the system components in-
dividually, but more importantly, ensure that they cooperate in harmony. The chains of
reactive systems are no stronger than their weakest links. Team effort with joint com-
mitment is the way to succeed in reactive development, but it requires one very essential
ingredient, prioritized corporate backing.
7.2
Actor design
Actor systems open up a lot of opportunities being scoped responsibility, responsiveness
through asynchronous message passing and controlled concurrency, but in order to take
advantage of these traits, the systems have to be designed carefully. As software engineers
we are taught to analyze and to design before implementing. This course of action applies
to actor system in particularly.
7.2.1
Always model first
During the project, we have affirmed the importance of model creation, models being
UML diagrams or other visual representations. Managing code alone is an impossible
task and will surely introduce failures and bottlenecks. Having posters of structural and
behavioral designs is a necessity, as they are great tools to achieve agreement and to spot
any kind of potential inexpediency in the process. There are no standardized ways of
representing actors, but have found a mix off sequence diagrams, activity diagrams and
hybrid diagrams to be very helpful. Implementing actors without modelling first should
be avoided at all costs.
7.2.2
Sharing mutable state across actors is forbidden
The power of actor systems is the ability to scope responsibility. A part of the design
process is to identify relevant actors and to ensure that associated data is accessed through
these actors alone. One single database table row should not be accessed by more than
one actor. Non shared mutable states ensure consistency of data and withstands race
conditions. Breaking this pattern will create immense holes in the system. Without great
documentation, this can be very challenging, so remember to model first.
7.2.3
Granularity and identification of actors
Actor granularity has great impact on the responsiveness and performance of a system,
as mentioned in Section 4.1.1 on page 81. The identification process should focus on the
domain but have technology in mind. If one should have trouble identifying actors, think
110
ACTOR DESIGN
==PAGE:133==
aggregates and services (Domain Driven Design), as they have scoped responsibilities.
A DDD aggregate does not permit modification of underlying entities, an attribute which
fits an actor very well. A DDD service is stateless and easily implemented by a worker
actor. If an entity is thought of as being concurrent in real life, or if it needs failure
handling, then it is an actor. But be aware, if the actor granularity is too fine, it will
require a lot message passing and data aggregation, but if it is too rough, then you are not
taking advantage of the powers of actors.
7.2.4
Actor maintenance
Actor systems consist of many actors and they are hard to maintain. Without thorough
consideration one can introduce memory leaks and inconsistency. Actors should be ter-
minated the moment they are done. Unused actors take up memory and remain in the
actor system address registration. Actor child look-ups should be fast, so utilize the actor
system to locate the addresses rather than iterating through children manually. Actor look-
ups should not be exaggerate. Model and use proper message paths through the system
instead of sending messages directly to random actors. Consider your implementation
thoroughly in order to evade duplicate actor instances. Use timeouts to detect absent
messages and failed actors.
7.2.5
Combining actor system with non-actor systems
Actor systems are technology specific, so make sure that abstraction boundaries are cre-
ated in order to communicate with them. These abstractions should expose nothing of
messages or actors. In the payroll system we use the ActorProvider as a intermediate
between the API and the actor subsystems. This boundary creates the illusion of requests
being synchronous, but the inner implementation is actual asynchronous. If possible, try
to be reactive in all aspects of the system e.g. use web sockets in the UI and API in order
to achieve a full stack of message handling.
7.2.6
Event sourcing and persistent actors
Event sourcing and persistent actors are used to keep actors stateful even when systems
fail. They can be designed as sagas controlling long-lived transactions or they can be data
driven CRUD actors. Be sure to use a combination of event sourcing and snapshotting in
order to do fast recoveries (CRUD actors only). Be aware of the amount of events thrown,
as they are persisted as single INSERTS and therefore have a huge impact on performance
due to the expensive database IO communication.

7.2.7
Flow control and asynchronous communication
Apply routers to achieve message flow control. Use consistent hashing in order to cre-
ate message paths through the system and round-robin or random routing to distribute
load between actors. Finally use broadcasting to broadcast messages to all associated
routees (required to terminate all). Use state machines to regulate asynchronous message
communication.
7.3
Messages
The use of messages is an essential part in developing reactive systems. This section
outlines some of the important considerations in regard to this.
7.3.1
How to handle return address
It is important to have a clear strategy from the start on how to handle the return address of
a message, i.e. the address of the initiating actor of a request. This was briefly mentioned
in Section 4.2.2 on page 85 regarding respond patterns. One way to manage this is to keep
actors responsible for mapping messages to the correct receiver, by sending a respond
back through the chain of actors that delivered the message. However, this requires a
great amount of bookkeeping, as each message must be uniquely mapped in all involved
actors. Instead, we found that the most suitable approach was to have a MetaData field on
all messages containing the address, and thereby letting the return address always follow
a message. This makes the actor responsible for replying to the message able to do it
directly to the requesting actor, and avoids a lot of bookkeeping.
Managing return address in flow of different messages
When a request in terms of a message is sent, the type of the message might change
during the execution of the request. For instance, when sending a GetEmployerCom-
mandMessage to an employer actor, the employer actor forwards the message to the
database actor, which in return replies the employer with a EmployerDocumentMessage.
Here the message type has changed, but is still part of the same request. In our case, since
we store the return address in the messages, we have to make sure that new messages in
a flow are populated with the address, in order to ensure that the address always follows
the messages in the flow. How to deal with this depends on how the return address is
handled. The point here is simply to highlight that this is a concern one needs to consider
when developing message driven architectures.
112
MESSAGES
==PAGE:135==
7.3.2
Message construction
Always have a clear approach and naming convention when construction messages. The
name of a message should convey a clear definition of what the message represents, as
well as the type of the message should always be reflected in the name. For instance,
GetEmployerCommandMessage clearly tells that it is a command message for request-
ing a specific employer. By following the same pattern helps easing the development and
makes it more intuitive.
7.3.3
Messages should be immutable
It is important to make messages immutable, meaning that once a message is created, it
should not be possible to change the contents of the message. This is due to the fact that
the same message can be sent to several actors concurrently, and in case the message is
not immutable and one of the actors makes a change in the message, this change will
also apply in the message received by all other actors. Thus, it can cause invalid and
unpredictable states in the messages, leading to incorrect behavior of actors. The extent
of this issue depends on the technology used, e.g. in C# messages (represented as objects)
are not immutable and therefore has to be dealt with.
7.3.4
Fast message processing
As previously mentioned in Section 4.1.3 on page 83, it is important to process mes-
sages fast in order to keep actors responsive. Always strive for optimizing the message
processing and delegate long running operations and blocking calls to dedicated actors
where possible. This is an important factor in achieving responsiveness.
7.3.5
Message size
Try as far as possible to keep messages small and avoid bulking data that can be send as
individual messages, into the same message. As explained in Section 4.2.3 on page 85,
it provides better responsiveness and performance when the receiving end can start pro-
cessing the messages while the sending end is sending messages.

A lot of new issues arise when going from the traditional, sequential way of programming
to developing a reactive system. This section highlights some of the important topics in
relation to system monitoring.

7.4.1
Debugging
It is important to say that reactive systems are harder to debug due to its asynchronous
nature. It is not possible for the compiler to follow the callstack, as it does not know
where a message is sent to. Thus, there is no such thing as ”stepping into” when sending
a message. Therefore, one needs to know where the message is sent and set breakpoints in
the individual actors in order to follow a message. We have not found an easier approach
for this, hence this section is just to stress that this is something one needs to be aware of.
7.4.2
Distributed tracing
If a reactive system is to be run in production, it is very important to have a mechanism in
place for distributed tracing, such as ZipKin [40], in order to monitor the behavior of the
system and to troubleshoot problems. Since requests often span multiple subsystems, it
is hard to follow a request through the system. Having no distributed tracing only allows
for external monitoring, which only provides the overall response times, and no insights
into the system. Distributed tracing helps giving an overview of how a request is handled
inside the system, by assigning each request to a request id which follows the request until
the end. This makes it possible to time requests between subsystems and therefore useful
for finding bottlenecks and to get a detailed overview of the system performance.
7.4.3
Lost messages
A great challenge when developing reactive systems is how to handle lost messages. We
simply cannot know if a message will ever be replied. If a message is somehow lost,
either by sending it to the wrong actor or if the receiving actor fails, a response will not be
sent back. This is a problem because an actor most likely expects a response, and if the
request e.g. comes from the API, it will simply hang forever without getting a response
back. For this reason, it is important to ensure that a response always is sent back, no
matter the outcome of the message, in order to know if something went wrong. There are
several approaches for this. First of all, whenever an actor fails, a message containing
a description of the error can be sent back, making sure that the requesting actor always
gets a response in case of failure. Secondly, if a message for some reason is sent to an
actor that does not know how to handle it, a message representing such scenario could be
sent back. This requires, however, that the requesting actor knows how to handle these
messages. Having such mechanisms in place ensure that sending a message always yields
a response back, even if the message is lost.

7.4.4
Monitoring subsystems
We found it useful to have a status message that can be sent to each subsystem in order to
monitor whether subsystems are online and responsive. By sending a AreYouThereCon-
versationMessage, a subsystem will reply with a YesImHereConversationMessage
in case it is online. This gives a quick overview of the system health.
7.5
Technology
Design is a critical matter, but actor systems introduce several technology specific con-
cerns which have impact on performance and the overall experience.
7.5.1
Awareness of cross platform technologies
Be aware of platform specific technologies. Using a mature technology such as AKKA
brings a lot of features, but the framework is limited by the language it is written in.
Proto.Actor focus on platform independence using protobuf messages. This way, the
actor components can be realized using different technologies. There is no single truth in
this, but be aware of potential problems.
7.5.2
Use await/async
It is important to use non-blocking features when doing IO operations, such as calling a
database, as it will release the thread and make it available to do other work while waiting
for the database to respond. This helps optimizing responsiveness and performance in the
system.
7.5.3
Performance impact of standard dependency injection frameworks
It is almost inevitable to use dependency injection (DI) in modern software systems, but
keep in mind that actor systems usually consist of thousands or even millions of actors and
it will end up being very expensive to inject object instances in each of them. Standard
DI frameworks can prove to be very slow in this context. It is recommended to use built
in functionality (shipped with the various actor frameworks).
7.5.4
Deployment of actor systems
Be sure to use a stable environment. Microsoft web applications running on IIS are not
recommended for housing actor systems as they can be recycled and restarted sponta-
neously. Windows services are very useful (running in virtual machines) as they are ro-
bust. Various cloud services, e.g. Azure Worker Roles or Docker containers are applicable
for actor systems, but be aware of requirement for manageable TCP ports.

7.5.5
Database access
It is essential that database experiments are conducted early in the development/proto-
typing process in order to spot potential limitations. The number of concurrent actors
communicating with the database depends on the functionality of the system. Too many
actors requesting the database at the same time will lead to timeouts. Consider if the
concept of throttled database access will suit your system.
7.5.6
gRCP message batch size
Proto.Actor makes use of the open-source communication framework gRCP [26] to han-
dle communication between actors. When sending many messages at the same time, the
framework bundles together several messages and sends them in a batch. This batch size
can be specified and is default set to 1000 in Proto.Actor. However, gRPC has a limit on
4MB for each message, which can become a problem if the total total size of the bundled
messages exceeds this limit. Therefore, we have limited this batch size to 25. In relation
to this, experiments showed that this did not have a noticeable impact on the performance.
7.5.7
How to combine virtual machines
An approach to achieve responsiveness is to scale the system. Since each employer is
represented as an actor, we can spread out the actors on several virtual machines. This
way, each virtual machine will only be responsible for a subset of the employers, relieving
the load of each machine. Figure 7.1 shows an overall and naive example of this where
four virtual machines constitutes the system, each maintaining employers starting with
specific letters. This approach of using alphabetic partitioning is just an example, in fact
any strategy works as long as the employers are partitioned evenly. A given number of
API instances are created in order to manage heavy user loads.

8
Conclusion
By applying the principles of the reactive manifesto, one can produce a system with the
properties of being responsive and resilient through a message driven and elastic design,
based on best practice and a common vocabulary. Reactive systems can be realized using
the concept of actors defined by the actor model. Actor systems relate to the real world,
leading to more intuitive systems designs. As a product of this thesis, a payroll system
based on the principles of the reactive manifesto and the actor model has been realized.
With the distributed nature of reactive systems comes the problem of guaranteeing re-
sponsiveness and correctness. The problem can be eliminated. It will, however, require
carefully thought out system design and a willingness to adapt to the paradigm of reactive.
Guaranteeing responsiveness
Responsiveness is achieved by identifying domain bounded contexts. Bounded contexts
are represented by subsystems. In each bounded context, an aggregate has been identi-
fied as an actor. This way, each employer, payroll and payroll result is represented by
a uniquely scoped actor. The optimal granularity of actors has been identified in order
to avoid overhead in message exchange. Responsibility has been spread across numer-
ous actor instantiations, and routers based on consistent hashing are utilized to create
fast message paths. Message size is kept to a minimum. Actors are designed to do fast
message processing and to keep state in-memory. Intensive IO operations are delegated
to other actors when possible. Dedicated database actors are created with the purpose
of throttling database access. The actor design is scalable, making it possible to scatter
actors across virtual machines in order to uphold responsiveness as the system grows.
==PAGE:140==
Guaranteeing correctness
Correctness is achieved based on the characteristics of actors being deterministic and the
fact that messages are processed sequentially. Furthermore, failure scenarios are dealt
with using long lived transactions realized by saga actors. Event sourcing ensures that
the system is always able to recover from failure. Race conditions are avoided, as spe-
cific data is handled as a state in the scope of dedicated actors. Invariants are defined
and satisfied. Payroll data can not be modified during payroll runs due to their state of
being approved. Modification of upcoming payrolls is still possible and the systems stays
responsive. Master data in the form of tax cards, salaries and pensions are grouped in
unique configurations. Configurations are not modified, they are copied and version con-
trolled. Failed payroll runs are recoverable and make use of previous known versions of
configurations, ensuring that the payroll point of reference stay the same.
Research contributions
This thesis stands as a reference for building reactive systems. It has gathered necessary
concepts and principles required to understand the nature of reactive actor systems, and
shows how a specific domain is modelled using domain driven design and realized by
actors with respect to responsiveness and correctness. Pitfalls, best practice and expe-
riences have been formulated in a guide to reactive development. By reading this thesis
a software engineer is able to comprehend the paradigm of reactive and to quickly start
developing without making fundamental mistakes.

hourly income.
