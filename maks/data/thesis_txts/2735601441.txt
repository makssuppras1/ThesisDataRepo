Abstract
The EU aims to be climate-neutral by 2050. As the construction sector is responsible for more
than 1/3 of all generated waste in the EU, it is crucial to quantify the waste flow properly
for transitioning towards a circular economy. This master thesis characterizes the genera-
tion and management of Construction and Demolition waste (CDW) in Europe, with an
in-depth focus on Danish waste management. The purpose of the research is to quantify
the generation of CDW from buildings in Europe. The thesis employs data collection and
a bottom-up method to calculate the generation of CDW using case studies from Den-
mark and the building stock. Besides this, several research interviews were conducted with
stakeholders in the sector to describe the overview of waste handling processes and waste
treatment practices. The main findings include an overview of the generation of CDW from
European countries by using the Eurostat database and a characterization of the Danish
CDW from the ADS. The findings are used to compare the case studies and the bottom-
up approach. The studies have a special focus on carbon-intensive and environmental
impactful materials concrete, brick, metal, glass, insulation and wood. The complemen-
tary research shows a larger generation of these waste materials in Denmark compared
to the existing databases. The thesis provides insight into the challenges surrounding CDW
practises and statistical limitations. It highlights the need for standardized data collection
and the current practices in the industry that can enhance sustainable solutions in the
construction and demolition sector.

1
Introduction
The construction sector and the built environments use many resources and are responsi-
ble for using 50% of all extracted materials in the EU. The construction sector is responsible
for 35% of all generated waste in the EU [1]. The EU acts on climate change and wants to
become climate-neutral by 2050. This is done through green climate initiatives and legally
binding targets, that shall uphold the countries’ responsibility to uphold the Paris Agree-
ment [2]. All economic sectors must reduce their emissions to uphold these initiatives and
this includes the construction sector. The transition to a circular economy is a key element
in these initiatives. The construction sector’s transition to a circular economy must include
a reduction in resources and keeping the current materials and products in the economy
by making use of the generated waste [3].
The need to address the challenges associated with Construction and Demolition waste
(CDW) and the European Union’s targets for waste reduction and recycling [4] emphasizes
the importance of characterizing CDW generation and flows from buildings. This thesis aims
to provide additional information on the CDW generation and flows from buildings. Cur-
rently, there exists data on total CDW and CDW fractions from Affaldsdatasystemet (ADS)
for Denmark and Eurostat for the European countries. However, these statistics have some
important limitations.
First, the existing databases aggregate waste from infrastructure projects and buildings to-
gether. Therefore, one will have to make assumptions regarding the share of building waste
from the data, to analyze CDW from buildings. Moreover, for the European Statistics, it is
not possible to investigate the CDW from concrete, brick and other mixed fractions in the
European countries. This is because the data for Europe is dividing all major minerals into
one wide category (minerals).
Therefore, this thesis takes a new approach, estimating the total CDW and CDW fractions
in Denmark using a bottom-up approach with case studies. Thereby, the thesis provides
a new measure of CDW waste and CDW fractions, which can be viewed as a comple-
mentary measure of CDW to the existing databases. In particular, the bottom-up measure
contains information on CDW generated from buildings with a focus on the materials: con-
crete, brick, metal, glass, insulation and wood. To this end, the thesis contributes with new
knowledge on the resources that can be used to transform the construction sector into a
circular economy.
Former research has used CDW data from existing databases, for instance, Caro et al.

The methodology used for this thesis involves a bottom-up approach to calculate a new

ensures a thorough investigation of the generation of CDW.
This thesis contributes to the knowledge of the current landscape of CDW in several ways.
First, the thesis characterizes the latest CDW flows and tendencies in the EU on both an ag-

the generation of CDW from buildings by countries. For Denmark, as mentioned, the thesis
uses a bottom-up approach to estimate the current CDW generated in Denmark by using
the framework for the generation of waste from buildings which is shown in figure 2. The
generated waste is found from the average of the case studies used for the calculation
of the CDW in Denmark shown in figure 1. The data is based on the building types: single-
family houses, terraced houses, apartments and offices. The building types are divided into
phases of the lifecycle of the building, where waste can be generated in the construction
of new buildings, the renovation of buildings and the demolition of buildings. Together with
the characterization of CDW data the waste treatment investigated and the challenges
that involve the handling of waste in a Danish context with different stakeholders.
As mentioned, the bottom-up measure of CDW and CDW fractions from buildings in Den-
mark provides a new complementary measure to the existing databases on CDW. Interest-
ingly, the bottom-up measure of total CDW deviates quite significantly from the CDW from
existing databases. In itself, this highlights the importance of calculating complementary
measures of total CDW. However, it is important to note, that there is also uncertainty re-
garding the bottom-up estimate. First, the limited number of case studies for the bottom-
up estimation introduces some uncertainty to the generality of the results. Moreover, some
critical assumptions are necessary to use the bottom-up approach. For future research, it
should be considered to formulate clear guidelines on which case studies to include in the
bottom-up approach.

Method
This thesis describes data on waste generation, waste flows and building stock information
in European countries. The thesis aims to characterize the intensity, volume and generation
of Construction and Demolition waste (CDW), including creating a new bottom-up mea-
sure of CDW. The following sections include an outline of the methods used to collect the
data. Moreover, the section describes the methods used for interviewing key stakeholders
in the sector.
2.1
Data Collection
The main objective of the project was to collect secondary data from different databases
on CDW. Therefore, this section describes the approach to collecting data and informa-
tion on CDW in European countries. Some of the data was collected by previous papers,
i.e. the thesis partly uses secondary data [6]. National and European statistics data was
collected from various datasets. The primary sources were Eurostat [7] Affaldatasystemet
(ADS) [8], European Environmental Agency (EEA) [9] and national agencies like the Dan-
ish Environmental Protection Agency [10]. Some of the data have previously been used
in papers by the European Commission and in other scientific papers. Damgaard et al.
[11] processes data on multiple CDW flows from various years and has been conducted
with a similar approach as in this paper. The European waste statistics, which contain data
on waste, were used together with data from Eurostat. The Danish CDW was gathered
from Affaldsdatasystemet (ADS) [8]. ADS is a Danish database where companies and mu-
nicipalities report waste data. This database contains CDW that were both generated by
buildings and larger infrastructure projects, roads and other activities in the construction
sector. Therefore, it was necessary to assume that some of the data was not only gener-
ated by buildings. Waste fractions, like glass and bricks, were assumed to be generated
fully by buildings. However, in reality, a fraction of the waste from glass and bricks etc.
would be generated by other activities.
The Building stock data was collected from the Danish national statistics [12] and Euro-
pean statistics [13].
The data on building types was used to calculate the amount of
CDW that was generated from buildings in different EU member states. Building stock data
was divided into groups of houses, with special emphasis on single-family houses, terraced
houses, apartment buildings and offices. This method was primarily performed with Dan-
ish building stock data and was calculated per year, per square meter. Overall, the data

2.3
Interviews
Research interviews were performed to gain an understanding of the CDW flow and dis-
cuss the role of CDW with different stakeholders in the sector. The interviews were used as
a quantitative and qualitative data research method. For this project, the important part
of the interviews was to gain perspective on the waste cycle from the product developer
to the waste treatment plant and the rest of the life cycle of CDW.
The interviewing of stakeholders was used to focus on the specific roles in the waste man-
agement process. The interviews were used to find key information from specialized peo-
ple, who have expert knowledge in their field of work. The interviews were conducted
individually on Teams or in person. Interviews were performed with a variation to the ques-
tionnaire depending on the person’s relations to CDW. Some general questions were used
to identify each person’s role in the industry and their specific know-how. Other questions
emerged naturally depending on the flow of the interview and were not planned. This
gave the interviews an exploratory purpose with an unstructured and flexible conversa-
tion. However, it was still important for the interviewer to guide the interview to collect the
necessary information and data from the interview. The interviews were a supplementary
research tool and worked as a personal verification or humanization of other data col-
lected. [17]. A table with an overview of the extracted information through the interviews
can be seen in appendix 15.
The methods performed in this thesis have been elaborated on and work as a foundation
for further analysis of CDW. The data collection included case studies and secondary data
from several databases, such as the Danish National Statistics. The collected data of total
CDW and waste fractions has been summarised in the CDW data framework. The inter-
views worked as a supplementary research tool and helped to identify knowledge gaps in
CDW theory.

Theory
This section presents theory on waste production, waste treatment and waste manage-
ment, including a description of the EU waste framework and how it can contribute to
limiting Construction and Demolition waste (CDW) in the EU.
3.1
Waste Production
The generation of CDW comes from three phases of the lifecycle of figure building. The
waste generation is shown in figure 2.
Figure 2: Framework for generation of waste from buildings
The first phase is the building construction phase. In this phase, new construction material
from cutoffs or extra material waste is placed in containers and sorted at the construction
site. The production of products or materials for the parts in the building is not taken into
account in this part of the lifecycle. The CDW from this phase is only the extra material
produced at the construction site.
3

13
==PAGE:15==

The second phase, where CDW can be generated from buildings, is during the use phase.
In this phase, the generation of waste occurs from the replacement of components or
building materials or during renovations. ’’Renovations can vary a lot in size. They can
range from large-scale renovations, where the building is stripped for everything, down to
the structural framework to smaller projects like replacing a roof construction or all the inte-
rior walls and installation” (Thomas Sinding, Development Manager - J. Jensen, Research
interview). As such there is a considerable variation in the CDW from renovations, but the
waste treatment is similar to a demolition case.
The end-of-life phase of the building, where it is demolished, is the last phase where CDW
can be generated in this framework. Demolition waste occurs when the whole building is
demolished. If only parts of the buildings are demolished, the waste is considered to be
generated in the use phase as some parts of the buildings will still be in use. The property
where the buildings were placed can be handed over to the owner in various stages fol-
lowing the contract situation. This includes whether the installations and sewers should be
removed as well or the foundation from the building should be intact.
3.2
Waste Treatment
CDW generated from new constructions, renovations or demolition requires effective man-
agement and treatment. The treatment varies for different waste material fractions and
between countries because of different regulations and guidelines. The European Union
released a framework on waste treatment and a structured approach to categorise waste
management processes optimally. This is elaborated in the section where EU waste regu-
lation is presented 3.3.
The management of CDW can be separated into the following categories: new construc-
tion products and materials, recovery into other products and energy, or disposal. These
categories define CDW and enhance resource efficiency. The waste treatment process
keeps the resources in use for as long as possible or extracts additional value from the
material through incineration or regenerated material. In the recovery process, there are
different subcategories like material recovery and backfilling, which is part of the European
aim to increase the recycling and reuse of CDW by 70% [4].
The waste treatment of different materials is essential for paving the road of the construc-
tion sector towards becoming more sustainable and working towards a circular economy
in the EU. This thesis investigates specific resources and treatments focusing on the six CDW
materials from the data framework in section 3.1 and other relevant CDW fractions. The

3.3
Waste Framework EU
Waste from buildings has to be separated into specific waste fractions. Countries in the
European Union must follow the main Waste Framework Directive[4]. This framework in-
cludes all waste fractions and industries including the construction industry. The directive
was adopted in 2008 and has since been updated and extended in 2014, 2015, 2018 and
February 2024. There are several targets for the frameworks of which three of the key ones
are listed below for the 2018 version (which were not altered in the 2024 version):
• by 2020, the preparing for re-use and the recycling of waste materials (such as paper,
metal, plastic and glass) from households shall be increased to a minimum of overall
50 % by weight
• by 2020, the preparing for re-use, recycling and other material recovery, including
backfilling operations using waste to substitute other materials, of non-hazardous con-
struction and demolition waste shall be increased to a minimum of 70 % by weight
• by 2025, the preparing for re-use and the recycling of municipal waste shall be in-
creased to a minimum of 55 %, 60% and 65% by weight by 2025, 2030 and 2035 re-
spectively
For the construction industry, the second target of the ones listed above was the most
important, as the industry was affected by these targets in terms of an increased need for
reuse and recycling of construction and demolition waste. Currently, Danmark is on the
path to completing the targets set for 2025, 2030 and 2035 [18].
The Waste framework also states basic principles on how to handle CDW. The principles
of waste handling state the following: Waste handling must happen without endangering
human health and harming the environment, without risk to water, air, soil, plants or animals,
without causing a nuisance through noise or odours, and without adversely affecting the
countryside or places of special interest. The people within the construction sector must
be aware of these principles when they are treating CDW, from the construction of the
buildings to the recycling facilities [4].
A very significant part of The EU’s Waste Framework Directive is the waste hierarchy, which is
a structured approach on how to categorise waste. The waste hierarchy is structured as a

1. Prevention: Prevention of generating waste is the main priority for waste manage-
ment, as it directly mitigates the environmental impact of waste by reducing the need
for landfill etc. Measures to prevent waste are taken before the material or product
becomes waste and are essential for reducing the amount of waste generated. The
initiative includes extending the materials’ lifetime and minimizing the waste gener-
ated by the material or product. The material also needs to remove impacts that
could generate harmful substances in the waste or negative impact on the environ-
ment, human health etc.
2. Preparing for reuse: Preparing for reuse is a strategy which includes checking, clean-
ing and preparing the product or materials which end up as waste. Eventually, the
goal of the generated waste is that no further processing of the waste is needed.
3. Recycling: Recycling includes the recovery operations of waste materials that are
reprocessed into original products, materials or for other purposes. The recycling step
does not include recovery of energy, reprocessing of waste material into fuels or back-
filling.
4. Recovery: Recovery is the operation where waste is replacing materials that would
have otherwise been used for a particular function. This could be energy recovery or
backfilling operations where a material is used as an alternative.

The Waste Framework Directive specifies when a product or material is considered waste,
and when the product or material becomes a new product or a secondary raw material
for new use. The end-of-waste criteria are important to determine when materials are no
longer considered waste, in which case they are no longer subject to waste regulation.
The specific criterias are listed below: [4].
• The substance or object is commonly used for a specific purpose
• There is a market or demand for the substance or object
• The substance or object fulfils technical and legal requirements for the specific pur-
pose intended
• The new use will not have a negative impact on the environment or human health.
Several countries are at risk of not meeting the required waste recycling rates in 2025 and
landfill targets for 2035. The following nine countries are on track to fulfilling the goals: Aus-
tria, Belgium, Czechia, Denmark, Germany, Italy, Luxembourg, The Netherlands, and Slove-
nia. The remaining 18 countries in the EU have been presented with recommendations and
support by the commission to reach the targets [20].
3.3.1
The EU CDW protocol and guidlines
The European Commission introduced a specific protocol and guidelines for CDW. The
CDW accounts for more than a third of all waste in the EU by volume which makes it
the largest waste stream. The protocol and guidelines for the industry are non-binding.
However, the initiative aims to increase the quality of recycled CDW and to improve CDW
management by waste identification, source preparation, collection, logistics, processing,
quality management and relevant and appropriate policy and framework conditions. [21]
The relevant stakeholders include the construction sector (demolition and entrepreneurs),

protocol and guidelines also influence the public municipalities, national institutions as well

The construction sector is known for dealing with hazardous materials. The guidelines ex-

3.3.2
EU selective demolition promotion
The EU waste framework influences the construction sector in several ways. First of all, the
CDW is not considered municipal waste and thus not a part of the targets for this specific
waste stream. CDW is waste generated from construction and demolition activities. The
Member states of the European Union are encouraged to promote selective demolition.
This will result in correct and safe handling of hazardous materials and prepare for reuse and
recycling of other materials. This ensures the potential for sorting systems of CDW materials,
which can be used in other products or as secondary materials [4].
3.3.3
EU CDW Audits guideline
The EU has developed a guideline for audits that assess and manage waste generated
from construction and demolition activities. The guideline is aligned with the objectives of
the Waste Framework Directive with the established target of 70% of CDW. The guidelines
can help all stakeholders in the waste management process and explain the procedures
and importance of a waste audition before the renovation or demolition of a building.
The waste must be assessed into types, quantity and potential hazards of the CDW in the
building [23]. The audit must be done before the building is demolished or renovated,
e.g. a screening of the building. It is the owner’s responsibility to perform the audit, as
the owner most commonly owns the building materials. ”Most often an entrepreneur or

4.3.1
CDW from Infrastructure Activities
The data collected for this thesis are only supposed to look at CDW from buildings. The
framework for CDW data treatment states that CDW from infrastructure needs to be sortet
into CDW from buildings and CDW from infrastructure. The theory behind this is based on
the literature study performed by Cristóbal et al. in the JRC Technical Report on Techno-
economic and environmental assessment of construction and demolition waste manage-
ment in the European Union [31]. The literature study tries to find a correlation between
the material waste fractions for concrete, Asphalt and ballast for railroads and infrastruc-
ture CDW and management. The waste from infrastructure includes the construction and
demolition of bridges, tunnels, roads etc.
The asphalt waste fraction mostly consists of asphalt paint, asphalt from roofing and asphalt
from roads that includes bitumen binders and aggregates from road construction [32]. Bal-
last from railways is confirmed as CDW from construction and rearrangement of railways in
Austria, Germany and Sweden according to BIO Deloitte report [33]. Besides these is con-
crete also a waste fraction, that occurs in both infrastructure and building waste. Because
it is reported together it is not easy to find the quantity of concrete coming from building
and infrastructure separately [31]. It has not been possible to find sources on the amount
of reported waste that originates from infrastructure and the amount that originates from
building waste. Therefore, the whole waste fraction is taken into account in the calcula-
tions and assumed to come from building waste.
In a European context has it not been possible to find the amount of CDW that comes from
infrastructure using the Eurostat database [7]. This is due to it being reported as part of the
mineral fraction and not as part of an individual category [31]. The categories for asphalt
and ballast from railways have not been taken into account in this thesis as it is considered
to come from infrastructure projects.
The national statistics for Danmark use the same methodology for separating the CDW
from buildings and infrastructure. It is described in the section 4.1.2.

bricks also have the possibility of being reused as there are standardised dimensions for
bricks in Denmark and other European countries.
The first step in the treatment of the Bricks is to see if the bricks can be reused directly.
The bricks in a masonry wall are put together with mortar and this has to be able to be
separated from each of the bricks. Traditional Morter in Denmark is made from lime and is
porous. Masonry from before 1950 typically has this type and it makes the bricks able to be
used for direct reuse. Cement mortar on the other hand is difficult to clean from the bricks
and makes it therefore a high risk of damaging them in the demolition or treatment pro-
cess [49]. Many companies are working with direct reuse of the stones and there are also
alternative solutions in Denmark companies like Gamle Mursten, Genbrugssten and Gen-
brugsmursten etc. sell reused bricks with a CE-mark, according to European Assessment
Documents (EAD) [49]. In Denmark, alternative aesthetic examples for recycling bricks are
available, for example, the resource rows, which remove brick facades and replace them
on the exterior facade of an apartment building in Copenhagen shown in figure 8. The
project also uses other elements in the design to optimise the potential of the resources
used in the whole building’s lifecycle [50].

Glass:
In buildings, glass is found in windows mostly. It can also appear in some offices or houses
with glass walls. This type of glass is also referred to as flat glass compared to container
glass from bottles. 90% of the glass in the world is soda-lime silicate glass and consists of sili-
con, calcium, sodium and other materials. This glass has good light transmittance and has
a smooth surface good for windows and facades [54]. The materials are melted with fur-
naces to get the raw material to melt simultaneously at 1600 degrees Celsius. The melted
glass can after this be formed into the desired product or be molted at lower heat. Glass
for buildings sometimes needs a type of coding surface as well to make it more resistant
or for better transmittance. Glass has the potential os be recycled many times and can
replace virgin materials saving energy in the manufacturing process [54]. Even if a small
portion of the CDW has glass the potential to become a closed-loop construction material
if the pure glass can be dismantled correctly and treated to remove potential pollution.
At the construction site glass i sorted as windows. The frame of windows can contain haz-

the windows are clean are they transported to the waste treatment facilities, where the
glass is sorted from the frames with machinery. The frames are put into different fractions
for reuse, recycling or incineration if they are made of metal or wood. The glass can be
recycled into new windows through a new melting process or as mineral wool (glass wool).
The production of glass wool is made of fibres of new and recycled glass and used as insu-

Insulation:
The material fraction from insulation material from buildings is low. Insulation material in

materials” (Thomas Sinding, Development Manager - J. Jensen, Research interview). The
insulation material has a low density and it takes up a lot of space compared to the vol-

different recycling objectives.
If the insulation material does not come in as a clean fraction, can it be sorted from the
mixed waste fractions. Here it can be sorted into different categories. Glass wool can be
recycled to Leca. Glass wool can be added as a raw material in the manufacturing and
production of new insulating Leca blocks [56]. "Stone wool (Rockwool) can also be re-

Denmark (and Europe) at RSG Nordic waste treatment facilities with approval from Rock-
wool. The insulation is sorted and treated at the waste facilities before it can be sent back
to the Rockwool factory, where it can be inserted as a replacement for raw material [57].
Insulation is also a small waste fraction and has not been considered a renewable material.

Wood:
Wood waste generated from CDW of buildings comes in different waste categories. The

wood. Besides this can also be mixed with glass, and plastic that is treated with dangerous
substances [60]. Wood from old windows might also contain PCB, as it was common to use
the in the window joints. Therefore has it to be treated correctly on the recycling facilities
and sent to landfill [61].
Estimations from the Danish Environmental Agency from 2018 are that there is generated
395,000 tons of recycled wood in Denmark. 38% of the wood is used in Danish chipboard
production and the rest is exported mostly to Germany. The majority of the wood waste
(67%) is recycled as large burnable material (sort brændbart) and there is a potential to
recycle 100,000 tons of wood waste in mixed waste fractions [60].
The waste treatement of wood waste from construction and demolition activities are in-
fluenced by the incineration business as wood can be used for energy recovery. At the
construction site is the wood waste often sorted in mixed fractions or as the burnable ma-
terial. The burnable material will be driven directly to the energy recovery facility and the
mixed fractions are sorted at the waste facilities. From the clean wood fraction is it possible
to recycle 100% of the wood to chipboards and the impregnated wood is 80% recyclable

Conclusion
Using European statistics, this thesis provides an overview of the generation of CDW in Euro-
pean countries by using different approaches, where the waste fractions are displayed and
characterized. The main waste category in all European countries is the mineral waste cat-
egory which contributes to 305,650,000 tons (91%) of the total waste generated by coun-
tries in the European Union in 2020. This waste category is a part of different large waste
fractions including concrete, brick, tiles etc. These waste fractions are more in-depth de-
scribed in the Danish national statistics from Affaldsdatasystemet (ADS). These fractions are
described in section 4.3.3. Both the European statistics and ADS show an increase in total
CDW generated by buildings since 2010.
One of the main purposes of this thesis was to calculate waste fractions and total CDW
in Denmark using a bottom-up method and compare the calculated waste fractions to
waste fractions from existing databases from ADS and Eurostat. The bottom-up method
uses material intensity from Danish case studies and the Danish building stock data from
Statistics Denmark. The bottom-up method uses the latest available data on case studies,
building stock and renovation rates, and surprisingly shows a significant difference in the
waste fractions and total CDW compared to the waste fractions in ADS and Eurostat. This
bottom-up approach provides additional information about CDW and shall be seen as a
complementary measure of CDW to the existing databases. The bottom-up estimations
calculate the generation of waste from the different processes where the waste can be
generated according to figure 2 in the construction-, renovation- and demolition of build-
ings. The CDW is then calculated for four building types: single-family houses, terraced
houses, apartments and offices. The findings from these estimations show that the largest
generation of CDW comes from the demolition of single-family houses (1,591,741 tons) and
the renovation of offices (884,879 tons).
When comparing the estimations from the bottom-up approach with the other databases
it is clear that this method estimates a larger generation of CDW. The comparison of the
six waste materials is shown in table 11 and figure 11. The complexity and limitations of the
data from ADS and Eurostat are explained and the assumptions that have to be made
when comparing these very different statistics.
For future studies, it is recommended to use a larger sample size of case studies for all
building types, which can ensure a unified material intensity for the bottom-up approach.
However, there has to be clear guidelines for the data collection of the material intensity
and the square meters especially related to the renovation of buildings.
