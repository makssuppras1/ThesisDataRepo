Abstract
Understanding the conditions governing star formation is key to understanding the evolution of
galaxies. As stars are expected to form from dense, molecular hydrogen gas, understanding the
drivers of star formation will inevitably require us to characterize the conditions and especially
densities of the molecular gas reservoirs in the interstellar medium (ISM). Characterizing the
ISM in turn requires observations of multiple rotational transitions of not only CO and its iso-
topologues, but also dense gas tracers like HCN and HCO+, combined with radiative transfer
modelling. However, there is a dearth of large, systematic surveys that combine these elements.
This thesis represents a key ﬁrst step towards such a study.
Our galaxy sample consists of 15 local (U)LIRGs, with IR luminosities in the range 11.04 →
log10(LIR/ L→) →12.47. Our molecular line data consists of single-dish observations of CO,
HCN and HCO+ up to J = 4 ↑3, HNC, CS and 13CO up to J = 3 ↑2 and a handful of
C18O(1-0) observations, obtained from an extensive literature search in combination with hith-
erto unpublished IRAM-30m and JCMT observations. We analyze our data with two versions
of the publicly available radiative transfer code Dense Gas Toolbox (Puschnig, 2022): version
1.7, which assumes ﬁxed optical depths and version 2, which solves for the optical depths as
free parameters. Dense Gas Toolbox minimizes the observed line ratios against a grid of mod-
elled line ratios from RADEX (van der Tak et al., 2007) with the LVG geometry and assumes a
full density distribution rather than a single-density medium. For both versions, we assume a
lognormal+powerlaw (LN+PL) distribution and estimate the output parameters using Bayesian
inference in the form of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling.
We estimate dense gas fractions by integrating over the mass-weighted distributions from a
threshold of ndense ↓104.5 cm↑3. We obtain intensity-H2 column density CO conversion factors,
XCO (and in turn luminosity-mass conversion factors, ωCO), from the CO emissivity associated
with the best-ﬁt parameters from the MCMC sampling. We then obtain HCN-dense gas conver-
sion factors from the calculated total molecular gas masses and dense gas fractions. We obtain a
mean CO conversion factor of ωCO = (1.6 ± 0.5) M→(K km s↑1 pc↑2)↑1, slightly higher than the
standard (U)LIRG conversion factor of ωCO = 0.8 M→(K km s↑1 pc↑2)↑1 (Downes et al., 1998),
with associated molecular gas masses in the range Mmol ↔(0.3 ↑22) ↗109 M→. Our estimates
of the dense gas fraction span the range fdense ↔0.2 ↑0.9, indicating that a signiﬁcant fraction
to the vast majority of the molecular gas in (U)LIRGs is in the dense phase.
We obtain a
mean dense gas conversion factor of ωHCN = (5.9 ± 0.3) M→(K km s↑1 pc↑2)↑1, with dense mass
estimates in the range Mdense ↔(3 ↑87) ↗108 M→. We also ﬁnd a weak negative correlation
between the dense gas fraction and the depletion timescale, though our sample size is too low
to make any deﬁnitive conclusions. Our results indicate a need of isotopologue observations, as
we ﬁnd signiﬁcant degeneracies in the estimated parameters, with only 8 galaxies converging for
v. 1.7 and none converging for v. 2, as well as three galaxies having unrealistically low fdense
estimates. They also highlight the importance of assumptions pertaining the optical depths and
abundances. While it is known that isotopical lines such as 13CO C18O are able to break the
aforementioned degeneracies, this was not possible in our case, since the observed line ratios were
outside the range of the model grid. We discuss potential improvements in future models as well
as mitigating factors without adding a dramatic increase in complexity to the model grids.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Star formation plays a critical role in the formation and evolution of galaxies. However, due to
observational constraints, the exact mechanisms and conditions which govern the star formation
rate (SFR) and star formation e!ciency (SFE) of galaxies, both in the local Universe and across
cosmic time, remain an open question. A particularly enigmatic piece of this puzzle comes in
the form of (ultra) luminous infrared galaxies ((U)LIRGs) which exhibit SFRs orders of magni-
tude higher than typical main sequence (MS) galaxies in the local Universe, as evident by their
extremely high infrared (IR) luminosities of LIR > 1011 L→, indicating high obscured star forma-
tion unless contaminated by actice galactic nuclei (AGN) activity. These extreme starbursts are
the closest local analogues to the (U)LIRGs/submillimeter galaxies (SMGs) that dominated the
cosmic star formation rate density (cSFRD; Lonsdale et al., 2006) during the cosmic noon epoch
(redshifts z ↘1 ↑3) where the Universe underwent its most intense period of star formation
(e.g. Casey et al., 2018). Understanding the drivers of SFR in these extreme galaxies is therefore
key to broadening our overall understanding of the mechanisms and conditions determining the
SFR of galaxies. As stars form from molecular hydrogen, H2, investigations of the drivers of
star formation will inevitably require us to probe the distributions and densities of molecular
hydrogen.
In the Milky Way, the volume of the interstellar medium (ISM) is dominated by a hot ionized
phase with densities n < 0.01 cm↑3 and a combination of a warm ionized phase and a warm neu-
tral phase (Kennicutt et al., 2012). The hydrogen mass is mostly dominated by neutral atomic
hydrogen, with MHI/MH2 ↔6 (Misiriotis et al., 2006). Giant Molecular Clouds (GMCs) are
the primary drivers of star formation and represent the coldest and densest parts of the Milky
Way ISM, with temperatures T ↘10 K and densities n > 30 cm↑3. Starbursts and (U)LIRGs
however are fundamentally di”erent from normal star forming galaxies like the Milky Way, in
that their ISM is almost fully molecular (Krumholz et al., 2005), with a signiﬁcant fraction of
the molecular gas being in a dense phase (e.g. Greve et al., 2009).
Probing the molecular gas contents and densities however represents a fundamental issue in the
study of star formation, as we for the most part cannot observe H2 directly. Due to rotational
symmetries, H2 molecules can only emit weak quadrupole emission, and the lowest permitted
rotational transition (J = 2↑0) requires temperatures of ↔500 K, much hotter than the average
temperature of the cold, dense GMCs that dominate star formation (Krumholz, 2015). Studies
of the molecular gas mass have therefore traditionally relied on the rotational lines of tracer
molecules like CO and its isotopologues, such as e.g. Stark et al., 1987, Mao et al., 2010 and
Israel, 2020. However, CO traces the total molecular gas mass, particularly the di”use mass,
whereas star formation relies on dense gas, whether that be some threshold density as proposed
by e.g. Gao et al., 2004b or depending on the mean density of the cloud (Krumholz et al.,
2005). The advent of large, sensitive (sub)mm telescopes like the IRAM-30m telescope has in
turn allowed us to observe the rotational transitions of so-called dense gas tracers like HCN,
HNC and HCO+, which require higher densities for excitation. Large, systematic surveys of the
1
==PAGE:8==
dense gas tracers include Gao et al., 2004a, Baan et al., 2008 and Israel, 2023. Some studies
go a step further and combine multi-J observations of dense gas tracers with radiative transfer
modelling to accurately assess the densities and conditions of the probed molecular gas, such as
Greve et al., 2009 in their study of NGC 6240 and Arp 220. However, there is a dearth of studies
which combine systematic surveys of a large number of galaxies with high-J transitions of both
CO and the dense gas tracers with radiative transfer modelling. Studies like e.g. Israel, 2020
use radiative transfer modelling on a large number of galaxies, but omit the dense gas tracers,
while e.g. Israel, 2023 adds the dense gas tracers but omits the radiative transfer modelling.
This thesis represents a key ﬁrst step towards such a study.
We use single-dish observations of CO, HCO+ and HCN up to J = 4 ↑3, CS, HNC and 13CO
up to J = 3 ↑2 as well as a handful of C18O(1-0) observations obtained from an extensive
literature search in combination with until now unpublished observations from IRAM-30m and
JCMT. Our molecular transition data is obtained from a sample of 13 local LIRGs and two local
ULIRGs, representing some of the most extreme galaxies in the local Universe when it comes to
star formation. We analyse these observations using the modern radiative transfer code Dense
Gas Toolbox (van der Tak et al., 2007; Leroy et al., 2017; Puschnig, 2022), which minimizes
observed line ratios against a grid of calculated ratios from RADEX and assumes a full density
distribution in the form of either a lognormal (LN) or lognormal with a powerlaw tail at higher
densities (LN+PL) and an isothermal medium. We use two versions of Dense Gas Toolbox: the
ﬁrst one is the publicly available version 1.7, which includes models for molecular lines up to
J = 3 ↑2 and ﬁxed optical depths, and which we have modiﬁed slightly to increase performance
and convergence. The second one is the only just published version 2+, for which we were given
early access, and which extends the model grids to the J = 4 ↑3 transitions and solves for the
optical depths as free parameters. We estimate the output mean densities, kinetic temperatures
and distribution widths using Bayesian inference in the form of Markov Chain Monte Carlo
(MCMC) sampling. With these outputs, we are able to not only obtain the associated dense
gas fractions, which we deﬁne as the mass fraction of gas at densities n ↓104.5 cm↑3, but also
the total and dense molecular gas masses and their luminosity-to-mass conversion factors, ωCO
and ωHCN. With these, we are able to investigate the molecular gas reservoirs in the central
molecular zones covered by the telescope beams which fuel the exceptional star formation in
these extreme galaxies.
The report is structured as follows: we outline the fundamentals behind radiative transfer mod-
elling, Bayesian inference using MCMC sampling and the speciﬁc implementations and assump-
tions of Dense Gas Toolbox in chapter 2. In chapter 3, we present the properties of our galaxy
sample as well as the molecular line transition data and our basic data processing. We explain
our methodology in detail in chapter 4, including correcting the line ratios for di”erent beam
sizes, changes to the default Dense Gas Toolbox code as well as calculating the dense gas frac-
tions and the associated conversion factors. We present our ﬁndings in chapter 5. We discuss our
ﬁndings in chapter 6, including comparisons with previous ﬁndings, possible reasons for issues
of non-convergence and degeneracies as well as suggestions for future work. Lastly, we conclude
in chapter 7.
Unless explicitly stated otherwise, use of the term density will refer to the density of molecu-
lar hydrogen, CO refers to its main isotopologue 12C16O, and luminosities etc. will refer to the
J = 1↑0 transition, i.e. L↓

Chapter 2
Theory
In this chapter, we outline the basic theory underpinning Dense Gas Toolbox and our parameter
estimation. We ﬁrst outline the fundamentals behind (non-LTE) radiative transfer modelling
in section 2.1, the foundation for the calculation of the Dense Gas Toolbox model grids. We
then present the methods and theory behind MCMC sampling in section 2.2. The derivation
of the ﬁnal expression used for MCMC sampling introduces some formal deﬁnitions of a variety
of di”erent probability density functions (pdfs), but the most important takeaways are the
introduction of the prior and the likelihood, the implementations of which are relatively straight
forward. Lastly, we introduce the speciﬁc implementations of Dense Gas Toolbox in section 2.3,
including the introduction of a density distribution.
2.1
Radiative transfer
In order for tools like Dense Gas Toolbox or RADEX to model the physical environments of
molecular clouds, we need an underlying theoretical model for how properties like density and
temperature can excite molecules and lead to line emissions.
The simplest way to estimate
properties like molecular column density from some observed line intensity is through local
thermal equilibrium (LTE) models which assume a single excitation temperature Tex equal to
the kinetic temperature Tkin. While the LTE method is fast, it implies a Boltzmann distribution
for the level population, which often does not hold (van der Tak, 2011). We must therefore
use more sophisticated methods, which involve solving for the level populations in a statistical
equilibrium and the radiative transfer equations.
2.1.1
Level populations
Before writing the equations of statistical equilibrium, it is necessary to elaborate on some terms.
When we discuss transitions of some molecule, we mean transitions between the rotational levels,
which are indexed by the rotational angular momentum quantum number J.
For diatomic
molecules like CO or CS, the energy of level J has the simple expression EJ = hBJ(J + 1),
where h is Planck’s constant and B is the rotation constant of the molecule (Goldreich et al.,
1974). The level population is then the fraction of molecules at some energy level J, usually
denoted nu or nl for the upper and lower levels, respectively.
With these terms clariﬁed, let us now move on to describing the level populations. The excited
energy levels of a molecule can be populated by collisions and radiation, where the former can
either excite or de-excite a molecule. We therefore need to take both the radiation ﬁeld and
densities of collision partners into account. Collisions are described by the collision rates Cul in
per second per molecule of the species of interest. The subscript ul refers to the transition from

2.1.3
Escape probability
One practical way of decoupling the radiative transfer and level populations is to introduce some
probability of photon escape from the source, ↽. This method has a rather intuitive argument

MCMC sampling
To estimate model parameters like the mean density, distribution width and kinetic temperature
(as well as optical depths for version 2+), Dense Gas Toolbox allows the user to make use of
Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. While it does allow the user to infer parameters
through a simple ε2-minimization, this has two main issues. First, such a method may have
several local minimum solutions, which makes it di!cult to determine which solution is ”best”
(Aster et al., 2012). Second, there is no inherent uncertainty provided with the estimated model
parameters aside from the ε2 goodness-of-ﬁt and the ε2 distribution itself. Both of these issues
can be mitigated with the use of MCMC sampling.
MCMC sampling is based on Bayesian inference, and instead of a single output model, the
output is an assumed representative sample of a posterior model probability density function
(pdf). MCMC sampling is fundamentally based on Bayes’ theorem:

2.3
Dense Gas Toolbox
With the basic underlying theory behind radiative transfer and MCMC sampling outlined, we
will now dive further into the speciﬁcs of how Dense Gas Toolbox uses line observed intensities
to infer the properties of molecular gas. We ﬁrst outline the broad strokes. We then detail
how emission from a single density is predicted and lastly explain how this is combined into an
assumed distribution of densities.
In Dense Gas Toolbox, molecular cloud properties are inferred using line ratios, which has the
beneﬁt of not requiring absolute abundances of the dense gas tracers. The observed line ratios are
minimized against radiative transfer models which assume an isothermal gas and an underlying
density distribution. These models are based on RADEX (van der Tak et al., 2007) using the LVG
geometry and use molecular data from the Leiden Atomic and Molecular Database (LAMDA;
Sch¨oier et al., 2005). The molecular abundances utilized are calibrated through observations
of the EMPIRE survey (Jim´enez-Donaire et al., 2017). Versions 1.7 and below of Dense Gas
Toolbox also use the optical depths from this survey, while versions 2+ solve for the optical
depths as free parameters, though in a relatively conﬁned grid based on the EMPIRE estimates.
The foundation of the model is a grid of one-zone models with a ﬁxed density for which the
emissivity is calculated, the details of which we outline in the next section.
The methods
employed by Dense Gas Toolbox are based on those from Leroy et al., 2017, and as there are as
of yet no papers published detailing the speciﬁcs, the next few sections will rely heavily on the
descriptions outlined in Leroy et al., 2017.

the total volume (Padoan et al., 2002). An example of the shift from a volume-weighted to
a mass-weighted distribution is shown in ﬁg. 2.4 with mean density n0 = 103 cm↑3 and width
ϑ = 0.8 dex, adopted from Leroy et al., 2017. Of note is the fact that the distribution shown in
ﬁg. 2.4 is not normalized with regards to its integral, but rather to its amplitude.

Our galaxy sample consists of 15 local galaxies in total, with 13 designated as LIRGs (1011 L→<

The sample is primarily
based o” the sample from an unﬁnished paper by the DeMoGas team (Leonidaki et al., n.d.),

S60 > 11.65 Jy were chosen, while the cut-o” was S60 > 16.4 Jy for LIRGs. For our sample,
the selection criteria from HerCULES were rather pragmatic: we selected galaxies with enough
observed lines in the transitions relevant for Dense Gas Toolbox, preferably with more than one
transition for each molecule. This ensures that we have enough degrees of freedom and can
realistically solve for the optical depth, for which we require more than one transition for each
molecular species. Regarding the observations, we also had the extra requirement of having
actual detections of the transitions, as Dense Gas Toolbox does not allow the inclusion of upper
limits.

3.2
Molecular transition data
Our molecular transition data consists of single-dish observations of the relevant J-transitions
(up to J = 4 ↑3) from the dense gas tracers CS, HCO+, HCN and HNC as well as CO, 13CO
and C18O from the published literature. The original foundation of our data stems from the
compilation of single-dish observations of dense gas tracers by the DeMoGas team (Leonidaki et
al., n.d.), which we supplemented with additional single-dish data through an extensive literature
search along with CO and isotopologue data, the former from the observations and literature
compilations by Kamenetzky et al., 2016 and Israel, 2020 in particular. However, we eventually
replaced, where possible, the compiled data with CO and 13CO data from Israel, 2020 as well
as HCO+, HCN and HNC data from Israel, 2023.
Our reasoning behind this was two-fold:
ﬁrst, it provided as much internal consistency as possible within our dataset. Intensity values
across di”erent surveys can sometimes vary signiﬁcantly, even at similar apertures. Di”erences in
data reduction can also lead to other inconsistencies, such as error handling. Some studies only
reported the errors on the Gaussian ﬁts while neglecting the errors on the actual data, which lead
to wildly underestimated errors and SNRs in the 100s, even on faint lines. We therefore sought
to eliminate these inconsistencies by drawing as much of our data as possible from the same
studies. The second and perhaps most important reason was aperture correction. Comparing
observations of di”erent molecules across the molecular transitions and di”erent telescopes will
naturally lead to vastly di”erent beam sizes.
Relevant beam corrections must therefore be
applied to the observations before they can be compared in any meaningful way. In this regard,
the observations from Israel, 2020 and Israel, 2023 have the beneﬁt of either being observed
natively at or extrapolated to resolutions of 22”, allowing us to better compare the observations.
Of note is that some of the data we use from Israel, 2023 are originally observations by P´erez-
Beaupuits et al., 2007 Krips et al., 2008, Graci´a-Carpio et al., 2008 and Li et al., 2019. While
the two studies mostly use data from IRAM-30m and JCMT, not all of our sample overlaps with
the two studies, and we therefore use data from a variety of di”erent telescopes. Although some
telescope names like the IRAM-30m are seemingly obvious, we summarize the telescopes used
as well as their abbreviations and dish sizes in table 3.3.

4.1.1
Correcting line ratios
As we replaced a signiﬁcant portion of our sample with the data from Israel, 2020 and Israel,
2023, most of our data has already been re-scaled to 22” apertures. However, as this does not
cover the entirety of our data sample, especially in the case of molecules like CS and galaxies
like NGC 4418, these observations must also be re-scaled in order for line ratios to be computed
and compared in any meaningful way. We follow similar procedures to those found in Aalto
et al., 2007, Costagliola et al., 2011, Lindberg et al., 2011 and Puschnig et al., 2020 and scale
the observations with ς2
line/ς2
CO where ς2
line is the HPBW of the line intensity in question and
ς2
CO is the HPBW of the CO(1-0) line intensity, as all line ratios in Dense Gas Toolbox are
calculated with regards to CO(1-0) by default. Our method does di”er slightly from those in the
aforementioned papers: the ﬁrst three also add the source size of the transition in quadrature,
something we have neglected as we could not ﬁnd reliable estimates of the source sizes of our
galaxies in all the relevant transitions. However, as the majority of our galaxy sample is located
at distances signiﬁcantly larger than 30 Mpc, this does not cause a notable error (see Appendix
A in Lindberg et al., 2011 for an excellent discussion regarding unknown source sizes when cal-
culating line ratios). Puschnig et al., 2020 multiply both the numerator and denominator with
an aperture correction, CAP, calculated based on the methods presented in Lisenfeld et al., 2011
and Stark et al., 2013. Our reasoning for not including the aperture corrections are two-fold:
ﬁrst, aperture corrections are applied when inferring average properties of the source, while we
are mainly interested in the central molecular zones of our sample, i.e. simply the area covered
by the beam. Second, the methods presented in Lisenfeld et al., 2011, Stark et al., 2013 and
Puschnig et al., 2020 rely on assuming some analytic function for the spatial distribution of
the transition, of which we only have reliable estimates for the ﬁrst two transitions of CO (J.
Puschnig, personal communication, Dec. 22, 2024). These methods therefore only realistically
apply an aperture correction to these two transitions, assuming that all emission from other
transitions are covered by the beam. As our CO(1-0) CAP values covered the range from ↘1 to
↘40, we concluded that such aperture corrections were not appropriate for our sample. While
there are other methods for applying aperture corrections, such as using Herschel FIR continuum
maps as in Zhang, 2013, those proved beyond the scope of this thesis.
As mentioned in section 3.2, some sources in our original sample only listed uncertainties on the
Gaussian ﬁts of the line rather than uncertainties reﬂecting the actual data, resulting in unrealis-
tic SNRs in the 100’s, even on faint lines. As Dense Gas Toolbox performs a ε2-minimization and
computes the likelihood for the MCMC sampling based in the resulting ε2 values, this caused
these lines to completely dominate the ﬁts when running Dense Gas Toolbox. We therefore

proved unnecessary when we replaced most of our data sample with the observations from Is-

observational.
After correcting for the beam sizes, we run our sample through both versions 1.7 and 2 of Dense

4.1.2
Version 1.7
We run our galaxy sample with the beam-corrected observations through version 1.7 of Dense
Gas Toolbox with the lognormal+powerlaw density distribution model and with both density,
temperature and width as free parameters. For each galaxy, we perform MCMC sampling to both
ensure as accurate results as possible and to get the inherent uncertainty estimations provided
by this sampling. We perform this sampling with nsims = 2 ↗104 and increase the number of
walkers from the standard 50 to 75, resulting in nsims ↗nwalkers = 1.5 ↗106 samples in total
for each free parameter. To ensure that our samples are uncorrelated and representative of our
posterior distribution, we implement a burn-in of of 10 times the maximum autocorrelation time
calculated by emcee and thin our samples by the mean autocorrelation time.
To get a sense of how our data spans the model space covered by version 1.7 of Dense Gas
Toolbox, we plot our observed line ratios against the model line ratios covered by version 1.71,
i.e. the ﬁrst 3 transitions of CO, 13CO, C18O, CS, HCO+, HCN and HNC. We show these plots
in ﬁgs. 4.1, 4.2, 4.3, 4.4, 4.5 and 4.6. We make the somewhat arbitrary choice to plot the non-CO
lines against the CO(3-2)/CO(1-0) line ratio. As we do not have a CO(3-2) observation for NGC
7552, that galaxy is omitted.
While a good portion of our data lies roughly within the model space covered by Dense Gas
Toolbox, there are of course quite a few outliers. Besides the occasional high line ratios such as
for HCO+(3-2) in ﬁg. 4.4, three of our galaxies have CO(3-2)/CO(1-0) ratios > 1, and 5 have
ratios > 0.8, the maximum value in the modelled line ratios. While this is deﬁnitely not covered
by the model space of the modelled line ratios and indeed represent measurements which should
be taken with a grain of salt, the plots are shown without error bars as they are mostly meant
to generally showcase the model space covered by Dense Gas Toolbox, and the ±1ϑ uncertainty
ranges do bring them closer to realistic values. It should also be mentioned that these ﬁgures are
2D projections of a N ↑1-dimensional model space, where N is the number of observed lines,
so some deviation is expected across these projections. Another interesting case is that of the
13CO/CO line ratios in ﬁg. 4.1, in particular the J = 1 ↑0 transition, where the observed line
ratios are all generally quite low compared to the modelled line ratios. As 13CO is optically thin
while CO is not necessarily optically thin (and indeed is modelled as the most optically thick
line in Dense Gas Toolbox), the 13CO/CO intensity ratio therefore essentially acts as an upper
limit on the 13CO/CO abundance ratio. That our observed line ratios are consistently lower
than the modelled line ratios could therefore indicate that the assumed 13CO/CO abundance
ratio of 1/50 is higher than the actual abundance ratio in our sample.

obtaining the ﬁnal positions and associated probabilities of the walkers.

initialized them in a Gaussian ball scaled to the magnitude of the individual parameters around
the position with the highest associated probability. We repeated this process 2↑3 times before
performing the full sampling run. The output chain of the full sampling run can be found in
ﬁg. 4.9.

implementation of the MCMC sampling with manually deﬁned step sizes.

way the probabilities are calculated: if there is not a steep enough gradient in the probabilities
across the model space, the walkers will not converge to some high-probability subspace but will
instead perform a random walk across the entire space, as we see in ﬁg. 4.11. The problem might
also simply be the temperature range, as version 2 of Dense Gas Toolbox only allows kinetic
temperatures up to 30 K, while 1.7 allows temperatures up to 50 K, or that the CS molecule is
more crucial for accurate parameter estimation than we had initially thought. Whatever the
possible reason, we were unable to further explore possible solutions to the convergence problem
due to time constraints. For the rest of this paper, we will therefore focus on the results from
version 1.7. We present the resulting corner plots and density distributions in chapter 5 and
discuss further possibilities for the non-convergence issues in section 6.2. Meanwhile, we will
now present our methodology for calculating the dense gas fraction fdense in the next section.

Chapter 6
Discussion
In chapter 5, we used the output MCMC samples to model the molecular density distributions
and calculate the associated dense gas fractions. We found convergence for 8/15 galaxies and
proceeded to use the best-ﬁt parameters to calculate XCO and ωCO and in conjunction their
associated molecular gas masses, as well as dense gas conversion factors, ωHCN. We discuss our
overall ﬁndings in section 6.1, compare to previous studies and touch on their validity. We then
discuss the overall validity of our models, assumptions and methodology in section 6.2.
6.1
Conversion factors and molecular gas masses
6.1.1
The total molecular gas mass
Per the overview in table 5.4, we ﬁnd a mean CO conversion factor of ωCO = 1.6 ± 0.5, slightly
higher than the standard (U)LIRG value of ωCO = 0.8 from Downes et al., 1998, though their
upper values are within the uncertainty range of our estimate, and still lower than the standard
Galactic (Milky Way) value of ωCO = 4.8 (Solomon et al., 1991; Young et al., 1991). Our values
are however more in agreement with those found by Papadopoulos et al., 2012b, who ﬁnd a
mean value of ωCO = 1.3 ± 0.4 for their one-phase modelling. They also argue that CO conver-
sion factors in (U)LIRGs can reach near-Galactic (i.e. Milky Way) values, leading to a severe
underestimation of the molecular gas content when using exclusively low-J CO lines.
As we ended up basing a signiﬁcant portion of our sample on the data from Israel, 2020 and
Israel, 2023, it is logical to compare our conversion factors to those found by F.P. Israel in the
former paper. Before doing so, we should outline a few key di”erences in methodology between
this thesis and Israel, 2020. First, the 2020 paper only uses CO and 13CO data, as the dense
gas tracers in the 2023 paper are used to analyse intensity and luminosity relations and ra-
tios rather than radiative transfer modelling. Second, the 2020 paper uses RADEX to compute
a grid of two-phase models to compare their line ratios to for two di”erent assumed CO/13CO
abundance ratios of 40 and 80. In contrast, we assume a full density distribution but a single
kinetic temperature and a ﬁxed abundance ratio of 50 (see table 2.2). We discuss our abundance
assumptions and their impact further in section 6.2. Of note is that they found poor ﬁts for
NGC 1614 at both abundances, in line with our ﬁndings where we were unable to obtain tight
constraints on the density (see ﬁg. D.1 in appendix D). In the end, Israel, 2020 had enough CO
and 13CO observations to obtain solutions for three galaxies in our (converged) sample: NGC
1068, 2623 and 6240. We summarize their results for these galaxies in table 6.1. We list the
parameters of both phases (1 & 2), their relative contributions (f12), and the calculated CO
conversion factors, XCO and ωCO. A few obvious di”erences are immediately clear. First, while
NGC 1068 and 2623 were ﬁtted with a somewhat lower 13CO/CO abundance ratio than what is

We showed the dense gas fractions, fdense, as a function of φdepl in ﬁg. 5.9, with and without the
unrealistically low values of NGC 34, 3256 and 7552. We found a negative correlation between
the two parameters, with Spearman’s rank correlation coe!cients of ⇀= ↑0.52 and ⇀= ↑0.80
for the cases with and without the three galaxies, respectively. As φdepl is the inverse of the star
formation e!ciency SFE, this negative correlation seems to agree with the density threshold
model of star formation (called so by Usero et al., 2015), where the SFE is determined by the
amount of molecular gas above a certain threshold density (i.e. our dense gas limit of 104.5 cm↑3),
a view advocated for by e.g. Gao et al., 2004b, Wu et al., 2005, Lada et al., 2010 and Lada et al.,
2012.
In this view, the SFE is correlated with the dense gas fraction. Mattern et al., 2024 also come to
similar conclusions in their survey of Milky Way GMCs. This is in contrast with the turbulence
regulated models, such as those by Krumholz et al., 2005, Krumholz et al., 2007 and Federrath
et al., 2012, where the SFE depends on the average density of a molecular cloud as well as the
Mach number, meaning the SFE is determined by average cloud properties rather than some
absolute density threshold. This view is supported by e.g. Usero et al., 2015, whose survey of
HCN(1-0) in star-forming galaxies found that the dense gas SFE was ↘6 ↑8 times lower near
galaxy centres, associated with high dense gas fractions, than it was in the outer regions, as well
as Bigiel et al., 2016 in their study of M51.
While our ﬁndings seem to lean more towards the density threshold model than the turbulence
regulated model, there are a few critical caveats to keep in mind. First is the fact that our sam-
ple size is low, with only 5 galaxies after the exclusions of NGC 34, 3256 and 7552. Even with
their inclusion, the sample size is still only 8, and we then have their extremely low, unrealistic
dense gas fractions a”ecting the results. Our p-values are also too high to conclude anything
at a 95% signiﬁcance level. Second is the fact that we actually implicitly assume turbulence
regulated models (or rather, gravoturbulent models) in some aspects of our methodology, as
turbulent GMCs are expected to follow the lognormal distributions outlined by e.g. Krumholz
et al., 2005. The transition to a powerlaw distribution, which describes gravitationally unstable,
free-falling density structures (Burkhart et al., 2019) also depends on the derived mean volume
density rather than ﬁxed threshold density such as our deﬁned minimum density for ”dense”
gas. Therefore, while the results seemingly lean more toward the threshold density models of
star formation, we do not have enough statistical certainty for a deﬁnitive answer.
All of the parameters discussed so far fundamentally rely on our estimated XCO and ωCO con-
version factors. While we do not ﬁnd too much scatter in our estimated values, we caution

6.2
Methodology and assumptions
In section 6.1, we discussed our overall ﬁndings, with a focus on the total and dense molecular
gas masses and their respective luminosity-to-mass conversion factors. We found some variation
between our derived values and values from the literature, including studies where we have
overlap in data. A signiﬁcant cause of this variation is the choice of models and the underlying
assumptions in those models, which we will now discuss in this section. We ﬁrst discuss the
non-convergence of a signiﬁcant portion of our sample for both utilized versions of Dense Gas
Toolbox. We then discuss the underlying assumptions in Dense Gas Toolbox, including optical
depths, relative abundances, estimations of Tkin and the choice of density distribution.
6.2.1
The issue of non-convergence
For both versions 1.7 and 2 of Dense Gas Toolbox, we experienced major issues of convergence:
in the case of the former, only 8/15 galaxies converged, while none of our galaxies converged
for the latter. We have already presented a variety of di”erent attempts to ﬁx this behaviour
in section 4.1.3, so the issue must lie elsewhere. As previously mentioned, the non-convergence
was associated with high likelihoods throughout almost the entire model space, which resulted
in a behaviour more resembling a random walk rather than convergence to some constrained
solution area. The behaviour was also not due to the observed line ratios not being covered by
the modelled ratios of Dense Gas Toolbox: in fact, as discussed in section 6.1.1, the galaxies
that converged for version 1.7 were generally the ones with observed line ratios not sampled by
the model. Both versions of Dense Gas Toolbox assume a uniform prior, meaning the posterior
peaks at the maximum likelihood estimate. The likelihood, in turn, is calculated from a ε2
estimate. This method is not unique to Dense Gas Toolbox, as it is also utilized by e.g. Mendel
et al., 2014 in their stellar mass estimates from the SDSS catalogue, as well as by Tunnard
et al., 2015. It does however mean that if the ε2-estimated parameters are not unique, the
resulting MCMC sampling will also lead to undetermined parameters. The issue immediately
becomes apparent when we consider the ε2-diagnostics ﬁgures in ﬁg. 5.4 for IRAS 17208-0014
as well as the rest in appendix E: similarly low ε2 estimates are found for almost the full
parameter range.
This represents a clear degeneracy between the density, temperature and
width (not shown in those ﬁgures), where the same observed line ratios can arise from a wide
variety of di”erent excitation conditions and combinations of density and temperature. This
degeneracy between especially density and temperature is a known issue in radiative transfer
modelling in general, as well as RADEX in particular, the backbone of the Dense Gas Toolbox
model grids. Viti, 2017 calculated a grid of line ratios from RADEX and found that line intensity
ratios were not unique across the grid, representing clear degeneracies in the RADEX solutions.
Crucially, they also found that only relying on line ratios could lead to erroneous conclusions
on the physical conditions of the emitting gas, and that the individual intensities as well as
fractional abundances should be taken into account. Israel, 2020 also mention the degeneracy
between density and temperature in their survey and argue that optically thin isotopologues
like 13CO are needed to break this degeneracy. As we already have observations of 13CO for
a large portion of our galaxies, as well as C18O for a few, this indicates that we might require
additional isotopologues. In Tunnard et al., 2015, the use of the more optically thin isotopologues
H13CN and H13CO+ allowed them to break this degeneracy in their radiative transfer modeling.
However, this represents an additional challenge, as these isotopologues are extremely faint;
dense gas tracers like HCN and HCO+ are already faint enough that their use as tracers has
only become possible within the last few decades, and, although their isotopologues are brighter
than the [13C]/[C] isotope ratio would make them seem due to them being optically thin, they
are still exceptionally faint. Tunnard et al., 2015 were only able to obtain upper limits for their
observations, even though they used the IRAM Plateau de Bure Interferometer (PdBI) instead of
single-dish observations. The use of these isotopologues to break the degeneracy is therefore more
61
==PAGE:68==
likely limited to Northern Extended Millimeter Array (NOEMA, the successor of the PdBI) and
Atacama Large Millimeter/submillimeter Array (ALMA) observations of the closest (U)LIRGs,
which is hindered by ALMA’s small ﬁeld of view. An alternative way to break this degeneracy
could to do as suggested by Viti, 2017 and incorporate the individual intensity of at least one
tracer, as was also done in Tiwari et al., 2018, where they incorporated the absolute intensity of
CO. This however requires knowing the absolute abundance of the speciﬁc tracer (Leroy et al.,
2017). In our case, the line ratios of the CO isotopologues were not covered by the modelled line
ratios, most likely due to the assumed abundances. As such, the degeneracies might simply be a
consequence of the abundance assumptions, rather than a requirement of further isotopologues.
Another way of breaking the degeneracy which does not strictly involve the radiative transfer
modelling process could be the introduction of a non-uniform prior, which would mean that
the posterior distribution is not completely governed by the likelihood. Introducing a di”erent
prior should however be applied with caution: as one might recall from section 2.2, the prior
distribution ⇀(m) represents our prerequisite knowledge of the model. Empirically estimated
priors, such as those used for crosshole GPR tomography in geophysics (see e.g. Hansen et
al., 2013) can provide necessary constraints on the posterior distribution. However, if a non-
empirically estimated prior is applied carelessly, it can introduce a signiﬁcant bias. As empirically
estimated priors are, of course, harder to obtain in the ﬁeld of astrophysics than in geophysics,
we must be careful to only assign priors that are motivated by physics. One such constraint on
the prior could be limiting the allowed range for Tkin based on estimates of the dust temperature
Td through dust continuum spectral energy distributions (SEDs), as we generally expect Tkin ↫
Td.
In our case, ﬁxing Tkin did not break the degeneracy in section 4.1.3, indicating either
an additional degeneracy with regards to the width of the distribution or that our temperature
estimate was simply a poor ﬁt. That the ε2 values were all similar over a wide range of parameters
in ﬁg. 5.4 rather than showing distinct minima for various permutations of parameters might
also indicate a deeper underlying issue in using Dense Gas Toolbox for our sample rather than
the classic issues of degeneracy with radiative transfer modelling. Whatever the cause of, or ﬁx
to, these issues of convergence, we for now leave it up to future studies. For now, we will discuss
the validity and impact of the assumptions behind Dense Gas Toolbox in section 6.2.2.
Before doing so, however, we feel the need to stress that in this study, we used two di”erent
versions of a state of the art radiative transfer software using observations of 4 di”erent dense
gas tracers up to transitions J = 4 ↑3 combined with both CO and its isotopologues 13CO and
C18O. Our sample spans a wide array of both critical/e”ective excitation densities, excitation
temperatures as well as (assumed) optical depths (see tables 2.1 and 2.2). We should in theory
have enough observations to tightly constrain the estimated parameters. Despite this, only 8/15
galaxies in our sample converged for the version with ﬁxed optical depths, and none of the
galaxies converged for the even more sophisticated version which solved for the optical depth of
each molecule as a free parameter. Had we simply performed a ε2-minimization for our observed
line ratios versus the calculated model grid and accepted the ”best-ﬁt” parameters at face value
without running further diagnostics on the validity of the results, we would have been left with
estimates that are a poor reﬂection on the physical conditions of the central molecular zones
of our sample, in the best of cases. Our results therefore highlight the need to be wary of the
outputs when using a radiative transfer code and not trust them blindly.
This is of course even without getting into the overall accuracy of radiative transfer modelling, in
particular RADEX. Tunnard et al., 2016 investigated the ability of RADEX with the LVG geometry
to recover the ”true” conditions from a set of generated HCN and CO observations using both
grid and MCMC methods. They found that, even under generous assumptions, RADEX struggled
to recover any parameter better than within half a dex, with no evidence of a systematic o”set
that one would be able to correct for.
Even in the best of cases and under the most valid
assumptions of abundances and optical depths as well as disregarding the assumptions of a few
zones/phases of ﬁxed density and kinetic temperature, there is an inherent uncertainty in the
parameters estimated through radiative transfer modelling that one should keep in mind.

6.2.2
The assumptions behind Dense Gas Toolbox
The assumed molecular (and isotopologue) abundances as well as optical depths will of course
have a signiﬁcant impact on the estimated result. If we recall from section 2.1, under the escape
probability approximation (which we use via the LVG geometry), the escape probability is a
function of optical depth. The optical depth therefore governs the fraction of photons from some
excited molecular species we are able to observe, meaning it has an obvious importance on the
molecular cloud conditions inferred through radiative transfer modelling. After all, if we observe
a particularly bright HCN(1-0) line, is that because the density and temperature conditions are
just right to excite that particular transition, or because the transition is optically thin and
almost all the emitted photons are able to escape from the cloud? This is also why optically thin
isotopologues are able to break the degeneracy discussed in section 6.2.1, as the isotopologues
have similar (and in our case, assumed equal) excitation conditions.
Much of the same is true for the abundance, as a given transition might be exceptionally bright
simply because there is a high abundance of the emitting molecule. We summarize the assumed
abundances and optical depths in table 2.2. In the case of the abundances, we already have
some evidence that the [C]/[13C] isotope ratio assumed is too low, e.g. due to the low 13CO/CO
line ratios in ﬁg. 4.1 and the ﬁndings by Henkel et al., 2014, Sliwa et al., 2014 and Tunnard
et al., 2015. In their radiative transfer modelling with NGC 1068 as a case study, Butterworth
et al., 2022 ﬁnd that variations in line ratios are to a higher degree caused by assumed fractional
abundances rather than di”erent densities or temperatures.
It is at this point worth pointing where the assumed fractional abundances and optical depths
in Dense Gas Toolbox are from. They are in fact calibrated through observations of the EM-
PIRE survey (e.g. Jim´enez-Donaire et al., 2017), which is a sample of 9 nearby, star forming
disc galaxies. This detail is important: the assumed fractional abundances and optical depths
in Dense Gas Toolbox are not based on any observations of (U)LIRGs, but instead only local
disc galaxies. This explains why e.g. our observed 13CO/CO and C18O ratios (ﬁgs. 4.1 and 4.2)
are so far outside the modelled line ratio space. The same is true for the optical depths, which
are ﬁxed in version 1.7 of Dense Gas Toolbox, i.e. the version with which we were able to obtain
results. The issue is compounded by the fact that the optical depth for a given emitter can
vary strongly between galaxies, even of the same type. Bemis et al., 2024 ﬁnd that CO is mod-
erately optically thin in extreme galaxies, and that the observed CO intensity varies strongly
with the optical depths. Israel, 2023 overall ﬁnd low optical depths for their observed dense gas
tracers. On the other hand, while Greve et al., 2009 argue that the bulk of the CO emission in
ULIRG(s)/extreme starbursts comes from a di”use phase with CO optical depths φ ↘1, they
also ﬁnd high HCN(1-0) optical depths for NGC 6240, with φ ∞3↑29, which is in stark contrast
to the optical depths from table 2.2, where CO is the most optically thick line, and where HCN
has optical depths of φHCN = 0.8.
Version 2 of Dense Gas Toolbox attempts to mitigate this by solving for the optical depth as
a free parameter. While this is an improvement over treating the optical depths as ﬁxed, it
does not solve the problem entirely, even ignoring the fact that none of the galaxies in our sam-
ple converged with this version. The introduction of these extra free parameters that had to
be included in the model grids caused their ﬁle size to increase dramatically, from ↔1 MB to
↔10 ↑40 GB depending on the chosen model, which is despite the allowed temperature range
being cut in half and many of the molecules being split into di”erent models. This is the cause
of the somewhat confusing overview in table 2.3. The minimum RAM requirements are approx-
imately double the disc space requirements. This dramatic increase in model complexity and
size as a function of the optical depths means that the corresponding allowed size and dynamic

model grids with abundances, optical depth ranges and temperatures appropriate for a few select
galaxy types, such as local disc galaxies, (U)LIRGs, etc. Calculating and customizing these on
the ﬂy would require not only a working installation of RADEX but also extra time for model
grid calculations and interfacing with RADEX, the latter of which was found by Tunnard, 2016 to

The galaxy template model would also enable a more appropriate temperature range. The max-
imum kinetic temperature in version 2 of Dense Gas Toolbox is Tkin,max = 30 K, which is not
appropriate in the case of our sample, as the molecular gas in starbursts and luminous galaxies
is usually warmer than in normal star forming galaxies (Bradford et al., 2003; Rangwala et al.,
2011; Bolatto et al., 2013). On the subject of kinetic temperature, the temperature estimation
is one of the weaker aspects of Dense Gas Toolbox, with the assumption of a single, ﬁxed tem-
perature within the beam. Israel, 2020 for example argue for implementing a smoothly changing
temperature. While it is di!cult to implement multi-zone temperature estimates without in-
creasing the complexity of the model even further, one compromise could be a density-dependant
temperature distribution, where the inferred parameter is some average or otherwise reference
temperature.
One of the more sensible assumptions of Dense Gas Toolbox is the implementation of a full den-
sity distribution, as described in section 2.3.2. However, as mentioned in that section, the beam
will inevitably cover more than one GMC, meaning the ”true” density distribution within the
beam will take on a more complicated form. Capturing the behaviour of the density distribution
is key for modelling the environments of GMCs, with e.g. Juneau et al., 2009 ﬁnding that the
molecular density distribution may be the key primary physical mechanism driving the observed
line ratios in (U)LIRGs. A way of compensating for this without introducing too much further
complexity could be to solve for a weighted linear combination of a few (2 ↑3) density distribu-
tions. If the full, combined density distribution turns out to be too complicated to be described
by such a method, one could go a step further and solve for a weighted linear combination of
template distributions with ﬁxed widths and mean densities, such as e.g. EAZY (Brammer et al.,
2008) does for its SED ﬁtting and redshift estimation.
6.3
Future outlook
As this thesis represents the ﬁrst steps towards a large, systematic survey of local galaxies com-
bining multi-line, high-J transitions and radiative transfer modelling, there is ample opportunity
for expanding the scope. The ﬁrst and most obvious step is to expand the sample size. Due to
our relatively limited sample size and issues of non-convergence, we often ended up having to
draw conclusions from the results of 4 ↑5 galaxies. Including the rest of the samples from the
preliminary study by Leonidaki et al., n.d. as well as the observations from the Israel, 2020 and
Israel, 2023 surveys would increase the sample size to ↔40 ↑50 galaxies. As the data is already
processed and in the latter cases already converted to units of Tmb, it could be added to the
pipeline without much e”ort. The inclusion of less extreme, non-(U)LIRG galaxies (as in the
64
==PAGE:71==
EMPIRE survey for normal star forming galaxies) would also allow us to probe the conditions
of galaxy types that have not been studies as extensively previously and therefore represent an
as of yet unexplored parameter space.
In chapter 6, we discussed the importance of sophisticated radiative transfer modelling which
can account for the variations in abundances, optical depths as well as the complexity of density
distributions found within a single beam. While version 2 of Dense Gas Toolbox is a step in this
direction, with its treatment of the optical depth as a free parameter, more can still be done, as
highlighted by our success (or lack thereof) with that version. Although we discussed a myriad
of suggestions in chapter 6, we particularly want to highlight the suggestions of shared optical
depth range and the linear combinations of distributions.
The dramatic increase in size and complexity of the model grids with the introduction of the op-
tical depths as free parameters hints at a dimensionality problem in radiative transfer modelling,
which makes more sophisticated models di!cult to implement without only covering a severely
limited parameter range. This dimensionality problem hints at the advantageous use of machine
learning (ML) algorithms, either unsupervised ML such as principal component analysis (PCA)
or Gaussian mixture modelling (GMM), or supervised ML in the form of (convolutional) neural
networks. For now, we leave further details up for future work.
As mentioned earlier, including observations from normal star forming galaxies would allow us to
probe the conditions of an as of yet mostly unexplored parameter space. However, observations
of dense gas tracers from these galaxies are more di!cult to obtain with our current facilities
due to how much fainter they are than in e.g. (U)LIRGs. In that regard, IRAM-30m is still our
best tool, as both JCMT and LMT lack heterodyne receivers like EMIR, though the latter could
be a powerful observational tool if equipped with such a receiver due to its large 50 m aperture.
Inferometric arrays like NOEMA and ALMA o”er much higher resolution and sensitivity, but
with their limited FoV, large systematic surveys would require massive investments in observa-
tional time. They would, however, allow us to probe the conditions of the higher-z submillimeter
galaxies which are believed to have accounted for a signiﬁcant fraction of the cSFRD at z ↘2.
In the future, the proposed Atacama Large Aperture Submillimeter Telescope (AtLAST; e.g.
Mroczkowski et al., 2024) will no doubt allow us to better probe the star forming conditions
of the local Universe, while interferometric arrays like the Next Generation Very Large Array
(ngVLA) and the Square Kilometer Array (SKA) as well as newly deployed ALMA Band 1
(Huang et al., 2016) will allow us to improve our understanding of the cosmic baryon cycle for
higher-z galaxies.
In short: while the IRAM-30m telescope is still our best tool for probing the molecular gas den-
sities and conditions for star formation, with its large dish size and heterodyne receiver, there is
still ample opportunity for furthering our understanding of the conditions driving star formation
with publically available datasets and more sophisticated radiative transfer models.

Chapter 7
Conclusions
We present the ﬁrst steps towards investigating the molecular gas properties of a large, systematic
survey of galaxies combining multi-J observations of CO, its isotopologues and dense gas tracers,
combined with radiative transfer modelling. We combine data from an extensive literature search
with so-far unpublished IRAM-30m and JCMT data, consisting of observations of CO, HCN
and HCO+ up to J = 4 ↑3, CS, HNC and 13CO up to J = 3 ↑2 as well as a handful of
C18O observations. Our galaxy sample consists of 15 local LIRGs+ULIRGs with mean redshift
z ↔0.015, representing the extreme ends of both the IR luminosity function and galaxy main
sequence in terms of star formation in the local Universe. We analyse our data using two versions
(1.7 and 2) of the modern radiative transfer code Dense Gas Toolbox, which assumes a full LN or
LN+PL distribution of densities, where we utilize the latter. Dense Gas Toolbox minimizes the
observed line ratios against a model grid calculated in RADEX with the LVG geometry and outputs
the mean density, kinetic temperature and width of the density distribution, with version 2 also
solving for the optical depths. We estimate the output parameters via Bayesian inference in the
form of Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) sampling. We estimate the dense gas fractions
by integrating the density distributions from a threshold density of ndense ↓104.5 cm↑3 and
calculate the XCO and by extension ωCO conversion factors from the best-ﬁt CO(1-0) emissivity.
We ﬁnd a mean luminosity-to-mass conversion factor of ωCO = 1.6 ± 0.5 M→(K km s↑1 pc↑2)↑1
and associated molecular gas masses in the range Mmol ↔(0.3 ↑22) ↗109 M→. Our estimated
dense gas fractions indicate that a signiﬁcant portion of the molecular gas in these galaxies is in
the dense phase, with our most realistic estimates spanning the range fdense ↔0.2↑0.9. We ﬁnd
a mean dense gas conversion factor of ωHCN = (5.9 ± 0.3) M→(K km s↑1 pc↑2)↑1 and dense gas
masses in the range Mdense ↔(3↑87)↗108 M→. Our results highlight the impact of assumptions
like fractional abundances and optical depths, as only 8/15 galaxies converged to constrained
solutions for version 1.7, while none of the galaxies converged for version 2.
Of the 8 that
converged, 3 had highly unrealistic estimates of the dense gas fraction, with fdense ↘10↑3. The
7 galaxies that did not converge were also the ones with observations best covered by the modelled
line ratio grid. This indicates a signiﬁcant degeneracy in parameter estimation, requiring the use
of isotopologues. As the assumed abundances in Dense Gas Toolbox are based on the EMPIRE
survey of normal, star-forming disc galaxies, our 13CO observations were far outside the modelled
line ratio space and were thus unable to break this degeneracy. We also discuss potential ﬁxes
and improvements, such as modelling the density distribution as a weighted linear combination
of template distributions, as well as discuss possible next steps for future work.
