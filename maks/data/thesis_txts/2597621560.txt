ABSTRACT

Abstract
The growing need for alternatives to conventional use of pesticides and fertilizers, have resulted in
an increased interest for plant growth promoting bacteria. In this project was four ﬂourescent pseu-
domonas strains (P. sp. 6.1, P. sp. 6.3, P. sp. 6.7 and P. sp. 6.10) analysed for plant growth promoting
(PGP) traits, using bioinformatic tools, and a model for further evaluation was proposed. The anti-
SMASH 5.0 Pipeline was used to predict the presences of secondary metabolites, synthesised by non-
ribosomal peptide synthases (NRPSs) and polikitide synthases (PKSs). Multiple seconday metabo-
lites were found in all four genomes, of which several sideorphores and cyclic lipopeptides were
assocatedd with PGP. The four strains were further analyzed by BLASTing their genomes against
a mannualy made database of known PGP and phytopathogenic traits. Genes for phosphate solubi-
lization and auxine production were found in all four strains. No genes for nitrogen ﬁxation, Type 3
Secretion Systems or phytotoxins were found. Additionally genes for hydrogen cyanide and chitinase
were found in one of the strains. Of the four strains strains P. sp. 10 showed the most promesing
results as a biofertilizer, while P. sp. 6.1 show potenatial as a biocontrole strains though strong iron
sequestering and induction of systemic resistance in plants. A system for further testing the strains
ability to stabilize in a natural soil community and their effect against a phytopathogenic fusarium,
was proposed and a protocol for how to test the system was made.

AIM OF STUDY
3
Aim of study
The orginial aim of this study was to evaluate the potential of Pseudomonas DTU9.1 in a barley-plant
model system infected with plant phytogenic fusarium, in the presence of a natural soil community.
The original project was cut short, because the corona pandemic hit one month into the project. A
bioinformatic analysis was therefore added due to the Corona pandemic limiting lab access.
This project now aims to ﬁnd and characterise Pseoudomonas strains for their potential use as plant
growth promotors. For this, two approaches were used: bioinformatic analysis and in situ testing.
In chapter 5, bioinfomartic tools was used to characterise four Pseudomonas strains, isolated from
sugar beet rhizospheres strains, for direct and indirect plant growth promoting traits. The antiSMASH
5.0 Pipeline was used to predict the presence of NRPSs and PKSs in the strains. The results and their
plant growth promoting relevance was manually evaluated. A database of known PGP and phy-
topathogenic traits was constructed and used to detect known PGP and phytopathogenic traits using
BLAST. The results was used to evaluate the strains potential usage as plant growth promoting strains.
Chapter 6, is a literature study based on previous projects and available research in the ﬁeld. Both
in vitro and in vivo methods for testing the strains potential as PGP strains are proposed. Assays
for further phenotypic evaluation of PGP traits identiﬁed in the four Pseudomonas sugar beet rhizo-
sphere strains are proposed. Dual culture plate assays and cross assays are proposed as methods for
in vitro testing of interactions between the individual strains and literature is used to predict them .
A lab-scale plant model system designed in a previous project for in vivo testing is introduced and
methods for inoculation and quantifying root colonisation of the proposed PGP Pseudomonas strains,
the artiﬁcial soil community and the phytopathogenic Fusarium strains are proposed ˙With this sys-
tem, a lab-scale plant model system is proposed to monitor and quantify fungal diseases in Barley
and growth and root colonisation of selected Pseudomonas biocontrol strains in the Barley model
system are predicted from literature. The effects of potential biocontrol strain on the natural micro-
bial community in soil is likewise predicted from literature. The hypothesised results will be used to
predict the proposed Pseudomonas biocontrol strains’ ability to stabilize in a natural soil community
and suppress Fusarium diseases in Barley.

INTRODUCTION
4
Introduction
4.1
The need for plant growth promoting organisms
The growing human population is estimated to have reached nearly 10 billion people by 2050. This
combined with increasing living standards, pressures the global food production to increase by at
least 70 %. Right now, food production is responsible for around 25 % of the global greenhouse gas
emissions and the expansion of farming areas into previously uncultivated nature is responsible for 60
% of global land based biodiversity loss [1]. Increasing areas for farming is therefore not a sustainable
solution. Instead the yield of the area already used must be increased.
The most effective measure used to day is application of chemical fertilizers. However excessive and
incorrect use of fertilizers in agriculture has lead to leakage of nitrates and pesticides to groundwater
and other water sources which has detrimental effects on the environment [2]. Another effective mea-
sure is disease protection. Every year at least 20 – 40 % of crops are lost world wide either directly or
indirectly due to plant diseases [1]. This is estimated to be 40 billion dollars worth of crops. A large
percentage of crops are cereals because of their use as both feed and food [3]. Of these are wheat and
barley the most produced and consumed cereals in the world, making up approximately one–third of
the world’s total grain production [4]. An estimated 135.65 million ton barley is yearly produced and
most commonly used as animal feed and in Beer production [5].
One of the biggest threats to cereal production is fungal plant diseases caused by the species Fusarium.
Fusarium species are present in 2 spots of the top 10 fungal pathogens in molecular plant pathology,
the fourth being Fusarium graminearum and the ﬁfth being Fusarium oxysporum [5]. Fusarium
causes loss in both yield and grain quality by reducing plant growth, lowering protein content and
mycotoxin contamination of grain [4]. Crop rotation and pesticides are the most common measure of
prevention. Effective preventative measures by crop rotation have had limited effect due to the broad
host range of Fusarium species and the ability of Fusarium to stay dormant in the soil as spores for
up to 4 years. The use of pesticides, have been of tremendous value to mankind enabling control of
multiple phytopathogenic organisms. However over the last decade it has become clear that its usage
have had unintended effects on the environment and human health. Since the crop yield must increase
to meet rising population levels and living standards, alternative measures are called for.
Such measure could be the use plant growth promoting bacteria (PGPB), which is deﬁned as the
use of microorganisms capable of increasing plant nutrient uptake (biofertilizers) or inhibiting plant
pathogens (biocontrol or biopesticides). Over the last 50 years, a few hundred strains have been
shown effective against soil born pathogens in literature, however only a few are used in existing
biopesticides on the market [6]. A major factor contributing to these inconsistencies is the inability
of the strains to stabilize in the existing root communities when introduced to a new environment[7].
A model for studying such interactions is therefore needed.

INTRODUCTION
4.2
The Rhizosphere and its complex microbiota
The rhizosphere is deﬁned as the narrow zone in which the plant roots effect the surrounding soil.
This zone is estimated to be a only a few millimeters wide but has been described as one of the most
dynamic interfaces on Earth [8]. Plants release multiple compounds into the rhizosphere, generally
described as exudates, of which a substantial part is photosynthesised carbon [9]. Most unplanted soil,
also called bulk soil, are extremely low in readily available carbon [10]. Consequently, the carbon
rich exudates makes it favourable for microorganisms to colonise the plant roots, which results in the
number of microorganisms in the rhizosphere being one to two orders of magnitude higher than in
bulk soil [11].
Figure 1: Illustration depicting the narrow zone of soil surrounding roots that is inﬂuenced
by plant roots, called the rhizosphere.
The ﬁgure zooms in on the rhizosphere, containing
pathogenic and symbiotic bacteria and fungi. The root colonizing microorganisms are concen-
trated at the root tip and along the root at sites of cell elongation [8]. The second magniﬁcation
depicts arbuscular mycorrhizal fungi (AMF) that colonize inside the roots.
Exudate generally contain a combination of organic acids such as citrate, malate, succinate, pyruvate,
fumarate, oxalate and acetate, sugars such as glucose, xylose, fructose, maltose, sucrose, galactose
and ribose, different α-amino acids, nucleobases and vitamins such as thiamin and biotin, but the
composition varies between plant species [12]. In the seedling stage, root exudates can represent
between 30 and 40 % of the plant’s photosynthates. In young plants, the release of these molecules
is quite signiﬁcant as it corresponds to up to 30 % of total biomass production [13]. The release of
these compounds, pays off because it allows plants to effect and communicate with the rhizosphere
microorganisms. The bacteria in return supports the plant with important nutrients such as ﬁxed ni-
trogen and phosphate, as well as protection from plantpathogens. Thereby allowing the plant to get
aid in overcoming environmental stresses, such as pathogen attack, drought, nutrient acquisition, and
metal toxicity [14].
The microorganisms does not colonize roots evenly, but exists in concentrated populations where ex-
udates gets released or where there is other forms for rhizodeposits such as dead cells or leakage from
wounds. The secretion of exudates is concentrated on living root hairs, the tip of the roots and from

4.3
Phytopathogens
Even though the plants exude sugars into the rhizosphere, the rhizosphere is frequently a nutrient
limited environment. Soil born phytopathogens escapes the competition for the scarce nutrients in
the rhizosphere by colonizing and multiplying inside of the nutrient rich plants. Phytopathogens
exist within most domains of microorganisms, including bacteria, fungi, nematodes and viruses [17].
Bacterial plant pathogens such as Erwinia carotovora, Dickeya solani and Pseudomonas syringae
pv. syringae penetrate the plant cell walls by secreting a variety of cell wall-degrading enzymes
(CWDEs), including pectinases and cellulases, via a type 2 secretion system (T2SS). Other important
virulence factors include phytotoxins and the type 3 secretion system (T3SS) which functions as a
needle for injection of biologically active effector molecules into the host cells [17], see ﬁgure 3.
Figure 3: Illustration of three common bacterial plant pathogenic virulence mechanisms leading
to altered hos cell physiology. Left: secretion of CWDEs by T2SS. Middle: secretion of effector
molecules by a T3SS. Left: secretion of Toxins and plant hormones [17].

4.3.1
Phytopathogenic Fusarium
Fusarium is a large genus of ﬁlamentous fungi, part of a group of fungi referred to as hyphomycetes
or mold. Most fusarium species are harmless and relatively abundant members of the soil microbial
community, where they act out their role as saprobes, by processing dead organic matter [18]. Nor-
mal growth happens in haploid mycellium but under harsh environmental conditions it can switch to
sporulation. The different life stages of Fusarium are divided into growth in form of mycellium, spore
production and a dormant stage as spores see ﬁgure 4 [18].
Figure 4: Generalized life cycle of Fusarium [18]
Multiple species of Fusarium spp. are however plant pathogens. Fusarium spp. can cause direct
losses resulting from seedling foot and stalk rots in many different crops, however the biggest losses
are due to severe reduction in yield and grain quality from head blight(HB) of cereals such as wheat
and barley [19]. 17 Fusarium species that either infect cereals individually or in groups are associ-
ated with Fusarium head blight (FHB). Three of the most important species globally and locally are
Fusarium graminearum, Fusarium culmorum and Fusarium oxysporum.
FHB is a soil born disease, that reaches ﬂowering cereals by either raindrop splashes, that have been
shown to be able to carry spores up to 1 m horizontal and 60 cm vertical, or by discharging airborne
ascospores, see ﬁgure 5 . Fields are commonly infected through leftover crop residue from previous
infected crops, where spores can lay dormant for up to 4 years, or infected seeds. FHB is initiated by
airborne spores landing on ﬂowering spikelets of cereals, multiplying, and entering the plant through
natural openings. Following infection, the fungus expresses genes for mycotoxin biosynthesis. The
primarily mycotoxin groups of concern are trichothecenes, zearalenones and fumonisins of which
deoxynivalenol (DON) is one of the most common [20]. DON is a virulence factor in cereals, causing
tissue degradation, thereby allowing the fungus to spread within the cernals. Mycotoxins such as
DON are toxic to both humans and animal and strict regulations for maximum levels of mycotoxin

4.4
Suppressive soil and plant growth promotion
The rhizosphere can be seen as the plants ﬁrst line of defence against soil-born pathogens [8]. This is
most evident from disease suppressing soils, which are deﬁned by having no disease symptoms even
though plant pathogens are present in the soil [16]. Soils ability to suppress diseases can be general,
where the total microbial community is responsible for the suppression and is not transferable between
soils, or it can be speciﬁc, where a single or a selected group of microorganisms are responsible for
the suppression and is transferable [22]. That a single or a few strains could be transferred to new soil
to obtain plant disease suppression in soil have lead to the concept of biocontrol strains. Biocontrol
microorganisms are described as indirect plant growth promotion (PGP) because of their ability to
promote plant growth through prevention of plant diseases. Direct PGP microorganisms are called
biofertilizers because of their ability to make nutrients available for plants and induce growth through
production of plant hormones [12]. Bacteria belonging to the genera Bacillus and Pseudomonas are
some of the proposed bacteria responsible for PGP [23].

4.5
Plant growth promoting Flourescent Pseudomonas
Pseudomonas are aerobic, gram-negative bacteria ubiquis in agricultural soils [24]. Fluorescent Pseu-
domonas spp. make up a diverse group of bacteria that can be distinguished from other pseudomonas
by their ability to produce pyoverdine, a ﬂuorescent yellow-green pigment [25]. Flourescent Pseu-
domonas are considered good candidates for PGP bacteria because of their ability to i) be massed
produced; ii) rapidly utilize root exudates; (iii) colonize roots; (iv) compete aggressively with other
rhizosphere microorganisms; and (v) adapt to environmental stresses [24]. A drawback with the use
of Pseudomonas as oppose to other bacteria such as Bacillus, is its inability to produce spores, which
would allow easy manufacturing and storage [24].
Flourescent Pseudomonas have speciﬁcally been of interest due too their direct PGP by eg. nitrogen
ﬁxation and phosphate solubilisation; and indirect PGP by eg. inhibiting phytopathogenic or promot-
ing the plants own defence mechanisms [16], see ﬁgure 6. Multiple PGP ﬂuorescent Pseudomonas
have been isolated and some are commercially available for example as one of the main active ingre-
dients in Bio-Save and BlightBan [26]. Through extensive research over the last 50 years, many traits
for PGP have been identiﬁed and characterized. The following section will go into each of these traits.

INTRODUCTION
4.5.1
Direct plant growth promotion
Direct growth promotion can be through solubilization of inorganic phosphate, nitrogen ﬁxation or
the synthesis of phytohormones. Multiple of these traits have been found in ﬂuorescent Pseudomonas.
To do a genetic analysis of the potential in strains, the genotype of such traits will be described in the
following sections.
Phosphate solubilizatio
Phosphorus (P) is an essential macronutrient needed for plant growth and development. Most agricul-
tural soils contain large reserves of Phosphorus, a considerable part has accumulated as a consequence
of regular applications of P fertilizers. Large amounts of soluble inorganic phosphate are however still
applied to soil as chemical fertilizer because it is rapidly immobilized (ﬁxed) to insoluble forms upon
its application in the soil due to its reaction with aluminum and iron. Thereby soon after application
becoming unavailable to plants [27]. Improving soil fertility by releasing bound Phosphorus through
microbial phosphate solublization is therefore an important method for increasing crop yield.
Several bacteria have been reported to be Phosphate solubilisers from the genera Pseudomonas, Bacil-
lus and Rhizobium, these are among the most powerful phosphate solubilizers [27]. The solubliza-
tion of organic phosphates by Pseudomonas is mediated through the production of enzymes such as
phytase and phosphonacetate hydrolase. These enzymes are in principal used for the production of
organic acids and acid phosphatases, which solubilize inorganic phosphates. Gluconic acid (GA) is
the most frequent agent of inorganic phosphate solubilization [28]. GA solubilises phosphate by de-
creasing the pH of the soil.
The biosynthesis of GA is activated by glucose dehydrogenase (GCD) and its co-factor Pyrroloquino-
line quinone (PQQ). The enzyme GCD is codede for by a single gene (gcd), while the production of
the PQQ molecule is encoded in the pqq operon which consists of six core genes pqqA,B,C,D,E, and
F, of which PqqA, PqqC, PqqD, and PqqE are essential [28]. Different composistions of the cluster is
seen across different ﬂuorescent pseudomonas species, see ﬁgure 7.
Figure 7: Comparison the pqq gene clusters found in P. ﬂuorescens B16 and P. ﬂuorescens Pf0-1
[29]

get ﬁxed nitrogen from atmospheric nitrogen ﬁxating microorganisms. Microbiological nitrogen ﬁx-
ation, is the ATP-dependent reduction of dinitrogen (N2) to Ammonia (NH3) by the nitrogenase

Phytohormone production
Other than improving nutrient uptake, rhizosphere bacteria can also directly impact plant growth
through secretion of plant hormones. The plant hormone, auxin is a fundamental compound that
modulates plant growth. The most important and most common auxine is indole-3-acetic acid (IAA),
produced by plants and microorganisms including bacteria and fungi [37]. IAA regulates ethylene
levels in plants. Ethylene signaling is the plants system for balancing resources between growth and
stress resistance. When stressed, for example by nutrient limitations or plant pathogenes, plants up-
regulate ethylene levels, which triggers a range of physiological adaptations. Microorganisms can
impact plant ethylene signaling via secreting IAA, which increase ethylene levels in plants, or by
producing the enzyme 1-aminocyclopropane-1 carboxylic acid (ACC) deaminase, which cleaves the
direct precursor of ethylene, thereby decreasing ethylene levels [38].
IAA is an important PGP trait because of its ability to trigger lateral root formation, root elongation
and the development of root hair. This anchor plants in soil and increases the surface area through
which water, minerals and nutrients can be taken up [39]. It is important to note that production of
IAA has also been linked to plant pathogenic bacteria. One of the presumed modes of pathogenesity
is the induction of different enzymes such as, endo-β-1,4- glucanases (EGases), xyloglucan endo-
transglycosylases (XETs) by IAA, which cause cell wall loosening by depolymerization of plant wall
polysaccharides [40].
IAA can be produced from tryptophan via four interlinking tryptophane-dependent pathways, in-
cluding the indole-3-acetamide (IAM), indole-3-pyruvic acid (IPA), tryptamine (TAM) and indole-3-
acetaldoxime (IAOX) pathways, or through a tryphtophane-independent pathway. The genes and
enzymes for the tryptophan-independent pathway has not yet been identiﬁed [41].

Hydrogen cyanide (HCN) is produced by certain ﬂuorescent Pseudomonas. HCN partially ionizes in

Lytic enzyme production
Multiple ﬂuorescent Pseudomonas also produce extracellular enzymes such as exo-1,3-0-D-glucosidase
(B-1,3-glucanase) and B-N- acethyl-D-glucosaminidase (chitinase) [64]. The Fungal cell walls are

enzymes that break down glycosidic bonds in chitin and protease break down peptide bonds in pro-
teins. Enzymatic lysis of fungal cell walls through extracellular enzymes has been implicated as the
main mechanism of biocontrol by bacterial agents Pseudomonas stutzeri against Fusarium solani, an
agent of plant root rot [65] and has been linked to disease suppressiveness in soil [66]. Chitinase and
B-1,3-glucanase are mainly biosynthesised by ChiC [64] and gluA [67] , respectively.

Phenazines are versitile secondary metabolites that are mainly produced by Pseudomonas and Strep-

have the production of phenazine-1-carboxylic acid (PCA), phenazine-1-carboxamide (PCN), hy-

been shown to be directly involved in the reduction of several diseases caused by fungi, oomycetes
and bacteria [68].

Cyclic lipopeptides:
Cyclic lipopeptides (CLPs) constitute a structurally diverse group of metabolites produced by various
bacteria and fungi, including Streptomyces, Pseudomonas and Bacillus. Their antimicrobial, cyto-
toxic, anti-tumor, immunosuppressant and surfactant properties make them interesting in many ﬁelds
including plant growth promotion. [70]
CLPs share a basic structure; they consist of a hydrophobic and a hydrophilic moiety, a lipid tail
linked to a short cyclic oligopeptide. The cyclic olligopeptide is a lactone ring formed between two
amino acids in the peptide chain. They have high structural diversity due to differences in the length
and composition of the lipid tail and to variations in the number, type, and conﬁguration of the amino
acids in the cyclic olligopeptide [71]. Based on structural characteristics, the cyclic LPs produced
by Pseudomonas are classiﬁed into fourteen major groups, with multiple structurally homologous
members. The deﬁning structure is the number of amino acids in the cyclic oligopeptide. From
shortest to longest the 14 groups are: Bananamide, Pseudofactin, Syringomycin, Viscosin, Orfamide,
Amphisin, Putisolvin, Entolysin, Xantholysin, Tolaasin, Fuscopeptin, Corpeptin, Syringopeptin 22A
and Syringopeptin 25A. A few are depicted in ﬁgure 16.

The three main natural functions of LPs described to date are their role in antagonism towards other
microorganisms, motility and attachment to surfaces [70]. CLPs primary mode of antagonism is pore
formation in membranes, leading to an imbalance in transmembrane ion ﬂuxes and cell death. In vitro
lytic and growth-inhibitory activities against a broad range of microorganisms, including viruses, my-
coplasmas, bacteria, fungi and oomycetes have been found. Even though cyclic LPs produced by
Pseudomonas species have mostly been studied for activity against human pathogens, multiple stud-
ies have found similar antagonism against plant pathogens. For example, the cyclic LP fengycins
has been shown to have activity against the plant pathogenic Fusarium graminearum [78]. Another
example is the dual-functioning compund amphisin, which with its biosurfactant and antifungal prop-
erties have been shown to exhibits antagonism towards the root-pathogenic fungi Pythium ultimum
and Rhizoctonia solani, who among other cause damping-off disease in sugar beet seedlings [79]. In
Pseudomonas, CLPs also play an important role in in surface attachment and bioﬁlm formation on the
roots. The role is however dependent on the CLP. A Massetolide deﬁcient Pseudomonas ﬂuorescent
have for example been shown to have reduced bioﬁlm formation [80] while a putisolvin deﬁcient
Pseudomonas putida have an increased bioﬁlm formation [81].

Most LPs are biosynthesized nonribosomally on large multienzyme complexes called peptide syn-
thetases (nonribosomal peptide synthetases, NRPSs). NRPSs consist of modules and in most cases
each module corospond to an amino acid in the peptidechain. The modules consist of an adenylation
(A) domain responsible for selection of the amino acid, and a thiolation (T) domain responsible for
adding the amino acid to the growing peptide chain, through thioesteriﬁcation. The active site of the
A domains, determines the selected aminoacids. After multiple domains collectively have generated
a lipopeptide, it is cleaved at the end of the assembly line by a thioesterase (TE). In most cases, the
TE domains also catalyze the cyclization of the mature peptide product, resulting in the release of a
cyclic LP (CLP) [70]. An illustration of how the models step wise together make a peptide chain is

Regulatory systems
In Pseudomonas, the GacA/GacS two-component regulatory system controls the production of sec-

studied, little is known about the signals that trigger LP biosynthesis. Studies by Koch et al. (2002)

5.1
Introduction
The ﬁrst part of this project consist of a whole-genome analysis of four ﬂuorescent Pseudomonas
isolated from sugar beet rhizosheres, to determine their potential as plant growth promoting (PGP)
strains.
The genomes were analyzed using the secondary metabolite predictor antiSMASH and
through BLAST of a manually made gene database. The genes chosen to be most important for
PGP was chosen to be for phosphate solubilization (gcd and pqqFABCDE), nitrogen ﬁxation (nifQB-
HUSW), auxin production (acdS, iaaMH, nit, nthAB, phe, ami, aldA, ippdC), hydrogen cyanide
production (hcnABC), phenazine production (phzEDFBG), 2,4-diacetylphloroglucinol (phlACBDE),
pyoluteorin (pltABC), pyrrolnitrin (prnABCD) and lytic enzyme production (chiC and gluA). These
were chosen due to their importance in PGP as indicated by their prevalence in current literature [85]
[86] [87] [88] [24].

5.2
Methods
5.2.1
Strains
Fluorescent Pseudomonas was previously isolated from sugar beet rhizosphere. Sugar beets were
harvested from Skælskør (5514’50.0”N, 1119’06.3”E) where after ﬂuorescent Pseudomonas was
screened for on Kings B plates and veriﬁed by 16S rRNA sequencing. Four strains was chosen
for whole genome sequencing based on their antagonism against three agricultural relevant plant
pathogens (Pectobacterium carotovorm, Dickeya solani and Xanthomonas campestris). Antagonism
was tested with a chloroform-based inhibition assay, modiﬁed from Li et al. [73].
5.2.2
Phylogenetic analysis
To identify sub-species level phylogeny, phylogenetic trees based on the rpoD gene sequence were
constructed using the Maximum Likelihood method in CLC workbench with standard settings (Boot-
strap values of 100 replications). The associated Pseudomonas sub-group, based on the classiﬁcation
by Hesse et al. 2018, was identiﬁed using the Basic Local Alignment Search Tool (BLAST) V2.10.1
[89].
5.2.3
Genome Analysis of Secondary Metabolite Clusters
Secondary metabolite clusters was predicted using the antiSMASH 5.0 Pipeline (Antibiotics and Sec-
ondary Metabolite Analysis Shell) with default settings. To ensure that clusters was complete and
functioning, a more detailed annotation was created by using NCBI BLAST or The Minimum Infor-
mation about a Biosynthetic Gene cluster (MIBiG) database.
5.2.4
Genome Analysis of plant growth promoting traits
To analyse the genomes for plant growth promoting traits which antiSMASH does not look for, a
database with a selection of genes shown to be associated with plant growth-promotion was created,
see table 1. The PGP traits of interest was for direct PGP though phospahte solubilization, nitrogen
ﬁxation and auxine production, and indirect PGP through hydrogen cyanide and lytic enzyme pro-
duction. Reference genes for the individual traits were chosen from Pseudomonas spp. based on
puplished work showing that deletions of the genes prevented the strains to perform the speciﬁc func-
tion. tBLASTn with a cutoff E-value of 1E-20 was used to identify the presence of the selected plant
growth-promoting, before manually veriﬁcation of cluster structures.
5.2.5
Analysis for phytopathogenic traits
To analyse the strains for potential plant pathogenic traits, VFanalyser was used to search for speciﬁ-
cally the type 3 secretion system (T3SS) and multiple plant toxins.

acterized by having a lower afﬁnity for iron than pyoverdine [94]. The achromobactin biosynthetic
gene cluster has been well characterized in Erwinia chrysanthemi [95], Sodalis glossinidius [95] and
Pseudomonas syringae [93]; and homologous clusters have been reported in Pseudomonas chloro-
raphis subsp. aurantiaca ARS-38 [96] and Pseudomonas chlororaphis Lzh-T5 [97], but to the best
of our knowledge it has not been reported in P. frederiksbergensis or any other Pseudomonas in the
sup-group P. mandelii, before now.
Two cyclic lipopeptides
In Pseudomonas sp. 6.1 and Pseudomonas sp. 6.3, antiSMASH identiﬁed NRPS biosynthetic gene
clusters involved in the production of cyclic lipopeptides (CLPs). Both clusters are composed of three
NRPS-encoding ORFs and ﬂanked by regulatory and transport-related genes. See ﬁgure 40a and 41a
in appendix. The best hit according to antiSMASH was a banamide synthase with 100% of genes
showing similarity, and a lokisin synthase with 70% of genes showed similarity, respectively.
Cyclic lipopeptides is a large group of secondary metabolites that are chemically diverse, but obey
a common structural blue-print. From these structures 14 distinct groups are deﬁned by the length
of their amino acid chain (8-25 aa). What tells the different cyclic lipopeptides apart can be a single
amino acid. This makes it hard for antiSMASH to tell the different clusters apart, which is apparent
from the antiSMASH KnownClusterBlast results (see ﬁgure 40b and 41B in appendix), where multi-
ple gene clusters had high similarity.

Quorum sensing
A quorum sensing cluster with a homoserine lactone (AHL) and a regulator was identiﬁed by anti-
SMASH in Pseudomonas sp. 6.1, region 1, see ﬁgure 23.
Figure 23: AntiSMASH result for a homoserine lactone biosynthesis gene identiﬁed in Pseu-
domonas sp. 6.1.
Through further manual investigation of the surrounding genes, it was shown that Pseudomonas sp.
6.1 contains both a LasI and a LasR gene. These two genes make up the LasI/R quorum sensing
system, where LasI directs the synthesis of N-(3-oxo- dodecanoyl)-homoserine lactone (3-oxo-C12-
HSL), which binds and activates the cognate response regulator LasR, resulting in the regulation of
virulence genes and bioﬁlm formation [98]. The LasI/R is a common quorum sensing system in Pseu-
domonas along with the RhlI/R system, although the latter was not found i Pseudomonas sp. 6.1.
The putisolvin cluster in Pseudomoas sp. 6.1 was also related to bioﬁlm production, therefore it was
interesting to investigate whether this LasI/R cluster had been shown to regulate the expression of
putisolvin. This relationship have been tested by Dubern et. al in 2016 [99] in Pseudomonas putida
PCL1445. In the study strains with mutations in LasI/R homologs, ppuI and ppuR, showed a severe
reduction of putisolvin production and an expression analysis of the putisolvin biosynthetic gene in a
ppuI background showed decreased expression, which could be complemented by the addition of syn-
thetic 3-oxo-C10 -N-acyl homoserine lactone (3-oxo-C10 -AHL) or 3-oxo-C12 -AHL to the medium.
With this they had shown that the ppuI and ppuR are putisolvin regulators. An additional gene was
found in the ppuI and ppuR, namley rsaL, which was found to be a repressor of ppuI by seeing that
an rsaL mutant overproduced AHLs.
A BLAST of this ppuI-rsaL-ppuR quorum-sensing system against the Pseudomonas sp. 6.1, con-
ﬁrmed that the three genes are homologs, see table 6. It is therefore hypothesised that the ppuI-rsaL-
ppuR quorum-sensing system in Pseudomonas putida strain 6.1, regulates putisolvin synthesis, in a
similar fasion as in Pseudomonas putida PCL1445. This further validating the presence of putisolvin
in P. sp. 6.1.

5.4.1
Evaluation of potential as biofertilizers
In all four genomes the genes for phosphate solubilization and auxin production was found while no
nitrogen ﬁxation genes were detected.
The lack of nitrogen ﬁxation in the four strains correlate with what others have found. A metagenomic
analysis of sugarbeet rhizosphere by Tsurumaru et al.[87] detected no nitrogen ﬁxation related genes
in the entire rhizobiom. Screening for any species of nitrogen ﬁxating biofertilizers in sugar beet
rhizospheres is therefore not optimal. The need for such biofertilizers are however still as important
as ever and multiple studies have shown that addition of nitrogen ﬁxating bacteria to sugar beet ﬁelds
increase the yields [105, 106].

That all four strains had the genes for phosphate solubilization and auxin production also correlates
with what others have found. A metagenomic analysis of sugarbeet rhizosphere by Tsurumaru et
al.[87] found a high concentration phosphate solubilization and IAA production related genes. This
correlates with a study by Claassen et al. 1990 [107] which found in vivo that sugar beets are able to
adapt to an insufﬁcient supply of phosphate. At the early growth stages the lack of added phosphate
decreased plant growth, however at later growth stages little difference in growth rates were seen be-
tween deﬁcient and well-supplied plants. This adaption was due to an improved nutrient uptake, by
increased root hair growth and phosphate solubilizing microorganisms.
Of the four strains P. sp. 6.10 have the most direct plant growth promoting traits, as it has three
pathways for auxine production (the IAM, IAN and TSO pathway) as well as the presence of two pqq
clusters for phosphate solubilization, both with pqqB and pqqE.
5.4.2
Potential as biocontrol strains
The number of known antimicrobial traits found in the four strains are not many. They all have
pyoverdine, additionally Pseudomonas sp. 6.1 has the CLP putisolvin, Pseudomonas sp. 6.3 has
HCN, chitinase and either the CLP amphisin or anikasin, Pseudomonas sp. 6.7 has non and 6.10
has an additional siderophore, achromobactin. When compared to known biocontrol strains such
as Pseudomonas protegens Strain Pf-5 that produces the seven antimicrobial metabolites 2,4- di-
acetylphoroglucinol, pyrrolnitrin, pyoluteorin, hydrogen cyanide, rhizoxin, orfamide A, and toxoﬂavi
[108], then it is hard to see how these strains could be used for biocontrol.
Biocontrol strains with few secondary metabolites do however exist. One of such strains is Pseu-
domonas capeferrum WCS358 (previously annotated as Pseudomonas putida WCS358), which in the
last 30 years has been used as a model strain for PGPR-mediated induced systemic resistance (ISR)
and biological control of soil-borne pathogens via siderophore-mediated competition for iron [109].
Just like Pseudomoans capeferrum sp. 6.1, Pseudomonas capeferrum WCS358 only produce pyover-
dine and putisolvin I/II. Its role as PGP has partly been linked to its aggressive sequestration of iron
and its ability to eliciting ISR multiple plants (Arabidopsis, Eucalypt, Grape vine, Tobacco, Bean and
Tomato) [109]. Its capacity to control plant pathogens through siderophore-mediated competition for
iron has been correlated with its unusual strategy for acquiring iron. P. WCS358 can only produce one
siderophore, pyoverdine PVD358. However, very few Pseudomonas strains seem to be able to use
this pyoverdine. Berendtensen et al. [109] has found that only 1 % of over 1000 randomly isolated
Pseudomonas strains could utilize PVD358. This is signiﬁcantly different from the pyoverdine from
another strain they tested, P. WCS374, where 30 % of the strains were able to use the PVD374. In ad-
dition, WCS358 was able to utilize the pyoverdines produced by a wide range of other Pseudomonas
strains.
The only other secondary metabolite produced by P. sp. 6.1 and P. WCS358 is Putisolvin I and II
which have shown to inhibit bioﬁlm formation of different Pseudomonas species and break down ex-
isting Pseudomonas bioﬁlms [81]. Such traits are important for many ﬁelds of research as well as for
colonization of the rhizosphere and the roots of plants, due to the ability to compete for surface area.
Putisolvins produced by P. putida strain PCL1445, have shown no antibacterial effects against mul-
tiple Gram-negative bacteria [70]. The inhibition of the bacterial plant pathogens P. cartovorum and
D. solani, are therefore presumed to be caused by iron limitation caused by the produced Pyoverdine.

Pseudomonas sp. 6.3 can apart from pyoverdine produce HCN, chitinase and either the antifungal
CLP amphisin [79] or the amoebicidal anikasin [82]. The most likely CLP being anikasin. The
antibacterial activity of anikasin have been tested against Bacillus subtilis, Staphylococcus aureus,
Escherichia coli, Pseudomonas aeruginosa, Enterococcus faecium and Mycobacterium vaccae [82].
Of which only E. faecium was inhibited. Antifungal activity has been tested against Sporoidiobolus
salmonicolor, Candida albicans and Penicillium notatum [82]. No antifungal activity was seen. P. sp.
6.3’s inhibition of X. campestris and D. solani are therefor not explained by the prodution of anikasin
but rather the antimicrobial activity of HCN. The antibacterial activity of HCN, the antifungal activity
of chitinase and the amoebicidal activity of anikasin, suggest that P. sp. 6.3 could be used as a broad
spectrum biocontrole strain. This a weak claim and would need further research to determine.
No antimicrobial metabolites was identiﬁed in P. sp. 6.7 and P. sp. 6.10. Their inhibition of D. solani
and X. campestris are therefore hard to explain. The inhibition could be due to iron sequesting by
pyoverdine production. The inhibition results was described as Weak or hazy zone for 6.7 and clear
zone for 6.10 (Nina Feldthaus, personal communication). If the inhibition is caused by iron sequester-
ing then that would explain why 6.10 show a stronger inhibition zone, due to its additional sideophore
achromobactin. Correlating inhibition with siderophore production should however be followed up
with siderophore deﬁcient mutants, since not all ﬂourescent pseudomonas have antimicrobial activity
but all produce siderophore.
Another explanation could be that there is some antimicrobial metabolite which was neither found
with antiSMASH or the manually made database. Some of the excluded antiSMASH results could
perhaps be important. The excluded results contained pigments and bacteriocins. Bacteriocins are
antimicrobial compound that targets closely related species. These are not expected to effect any of
the plant pathogens, but could have played a role anyway.
Only well known PGP traits was manually searched for. A wider target of genes with a broader PGP
effect could have been added to the database.
5.4.3
Limitation of bioinformatic analysis
Bioinformatics is a fast and efﬁcient way to screen for plant growth promoting traits, but it has its
limitations. Firstly is the difference between Geno- and phenotype. Identifying the presence of a
secundary metabolite cluster, does not indicate that the strain is a good producer of that metabolite.
Further in vitro testing would be needed. In the four sugar beet isolated strains, the most important
traits to be tested in vitro: for P. sp. 6.1 is CLP; for P. sp. 6.3 is CLP and chitinase; for P. sp. 6.10
is IAA and phospahte production. Methods for in vitro testing of CLP production, phospahte solubi-
lization, auxin production and hydrogen cyanide production are proposed in captor 6.2.2
Secondly the four strains potential as PGP strains has only been evaluated based on genetic markers
for direct and indirect PGP, not root colonization or ability to stabilize if introduced to a new rhizo-
sphere. This would need further testing. A system for testing such traits is proposed in chapter 6.

6.2.4
The plant-root system
Sterilisation and germination of seeds
To ensure no bacteria or fungi is transferred to the sterile system, the seeds are surface sterilised be-
fore use.
Transfer no more than than ten intact seeds showing no disease symptoms to a 25 ml falcon tube.
Add 70 percent ethanol up to 10 ml, and shake for 30s. Remove ethanol and rinse seeds twice with
sterile water. Gently shake seeds in sterile water for 30 min. Remove water and add 1.5 percent
sodium hypochlorite, supplemented with 0.1 percent Tween80 up to 10 ml and shake gently for 0.5h.
Remove the liquid and rinse seeds twice in sterile water. Add sterile water up too 10 ml and shake
gently for 0.5h. Repeat twice.
Germinate sterile seeds on LB plates at room temperature for 48h. Only seedlings with 0.5-1 cm root,
showing no growth of bacteria or fungi should be chosen for further experiments.
Measuring plant growth
Plant growth is determined by the length and weight of the shoot and root of the seedlings.
Transfer surface sterilized and germinated seed to a falcon tube containing 45 ml 1/2 Murashige &
Skoog Medium (MS) (X % agar). Grow seed for 7 days in a growth chamber with a 12h light/ 12h
dark light cycle (100 µmol/m²/s, 25% blue, 35 % green and 40 % red light) at room temperature (avr.
18 night/ 21 day). After seven days, cut sprouts as close as possible to the seed with a sterilised
scissor. Measure length with a ruler. Pat dry the spouts with a paper towel before weighing.
To measure root growth, release ﬁrst the MS media into a sterilized beaker. Release roots from the
MS media with sterilized tweezers. Measure the primary root with a sterilised ruler. Before weighing
the roots, pat dry and remove any left over MS medium.

pathogenic Fusarium species and a native community in the rhizosphere of Barley and to test selected
PGP strains in the system.

The plant root system
A lab-scale plant root system was developed in a previous project [120].

6.3.4
Single inoculation
To understand the effect when all four parties (plant, biocontrol, Fusarium and community) are mixed,
the individual strains ability to colonize the roots and their effect on the plant must ﬁrst be measured.
Then the individuals effect on each other must be understood, before mixing them all together. In the
following section, methods for inoculation and measuring root colonization of the individual strains
are proposed and potential results are hypothesised based on literature.
Inoculation with Fusarium strains
To see effect of the Fusarium strains on the plant alone and compare ability to colonize roots, the
Fusarium strains should be inoculated to the system individually.
The most intuitive approach would, as described in section 6.2.5, be to harvest Fusarium spores, di-
lute the spore suspension to approx. 10 8 spore/ml. The system could then be inoculated by coating
surface sterilized germinated seedlings. This would allow inoculation of the system with a speciﬁc
concentration of Fusarium.
In a study by Henkes et. al. [118] Fusarium graminearum spores was added to a barley-root system
ﬁlled with sterile hydroponic solution containing 50% Hoagland (a soil nutrient solution similar to
the MS media), highly visible browning of the entire barley root system was seen after 7 days, see
ﬁgure 30 a. F. graminearum infection led to a lower plant weight of the shoot and the root system
1 week after infection, suggesting that the pathogen overcame the plants defences and successfully
established a parasitic infection. Infection of the system caused root weight loss of 33 % compared
to non-infected roots. An alternative method could be to spread the spores on water agar plates and
let the barley grow vertically instead, as done by Roberto et al. [117] see ﬁgure 30 b and c. At day 7
the disease level was ranked both by counting seedling death and on a disease severity rank system,
as explained in section 6.2.7.
The results from Henkes et. al. suggest that inoculation of Fusarium through coating in a spore
solution is possible. By inoculation of a speciﬁc concentration of Fusarium it would be easy to
replicate and control. This would also allow initial concentrations to be comparable to bacterial
inoculations. The method by Roberto et al. would require that the set up of using falcon tubes for
growth would have to be changed. The method by Henkes et al. is therefore preferred. Using the
ranking system by Roberto et al. would allow easy quantiﬁcation of disease levels. This method is
therefore proposed.

The artiﬁcial soil community was designed for maize rhizosphere not barley rhizosphere, and it is

The simpliﬁed synthetic community was constructed from the rhizosphere of maize seedlings grown

the individual strains also can colonize roots of barley grown in MS medium. Roberto et al [117]

cloacae and at day 10 favoring Ochrobactrum pituitosum, see ﬁgure 31. It is therefore expected that
composition changes would also happen when inoculated into barley root system.

6.3.5
Preliminary testing on plates
To determine the effect of the individual strains, on each other, before co-inoculating them in to the
system, two plate assays could be conducted. This would give valuable information for understanding
dynamics and validate results in the plant system.
Dual culture assay for biocontrol stains vs. Fusarium strains
A simple method to test antagonism of the Pseudomonas strains against the Fusarium strains would
be with the use of a dual culture assay. Such assays can be done in many different ways but a simple
and commonly used way would, as described in section 6.2.3, be to make a line of Pseudomonas O/N
culture in one side of a LB plate and spot Fusarium on the other side, at a determined distance and
then compare the radius of the Fusarium to a control plate without Pseudomonas, see ﬁgure 27.
Dual culture assay for community vs. Fusarium strains
To validate that suppression of the Fusarium spp. caused by the PGP strains, it is important to know
if the community itself can suppress Fusarium spp.
The same method as for the biocontrol strains can be used, to make it easily comparable. Spotting a
suspension with equal concentration of all community strains could show the combined effect of the
community against Fusarium.
Cross assay for biocontrol strains vs. community
It is important to measure the effect of the PGP strains on the community to further evaluate the PGP
strains ability to stay stable in the rhizosphere. Roberto et al. found that removal of E. cloacae lead
to signiﬁcant changes in community composition. It is therefor especially important to know whether
the PGP strain inhibit this bacteria.
Multiple assays exist for testing antagonism, symbiosis and neutralism between bacteria. A common
method is plating a lawn of one strain and then spotting the strain of interest on it to measure clear-
ing zones. This method is however not ideal for testing 7x7 strains. A more ideal method in this
case would be a simple cross assay, described in section 6.2.3, where the different strains in pairs are
crossed with each other and then compared to streaks of the strains alone, see ﬁgure 28. In this way
an increased growth of one or both of the strain suggest symbioses, a decreased growth of one of the
strains indicate antagonism and no different indicate neutralism.

vironmental factors such as available carbon, type of carbon sources, nutrient limitation, cognizable
surfaces, pH levels and oxygen levels. By co-inoculation of the strains into the plant root system the
interaction when in close contact with roots could be determined.

al. found infection of Fusarium reduced the root biomass but that pre-inoculation of the system with
DAPG producing Pseudomonas CHA0, before inoculation with Fusarium, reduced the root weight
loss without preventing colonization of F. graminearum. An inoculation with a DAPG deﬁcient CHA0
did not show loss of reduced root weight. As explained earlier DAPG have been shown to induce sys-
temic resistance in plants. Henkes et al. found the the root weight loss was caused by a diverted
carbon delivery from the infected roots to the healthy roots, maintaining the same total photosyn-
thetically supplied carbon. Plants do this to slow down the infection until the plants own defence
mechanisms are expressed, while healthy roots are being supplied with more energy to prevent fur-
ther infection. Inoculation of of the DAPG producing strain stopped the diversion of carbon, causing
no root weight loss, even though Fusarium maintained infection of the roots.

FUTURE PERSPECTIVES
7
Conclusion
Using antiSMASH and a manually made database, the strains was examined for PGP traits. Genes for
puoverdine, phospate solobilization and auxine production were found in all four strains and no genes
for nitrogen ﬁxation T3SS and phytotoxins were found. An additional siderophore, achromobactine
was found in P. sp. 6.10, which interestingly had not previously been described in the P. mandellii
group. Additionally, genes for CLPs belonging to the amphisin and putisolvin group was identiﬁed
in P. sp. 6.1 and P. sp. 6.3, respectively. A ppuI-rsaL-ppuR quorum-sensing system was found in P.
sp. 6.1, and is hypothesised to regulate putisolvin synthesis.
Of the four strains, Pseudomonas sp. 6.1, which resembles the model strain Pseudomons WCS35, is
proposed as a potential PGP strain due to its strong iron sequestering and induced systemic resistance
in plants. Additionally P. sp. 6.3 is proposed to be a broad spectrum biocontrol strain due to the genes
found for HCN (antibacterial), chitinase (antifungal) and anikasin (amoebicidal) production.
The bioinformatic results can not fully explain the empirical found antagonism of the four strains nor
be used to predict if the strains can successfully colonize the rhizosphere, if reintroduced into foreign
soil. Therefore a protocol to further test the strains in vivo has been outlined in section 6, but due to
Covid-19 the test was sadly not performed.
8
Future perspectives
The increasing demand for sustainable alternatives to conventional pesticides and fertilizers, can be
met with biocontrol and biofertilizers. Bioinformatic tools allow for high through-put screening of
potential PGP bacteria and an increasing number of bacteria have been found to have the traits needed
for biocontrol and biofertilization. Many of them have shown promising results in small-scale trials.
Results from large ﬁeld experiments are however often less promising. Artiﬁcially introduced PGP
rhizobacteria have been found to initially colonize roots at 10 7 - 10 8 CFU g–1, but these levels de-
cline within few weeks [16]. A major factor contributing to these inconsistencies are the challenges
of the PGP strains to stabilize in a foreign rhizosphere. The phenotypes and genotypes needed to
adapt and respond to alterations in the physical, chemical and biological properties of the rhizosphere
are not jet fully understood. The lab-scale root-soil system allow further understanding of the com-
plex phenotypes needed for successful root colonization. The system has however not been validated.
Validating the proposed system is therefore the main future prospect because it supplies valuable in-
formation for how well the proposed PGP strains would do in future trials. The proposed lab-scale
root-soil system is supposed to represent a simpliﬁed rhizosphere with only a few variables, to ensure
reproducible results. The system will however never replace the need for greenhouse and ﬁeld trial
and is not meant to. It can only be used as an additional step after in vitro tests and before greenhouse
trials. To obtain a ﬁnal commercial product, greenhouse trials, ﬁeld test, mass production and distri-
bution, are future steps to be taken.
