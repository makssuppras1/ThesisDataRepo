Abstract
Thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs) are promising carriers for the localised delivery of
drugs in response to externally applied heat. This thesis investigates the use of TSLs
to encapsulate and thermally release clenbuterol, a β2­adrenergic agonist relevant to
the treatment of neuromuscular disorders. Liposomes composed of DPPC and DSPE­
mPEG2000 were prepared and actively loaded with clenbuterol using an ammonium sul­
fate gradient. The resulting formulations were evaluated in terms of particle size, encap­
sulation efficiency, and temperature­triggered drug release.
Dynamic light scattering measurements indicated that the liposomes were monodisperse
and within the desired size range. Encapsulation efficiency studies showed efficient en­
capsulation for most formulations. Thermal release studies showed a marked increase in
drug release at 42°C compared to 37°C, consistent with the phase transition temperature
of DPPC­based bilayers. However, quantification by HPLC during final analyses revealed
significant variability in calculated drug concentrations across replicate injections. This
inconsistency was traced to instrumental factors, likely involving the autosampler, and
limited the reliability of encapsulation and release measurements.
Despite this analytical limitation, the study demonstrates the feasibility of using TSLs for
the controlled release of clenbuterol. The results support further exploration of TSL­based
systems for localised therapy, while highlighting the need for improved analytical consis­
tency and protocol optimisation.

1
Introduction
The neuromuscular junction (NMJ) is a specialised synapse that mediates communication
between motor neurons and skeletal muscle fibres. Disruption of this junction plays a cen­
tral role in a variety of neuromuscular disorders, including myasthenia gravis, amyotrophic
lateral sclerosis, and certain congenital myopathies. Pharmacological modulation of NMJ
signalling has shown therapeutic benefit in several cases, but systemic administration
of such agents often produces off­target effects. This challenge underscores the need
for drug delivery systems that enable precise, localised administration to neuromuscular
interfaces.
Clenbuterol is a selective β2­adrenergic agonist with potential for modulating muscle func­
tion, promoting regeneration, and supporting neuromuscular repair. While traditionally
used as a bronchodilator, its anabolic and neuroprotective properties have attracted in­
terest in the context of muscle­wasting and neuromuscular conditions. However, clen­
buterol’s systemic use is limited by dose­dependent side effects, particularly on the car­
diovascular system. This makes clenbuterol an ideal candidate for localised delivery sys­
tems that can enhance the therapeutic index by targeting the site of action while minimis­
ing systemic exposure.
Targeted drug delivery systems offer a means of concentrating pharmacologically active
compounds at specific tissues or cell types, thereby improving efficacy and reducing ad­
verse effects. Among these, liposomes have emerged as versatile nanocarriers capable
of encapsulating both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs. Their biocompatibility, tunable
composition, and ability to alter pharmacokinetics make them attractive for controlled drug
delivery applications. Further refinement of this concept has led to the development of
stimuli­responsive liposomes, which release their payload in response to specific environ­
mental triggers.
Thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs), in particular, respond to mild local hyperthermia by
undergoing structural changes that increase membrane permeability, enabling rapid drug
release at elevated temperatures. By exploiting the phase transition properties of lipids
such as dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC), TSLs can be engineered to release their
cargo at temperatures just above physiological baseline, typically around 39­42°C. This
enables spatial and temporal control over drug delivery when combined with heat­generating
modalities such as focused ultrasound.
Despite their promise, several challenges remain in the development of thermosensitive
liposomal drug systems. Drug loading efficiency must be optimised for each compound,
particularly when using active (remote) loading methods that depend on the physicochem­
ical properties of the drug, such as pKa and lipophilicity. Furthermore, the physical stability
of liposomes must be maintained under physiological conditions, and the release profile
must be precisely tuned to the thermal activation window.
This thesis investigates the formulation and characterisation of thermosensitive liposomes
for the localised delivery of clenbuterol. The project focuses on optimising liposome size,
encapsulation efficiency, and release behaviour in response to temperature changes. By
combining the therapeutic potential of clenbuterol with the targeting capabilities of TSLs,
this study aims to evaluate the feasibility of a heat­activated delivery system for neuro­
muscular applications.

Background
2.1
Drug Delivery Systems
Very few drugs reach their intended site of action unaided and have to be incorporated into
a drug delivery system to escape uptake or clearance by the immune system. The term
drug delivery system (DDS) describes a means of delivering an active pharmaceutical
ingredient to its site of action. Recently, nanocarriers, a DDS which focuses on encap­
sulating drugs into nano­sized carriers, have become an interesting topic of research,
as they have shown immense capabilities to increase bioavailability, reduce off­target ef­
fects, and decrease the toxic effects of drugs. As new drug molecules become more and
more complex, the need for efficient delivery is becoming increasingly important.
2.2
Targeted drug delivery
Targeted drug delivery refers to therapeutic strategies aimed at concentrating pharma­
cologically active agents at specific tissues, cells, or pathological sites while minimising
distribution to healthy tissues. This approach enhances therapeutic efficacy and reduces
systemic side effects, particularly in treatments where high systemic exposure would be
harmful or inefficient [1, 2]. Targeting mechanisms can be passive, such as leveraging the
enhanced permeability and retention (EPR) effect in tumours or inflamed tissues, or ac­
tive, involving the use of molecular ligands that bind selectively to receptors on target cells
[3, 4, 2]. Advances in nanomedicine have facilitated the development of carrier systems,
such as liposomes, micelles, polymer­based nanoparticles, protein­based nanocarriers,
and metallic nanoparticles, that improve drug stability, circulation time, and tissue speci­
ficity [1, 5, 6]. Among these, liposomes have gained significant clinical traction due to
their biocompatibility, tunable properties, and adaptability for both passive and triggered
release, making them particularly suitable for temperature­ or pH­responsive delivery sys­
tems [1].
2.3
Liposomes
Liposomes, first described in 1965 by Bangham et al. [7], are among the most widely stud­
ied nanocarriers in drug delivery. They are spherical vesicles, composed of one or more
phospholipid bilayers that, due to their amphiphilic nature, form spontaneously when lipids
are hydrated in aqueous media. The hydrophobic tails orient inward, shielded from water,
while hydrophilic heads face outward toward the aqueous environment. This means that
not only can they encapsulate hydrophilic drugs in their aqueous core, but lipophilic drugs
can be encapsulated within the bilayer as well (figure 2.1) [4].

As drug carriers, liposomes provide protection from physiological degradation, prolong
drug half­life, allow for controlled release, and exhibit excellent biocompatibility [9, 10, 8,
4]. They can also be engineered to target diseased tissue actively or passively, minimising
off­target effects and improving therapeutic efficacy [2, 4, 11, 12].
A key limitation of liposomes is their efficient recognition by the mononuclear phagocyte
system (MPS) which recognize surface­bound opsonins, and subsequent clearance via
the reticuloendothelial system (RES) [4, 13]. PEGylation, the addition of the hydrophilic
polymer polyethylene­glycol (PEG) to the surface of liposomes, is a popular modification
of the classical liposome. Possessing a flexible chain that occupies the space immediately
adjacent to the liposome surface, PEG tends to impede aggregation of liposomes and
sterically hinder other macromolecules from this space. Therefore, access and binding of
blood plasma opsonins to the liposome surface are challenged, and thus interactions of
MPS macrophages with such liposomes are inhibited, thereby reducing MPS uptake and
RES clearance, and consequently achieving long­time circulation [13, 14, 15].
The inclusion of cholesterol may help stabilise the membrane [4, 9]. The forces that
govern bilayer assembly include hydrophobic interactions, electrostatics, van der Waals
forces, hydrogen bonding, hydration, and steric constraints [16].

2.4
Alternatives to Liposomes
Liposomes are just one among many nanocarriers that have achieved clinical success
or remain under active development. The broader field of nanocarrier­based drug deliv­
ery has seen significant advances in recent years, with notable examples including lipid
nanoparticles (LNPs), protein­based nanoparticles, and metallic nanoparticles.
2.4.1
Lipid­based Nanoparticles
Lipid nanoparticles (LNP’s) are structurally similar to liposomes; they are formed of lipids,
like liposomes, but do not possess the defining lipid bilayer of liposomes. They can vary
greatly in composition and form. Some LNPs may display regions of bilayer, monolayer,
or even more complex hybrid structures, resulting in a lipid dense core which is ideal for
e.g. nucleic acid encapsulation. Some lipid nanoparticle DDS’s are drug­lipid complexes
where drug is bound straight to lipids [17], two LNP formulations containing ionizable lipids
(iLNPs) were approved for mRNA vaccine delivery in the COVID­19 epidemic [18]. Other
examples are solid lipid nanoparticles (SLPs) and nanostructured lipid­carriers (NLCs),
which consist of solid lipid matrices and a combination of solid and liquid lipids respec­
tively, both are designed for a slow and sustained release of drug. No such formulations
have been approved yet, though they show promise for improving solubility and bioavail­
ability of hydrophobic drugs [19]. Figure 2.3 compares the different types of lipid­based
particles mentioned.
Figure 2.3: Comparison of different types of lipid­based nanocarriers. Liposomes and lipid
nanoparticles (LNPs) are structurally similar, though LNPs lack the characteristic bilayer

both slow­release DDS formulations which have a matrix­like, lipid­dense, lipophilic core

2.4.2
Protein­based Nanoparticles
While no formulation has yet been approved for use in the clinic, protein­based nanopar­
ticles (see figure 2.4 for a comparison of ferritin drug conjugates (FDCs), an example of a
protein­based nanoparticle, to liposomes) are an emerging field of nanocarriers. Ferritin,
a natural protein with a hollow nanocage structure, exhibits some of the same exciting ad­
vantages as liposomes, such as excellent biocompatibility and encapsulation efficiency.

2.4.3
Metallic nanoparticles
Metallic nanoparticles are another nanocarrier type which have been extensively studied,
although mostly for oncological applications. They are colloidal particles ranging from 10
to 1000 nm in size. The most common metallic NPs used in or studied for drug delivery
are composed of: gold, silver, iron oxide, platinum, zinc oxide, or titanium dioxide. A
therapeutic agent can be either dispersed or encapsulated within a shell, either covalently
attached to the surface of the NP, or encapsulated within the particle to deliver the drug at
the target site. Metallic NPs offer a variety of applications within the diagnosis of cancer as
well as therapy, for instance, utilisation of magnetic resonance imaging (MRI) and colloidal
mediators for magnetic hyperthermia effect using magnetic NPs by photo­thermal ablation
(PTA) therapy. A significant drawback of metallic NPs is the potential toxicity from NP
accumulation in certain cell types [6].

2.5
Thermosensitive Systems
Stimuli­responsive liposomes have become a promising prospect in drug delivery. They
aim to release their payload when they are stimulated by either an external source or a
physiological trait of the tissue or cells they are intended to treat. This can, and has been,
done in a number of different ways. Some notable stimuli that have been researched are
thermal, pH, redox reactions, unique enzyme activity, and light. These stimuli generally
cause membrane defects in the lipid bilayer which lead to the drug diffusing out of the
liposomes [13].
Thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs) have a characteristic phase transition temperature
(Tm) at which they transition from a solid­ordered gel phase (Lβ) to a disordered liquid­
crystalline phase (Lα). TSLs are designed to remain intact at physiological temperatures
and to release their contents upon heating to their Tm, typically between 39 and 42°C [22,
23]. This feature makes them suitable for targeted therapy using localised heat, such as
in hyperthermia­treated tumours, potentially inflamed tissues, or in conjunction with ex­
ternal heat sources. To avoid additional harm, the phase transition temperature must be
high enough to prevent premature leakage, but low enough to avoid damage to healthy
cells [23].
2.5.1
Mechanisms of thermosensitivity
Figure 2.6: Phospholipid bilayer structure at varying temperatures relative to the phase
transition temperature. While permeability is high at temperatures above the Tm, it is
highest at around the Tm [22]
In the solid­ordered gel phase of the liposome, Lβ, the lipid molecules are tightly packed
in an orderly fashion in the bilayer, with extended hydrocarbon chains. The mobility of the
lipids within the bilayer increases slowly with temperature as the bilayer is heated. When
the temperature approaches Tm, the orientation of the C­C single bonds in the hydro­
carbon chains moves from a ’trans’ to a ’gauche’ configuration. Leaky grain boundaries
develop between still solid gel­phase domains and disordered, melting­, liquid­lipid do­
mains, Lα. Incompatible lipid packing characterises the lipids at these interfaces, and the
membrane becomes highly permeable at these boundaries, even more so than at higher
temperatures where the entire bilayer has reached the Lα phase. Similarly to the Lβ
phase, individual lipid molecules are still restricted in the Lα to the two­dimensional plane
of the membrane, but are able to move freely and rapidly within the membrane planel,
resulting in a mostly fluid membrane that is permeable to drugs throughout [24, 25, 23].

==PAGE:19==
Importantly, the liposomal membrane is not a homogeneous sheet of lipids, but a granular
structure. In the liposome production process, powdered lipids are hydrated at temper­
atures above Tm. This forms large multilamellar vesicles (LMVs), which are extruded to
reduce their size to small unilamellar vesicles (SUVs), which is the desirable final form of
many liposomal formulations. As temperatures slowly drop down to Tm, the lipid mem­
brane does not solidify uniformly, but nucleates out of solid grains that form within the
liquid membrane. These individual domains of solid phase lipids, or ‘grains’, orient them­
selves in lattice­like structures. As the transition approaches completion, grain bound­
aries are established between the hardening domains, and the grains meet at different
orientations, similarly to the solidification of metal lattices. These imperfections in the ar­
rangement between the growing solid­phase grains produce planar defects at the grain
boundaries [23].
When the liposomes are heated again, e.g. to induce permeability and deliver a drug after
administration of a liposome formulation, melting initiates at these crystal structure bound­
aries (defects) that were formed during cooling in the production process. Upon heating,
new solid/liquid boundaries also formed within the crystalline solid domains in the planar
bilayer sheets that are not grain­derived as the solid­phase begins to melt (transforming
from Lβ to Lα). The presence of the crystal structure imperfections at the grain boundaries
accelerates this process and thus enhances the permeability of the membrane during the
phase transition [26, 23].
2.6
Historical Liposomal Applications
2.6.1
Cancer
Antitumour drugs tend to have serious side effects, somewhat restricting their clinical
use. Due to the impressive properties of liposomes, some of which mentioned above,
they can be used as a drug carrier in tumour therapy and have been widely used and
studied in tumours. In recent years, with the rapid development of liposomal technology,
many liposome­based therapeutics have been successfully transformed into clinical appli­
cations, and many liposome products are undergoing clinical trials. Doxil®, a formulation
containing DOX hydrochloride, is the first FDA­approved nano­scale drug delivery system
based on liposome technology. Sequus Pharmaceuticals originally developed Doxil® in
1995 as an intravenous injection for the management of advanced ovarian cancer, mul­
tiple myeloma, and HIV­associated Kaposi’s sarcoma. Since then, many more liposomal
products have been developed and applied against cancer in clinical practice, such as
Myocet®, Depocyt®, DaunoXome®, and Onivyde® [27, 28].
2.6.2
Pain
Liposomal formulations of local anaesthetics, especially bupivacaine, have been devel­
oped and clinically tested. These formulations provide significantly prolonged pain relief
after surgical procedures and in chronic pain settings, such as cancer pain [29]. For in­
stance, in 2011, Gorfine et.al. performed a multicenter, randomized, double­blind clinical
trial which showed that liposomal bupivacaine significantly reduced pain and opioid re­
quirements for up to 72 hours after hemorrhoidectomy [30]. Similar benefits have been
reported for chronic cancer pain, with liposomal bupivacaine providing pain relief for up to
11 hours, compared to 4 hours for the plain drug [29]. Exparel® (liposomal bupivacaine)
was first approved by the FDA in 2011 and remained the only FDA­approved liposomal
pain relief medication until 2024, when the FDA approved the first generic liposomal bupi­
vacaine formulation. Liposomal morphine sulfate had previously been approved in 2004,
but was discontinued due to manufacturing difficulties [31].

2.6.3
Infectious diseases
Liposomes have become key vehicles in the clinical management of infectious diseases,
primarily due to their ability to improve drug pharmacokinetics, reduce toxicity, and target
delivery to sites of infection. The most prominent example is liposomal amphotericin B
(AmBisome®), which is globally approved for severe systemic fungal infections and is
now the standard of care for invasive mycoses in immunocompromised patients or those
with renal impairment. This formulation offers a markedly improved safety profile over
conventional amphotericin B, reducing toxicity, particularly nephrotoxicity, and enabling
higher dosing [32]. Another notable product is amikacin liposome inhalation suspension
(Arikayce®), approved for refractory Mycobacterium avium complex lung disease, which
enables high local concentrations in the lungs while minimising systemic toxicity [33].
A critical feature of liposomal carriers for treating infections caused by intracellular pathogens
is their natural uptake by the mononuclear phagocyte system (MPS), especially by macrophages
in the liver and spleen, as liposomes are preferentially taken up by these endocytic cells,
allowing for targeted drug delivery directly to sites where pathogens may reside [15]. As
previously discussed, macrophage uptake is usually a challenge of liposomal develop­
ment, so in cases where macrophage uptake is the desired outcome, PEGylation is not
utilised.
Liposomal technology has also been incorporated into vaccines such as Epaxal® (hepati­
tis A) and Inflexal® (influenza), where it enhances antigen delivery and immunogenicity.
Both vaccines have demonstrated high immunogenicity and excellent tolerability in clini­
cal use, with Epaxal® and Inflexal® representing established, licensed liposomal vaccine
products. Several other liposomal vaccines are currently under development, including
vaccines against influenza, diphtheria, tetanus, hepatitis A and B, rabies, tuberculosis, in­
testinal infections, etc., reflecting the ongoing expansion of this platform in disease man­
agement and prevention [34].
2.6.4
Other Areas of Interest
Beyond oncology, pain, and infection, Liposomes have also been investigated extensively
as delivery vehicles in cardiovascular, neurological, and autoimmune disease contexts.
In cardiovascular medicine, liposomal systems have been evaluated for targeted deliv­
ery of various drugs to treat disorders such as restenosis, myocardial infarction (MI), and
atherosclerosis [35]. In neurology, liposomes are being engineered to cross the blood­
brain barrier (BBB), using receptor­mediated or adsorptive pathways to deliver therapeu­
tics for conditions such as Alzheimer’s, Parkinson’s, and cerebral ischemia. Liposomes
targeting transferrin receptor (TfR) are the most investigated for liposomes crossing the
BBB, because of the abundance of TfR on the BBB [35, 36]. For autoimmune and inflam­
matory disorders, liposomal formulations (sometimes surface­modified to mimic apoptotic
cells or target inflamed tissues) have shown promise in preclinical models, improving de­

of autoimmune disorders which have been investigated for liposomal delivery are: type

structural versatility, biocompatibility, and tunable pharmacokinetics make liposomes a
versatile platform in nanomedicine research across a broad spectrum of disease areas.
2.6.5
Clinical Progress of Thermosensitive Liposomes
Although thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs) have been studied for several decades and
are widely considered a promising platform for targeted drug delivery, no TSL­based for­
mulation has received full regulatory approval from the FDA or EMA as of 2025. However,
there are several important candidates that have entered clinical trials, and their devel­

opment offers valuable insight into both the potential and limitations of TSLs in real­world
settings.
ThermoDox® – The Most Advanced TSL Formulation
ThermoDox® is the most clinically advanced TSL formulation, a heat­triggered liposomal
formulation of doxorubicin developed by Celsion Corporation. ThermoDox is based on
a lipid composition of DPPC, MSPC, and DSPE­mPEG2000, which enables rapid drug
release when exposed to temperatures around 41–42°C. It has been evaluated in combi­
nation with local heating techniques such as radiofrequency ablation (RFA) and focused
ultrasound (FUS) in patients with hepatocellular carcinoma, as well as breast cancer and
recurrent chest wall breast cancer [38, 39].
ThermoDox has undergone multiple clinical trials, including two Phase III trials known as
HEAT and OPTIMA. Unfortunately, both studies failed to meet their primary endpoints.
However, later analysis of HEAT showed improved outcomes in a subgroup of patients
who received prolonged thermal exposure. These findings have kept interest in Thermo­
Dox alive, although it has not progressed to regulatory approval [38].
Other TSL­Based Candidates
Another TSL­based product in development is a liposomal formulation of vinorelbine. This
candidate is intended for use in soft tissue sarcoma and other solid tumours. Like Ther­
moDox, it uses a DPPC­based lipid system and is designed for combination with localised
heating. This formulation remains in preclinical or early clinical development [40]. Some
other investigational TSLs include formulations for antibiotics [41] and nucleic acids [42],
but none have advanced beyond early­stage trials.
Barriers to Regulatory Approval
The lack of approved TSLs is mostly related to practical and clinical challenges. Reliable
delivery of heat to internal tissues remains difficult. This leads to variability in drug release,
which affects the consistency of clinical trial outcomes. Regulatory bodies require strong
and predictable data, and this has been a major hurdle for TSL developers [43, 38].
In addition, the production of TSLs must, as with any other pharmaceutical product, meet
the strict pharmaceutical standards of regulatory bodies, including control of size, drug
encapsulation, release kinetics, and stability. Achieving this consistency at large scale
can be complex, especially for formulations that are highly sensitive to temperature and
composition.
Outlook
While no TSL product has yet reached the market, their clinical development is ongo­
ing, and the underlying technology continues to evolve. Improvements in hyperthermia
devices, image­guided heating, and liposome formulation may lead to better clinical per­
formance in the future. TSLs remain one of the most promising approaches for achieving
controlled, site­specific drug delivery in oncology and potentially other fields such as neu­
rology or regenerative medicine.

Extensive angiogenesis and resulting hypervascularity, other defective vascular architec­

ators of permeability are pathophysiological characteristics which have been observed to
be universal in solid tumours. This describes an important concept in liposome develop­
ment for oncological applications, called the enhanced permeation and retention (EPR)
effect [3]. This effect is what makes liposomes such an effective DDS for anticancer drugs,
as systemically administered liposomes will accumulate in and around tumour tissue be­
cause of increased blood­flow and poor lymphatic drainage. While the EPR effect is less

2.8
The Neuromuscular Junction
The neuromuscular junction (NMJ) is a specialised chemical synapse that enables com­
munication between motor neurons and skeletal muscle fibers, thus playing a critical role
in voluntary movement. Structurally, it comprises the presynaptic motor neuron terminal,
the postsynaptic region of the muscle membrane, and associated terminal Schwann cells.
These elements work together to ensure precise and efficient signal transmission [44].
When an action potential reaches the motor neuron terminal, voltage­dependent calcium
channels open, allowing calcium to enter the presynaptic terminal. This calcium influx is
essential for triggering the release of acetylcholine (ACh), the primary neurotransmitter
at the neuromuscular junction, which then diffuses across the synaptic cleft to activate
nicotinic receptors on the muscle fiber membrane region, known as the ”motor end plate”,
which is characterized by thickening and extensive folding into junctional folds. The re­
sulting depolarisation initiates intracellular calcium release within the muscle, ultimately
leading to contraction [44, 45, 46]. The dependence of ACh release on calcium entry
was first firmly established by Katz and Miledi, who argued that the amount of transmitter
released correlates with the quantity of calcium entering the nerve terminal during depo­
larisation [45].
The NMJ is also one of the earliest affected sites in various neuromuscular disorders.
In models of amyotrophic lateral sclerosis (ALS), NMJ deterioration has been observed
before detectable motor neuron loss, suggesting that synaptic degeneration may play a
role in early disease progression [44].
Due to its relative accessibility and well­characterised molecular organization, the NMJ
has long served as a model for studying synaptic formation, function, and maintenance.
It has contributed significantly to our understanding of synaptic biology, including insights
applicable to less accessible central synapses [47].
Although some drugs have shown potential to protect or restore NMJs, their use is often
limited by side effects due to their action in tissues outside the muscle. This has led to
growing interest in targeted delivery systems, such as TSLs, which release their contents
in response to local heating and may one day allow more precise drug delivery to NMJs,
reducing off­target effects and improving treatment safety [48].

2.8.2
Current Strategies for Drug Delivery to the Neuromuscular Junction
Currently approved therapies for neuromuscular disorders predominantly rely on systemic
drug administration via oral or intravenous delivery. While these methods enable broad
biodistribution, they are frequently associated with suboptimal efficacy and undesirable
systemic side effects due to the lack of site­specific targeting and large doses required
due to systemic administration [52]. Although the molecular architecture of the neuromus­
cular junction (NMJ) is well­characterized, encompassing targets such as acetylcholine
receptor (AChR), muscle­specific kinase (MUSK), they are not expressed exclusively in
the NMJs, making selective NMJ targeting with these targets difficult [48, 53, 54]. Some
other difficulties in development of such strategies may be the presence of physiological
barriers as NMJ’s lie deep within skeletal muscle tissue with multiple layers of protective
tissue, the rapid clearance of nanocarrieres, and the high doses that may be required to
reach sufficient levels of drug accumulation at NMJ’s, increasing the risk and severity of
side­effects.
Many anaesthetics used for muscle relaxation exert their effects at neuromuscular junc­
tions [46].

2.10
Design Choices
2.10.1
Dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine
1,2­dipalmitoyl­sn­glycero­3­phosphocholine (DPPC) (figure 2.8) is one of the most com­
monly used neutral phospholipids in thermosensitive liposomal formulations due to its
well­characterised phase transition temperature (Tm) of approximately 41.5°C [67]. This
temperature aligns well with the mild hyperthermia window used in externally triggered
drug delivery, allowing for stable encapsulation at physiological temperature (37°C) and
rapid drug release when heated locally to temperatures around the Tm. Furthermore,
DPPC forms robust bilayers that support consistent vesicle formation and remote loading
protocols.

2.10.2
Poly(ethylene glycol)–distearoylphosphatidylethanolamine
As previously mentioned, the classical liposome is rapidly cleared from circulation, which
is a major challenge with their application in drug delivery where liposomes need to circu­
late for a relatively long time to allow for the delivery of drug in therapeutic doses to their
intended site of action.
The addition of 1,2­distearoyl­sn­glycero­3­phosphoethanolamine conjugated with polyethy­
lene glycol 2000 (DSPE­mPEG2000) provides a steric barrier around the liposome, thereby
reducing opsonisation and subsequent uptake by the mononuclear phagocyte system
(MPS). PEGylation helps extend liposome circulation time, improving the chance of reach­
ing the intended tissue before drug release [68]. A ~5 mol% DSPE­mPEG2000 composi­
tion is commonly used and has been shown to balance circulation time with stability and
payload release kinetics. Below 4 mol% the PEG adopts a mushroom­like configuration,
which is about 3.5 nm thick. As the concentration is raised to 4­8 mol%, the configuration
moves to a brush­like shape of about 4.5­10 nm thickness, which is more effective in hin­
dering opsonisation. A mol% above this level induces the formation of micelles instead of
liposomes, which are not desirable for the encapsulation of weak bases [9].
Figure 2.9: Structure of DSPE­mPEG2000. The image is adapted from [23]. The figure
shows a DSPE phospholipid with a polyethylene glycol chain attached to its polar head
group, sterically hindering the accumulation of plasma opsonins on the liposomal sur­
face and thereby reducing the uptake of liposomes by macrophages and increasing the
circulation time of the liposomal formulation [68].
PEGylation has been the gold standard for increasing the circulation time of liposomes,
though recent research has suggested that there may be an adverse immunogenic re­
sponse to PEGylated drug delivery systems induced after systemic administration which
leads to complement activation and/or anti­PEG antibody production, resulting in hyper­
sensitivity reactions and lack of therapeutic efficacy of the drug [69]. This needs further
validation before action needs to be taken, but should be monitored closely for updates.

Because NMJs reside in healthy skeletal muscle without the leaky vasculature associ­

that release their encapsulated drug specifically at the NMJ in response to local heating.

particularly relevant for drugs like clenbuterol that have widespread physiological effects

3.2
Liposome Production
40 mM liposomal solutions were prepared by direct hydration of a dry lipid powder con­
sisting of 95:5 mol% dipalmitoylphosphatidylcholine (DPPC) and DSPE­mPEG2000. The
lipids were hydrated in 250 mM ammonium sulfate solution (pH 5.5) at 60°C for 1 hour.
Hydration was carried out in a glass vial placed within an aluminium heating block to en­
sure uniform and consistent temperature. A magnetic stir bar was used to continuously
stir the mixture on a stir plate throughout the hydration period. The vial was covered with
aluminium foil to minimise heat loss and maintain stable thermal conditions.
Following hydration, the multilamellar vesicles were downsized using high­pressure ex­
trusion. The suspension was heated to 70°C and sequentially extruded through poly­
carbonate membranes with 800 nm, 400 nm, and 100 nm pore sizes to produce small
unilamellar vesicles (SUVs) with a uniform size distribution.
To establish a transmembrane ammonium sulfate gradient, the extruded liposomes were
subjected to dialysis against 500 mL phosphate­buffered saline (PBS, pH 7.4) at 4°C for
16 hours, with a single buffer exchange after 4 hours.
3.3
Remote Loading
Clenbuterol hydrochloride was actively loaded into the preformed liposomes using a trans­
membrane ammonium sulfate gradient, a method widely adopted for loading weakly basic
drugs into PEGylated liposomes [72, 73]. This technique relies on the passive diffusion of
ammonia (NH3) from the vesicle core, resulting in proton accumulation and a decrease
in internal pH, and thereby the creation of a proton gradient. Weak bases such as clen­
buterol become protonated upon entering the acidic core and are retained in their charged
form, unable to diffuse back across the lipid bilayer [72, 74].
To determine optimal incubation temperature and duration of remote loading, a series of
preliminary experiments were done to evaluate the effects of temperature and loading du­
ration on encapsulation efficiency with liposomes made from a stealth mixture, containing
HSPC, cholesterol, and DSPE­mPEG2000, where 45°C and 60°C were compared, as
were 30 and 60 minutes. A combination of 45°C loading temperature and a 60 minute
duration consistently yielded the highest encapsulation efficiency. These conditions were
selected based on the expected behaviour of the lipid bilayer near its phase transition
temperature (Tm ≈41.5°C for DPPC), where membrane permeability is enhanced and
loading is more efficient.

3.3.1
Advantages and Limitations of Remote Loading
Active (remote) loading of liposomal drugs, offers significantly higher encapsulation ef­
ficiency and allows for greater intraliposomal drug concentrations compared to passive
loading, which relies on drug partitioning during liposome formation [75]. These charac­
teristics have enabled the success of formulations like Doxil®, where remote loading of
doxorubicin via an ammonium sulfate gradient results in drug­to­lipid ratios and retention
properties that passive loading cannot match [73, 75]. However, both loading methods
require post­processing, such as dialysis or gel filtration, to remove unencapsulated drug
[75].
Remote loading is best suited for weakly basic, amphiphilic compounds that can cross the
lipid bilayer in their neutral form and become protonated and trapped inside the vesicle
core. Clenbuterol, with a pKa of approximately 9.6 and a logD of ca. 0.14 [55, 57], fits
this profile in principle, due to its moderate lipophilicity and basicity [76]. However, this
relatively high pKa may reduce the fraction of neutral (membrane­permeable) species
at physiological and loading pH conditions, possibly leading to lower encapsulation effi­
ciencies than are observed with doxorubicin or similar compounds with lower pKa values
[73].

divided by the initial mass of drug added to determine the encapsulation efficiency as a
percentage.
For the release studies, the average HPLC peak area from triplicate measurements is
similarly applied to the calibration curve to obtain the drug concentration in the release
medium, which is then multiplied by the PBS volume to yield the total amount of drug
released from the liposomes.

Correcting for Lipid Loss
During extrusion and dialysis, a substantial portion of lipid is lost, reducing the actual lipid
concentration in solution. This loss must be accounted for when calculating the amount
of drug required to achieve the intended drug:lipid molar ratios. Because the primary
lipid used, DPPC, is non­UV active and thus not directly quantifiable via standard UV­
Vis or HPLC detection methods, a separate liposomal formulation containing cholesterol

was prepared to estimate lipid loss. Cholesterol, being UV­active and chemically stable,
serves as a suitable surrogate marker.
To quantify lipid loss during extrusion and dialysis, liposomes made from a ”stealth mix”
acquired from Avanti Polar Lipids, consisting of HSPC:DSPE­mPEG2000:cholesterol at
a 3:1:1 molar ratio were prepared. Importantly, this mixture contains cholesterol, which is
suitable to use as a surrogate for the undetectable DPPC. Samples were collected during

liquid chromatography (HPLC) with the same column used for EE and thermal release

210 nm, where cholesterol exhibits reliable UV absorbance. A standard calibration curve
was established from known cholesterol concentrations to quantify sample content. The
relative change in cholesterol concentration revealed a 38% lipid loss during processing.
For the purposes of this project, it is assumed that this loss percentage is consistent across
all lipid components, including DPPC and DSPE­PEG. As a result, nominal lipid concen­
trations were adjusted accordingly: for example, a liposome solution initially formulated at
40 mM is treated as having an effective lipid concentration of 24.7 mM post­processing.
This corrected concentration was then used in all drug­loading calculations to accurately

5
Discussions
This study aimed to develop thermosensitive liposomes capable of encapsulating clen­
buterol hydrochloride and releasing it in response to mild hyperthermia. The results from
characterisation experiments, including encapsulation efficiency (EE), size distribution,
and thermal release, are discussed in this chapter, along with notes on analytical preci­
sion and practical considerations for in vivo applications.
5.1
Encapsulation Efficiency
The observed encapsulation efficiencies (EE) ranged from 39% to 88%. Modest EE val­
ues were expected given the relatively high pKa of clenbuterol hydrochloride (9.63). Weak
bases with high pKa values tend to exist predominantly in their charged form at neutral
pH, which limits their ability to be actively loaded into liposomes using transmembrane ion
gradients. This is because charged species generally show low permeability across the
hydrophobic lipid bilayer, and remote loading usually relies on the neutral form of weak
bases to diffuse into the liposome interior, where they become protonated and trapped by
the ammonium sulfate gradient, and a higher pKa means that at at any given moment,
fewer molecules are in the uncharged form under physiological or mildly acidic conditions.
Thus, very high EE was not anticipated. However, several factors likely contributed to the
improved loading performance observed in this formulation. Most notably, the steep trans­
membrane ion gradient (250 mM ammonium sulfate) provided a strong driving force for
remote loading, the efficient removal of unencapsulated ammonium sulfate through ex­
tended dialysis likely intensified the gradient, further promoting protonation and trapping
of the drug within the liposome interior, and the longer remote loading duration gave the
drug more time to accumulate within the liposomes. While clenbuterol’s pKa may cause
difficulties passing into the liposomes, it does also mean that once within, the drug is more
likely to stay there and not diffuse back out.
Among the various conditions tested, liposomes loaded at 45°C for 60 minutes consis­
tently outperformed other combinations of temperature and duration. In contrast, shorter
loading durations (30 minutes) and temperatures greater than Tm resulted in lower EE,
suggesting insufficient time or a lack of grain boundaries to allow unprotonated clenbuterol
to saturate the internal space (Table 4.3). This trend confirms that both the duration of
loading and the temperature at which loading is performed significantly affect encapsula­
tion efficiency. The decision to load at 45°C, just above the Tm, rather than at the actual
phase transition temperature of DPPC of 41.5°C, was based on uncertainty regarding
heat transfer efficiency from the heating plate to the liposome solution. Performing re­
mote loading at 45°resulted in higher EE than 60°C, which is likely due to the membrane
permeability being at its highest at the Tm, because of the especially permeable grain
boundaries which are present at the phase transition temperature, and not above, where
the whole liposome, or most of it, is in the liquid disordered phase [22]. The high perme­
ability of grain boundaries may partially explain the higher­than­anticipated EE. Further
experimentation and more precise condition characterisation/control would likely enable
further encapsulation optimisation.
Interestingly, a lower drug­to­lipid ratio tended to result in a higher encapsulation efficiency
(table 4.7). However, higher EE does not mean that more clenbuterol was encapsulated.
In fact, even with the lower EE, higher drug­to­lipid ratios consistently delivered more
clenbuterol to the liposomal core. Liposomes loaded at 1:20 drug­to­lipid ratio showed
40
Thermosensitive liposomes for localised treatment of neuromuscular disorders.
==PAGE:50==
the highest EE, however, as mentioned, even with their high EE, the actual amount of
drug encapsulated is still lower than at the other ratios, see equations 4.1­4.5 and table
4.8.
So even at its moderate 39% EE, 1:3 outperforms every other drug­to­lipid molar ratio
surveyed in terms of total mass of encapsulated drug with 1.0 mg/mL of clenbuterol suc­
cessfully remote loaded, compared to 0.7 mg/mL, 0.6 mg/mL, and 0.3 mg/mL for 1:5, 1:7,
and 1:20, respectively, with the exception of 1:3.5, which had a notably higher EE, which
was likely the result of instrumental faults, rather than innate differences between these
molar ratios.
The vast difference in EE between the 1:3 and 1:3.5 formulations, as seen in tables 4.5
and 4.7, is likely mostly explained by the failure of the HPLC in late May/early June (Table
4.10), where the measured concentrations were considerably lower than expected and
had very high variability between measurements. On top of that, the high measured EE
of the 1:3.5 formulation is somewhat questionable, as excluding that formulation, the ob­
served trend is an increase in encapsulation efficiency with a lowering drug­to­lipid ratio.
Nevertheless, in this thesis, the drug:lipid ratios of 1:3 and 1:3.5 outperformed the other
drug:lipid ratios tested in terms of total mass of drug encapsulated within liposomes, as
shown in equations 4.1­4.5.
A question of waste is one that needs to be asked. While the observed trend is that
higher drug:lipid ratios tend to encapsulate more drug, they also tend to have a lower
encapsulation efficiency, leaving a significant amount of drug outside the liposomes to be
dialysed away, and thus wasted. While a higher efficiency is desirable from a cost and
engineering rigour standpoint, fewer drug molecules per liposome mean a higher volume
and higher lipid dose, which might be undesirable safety­wise. This remains a point of
contention and needs to be settled before animal studies are conducted.
It should be noted that the encapsulation efficiency reported in these experiments may be
slightly overestimated, as no additional purification step was employed to remove drug po­
tentially adsorbed to the exterior surface of the liposomes. Future studies could address
this limitation by incorporating size­exclusion chromatography or repeated washes with
ammonium sulfate to better distinguish true encapsulated drug from surface­associated
material. However, the significance of this potential overestimation is uncertain. The
release profiles at physiological temperatures did not indicate excessive early­phase re­
lease that might suggest detachment of surface­bound drug. If any drug is indeed ad­
sorbed to the liposome surface but remains stably associated during circulation and does
not interact nonspecifically en route to the target tissue, its presence may have negligible
impact on therapeutic performance or interpretation of the results.
5.2
Thermosensitivity and Release Kinetics
Thermosensitive release was observed, with up to 48 µg of drug released after 4 hours,
and 96 µg after 20 hours at 42°C, and a mere 3.7 µg and 4.1 µg after 4 and 20 hours re­
spectively at 37 °C. Corresponding with DPPC’s Tm of ~41.5 [67]. While this indicates a
functioning thermoresponsive system, the rate may be insufficient for some clinical appli­
cations requiring rapid release.
The 1:20 drug­to­lipid formulation was not used for these studies as (even with its high
EE) the total amount of clenbuterol loaded was still smaller than in the higher drug­to­lipid
ratio formulations and so the 1:20 formulation was not considered relevant for further use.
There were two graphs of release at 37°C provided in the results chapters (Figures 4.5,

The standard deviation for drug release at 42°C is relatively high. For the 1:3 drug­to­lipid
molar ratio formulation, the measured amount of drug released at 20 hours was 96 µg
on average, with a standard deviation of ±28 µg. This means that, assuming a normal
distribution, approximately 68% of individual measurements are expected to fall between
68 and 124 µg. However, it is important to contextualize this range using the known en­
capsulation efficiency. For the 1:3 formulation, the encapsulation efficiency was 39%. To
prepare 100 µL of liposomes with this ratio, 258 µg of clenbuterol is introduced, which,
given the 39% EE, yields approximately 100 µg of drug encapsulated. Consequently, any
apparent release values exceeding this amount are not physically possible and likely re­
flect experimental variability and/or measurement noise. The realistic upper bound for
released drug, therefore, is about 100 µg. In light of this, the actual release after 20 hours
at 42°C is likely within the range of 68–100 µg, corresponding to an approximate 68–100%
release of the encapsulated clenbuterol. Given the statistical spread, complete release is
unlikely, but high release efficiency is still evident.
Comparing these results to the rate of release at 37°C, we observe that the release at 42°C
is almost two orders of magnitude greater, with 1:3 releasing only ~1 µg if we remove the
outlier as seen in Figure 4.6, if we only take the raw data, the release at 42°C is still ~25x
greater than at 37°C (figures 4.4 and 4.5).
Similarly, for the 1:7 drug­to­lipid molar ratio formulation, drug release at 42°C after 20
hours showed a standard deviation of ±22 µg, with measured release values ranging from
24 to 68 µg. This again reflects considerable variability in the thermally induced release.
In contrast, the corresponding measurements at 37°C exhibited much lower variability,
with an average release of 1.9 µg and a standard deviation of only ±0.1 µg. For the 1:5
formulation, the release at 42°C was measured at 53 µg with a standard deviation of ±8 µg,
whereas the release at physiological temperature was 3.3 µg with a standard deviation of
±0.2 µg. These results underscore the thermosensitive nature of the formulations, with
minimal leakage at 37°C and significantly greater release upon heating. The relatively
high standard deviations at 42°C, especially for the 1:3 and 1:7 formulations, likely reflect
the failings of the HPLC at the end of the project and highlight the need for a reliably
functional measurement apparatus in future studies.

Enhancing release kinetics could involve adjusting lipid composition. For instance, adding
another species of lipids such as DSPC or HSPC to the bilayer can be a good way to
promote rapid release near Tm due to the packing defects associated with combining
different lipids [23]. ThermoDox®, included a lysolipid in their formulation, likely for this
reason [77]. Another potential strategy is reducing PEG content, as PEG may reduce
membrane permeability.

cholesterol content: while it stabilizes membranes against premature leakage, it can re­

defects that result from combining different types of lipids, which has already been dis­
cussed. As the phospholipid bilayer tends to be more permeable at the Tm than above or
below it, it is possible that using the actual Tm of the formulation would result in a faster
release of drug and thus show a more pronounced thermal sensitivity. This is something
that requires validation and is something that should be studied going forward.

5.3
Notes on Analytical Precision
High standard deviations and lower­than­expected measured concentrations during the
last rounds of HPLC analysis raised suspicions that the HPLC was providing inaccurate
results. Because of this, a series of accuracy & precision tests were performed. Four
sample vials were used: one containing a 1.00 mg/mL solution of celnbuterol hydrochlo­
ride, and the second a 1:1 dilution (0.50 mg/mL), the third a 1:20 dilution (0.05 mg/mL),
and the last a vial of a sample which had been measured a week earlier (Table 4.10). Each
sample was injected numerous consecutive times using the autosampler under identical
conditions. Despite using a validated method and a calibration curve that had consistently
produced accurate results since February, the calculated concentrations from these injec­
tions were substantially and inconsistently underestimated, ranging from approximately
0.3 µg/mL to 51.3 µg/mL, far below the expected concentrations.
Chromatographic performance indicators such as peak shape, baseline noise, and re­
tention time were consistent throughout the analysis, with sharp peaks appearing at the
expected 5.00­minute mark. System pressure was also stable and within the typical op­
erating range, ruling out flow or column­related issues. No changes had been made to
the method, mobile phase, or sample preparation procedure.
This combination of symptoms, sharp peaks, stable retention time, and consistent method
parameters, but widely varying and drastically low area under the curve (AUC) values,
strongly suggests a failure within the injection system itself. Autosampler malfunction
was considered the most likely cause. Potential explanations include the presence of air
bubbles in the autosampler syringe, a leaking or worn syringe seal, a partially blocked
needle, or a malfunctioning injection valve. These faults could result in partial or incon­
sistent sample drawing and delivery, causing the erratic and severely underestimated
quantification observed.
Notably, a few days prior to this test, unusually high AUCs had been recorded for a similar
sample, suggesting the autosampler was already behaving unreliably. These inconsis­
tencies further support the conclusion that the fault lies not in the analytical method or
calibration, but in the physical delivery of the sample. However, other possibilities, such
as malfunction of the UV detector, fluctuations in lamp intensity, and undetected electronic
misfires, cannot be ruled out. Further diagnostic testing would be required to confirm the
precise cause of the inconsistency.
5.4
Liposome Size and Suitability
DLS measurements indicated a hydrodynamic diameter slightly above 130 nm, which is
widely considered appropriate for systemic delivery due to efficient vascular transport and
reduced risk of rapid clearance [70]. Though the EPR effect is less relevant in skeletal
muscle, small liposomes remain critical for effective vascular navigation and uptake by
interstitial spaces [70]. The EPR effect, more prominent in tumour and inflamed tissues,
refers to the tendency of nanoparticles to accumulate at sites with leaky vasculature and
poor lymphatic drainage [3]. While it is a cornerstone for liposome delivery in oncology,
and thus much discussed in liposomal literature, its contribution in non­cancerous muscle
tissue or the NMJ is likely minimal.
The slight increase above the pore size of 100 nm used in extrusion could be the result
of PEG­induced steric repulsion or minor inconsistencies during extrusion. Importantly,
the low PDI (0.061) and high autocorrelation intercept (>0.9) confirm a monodisperse
population, which is desirable for predictable biodistribution.

Size is particularly relevant for targeting neuromuscular junctions (NMJs), which are lo­

although this is highly tissue­specific. Moreover, minimizing liposome size is beneficial in
preclinical models, such as mice, where injection volume is limited and smaller particles
pose fewer risks of embolism or off­target accumulation [78, 79, 80].
5.5
Comparison of Results to ThermoDox®
ThermoDox® is a clinically evaluated thermosensitive liposomal formulation of doxoru­
bicin designed for heat­triggered drug release. Its composition, DPPC, MSPC, and DSPE­
PEG2000 in an 86:10:4 molar ratio, has become a reference point for TSL development.
Al­Jamal et.al. [77] replicated the Thermodox® liposome composition and reported their
findings on encapsulation efficiency and release kinetics. Doxorubicin is remote­loaded
into Thermodox® using an ammonium sulfate gradient, achieving encapsulation efficien­
cies of close to 100% with drug­to­lipid ratios of approximately 1:20 [77].
In the present study, encapsulation efficiencies for clenbuterol­loaded TSLs were notably
lower than those reported for ThermoDox®. Depending on the drug­to­lipid ratio and
loading conditions, encapsulation efficiencies ranged from 39% to 88%. The lower en­
capsulation observed here may be attributed to clenbuterol’s physicochemical properties,
particularly its pKa of 9.6, which is near the upper range considered optimal for remote
loading [76]. While still sufficient for active loading, this pKa is less favourable than doxoru­
bicin’s (8.3), potentially limiting the fraction of drug that can permeate the lipid membrane
in its neutral form before being trapped inside the acidic liposomal core.
In terms of release kinetics, ThermoDox® demonstrates extremely rapid release behaviour
at mild hyperthermic temperatures. Studies have shown that close to 100% of the en­
capsulated doxorubicin is released within just one minute of exposure to 42°C, but its
stability at 37°C was not impressive with 100% of drug released within 120 minutes [77].
In contrast, the TSLs in this project displayed slower release profiles. At 42°C, cumula­
tive release ranged from 76% to 96% over 20 hours, depending on the formulation, with
only ~3% released after 30 minutes, and physiological stability is markedly better, with
very limited release after 20 hours at 37°C. The difference in release rate may be due to
multiple factors, including differences in membrane composition, absence of an optimised
release enhancer like MSPC, the characteristics of clenbuterol compared to doxorubicin
­ such as its lower pKa, or both.
Issues with the HPLC, as discussed in section 5.3, likely resulted in lower measured
amounts of clenbuterol encapsulated and released than in reality, further exaggerating
the apparent gap in performance between ThermoDox® and this project.
These differences highlight the challenge of achieving ThermoDox®­like performance
with non­doxorubicin compounds. While Thermodox® sets a high benchmark for encap­
sulation efficiency and release sharpness, results from this study demonstrate that alter­
native compounds like clenbuterol can still be encapsulated and released in a temperature­
sensitive fashion, albeit with further optimization required to enhance both loading and
triggered release.
5.6
In Vivo Considerations
A critical factor in the application of thermosensitive liposomes in vivo is the concept of
thermal dose, which quantifies the biological effect of heat exposure as a function of both
temperature and time. Originally formalised by Sapareto and Dewey in 1984, thermal
Thermosensitive liposomes for localised treatment of neuromuscular disorders.
45
==PAGE:55==
dose is expressed as equivalent minutes at 43°C, allowing different heating regimens to
be compared based on their biological impact. This metric is particularly relevant for ther­
mosensitive liposome systems, where drug release is tightly coupled to membrane phase
transitions that occur within a narrow temperature window, typically around 42°C. In a
clinical or preclinical setting, ensuring that the targeted tissue receives a sufficient ther­
mal dose is essential for reliable drug release, while simultaneously avoiding excessive
heating that could damage healthy tissue [81].
The biological effects of hyperthermia are strongly dependent on both temperature and
duration, and are well described using the thermal dose equation from Ta and Porter [23],
adapted from Sapareto and Dewey [81], equation 5.1:
t43°C = ∆t × R(43−T)
(5.1)
Where t43°C is the thermal dose of the exposure in equivalent minutes at 43°C, ∆t is the
duration of the exposure (min), R is a constant set to 0.25 when T is below 43°C and
0.5 when T is 43°C or higher, and T is the average temperature of the exposure over ∆t.
This relationship highlights the exponential nature of thermal injury risk with increasing
temperature.
This standardisation enables comparisons between protocols that differ in temperature
but yield similar biological effects. According to compiled data across preclinical models,
including muscle tissue, the onset of necrosis begins around 0.5­30 minutes t43 while mod­
erate damage becomes more likely at 60­240 minutes t43, and more severe damage for
t43 beyond 240 minutes [23]. Applying this framework to the present study, a continuous
heating regime at 41.5–42°C for 3 to 4 hours corresponds to an estimated 20–60 minutes
t43. This range remains below the commonly cited damage threshold and supports the
feasibility of achieving thermosensitive liposomal drug release in vivo without incurring
significant thermal injury to surrounding tissue, provided heating remains localised and
well­controlled.
As the thermal dose increases exponentially with increasing temperature, every degree
matters, so future work should aim to lower the phase transition temperature to reduce
the thermal dose.
For in vivo studies in mice, intravenous administration via the tail vein typically limits in­
jection volume to approximately 200 µL, or 5 mL/kg [78, 79]. This volume constraint
necessitates relatively high encapsulation of drug to deliver a sufficient payload.
No precise EC50 values have been reported for clenbuterol NMJ activity in mice, though
Arch and Kaumann et.al. report an EC50 of appx. 2.5 µM for relaxation of rat uterus [82].
Lynch et.al. dosed their mice with clenbuterol at 2 mg/kg in their study. In their words
an excessive dose to mimic misuse of the compound by athletes [83], which, assuming a
mouse weighs 25­30g, means a 50­60 µg dose of clenbuterol. Taking this all into consid­
eration, a 50 µg dose in a maximum 200 µL solution, or 0.2 mg/mL, can be a good target
concentration.
Depending on how quickly the dose should be delivered, two options are available if the
release study results are to be believed. 1:3 drug­to­lipid ratio released just over 48 µg
after four hours at 42°C, and the 1:5 formulation released 55 µg in 20 hours.
The 1:3 drug:lipid ratio formulation contains around 2580 µg/mL, or 516 µg for 200 µL. At
39% EE, ~200 µg is encapsulated. With a ~50% release after 4 hours, the effective dose

5.7
Further Research & Optimization
Further refinement of the formulation could explore:
• Inclusion of other lipid­species to accelerate thermally triggered release and reduce
the thermal dose.
• Cholesterol incorporation, which could increase liposome stability and reduce drug
leakage in vivo.
• Optimisation of PEG chain length and density, balancing stealth properties with re­
lease performance.
• Tighter control of particle size via extrusion or microfluidic techniques.
• Further stability studies at 4°C, room temperature, and physiological temperatures
are needed.
• Determination of a precise Tm.
• Explore higher drug­to­lipid ratios to find the maximum loading capacity of the lipo­
somal formulation.
Taken together, these findings indicate that the formulation is a viable platform for further
exploration in targeted neuromuscular drug delivery.

6
Conclusions
This thesis investigated the potential of thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs) as a delivery
platform for clenbuterol, a β2­adrenergic agonist of interest in the treatment of neuromus­
cular disorders. The primary aim was to evaluate the feasibility of encapsulating clen­
buterol using remote loading techniques and to assess the thermal release behaviour of
the resulting formulations under physiologically relevant temperature conditions.
Encapsulation efficiency and thermosensitive release measurements showed consider­
able variability, and repeated HPLC analyses produced inconsistently calculated concen­
trations, often far below expected values. Chromatographic peaks were sharp, retention
times were consistent, and system pressure remained stable, suggesting a possible fault
in the autosampler injection system rather than method­specific errors. These instru­
mental inconsistencies limited the ability to draw strong conclusions regarding absolute
encapsulation values and release percentages.
Despite these limitations, the thermal release data followed a clear temperature­dependent
pattern. Liposomes remained largely stable at 37°C and exhibited significantly increased
release at 42°C, consistent with the phase transition behaviour of DPPC­containing bi­
layers. Particle size and polydispersity index (PDI) remained within acceptable ranges
throughout, supporting the physical stability of the liposomal formulation under the tested
conditions.
In summary, this study demonstrates that thermosensitive liposomes are a viable can­
didate for heat­triggered release of clenbuterol, but technical improvements in analytical
precision are essential for robust evaluation of encapsulation and release performance.
The findings highlight both the promise and the challenges of developing TSL­based drug
delivery systems for localised therapy.
Further work should aim to (i) further optimize the remote loading protocol for clenbuterol;
(ii) evaluate the liposome composition to allow for a sharper release of drug when stimu­
lated while maintaining stability at physiological temperatures; (iii) resolve injection vari­
ability through improved HPLC system maintenance or validation; and (iiii) explore in vitro
or in vivo models to assess release kinetics and stability under physiological conditions.
