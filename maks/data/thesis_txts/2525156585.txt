Abstract
This thesis presents a model describing the heat dynamics of a house using
a grey-box model. The grey-box model combines physical knowledge and in-
formation contained in data. The dataset used to Ô¨Āt the model comes from an
experiment conducted at FlexHouse located at DTU Ris√ł campus. The model is
Ô¨Ātted using the R software package CTSM-R with a forward selection procedure
where the model complexity is increased iteratively. The parameters estimated
include building characteristics including thermal resistances and heat capac-
ities. The model parameters are validated by comparison to previously made
similar models and calculated physical values.

Chapter 1
Introduction
With the whole world increasingly focusing on sustainable energy consumption
the need for using the available energy eÔ¨Éciently has become ever more im-
portant. An aspect of everyday life where this eÔ¨Éciency has deÔ¨Ānite room for
improvement is the heat consumption of houses. Using utilities more sparingly
can lead to much more eÔ¨Écient energy consumption. In this paper a thermo-
statically controlled stochastic model is introduced. The aim of the model is to
be able to accurately describe and to a certain degree predict the heat dynamics
and temperature of a house.
The data used for the model building comes from experiments made at Flex-
House which is a part of the SYSLAB laboratory at the DTU Ris√ł campus.
The experimental data gathered at FLexHouse during this particular experi-
ment comprises of 5 diÔ¨Äerent experimental setups. The experimental setup and
design is discussed further in [Mad10a] while the experimental data from these
5 particular experiments is introduced in [Mad10b]. Of these 5 experiments, 2
include the use of a pseudorandom binary sequence (PRBS) signal to control the
heater output. The PRBS-signal is frequently used when estimating parameters
in systems of this kind. The PRBS-signal is used to try and create optimal
conditions for the parameter estimation.
The other 3 experiment setups in [Mad10b] consist of thermostatically controlled
output from the heaters. A temperature setpoint is chosen, in this case 20‚ó¶C,

and the heater output is determined by whether the measured indoor temper-
ature is above or below the temperature setpoint.
This experimental setup
is designed to imitate actual living conditions, where the indoor temperature
remains relatively constant.
The experimental data from the FlexHouse experiments has been used in previ-
ous work, including [Mad11]. In this paper, the PRBS-signal data was used to
identify models for the building‚Äôs heat dynamics. The models were determined
iteratively with a forward selection procedure where the starting model was a
simple single state model. The 3-state model and subsequent extensions thereof
resulted in reliable estimates.
Other work in this Ô¨Āeld includes [Par12]. In this Master‚Äôs thesis a PRBS-signal
experiment conducted in FlexHouse was also used. The models implemented in
this thesis were multiroom models where the system parameters were estimated
for each room seperately. Similarly, the model selection advanced iteratively
with a forward selection procedure. In this particular case, the 1-state model
was selected, as the model performance was not improved signiÔ¨Ācantly by adding
more states. The selected model was then used for a model predictive control
(MPC) analysis.
In the present thesis a new set of grey-box models are introduced, which are
suited for thermostatic controlled systems. Such models are important, since
most building heating systems are thermostatically controlled, hence for charac-
terization and model predictive control (MPC) such models are key components
[J√ł12]. The model is found by using a forward selection procedure in which the
model complexity is increased iteratively.
The model structure is built on previous work in the Ô¨Āeld including: [Par12]
where a multi-room model is Ô¨Ātted and used for economic model predictive
control (EMPC), [She19] where a similar model is Ô¨Ātted to data from a ther-
mostatically controlled experiment and [Mad11] where a models are Ô¨Ātted to
heating output controlled by a pseudorandom binary sequence (PRBS) signal.
A new feature of the present work is the application of the Sigmoid function for
modelling thermostatic control and a thorough analysis of the grey-box mod-
elling of heat dynamics of the FlexHouse, using the thermostatically controlled
experiments. The parameters are validated by comparison to previous work and
calculated physical values based on the physical properties of FlexHouse.

The integral part implemented in the models applied in the analysis is the use
of heat to steady the interior temperature of a house and thus the transfer of
energy from the heaters to the air inside the building.
Energy transfer by heat can be described as the interaction between a closed
system with its surroundings in a way that cannot be described as work, i.e.

4.1
Grey-box model
A grey-box model in its simplest sense is a combination of a black-box and a
white-box model.
A black-box model is based solely on statistical models and is viewed in terms
of its inputs and outputs. No prior knowledge is used and only the information
contained in the data is used to construct the model. The unfavourable char-
acteristic of black-box models is that the model and its parameters cannot be
directly interpreted as physical quantities.
A white-box model on the other hand makes use of physical knowledge of the
system. The governing equations of the system thus come from prior knowledge
of the dynamics of the system and the parameters can be directly interpreted.
A grey-box model, as the name suggests, combines the attributes of both of these
==PAGE:26==
18
System IdentiÔ¨Ācation
model types i.e. the model combines prior physical knowledge and information
found in the data. In this case, the physical knowledge of the system consists
of the heat dynamics of the building.

This section provides a detailed explanation of the model development process

5.1
Model selection procedure
When developing a model that provides the best Ô¨Āt to the data there are a few
diÔ¨Äerent methods to choose from. These include forward selection, backward
selection and a combination of both. The backward selection procedure involves
starting with a full model containing all candidate variables. The insigniÔ¨Ācant
variables are then iteratively removed using a model Ô¨Āt criterion, often the p-
value, until an adequate model is reached. A forward selection procedure starts
with a null model, the opposite of the backward selection procedure, which is
then extended by adding the variable that provides the most improvement in
the model Ô¨Āt. This process continues until the model Ô¨Āt does not improve when
adding an additional variable[MT11].
The selection procedure used for the model development in this paper is the
forward selection procedure. This method is preferred since it is more convenient
==PAGE:36==
28
Model development
to start with the simplest model and is the method that has been used in Ô¨Ātting
similar models previously (see e.g. [Mad11], [Hol95]).
The model development process thus starts with a single-state model which is
then extended upon by adding more states. The added state in each step of the
iteration process is the one that increases the likelihood of the model the most.
The increasingly complex model is then compared to the previous model with
a likelihood ratio test to determine if the larger model performs signiÔ¨Ācantly
better than the simpler model. The process is stopped once the more complex
model does not perform signiÔ¨Ācantly better than the previous model.

Chapter 7
Conclusion
The goal of this thesis was to Ô¨Āt a grey-box stochastic diÔ¨Äerential equation
model to a data set made from a thermostatically controlled experiment. The
data consisted of indoor temperature measurements as well as weather data,
including the ambient outdoor temperature and solar radiation. One of the ad-
ditions to the model was the use of a sigmoid function in order to accurately
describe the thermostatic control. The model was developed iteratively using
a forward selection procedure until acceptable results were obtained. The pa-
rameter estimates were validated by comparing them to previous work. The
physical properties of the house where the experiment was conducted were also
used to calculate estimates of the model parameters.
Chapter 2 introduced the experimental and weather data used for the model
Ô¨Ātting.
This included a discussion of the variables and a description of the
experimental setup.
Chapters 3 and 4 described the theory behind the model structure, both the
thermodynamic principles used in the model equations and the system identiÔ¨Ā-
cation methods.
Chapter 5 described the model selection procedure and Chapter 6 showed the
results of the selected model. The selected model was a relatively simple 2-
state model where the states represented the interior and envelope temperatures
==PAGE:66==
58
Conclusion
respectively. The model was shown to be suitable for prediction although some
of the estimated building characteristics were inaccurate. Ideas for improving
the estimates were then presented.
Overall the model describes the heat dynamics of the building well and produces
reasonable parameter estimates.
