ABSTRACT 
The use of fluorescent proteins (FPs) in lactic acid bacteria (LAB) has been considerably increasing 
in the last decades for potential applications, such as visualizing gene expression and studying strain 
competition. The behaviour of FPs in LAB is still uncertain, although it has been proved to be stable 
in LAB and to not influence the host fitness. Therefore, this project aimed to study the effect of the 
bacteriostatic chloramphenicol (Cm) on the growth of L. lactis strains to analyse the maturation of 
GFP and mCherry by optical density (OD) and flow cytometry. According to the results of the OD, 
the strains did not grow in presence of Cm, resulting in growth rate constant values between 0.0007 
and 0.0021, in comparison with the strains that did not have Cm added, which had a growth rate 
constant between 0.0056 and 0.0059. Regarding the flow cytometry data, the events detected from 
all strains did not vary over time, showing insignificant increment or decrease. The EGFP showed a 
maturation time of 114 minutes, whereas the mCherry showed a maturation time of 74 minutes. In 
conclusion, this study shows that OD measurements may provide more reliable results than flow 
cytometry in terms of bacterial growth. The application of flow cytometry to evaluate the 
fluorescence and the maturation time of the FPs might be relevant for an optimization of analysis of 
fluorescent bacteria, however, further studies are necessary to implement this technique.

1. INTRODUCTION  
 
1.1. 
Use of LAB in the food industry and research 
 
Lactic acid bacteria are safe microorganisms commonly used in the production of fermented and 
probiotic foods (Wessels et al., 2004).  Their use for preserving food and giving health benefits have 
been observed from many years since antiquity. These bacteria are the target of research not only 
because of their properties, but because they have been used extensively in genetic engineering 
providing excellent results (Fan et al., 2022; Ma et al., 2022; Zhou & Han, 2022). They grow under 
standard conditions, among other characteristics, they are aerotolerant, acid tolerant, acid lactic 
producers (König & Fröhlich, 2017). The most studied LAB with biotechnological interest is 
Lactobacillus spp., although other species belonging to Lactococcus and Enterococcus are also a focus of 
interest in the food industry (José M. Landete et al., 2015). The lactic acid bacteria Lactococcus lactis has 
been widely used for overexpression of homologous and heterologous proteins due to its potential 
as a host (Mierau et al., 2005; Morello et al., 2008; Song et al., 2017), using protein recombination 
techniques (Morello et al., 2008). In addition, this bacteria is of big interest because it is easy and 
cheap to grow (it grows at 30ºC, with a doubling time of 35-60 min (M. Gasson & Vos, 2012) and it 
is increasingly being used in modern biotechnological applications). It has contributed to genetic 
engineering tools development and its molecular characterization (Geertsma & Poolman, 2007; Kok 
et al., 2005; Kuipers et al., 2002; Mierau & Kleerebezem, 2005). 
 
1.2. 
Genetic engineering: the importance of reporter genes 
 
Genetic engineering has been used broadly in research, industry and medicine, among others, 
approaching tremendous advantages and new possibilities to improve our day-to-day lives (Stanley 
N Cohen & Goldstein, 2013). It can be used in a great range of plants, animals, and microorganisms, 
being bacteria the first organism genetically modified (S. N. Cohen et al., 1973). Since then, many 
approaches have been achieved, such as production of medical drugs and enzymes that processes 
food, to analyse gene function and creation of improved new food plants, also named as genetically 
modified (GM) food  (Ranjha et al., 2022; Tajima et al., 2022; Yali & Insti, 2022). One of the potential 
technologies more widely applied is deoxyribonucleic acid (DNA) cloning (also known as 
Recombinant DNA technology, RDT) in which a sequence or multiple sequences of DNA are 
copied can be copied , assembled in a plasmid and inserted into a bacteria. Thus, the bacteria can 
replicate the DNA sequence acquired and encode it into specific proteins of high interest. The arrival 
of RDT has brought remarkable changes in the lives of mankind, mainly by innovation and strategies 
to address improvement and benefits in health, food and environment (Wetzel, 1980). When it comes

to select the transformed bacteria, selective markers are commonly used for growing bacteria into 
specific conditions, such as antibiotic resistance genes or genes encoding enzymes such as Escherichia 
coli lacZ gene, these kind of enzymes can degrade specific substrates, like lactose, often used for 
prokaryotic regulation (Clark & Pazdernik, 2013). Alongside these, reporter genes for detecting 
transformed bacteria are also widely used due to the interest in marking and screening successfully 
transfected cells, as well as studying the regulation of gene expression (Phillips, 2001). Most used 
reporter genes that induce visually identifiable characteristics usually involve fluorescent and 
luminescent proteins, which can permit tracking bacterial cells in vivo and in vitro and provide valuable 
imaging (S. Li et al., 2018). Green fluorescent protein (GFP), for example, causes cells that express it 
to glow green under ultraviolet (UV) light. Yellow and red versions, such as Venus (Nagai et al., 2002) 
or mCherry (Mohedano et al., 2015), are also available, allowing the investigation of multiple genes 
at once (Stewart et al., 2009). 
The development of FPs and luciferases began with biochemical characterization studies in the 1970s 
(Gilad et al., 2007). Since then, FPs have made possible localize certain proteins in living organisms, 
tissues, and cells; follow protein dynamics and study gene expression, among others (Wu et al., 2013). 
The GFP, from the jellyfish (Aequorea victoria), has been known since the 1950s (Shimomura, 2005). 
Since their discovery, natural FPs found in marine organisms have been potentially improved in 
brightness, colour and photostability. Compared to synthetic fluorescent markers, FPs are non-toxic, 
non-destructive and minimally invasive method that does not requires exogenously added substrates 
or cofactors, which can also be genetically encoded to be produced by a cell itself  (Chalfie et al., 
1994; Spano et al., 2019). Its first cloning and expression was in Escherichia coli (Prasher et al., 1992) 
and since then, different applications of GFP-like FPs and their variants have been used and studied 
in a great range of bacterial species (Chalfie et al., 1994). Among all the applications, FPs have been 
used as biosensors systems to monitor intracellular physiological parameters, like the pH, the oxygen 
level or the antioxidant activity (Crone et al., 2013; Germond et al., 2016; Potzkei et al., 2012). As 
reporters of the gene expression and transcription regulation (Chalfie et al., 1994; Mohedano et al., 
2015; Ruiz-Cruz et al., 2010; Uliczka et al., 2011; Webb et al., 1995). As biomarkers for imaging 
subcellular location of fusion proteins and their targets (Belardinelli & Jackson, 2017; Kapanidis et 
al., 2018; Phillips, 2001; Webb et al., 1995). For direct detection and quantification of plasmid 
conjugation and plasmid loss (Bahl et al., 2004; Karimi et al., 2016; Nancharaiah et al., 2003; Sørensen 
et al., 2003), and for tracking bacterial cells in vivo and  in vitro competitions and interactomic studies 
(Campbell-Valois & Sansonetti, 2014; Jarchum, 2015; Russo et al., 2015; Singer et al., 2010).

1.3. 
Understanding the maturation of FPs 
 
A typical FP is a globular protein with a 11-stranded β-barrel structure that encircles a central α-helix 
containing the chromophore (e.g., Figure 1b,c) (Miyawaki et al., 2012; Thorn, 2017; Zimmer, 2002a). 
The mature form of the FP is generated by a spontaneous self-modification process that is essentially 
irreversible (Zhang et al., 2006). Numerous studies have aimed at establishing the mechanism of the 
chromophore formation, until it was proved that in different marine organisms the formation of 
chromophores in FPs follows an identical path (Pletnev et al., 2010). The newly synthesized FP 
polypeptide has to undergo a maturation process folding into the β-barrel and forming the 
chromophore (Figure 1a) by an autocatalytic mechanism that only requires oxygen (usually the rate-
limiting step), consisting of successive cyclisation, oxidation and dehydration reactions within a 
tripeptide located in the central α-helix (Barondeau et al., 2003; Miyawaki et al., 2012; Zimmer, 
2002b). There are no known cofactors or enzymatic components required for this autocatalytic 
process that involves catalysis by specific amino acid residues present in all FPs (Pletnev et al., 2010). 
Therefore, the maturation process is generated in an O2-dependent manner (B. Li et al., 2012; 
Titushin et al., 2012), and oxygen is the only limiting factor. Oxygen is needed to dehydrogenate the 
α,β bond of residue 66, and residues 65-67 are the ones that become the chromophore (Heim et al., 
1994; Inouye & Tsuji, 1994; Reid & Flynn, 1997; Kent et al., 2008). Scott et al., 1998 reported that 
LAB can grow anaerobically with no fluorescence but were able to fluoresce upon subsequent 
exposure to air, which is consistent with the nature of the FP. 
 
1.4. 
FP variants  
 
New variants of the original FPs (mainly from GFP) have been engineered to increase the range of 
potential application of the FPs, showing alterations in their oligomeric state or in their intracellular 
stability, producing different emission colors and improving brightness, photostability or maturation 
rates (Spano et al., 2019). A wide range of plasmids encoding a great variety of FPs were constructed 
and transform in Gram-negative bacteria, where these proteins have been extensively characterized 
in order to find the optimal conditions for their in vivo and in vitro applications (Barbier & Damron, 
2016; Singer et al., 2010). Red fluorescent variants of the wild-type (wt) GFP have been obtained by 
substituting Ser65 for Ala, Gly, Ile, Thr, or Cys (Cormack et al., 1996). Among them, GFPmut1 (also 
termed enhanced green fluorescent protein, EGFP, Figure 1a), which contains both the S65T and 
F64L mutations resulting in a red-shifted excitation GFP with increased brightness and protein 
solubility, was early constructed and has been widely used (Miller & Lindow, 1997). Therefore, GFP 
variants can be combined with red FPs (Figure 1c), like DsRed from Indo-Pacific reef coral Discosoma 
sp. and its improved monomeric variant mCherry (Shaner et al., 2004; Singer et al., 2010), for 
simultaneous fluorescent tagging of bacterial cells (Doherty et al., 2010; Nancharaiah et al., 2003). An 
==PAGE:8==
Alba Esteban s202252                                                                             Master thesis – Spring 2022

attractive property of some RFPs is that their red fluorescence can be turned on or modulated by 
illumination at specific wavelengths (Miyawaki et al., 2012). Other variants, such as an improved 
version of the YFP named “Venus”, have been developed by mutagenesis, containing also the novel 
mutation F46L, which at 37ºC greatly accelerates oxidation of the chromophore and the FP have 
higher resistance to environment (Nagai et al., 2002). Apparently, GFP variants and mCherry have 
been tested for optimal or sporulation-stage-specific fluorescent labeling of the Gram-positive 
bacteria Bacillus subtilis (Doherty et al., 2010; Overkamp et al., 2013). The combination of these two 
FPs has been also used for cell tracking and monitoring gene expression in Gram-positive bacteria 
belonging to the Bifidobacterium genus or to the heterogeneous group of LAB, which are commonly 
employed in the fermentation food industry or as probiotics (José Ma Landete et al., 2016).

1.5. 
The use of FPs in LAB 
 
A main drawback of using GFP-like FPs in bifidobacteria and LAB, which are generally cultivated 
under low-aeration or anaerobic conditions and produce lactic acid, is that the formation of the 
fluorophore depends on the presence of oxygen and its activity is sensitive to low pH (Doherty et al., 
2010; Pérez-Arellano & Pérez-Martínez, 2003; Shinoda et al., 2018). Thus, neutralization and/or 
aeration subsequent to bacterial growth may be required for detecting and quantifying the 
==PAGE:9==
Alba Esteban s202252                                                                             Master thesis – Spring 2022 
9 
 
fluorescence emission in bacteria (Fernández De Palencia et al., 2000). Apart from this, very few 
studies have been performed to study the features of the FPs variants in LAB and there is a general 
lack of information relative to the maturation rate or to the intracellular stability of the FPs in these 
bacteria. These characteristics can change depending on the external conditions and the genetic 
background, which have been disregarded. 
Spano et al., 2019 studied the maturation and stability of both EGFP (a variant of GFP with S65T 
and F64L mutations) and mCherry in L. lactis at 30ºC, using Varioskan Flash system (Thermo Fisher 
Scientific, Waltham,MA, United States), which is an instrument that measures absorbance and 
fluorescence intensity (Spano et al., 2019). They proved that EGFP could take 0.56 hours to mature, 
whereas mCherry needed 2.88 hours to get fluorescent (Spano et al., 2019). Similar studies using 
Escherichia coli, also demonstrate that there was required longer time for maturation of mCherry 
compared to EGFP, which has been attributed to a second slow oxidation step occurring during 
formation of the red FP chromophore (Balleza et al., 2017a; Hebisch et al., 2013). Other studies 
suggested that GFP matures after 46 min using a purified preparation of GFP-trix derived from 
inclusion bodies by using a fluorimeter (Zhang et al., 2006), and 120 min for wt GFP and 27 min for 
GFP-S65T in transformed Escherichia coli by fluorescence resonance energy transfer (Heim et al., 1994; 
Kilgard et al., 1995). Spano et al., 2019 used L. lactis MG1353 host to study how different plasmid 
constructs with GFP and mCherry genes behave, obtaining two similar plasmid constructs containing 
mainly pMV158 replicon, maltose operon transcriptional repressor (malR, a pneumococcal 
chromosomal gene encoding MalR protein that represses transcription from promoter PM in absence 
of maltose) and Ery resistant gene (Spano et al., 2019). They concluded that the synthesis of the FPs 
encoded by these plasmids did not significantly decrease the host fitness and by applying inducible 
expression vectors, the FPs showed to be very stable in L. lactis (Spano et al., 2019). Therefore, 
encoding FPs does barely affect the normal metabolism of the host (L. lactis). Apart from that, the 
growth of this bacteria containing these plasmids proved that fluorescent proteins can be encoded in 
presence of maltose, as an example of a bacterial regulator protein (Goda et al., 1998). 
Many studies have used red and green FPs simultaneously to distinguish between two different cells 
or groups of cells with each color (Hoffman, 2015; Hoffman & Bouvet, 2016; Platonova et al., 2015) 
and, furthermore, among LAB, José M. Landete et al., 2015 reported that L. lactis showed the highest 
rate of fluorescence when transformed with a plasmid containing the fluorescent gene. Other studies 
obtained successfully L. lactis fluorescent by transformation with fruitful imaging (García-cayuela et 
al., 2012; Geoffroy et al., 2000; Scott et al., 2000; Scott y et al., 1998), used GFP and mCherry for 
tracking LAB (Fernández De Palencia et al., 2000; José Ma Landete et al., 2016; Mohedano et al., 
2015), proved its use in cheese (De Palencia et al., 2004) and even in fermented meat products (Gory 
et al., 2001). However, little is known about the behavior of fluorescent proteins in LAB, and when 
it comes to finding the best marker for practical applications, it is required a detailed study and 
==PAGE:10==
Alba Esteban s202252                                                                             Master thesis – Spring 2022 
10 
 
comparative tests of FPs, where it is important to consider the influence of main environmental 
factors (José Ma Landete et al., 2016).  
There is clear evidence that information about the behaviour of FPs is uncertain and imprecise, which 
is relevant for interesting studies when it comes to monitor gene expression and strain competition 
visually, especially in LAB because of its interest in food industry and research. However, some 
techniques such as flow cytometry is proved to give specific detailed and valuable data of how 
fluorescence performs in LAB, as well as other features. This project aimed to study the behaviour 
of two known and well-studied FPs (EGFP and mCherry) in L. lactis using the strains containing 
pLS1ROM-GFP and pLS1ROM-mCherry, under selective conditions, monitoring their growth and 
their fluorescence using the flow cytometer and measuring their optical density. Accordingly, further 
approaches can be considered for implementing FPs in LAB and a better understanding of how 
useful flow cytometry can be for monitoring in a matter of minutes either fluorescence or bacterial 
growth, among other possible features.

2. MATERIAL AND METHODS 
 
2.1. 
Bacterial strains and growth conditions  
 
The bacterial strains used in this project are the free-plasmid L. lactis subsp. cremoris MG1363 (M. J. 
Gasson et al., 1983) as control and two strains (also L. lactis subsp. cremoris MG1363) containing the 
plasmid pLS1ROM-GFP and pLS1ROM-mCherry respectively obtained from the library of strains 
from Molecular Microbiology and Infection Biology Department (Centro de Investigaciones 
Biológicas, Consejo Superior de Investigaciones Científicas, Madrid, Spain). Plasmid constructions 
used in this study and their main features are listed in Table 1, and represented in Figure 2. Lactococcus 
lactis strains containing  pLS1ROM-GFP and pLS1ROM-mCherry were chosen because they have 
been tested previously, showing interesting characteristics (Spano et al., 2019).  
All strains, either containing plasmid or not, were cultivated in M17 broth medium (a mix of 
polypeptone, phytone peptone, yeast extract, beef extract, lactose, ascorbic acid, β-disodium 
glycerophosphate and MgSO4, (Terzaghi & Sandine, 1975) supplemented with 4% maltose (M17M) 
at 30ºC for 2 days in tubes of 15 mL, without aeration nor shaking. For those containing either 
pLS1ROM-GFP or pLS1ROM-mCherry, erythromycin (5µl/mL) was added for optimal induction 
conditions. Growth was monitored with a spectrophotometer (Novaspec Pro, Fisher Scientific) by 
checking OD at 600nm for all cultures. Absorbance and fluorescence of the samples were measured 
in a flow cytometer (MACSQuant® VYB, Miltenyi Biotec), as well as their growth by counting the 
number of events per mL represented as number of cells (illustration in Figure 4 ).

2.2. 
Estimation of the growth rate of L. lactis by Optical Density  
 
Bacterial cells were grown in the appropriate selective media (see section Bacterial strains and growth 
conditions). After 2 days of incubation, each culture was diluted 1:10 in three replicates to an OD of 
0.2~0.3 in the same selective media in tubes of 15 mL and kept at room temperature (18ºC). The 
antibiotic chloramphenicol (Cm) (5µl/mL) was added to two of the three replicates from each strain 
(named as R1 and R2, Table 2), leaving one as control for each strain. Bacterial growth was 
monitored with a spectrophotometer by checking OD at 600nm for all samples every 15-30 minutes 
for 4 hours, starting the first analysis once the strains were kept at room temperature and Cm was 
added at two of the three replicates (R1 and R2). An amount of 1 mL of each sample was transferred 
into a cuvette for spectrophotometry analysis.  This time span between analysis has been set since 
there is a required and unspecific time of maturation for the fluorescence proteins GFP and mCherry, 
being the last one the longest to mature. The bacterial growth rate constant (µ) was calculated during 
the lag and the exponential growth phases, using the following equation: 
ln(ODt / OD0) = µ (t − t0) 
    
                     (1) 
where the ODt and OD0 correspond to the optical density of the cultures at the times t and t0, 
respectively, and µ is the slope of the linear regression that fits in the plot of the values ln(ODt / OD0) 
against the incubation time (t). The same equation can also be interpreted as:

2.3. 
Estimation of the bacterial growth and FPs maturation times in L. lactis by 
Flow cytometry 
 
The strains containing pLS1ROM-GFP and pLS1ROM-mCherry were grown in the appropriate 
selective media (see section Bacterial strains and growth conditions). This media was chosen because 
these two strains contain the gene malR encoding for MalR protein that, in absence of maltose, 
represses transcription from promoters PM, leading to non-fluorescent cells. Accordingly, each 
culture was diluted 1:10 in three replicates to an OD of 0.2~0.3 in the same selective media and kept 
at room temperature (18ºC). The first analysis was performed once the strains were kept at room 
temperature and Cm was added to two of the three replicates (R1 and R2). For each analysis 
performed every 30-40 minutes, an aliquot of each culture was diluted with PBS in a 96-well plate. 
The bacterial growth was monitored associating the number of events detected as probable cells. 
These events were detected by the flow cytometer, which collects forward scatter light (FSC) and 
side scatter light (SSC) of the particles flowing throughout the instrument. The number of fluorescent 
cells for GFP and mCherry were analysed also with the flow cytometer by detecting the number of 
fluorescent cells considered as probable cells. The instrument provides with three internal lasers of 
different wavelengths: 405 nm, 488 nm and 561 nm. It also has different filters for detecting different 
fluorescence: 525/50 nm for green fluorescence and 615/20 nm for red fluorescence. 
 
2.4. 
Data analysis 
 
Data obtained from the flow cytometer (see section Bacterial strains and growth conditions) was 
exported from MACSquantify™ Software (v2.11 MACSQuant® VYB, Miltenyi Biotec) to Microsoft 
Excel. Data analysis from both spectrophotometer and flow cytometer were carried out in Microsoft 
Excel. Graphic designs were performed both also in Microsoft Excel and in GraphPad Software 
(v9.3.1. San Diego, CA, United States).

3. RESULTS 
 
3.1. 
Effect of the presence of Chloramphenicol on bacterial growth by Optical 
Density 
 
To evaluate the effect of Cm (bacteriostatic agent) on the growth of different L. lactis strains, the 
bacterial cells were grown in the appropriate selective media (see section Bacterial strains and growth 
conditions) at 30ºC for 2 days. During incubation, the samples were not shaken nor with aeration. 
To assess the growth of all samples, the OD was measured over 4 hours and the growth curve of all 
samples were calculated (Figure 3). The different trending lines from the different strains were 
compared. The strains containing the plasmid pLS1ROM-GFP or pLS1ROM-mCherry were 
incubated before and during the analysis under selective pressure in presence of Ery, since they are 
Ery resistant, so it can be ensured that the bacteria grown in this selective media still contain the 
plasmid (meaning that all cells might contain the fluorescent gene). 
As an overview, it can be observed that all strains grow at 18ºC over 4 hours at different rates (Table 
2).  The strains that had Cm added barely grew, while the strains which did not have Cm in their 
media grew considerably (Figure 3). The samples in presence of Cm had a low growth rate constant 
(µ) of 0.0007-0.0021, whereas the ones without it had a µ of up to 8 times greater (0.0056 – 0.0059). 
Among the samples in presence of Cm (named as “R1 + Cm” and “R2 + Cm” shown in Figure 3), 
there is a very similar growth rate constant, with the exception of one replicate of pLS1ROM-GFP 
(R1) that had a µ of 0.0021, resulting in an average of 0.0012 approximately. On the other hand, 
among the samples which did not have Cm added (control samples), had also a very similar growth 
rate constant, reaching the limit of the OD detection after 4 hours of analysis with no dilution in 
between (OD≈1). In addition, it can be observed that one of the replicates of the strain PLS1ROM-
GFP with Cm added presented an OD of 0.12, which is much lower than the other samples that also 
contained Cm in their media.

Estimation of the bacterial growth and FPs maturation times in L. lactis by 
Flow cytometry 
 
In order to monitor the bacterial growth, the strains of L. lactis containing PLS1ROM-GFP and 
PLS1ROM-mCherry were grown in selective media (see section Bacterial strains and growth 
conditions) at 30ºC for 2 days. The first analysis was performed once the strains were kept at room 
temperature and Cm was added to two of the three replicates (R1 and R2). The bacterial growth and 
the number of fluorescent cells were analysed with the flow cytometer by counting the number of 
events that correspond to the number of cells (according to their FSC and SSC) and counting which 
of them show fluorescence (illustration of the selected events and fluorescent events is presented in 
Figure 4). 
The number of events detected as probable cells from the total number of events detected are selected 
with a specific area (Figure 4a) and described as percentage. They are displayed in a plot of forward 
versus side scatter (two-dimensional density plot). The FSC refers to size of the events and the SSC 
refers to complexity of the events.  From this amount selected, the number of events that emit 
fluorescence are detected at different wavelengths and presented as percentage of fluorescent events 
(Figure 4b, c) from the total of the area preselected as number of probable cells. The graphs 
extracted from the flow cytometer software can visually differentiate between red fluorescent events, 
green-fluorescent events and non-fluorescent events. The samples chosen for the graph 
representation are the strain containing pLS1ROM-mCherry (R2) at minute 152 (Figure 4a, b) and 
the strain containing pLS1ROM-GFP (R2) at minute 152 as well (Figure 4c). The percentage of 
events detected as cells from the strain pLS1ROM-mCherry (R2) is 97.97%, named or categorized as 
“cells”. The percentage of red fluorescent events from this sample is 88.76%, although a percentage 
of 2.81% green-fluorescent events have been also detected. The rest (3.37%) are represented as non-
fluorescent events. For the sample pLS1ROM-GFP (R2), a percentage of 75.16% green-fluorescent 
events was detected. However, a percentage of 0.14% (quite less compared with the sample 
pLS1ROM-mCherry [R2]) have been detected as red fluorescent events. The rest (1.96%) are non-
fluorescent events. The pattern of showing a small % of fluorescence either for green or for red 
fluorescence has been observed in all samples. The area between -1 and 1 from the X and Y axis 
(Figure 4b, c) have been discarded, as they are considered as “noise”, dust or particles that do not 
represent cells. 
To visualize and monitor the bacterial growth using the flow cytometer, the number of events have 
been detected, in which some of them have been presented as probable cells and named as count

change in count/mL over time after 4 hours, in other words, there is no growth observed in any of

4. DISCUSSION 
During the last two decades, there has been a notorious increment of the use of vectors in LAB as 
genetic tools for gene expression and for tracking bacterial cells, even for imaging and detecting cells 
by fluorescence emission. However, there is still a general lack of information about the behaviour 
of fluorescent proteins once transformed in bacteria (stability, maturation time, intensity, etc.), 
conditioning their potential applications. The maturation kinetics and intracellular stabilities of the 
most common FPs (such as EGFP and mCherry used in this project) are still missing in most LAB, 
apart from being influenced by the intracellular environment and the external conditions (such as 
oxygen viability, temperature, etc.) (Balleza et al., 2017a; Hebisch et al., 2013). This is critical for the 
use and optimization of biomarker proteins, since it is crucial for reliable results and avoiding 
mistaken qualification and quantification of emitted fluorescent bacteria. Fluorescent labelled LAB 
has been previously analysed by fluorescence microscopy, flow cytometry and fluorometer (Holm & 
Jespersen, 2003; Karimi et al., 2016; José M. Landete et al., 2015), although few studies have been 
reported using flow cytometry in LAB for detection and quantification of fluorescence (Holm & 
Jespersen, 2003; Karimi et al., 2016). In this project, two strains of L. lactis, which contained 
pLS1ROM-GFP and PLS1ROM-mCherry were tested using flow cytometry. In order to induce 
expression of the FP genes, the strains were grown in M17 supplemented with maltose at 30ºC 
without aeration, as they contain the transcriptional repressor MalR (in absence of maltose it inhibits 
the ability of encoding FP). In addition, they were exposed to Cm to stop the growth and track how 
the FPs matured over time. To monitor the growth of the bacteria, both OD and flow cytometry was 
used.  These two strains have been previously studied by Spano et al., 2019 using other techniques 
(fluorescence microscopy and a microplate plate reader), providing their segregational stability, PCN, 
the effect of the vectors on host fitness and fluorescence intensity. It was proved that these two 
vectors had very low effect on the host fitness and the fluorescent proteins were very stable in this 
bacterium (Spano et al., 2019). Same as other studies, the GFP takes shorter time to mature compared 
to the mCherry (Balleza et al., 2017b; Spano et al., 2019). Based on the data of the two strains chosen 
for this study, EGFP required 0.56 hours to mature, whereas mCherry needed 2.88 hours to matures, 
in other words, until 3 times more than EGFP to mature (Spano et al., 2019). 
Based on the data obtained from the OD analysis(Figure 3, Table 2), it has been observed that OD 
measurement has been performed successfully, proving that the exposure of Cm stopped the growth 
of all strains, including the free-plasmid, since the OD barely change during 4 hours (only an 
increment of the OD of 0.1 approximately in all the cultures with Cm added). This was required for 
ensuring a proper analysis of the fluorescence maintaining a same number of cells, so the maturation 
time could be tracked. Although the samples were stored at 18ºC (being 30ºC the temperature of 
optimal growth for L. lactis), they strains not exposed to Cm kept growing quite fast during 4 hours, 
reaching an OD of 1 after 4 hours. 
==PAGE:21==
Alba Esteban s202252                                                                             Master thesis – Spring 2022 
21 
 
On the other hand, based on the analysis using flow cytometry to monitor the bacterial growth and 
the fluorescence emission(Figure 5, Figure 6), any of the strains did grow during the 4 hours of 
analysis. Even the strains that were not exposed to Cm have not been detected growing. Apart from 
that, the free-plasmid L. lactis strain showed a strange increment of 2 logs only after 29 minutes, which 
cannot be related as growth since the growth time for L. lactis requires from 35 minutes to 60 minutes 
(1 log of increment in at least 30 minutes at 30ºC). This might have occurred due to the few events 
detected in all samples (< 3,000 events in all samples), or the small amount of sample taken from the 
culture before diluting in the 96-well plate, which was quite small taking into account the whole 
culture. However, a great number of events were detected as cells from all samples, compared with 
the total of events detected, reaching between 80 and 99% in almost all analysis (not shown in 
Figures). 
Regarding the fluorescence of the two fluorescent labelled L. lactis strains (illustration of the selection 
of cells and fluorescence displayed in Figure 4), fluorescent events were successfully detected and 
differentiated by the flow cytometer. As expected, the fluorescence increased over time in all samples 
at different times, although the red labelled strain which did not have Cm added show a decrease 
after the 2 hours. As expected, the L. lactis strains that were not exposed to Cm showed slower 
increment of the fluorescence in comparison with the ones that had Cm added. This occurred because 
the bacterial growth stopped after the exposure of the Cm, so only the cells remained could produce 
the FP and let it mature over time. Compared to previous studies, the EGFP took longer to mature 
than mCherry, which was not expected from the beginning, although the number of replicates might 
have not been enough to have this outcome as conclusive data for stating that mCherry can mature 
faster than EGFP. Furthermore, the increment in fluorescence during the first hour occurred faster 
in mCherry (from 10% to almost 90%) than in EGFP (from around 50% to 60-90%). The reason 
why this could have happened could be because the area selected for both red and green fluorescence 
could have rejected events with low fluorescence, leading to a low % of fluorescent events from the 
beginning, as not all the FPs have matured yet. The decrease of red fluorescence in the straining 
containing pLS1ROM-mCherry without Cm could have been occurred because the culture kept 
growing during the analysis (as it was not forced to stop the growth by adding Cm), so the % of 
fluorescent events are lower as the FP requires minimum time to mature for fluorescent emission. 
Furthermore, L. lactis  is aerotolerant, so it can keep growing under anaerobic conditions, leading to 
an anoxic environment. Thus, the mCherry would need more time to mature, or it might not finally 
mature because of the lack of oxygen after some time. 
In addition, as observed in Figure 4, red fluorescent strains presented few green fluorescent events, 
as well as the green fluorescent strains with few red fluorescent events detected. This could have 
occurred by possible cross-contamination of samples or by detecting events in between red and green 
fluorescence close to the X=1 and Y=1 point, among other possible causes. 
==PAGE:22==
Alba Esteban s202252                                                                             Master thesis – Spring 2022 
22 
 
For further perspective, more replicates might be needed for obtaining more conclusive or reliable 
data regarding the maturation time of EGFP and mCherry. Additionally, less diluted samples could 
also be considered when measuring in the flow cytometer, so greater number of events could be 
detected, and a more accurate number of events could be observed for monitoring both bacterial 
growth and fluorescence emission. Accordingly, this project could be of relevant importance when 
analysing fluorescent LAB with flow cytometry for a better understanding and analysis or 
experimental design optimization. Furthermore, although flow cytometry is a fast technique for 
analysing fluorescence, it takes around 2-3 minutes analysis per samples, meaning that the more 
samples are used, the more time is required for monitoring growth or increment of any feature. This 
explains the reason why the fluorescence was analysed every 30-40 minutes. However, the maximum 
fluorescence has been successfully measured by flow cytometry, showing great potential for using 
fluorescence as a biomarker in LAB for many purposes, such as detecting gene expression, 
differentiate different strains in a same culture by using different FPs and for using a rapid technique 
to  verify fluorescence and other features at the same time (cell size, fluorescence intensity, etc.).

5. CONCLUSION 
Marking LAB with fluorescence is an incredible tool for tracking cells and differentiate among 
different strains. It can be useful for in vitro and in vivo studies, such as imaging biofilm formation, 
studying strains competition, visualizing survival cells under stress conditions, etc. This project 
showed two different outcomes by implementing two different techniques, in which the OD, 
although being an old-fashioned method, seems to give more reliable data than flow cytometry in 
terms of bacterial growth. The addition of Cm has proved to stop the growth of these two L. lactis  
strains containing pLS1ROM-GFP and pL1SROM-mCherry, which can be relevant for further 
studies of the FPs behaviour either in L. lactis strains or other bacteria with a different selective marker 
to ensure a proper monitoring of the fluorescence. Fluorescence has been detected at the same time 
using flow cytometry, which is an enormous advantage in research. However, the exportation and 
the management of the data obtained has taken longer than expected due to the sophisticated 
software of the flow cytometer. The maturation time of the two FPs (EGFP and mCherry) has been 
calculated based on the results obtained. The results must be interpreted carefully, since not enough 
replicates were used to be able to provide the actual maturation time of the two FPs studied, as fewer 
replicates were used in the project. It must be considered the experimental design and the sampling 
process for obtaining more accurate and reliable data. This knowledge might be crucial for making 
an optimized analysis of fluorescent bacteria using flow cytometry.
