ABSTRACT
The infrastructure sector plays a central role
in climate change, with CO₂-intensive
materials and processes contributing
substantially to global emissions. In
response, municipalities and developers are
increasingly seeking data-driven
approaches to quantify emissions and
identify reduction pathways for decision-
making processes. This study develops a
framework to quantify material flows and
CO₂ emissions across infrastructure
projects, and assess reduction actions by
balancing reduction potentials, cost-
effectiveness, and feasibility of
implementation, applying material flow
analysis (MFA), life cycle assessment (LCA),
and life cycle cost (LCC) methodologies.
This framework is applied to the Technical
and Environmental Administration (TMF) of
Copenhagen Municipality, which has set a
30% CO₂ reduction target by 2028
according to current levels. By analyzing
tender submission data from 37
infrastructure projects, the study estimates
TMF’s portfolio emissions and identifies
potential reduction pathways. Key findings
reveal an annual emission of 6,500 tCO₂
from roads, bicycle lanes, and pavements
and highlight the effectiveness of targeting
asphalt, concrete foundations, concrete
pavement slabs, and fuel source for road-
going machinery.
In total, 13 reduction actions was
identified and assessed, with purposed
actions for immediate piloting and
implementation including: FutureCem
for concrete foundation and pavement
applications, bitumen-stabilized
material (BSM-KMA) for asphalt base
layers, and HVO-UCO for road-going
machinery. Although implementing
these reduction actions presents a viable
pathway to achieve TMF's reduction
target by reducing annual emissions by
39%, theory and reality often diverge. 
While reduction potentials are
quantified based on EPD data and LCA
modelling, real-world uncertainties and
risks, such as unforeseen costs, model
assumptions, and implementation
challenges, can negate cost-
effectiveness and realized CO₂
reductions. To bridge the gap between
reduction potentials and realized
reductions, the study discusses and
suggests the use of a CO₂/DKK KPI to
baseline current emissions and monitor
realized reductions post-implementation
of reduction actions. Ultimately, this
study provides a data-driven framework
for assessing and reducing CO₂
emissions from TMF's portfolio, while
contributing to a broader development
of sustainable urban infrastructure.

Introduction
Amidst the global climate crisis, reducing greenhouse gas (GHG) emissions has become a central
agenda for governments, industries, and individuals around the world. This urgency has been pro-
pelled by numerous international agreements, such as the Kyoto Protocol, 1997 and Paris Agreement,
2015, and reports from the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC, 2023). The message
is loud and clear, calling for a rapid decarbonization across all branches of society. According to
major reports from the United Nations Environment Programme (UNEP, 2023) and World Green
Building Council (WGBC, 2019), the built environment, referring to building and infrastructure ac-
tivities, is responsible for approximately 37-39% of global CO2 emissions, giving it a vital role to play
in responding to this urgency.
Figure 1: Global share of buildings and construction operational and process CO2 emissions (UNEP, 2023)
While reports have primarily focused on the role of emissions from operating buildings (operational
carbon), the contribution of the physical materials (embodied carbon) from constructing infrastruc-
ture, such as roads, bridges, tunnels, and urban spaces, has not been explicitly isolated from the built
environment. However, considering the similar material consumption and reliance on CO2-intensive
construction materials, such as concrete, asphalt, granite, and steel (Ortlepp et al., 2015; Tredje Natur,
2024), the infrastructure sector is an inherent part of the problem, and thus, holds a responsibility for
reducing emissions.
1.1
The importance of data-driven decision-making
There is no denying that embodied carbon presents, yet, another complex problem, making confi-
dent and data-driven decision-making inherently more important than ever before (Diván, 2015; Jia
et al., 2015; Porto de Albuquerque et al., 2021; Szukits & Móricz, 2024). And while life-cycle thinking
methodologies like life cycle assessment (LCA) and material flow analysis (MFA) have been widely
recognized as fundamental approaches for quantitative sustainability assessments (QSA) (Prysh-

of data, as well as bridging the gap between data and decision-makers (UNEP, 2023). As highlighted
by UNEP, 2023, challenges remain for a systematic approach of reducing emissions from embodied
carbon. These include the lack of a standardized baseline for assessing embodied carbon, such as

Aiming to address these challenges and provide decision-makers with transparent and third-party

While these tools aim to provide decision-makers with the necessary environmental data for assess-
ing trade-offs and decarbonize the built environment, challenges still remain to baseline the assess-

1.2
TMF’s role in sustainable infrastructure development
As one of the major Danish infrastructure developers, the Technical and Environmental Administra-
tion (da: Teknik- og Miljøforvaltningen, TMF) is the responsible administration for planning, construct-
ing, and maintaining Copenhagen Municipality’s infrastructure, representing the urban context of
embodied carbon. TMF administrates approximately 550 active projects throughout the year, with
an annual budget of 1 billion DKK. Projects include roads, pavements, bridges, tunnels, and rainwa-
ter management, typically referred to as "everything in-between buildings". Adhering to the Danish
Climate Act, 2021, and Copenhagen’s efforts of achieving carbon neutrality by the current year of
2025 (CPH2025, 2022), TMF has worked towards building a capacity to participate in data collec-
tion and incorporating QSA into its decision-making processes. Recognizing its role in sustainable
infrastructure development, TMF has established several sustainable initiatives including:
• TMF’s use of internal reuse and recycling facilities, namely storage facility at Selinevej and
KMC Nordhavn. Initially established due to an economic incentive, but has proven to be a
major key for reducing CO2 emissions across TMF’s portfolio. These include reusing granite
pavement elements at Selinevej and crushing asphalt and concrete waste into road filling at
KMC Nordhavn.
• Building capacity by in-sourcing a sustainability team to incorporate QSA into decision-making
processes and set sustainable strategies. This has led to a 30% CO2 reduction target for its
projects by 2028 according to current levels.
• TMF has mandated the use of 2nd-generation biofuel, namely HVO-100, within construction
sites, with a long-term goal of full electrification of construction activities.
• A critical evaluation of TMF’s technical guidelines and standards is currently underway to
incorporate sustainable practices across TMF’s portfolio.
• Recently mandated LCA to be conducted for all projects, currently working on establishing
data-mechanisms to support this requirement.
For the past 3 years, TMF’s work with LCA has been at a project level aiming to reduce CO2 emissions
from it’s projects exceeding 20 million DKK. This has provided valuable insights into CO2 emission
trends, the possibilities of LCA data, and how it can used to describe the general portfolio of TMF’s
projects. This has led to a need of establishing a baseline for benchmarking emissions, as well as
efficiently reducing emissions from its portfolio, ultimately, leading to the commissioning of this
study.

1.3
Goal of study
The goal of this study is to expand TMF’s current sustainability efforts by establishing a data-driven
approach that connects existing data flows with a holistic assessment of material streams and CO2
emissions across TMF’s infrastructure portfolio. Following insights from this, the study aims to pro-
vide a structured framework to identify and evaluate reduction potential, cost-effectiveness, and
implementation feasibility of multiple reduction pathways (reduction actions) for achieving TMF’s
reduction targets. This study addresses the following research question:
"How can a data-driven framework be established to quantify material streams and CO2 emissions across
TMF’s infrastructure portfolio and assess cost-effective pathways to achieve its 30% CO2 reduction target by
2028 and beyond?"
To address the research question, this study is structured into two parts, each with distinct but inter-
related objectives.
Portfolio mapping and hotspot analysis:
• Collect data on contractor services, processes, material use, cradles, and graves from recent
infrastructure projects within TMF’s project portfolio.
• Conduct MFAs on collected projects to gain an overview of material streams around construc-
tion sites and identify current waste handling practices.
• Conduct LCA on collected projects to quantify material specific and life cycle stage emissions.
• Establish an approach of estimating annual material streams and emissions of TMF’s project
portfolio.
• Assess MFA and LCA results to identify the most efficient pathways for reducing emissions.
• Establish and suggest a data-driven approach to baseline portfolio emissions.
Evaluation of reduction pathways:
• Evaluate emerging material and process-oriented innovations (reduction actions) for identified
reduction pathways.
• Conduct LCA and techno-economic analysis to assess the reduction potential and cost-effectiveness
of identified reduction actions.
• Critically assess obstacles and risks to evaluate the feasibility of reduction actions.
• Establish and suggest a communicable approach of bringing results to decision makers.

1.4
Scope of study
This study focuses on identifying reduction pathways and evaluating their corresponding reduction
actions with the core purpose of reducing environmental impacts from TMF’s infrastructure projects.
However, several aspects fall outside the scope of this study:
• Implementation of reduction actions: This study focuses on the identification and evaluation
of reduction actions rather than their implementation. It is acknowledged that implementation
planning, funding, stakeholder management, and risk mitigation are essentials for the success
of introducing changes in big organizations.
• Other environmental impacts: This study evaluates portfolio impacts and reduction actions
based on global warming potential (CO2 emissions) due to data availability in EPDs and scien-
tific literature. However, only focusing on reducing CO2 emissions may risk problem-shifting,
where improvements in one area come at the expense of another.
• Other project types: This study is limited to TMF’s projects dealing with roads, bicycle lanes,
and pavements, as enterprise data availability does not encompass other types of infrastructure
projects, such as bridges, tunnels, parks, and rainwater management. These types involve
different material compositions and emissions, thus different reduction pathways and actions.
These exclusions are acknowledged and will be described and discussed further in relevant sections.
Future endeavors of the work presented in this study should integrate these aspects.

Methodology
This section describes the methodological framework applied to achieve a holistic and data-driven
assessment of reduction pathways and actions within TMF’s portfolio. However, some background
and theorem are necessary to describe, as these highly relate to the results of this study.
Section 2.1 describes the general methodology of LCA and MFA. Section 2.2 describes the LCA and
MFA tool applied for the purpose of this study. Section 2.3 outlines data collection and how LCA
and MFA results are used to estimate TMF’s portfolio and identify reduction pathways. Section 2.4
outlines the framework for identifying and assessing reduction action.
2.1
Quantitative sustainability assessment
2.1.1
Life cycle assessment
In its simplicity, life cycle assessment (LCA) is a holistic methodology used to quantify potential en-
vironmental impacts associated with a product, process, or system throughout its entire life cycle.
Depending on the purpose and application of the LCA, it can have many facets and forms based
on different methodologies and principles. In current time, most LCAs follow the framework and
guidelines set by ISO 14040 and the European Commission’s ILCD guidelines (EC-JRC, 2010), which
is further outlined for "safe" navigation by handbooks (Hauschild et al., 2018; Jolliet et al., 2016). The

Environmental product deceleration
With the goal to streamline goal and scope definition, inventory analysis, and impact assessment
methodology of LCAs, as well as provide reliable data to decision-makers, the environmental prod-
uct deceleration (EPD) system was introduced by ISO 14025. This ensures that LCA results follow
the same methodologies and are presented in an identical way, ensuring transparency and compara-
bility. Further developed to meet the needs of the construction and infrastructure sector, the standard
DS/EN 15804 + A2:2021, 2021, describes the governing rules of the methodological choices of LCA
and how results are presented for construction materials. For the relevancy of this study, three key
rules of EN15804 are necessary to describe:
1. Life cycle stages: EN15804 divides life cycle stages into distinct modules (A1, A2, A3, B1, etc.)
in order to highlight important processes related to construction materials, such as construction
and waste processing. At the current stage of EPD development in Denmark, EPDs does not
yet include all life cycle stages (Figure 4). Particularly, the B-module is calculated according to

crushing the concrete. EN15804 applies the cut-off approach, which implies that all impacts are
allocated to the primary user of the material (polluter pays principle), while the receiver gets a
burden-free material (Hermansson et al., 2022). The cut-off approach has received some criti-
cism, as it fails to incentivize the production of recycled materials for the primary user. While
the D-module aims to give the primary user an incentive to improve recycling practices through
credited reductions, it is required to be reported separately from LCA results and belongs to the
next product system according to the cut-off approach.
2.1.2
Material flow analysis
Material Flow Analysis (MFA) is a systematic methodology used to quantify and visualize the flows
and stocks of materials within a system. By mapping material flows between processes, MFA has
shown useful for organizations to comprehend material consumption and waste generation as well
as connect circular processes (Khlifa et al., 2024). It has been widely applied in waste management
systems to improve recycling efforts and optimize resource utilization (Moriguchi & Hashimoto,
2016).
In MFA, material flows are typically categorized into input flows, stocks, and output flows, represent-
ing a material entering, accumulating within, and then leaving the system, respectively. If the mate-
rial composition is known, the flow can then be further broken down into specific sub-components
(e.g. bitumen in asphalt flows). MFA is based on the principle of mass conservation, which defines
that matter cannot be created or disappear in an isolated system (Lombardi et al., 2024). Essentially,
implying that the sum of stocks and flows and stocks should equal 0.

Part 1: Portfolio and hotspot analysis
The portfolio and hotspot analysis serves as a crucial component in the data-driven approach of
this study. Identifying and quantifying material streams and CO2-intensive materials and processes
across TMF’s infrastructure portfolio provides key insights in areas where reduction actions are most
efficient. These are referred to as high-impact opportunities. MFA brings a holistic approach to
system analysis by visualizing flows and stocks around the construction sites which, in turn, can be
used to identify key resource streams, inefficient waste handling processes, or connecting streams to
improve circularity. Complementary to this, LCA brings key figures on how materials and processes
contribute to CO2 emissions across different life cycle stages, enlightening where problems occur
and which solutions can be applied. For instance, high impacts in production stage (A1-A3) would
require a rethinking- or reducing solution, while high impacts in deconstruction stage (A4-out, A5-
out) would require an optimization of waste handling processes.

LCA results to a portfolio analysis that reflects real-time annual material streams and CO2 emissions,
as well as its implication and limitation to the scope of this study.

2.4.1
Problem identification, success criteria, and literature review
The first step involves scoping the high-impact opportunity at hand, as found in the portfolio analy-
sis, and identifying potential solutions. This step is crucial as different opportunities presents differ-
ent challenges, such as high environmental impact during production or inefficient waste handling
processes. To ensure that the identified reduction actions addresses the opportunity, general suc-
cess criteria regarding CO2 reduction potential and cost-effectiveness are established, but additional
criteria are also tailored to the specific opportunity.
To support this, a comprehensive literature review is conducted, focusing on technological and
process-oriented innovations in the infrastructure sector. The review begins with broader literature,
such as review studies, and then narrowing down to practical solutions that has proven to reduce
CO2 emissions, either through EPDs or scientific literature. The feasibility of these solutions within a
municipal context is also considered.
2.4.2
LCA and baseline establishment
Three major infrastructure developers, namely Vejdirektoratet, Bygningsstyrelsen, and BaneDan-
mark, have established a common framework for the development and assessment of reduction ac-
tions. The framework aims to align the methodology of balancing CO2 reductions and socioeconomic
costs, ensuring methodological consistency of reduction action (Transportministeriet, 2024a).
This framework describes the LCA approach and defines some of the components to be included in
the techno-economic analysis. Given that KK-LCA is aligned with the impact assessment methodol-
ogy of Vejdirektoratet’s LCA tool, InfraLCA, it is logical to adopt the same approach for evaluating
reduction actions in this study.
Common LCA approach and baseline-setting
The framework outlines general LCA methodology as established in this study, but importantly high-
lights the importance of matching the temporal scope of reduction actions with a baseline scenario. It
states that it can be scoped according to the need of the specific reduction action at hand, but it needs
to encompass all relevant benefits and drawback from the reduction action’s life cycle. This relates to
cases when comparing materials with different lifespans than those in the baseline model. Further-
more, the framework recommends using established LCA tools such as InfraLCA or LCAbyg. For
the purpose of this study, KK-LCA is considered as an established and reliable LCA tool.
To evaluate the reductions of reduction actions, a baseline scenario is required. An approach for es-
tablishing such is either unclear or simply not disclosed in the common framework. For the purpose

of this study, the portfolio analysis is applied as the baseline scenario, as it reflects current annual

Conducting LCAs for reduction actions
To ensure compatibility with the common framework, a "normal" LCA is conducted for each reduc-
tion action in KK-LCA, including all life cycle stages (including the C-module). These are based on
their corresponding EPD or data from scientific literature. Rather than the traditionally-used func-
tional unit as the basis for comparison, material flows from the portfolio analysis is used as input
flows for the reduction actions. For example, if a reduction action aims to replacing all concrete
pavement slabs with a low-carbon alternative, the annual material flows for the current slab are used
as reference. If the alternative material involves a different density, a different unit, such as m2 is
used. The results of these LCAs are then compared with a baseline scenario based on currently used
EPDs and portfolio analysis data.
Translating LCA results to the portfolio analysis
While the LCA results, at this point, are compatible with the common framework, they must be
translated to the portfolio analysis in order to reflect their effect on TMF’s portfolio. This translation
follows the same methodology established in 2.3 and involves:
• Converting EoL results (C1-C4) into deconstruction emissions (A4-out + A5-out).
• Allocating emission credits from the D-module into the production stage (A1-A3).
While this approach deviates from standard LCA methodology, it is the only work around to quantify
how improved material recycling in EoL contributes to emission reductions across TMF’s portfolio.
Allocating the D-module to the production stage introduces an important implication, as it conflict
with the cut-off approach. This relates to reduction actions that improves recycling processes out-
side TMF’s system boundary, and is underscored throughout this study and further discussed in
Section 5.7.
2.4.3

In parallel with the LCA, a techno-economic analysis is conducted in order to estimate the economic
costs and savings of implementing reduction actions. The techno-economic analysis follows a life
cycle cost (LCC) approach as presented in the common framework, which is based on guidelines
from Finansministeriet, 2023, ensuring that the economic costs of reduction actions are assessed over
their full life cycle. This is logical, as it aligns with life cycle approach for LCA, allowing for grouping
costs and savings according to life cycle stages.

However, the common framework does not specify important considerations such as which cost
components needs to be included or guidance of appropriate data sources and estimations. For the
purpose of this study, the following are inclusions and exclusions for LCC of reduction actions:
• Initial costs: upfront costs of alternative material, process, and machinery.
• Operational costs: Change in operational costs as a result of changes in processes, maintenance
needs or lifetime of material.
• Cost savings: Economic saving or revenues related to cheaper materials, processes, or produc-
tion of recycled materials.
• Exclusions: Internal resources, such as project manager and administrative costs, associated
with planning, training, and communicating with stakeholders are excluded from the LCC.
Furthermore, to ensure reliability and documentation of data sources, the following hierarchy of cost
estimations is applied for quantifying costs and savings of implementing reduction actions:
1. quotation or estimates sourced directly from producer or supplier of alternative material or
process.
2. invoices or cost estimation from developer that has experience with the alternative material or
process.
3. cost estimation based on average prices from tender submission data.
For the baseline scenario, LCC are based on averaged prices from the 37 project’s tender submissions.

4.1
Concrete pavement slab
4.1.1
Problem identification and success criteria
Concrete pavement slabs plays a defining element in both the architectural identity of the city and
its functionality. The distinctive red-tinted slabs allows for easy removal and re-installation, making
them particularly suited for frequent maintenance of utilities and pipelines beneath the pavement.
Additionally, concrete slabs presents a cheap solution for paving areas only costing 80 DKK per slab
(160 DKK m−2), or 260 DKK m−2 with transportation and full installation. However, slabs are very
prone to cracking when exposed to heavy vehicles driving onto pavements, leading to a drastically
shortened lifetime than anticipated. Furthermore, the slabs reliance on virgin materials contributes
significantly to the annual CO2 emissions of TMF’s infrastructure projects, alone accounting for 1,200
tCO2 (17%). Annually, 10,000 tonnes of virgin slabs are installed, while 7,600 tonnes are decon-
structed and downcycled into gravel substitutes.
The key challenge lies in addressing this high CO2 footprint, while maintaining the visual and psy-
chical functionalities of the slabs. To ensure the success of any proposed solutions, the following
criteria are assessed:
• Preservation of functionality: The solution must not change the visual perception or interfere
with the maintenance process required for utility and pipeline work.
• Durability: Must offer equal or better strength and lifetime compared to the current slabs.
• Significant CO2 reduction: The proposed solution must deliver CO2 reduction documented in
EPDs or scientific literature.
• Cost-effective: The solution must be economic feasible and present a cost-effective business
case for TMF’s portfolio.
• Ease of implementation: The solution must change as few process as possible, minimizing
resistance from stakeholders.

in Copenhagen Municipality: Green concrete referring to the utilization of supplementary cemen-
titious by-products from other production chains e.g. fly-ash or slag. This approach showed to
reduce clinker content by up to 50%. Developed by Aalborg Portland, FutureCem is an alternative

4.2
Concrete foundation
4.2.1
Problem identification and success criteria
The use of concrete foundation, particularly earth-moist or zero-slump concrete, is a critical com-
ponent in curbstone and granite paving applications designed to provide structural stability and
durability at risk of heavy vehicle stressors. The rigidity of the material allows for easy installation of
granite elements, as the stones can be directly set and hardened into place. In current technical stan-
dards, TMF requires a technical strength of 30 MPa (TMF, 2024). As shown in the portfolio analysis,
concrete for pavements and curbstone constitutes one of the major mass flows of 13,000 t yr−1 and
the single largest material-specific contributor to emissions with a total impact of 1,540 tCO2 yr−1.
Figure 17: Typical dimensioning of curbstone concrete foundation. Not a TMF standard (Pavingexpert, 2024)
The issue stems from a precautionary principle accounting for the risk of heavy vehicles driving over
curbstones. As illustrated in Figure 17, more concrete is used than granite in curbstone applications.
While this ensures durability, it also leads to over-engineering with higher material consumption
and emissions than necessary in many applications. Moreover, the issue is worsened by the frequent
renovation or replacement of curbstones before reaching their full technical lifetime (Momotaz et al.,
2023). This is often driven by other parts of the infrastructure needing renovation or unforeseen wear
and tear from vehicle collision, rather than the structural failure of the curbstone and foundation.
This means that the embedded CO2 in the concrete foundation over it’s 100 year technical lifetime is
wasted (IBF, 2022; Unicon, 2022), which might be the one reason to the high material flows of granite
curbstones, and thus concrete foundation.
To ensure the success of any proposed reduction actions, the following criteria are assessed:
• Structural performance: Maintain the required strength (30 MPa) and durability to support
curbstones and granite paving under vehicle loads.
• Concrete rigidity: The solution must maintain the workability of the material to ensure ease of
handling and installation at construction sites.

4.2.2
Literature review
Concrete foundations in curbstone and pavement applications represent yet another challenge of
cement-based constructions. In this challenge, circular concrete is also relevant as concrete waste
is generated in the same manner as concrete pavement slabs. FutureCem was also identified as a
promising low-carbon alternative for cement-based materials. This cement have been adopted by
Unicon in their UniGreen product line, which includes earth-moist concrete specifically developed
for curbstone applications with a compressive strength of 30 MPa, which meets TMF’s technical
standard for concrete foundations. A substantial reduction is documented, lowering emissions from
current earth-moist concrete of 220 kgCO2 t−1 (IBF, 2022; Unicon, 2022) to 165 kgCO2 t−1 (Unicon,
2024). Furthermore, it is available in compressive strengths ranging from 12 MPa to 30 MPa.
The high infrastructure load in Copenhagen remains a relevant concern to consider. However, in
some areas, the compressive strength requirement of 30 MPa in Copenhagen Municipality may be
unnecessarily high. A comparison with standards outside of Denmark reveals that curbstones re-
quirements, specifically for concrete curbstones, ranges from 17 to 40 MPa depending on country
and authority (Momotaz et al., 2022). This suggest that current standards may represent an over-
engineered precaution, leading to excessive material use and emissions. Therefore, a reduction action
looking at lower compressive strength requirements is investigated.

4.3
Asphalt base layer
4.3.1
Problem identification and success criteria
Asphalt plays a critical role in the construction of roads and bicycle lanes, including three primary
layers: wearing, binder, and base layer. Each layer serves its own structural purpose, with the base
layer acting as the foundation for the upper layers, ensuring load distribution and stability. Despite
the significant contribution of asphalt to annual CO2 emissions, no reduction action targeting all
asphalt layers collectively was identified.
The base layer is commonly constructed using virgin stone aggregates and bound together with a
bitumen binder. The materials are mixed using a thermal mixing process reaching temperature of up
to 190 oC, resulting in high energy consumption. Annually, 14,200 tonnes of GAB is used to construct
asphalt base layers, leading to a CO2 emission of 570 tCO2 yr−1.
For reduction actions dealing with asphalt base layer, the following success criteria are assessed:
• Structural Performance: Maintain or improve the structural performance and durability re-
quired for load distribution in both road and bicycle lane applications
• CO2 reduction: Deliver a significant reduction in CO2 emissions compared to current base layer
materials.
• Material circularity: Incorporates the use of recycled materials, reducing the reliance on virgin
aggregates.
• Cost-effective: The solution must be economic feasible and present a cost-effective business
case for TMF’s portfolio.
• Ease of implementation: The solution must change as few processes as possible, minimizing
resistance from stakeholders.
4.3.2
Literature review
Over the last decade, the use of reclaimed asphalt pavement (RAP) in road filling has increased,
with Europe generating approximately 50 Mt of RAP annually (Hussain & Yanjun, 2013), with Den-
mark being identified as one of the countries with most experience utilizing RAP in road building
(Tsakoumaki & Plati, 2024). RAP refers to the same utilization of crushed concrete and asphalt as
substitute for natural aggregates. In the case of TMF, utilizing RAP has shown both economic and
environmental benefits (other than CO2) compared to using virgin gravel.
A recent innovation in this field is the adoption of cold-mixed bitumen stabilized material (BSM-
KMA) for asphalt base layers. BSM-KMA is an in-situ solution that directly recycles asphalt into

4.4.2
Literature review
Hydrotreated vegetable oil (HVO) has emerged as an alternative to conventional diesel fuel and is
already fully implemented by TMF within it’s construction sites as of 2024. TMF require the use
of second-generation HVO-100, which is derived from residual byproducts or waste (residual feed-
stock), such as slaughterhouse by-products, fish factory waste, and used cooking oil (Naveed et al.,
2023). This is crucial as it does not compete with food production, which would lead to indirect CO2
emissions. The main advantage of HVO lies in its direct compatibility with existing diesel engines,
making it easy to adopt by contractors and freight companies. Furthermore, a reduction certificate
promising 95% CO2 reduction was obtained from a Danish HVO supplier (Biofuel Express). How-
ever, literature indicates that CO2 reduction of HVO heavily depends on which feedstock is used,
how its produced, and distributed. For instance, one study found that reductions ranged from 7.7%
for HVO blends (blends not disclosed) to 93% for HVO using 100% used cooking oil (HVO-UCO) as
feedstock (Kourkoumpas et al., 2024). Currently, HVO is sold by Danish suppliers under the name
HVO Biodiesel (UCO) (OK, 2025b).
Despite its promising reduction, concerns remain about the limited availability of residual feedstock
to produce HVO at a scale that could replace the entirety of conventional diesel (Soam & Hillman,
2019). This scarcity could negate adoption by industry stakeholders, as low supply and high demand
implies a risk of increased costs. However, the opposite is observed when looking at price develop-
ment of HVO. From 2022 to 2024, the price premium of HVO-UCO compared to conventional diesel
have been stable around 50%. Since then, the price point of HVO-UCO have dropped drastically
from approximately 25 to 15 DKK L−1, corresponding to a price premium below 30% (Figure 21).
The reason for this can have multiple explanations, but either way, it’s becoming more attractive to
use HVO in diesel engines.

Building upon TMF’s existing initiative to reduce emissions by requiring HVO for construction ma-

Heavy Electric Vehicles (HEVs) are a rapidly developing technology and a relevant alternative within
the temporal scope of this thesis. Projections by Green Transition Denmark, 2024, estimate that by
2030, 25% of trucks will be electric, rising to 50% by 2035. The prerequisite of HEVs advantages lies in
it being derived from green energy, which Denmarks’s energy grid allows for. The main advantage
is that green electricity in Denmark is assumed to be less scarce compared to the residual feedstock
of HVO.
Promising CO2 reduction potentials have been shown for HEVs. Scania, 2023 reports a break-even
point with diesel trucks at 37,000 km with green electricity and 59,000 km with average European grid
mix. In a comprehensive study of truck transportation in urban areas revealed a 91% CO2 reduction
compared to diesel trucks, with similar total costs of ownership (TCO) (Lyu et al., 2023). Similar
reduction have also been found in Danish studies (Region Hovedstaden, 2023).
Currently, HEVs are not yet viable for immidiate implementation due to several challenges. The lack
of charging infrastructure remains a significant barrier for adoption, requiring substantial govern-
mental investments to establish (Konstantinou & Gkritza, 2023). For example, it is estimated that
50,000 charging stations are needed across EU to support a 15% share of HVEs in long-haul opera-
tions (Shoman et al., 2023). Additionally, the high upfront costs of transitioning an entire truck fleet
to HEVs pose a substantial economic investment for freight companies. To address this challenge,
freight companies needs to develop new business models that distribute costs across participating
stakeholders, hereunder municipalities (Werner & Onufrey, 2022). While pushing electrification of
freight trucks are beyond TMF’s jurisdiction and economic capabilities, it can advocate for such in-
vestments by aligning economic incentives with these new business models to encourage freight
companies to take the risk of investing in electric fleets.

Obstacles and risk assessment
HVO from used cooking oil
The main obstacle for implementing HVO lies in gaining support and cooperation from truck fleet
owners. In previous market dialogues, concerns have been raised about truck producers revoking
warranties when using HVO. However, TMF’s implementation of HVO for machinery within con-
struction sites has not reported any issues related to this concern. Yet, the risk remains and can lead

reduction action.
Another important consideration is to confirm that biodiesel suppliers can meet the projected annual
demand of approximately 500,000 liters of HVO-UCO. Furthermore, there is a risk that truck owners
might not use HVO-UCO as required. While field test kits exist for checking for HVO in diesel
engines, no such test is available for differentiating between HVO types. Hypothetically, trucks could
run on other HVO blends or conventional diesel instead of HVO-UCO, which would significantly
negate CO2 reductions.
Heavy Electric Vehicles
The main obstacle for HEVs lies in the substantial governmental and fleet owner investments re-
quired to establish a charging infrastructure and electric truck fleets, which is outside of TMF’s di-
rect control. And although literature projects that HEVs will become economically competitive with
diesel trucks in the future, slow technological advancements present a significant risk. For now, the
main risk remains in the technological readiness of HEVs.

5
Discussion
The aim of this study was to identify high-impact opportunities for TMF’s portfolio and assess the
reduction potential and cost-effectiveness of reduction action. The goal of this was to present various
ways of reducing CO2 emissions contributing towards TMF’s 2028 and future targets.
This section discusses the interaction between the identified reduction actions in order to recommend
the most effective and combined pathway of reduction actions. Additionally, transitioning from the-
oretical reduction potentials to realized reductions is discussed, presenting a method for monitoring
the progress and reductions of future implemented reduction actions. A critical evaluation of the
robustness of the analysis is presented, including implications of main findings, uncertainties, tem-
poral fluctuations of KPIs, and methodological choices. Lastly, the discussion addresses the limited
scope of this study, identifying gaps and outlining future work.
5.1
Interactions, conflicts, and synergies of reduction actions
For now, each reduction action has been individually presented and assessed for its CO2 reduction
potential. However, combining multiple actions has some implications due to interactions, conflicts,
and synergies. As TMF moves forward by implementing reduction actions, it is important to consider
that one action can limit or impact the benefits of other actions.
Conflicts occur when reduction actions changes the same material flow or life cycle stage of a ma-
terial or depend on overlapping material flows. For instance, Circular Asphalt and BSM-KMA both
rely on asphalt waste streams from construction sites for producing new asphalt. Since the same
waste cannot be used for both processes, these actions are incompatible, meaning that implementing
either would directly make the other infeasible. And even though it is possible to recycle an estab-
lished BSM layer through circular asphalt, the reduction potential is substantial lowered. As per
LCA methodology, previous credited reductions from recycled materials cannot be credited twice.
This is an important aspect to highlight during decision-making when selecting actions for reducing
emissions.
Synergies occur when one reduction action mediates the drawbacks of another. For example, im-
proved recycling of circular concrete requires longer transportation distances of concrete waste from
construction sites. This drawback can be reduced by simultaneously implementing HVO for road-
going machinery. Moreover, reduction actions that targets different life cycle stages of the same ma-
terial can also be considered as synergetic. For instance, implementing a new FutureCem pavement
slab, elongating lifetime by requiring reuse of slabs, and improving recycling with circular concrete
could theoretically amplify CO$_2$ reduction potentials. However, combining these actions would
01-03-2025
page 66 of 98
==PAGE:70==
Master’s Thesis
Technical University of Denmark
alter the basis for comparison, requiring an entirely new LCA model to assess the combined reduc-
tion potentials and economic outcomes. This demonstrates the possibility for synergies but also the
complexity in quantifying their collective reduction potentials.
While this thesis presents a manageable set of actions, TMF’s long term strategy involves a substantial
larger number of reduction actions targeting different materials, life cycle stages, or other processes.
As the number of reduction actions increase, it becomes more crucial to develop a framework for
assessing conflicts and synergies between reduction actions. This might be done by flagging reduc-
tion actions with involved material flows or flow dependencies, automating conflict detection in a
database. For now, the reduction actions are not modeled for conflicts and synergies, which implies
that only one action from each material can be combined in order to avoid misleading reduction
potentials and conflicts.
5.2
Short- and long term roadmap of combining reduction actions for CO2 targets
The main question of whether a 30% reduction in CO2 emissions is achievable by 2028 remains unan-
swered. While the individual reduction actions provide viable ways to reduce emissions, combining
a pathway of multiple actions is required in order to meet 2028 and future targets.
Based on the assessment of each reduction action, as well as conflicts and synergies discussed, a
short-term prioritization of reduction actions is recommended. This includes FutureCem pavement
slabs and foundations, BSM-KMA for base layer construction, and HVO for road-going machinery.
These actions present significant reduction potentials and was suggested for immediate pilot-testing
and implementation.
However, the ultimate decision on which actions to pursue and implement lies with decision-makers,
making it inherently important to present reduction actions and reduction potentials in a compre-
hendible way that is intuitive to non-experts. To support future decision-making processes and eval-
uate combinations of reduction actions, an Excel-based tool for visualizing the combined reduction
potential of multiple reduction action was developed (Figure 23). The tool allows for combining mul-
tiple actions with different implementation timelines and directly compares with annual emissions
and reduction targets. References found in Appendix D.4.

5.3
Monitoring realized reductions in portfolio emissions
For now, the analysis has remained in the theoretical world, where CO2 reductions are described as
reduction potentials. However, once the proposed reduction actions are implemented, these theoreti-
cal potentials will transition into realized CO2 reductions. However, theory and reality often diverge,
meaning that reduction potentials might deviate from realized reductions due to multiple aspects
explored in this section. Therefore, tracking and monitoring these are crucial in order to verify that
reduction actions deliver the expected reduction potentials and ensure accountability of progress to-
ward TMF’s 2028 and future targets. One approach for achieving this involves the CO2/DKK KPI
identified in Section 3.2.3 which reflects the relationship between CO2 emissions and the economic
scope of TMF’s infrastructure projects.

The strong linear correlation of the CO2 KPI makes it a robust metric for this purpose. As reduction
actions such as FutureCem concrete and HVO for road-going machinery are implemented, the KPI

5.3.2
Expanding the scope to other project types
The scope of this analysis have been limited to include roads, bicycle lanes, and pavements due to
data availability, but TMF project portfolio encompasses a much broader span of project types such
as rainwater management, bridges, tunnels, parks, and much more. These project types represents a
gap in this study, as they account for approximately the same capital invest as roads, bicycle lanes,
and pavements. As the analysis of this study is automated, drawing data from a LCA result database,
it would be possible to include and differentiate between project types. This means that material
flows, major drivers of CO2 emissions, and KPIs could be generated in real time, and used to identify
specific reduction actions for each project type. For instance, it would become possible to quantify
the importance of steel reinforcement in projects dealing with bridges and tunnels. If we assume the
same KPI for other project types, TMF’s annual CO2 emission would be 14,300 tCO2 yr−1 instead of
the presented 6,500 tCO2 yr−1.

as these projects deals with lower CO2 intensive materials and processes, such as soil and gravel.
Conversely, bridges and tunnel yields a higher KPI as these projects deals with higher amounts of
concrete, steel, and steel reinforcement. This variation is illustrated in Figure 27 and introduces an
important consideration of KPI fluctuations due to project type prioritization.

The KPI presents a promising tracking tool, however monitoring reductions over time are not that

TMF’s portfolio. While factors, such as project prioritization, are measurable for each year, future
comparisons is eminent to be influenced by factors such as material price inflation, advancement in
EPD data, or changes in LCA assumptions and methodologies. For instance, as material prices in-
creases due to inflation, the KPI ratio naturally decreases, showing a wrong picture of an improved
KPI. And as product specific EPDs gets available, portfolio emissions and future KPI will change
accordingly.

implemented reduction actions. For this reason, it is even more important to establish a framework
that accounts for these fluctuations when comparing future KPIs with a baseline, especially as it
directly impacts the amount of realized CO2 reduction of future implemented reduction actions. A

5.4
General uncertainties: evaluating the robustness of findings
As with any other study, this thesis is subject to general uncertainties that need to be critically eval-
uated to assess the robustness of its main findings. In this study, the main findings include an-
nual emissions, reduction potentials of identified actions, economic estimates, and most important,
whether TMF’s future CO2 reduction targets are realistically achievable.
Uncertainties of varying degrees are present across all components of this analysis, though only
quantified for the reduction potential of the identified actions. One example of disregarded uncer-
tainty lies within emissions from external waste treatment processes. As outlined in Section 3.1.2,
these emissions are modeled using an average of multiple scenarios, including longer transportation
distances and varying waste treatment processes. For instance, asphalt waste could be recycled into
new asphalt at factories, downcycled into aggregates at external sites, landfilled, or even sent back to
KMC (Figure 28). Such variation introduces uncertainty in the portfolio analysis, thus, the baseline
compared to each reduction action which impacts the accuracy of the reduction potentials.

5.5
Counteracting effects of reduction actions
While reduction actions aim to lower CO2 emissions, cost-saving actions introduce a risk of unin-
tended counteracting effects. The issue lies in the fact, that financial savings mainly remain in the
given project’s budget or flows back to TMF for other projects. This opens the possibility for those
savings to be used towards more CO2-intensive materials or processes, potentially negating the ini-
tially intended reduction.
An example of this was observed in 2024 during the implementation of a smarter soil management
reduction action, where the "50 cm rule" was revoked, essentially reducing the amount of virgin and
contaminated soil transported to and from TMF’s projects. In a park development project, this action
freed up a substantial amount of its budget. And since soil has relatively low CO2 emissions com-
pared to other construction materials, the available budget could potentially be used for other con-
struction materials with higher emissions. Although this project has not been followed up upon, this
example highlights the importance of considering the risk of counteracting effects of cost-savings,
especially for reduction actions such as BSM-KMA.
While this was not observed for any of the identified reduction actions, the same concept applies for
action that simultaneously is cost-saving and reduces CO2 emissions. Quantifying the implication of
this is very challenging as it would require a substantial amount of resources to track where savings
are allocated to in each individual project. However, it is theoretically possible to estimate this by
leveraging the CO2/DKK KPI. As this metric averages CO2 emissions per DKK spent, it could be
applied to give an estimate of indirect emissions associated with cost-saving reduction actions.
Constructing roads, bicycle lanes, and pavements was found to emit 15.9 tCO2 per million DKK
used. This means that per million DKK saved in projects, an average of 15.9 tCO2 can be expected
to be emitted additionally. Furthermore, we can also consider this value as a threshold for reduction

5.6.1
Why TMF faces higher reduction costs
TMF’s system is characterized by major circular flows, high recycling rates, and low transportation
distances for most materials, as identified through the portfolio analysis, making it a more optimized
system compared to other Danish Developers’. When comparing TMF’s CO2 KPI of 15.9 tCO2 per
million DKK to Aarhus Municipality’s 42 tCO2 per million DKK, it is evident that TMF’s system
has a better CO2-efficiency (Klitgaard, J., personal communication, 2025). This difference can imply
two considerations: either TMF includes far fewer CO2 emissions in it’s LCAs compared to Aarhus
Municipality, or that TMF have already improved key parts of its system. In this discussion, the latter
explanation is considered. No KPI was found for other Danish developers, such as Vejdirektoratet,
Bygningsstyrelsen, and Banedanmark.
This optimization has already captured many of the low-hanging reductions (e.g. internal reuse of
granite and better transportation logistics), making it inherently more challenging to further reduce
emissions for TMF. For this reason, a general higher shadow price can be expected. TMF faces a de-
cision: It can either adopt similar to other developers’ WTP and communicate that reduction should
occur in other sectors, or take a leadership role by raising the bar, explore ambitious reduction actions
and push the development of sustainable infrastructure.
5.6.2
Why TMF should raise the bar
Unlike other municipalities, TMF has the capacity, resources, and a committed team determined to
carry out the implementation of reduction actions across it’s portfolio. This uniquely positions TMF
to test more ambitious ways of reducing emissions. Additionally, TMF has an interest in taking a
leadership role. by raising the bar, TMF can encourage other municipalities and developers to in-
corporate successfully implemented reduction actions, substantially reducing emissions at a broader
scale.
By its efforts and substantial capital investments, such as in-sourcing a sustainability team, imple-
menting HVO- and electric machinery on construction sites, and mandating a thorough environmen-
tal evaluation of its own technical standards, this suggests that political framework in Copenhagen
Municipality is committed to reduce CO2 in the infrastructure sector. Given TMF’s vision of sus-
tainability under “Our Copenhagen”, not setting a strict WTP aligns with its long-term goals and
demonstrates its commitment to reduce emissions by fully exploring reduction opportunities. Ambi-
tious goal setting requires equally ambitious efforts, and if TMF is to meet its 2028 and future targets,
it must continue its WTP for reduction actions with high shadow prices.

5.7
Cut-off methodology as a barrier for circularity and reduction actions
As presented in Section 4.1.5, the cut-off methodology adopted by EPD operators, TMF, and other
Danish infrastructure developers fails to include the benefits of sending materials to recycling outside
the system boundary, which is very relevant for reduction actions such as circular concrete and as-
phalt. The choice of methodology governs how emissions and credits are allocated between product
systems (Figure 30).
Figure 30: Common approaches to allocate emissions and credits in LCA between primary and secondary
system (Corona et al., 2019)
The Cut-off methodology follows the polluter pays principle, meaning that emissions associated with
production and use are fully allocated to the primary product system, and any materials that are
reused or recycled in the secondary product system are treated as burden-free. Multiple allocation
approaches exists with their respective pros and cons, but the Cut-off approach has been widely
adopted in the construction and infrastructure sector due to its easy-to-follow framework and low
data requirements, which simplifies the creation of EPDs and LCAs as EPD commissioners only have
to gather data for it’s own respective systems.
However, this simplicity comes with a significant flaw. It incentivizes the use of recycled materi-
als as burden-free, but fails to incentivize the production of recycled materials outside the system
boundary. For reduction actions where recycling happens externally, such as circular concrete and

ternally recycled materials are not exclusively brought back into the system, TMF would be paying

This presents a clear barrier for reduction actions that aims to improve external waste handling prac-

5.8
Beyond CO2 emissions
While CO2 emissions has been the main focus of current thesis, it is important to acknowledge that
a truly comprehensive environmental assessment requires going beyond CO2. Infrastructure devel-
opment affects multiple dimensions of environmental concerns, including resource depletion, air
pollution, waster use, and ecosystem impacts.
As infrastructure projects rely heavily on virgin materials such as gravel, granite, and bitumen, one
key impact category worth considering is depletion of abiotic resources. Gravel pits are a rising con-
cern in Denmark, which implies that future decision-making processes may evaluate and prioritize
resource depletion at the same level as CO2 emissions (Teknologisk Institut, 2024). Similarly, local
and regional impact categories such as particulate matter formation (air pollution) and bio-diversity
are gaining a lot of traction in the environmental debate, and might become key impact categories in
future infrastructure projects.
While it is possible to calculate and evaluate these impact categories in current LCA tools, the lim-
itation lies in the lack of comprehensive EPD data, as the industry is still transitioning to the new
EPD standard. When sufficient EPD data is obtained, TMF would be able to identify if reduction
actions gives rise to problem-shifting, where CO2 reduction could lead to unintended increases in
other impact categories. For instance, adopting a low carbon granite paving stone could lead to
higher hazardous emissions, which would otherwise be neglected if only CO2 is considered.
Multiple established LCA approaches, such as normalization, monetization, and absolute sustain-
ability assessment, can be applied to evaluate and compare problem-shifting of the proposed and
future reduction actions. However, their results are only as robust and complete as the background
data, limiting the conclusiveness needed for decision-making purposes. For this reason, the ability

Conclusion
This thesis presented a holistic analysis of CO2 emissions from TMF’s infrastructure projects, iden-
tifying key material flows and processes driving emissions and proposing pathways of reducing
emissions towards 2028 and future targets. The key contribution of this work lies in the establish-
ment of a data-driven framework that utilizes tender submissions from TMF’s projects to identify
high-impact reduction opportunities, evaluate reduction action potentials, and measure realized re-
ductions post-implementation of reduction actions.
Key findings and contributions
The portfolio mapping revealed that annual emissions of 6,950 tCO2 are primarily driven by asphalt
materials (33%), concrete foundations (24%), and concrete pavement slabs (17%). With emissions pre-
dominantly occurring in production stage (A1-A3) and transportation stage (A4), targeting related
processes for these materials represents the most efficient pathway of emission reduction.
In total, 13 reduction actions were identified and evaluated for the high impact of concrete pave-
ment slabs, concrete foundations, asphalt base layer, and road-going machinery. The 4 recommended
reduction actions, that is, FutureCem pavement slabs and concrete foundations, BSM-KMA for as-
phalt base layers, and HVO for road-going machinery, demonstrate that TMF’s 30% reduction in
CO2 emissions by 2028 is achievable. However, some technical aspects requiring pilot testing and
cost verification is needed before implementation can start.
The techno-economic analysis highlighted that some reduction actions require economic investments
above the general willingness to pay (WTP) of 1, 500 DKK tCO−1
2
reduced. It was recommended that
TMF should lead by example, recognizing that ambitious goal-setting requires equally ambitious ac-
tions. By testing and implementing ambitious reduction actions, TMF can achieve its own reduction
targets but also pave the way for other municipalities and developers, driving industry-wide CO2
reductions on a much broader scale.
Challenges and limitations
This thesis also introduced the importance of considering and dealing with key challenges, includ-
ing uncertainties in LCA and techno-economic estimates, allocation methodology as a barrier for
circularity, and potential counteracting effects of cost-saving reduction actions. As a result of these
challenges, realized CO2 reductions of implemented actions are expected to deviate from the theo-
retical reduction potentials found in this study.

It was recommended to utilize the CO2/DKK KPI to account for this deviation. Furthermore, it en-
ables the ability to monitor realized reductions over time, providing a metric for CO2 accounting
and progress towards reduction targets. However, the accuracy of reduction tracking depends on
establishing a robust baseline and addressing the natural fluctuations due to inflation, project priori-

Future work and final remarks
While this thesis showed a clear pathway of significantly reducing CO2 emissions, high-impact op-
portunities for asphalt, concrete, and granite remain to be examined. And as TMF progresses towards
its 2028 and future reduction targets, gathering and analyzing more project data will be essential to
enable the scope of this thesis’ findings to include other types of projects, possibly revealing new
high-impact reduction opportunities. Progressing from analysis to implementation, the next steps
consist of exploring funding opportunities, running pilot tests, managing important stakeholders,
and evaluating implementation risks. Realizing and the success of the proposed and future reduc-
tion activities depends on addressing these during the implementation.
Ultimately, TMF has the resources and opportunity to push the development of sustainable infras-

TMF can remain on its path towards future CO2 targets and contribute to broader reductions.
