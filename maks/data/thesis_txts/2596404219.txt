Summary
Since the 2008 financial crisis, risk-based and risk factor related portfolio construction
strategies have seen an increase within the asset management sector.
Risk-Based methods such as risk parity are now an integral part of mutual and
hedge fund investment strategies. Risk parity, at the asset level, provides an alter-
native form of diversification by matching the risk contributions of all the portfolio
assets.
On the other hand, risk factors, traditionally used for asset pricing, have in the
last decade seen more use in portfolio construction methods with investors looking to
gain exposure to systematic risk premia by investing in factor-based methods.
In this thesis, the aim is to combine both risk parity and factor investing in a
portfolio construction method known as Factor Risk Parity. This multi-factor strategy
matches the risk contributions of a set of chosen factors towards the total risk of the
portfolio. To test the performance of such a strategy, a backtest on a portfolio is
implemented using the S&P 500 constituents over the last 15 years and then compared
to other methods used as benchmarks.
Also, to study the correlation between risk factors a correlation hierarchical clus-
tering algorithm is implemented and the factors used on the backtest are chosen based
on this cluster analysis.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In Capitalism and Free-market economies, one of the significant life decisions that
an individual faces is characterized by their choices of how to allocate resources and
wealth.
The rising amount of opportunities for investment accessible to an individual or
to a company provided by globalism and technology has caused the complexity of
resource and wealth allocation decisions to increase tremendously and because of
that we have seen an exponential rise of both academic and commercial research in
economic and financial topics.
Institutions, as collective entities, face the same asset allocation problem as the
common individual with added diﬀiculties and complexity. A more in depth anal-
ysis of the risks and liabilities taken by an institution is often required. Such an
analysis may be performed due to regulations required by the country(ies) in which
said institution operates or because of internal auditing and protocols established to
increase risk accountability and to optimize it’s resource allocation strategy. Also,
institutions often have stricter self-imposed constrains in terms of liquidity, social
awareness, environmental footprint etc. All the referenced above make the topic of
resource allocation very broad. Over the years several investment school of thoughts
have emerged and as we enter new and more innovative periods of time the market
itself requiring constant adaptation. What worked before for investors may not work
now due to better information and market access.
1.1
Portfolio Construction
In 1952 Harry Markovitz published an article on The Journal of Finance titled ”Portfo-
lio Selection” (Markowitz 1952). His article represented the birth of Modern Portfolio
Theory which is often the starting point for students when learning about asset al-
location. In this article, Markovitz introduced Mean-Variance Analysis and the idea
of diversification. In broad terms Markovitz introduced the idea that, while keeping
the variance (risk measure) the same, one could achieve higher portfolio returns (or
another measure) by choosing an optimal collection of assets, thus diversifying. An
illustration taken directly from Markowitz (1952) paper can be found in figure 1.1
Markovitz argued that a rational investor would always want to allocate its re-
sources to a portfolio in the eﬀicient frontier no matter its risk aversion because for

Later, W. F. Sharpe (1964) introduced the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM),

Since the origin of Modern portfolio theory, the topic of portfolio construction has
seen huge changes. A more complex market and market instruments (such as ETF’s),
as well as new technologies, have changed the way institutions and individuals manage
their assets tremendously over the years.
In the previous decade, machine learning and A.I. and pattern recognition based
techniques have been more and more in the toolset of portfolio management profes-
sionals in the hedge funds industry. Such methods allow for the integration of more
complex data sets besides a spreadsheet type data source.
1.1.1
Risk Factors
The importance of risk factors in investing has been increasing over the past few
decades and represents an area of studies with frequent updates as new factors are
constantly proposed in academia.
Historically risk factors have been used primarily as tools for asset pricing and
described in models that expand on the CAPM such as the ones proposed by Fama

The use of factors withing for portfolio construction strategies is more recent
as investors seek exposure to systematic risk using risk premia factors as tools for
such exposure (Homescu 2015)., as opposed to the traditional role of factors for
asset pricing and forecasting.
The term risk premia factors, according to Bender
et al. (2013), is used for factors that generate risk premium over long periods and

This topic won’t be covered in this project but by a brief read of such topic, one can
observe that some of the alternative risk premia described in such strategies, such as
in Reid & Zwan (2019), is related to long/short exposure to risk factors such as e.g.,
momentum, value, etc.
1.1.2
Risk-Based portfolio construction
According to Luo & Mesomeris (2015) Risk-based portfolio construction methods
have seen a huge increase in use by investors after the 2008 financial crisis. Risk-
based strategies opt to base its portfolio allocation on constrained optimization of
certain risk metrics instead of the more common return-to-risk approach.
Around the 2008 crisis volatility surged to point not seen since in previous decades
and many of the strategies that have been working before underperformed with in-
vestors losing gains from previous years. One can see why, in such a setting, risk-based
strategies increased in popularity as investors were looking for more secure protection
against volatility.
Since then, in particular, 3 main risk-based strategies have stood out in academia:
• Minimum variance: creating a portfolio with the intent of minimizing the volatil-
ity of its returns
• Maximum diversification: where a diversification ratio is maximized.
• Risk Parity: creates a portfolio where all assets contribute the same amount of
risk to the portfolio total risk.
On this project, the focus will be on the Risk Parity strategy and on how we can
incorporate factor investing in such a strategy.
1.2
Problem Definition
The Risk Parity method and factor investing as previously described have been in-
creasingly used by institutions and studied in academia. Nonetheless, the combination
of these two approached to portfolio construction has not been studied extensively. In
the last 5 years, some research on this topic has surfaced. In my view, the article by

T. Roncalli & Weisang (2016) has spurred some interest in this topic. This project
represents an attempt to contribute to the discussion regarding the application of
Factors to Risk Parity strategies.
Thus, the main objective here is to create a risk parity strategy based on factors
(Factor Risk Parity — FRP). That is, to create a portfolio construction strategy

2.1
Risk Parity and Risk Budgeting
Risk Parity is a particular case of the Risk Budgeting approach where the risk con-
tributions for all assets are equal. Thus, let us start by introducing the concepts of
Risk Budgeting and then how Risk Parity relates to it.
As indicated by Thierry Roncalli (2016), Risk Budgeting is one of the three Budget-
ing methods for asset allocation . The others are Weight Budgeting and Performance
Budgeting.
A Weight Budgeting strategy simply constructs a portfolio by directly selecting
the amount invested into each asset. An example of a Weight budgeting portfolio
method is an Equally Weighted portfolio where all assets have the same weights.
Another example is the commonly used 60/40 stocks and bonds allocation.
Performance Budgeting methods are based on the contributions of different assets
to the portfolio return, i.e., the assets performance contributions to the overall port-
folio return. As an illustration lets say we want a portfolio with 10% returns where
an investment in an S&P500 index fund and an investment in a government-issued
bond have respectively 8% and 2% performance contributions.
Risk budgeting, instead of weight and performance contributions, uses risk con-
tributions to construct a portfolio. The risk contribution of an asset to a portfolio is
the fraction of portfolio risk for which it is responsible. The following is a description
of how to calculate Risk Contributions as constructed by Thierry Roncalli (2016).

2.2
Risk Factors
Homescu (2015) does a good job illustrating that Risk Factors are extremely useful
to map the macroeconomic landscape. They help us see the overall picture of where
the market is going and explain the variations in individual assets.
==PAGE:20==
10
2 Theoretical Framework
According to Bhansali (2011), 4 or 5 risk factors are enough to explain almost 70%
of the variation of a lot of assets. This was shown by computing the statistical Prin-
cipal Components of major Asset Class Indexes on the 1960 to 2010 interval. Most
of the excess returns in portfolios with a big number of assets can be associated with
factors. As a portfolio growths and gets more diluted between assets the tendency
for its excess returns to be explained by risk factors increases as well.
When an investor invests in a huge number of assets this can be viewed as an in-
vestment in certain factors. If those assets have similarities then this factor allocation
is even clearer. For example, if an investor chooses to invest in the best perform-
ing assets in the last month, then this is similar to saying that this investor chose
to allocate a big proportion of its capital to the Momentum factor (described in 2.2.3).
The risk factor investing rationale is to create a portfolio where an investor gains
exposure to different factors. i.e., the portfolio is diversified in regards to risk factors.
Since different factors tend to be correlated to different macroeconomic phenomena
then an investor has diversified exposure to potential macroeconomic shocks and can
capture gains or prevent too many losses in the case of an event that influences a
particular factor. The previous is important because often investors suffer avoidable
losses because they were unknowingly overly exposed to a single factor.
In the last few decades, a huge plethora of risk factor has been proposed by
academia. As a way to express this huge diversity of factors the term ”Factor Jungle”
is often used.
Risk factors can be agglomerated in different ways. For the scope of this project
the main distinction made is between statistical and non-statistical risk factors. Also,
more emphasis will be given to factors related to the stock market.
2.2.1
The Fama-French 3 Factor Model
The 3 factor Fama French model, first described in Fama & French (1993), is an
expansion of the Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) adding 2 new factors to the
later.
On this, and the next sections, equities and the stock market will be considered as
our assets since that is the asset class where the model the 3 Factor Model that Fama
and French developed in 1993 and later, the 5 factor model proposed by then in 2015
have more impact.
The model Fama and French proposed is represented by the following time series
regression from Fama & French (2015):
==PAGE:21==
2.2 Risk Factors
11
Rit −RF t = ai + bi(RMt −RF t) + siSMBt + hiHMLt + eit
(2.11)
Where Rit represents the return of an equity (or portfolio) i at time t, RF t is
the risk free rate, RMt are the value-weighted market returns (e.g., S&P 500 index
returns). The Factor exposures are defined by bi, si and hi and ai is the intercept of
the model. The residual error is characterized by eit.
The 3 factors present in the model are:
• RMt −RF t: The excess returns obtained by investing in the market instead
of investing in a risk-free instrument. Often the risk-free instruments used are
government-issued treasury securities (e.g., In the United States, treasury bills,
or T-bills). The Fama and French 3 factor model and the CAPM have this
factor in common. It represents the performance of the entire market;
• SMBt (Small Minus Big): Returns of a portfolio of Small stocks minus the
returns of a portfolio of Big stocks. As Fama & French (1993) describe, this
factor is related to the size of publicly traded companies and stems from the
fact that, over a long period, small-cap companies perform better than big-cap
companies. It may also be referred to as the size factor;
• HMLt (High Minus Low): Difference between the returns of a portfolio of
stocks with high book-to-market ratio (B/M) and a portfolio of stocks with low
book-to-market ratio. As mentioned by Fama & French (2015), the rationale
behind this factor is the fact that, in the long term, stocks with high book-
to-market ratios are expected to provide higher returns. This factor measures
exposure to the value effect of a company. Companies with high book-to-market
ratios are considered to be value stocks and, on the other side, companies with
low book-to-market ratios are considered growth stocks. In the long term, value
stocks outperform growth stocks. This is often also referred to as the value factor
Fama and French originally proposed their 3 Factor Model and argued that it ex-
plained what was previously considered anomalies in the CAPM model. Nonetheless,
anomalies and/or shortcomings have also been attributed to the Fama French 3 factor
model. Thus, models that build on top of the later have been proposed in the same
way that Fama French 3 factor model built on the CAPM concept. Out of these, the
following will be analyzed in more detail
• The 5 factor model by Fama & French (2015) expands their previous model by
adding what they refer to the profitability and investment factors (see 2.2.2);
• The Carhart 4 factor model, first described in Carhart (1997), introduces a
momentum factor to the the existing Fama French 3 factor model (see 2.2.3);

2.2.2
Fama and French 5 factor model
In their paper ”A five-factor asset pricing model” published by the Journal of Finan-
cial Economics, Fama & French (2015) extended their previous work by expanding
the model first introduces in Fama & French (1993).
The Fama French 3 factor model was shown by multiple sources to be lacking
variations in its equity returns related to profitability and investment factors. To
approach that an expansion to the previous time series regression (2.11) was proposed.
From Fama & French (2015):
Rit−RF t = ai+bi(RMt−RF t)+siSMBt+hiHMLt+riRMWt+ciCMAt+eit (2.12)
Where the 2 new factors are:
• RMWt (Robust Minus Weak): Difference between the returns of a portfolio
of stocks with robust profitability and the returns of a portfolio of stocks with
weak profitability. Profitability in this case is a measure of the gains generated
by a company (present in the Income Statement that every public company has
the obligation to release);
• CMAt (Conservative Minus Aggressive): Difference between the returns
of a portfolio of low investment firms and the returns of a portfolio with high
investment firms. Low and High investment firms are respectively classified as
Conservative and Aggressive.
The exposures to new factors in (2.12) are represented by ri and ci.
2.2.3
Carhart 4 factor model and the Momentum factor
In Carhart (1997), a new model expanding the Fama and French 3 factor model (2.11)
was proposed. Carhart argues that mutual funds with higher 1-year returns obtain
them, not because they are successfully exposed to momentum, but because, mostly
by chance, they hold positions in the winning stocks of the previous year.
A big shortcoming of the Fama French 3 factor model is the not inclusion of a
momentum factor. Carhart proposed the following:
Rit −RF t = ai + bi(RMt −RF t) + siSMBt + hiHMLt + piMOMt + eit
(2.13)
Where the definitions in (2.11) apply, MOMt is the momemtum factor and pi is
the momemtum factor exposure. Using the methodology described by Carhart (1997),
MOM is the 1-year momentum factor of equity returns.

Often, in literature, momentum is also named UMD or Up minus Down due to
it being calculated by taking a portfolio of the returns of the best-performing stocks
of 1 period of time and subtracting a portfolio of the worst-performing stocks of that
same period.
The momentum factor has become a very commonly used factor by a lot of dif-
ferent models from the more classic Asset Pricing Models to the newer Alternative
Factor Premia strategies. This is due to the merit of its performance.
Carhart (1997) argues, in favor of the momentum risk factor performance, that by
simply buying the top 10% best performing mutual funds and shorting the bottom
10% of the previous year, an yearly returns of 8% is obtainable out of which 4.6% is
related to the value and momentum factors and 1.7% is due to differences in expenses
and transaction costs.
2.2.4
Quality as a risk factor
In their paper, Asness, Frazzini, & Pedersen (2019) suggest that the quality of a stock
has good characteristics to be used as a factor. They show that high quality stocks
have a tendency to be underpriced while low-quality stocks tend to be underpriced
which without any further analysis seems to characterize a market anomaly. Also,
the authors found no robust evidence that high-quality stocks have higher risk when
compared to low-quality stocks.
A portfolio composed of long positions of high-quality stocks and short positions
of low-quality stocks has a good return-to-volatility relation. This type of portfolio
can be used as a risk factor that gives quality exposure and can be used in multi-factor
methods. Such risk factor is known as Quality minus Junk (QMJ).
Quality is built by Asness, Frazzini, & Pedersen (2019) as a combination 3 char-
acteristics of stocks: profitability, growth and safety. Profitability is obtained as the
profits per monetary unit of book-value of a company. In Asness, Frazzini, & Peder-
sen (2019) the growth measure used is the 5-year growth of the profitability measure.
Safety composed by measures of low market beta, low leverage, low bankruptcy risk
and low volatility of returns on investment.
Some of the components used to build the Quality Minus Junk factor might create
unwanted correlation with factors previously described. This will be dealt with later
in the factor selection process.

2.2.6
The Devil in the HML factor
This section follows the work of Asness & Frazzini (2013). The authors propose an
improvement on the calculation of the High Minus Low (HML) factor previously
introduced in by Fama and French in their 3 factor model. In the remainder of this
project, this factor will be referenced as the HML_Devil factor as an allusion to the
title of the original article: ”The Devil in HML’s Details”.
In Fama & French (1993), previously described in 2.2.1, HML is calculated using
each company’s Book and price values from the end of the previous year. i.e., the
book to price ratio (B/P), for all companies, is updated on an yearly basis. As a
consequence, the portfolio used to obtain the HML’s returns is also rebalanced on a
yearly basis.
One cannot calculate the B/P ratio for a current date since the Book value is not
available until a company releases it’s financial statements. Nonetheless, the current
market price of a public company is always attainable.
In their article, Asness & Frazzini (2013) present empirical evidence that using
current prices to update B/P ratios on a monthly bases with lagged Book values on
the HML calculation improves the performance of the 5 Factor Fama french model
(section 2.2.2).
2.3
Factor Risk Parity and Factor Risk Budgeting
Factor Risk Parity represents an attempt of merging two different approaches to
portfolio diversification: Factor investing and Risk parity. This attempt is backed by
the fact that both of those strategies have shown promising results.
As a reminder, the objective of the Risk Parity strategy (section 2.1) was to match
the risk contributions of a set of assets in a portfolio. That is, we are calculating risk
contributions of individual assets such as stocks, fixed income, currencies, or asset
holders such as market index funds, ETFs, Mutual funds, etc.
The aim of Factor Risk Parity is to, instead of constructing a portfolio where the
Risk Contributions of assets are to be equal, a portfolio where the risk contributions

2.5
Backtesting
A major distinction between finance-related topics (and economics in general) and a
pure science subject is the fact that it is impossible to conduct experimental tests on
theories.
Access to financial data is very limited. Data can not be generated by experimen-
tation (pure sciences can, for example, produce data in a lab or by collecting samples).
Also, if a hypothesis is verified for a collection of financial data it is not guaranteed
that such a hypothesis will originate similar results in the future.
The fact of the matter is that, in finance and economics, only one real path of
observations exits and one must work around the limitations that ensue from that.
All of the above makes achieving a consensus in financial topics much more diﬀicult
than in other sciences. Ideology and political biases might also interfere with one’s
inferences withing such topics.
The best tool a portfolio manager has to give fundamental to its portfolio construc-
tion strategies is backtesting. Backtesting relies on historical data to test investment
strategies. Following Prado (2018a,b, 2019b), there are 3 types of backtesting that
can be performed:
2.5.1
Walk-forward method
The walk-forward method is the simpler backtesting of the 3. In this method, the
investment strategy is applied over a past period of time.
One should be careful
to avoid including biased information from the period used in backtesting as such
will likely lead to overfitting. For example, if we chose to backtest, using the Walk-
forward method, over the 2005-2020 period one should avoid making decisions on the
investment strategy used based on events or data from the same period (e.g., 2007-
2008 financial crisis). In our case, the 2005-2020 period is reserved for backtesting,
and decisions regarding factors are made using data from 1990-2004.
We could say that the main assumption of the walk-forward method is that history
repeats itself. Such an assumption is rather weak as there is no guarantee about the
market behavior for future times. For example, if an investor chose to backtest a new
strategy over the last decade, they might be overwhelmed by the strategy performance
in 2020 (high global market stress due to worldwide lockdowns). Therefore, when
doing this sort of backtesting, it is a good idea to do some sort of stress testing before
implementing any strategy or have both bearish and bullish market periods included
in the backtest.
Results from a walk-forward backtest should be taken more as descriptive instead
of inferential.

20

2.5.2
Resampling method
The Resampling method works by simulating several future observation paths. Even
if the focus is on a future period, the future scenarios simulated are created by resam-
pling observations from the past.
Cross-validation and bootstrapping are commonly used methods for resampling
financial data that have seen a lot of use outside financial backtesting applications as
well(e.g., used extensively in machine learning application).
According to Prado (2019b) an advantage of the Resampling method is that over-
fitting is less likely to occur when compared to the walk-forward method. Also, the
fact that an investor has a plethora of different future scenarios available enables
them to build a strategy with more inelastic returns across market cycles.
The main problem with the resampling method is that due to the limited nature
of the historical data used for resampling the simulations obtained might, like past
observations, not be a good representation of the actual future financial data.
Another problem more specific to this project is that to make good use of simula-
tions one must run algorithms multiple times which creates a problem for a method
like Factor Risk Parity as we will see later 3.
2.5.3
Monte Carlo method
In the Monte Carlo backtesting method, like in the resampling previously, future
simulations are created.
The difference is that such simulations involve more in-
depth statistical and theoretical knowledge on the market. Besides historical data
of firms, other parameters are used such as relations between firms, industry sector,
supply chain commonalities, etc.
Out of the three methods here describes Monte Carlo is the most complex but it
can be argued that it is also the stronger amongst the 3 if properly implemented.
Due to being a method that gives origins to several simulation paths its application
to our Factor Risk Parity present the same challenges as the resampling method.

Moreover, the fact that we are using pre-built factors for the backtesting period
it is not possible to use such factor returns directly on simulated data (factor returns
data would have to be part of each simulation as well).

In the present section, I propose the use of cluster analysis methods for factor

Cluster analysis as a technique precedes technological advances such as the per-
sonal computer, and it has been used in a plethora of areas from social sciences,
biology, and many more. It gained a lot of popularity as a frequently used method
within machine learning algorithms.
Following Wilks (2011) cluster analysis is a data analysis tool that attempts to
group data identifiers in different levels of aggregation. In general, clustering works
by calculating the distance between each pair of identifiers. One is not constrained to
the Euclidean distance. A lot of different types of distances have been proposed over
the years. Pairwise distances are cached into a matrix, the distance matrix. Then,
iteratively, the pair of identifiers with the smalled distance in the distance matrix is
clustered together and those two identifiers are replaced by the cluster in the distance
matrix (several methods exist to assign distances between clusters).

correlation is undesirable.

by investing in factor A, an investor is not only gaining exposure to factor A but
also to a factor B to which A is positively correlated. Assume that another factor,
C, is negatively correlated to A. Then by investing in A one will reduce it’s exposure
to C which is also unwanted in a Factor Risk Parity model as the exposure to A is,
proportionally to the total portfolio factor exposure, too high (underexposure to C
and, thus, overexposure to risk factor A).

that penalizes both negative and positive correlation. I propose the distance present

4.5
Maximum Drawdown
The concept of Drawdown has been previously explained in section 3.1. Recall that
drawdown is, at any given time, a measure of the loss percentage between the last
peak in price (or cumulative returns) and the current price (or cumulative return).
The maximum drawdown is the value of the biggest (negative) drawdown in the
entire period the portfolio is in place. It can be seen as the biggest loss an investor
might have suffered by investing in the portfolio. To incur such loss an investor would
have to be made in the peak before the maximum drawdown and retired on the valley
date (the lower cumulative) of the largest drawdown.
The maximum drawdown is an important measure to evaluate the potential for
losses of a portfolio and more risk-averse investors will pay extra attention to such
indicator.
Such as volatility is used in the Sharpe ratio calculation, the maximum drawdown
equivalent is the Calmar Ratio.

If a portfolio is relatively stable over an entire period but has a very accentuated
quick peak to valley drawdown the Sharpe ratio won’t be affected nearly as much as
the Calmar ratio. Therefore it is useful to observe both ratios.

Value at Risk (VaR)
In brief terms Value at risk (VaR) is a risk measure used to evaluate the risk of
incurring extreme losses given a confidence level. The most used confidence level is
95% (followed by 99%). In simple terms, in our portfolios VaR is obtained, for a
confidence level of 95%, as the 5th percentile of the distribution of daily returns. For
the example of the EW portfolio, in table 4.1, we can see that the daily VaR is 1.82%.

4.13
Alpha & Beta
The terms alpha (α) and beta (β) used in asset management originate from the
concept of linear regression. As for performance measures, for the case in question
stands from equation 4.16.
r = α + βrm + ϵ
(4.16)
where r is a vector of return of a portfolio (or asset outside our scope), rm is the
vector of market returns (S&P 500 index in our case), and ϵ is the regression error.
The Capital Asset Pricing Model (CAPM) recontructs such regression with the
inclusion of the risk free rate:
r −rF = α + β(rm −rF ) + ϵ
(4.17)
Note that the Fama/French and Carhart equations (2.11, 2.13 & 2.12) previously
mentioned are derivation of the CAPM but with the inclusion of multiple risk factors
in the regression. The α and β estimated from 4.17, after annualizing α are our desire
measures.
Beta measures the degree to which the volatility of the portfolio is related to the
volatility of the market (systematic risk in the portfolio). If β is higher then the
portfolio is very reactionary to market shifts and less reactionary the lower it is.
Alpha, the intercept in the CAPM equation, reflects the portion of returns not
related to the market.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
The main objective set for this project was to create a portfolio that used a risk
parity approach on risk factor and that outperformed, not only the market, but also
a risk parity portfolio on the equity level. The FRP portfolio implemented was has
less inherent risk that the RP portfolio but such came as a consequence of also lower
annualized returns (11.89 % for the FRP and 13.54% for the FRP). The sharpe ration
obtained was aproximadelly for both this portfolios. THe maximum drawdown for
the FRP portfolio was −33.3% which represents a big improvement from the RP’s
−46.8%. The lower risk of the FRP portfolio was enough to counterbalance the lower
returns generating higher Calmar and Tail ratios.
The problem with the first implementation of the FRP was that Risk factor
weights were too restricted. Such is evident by looking at the structure of the risk con-
tributions of factors over the portfolio’s backtest period. Then, by allowing negative
values of factors (which can be interpreted as shorting risk factors) until a limit of
−1
m for each of the m factors the results obtained were much better without intaking
any more leverage or increasing equities shorting budget.
With this relaxation in place annualized returns amounted to 14.99% and the
annual volatility was reduced to 13.46%. This portfolio not only outperformed the
previous FRP but also the RP portfolio, hence fulfilling the objective of the thesis.
On the 2008 financial crisis the max drawdown was of −26.96% which, while still
high, is an improvement from all other portfolios presented here. The alpha from
FRP was also higher than for the RP. In RP beta was 0.94, close to the S&P 500
index returns while in the FRP, beta was much lower (0.46 for the best performing
portfolio) which indicates a bigger detachment to market volatility.
The attempt to cluster factors by intersection (combining correlated factors) gen-
erated the portfolio with the lowest volatility but at the cost of lower returns. And,
the attempt to cluster factors by making them share risk contributions was unsuc-
cessful in generating a good portfolio. There was clearly some flaws with this last
FRP strategy regarding overexposure to certain factors. This gives the perspective
that it is better to use a more limited set of uncorrelated risk factors than to try to
include too many factors in the same portfolio.

6 Conclusion
6.1
Future Work
The selection of factors and assets within this project was rather limited. The fact
that only big cap stocks were used (S&P 500) might have limited the influence of
certain factors such as the size factor (SMB) that was of the constituents of the FRP
approach here applied.
The fact that the FRP strategy as implemented takes between 20 to 60 minutes
(depending on the factors used) to fully run on the S&P 500 for out backtest period
of 15 years made certain analysis impossible. Also, if implemented on a larger set
of assets the amount of running time might be unfeasible even for each portfolio
re structuring period. Most of the algorithm relies of performing the same tasks a
number of times. Therefore, a way to expedite running times would be to parallelize
the algorithm.
During the development of this project the world was hit by a lockdown due to the
coronavirus Covid-19. This caused many sectors of the economy to slowdown or even
shutdown causing a financial crisis that has been regarded as worse than the 2008
financial crisis. The global economy is currently facing a recession and the future is
the full extent of the damage to the world economy is not yet known. It will be of
interest to see how this FRP approach will behave on such period.
6.2
Self-Critique
In this small section I will go over some of the parts of this project that I find particular
weak and what I could have done better or done differently.
In the beginning of the project my approach to literature was too broad which
caused some of the topics I read about not to be implemented and perhaps not enough
depth was given to the ones that ended up in the project.
At the start of this project I decided to code my implementation in python. At
that time I had almost no experience with this language and initially I focused to
much on using existing packages that later did not provide the flexibility I wanted
regarding leverage and shorting. At the end of this project this is still the case for the
RP method. Too much time was used trying to build my code around this packages
before realizing that I would be better off building my own methods.
There was to much hesitation regarding how to report my results, which caused
the writing of my implementation chapter to be too rushed. Leverage and shorting
across portfolios could have been reported better.
