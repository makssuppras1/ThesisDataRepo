Abstract
The consumption of building materials has a signiﬁcant and increasing impact on the overall environ-
mental impacts of buildings, therefore it is important already in the early design stage to make informed
and environmentally friendly decisions related to the choice of materials.
The purpose of this project is to develop guidelines that enable sustainable decisions in the ﬁrst stages
of the building design. The guidelines have information about which components in the building that
need to be in focus to make the most sustainable choices when constructing a building. The guidelines
are based on hotspot analyses made on three different types of buildings: a single family house, a nine
story residential building and a multifunctional building. From each building type there are made two
different versions of the buildings, to see if the hotspot is changing, if some of the components in the
buildings are changed or if hotspots in the buildings are optimized. Besides making the hotspot analyses
of the buildings a comparison is made to compare the professional LCA tool SimaPro and the ﬁeld tool
LCAbyg.
The hotspot analyses for the buildings show that the hotspots for the single family house are the use
stage (the operation) and the outer walls. For the nine story residential building, the hotspots are the
outer walls and the inner walls that are non load-bearing and for the multifunctional building they are
the use stage and the outer walls. The hotspots for the buildings do not change when the components,
where the hotspot occurs, are optimized.
When comparing the two LCA tools SimaPro and LCAbyg it is discovered that the buildings total results
from SimaPro for all the buildings is higher than the buildings total results from LCAbyg. Both LCA
tools have, in this project, the same trends when looking at the results for the components and both
programs shows the same hotspots for the buildings.
Keywords
Life Cycle Assessment on buildings, Hotspot analysis on buildings, Comparison between LCAbyg and
SimaPro.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation for the study
10 to 15 percent of the entire CO2 emissions come from traditional building materials. When consider-
ing transportation and the building processes as well, the number just increases [1]. The consumption of
building materials have a signiﬁcant and increasing impact on the overall environmental impact of new
and renovated buildings. Therefore it is important already in the early design stage to make informed
decisions related to the choice of materials. However, in order to make informed decisions more knowl-
edge about where the environmental impacts occur in buildings and which material composition is best
for the environment is needed.
More knowledge about the hotspots in the different types of buildings is needed to make good and
important choices when new buildings are build, therefore hotspot analyses are made. These analyses
shows the components in the buildings that have the biggest environmental impact. This report will
provide knowledge about three different types of buildings: a single family house, a nine story residential
building and a multifunctional building. This will be explained in detail in the next section 1.2 Case study
and in section 2 Presentation of scenarios.
1.2
Case study
This project was made as a part of a master thesis for the MSc in Architectural engineering at the
Technical University of Denmark, DTU, in cooperation with Niras. The target audiences for this project
are consulting building engineers.
The purpose of this project was to develop guidelines that enable sustainable decisions in the ﬁrst stages
of the building design. The guidelines contain information about which component of the building that
need to be in focus to make the most sustainable choices. Furthermore, it will also be possible to see
which material composition that has the least impact on the Life Cycle and therefore will be the most
sustainable choice.
This case study was based on different types of buildings. The data for the buildings was received from
Niras. A Life Cycle Assessment was conducted for a cradle to grave analysis for the different scenarios
for the buildings.
The study consisted of three building types and is presented as scenario 1,2 and 3. Additionally, two
different scenarios for each building type was made as seen below.
Scenario 1 is a single family house.
• Scenario 1A: is a single family house made of wood.
• Scenario 1B: is a single family house made of stone.
Scenario 2 is a nine story residential building.
• Scenario 2A: is a nine story residential building - Original, which is made of stone.
• Scenario 2B: is a nine story residential building - Optimized, with have a steel construction.
Scenario 3 is a multifunctional building consisting of an ofﬁce, hotel, cafe, super market, parking in
the basement and rooms for technical installations.
• Scenario 3A: is a multifunctional building - Original.
• Scenario 3B: is a multifunctional building - Optimized, with smaller number of outer wall- and
ﬂoor types.

3.1
Life Cycle Assessment on buildings
Life Cycle assessment, also called LCA, is a holistic method to evaluate the environmental impact on
products, product systems or services. In this project, it is included in the hole product systems lifespan.
This is also called a cradle to grave analysis, which means the analysis is looking at all the processes
and emissions from the extraction of the materials to the End of Life (EoL) phase for the product or
products. For this project it means that the analyses are looking at everything from the excavations of
raw materials to the demolition and EoL of the building. All the main steps between the two stages can
be seen in Figure 13, which shows all the typical stages for a LCA on a building.
Figure 13: Life Cycle for buildings [13]
As seen in Figure 13 the main stages of a LCA for a building are:
1. The product stage, where all the raw materials are excavated and new materials/products are
made. This stage includes the fossils fuels for transportation, the emissions of gasses in the pro-
duction, the waste of materials in the production as well as the energy from the excavation and the
production that are contributing to the environmental impacts.
2. The construction stage, where the building is constructed and everything is installed. In this
stage it is the energy, the material waste and the fossil fuels for the crane and transportation that
are contributing to the environmental impacts.
3. The use stage, where the maintenance, repair, replacement, energy, heat and water use is included.
In this stage it is the water consumption, fossil fuels and renewable energy used to make heat and
electricity and all the impacts there occur in the production stage which are contributing to the
environmental impacts.
4. The EoL stage, where the demolition, waste sorting and landﬁll are included. In this stage it
is the fossils fuels used for transportation and gasses from landﬁll that are contributing to the
environmental impacts.
12
==PAGE:21==
5. The next product system, or the Beneﬁt and load beyond the system boundary as it is called in
Figure 13, where some of the materials are recycled or incinerated and used as heating. In this
stage it is the energy used in the incineration of the materials and the energy used to recycle or
renew materials are contributing to the environmental impacts.
These stages is divided into smaller stages in this project, to make it easier to handle a big product
system which contain many processes and materials.

3.2
Simulations tools
In this project two different LCA simulation tools are used, a professional tool and a ﬁeld tool, which
will be presented in this section.
3.2.1
SimaPro
SimaPro is a professional LCA tool that analyse the environmental aspects of products, product systems
or services. SimaPro is a tool in which all the processes are made by the user and the process can be
assembled and modulated as the user would like within the framework of the software. SimaPro is used
in many different companies and for many kinds of environmental investigations. SimaPro is available
in multiple versions and have included a large set of inventory data and impact assessment methods,
SimaPro’s data is from ecoinvent version 3.5 (2018) [10].
The version used in this project is SimaPro 8.5.
In SimaPro the Cut-off system is used, which means that the primary production of the materials are
always allocated to the primary user of the material. If a material is recycled, the primary producer
does not receive any credit for the provision of any recyclable materials. As a consequence, recyclable
materials are available burden-free to recycling processes, and secondary, the recycled, materials bear
only the impacts of the recycling processes and the producers of wastes do not receive any credit for
recycling or reusing of products resulting out of any waste treatment. Nevertheless, the incineration is
allocated completely to the treatment of the waste, and therefore the burdens lay with the waste producer.
The heat comes burden-free [2].
3.2.2
LCAbyg
LCAbyg is a LCA software that is developed to make simple and fast LCA on buildings by using
a simple set of data. The data used in LCAbyg are from ¨OKOBAUDAT, in which the data mainly
are collected in Germany. With LCAbyg it is possible to calculate a building’s environmental proﬁle
and resource consumption. The information needed are information about the building parts and the
buildings energy consumption. The tool automatically takes care of the LCA calculations and gather the
results in a report [14].
When making a LCA in LCAbyg it is important to know that some simpliﬁcations in the program are
made. The simpliﬁcations are due to lack of data and practical reasons [5]. The stages that are taking
into consideration can be seen in Figure 15. Some of the stages are not considered because adequate
data is missing, and experience has shown that some of these stages have a very small impact on the end
result [5].
Figure 15: The buildings life cycle phases and organization of the different modules which includes in the LCA of
a building which follows DGNB [5]

og Byggestyrelsen. LCAbyg uses the method CML 2001, World 2000 (update: April 2015). The version

3.2.3
Lifespan
The lifespan for the materials used in LCAbyg and SimaPro is the Lifespan table from the Statens
Byggeinstitut (SBI), which is implemented in LCAbyg. The lifespan of the materials is from the time a
building part is installed, to the time it is dismounted or do not life up to the wishes properties.
In LCAbyg the calculation period is determined according to EN1578:2012, where the lifespan of the
element is looked at in the perspective of the building’s lifespan. Materials are not replaced in the last
1/3 of the building’s lifespan, unless the element has a short lifespan [11].

4.2
Scope and design
The deliverable that will accompany this study is a LCA of three different types of buildings which is
subdivided into two different versions of the buildings, in which the hotspots and the material choices
will be analysed. The LCA will be made using two different programs, LCAbyg and SimaPro. Addi-
tionally, a comparison between the two programs will be made and the results will be evaluated.
4.3
Life Cycle Inventory
The life cycle inventory consists of:
• A product stage, in which the raw materials are extracted and the products are produced.
• A assembly stage, in which the buildings are constructed and all the building elements and com-
ponents are assembled.
• A use stage, in which the buildings are in operation, which consist of an electricity and heat
consumption.
• A replacement stage, in which the materials are replaced. The lifespan for the materials are de-
cided by using a lifespan standard.
• A demolition stage, in which the buildings are demolished.
• A sorting stage in which the building elements are sorted.
• A EoL stage, in which the building elements is recycled, incinerated or driven to landﬁll.
Between many of the stages there is a transportation stage. The ﬂow of the life cycle inventory can be
seen in Figure 16.

Single family house: It is assumed for a single family house that it takes 4 months to construct the
building [6]. There are used 3 hand tools and they are used in 80 workdays, 8 hours a day and the crane

Nine story residential building: For the nine story residential building it is assumed that it takes 1.5
year to construct the building [6] and there are used 10 hand tools and they are used in one year, 8 hours
on workdays. A crane is in use one year, 8 hours every workday.
Multifunctional building: It is assumed for the multifunctional building that it takes 2 years to con-

4.11
End of life
The EoL stage is where all the materials are recycled, incinerated or driven to the landﬁll. If materials
are recycled or incinerated will avoided processes occur. Avoided processes are gained material. For
example, 95 percent of the steel used in this analysis are recycled, meaning 95 percent are used to
make new steel. Therefore an avoided process of new steel occurs. This also happens when materials
are incinerated, instead of an avoided process consisting of the materials that are incinerated, as in the
recycling process, it is heat to the district heating system which are gained. When materials are deposed
there are nothing to gain, therefore do avoided processes not occur here. When materials are incinerated,
only emissions of different gases, depending of which materials that is deposed, are released.
The EoL stage is different for each material and can also be different between the two programs LCAbyg
and SimaPro. An example of this can be that the insulation of mineral wool in LCAbyg has EoL process
called Affald, bygge, deponering and in SimaPro the EoL process is Waste mineral wool [CH], treatment
of, recycling, Cut-off, U and a avoided process Glass wool mat [GLO], market for, Cut-off, U. This means
that the mineral wool in LCAbyg is not recycled but it is in SimaPro. This differences appears because
generic LCAbyg processes are chosen.
The EoL processes for the materials in LCAbyg can be seen in Appendix B for all the scenarios and in
SimaPro in Appendix D.

5.1
Assumptions
Many assumptions are made for the input for the LCA in this project. If the input for an LCA is
unrealistic, the end result for the analyses will also be unrealistic. Therefore it is important to have
realistic input. To ensure the input in this project is realistic a wide range of knowledge from the
employees at Niras is drawn on to qualify the input.
The lifespan table from Statens Byggeinstitut [11] is used for all the products except the wooden facade
for scenario 1, because the facade in scenario 1 is not treated with paint or wood oil, like the wooden
facades in the lifespan table. Therefore the facade are split into two where 75 percent of the facade has
a lifespan of 50 years, while the last 25 percent has a lifespan of 20 years.
5.1.1
Limitations
The study is delimited by focusing on the main components and elements in a building. This means
that the analyses are not including screws, connections and small parts. This decision is based on the
knowledge that these parts has a very small impact on the overall analyses. This corresponds to the
simpliﬁed method described in DGNB [4], the differences between this method and how this project is
made is that DGNB accounts for this by multiplying the LCA result with a factor of 1.1.
Not all the products could be found in the database in LCAbyg or SimaPro, so similar products or
simpliﬁed products has been chosen. The actual product chosen in LCAbyg and SimaPro can be seen in
Appendices A for LCAbyg and C for SimaPro.
It was not possible to ﬁnd an EPD for a Vinyl ﬂooring to LCAbyg, so a linoleum ﬂoor was used. In
SimaPro it wasn’t possible to ﬁnd a linoleum ﬂoor, hence a vinyl ﬂooring was used.

Comparison of scenarios
In this section the results from SimaPro are compared within Scenario 1, Scenario 2 and Scenario 3
and shown for the environmental impact categories: Human health, Ecosystems and Resources using
method ReCiPe 2016 Endpoint (H) V1.02.
The results for Human Health are given in DALY, which means the Disability-adjusted life year, this can
be thought of as a measurement of the gap between current health status and an ideal health situation
where the entire population lives to an advanced age, free of disease and disability [17]. The results
for Ecosystems are giving in species.yr, which is a method to measure an extinction rate. There are
approximately one extinction estimated per million species-years. This means that if there are a million
species on Earth, one would go extinct every year, while if there was only one species, it would go
extinct in one million years [7]. The results for Resources are giving in USD2013, which are US dollars
for the reference year 2013.
It has not been possible to investigate which emissions in the different materials that have an impact
on the total result. This has not been possible because the method chosen for the model makes is very
difﬁcult.
The results from the analyses can be seen in Tables in Appendices J and the results for all the categories
using ReCiPe 2016 Midpoint (H), can be seen in Appendices K.

Sensitivity analyses
The sensitivity analyses are made to see if the mass input in the LCA is sensitive for the outer walls,
inner walls and the transportation for all the scenarios. The outer and inner wall are chosen because
it was seen in the result from Section 7 Results that these components are the hotspots in most of the
scenarios. The transportation are chosen because the assumption for the distances in the transportation
is based on which producers, reuse facilities, recycle-, land ﬁlling- and incineration plants that could be
found on Google maps and the municipalities homepages how to handle building waste. There is not
made sensitivity analyses for the buildings use although it is the hotspot in 4 out of 6 scenarios because
this stage is a calculated energy frame, which is calculated by standard calculation which is not looked
at in this project.
The sensitivity analyses are calculated for the buildings total result, to investigate if there is big changes
for the buildings total result when the mass in the components are changed. If there are big changes in
the buildings total result are the mass input for that component sensitive.
The sensitivity analyses in this section are made in SimaPro with method CML-IA baseline V3.05 /
EU25 and are only showed for the environmental impact Global Warming Potential.

Discussion
The discussion is divided into four main sections. In the Modelling section there will be discussed
which other methods or initiatives that could have been used in the project. The next section Discussion
of results from LCAbyg and SimaPro looks at the result from Section 7.1, the penultimate section Com-
parison of Scenarios discusses the result from Section 7.2. The last section Discussion of the sensitivity
analyses looks at the sensitivity analyses made in Section 8 and discuss the result from the analyses.
9.1
Modelling
The use stage in SimaPro could be more precise if it was possible to make a dynamic heat and electricity
mix. When looking at the future dynamic heat and electricity mix used in this project it can be seen that
the difference between the estimations made for year 25, as chosen for the use stage in the project, and
the ﬁnal year is very small. If another estimation was used for a future dynamic heat and electricity mix
it could make a differences for the result. If the electricity and heat mix were more environmentally
friendly the use stage (operation) would have less impact on the buildings total result.
If another lifespan was used in this project, the results would have been different. There are different
variation of lifespans, a technical lifespan, a functional lifespan, a economic lifespan and a aesthetic
lifespan, these lifespans are looked at and compared in the article Levetider af bygningsdele ved vurder-
ing af bæredygtighed og totaløkonomi written by byggeforskningsinstitut (SBI)[11] where it is showed
how complex it is to set a lifespan for a material, because many parameters have a bearing in this matter.
One of the challenges with the lifespan are that it can differ where and how the materials are used, the
lifespan also depends on the maintenance of the materials. It can also depend on where the building
is build, if the building is build near beaches, materials there can handle the high amount of salt need
to be chosen. There is also places in the buildings where the lifespan do not matter, for example if the
insulation’s lifespan in a stone walls is expired it will not be replaced. This is due to that it is not possible
to replace it without destroying the concrete or bricks in the stone wall which has a higher lifespan than
the insulation. From the analyses it can be seen that the amount of material used has a big impact on the
buildings total result.
There occurs a paradox when looking at the lifespan used in this project, because the estimations made
in the lifespan from SBI [11] does not always comply with the calculation method for load-bearing
structures or the real life experience. The lifespan for a load-bearing structure according to the lifespan
from SBI [11] is 120 years, but the lifespan within the calculation for the carrying capacity is only 50
years. This means that the lifespan for load bearing structures are not with certainty 120 years, but may
only be 50 years. An example where the lifespan from SBI does not comply with the real life experience
is for a facade of bricks and mortar which has a lifespan of 120 years, but in reality there is a need for
replacing some of the bricks and adding new mortar before the span of 120 year.
The EoL is based on knowledge from different manufactures and from employees at Niras. It is possible
that the materials or products used in this project has another EoL stage in other contexts or for another
building. When the buildings are demolished it is not certain that the materials will be sorted correctly or
recycled, incineration or land ﬁlling as it is chosen in this project and therefore the buildings may have
a higher or lower environmental impact in reality. There is an expectation that the recycling of materials
will increasingly become more in focus and the use of recycled materials in the future as well, which in
return will make new buildings more environmentally friendly.
In the modelling method, used in SimaPro, it has not been possible to see which emissions comes
from the different material choices inside the components. If more time was at hand or this problem
was discovered earlier in the project the building would have been remodelled in another way to make
it possible to investigate this. One possibility of the remodelling could have been to modulate each
material/product and its replacement stage and EoL stage in one process instead of dividing it into
components.

9.3
Comparison of scenarios
When making LCA on buildings it is important to compare the total building result, because when ma-
terials are changed in the different components it can change other stages for example the transportation
stage.
Some of the environmentally heavy materials does not have the most environmental impact on the total
building result, because a big part of the material can be recycled in the EoL stage. This occurs for
example regarding the steel, where 95 percent of the materials are recycled. The bricks and concrete are
recycled as well as the steel, but the bricks and concrete are recycled as gravel. The differences between
the two recycling processes are that the avoided processes have a different impact on the result. Steel is
a very environmentally heavy process, so when steel can be recycled another steel production is avoided
and lot of environmental impacts is saved compared to if raw materials was needed. When concrete or
bricks is recycled as gravel, there is not save as much as in the recycling process, because the extraction
of gravel does not have as heavy burden production like steel.
From the comparison of the scenarios it was seen that Scenario 1A had less impact on the overall
environmental impact than Scenario 1B and the hotspots for both scenarios were the use and the outer
walls for all three environmental impacts. It was also seen that scenario 2B has less impact on the overall
environmental impact than Scenario 2A and the hotspots for both scenarios were the outer walls and the
inner walls that are non load-bearing for all three environmental impacts. For Scenario 3 it was seen that
Scenario 3B had less impact on the overall environmental impact than Scenario 3A and the hotspots for
both scenarios for all three environmental impacts were the use and the outer walls. In the comparison
it was seen that the hotspots does not change when optimizing the building components in this project.

There is not made a sensitivity analyses on all the components in the buildings because the outer and
inner walls have the biggest impact on the total results for the buildings, except the use which is not

Conclusion
It can be concluded that the results of the analyses made in SimaPro have a bigger impact on the envi-
ronment than the anlyses LCAbyg in all scenarios. Both programs have, in this project, the same trends
when looking at the results for the building components. Both programs, in almost all the scenarios,
also showed the same hotspots for the buildings. In the comparison it was seen that the hotspots does
not change when optimizing the building components that was the hotspot in the scenarios.
It can be concluded that a single family house made of wood overall has less environmental impacts than
a single family house made of stone. However the single family house of wood has a bigger impact on
the impact category Ecosystems, which can be explained by that the single family house of wood uses
a bigger amount of materials than the single family house of stone, because wood has a shorter lifespan
than bricks and concrete. The hotspot for both houses are the use stage and next the outer walls.
When comparing Scenario 2A and 2B, the nine story residential buildings, Scenario 2B are the most sus-
tainable solution. The differences between the scenarios are very small for the impacts categories Human
Health and Ecosystems, but there are a differences when looking at the impact category Resources. The
hotspot for both scenarios are the outer walls and next the inner walls that are non load-bearing, this
does not change by changing the outer walls from concrete to a steel structure.
When comparing Scenario 3A and 3B, Scenario 3B is the most sustainable solution. The differences
between the scenarios are very small for the impacts categories Human Health and Ecosystem, but there
is a difference when looking at the impact category Resources. The hotspot for both scenarios are the
use and next the outer walls, this does not change by optimizing the outer walls.
10.1
Design Guide for Sustainable Buildings
These conclusions for the Design Guide for Sustainable Buildings are based on the analyses made in
LCAbyg and SimaPro and the comparisons of the scenarios made in SimaPro.
10.1.1
Single family house
When building a single family house no matter if it is a wooden house or a stone house the most
important components in the building are the outer walls, therefore it is very important to choose envi-
ronmentally friendly materials or products here. The next building component that needs to be look at
in the design phase differs depending on the material used in the building. If it is a wooden house it is
the inner walls that needs to be a focus on, but if it is a stone house it is the ground slab, the inner walls
and the roof that needs to be in focus.
10.1.2
Nine story residential building
When building a nine story residential building it is also the outer walls that need to be in focus, even if
the outer walls are made of stone or steel. So here it is very important to choose environmental friendly
materials or products. The load-bearing inner walls for this building have the same structure as the
outer walls, therefore these materials should be the same as the outer walls. Another important focus
area when building this type of building is the inner walls which are non load-bearing, these walls has a
bigger percentage impact on the overall result for the nine story residential building with a steel structure
than on a nine story residential building made of stone, but the inner walls should still be in focus for
both scenarios.
10.1.3
Multifunctional building
When building a multifunctional building it is the outer walls as well as, in the other building types, that
need to be in focus as the ﬁrst thing in the early design phase. In this building there is already thought
of this by using recycled bricks in the heavy walls, but still other alternatives are proven better than the
92
==PAGE:101==
heavy outer wall composition used in the original multifunctional building. The next component that
needs to be in focus is the ﬂoors for the original building, here can it be seen from the optimized building,
that by changing the many types of ﬂooring to a fewer amount and by chosen the most environmentally
friendly ﬂooring types the environmental impact can be minimized. If the ﬂoors are optimized as in the
multifunctional building that is optimized the focus next should be on the inner walls and the solar cells,
therefore these building components should also be in focus when designing this type of building.
