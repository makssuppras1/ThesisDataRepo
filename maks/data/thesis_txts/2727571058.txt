Abstract
The current thesis aims to investigate the suitability of a novel friction grip connection detail
for directly connecting boat landings to wind turbine monopiles, eliminating the need for a
transition piece. This innovative connection detail has the potential to reduce manufacturing
and installation costs of offshore wind turbine monopile foundations without relying on bolts
or welding. The primary focus is to assess the load-carrying performance of the connection
detail under multi-axial loading conditions. A 2D finite element model of the grip connec-
tion detail is generated, incorporating geometric non-linear analysis and non-linear contact
conditions. Initially, an investigation is conducted to assess the load-carrying capacity and
displacement behavior of the rotational key parts under uni-axial loading conditions, iden-
tifying distinct failure modes and their causes. The study progresses to evaluate the grip
connection’s performance under multi-axial loading conditions, arising from wave loads, run-
up loads, and vessel forces, based on three distinct loading sequence scenarios, considering
also design gaps. The findings serve as a basis for assessing the feasibility and performance
of the novel friction grip connection. Notably, a strong coupling between bending moments
and axial forces is observed, affecting the overall connection performance. This coupling
phenomenon highlights a limitation in the connection’s bending moment carrying capacity
under multi-axial loading conditions while rendering it inefficient in resisting pure bending
moments in sequential loading conditions.

Grip connection detail at the boat landing comprising four fundamental com-
ponents.
1) Rotational cam: Mechanical rotary key part featuring an in-
creasing radius that effectively limits the connection’s vertical upward motion
by activating a friction-based self-locking mechanism. 2) Inner tube: Base
structure accommodating the rotational cams through bolted joints. 3) Outer
tube: Secondary structure securely attached to the monopile. 4) Ring: Sup-
port structure at the top and bottom of the connection which contributes to
the system’s performance under multi-axial loading conditions. . . . . . . . .

1.1
Background
Monopile foundations are widely used in the offshore wind industry, being the most common
foundation type for offshore wind turbine generators (WTG), as they offer several advan-
tages compared to other offshore WTG foundation configurations. These advantages include
structural simplicity and cost-effectiveness in terms of manufacturing, transportation, and
installation [3, 4]. The concept of a transition piece (TP)-less monopile has emerged as an
innovative alternative that eliminates the need for a separate transition piece.

The mechanical rotary key parts, known as rotational cams, are designed with increasing
radii and play a critical role in establishing a reliable linkage. The primary purpose of the
cams is to confine the vertical upward movement of the connection to the direction of the
applied forces by activating the self-locking capability, which relies on friction. The variation
in cam radii plays a crucial role in achieving the desired self-locking effect. Since the cam
radius increases, it enhances the frictional resistance between the cams and the outer tube
surface.
The inner tube serves as a base structure, accommodating the rotational cams
through bolted joints, while the outer tube serves as the structure securely attached to the
monopile. Finally, rings are located at the top and bottom edges of the connection and serve
as supportive structures, particularly under multi-axial loading conditions.

1.3
Literature review
Self-locking mechanisms are an important class of mechanical devices that have been widely
used in various fields, for instance in automotive engineering, to improve the reliability and
safety of machines and equipment [5,6].
These are engineered to restrict any undesired motion in one direction while permitting free
movement in the opposite direction. These mechanisms typically consist of two interacting
components: a driver and a driven member. The driver exerts a force on the driven member,
resulting in its motion toward the intended direction [7]. Nonetheless, when an external force
attempts to move the driven member in the opposite direction, the self-locking mechanism
engages and prevents the movement [1].
In the realm of pipeline engineering, pipelines are commonly utilized for a variety of purposes
including water supply, transportation of oil and gas, and heat exchange [8,9]. In-pipe robots
have proven to be appropriate for tasks related to inspecting and maintaining pipelines [10].
A wide range of different in-pipe robot configurations are introduced in [11], following a
chronological order back to the 1950s, where the initial automated device that could gather
data regarding the state of the internal surface of pipelines consisted of a pig-type in-pipe
robot [12]. Additional types derived later on, such as the wheel type [13], walking type [14]
as well as inchworm type [15].
The peristaltic motion of inchworm in-pipe robots effectively utilizes friction by maximizing
the contact area between the robot and the inner wall of the pipe [16]. The development of
self-locking mechanisms continued till nowadays, with a number of different configurations,
aiming to optimize the traction ability and fast movement of the in-pipe robot, including
elastic leg clamps [17], wedge-based [18] as well as cam-based locomotion system [1]. Figure
3 illustrates the locomotion principle of continuous propulsion in-pipe robot, illustrating the
structure of the cam locking mechanism as well as the mechanical model of the cam [1].

The study specifically investigates the suitability of applying inchworm-type

the boat landing to the monopile, the incentive is to simplify the installation process of the
TP-less design, reduce the number of components and avoid on-site welding and bolting
operations [19]. This streamlined approach has the potential to significantly reduce manu-
facturing and installation cost and time associated with the monopile foundation [20].

Mesh discretization
The domain of interest is divided into a finite number of elements, which represents the

more accurate solutions as they capture the behavior of the design model more precisely
across its physical domain. However, this comes at the cost of longer runtimes. Therefore, it
is crucial to find a balance between accuracy and computational efficiency. In this study, a
range of decreasing element sizes is examined, as seen in table 13 in the Appendix, and the
normal pressure of the contact point is measured, shown in figure 51 in the Appendix. Based
on the analysis, a characteristic size of 0.5 mm is chosen. It is stated that more detailed
investigations were carried out at the contact area by further reducing the element size to

2.4.3
Contact interaction
The interaction between contacting surfaces comprises two distinct components: the normal
component, which acts perpendicular to the surfaces, and the tangential component, which
acts parallel to the surfaces. The tangential component is defined by relative motion, or
sliding, between the surfaces, and can result in the generation of frictional shear stresses.
Each contact interaction can be associated with a specific contact property that describes
the model used to represent the interaction between the contacting surfaces [2]. Based on
ABAQUS-provided contact interaction models, the behavior normal and tangential to the
surfaces is introduced below.
2.4.4
Pressure-overclosure relationship
The pressure-overclosure formulation in contact interaction refers to the modeling of the
normal component of contact forces between surfaces and describes how the contact pressure
distribution evolves as the surfaces come into contact and deform. This formulation takes into
account the deformation and penetration of the contacting surfaces [2]. Direct enforcement
of "hard" contact is applied in the current analysis by minimizing the penetration depth,
since it directly enforces the contact constraint when the clearance between two surfaces
becomes zero. The contact pressure-clearance relationship is illustrated in figure 7.

2.4.5
Frictional behaviour
The frictional behavior in contact interaction refers specifically to the modeling of tangential
contact forces or friction tractions between surfaces. It considers the relative motion between
the surfaces and captures the generation of frictional shear stresses, allowing for the simula-
tion of frictional effects. The interaction between the contacting surfaces is simulated using
the Coulomb friction model, which describes the frictional behavior between the surfaces
using a friction coefficient and is given in equation 29 [2],
τ = µsσn
(29)
In the context of frictional contact between two surfaces, the sticking regime is defined as
the state where the applied shear stresses are below a critical value. Under these conditions,
the surfaces remain in contact without any relative motion or slip.

Accurately simulating ideal friction behavior can be challenging [2], and as a default ap-

"elastic slip" is a finite element concept rather than a physical phenomenon, serving as a
means to address potential non-convergence problems.

2.4.6
Quasi-static analysis
A quasi-static analysis is a type of dynamic analysis that considers the response of a structure
or system under time-varying loading conditions that change slowly with time so that it
captures the dynamic response of the system without the need for a full dynamic analysis.
An application of a quasi-static solver introduces certain modifications to the governing
equations of dynamic systems [21],
[Mm]¨ϕ + [Cv] ˙ϕ + [Kg]ϕ = F(tq)
(30)
In the context of quasi-static analysis, the governing equation becomes time-independent
since the time-dependent (dynamic) terms, such as acceleration ¨ϕ and velocity ˙ϕ, are con-
sidered negligible compared to the rest terms.
In the current thesis, a dynamic implicit analysis is carried out utilizing a quasi-static type
application to determine the final static response. This provides stability to the system by
considerable energy dissipation and improves convergence behavior since the inertia effects
are primarily introduced to regularize unstable behavior.
The equilibrium equations are
solved for each time increment iteratively using Newton’s method, giving the advantage of
an unconditionally stable solver [2]. Automatic time incrementation is used based on the
convergence behavior of Newton’s iterations, while a high temporal resolution of 1e-4 with a
maximum increment size of 5e-3 is used to effectively capture the system’s behavior over the
total simulation time. The load increment size is proportional to the time increment size,
while a smooth step amplitude is utilized to define gradual changes in loading.

2.7.7
Prevailing mechanisms of shear tractions at the interface
For the same examined case of Er = 210 GPa, in order to gain deeper insights into the
prevailing mechanisms at the interface, two sensitivity analyses are conducted: the Poisson’s
ratio and the slip-prevented study.
The Poisson’s ratio sensitivity analysis investigates the effects of different Poisson’s ratios
on the generated normal pressure, and consequentially shear tractions along the contact
area. The objective of this analysis is to examine the impact of the distribution of shear
tractions by increasing the normal contact pressure and reveal the predominant mechanisms.
Subsequently, the slip-prevented analysis centers on manipulating the friction coefficient µs.
The primary objective of the latter is to achieve higher resistance to relative motion and
investigate potential shear traction redistribution.
The investigation of the two following sections is limited in scope, focusing primarily on
specific cases and concepts, indicating a need for further research.

Nevertheless, analyzing the behavior and determining the reaction forces of this complex
2D structure poses significant challenges. To address this complexity, a rod simplification
model is utilized to gain insight into the overall structural behavior, while the gravity load
is omitted. The rod simplification approach involves approximating the 2D structure as a
simplified rod system, which allows for a more manageable analysis while still capturing the
fundamental characteristics of the original structure. Therefore, the reaction forces at the
supports are determined by satisfying force and moment equilibrium.
Figure 16(top) demonstrates the original rod model representing the grip connection as well
as a magnified model (bottom) as regards the angle of friction α of the inclined rods. The
latter is solely and intentionally made to increase the visual representation of the angles. In
fact, this model is built upon the equivalent analytical rod model for an individual deformable
cam on a rigid surface (figure 10). The inclusion of frictional slider elements at both sides of
the connection symbolically represents again the "locking" and "sliding" states. Additionally,
the presence of middle roller support acts as a constraint, deterring the structure from
moving horizontally. This prevents rigid body motion, ensures self-locking, and secures that
the applied bending moment conditions or asymmetrical loads do not lead to structural
collapse.
The inclusion of inclined rods, which act as a mechanism of load transfer, is
essential for ensuring the stability of the entire system when subjected to external forces.
These rods play a vital role by allowing the cams to transfer compressive loads, stemming
from the radial forces, to the outer wall surface. The subsequent investigation builds upon
the findings presented in sections 4.4.6 and 4.4.2, which highlighted two distinct failure
modes: one occurring during small deformations and the other during large deformations.
To further understand the behavior of the grip connection, the analysis is divided into these
deformation states, allowing for a detailed examination of the connection’s response in each
case.

• Linear: The linear spring demonstrates a direct relationship between rotation and

response to any rotation, assuming ideal conditions where the rings are precisely posi-
tioned and tightly secured against the outer tube surface, eliminating any design gaps

rings and the outer tube surface.
• Convex(non-linear): Similar to the linear one, the non-linear spring is immediately
activated even with minimal rotation.
However, it requires a much lower moment

exponent n is made without specific constraints in order to achieve a convex curve that
exhibits a "hardening" effect. By setting n = 1.7, the resulting curve demonstrates an
increasing stiffness, ensuring a resisting response.

After describing the key methodology implemented, three distinct loading sequence scenarios
used in this study are presented. Multiple cases are considered for different combinations
of (M, N), where the bending moments are obtained using the rotation-controlled approach
consistently applied across the following scenarios.
It is worth noting that the springs’
influence is solely examined for the sequential loading scenario. In addition, it is important
to state that the numerical findings presented in the following studies are based on the 2D
numerical grip connection model.
Sequential loading scenario
A sequential loading scenario refers to a specific sequence in which external loads are applied
to the grip connection. It involves a step-by-step process where an initial axial force is ap-
plied, followed by the subsequent application of a bending moment. This loading sequence
enables a controlled evaluation of the influence of axial force on the connection’s ability
to withstand bending moments. In the current analysis, the maximum bending moment-
carrying capacity of the grip connection is determined through a rotation-controlled simula-
tion.
Pre-axial followed by simultaneous loading scenario
To investigate the influence of loading history, the pre-axial followed by simultaneous loading
scenario is introduced as a modification to the previous sequential loading scenario, while still
maintaining certain similarities. In particular, the loading sequence begins with an initial
pre-axial load, which introduces a specific level of axial force to the connection. Subsequently,

Multi-axial loading investigation of grip connection: Analysis procedure
a simultaneous application of both axial force and bending moment is initiated.
Simultaneous loading scenario from the onset
In this last loading scenario, axial force and bending moment are applied simultaneously
from the initial stage. This loading sequence is designed aiming to simulate more realistic
loading conditions. The analysis is performed by implementing and evaluating two differ-
ent approaches: rotation-controlled simulation and moment-controlled simulation. In the
rotation-controlled simulation, a constant rotational displacement is applied while varying
the axial forces. In the moment-controlled simulation, a constant bending moment is applied
while varying the axial forces. Once again, these analyses are conducted to investigate the
connection’s ability to withstand bending moments and to examine the dependency of the
axial force on its capability in this regard.

4.1
Derived cam profile
From the initial derivation of the cam profile in figure 24, it becomes evident that the cam
design parameter plays a crucial role in shaping the profile slope.
Specifically, a higher
value of the design parameter leads to a more gentle slope. This relationship emphasizes the
significant influence of the cam design parameter on the overall cam shape.
Furthermore, it is worth noting that the derivation of the cam profile highlights a distinct
advantage of the rotational cam. Regardless of the rotational angle Dθ around the center
of rotation O, the rotational cam maintains a stable contact angle, as shown in figure 25.
This characteristic of the rotational cam enables its operation across a wide range of rotating
angles and makes it able to deal with fabrication and installation tolerances. The reason
behind this lies in the fact that the slope of the exponential function describing the cam
profile is always zero at the contact point.
4.2
Hertzian model evaluation
The normal pressure and the shear traction distribution are shown in figures 53 and 54, re-
spectively, in the Appendix. The former is symmetric, and its maximum value is compared
with the analytical calculation, matching precisely. The latter is also seen in figure 54 in
the Appendix, changing the sign from the left to the right side creating a skew-symmetric
pattern. This symmetrical distribution with opposite directions on either side results in zero
integral, indicating zero net shear force. The numerical simulation results demonstrate a
close agreement between the maximum contact pressure and the contact half-width obtained
from the numerical model and the corresponding values predicted by the Hertzian analyt-
ical equation. This indicates the reliability of the numerical model for conducting further
analysis. Any slight deviations observed can be attributed to the numerical discretization in
representing the contact area.

4.4.2
Eccentricity study: Validation of analytical and numerical models
The failure mode observed in both the analytical and numerical models (figure 27a) demon-
strates features that can be identified as a widely acknowledged form of instability mode
in structural systems, namely "snap-through" mode. "Snap-through" refers to a type of
failure mode where an elastic system, subjected to specific loading conditions, can transition
from an equilibrium state to a non-adjacent equilibrium configuration [29]. Since large-scale
deformations, as well as a significant reduction in the load-carrying capacity of the structure,
occur, "snap-through" is considered a global failure mode caused by geometric non-linear
shortening of the equivalent rod.
Drawing from the aforementioned failure mode, the axial force-eccentricity response is de-
scribed by a monotonically increased concave curve followed by a peak value, upon which
it decreases and cannot be further increased. This "softening" effect as well as the local
load-bearing maximum value stems from the geometrically non-linear deformation behavior
of the rod, the length of which is decreasing (rod "shortening") as a function of imposed
displacement at point O, ux. Nevertheless, while the eccentricity decreases, the contact force
is monotonically increasing, albeit at a slower rate (figure 27b). Such a phenomenon repre-
sents a competing mechanism, and eventually, the external load must be reduced to sustain

4.4.4
Hollomon spring implementation
Figure 29 demonstrates that the analytical Hollomon model, with a calibrated Hollomon
exponent of n = 0.175, provides a satisfactory approximation of the numerical results, par-
ticularly for large deformations when the eccentricity is reduced. However, it is evident that
at the initial stages (pre-peak), a noticeable "hardening" effect is observed, which is exclu-
sively captured by the 2D numerical model. The discrepancy between the two models arises
from their differing treatment of contact interaction. The analytical model, as described in
the eccentricity study in section 2.7.2, defines the contact as a "stationary" point, whereas
the 2D numerical model considers the contact as a surface. Consequently, the analytical
model exhibits the following inherent limitations that arise from this fundamental difference
in contact modeling:
• "Increasing radius" effect: Despite the shortening behavior, since the cam rolling mo-
tion entails a change in the cam profile radius in the undeformed configuration (equa-
tion 26 and figure 58 in Appendix), the increasing radius during deformation is crucial
to be considered by taking into account the additional rolling angle Dθ (figure 11).
• "Hardening" effect: The contact interaction is, in fact, determined by a contact inter-
face, which grows in magnitude as the deformation progresses, leading to changes in
the effective stiffness. The analytical model in its current form does not consider the
effect of increasing contact area, which is related to the modeling assumption that EA
is constant and the contact point is stationary.

4.4.6
Investigation of load-carrying capacity for a stiff cam - Slip failure mode
unveiling
Figure 33 makes clear that a second critical failure mode is unveiled, the so-called slip
failure mode. In the context of slip-induced failure, it should be noted that this phenomenon
is fundamentally distinct from the "snap-through" effect and is considerably more complex.
This is evident in the noticeable differences observed in the load-displacement response.
A significantly low axial force-carrying capacity is reached before it fails, corresponding
to small deformations, followed by an abrupt drop. This signifies the point at which the
frictional resistance between the contacting bodies is overcome, which indicates a loss of
contact. Slip failure is a localized phenomenon that is highly dependent on the intricate
mechanics of tractions in the contact zone (shear traction distribution) and is characterized
by the relative movement or sliding between the contacting surfaces when the applied force
exceeds the frictional resistance. The "hardening" effect is again captured, as indicated by
the presence of a convex curve, compared to a linear trendline at the initial stages.
The slip occurrence is also justified by the effective friction coefficient µeff during cam
deformation.
As the cam deforms, the effective friction coefficient adapts to reflect the
evolving frictional behavior, satisfying always the maximum available limit that Coulomb
law imposes, µeff ≤µs. Initially, as the force is gradually applied, the available generated
friction increases at a relatively fast rate, which eventually reaches its maximum value,
dictated by µs. This maximum value signifies the point where the frictional forces cannot
increase further, even with higher applied loads, thus, slip failure occurs.
Page 64 of 76
==PAGE:83==
4.4
Deformable cam - rigid surface formulation
It becomes evident that slip failure is typically detected prior to the occurrence of the "snap-
through" failure mode.
The former is observed during the early stages of deformation,
characterized by small displacements compared to the latter, which is encountered at later
stages when larger deformations take place. Therefore, the axial load-carrying performance
of an individual cam is limited by the presence of two distinct failure modes, which occur at
different stages of deformation.
4.4.7
Shear tractions at the interface: Poisson’s ratio study
During the rolling action, as the cam is compressed against the surface, the material un-
dergoes lateral displacement in both directions, attributed to the Poisson effect.
This
compression-induced lateral displacement gives rise to contact and consequentially shear
tractions at the interface, as the material attempts to laterally displace.
From figure 34 is observed that as ν increases, the maximum carrying capacity also increases
due to larger normal stresses generated. To investigate the behavior near the point of failure
for ν = 0.1, a specific analysis is conducted at a selected displacement, as indicated by the
annotation arrow and the dashed line.
Figure 35a highlights a finding that requires significant emphasis. A higher Poisson’s ratio
results in larger contact pressure, and consequentially shear tractions, due to the tendency
of the material to expand laterally, to a greater extent, at the opposite side of the rolling
direction, where the contact area is smaller. Nevertheless, figure 35b makes evident that
the effective friction coefficient is overwhelmed, with a large area of the contact region
experiencing relative sliding. This resulting sliding indicates that there is a larger increase
of the shear tractions on the left-hand side, tending to redistribute, albeit restricted by the
imposed maximum µs.
Here comes the second key finding, which justifies the right-to-left prevented mechanism of
stress redistribution. This competing mechanism involves an interplay between the shear
tractions generated from pure kinematic considerations and the contact pressure’s capacity
to induce frictional shear in accordance with the Coulomb law. Therefore, when the frictional
limit is exceeded, the shear stress distribution tends to align with the prevailing Coulomb
friction law, following a linear dependence with the normal stresses governed by a scaling
factor of µs = 0.1, as seen in figure 35a.
4.4.8
Shear tractions at the interface: Slip-prevented study
Figure 36a makes evident that the generated friction increases proportionally with the friction
coefficient, thus, a higher µs yields a greater magnitude of frictional force. Figure 36b depicts
a zoom-in view of figure 36a, focusing now on the initial stages of the deformation, where slip
occurs. It is justified that, for a limited coefficient µs=0.1, the no-slip condition N ≤Tµs=0.1
is not satisfied, as the ratio surpasses unity. Of particular interest is the case of µs=0.8, as
it provides a higher resistance to relative motion, minimizing or eliminating slip effects and
aiming to obtain interlocking between the contacting surfaces.

their cumulative effect generates a net friction force that opposes the externally applied force.
Figure 37b depicts the behavior of the effective friction coefficient. The pattern closely aligns
with that of the shear stresses, where for µc = µs = 0.1, it reaches the maximum available
friction along the contact area. However, even for a relatively high value of µs=0.8, some
sliding between the surfaces is still observed, indicating that complete interlocking has not
yet been achieved. Nevertheless, the friction provided by µs = 0.8 is sufficient to handle
the applied force, as justified in figure 36a (purple curve). It should be also stated that
an effective sliding is present for the case µs = 0.1, justifying the slip occurrence, which is
vanished for the other two cases.
Figure 38 demonstrates the propagation of shear tractions distribution for both the slip case
and the slip-prevented study, confirming the investigation into traction redistribution. The
shear tractions propagate gradually over successive time increments during the initial stages
of deformation. The top-left figure corresponds to the slip case, where the cam is calibrated
for a friction coefficient µc = 0.1, and the material stiffness is E = 210 GPa. In this case,
it is evident that the Coulomb law dominates. On the other hand, the remaining figures
represent the slip-prevented study, where either the cam has low material stiffness or the
friction coefficient is larger. It can be observed that the right-to-left mechanism of stress
redistribution is prominently displayed when the friction resistance is higher than the cam
design parameter, with the shear tractions aligning with the predictions of the Hertzian
solution.
The chosen design parameter for the cam profile calibration is proved to be strongly sensitive
to the friction coefficient applied between the contacting surfaces. This indicates that the
value of µs significantly influences the appropriate choice of µc since it is evident that even
when µc = µs, it is insufficient to prevent slip failure.
Therefore, it can be concluded
that selecting a suitable cam design parameter that is lower than the friction coefficient
can effectively prevent the occurrence of the slip mode. This ensures that only the "snap-
through" failure mechanism is considered in the analysis.
Figure 39 makes it obvious that the stiff cam response is capable of capturing the "snap-
through" failure mode. This analysis is conducted considering large displacements and a
friction coefficient of µs = 0.8, indicating that "snap-through" mode can occur in any cam
model configuration, provided that slip is prevented.

Conclusion
A concise overview of the major key findings in sections 3 and 4 is presented in a bullet
point-style format below.
• Individual cam performance and governing failure modes
➯The cam profile design allows the rotational cam to function effectively across a wide
range of angles, accommodating variations in clearance heights based on its derived
function.
➯In a rigid locking state, the rotational cam is capable of withstanding infinitely increas-
ing axial forces without experiencing slip.
➯For a deformable cam on a rigid surface, the eccentricity reduction effect is prominent
at large deformations and is independent of the cam’s material stiffness.
➯For small deformations, an initial "hardening" effect is observed due to the increased
effective stiffness resulting from the expansion of the contact area.
➯A "snap-through" failure mode is revealed as a global instability mode arising from
significant geometrically non-linear deformations occurring at a large scale. This failure
mode can occur in any cam configuration, provided that slip is prevented.
➯A localized slip failure mode is observed at the contact area, characterized by the
distribution of shear tractions, preceding the "snap-through" failure mode.
➯A competing mechanism exists between friction shear tractions, governed by Coulomb
friction law and those suggested by the Hertz solution. When the friction coefficient
equals the cam design parameter, Coulomb law predominates, while for higher values,
Hertzian tractions prevail due to shear traction redistribution.
• Grip connection performance subjected to multi-axial loading conditions
➯The maximum bending moment-carrying capacity of the connection related to axial
force shows a piece-wise linear response for small deformations and sequential loading
conditions.
➯The interplay of axial force and bending moment creates a coupled loading behavior
that prevents the connection from sustaining pure bending moment without axial force,
emphasizing their strong interdependence.
➯The inclusion of rings, modeled as rotational springs, increases the bending moment
capacity of the connection by providing additional strength and support.
➯Significant rotations introduce second-order effects caused by the offset of contact
points, leading to an additional opposing moment that diminishes the overall moment
capacity of the connection.

Future work
The present MSc thesis has established a foundation for comprehending and evaluating the
unveiled failure modes and the response behavior of the grip connection detail under multi-
axial loading conditions, utilizing the existing methodologies and tools described in this
study. However, to gain deeper insights into the complexity of this subject and overcome
certain identified limitations, further research and exploration are required. Hence, the future
work is segmented into two sections: the first focuses on proposing new directions to the
existing models by addressing their limitations and refining the underlying assumptions. This
aims to achieve more accurate results by enhancing the methodologies that are currently in
use. The second section refers to unexplored approaches and considers those that have been
simplified, overlooked, or not previously considered. This section provides an opportunity
to broaden the scope of analysis and discover new perspectives and methodologies.
In the initial section, the slip failure mode was found to be influenced by the cam design
parameter and friction coefficient. Future work should focus on optimizing the cam profile
calibration by lowering the design parameter below the friction coefficient. This optimiza-
tion aims to eliminate slip failure during small deformations, enabling the cam to withstand
larger deformations where only the "snap-through" failure mode occurs. In addition, further
research focus should be directed toward two specific areas in the current study; the interac-
tion between a rigid cam and a deformable surface and the fully elastic bodies. These areas
have received limited investigation, and exploring them in more depth could provide valuable
insights and advancements in their respective fields. Regarding the multi-axial loading inves-
tigation, it is suggested to consider the inclusion of a translation spring attached to the inner
tube, so that the idealization of its rigidity is prevented and the incorporation of flexibility
in the tangential motion is allowed. Shear forces can be also incorporated into the study
and examine their impact on the overall system behavior. Lastly, it is highly recommended
to incorporate the second-order effects into the analytical solutions. This enhancement will
provide a more precise estimation of the connection’s bending moment carrying capacity.
Continuing to the second section, since the grip connection is exposed to harsh environmental
conditions, particularly in offshore applications, it is recommended to investigate the effects
of corrosion on the performance of the connection. This can involve conducting corrosion-
fatigue analysis, studying the degradation of material properties over time, and assessing the
long-term durability of the connection in corrosive environments. Furthermore, the present
thesis is conducted within the framework of elasticity theory, disregarding any permanent
deformation or plasticity. However, to further explore potential yield failure, an examination
of the stress field in the contact area could be conducted, taking into account the material’s
elastic limit. Moreover, experimental tests could be performed to validate the findings of the
current study and capture any potential deviations.
