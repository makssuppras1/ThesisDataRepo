1 ABSTRACT  
 
This master’s thesis documents the development of a Sustainable Development Goal (SDG) 
assessment tool for Small and Medium Sized Enterprises (SMEs). Conducted by a master’s thesis 
student in MSc Design and Innovation at the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), in collaboration 
with United Nations Development Department Nordic Representation Office (UNDP NRO) and 
Monitor Deloitte, to support their pilot project, the SDG Accelerator Program. Findings from the 
program shows that the SMEs are driven by the opportunity for innovation but need further support 
in their project selection. The aim for this project was to develop a tool that can support the SMEs in 
their contribution to the UN 2030 Agenda to make impact-based decision. 
The first part of the thesis identifies the gap in supporting SMEs towards sustainability business, which 
shows a need to enhance the SMEs understanding of how they impact, or how they could impact, the 
SDGs. Based on this, a literature research was made to understand the SMEs needs and drivers 
towards SDG implementation. Key findings show that the SMEs limited amount of resources calls for 
simpler solutions that focus on sustainable business opportunities.  
A systematic literature review identifies gaps between existing tools and observed needs. Key 
learnings show a need for tools that support SMEs in their understanding of how the SDGs relate to 
their company and how the company can contribute to the global challenges and tools that focus on 
business opportunity rather than risks.  
A design was developed through conceptualization, prototyping and testing. The final concept is an 
assessment tool that supports the user in the identification of SDG focus. The first part of the 
assessment filters a list of SDG related questions to where the firm can have most potential for SDG 
contribution, and secondly guides the user to assess their potential for sustainable business 
opportunity. As a result, a visual presentation supports the user in the evaluation and identification of 
focus SDG for the following innovation process. For scalability and easy access, is the tool suggested 
to be available through an online platform. 
The final design is evaluated against the design specification and compared with the existing tools and 
suggestions for further work. Emphasizing the need for setting the tool in context, and support the 
further innovation process from idea to implementation.

2 RESUME 
 
Dette kandidat speciale dokumenterer udviklingen af et vurderingsværktøj for FN’s verdensmål for 
bæredygtig udvikling (verdensmål) for små og mellemstore virksomheder (SMV’er). Gennemført af en 
kandidatstuderende i MSc Design og Innovation ved Danmarks Tekniske Universitet (DTU), i 
samarbejde med FN’s udviklingsafdeling nordiske repræsentationskontor (UNDP NRO) og Monitor 
Deloitte, i forbindelse med deres pilotprojekt, SDG Accelerator Programme, der sigter mod at 
fremskynde SMV'ernes bidrag til verdensmålene. Resultater fra programmet viser, at de små og 
mellemstore virksomheder er drevet af muligheden for innovation, men har brug for yderligere støtte 
i udvælgelsesprocessen af projekter for at tage impact baserede beslutninger. Formålet med dette 
projekt var at udvikle et værktøj, der kan støtte de små og mellemstore virksomheder i deres bidrag 
til verdenmålene ved støtte SMV’erne i at identificere SDG med stort potentiale. 
Den første del af specialet identificerer mangler til at støtte SMV’er i bæredygtighedsimplementering, 
hvilket viser, at det er nødvendigt, med en forbedret egen forståelse for, hvordan de påvirker eller 
hvordan de kan påvirke de 17 verdensmål. På baggrund af dette blev en litteratur undersøgelse udført, 
for at forstå SMV’ernes behov og drivkraft for implementering af verdensmålene. Undersøgelsen 
viser, at små og mellemstore virksomheder begrænsede mængder ressourcer kræver enklere 
løsninger, som kan understøtte deres forståelse. 
En systematisk litteraturundersøgelse resultere i identifikation af mangle af eksisterende værktøjer 
der understøtter SMV'er i deres forståelse af, hvordan verdensmålene vedrører deres virksomhed, og 
hvordan virksomheden kan bidrage til de globale udfordringer. Samt værktøjer, der fokuserer på 
forretningsmuligheder frem for risiko. 
Baseret på disse mangler blev design udviklet gennem konceptudvikling, prototyper og testning. Det 
endelige koncept er et vurderingsværktøj, som understøtter brugeren i identifikationen af SDG fokus. 
Den første del af vurderingen filtrerer en liste over SDG-relaterede spørgsmål baseret på hvilke 
verdensmål virksomheden har størst potentiale for at bidrag til, for det andet fører brugeren igennem 
en række spørgsmål for at vurdere en deres potentiale for bæredygtig forretningsmuligheder. Som 
resultat understøtter en visuel præsentation brugeren i evalueringen og identificeringen af fokus SDG 
for den følgende innovationsproces. For skalerbarhed og nem adgang er værktøjet foreslået at være 
tilgængeligt via en online platform. 
Det endelige design vurderes mod designspecifikationen og sammenlignes med de eksisterende 
værktøjer og forslag til videre arbejde. Understreger behovet for at støtte den videre 
innovationsproces fra idé til implementering

11 INTRODUCTION 
 
Sustainable Development (SD) is essentially a development that allow us to meet the present and 
future generation’s needs, as it was first defined by the Brundtland Commission in 1987;  “Sustainable 
development is development that meets the needs of the present without compromising the ability of 
future generations to meet their own needs.” (World Commission on Environment (WCED), 1987), and 
a recognition of the increasing need for system changes. In order to move towards SD, global action 
and initiatives is needed. That is why the 197 member states of United Nations in 2015 adopted the 
Resolution 70/1; "Transforming our World: the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development."(UNGA, 
2015), a list of 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) which need to be met before 2030, covering 
the broad understanding of sustainability of both people, prosperity, planet, peace and partnerships. 
To achieve the SDGs not only governmental action is required, business is a vital partner in achieving 
the Sustainable Development Goals (Ban Ki-Moon, United Nations Secretary-General), Companies 
contributes through their core activities in both the economic, environmental and social system 
through the entire value chain  
The Small and Medium Sized Enterprises (SMEs) have been estimated to contribute with up to 70% of 
global pollution collectively (Johnson and Schaltegger, 2016). Yet only 10% of the total number of 
sustainability reports in the GRI Sustainability Disclosure Database is from SMEs (Global Reporting 
Initiative, 2016). The SMEs focus on the local and short term market so the long term perspective of 
SD, can make the SDGs overwhelming (Pedersen, 2018). And since the SDGs are a political framework 
this can make it too complex to integrate and translate into actionable business strategy (Verboven 
and Vanherck, 2016a; Amanda Wiliams, 2018). 
The SDGs offers great potential for business as they are a way to “look into the future” and find future 
business opportunities (Pedersen, 2018). But to truly make businesses  contribute, radical change is 
needed in moving from compliance and risk driven sustainability management to integrate 
sustainability into business strategy (Dyllick and Muff, 2016).  The complex framework tempts the 
companies to choose the SDGs that lay closes to their main business activities, which led to the risk of 
companies “cherry picking” according to own interest rather than SDG contribution, in fact a study by 
PwC show that only 1% of companies assess their impact across all 17 SDGs (PwC, 2016). To support 
the SMEs in the contribution, a need for a structured approached to understand, identify and prioritize 
action, is needed.   
To support this process of implementing, research shows that company requires a structured 
framework or management system (Azapagic and Perdan, 2011; Witjes, Vermeulen and Cramer, 2017; 
Hernandez-Vivanco, Bernardo and Cruz-C Azares, 2018), but most studies and tools focus on large 
organizations and their behavior (Becherer and Helms, no date). To reach the SMEs a new approach 
is needed, it is necessary with a simpler and more straightforward approach (Hillary, 2000). 
To support the SMEs in the SDGs contribution through strategic implementation, the United Nations 
Development Program (UNDP) and Monitor Deloitte have developed a program to accelerate the 
implementation, the SDG Accelerator. The program realizes that to engage the SMEs the focus will 
need to move from compliance and risk to SDGs as driver for business opportunities and innovation. 
But findings from the initial round of the program implies a need for supporting the SMEs further in 
the process towards impact-based decision when prioritizing projects. This master thesis will aim to 
develop a tool to support the SMEs in the process of identifying SDG priorities which they can have 
the biggest potential.

12 METHODOLOGY 
This chapter presents the design research methodology that have structured and guided the research 
and design process towards the objective. 
The research objective of this thesis is to develop a tool to support SMEs in their contribution to the 
UN 2030 Agenda, through the identification of areas with the biggest potential impact, SDG Hotspots, 
supporting them to make impact-based decisions. To answer this, a research question was 
formulated: 
How to support the SMEs in their identification of the SDGs with their biggest potential impact? 
12.1 DESIGN APPROACH 
The design process has been led by a problem-solving approach, with the focus on understanding the 
challenges and the context of which the solution will function, as a stepping stone to develop a 
solution that meets the needs. This process is explained and illustrated by the double diamond process 
for design thinking (Figure 1 Double diamond). Which guides the creative process through a combined 
divergent and convergent thinking approached, where the divergent opens up and the convergent 
narrowing down. First by focus on the problem space and then the solution space. The process is 
divided into four phases as shown on the figure. 
The design is built on a user centered approach, based on the learnings from the SDG Accelerator from 
selected participates from the program. Potential users are involved in the problem definition from 
interviews to understand the needs, and in the solution space through testing and iterations.  
The illustration (Figure 1 Double diamond), below shows the design process using the relation to the 
double diamond. The process is illustrated as a consecutive model, where one area feeds into the 
next, however in reality the process should be described as an iterative process where new knowledge 
have led to further research and understanding. 
The double diamond can be described with four phases, discover, define, develop and deliver, which 
is used to structure the process into four work packages, is described under the figure. The four work 
packages are also used to structure the report.

Work Package 1: Discover  
The discovery phase aims to understand the challenges related to the design and the problem space 
through divergence thinking. In this work package a context analysis is made based on a literature 
review to understand the challenges of SDG implementation in SMEs and the context of the SDG 
Accelerator process. To analyses gaps in the problem space, a user activity cycle and an ecosystem 
mapping is used to understand the context and how they relate to each other.  
 
Work Package 2: Define  
The define phase aims to outline what the design should accomplish, through convergent thinking. To 
do so an analysis of the stakeholder’s requirements is made based on semi structured interviews with 
UNDP, Deloitte and two case companies, RGS Nordic and Plastix along literature review on the SME 
needs.  The client objectives have been structured using Nigel Cross design method (Nigel Cross, 2000), 
the objective tree.  
As part of the define phase, a definition on impact, impact measurement and impact tools is made, 
based on literature review.  
The stakeholder needs is translated into tangible design criteria using the method Quality Function 
Deployment (QFD) (Nigel Cross, 2000) which together with the impact definition is base for the design 
specification. 
Based on the design specification, a systematic literature review (Kitchenham, 2012) of existing tools 
is made to understand best practice and to define gaps between the existing solutions and the desired 
criteria from the design specification.    
 
Work Package 3: Design and deliver 
This work package includes both the design and deliver part of the double diamond. The design phase 
is where the solution space is opened by a divergent thinking to develop and explore the possible 
solutions, and the deliver phase is a the convergent process of choosing, testing and narrowing down 
to a single solution that meet the needs. 
 To open the design scope a function analysis (Nigel Cross, 2000) is used to first define the scope of 
the design and open the scope by analyzing sub functions.   
The conceptualization is based on the design methods brainstorming and morphology charts and is 
evaluated by the use of weighting scheme based on Nigel Cross design methods(Nigel Cross, 2000). 
Additional literature research is used in order to detail the concept further.  
Prototyping is used as boundary object to mediate the concept, in order to gain feedback from test 
users, along a technical test for color blindness. The feedback is used in a iterations of improving and 
testing the concept.  
The final solution is evaluated up against the design specification, and compared with the existing 
solutions using a value strategy canvas.  
 
Work Package 4: Evaluate  
The fourth work package covers the reflection of the final solution and the approach and method used 
doing the project. This work package will also include the final conclusion and suggestions for further 
work.

13.1 SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT 
At the United Nation (UN) summit in Stockholm in 1972, was the first-time human actions were 
recognized to impact the environment. And in 1987 was sustainability first formulated by the World 
Commission on Environment and Development (WCED) commonly referred to as Brundtland 
Commission, “Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without 
compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.” (World Commission on 
Environment (WCED), 1987). The recognition of sustainable development (SD) as the human essential 
needs, moves the previous understand from a focus on only environmental aspects to three essential 
pillars of sustainability, the social, economic and environmental aspects (Figure 2). To understand the 
complexity of sustainability, we will need to understand the society as an interlinked system where 
the three pillars of sustainability is interdependent and cannot exist without the other, and all 
essential to achieve SD.  
 
The latest IPCC report from October 2018 was a wakeup call: “The world’s leading climate scientists 
have warned there is only 12 years to be kept to a maximum of 1.5C and avoid catastrophic 
environmental breakdown.” (IPCC Oct 2018), together with the rapid growth in population worldwide, 
which is expected to grow from 7.6 billion to 8.6 billion from today to 2030 (UN, no date), alongside 
an increasing living standard, this will rise the demand of for goods and services. Alarming factors, 
which call for global action.  
To achieve SD, we need system innovation. It requires long term planning and understanding of 
complex systems and their dynamics. From resource contains, climate change and air pollution to 
raising population and the need of reduce hunger and unemployment. There is a need for societal

13.1.1 THE UNITED NATIONS SUSTAINABLE DEVELOPMENT GOALS  
The need to address these environmental, social and economic challenges have resulted in important 
global initiatives among others the 17 UN Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs). At the UN General 
Assembly 25th September 2015, the 197 member states adopted the Resolution 70/1 "Transforming 
our World: the 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development."(UNGA, 2015) Also referred to as Agenda 
2030, the Sustainable Development Goals, SDGs or the Global Goals. The resolution contains a list of 
17 overreaching global goals and 169 specific targets related to each goal to be reached before 2030. 
The goals is a pledge to leave no one behind and is based on the principles of being people -centered, 
universal and transformative by balancing all the three dimensions of sustainability the social, 
economic and environmental aspects. – In other words, a plan for taking care of the planet, people 
and prosperity, peace and partnerships (UNGA, 2015).  
The SDGs are universal, integrated and indivisible and balancing the three pillars of sustainability and 
add two additional dimensions: peace and partnership. This is summarized to the Five P’s dimensions. 
(One World Centre, no date). The understanding of the goals as integrated, is essential to understand 
the impact an action might have. An example could be the implementation of new form of sustainable 
energy, which is both carbon free and pollution free. This solution not only impact the sustainable 
energy targets (7.1, 7.2, 7.3) but also contribute to the wellbeing goal of reducing air pollution (3.9). 
However, interlinkage between goals can also have negative consequence, and should be considered 
when implementing new actions (‘A guide to SDG interactions: from science to implementation’, 
2017).

The SDGs are ambitious and have a short deadline. But a similar plan has already been realized. In 
2000 world leaders came together in the UN headquarter in New York to adopt the UN Millennium 
Declaration, containing 8 Millennium Development Goals (MDGs) which had to be reach before 2015. 
The final report conducted after the 15 years period, confirms that goal-setting can lift millions of 
people out of poverty, cut the amount of undernutrition people in half, and provide access to clean 
drinking water (UNDP, no date b). The format of the SDGs is the same as the MDGs, but the SDGs 
require change in all countries, both developing and developed countries, where the MDGs focused 
on the developing countries and governmental action. The SDGs requires all countries and all 
stakeholders to act and collaborate (UNGA, 2015). 
13.1.2 ENSURE PROGRESS  
To achieve the global goals, the UN member states agreed as part of the Resolution 70/1 to develop 
a list of measurable indicators for each target. Alongside the goals, there in total 232 indicators, which 
is used to help the member states progress on the SDGs, and to have a common ground for measuring 
(United Nations, 2017). 
The SDGs is a global framework but implemented on a national level. This is executed by all member 
states have made their own national action plan. In the Nordic countries the report “Bumps On The 
Road 2030” explain the national challenges to solve to reach the commitment, in the Nordic region 
the focus should be on the main challenges of SDG 2, 7, 12, 13, 14, and 15 (Larsen, Alslund-Lanthén 
and Nordisk Ministerråd, 2017). Based on this the Danish government have made national action plan 
“Handlings plan for FN’s verdensmål” (2017) and “DANIDA: The world 2030” (2017).   
Each member state is responsible for managing statistics on the progress. In Denmark “Statistics 
Denmark” provides national data, statistics for each target, to track the progress (Danmarks Statistik, 
no date). The Sustainable Development Solution Network (SDSN) have made a report and interactive 
tool to get an overview of the status towards the goals are challenged on a country level (United

13.2 SUSTAINABLE BUSINESS 
It is not new for businesses to work with sustainability, the first major milestones were in 1997 and 
1999 where respectively the GRI, Global Reporting Initiative and UN Global Compact was founded. 
Both which set the standard for sustainable reporting and monitoring. But implementing sustainability 
as core of a business, can still be a difficult task, the majority of companies is still on either a 
compliance and efficiency level, where more effort is needed to move to the level of strategic or 
implementation (Idil Gaziulusoy, Boyle and Mcdowall, 2013, Bos-brouwers, 2010).  
The main aim for an enterprise is to create value through the trade of products and services for the 
society. But companies tempt to perceive value creation narrowly focusing on optimizing short term 
financial performance without considering the needs for a sustainable long-term success(Porter and 
Kramer, 2011). In the last decades it has become the norm from a business and economist perspective, 
to consider global sustainability challenges not as a business responsibility.  Economist have 
legitimized the idea that providing societal benefits will add constrain or adding costs to the company, 
which does not meet the aim of profit maximization. Along this is the idea “externalities” indicates 
that a company should not bear this extra burden but be the government’s responsibility to encourage 
the change by taxes, regulation and penalties. All together have ideas like these lead to business who 
excludes the social and environmental considerations from their strategy and core business. Some 
companies incorporate sustainable aspects through corporate responsivity programs, but mainly as a 
reaction to external pressure and to improve reputation (Porter and Kramer, 2011). 
“Business is a vital partner in achieving the Sustainable Development Goals. Companies can 
contribute through their core activities, and we ask companies everywhere to assess their 
impact, set ambitious goals and communicate transparently about the results”. (Ban Ki-Moon, 
United Nations Secretary-General (GRI, UN Global Compact and WBCSD, 2016)). 
This is how Ban Ki-Moon, former secretary General of the UN, state the importance of business 
involvement to achieve the global goals.  No firm is an island, companies are an integrated and major 
part of the system. But to understand how business can contribute to SD and what this can mean for 
business, we will look at the different ways companies integrate the framework.  
Implementing sustainability in business can come with strong competitive advantage in the market 
and can affect either tangible areas such as cost reduction or more intangible values such as brand 
reputation. But we still need to differentiate based on how effective contribution the companies make 
to SD, and to do so sustainability will need to be implemented into the strategy and core business.  To 
clarify the meaning of sustainable business Dyllick and Muff  (2015) have defined three levels of 
implementation, from business as usual to true business sustainability. 
Business as usual: 
 
 
Focus on economic view and profit maximization

and reduction of energy, transport etc.

Consider value creation beyond economic value by including social and 
environmental value creation. Based on the Triple bottom line (Elkington;

Business

“True business sustainability”, Shift perspective from wanting to minimize its

To move companies towards a SDG contribution we need to understand how this can create value for

13.2.1 VALUE CHAIN 
Creating value for the customer is the essence of a business, it is the reason products differentiate 
from each other and the reason the customer buys the product. The term value chain was formulated 
by Porter (1985), as a model to identify and measure value adding activities for to increase the 
competitive advantage and includes all the activities that provide value to the product or service a 
company is providing. Porter separate the actives in primary activities that directly relate to the 
product or service and support activities that covers firm’s infrastructure supporting the primary 
actives.

Primary actives  
The primary activities cover the activites related to the product from receiving materials to after sale 
services. First is the inbound logistics, the receiving of goods, the inspection and their storage, next is 
the operations of the product production, when a product is ready the outbound logistics covers the 
activities from finish product to customer, the order processing, packaging and shipping.  
To sell the product the marketing & sales add value through activities related to distribution channels, 
pricing, and promotion. Last the company can provide services such as repairs, training or 
maintenance. 
Support activities 
The support activates is all necessary for the chain of primary activates to function. The firm 
infrastructure supports both primary and support actives covering management, planning, finance, 
the human resources the activates related to staff from recruitment, training and motivation, the 
technology development including product development and supporting systems to keep track on 
products in stuck eg., and last the procurement of material that supporting all stages of primary 
activities.

Value system 
The value chain covers both the internal and external activities for value creation. The firm does not 
exist in isolation and should be seen as a network connecting different organizations through the 
exchange of value. The supplier’s value chain connected to the firm’s and goes own to the retailer and 
the customer (Topple et al., no date). 
 
13.2.2 TRIPLE BOTTOM LINE 
Triple bottom Line (TBL) reflect business effect on the economic, social and environmental 
sustainability, in a measurable matter that can quantify sustainability for business, made by Elkington 
(1998).  
Economic bottom line 
The companies are a crucial part of the economic system, both on a micro and macro- level. On a 
micro level for the individual employer’s private economy and for the company’s performance sale, 
turn-over and profit. And on a macro level both national and international trough tax contribution, 
employment and GDP (Azapagic and Perdan, 2011). Economic capital can be measured in the total 
value of assets, physical capital, financial capital, knowledge economy- human capital, skills and 
knowledge (Elkington; John, 1998). 
Environmental bottom line 
The environmental changes can directly affect the companies’ access to resources or indirectly effect 
the social and economic system. The company could affect the environment through land use, 
pollution, resource use, waste handling (Azapagic and Perdan, 2011). The environmental bottom line 
can be measured in natural capital, when the company affect the surrounding ecosystem  (Elkington; 
John, 1998) 
Social bottom line 
Social changes can affect the company through change in customer needs or employees well-being. 
And the enterprise influence on the social system is both connected to the health and safety of 
employees over the value chain, and their customers through the products they sell. The social effect 
of business also covers aspects like slavery, child labor or working conditions. A socially responsible 
company should consider the responsibility they have to the community and society. (Azapagic and 
Perdan, 2011). The social bottom line can be measured in human capital: public health, skills and 
education, society health and wealth creation potential  (Elkington; John, 1998). 
13.2.3 SHARED VALUE CREATION  
A shared value perspective places sustainability as center for value creation and create mutual 
benefits both for a profitable business and the society. Due to mutual dependence, this requires a 
holistic approach and an understanding that sustainability in the core business cannot occur only 
within a company, this requires a change of perspective to adopt a network approach on the basis of 
transparency, collaboration, and flexibility (Porter and Kramer, 2011). 
The principle of shared value involves creation economic value in a way that it also creates value for 
society, by addressing its needs and challenges. By changing policies and operating practices and 
enhance the competitiveness of a company, by opening of new opportunities, gain efficiency, create 
differentiation and expand markets. This will not only change the approach for the single company, 
==PAGE:21==
20 
 
but drive of the next wave of innovation and productivity growth in the global economy (Porter and 
Kramer, 2011).  
Business should still act as business, and do what they do best, create value through trade of products 
or services.  But the basic understanding of differentiation in a market based on value creation have 
over looked the opportunity to meet fundamental societal needs, while the focus has been on profit 
maximization. This calls for a need to recognize that sustainable challenges can harm and create 
internal costs for the firm, such as wasted energy, raw material, costly accidents. And can help 
innovate through using new technologies or operating methods and management approaches to 
increase their productivity and expand their market. The company should also recognize that the 
health of the community surrounding it, will have a positive effect on the business. A business needs 
the successful community and the community needs successful businesses to provide jobs and wealth 
(Porter and Kramer, 2011). 
There is generally three ways to create shared value according to Porter and Kramer (2011) by 
reconceiving products and markets, redefining productivity in the value chain or enabling local cluster 
development.  
Reconceiving product and markets 
To achieve the SDGs there is a need for better health, housing, nutrition, energy e.g., a long list of 
unmet needs in global growing markets, with many new innovation opportunities.  This specially 
applies in emerging markets or developing countries where the societal needs or more pressing, and 
the market have not yet been recognized, but this also applies to disadvantaged areas of developed 
countries, such as poor urban areas (Porter and Kramer, 2011). 
Productivity in the value chain  
A company’s value chain affects and is affected by challenges such as natural resource and water use, 
health and safety, working conditions and equal treatment in the workspace, this can be explained by 
the following areas: 
Energy use: reexamine the energy use in processes, transportation, buildings, supply chains, 
distribution channels, or support services can be achieved by applying new technologies and lead to 
cost reduction. 
Logistics: shipping can be expensive and add unnecessary complexity, cost and time. New logistical 
systems or redesign can reduce shipping.  
Resource use: use of water, raw materials and packaging, recycling and reuse. The use of material is 
both costly for the society and the business. 
Procurement: Traditionally procurement have been aimed to drive down prices and outsourcing to 
suppliers in lower wages locations. Firms starts to recognize that this cannot remain productive or 
sustain or improve quality. Instead of by improving the production can create stronger suppliers.  
Distribution: Rethinking the way we distribute can have huge impact. Moving from paper to e-product 
or finding ways to distribute in nontraditional markets is a way to enter new markets.  
Employee productivity: Keeping wage level and benefits down directly affect the opportunity to 
advance of the employee’s productivity. Cutting down health care increases lost workdays. When the 
company invest in solutions that minimize pollution this will increase the employees’ health and 
minimize lost productivity.   
==PAGE:22==

Location: The outsourcing has moved production to the cheapest location without concerning local 
communities. Moving the production closer can reduce transport and easy restock smaller quantities.  
Local cluster development  
A company’s success is strongly related to the support and interactions with the society, companies 
and infrastructure that surround it. A geographic concentration of firms, academic programs, trade 
associations or standard originations, NGOs e.g. is defined as a “clusters”. The clusters have a strong 
connection to both the productivity and innovation.  
The local cluster does not only affect the innovation but can also create internal costs. If a company is 
surrounded by poor public education or infrastructure will add costs for staff training and logistics. Or 
a high poverty can limit the demands for products and leading to environmental degradation, 
unhealthy workers and high security costs. The company will benefit for contributing to build a strong 
local cluster and a more open and transparent markets. 
 
Creating shared value goes far beyond compliance, by looking at the opportunity to create economic 
value through creating societal value. This way of thinking calls for a new way of understanding 
customers, productivity and the external influence on corporate success. And the starting point is to 
identify all the societal needs, benefits and harms that could relate to the firm in a constantly changing 
system (Porter and Kramer, 2011). 
13.2.4 TRUE BUSINESS SUSTAINABILITY  
The third level “true business sustainability”(Dyllick and Muff, 2015), is built on the is a need to embed 
sustainability into the value chain and core strategy, beyond economic value. With a focus not only on 
short term risks but a need for business to contribute to the macro level challenges(Dyllick and Muff, 
2016).   
To achieve this companies will have to ask them self; How can we contribute to regional or global SD? 
Where the company start to shift perspective from wanting to minimize its negative impacts to 
understanding how it can create a significant positive impact in critical and relevant areas for society 
and the planet. The fundamental shift from 2.0 to 3.0 is the change from an inside out to an outside 
in perspective, where the company first looks first at the external environment within which it 
operates and then consider how they can contribute with its competencies. This can either be on an 
individual company level through innovation in product or process or sectorial or cross sectorial level 
to be part of changing the common approach.  
To achieve this Dyllick and Muff  (2015)has found three challenges that disconnect the companies 
from the society and sustainability agenda: 
• 
Poor integration of the three pillars of sustainability.  
• 
Missing integration of the macro level within the organizational micro level, the company 
focus on its own business, without the big global context.   
• 
The focus for business is driven by economic value creation, in a short-term planning.

13.3 SUPPORTING BUSINESS IMPLEMENTATION  
The private sector engagement with sustainable development is growing, yet little knowledge on how 
this organizations translate sustainability or the SDGs into their daily practices, or how these 
businesses identifying and addressing different issues (Topple et al., no date).  
Moving from CSR as a side project to integrate in to the strategy, requires a completely new approach 
and system understanding of the change and how it influences the company. To support this process 
and understanding, research shows that company requires a structured framework or management 
system (Azapagic and Perdan, 2011; Witjes, Vermeulen and Cramer, 2017; Hernandez-Vivanco, 
Bernardo and Cruz-C Azares, 2018).  
The range of sustainable management tools is broad, since they cover different areas of the process 
of implementation or management. According to a literature review from Hörisch et al. (2015), can 
the tools be organized based on the purpose they serve. There is generally four types of tools; 
accounting tools, indicators, product design and communication tools. 
Accounting tools: The first kind of tool is the sustainable accounting tools. They provide information 
to helping the company measure and track changes in the current performance, from energy use to 
analyzing product life cycle using e.g. LCA. Generally this can be used to highlight negative impacts. 
Indicators: To support the decision making the indicators helps to put the data into context and 
compare with alternatives or competitors. By doing so the indicators can help identify less harmful 
alternatives, among others can be mentioned eco-indicators, eco-efficiency indicators or sustainability 
indicators. 
Product design: The third group of tools support the company in improving the aspects of the design 
or service, by reducing the negative impacts. This group of tools include sustainable supply chain 
management, sustainable design, and eco-design. 
Communication: The communication and reporting tools support the company in how to 
communicate their impact to stakeholders. This include publishing of sustainability reports, 
sustainability labels. 
13.3.1 SUSTAINABLE BUSINESS IN SMALL AND MEDIUM SIZED ENTERPRISE (SME)  
The SMEs have been estimated to contribute with up to 70% of global pollution collectively (Johnson 
and Schaltegger, 2016). SMEs is defined by the European Commission by the number of employees to 
be between 10 -250 employees together with specifications for the annual turnover and annual 
balance sheet (European Commission, no date). Worldwide the SMEs adds up and account for more 
than 90% of all business and 50 % of employment and is often a major part of the supply chain of 
larger companies (IFC, no date). Zooming in at Europe 99% of all business in Europe is defined as SME 
(European Commission, no date). Taken a look at Denmark it is common that a company only have a 
small amount of employees. According to Danish Statistics, 92% of all companies in Denmark have less 
than 10 employees and only 0.7 % have more than 100 employees (Folketinget-Erhvervsudvalget, 
2005).  
Studies shows that SMEs green core competences, green innovation performance and green image is 
significantly less than large corporations, and are less likely to examine their environmental impact 
(Becherer and Helms, no date). Only 10% of the total number of sustainability reports in the GRI 
Sustainability Disclosure Database is from SMEs while the rest from large and multinational 
organizations (Global Reporting Initiative, 2016). And only 18% of the companies registered in EMAS 
(ISO standard environmental management system) in EU is SMEs (Hillary, 2000).

13.4 THE ROLE OF THE SDGS TOWARDS SUSTAINABLE BUSINESS 
The SDGs offers the business who would like to move towards sustainable business a framework as

understanding of sustainability as all the three pillars, they set specific targets for global challenges

For business can the SDGs be seen as “look in to the future”(Pedersen, 2018) both in term of future

But considering SDG implementation is not only a matter of future regulations. According to Pedersen

the company to find unmet needs. Research from the report Better Business Better World estimates 
that achieving the SDGs could open market opportunities in 60 market hotspots worth an estimated 
US$12 trillion by 2030 in business savings and revenue(Business and Sustainable Development

13.4.1 THE ADVANTAGE OF BEING SMALL 
The SMEs have an advantage, the size of the organization have a strong influence of the ability to

innovation management of rapid decision making combined with building network linkage to 
compensate for the resource limitations (Tidd; Joe, Bessant and Pavitt, 2001).

13.5.2 ECO SYSTEM MAPPING  
To understand the challenges in the current socio-technical system an ecosystem mapping helps to 
illustrates the interconnections between actors and identify gaps.  
With a system thinking approach to sustainability the ecosystem is encapsulated in an environmental 
sphere, to illustrate that all actors navigate with in the same space of planetary boundary (Raworth, 
2012).  
The ecosystem is separated in two arenas, the UN arena and the SMEs own arena. Looking at the UN 
area we have the UN as an umbrella.  As the name implies is the UN driven by its member states the 
local government, which therefore have an important role both in the decision making and the 
implementation. Since the UN is both the largest and most international organization in the world, it 
is organized in a several agencies. In this case the focus will be on the UNDP, more specifically on the 
regional institute of the global UNDP. The role of the regional institutions (UNDP Nordic) is to support 
the implementation nationally and link the local initiatives together on a global scale, through their 
work in 170 countries and territories.  
The SDGs is a UN initiative, the intergovernmental position of the UN have an important role in the 
journey towards sustainable development, it makes it possible to have a global holistic understanding, 
and apply this to the local initiatives. The important role of the actual execution and implementation 
of the goals is the local government responsibility on a national level. 
The SME is illustrated in its value chain, to give an overview of the potential areas of SDG impact which 
not only is limited to the internal production activities but include the stages from suppliers to end of 
use of the product life. Creating value for the customer is the essence of a business, it’s the reason 
products differentiate from each other and the reason the customer buys one product instead of 
another and lead to competitive advantage. The companies is a focal part of the society not only do 
they create economic value but also affects both the social and environmental system. The society 
also creates the frame for the SME which in they operate, both through regulations, the market and 
the access to resources.   
An observed gap in the system is the contribution from the SMEs to the SDGs. SMEs is as stated earlier

14.1.2.2 CHARACTERISTICS

Characteristics for SMEs is that they operate usually locally and unlike large firms, tend to be

more agile then large firms (Tidd; Joe, Bessant and Pavitt, 2001). 
What characterize the end-users is that they may have different tasks at hand and cannot be expected 
to have prior knowledge or technical background within sustainability management or innovation 
management. Small companies does often not have their own R&D department, in fact does only 16

department”.  So to define the end-user of the tool, this depends on the size of the company. In the

14.2 MEASURING IMPACT 
Working towards sustainable development have for long been broadly accepted, yet the process on 
how to measure progress to achieve the SD is not yet a common definition. In this section the terms 
relevant to define impact measurement will be described based on literature review. 
14.2.1 SDG IMPACT 
An impact is defined as an effect or change on something, which in this case is the change in relation 
to sustainable development. When discussing impact it should be specified in the case of positive or 
negative effect. However in most cases this can easily be determined by the context, depending on 
the contribution to the sustainable development, the goal is to avoid negative impacts and maximize 
positive impacts. But in order to measure impact it is necessary to have a common understanding on 
how to define and measure this effect.. 
SDG impact is then defined as an effect that contributes to achieving the SDGs. The SDG impact can 
be quantified by the use of the selected 232 indicators, which is directly connected to each of the SDG 
targets. 
 
14.2.2 IMPACT MEASUREMENT  
When considering SDG impact for a project, a guideline for companies to understand and evaluate the 
potential impact have been made by leading standard setting originations, the network IMP (Impact 
Management Project, no date). They have defined five dimensions to be aware of when measuring 
impact. First the organization must define what is the desired outcome, which SDGs are targeted? 
Then they must consider who the involved stakeholders are? Third is the how much of the outcome 
occurs, such as duration of the project, and how many does it effect. The fourth dimension is to 
consider the actual contribution, would the change had happened anyways? And the last dimension 
is to consider the risk of implementation if the impact does not occur as expected or have not intended 
negative effect on other areas, since a new action or technology can sometimes have a negative 
behavioral or systematic effect after implementation, this is called the rebound effect.  
Impact can be measured by indicators. The SDG indicators in the SDG framework is targeted an 
international or national level, but to measure progress towards the SDGs on a local or corporate level 
different set of indicators may be needed. There exist quite a few different sets of indicators, as many 
indicators is developed for a specific company or sector (Azapagic and Perdan, 2011). In order to 
support the companies in selecting indicators, an overview on existing business indicators and the 
relation to each SDG have been collected at the SDG Compass platform (GRI, UN Global Compact and 
WBCSD, 2016).  
As example can be mentioned the WBCSD’s eco-efficiency indicators, who focus on measuring 
resource efficiency, the minimizing the resources used in producing, and resource productivity, the 
efficiency of economic activities in generating added value from the use of resources.  
On some impact categories can it be challenging to find indictors, while the economic and 
environmental performances are relatively easy to find indicators for and to measure, it can be more 
challenging to measure the social sustainability aspects since they can be difficult to quantify (Azapagic 
and Perdan, 2011), an example of an indicator framework that covers the social aspects, is the GRI 
sustainability indicators (Azapagic and Perdan, 2011).

14.2.3 IMPACT ASSESSMENT TOOLS  
A sustainability assessment tool is defined as a decision support tool that directs decision making 
toward sustainability. The term is however more commonly used as an umbrella for different 
procedures, practices, processes, methodologies, methods, frameworks, and tools that focus on 
measuring or promoting sustainability (Moldavska and Welo, 2019). Overall does the variety of 
already existing sustainability management tools covers tools for analysis and evaluation (e.g. LCA), 
tools for action (e.g. EMS) and tools for communication (e.g. sustainability reporting) (Johnson and 
Schaltegger, 2016), where the focus for this project will be on tools for evaluation. 
A sustainable assessment tool can be defined based on different parameters; the target group, 
purpose, project scope and process. The target group can be on different levels such as country, city, 
and company level (Moldavska and Welo, 2019).  The tools variate in scope, some tools focus on a 
specific product, a specific part of the supply chain or the value chain. The tools also differentiate in 
the sustainability scope, where some tools focus on a specific area of sustainability such as climate 
change or gender equality and others have a broader scope of sustainability. A last aspect is the tools 
can be defined based on the temporal perspective of when the assessment is used, this can be 
separated on three phases: ex-ante (pre-project “predict the future), in-itinere (doing the project 
observe if change is taking place with criteria to monitor) and ex-post (post project, measuring effect 
after change both short and medium term effect). (Biénabe, Rival and Loeillet, 2017). 
To limit the scope to have relevance for the project, a tool will in this thesis be scoped to: 
• 
Target group: the focus will be on tools addressing SMEs and is there for most relevant to look 
at the tools targeting a company level.  
• 
Scope: The scope of the value chain  
• 
Sustainability focus: In this study only tools that have a broad definition of sustainability  
• 
Time: Here the ex-ante is interesting are useful since it influence the decision process for 
direct the innovation project. 
 
14.2.4 SDG BUSINESS OPPORTUNITY  
The large framework of the SDGs have many touch points with the value chain, and there is no doubt 
the SD will be the driver for innovation (Porter and Kramer, 2011) as described in section 0. Both public 
and private organizations will establish SDG-related projects and lead new or growth of existing 
markets. 
 
Referring to the shared value definition, a business opportunities will have to create competitive 
advantage through reconceiving product and markets, productivity in the value chain or local cluster 
development. In any way sustainable innovation is needed, covering a new or significantly improved 
production, deliver method or capabilities of goods or services (OECD cited in Hernandez-Vivanco, 
Bernardo and Cruz-C Azares, 2018). 
 
To define the SDG Business opportunity, the definition of “sustainable innovation” and “eco-
innovaiton” have been used as a baseline: 
 
Sustainable innovation can according to Hernandez-Vivanco, Bernardo and Cruz-C Azares, 
(2018) be defined as the utilization of technological solutions to minimize environmental and social 
impacts of manufacturing operation as well as reduce the impact from the design of the entire life 
cycle, and most contribute to the TBL with measurable improvements.

Eco-innovation according to Pigosso, Schmiegelow and Andersen Eco-innovation (2018) is

14.4.1 RESULT OF LITERATURE REVIEW 
The result of the structured research shows that not many tools already exist to assess the SDG impact

implementation in SME’s, only one focus on SMEs implementation of environment (Zorpas, 2009). 
And 5 support a hotspot analysis.  
In the 16 articles, around 25 tools have been identified, where 3 was relevant to look further into 
based on the exclusion criteria. Tools that does not focus on the assessment of impact and

14.4.1.1 LIFE CYCLE-BASED TOOLS 
Lifecycle thinking and life cycle analysis (LCA) or is one of the most traditional way of working with 
assessing sustainability of a company life cycle, an important tool and should be part of decision-
making (Pesonen and Horn, no date). However its criticized for being both resource consuming and 
difficult to understand, LCAs are valuable assessment tools, in some cases, they cannot be used due 
to lack of time or resources (Pesonen and Horn, no date), since the LCA is based on quantities data 
and require prior knowledge in the architecture of the tool. According to Pesonen and Horn “For 
practical decision-making in early phases, there is a demand for less complicated, thus more widely 
utilizable, tools in situations in which preliminary analyses” (Pesonen and Horn, no date). Many tools 
have been developed to be both quicker and less resource demanding tools to address the life cycle. 
From the literature research three of such tools where fined, the Hotspot Analysis (HSA) (Rohn et al., 
2014), the Sustainability SWOT (Pesonen and Horn, no date) and the 7-step approach (McAloone and 
Bey, 2009) additionally one tools where added from grey literature, who assess the SDGs more 
specifically namely the step 2 “define priorities” from the SDG compass (GRI, UN Global Compact and 
WBCSD, 2016).  
Hot Spot analysis HSA 
The HSA is a method to identify social and ecological problems along the entire life cycle of a product. 
The tools main focus is on locating long-term and mid-term risks in value chains by using a brought 
range of key indicators. The method is divided into five steps (1) first step is to determine the aim of 
scope where the life cycle phases is defined and categories to asses, this can be supported by scientific 
==PAGE:46==
45 
 
literature to determine typical process. (2) Step two is a literature review, both secondary such as 
trade unions and primary internal data e.g. the year report, practically this is done in a table to gain 
an overview. When the data is collected for each category is given an index score of relevance from 1-
3. (3) step three is to now weight the life cycle steps by the same index score. (4) The hotspot is then 
determined by multiplying the category value with the value of the phase. (5) All values between 6 -9 
is defined as hotspots and the final step is the present and evaluate with stakeholders (Rohn et al., 
2014). 
Sustainability SWOT 
Sustainability SWOT (Pesonen and Horn, no date) is made to make the lifecycle approach quicker and 
easy to understand as is tailed to the well know business decision tool SWOT (Strengths, Weaknesses, 
Opportunities, Threats).  The tool can be used by a company on either a single product or a product 
group. The method is split into five stages: (1) Identification of product life cycle stages (2) 
Identification of sustainability impacts from all three pillars both now and in the future (3) Significance 
assessment of the sustainability impacts by a score from 1-3 per impact (4) Compilation of the 
Sustainability SWOT. The information is collected in a single matrix, using colours or symbols to define 
the life cycle stage. The tool is based on qualitative and semi-quantitative data, but is suggested to be 
carried out as a brainstorm between the business and sustainability experts.  
7-step approach 
Systematic and creative 7-step approach to identify the company's potential for creating synergy 
between environmental improvement and business creation (McAloone and Bey, 2009). (1) Use 
context describes who how and what is used, (2) step 2 is to create an overview of product life cycle 
and significant impacts. The impacts is find by brainstorming for each phase. (3) step 3 is to organize 
the impacts according to the MECO-matrix categories: material, energy, chemicals and other, and 
identify the top 5. (4) step 4 is to identify relevant stakeholders and sketch a stakeholder network, (5) 
step 5: quantify by use of LCA- based method or similar. (6) the next step is to ideate and create new 
concepts based on the assesment, (7) the last step is to develop a strategy by use of a spider web and 
plot the current level and the goal for each environmental factor. 
SDG Compass step 2 – Define Priorities  
SDG Compass (grey litt.) (GRI, UN Global Compact and WBCSD, 2016) step 2 define priorities: (1) map 
the value chain to identify impact areas, sort between negative and positive impacts (2) select 
indicators for each of the identified high impact areas using the SDG Compass inventory (webpage), 
when choosing indicators its suggested to use the Logic Model and consider availability of data and 
combine lagging and leading indicators (3) the last step is to define priorities, consider the likelihood 
of the impacts and the stakeholder interest and consider the business opportunities before choosing. 
14.4.1.2 SURVEY / QUESTION-BASED TOOLS: 
Ramboll SDG impact assessment tool (grey litt) (Ramboll, no date) and B-impact assessment tool (grey 
litt) (GLOBAL VALUE, no date). In the Ramboll the output is a custom made report where in the B-
impact it is a dashboard where you can go back to your result or edit your answers. 
Ramboll SDG Impact Assessment Tool 
Ramboll SDG Impact Assessment tool (grey literature) (Ramboll, no date) is a questioner based self-
assessment tool that address the companies impact with in three categories: products and service, 
manufacturing and value chain and business administration. The assessment also asks for your sector 
and geographical area, however it is an early version that focus on building materials or 
==PAGE:47==
46 
 
pharmaceuticals, but is intended to be expanded to address other industries as well. The result of the 
assessment is a report that the user can receive by email, the report include an introduction to the 
SDGs and a list of the SDGs the company directly impact or indirectly either support or have potential 
in supporting. 
B impact assessment  
B impact assessment is a questionnaire based self-assessment tool that focuses on assessing a 
company’s sustainability management system and sustainability performance (grey literature) 
(GLOBAL VALUE, no date). The questionnaire is structured along five business thematic areas. To tailor 
the assessment to the user there is around 40 versions of the tool, depending on the size of the 
company, the operating in emerging or developed markets and manufacturing or services industries. 
The tool can be implemented within a few week. It is facilitated by an online platform that guides the 
user through the questionnaire and results in a score on a scale from 0-200, which also works as a 
benchmark for similar companies.  
14.4.1.3 LIST BASED TOOLS 
Future Fit argues (Future Fit, 2018) that a survey based approach can limit the result since it rely on 
the way the questions are formulated. In order to avoid this a category of “list based tools” have been 
identified. The list based tools provide the user with a list of criteria that the company should consider. 
Two such tools as been identified the Future Fit (grey literature) (Future Fit, 2018), and the GRI SDG 
Business report analysis (grey literature) (GRI, no date). 
The Future-Fit business benchmark tool 
The Future-Fit business benchmark tool(Future Fit, 2018) consist of 23 Break Even Goals. The goals is 
meant as a minimum threshold that any company most reach to consider them self-sustainable and 
20 Positive Pursuit, for what companies may implement to have a bigger impact. They are split into 
four areas: fostering well-being, respecting nature, optimizing resources, and strengthening society. 
For each goal a “action guide” can be downloaded to support the company in the implementation. 
SDG Business Report analysis 
GRI and UN Global Compact have made the SDG Business Report analysis, which contain two 
documents: An analysis of the goals and targets, which contain a list of possible actions per SDG Target 
and a list of possible gaps and how to report them. The other document “integrating the SDGs into 
Corporate Reporting A practical Guide” provide a structured approach to reporting. The guide is ment 
for companies to understand their potential impacts on the SDG targets in order to define priorities 
and how to report progress. 
14.4.1.4 FILTER BY RELEVANCE

the list of SDGs according to criteria the user fill in.  
SDG Selector 
PWC SDG selector (grey litt) (PwC, no date) The SDG Selector is created by the consultancy PwC, a 
quick internet based tool that sort the tools based on industry, country or them. 
GAPFRAME 
GAPFRAME (grey litt) (Raworth, 2012), suggest being used by business as a look up tool and pointer 
for direction based on the region they are operating. (Muff, Kapalka and Dyllick, 2017a)

14.4.2.2 LEARN FROM BEST PRACTICE TOOLS 
The second purpose of the literature review is to understand current available method, to due so three 
elements have been the focus for the comparison, the data collection, the user group and the steps 
of the process.    
14.4.2.2.1 DATA COLLECTION  
Looking at the data collection, most of the tools are based on qualitative or semi-quantitative data  
(i.e., relative scale such as ‘low’, ‘medium’, ‘high’ for, for example, ‘customer satisfaction’, 
‘organizational image’). Few tools however encourage to use qualitative measures as part of the 
assessment. This is the SDG Compass who encourage LCA based decisions for the identification of 
impacts before the significant assessment.  And in the fourth step the 7-step approach suggest 
verifying the result using a quantitative tool such as LCA or a simplified LCA. 
14.4.2.2.2 THE USER 
Another aspect to look at is, who is involved in the process. In the HSA and Sustainability SWOT they 
suggest a combination of the company and sustainability expert (Pesonen and Horn, no date). In the 
7-step approach it is suggested that all staff who play an active role in the areas of the company, which 
may be linked to the product’s environmental impacts should be present at the workshop (McAloone 
and Bey, 2009).  
It is recommended by the SDG Compass (GRI, UN Global Compact and WBCSD, 2016) to include both 
external and internal stakeholders, however they should be prioritizes based on who may be impacted 
by its decisions and activities, as well as consider the view of stakeholder who are unable to “speak” 
such as future generations or the eco-system. 
One article focus on the importance of involving the end user by the use of co-creation  (Arnold, 2014), 
since co-creation changes the perspective from organization-based to (value) ‘chain-based’ 
production processes and focuses on the vertical chain-production within inter organizational 
relations as well. Such as innovation workshops, web community or idea competitions. 
14.4.2.2.3 PROCESS STEPS 
All the tools have a different approach to assessing impact, but in order to understand what a tool 
should contain, an analysis of steps towards to result have been made (Appendix E). This results in 
overall five general steps generalized for the tools. Not all tools contain all the steps.  
The first step is to define sustainability areas to asses, by mean the scope of sustainability that the 
company should consider. The HCA offers a list of impact categories to consider for the assessment 
which is also the case for the SD based tools that consider the SDG goals or targets as the scope of 
assessment. However it is not all tools that provide a framework for this step, 3 out of the 10 tools 
suggest the company to perform a structured brainstorming based on a mapped value chain. 
The second step identified is to find touchpoints between company and the sustainability areas to 
assess. This is generally done in two ways, either in a workshop setting where the value chain is 
mapped out and the impact areas are mapped, or using the survey based tool where questions are 
asked directly related to business activities. Although this is also on the list of identified “gaps”, it is 
still included in this steps. This is because the tools that do not support the process of identifying 
touchpoints, still expect this to be part of the process. An example is the Sustainable SWOT that 
assume the hotspots is found beforehand, or the list based that due not provide additional support to 
actually locate the touchpoints.

The next step of the design process is to understand how a solution can fulfill the needs and fill this 
gap. To make this more tangible, a functional analysis is carried out, to split the task in to bids, before 
generating ideas and concepts. The final concept are chosen based on the design criteria, and further

15.1 FUNCTIONAL ANALYSIS: OPENING THE BLACK BOX  
A function analysis (Nigel Cross, 2000) helps to understand to define the scope of the design, by 
defining what the solution should be based on and what it should accomplish, as shown on the 
diagram below (Figure 12 Black box).  
 
The desired output of the tool is a prioritization of the SDG hotspots in the company’s value chain 
based on the business opportunity. Business opportunity as earlier defined, covers the possibility to 
achieve competitive advantage in a current or new market by applying a new or significant change to 
either the product/ service or processes across the value chain that leads to a measurable contribution 
for the UN Sustainable Development Goals. 
 
The tools will have to function as a boundary object, an object that support the translation and 
understanding, between the SDGs and the SMEs who through the tool gain an understanding of how 
they can relate to the SDGs and how they can contribute. The tool should do this by making the 
complex framework tangible and possible to understand for business. After the assessment the tool 
should be a boundary object between the SME and the co-works or potential consultants to 
communicate the result and translate it in to strategy.  
 
The inputs available can be split in two areas, the internal inputs from the company and the external 
available data. Looking at the external data we have the SDG framework, SDG or sustainability 
statistics and scientific articles, as well as other quantitative data such geographical data collected on 
the SDGs or trade unions. Externally the use of sustainability experts could also be considered. 
Internally from the company we have the knowledge from the management, cross company 
employees and customers, suppliers and other external stakeholders, although not all of them will be 
easy to reach with the scope of this solution. It would therefore be more manageable for an initial 
assessment to focus on the management and cross company employees. Internally they may also have 
access to relevant data from annual reports or data collection, however as specified in the design 
specification this should not be a requirement for the solution (appendix D). The analysis of existing 
tools gave inputs for defining the input. Most of the tools is based on qualitative data, semi-
quantitative data. The involved stakeholders varies, from a broad range of internal staff, to external 
stakeholders from the suppliers or customer involvement and sustainability experts.

To transfer this in the context of assessing SDG hotspots the black box can be broken down to these 
sub functions:  
1) Define the impact areas to assess 
Define the impact area to assess, it is important to set the scope clear and define the level of 
detail. Even though the assessment should asses the SDG impacts, the categories may still be 
different from the 17 overreaching goals. 
 
2) Find touchpoints between the business and the impact area 
A long list of impact areas will not all be relevant for the specific company who wish to find 
their SDG with the highest sustainable business potential. First step is to limit the list by finding 
the SDGs that may have touchpoints with the firm. A touchpoint can be areas where the 
company have possibility to reduce their impact of current product or processes or new 
opportunities e.g. by applying product to a new emerging market. 
 
3) Assess the significance 
Assess the touchpoints to find hotspots, by addressing their potential for business opportunity 
for the firm, to understand which of the touchpoints may be relevant to prioritize.  
 
4) Evaluate the hotspots 
Evaluate the hotspots by comparing the assessment of the hotspots. The evaluation should 
also support the users understanding of the impact, include the interconnection between 
goals.

5) Choose priorities

15.3 DETAILING CONCEPT  
In this section the chosen concept will be detailed by looking in the design of the filters, create impact 
areas, evaluation criteria for the assessment and how the result should be presented.  
15.3.1 FILTERS 
The filter are chosen to be one for the sector and one for the geographical area. Other filters that have 
been considered is “size of company” (amount of employees or turn over) since this is used in the 
Ramboll tool and B assessment. However their data is not available on how they relate to the SDGs. 
An overview of both filters and their relation to the SDGs can be found in appendix G. 
 
15.3.1.1 GEOGRAPHICAL FILTER 
The geographical progress and hotspots can be measured based on the SDG indicators (United 
Nations, 2017).  
Different sources, already collect this data, where to have been compared, the GAP frame data and 
the SDG Index. The GAP frame provides a score for each impact area and each country which explains 
if the country is in a “safe”, “critical” or “threat” state, inspired by the planetary boundary theory 
(Muff, Kapalka and Dyllick, 2017b). The SDG index report (Sachs, J., Schmidt-Traub, G., Kroll, C., 
Lafortune, G., Fuller, 2018) provides information of progress towards the SDGs, by using the indicators 
and compare with the 2030 goal, they highlight the areas which need the most focus in order to 
achieve the goals.  
 
Since the SDG Index is directly related to the progress towards SDGs, and moreover is updated 
annually, this data source is choses for the tool. The data level available can be narrowed to a national 
level, but is for the design of the tool chosen to be on a region level to limit the complexity.  The data 
for the filter can be find in the SDG Index  methodological paper (Sachs, J., Schmidt-Traub, G., Kroll, 
C., Lafortune, G., Fuller, 2018), the relations to SDGs can be found in appendix G. 
 
15.3.1.2 SECTOR FILTER  
The existing data available that evaluate SDG hotspots for a sector or industry, have only been found 
from the SDG selector, which provides a list of “biggest challenges” and “biggest opportunities” for a 
list of industries. However the list does not specifically manufacturing companies and is based on 
qualitative data of business own perception of the SDGs, that completely leave out five of the SDGs. 
 
To generate the filters the categories of sectors is based on the UN calcification of manufacturing 
sectors (United Nations, 2008). The correlation between each of the manufacturing sectors is then 
estimated based on research, which can be find in appendix H. The correlations mark the hotspots 
where the industry have a potential for influence. This can either be duo to a tendency to having a 
negative impact on the SDG or a potential to gain new opportunities.  
To the option list for the filter the possibility to choose “other” is also added, for companies who do 
not fall in the categories.  
 
15.3.2 IMPACT AREAS 
Taking a starting point at the SDGs and the underlying 169 targets it clear that each SDG covers several 
different areas, to mention an example SDG 3 “Good Health and Well-being”, covers both maternal 
death, epidemics, use of drugs, alcohol, traffic accidents and air pollution. To make a target like this 
more tangible for the business to understand in a survey format, impact areas have been created for 
each SDG. The impact areas is generated by inspiration from the 169 SDG targets, where areas directly 
related to a governmental level have been excluded. This results in a list of 42 sub areas divided on 
the 17 SDGs, an average of 2.5 impact area per SDG. They can be found in appendix I.

15.3.4 EVALUATION SCALE 
To make the data comparable across indicators, a normalization can be made by rating each variable 
using the same scale. The choose of scale have a direct influence on the responses and impact the 
overall survey data quality, specially the numbers of response options, labeling of options or the 
provision of an explicit ‘‘don’t know’’ option, is just some of the considerations which should be 
considered. To understand this complexity a literature review was found by DeCastellarnau (2018) 
which looks at the classification of response scales effect on data quality. The literature review both 
gives a good understanding of possible alternatives and an indication of how each effect the data 
==PAGE:64==
63 
 
quality. Therefore a morphology chart is made to visually have an overview of the alternatives (Table 
8 Scale design).  
Looking at the labeling different options is considered, if the evaluation should be on a “agree – 
disagree”, “low to high potential”, simply add numbers and rather the scale should be partly or fully 
labeled. The scale is chosen to be from “low potential to high potential” and only partially labelled, 
means to have a start and mid label. The reason is that it reduce the cognitive demand to have a 
specific scale – and to not have a fully labelled scale.  
The second area is the design of the scale length. First of all it should be considered of the scale should 
be an even or uneven number. Where an uneven number is chosen, to have an implicit neutral 
alternative in the middle. According to the literature review too few categories can make it difficult to 
differentiate between responses where too many categories reduce the clarity of what the options 
mean. When having an item specific labels (like the high – low potential) and not a (agree- disagree) 
the research show that the data quality does not improve for more than 7 points. Because of this the 
scale length is set to 7. However the longer the scale the more complex it is for the user and other 
implications such as the length if the device screen should also be taken into considerations, this is a 
relevant aspect to evaluate for the test.   
The cognitive perception is closely connected to the visualization of the scale. If the scale is a chose 
between points, a slider, text box, drop down or drag and drop option.  The scale is chosen to be point 
selection, horizontal scale from low to high. The reason is that the study show this is easy to perceive 
for the user. What should be considered where is the future perspective of unrolling the tool in a 
international context, where the reading direction might be different, this can affect the perception 
of scale and in worst case influence the result.

between the designer and the tester, which makes it possible to communicate the design and gain 
valuable feedback for further work. When deciding on a prototype it is important to consider the 
purpose of the test and how this should be reflected in the model. The prototype should be possible 
to adopt and change along the testing and improves, or it is sometimes relevant to considered making 
different prototypes for different test groups.

For the chosen concept two kind of prototypes is created, a visual and a functional model. The visual

advantage to be possible to quickly adapt to changes along the testing. The functional prototype have 
the purpose of showcase how the solution will function, by offer the key elements of the solution. The

be used, try to answer questions and get a result.

15.5 FINAL DESIGN  
After testing, the changes have been implemented in to the design. In this section the “final” design

The final concept of the SDG Impact Assessment, support SMEs to assess which SDGs they should 
prioritize based on potential contribution to the SDGs and the business opportunity. The assessment 
takes an outside in perspective by taking a starting point in those global challenges with urgent need 
for action, that have relevance for the firm. And evaluating this list based on their potential for creating 
business opportunity. 
The user of the assessment can either be a single employee but for a more in depend assessment and 
understanding, it is suggested that the management along with a group of cross company employees 
all fill in the assessment, and evaluate based on a collected result presentation.

15.5.1 IMPACT FILTERS  
The first part, the impact filters, ask the user to choose the area of main business from a list of 
geographical regional areas. This filter is based on data from the SDG Index, and will sort the SDGs 
which is furthest away from being met by the geographical area by 2030. The second filter ask the 
user to fill in the sector they belong to, the user can use from a list of manufacturing sectors. This filter 
is based on research and will chose the SDGs that the sector have the biggest impact or opportunity 
to impact. For both filters there is an option to choose “other”, which will select all SDGs.  
The filters will make sure to leave no goal behind, by only focusing on the once which need the rapid 
progress, and will also guide the company to the areas the country will focus on, which can open 
potential funding or support.  
15.5.2 OPPORTUNITY ASSESSMENT  
The second part is the opportunity assessment, which guide the user to locate the SDGs that 
potentially could led to a new business opportunity. The assessment guides the user through a list of 
SDG impact areas, and is asked for each of them to assess the potential for financial performance, cost 
reduction, brand reputation, satisfying customers or satisfying employees. Since some ideas might 
come along the way an option to comment is added. Some areas might not be relevant for the specific 
company do to the generic filters, which is why the option of choosing not relevant can be chosen  
The amount of questions will depends on the chosen impact filters, but is estimated to take around 
30-45 min. in average, but can take up to 2 hours for the entire assessment. 
15.5.3 EVALUATE RESULT 
The result is visually presented for the user, who can compare the overall result for the SDGs or chose 
the business opportunity criteria they might find most relevant. They can also find a comparison 
between the top 5 impact areas.  Along with the overview additional information about the SDG 
interactions and inspiration can be find for each of the SDGs.   
Based on the evaluation the user can choose focus SDG, which they can use for a following innovation 
process or strategical goal setting.

16.1.2 FINAL DESIGN  
In this section a reflection on how the final solution meet the goal is discussed. The initial aim of this 
project was to develop a tool to support SMEs in their contribution to the UN 2030 Agenda through 
the identification of SDG hotspots in their value chain.  
The final design is a SDG impact assessment tool that support SMEs to find the SDGs where they 
potentially could have the biggest contribution assessed based on the potential business opportunity.  
Support SMEs in their contribution to the UN 2030 Agenda  
The tool support the SMEs contribution to the 2030 Agenda, by support them in the navigation 
through the complex framework. The tool do so by directing the user to where they potentially should 
be able to have the biggest contribution through the impact filters as well as making them relevant 
for company through a focus on the business value creation. 
Where this tool might support the process of identifying business opportunities while contributing to 
the SDGs, a critical pitfall to be aware of is the concern of greenwashing. Since the tool does not assess 
and compare the firms existing negative sustainability impacts, the risk of creating positive impact for 
one SDG through a new innovation project, while on other SDGs have big potential for reducing a 
negative impact. This increase the risk of greenwashing, since the company will appear to doing good 
while “hiding” negative impacts. While greenwashing maybe sound like an intended action, it should 
be mentioned that this could also be a consequence of simply not being aware of the negative impact, 
since the assessment tool is voluntary and used internally, this might lead to selective interpretation 
as well (Topple et al., no date).  
Nevertheless, not all problems can be solved by one single tool. Other tools might be better to find 
the negative impacts the firm should mitigate or future risks the company should adapt to. This have 
not been the focus for the development of this tool, but should be kept in mind when considering the 
overall SDG contribution. 
The focus on finding potential business opportunity, lower the risk of projects being a onetime charity 
donation, but potentially an economically sustainable and scalable solution. One might argue that the 
focus on business opportunities and innovation makes the SDGs relevant and more tangible for 
businesses, which could trigger an initial consideration for implementing sustainably into the strategy, 
which can then lead to further and broader implementation in the organization.  
Another important pitfall to consider, is the risk of the filters sort out the SDG that might be highly 
relevant for the specific company in a certain context. This risk is lower by adding the function of 
personalize the list of SDGs, by the possibility to add to the list. 
The key to truly contribute to the 2030 agenda, is a good implementation of the tool. This is both 
through spreading the word through relevant originations, getting SMEs onboard, support the SMEs 
in interpret the result and in the development from idea to implementation.  
Identification of SDG hotspots in their value chain 
The tool support the identification of SDG hotspots in their value chain and beyond, through the 
criteria that covers a broad range of the value chain. However this could be further enhances with 
more explicit questions that directly relate to the value chain activities.  Through the project new 
knowledge was discovered, that the biggest potential contribution may not be found only by 
improving and focus on the firm’s own value chain of activities. Which have expanded the aim to 
identification of SDG hotspots in their existing value chain and the potential of new market creation.

immediate next step can be found. 
 
In conclusion, the gap of supporting the SMEs in their contribution to the SDGs need rapid action. This 
tool have the purpose of supporting SMEs in their navigation, through simplification of its complexity 
and making it tangible for business by highlighting the opportunities they offer rather than risk. This 
tools was never meant to stand alone, but should be set in context with a following innovation process 
that guides the user further from ideation to implementation.

16.3 RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FURTHER WORK  
Based on the evaluation suggestions for the next step to further enhance the tool will be described on 
this section. 
16.3.1 FURTHER TESTING AND PILOT  
The immediate next step of the design process is to test with the SMEs, in order to make sure it meets 
the needs and desire. This would recommended to include different target groups; (1) SMEs who have 
been part of the first round of the SDG Accelerator program, (2) SMEs who will be part of the next 
round of the program and (3) SMEs who  is not part of the program but wish to implement SDGs. It is 
suggested to use the start of the next round of the SDG Accelerator as pilot testing for the 16 SMEs.   
Additionally it is suggested to consider the range of SMEs to work in diverse sectors, geographical 
areas and with different backgrounds, since different “languages” and cultures might affect the 
interpretation of the assessment, and would be valuable to make the tool robust enough to stand 
alone in a potential international use.  
16.3.2 FURTHER SUPPORT OF THE CONTEXT UNDERSTANDING   
As discussed in the previous section, the outside in perspective can make it difficult for companies to 
fully understand how they can relate to their current business activities. The criteria was evaluated 
through the testing to capture the business context, however this could be further elaborated in the 
tool by implementing examples of ideas for how the impact area might be solved or how it could relate 
the business. Another recommendation is to consider if the sector categories to be more specific, or 
filter the impact area rather than the SDGs to ensure questions with higher relevance for the context.  
To support the process further towards measurable contribution, the chosen SDG or SDG impact area, 
could be added with a list of indicators suggested to measure the progress. 
16.3.3 ENHANCE FURTHER INNOVATION JOURNEY 
It is recommend to consider how the immediate next step of the innovation journey could be further 
supported, to ensure a larger potential impact from the tool. It is recommended to consider further 
support in these four areas: Innovation resource allocation, innovation tools, goal setting and network. 
According to innovation theory (Tidd; Joe, Bessant and Pavitt, 2001) can the innovation journey be 
generalized to five steps. The immediate next step after choosing a focus, is to allocate resources to 
the project either found internally or externally. The next step is the ideation process. It should be 
considered how this process could be further supported, one aspect could be to help the SME to prior 
to the assessment to consider the available resources. It might also be relevant to refer the company 
to how they can find investment opportunities, innovation or sustainability consultancy. 
The process of setting goals and roadmap, might also be relevant to support. This could be included 
in the tool or include a suggestion to other relevant tools, such as UNDP’s BCtA Impact Lab(UNDP, no 
date a), a online tool that help the SDG goal setting and road mapping. Maybe other tools could be 
interesting to refer to as a tool box. 
Innovation often grow from a combination of knowledge areas put together (Tidd; Joe, Bessant and 
Pavitt, 2001). Connecting the user to the possibility to network with other companies in similar 
situations or find potential partners, would enhance the possibility for innovation. This could be 
supported by networking events or refer to existing SDG partnership platforms.

16.3.4 ENHANCE THE RETURN  
Further development should look in to enhancing the return of the user, in order to ensure continues 
process of new innovation projects. Suggestions include send out notifications, or include a possibility 
to roadmap several impact areas on a prioritized list.  
16.3.5 FROM MVP TO FINAL TOOL 
In order to move the product from minimum viable product (MVP) to the suggested final layout, a 
few processes is needed. It is recommended to make an expert review of the sector filter, the 
current data set is based on estimations which should be reviewed optimally with sector specific 
sustainability experts.   
In order to move from a excel platform to a web application, this can be achieved either by a simple 
wep app that is estimated by a back end developer would take around a fulltime week, 5 % for the 
functionality and 95% for the presentation and styling. Additional time would be added if new 
functions are added or if the tool should be usable from a mobile (Andreas Mønster, back end 
developer, Ariofit).  
To avoid update, the final tool is recommend to be build using open API from the SDG Index, this 
would allow the tool to stay updated to the geographical data2  
16.3.6 OTHER APPLICATIONS  
The tool have been developed with the target group manufacturing SMEs. However it is 
recommended for further work to consider other possible application who might find the tool 
relevant. 
Other target groups than might be relevant to consider is to expand to other sectors then 
manufacturing, a suggested tool for entrepreneurs who wish to find ideas for a start-up. Public 
organizations or NGOs who will like to find new project, might also be relevant, however would have 
a different focus then business opportunities.   
The development have been based on the SDG Accelerator participates, to apply the tool for SMEs in 
general one might consider if the innovation process and measurable contribution should be further 
enhanced to be able to stand alone, but still led to impactful project implementation.
