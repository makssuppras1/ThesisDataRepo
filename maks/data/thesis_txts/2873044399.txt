Summary
Riverbank Filtration (RBF) is a natural and cost-effective water treatment pro-
cess in which river water infiltrates through the riverbed into adjacent aquifers
before being extracted via wells. During this subsurface flow, contaminants are
attenuated through filtration, sorption, microbial degradation, and other geo-
chemical processes. However, the ability of RBF to retain persistent and mobile
chemicals (PMCs), such as pharmaceuticals and industrial pollutants, remains
limited and highly dependent on local hydrogeological conditions. This study
aims to investigate how aquifer parameters influence contaminant transport
and sorption behavior in RBF systems. A dual-scale modeling approach was
implemented in COMSOL Multiphysics to analyze how aquifer properties and
key transport parameters influence contaminant behavior in RBF systems. The
laboratory-scale model focused on breakthrough curves under controlled condi-
tions, while the field-scale model extended the analysis to realistic RBF settings,
including operational factors such as pumping rate, and well spacing. Simula-
tions were conducted using a conservative tracer and three representative com-
pounds (carbamazepine, melamine, 4-nitrophenol) with distinct physicochemi-
cal properties. Results showed that travel time and solute breakthrough are
mainly influenced by travel distance, parameters which control the water flow
velocity and retardation factors governed by sorption coefficient and soil density.
Moreover, realistic RBF simulations demostrated that increasing the distance
between river and wells, or reducing pumping rates, leads to longer residence
times and greater removal of degradable compounds. Carbamazepine exhib-
ited strong retardation due to higher sorption, while melamine and 4-nitrophenol
were affected by degradation. The findings highlight that optimizing well layout
and key transport parameters can significantly improve contaminant removal
efficiency in RBF systems, contributing to safer and more reliable groundwater
extraction.

Introduction
Access to clean and safe drinking water remains a global challenge, largely
due to pressures from industrial and agricultural activities, as well as urban ex-
pansion. These human-driven factors release dangerous and toxic substances
into the environment, which end up polluting water sources such as rivers and
aquifers. Natural groundwater is considered a valuable source of drinking wa-
ter, as it typically has low pathogen content, a refreshing taste, and is generally
clearer than surface water (Schmidt et al., 2003). However, it is not always avail-
able in sufficient quantities to meet the needs of urban areas, especially large
cities, or regions with limited water infrastructure (Schmidt et al., 2003). On the
other hand, surface water is more abundant but more vulnerable to pollution
and seasonal variation in quality (Schmidt et al., 2003; Kuehn & Mueller, 2000).
To combine the reliability of groundwater with the availability of surface water,
methods like River Bank Filtration (RBF) have been developed and widely ap-
plied. RBF is a natural water treatment process in which pumping wells are in-
stalled near a river to extract groundwater from the adjacent aquifer. The pump-
ing action creates a pressure head difference between the river and aquifer,
inducing river water to infiltrate through the riverbed and banks into the sub-
surface. As a result, the extracted water is a mixture of preexisting ground-
water from the aquifer and river water that has passed through the surrounding
aquifer (Ray & Schubert, 2006). This process enhances water quality by remov-
ing contaminants through physical filtration, dilution, adsorption, and microbial
degradation (Gao et al., 2025).
RBF has been widely used in Europe, North America, and Asia as a sustainable
and cost-effective alternative to conventional water treatment methods (Schmidt
et al., 2003; Gao et al., 2025). It may be the most practical purification option
for water treatment (Uwimpaye et al., 2025), and in many cases, it also serves
effectively as a pre-treatment process.
Persistent and mobile chemicals (PMCs), such as pharmaceuticals, pesticides,
and industrial compounds, pose increasing concerns for drinking water safety.
While RBF systems are effective in removing many suspended solids, bac-
teria, and some organic matter, they often face limitations in retaining PMCs
due to their high mobility and resistance to degradation (Schmidt et al., 2003;
Reemtsma et al., 2024; Uwimpaye et al., 2025). The behavior of each chemical
depends strongly on its physicochemical properties. Substances like atrazine,
carbamazepine, hexafluorophosphate, and 2-pyrrolidone have been reported
to pass through RBF systems with limited attenuation, potentially reaching
groundwater supplies untreated (Schmidt et al., 2003; Reemtsma et al., 2024).
Understanding how these compounds interact with the subsurface environment
is essential for evaluating the performance of RBF and improving contaminant
risk management.
One of the most important mechanisms influencing the transport and removal of
organic contaminants in the subsurface is sorption, which includes adsorption,

absorption, and ion exchange (Tinsley, 2004). Sorption delays the movement
of pollutants relative to the flow of groundwater, a phenomenon known as retar-
dation. The extent of sorption depends on several factors, including the physic-
ochemical properties of the sorbate (e.g., water solubility, polarity, lipophilic-
ity), the mineral and organic composition of the soil, the presence of functional
groups, and the hydrologic and residence time conditions (Verstraeten et al.,
2002). Hydrophobic compounds tend to be strongly adsorbed by organic mat-
ter, and their sorption potential is often correlated with the octanol-water parti-
tion coefficient (Kow) (Chiou et al., 1982). Sorption is commonly modeled using
the Freundlich isotherm, and for linear behavior (N = 1), the distribution coef-
ficient Kd is used. Kd can be estimated as the product of the organic carbon
partition coefficient (Koc) and the fraction of organic carbon in the medium (foc),
i.e., Kd = Koc · foc (Karickhoff et al., 1979). The resulting delay in contaminant
transport is described by the retardation factor R, calculated as:
R = 1 + ρb
ϕ Kd
where ϕ is the porosity of the medium and ρb is the bulk density of the solid
phase (Verstraeten et al., 2002).
Since these processes directly influence breakthrough behavior, travel time,
and overall water quality at the well, modeling sorption and transport in RBF
systems provides essential insights for their evaluation.To better understand
these mechanisms, this study employs a two-dimensional (2-D) numerical mod-
eling approach in COMSOL Multiphysics, examining two simulation models.
The first is a laboratory-scale column model that explores how aquifer prop-
erties such as porosity, hydraulic conductivity, dispersivity, and Kd affect sorp-
tion processes and BTCs. The second is a realistic RBF system model, de-
veloped using field-derived parameters, which simulates the behavior of se-
lected contaminants under natural gradient conditions. In both cases, a non-
reactive tracer is used to characterize the flow and transport behavior, while
representative organic compounds are used to evaluate their retardation be-
havior and the influence of sorption on their mobility. Three organic compounds
-carbamazepine, melamine, and 4-nitrophenol- are selected for this study due
to their frequent detection in surface water and groundwater and their contrast-
ing physicochemical properties. Carbamazepine is a pharmaceutical commonly
found on both treated and untreated water due to its persistence and resistance
to degradation (Bizi & El Bachra, 2021; Zulkifli et al., 2018). Melamine, an indus-
trial compound, is frequently found in runoff and stream waters, especially near
residential areas, largely due to its presence in plastics (Seitz & Winzenbacher,
2017), fertilisers and algaecides (L¨utjens et al., 2023). While considered persis-
tent under many conditions, melamine can also originate from the degradation
of other compounds such as cyromazine (L¨utjens et al., 2023). 4-Nitrophenol, a
metabolite and industrial intermediate, is expected to biodegrade in both water
and soil (Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease Registry (ATSDR), 2023).
Their dinstinct profiles in terms of molecular weight, polarity, log Kow, and pKa
allow for a comparative assessment of sorption and retardation behavior in

porous media during RBF.
Although the interaction between organic compounds and aquifer media in-
volves complex chemical behavior, the focus of this thesis lies primarily in the
hydrological and physical processes that govern contaminant transport. Em-
phasis is placed on understanding how groundwater flow, hydraulic properties,
and sorption influence the fate of contaminants during riverbank filtration. The
chemical behavior of selected substances is considered mainly in terms of its
impact on mobility and retardation, rather than from a mechanistic or molecular
chemistry perspective. This work aims to analyze how aquifer properties affect
sorption and transport, evaluate the influence of key hydrological parameters,
and investigate the retardation of representative contaminants in riverbank fil-
tration systems by simulating breakthrough behavior under realistic conditions
through a dual-scale numerical modeling approach.

the state of the art on RBF. Section 3 provides a detailed description of the

pares these results, highlighting limitations and suggesting directions for future
research. Finally, Section 6 concludes the study, and Section 7 (Appendix) de-
fines all symbols and notations used throughout the manuscript.

2
State of the Art
River Bank Filtration (RBF) is a natural water treatment process that involves the
abstraction of water from an aquifer that is hydraulically connected to a nearby
river. Figure 1 illustrates the schematic diagram of river bank filtration processes
(Jeyakumar et al., 2017). IIn a typical riverbank filtration (RBF) system, wells
are placed near a river to pump water from the nearby underground aquifer. As
water from the river slowly moves through the riverbank and soil, it is naturally
cleaned. Along the way, particles, harmful microbes, and some pollutants are
removed by physical filtering, chemical reactions, and biological activity in the
soil. By the time the water reaches the wells, its quality is much improved.
Figure 1: Schematic diagram of showing river bank filtration processes.
Across Europe, and particularly in Germany, RBF has been used for decades
as a reliable barrier against contaminants in drinking water production. Approxi-
mately 16% of drinking water in Germany is derived from bank filtrate or infiltrate
(Kuehn & Mueller, 2000). This method capitalizes on the underground passage
of surface water through riverbanks and soil, harnessing natural attenuation
mechanisms for contaminant removal.
Globally, RBF is widely used across different countries to tackle local water sup-
ply challenges. In India, it provides safer drinking water to cities on the Ganga
Plains facing pollution and irrigation stress, often without additional treatment
(Jeyakumar et al., 2017). China employs vertical and horizontal wells in flood-
plains and basins to sustainably reduce pathogens, metals, and organic pollu-
tants (Hu et al., 2016). In Egypt, RBF offers a low-cost pre-treatment option for
polluted surface waters along the Nile and canals, benefiting from favorable hy-
drogeology (Ghodeif et al., 2016). The cost-effectiveness of RBF and its ability
to remove suspended solids and organic contaminants also make it a valuable
technology in developing regions (Ahmed & Marhaba, 2017). These examples
9
==PAGE:11==
underscore RBF’s adaptability to varied hydrogeological conditions and water
quality issues worldwide.
However, the efficiency of RBF depends greatly on local hydrogeological condi-
tions. Factors such as aquifer thickness, soil porosity, and hydraulic conductiv-
ity play a critical role in determining the residence time of water underground,
which in turn influences the removal of various contaminants (Uwimpaye et al.,
2025). The residence time in RBF systems usually ranges from 5 to 100 days,
depending on how long the water travels underground. The exact time depends
on factors like well distance and pumping rate(Kuehn & Mueller, 2000; Eckert &
Irmscher, 2006).
Numerical modeling has become an increasingly valuable tool for assessing
the performance of RBF systems, allowing the investigation of flow paths, resi-
dence times, and contaminant attenuation under variable hydrogeological con-
ditions. Models such as MODFLOW or COMSOL are commonly used to sim-
ulate groundwater flow, coupled with solute transport modules to evaluate the
fate of contaminants(Jaramillo et al., 2019). These models help to optimize well
placement, pumping rates, and to assess the influence of aquifer properties
(e.g., porosity, dispersivity, sorption parameters) on pollutant breakthrough. Es-
pecially for persistent and mobile compounds, such simulations are critical to
predict behavior under long-term scenarios and variable river stages. Recent
approaches have incorporated layered stratigraphy, varying hydraulic conduc-
tivities, and reactive transport processes to improve model realism and predic-
tive capacity.
During underground passage, water undergoes natural removal of pathogens
and micropollutants via filtration, sorption, and biodegradation.
In aerobic
aquifers, ammonium is oxidized to nitrate by nitrifying bacteria, while microbial
activity in the infiltration zone reduces biological regrowth (?).
Inorganic substances such as iron, manganese, and heavy metals are typi-
cally retained via sorption processes during soil passage (Schmidt et al., 2003).
Under anaerobic conditions, however, iron and manganese may become mo-
bilized, requiring removal through aeration or chemical treatment (Kuehn &
Mueller, 2000). Nitrates can be naturally eliminated via denitrification under
anoxic conditions, reducing the need for costly technologies such as ion ex-
change or reverse osmosis.
Organic pollutants show varied behavior depending on their properties. While
some compounds such as lindane are effectively biodegraded, polar and persis-
tent compounds like carbamazepine often pass through the subsurface with lit-
tle to no attenuation (Kuehn & Mueller, 2000). Studies in Germany have shown
removal efficiencies between 10% and 100%, depending on the compound and
site conditions Schmidt et al. (2003).
RBF systems act as a natural buffer against sudden spikes in river contaminant
concentrations, such as those caused by industrial spills or stormwater runoff.
This buffering effect arises from variable flow paths and retention times within
10
==PAGE:12==
the porous aquifer, which delay and disperse the contaminant plume before it
reaches the production wells. As a result, peak concentrations observed in the
river are typically reduced and stretched over time in the bank filtrate(Kuehn
& Mueller, 2000). Notably, a 1986 Rhine contamination event involving 1,2-
dichloroethane demonstrated significant attenuation and delay in the bank fil-
trate. The variability in porosity and flow paths within the soil matrix contributes
further to this natural damping of peak concentrations Eckert & Irmscher (2006).
Despite its many advantages, RBF is not universally effective for all contami-
nants. Water drawn from areas with intense human activity may contain ele-
vated levels of ammonia, organic matter, and micropollutants that are not fully
removed by bank filtration alone (Kuehn & Mueller, 2000). Post-treatment pro-
cesses, such as activated carbon filtration, ozonation, or aeration, are often
necessary to meet drinking water standards.
Dynamic interactions between the river and adjacent aquifer also significantly
influence RBF performance. Fluctuating river levels alter hydraulic gradients
and infiltration velocities, which affect residence times and redox conditions
within the subsurface (Schubert, 2002; Eckert & Irmscher, 2006). River stage
variations can lead to the development of aerobic or anoxic zones, impacting
the speciation and mobility of contaminants. These dynamics were well demon-
strated at the Flehe waterworks in D¨usseldorf, where riverbed clogging due to
organic matter and suspended solids reduced yield and altered subsurface re-
dox status.
Age stratification of bank-filtered water, observed through depth-oriented sam-
pling, helps buffer variations in river water quality. This stratification leads to a
mixing of younger and older water parcels, smoothing out fluctuations in con-
taminants and temperature (Schubert, 2002).
Field studies in the Neuwied Basin and the Lower Rhine region provided valu-
able hydraulic data: aquifer thicknesses ranged from 10–20 m, hydraulic con-
ductivities varied from 0.004 to 0.02 m/s, and infiltration rates reached up to
2400 L/s/km under natural conditions (Schubert, 2002). Bank storage events
during flood waves were shown to store over 106 m3 of water temporarily in the
aquifer, significantly affecting recharge patterns and the timing of bank filtrate
discharge.
Investigations of riverbed structure revealed three distinct zones of permeabil-
ity: fully clogged, partially clogged, and highly permeable active zones, each
with different implications for infiltration dynamics and treatment performance
(Schubert, 2002). These zones are shaped by flow conditions and sediment
deposition. Suspended solids concentrations in the Rhine can exceed 400 g/m³
during flood conditions, contributing to physical clogging and reducing infiltra-
tion efficiency (Schubert, 2002).
Biological removal mechanisms are also critical. At the Flehe site, microbial ac-
tivity was concentrated within the first meter of the infiltration path. The oxida-
tion of organic carbon occurred rapidly beneath the riverbed, with little change
11
==PAGE:13==
detected further along the flow path, resembling processes found in slow sand
filtration (Eckert & Irmscher, 2006). Pathogens such as bacteria, viruses, and
protozoa are efficiently removed during RBF, with bacterial reductions of up to
3-log and near-complete parasite removal (Eckert & Irmscher, 2006). Seasonal
shifts in river water temperature and organic load influence microbial activity
and, consequently, water quality.
Flood events are associated with reduced residence time and weaker purifica-
tion, but even under extreme hydrological conditions, the combination of nat-
ural and technical treatment has proven reliable (Eckert & Irmscher, 2006).
The long-standing experience at the D¨usseldorf waterworks, spanning over 130
years, demonstrates the resilience and adaptability of RBF as a core compo-
nent of safe drinking water supply.
In conclusion, riverbank filtration represents a sustainable and efficient pre-
treatment step within drinking water production systems, particularly in regions
with favorable hydrogeological conditions. However, for persistent and mobile
contaminants, it must be integrated with advanced post-treatment technologies
to ensure long-term water quality protection.

3.1
Experimental Procedure
To evaluate the mobility of selected PMT substances under realistic RBF condi-
tions, laboratory-scale column experiments are being conducted by Fraunhofer
IME in Germany as part of a collaborative research project (STREAM project).
These tests are designed to simulate contaminant transport through saturated
aquifer material and provide reliable data for investigating the sorption behav-
ior and mobility of the selected substances under conditions representative of
natural RBF systems. This section describes the experimental setup and pro-
cedures used to obtain these data.
The experiments will be conducted by the Fraunhofer IME in Germany, using
a laboratory column setup to investigate the transport behavior of the selected
compounds through saturated aquifer material. The laboratory column setup
used for these experiments is shown in Figure 2 (Deutsches Institut f¨ur Nor-
mung e.V. (DIN), 2023).
The setup consists of a vertically oriented column
packed with natural aquifer material collected from RBF sites. The column has
an internal diameter of 14 cm and is packed up to a height of approximately
40 cm. A 5 cm quartz sand layer is placed at the bottom, above a stainless-
steel mesh, to support filtration. The aquifer material is added stepwise and
compacted to ensure uniform density. Another stainless-steel mesh is placed
on top of the packed material to maintain stability during flow. The column is
saturated with upward flow of tap water. Once saturation is achieved, a tracer
(kBr) and the test item ( mixture of the three compounds) dissolved in the elu-
ent, with concentrations of 0.2 mg/L and 1 mg/L, respectively, are loaded in
the column with a constant flow rate at 1.107 mL/min. Effluent samples are
collected every 2 hours. The electrical conductivity of the eluate is measured.
The load of the mixture is terminated when one of the compounds is detected
in the effluent. The electrical conductivity of the eluate is measured, and the
eluate is stabilised with Acetonitrile before chemical analysis.

3.2.2
Data from search about realistic RBF system
This section compiles representative values from the literature to support the
setup of a realistic Riverbank Filtration (RBF) system. The collected data in-
clude typical hydraulic properties of aquifers, operational parameters of RBF
wells, as well as characteristics of the selected compounds (melamine, 4-
nitrophenol and carbamazepine). Note that these parameters are used as input
values for the realistic RBF simulations.
Aquifer and well data
In the Neuwieder Becken in Middle Rhine region, the aquifer has the following
characteristics, which are also representative for the lower Rhine region: thick-
ness b = 10 −15 m, and hydraulic conductivity K = 4 · 10−3 −2 · 10−2 (Schubert,
2002). A well gallery of 70 vertical wells is situated at distance drw ≈50 m par-
allel to the riverbank of the Rhine river in the area of D¨usseldorf, with average
pumping rate Q ≈500 m3/d. The total length of the gallery is 1.8 km (average
spacing between wells dww = 26 m) (Schubert, 2002). Modelling simulations
for the same area have resulted the values for dispersivities, αL = 2.0 m (lon-
gitudinal) and αL = 0.2 m (transversal). In Targau site, a system of 42 vertical
wells is located with maximum pumping rate per well, Q ≈3570 m3/d, and a
distance from the riverbank, drw = 250 −300 m (Ray et al., 2002). Note that the
representative porosity used for the realistic RBF simulations is the same as in
laboratory setup simulations, ϕ = 0.25 L/L.
Data for selected compounds
In soil, melamine may be considered as non-persistent or very persistent with
half-life time thl = 46 −211 d at 20 oC. The average melamine concentrations
for the river Rhine (2017) ranged from 0.3 µg/L (Weil at the Rhein) to 3.2 µg/L
(Worm In Rhine) in 2017 (L¨utjens et al., 2023). For the realistic RBF simulations
of melamine the values co = 1.5 µg/L and thl = 120 d are considered, as they
are close to their average values.
In top soil, the half-life of 4-nitrophenol is 1 −3 d under aerobic conditions and
14 d under anaerobic, whereas in subsoils, the half-life of 4-nitrophenol is about
40 d under aerobic conditions and even slower under anaerobic conditions. In
USA, from 2000 to 2022, the maximum concentration of 4-nitrophenol has been
detected in surface water is co = 27 µg/L, and the average concentration for
the same period is co ≈1.4 µg/L (Agency for Toxic Substances and Disease
Registry (ATSDR), 2023). For the realistic RBF simulations of 4-nitrophenol the
values co = 1.5 µg/L and thl = 40 d are considered.

carbamazepine concentration was 0.084 µg/L, whereas the maximum exceeds
0.1 µg/L. The median removal of carbamazepine in RBF was 9 % (Reemtsma
et al., 2024), indicating its persistence through the aquifer system. For the pur-

3.3
COMSOL set up
3.3.1
Lab column
To simulate groundwater flow and solute transport in the laboratory soil col-
umn, COMSOL Multiphysics 6.3 is used with a two-dimensional axisymmetric
geometry. The domain is modeled as a vertical cross-section of a cylindrical
column with dimensions 0.025 m (radius) at x-direction and 0.50 m (height) at
z-direction, representing the geometry of the cylindrical soil column used in lab-
oratory experiments; see Figure 3. The vertical axis corresponds to the axis of
symmetry, and the opposite vertical edge represents the outer boundary of the
soil column.
17
==PAGE:19==
Figure 3: (a) 2D laboratory set up, (b) 3D laboratory set up.
Flow is assumed to follow Darcy’s Law, under steady-state and fully saturated
conditions.
The porous medium is considered homogeneous and isotropic
with respect to hydraulic conductivity. Under these assumptions, the governing
equation for hydraulic head h simplifies to the steady-state Laplace equation:
∇· (−K∇h) = 0
(4)
A constant head is imposed at z = 0.50 m (h = 0 m), and the head at z = 0 m
(h = hbottom) is adjusted depending on the desired flow rate. No-flow (zero-
flux) conditions are applied at x = 0.025 m. A stationary study is performed to
compute the velocity field from the hydraulic head solution, which is used as
input in the solute transport simulation.
The transport of the solute is modeled using the advection-dispersion equation
with linear equilibrium sorption:
ϕR∂c
∂t + ∇· (−ϕD∇c + ϕvc) = −ϕλc
(5)
where c is the solute concentration, ϕ is the porosity, R is the retardation fac-
tor, v is the velocity field obtained from the flow solution, D is the hydrodynamic
dispersion tensor, and λ is the first-order degradation rate constant. The disper-
sion tensor D is defined as diagonal, with components Drr and Dzz represent-
ing radial and vertical dispersion, respectively, including the effect of molecular
diffusion and mechanical dispersion.
The initial concentration in the domain is set to zero. At z = 0 m (inlet), the so-
lute concentration is set equal to cin, which is 10 µg/L in the baseline case. For
pulse injections, a time-dependent solute concentration is prescribed depend-
ing on the injection scenario. A no-flux condition is assigned at x = 0.025 m. At
z = 0.50 m (outlet), an advective flux boundary condition is applied assuming
no interaction with the boundary, namely:
18
==PAGE:20==
∇c = 0
(6)
The model is solved using a time-dependent study with a constant time step
(e.g., 1 min). BTCs are extracted at the axisymmetric outlet point (x = 0 m,
z = 0.50 m), for various simulation scenarios.
Mesh refinement of laboratory set up model
The finite element method is used to solve both the flow and transport equa-
tions. An extra fine triangular mesh is applied, with a geometry scaling ratio of
2:1 (r:z) to capture the cylindrical geometry accurately.

3.3.2
Field-Scale RBF Model (Conceptual Description)
A second numerical model is developed in COMSOL Multiphysics to simulate a
realistic riverbank filtration (RBF) system at field scale. Figure 4 represents the
graph of the conceptual model. The domain is defined as a two-dimensional
rectangular area in plan view, representing a horizontal cross-section of the
aquifer perpendicular to the river.
19
==PAGE:21==
Figure 4: Conceptual model graph.
The y-axis corresponds to the riverbank, assumed to maintain a constant hy-
draulic head. A vertical pumping well is placed at x = drw = +50 m and y = 0 m.
The boundaries at y = dww/2 = +50 m and y = −dww/2 = −50 m are assigned
no-flow (Neumann) conditions, reflecting the assumption that the model repre-
sents a repeating segment of an infinite series of identical, evenly spaced wells
along the riverbank. This symmetry simplifies the system and allows lateral iso-
lation of the model domain. The boundary at x = L = +500 m is assigned a
constant head to represent far-field aquifer conditions.
Flow is modeled under fully saturated, steady-state conditions according to
Darcy’s law. Solute transport is governed by advection, dispersion, sorption,
and first-order degradation, similarly to the laboratory model. The governing
transport equation is:
ϕR∂c
∂t + ∇· (−ϕD∇c + ϕvc) = −ϕλc
(7)
Unlike the laboratory-scale model, where dispersion is assumed to be isotropic
and simplified, the field-scale model implements the full dispersion tensor. This
includes both longitudinal and transverse dispersivity components, allowing
more accurate representation of solute spreading due to spatial variability in
flow paths, especially near the well capture zone.
The model is solved using a time-dependent study, with a time step of 1 d during
the first year and 7 d thereafter. BTCs are extracted at two locations: (i) the point
closest to the river at the pumping well interface (x = 49.25 m, y = 0 m), and (ii)
the perimeter of the well itself (representing the water entering the well).
Mesh refinement of realistic RBF model
The finite element method is used to solve both the flow and transport equa-
tions. A fine triangular mesh is applied throughout the domain, maintaining a
geometry scaling ratio of 1:1 (x:y). To improve accuracy in regions with ex-
pected sharp gradients, an extremely fine mesh is applied along the domain
boundaries at x = 0 m and x = 500 m, as well as in the vicinity of the pumping
well (see Figure 5).

3.3.3
Outputs of models
The BTCs are used to evaluate the timing and magnitude of contaminant arrival
under different scenarios, in comparison with the water travel time and the initial
solute concentration at the source.
The water travel time, ts, is estimated based on the seepage velocity vs through
the porous medium and is defined as:
ts = ℓ
vs
(8)
where ℓis the flow path length through the porous medium, and the seepage
velocity is given by:
vs = Q
A ϕ
(9)
Here, Q is the volumetric flow rate, A is the cross-sectional area perpendicular
to flow, and ϕ is the porosity of the medium.
To characterize the BT behavior, the following indicators are defined and ex-
tracted from the BTCs at each observation point:
• tBT: the breakthrough time, defined as the time at which the solute con-
centration first exceeds the detection limit at the outlet or monitoring loca-
tion.
• cpeak: the peak concentration observed in the BTC. The precision of this
value depends on the assumed detection threshold.
• t0.5: the time at which the solute concentration reaches half of the maxi-
mum value (cpeak/2) in the BTC.
• tmax: the time at which the solute concentration reaches practically the
maximum solute concentration, cpeak. It also depends on the detection
limit.
• ttot,BTC: the total observed duration of the BTC, defined as the time when
solute concentration drops below the detection limit following the initial
pulse. This metric is only analyzed for the lab-scale case.
3.4
Sensitivity Analysis
In this section, a sensitivity analysis is conducted to evaluate the influence of
selected parameters on the behavior of both the laboratory-scale and the realis-
tic RBF models. The aim is to identify which parameters most significantly affect
the model outputs related to solute transport. The analysis focuses on t0.5 and
cpeak, while tBT, tmax, and ttot,BTC are excluded due to their strong dependence
on the detection limit, which may lead to inconsistencies in interpretation.

using the ratio between the relative change in a model output and the corre-
sponding relative change in the input parameter. The formula used is:
Sx =

where xref is the reference value of the parameter, xmod is the modified value
of the parameter, yref is the model output corresponding to the reference case,
and ymod is the model output corresponding to the modified input.
For each parameter, two modified values are tested relative to the reference
case, corresponding to a +30% and a −30% change for each reference pa-
rameter. This allows the calculation of two sensitivity indexes per parameter,
capturing the effect of both an increase and a decrease of the parameter.

5
Discussion
5.1
Interpretetion
This section provides an interpretation of the simulation results, focusing on
how changes in key parameters influence the solute transport behavior and the
shape of the BTCs.
5.1.1
Discussion of lab scale model parameters
Figure 6(a) shows how hydraulic conductivity K affects solute transport. As
K increases, solute transport accelerates, leading to earlier solute arrival tBT
at the outlet, steeper BTC, and shorter time tmax. As K increases from 10−4 to
10−2 m/s, the water travel time ts decreases from 29.76 h to 0.30 h, inversely pro-
portional to K, as expected from Darcy’s law under constant hydraulic gradient
and cross-section area of column. Similarly, t0.5 scale proportionally with ts, as
t0.5 drops from 24.53 h to 0.25 h maintaining nearly constant ratio: ts/t0.5 ≈1.20.
However, in the case of tBT, the ratio ts/tBT varies between 2.86 and 3.75 over
the same range of K values. The outlet concentration at ts consistently reaches
approximately 0.68 cmax across all cases, indicating that ts is a good proxy for the
cpeak under non-sorbing conditions.
Figure 6(b) presents the BTCs corresponding to different porosity values ϕ. As
ϕ increases from 0.2 to 0.4, solute transport slows down, and the BTCs shift
to the right. This delay is due to the increased void volume, which reduces
the seepage velocity for a given hydraulic gradient and hydraulic conductivity.
Specifically, the water travel time, ts, increases from 1.98 h to 3.97 h, while t0.5
increases from 1.65 h to 3.28 h, and tBT increases from 0.65 h to 1.3 h. So, all BT
times scale linearly with ϕ, maintaining constant the ratios: ts/t0.5 ≈1.20 and
ts/tBT ≈3.05. Moreover, the ts also represents the time when the concentration
at outlet reaches at 0.68 cpeak, as in the case of hydraulic conductivity. These
observations show that there is a proportional relationship between porosity
and the time required for the outlet concentration to reach at specific levels.
Figure 6(c) shows the influence of the sorption coefficient (Kd) on solute trans-
port. As Kd increases from 0 to 2.0 L/kg, the BTC shift to the right and become
more elongated, due to increased retention.
The corresponding retardation
factor R rises from 1 to 18.67, causing proportional delays in BT times: tBT
increases from 0.97 h to 12.98 h and t0.5 from 2.47 h to 32.78 h. These times
scale linearly with R. The water travel time ts remains the same (2.97 h) for all
cases, as it depends more on the hydraulic parameters than on sorption. While
c(ts) ≈0.68 cmax for Kd = 0, it drops near zero for higher Kd, showing that ts is
no longer representative of cpeak under significant sorption.
Figure 6(d) illustrates the impact of longitudinal dispersivity (αL) on the shape
47
==PAGE:49==
of the BTCs for a soil column of fixed dimensions and height. As αL decreases
from 0.05 to 0.005 m, the Peclet number, defined as Pe = ∆z/αL and expresses
the relative importance of advection versus dispersion, increases from 10 to
100, indicating a shift toward advection-dominated transport. In such cases, the
BTCs become steeper, and the solute arrives more sharply at the outlet. This is
reflected in the decreasing ratios: t0.5/tBT drops from 2.55 to 1.38, tmax/t0.5 from
1.98 to 1.26, and ts/t0.5 from 1.20 to 1.01. Simultaneously, the outlet concentra-
tion at ts decreases from 67.5 % to 54.7 % of cpeak, showing that as dispersion
weakens, ts becomes a better proxy for t0.5 and peak arrival. Overall, in cases
where Pe ≫1 (e.g., Pe > 100), advection dominates and the solute moves
rapidly with minimal spreading. However, for Pe ≈1–10, as in the baseline case
(Pe = 10), dispersion becomes significant, influencing both the timing and the
shape of the BTC.
Figures 6(e)-(f) show that neither the molecular diffusion coefficient (Dm) nor the
degradation half-life time (thl) have a noticeable effect on the BTCs at laboratory
scale. Despite varying Dm across one order of magnitude (from 2.5 · 10−10 to
2.0 · 10−9 m2/s), and thl from 60 to 180 d, the resulting BTCs remain virtually
identical. This suggests that, under the given conditions, these parameters do
not significantly influence solute transport and can be considered negligible in
lab-scale simulations.
Figure 7(a) illustrates the impact of hydraulic head difference ∆h (and corre-
sponding flow rate Q) on BTCs for a 4-hour constant pulse injection. As ∆h
increases, the flow rate rises, leading to sharper, earlier, and shorter solute
BTCs. For instance, as ∆h increases from 10−4 m to 10−3 m , the normalized
peak concentration, cpeak/cin, increases tenfold from 0.024 to 0.240, while the
time to peak tmax drops from 6 to 0.68 d. This demonstrates that higher flow
increases solute delivery speed and concentration at the outlet.
Figure 7(b) shows the breakthrough curves (BTCs) resulting from different input
pulse durations tp. As tp increases, the peak concentration increases and the
delay tmax−tp becomes shorter, from 1.6 h at tp = 1 h to 0.9 h at tp = 4 h. For the
shorter pulses (tp < ts = 2.97 h), a significant portion of the solute mass contin-
ues to arrive after the end of the solute injection, increasing the concentration
by approximately 0.39 cin. For the longer pulses,tp = 3 h and tp = 4 h, this
concentration increase is much smaller, 0.18 cin and 0.07 cin, respectively. This
indicates that the outlet response becomes more immediate for higher pulse
durations than travel time.
In Figure 7(d), the relationship between flow rate Q and the total duration of
the BTC, ttot,BTC is illustrated for different sorption scenarios and hydraulic
conductivities, under a 4-hour constant pulse injection of 10 µg/L.
Each
dataset corresponds to a specific combination of hydraulic conductivity K and
sorption coefficient Kd, with all other parameters remain consistent with the
baseline case.
Among the simulated scenarios are non-sorbing conditions
(Kd = 0.0 L/kg, R = 1.0) as well as sorbing cases representing the three
chemicals: melamine (Kd = 0.13 L/kg), 4-nitrophenol (Kd = 0.41 L/kg), and
carbamazepine (Kd = 1.0 L/kg and R = 7.2). The corresponding retardation
48
==PAGE:50==
factors are R = 1.8 (melamine), R = 3.5 (4-nitrophenol), and R = 7.2 (car-
bamazepine).
For the non-sorbing cases, curves corresponding to different
hydraulic conductivities collapse onto a single trend, confirming that for a fixed
Q, the BTC duration is independent of K, consistent with Darcy’s law, since the
hydraulic gradient adjusts to maintain the same velocity. In contrast, as the Kd
increases, the ttot,BTC become singificantly longer for the same flow rate. The
fitted curves in Figure 7(d) show that this extension in ttot,BTC approximately
scales with the retardation factor R. For example, at a given flow rate of approx-
imately 10 mL/h, the total duration of BTC increases from about 2 d for R = 1.0
to 7 d for R = 3.5, showing a linear relationship ttot,BTC(R) ≈R · ttot,BTC(R = 1).
This highlights the important role of sorption in extending solute residence time.
Figure 7(c) complements the analysis by showing the tBT across different flow
rates and sorption scenarios. As in the case of ttot,BTC, the tBT increases with the
sorption coefficient Kd, due to retardation of the solute front. For a given flow
rate, the simulations reveal a clear scaling behavior: tBT(R) ≈R · tBT(R = 1).
This confirms that sorption delays the initial arrival of the solute, not just its total
residence time.
In Table 14, cases 1-3 illustrate the influence of varying flow rates on solute
transport in non-sorbing conditions.
The baseline parameters were used in
these simulations, while the flow rate was adjusted such that the solute pulse
terminated when the concentration at the outlet reached at the detection limit.
Under these non-sorbing conditions, it is observed that the peak concentration
at the outlet remained constant across all cases, while the BT times changes
inversely with the flow rate. Case 4 uses the same flow rate with case 2 (Q =
0.06 L/h), but it is under sorbing conditions (Kd = 0.2 L/kg), and the pulse
duration adjusted as in the precious cases. It is observed that only the BT times
are affected, while the concentration peak remains the same. Cases 5-7 are
also simulated with flow Q = 0.06 L/h, and with solute concentration of pulse

Discussion of scenarios varying porosity, sorption coefficient and pump-
ing rate
Sensitivity analysis of both models indicates that porosity does not have a dom-
inant influence on the BTC results. A 30 % decrease in porosity, from 0.30 to
0.21, results in sensitivity indices for the time, t0.5, at which the solute concen-

==PAGE:52==
tration reaches half of its peak value (cpeak/2), of −0.16 for the laboratory model,
−0.20 for the nearest well point (realistic RBF model), and −0.22 for the pump-
ing water (realistic RBF model), respectively (see Tables 16, 18, and 20). This
reduction in porosity leads to a significant increase, approximately 50 %, in the
retardation factor R, from 5.3 to 8.0.
In comparison, increasing the sorption coefficient Kd by 30 %, from 1.0 to 1.3,
yields sensitivity indices of approximately 0.80 across all cases, and increases R
by about 30 %, reaching a value of 6.6. These results suggest that although Kd
has a higher sensitivity index than porosity ϕ, its overall impact on R is smaller.
This is likely because a decrease in porosity results in higher seepage velocity,
which in turn has a stronger effect on the transports process. To further explore
the interplay between porosity, sorption, and seepage velocity, additional sce-
narios were simulated (see Table 21). In one scenario, the sorption coefficient
Kd is increased to 1.6 in order to match the higher retardation factor, R = 8.0,
resulting from a 30 %, reduction in porosity. Simultaneously, the pumping rate
is increased by 30 % to simulate the associated increase in seepage velocity
caused by lower porosity. In a second scenario, only the pumping rate is in-
creased by 30 %, without changing Kd, to isolate the effect of velocity. The
resulting BTCs at the well point nearest to the river and for the pumping water,
for each scenario in Table 21, are shown in Figure 12.
As illustrated in Figure 12, the BTCs from Scenario 1 (see purple line - in-

and at the pumping well (Figure 12(b). This supports the assumption that the
increased seepage velocity due to reduced porosity can be effectively achieved

t0.25 increases from 90 d to 113 d (≈25 %).
It is also evident that the scenarios that affect the seepage velocity, by reducing
porosity or increasing pumping rate, lead to similar peak concentrations. In
contrast, increasing only the sorption coefficient (Kd) delays solute arrival but
does not reduce the peak concentration. Thus, sorption affects timing, while
velocity affects both timing and spreading.

5.2
Limitations
Despite the valuable insights provided by the simulations, the study has some
limitations related to simplifying assumptions and the lack of experimental cali-
bration.
Due to delays in acquiring experimental data, the models were not calibrated or
validated against observations, and the resulting simulations should be consid-
ered as theoretical. Both the laboratory-scale and realistic RBF models were
developed under simplified assumptions, treating the porous medium as homo-
geneous and isotropic, without accounting for stratification or heterogeneity.
In the realistic RBF simulations, temporal variations such as seasonal fluc-
tuations in river surface, rainfall events that recharges the aquifer, and spa-
tial changes in groundwater table elevation were not considered. Instead, the
groundwater level at a certain distance from the river was assumed equal to the
river level, and flow conditions were kept steady-state with a constant pump-
ing rate throughout. These assumptions, while simplifying the modeling pro-
cess, may overlook important dynamics that affect hydraulic gradients and so-
lute travel times.
Chemical properties of the compounds were assumed uniform across the do-
main and constant over time. Potential variations in sorption behavior due to
environmental conditions such as pH, temperature, or aerobic/anoxic condi-
tions were not included. All compounds were also assumed to enter the system
at the same concentration from the river, regardless of potential differences in
their actual presence in surface waters.
The spatial layout of wells was treated uniformly, and individual well charac-
teristics such as varying pumping rates or distances from the river were not ex-
plored. A simplified approach using widely spaced wells and no-flow boundaries
at the domain edges was used to approximate isolated well behavior. More re-
alistic configurations, including interacting wells or clustered pumping systems,
were beyond the scope of this study.
53
==PAGE:55==
Additionally, small-scale processes near the river–aquifer interface, such as bi-
ological activity, sediment clogging, or changes in infiltration capacity, were not
considered in the models, although such processes can significantly affect local
flow and contaminant attenuation. Parameters such as porosity and dispersiv-
ity were assumed spatially constant across the entire domain in both modeling
approaches.
5.3
Outlook and further research
To improve the reliability and applicability of the presented findings, future work
should focus on validating the simulation results with both experimental or field
observations. Laboratory experiments and site-specific measurements would
help calibrate model parameters and assess the accuracy of theoretical pre-
dictions. Enhancing the model framework with spatial heterogeneity, seasonal
fluctuations, and variable boundary conditions into the RBF model would allow
for more realistic representation of natural RBF systems. Moreover, integrating
reactive transport processes that account for pH affections, aerobic and redox
conditions, and biodegradation or sorption behavior of compounds under differ-
ent conditions would enhance the accuracy of simulations for diverse compound
types. Future studies could also investigate the effects of spatially distributed
well networks, variable pumping schemes, and potential clogging processes at
the river–aquifer interface. Such improvements would support the development
of more accurate and applicable tools for groundwater protection and RBF sys-
tem design, under changing hydrogeological and environmental conditions.

6
Conclusions
This study investigated the influence of sorption and transport processes on
the retardation of PMCs in RBF systems, using a dual-scale modeling ap-
proach. Two numerical models were developed in COMSOL Multiphysics: a
laboratory-scale column model and a realistic RBF system model. These mod-
els simulated the behavior of a tracer and selected contaminants (melamine,
4-nitrophenol, carbamazepine) by varying the properties of solute (i.e. concen-
tration, sorption, etc.). The primary objective was to quantify how key hydraulic
and transport parameters, such as porosity, sorption coefficient, flow rate, and
travel distance, affect BTCs, with emphasis on the half-peak arrival time, t0.5.
In the laboratory model, simulation results and sensitivity analysis demostrated
that the most influential parameters on BT behavior was the travel height of the
lab column ∆z, the hydraulic conductivity K , and the hydraulic head difference
∆h, as the former affects the travel distance and the other two parameters con-
trol the flow velocity (Darcy’s Law). Also, the sorption coefficient Kd, and soil
density ρs, which control the retardation factor R, had an important effect on the
BT times. The peak concentrations cpeak only affected by the initial concentra-
tion of solute, cin.
In realistic RBF model, sensitivity analysis confirmed that similar parameters
control the travel times of the solute and the peak concentrations. The most
influential parameters were the distance river-well, drw, which affect the travel
distance of the solute through the aquifer, and the well pumping rate Q, which
controls the flow velocity. The thickness of aquifer b, sorption coefficient Kd,
and soil density ρs had important impact on the solute residence time. The
parameter well-spacing, dww, exhibited a noticeable influence on the BT times,
and the peak concentrations of solutes were affected only by their concentration
in the river, co.
In both models, the porosity, ϕ, had limited influence on BTCs. As it was inves-
tigated, this was because changes in porosity affect the water flow velocity and
retardation factor in a way, that produces opposing effects on solute transport,
balancing each other impact on BTC.
The realistic RBF simulations explored the effect of well positioning and the
well pumping rate for the selected chemicals on BTCs in RBF systems. The
simulations showed that increasing the river-well distance or decreasing the
pumping rate led to longer residence times, resulting higher removal of all com-
pounds, especially degradable compounds like melamine and 4-nitrophenol.
Conversely, placing wells too close to the river and too close to each other,
combined with high pumping rates, proved to be an inefficient strategy, as it re-
sulted in short travel times and limited attenuation. It was found that increasing
the distance between the wells and the pumping rate results better removal of
degradable chemicals.
Overall, the study highlights that optimizing well layout and key transport param-

eters can significantly improve contaminant removal efficiency in RBF systems,
contributing to safer and more reliable groundwater extraction.
