ABSTRACT 
This study means to it investigate the growth characteristics of a new strain, Cutaneotrichosporon 
oleaginosus LGF 11, which has been isolated from a Nigerian palm wine production to see if it has potential 
as an industrial fermentation platform. 
Investigating the growth morphology and stationary morphology showed circular and rod shaped 
organisms, in plates and shake flasks alike. The shake flask and 1 litre fermentation showed only 
homologous growth, no pellet, filament or floc were found. BioLector® experiments, which give a high 
throughput, were used to investigate the inhibitory effect of furfural, a potentially inhibiting by-product. At 
0,5 g/L the lag phase was elongated compared to the control experiment, but the growth rate was not 
affected. The same system was used to explore the options for growth in high pH in YNB, YPD and CBS 
minimal media. The YNB medium with all amino acids had growth in the highest concentration at pH 10.  
Experiments were conducted to explore the use of several different carbon sources, and subsequently to 
investigate if there is a significant difference in the growth rates. Of the tested carbon sources when using 
off-gas analysis for growth rates, glucose, lactose, galactose, sucrose, xylose, and glycerol had no significant 
difference. Sucrose had the fastest growth rate of 0,2551 h-1 ± 0,0135, with arabinose being the only tested 
carbon source that had a slower growth rate.  
Investigating cheaper alternative complex media, hydrolysed spent beer grain from beer production and 
hydrolysed fungal biomass from Talaromycers atroroseus gave growth rates of 0,3450 h-1  ± 0,0213 and 
0,3220 h-1  ± 0,0119 respectively.  
RESUME  
Dette studie vil undersøge vækst karakteristika for den nye stamme Cutaneotrichosporon oleaginosus LGF 
11, som er isoleret fra nigeriansk palmevin produktion, og undersøge, om den har relevans som industriel 
fermenterings platform.  
Undersøgelser om vækst morfologi og stationær morfologi, viser cirkulær og stav formet vækst på både 
plader og i shake flasker. 1 liter fermenteringer og shake flakser viste kun homologis vækst, og ingen pellet, 
filament eller biofilm. BioLector eksperimenter, der gav et bredt undersøgelsesfelt, blev brugt til at teste 
furfural inhibering, som er et potentielt biprodukt af hydrolysering. Ved 0.5 g/L er der en længere lag fase, 
men vækstraten er ikke påvirket af denne koncentration ved sammenligning med kontrol eksperimentet.

undersøgt. En serie af eksperimenter, som skulle undersøge forskellige kulstofkilder og samtidig undersøge,

2 INTRODUCTION  
In the biotechnology industry, with tools such as CRISPR and the continuous reduction of the price of full 
genome sequencing, the well-known and well-studied organisms that historically have been used for a wide 
range of products might give way for new and more specialised organisms. New organisms which naturally 
produce a desired product that they are evolutionary prone for could be used in an industrial setting, 
instead of over-engineering an already known organism.  
The aim of this project is to investigate a potential new cell factory, Cryptococcus oleaginosus LGF 11. Key 
properties will be investigated for how different nutrients are utilised, the organism’s ability to grow at 
various pH values and temperatures. Along with this, preparations for a lipid analysis are made to see if the 
C. oleaginosus LGF 11 isolate produces high amounts lipids. This approach is a reasonable first line of 
investigation when exploring industrial relevance and could be applied for new isolates as well to find other 
natural producers. 
To have an industrially viable organism, often large amount of genetic work must to be done. This is often a 
very costly endeavour. Therefore, before investing large amounts of resources, the organism should be 
evaluated thoroughly. Ideally, the organism should have the potential to utilise cheap substrates and grow 
in a liquid fermentor, while turning a profit. If the requirements are not met, it would be more cost efficient 
to search for new potential isolates. The experiments will focus on the growth rate (µ) and the utilisation of 
various carbon sources, along with the natural production of lipids.  
2.1 AIM  
As an overall aim the research project sets out to investigate whether there is a potential for C. oleaginosus 
LGF 11 to be an industrial fermentation platform.  
The morphology of the organism will be done on two plates and a series of shakes flasks experiments to 
ensure that it remains similar over time. One set of the plates and one set of the shake flasks will be 
incubated for an extensive amount of time to investigate whether there is a change of morphology from 
nutrition depravation and extended life span.  
Exploratory experiments will be conducted in 1500 µL fermentations in a BioLector® to test the inhibitory 
compound furfural, which is a by-product of the hydrolysation of biomass. 
Along with this, also as 1500 µL micro fermentations, a series of exploratory experiments with high pH will 
be carried out to investigate the upper pH limit at which the organism can live, and how the growth rate is 
affected. A further benefit of testing pH tolerance is that hydrolysation uses either acid that needed to be 
neutralised, or alkaline to release more macronutrients.  
A secondary supportive objective was to prepare for liquid profiling. Exploratory experiments will be done 
in 1500 µL, along with a nitrogen limiting micro fermentation. If the furfural is heavily limiting the formation 
of lipids or the high pH alters the lipid composition, the rate of utilisation would be affected. Nitrogen 
limitation could affect the production of lipids, therefore this aspect of the organism is relevant to explore 
(Kourist et al. 2015).  
To giver further evidence for the potential of C. oleaginosus LGF 11 as a platform for fermentation, a series 
of 1 litre fermentations with different carbon sources will be carried out. The first step is to investigate

A set of simple fermentations utilising two combinations of pure carbon sources will be carried out to

3 BACKGROUND  
The isolate investigated in this thesis was obtained from Nigeria from palm wine production, and selected 
for fast growth. The very first isolate of C. oleaginosus originates from a dairy farm in the state of Iowa, 
United States, with the original name Candida curvata (ATCC 20509). 
When assessing a new isolate of a known organism, its properties are examined to investigate if it has any 
potential as an industrial producer of for example primary metabolites, secondary metabolites, acids, 
antibiotics, proteins, or pharmaceuticals. For C. oleaginosus LGF 11 high amount of lipids would be a 
reasonable choice of research, since it is a natural producer of these secondary metabolites.  
First and foremost, it should be investigated whether a useable product is produced naturally, just like in 
the case of penicillin, produced by a Penicillium fungus. There are also opportunities in heterologous 
pathways to produce an otherwise “unnatural” product. To do this, already well-defined pathways should 
be examined, to see if it would give a high yield in a stable platform, without altering the organism too 
extensively.  
3.1 INTRODUCTION TO CUTANEOTRICHOSPORON OLEAGINOSUS  
This organism has been re-classified several times, therefore it is also known as Trichosporon oleaginosus, 
Cryptococcus curvatus, Apiotrichum curvatum or Candida curvata (Bracharz et al. 2017). For the remainder 
of the thesis it will be referred to as “C. oleaginosus”, unless it is quoted directly from another article. 
C. oleaginosus is an unconventional yeast, but it is gaining popularity in the production of lipids for uses 
such as biodiesel. It also has the potential to produce more expensive and specialised oils. Yeast belong to 
the kingdom Fungi and are unicellular eukaryote organisms. Unlike the more traditionally used 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae that belongs to the phylum of Ascomycota, C. oleaginosus belongs to 
Basidiomycota, along with other fungi like Agaricus bisporus (commonly referred to as champignon 
mushroom), and other edible mushrooms. C. oleaginosus is in the family Trichosporonaceae and genus 
Cutaneotrichosporon, and it does not show sexual reproduction or basidiocarps (Bracharz et al. 2017).  
The genome of the particular isolate that will be the focus of the experimental setup has not yet been 
sequenced, but in the “1000 Fungal Genomes Project” C. oleaginosus IBC0246 was sequenced (Kourist et al. 
2015). This strain can achieve an up to 70% w/w lipid concentration per dry weight, the ability to store 
lipids is present, and it is also able to utilise several different carbon sources. Since these organisms are 
from the same species, some underlying similarities are assumed. Until a full genome sequence and 
annotation are made, this will be the accepted data source.  
C. oleaginosus IBC0246 has a total of 19,8 mbp containing about 8,322 identified coding genes, with a gas 
chromatography (GC) content of 60,75%  as described by (Kourist et al. 2015). Under normal, non-limited 
growth conditions the cell walls are mostly made up of three neutral carbohydrates, glucose, mannose and 
hexose (63% w/w), but also glucosamine (9% w/w), glucuronic acid (13% w/w) and 11 % w/w proteins. 
There have been no alterations on the cell wall composition reported when the organism experiences 
nitrogen limitations, although there are endoglucanases which are upregulated (Bracharz et al. 2017).

3.3 LIPIDS  
3.3.1 
Biosynthesis of lipids 
There are naturally many of the same enzymes and pathway steps used to make storage lipids, as to make 
cell membrane (Hapala et al. 2013). There may be other pathways to produce lipids, but both C. gattii and 
Trichoderma reesei have genes for the same pathway (both annotated and part of the KEGG database) and 
they are closely related to C. oleaginosus LGF 11. 
The first step in this pathway is going through the TCA cycle. As a side-product, citrate is made, and first 
intermediate to get to acetyl-CoA. Since the lipids are formed in the cytoplasm of the organism, this can 
cause a limiting step, since acetyl-CoA is made inside the mitochondria and there are no known 
transporters to the cytoplasm (Bracharz et al. 2017). To circumvent this, it is transported as citrate out of 
the mitochondria, and the enzyme citrate lyase cleaves it with the use of ATP into acetyl-CoA and malate 
(Ratledge 2002). The enzyme that makes the reaction is in the cytoplasm, ensuring the building blocks in a 
series of reactions starts that will start with the acetyl-CoA. To make this reaction continue, the pool of 
oxaloacetate inside the microconidia needs to be maintained. After the citrate has been transported out of 
the mitochondria, and with the citrate lyase ensuring that there is acetyl-CoA in the cytoplasm, there are 
two enzymatic steps; first, malate dehydrogenase (MDH) turning the oxaloacetate by-product into malate.

3.3.2

be limited. They are some of the most needed elements that can be easily controlled, and there are 
systems that govern regulation in limiting conditions. Oxygen limitation would be an option, but it is more 
complicated as there is a risk that cells would still access some.  
When using complex media there will be several trace elements. Trace elements are not viable options as 
limiting factors, since only minute amounts are needed, and are therefore difficult to control. 
Consequently, the two ideal options would either be nitrogen or phosphorus.

3.4 BIOREFINERY AND ALTERNATIVE NUTRITION SOURCES 
In the modern society, there are several challenges, such as the lack of food, or pollution and waste. Huge 
efforts and investments are made to find a greener way of during business. A significant step would be 
utilising every part of an organism during production, including by-products or exploring ways to repurpose 
waste products to gain value.  
The traditional feed used is a pure or relatively pure substrate, which ensures that the fermentations are 
comparable. Complex media from various sources might give less predictable outcomes, so a stable 
fermentation platform would mitigate some of the variance.  
There is little room for improvement with genetic alteration to how efficiently the carbon source is utilised 
for the production of lipids. However, one area that can be greatly improved is the feed for the organism. 
The optimal setting would be with pure substrates of high quality, but it is quite costly, especially when the 
end product is relatively low price with a small financial margin. To decrease the cost of utilising pure 
sugars, alternatives can be used, such as old biomass from fermentations, leftover grains from beer making, 
grain straws, or even sewage sludge.  
First generation biofuels are made from sugars and oils acquired with a standard method, and most of this 
can be used as human nutrients. The concept of second-generation biofuel is to use renewable materials 
after undergoing a treatment for fermentations or as a heat source.  
Figure 7 shows a set of graphs indicating the amount of by-products produced from agriculture in Denmark, 
from 2006 to 2017. The graph on the left shows the residual production of Danish agriculture, and on the 
right is an overview of how the residues are used. Approximately 5-6 million tons of residual material fresh 
weight was available every year, with 32-50% being unused(Bastidas-Oyanedel & Schmidt 2019). This 
means a minimum of 1,5 million tons of unused material annually. The composition of materials includes 
various organisms, including unused parts of plants, animals, and microorganisms. To utilise all these 
different energy and nutrient sources for a microbial fermentation, a robust platform would be optimal, 
since there is less need to process and purify the food stock, thereby saving costs.

3.5 MEDIA AND PATHWAYS  
In a modern setting the term fermentation is becoming a broad concept, stretching from alcohol making 
with yeasts, all the way to using human cell cultures to create highly specialised medicine. Overall, it is the 
transformation of a given substrate into a product using living organisms  
Overall there are two major groups of fermentation types, aerobic and anaerobic. There are three groups 
of organisms that would fall into these categories. One type of organism needs oxygen to live, one does not 
need it and in some cases oxygen is toxic to it, and some organisms can utilise oxygen as a reducing agent 
and also live without it (Stanbury et al. 2016). 
3.5.1 
Nutrient requirements  
There are two types media: defined and complex. Defined media is composed of pure elements in known 
amounts, that combined will contain everything needed for the organism to survive. The advantage of this 
media is that the composition is completely known. This medium is often clear with little foaming and 
fewer impurities, making the downstream processes easier. This kind of medium is often more expensive 
since it is made of pure elements and is used for research and high value products. It is ideal for research, 
because it contributes to higher degrees of reproducibility and control. The FDA and EFSA often a demand 
very specific and controlled environments and parameters for the production of medicine. According to the 
Biotechnology Inspection Guide (11/91) from the FDA, “Raw materials should not contain any undesirable 
and toxic components that may be carried through the cell culture, fermentation and the purification 
process to the finished product”(Fish 2014). Since there could be some part of complex media that is 
undesired, this is one example of the complications that can arise when utilising it for medicine.  
Alternatively, complex or “natural” media is used. This is not chemically defined, and is often a cheaper 
product such molasses, high fructose corn starch, or yeast extract (Stanbury et al. 2016). One of the 
advantages of using this kind of media is that there are more complex elements in it, for example whole 
amino acids, or even oils. If the organism can transport it into the cell, it does not have to stylise it de novo. 
This means a faster growth rate and less energy “wasted”. Since there are only a few complex media that

Water 
All life as we know it is depending on water in some shape or form, with nearly 80% of the mass of viable 
organisms being water (Topiwala & Khosrovi 1978). This is also the medium in which salts and sugars are 
dissolved. There is a cost associated with the water used for the fermentation, recycling of the water from 
former fermentations is common. Further benefits and cost reductions can be achieved, if there are 
leftover nutrients not absorbed by the former fermentation (Topiwala & Khosrovi 1978). 
Energy 
There are two methods for the organism to acquire energy. The first and most common is the oxidisation of 
elements from the medium, and the second is from light. The most common source of energy will therefore 
be the carbon source, but also lipids and proteins/amino acids from a complex medium. It is not only 
restricted to salts and sugars in the liquid, but also gasses that are being passed through the liquid can be 
utilised. It is the alteration of these molecules into either products or fully oxidised CO2, that gives the cells 
energy. The glycolysis is the backbone of the energy conversion inside the cell, and it is from this pathway 
most of the intermediates that is being used for production of products originate from. The metabolic 
pathway will be further described later in Section 3.5.3, “Metabolic pathways”. 
Carbon 
Organic chemistry is the chemistry of carbon, and this atom makes up around half of the dry weight of the 
organism, as shown on Table 1. The rate at which carbon is metabolised can affect the production, where 
high concentrations of readily available sugars give a low production of secondary metabolites. This can be 
circumvented by utilising different carbon sources, which are not metabolised as rapidly, giving a limitation 
to the organism. Alternatively, a continuously or a semi continuously feeding profile can be used to 
introduce a limiting factor, and the secondary product can be made with rapidly metabolised feed. 
If there is a mixture of two or more carbon sources, the organism will often utilise the carbon source which 
gives the highest growth rate first. This carbon exclusivity also affects other pathways inside the cells, by up 
or down regulating systems ranging from membrane transporters for sugars, to expression of genes.  
This could be used for genetic two-step fermentation, where the first stage is set up to produce biomass 
and the second for maximising production (Stanbury et al. 2016). The simple sugars can be utilised directly 
into the TCA cycle, but these are also associated with the highest costs. More complex media that is 
untreated is made up of longer polysaccharide chains, which need to be broken down into simpler sugars 
before it can be utilised. This can either be done by pre-treatment before it is introduced to the organism, 
or by enzymatic reactions provided by the organism (Stanbury et al. 2016). 
Oils are also an alternative for carbohydrates, which has both advantages and disadvantages. The energy 
density is higher in oils, and they have some antifoam properties, but the dissolvability of oxygen is 
reduced. Also, unused oils can make complications with the downstream processes.

organisms can utilise inorganic nitrogen sources, some can only utilise organic nitrogen, and some more 
specialised ones can also use nitrogen from the air. As seen with carbon, the limitations can heavy affect 
the expressions of genes. This can be used as described above to ensure a higher formation of products. 
Organisms will utilise the nitrogen to make among others amino acids. Even though an organism can utilise

Depending on the medium used and how pure or impure it is, there may be a need to add several

are typically sufficient. If the final product or a given pathway contains large amounts of a trace metal or 
micronutrients, this should be added accordingly (Stanbury et al. 2016).  
3.5.3 
Metabolic pathways  
Inside the cell there are two major categories of building elements needed for making products. The first is 
chemical energy, that can be found in the shape of ATP, NAD, NADP, etc. Secondly, there are twelve 
precursor metabolites which function as the core building blocks and can be modified to suite specific 
needs. These are: phosphoenol-pyruvate, glucose-6-phosphate, fructose-6-phosphate, erythrose-4-
phosphate, glyceraldehyde-3-phosphate, oxaloacetate, ribose-5-phosphate, pyruvate, acetyl-CoA, 2-
oxoglutarate, succinyl-CoA, and 3-phosphoglycerate (Whitacre 2012). 
Figure 8 shows the a ‘’bow-tie’’ model of how metabolism works. The trans* stands for transcription and

utilised to make either precursors, carriers, or CO2 for a full energy consumption. All of this is under heavy 
regulation, depending on a wide range of factors such as the carbon source, nitrogen amounts, levels on 
intermediates etc. This level of regulations is also feedback that can influence the amount of product. The 
higher the product concentration, the more down-regulated the pathway may be.

3.6 FERMENTATION THEORY 
There are several models and kinetics, which describe the elements of fermentation. These models provide 
an overview of the fermentation process, and potential optimisation strategies.  
3.6.1 
The Black Box Model 
This is a simpler model, that only sets out to investigate three things: input, output, and the resulting 
biomass. It is illustrated in Figure 9. 
The inner working of an organism is very complex, and there are many factors including the uptake of 
sugars, oxygen, and other components for producing proteins, DNA, and products. The Black Box Model 
does not take any of these into account.  
 
Figure 9: The Black Box Model of an anaerobic organism. (Villadsen et al. 2011) 
A key part of this model is that everything that enters the system will be transformed into one of the 
outputs. No matter ever disappears. There is also a mathematic element to the model that describes rate at 
which the biomass and the product is made, and a rate at which the substrate is being consumed.  
“Substrate” covers every element used by the organism, including sugars, a nitrogen source, and trace 
elements metals. If there is a gaseous element used for growth such as methane, it will also be categorised 
in the substrates. Oxygen is listed separately, because not all fermentations utilise oxygen.  
“Extracellular micro and macromolecules” involve everything secreted. It can be micro molecules such as 
ethanol, or macromolecules like proteins. The biomass and metabolites refer to the materials inside the 
cell. In the case of lipids, the product is not secreted and therefore would be listed under “metabolites”. 
The biomass refers to the dry cell weight.

3.6.3 
Types of fermentations  
There are three overall kinds of fermentation: batch, fed-batch, and continuous fermentation (chemostat). 
Figure 10 illustrates the three main types of fermentation setups. On the image a) represents a batch, 
where the biomass, substrate, and product depend on time volume constant. The image marked b) is fed-
batch, where the biomass, substrate, product, and volume depend on time. Image c) shows continuous 
fermentation with all parameters constant. 
 
Figure 10: Fermentation types. a) batch, b) fed-batch, c) continuous fermentation (Teixeira et al. 2013) 
Batch cultivation 
Batch fermentation is the most commonly used type and is the simplest of the three. In the batch all the 
nutrients are added in the beginning, and at the end of the fermentation everything is removed. There is 
addition of oxygen and acid/ alkaline to regulate the system, so technically it is not truly a batch system.  
The equation described in Section 3.6.3 can be applied here, with minor alterations. Since there is no feed 
into the system, this part of the equations can be removed. Likewise, there is nothing removed from the 
fermentation so the exit part of the equation can also be removed. The main advantage of this setup is the 
low risk of contamination, since no addition is done during the fermentation. Also, the calculations are 
relatively simple and straight-forward (Stanbury et al. 2016). 
High glucose concentration or other nutrients can have an inhibitory effect. Likewise, this type of 
fermentation is not optimal if the product is toxic since there is no removal, and the build-up would result 
in inhibition and in extreme cases lead to cell death. An alternative to this is continuous fermentation 
described below (Villadsen et al. 2011). 
Fed batch 
The fed batch will often start out as a normal batch, until there is a high biomass concentration. At this 
point, fresh medium will be added to the fermentation. This kind of fermentation can give a prolonged 
exponential phase and stationary phase, since new medium can be added to prolong the specific desired 
phase. The only limiting factor to a fed batch is the size of the fermentor, which includes the addition of 
oxygen and the stirring of the tank (Stanbury et al. 2016). 
The equation described in Section 3.6.3 can be applied to a fed batch setup, but since there is no removal 
of broth, the minus vexit is 0. The volume is depending on the time factor, and the vfeed does not need to be 
constant. This makes the calculation of the fed batch the most complex of the three (Villadsen et al. 2011).

Continuous Fermentation  
During the third kind of fermentation there is medium being removed and added, so in theory this can 
continue forever.  
For the first kind of continuous fermentation, the equation from Section 3.6.3 is split up in three segments: 
the addition of feed, and conversion of feed to product/biomass and the removal of product/biomass. In 
the chemostat all equations will equal to 0, since there is no net build-up of biomass or product in the 
fermentor, and no net removal of substrate (Villadsen et al. 2011). The continuous fermentation type 
requires less re-inoculation time and a less overall time for lag phase, since it only needs to happen once 
per fermentation (Stanbury et al. 2016).

rpm rotation at a 3 mm can mimic the aeration also found in a large-scale fermentor.

4 MATERIALS AND METHODS 
4.1 STRAIN 
The strain used is Cutaneotrichosporon oleaginosus (LGF 11), isolated from Nigeria. The original isolate was 
one of many which were all isolated for fast growth, so they could have industrial interest. The strain was 
provided by Associate Professor José Luis Martinez Ruiz, Technical University of Denmark from a -80 °C 
glycerol stock.  
Three main media recipes were used in the experimental work, these are; YPD medium, YNB medium, and 
CBS medium. All media and media components in this study were prepared from stock solution. For 
adjusting the pH of the stocks, unless stated otherwise, 2M sulfuric acid and 2M sodium hydroxide were 
used. 
4.1.1 
Trace metal solution and vitamin solution 
The trace metals were made in a 1000 mL water solution, with a pH of 6. The salts were added in the 
following order and the pH reset to 6 after each addition; FeSO4 7H2O: 3,0 g, ZnSO4*7H2O: 4,5 g, 
CaCl2*6H2O: 4,5 g, MnCl2*2H2O: 0,84 g, CoCl2*6H2O: 0,3 g, CuSO4*5H2O: 0,3 g, Na2MoO4*2H2O: 0,4 g, 
H3MO3: 1,0 g, KI: 0,1 g, and Na2EDTA: 15g. The pH was adjusted to 4 using 2M sulfuric acid, and the mixture 
was autoclaved. 
The vitamin solution contained 25 mg D-biotin dissolved in 10 ml 0,1M NaOH and 400mL H2O. The pH was 
adjusted to 6,5 with HCl, and the following vitamins were added: 
Ca-pantohenate: 500 mg, thiamine-HCl: 500 mg, pyridoxine-HCl: 500 mg, nicotinic acid: 500 mg, p-
Aminobenzoic acid: 100 mg. After the addition of vitamins, the pH was readjusted to 6,5, and 12,5 g of m-
inositol was added. Afterwards, the pH was readjusted to 6, and demineralised water was used to fill up the 
volume to 500 mL. The solution was then sterilised through a 0,22 µm filter and stored cold.  
4.1.2 
YPD Medium 
The YPD (yeast extract peptone dextrose) medium contained 20 g/L BactoTM peptone, 10 g/L BactoTM Yeast 
extract with 1 ml/L of Sigma 204 antifoam and filled up to 900 ml with demineralised water. Separately, 20 
g/L WWR chemical D-glucose was dissolved in 100 mL demineralised water. Separately they were 
autoclaved and combined in a LAF bench. 
4.1.3 
YNB Medium 
The YNB (yeast nitrogen base) medium contained 16 g/L BD Difco™ Yeast Nitrogen Base with all amino 
acids and filled up to 900 ml with demineralised water with 1 ml/l Sigma 204 antifoam. Separately, 20 g/L 
WWR chemical D-glucose was dissolved in 100 ml demineralised water. It was separately autoclaved and 
combined in a LAF bench. 
4.1.4 
CBS Minimal Medium 
For the minimal medium the CBS (Centraal Bureau voor Schimmelcultures/ Central Bureau for Fungi 
cultures) standard (Verduyn et al. 1992) was used, containing 5 g/L Applichem (NH4)2SO4, 3 g/L Applichem 
K2HPO4, and 0,5 g/L Applichem Mg SO4*7 H2O. The salts were mixed in 900 ml demineralised water, with 
1ml/L trace metals and vitamins as described above, as well as 1 ml/L Sigma 204 antifoam. 100 ml of 
carbon source with a concentration of 20 g/L was separately autoclaved unless stated otherwise.

added. The samples were vortexed for 5 seconds to get mixed, and then for 15 minutes with a rotator, and 
centrifuged for 5 minutes at 2500 RPM at 15 C°. 2 ml hexane (top phase) was removed with a Pasteur 
pipette to a new glass tube, then 2 ml of Milli-Q water was added to the hexane phase to wash it. The 
vortexing and centrifugation step for 2500 RPM at 15 C° was repeated. The samples were stored at -20 C°.

4.7 DATA TREATMENT AND ANALYSIS  
4.7.1 
BioLector and determining growth rates using light scattering  
All graphs for the BioLector experiments were created in R-script, with time plotted on the x-axis and light 
scattering on the y-axis. To get the growth rate, the natural logarithm to the light scattering unit was 
calculated and plotted on y-axis, and unaltered time on the x-axis was used to create a trendline. The slope 
of the line represents the growth rate. The transformation of the data for removal of outliers and cleaning 
up the data was done in R-script, and the growth rate calculations and trendline addition were all done in 
Microsoft Excel. See Appendix IV and V for the R-script code. 
4.7.2 
Determining growth rates using OD600 
On the graphs, the optical density was plotted against the time, and the dilution of the samples was also 
factored in to the calculation, giving an exponential growth phase. To transform this exponential growth 
phase into a straight line, the natural logarithm was calculated for the optical density. The transformed 
optical density data plotted on the y-axes, against unaltered time on the x-axis would give a trendline, and 
the slope represents the growth rate.    
4.7.3 
Determining growth rates using biomass  
To determine the growth rates from the biomass, first the units must be converted to g/L. Afterwards, 
natural logarithm to the biomass concentration was calculated and the transformed data just like the 
optical density and plotted against the time. The slope of the line represents the growth rate.  
4.7.4 
Determining growth rates using off-gas 
The off-gas was analysed with a mass spectrometer (MS) (Thermo Scientific, model: Prima Pro), and logged 
as individual percentages of the measured gasses and other volatile compounds to corresponding time 
points. To calculate the growth rate, the amount of accumulated CO2 was used. This was done by first 
converting the percentage to an amount in g/L, calculated from the known amount of air being passed 
through the system. The calculation is shown in detail in Appendix III. The natural logarithm to the 
accumulated CO2 is plotted against time, and the slope represents the growth rate. The plot is expected to 
have a straight line, and the incline or decline of the line indicates the growth rate (Knudsen 2015).  
4.7.5 
Statistical significance testing  
Two types of mean variance testing were used: t-tests when comparing two variables, and Analysis of 
Variance (ANOVA) tests for three or more variables. The t-test is essentially a signal-to-noise ratio, 
comparing the means of two datasets (Miceli et al. 2016). The null-hypothesis in each case stated that 
there is no difference between the tested datasets. The accepted level of significance was 95%, meaning 
that if the p-value was under 0,05 the null hypothesis was rejected.  
By running multiple t-test on a data set, the chance for getting Type I errors (“false positives”) increases. 
For three or more sets of samples, ANOVA tests were used which corrects for the error margins of multiple 
t-tests. For an ANOVA test to be useable, the data needs to follow a normal distribution (Saravanakumar et 
al. 2013). There is only maximum three data points for each fermentation, the skewness (symmetry) is 
therefore very unreliable and kurtosis (height of the peak) could not be factored in to the calculation. 
Therefore, it is assumed that the data follows a normal distribution. 
All the calculations were made in Excel 2013.

6 DISCUSSION  
6.1 THE MORPHOLOGY OF C. OLEAGINOSUS  
When observing the morphology of the organism on a plate (Figure 12), a smooth and lighter colour texture 
is seen. There is a much darker colouration on the cells shown on (Figure 13), and the colonies start to get a 
wrinkly surface texture. This could be caused by evaporation, which happens because there is no humidity 
added to the incubator, so water is drawn from the cells and the agar plate, affecting the texture.  
The cells seen on the left part of Figures 12 and 13, under the microscope are having a different cell 
density. This could be caused by human error, since the colonies are larger on the plate that have been 
incubated longer There are also cells which appear look darker under the microscope, which could be 
explained by the evaporation.  
The 72 hours plate (Figure 12) had more rod-shaped cells located in sets of two. This could be due to the 
cell division, since it is not seen on the plate which has been subjected to 30 °C for 19 days (Figure 13), 
where more of the cells are rounder and individual. It is not known whether this effect is due to the time 
factor or the lack of nutrients. 
In the shake flask experiments with 44 day (Figure 21) and 72 hour (Figure 14 to 20) incubation times and 
different carbon sources, a circular/rod shaped morphology was observed with no changes. In all the 
different experiments the cells were rounded.  
Only in the experiment with arabinose did the cells appear smaller than in other carbon sources. In the 44-
day experiment (Figure 21), the cells were still rounded, but the colour of the shake flask had a browner 
nuance compared to the other flasks. The high cell density is due to all the glucose being utilised, and 
evaporation from the long time in an incubator. This browner colour was observed in both the shake flask 
after 44 days, as well as on the plates after 19 days. The timing of when this shift in colour happens has not 
been further investigated.  
None of the fermentations in the 1 litre scale showed any pellet growth, filamentous growth, or floc 
(biofilm). When there were indications of a contamination, samples were investigated under a microscope. 
All examined samples showed similar rounded cells, which is the morphology known for many yeast 
species.  
The similarity in morphology across the experiments suggests that this circular or rod shape would remain 
over longer fermentations. For a continuous fermentation it would be advantageous if no complication 
happened due to changed morphology. With the cells being more individual and not having a filament 
growth the equipment stirring, oxidation, and distribution of the added nutrients would be more even.

6.2.1 
Furfural experiment  
The aim of the furfural experiments was to investigate the inhibitory effect on the growth rate of C. 
oleaginosus. It was not possible to get accurate data from the experiments with a higher than 1 g/L furfural 
concentration, because of the dissolving of the plastic of the micro tray (Japan Chemical Industry Ecology-
Toxicology & Information Center, 1992). Since the high concentration of furfural is not dissolvable in water, 
the relatively pure furfural created precipitant at the bottom of the plate. The light scattering used for 
detecting the biomass is at the bottom, so it was significantly affected by the dissolving micro tray. This 
could explain the strange increase in light scattering.  
 In Figure 22 the “4_blank” sample had a well with no light scattering. This could be due to either no added 
furfural by mistake, or the furfural could have been stuck to the semi permeated membrane. Apart from 
that blank sample, the remaining two blank 4 g/L furfural samples followed the same light scattering trend 
seen in the standard 4 g/L furfural with cells, and there is no observed growth until 0,5 g/L furfural.  
The 0,5 g/L furfural experiment had a longer lag phase than any of the other concentrations, so not only is 
the growth rate affected but also the lag phase. Therefore, there is a possibility that if the tray would have 
been incubated longer, biomass in higher concentrations could have also been achieved. The reason that 
this longer lag phase is observed could be because of the shake flask used for the inoculum did not contain 
any furfural. Without furfural the organism needs to adjust to a different environment, and this can result 
in a longer lag phase.  
Similar tendencies can be observed if there is a difference in the carbon source utilised in the shake flask 
which is later being used for inoculum. The growth rate is higher, but not significantly different to some the 
other concentrations tested (presented in Table 4). The longer growth could also be due to the inhibitory 
effect of furfural, whereby the organism is neutralising the chemical, prolonging the lag phase.  
Since furfural is a by-product from the hydrolysation of cellulose and hemicellulose, inhibition even at very 
low concentration of furfural would have meant that the alternative cheaper media might not be an option, 
and more expensive media would be needed instead.  
6.2.2 
High pH  
Growth at high pH has not been reported for C. oleaginosus prior to this study. Growth at higher pH is the 
novelty of the organism, therefore the experiments will mainly focus on this aspect, and if there is a desire 
for growth at a lower pH, other isolates and organisms would be more favourable over this one.  
The optimum pH value for C. oleaginosus is between 4,8 and 7 (Bracharz et al. 2017), but there are other 
organisms that also can utilise waste and produce lipids at levels as low as pH 3 (Santamauro et al. 2014). 
In Figure 24 growth at pH 9,5 and 10 were only observed when utilising the YNB medium, that contains all 
amino acids. The growth observed at pH 9 is quite odd, whereby one of the wells produced growth after 20 
hours, and the remaining ones did not show growth until around 60 hours. A reason that growth at pH 9,5 
and 10 was only observed in YNB could be one or more amino acids which cannot be synthesised at high pH

these conditions are not yet activated. Alternatively, some of the amino acids could have changed charge in

What can be seen in both YPD and minimal experiments is that the highest growth rate is at pH 8, but not

When comparing the YNB and CBS minimal media, the final light scattering had a lower maximum peak

observed in the 1 litre fermentation, where the same tendencies are seen in the YPD fermentations.  
The two different BioLector fermentations that have the same minimal media composition (Figure 25) were 
made from the exact same media stored at 5 °C. The first fermentations (left graph of Figure 25) were done 
with newly made broth filtered into sterile 50 ml flacon tubes. Before the fermentation on the right graph, 
the liquid was filtered again through a sterile 0,22 µm filter. The underlying reason for this large difference 
in both specific growth rate and in lag phase is unknown, but there was a difference in the inoculum and 
the flower plate. There is a difference in the initial light scattering seen easily in Figure 27 to 30 with the 
C/N experiments, indicating a difference in the plates. Since the biomass was mainly located on the sides of 
the plate, it is difficult to determine how large the uncertainty is, but it is there. 
There seems to be an effect on the YPD media at high pH, when comparing the growth rates of the 
BioLector and 1 litre fermentation. In the 1 litre fermentor the YPD media had a faster growth rate than any 
other non-complex media, at the high pH it had the slowest.  
If there are larger fragments which cannot be transported and need enzymatic cleaving before it can be 
utilised by the cell, this might cause a bottle neck. If these enzymes are pH sensitive because likely the 
cleaving site or the overall structure is affected, and they operate at a slower rate, this will result in a bottle 
neck and affect the overall growth rate. The transporters for simple amino acids are operational since a 
higher growth rate is seen in the YNB media. There could also be YPD broken down by the high pH to either 
unusable substance or turning the YPD toxic. 
The growth in high pH medium gives opportunities for producing products which are more stable at higher 
pH, such as some pharmaceuticals.  
In addition, there are three cheap main methods for hydrolysation of complex biomass: acids, neutral, and 
alkaline. The organism’s ability to grow at high pH allows for the neutralisation of the acids from the 
hydrolysation, or for leaving the alkaline in the fermentation, thereby reducing the amount of acid used.

Nitrogen limitation  
Since there was so much biomass in the plate rings, it is very difficult to describe what is happening from a 
growth perspective. As can be seen in Figures 27 to 30, there was not an increase of more than 3 LCU from

A possible explanation for the ring formation is foaming in the well. Sigma 204 antifoam was added to

6.3  1 LITRE EXPERIMENTS 
A large number of experiments have been conducted throughout this research project. Due to the 
exploratory nature of the study, a lot of them have been unsuccessful, and provided no useful data. In 
these cases, the learnings from the issues have been noted, and consequent setups were adjusted 
accordingly. The data presented in this study comes from the fermentations which resulted in relevant 
information. Data from contaminations, fermentations with no growth, missing/insufficient data due to 
disabling of off-gas because of foaming or pH spikes were not used.  
The dry weight biomass data showed very inconsistent measurements. The YPD in Figure 31 is an example 
of the biomass appearing to increase and decrease. This tendency is apparent in all the carbon source 
experiments where biomass was measured. For the subsequent experiments the biomass was not 
measured since it requires a minimum of 4 ml of liquid and is inaccurate.  
Growth can still be seen on the biomass measurements. Although it does not follow any straight or 
exponential line, there was an increase in the biomass found in all experiments where measurements were 
made. This means that even if it is not possible to get accurate growth rate measurements from the 
biomass, it can be observed that something larger than 0,22µm was produced and got caught on the filter. 
During the experiment with the mixed carbon sources glucose and arabinose (Figure 40), samples were 
taken to attempt to measure the dry weight. Every sample took over 30 minutes to analyse, and the 
vacuum pump and several filters broke. As only one machine was available, the samples had been out for 
over 90 minutes before the last liquid had passed, and the sample set was no longer viable.  
It is uncommon for the samples to need such a long time to pass through the filter. When comparing the 
biomass growth of C. oleaginosus with Y.Lipolytica from previously studies conducted by the author, the 
samples did not take that long even with high cell density. This suggests that an unknown product is filling 
the filter and leads to blockages, which in turn causes uncertainties with the sampling. For the same reason, 
no final dry weight was calculated due to the lack of consistency in the measurements. Before an accurate 
way of measuring the biomass is found, the numbers will not give an accurate picture.  
No final conclusion should be made on uncertain data, as it may lead to incorrect assumptions or futile 
future experiments.

The HPLC data is also inconsistent, by going both up and down. Some samples had an increase in carbon

There is a difference between the off-gas data and the OD600 growth rate. This may be due to various 
reasons, such as a difference in what is measured, since none of the methods are directly measuring the 
actual cell count. The method of sampling also varies, and the equipment used for the analysis can all affect 
the data.  
The off-gas is a mixture of all the gasses captured from the fermentor, and the percentage of the CO2 is 
isolated. This measurement can be used to calculate how many moles of carbon is released from the 
fermentation over a given time. With the assumption that all the cells are growing and multiplying at the 
same rate, and that every individual cell is releasing the same amount of CO2, this can then be used to 
calculate a growth rate. Furthermore, it is assumed that there is no formation of by-product that would 
release CO2. The products that are made, as long as they are made in proportion to the cell concentration, 
do not make a difference to the growth rate because it is calculated with the natural logarithm.  
All cells need energy to maintain themselves, such as producing new enzymes, repairing damages etc. The 
longer the cells have been alive, the more repairs must naturally occur. Also, the more cells there are, the 
more maintenance there will be in the overall cell culture. This may result in a slightly higher growth rate 
than should be observed, since this CO2 is not used evenly by old and new cells. 
The OD600 measurement works by sending a beam of light at a known wavelength through a sample, 
measuring the amount of light that comes through, and thereby calculating the 
amount which was absorbed. A blank sample with media, but without cells is used 
for the calibration, to account for elements from the media which could also block 
the light. 
There are four large potentials for error in this setup. Firstly, everything that blocks 
the light beam is read as the organism. This includes dead cells, and potential 
secreted elements that all have absorption at 600 nm. There is no difference in the 
OD600, so this will introduce an error to the measurements. The second error factor 
is too much biomass in the sample. If there are two or more organisms in line, the 
measurement will only show one.  
A similar limitation is that if a beam hits a cell, and the reflected light hits a different 
cell before it is measured, it will result in an error indicating that there are fewer 
cells than there are. These two effects are illustrated in Figure 46. 
Lastly, all samples need to be diluted to avoid the aforementioned issues, which 
introduces the potential for human error. Growth rates on different carbon sources 
The growth rate measurements from the OD600 and from the CO2 are different in 
some of the fermentations, which could be due to some of these errors. Both faster 
and slower growth rates have been observed from OD600 compared to off-gas, so 
the difference is not consistently biased to one side.

The null hypothesis states that the OD600 and off-gas values are not significantly different. A two-tail t-test

Alternative media  
Biomass from T. atroroseus was hydrolysed, used as carbon source, and compared to YPD medium (Figure 
47) was compared to YPD media. B1-B3 was the hydrolysed biomass with 20 g/L glucose. A1-A3 was YPD

therefore the scrapings of the biomass from the filters are expected to contain some cellulose residues. 
The closest experiment that can be used for comparison is the fermentation using YPD medium, since they 
both are both complex media and have glucose as a carbon source.  
Figure 47 shows the CO2 off-gas data from the YPD medium and the hydrolysed biomass experiment. The 
decreasing curve on the hydrolysed biomass shows more of a downward curve in stationary phase, unlike 
the YPD which does not have a reduction in the CO2 produced. The YPD medium is possibly more broken 
down, so the further enzymatic breakdown to utilise all the nutrients is possible.  
The hydrolysed biomass has “only” been autoclaved at 121 for 20 min. The YPD is also declining, but there 
is more material that elongates the stationary phase. At the stationary phase the limiting factor could be 
the reaction rate at which the enzymes can break down the media components. When a given nutrient 
source has been depleted with no available alternatives, there would be a decrease in off-gas CO2, since no 
new cells were made.

6.3.3 
Temperature experiments  
There were several different temperatures tested, but the 40 °C and 45 °C fermentations did not have any 
growth after 4 days, so these experiments were discontinued. It is possible that heat-shock resulted in cell 
death, since the inoculum was prepared in a LAF bench. In the LAF there is a sterile airflow over stainless 
steel tables, which is colder than the surrounding area. This would lower the temperature of the inoculum 
from the 30 °C in the shake flask. First a significant decrease in the temperature from the incubation, 
followed by a heat shock in 40 °C and 45 °C liquid could have caused damage to the organism. The growth 
rate is very low in the 35 °C fermentation setup, which could also simply mean that the organism cannot 
survive in such a high temperature, due to denaturing of protein, alteration of cell membrane etc. 
To investigate whether temperature causes a difference in the growth rate, increments of 5 °C were tested 
in the fermentations. This was to investigate how the temperature gradient that comes with the cooling or 
heating of the fermentation affects the organism. 
A t-test was performed with the null hypothesis stating that there is no difference in the growth rates 
between a 30 °C fermentation and 25 °C or 35 °C. In the case of the 35 °C fermentation the null hypothesis 
can be rejected, with a p-value of 0,0005. However, the fermentation at 25 °C had a p-value of 0,6198 
showing no statistical significance, and the hypothesis cannot be rejected. This means that there is an 
operation window of at least 5 °C, where the growth rate is unaffected by the change in temperature.

7 CONCLUSION 
During the investigation of the morphology of C. oleaginosus, it was only observed to have either rod or 
spherical cells in shake flask fermentations. This applied to all tested carbon sources, long fermentation 
times, and randomly taken samples from 1 litre bioreactor fermentations. 
There was no inhibition of the growth rate with furfural for up to 0,5 g/L concentration, but the lag phase 
was prolonged. Higher concentrations were also tested, but the results were inconclusive, due to 
interference created by the furfural.  
Growth was registered in up to pH 10 utilising YBN medium containing all amino acids, and up to pH 9 for 
minimal medium. There was no decrease in the growth rate at high pH compared to pH 5. This makes the 
organism ideal for growth on alkaline-hydrolysed media, or acid-hydrolysed media after strong 
neutralisation. 
When comparing pure carbon sources, statistically significant differences were detected for both the off-
gas and OD600 measurements. Based on off-gas data the only carbon source with a significantly lower 
growth rate was arabinose, and the fastest growth rate was measured in sucrose at 0,25515 h-1.  
Based on OD600 measurements, lactose also was statically significantly lower, and the fastest growth rate 
was detected in xylose at 0,2611 h-1. In summary, the highest growth rates were observed when sucrose or 
xylose was the carbon source. The latter is found in cheap complex plant biomass and is therefore a 
desirable carbon source for C. oleaginosus. As a pure carbon source, sucrose is a desirable choice in 
Denmark with a large sugar beet production.  
The mixed carbon source experiment with glucose and xylose showed no significant difference in the 
growth rate from the off-gas data between the individual growth rates and the mixture. The OD600 data 
showed a difference between glucose and xylose, favouring xylose. There was no difference between 
xylose and the mixture, or glucose and the mixture. Again, this is a desirable trait since xylose is cheaper, 
and if there is cellulose remaining that is broken down to glucose, it will not affect the growth rate. 
Testing a mixture of arabinose and glucose, there was no statistically significant difference in the growth 
rate of pure glucose and the arabinose/glucose mixture, but a significantly lower growth rate was observed 
in arabinose, which was expected based on the single carbon source results. A shoulder was detected on 
the off-gas data with the mixture that is not seen when utilising pure glucose, which is suspected to stem 
from the utilisation of arabinose after all the glucose had been used.  
The tests conducted to investigate temperature stability showed no difference in the growth rate between 
25 °C and the baseline at 30 °C, but a reduction was observed at 35 °C. This confirms that the ideal 
temperature for fermentation is between 25 °C and 30 °C. 
Testing a more economically viable crude glycerol as a carbon source proved unsuccessful with a 
significantly lower growth rate than with pure glycerol. Although crude glycerol is cheaper, the reduction in 
growth rate is so substantial, that it would likely offset any benefits from this alternative. 
Alternatives to traditional pure carbon sources and defined media were also explored, experiments utilising 
used beer spent grain and hydrolysed fungal biomass were compared to traditional YPD medium as a 
control. The null hypothesis stating that there is no difference between the growth rates of the three 
above-mentioned complex media could not be rejected. With growth rates that are all above 0,32h-1 and a 
higher growth rate than on any other minimal media tested, it seems like a viable option for a cheap and 
fast growth media.

8 FOLLOW UP EXPERIMENTS AND FUTURE PERSPECTIVE 
8.1 DNA SEQUENCE AND GENETIC MANIPULATION  
A natural first step for future research is to get a full DNA sequence and annotation of this train of C. 
oleaginosus. This would serve to confirm that the genes for the high lipid production under nitrogen 
limitation are present, and indicate whether this can be manipulated.  
One of the particularly important genes would be malic enzyme (ME) which is used for regeneration to get 
sufficient amounts of NADPH.  
For genetic manipulation many of the tools like CRISPR work with two systems which are used for the 
repairing of DNA damage. The homologous recombination uses a repair system to insert genes into the 
genome. Without this system manipulations would be more costly and time consuming, and the organism’s 
potential as a platform that can fill multiple roles as a production organism might not be as strong. 
Therefore, if there are genes for homologous recombination, vectors and protocols for genetic 
manipulation should be investigated.  
An important factor for an organism used for large scale production is not making any toxins. If it does, the 
level of security for all laboratories and production facilities would require a higher standard. If there are 
genes relating to toxic compounds, it could hinder the approval process for the lipids as a food additive or 
as a supplement.  
8.2 ABOVE 1 G/L FURFURAL FERMENTATION 
The equipment posed a limitation to furfural experiments, since concentrations above 1 g/L dissolved the 
flower plate. To investigate if the growth rate is affected by levels higher than 1 g/L furfural, flower plates 
from non-soluble materials are recommended, such as glass or a different plastic.  
An alternative approach is to conduct the fermentation in the 1 litre system. However, since furfural is 
suspected cancerogenic, acutely toxic, and harmful on skin, testing it in 1 litre fermentations outside a 
ventilation hood would be inadvisable.  
To try to circumvent the inhibition and increase of lag phase of furfural, a series of experiments of 
increasing concentrations could through evolutionary pressure address this. This can be done either on 
plates with increasing concentrations, or in shake flasks. A different solution is continuous fermentation 
with increasing furfural concentration added to the medium. 
8.3 PH EXPERIMENTS  
To further investigate the effect of pH on C. oleaginosus, a series of high and low pH fermentations in 1 litre 
scale is recommended. Since there was growth observed at pH 10 in YNB media, an upper limit has not 
been established. In the high pH fermentation series, the only medium that had growth over pH 9,5 was

8.4 IMPROVEMENT ON SAMPLING AND PROTOCOLS  
To obtain a good and reliable biomass data and get growth rate data from this source, a better protocol is 
needed. The solution might be to have several washing steps, and spinning down samples. It is currently 
unknown what is causing the large deviations in the sampling, therefore improvements to the protocol in 
this regard would be worthwhile. 
Along with the alteration to the biomass protocol, either a different approach for the carbon concentration 
measurements is needed, or the machines needs to be serviced/replaced.  
8.5 LIPID OPTIMISATION 
Since C. oleaginosus is known for its lipid production (Saravanakumar et al. 2013), it would be ideal to 
further investigate a medium which would be optimal for lipid production. Samples have been prepared 
and methylated, and are available to serve as a good starting point for further studies. These samples are 
from the nitrogen limitation experiment, the furfural inhibition experiment, and the pH experiment.  
Based on the findings in this research, a hypothesis for the effect of nitrogen limitation is that the higher 
the C/N, the more lipids are produced. If the lipid analysis confirms this, then a promoter section is 
activated with nitrogen limitation as described in Section 3.3.2. In this case, a new series of experiments are 
recommended with the aim of optimising the production of lipids, and finding the middle ground of 
maximum cell density that gives the highest lipid content. It is also recommended to simultaneously 
investigate the levels of ME, and FAS 1 and 2, and see if there is an increase matching the findings from 
(Kourist et al. 2015). 
If it is not confirmed, it would mean that a nitrogen limitation has not been reached or that the nitrogen 
limited gene section is not there.  
The lipid analysis would also be relevant with regards to furfural inhibition. If there is a significant reduction 
in the lipid produced when exposed to furfural, the hydrolysed biomass might not be ideal as a carbon 
source. Since it is an inhibitory molecule, it might also have the opposite effect or alter the lipid 
composition, and this feature could be manipulated.  
The pH experiments at pH 8-10 might give a different lipid profile if shorter or longer chains are more 
predominant, or the saturation pattern changes. This could lead to a manipulation strategy to get the most 
valuable lipid structures.  
In summary, the organism has a huge potential both as a research topic and as a promising industrial 
platform. Further research is highly recommended.

APPENDIX I: REDUX CALCULATION  
The chemical formula of oleic acid is C18H34O2. To make the redux calculation it needs to be on a cmol basis.  
C18H34O2 is divided by 18 for the carbon, giving a Cmol formula of CH1,88O0,11. The Cmol redux of C18H34O2 will 
then be 5,66.  
For glucose C6H12O6 divided by 6 will be CH2O and have a redux of 4.  
4 𝐶𝑚𝑜𝑙
5.66 𝐶𝑚𝑜𝑙= 0,7067 𝐶𝑚𝑜𝑙
𝐶𝑚𝑜𝑙 
The two numbers are then divided, which gives a final Cmol/Cmol. Thereby the maximum theoretical yield 
is 0,7067 Cmol/Cmol.  
To calculate the gram per gram, 1 Cmol of sugar will give 0.7067 Cmol of lipid with a maximal theoretical 
yield. One Cmol of glucose is 30 g/Cmol, and for lipid it is 15,64 g/Cmol. This is then multiplied by the 
0.7067 factor, which results in 11,052 grams of lipid per Cmol.  
When multiplied by 3,33 to get 100 grams of glucose, it results in 36,84 grams of lipid per 100 grams of 
glucose.
