Abstract
Football, is one of the most beautiful and popular sports. As a popular sport, it is normal
and expected that professional football attracts the attention of many people from the
business field. In the 21st century, the importance of business in football has only been
enhanced. One part of doing business in football is for the clubs to exploit the football
market for the recruitment of new players at the minimum possible cost. This master
thesis aims to investigate if and how, various machine learning models can help football
clubs be viable during transfer periods by predicting a certain market value range for the
player so that the club can negotiate accordingly. At first we provide a brief introduction
to the basics of football and a description of previous similar projects conducted trying to
give a clear answer to the very same problem. Unlike other projects, this research heavily
relies on the data that have or share the same idea as the expected goals metric, which is
a relatively new statistical model and is very popular among football analysts. At first we
have applied various linear regression models to determine whether there is a significant
correlation between the input features the target data. We then, proceeded following a
different approach, formulating the problem as a classification one with the classes being
high and low (High market value price and low market value price). After that, in the
following step, multiclass classification is used trying to predict whether the future market
value of a professional football player will be classified as Low,Medium or High. In the
last experimental step of this research it has been utilized the design of a simple neural
network. The goal behind that action is to investigate the behaviour of a simple neural
network given the dataset that has been build from two trustworthy sources for the purpose
of this project. On to the final chapters of this report, there are presented several future
plans for the development of the current project. Following all the aforementioned steps,
this report thoroughly presents the findings of the main analysis, evaluate each approach
separately and reports the conclusions in a concise manner.
Keywords: Football, Market value, Data science, Market value prediction, Market value
classification

Introduction
It was August 3rd 2017 when Neymar, a Barcelona player at that time, agreed to move to
France and play for Paris Saint Germain football club. The move was completed after the
French giants payed an all time world record fee of 222 million вӮ¬ [1] for the player. It was
obvious that the french team brought Neymar onboard having on their mind international
titles (e.g. Champions League), as they were already dominating their domestic league at
the time. Fast forward, 6 years later, the french team has still not achieved some notable
success in international competitions trophies, and from time to time there have been
reports about the frustration of the club with their star player Neymar. The question that
has been in the mind of many football fans and experts for a long time on this topic is:
Was Neymar worth the money spent on his transfer? Everybody can have his say, but it
is difficult to judge oneвҖҷs action in football as it is a constantly changing environment and
profession with a lot of risk in every aspect.
In their try to decrease the risk levels, football clubs have deployed various ways to manВӯ
age these risks. One of these ways is also the introduction of data analytics and data
science in football. Although data and statistics have been around for quite some time in
football, clubs have started utilizing data thoroughly to make data driven decision after the
21st century. Football clubs, have used data science in multiple sections such as tactics,
player performance, financial sector,etc. In this project we will focus on exploring how
data science can be useful in football transfers related tasks.
Taking a close look to the tables on Figure 1.1. and Figure 1.2, it helps to understand
where the motivation for this project come from. As many clubs spend huge amount of
money on transfer periods, we would like to investigate if such a strategy is viable for
football clubs.

the 10 teams with the highest expenditures during the seasons 2000/2001Вӯ2023/2024

this project is to investigate whether certain types of machine learning models that have
bee previously proved to be efficient in other similar projects, can predict the future market

from open data, and how accurate these predictions can be. The first intention is to build
at least one ML model that could achieve satisfying operational scores in order to be
used from professional football clubs ( but also from football clubs of every level) to make
data driven decision in the negotiation table, however as this is a research project, any
conclusion drawn after the final results will be valuable for further investigation.

Related Work
In this chapter we present some previously carried out research projects related to our
topic, that have been published in scientific papers/websites. Reviewing prior work on
similar projects it is a necessary step for the continuation of our research.
2.1
Literature
One of the most important thing in a project like this is to use the correct features that will
uplift the evaluation metrics of the model. An interesting research from Majewski[3] breaks
down a group of factors that determined the market value of the 150 most valuable forward
players back at the time of the research. It appears that that the clubвҖҷs international ranking
in the FIFA classification list can well affect the individual players market value. Another
factor that applies only to the best five players, is the playersвҖҷ popularity Which according
to the results of the study, it can have a huge effect. The author in [3] has followed a
linear regression model and used three different model evaluation models which are the
following ones: Ordinary least square (OLS), generalized least square (GLS) and feasible
generalized least square with correction for heteroscedasticity (FGLS).
Following the conclusion from the aforementioned research, a project conducted by Singh
and Lamba [4] is using a popularity parameter in their dataset which is obtained from the
video game FIFA 18. In combination with that dataset, the authors in [4] also use data
collected from TM. One of the most important observations of this study is that during the
exploratory analysis was observed the correlation of all the parameters with the target
variable that was the вҖқcurrent market valueвҖқ. The authors decided to rely on these numВӯ
bers as in their final analysis they included only parameters that had at least 0.5 correlation
value. It is important however to mention here that the statistical analysis in this project
confirms the conclusion from [3] as popularity indicators are amongst the highest related
parameters to the market value of the players. In the main analysis, five regression modВӯ
els were used which are namely: Linear Regression, Ridge Regression, Decision Tree,
Random Forest, Gradient Boost. In the final results of [4] it is reported that by including
crowdsourcing, popularity and previous year statistics variables an astonishing 0.89 RВӯ
squared value was achieved from the Random Forest model, which was also the best
performing model.
Behravan and Razavi[5] present a different approach in the market evaluation process as
their methodology and model consist two phases. In the first phase the authors use an auВӯ
tomatic clustering algorithm named PSO to split the players in four clusters regarding ther
position. Therefore the players have been split into Goalkeepers(GK), Defenders(DF),
Midfielders(MF) and Forwards/Strikers(FW). Just like in [4], the authors have used data
obtained from the same video game but a more recent version of that (FIFA 20).In the
second phase of the project the authors use a support vector regression model for their
analysis. What is an important contribution to this project is that for the four different clusВӯ
ters the authors have used different features to perform their analysis. For the evaluation
of the model there were used five evaluation metrics, namely: MSE, RMSE, MAE, MAPE,
and R2. Making use of the Matlab R2019b software, the analysis showed that the model
was able to outperform 3 models from prior projects. by achieving an RВӯsquared value of
74%.
Following a similar approach, AlВӯAsadi and Tasdemir[6] have utilized four regression modВӯ
els (Linear regression, Multiple linear regression, Regression tree and Random forest reВӯ
5
==PAGE:16==
gression) to see whether the same dataset (FIFA 20) can achieve better results. A split
of 80%Вӯ20% is applied in the dataset in order to fit the models. The comparison of their
own models points to the random forest regression as the most successful one. With a
94% RВӯsquared value and lower MAE and RMSE values, the random forest regression is
clearly achieving greater results than the model of Behravan and Razavi[5] even though
AlВӯAsadi and Tasdemir[6] are using 48 less features in their final analyzed dataset.
It seems that MГјller, Simons and Weinmann ave been inspired by MajewskiвҖҷs project[3]
as they use the same feature design logic for their dataset. In that project the authors
aim to determine whether their suggested model have can be more accurate than crowd
estimation by using as an input TM data. The main contribution in their project is the creВӯ
ation of a multilevel regression model. At first the authors have taken into consideration
only the effect of previous year players market evaluation. In the second stage the have
added the player characteristic parameters. In the third stage player performance indiВӯ
cators are added and finally in the fourth stage the authors have make use of the player
popularity factor as well. In the final chapters of their publication, the authors reveal their
conclusion as their suggested model is more efficient in low price evaluations but it falls
short to crowd estimates in cases of high market price evaluations.
A very interesting study from Inan and Cavas investigate the market value estimation
through an artificial neural network model[7]. The authors use a combination of TM and
Opta data from 306 games and 358 midfield players from the Turkish Super League during
the season 2018Вӯ2019. The number of the analyzed players was reduced to 236 after
the exploratory and the preprocessing stages. Simulated data were used to assist the
authors once they found out that it was not possible to design an effective ANN with the
data they had at the moment. An important information underlined in this study is that the
injuries have a negative impact in the players market evaluation even though the number
of injuries of player is not indicated in most of the datasets.
2.2
Conclusion
In summary, it is obvious that a wide range of regression models seems to be suitable
for research topic such as football playersвҖҷ market evaluation. In the reported reviewed
literature we can see that in a sport like football where responsibilities and player characВӯ
teristics vary from position to position, splitting the data into clusters where the number of
cluster will be the number of the player positions in a dataset will only provide more accuВӯ
rate results. It is also worth mentioning, that the correct feature selection for the analysis
depending on the statistical analysis is of big importance for the final results. This thesis
takes into consideration these findings while trying to explore other ways as well to predict
playersвҖҷ market values.

3.1
Football as a sport
Football is one of the most ancient and worldwide known, played and supported sports.
We see the first footprints of football as a game in the ancient Chinese civilization[8].
There are also evidences of similar games to football played with a ball in ancient Greece
and ancient Rome. However, according to Giulianotti & Robertson[9] it is no earlier than
the 14th century that the origins of modern football were spotted in Britain. As football conВӯ
tinued to develop through the years, multiple football confederations were formed around
the globe to handle and resolve organizational and legal issues so that the football is acВӯ
cepted and played from all ages and genders in as many countries as possible. These
confederations, aim also to promote the sport of football in as many countries as possible
and develop football as much as they can where there is the need for it. They are unable
to achieve great result however without the help of each countryвҖҷs football union. ThereВӯ
fore, in every country that football is played, there is a governing body or a football union
as it is named in a lot of countries (e.g. DBU Вӯ Dansk Boldspil Union) where it cooperates
with a larger football confederation. Amongst others, these unions often set their own
regulations about transfer periods and the process of a playerвҖҷs transfer.

associations of a separate continent. In the table(number) we can see all the names of

world they are responsible for. As mentioned in the previous section these confederaВӯ

3.3
Legislation related to football players transfers
The aforementioned confederations and all the football associations are constantly workВӯ
ing on establishing regulations that will allow every country and its football clubs to be
competitive. It is not a secret however, that in football have always existed controversial
regulations such as the Bosman[10] case for example. A newly established regulation
7
==PAGE:18==
from UEFA that is quite relevant to this project is mentioned in[11]. According to UEFA,
European football clubs, before 1st of July 2023, were allowed to split the total payment
of a playerвҖҷs transfer in as many years as the playerвҖҷs new contract was. This rule inВӯ
deed was helpful for football clubs to secure sustainability over the years. The problem
however was that such a rule, even though promoted sustainability in all football clubs,
it was quite handy mostly to football clubs with an advanced financial status as the clubs
could spend a lot of money to sign a player but the payment would be completed through
installments, with the final annual cost being relatively low. Apart from that, in the meanВӯ
time these clubs would be able to financially cover these annual expenses through their
revenue. With the new regulation however every team from a country that is part of UEFA
has to cover the expenses of a transferred player in 5 years since the day that the player
is signed. With that saying, this new regulation allows вҖқsmallerвҖқ clubs to be competitive
not only in the football field but also in the transfer market as well as the big teams will
have to think twice before spending a lot of money to sign a player, otherwise there will
be the risk of big financial downgrade of the club.

Football, that started as a form of entertainment for lower and middle classes, is now

Discussion
In this chapter we discuss the main components of the project, important setbacks that
caused increased time consumption as well as future plans to further develop or scale the
project. Вӯdata from international competitions Вӯmore event data Вӯdata from national teams
competition Вӯapply new models.
7.1
Setbacks
The most important issue of the project was the collection of data from TM website. In
contrast to the FBref website where all the needed data are in the same page and acВӯ
cessible, TM website had no such option to download data, and in addition to this, every
player has his own profile on the website. Hence, it was needed to visit each profile one
by one and perform the scraping technique. Building on this, even though accessing the
websiteвҖҷs structure was easy enough, it was difficult to find where were the data located in
the html code. Eventually the data is hidden in specific scripts on the html code. And even
when this issue was taken care of, the data extraction kept challenging the project as not
all the playerвҖҷs profiles (website links) had the data hidden in the same script. Thus the
profile link of each one of the all the players had to be inspected in order to find where the
data is. This challenge was by far the higher time consuming process of the whole project
because even when the location of the data on each link was found, the coding environВӯ
ment and software was not able to keep up with the amount of data that was extracting
and it was only scraping few links at a time. With that saying, the same process had to be
repeated multiple times before all the important data were extracted. Talking about time
consumption, once the final dataset was constructed, finding and separating players with
same name was another challenge. Although resolving this issue did not take that much
time as the previous one, what was bad about it is the confusion it caused as the issue
was identified in a relatively late stage, during preprocessing when the analysis showed
that some players in the same season were for example part of 3 or 4 teams. So when
this happened, we had to take a few steps back, before moving into the main part of the
research.
All the previous issues, are related to the data of the project. When it comes to the plan
and strategy of the project, it was quite challenging after the interview with Mr Rigg, to
approach the project in a different way. Initially the project was planned to be a pure
regression problem. The main challenge here was that by the time the interview took
place, some steps of the project were already initiated and once the main plan of the
project changed, this steps had also to be approached differently if not completely be
abandoned. However the interview, heavily affected the planning and we can say that
eventually it lead to a better structure of the project plan and strategy. Moving in the main
analysis of the project, it was expected in the beginning of the project that the size of the
dataset would be bigger than the final one. It is believed that the significant reduction of
the dataset strongly affected the final results of the used machine learning models.
7.2
Future Work
Stepping on the last challenge mentioned in the previous section, we consider as the first
improvement step of this project to be the addition of more observations. This consist of
two stages. First, it would be wise to increase the analysis time range, say from 3 years to
5 years consistent performances in the higher level of professional football. Second stage,

is the addition of data from all the international competitions that the collected players will
have been participating at. This competitions could be internal european competitions
such as the competitions held by UEFA, or other international competitions organized
from FIFA. Another step forward that can be taken to improve the final findings of the
project, is to target each time a very specific group of players. For example it could be
that in the future this project is used to only predict values of players that only perform in

Conclusions
This project inspect the ability of several machine learning models to predict the market
value of professional football players playing in the five most popular football leagues in
Europe. The models use a dataset of 2729 observations and 38 features for training and
testing. The target variable is called вҖқValueвҖқ and contains the market value prices of each
player of the dataset. First finding of the report touches on the correlation between the
target variable and every other non categorical feature. The correlation matrix in chapter
4 indicated that no feature has a relation higher than 0.5 with the target variable. And
in combination with relatively small to medium size dataset and the pairplot provided in
Appendix B confirming that there no linear relationship between most of the features and
the target variable, they justify the poor performances of the first approach.
Comparing the first two regression approaches of the project we can confirm that the size
of the dataset plays indeed a very crucial role in the performance of the machine learning
models. For the first approach all the categorical data has been excluded from the dataset
as they were data nonВӯperformance data and the goal was to investigate whether that
would have an impact on the performance of the models. In the second approach were
the categorical data were encoded with oneВӯhot encoder, the dataset was enlarged almost
by 7.5 times more than its initial size. This change, saw the two best performing models
(Lasso and Gradient Boosting) improve their performances by more than 20% each by
achieving an RВӯsquared value of approximately 0.53 and 0.51 respectively.
We then applied binary classification with the help of the 5 best performing models of the
second approach: Lasso, Ridge, kВӯNN, Random Forest and Gradient Boosting. The two
classes of the approach were named High for market values bigger than 16 million euro
and Low for market values smaller than that. The results showed that most of the models
were able to correctly predict high market values but there was a struggle to predict low
prices from most of them. That most probably happens due to the existence of numerous
outliers that were detected during preprocessing using the DBSCAN technique, see figure
x. A fact worth mentioning is that, in this approach again, Lasso and Gradient Boosting
models showed better predictive ability than the rest of the models
In the fourth approach of the model, multiclass classification was implemented again in
the 5 models that were used in binary classification. The prediction classes are now High
(again for prices of 16 million euro and above), Low (for market values with a cost less
than 3.5 million euro), and Medium (for market values between 3.5 and 16 million euro).
As expected, we saw a fall on the final results of these approach as now the problem is
more complex by adding one more class. However Lasso and Gradient Boosting models
were once again able to score descent f1 values for High (0.76 and 0.67 respectively)
and Medium (0.72 and 0.70 respectively) class. The models once again were struggling
again to predict Low market values, but this time the Gradient Boosting model was better
at predicting low values. Just like in the third approach there is a belief that the outliers
lying in the low levels of the scatterplot in chapter 4, have a big impact to predictive ability
of the models. Furthermore in both approaches of the project, third and fourth, we can
also explain the poor performance in predicting low values considering the imbalanced
class distribution that is a result of the quantiles split. As mentioned earlier in the report
in the second approach, the 66th quantile was used as the splitting line of classes Hign
and Low, whereas in the multiclass classification approach it was used the 20 quantile
to separate the low values from the medium values, and the 66th quantile to identify the
45
==PAGE:56==
medium and the high market values. In the very same approach we continued by tuning
the hyperparameters of Gradient Boosting model with the help of RandomizedSearchCV
technique. The results were impressing as the model was able to achieve an overall f1
score of approximately 83%. Therefore we conclude that tuning the hyperparameters is
an essential step when utilizing machine learning models.
In the last approach it is used a simple neural network design. The network consist of 3
hidden layers with 8 neurons on the first layer, 16 neurons on the second layer and 32
neurons or units on the third hidden layer. on every hidden layer in each neuron ReLU
actiovation function is being used before sending the results to the next layer, while in the
final layer the model uses the softmax activation function. Without tuning the hyperpaВӯ
rameters we can observe that the neural network is more efficient in predicting the low
and the high values when compared to the other models during the multiclass classificaВӯ
tion approach. However, despite the fact the it is predictive ability of medium values is
outperformed by other models, it still achieves a good f1 score percentage. By taking a
look on the performance results of the neural network and compare it to the other models
performance, we can conclude that the neural network has a more balanced predictive
ability as it is able to have good predicting ability for all three classes.
Overall we can conclude that the Gradient Boosting multiclass classifier, the Lasso clasВӯ
sifier and the Neural Network that have been used in this project in combination with the
dataset that has been constructed for the purpose of the research can yield reasonable
performance results. The tuned Gradient Boosting classifier underlines the importance
of hyperparameter tuning as it helps the models to achieve better results. Nonetheless,
even though the models have achieved good results, as that the most importance finding
of the research is considered to be the importance of the features PrgP and PrgR (proВӯ
gressive passes and progressive runs) in the success of the tuned GB. These two features
combined consist of more than 15% of the most important features for the model. FurtherВӯ
more, we can see that the success of the Gradient Boosting model highly relies in features
that introduce the expected goalвҖҷs logic. With that saying, we can state that using data
with the expected goalвҖҷs logic to train machine learning models, can lead to good model
performance results.
