Abstract
This project aims, to integrate sustainability in pre-phase in the planning of districts,
creating a linkage and integration of diﬀerent ﬁelds.
This is increasing the level of
information used when formulating strategies and master plans. The aim is to create a
tool, that can map the renovation potential for each building. It must therefor be able
to handle indicators, that may be conﬂicting, and output the distance to an ideal best
performing building. Along with this, the aim is to seem how much information can be
derived from public records, and data in social housing associations.
The method of Multi-Criteria Decision Making with a focus on Technique of Order
Preference Similarity to the Ideal Solution (TOPSIS) is explored and a novel approach
is taken. Understanding which indicators to include, and how buildings and districts can
already be assessed in relation to sustainability today, was studied. The methods already
existing falls short on the pre-phase implementation due to complexity in indicator
assessments. It was found that indicators representing all three branches of sustainable
must be included.
Through the study, it was found, that the developed tool “Potentials for Renovation with
Indicators for Sustainability with use of TOPSIS” (PRIS-TOPSIS) provides a plausible
method for investigating the renovation potentials and formulate strategies based on it.

Resume på dansk
Projektet, Kortlægning af bæredygtige renoveringspotentialer i byplanlægning i de indle-
dende faser, har til formål at integrere bæredygtighed i de tidlige faser af byplanlægning
for at skabe en grænseﬂade og integration af forskellige fagligheder. Det øger informa-
tionsniveauet tilgængeligt, når udviklingsstrategier og masterplaner formuleres. Målet
er at udvikle et værktøj, der kan kortlægge renoveringspotentialerne for hver enkelt
bygning. Det er derfor nødvendigt, at det kan håndtere indikatorer, der kan være mod-
sættende, og returnere længden til den ideelle bedst præsterende bygning. Yderligere,
søger projektet, at undersøge, hvor meget information, der kan anvendes fra oﬀentlige
databaser og data hos almene boligselskaber.
Metoden Multi-Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) med fokus på Technique of Order
Preference Similarity to the Ideal Solution (TOPSIS) er udforsket og en ny tilgang bliver
taget. Det er undersøgt, hvilke indikatorer, der bør inkluderes, og hvordan bygninger og
områder vurderes i dag i relation til bæredygtighed. De allerede eksisterende metoder
kommer til kort ved implementering i de indledende faser på grund af kompleksiteten i
indikatorvurderingerne. Indikatorerne skal repræsentere alle grene af bæredygtighed.
Gennem studiet, kan det konkluderes, at “Potentials for Renovation with Indicators for
Sustainability with use of TOPSIS” (PRIS-TOPSIS) giver en plausibel metode til at
undersøge renoveringspotentialer og formulere strategier på baggrund heraf.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
In urban planning, many diﬀerent goals, ambitions and strategies are formulated in
municipal plans, national regulations and standards. They relate to quality of life, so-
cioeconomic challenges, urban growth, crime, traﬃc, energy consumption, environmental
sustainability and many more. Urban planning deals with both new and existing neigh-
borhoods. When looking at an area with already existing building stock, the question
of whether to demolish, build new, renovate or transform arise. The buildings can be of
cultural importance, the technical condition may be outdated and the energy use may
be high.
Sustainable buildings and cities, as well as sustainability within architecture and the
building industry, is a widely debated topic. 40% of the CO2 emissions are related to
the building sector.[1][2] Often the goal is to reduce the carbon footprint and the energy
consumption of the new building, but sustainability is much more than that. Sustain-
ability is often divided into three subcategories: environmental, social and economic
sustainability, and all must be taken into consideration.
The motivation behind this study is to help facilitate early stage integration of sustain-
able renovations in the pre-phase of urban planning.
1.2
Case Area
A premise of the study is a case study of the social housing area Tingbjerg. Approxi-
mately 20% of housing in Denmark is social housing, and the majority was build between
1950-1980.[3][4] The social housing is an interesting case, as many areas selected for devel-
opment includes social housing, and many social housing areas are set to be renovated
within the near future.[5] Using a case gives the opportunity to study available data
and to test the developed method. This means, that the project is focused at housing
associations in the urban or near urban context.
1.3
Objectives
The main objective is to develop a method applicable in the early district planning
to outline sustainable renovation potentials through mapping of indicators and ﬁnally
formulate development strategies based on these.

Speciﬁcally, to establish an interdisciplinary linkage through the use of MCDM method
TOPSIS (Technique of Order Preference Similarity to the Ideal Solution) in order to

• Is it possible to formulate renovation potentials based on the analysis?
• Can the potentials help formulate strategies?
2. How does the diﬀerent sustainability groups inﬂuence the renovation potentials?
3. How far can you get with the information available from social housing corporation
and public databases?
4. Is the necessary data available?

Background
This chapter brieﬂy introduces the process of urban development and design process,
followed by an introduction to Multi-Criteria Decision Making including speciﬁcation
and example of TOPSIS. It then introduces how sustainability can be deﬁned and how
it is assessed today with standard framework, tools and examples of certiﬁcations on
both building and neighborhood level. The study is performed in order to investigate
relevant information for the development of an appropriate tool.
2.1
Urban Development and Design Process
This project aims at deﬁning a new tool, that should be used in the early stages of
renovation projects and urban planning. It is therefore relevant to brieﬂy look at the
typical process of urban planning and renovation cases. Typically, in urban planning in
Denmark, land or urban area is selected for development or renewal based on the growth
of the city, socioeconomic challenges, infrastructure projects, new needs and change in
industrial areas or other, and are mostly selected by legislators or municipalities.
In Denmark, a number of areas are selected for development based on socioeconomic
challenges such as the level of marginalization. An area is deﬁned as marginalized if two
out of four criteria are met. The criteria deals with; 1) detachment from the job market
or education, 2) crime, 3) high amount of residents with only a primary education, and
4) income level.[6]
Furthermore, the marginalized urban area is deﬁned as a ghetto, if more than 50% of the
residents have non-Western backgrounds, and as a “hard-ghetto” if the area has been
a ghetto for the past four years. For areas deﬁned as a “hard ghetto”, it is mandatory
to reduce the number of social housing to 40%.[7] This is part of the legislation passed
in the eﬀort to eliminate parallel societies.[8][6] Each year in December, the so called
ghetto list becomes public.
The topic of ghettos and hard ghettos are widely debated in the public by politicians,
housing associations and other stakeholders. It is relevant for this project, as it inﬂu-
ences the case area Tingbjerg, since it is part of the ghetto list as a hard ghetto where
development must happen. Furthermore, the legislation sets some boundaries that can
be relevant for investigations and indicators. However, this project does not go into
further detail of the politics related to the ghetto plans.
After an area is selected for development, whether based on socioeconomic parameters,
decommissioning of industry or other, a master plan is derived as a framework often

including which buildings to renovate or demolish, and where to build new, along with
a vision for the area. When the master plan is passed and implemented into legislative
plans, the buildings are detailed and built. Public participation is integrated into the
process. Often a public meeting is held before the master plan is formulated, in order to
include the locals in the decision making. Before any legislative local plans are enacted,
a public hearing process is carried out, where everyone can formulate a consultation
response.
Note that the planning process is subject to change from municipality to
municipality and from case to case.
Nielsen presents the phase involved in a renovation case as seen in Figure 2.1. Here,
it is important to notice the pre-phase, as this study focuses on this phase. The pre-
phase consist of setting the scope for the project and performing building diagnostics
afterwards, leading into the design phase.[9] The focus of the PhD Early Stage Decision
Support for Sustainable Building Renovation. by Gade (formerly called Nielsen) was
the development of REDIS, a decision support tool, and is a value-based group decision
support tool, to support the weighting process and help prioritize which buildings to
renovate within a building portfolio.[10] The aim of this study aligns with the PhD,
but the criteria setup and the how to prioritize and map the results of the building
diagnostics and investigations diﬀer as they are of structural and economic character.
2.1.1
New Initiatives
New initiatives have been formulated to address the rise of awareness – and interest –
in environmental sustainability. The awareness on climate change and the eco-system is
part of the developments and with the passing of the Danish Climate Act in December
2019, this is merely highlighted further.[11][12] Sustainability, what it is, and how it is
assessed, will be presented in Section 2.2 and 2.4. Many initiatives are formulated in
order to facilitate an increase in sustainable cities and buildings. Among these, on a
building scale, are urban mining, upcycling, transformation, reuse and recycling. These
initiatives are brieﬂy deﬁned in this section.
2.1.1.1
Urban mining potential
The idea of urban mining is often associated with harvesting metals from Waste Electrical
and Electronic Equipment (WEEE). However, due to the increased focus on sustainabil-
ity and material management the concept of urban mining has spread to the building

2.3
Multiple Criteria Decision Making
In order to handle complex matters, with inﬂuences from many ﬁelds, that may conﬂict
with each other, the methods of Multiple Criteria Decision Making (MCDM) can be
useful.
MCDM is a set of methods used to evaluate possible solutions based on their performance
in a range of criteria. The criteria can often be of conﬂicting nature. The solutions are
generally ranked on overall performance across the criteria. When assessing with MCDM,
it is necessary to establish a set of criteria and determine how they are evaluated and
weighted, and then rank them based on their performance in each criteria.[17][18] A well
known example of this is; cost being one criteria and quality being another, hence telling
a story of “most value for money”.
A range of diﬀerent methods exist and are presented by Papathanasiou and Ploskas[18].
All of these methods are standardized and general applicable. This is in contrast to other
methods of evaluation that can be considered MCDM, since they are applied speciﬁcally.
Examples of these are SAVE, DGNB and SCI which will be covered later in Section 2.4.

SAVE
The cultural and architectural value of the buildings are of great importance

development plans. The municipality is responsible for the survey, and the

speciality in building renovation.[24]

disposal. For single building materials on the market, Environmental Product

An LCA can serve as tool to compare the environmental impact from diﬀerent
possible scenarios such as a facade of wood versus steel.

Discussion
As presented, a number of approaches exist to utilize the LCA.
Should the building materials already used be included into the LCA? And is it even
relevant to perform an LCA of the existing building materials in the building? How can
the information better anything and change anything? Or can it cause fallacy? Using
wood is often considered an environmental friendly material as the production of the
uses less energy and emits less CO2 (the wood accumulate CO2 while growing) than
concrete production, but a lot more CO2 is emitted at the end-of-life stage as the CO2
accumulated is realeased. A brick and concrete building have no accumulated CO2 and
is therefore more “easely” disposed. On the other hand, the production of the concrete
have already emitted the CO2 and the CO2 can therefore be seen as already invested.
Furthermore, the lifetime is also very essential and this is longer for brick and concrete –
especially if maintained properly. Nonetheless, making a material inventory will beneﬁt
the understanding of the urban mining potential. This way the existing building mass
is seen as a resource with embodied energy and CO2.

Within social sustainability, the framework for buildings in the European Standard are

3
Approach
The investigations in Chapter 2 presents drawbacks and beneﬁts of diﬀerent approaches
and tools. The level of data and information needed in the many existing certiﬁcations,
indexes and tools can easily be a barrier for integration into the pre-phase. This gives
rise to the development of a new approach, that addresses the pre-phase of urban de-
velopment, by mapping the potentials for renovation in the building stock integrating
social, environmental and economic sustainability.
The focus of this chapter is to develop, present and demonstrate the novel approach
“Potentials for Renovation with Indicators for Sustainability with use of TOPSIS” (PRIS-
TOPSIS). This includes the process of rethinking MCDM to handle the already existing
buildings and their current status. An important aspect of this study is to establish
a method for systematic cross analyzing of the existing building mass in order to ﬁnd
out what potentials they hold before coming up with any design and planning solutions.
Furthermore, the method should be open, changeable and scalable.
This chapter looks at the appropriate consideration that must be done for; 1) applying
MCDM on existing buildings, 2) introduction of reference scale for indicators, 3) the
selection of indicators, 4) the data available, 5) the evaluation and weighting of indicators,
and 6) the interpretation of results as potentials and strategies.
3.1
Rethinking MCDM and TOPSIS
The background of the project is to take the MCDM TOPSIS (see Section 2.3) and
rethink it and use it to generate scenarios based on the diﬀerent indicators and a pre-
ﬁxed ideal and anti-ideal situation, instead of evaluating diﬀerent scenarios. Figure 3.1
illustrates the new approach where the buildings become the scenarios investigated.
When rethinking the method, each building in itself is investigated, and hence “become”
the diﬀerent scenarios known from the normal approach. Following the approach of
MCDM in Chapter 2 and advised by Papathanasiou and Ploskas[18], the buildings are
being ranked in comparison to one another. This is a good way to handle the com-
parison between already formulated solutions. In PRIS-TOPSIS, the solutions are not
formulated and cannot be tested. If the buildings are simply compared to one-another,
the perspective is lost and it can be diﬃcult to determine whether an (homogeneous)
area is performing well or not, and therefore no conclusion to whether the area needs
renovation, transformation, demolition or a mix can be made based on it.

3.3
Renovation potential
The renovation potential is a means to understand the existing building mass and which
building perform well, i.e. which can beneﬁt from a renovation or other action. The
renovation potential expresses the potential for bettering the area and building by look-
ing the many aspects of at the building. It is an instrument for working out how the
building is performing at multiple levels and illustrating what can be improved. This
can help to better understand and ﬁnd target areas and target issues speciﬁcally. It also
informs on signiﬁcant social matters that are important, but may be diﬃcult to quan-
tify, and can be forgotten, when it all comes down to technical performance and money.
It uses systematic approach that can ensure interdisciplinary actions. The purpose of
PRIS-TOPSIS, as introduced in Section 3.1, is to map the renovation potentials in each
building of an urban area. The renovation potential is highest when the buildings are

3.4
Deriving Strategies
Looking at the renovation potentials and how each indicator performs, strategies can be
formulated. The overall resulting renovation potential gives a good indication for each
building, but the mapping of the individual indicators for each building is also useful
for targeting the strategies at the right areas. In order to do so, one can ask oneself the
following questions:
• Which indicators have the lowest and highest score?
• Are the scores within an acceptable range or is a large room for improvement?
• If some indicators are eminent to handle, should others be dealt with at the same
time giving a more strategic renovation? E.g. building edge zones at the same
time as added insulation.
• Are some building in such a state or shaped in such a way that they do not become
proﬁtable to keep?
If desirable, it is advisable to conduct the TOPSIS analysis again for the derived strategy
or strategies in order to see if the desired eﬀect is obtained. This however implies that,
the new and thought of strategy can be concertized into scores.

A mixed city is a city with a diverse demography and housing. This concerns income,
ethnic and social background, size of household, age etc. The mixed city also includes
the mix of diﬀerent functions such as daycare, schools, shops, services, oﬃces and so on.

A number of criteria for the resident composition in public housing has been determined

number of initiatives to avoid parallel communities – including a limit of 40% on the
number of family units in public housing areas deﬁned as a severe ghetto – was passed
in November 2018.[7]
Both the ownership distribution and the resident composition are relevant when measur-

Description
The indoor environment of the building has a great impact on the resident’s comfort and
health. Hence, it can be associated with social sustainability. A number of European
Standards exist for new buildings. specifying necessary ventilation, thermal environment,

the indoor climate and air quality. DGNB covers thermal comfort based on standards,
indoor air quality in form of Total Volatile Organic Compounds (VOC), visual comfort
and user control.[29] However, there is no guarantee of the state, when looking at existing
buildings as the performance will depend on age, typology and other factors.
The
materials and building techniques of the past vary and some have proven to work while
others fall short. This means, that a screening and registration of the building can prove
very useful and necessary.
The newly developed IEQ Compass operates with four categories of the indoor environ-
ment; acoustic, thermal and visual indoor environment, along with indoor air quality[64].
The idea behind the tool is to be able to assess existing buildings and how they can bene-
ﬁt from renovation.[65] The layout of the tool is similar to what is known from the energy
label. Unfortunately, this tool is not fully studied and implemented as a standard for
building registrations and legislation such as the energy label.

Energy Use
Description
The energy use of the building is a factor both in terms of economy and environmental
impact, as energy cost money and the energy production can impact the climate change
depending on the source of energy.
The motivation behind making an energy renovation is not always to reduce emissions
and save money, but to increase the comfort by reducing draft, cold surfaces, mold and
the possibility to place furniture in all corners against all walls.[77] [78].
However, these factors are included in the indicator for indoor environmental quality in
Section 4.7. Furthermore, the proﬁt and impact on the environment may be naturally
reduced in the future due to energy supply becoming more and more renewable.[79] This
argues for a lower weight of the energy use as a factor for the potentials, but for now it
is still considered proﬁtable.[79].
For new buildings, a calculation of the energy use must be made and it must comply with
the newest building regulations.[80] It is mandatory for new buildings to have an energy
label. For older buildings, the energy label is mandatory for rental properties and when
the building goes on the private housing market.[81] An energy label informs about the
theoretical energy usage of the building and is performed by an energy consultant.[82]
The use of energy in a building is used for heating and building operation, which only
includes ventilation and pumps. The energy labels can be found online, which makes it
easily accessible, hence makes it an attractive measurement for the energy use. However,
the margin of error in the energy labels produced is fairly high, but declining from year
to year for example going from 66% with no errors in 2013/14 to 77% in 2018.[83][84][85]

Climate Change
Description
As argued throughout the project, the environmental sustainability is critical. In par-
ticular, how to reduce the climate change is important.
Traditionally, the LCA, as
introduced in Chapter 2, is used when building new or using new materials in renova-
tions in order to assess the environmental impacts of diﬀerent scenarios and materials,
along with quantiﬁcation and certiﬁcation of sustainability. In relation to the renovation
potential it is relevant to study the existing building stock as well. The main reason for
this is that the building stock should be seen as a resource where already investments of
environmental impacts are made. The investments of resources, CO2 and energy should
be recognized.
Another indicator could be the percentage of lifetime left. An example in this case study
would be: 120 years - 60 years = 60 years left. Furthermore, the lifetime left is a simple
indicator easy to perform.
Finally, another possible indicator could be the distance in the LCA between the least
renovation needed to preserve the building and a new reference building.

Strategies
Based on the renovation potentials in Figure 5.16 a series of renovation strategies can
be established.
All buildings have a relative distance to the ideal situation (C∗), between 0.41 and 0.54,
and all buildings in Tingbjerg I performs better than buildings in Tingbjerg II. This
is an expected result, as Tingbjerg I is newly renovated where issues regarding indoor
environmental quality, energy use and maintenance of building was dealt with leading
to a better initial input score.
The criteria cultural heritage, perceived level of safety, mixed city and climate change
do not diﬀer from building to building, as the main inﬂuence is on neighborhood level
for all except climate change. The climate change was however expected to diﬀer more,
as the buildings in Tingbjerg I are newly renovated, increasing the materials used. The
density is generally low in the area, presenting the possibility of densiﬁcation throughout
the area. Taking a closer look at Tingbjerg II and worst performing buildings – the ones
with the greatest renovation potential – are no. 26, 8, 2, 3, 10, 11, 1 and 9. Therefore,
Tingbjerg II and in particular these buildings, are in focus along with the neighborhood
level indicators. Based on this, the following initiatives are possible, but not limited to:
• Increasing building mass with soft edges and application of new functions and
ownership in order to increase density, mixed city, edge zones and safety.
This can be obtained by adding building mass in between building, demolish and
build new or – if the structure allows it – add new mass building to existing
buildings keeping the building footprint.
• Improving general access to and from the area and opening the area to increase
mobility.
This can be obtained by creating better openings through T2-B26, T2-B8, T2-B9,
T2-B10 and T2-B11.
• Decrease energy use and increase indoor environmental quality either by renovation
or introducing new building mass.
Given the climate change, the resources invested correspond to the best reference build-
ing in DGNB, and is therefor not a hindrance to demolish, but looking at the cultural
heritage the buildings should be preserved. However, for obtaining an increase in densiﬁ-
cation and mobility, T2-B26 is quite a hurdle. Furthermore, it has a bad indoor climate
and high energy consumption, but it has great accessibility. This opens the possibility of
demolishing this building or possibly remove sections of the building, allowing for easier
access, but this will decrease the density. However, removing sections or creating better
and more visibly paths and roads through T2-B8, T2-B9, T2-B10 and T2-B11 could
prove a good option.

Discussion
The general methods used, the indicators, the urban planning integration and other
related topics are discussed in this chapter.
Overall this method is to used as an estimation and way to visualize, discuss values
that can be brought in from the beginning and continuously evaluated throughout the
process.
A beneﬁt from the developed approach is the ﬂexibility it oﬀers. The indicators can
be adapted to a various amount of available data, the weighting application can be
controlled in many diﬀerent ways and certiﬁcation goals can be incorporated However,
the approach also gives rise to the paradox; the level of information and detail early
in the planning process is needed to estimate quite technical things, which is normally
done later in the process. In order to make informed and qualiﬁed decisions the data
must be suﬃcient, technical and of a decent quality. BBR is expected to be of a high
quality in the early stages, but the information in BBR also relies on the owner of
the building, reducing the quality. Furthermore, discrepancy between BBR and Energy
Label was found, along with discrepancy within BBR e.g. the total building area was
lower than the total dwelling area in building 7 in Tingbjerg I. The margin of error for
the Energy Label was presented and discussed in Section 4.10. However, on an overall
scale, a large number of data is available and is quite reliable. And, as projects often are
informed from a more or less random cluster of information depending on the developer,
the systematization of data creating an interdisciplinary transparent platform is very
beneﬁcial. The transparency in the approach is obtained by letting the mathematics be
open and freely present the weighting. Hence, the PRIS-TOPSIS approach is not about
the indicators, but rather the technique, the systematization and transparency, which
also allows for the approach to be scalable and adaptive in relation to the number of
buildings, the number of criteria and to shifting goals as society changes and diﬀer from
country to country.
A barrier is the systematic integration of the data and the process of going through all
the data available. Implementation of Building Information Modelling (BIM) and GIS
will ease this and allow for better integration and possibly more and better indicator
analysis. Many of the included indicators could have been assessed in a more beneﬁcial
way given more time and better data. Furthermore, the ﬁnal strategy might be analyzed
to see if the desired eﬀect is achieved. Creating BIM in the early stages and pre-phases
is not unheard of and the model can gradually be updated and used throughout the
project. This all means that the PRIS-TOPSIS tool can be more eﬃcient.
The focus through the study was the renovation potential, but leaves out the transfor-

Conclusion
Through a novel approach to TOPSIS and MCDM which analyzes the existing build-
ing mass along with a best and worst performance building, it is possible to map the
renovation potentials and inform the decision maker in the early stages of urban plan-
ning. The renovation potentials along with the weighted indicator scores can be used
to formulate development strategies. This is done by looking at 1) how far from the
ideal best performance the individual building is in order to determine if it will beneﬁt
from a renovation and 2) the indicators contributing the most to ﬁnd out what eﬀorts
to implement in the renovation.
The three sustainability groups (environmental, social and economical) all have an in-
ﬂuence on the ﬁnal result. In the case study, the classic renovation indicators such as
energy use, indoor environmental quality and integrity of structures are of great impor-
tance to the individual building, but the indicators mobility, edge zones and mixed city
was essential for the renovation potential and in the formulation of strategies in this
case. This highlights the statement, that sustainable solutions should be found when
integrating both environmental, economical and social aspects.
The data available through public databases and social housing corporation goes a long
way for many analysis, but additional data analyze was necessary. Furthermore, the
available data was scattered across many diﬀerent sources, programs and formats that
was time consuming to manually overcome. This is a barrier for further implementation.
The PRIS-TOPSIS approach can be used as a communicative tool to integrate decision
making in the pre-phases of urban planning and establish an interdisciplinary linkage.
PRIS-TOPSIS can be used to understand which buildings have a large renovation po-
tential and for which parameters the performance can be improved. Furthermore, the
user can apply weighting to certain criteria and a large number of stakeholders and
experts have a shared platform for knowledge. It seems plausible, that PRIS-TOPSIS
can be used to visualize and debate interdisciplinary linkage and assessments, in the
pre-phase in the planning of an area selected for development, on an informed basis
with transparency, scalability and ﬂexibility.

Recommendations for further
investigations
A main drawback of the approach is the data gathering and analysis, that can be barrier
for further implementation into the circular economy. A more overall goal for further
study is the beneﬁt of a publicly owned record or expansion of BBR containing the urban
stock. This could be beneﬁcial in helping developers, politicians, designers, architects
and engineers to more easily map the materials in building Inspiration can be drawn
from the Energy Label and the calculations and programs behind them as most of the
information is contained in them.
For the PRIS-TOPSIS tool the following investigation are of interest:
• Integrating the urban mining potential in the approach shows great potential, but
also a further investigation of the reuse potential could be interesting, as it is
a resulting factor of the derived strategies. Perhaps by zooming in on buildings
deemed to be demolished and renovated in order to map the reuse potential.
• Implement LCC into the case and investigate the eﬀect.
• Investigate how PRIS-TOPSIS can handle buildings that might not have some of
the indicators such as garages and energy use.
• BIM implementation or programming in Rhino/Grasshopper or GIS
• Investigate the best practice for weighting process and implement in to the tool.
• Evaluate renovation strategies in order to see if the desired goal is obtained.
• Further study to expand the process to former industrial buildings and include
transformation potentials.
