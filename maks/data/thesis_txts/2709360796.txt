Abstract 
Anaerobic digestion is an established waste management technology, which produces renewable 
energy in the form of biogas. This study aims to quantify the environmental impacts of treating 
and utilising biogas in Denmark. 17 scenarios are investigated, including biogas combustion, 
biogas upgrading into biomethane, as well as carbon capture, utilisation, and storage (CCUS) 
options for the CO2 fraction. Life cycle assessment (LCA) is used to evaluate the scenarios and 
the results are subjected to sensitivity-, uncertainty- and energy system analysis. The 
interpretation of the results focuses on the global warming (GW) impacts and shortly discusses 
other impact categories. 
The values and conclusions presented in the following paragraph are based on the GW impacts 
of the investigated scenarios in the baseline energy system, corresponding approx. to the energy 
system of 2020. However, processes which are identical in all scenarios are excluded from the 
modelling, as per LCA standards. Overall, the investigated scenarios are heavily influenced by 
the energy displacement and the CCUS impacts. The CCUS scenarios generally have the largest 
benefit in the GW impact category, as they contribute with a saving ranging from 1300-2100 kg 
CO2-eq./1000 Nm3 biogas treated. Overall, the methanation scenarios have the largest 
environmental benefits. Both combustion and biogas upgrading scenarios are amended with 
carbon capture and storage (CCS), resulting in substantial savings, which are robust across the 
changing energy systems. The biogas upgrading scenarios without CCUS also show a 
substantial benefit to the system, mainly due to the displacement of natural gas. The combustion 
scenarios show considerably less environmental benefits, despite the amendment with CCS, and 
they are associated with substantially higher uncertainties than the remaining scenarios.  
The net results of the remaining environmental impact categories are briefly assessed. Despite 
substantial internal differences, the carbon capture and utilisation (CCU) scenarios demonstrate 
the overall largest environmental benefits based on the ranking of the scenarios. The biogas 
upgrading scenarios without CCUS generally follow the CCU scenarios and outperforms the 
CCS scenarios in several of the environmental impact categories, contrary to the tendency of the 
GW impact category. The combustion scenarios generally perform worse than the remaining 
investigated scenarios, posing a relatively large burden to several impact categories.  
Issues related to the investigated technologies, biomass competition and the role of biogenic 
CO2 in the future are discussed, underlining the importance of eliminating energy crops from 
the biogas feedstock, and not using the deployment of CCUS as an excuse to maintain 
unsustainable habits. The following main recommendations are given: 1) Upgrading biogas to 
biomethane has environmental benefits. 2) Utilising the CO2 fraction from the biogas upgrading 
process has additional environmental benefits, but also trade-offs. 3) Methanation appears to be 
the most environmentally beneficial biogas treatment, from the assessed technologies. However, 
it is very dependent on the energy system in which it operates. 
A simplified calculation for the potential of CCS amended on 50% of the Danish biomethane 
capacity results in a GW saving of approximately 0.3 million tons CO2-eq. yearly, which 
constitutes a large contribution to the Danish Government’s goal of achieving a CCUS saving of 
0.4 million tons CO2-eq. yearly in 2025. Despite the scattered nature of biomethane plants as a 
CO2 source, they still demonstrate substantial potential for CCUS in Denmark, as the CO2 is 
biogenic and already captured in the biogas upgrading process. Utilising the CO2 fraction may 
provide substantial environmental benefits and can contribute to the mitigation of dangerous 
climate change.

Sammenfatning 
Anaerob nedbrydning er en veletableret affaldsbehandlingsform, der producerer vedvarende 
energi i form af biogas. Formålet med denne opgave er at kvantificere de miljømæssige 
konsekvenser af at behandle og bruge biogas i Danmark. Dette undersøges vha. 17 scenarier, 
der indebærer forbrænding af biogas, opgradering af biogas til bionaturgas, såvel som CO2 
fangst, anvendelse og lagring (carbon capture, utilisation, and storage (CCUS)) af CO2-andelen 
af biogas. Livcyklusanalyse (LCA) bruges til at vurdere scenarierne, og resultaterne underkastes 
sensitivitets-, usikkerheds- og energisystemanalyse. Fortolkningen af resultaterne fokuserer på 
miljøpåvirkningskategorien global opvarmning, men vurderer også kort resultaterne i andre 
kategorier. 
De konklusioner og værdier, der præsenteres i det følgende tekstafsnit, er baseret på 
miljøpåvirkningskategorien global opvarmning og grundenergisystemet, som omtrent svarer til 
år 2020. De processer der er identiske i alle scenarier, er dog udeladt, i henhold til LCA-
standarder. Generelt er det energisubstitutionen og påvirkninger fra CCUS, der bidrager mest til 
resultaterne. Samlet set resulterer CCUS scenarierne i de største miljømæssige fordele, da de 
medfører en besparelse på ca. 1300-2100 kg CO2-ækvivalenter/1000 Nm3 biogas behandlet. 
Både biogasforbrændings- og opgraderingsscenarierne kombineres med CO2 fangst og lagring 
(carbon capture and storage (CCS)), hvilket giver en miljømæssig besparelse som er robust i 
samtlige energisystemer. Biogasopgraderingsscenarierne uden CCUS resulterer også i en 
betragtelig miljømæssig gevinst, hovedsageligt pga. substitution af naturgas. 
Biogasforbrændingsscenarierne udviser færre miljømæssige fordele, og derudover er de 
forbundet med betydeligt større usikkerheder, sammenlignet med de resterende scenarier. 
Nettoresultaterne af de resterende miljøpåvirkningskategorier vurderes også kortfattet. På trods 
af betydelige interne forskelle, udviser scenarierne med CO2 fangst og anvendelse (carbon 
capture and utilisation (CCU)) samlet set de største miljømæssige gevinster, baseret på en 
rangering af scenarierne. Især metanationsscenarierne præsterer fordelagtigt. 
Opgraderingsscenarierne uden CCUS rangerer efter CCU scenarierne, og overgår CCS 
scenarierne i flere miljøpåvirknings-kategorier, modsat tendensen set i miljøpåvirknings-
kategorien global opvarmning. Generelt set præsterer forbrændingsscenarierne dårligere end de 
resterende scenarier, og de udgør endda en byrde i flere miljøpåvirkningskategorier. 
Problemstillinger angående de undersøgte teknologier, og konkurrence mellem forskellig brug 
af biomasse diskuteres, og derved betones vigtigheden i at eliminere energiafgrøder fra produk-
tionen af biogas, samt ikke at bruge CCUS som en undskyldning for at bibeholde nuværende 
miljøskadelige vaner. De følgende anbefalinger gives: 1) Opgradering af biogas til bionaturgas 
har miljømæssige fordele. 2) Anvendelse af CO2-fraktionen til CCUS har yderligere miljø-
mæssige fordele, men også belastninger. 3) Metanation synes at være den mest fordelagtige 
behandling, men processen er også stærkt afhængig af elektricitetsinputtets miljøpåvirkninger. 
En forenklet estimering af potentialet ved at udstyre 50% af den danske bionaturgaskapacitet 
med CCS viser en klimabesparelse på ca. 0,3 mio. ton CO2-ækv. årligt, hvilket udgør et stort 
bidrag til Folketingets mål om at spare 0,4 mio. ton CO2-ækv. årligt i 2025 vha. CCUS. På trods 
af at bionaturgasanlæg udgør en spredt og relativ lille kilde til CO2, udviser de stadig potentiale 
for at indgå i CCUS projekter i Danmark. Dette er især grundet CO2-gassens biogene status, 
samt at den allerede er indfanget, som en del af biogasopgraderingen. Anvendelse af CO2 
fraktionen fra biogas i CCUS-processer kan levere betragtelige miljømæssige fordele, og kan 
dermed bidrage til at lempe de negative påvirkninger fra klimaforandringer.

1  Introduction 
Biogas production is a mature technology, providing both treatment of organic waste and 
renewable energy production through anaerobic digestion (AD) of organic materials (Al Seadi 
et al., 2013). The production and use of biogas can contribute to the transition from fossil to 
renewable energy, which is a key form of mitigation against climate change (Intergovernmental 
Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) 2018). According to Biogas Danmark (2020), upgrading 
biogas to biomethane has additional benefits e.g. decoupling of gas production and consumption 
and defossilisation of the heavy energy uses, which currently rely on natural gas (NG). Further, 
injecting biomethane into the NG grid provides existing infrastructure for energy storage and 
distribution. The biogenic CO2 stream from biogas upgrading is commonly considered a waste 
product and emitted to the environment (Sun et al., 2015), but could instead be utilised or 
stored. 
Carbon capture and storage (CCS) is the removal of carbon from the carbon cycle over long 
time scales by capturing and storing it. The carbon is commonly captured at large point sources, 
e.g., power plants, or from the atmosphere, and it is typically stored in suitable geological 
formations deep in the underground (Pour, Webley and Cook, 2017). CCS is considered a key 
technology for reaching the goals of the Paris agreement (European Biofuels Technology 
Platform, 2012; Pour, Webley and Cook, 2018b). Bioenergy with carbon capture and storage 
(BECCS) is a specification of CCS, where the carbon is captured from bioenergy processes, e.g. 
biogas or ethanol production, and therefore the carbon is biogenic of nature (Kemper, 2015). 
Consequently, BECCS establishes a net negative carbon flow from the atmosphere to the 
underground (Kemper, 2015; Pour, Webley and Cook, 2018b). 
Carbon capture and utilisation (CCU) is similar to CCS, but instead of storing the carbon, it is 
utilised industrially e.g., as a basis for fuel- or chemical production, or for direct utilisation. 
CCU offers a way to “recycle” the carbon and may offer substantial climate benefits, depending 
on what it substitutes and the energy system considered (Christensen and Bisinella, 2021).  
Power to gas (PtG) is an umbrella term, covering the conversion and storage of energy in 
gasses, such as H2 or CH4 (Thema et al., 2019). Mainly renewable energy is used to electrolyse 
water into oxygen and hydrogen, thus storing part of the energy in the gases. The hydrogen can 
be used as an energy carrier itself, or as a feedstock to produce other fuels or chemicals. A 
common route is the methanation process, where H2 reacts with CO2 to form CH4, often called 
synthetic natural gas (SNG), with the same benefits as biomethane (Parra et al., 2017; Thema et 
al., 2019). Methanation also offers a chance to partly dissociate the production of methane from 
biomass availability (Angelidaki et al., 2018).  
1.1 Biogas and biomethane in Denmark 
Denmark had 141 biogas plants in 2019, producing 1842 GWh biogas (European Biogas 
Association, 2021). In November 2020, 48 of the biogas plants upgraded their biogas 
production to biomethane, permitting injection into the NG grid. The Danish biogas upgrading 
plants utilise three upgrading technologies: amine scrubbing, water scrubbing and membrane 
permeation (Dansk Gasteknisk Center, 2020). Approximately 21% of the gas in the Danish NG 
grid was biomethane at the end of 2020 (Kvist, 2021), and this value is expected to rise to at 
least 30-40% in 2040 (Hansen, 2020). However, the potential is substantially higher if more of 
the Danish biogas plants amend their production to include biogas upgrading (Hansen, 2020). 
According to Energinet (2020a), the entire Danish gas use will be covered by so-called green 
gases, which encompasses biomethane, SNG, hydrogen etc. in 2040.

Only one of the biomethane producing plants in Denmark is currently utilizing part of the 
captured CO2 for food production (Nielsen, 2021), so the potential for CCUS in combination 
with biomethane production is nearly unexploited in Denmark. 
1.2 Carbon storage potential in Denmark 
According to Wendorff (no date), the Danish underground can contain approximately 22 Gt 
CO2, corresponding to approximately 500 years of the current Danish CO2 emissions. Further, 
according to Anthonsen et al. (2013), the CO2 storage capacity for Denmark and Norway is 212 
Gt, primarily in offshore basins. Therefore, there appears to be ample potential for CCS in 
Denmark. For more details on CCS in Denmark, see Appendix F.13. 
2 Review 
The basis for this assessment is the treatment and upgrading of biogas, which is investigated in 
several papers: Hernø (2020) provides a survey of the entire Danish value chain from biomass 
to biomethane including energy and mass balances for amine scrubbers, water scrubbers and 
membrane permeation plants in different sizes. Further, environmental and economical 
optimisation potentials are identified. Lombardi and Francini (2020) perform a LCA and life 
cycle costing of biogas upgrading into biomethane using five different biogas upgrading 
technologies (water scrubbing, amine scrubbing, potassium carbonate scrubbing, pressure swing 
adsorption, and membrane permeation). All the assessed upgrading technologies proved to have 
environmental benefits, but amine scrubbing was the overall most beneficial, mainly due to low 
CH4 losses.  
The CCUS is also an important part of this assessment, and it is a subject of intense research. 
Several papers focus on carbon capture (CC) at fossil or partly fossil CO2 sources: Bisinella et 
al. (2021a) perform a consequential LCA of amending municipal solid waste incineration 
(MSWI) with amine-based CCS, focussing on changes in energy production and global 
warming (GW) impacts. The amendment with CCS resulted in substantial reductions in the GW 
impact category but at the expense of other impact categories and the energy output of the 
MSWI. Christensen and Bisinella (2021) perform a consequential LCA, focusing on GW 
impacts of a range of CCU processes with carbon captured from MSWI, including direct 
utilisation of CO2 and production of selected fuels and feedstock chemicals. The paper 
concluded that CCU substantially reduces the energy recovery of the MSWI, but also that the 
CCU processes lowered the overall GW impacts. Furthermore, the transportation of CO2 is a 
challenging aspect of the CCS value chain. Rambøll (2020) investigates Danish MSWI 
amended with CCS, looking at the value chain from transportation to injection, including 
different transportation options for the captured CO2. The paper also investigates the economic 
cost of CCS both in the short and long term, and it concludes that the technology choices and 
price of CCS is highly dependent on the specific CO2 storage site.  
Some PtG processes are not yet used on a commercial scale, and therefore experimental data is 
important in the assessment of their environmental potential. Lardon et al. (2018) provide 
experimental data based on a pilot-scale biological methanation facility, as well as a technical 
and economic analysis of the biological methanation process. Likewise, for the catalytic 
methanation process, Haldor Topsøe (2018) provide experimental results of a (small) full-scale 
catalytic methanation plant, including an energy balance of the plant, and up-scaling to a large 
facility. Both are used for the life cycle inventories (LCIs) of the respective methanation 
processes.

This study also attempts to put the CCUS of biogenic CO2 into a larger sustainability context 
and this subject is discussed thoroughly in Kemper (2015). The paper provides a review of 
BECCS opportunities; analysing feedstocks, technologies, and challenges; and provides a global 
status of current projects and potentials. The paper also discusses several key aspects regarding 
the sustainability of BECCS, amongst others the competition for bioresources, as well as the 
environmental, financial, and social aspects of bioresource management. The results include 
several recommendations regarding the sustainability of bioresources, the implementation of 
carbon taxes, and the importance of not relying on BECCS and disregarding other mitigation 
strategies. Likewise, Pour et al. (2017) holistically examines the impacts of a large-scale 
application of BECCS on the three pillars of sustainability (environmental, social and 
economic), as well as its potential to permanently store CO2 emissions. 
This study carefully models the treatment of biogas and biomethane via life cycle assessment 
(LCA) and includes usage of the CO2-fraction within CCS, methanation and direct utilisation of 
CO2, thus quantifying their environmental impacts. 
3 Materials and method 
3.1 LCA 
LCA is a standardized methodology for the assessment of the environmental impacts of goods 
or services. The assessment is based on quantification of emissions and resources consumed 
during the product’s lifetime, alternatively within the temporal scope of the assessment. A full 
LCA usually covers impacts on the natural environment, human health, and resource depletion, 
but the exact impact categories vary with the life cycle impact assessment (LCIA) methodology. 
LCA consists of four main phases, which are highly interdependent and form the iterative nature 
of the LCA, see Figure 1. The first phase is the goal and scope definition: the goal definition is 
the basis of the LCA and defines among other things the study’s application, limitations, 
decision context, target audience and influential actors (European Commission and Joint 
Research Center, 2010). The scope definition identifies the product or system under 
investigation and i.a. defines and describes the functional unit (FU), physical and temporal 
system boundaries, data requirements and modelling choices. The LCI contains the data 
collection and modelling and must comply with the goal and scope definition. There are two 
main types of LCA modelling: attributional and consequential, and the modelling choices must 
be applied consistently. The LCI is used as input to the third phase: the LCIA, where the LCI is 
converted to impact category results, commonly using LCA software (European Commission 
and Joint Research Center, 2010). Finally, the fourth phase analyses and interprets the LCIA 
results, by placing them in a larger context. Often the interpretation phase consists of a 
contribution analysis, as well as sensitivity- and uncertainty analysis in addition to a discussion 
of the results (European Commission and Joint Research Center, 2010).  
LCA is a tool commonly used to assess environmental impacts, but it is also criticised due to 
lack of transparency in data and assumptions, resulting in difficulties in the comparison of 
different LCAs (Corsten et al., 2013). These difficulties arise because the results of LCAs are 
very dependent on the modelling choices, such as differences in system boundaries, 
assumptions, models etc., thus making LCAs difficult to compare across different studies 
(Terlouw et al., 2021).

3.2 Goal and Scope definition 
This LCA study aims to assess and quantify the environmental impacts of several treatment and 
utilisation options for biogas in Denmark, and the report is intended for decision support 
regarding this subject. The driver of this assessment is the need to systematically gather 
information about the environmental impacts of biogas treatment and utilisation. 
Based on the LCA guidelines from European Commission (2010), this study adheres to decision 
context B: “meso/macro-level decision support”, as the implications of the findings, if applied, 
may impact the Danish energy system. As a result, the consequential modelling framework is 
applied. 
The object of assessment is a biogas system, specifically how the biogas is treated and utilised 
after it has been produced. Several technology configurations (TCs) are compared, including 
biogas combustion with heat and/or electricity recovery, three types of upgrading technologies 
and two types of methanation, in combination with and without CCS, resulting in 12 main TCs 
and 5 variations. A holistic modelling approach is used, meaning the investigated TCs do not 
cover existing plants, but rather give a broader overview of the technologies. See Table 1 for a 
summary of the key points of the investigated TCs.

The FU used in this assessment is the utilisation and treatment of 1000 Nm3 biogas, consisting

The system boundaries for the investigated TCs are presented in Figure 2, and more detailed 
system boundary figures are placed in Appendix A. The assessment starts with 1000 Nm3 
biogas and ends with the substitution of the produced energy carrier. The LCIA methodology 
used is the Environmental Footprint 3.0 (EF3.0) methodology with long term impacts (Fazio et 
al., 2018), and special attention is placed on GW impacts.  
A comparative process based LCA of 17 TCs with various treatments and utilisations of biogas 
is performed. This assessment is done using the consequential modelling framework, and 
accordingly, marginal processes are used for the background modelling. Furthermore, 
multifunctionalities are handled using system expansion.  
The economic and social impacts of the TCs are not investigated in this study. Further, a cut-off 
criterion of 20 kg CO2-eq. is applied throughout the modelling. Thus, through the iterative 
nature of LCA, the improvement of data collection is stopped if the change related to the 
parameter in question had an impact of less than 20 kg CO2-eq. Further, the storage process of 
the CCS modelling is limited to only impact the GW impact category, as no coherent data is 
found for the remaining impact categories. Several processes of the TCs are identical and are 
therefore excluded from the modelling, in line with recommendations from the International 
Reference Life Cycle Data System (European Commission and Joint Research Center, 2010). 
The excluded processes are: 
• 
Biomass production, collection, transportation, and pre-treatment. 
• 
AD production, including the initial desulphurisation with iron compounds in the AD 
reactor. 
• 
Digestate transportation, utilisation, and the resulting displacement of mineral fertiliser. 
• 
Construction and maintenance of equipment, infrastructure, and plants. 
However, all other gas cleaning, upgrading, treatment and utilisation processes are included 
within the system boundaries, as well as the products displaced by the modelled energy carriers.  
The technological maturity of the investigated technologies is generally current and commercial, 
apart from the solid oxide electrolysis cell (SOEC) process and the biomethanation, which is 
currently not used on a commercial scale. The geographical scope of the assessment is 
Denmark, but when available the ‘market for’ processes in Ecoinvent are used to reflect the 
international market. The temporal scope of this assessment is aimed at the near future: 2020-
2040, but it is based on literature data roughly from 2010-2020. Further, the technologies 
investigated may have a lifetime of 20-30 years, making the impacts more long-term. The 
modelled systems have capacities and efficiencies corresponding to approximately 2020, but the 
efficiency of several of the investigated technologies is expected to become higher during the 
temporal scope of this study. However, that development is outside the scope of the modelling. 
Furthermore, this is a dynamic field, meaning unforeseen, large-scale changes may happen 
quickly, e.g., concerning the energy system.

3.3.1 Biogenic and fossil carbon 
Emitted biogenic CO2 is assumed to have a neutral climate impact and to count negatively when 
it is permanently stored. Fossil CO2 is considered a load to the carbon balance when it is emitted 
to the atmosphere. The biogenic CO2 in CH4 is counted as the GW impact of CH4 subtracted 
2.75, corresponding to the relation between the molar masses of CO2 and CH4. Thus considering 
the biogenic nature of the CO2 resulting from the oxidation of biogenic CH4 (Muñoz and 
Schmidt, 2016).  
3.3.2 Waste composition and AD 
The waste composition utilised is based on the average, Danish biogas feedstock from 2014-
2018 given in Wenzel et al. (2020). The AD reactor is the basis for the production of biogas, 
and it is modelled using an existing EASETECH process for a thermophilic reactor. The 
resulting biogas is assumed to consist of 55% CH4 and 45% CO2. This is a simplification, as the 
content of impurities is disregarded in the biogas composition. However, the removal of water 
vapour and H2S is accounted for in the modelling.  
==PAGE:21==

Both the waste composition and the AD process are excluded from the modelling of this 
assessment; however, they are still needed as a basis in EASETECH. For more information on 
the waste composition and the modelling of the AD, see Appendix E.2 and E.3.  
3.3.3 Infrastructure 
The building and maintenance of the various plants and related infrastructure are excluded from 
this assessment. However, the building of the infrastructure typically has a substantial impact on 
the GW impacts during a facility’s lifetime, corresponding to approximately 10% of the overall 
life cycle impacts in the GW impact category (Agostini et al., 2020).  
3.3.4 Biogas conditioning 
A range of impurities may be present in raw biogas. Typically only the H2S and water are 
targeted in the processes of respectively desulphurisation and biogas drying (Angelidaki et al., 
2018). The most commonly used desulphurisation technologies in Denmark are the direct 
addition of iron oxides and/or salts to the AD reactor, and adsorption to activated carbon 
(Allegue and Hinge, 2014; Dansk Gasteknisk Center, 2020). See Appendix F.1 for more details 
on biogas cleaning in general. 
It is assumed in all TCs that sufficient iron compounds are added in the AD digester to obtain a 
H2S content of maximum 300 ppmv, which is well within its the technological limit (Allegue 
and Hinge, 2014), see Appendix E.4 for an estimate. This initial desulphurisation is assumed to 
be identical in all TCs and is therefore excluded from the modelling. The initial desulphurisation 
is assumed sufficient for the combustion TCs (TCs 1-4), as the H2S requirements for 
combustion are relatively low (<500 ppmv H2S) (Allegue and Hinge, 2014). However, precision 
desulphurisation is needed for the biogas upgrading and the CCU TCs (TCs 5-12). In line with 
Lombardi and Francini (2020), it is assumed that the precision desulphurization is achieved by 
activated carbon, impregnated with NaOH, which removes the remaining H2S (<300 ppmv) in 
the biogas. Further, it is assumed that the energy needed for the precision desulphurization is 
negligible, as no specific data was found for this. Therefore, only the materials of the precision 
desulphurisation are modelled, including treatment of spent activated carbon. See Appendix E.4 
for more details on the modelling of the precision desulphurisation. 
The removal of water in the biogas is important, as corrosive compounds may form upon 
aqueous contact (Petersson, 2013). The drying process is modelled from a simple 
thermodynamical approximation of the energy requirement of raising the temperature of the gas 
to 100°C, plus the latent heat of water evaporation, assuming a water content of maximum 4%. 
This results in relatively low energy demands, but it is used as an approximation. See Appendix 
F.3 for more details on biogas drying, and Appendix E.5 for the modelling of the drying process 
and how the dryers are placed in the investigated TCs.  
The gasses are compressed differently depending on the technologies used and the fate of the 
relevant gasses. See Appendix E.6 for details on the modelling of the gas compressions, as well 
as the related energy demands. 
3.3.5 Biogas flaring 
Flaring is commonly used to lessen the impacts from the emitted biogas, which cannot be 
utilised directly, e.g., due to increased pollutants or equipment maintenance. The flaring mainly 
emits CO2 and CO, but also hydrogen, water and unburned fuel may result, as well as more 
toxic compounds, e.g.  aromatic and volatile organic compounds (Ismail and Umukoro, 2012).

An Ecoinvent process for the flaring of NG is used as the basis for the modelling, and it is 
adjusted from fossil to biogenic carbon emissions, to reflect the change from NG to biogas. See 
Appendix E.7 for details on the modelling of the biogas flaring. 
3.3.6 Biogas combustion 
In TC1-4 the biogas is combusted in a combined heat and power (CHP) plant, modelled by an 
existing EASETECH process, which is based on Danmarks Miljøundersøgelser; European 
Environment Agency; Nielsen et al. (2013; n.d.; 2010). The efficiency of the plant is based on 
the CH4 content of the biogas. 35% of the energy in the CH4 is converted to electricity and 53% 
is recovered as heat, while the remaining energy is lost. These efficiencies are in line with 
(Henson, 2012; Lantz, 2012). TC1 and TC2 are not credited for any heat recovery, as the heat is 
assumed used internally and otherwise vented. For general information on biogas combustion, 
see Appendix F.4 and see Appendix E.8 for details on the modelling of the biogas combustion. 
3.3.7 Amine scrubber 
The amine scrubber uses an aqueous amine solution to chemically bind the CO2 in the liquid 
phase, thus concentrating the CH4 in the biogas to biomethane quality. The rich CO2- and amine 
solution is then regenerated at high heat, releasing the CO2, and allowing for recirculation of the 
amine solution (Bauer et al., 2013; Beil and Beyrich, 2013). Amine scrubbers can reach very 
high gas purities and very low CH4 losses. For more detailed information on the amine 
scrubbing technology, see Appendix F.5.   
The LCI for the amine scrubbing process is constructed based on Hernø (2020). The amine 
scrubber has a high heat demand for the regeneration of the amine solution, and it is assumed 
that 60% of the heat is recovered (Ryhl, 2021a). The modelled amine scrubber recovers 99.2% 
of the CH4 present in the biogas, and the resulting biomethane has a 98% CH4 purity on a dry 
basis. Further, the CO2 purity of the off-gas is 99.9%. For more detailed information about the 
modelling of the amine scrubber, see Appendix E.10. 
3.3.8 Water scrubber 
The water scrubber uses water as a solvent to scrub the CO2 from the biogas in the absorption 
column, thus concentrating the CH4 to biomethane quality. The aqueous CO2 solution is 
regenerated by injecting atmospheric air into the regeneration column, thus rapidly dropping the 
pressure, and releasing the CO2 back to its gaseous form. Water scrubbers can reach high gas 
purities, but the CH4 losses are typically higher than both amine scrubbers and membrane 
permeation units. The water is recirculated, resulting in low overall water usage (Bauer et al., 
2013; Beil and Beyrich, 2013). For more details on water scrubbers in general, see Appendix 
F.6. 
The LCI for the water scrubbing process is based on Hernø (2020). However, they assumed no 
water input to the water scrubbing process. Even though nearly closed water circuits are 
possible with modern water scrubbers (Jin et al., 2021), there is likely still some losses due to 
evaporation and uncontrolled losses. A water consumption of 0.4 L/Nm3 biogas (Danish Energy 
Agency and Energinet, 2020) is assumed for this study. The modelled water scrubber recovers 
98.3% of the CH4 present in the biogas, and the resulting biomethane has a 98% CH4 purity on a 
dry basis. Further, the CO2 purity of the off-gas is 98.8%. For more details about the modelling 
of the water scrubber, see Appendix E.11.

3.3.9 Membrane permeation 
Membrane permeation utilises differences in gas permeabilities for the separation process. The 
biogas is pressurised before entering the membrane unit, which typically consists of two or three 
membranes, allowing for optimisation of the gas concentrations. The membranes retain the CH4 
and let the CO2 and other impurities pass through, thus separating the gases. Membrane 
permeation can reach high gas purity and low CH4 losses. The membrane permeation units are 
relatively easy to operate but are vulnerable to clogging (Bauer et al., 2013; Beil and Beyrich, 
2013). For more details on membrane permeation units in general, see Appendix F.7. 
The LCI for the membrane permeation process is based on Hernø (2020). The modelled 
membrane permeation unit recovers 99.1% of the CH4 present in the biogas, and the resulting 
biomethane has a 98% CH4 purity on a dry basis. Further, the CO2 purity of the off-gas is 
99.4%. For more detailed information about the modelling of the membrane permeation unit, 
see Appendix E.12. 
3.3.10 Post-combustion amine scrubbing 
TC2 and TC4 utilise a post-combustion (PC) amine scrubber for CC. The amine scrubber is 
chosen for the PC CC, as this is the most common CC technology used in this context (Bisinella 
et al., 2021a). The PC amine scrubber is modelled based on the PC amine scrubber described in 
Reiter and Lindorfer (2015) according to Christensen and Bisinella (2021). The modelled PC 
amine scrubber captures 85% of the CO2, and the remaining CO2 is vented to the atmosphere. 
See Appendix E.13 for more details on the modelling of the PC amine scrubber. 
3.3.11 Direct CO2 utilisation 
Direct utilisation of CO2 has many applications, e.g., use in greenhouses for CO2 fertilisation, 
use in the food and drink industry, use as a frozen refrigerant etc. See Appendix F.14 for general 
information and other examples of direct CO2 utilisation. 
The TCs for direct utilisation of CO2 are modelled based on the approach described in 
Christensen and Bisinella (2021), and additional information can be found there. Due to the 
large variety of direct utilisations of CO2, a simplified modelling approach is used, to gain a 
more general view of the implications of direct CO2 utilisation. The direct CO2 utilisation TCs 
are modelled based on the amine scrubbing TC because this upgrading technology has the 
largest capacity in Denmark (Dansk Gasteknisk Center, 2020), but they might as well be 
amended to other biogas upgrading technologies. The off-gas from the amine scrubber has a 
CO2 purity of 99.9%, so no further cleaning of the CO2 is assumed. However, the utilisation of 
the CO2 is very dependent on its purity, e.g. if the captured CO2 is to be used for food, it must 
comply with strict codes and regulations formulated e.g. in European Industrial Gases 
Association (2020). Three variations of the direct utilisation of CO2 (a, b, and c) are outlined 
below, based on how the CO2 is used and what it substitutes: 
Local use of captured CO2, avoiding provision and combustion of fuel and substituting: 
a) Fossil CO2, modelled as NG. 
b) Biogenic CO2, modelled as wood chips. 
The local use of CO2 could e.g., be substituting the combustion of fuel in greenhouses 
during peak production for CO2 fertilisation. No transportation of the captured CO2 is 
accounted for in the local use TCs (TC5a-b). 
 
The other CO2 use is where the captured, biogenic CO2 from biomethane production is sold on 
the market, substituting fossil CC at an industrial facility, which in turn induces a release of the 
substituted CO2 to the environment.

The substitution is modelled using an Ecoinvent process for liquefied, fossil CO2 on the 
European market. The CO2 captured from the biogas upgrading is liquefied and transported to

The substituted CO2 is: 
c) From an industrial process with amine-based CC of fossil CO2. 
E.g., the substitution of CO2 captured from a fossil source used for refrigerants. 
 
It is assumed that the utilised CO2 is released into the atmosphere within the time scope of this

3.3.12 Methanation 
Methanation is the exothermic conversion of CO2 and H2 into CH4. The process is energy 
intensive, mainly due to the H2 production, and can serve as a type of energy storage (Parra et 
al., 2017). The methanation process can be catalysed both biologically and chemically, and both 
pure CO2 and raw biogas can be used as the CO2 source (Gutiérrez-Martín, Rodríguez-Antón 
and Legrand, 2020). See Appendix F.8 for more details on methanation in general, and 
Appendices F.9 and F.10 for general information on respectively biological and catalytic 
methanation. 
Both the biological- and catalytic methanation are modelled as direct processes utilising biogas 
and H2 as their primary feedstocks. See Appendix E.15 for general comments on the modelling 
of the methanation processes. The biological methanation is modelled based on experimental 
data from Lardon et al. (2018), detailing a pilot-scale facility utilising biogas from a Danish 
wastewater treatment plant. Likewise, the catalytic methanation is modelled based on 
experimental data covering a (small) full-scale Danish facility utilising biogas from AD, based 
on Haldor Topsøe (2018). The H2 is produced using water electrolysis. Two types of water 
electrolysis are considered in this assessment: alkaline water electrolysis (AEL), which is a 
mature technology (Kolb et al., 2021), and the SOEC, which is not yet commercial, but has a 
lower energy demand (Haldor Topsøe, 2018). Two different AEL units are modelled, as the 
utilities for the AEL process described in Lardon et al. (2018) are relatively high, compared to 
literature values. Therefore, the AEL unit described in Christensen and Bisinella (2021) is 
introduced as a less energy intensive alternative. The biological- and catalytic methanation 
processes are both modelled with AEL and SOEC technologies. For more details on the 
modelling of the electrolysis, see Appendix E.16. The oxygen produced as a by-product of the 
water electrolysis is valorised and assumed to substitute liquid oxygen on the market. Thus, the 
oxygen produced in the electrolysis process is liquefied, assuming no further purification of the 
O2 and an electricity demand of 0.5 kWh/kg O2 (Barsali et al., 2018) as the only input for the 
liquefaction process. The SNG resulting from the methanation processes is dried, compressed

3.3.14 Substitution of NG 
The substitution of NG in the upgrading and CCU TCs (TC 5-12) is modelled by displacing NG 
according to the ratio of the lower heating values (LHVs) of the biomethane and the NG, 
resulting in a substitution of approximately 91%. The substitution includes combustion impacts 
of both biomethane and NG, using the same combustion engine as for the combustion TCs (see 
Section 3.3.6) and considering the imperfect substitution between biomethane and NG.  
The biomethane is compressed to 80 bars prior to injection, to match the high pressure NG 
Ecoinvent process used for the substitution, and the high-pressure NG grid in Denmark (Danish 
Energy Agency and Energinet, 2021b). Transportation of the biomethane is also included in 
equal measure to the Ecoinvent process used. For more details on the modelling of the NG 
substitution, see Appendix E.20. 
3.3.15 Heat balance 
The internal heat use is quantified to assess how much of the heat assumed to be used internally 
in TC1 and TC2, is reasonably utilised. Two internal heat uses are investigated: heat required to 
maintain a thermophilic AD process (Banks, Heaven and Zhang, 2018) and room heating 
(European Commission, no date), resulting in the sum of 1804 MJ/FU, see Appendix E.21 for 
details. These internal heat uses are outside the system boundaries, but because of the 
assumption of the internally used heat in TC1-2; the upgrading and CCU TCs, as well as TC3-4, 
are extended to include the internally used heat as an energy penalty. The TC3-4 cover the 
energy penalty by utilising part of the heat recovered from the combustion process, whereas 
TCs 5-12 use heat from NG, in accordance with Ryhl (2021b). The remaining heat recovered in 
TC1 and TC2 is assumed vented. However, this estimate is fairly uncertain as it is expected to 
vary substantially with each plant. 
The amine scrubbers have a high heat use for the regeneration of the amine solution. It is 
assumed that the TCs using amine scrubbers are connected to the district heating system and 
that they recover and export 60% (Ryhl, 2021a) of the heat used for the regeneration of the 
amine solution, see Appendix E.21 for more details.

4.2.4 Biogas upgrading technologies 
The upgrading TCs (TC5, TC6-10) investigate three different biogas upgrading technologies:

scrubbing process is applied, the GW impact of the amine upgrading process would be 53 kg

permeation unit (TC9) has a GW impact that is more than double that of the amine scrubber, 
and the water scrubber (TC7) has almost three times the GW impact of the amine scrubber.

4.4 Sensitivity analysis 
4.4.1 Perturbation analysis 
A one-at-a-time perturbation analysis is performed on 53 unique parameters, and the majority of 
them are used in more than one TC. See Section 3.4.1 for the methods and formulas used for the 
perturbation analysis. The majority of the TCs, except TC1, have a negative impact score. Thus, 
for the majority of the TCs, a negative SR denotes a load to the system and a positive SR 
denotes a benefit to the system, compared to the initial result. For TC1, the interpretation is 
reversed. 
The combustion TCs (TC1-4) generally show higher SRs, likely due to their simplicity and 
relatively smaller GW impact results. The parameters tested from the combustion TCs, which 
have -1≥SR≥1, are presented in Table 7. Generally, the combustion TCs show high sensitivity 
for the engine-related parameters (EngLost, EngEEf, EngLostHeat), as these parameters directly 
relate to the impactful energy recovery. The parameters related to the engine are benchmarked 
against other gas-powered engines (Henson, 2012; Lantz, 2012) and are deemed reasonable. 
TC1 especially shows very high SRs. This is due to the simplicity of the system, as it consists of 
only four processes, but also because the total GW impact score is very close to 0 (4 CO2-eq.), 
resulting in dramatic SR values of more than 1000.

The quantification of the analytical parameter uncertainty is based on the subset of parameters, 
which proved to be most sensitive, and it is expected that they represent the overall uncertainty 
of the TCs, in accordance with Bisinella et al. (2016). The sensitive parameters are also 
generally related to the biogas treatment processes, which is the focus of this study. The 
negative sign of the majority of the CVs indicates that the GW impact score is negative, except 
for TC1 which has a positive impact score.  
The combustion TCs, and in particular TC1 and TC2, have very high parameter uncertainty, 
mainly due to the contribution of the parameters related to the combustion process, but the 
relatively low impact score also has a substantial influence on the CVs. 
The upgrading TCs generally have CVs of 3-4%. TC5c has a relatively higher uncertainty and 
the majority of the quantified uncertainty for this TC comes from the market-based substitution 
of CO2. The methanation TCs generally have slightly lower CVs of approximately 1%, except 
for TC11a, where the majority of the uncertainty comes from the H2 usage. 
The number of parameters used for the uncertainty assessment also plays a role. In Bisinella et 
al. (2016) the six most influential parameters are highlighted as a good compromise between the 
number of parameters assessed and the represented variance, based on a case study. These six 
parameters on average represent around 90% of the total analytical parameter variance, and as a 
result, six parameters are also the minimum number used to assess the TCs in this study. 
Assuming that the analytical uncertainty does indeed accurately indicate the overall uncertainty, 
the most uncertain TCs are by far the combustion TCs followed by the upgrading TCs and 
finally the methanation TCs.

The CCS TCs (TC2, TC4, TC6, TC8 and TC10) consistently demonstrate higher benefits than

process, the high-pressure compression, is assumed to be powered by oil (as this compression 
happens aboard the oil-fuelled ship) and is therefore not varied with the ESs. If it was, the CCS

their benefit to the system. The upgrading TCs (TC5-10) do not vary greatly along with the

the top, as the substantial energy requirement needed for the electrolysis becomes less 
impactful, and the benefit from the displacement of NG remains the same. The methanation TCs 
have potentially very large benefits, but their GW impacts also depend strongly on the ES. TC5a 
continually ranks fairly highly, due to the benefit of displacing both provision and combustion 
of NG, which is a substantial benefit in all ESs. Figures showing the movement of each TC

Sensitivity scenario 1 directly influences the ES, so it is tested in three different ESs, and the

of the TCs with the same magnitude, constituting a saving of 50-200 kg CO2- eq. from the fossil 
to the renewable ES. However, no change in the ranking of the TCs is observed compared to the 
standard TCs, denoting no large sensitivity related to this scenario.  
Sensitivity scenario 2 results in substantially decreased benefits for the methanation TCs 
amounting to around 700 kg CO2-eq. Scenario 2 changes the ranking of TC5-12, except for 
TC5a. Thus, the results proved very sensitive to this change, indicating that the O2 produced 
from methanation absolutely ought to be valorised. This is also supported by Lardon et al. 
(2018). 
Sensitivity scenario 3 results in a saving for the CCS TCs, but it does not influence the ranking 
of the TCs. Despite the relatively large saving of approximately 150-180 kg CO2-eq. for each 
CCS TC, this change is not sensitive enough to cause a shift in the ranking.

Sensitivity scenario 4 influences all the amine scrubbing TCs, and also directly influences the 
ES, thus it is tested in three ESs. Scenario 4a changes the ranking of several TCs (TC1, TC5a-c, 
TC6, TC7, TC8 and TC10), and thus show relatively high sensitivity in the fossil ES (ES1). 
Scenario 4b changes the ranking of TC5c, TC6, TC7 and TC10, and thus Scenario 4b also 
demonstrates relatively high sensitivity in the baseline ES (ES4). Scenario 4c does not influence 
the ranking of the TCs. Thus, the heat recovery of the amine scrubber demonstrates a large 
sensitivity in the fossil ES, but as the ES becomes more renewable, the sensitivity decreases, 
due to the smaller benefit of the displaced heat in the standard system. 
Sensitivity scenario 5 is included to investigate the effects of displacing biomethane instead of 
only NG, as this may happen towards the end of the temporal scope of this assessment. Scenario 
5 results in a load of approximately 100-200 kg CO2-eq. to all TCs producing biomethane/SNG, 
compared to the standard system. However, this change does not impact the ranking of the TCs, 
demonstrating that it is not sensitive enough to cause a change in the rankings.

E.g., several of the biogas upgrading technologies use anti-foaming agents (Bauer et al., 2013),

missing data on the consumables used in smaller quantities, but given their limited use, it also

The process of gas drying is used in all TCs, but it is modelled based on a simple 
thermodynamical extrapolation. In practice, there are many ways of drying gasses – ranging 
from thermal to physical processes. For instance, the gas might well be dried using water traps 
placed conveniently, where the water is likely to condense (Ryhl, 2021c), e.g. when the gas 
cools down, thus requiring no energy input. The impact of the utilised drying process is ≤10 kg 
CO2-eq. pr. TC due to the electricity use, and it was deemed an appropriate approximation. 
The storage of CO2 is restricted to only impact the GW impact category, as this technology is 
still relatively new, and no consistent data was found for the other environmental impact 
categories. The consequence of this is that mainly the GW impact category is considered in the 
interpretation phase, despite the remaining processes being suitable for a full LCA. However, 
according to Pour et al. (2018b) the contributions from CCS to e.g. acidification, ecosystem 
damage and GW are assessed to be very low if the storage site is well chosen and monitored. 
The data collection and modelling of the TCs with direct utilisation of CO2 (TC5a-b) is different 
from the remaining scenarios. A more general approach is used, focusing on the origin and fate 
of the displaced CO2, to encompass direct CO2 utilisation in general, as opposed to one specific 
utilisation. This also means that the completeness of these TCs is likely lower than the 
remaining TCs. 
Furthermore, the omitted processes, which are identical in all TCs and specified in Section 3.2, 
impact the completeness. A rough quantification of these contributions is made, see Appendix 
E.22 for details. It should be noted that the data collection for this estimation is not as detailed, 
as for the remaining modelling and is mainly based on general EASETECH- and Ecoinvent 
processes. The rough quantification of the collection and transportation of biomass, AD, initial 
desulphurisation, transportation of digestate, appliance on land and displacement of mineral 
fertilisers results in an additional GW load of around 700 kg CO2-eq. in total, which should be 
added to each TC. Keeping in mind the additional uncertainties, this implies that all the 
combustion TCs are a load to the system. 
Overall, considering the system approach of this assessment, the completeness is deemed to be 
sufficient to reach conclusions within the limitations and uncertainties discussed. 
4.8 Consistency check 
The consistency check serves to verify that data, methods, and assumptions are applied 
consistently throughout the modelling. 
The system boundaries and the allocation rules have presumptively been applied consistently. 
System expansion is used to handle the displaced products and in LCA, the credited products 
often have a large impact on the overall results. When appropriate, imperfect substitution has 
been considered, for instance with the substitution of NG. The substitution of heat and 
electricity, as well as CO2 in TC5a-c, is assumed to perfectly substitute their displaced products, 
as there is no apparent loss of quality.  
For the ES analysis, only electricity and heat are varied, but the gas system was also considered 
to be varied.  
==PAGE:46==
DTU Environment  
Anna Sofie Varling 
Page 34 of 47 
 
However, the pace of change in the gas sources is expected to be different compared to the 
electricity and heat systems. Further, the overall gas use in Denmark is expected to decrease 
considerably within the temporal scope of this assessment, as more processes are electrified and 
NG for heating purposes are phased out (Energinet, 2020a). According to Hansen (2020), the 
amount of biomethane in the gas grid will be 30-45% in 2040. Meanwhile Energinet (2020a) 
expects the entire Danish gas consumption to be covered by “green gases”, including both 
biomethane, but also H2 and other non-specified gasses, in 2040. Therefore, it seems reasonable 
to assume that biomethane displaces fossil NG within the time frame of this assessment.  
The geographical scope of the sources for the main technologies, specifically the biogas 
upgrading processes, the gas conditioning, and the methanation processes, are all based on 
Danish conditions, while the CCS process has a European focus. The Ecoinvent database is 
used extensively to model background processes, and whenever possible, market processes are 
utilised. Further, mainly a Danish or alternatively European focus is used for the background 
processes, but the geographical scope of the Ecoinvent processes varies significantly, ranging 
from processes specifically for Denmark (6/36), to European processes (22/36) and global 
processes (8/36).  
The temporal scope of the sources for the main technologies is generally recent, as they are 
from 2018-2021. However, the temporal scope of the background processes varies substantially 
(with start years from 1980-2018), but as all the Ecoinvent processes are from the Ecoinvent 
Database 3.7.1, the datasets are all extrapolated to apply to 2020. Further, considerable changes 
to the energy- and consumables consumption are expected for the technologies within the 
temporal scope of this assessment. The consumptions used for this assessment are in line with 
what is expected for technologies installed around 2020, but the technologies are likely to 
become more efficient over time, which is not considered in this assessment.  
The data used to model the biogas- and biomethane technologies generally have high 
technological representativeness, as the majority of the data is specific to the exact technologies. 
Outliers are the biogas engine, which is also used to model the NG combustion, and the MSWI 
PC amine scrubber, which is used for CC in the combustion TCs. However, these substitutes are 
expected to correlate fairly well with the targeted processes. Further, the methanation TCs are 
based on experimental reports, whereas the upgrading and remaining CCUS TCs are based on 
literature data. The geographical, temporal, and technological representativeness are also 
assessed using the pedigree matrix, see Section 4.8.1. 
Overall, the assumptions and modelling choices appear to have been applied consistently.

5 Further perspectives 
This section aims to discuss implications of an increased use of the technologies assessed and 
expands the scope to also discuss biomass use and the role of CCUS in the future. 
5.1 Gas technologies 
Several of the technologies investigated in this study are emerging technologies, and costs are 
expected to drop with an increase in efficiency and installed capacity (Kemper, 2015). 
According to Pour, Webley, and Cook (2018a), the capital cost of CCS is likely to decrease by 
30–50% by 2030. This may allow for an additional installed capacity of (BE)CCS.  
 
==PAGE:48==
DTU Environment  
Anna Sofie Varling 
Page 36 of 47 
 
However, BECCS typically result in both trade-offs and co-benefits to the environment 
(Robledo-Abad et al., 2017), and the challenges related to BECCS, such as land-use change 
(LUC) and how securely the carbon is fixed, must be considered for its large scale application to 
be sustainable. As a result, it is crucial that BECCS is not used as an excuse to continue current 
unsustainable practices, but rather a tool to mitigate unsafe climate change (Kemper, 2015). 
Thus, despite the benefits of the CCS TCs, their savings should not be used as an excuse to burn 
more fossil fuels. 
According to Bailera et al. (2017), PtG is viewed as a promising technology to store surplus 
renewable energy in gaseous products for the longer term. However, this requires the 
technologies applied to be flexible regarding intermittent production. The AEL unit, despite 
being the most mature electrolysis technology, does not work well with intermittent production 
(Reiter and Lindorfer, 2015). This necessitates the continued development of other electrolysis 
technologies, which are more suitable to the irregular nature of PtG, and ideally less energy 
intensive.  
As seen in Section 4.5, the energy system has a large effect on the GW impact of the 
methanation processes, mainly due to the electricity demand of the electrolysis. In a mostly 
renewable energy system, the environmental benefit of the methanation process is large, but it is 
important to remember that the Power to X (PtX) processes are only as environmentally 
sustainable as the electricity that powers them. Some PtX processes involve several 
transformations processes, resulting in advanced fuels and chemicals, but energy is still lost 
with every transformation. Further, the operators cannot control what electricity runs in the 
electricity grid, so the large focus on the storage of  “surplus” electricity might not reflect the 
complete truth. The more likely scenario is that the PtX technologies, such as methanation, are 
deployed when the grid reaches a certain percentage of renewable energy, resulting in the 
storage of both renewable and fossil energy. However, as the Danish energy grid moves towards 
increasingly renewable energy sources, this issue likely becomes less impactful. 
5.2 Biomass competition 
The biogas production of this study is based solely on waste products, but energy crops are 
often used to increase the methane yield. The utilisation of any biomass has impacts on the 
food-climate-water-energy nexus which can have far-reaching consequences (Kemper, 2015). 
The issues related to biomasses are many and involve i.a. direct and indirect LUC impacts, 
competition with e.g. food, feed, fibre and impacts on environmental justice (Kemper, 2015). 
According to Kemper (2015), the production of energy crops are in direct competition with food 
crops, and a transition towards using only waste products for the production of bioenergy is 
deemed beneficial. Furthermore, the use of energy crops for biogas production has a detrimental 
impact on its environmental sustainability, as the impacts from the production of the biomass 
must be included in the environmental assessment of the biogas (Kemper, 2015). This is also 
supported by the recommendations from the European Environment Agency Scientific 
Committee, stating that bioenergy production from waste materials should be encouraged to 
ensure the environmental sustainability of the bioenergy (European Environment Agency, 
2011).  
As a result, the Danish government has implemented a gradual reduction in the accepted level of 
energy crops in the feedstock for Danish biogas plants from a maximum of 12% in 2021 to a 
maximum of 4% in 2025 (Danish Parliament, 2021a).  
==PAGE:49==
DTU Environment  
Anna Sofie Varling 
Page 37 of 47 
 
According to Biogas Danmark (2020b), only 20% of the Danish production of livestock manure 
was used for biogas production in 2020, and it is estimated that there is enough waste biomass 
potential in Denmark to raise the biogas production from 20 petajoules in 2020 to 94 petajoule 
in 2040. The reduction or elimination of energy crops used for biogas production, and therefore 
the increased use of organic waste and by-products, can also to a certain extent evade issues 
related to biomass competition. Since for instance food, feed and fibre have no direct use of 
waste products such as manure and municipal or industrial organic waste. 
5.3 Applying CCS to Danish biomethane plants 
The Danish government has set aside 16 billion DKK specifically for CCUS, intending to 
achieve a reduction of 0.4 million tons CO2-eq. in 2025 and 0.9 million tons CO2-eq. in 2030 
(Danish Parliament, 2020).  
Considering a scenario, where approximately 50% of the Danish biomethane capacity is used 
for CCS, just over 0.5 million tons CO2 could be captured annually from this source. Bearing in 
mind the emissions related to transportation and storage, as quantified in this study, it 
corresponds to a reduction of 0.29 million tons CO2-eq./year, which is already captured, and 
ready for further transportation and storage. 
50% of the Danish biomethane capacity corresponds to the production from the 14 largest 
biomethane plants operating in November 2020 or a production capacity of 38200 Nm3 
biomethane pr. hour (Dansk Gasteknisk Center, 2020). Using the biogas composition from this 
thesis (55% CH4, 45% CO2), and assuming no other losses, these 14 biomethane plants could 
capture just over 0.5 million tons CO2 yearly. 83% of the capacity of the 14 largest biomethane 
plants are equipped with amine scrubbers (Dansk Gasteknisk Center, 2020), and based on the 
amine scrubbing TCs (TC5 and TC6), the GW saving is 475 kg CO2-eq./1000 Nm3 biogas 
treated, which then results in the sequestration of approximately 0.29 million tons CO2-eq./year.  
Due to the high concentration of biomethane plants in Jylland, there may well be a potential for 
creating clusters of larger biomethane plants, and maybe other large CO2 emitters. These 
clusters can e.g., share infrastructure and liquefaction equipment, and collectively arrange the 
further transportation to reap the benefits of large-scale CO2 production. This would also 
mitigate the challenge of the feasibility of having liquefaction equipment and gas storage 
options on every biomethane plant. One such hub could e.g. be placed around Esbjerg where 
there within a distance of 60 km are 5 larger biomethane plants (Dansk Gasteknisk Center, 
2020) with a CO2 production capacity (considering the same assumptions as above) of around 
0.17 million tons CO2 yearly.  
Utilising biomethane plants as a CO2 supply offers a relatively scattered and small CO2 source, 
e.g. compared to Amager Bakke in Copenhagen, which is expected to capture 0.5 million tons 
CO2 yearly, assuming a full-scale CC facility (Amager Ressource Center, 2021). However, 
biomethane plants have the large benefit of having already captured the CO2 in the process of 
concentrating the biogas into biomethane. Thus, there is no to little extra cost and impacts 
associated with the CC, which is typically the part of the value chain, that is the most expensive 
(Danish Energy Agency, 2022). Further, the CO2 captured from biomethane plants is biogenic, 
so the storage results in net negative emissions, which has a unique potential and is expected to 
become a valuable currency, depending on the future CO2-tax system (Kemper, 2015).

5.4 Biogenic CO2 in the future 
Large-scale implementation of BECCS and/or biogenic CCU requires a major production of 
bioenergy. In Denmark, the majority of bioenergy today comes from the incineration of solid 
biomass, but this energy source is expected to be reduced drastically in the near future, due to 
the gradual elimination of solid biomass based power plants (Dansk Energi, 2021). Despite the 
expected increase in biogas and biomethane production, biogenic CO2 is likely to become a 
limited resource over the next few decades (Dansk Energi, 2021), especially considering the 
many possibilities of utilising biogenic CO2 within PtX.  
At a recent forum hosted by the Danish Energy Agency (Danish Energy Agency, 2022), it was 
proposed that the captured, biogenic CO2, should be prioritized for CCU to make so-called 
green fuels to defossilise heavy transportation and industry. Based on the GW impact category, 
methanation and direct utilisation of CO2 do have more environmental benefits compared to 
CCS, especially in the more renewable ESs. However, using the biogenic CO2 for CCU does not 
utilise the unique potential of BECCS for creating permanent, net negative emissions. 
The distinction between biogenic and fossil carbon is a common convention in carbon 
accounting. Nevertheless, when the carbon reaches the atmosphere, there is no meaningful 
distinction between fossil and biogenic CO2, and they both contribute to the greenhouse effect 
(Agostini et al., 2020). The difference is found in the biogenic carbon having been recently 
taken from the atmosphere in contrast to the fossil carbon, which is removed from the 
underground and “added” to the carbon cycle (Agostini et al., 2020). This only emphasises the 
importance of capturing and utilising the biogenic CO2, as it may serve as the basis for several 
environmentally beneficial CCUS processes and, if not captured, it contributes to climate 
change. 
6 Conclusion 
The goal of this assessment was to quantify the environmental impacts of 17 different TCs for 
the utilisation and treatment of biogas in Denmark using LCA. The TCs include biogas 
combustion, three biomethane upgrading technologies, as well as CCUS processes for the CO2 
fraction. A special focus was placed on the GW impact score, and the results were subjected to 
sensitivity and uncertainty analysis, as well as investigated in different energy systems.  
Overall, the investigated TCs are dominated by the energy displacement, as well as the impacts 
from CCUS. The values presented in this paragraph are the net GW impacts of the investigated 
TCs in the baseline ES (ES4). However, the processes which are identical in all TCs are 
excluded from the modelling, as per LCA standards. The CCUS TCs resulted in the biggest 
environmental savings in the range of 1300–2100 kg CO2-eq./1000 Nm3 biogas treated. On the 
other end of the scale, the combustion TCs resulted in the smallest benefit to the system, 
ranging between approximately -400 to 0 kg CO2-eq./1000 Nm3 biogas treated, and they proved 
to have substantially more uncertainty than the remaining TCs. The BAU upgrading TCs result 
in a saving, in the range 1100-1300 kg CO2-eq./1000 Nm3 biogas treated, mainly due to the 
displacement of NG. Among the three biogas upgrading technologies assessed, the amine 
scrubber performed best, based on the GW impacts. The CCS TCs result in a saving of 400-
1700 kg CO2-eq./1000 Nm3 biogas treated, depending on whether the biogas is combusted or 
upgraded. The CCS processes provide a robust saving to the system in all the energy systems 
assessed. However, the modelling of the storage process only covers the GW impact category. 
Furthermore, the TCs with direct utilisation of CO2 result in a saving ranging between 1300-
2100 kg CO2-eq./1000 Nm3 biogas treated, depending on the nature of the CO2 displaced. 
==PAGE:51==
DTU Environment  
Anna Sofie Varling 
Page 39 of 47 
 
Finally, the methanation TCs generally resulted in the biggest saving to the system, ranging 
between 1500-2100 kg CO2-eq./1000 Nm3 biogas treated. However, the results of the 
methanation TCs are very dependent on the carbon footprint of the energy powering them, and 
on the valorisation of the O2 from the electrolysis. 
Based on the normalised results, the other impact categories overall show a ranking of the TCs, 
which is similar to the GW impact category. Very generally, the CCU TCs demonstrated the 
largest benefits, followed by the upgrading TCs and finally the combustion TCs. Nonetheless, 
the BAU upgrading TCs outperform the CCS TCs, contrary to what the GW impact category 
demonstrated. However, this is influenced by the CCS storage process only being modelled in 
the GW impact category. 
Challenges related to the treatment and utilisation of biogas were discussed, stressing especially 
the importance of eliminating energy crops from the biogas feedstock and the significance of 
not using CCUS as an excuse to maintain current unsustainable practices.  
A simplified assessment demonstrated that amending 50% of the Danish biomethane capacity in 
2020 with CCS could provide a saving of approximately 0.3 million tons CO2-eq. yearly, which 
is already captured and ready for further processing. This would contribute substantially to the 
Danish Government’s goal of achieving a saving of 0.4 million tons CO2-eq. yearly in 2025. 
In addition to the results reported above, and within the uncertainties discussed, the following 
recommendations are suggested: 
• 
Upgrading biogas to biomethane for injection into the NG grid is beneficial for the 
environment, compared with biogas combustion. 
• 
Utilising the CO2 resulting from the traditional biogas upgrading processes has 
additional environmental benefits, but also trade-offs. 
• 
Methanation of biogas appears to be the most environmentally beneficial biogas 
technology of the technologies assessed, however, it is very sensitive to the energy 
sources powering it.  
Despite the scattered and relatively modest CO2 production of biomethane plants, they still have 
potential for CCUS as they offer, not only renewable energy, but also the possibility of low or 
even negative emissions. Seeing as the CO2 resulting from biomethane plants is already 
captured, and releasing it contributes to climate change, it seems an oversight not to utilise this 
easily accessible potential for CCUS, especially in the first phases of CCUS implementation in 
Denmark.

C Energy Systems 
The energy systems (ES) used are from Bisinella et al. (2021b), as they consider similar 
technologies to this study. The supplementary material of their paper contains additional 
information on the reasoning behind the ESs. The marginal for the baseline energy system 
(ES4) is based on a Danish energy scenario presented in International Energy Agency  (2016b), 
which is a report focussing on future Nordic energy projections. The marginal baseline for both 
the heat and the electricity is based on the “2DS” scenario, which is a scenario with substantial 
initiatives to make the energy system more renewable, resulting in 50% certainty of limiting 
global warming to 2°C in 2100 (International Energy Agency, 2016a).  
Table 13 in Section 4.5 shows the energy sources used in the six investigated ESs, ranging from 
completely fossil (ES1-2) to completely renewable (ES5-6). The Ecoinvent processes used to 
model the electricity- and heat technologies used in the ESs are presented in Table 20 and Table 
21.

E.4 Desulphurisation 
For all TCs investigated, it is assumed that iron salts are added to the AD digester in sufficient 
quantity to reduce the H2S concentration to a maximum of 300 ppmv. This desulphurisation 
method is commonly used to lower the H2S concentration to 100-200 ppmv. However, lowering 
the concentration further requires a high amount of excess iron ions, so an additional technology 
is recommended instead (Allegue and Hinge, 2014).

that approximately 50% sulphur leaves the digester with the digestate (Möller and Müller, 
2012), the maximum concentration, assuming 50% of the S present in the biomass (1.646 kg S) 
is transformed completely into H2S, is 1.22 Nm3 H2S, see Equation 8.

Lombardi and Francini (2020). 4% maximum water content is also assumed for the biomethane 
and off-gas, as no data was found for these.

E.7 Flaring in torch 
An Ecoinvent process for the flaring of NG (“waste natural gas, sweet, treatment of waste 
natural gas, sweet, burned in production flare”) is used as a basis to model the flaring of 
biomethane, see also Appendix G for the modifications. The process is adjusted so the origin of 
the CO2-, CO- and CH4-emissions are biogenic instead of fossil, to reflect the fuel change from 
NG to biogas. The values are not changed, and the CO2 input to the flaring is assumed emitted 
to the atmosphere. The amount of gas sent to flaring depends on the treatment of the gas, but it 
is specified in the LCIs for the respective technologies.

E.10 Amine scrubbing 
The LCI used for the amine scrubbing process is constructed based on data from Hernø ( 2020), 
see Appendix E.9 for general comments.  
The amine scrubber requires a substantial heat input for the regeneration of the amine solution, 
and it is assumed that 60% of this heat input is recovered. This value falls within the common 
range of 50-60% given by Ryhl (2021a), which also states that it is possible to recover up to 
80% of the heat input if it is useable at the plant. Further, these values are in line with the ones 
presented in Bisinella et al. (2021a). 
The amine scrubbing process takes place at a pressure of 1-2 bars and the biomethane leaves the 
process at the same pressure (Lombardi and Francini, 2020), so no initial compression of the 
raw biogas is assumed. For an overview of the assumptions regarding gas compression for the 
amine scrubber TCs, see Appendix E.6.   
The required inputs of amine solution and lubricating oil listed in Hernø ( 2020) are given as 
economic values. These are converted to masses, by division of price/kg, under the assumption

E.15 Methanation 
For this study only direct methanation is modelled, meaning biogas is used as feedstock for the 
methanation processes. 2% of the SNG is assumed to be lost during the processes for both types 
of methanation. This value is chosen, because the average gas loss at agricultural biogas and 
biomethane plants is 2.1% (Gudmundsson et al., 2021), and it is assumed that the loss from a 
methanation plant is similar. 
One of the main purposes of PtX, and therefore also PtG, is to store energy when it has a high 
proportion of renewable energy, for use when the energy grid is more fossil-based. To optimise 
the methanation process, some amount of gas storage is needed for the intermediary gasses H2 
and biogas, to allow for maximum operation of the methanation unit (Lardon, Thorberg and 
Krosgaard, 2018). However, this internal storage and transportation are not included in this 
assessment. 
E.16 Electrolysis 
Two types of water electrolysis are considered for the production of H2, the AEL and the SOEC. 
The AEL is the most mature of the water electrolysis processes (Kolb et al., 2021), and the most 
cost-efficient (Iskov, Kvist and Bruun, 2019). The SOEC is not yet commercially available, 
however, it has a lower energy use compared to the AEL (Haldor Topsøe, 2018). According to 
Haldor Topsøe (2018), the SOEC is expected to be commercially available in the early 2020s. 
Two types of electrolysers, the AEL unit and the SOEC, are considered for both the biological 
and catalytic methanation units, resulting in four TCs, see Table 39. See Appendix E.17 and 
E.18 for details on the modelling of respectively the biological and the catalytic methanation.

E.18 Catalytic methanation 
The catalytic methanation is based on a small, but full-scale, boiling water reactor using a 
nickel-based catalyst (Haldor Topsøe, 2018). In Haldor Topsøe (2018) the small, experimental 
reactor is directly scaled to a large methanation unit treating 2500 Nm3 biogas/hour, and these 
scaled values are used as the basis for the LCI. However, the scaling assumes that a pressurised 
SOEC becomes available on the market, meaning the compression of H can be avoided (Haldor 
Topsøe, 2018). 
 
Catalytic methanation units are very sensitive towards impurities in the biogas but seeing as the 
precision desulphurisation process described in Appendix E.4 is assumed to completely remove 
the H2S content, no further cleaning is modelled. However, the experimental methanation unit 
employs both activated carbon desulphurisation and a sulphur guard to target other sulphur 
species than H2S (Dannesboe, Hansen and Johannsen, 2020). However, no data was found for 
this sulphur guard, so it is not considered. 
 
The CO2 content of the biogas used in Haldor Topsøe (2018) is 43%, which is lower than the 
CO2 content used in this study. Therefore, an additional 2% H2 is added to the LCI, including 
additional electricity for the electrolysis. Implications on the methanation process itself are not 
considered. 
The high-temperature heat input to the SOEC unit (≈3 MJ/Nm3 biogas) is in TC12a covered 
using the high-temperature heat output from the catalytic methanator, as in Haldor Topsøe 
(2018). In TC12b this high-temperature heat output from the methanator is exported as district 
heating. 
The energy balance presented in Haldor Topsøe (2018) does not include energy input to heat the 
methanator itself. It is therefore assumed that the heat from the methanation reactor is enough to 
keep the methanator at standby temperature. The energy demand and time required for a cold 
start is given as 15 kW and 6-8 hours. However even if this cold start happened three times a 
day, leaving no time for actual operation, the energy demand pr. Nm3 biogas treated in the full-
scale plant, considering 8000 operating hours would result in an energy input of only 0.007 
kWh/Nm3, see Equation 14. Therefore, this is excluded from the modelling.

E.19 CO2 transportation and injection 
The CO2 transportation is inspired by the Danish “Greensand” project, as described by 
Svendsen (2021a, 2021b).Biogas plants are a relatively scattered source of CO2, and the initial 
onshore transport system may well be trucks, due to the flexibility and low capital investment 
compared to pipelines (Østergaard and Stoedter-Rosien, 2021). The gas conditions for 
transportation in trucks and ships are commonly in the range of 15-20 bar and -25 to -30 °C, 
where the CO2 is liquid with a relatively high density (Østergaard and Stoedter-Rosien, 2021). 
 
The CO2 is assumed compressed and cooled to 15 bars and -28 °C at the biogas upgrading 
facility, and it is assumed that the waste heat is 100% recovered for district heating, in line with 
Rambøll (2020). The liquified CO2 is transported by truck 100 km to a harbour. Here it is 
transferred to the ship and sailed 1000 km to the place of injection, where it is heavily 
compressed aboard the ship to 200 bars, and it is assumed that enough heat can be recovered to 
avoid electrically heating the CO2 (the required heat input is about 30% of the waste heat 
produced during the compression, but alternatively, the seawater may also be used as a heat 
input).  
==PAGE:92==
DTU Environment  
Anna Sofie Varling

This last compression is powered by the ship’s motor, which is assumed to run on fuel oil, 
despite project Greensand’s ambition of using renewable marine fuels (Svendsen, 2021b). The 
CO2 is then injected into the underground, and the transferral from the ship to the well is 
included in the energy demand given by Svendsen (2021a). At a transport distance of 1000 km, 
approximately 2% of the transported CO2 is expected to be lost due to mainly ship- and storage 
boil-off (International Energy Agency and Mitsubishi heavy industries, 2004).

E.21 Heat balance 
The internal heat use is quantified to assess how much of the heat assumed to be used internally 
in TC1 and TC2 is actually utilised. The internal heat use is based on the parasitic heat demand 
of a thermophilic AD plant described in Banks, Heaven and Zhang (2018) and the estimated 
heat required to heat a large, non-residential facility of 5000 m3 (European Commission, no 
date). 
These two heat sinks are outside the system boundaries, but because of the assumption of the 
internally used heat in TC1-2, the upgrading and CCU TCs (TC5-12), as well as TC3 and TC4, 
which export the heat from biogas combustion, are extended to include the internally used heat, 
as per the system expansion guidelines. The upgrading and CCU TCs (TC5-12) are assumed to 
use NG for this internal heat demand (Ryhl, 2021b). However, TC3-4 utilise part of the heat 
recovered from the biogas combustion to cover the internal heat use and export the remaining, 
recovered heat. 
According to Ryhl (2021b), the heat demand to keep a thermophilic AD digester is 
approximately 5-10% of the energy production. Stephansen (2021) estimates that 20-25 kWh 
heat/ton wet biomass is used to heat a mesophilic AD reactor. Further, the heat required for 
room heating was estimated assuming non-residential buildings of various sizes (3000-5000 
m2), based on an estimated heat demand for room heating (European Commission, no date).

E.22 Quantification of excluded processes 
The collection, transportation, pre-treatment, AD and digestate management has been excluded 
from the modelling of this study, due to these processes being identical in all TCs. However, to 
gain a better understanding of the full system, a rough modelling of these processes is 
performed. Nevertheless, the data for this part of the system is not of the same quality as the 
remaining modelling. 
The biomass is still considered without impact, as it is 100% waste product. The collection- and 
transportation processes are based on generic rural collection and transportation of waste for 
100 km to the AD plant. The collection and transportation are based on Larsen et al. (2009), and 
assuming a diesel-driven collection vehicle of 10 tons, fulfilling the Euro3 standard. 
The modelling of pre-treatment of waste is excluded, as no dataset for this process was found, 
and the modelling of the AD plant is described in Section 3.3.2. However, the heating of the AD 
reactor is excluded from the process, as it is already included in the internal heat use. The initial 
desulphurisation with iron compounds is described in Appendix E.4. It is assumed that 100 g 
FeCl3 pr. 1 Nm3 raw biogas is enough to decrease the H2S content to at least 300 ppmv. This is 
supported by Allegue and Hinge (2014), which state that 120-160 g FeCl3 pr. 1 Nm3 raw biogas 
is enough to lower the H2S concentration from 2000 to 20 ppmv. The approximate initial H2S 
concentration is 1220 ppmv H2S, see Equation 8, resulting in a fairly conservative estimate. 
The same transportation process as described above is used to move the digestate 100 km, 
before it is applied on land, using a diesel-powered tractor. The use-on-land process is modelled 
using an existing EASETECH process for AD digestate, based mainly on Bruun et al. (2006). 
The following mineral fertiliser equivalents are assumed: 0.6 for nitrogen (average for Danish 
conditions in Brockmann, Négri and Hélias (2014)), 1 for potassium and 0.95 for phosphorus 
(Hajjaji et al., 2016), and the mineral fertiliser processes are updated with Ecoinvent data for the 
Danish market. The construction and maintenance of the plant, infrastructure etc. is excluded.  
This results in a GW impact of 703 kg CO2-eq. in total for the (otherwise excluded) processes, 
see Table 48. This means that each TC should have approximately 700 kg CO2-eq. added to its 
GW impact for a fuller picture of the biogas system.

F Technology descriptions 
F.1 General biogas cleaning 
Several impurities may be present in raw biogas such as H2O, N2, NH3 and H2S. Typically only 
the H2S is targeted in the biogas cleaning, in addition to drying of the biogas to remove the 
water content (Angelidaki et al., 2018). According to Haldor Topsøe (2018), many of the 
general biogas impurities (carbon monoxide, VOCs, halogenated and long-chain hydrocarbons, 
ammonia and siloxanes) are of little concern, when the biogas is sourced from a continuously 
operated AD facility mainly based on livestock manure and agricultural organic waste.  
 
F.2 H2S removal 
H2S is always present in raw biogas, typically because of the anaerobic reduction of proteins 
containing sulphur. H2S is toxic and highly corrosive and is often removed from the biogas, 
before use in e.g. combustion or upgrading technologies (Allegue and Hinge, 2014). H2S is 
typically present at a concentration of 1-10,000 ppmv in raw biogas but the concentration is 
highly dependent on the biomass and the conditions of the AD digestor (Angelidaki et al., 
2018). 
Overall, there are two main types of H2S removal technologies: physical/chemical- and 
biological technologies. The most commonly used technologies in Denmark are the addition of 
iron oxides and/or salts to the digester, and adsorption to activated carbon (Allegue and Hinge, 
2014; Dansk Gasteknisk Center, 2020). The activated carbon can also aid in removing and 
binding oxygen and nitrogen present in the biogas (Petersson, 2013). 
F.3 Water removal 
Due to the water present in the biomass, raw biogas is always saturated with water vapour, but 
the amount of water present depends on the remaining compounds, the temperature and the 
pressure of the biogas (Petersson, 2013). Both amine and water scrubbing technologies require 
drying after the upgrading process, due to the water used in the upgrading process (Beil and 
Beyrich, 2013). 
==PAGE:98==
DTU Environment  
Anna Sofie Varling 
xxxviii 
 
If the water is not removed, it not only lowers the energy density of the biogas/biomethane, but 
it may also condense and form corrosive compounds in the later treatment and utilisation of the 
gas (Petersson, 2013). The removal of water may also partially remove other impurities such as 
particles, ammonia and siloxanes, which are dissolvable in the water (Petersson, 2013).  
F.4 Biogas combustion 
Biogas can typically be combusted in stationary engines suitable for NG, such as boilers or CHP 
facilities (Kaparaju and Rintala, 2013). A conventional CHP engine consists of a piston, driving 
an electric generator with typical conversion efficiencies of 35% electricity and 50% heat, 
alternatively, the conversion efficiency of 75% can be reached if only heat is produced (Henson, 
2012). These efficiencies are supported by (Lantz, 2012), stating that typical conversion 
efficiencies in biogas driven engines are 30-40% electricity conversion and 35-55% heat 
conversion. However, drying the biogas is advisable before combustion (Henson, 2012). 
F.5 Amine scrubbing 
Amine scrubbing is a mature biogas upgrading technology, which has been used for sour gas 
separation since the 1970s (Bauer et al., 2013; Beil and Beyrich, 2013). It removes CO2 from 
the biogas in an exothermic process by both physically absorbing, and chemically binding it 
with covalent bonds (Abdeen et al., 2016). Commonly used amine solutions include 
monoethanolamine, methyldiethanolamine and diethanolamine (Beil and Beyrich, 2013).  
The process has two main components: the absorption column, where the CO2 present in the 
biogas is bound to the amine solution, and the stripper, where the CO2/amine solution is heated, 
thus releasing the CO2 from the amine solution. Subsequently, the amine solution can be cooled 
and recirculated (Bauer et al., 2013; Beil and Beyrich, 2013). However, some amine loss is 
expected due to degradation, and thus a small input of amine solution is required (Beil and 
Beyrich, 2013). According to Danish Energy Agency and Energinet (2020), this amine input is 
highly variable and dependent on the type of amine used, but generally ranging from 0.2-1.6 kg 
amine/ton CO2 removed.

F.6 Water scrubbing 
Water scrubbing is a mature biogas upgrading technology (Bauer et al., 2013), and the most 
commonly used biogas upgrading technology in Europe (Lombardi and Francini, 2020). A 
water scrubber facility consists of three main parts: the absorption column, where the CO2 and 
other compounds with high water solubility dissolves in the water under high pressure; the flash 
tank where some dissolved CH4 is recovered by rapidly lowering the pressure and recirculating 
the gas phase to the absorption column; and the desorption column where atmospheric air is let 
in, thus depressurising the column and releasing the CO2 from the water (Bauer et al., 2013). 
The water is then recircled, and in modern water scrubbers, almost complete recirculation of the 
water is possible, meaning the required water input is potentially very small (Jin et al., 2021). 
Water scrubbers are typically operated at 6-8 bars, and the biomethane typically leaves the 
water scrubbing process at  5-8 bars (Hoyer et al., 2016).

F.7 Membrane permeation 
Membrane permeation is a mature gas cleaning technology, which has been used for biogas 
upgrading since the 2000s (Bauer et al., 2013). Common membrane materials include polymers 
and carbon membranes (Bauer et al., 2013). Membrane permeation exploits the differences in 
the permeability of gasses. The biogas is cleaned, pressurised and led to the first membrane, 
which retains the CH4, allowing the impurities to permeate, thus separating the gasses (Beil and 
Beyrich, 2013). Membrane permeation for biogas upgrading commonly consists of 2- or 3-
membrane systems, to minimise the CH4 in the off-gas and increase the purity of the 
biomethane. The permeate may also be recirculated to further decrease the CH4 loss. 
The upgrading process occurs at 10-15 bar and the biomethane leaves at the same pressure 
(Lombardi and Francini, 2020). In Denmark, the process pressure is typically 15 bars (Hansen 
and Hernø, 2020). See Table 53 for an overview of a range of literature values for key 
consumables, presented for membrane permeation of 1 Nm3 biogas.

F.8 General methanation 
Methanation is an important technology within the PtG arsenal. Mainly renewable energy (often 
the aim is to use ‘surplus’ energy) is utilised for the production of hydrogen. The hydrogen can 
be used as an energy carrier itself, or it may be used for methanation, where it reacts 
exothermally with CO2 to produce CH4. The resulting SNG can then be injected into the NG 
grid on equal terms with NG (Parra et al., 2017; Thema et al., 2019). The CO2 source for the 
methanation process may have several different origins, including biogas plants, industrial 
facilities and power plants (Kolb et al., 2021). 
For the production of H2, water electrolysis is the most commonly used technology in a PtG 
context. Water electrolysis is the splitting of water into H2 and O2, see Equation 17 (Guilera et 
al., 2021).

Water electrolysis is a relatively energy intensive process, and it requires cleaned water, such as 
de-ionized water for the process to work optimally (Koj, Wulf and Zapp, 2019). There are 
several different approaches to water electrolysis, but the two main commercial technologies are 
the proton exchange membrane electrolysis and AEL (Guilera et al., 2021).  
A major benefit of methanation is the existing infrastructure for storage and distribution of the 
resulting CH4 in the NG grid (Parra et al., 2017). Biogas can be used directly as feedstock for 
the methanation process (direct methanation) with no inhibitory effects, thus avoiding the 
classical biogas upgrading process (Stangeland et al., 2017). Alternatively, the relatively pure 
CO2 stream (the off-gas) from conventional biogas upgrading can be used as the carbon input to 
the methanation process (Collet et al., 2017). The methanation can also happen in-situ, meaning 
that the H2 is added directly to the AD reactor, there are however still some operational flaws in 
this set-up (Angelidaki et al., 2018).

There are two main pathways for methanation: biological methanation and catalytic 
methanation. Both types of methanation can be achieved by direct methanation, where biogas is 
cleaned and used directly as the C-source. Alternatively, the CO2 can be separated and captured 
before the methanation, but this may not be economically feasible, as the cost of CC is likely 
higher than the slight price increase of the higher gas flow when using direct methanation 
(Gutiérrez-Martín, Rodríguez-Antón and Legrand, 2020). 
The production of SNG from methanation is currently not supported by the Danish subsidy 
system (Energistyrelsen, 2018), which might be a part of why the technologies are not yet 
widespread in Denmark. 
F.9 Biological methanation 
Biological methanation utilizes methanogenic microorganisms, CO2 and H2 to form CH4. A 
biological methanator normally operates at 37-65 °C, and 1-15 bars pressure.

Methanogens are relatively robust towards gas contaminants, but a key limitation to the process 
is the low gas-to-liquid mass transfer of hydrogen, implying that the reactors for biological 
methanation have to be larger than reactors for catalytic methanation (Thema et al., 2019). 
Mixed microbial populations can be used for biological methanation, so sterile conditions are 
not required. The biological methanation pathway is proven at the laboratory- and pilot-scale, 
but not commercially (Villadsen et al., 2019). 
F.10 Catalytic methanation 
Catalytic methanation is a mature technology. The catalytic methanation is also an exothermic 
reaction, and it often uses metal catalysts (Danish Energy Agency and Energinet, 2021c). The 
methanation of CO2 is performed at high temperature (180-350 °C), meaning it has a high 
energy demand (Reiter and Lindorfer, 2015). The metal catalysts require high gas purity, as they 
are highly sensitive to contaminants (e.g., H2S). The choice of catalyst is very influential on the 
efficiency of the overall process, and the most commonly used catalysts are nickel-based 
(Thema et al., 2019). 
F.11 CCS 
CCS is the process of capturing and sequestering carbon. The capture process typically happens 
at large point sources, such as power plants, or from the atmosphere in the process of direct air 
capture. The CO2 is then transported to a permanent sink, such as a suitable geological 
formation or a depleted oil or gas field for storage (Kemper, 2015). If the captured CO2 is 
biogenic, the subsequent storage results in net negative emissions. In contrast, if fossil CO2 is 
stored, the storage results in neutralising the CO2 emission (Dansk Energi, 2021), not counting 
the energy it takes to capture and store it.  
Injection of CO2 into the underground is considered an established technology used for 
enhanced oil recovery with CO2 (CO2-EOR), which is analogous with CCS (Taniguchi and 
Itaoka, 2016)  – although with the obvious difference of the goal. According to Núñez-López, 
Gil-Egui and Hosseini (2019), the actual energy use of injection of CO2 is very site-specific, as 
it depends strongly on depth, geology etc. The minimum pressure needed for CO2 injection into 
the underground in the context of CO2-EOR is 124 bars (Cooney et al., 2015), and several 
sources cite 150 bars in a CCS context (Pour, Webley and Cook, 2018b; Bisinella et al., 2021a). 
Various geological formations are considered suitable for the long term (>1000 years) storage of 
CO2, mainly saline aquifers with a cap rock layer for secure retention, and depleted oil and gas 
reservoirs both on- and off-shore (IPCC 2005). The geological formations considered for CCS 
are often found in min. 800-1000 m depth, as the CO2 injected at these depths behaves like a 
liquid (IPCC 2005). The CO2 is expected to become more securely sequestered over time, by 
several mechanisms: the high pressure, causing the CO2 to sink deeper into the deposit, the 
impermeable caprock physically retaining the CO2, and the process of carbonation, where the 
CO2 reacts with minerals, forming various carbonates (IPCC 2005). 
Even though it can be argued that the injection of CO2 into the underground is an established 
technology, practised since the 1970s, the migration patterns of CO2 over longer time scales are 
less well-known and therefore requires long-term monitoring in addition to great care in the 
selection of the sink (Taniguchi and Itaoka, 2016). However, it is very likely that the CO2 
injected in appropriate and properly managed reservoirs is retained at an efficiency >99% at 
both the 100 and 1000 year time scale, as well as at longer time scales (IPCC 2005). However, 
sudden emissions may happen due to failure of the injection well or storage seal, or due to 
natural disasters, such as volcanic eruptions or earthquakes (IPCC 2005).

Further, mismanaged injection wells may leak, and thus could degrade the groundwater quality 
locally, as well as influence the local ecosystems. But these risks can be efficiently mitigated by

F.12 BECCS 
BECCS is the capture of biogenic carbon from bioenergy production, followed by permanent 
sequestering, exactly like CCS, see Section F.11. Utilising BECCS establishes a flow of CO2 
from the atmosphere to the underground, thus providing net negative emissions (Pour, Webley 
and Cook, 2017). BECCS is a broad category, covering many combinations of feedstocks, 
bioenergy conversions, CC processes and storage options, all of which have different 
environmental impacts (Pour, Webley and Cook, 2017). 
BECCS offers an opportunity to remove historic CO2 from the atmosphere (Kemper, 2015). 
This represents increased flexibility within the climate scenarios, allowing for more temporal 
leniency as to when the climate goals are achieved, as well as an opportunity to offset fossil 
emissions from the sectors, which are difficult to fully defossilise. However, overshoot 
scenarios, where greenhouse gas emissions exceed the climate goals, pose a higher risk of 
crossing climate tipping points and ought to be avoided, if at all possible (Kemper, 2015). 
Further, BECCS must not be used as an excuse not to change the current unsustainable practices 
of society (Kemper, 2015; Pour, Webley and Cook, 2017). 
BECCS also faces considerable challenges, that must be managed well for its widespread 
application to be viable, e.g. the competition for biomass uses, increased water usage, direct and 
indirect LUC and economic feasibility (Kemper, 2015). However, considering past energy 
technology learning curves, it is evident that the cost typically decreases as the cumulative 
installed capacity increases (Kemper, 2015). BECCS also faces a challenge in its relatively low 
public acceptance, which is lower than fossil-based CCS (Kemper, 2015).  
F.13 CCS in Denmark 
According to Dansk Energi (2021), there is broad consensus among the government and the 
trade and industry in Denmark about the necessity of utilising CCS to reach the ambitious goal 
of achieving a 70% reduction of greenhouse gas emissions in Denmark in 2030. However, there 
are notable obstacles to the implementation of CCS in Denmark: CCS requires creating a new 
value chain for CC, transportation of CO2 and its storage, and currently, there are not yet 
sufficient economic incentives to capture and store CO2 (Danish Parliament, 2020, 2021b). The 
technical CO2 capture capacity for Denmark is expected to be 12-13 Mt CO2/year from 2030 
(Dansk Energi, 2021). 
F.14 CCU 
CCU is an umbrella term, covering the capture and utilisation of CO2, typically from industrial 
processes. CCU is depicted as a more economically feasible alternative to CCS, as the resulting 
products can be directly valorised (Cuéllar-Franca and Azapagic, 2015). CCU technologies 
convert the captured CO2 into various products, and the utilisations can be divided into direct 
usage and conversion into chemicals or fuels (Christensen and Bisinella, 2021). Examples of 
direct utilisation include use in the food and drink industry as additives or solvents, industrial 
uses as refrigerants or fire retardants, CO2 mineralisation, and use in greenhouses for CO2 
fertilization and biosequestration (Christensen and Bisinella, 2021).  
Examples of the conversion of CO2 into chemicals and fuels are many, including the production 
of methanol, formic acid, and various polymers (Cuéllar-Franca and Azapagic, 2015; Baena-
Moreno et al., 2019; Christensen and Bisinella, 2021).

The choice of utilisation typically depends on the purity of the CO2, alternatively on the 
possibility of gas cleaning (Cuéllar-Franca and Azapagic, 2015). 
According to Hoyer et al. (2016), the food industry is not the most obvious utilisation for 
captured CO2 from biomethane production, because the CO2 must be extensively cleaned for 
this use. Further, using CO2 from biomethane may have an image problem, due to its origins in 
manure and other types of waste. International agreed requirements for the purity of CO2 used in 
beverages can be found in Ringo (2000). 
There is some overlap between CCS and CCU, e.g., CO2-EOR and mineral carbonation can be 
categorised as both CCS and CCU, depending on the circumstances. CO2-EOR is the injection 
of CO2 into nearly depleted oil- or gas fields to force more oil or gas out, and mineral 
carbonation is CO2 reacting with metal oxides to form carbonates (Cuéllar-Franca and 
Azapagic, 2015). However, categorising CO2-EOR as CCUS is questionable, as the goal is to 
extract additional fossil fuels.
