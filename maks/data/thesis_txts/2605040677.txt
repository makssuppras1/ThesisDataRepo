Abstract
More and more Wind-power generation plants have been built worldwide. New wind
turbines utilise IEC type-4 full converter system to export power to the grid. The
converter system electrically decouples the turbine mechanical system from the grid
and has drawn attention in modelling and stability studies. A converter system needs
to synchronise to the grid phases, which is often accomplished using a phase-locked
loop (PLL). The stability of the joint converter and PLL system is a function of
amongst other the uncertain grid impedance, partly driven by the electrical network,
i.e. transmission cables, passive equipment and other turbines. The variability in
the impedance brings a challenge to guarantee system stability using classic analysis
methods, such as the impedance and eigenvalue based method.
This thesis presents an overview of robust stability and performance analysis of
the grid-connected converter system. The analysis is based on the linearised model
of a simplified system. The phase-locked loop controller and current controller are
designed to synchronise the converter system with the grid, and control the current. A
hypothetical transmission grid is simplified and modeled to obtain the grid equivalent
impedance. A mathematical model of the grid-connected convert system is derived
in order to analyse the nominal system’s dynamic performance. The variation of the
grid impedance is modeled to determine the uncertainty perturbation to the system
dynamic. The system’s robustness analysis determines whether the system is able to
achieve the performance and stability with the considered perturbations.
In this project, the hypothetical transmission grid is simplified and assumed to
have a variation in the grid impedance of ±30%.
For the considered uncertainty,
the converter system is robust unstable when the PLL bandwidth is 10 Hz and the
current control loop bandwidth is 100 Hz. In addition, increasing the current control’s
loop bandwidth improves the nominal control system’s performance at the cost of
reducing the margin of the system’s robustness.
The system with wider current
control bandwidth is more sensitive to the model uncertainty, and when the current
control loop bandwidth is at 150 Hz, the control system cannot achieve the robust
performance.
Overall, the thesis provides a reasonable method when analysing a
system’s robustness.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The energy demand is continuously increasing; however, fossil energy sources such
as coal and oil are gradually depleted. In contrast, the usage of renewable energy
is becoming more and more emphasised. In the development of various new energy
sources, the wind power generation method is with relatively mature technology and
has large-scale commercial development conditions[15]. Nowadays, more and more
wind-turbine (WT) generators have been built worldwide.
In the power generation process, the wind’s kinetic energy is captured by the
rotor blades and transferred to the mechanical system of the turbine. A generator
converts the mechanical energy to electrical energy at a variable frequency which is
then transferred to the 50/60 Hz electrical grid by first converting it to DC, and then
from DC to AC at the right frequency in a tightly controlled process. The converter
process has many advantages, one is the capability of supporting the grid by injecting
reactive power, and another is higher efficiency[16].
Figure 1.1 shows an example of an off-shore wind-turbine with a grid-connected
converter integrated with the transmission grid.
When connecting a WT to the
transmission grid, the synchronous operation of the power converter must be strictly
controlled. The amplitude, frequency, and phase of the grid voltage are essential in
the synchronisation process, which is vital to ensure the converter system’s perfor-
mance. Therefore, the extracted voltage signal affects the converter system’s perfor-
mance, then affects the grid-connected operation, furthermore influences the entire
power system. However, the transmission grid is a complex dynamic system, and the
grid voltage is susceptible to various adverse factors, such as large grid impedance,
harmonics distortion, Etc.. Thus, the synchronisation algorithm must have a good
dynamic performance to ensure the converter and grid working in harmony.

Figure 1.1. Grid connection of off-shore wind-turbine
In the literature, for renewable-power system stability study, the impedance and
eigenvalue-based analysis are the most well-known methods. In the impedance-based
analysis, the stability is predicted by analysing whether the ratio of grid impedance
and grid-tied converter output satisfies the Nyquist stability criterion or not[10]. The
eigenvalue-based analysis identifies the system’s stability by using the eigenvalues of
the state-space system parameters.
In practice, there are many uncertainties when modelling a transmission grid af-
fecting the quality of the grid voltage. This thesis focuses on analysing the stability
of the converter system integrated with an uncertain grid, more specifically, the un-
certain transmission grid impedance. The project uses the robust analysis method.
Compared with the traditional methods, the robust analysis offers the advantage of
guaranteeing the analysed system’s stability for all considered perturbations.
The project’s main objectives consist of two parts, the system modelling, and
robust analysis. The first part includes the transmission grid and the grid-connected
converter modelling. In the second part, the robust stability and performance of the
modelled system is analysed based on the considered uncertainty perturbation.
1.1
Problem statement
The following questions will be answered and used as a guideline throughout the
project.
• How should a grid-connected power converter and grid been downscaled and
modelled, such that uncertainty in the grid impedance which is caused by the
long transmission cable between an off-shore wind-farm and a power-grid, is
presented? Which elements must be taken into consideration and which are
not?
• In a balanced three-phase grid voltage condition, when a fault occurs, how
should the converter system been controlled, such that the system maintains
synchronised with the grid and the current is able to maintain at the reference
level?

CHAPTER 2
Phase locked loop
This chapter is about the phase-locked loop(PLL), which is the essential unit for
the grid-connected converter system’s synchronisation operation.
In this chapter,
the structure of the selected PLL is firstly introduced. Secondly, the rotating frame
and stationary reference frame transformation are described. Then, the small-signal
model of PLL is established, and the controller is designed. Furthermore, the dynamic
model of PLL is tested in Simulink. Finally, the effect of the rotational frame on
the converter system is discussed.
2.1
PLL modelling
PLL typically consists of three essential parts: a phase detector (PD), a loop filter
(LF), and a voltage-controlled oscillator (VCO). Figure 2.1 shows the block diagram
description of a typical single-phase PLL.
Figure 2.1. Typical single-phase PLL
Focusing on the grid-connected applications, a significant challenge associated
with the PLL is how to precisely and fast estimate the phase and frequency when
the grid is disturbed.
Many different PLL structures have been proposed in the
literature[11], such as MAF-PLL and DQ-PLL, Etc.. Among these techniques, the
Synchronous-Reference Frame PLL (SRF-PLL) is one of the most popular and widely
used techniques owing to its simple digital realisation, low computational burden, and
effectiveness.

2.1.1
SRF-PLL
The SRF-PLL algorithm is used extensively in three-phase systems. In figure 2.2, the
block structure of SRF-PLL is shown [12].
Figure 2.2. SRF-PLL system
The SRF-PLL operates as a feedback servo system to instantaneously detect the
phase angle of the grid voltage. In this system, the three-phase grid voltages are
firstly measured. Then, the measured three-phase sinusoidal voltage is transformed
into DC quantities through the abc −dq transformation. The reason for utilising the
DC quantities is for designing a reliable and effective PI controller, such that the
phase error can be regulated. Meanwhile, the transformation allows the independent
control of the active component (d-axis) and reactive component (q-axis). The reac-
tive component contains information about the estimated phase angle error, and the
active component gives the amplitude information of the grid voltage in steady-state.
The abc −dq transformation is from the three-phase (A,B,C) to the stationary
frame (α, β) through the Clark transformation matrix and further to the synchronous
rotating reference frame (D,Q) through the Park transformation matrix.

PLL is an important part in the process when a grid-connected converter synchronises
with a grid. For modelling the converter system, it is beneficial by defining two frames:
the system frame and controller frame.
Figure 2.9. s frame and c frame

controller DQ frame is defined by the PLL, which tries to estimate the phase angle
and the frequency of the grid in order to reach synchronisation. The angle between

3.1.3
Modelling of the Voltage source inverter
The VSI connects to the power grid through a single power L filter [16]. In a power
converter, the DC power generated from the WT is converted to AC power by using
the VSI. The VSI uses PWM and H-bridge circuit to generate a square wave, and
utilising the filter techniques, and the waveform resembles a sine wave. A general
layout of such a process is illustrated:
==PAGE:24==
3.1 Structure of the control loop
19
Figure 3.7. Three-phase VSI
The main objective of a power converter is to produce an AC output from a DC
power supply. The output sine wave is controlled through PWM. To generate the
desired output, the power valves must be properly switched continuously. A standard
method for this purpose is the carrier-based PWM. It defines the on and off state
of the VSI one-leg switch by comparing a modulating signal m (desired AC output
voltage) and a carrier signal ∆m. If m > ∆m, the switch S+ is at on state and S- is
at off state; similarly, if m < ∆m, the switch S+ is at off and S- is at on state. The
switches are connected to each leg of an H-bridge as figure 3.8 shows.
Figure 3.8. H-bridge
The switches of any leg of the inverter cannot be switched on simultaneously, as
it would result in a short circuit across the DC voltage source. Similarly, to avoid
the undefined state in the VSI and undefined AC output line voltages, the switch
of any leg of the inverter cannot be switched off simultaneously. However, in order
to obtain the desired three-phase voltage, an L power filter is necessary to limit the
current harmonics which would arise due to the difference between the grid voltage
and PWM inverter voltage. Since the study does not focus on the designing of VSI,
the impedance of the filter Lf and Rf are selected base on existing work[16]. As a
==PAGE:25==
3.1 Structure of the control loop
20
result, this topology would provide a three-phase voltage with the desired amplitude,
phase, and frequency.
The same purpose can be achieved by replacing the PWM and the H-bridge with
an ideal controlled three-phase voltage source. This method offers the advantage of
reducing the modelling complexity by avoiding the switching mechanics and PWM.
Since the PWM and the H-bridge affect the converter system’s stability by the gen-
erated harmonics, and the effect of harmonics are not the focus of this study, the
technique is feasible.
The time delay caused by the switching process is considered and modelled with
Padé first-order approximation:

CHAPTER 5
Robustness analysis
In this chapter, the uncertainty in the transmission grid is firstly defined and mod-
elled as parametric uncertainty.
The robust stability of the closed-loop system is
justified using H∞norm of the M∆structure. Meanwhile, the system’s nominal per-
formance is evaluated based on the H∞norm of the weighted sensitivity function, and
robust performance is evaluated based on the H∞of the mixed sensitivity setup. The
controller’s effects on the system’s robust stability and performance are investigated.
5.1

Future work
In this project, in order to simply the modelling process as well as keeping the focus
on the main objective,numerous assumptions have been made. There are still open
questions needed to be answered.
• In the future work, it worth to study in depths how the PLL controller affects
the system’s robustness, i.e. utilising the root-locus analysis method to identify
the trajectory of the system poles, when changing the PLL’s bandwidth.
• The proposed method in this project is based on that the linearised model
is validate approximation around the operating point.
Since in large signal
modelling, the condition is no longer applied. More advanced technique such
as non-linear stability analysis should be used.
• The resonance caused by the long transmission cables at low frequency often
has a large impact on the system’s stability in practice.
• In non-ideal grid voltage condition, the SRF-PLL will no longer be able to
determine the phase angle correctly. A more advanced PLL technique should
be applied. Further more the abc-dq coordinate transformation will become
more complicated.
• Multiple uncertainties considered, including un-modelled dynamic’s uncertainty,
and the variability in the phase angle operating point is also modelled as an
uncertainty
• To expand the system model, where several converters are connected in parallel.
• It can utilise µ-analysis, which guarantee non-conservative analysis result.
• Instead of PI controller, implement H∞controller as the main control strategy.

Summary
Chapter 1, the background and motivation for this project are introduced.
Chapter 2 introduces two important transformations of the three-phase system,
the Clarke and the Park transformations, and their relation and usage in the PLL.
The dynamic model of the SRF-PLL is selected for this project and tuned according
to the open and closed loop bode plot. The system is tested in the time-domain
simulation with an input voltage of 60 kV and a phase jump of 10 degrees on all three
phases. The system design is proved to be stable and able to adequately track the
grid phase.
Chapter 3, the modelling of the transmission grid is firstly introduced. The prac-
tical transformer is modelled with the equivalent circuit. The impedance frequency
characteristic of the single and multiple PI-section(s) equivalent circuits are compared.
The result confirms that the single and multiple PI-sections have identical impedance
characteristics within a particular frequency range. The frequency range is bounded
by the cable length, cable coefficients. The transmission grid is represented with an
equivalent circuit, and the grid impedance is obtained. The current controller is de-
signed based on the closed-loop step response and bode plot. The designed system
is tested in the time-domain simulation with a current reference step and grid side
voltage of 60 kV with a phase jump of 10 degrees on all three phases. The test result
confirms that the designed system is able to synchronise with the grid and follow the
current reference.
Chapter 4, the system’s non-linear state space model is derived. According to
the step response comparison between the Simulink model and the non-linear state
space model, the derived model has a good accuracy. The model is linearised based on
the selected operating points. Two groups of systems, each with different controller
parameters, are implemented and linearised. The obtained LTI models are proved to
be stable, except when the PLL bandwidth is 70 Hz and current controller bandwidth
is 100 Hz. Moreover, it founds that the linearised model only approximates the system
model around the chosen operating point. The concluded stability and performance
of the system is therefore only validate for the select operating point. Furthermore,
the system is transformed into a feedback system composed by three subsystems,
and the transformed system is proved to have the exact dynamic as the original LTI
system.
Chapter 5, the linearised model’s robust stability and performance are evaluated.
The grid impedance with a variation of ±30% is identified as the uncertainty param-

Conclusion

eter of the linearised model. The complex uncertainty perturbation is presented with
the selected weighting function. The linearised controller plant is proved to be ade-
quate even though the grid impedance is uncertain. From the robust stability analysis,
it founds that the system is robust unstable when PLL bandwidth is 10 Hz, while
both a lower and wider PLL bandwidth are able to guarantee the system’s robust
stability. On the other hand, when increasing the current control loop bandwidth,
the system’s robustness margin becomes smaller (more difficult to satisfy the stabil-

performance analysis, the systems with lower current control bandwidth (50 Hz and
100 Hz) can achieve the performance specification with the considered uncertainty
perturbation. However, because the system becomes more sensitive to perturbation,
when current controller bandwidth is wider. The robust performance condition is not
satisfied for the systems with current control bandwidth at 150 Hz.
