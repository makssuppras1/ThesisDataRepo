Abstract
Robot assisted surgery is becoming ever more prevalent in medical applications. Total knee arthroplasty
surgery is one type of orthopedic surgery, that will gain high beneﬁts from the accuracy and efﬁciency of
robot assisted surgery. Total knee arthroplasty surgery is a surgical procedure whereby the diseased knee
joint is replaced with a prosthesis.
The purpose of this project is to investigate how robot technology and automation can be used to optimize
and improve an existing surgical procedure, so that it gains the efﬁciency and usability of the traditional
procedure while maintaining the accuracy and precision of CAS procedure. Thereby making total knee
arthroplasty surgery faster and more precise. Furthermore, design and implement a proof of concept system.
A proof-of-concept system was designed and implemented to acquire data points using camera and a laser
line projector. Isolating the laser line in the image using computer vision and gather 3D coordinates from 2D
projection of these on the image plane, proved successful. It was required to accomplish this with a precision
of 1.0mm.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Robot assisted surgery is becoming ever more prevalent in medical applications. Especially in microsurgery
where minimally-invasive surgical procedures are desired and high precision is required.
Orthopedic surgery is a ﬁeld where robot assistants have yet to be widely introduced. More speciﬁcally, total
knee arthroplasty surgery is an area that will gain high beneﬁts from the accuracy and efﬁciency that comes
from implementing a robot to assist during surgery.
Total knee arthroplasty surgery is a surgical procedure, where the diseased knee joint is replaced with
artiﬁcial components - referred to as the prosthesis. The knee is a revolute joint which provides single axis
rotation between the thigh bone (or femur) and the large bone of the lower leg (tibia). During a total knee
replacement, the end of the femur bone is removed and replaced with a metal shell. The end of tibia is also
removed and replaced with a synthetic meniscus and a metal stem 1.1(c). The knee joint is protected by a
kneecap, which - depending on the medical condition - may also be fully or partially replaced.
(a) Mechanical axis
(b) Knee joint
(c) Prosthesis
Figure 1.1: Diagrams of the mechanical axis, healthy knee joint and prosthesis.
The prosthesis placement is determined by the mechanical axis 1.1(a). The mechanical axis is a line drawn
from the center of the spherical hip joint to the center of the spherical ankle joint. The prosthesis must be
placed, so that its alignment is perpendicular to the mechanical axis. Furthermore, the prosthesis must be
rotated accordingly around the mechanical axis to ensure symmetric alignment with other knee joint, when
the knee joint is bent. This rotation may vary dependent on the patient (genu valgum or genu varum) and
must be determined by the shape of the femural condyle and tibia plateau 1.1(b).

CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
The synthetic meniscus is made of polyethylene (plastic) and is therefore prone to wear. To reduce wear
on the synthetic meniscus, force of the femoral and tibial components must be distributed uniformly along
the surface of the synthetic meniscus. Which means the risk of wear is highly correlated to the prosthesis
alignment. Surgery is needed to substitute the synthetic meniscus in the case that it is worn down to a level
of patient discomfort.
Study shows that the probability of need to do substitute the synthetic meniscus within a 15 period can be
minimized from a probability of 54% to 4.7%, by securing the precision of the prosthesis alignment within 3
degrees from the mechanical axis ().
1.1
Delimitation
A prosthesis alignment within 3 degrees from the mechanical axis can be achieved using Computer Assisted
Surgery (CAS). Unfortunately, CAS is quite time consuming compared to the traditional surgery procedure.
A traditional procedure last on average between 71 and 82 minutes (Sundberg et al. 2015). An investigation
made by the Danish Health Authority in 2007 shows that CAS on average lasts 27 minutes longer than the
traditional procedure (Jensen et al. 2007).
By analyzing the individual steps in the two surgical procedures in Appendix A, it becomes obvious that the
two surgical procedures share many sub-procedures and the main difference is way data about prosthesis
placement is acquired. The data acquisition of CAS is much more thorough and the data acquired has high
accuracy, whereas the data acquisition of the traditional procedure is based less accurate equipment and
relies heavily on the surgeons experience.
1.2
Purpose
The purpose of this project is to investigate how robot technology and automation can be used to optimize
and improve an existing surgical procedure, so that it gains the efﬁciency and usability of the traditional
procedure while maintaining the accuracy and precision of CAS procedure. Thereby making total knee
arthroplasty surgery faster and more precise. Furthermore, design and implement a proof of concept system.

CHAPTER 2
Analysis of Current Surgical Procedures
By analyzing the individual steps in CAS and traditional surgical procedures from Appendix A, it becomes
clear that the most time consuming steps in CAS compared to the traditional procedure, involves the data
acquisition.
CAS works by tracking the femur and tibia in 3D using an infra-red stereo camera setup, infra-red projection
and retro-reﬂective markers. Several instruments are tracked in real-time. Each instrument tracked in 3D has
an unique conﬁguration of markers, so that equipment type, position and rotation information is obtained
from the marker conﬁguration (referred to as arrays). Among these instruments are the so called femural
array and tibial array. These arrays are mounted directly into the femur and tibia bone, respectfully. Thereby
the position and rotation of the bones can be tracked in real-time. Another 3D tracked instrument is the
pointer. The pointer hand-held tool operated by the surgeon and it is used to track points in space. A second
hand operated instrument is also tracked in 3D, that instrument is called the cutting block. Cutting blocks
are used to guide the blade, when the bone is resectioned.
To summarize: Femural array (bone mounted on femur), Tibial array (bone mounted on tibia), Pointer tool
(hand operated instrument), Cutting blocks (hand operated instrument).
Figure 2.1: Picture showing 3D tracked instruments used in CAS
CAS data acquisition can be categorized into two main parts dependent on the method used to acquire data.
The ﬁrst part determines the mechanical axis and the second part determines the prosthesis placement along
and around the mechanical axis, as well as the desired prosthesis size.
The mechanical axis is found by determining the center of the hip joint and ankle joint. The center of the hip

Single landmarks are speciﬁc points located one the surface of femur and tibia, that needs to be acquired.
Whereas, multiple landmarks are whole surfaces that are acquired from gathering multiple points within a
certain area and model the surface. Moving the pointer in a circular motion, while keeping the tip of the
pointer in position, may increase accuracy of data point.
2.1
Identiﬁcation of bottlenecks
Data acquisition using the pointer is a time consuming process prone to errors, which shows the greatest
potential for improvement.
2.2
Requirements
Design a system that replaces the existing pointer based data acquisition system. System must be:
1. Faster than existing pointer based system. Preferably to a degree where CAS operating times can be
comparable to that of the traditional surgical procedure.
2. Minimum the same accuracy as existing system. Which is 1mm.
3. Design the system in such a way, that is entails the potential of extending its functions to replace even
more parts of surgical procedure.

CHAPTER 3
Design
There are several potential solutions to solving the problem presented in previous chapters. One possible
solution would be to perform CT scans pre-surgery and apply computer vision to gather landmarks. This
would decrease the duration of the surgery procedure but increase the total cost and number of consulta-
tions. Other potential solutions include real-time scanning peri-surgery. This could be achieved either by a
mechanical force sensing system or a camera based system with applied computer vision. The mechanical
force sensing system design was discarded because of concerns that it would be too slow.
A computer vision based system is therefore last remaining candidate. Such a system, shows great potential
for speed, accuracy ensures to be non-invasive since is will not be in physical contact with patient. Mounting
this system on a robot arm, further automates the data acquisition and increases the accuracy. The Inclusion
of robot arm further ensures potential for future development of automating more of the tasks currently
performed by surgeons during this procedure.

CHAPTER 4
Robot Arm Control
This chapter covers the design choices and implementation of functions related to the robot arm.
The system is designed for an UR-5 robot arm from Universal Robots. Controlling the robot arm is done
through existing driver software. Control input is passed to the driver software in the form of the desired
position of end-effector.
4.1
Forward and inverse kinematics
The problem of kinematics is to apply geometry in order to describe the motion of the manipulator without
consideration of the forces and torques causing the robot motion. Forward kinematics can be describes as, a
way to determine the position and orientation (pose) of the end-effector in Cartesian coordinates, given the
values of the joint variables of the robot. Opposite forward kinematic, inverse kinematic transformation is
that of ﬁnding the joint variables in terms of the end-effector position and orientation. (Spong et al. n.d.).
The forward kinematic solution is found using Denavit-Hartenberg convention, where a transformation
matrix can be describes using the so called Denavit–Hartenberg parameters, which describes the relation
between a joint and a reference frame. These parameters include constant values and joint variables.
Inverse kinematics is somewhat more troublesome and does not follow distinct pattern when solving an
inverse kinematics problem. Instead, it relies on mathematical isolation of joint variables using geometrical
equations given by the forward kinematic matrix of a given system, in order to determine the Cartesian pose
as a function of joint variables.
The UR5 robot arm does provide support for utilizing built-it Forward and inverse kinematics. Even so,
kinematics was implemented in C++ using a UR robots kinematic framework (?). The framework includes
methods of solving both inverse and forward kinematics problems. The forward kinematic function returns
the position of the end-effector relative to the base frame. The inverse kinematic function returns all possible
solutions for a given end-effector position.
The pose selected from inverse kinematics solutions is the one that results in the least movement of the robot.

5.1
Identifying laser beam in image
The ﬁrst crucial step of the computer vision is to determine center of laser line beam in each horizontal
column of the image and thereby acquire one point per pixel of camera resolution vertically (v direction)
and at sub-pixel precision horizontally (u direction) of image plane (u, v). This is done by applying a series
of image processing algorithms.

CHAPTER 5. COMPUTER VISION
Color ﬁltering in software has its limitations. An arbitrary color may consist of one (monochromatic) or
multiple wavelengths (polychromatic). Similar to the human eye, a camera sensor captures red, green
and blue wavelengths. Therefore, the camera can not distinguish a single wavelength color from a color
composed of two or several wavelengths. Hence, full spectral information is not available for each pixel,
only coordinates in a color space.
To account for this, a different approach - from wavelength ﬁltering - must be followed. Since the laser
is green, a color ﬁlter is applied by splitting the red, green and blue channels of the image in RGB color
space. A RGB value corresponding to that of a color with 532nm as its dominant wavelength, is given as:
RGB = (80, 255, 0) (Bruton n.d.), since it is slightly shifted towards the red end of the electromagnetic
spectrum. Through further investigations, it was determined that the red color of the laser was barely
noticeable and ﬁltering in the red spectrum was discarded. A bandpass ﬁlter is then applied to the only green
channel to roughly isolate the laser beam by means of intensity.

5.1.2
Canny edge detection
The Canny edge detector was invented by John Canny in 1983 (Siegwart et al. n.d.). It uses a multi-stage
algorithm to detect a wide range of edges in images, with the goals of:
• Maximiza signal-to-noise ratio.
• High precision on location of edges.
• Minimize no. of edge responses with each edge.
OpenCV has an function for using utilizing Canny edge detection.
Figure 5.4: Canny edge detector mask
Applying the Canny algorithm on the source image, yields the mask in Figure 5.4. It is clear that the edge
mask displays not just the outline of the laser beam, but also the outline of the bone. It should be noted that
this outline is quite clear partially because the object of scanning is an artiﬁcial bone in an experimental
setting. In a practical setting, the edges between bone and tissue will be much more blurred.
5.1.3
Combined color and edge mask
By combining the edge mask and color mask and performing a series of computer vision operations, the
beam can be identiﬁed. First, the edge and color mask are combined through pixel-wise logical and. The
following algorithm is then applied to the resulting mask:
1. Mask is dilated
2. Mask is inverted
3. Mask is ﬂooded
4. Mask is eroded.
5. Mask is inverted.
The above algorithm closes the edges around the beam and ﬁlls it. Single pixel errors are eliminated by
morphological operation; ﬁrst dilating and later eroding the mask. By ﬂooding the inversed mask (Figure
5.5(a)) while it is eroded, ensures that the edges around the beam is closed and therefore does not get ﬂooded.

5.1.4
Gaussian blur
Applying a Gaussian blur to an image is the act of convolving an image with a Gaussian function. It is often
used to smoothen images. The scattering seen in the cross-section of a laser line can similarly be perceived
as a Gaussian distribution, from the image pixel intensity perspective. Thus, ﬁtting a Gaussian proﬁle to the
intensity map of the laser line scattering proves to detect laser line center position with sub-pixel resolution
and accuracy (Molder et al. n.d.).

a representative intensity map. The resulting dilated mask is then applied to the green channel of the source

5.3.1
Optical analysis of scanned medium
The boundaries of the scan is determined using an optical light scattering and absorption analysis. This
analysis is tool which provides information regarding the material of the scanned object. This is done by
measuring the width (scattering) and intensity (absorption) of the laser beam in on the scanned object.
Optical properties of biological tissue differs dependent on cell type and density. In this project, there will be
differentiated between three different types of tissue; bone, skin and soft tissues.
The correlation between wavelength of light source and scattering coefﬁcient µs is can be approximated
using Rayleigh and Mie scattering

5.4
Model generator and ﬁt
The model generator is a part of CAS that generates model of the knee and acquire landmarks using surface
matching between data points and a generalized model. Data points from the point cloud are parsed to
CAS system model generator and together with the following information, the position of the prosthesis is
determined:
1. Center of ankle relative to tibia
2. Center of hip relative to femur
3. Distance between ankle and hip when leg is stretched.
5.4.1
Surface matching
The surface matching problem is complex computer vision problem, that matches objects based on points,
lines, curves surfaces and volumes. With respect to this project, the CAS system performs surface matching
based on points and the data acquisition part of the project is therefore design accordingly. Matching 3D
points to a corresponding point on 3D surface model is a difﬁcult, since there is no unique solution. Added
to the equation is the fact that the surface of bones is highly patient dependent. Surface matching data points
and knee models is therefore not within the scope of this project.

CHAPTER 6
Path planner
The information gathered by the computer vision part in the previous chapter forms the foundation of the
information needed by the path planner and movement control of the robot arm. This chapter describes the
path planner and movement of the robot arm.
The path planner is responsible for moving the end-effector from one point Pold to another Pnew at each
iteration. The new point Pnew is then transformed to joint coordinates using inverse kinematics and passed
to the robot controller.
The end-effector position can be determined relative to the CAS coordinate since the tool array is mounted
on the end-effector and the tool array is given in the CAS frame by the CAS system. The camera is also
mounted on the end-effector, thus having a constant transformation matrices to describe to relation between
all three end-effector mounted devices.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
The goal of this project was to investigate how robot technology and automation can be used to optimize
and improve existing total knee arthroplasty (TKA) surgical procedures, so that it gains the efﬁciency and
usability of the traditional procedure while maintaining the accuracy and precision of CAS procedure. In
order to solve this problem, the project was split into smaller sub-problems.
An analysis of the procedures currently applied, during TKA surgery, was performed to determine what parts
of the procedure showed greatest potential for improvement. It was found that developing an add-on for the
existing CAS system, to acquire data point fast and with high accuracy, showed the best potential. Imple-
menting the data acquisition system on a robot-arm secures a foundation for future work of implementing
more of the functions performed by the surgeon today.
A proof-of-concept system was designed and implemented to acquire data points using a camera and a laser
line projector. Isolating the laser line in the image using computer vision and gather 3D coordinates from
the 2D projection of these on the image plane, proved successful. It was required to accomplish this with a
precision of 1mm and speed greater than that of CAS. A precision < 1mm and sampling time faster than that
of CAS was accomplished.
Furthermore, a robot arm controller was designed and implemented, but unfortunately not tested properly
due to insufﬁcient time.
All in all, this project proves to provide a data acquisition system that can be integrated into CAS. the
computer vision based data acquisition system was designed and implemented to acquire key data points
related to determining the placement of the prosthesis and therefore where to place the cutting blocks.

CHAPTER 8
Future Work
Much time was put into investigating possible solutions, before setteling in the one presented in this project.
One of the reasons for deciding on this particular solution, is that it open up many new possible expansions
and future work. Some of these suggestions, are purely improvements to current systems, whereas others are
stand-alone projects to complement this project with regards to further automate the surgical procedure of
the total knee arthroplasty surgery.
8.1
Optimize ﬁltering
Future work could be to further optimize the ﬁltering part of the applied computer vision, in order to remove
noise and improve results. One expansion of such, will be to apply an optical lens ﬁlter to the camera with
a transmission corresponding to the 532nm wavelength of the line laser. This will easy the detection of
the center of the beam, since corresponding sums of wavelengths to that of the laser will be discarded, and
therefore not present in the spectral data.
8.2
Robot performed surgery
Since the system developed in this project was developed to assist in ﬁnding the prosthesis placement and
orientation, the next logical step - following the steps of the surgical procedure - and an evident expansion,
would be to use either another or the same robot arm for placing the cutting blocks. Using a robot arm to
place the cutting blocks will also eliminate the need of having several specialized cutting blocks for each cut,
which means a single universal cutting block could be used.
Further developing this would be to program the robot arm to perform the cut itself - with or without cutting
blocks. Or even ﬁxating the prosthesis after the cut has been performed. This does raise some safety concerns,
since surgical procedures are highly complex and there could be complications that only an experienced
surgeon can solve. Hence, the practical use might be limited.
8.3
Complementary stereo vision system
Since this project relies heavily on the CAS system and does not look at possible improvements of it, an
evident expansion would be to a design and implement a system that utilizes a stereo vision similar to that
