ABSTRACT
The demand to minimize greenhouse gas emissions and decarbonize the electrical system
has grown dramatically in recent years. For this to be achieved, the installation of renewable
energy resources, such as solar and wind, must increase and the conventional power plants,
such as coal- or gas-fired generators, need to be phased out. Due to the presence of variable
power flows, the operation of these renewable energy resources, which are inverter-based,
complicates and threatens the system’s stability.
Traditionally, the aforementioned conventional power plants are responsible for the
black-start services full filling the system operator’s criteria.
However the transition
to inverter based systems, requires the research of alternative solutions to provide black
start service. To this direction, extensive research is conducted to facilitate, among others,
the participation of wind turbines in the black-start market. All wind turbines in operation
today are grid following, namely they rely on an externally controlled AC grid voltage for
their stable operation. However, through grid-forming control capabilities wind turbines
will be able to contribute to creating and controlling the AC system’s voltage magnitude
and frequency.
The scope of this thesis is to test grid forming control algorithms’ performance in defined
simulation scenarios and test them in DTU Wind’s AC/DC WindPowerLab lab converters.
Having verified their operation through simulation tools study cases are adapted and tested
in the lab converters. This project could contribute in the offshore wind farms research
area focusing on the black start capabilities of the wind turbines. The obtained results
show that the converters are able to provide grid forming services, also they are able
to operate in parallel to form the grid, with quite promising performance. Finally, the
conclusions of this study could be used as the baseline for testing additional grid forming
control architectures, and, also, the parallel operation of converters to perform grid forming
control could be investigated further.

1
INTRODUCTION
1.1
Motivation
Over the past years the need for the greenhouse gas emissions to be reduced and for the
decarbonization the electrical system has increased significantly. As a consequence of
this need the installation of renewable energy (RE) resources, such as solar and wind,
has grown considerably. Wind farms (WFs) and photovoltaic parks are inverter-based
resources (IBR). This high level of IBR integration into the power system complicates not
only its operation, but also, challenges its reliability and stability, due to the introduction
of variable power flows produced by stochastic energy sources [1]. Furthermore, this
high-volume penetration of RE sources, that aims to replace the conventional synchronous
power production, causes the reduction of reactive power reserves, which, especially during
system outages, destabilizes the energy transmission from large-scale RE systems, such
as offshore wind power plants (OWPPs) [2]. Additionally, power electronic converters,
which provide the inertial decoupling from the grid, however, their use causes the transient
stability of the system to be reduced, increasing that way the risk of wide-area blackouts
[1].
Coal- or gas-fired generators and pumped-hydro storage have traditionally provided black-
start service, since they are able to meet the technical criteria set by the system operators.
However, the transition to a more sustainable energy production, leads to large conventional
generation plants being replaced by IBRs and non-traditional technologies. In that case,
the black-start services originated from conventional power plants are more costly and
time consuming, since deactivated conventional units consume time and fuel to start their
operation in case of emergency. For that reason, alternative solutions for enabling black
start must be introduced, to facilitate, among others, the participation of RE sources in
the black-start market.
In power systems with high RE penetration and if the system is able to support the energy
provision by alternative solutions, instead of the conventional power plants, black start and
islanding operation requirements have been established as options for wind power plants in
the ENTSO-E network codes [3]. State-of-the-art wind turbines (WTs) are already capable
of providing some services included in the restoration process such as:
• fast frequency response
• low-voltage ride-through (LVRT)
and can, also, meet more advanced criteria required by the grid codes, [4] such as:
• inertia emulation
• power oscillation damping
• reactive current injection
1

1. Introduction
According to [5] these capabilities can be achieved by leveraging the advanced functionalities
of the full-scale power electronic converters of modern WTs, while [6] highlights that WTs
with fast start-up capabilities could be included at an earlier stage of the restoration
process to provide reactive power support and pickup load. In that way they could decrease
the impact of a blackout event on the system, by reducing the restoration time and the
amount load that could not be supplied. However, the grid is generally not stable enough
during a blackout, so connecting the currently popular grid following WTs at the start
of the restoration phase could result in a recurrence of a blackout, and possibly cascaded
outages [7]. For that reason, grid forming control of WTs, which allows them to operate
together as an AC voltage source without relying on an external grid and supply load in a
power island, can instead help WPPs participate from an early stage in effective bottom-up
network energization.
Figure 1.1. Offshore Wind Farm Connected Through HVDC Network. [8]
Grid forming control of converters has been extensively investigated in order to integrate
RE sources at the distribution level for micro grids. According to [9], grid forming control
has only recently been included in high power applications, especially addressing black-start
energization by high-power grid forming renewable sources connected at the transmission
level. Thus, due to the fact that the risks of uncontrollable islanding are high even at
the distribution level, this technology has only recently been included in studies against
blackouts [10]. Furthermore, the majority of existing micro grids are AC systems, with
DC systems only recently gaining attention, as they provide the micro grid with greater
operational and control flexibility, strengthening its role in ensuring the reliability of future
power grids [9].
Traditionally, the majority of the OWPPs are connected to the grid through High Voltage
Alternate Current (HVAC). High Voltage Direct Current (HVDC) systems are becoming
more popular for the connection of OWPPs over the past few years. Even though HVDC
was used initially for bulk power transfer, they are gaining popularity in connecting OWPPs
due to the following advantages compared to HVAC [11]:
• fewer cables are required for the transmission of the same amount of power
• there are no reactive power losses

The extensive use of power electronic converters causes the increase of the harmonic

1.2
Thesis description and main objectives
All wind turbines in operation today are grid following, namely they rely on an externally
controlled AC grid voltage for their stable operation. However, considering the steadily
increasing share of IBR, new capabilities will be required. One of such capabilities is
grid-forming control, through which WTs contribute to creating and controlling the AC
system’s voltage magnitude and frequency.
Several grid-forming control algorithms have been proposed, among them Virtual
Synchronous Machine (VSM), Direct Power Control (DPC), Distributed PLL-based (dPLL)
Control and Power Synchronization Control (PSC), each one of them with their own
characteristics. Their applicability and performance in energizing the transmission and
onshore load has been investigated in [9].
The scope of this thesis is to test some of the aforementioned grid forming control algorithms
and implement them in a lab environment. DTU Wind’s AC/DC WindPowerLab laboratory,
where the experimental part of this thesis is implemented, will be presented in Section 1.3.
More specifically, the main objectives are:
• Implement grid forming control in the dq reference frame and compare the
performance with the integrated PR based control of the lab converters
• Perform simulations in Simulink© to test the performance of different grid forming
algorithms and evaluate their performance for specific test cases
• Adapt, implement and test these grid forming control architectures on a scaled-down
converter-based laboratory setup and compare the results with the simulation results
The grid forming control architectures that will be tested in this thesis are:
1. Fixed frequency grid forming control
2. Virtual synchronous machine grid forming control
3. Distributed PLL-based grid forming control

1.4
Thesis structure
The thesis is organized in 5 chapters including this introduction. After the general overview
of the Introduction, which states the motivation and the main objectives of this project,
the most general to the most specific aspects of the topic are discussed. More specifically,
the second chapter provides the reader with an overview of the voltage sourced converter
(VSC) model that is used for this study and the background knowledge for its control.
Following the principles of grid forming control are introduced and the control architectures
that are used for this thesis are presented, and, then, the simulation and lab results are
discussed. Finally, the conclusions and discussion regarding future work are presented.
The detailed description of each chapter is:
• Chapter 2: Introduces to the voltage source converter average model and provides
the theoretical background of its main control system components.
• Chapter 3: Presents the grid forming control principles and the dPLL and VSM
grid forming control architectures in detail.
• Chapter 4: Presents the Simulink© and the lab results, provides the comparison of
the two, where applicable.
• Chapter 5: The conclusions that are extracted from the thesis results are discussed,
including the main experiences when dealing with real-time simulations, and
additionally gives proposals for future investigation on the topic.

2
VSC AVERAGE MODEL
This chapter contains the theoretical background regarding the Voltage Source Converter
(VSC) average model that is utilized for the experimental part of this thesis and some
background knowledge regarding its control.
Comparing the Current Source converters (CSC), operating with thyristors, that were
previously broadly used for power transmission between AC and DC circuits, CSCs are
not able to control the reactive power and they cannot form the grid, as VSC models can.
Additionally, the development of Insulated-Gate Bipolar Transistors (IGBTs) resulted in
the improvement of the controlability of the converters due to their ability to utilize higher
switching frequency. In the majority of applications above 1 MVA Multilevel Converters
(MMC) are used. However, in power system studies that power electronics switching
transients, e.g. harmonic distortion, are not of interest, the average converter model can
be used instead of the MMCs.
The principle operation of the VSC average model is presented in detail in [17] while more
accurate models can be found in [18].
2.1
Two level voltage source converter average model
The average converter is a controlled voltage source, which means that it receives voltage
signals directly rather than switching pulses as in the detailed switching model. This
results in the average converter output voltage being a pure sinusoidal wave, since IGBT
switching is not represented. For both the average and the detailed model the control
circuits are similar, the only difference is that the switching model receives inputs usually
varying between -1 and 1, while the average model receives directly the AC voltage in
Volts. The main benefit of employing an average model is the reduction in simulation time
due to the use of bigger time steps and the ability to reduce modeling requirements, which
is particularly appealing for real-time modeling applications.

is connected to the grid.

2.2
Theoretical background of the VSC control system
In this section the basic components related to the control of the VSC system will be
described. More specifically, the reference frames, the PI controllers used for the control
implementation and the phase locked loop (PLL) will be presented.
2.2.1
Reference frames
The three-phase VSC control deals with simultaneous control of three phase signals,
sinusoidal voltage or current signals. For this purpose, the controller needs to be of higher
order and bandwidth if a sinusoidal command is to be tracked. The αβ and the dq frames
are the two main classes of two-dimensional frames that can provide a solution to this
issue.
The αβ frame transforms the problem of controlling a system of three half-bridge converters
to controlling two equivalent subsystems. On the other hand, the dq frame transforms the
problem to an equivalent DC command tracking problem, enabling the use of PI controllers.
Also, the representation of VSC systems in the dq frame enables analysis and design tasks
based on approaches commonly employed for power systems. The control in αβ frame has
the feature of reducing the number of required control loops from three to two. However,
the reference, feedback, and feed-forward signals are in general sinusoidal functions of
time. Therefore, to achieve a satisfactory performance and small steady-state errors, the
controllers need to be of high orders, and the closed-loop bandwidths must be adequately
larger than the frequency of the references. The dq frame based control offers a solution to
this problem. The signals are assumed to be DC waveforms under steady-state conditions,
allowing the use of controllers with simpler structures and lower dynamic orders. Moreover,
zero steady-state tracking error can be achieved if integral terms are included [21].

2.2.2
PI and PR controllers
In this section the principles of the PI and PR controllers will be discussed.
PI controllers
Proportional–Integral–Differential controller (PID controller or three-term controller) is
a control loop mechanism employing feedback and is widely used in industrial control
systems and a variety of other applications requiring continuously modulated control.
These controllers constitute mature technologies that have been widely employed in recent
decades. The proportional P controller is used in the control system of critical power
system components like traditional generator droop controllers. The error signal, which
is obtained by subtracting the real measurement from a reference value, is fed into PI
controllers, where depending on the type of controller (P, PI or PID) the error is integrated
or fed into the derivative term. In the case of P term, it is proportional to the error,
namely if the positive error is significant, the control output will be proportionally large
and positive. The I term accounts and integrates for the past values of the error signal.
11
==PAGE:24==
2. VSC average model
When the PI controller discriminates the error in αβ frame, and it calculates the desired
control command in the same αβ frame, it is called the PI controller in the stationary
frame. In cases where the PI controller discriminates the error in dq frame, and it calculates
the corresponding control command in the same dq frame, it is called the PI controller in
the synchronous frame [22]. For this report, the PI controller in the synchronous frame is
relevant.
The applicable range of the feedback gains for the PI controller is limited, thus, the
closed-loop bandwidth is finite. For this reason, the operation with AC signal references
introduces the phase and amplitude errors. In order to achieve an error-free control, it is
necessary to transform the corresponding signals in dq frame, at which they are constant
in steady state and the operation is error-free.
In principle the transfer function that dominates the operation of the PI controller is given
by the following equation:
GPI(s) = KP + KI

investigated, such as Ziegler–Nichols, Cohen–Coon and trial and error.
In the cases of DC signals being of interest, the performance of the PI controllers is optimal.

major disadvantages. They are not able to track sinusoidal references without a steady

possible. Using feed-forward into the control system results in the improvement of the
performance of the controller in the dynamic responsiveness and disturbance rejection.
When utilizing a feed-forward, it is possible to measure and account for disturbances before
they have a chance to damage the system, instead of waiting for the feedback from the

Phase-Locked Loop
A phase-locked loop (PLL) is a closed-loop system in which an internal oscillator is
controlled by a feedback loop to maintain the time of an external periodic signal. PLL
techniques are widely employed in a variety of fields, including communications, computers,
and modern electronics. They can generate synchronized frequencies with external periodic
occurrences, retrieve meaningful signals from distorted sources, and distribute clock-timing
pulses in complex control systems. Because it should work in sync with the grid, a grid-
connected power converter perfectly suits the PLL’s philosophy. It should phase-lock its
internal oscillator to a specific grid signal to provide an amplitude and phase-coherent
internal signal that may be used by various control system blocks. Moreover, the PLL
provides continuous information about the phase-angle and amplitude of the signal of
interest, generally the fundamental grid voltage, which allows space vector based controllers

3
GRID-FORMING
During the operation of a power system there are four factors that must be controlled and
balanced: voltage, frequency, active and reactive power. In the majority of the conventional
grids the voltage and the frequency are stabilized by interconnected synchronous generators.
Therefore, the converter-based generation units can rely on the voltage and frequency
provided and control the active and reactive power set-points.
The technological trend indicates that synchronous generation units are being phased out
in favor of converter-based generation [25]. Therefore, different control strategies must
be used in the converter-based generation units, in order to be able to provide the new
services, namely frequency and voltage control, rather than just support the system.
This chapter describes the grid forming control concept and provides the background for
the different control algorithms. The control architectures and the models used for this
thesis will be described in detail.
3.1
From grid following to grid forming
Power converters can be categorised as grid feeding, grid supporting, or grid forming based
on how they operate in an AC micro grid. The grid forming converters can be compared to
an ideal AC voltage source with a low-output impedance, setting the local grid’s amplitude
and frequency. Grid feeding power converters, on the other hand, are designed primarily for
delivering power to an energized grid. They can be thought of as a perfect current source
that is connected to the grid in parallel with A high impedance. It must be noted that in
this application this current source must be completely synchronized with the AC voltage at
the connection point, in order to regulate the active and reactive power exchanged with the
grid accurately. Finally, the grid supporting converters can be modeled either as an ideal
AC controlled current source in parallel with a shunt impedance, or as an ideal AC voltage
source in series with a link impedance. The output current/voltage of these converters
is regulated to keep the grid frequency and voltage amplitude close to their rated values [10].
The grid feeding control strategy is equivalent to grid following strategy that is most
widely used. Converters following this control strategy, in most cases, depend on the
synchronization with the main grid, using a synchronization unit for the estimation of the
system frequency and phase angle. In most of the cases frequency based synchronization is
used, namely a PLL is utilized. One of the main characteristics of grid following converters
is the absence of inertia. Grid following converters are able only to inject predetermined
power set-points regardless of load level, therefore they cannot ensure the stability of
the power system’s key variables, namely system frequency and voltage magnitude. Grid
feeding control requirements are currently set by transmission grid operators in grid-codes.

Grid supporting converters are considered to be an improvement of the grid following

for example, these converters won’t be able to regulate the voltage, since their goal is
to maintain voltage and frequency close to the predefined set-points, rather than control
these two parameters. On the other hand, the grid forming converters have the ability to
accurately control the output voltage amplitude and frequency creating, in that way, the
grid on their own.

As mentioned before, grid forming converters have the ability to form the grid, since they
are able to operate as voltage sources. This results in them constituting a very promising
technology to assist in higher renewable penetration in power systems and being broadly
used in low or zero inertia micro grids. The control logic is similar with that of a grid
following converter, however, what differentiates the two control strategies is mainly the
synchronization method that provides the correct rotation in the abc/dq transformation.

3.2.1
Structure of controlled frequency VSC system
According to [21], in controlled frequency VSC system, the voltage and the frequency are
controlled at the point of common coupling (PCC), resulting in the real and reactive power
exchanged at the PCC between the VSC system and the AC system being the by-products.
Some case studies that this control strategy is used, are:
• an islanded (off-grid) electronic linked distributed generation (DG) or distributed
energy storage (DES) unit that supplies a dedicated load or a cluster of loads
• a VSC-based HVDC converter system that provides a weak or passive AC system
• an uninterruptible power supply (UPS) that uses a VSC system as its power source
The control architecture that precedes the controlled frequency architecture is the grid
imposed frequency VSC system, in which the voltage at the PCC is formed but the
frequency is imposed by the AC system, according to [21]. This control architecture is used
mostly in electronically-coupled distributed generation units, VSC based HVDC systems
and FACTS controllers.

3.3
Grid forming control strategies
The control strategy currently used in the WTs, which is grid following especially in the
onshore WTs, instead of helping the restoration procedure can cause a blackout again. This
can happen because the grid at this phase is not stable enough and grid following WTs can
be proven to be a burden rather than support to the grid. However, the participation of
WTs in bottom-up network energization is possible if grid forming control is used, which
allows them to operate together acting as AC voltage sources without depending on an
external grid, being able to supply load in a power island. The control strategies that will
be discussed in this section are based on [9], which provides an analysis of the grid forming
control strategies applied to WT converters to enable WTs black start capability.
When the grid forming controller operates in islanded mode, the controller varies slowly
the phase angle and frequency of the island voltage during the re-connection transient
to re-synchronize with the grid voltage. For the synchronization with the grid, or if the
parallel operation of converters is considered, there are two types broadly used. The most
commonly used is the voltage-based synchronization, in which a PLL is used. Nevertheless,
voltage-based synchronization might perform purely at off-grid operation resulting in
instabilities, or unbalanced and distorted voltage conditions that can compromise system
stability. Power-based synchronization can be used instead, utilizing the structure of the
swing equation that rules synchronous machine dynamics. These two synchronization
methods can be equalized, considering that the PLL architecture can be altered to derive the
derivative term of the frequency, equivalent to inertia, and the speed variation, equivalent
to damping. This provides a more stable method, since it, also, allows the controller to
serve as the synchronization block.
Apart from the cascaded control, voltage CV and inner current loop CI, outer power control
loops CP and CQ act on the active (P) and reactive (Q) power regulation. The most
famous solution for the implementation of CP and CQ control loops is the droop control
scheme, initially used for synchronous generators operating in conventional grid, however
gaining a lot popularity in the micro grids operation. This method of three control levels
(three-level hierarchical control) depends on local measurements and does not require the
use of communication links, since it is based on the interconnecting impedance X
R ratio
for enabling power sharing in micro grids and simulates the self-regulation capability of
grid-connected synchronous generators. One issue that must be considered while utilizing
droop control scheme, is load-dependent frequency deviation, slow dynamic response caused
by filters used for power measurements and non-linear load-sharing caused by harmonics
under transients. A variable virtual impedance ZV adds harmonic droop characteristics
with additional damping, improving the performance of the outer power loops [29].
3.3.1

can provide the baseline for decentralized converter control schemes that are able to operate
both in grid connected and islanded conditions. The inertial characteristic of this control
architecture can enable frequency support provision and transient power sharing as primary
control actions. These functions can be based only on local measurements and do not

3.3.2
Distributed PLL-based (dPLL) control
A different approach than the VSM architecture is the dPLL, that uses voltage-based
synchronization, by utilizing a PLL for frequency control. According to [9], the d-axis
voltage reference is generated by the active power controller, while a frequency control
loop is used to control the q-axis voltage reference, instead of setting this to zero. In the
case of the measured q-axis voltage being larger than zero for a short time, the detected
frequency will increase. The output of the PLL provides an indication of the frequency
deviation from the set-point, as described in Equation 3.5:

When f < f∗, the frequency control produces a positive q-axis reference feeding to the AC
voltage controller. The voltage and current loops ensure that the q-axis voltage produced
by the converter will be according to its reference value produced by the frequency loop.
This results in an increase in the frequency measured by the PLL until becoming equal to
its reference. Similarly, when f > f∗, the frequency is reduced accordingly. This type of
frequency control is able to operate autonomously to contribute to the overall frequency
regulation. This control scheme can be quite flexible and provides a successful sequential
start-up of more than one WTs working together to form the grid. Also, provides an
automatic synchronization for WTs that are initially offline with minor influence on the
reactive power required to energize transformers and filters and ramp up the voltage and
start delivering active power to the grid [31]. In the Figure 3.8 the control structure of the
dPLL is depicted.

control loop provides the frequency control. However, for the experimental part of this
thesis, a different variant of the dPLL architecture has been adapted, employing active
power droop control in the frequency loop, while the d-axis voltage is provided by the
reactive power droop control. In Figure 3.9 the frequency control loop with the active

angle of the synchronous reference frame is following the angle of the system. If there is a
misalignment between these two, the PLL adjusts the angle used for the transformations
by changing accordingly the rotation speed of the frame, the frame is aligned with the grid
if the q-axis component is equal to zero. The PI controller of the PLL is responsible for
keeping the q-axis component equal to zero, which output is zero in steady state.

4.2.3
Discussion on VSM and dPLL simulation results
In this subsection a discussion regarding the simulation results presented in sub-sections
4.2.1 and 4.2.2 is presented. The comparison is based on the Figures 4.7 and 4.9 regarding
the voltage, and Figures 4.8 and 4.10 regarding the frequency.
In general, the presented VSM architecture has a more oscillatory behavior after each
simulation event, compared to the dPLL architecture. However, the controlled signals seem
to follow the given set-point at each case faster than the dPLL. This oscillatory behavior
of VSM could be attributed to the inertia and damping of the control, the values for the
relevant control parameters can be found in the appendix A. The oscillation mode T = Tα
kd
due to the inertia and the damping of the control could be the reason for these oscillations.
The VSM is tuned to be quite fast, so in that case damping is lesser and the control is
much more oscillatory. This could be proven to be an issue if the control is applied in a lab
converter. Another explanation for this oscillatory behavior could be the tuning of the outer
droops. More specifically, if the droop gains are reduced (kω, kf), then the oscillations
could be reduced. The dPLL architecture, on the other hand, presents an underdamped
voltage response (Figure 4.9), except at t=15sec when the load is disconnected, and the
reason for this oscillatory behavior could be the reduced damping at the system when the
load is disconnected. As far as the dPLL frequency (Figure 4.10) is concerned, it presents
oscillations after the load is disconnected and this could be attributed to the tuning of the
frequency control loop, since the PLL and the frequency control loop are strictly connected
in the dPLL architecture.

the integrated controllers, but for this project the mode C of the lab converters is used. So
in order to avoid dealing with this noise that would distort the results, the voltage control
disturbance feed-forwards are disabled. Finally, the DC side voltage is fixed by another

The current control loop of converter 2 is enabled before the voltage control loop to allow
the current flowing through the converter to be equal to zero. This is relevant for the
simulation, since the lab converter prior to the beginning of its operation is blocked, namely
resembles an open circuit and no current is flowing through it.
When the frequency control loop of converter 2 is enabled, converter 2 takes over and
actually controls the frequency. Gradually the PLL and the frequency control loop of
converter 1 are enabled, so converter 1 starts to participate in controlling the frequency
and the voltage of the q-axis. By controlling the q-axis component of the voltage (Vq)
to be equal to zero, the rotating reference frame is oriented so that its d-axis is aligned
with the alternating voltage. That way the magnitude of the voltage is controlled through
its d-axis component (Vd), while its frequency is imposed by the rate of change of the
(inverse) Park transformation angle (ωt), which is determined by the frequency reference.
During transients in the operation of the converter the rotating frame might be temporarily
misaligned resulting in Vq not being equal to zero. This error is eliminated by balancing
the active power in the load, extracting or consuming power depending, also, on what is
the state, in terms of power production or consumption, of the parallel converter.
In order to control the voltage magnitude, the reactive power loop injects or consumes
reactive power if needed. In this case that the load is mainly resistive but the fun of the
heat blower (load in the lab) might add some inductance to the load. Therefore, the load
in principle doesn’t consume reactive power and the excessive power is flowing through the
two converters. This could cause a problem in a real scenario, however for this thesis is
out of scope.

Comments on the results
• Step 1: The difference in the first step of the two startup sequences is that in the
second one only the operation of the first converter is initiated, while in the first
one both converters begin to operate. Thus, in Figures 4.17 and 4.18 oscillations
are present in both voltage components for the two converters, while in the second
case these oscillations are of much lower magnitude, since the transient is smaller.
In both cases the active and reactive power are equal to zero, since zero current is
flowing through the converters up to this instant. For the startup sequence No.1 the
PLL of converter 2 starts to impose the frequency, through the bias that acts as the
reference, until the frequency control is enabled.
• Step 2: In both cases, when the voltage reference is given, both converters follow
the reference, converter 1 because its voltage control is enabled and converter 2 since
it is the voltage seen from the rest of the system. In the case of the second startup
sequence, that converter 2 does not operate yet, the measured voltage is coming from
converter 1. At this instant, that the voltage of the system is the nominal, the load
consumes nominal power, equal to 6kW and converter 1 supplies the load. Converter
1, also, consumes reactive power.
• Step 3: In both sequences the reference for the active power is given to both controls,
while in the second sequence the PLL of converter 2 imposes the frequency to the
system, similar to the previously described event for startup sequence No.1. The
power reference is set to 3.5 kW and not 3 kW, following the nominal power of the
load, in order to counterbalance the losses in the system, since the power illustrated
in Figures 4.19 and 4.23 is the power seen by the converters, before the step-up

• Step 4: In startup sequence No.1 in this step the voltage control of the second
converter is initiated, causing an increase of the steady state voltage, allowing a small
steady state error, so the converters do not fight each other. In the second startup
sequence the operation of the second converter is initiated, causing the transient

control. For this to be achieved the droop gains must be equal for the two converters.
The behavior of the converters in the case of the second startup sequence is similar
to the one described before for the case of the first sequence.
• Step 8: This is the final step of startup sequence No.2 and now, also in this case, the
two converters share equally the power and the grid forming control responsibilities.

CONCLUSIONS AND FUTURE WORK
In this final chapter of the report, the main conclusions of the research work conducted
are presented. Moreover, a discussion on the future work in the field is included, based on
these conclusions and the overall experience gained on the subject.
5.1
Conclusions
Following the work presented in [12], where the control modes of the converters available
in the DTU Wind’s AC/DC WindPowerLab laboratory was duplicated and evaluated in
Simulink©, in this thesis the focus is on the grid forming control capabilities of the lab
converters, especially focusing on the 2 level converters. The integrated control of the 2
level lab converters is established in the αβ reference frame based on PR controllers.
The first objective of this project was to implement the cascaded control of the converters
in the dq reference frame, based in PI controllers. The control realized in the dq reference
frame is the starting point for the implementation of different control architectures other
than the integrated control of the lab converters. After the desired conversion to PI based
control was implemented it was compared with the performance of the PR based for a
sequence of events. The goal of the applied events was to test whether the PI based control
was able to change the operating point of the islanded system, changing the frequency and
then the voltage set-points, and if it is able to withstand a transient event, such as the
load disconnection, and maintain the stable operation of the system.
After it was confirmed that the performance of the PI based control was the expected one,
in some cases it even outperformed the PR based control, the focus was shifted towards the
implementation of grid forming control architectures and their experimental verification
in the lab environment. Firstly, the fixed frequency control architecture was tested in a
lab converter, where the converter had to form the grid and supply a resistive load in an
islanded system. For this to be implemented the mode C of the lab converters operation
had to be used, where the control signals are sent directly to the converter modulation
through the real time computer in the lab. Following a similar event sequence, the fixed
frequency control was able to adjust to the new operating set-points and withstand the
transient event of the load disconnection.
Having verified the operation of the main control loops, different control architectures were
initially simulated and then adapted in order to be tested in the lab. More specifically,
based on the analysis presented in [9] the VSM and dPLL grid forming control architectures
were initially simulated. The simulation results indicated that both control architectures
are able to impose the desired frequency and voltage set-points to the system and maintain
them after the transient event of the load disconnection. However, differences in their
transient behaviours were observed and a qualitative discussion was presented. It has been
55
==PAGE:68==
5. Conclusions and future work
shown that the low bandwidth of standard cascaded control structure of dPLL reduces the
damping in the system and can bring the system closer to instability.
Finally, the dPLL architecture was tested in the lab converters, and, more specifically,
the parallel operation of grid forming converters was investigated. In this case two lab
converters were forming the grid and were supplying the same resistive load, as mentioned
before. Both converters were employing the dPLL control architecture, having the same
control parameters and receiving the same power references, in order to equally share the
load and work together in forming and controlling the grid, instead of one converter forming
the grid and the other one following. The startup sequence of this case was challenging,
since the control loops of the converters had to be initiated in a way that they won’t start
fighting each other on which converter will dominate. After applying a similar sequence
of events, as in the previous case, it was observed that the converters equally shared the
power and maintained the stable operation of the grid in different voltage and frequency
set-points. Also, the converters were able to withstand the transient event of the load
disconnection and maintain the islanded system in a stable condition. This is a promising
result and could be the starting point for the investigation of different lab setups and
different grid forming control algorithms.
Real-time applications are one of the most state-of-the-art procedures, in order to validate
offline models, such as systems developed in Simulink© software. It has been attempted
to comprehend the theory underlying situations in the software domain, and then when
implementing the model in the real-time computer, it was realised that the real-time world
discloses the consequences of the non-ideal qualities that exist in nature. Aside from the
results, the general technique for developing and building models for real-time use is not
so simple. Overall, the experiences gained through the current thesis project’s real-time
applications can be applied to research activities that will bring in the surface more grid
forming control capabilities and will test its capabilities in even more real-time scenarios.
5.2
Future work
In this section proposals for further research are given, in order to take advantage of the
obtained results of the current thesis. In particular:
• In the case of the parallel operation of grid forming converters employing dPLL
control architecture, a different setup could be used, namely a different type of load,
e.g. inductive, that will bring into the surface more challenges for the converters.
Also, electrical distance could be added between the converters, that would add
losses to the system, and then the behavior of the active and reactive power could
be further investigated. Also, the behavior of the reactive power could be further
examined and a scenario were it should be limited could be considered and how the
converters could assist with this task.
• Following the VSM simulation results, an attempt to test this architecture in the
lab converter was made, however the obtained results were not satisfactory, thus,
they are not presented in this report. For that reason, considering that VSM is a
very promising algorithm for grid forming, further investigation for the real time
simulations of VSM should be performed, in order to obtain the desired results.
56
==PAGE:69==
5. Conclusions and future work
• The parallel operation of grid forming converters in the lab should, also, be
investigated, but this time one converter employing the dPLL architecture and
the second one the VSM architecture. This is, also, a case study investigated in the
current project, however, more research should be done on how to make these two
different control algorithms, one uses voltage based synchronization while the other
power based synchronization with the grid, cooperate and equally share the power of
the load.
• Simulate different grid forming control architectures mentioned in [9], such as Direct
Power Control (DPC) and Power Synchronization Control (PSC), and, then, adapt
them for real time simulations in the lab converters.
• Regarding the DTU Wind’s AC/DC WindPowerLab lab, the high level of noise in
the load current measurements should be filtered out in order to be able to use
the disturbance feed-forwards in the voltage control loop, which might improve the
performance of the loop.
