Abstract
Rotor vibrations in wind turbines represent an important aspect to consider in
both the design and operational phase of these structures.
During a wind turbine’s lifetime vibrations are assessed by tailored monitoring
schemes, which make use of sensors placed across the structure to keep track of
its behaviour, enabling the controls to react before the occurrence of possible
malfunctions.
This work addresses the optimization of the rotor vibrations detection, an activ-
ity that can be disturbed by the harmonic excitations of the turbine due to the
rotor rotation. A misinterpretation of the vibration level can lead to reactions
of the controllers that move the operational set up out of its optimum.
Most of the modern wind turbines are equipped with accelerometers on the na-
celle and the hub. This study will then be based on the analysis of the data
acquirable from these devises.
Several tools and strategies have been adopted to analyse such data.
In all
the cases the investigation has exploited features that characterize the dynamic
behaviour of wind turbines.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Wind turbines are structures subjected to several kinds of dynamic loads. Turbulence pro-
vokes wind speed ﬂuctuations on time, variation of the mean wind velocity in the vertical
proﬁle, blades tower interaction, all of these actions produce dynamic forcing on the turbine.
On the other hand, the eﬀorts to maximise the eﬃciency of these machines have brought
to stretch the blades more and more, making these elements more slender and consequently
more vulnerable regard dynamic issues.
This means that negligence in the design and
monitoring of the dynamics of turbines can undermine the safety and eﬃciency of these
machines.
Vibrations can in extreme scenarios provoke critical load levels, creating a threat to
the integrity and safety of the turbine. However, even for less severe vibration levels, the
structure can suﬀer problems like fatigue, which manifest their eﬀects in the long term,
aﬀecting the durability of the turbine.
It is then clear the relevance of the assessment and control of vibrations.
This project focuses on the optimisation of an alarm system used on monitoring operations.
The signals acquired from accelerometers in the nacelle and hub are used to assess the status
of the turbine and feed the controls system for possible reactions. One of the more signiﬁcant
sources of rotor vibration is related to the edgewise modes excitation, which in general is
recognisable in the measurements of the side-side accelerations of the nacelle.
The major cause of excitation of the turbine is related to the harmonic forces that develop
for the revolutions of the rotor. In a 3-bladed wind turbine these forces rise at the frequency
equal to the triple of the rotational speed (3P), and its multiples.
When the the rotational speed is such that 3P approaches the frequency of the backward
edgewise (BWE) mode, the risk of resonance increases and assessment of the vibration level
becomes more important. At the same time, the assessment of the BWE mode excitation
is more clear because the side-side accelerations of the nacelle are aﬀected from both the
BWE mode excitation and the harmonic forces related to the rotor speed.
1.1
Scope of the research
The aim of this project is focused on the deﬁnition of a method able to identify and separate
the level of the blade’s edgewise vibrations from the others source of vibrations. The chal-
lenge stands on the fact that this must be done by assessing the sensors normally installed in
a wind turbine. These sensors are usually available in a limited number, and their location
is restricted to the tower top and hub, elements not only aﬀected by the rotor vibrations
1
==PAGE:18==
but also from the dynamics of other nature.
1.2
Motivations
In wind turbines the monitoring systems gather the information available from sensors and
evaluate the status of the structure based on these information.
In case the measured signals do not respect the assigned thresholds, an alarm is triggered
and the controllers bring the turbine back to ordinary operation.
This is done by varying rotor speed, power and blade’s pitch angle.
A misinterpretation of the rotor vibrations may lead to false positives and move the
turbine operational conﬁguration out from the optimum. An optimization of the monitoring
system’s capabilities would then beneﬁt the eﬃciency of the turbine.
1.3
Methodology
The methods presented are based on the knowledge of how an edgewise rotor whirling mode
will contribute to the response at the diﬀerent measurement points. Each method will be
tested to validate its goodness and robustness.
For this reason the project needs to be based on tools that: can simulate the behaviour of a
wind turbine with a good level of ﬁdelity and give the possibility to evaluate with accuracy
the entities of the modal components related to the rotor modes.
The behaviour of the turbine is simulated with HAWC2, the non linear aeroservoelastic
code developed and distributed by DTU Wind Energy [5]. The software, conceived and
designed to simulate the behaviour of vertical and horizontal wind turbines, oﬀers wide free-
dom to set up the structural model and external conditions. Further, the outputs of the
simulations can be evaluated in a great number of parameters that describe the behaviour
of the turbine all across it.

Theoretical background
2.1
Structural dynamics notions
The characteristics of the dynamic behaviour of a structure results from the forces whom the
structure is subjected and the inherent characteristics of the structure. The former is the
sum of the dynamic loads applied across the turbine; the latter are notoriously deﬁned by
natural frequencies, mode shapes, and damping, which are in turn deﬁned by the distribution
of masses, stiﬀness and damping over the structure. Natural frequencies, mode shapes, and
modal damping coeﬃcients represent the modal parameters of the structure.
In general, a structure experiences increments in its vibration level when the external
forces distribution resembles the shape of a mode and is applied with a frequency close to
the natural frequency of the mode itself. These conditions, combined with small values of
damping, determine the occurrence of the phenomena known as resonance, which leads to
vibrations escalation.
2.2
Wind turbine dynamics
For deﬁnition, a machine is a structure with parts that move relative to one another. This
feature implies that the dynamics of machines acquire slightly more complex characteristics.
This is due basically because gravity, centrifugal and gyroscopic forces change the charac-
teristics of the mechanical system over diﬀerent operational points, which implies that the
natural frequencies and mode shapes change as a function of the rotational speed.
Wind turbines are contained in this family.
In addiction, wind turbines are strongly aﬀected by aerodynamic forces, and this col-
locates this machines in a further subgroup, which gather the aeroelastic structures (and
machines). Aeroelasticity is, in fact, the discipline that studies bodies aﬀected by elastic,
inertial , but also aerodynamic forces, explaining the eﬀects and interaction of these three
kind of forces. In this kind of structures a further component of damping is introduced.
This additional term, known as aerodynamic damping, can strongly change between modes
and operational points.
2.2.1
Wind turbine modal analysis
The modal analysis of a machine can be performed once the mathematical model of the
system is deﬁned.

ment revolves around the elliptical trajectory with opposite direction of the rotor rotation.
Conversely, in forward modes the rotor center revolutions proceed in accordance with the
blades rotation.

3.3
Incoming wind characteristics
The incoming wind plays an important role on the dynamic excitation of a wind turbine.
The mean wind speed determines the operational point in which the turbine operates. It
follows that the wind speed aﬀects the mode shapes, their natural frequencies, and the
damping level (See Figure 3.3).
Another important characteristic of the wind regards the turbulence. The turbulence aﬀects
the dynamic behaviour of the turbine in two opposed ways:
• Low turbulence implies small variations of the rotational speed. For this reason the
harmonic forcing related to the rotor speed is more stable, favouring the development
of resonances.
• High turbulence winds excite the turbine with a higher intensity. This means that any
mode can be easily excited.
The selection of the mean speed and turbulence intensity (TI) have been driven by the at-
tempt to maximise the excitation of the edgewise rotor mode.
The selected value is 16 m/s. This value was chosen for the following reasons:
• Over 12 m/s the rotational speed is stable
• From 16 m/s on the damping level is approximately at its minimum
This is conﬁrmed in Figure 3.4, where the trends of the ABW component for a mean
speed of 12 and 16 m/s are compared.
The simulations have been run on the Nrel 5MW turbine with the rotor with original
damping. The seeds used on them are diﬀerent. However it is clear that with 16 m/s the
level of excitation is higher.

Method 1: Side-side accelerations
analysis
As introduced in Section 1.1, the scope of this study consists in the identiﬁcation of the
edgewise vibrations level when the 3P frequency is in the proximity of the BWE natural
frequency.
This is attempted by analysing the accelerations measured at the nacelle and hub, measure-
ments commonly available.
The analysis conducted will be based on data simulated both in Hawc2 and BHawC.
The ﬁrst is the non linear aeroservoelastic code developed by DTU Wind Energy, a tool
which oﬀers a great ﬂexibility in the choice of the situations to simulate, lending itself to
the project’s purposes.
The latter, BHawC, is the analogous code developed in-house at Siemens Wind Power. The
simulations in BHawC were instead used to have data from the turbine upon which the
scope of this project focuses on.
The method aims to detect the edgewise vibrations by analysing the side-side acceler-
ation. The analysis is done using the technique of the Short-Time Fourier Transformation
(STFT), which makes use of a moving window through time series.
4.1
Concept
The dynamic behaviour of a structure, and consequently its response in terms of accelera-
tions, result from the combination and interaction of the dynamics of the elements which
make up the structure itself.
In case the rotor speed is such that 3P is overlapped to the natural frequency of the backward
edgewise mode, the side-side accelerations are mainly related to the following contributions:
• Excitation of the side-side tower mode at its natural frequency
• Excitation of the backward edgewise rotor mode at its natural frequency
• Excitation of the side-side tower mode due to the rotational speed (at 3P and its
multiples)
Although the natural frequency of the side side tower mode is substantially lower that the
one of the 3P forcing, the tower top accelerations are highly inﬂuenced by the excitation of
14
==PAGE:31==
this mode at its natural frequency.
The backward edgewise mode excitation may in some extent be severe because for the
closeness between the frequency of the forcing, 3P, and the natural frequency of this mode.
However this cannot be given for grant because there are other factors that can limit this
contribution: ﬁrst, the damping associated to the backward edge mode can attenuate the
vibrations, especially in case this mode couples with a ﬂapwise one; second, turbulent ﬂow
implies a ﬂuctuating rotor speed. This means that the driving force may not remain at the
natural frequency of the mode for long periods, hence resonance would not build up.
The excitation of the edgewise rotor modes aﬀects the tower top accelerations mainly in the
side side direction, while the ﬂapwise modes aﬀect especially the for aft tower top behaviour.
Finally, the tower experiences an excitation of the side side tower mode at the frequency
of the harmonic forces (3P and its multiples). This forces are due to the blades-tower inter-
action, wind shear and turbulence sampling. The characteristics of the rotor excitation due
to turbulence were presented by Kristensen in [4].

4.3
Method execution
As mentioned in the presentation of the method, the complexity of the dynamics of a wind
turbine makes hard to interpret the mere reading of the accelerations in time domain. A
frequency analysis is then the ﬁrst step to ease its understanding.
This method attempts to ﬁnd a correspondence between the trend of the blades response
whirling component ABW and the side side tower top accelerations contents at ωBW (BWE
natural frequency).
The procedure assumes to track the trend of this parameters on time, thus the frequency
analyses must be performed in a moving window. The technique is known as Short-Time
Fourier Transformation (STFT) and, in general, is applied to remedy the weakness of the
Fourier analysis on the detection of non stationary eﬀects.
This approach represents a
compromise between the time and frequency analysis of a signal [2].

The whirling rotor amplitudes are determined based on the edgewise blades root bending

The choice of a small NFFT implies short time windows, consequently the extraction of
whirling components is averaged in short time spans, providing localized information. The
drawback of a small NFFT is related to the coarseness of the frequency spectrum.
The number of frequencies (Nf) by which the spectrum is discretised is equal to NFFT/2+1,
covering a frequency range that spans from 0 to Fs/2, where Fs indicates the sampling
frequency. Obviously the choice of a high NFFT brings to the opposite situation: high
frequency spectrum reﬁnement but averaging over long time segments.
The rate of the steps forward (Nstep) determines the extend of the overlapping between
windows and consequently the number of windows where the analysis is performed. In order
to obtain a ﬁne distribution of the whirling component values, small steps of the window
have to be taken.

The analysis of the side side accelerations content around the natural frequency of the BWE

The method application on data simulated with the Nrel 5MW model provided scarce

The application of the method to the Siemens turbine provided better results however the
method could not oﬀer a satisfying reliability.
The method was also applied on measured data. In this cases the detection improved sub-
stantially but the capability of the method should be validated with an analysis that involves
information from all the blades.
One of the main factor that undermines the eﬀectiveness of the method seems related
to the fact that the tower is strongly aﬀected by the dynamics of several diﬀerent causes.

Chapter 5
Method 2: Rotor center movement
ﬁtting
Assumed the side side accelerations can not provide satisfying information about the exci-
tation of the backward edgewise mode, this second method will focus on the rotor center
movement.
The idea is to evaluate the excitation of the rotor modes by observing the similarity be-
tween the actual motion of the rotor center and the whirling movement that characterizes
this point in the BWE mode.
In order to highlight this typical behaviour, the analysis addresses the relative motion of the
hub respect the tower top.
The sensors installed on wind turbines provide only information in terms of accelerations.
Nevertheless, for practical reasons this method is based on the analysis of positions. Should
the method be promising, its adaptation to accelerations will be done in a further stage.
5.1
Concept
The nomenclature of the asymmetric rotor modes is inspired from a peculiarity of the rotor
center motion: in the backward mode the rotor center movement experiences a circulatory
motion, revolving in the opposite sense to the rotation of the rotor. Inversely, in the forward
mode the revolutions of the rotor center have the same sense of the rotor rotation.
In a horizontal axis wind turbine the vertical and horizontal rigidity of the rotor support is
diﬀerent, this implies that the trace of the rotor center takes an elliptical shape. The ellipse
develops at the rate of the mode periodicity and its size is function of the excitation of the
mode itself.
A description of the characteristics of wind turbines mode shapes was given in Section 2.2.3.1.
The method attempts to track the excitation of the backward whirling mode by ﬁtting
the position of the rotor center into the ellipse associate to the backward edgewise mode.
The evaluation of the mode excitation is done by a single coeﬃcient, which accounts for the
goodness of the ﬁtting and the size of the ellipse.
In order to prove the eﬀectiveness of the method, the ﬁtting coeﬃcients are matched
with the trend of the amplitude of the BWE component ABW .
32
==PAGE:49==
5.2
Background theory
This method exploits the characteristics of the dynamic behaviour of the turbine in a speciﬁc
mode shape, the backward edgewise mode.
An overview of the theory of modal analysis of wind turbines was presented in Chapter 2.
One of the characteristics of the backward edgewise whirling mode stands on the fact that the
rotor center follows a whirling movements in the opposite direction of the rotor revolutions.
Also, the scale of the ellipse traced by rotor center is proportional to the mode excitation.
In order to isolate and assess such behaviour, the accelerations of the rotor center are
ﬁltered to keep only the content at the BWE natural frequency. This is done by means of
a second order band-pass ﬁlter.
5.2.1
Band-pass ﬁlter
A band-pass ﬁlter is a tool used in signal processing to attenuate or removing the frequency
content that lies out of a selected frequency band.
There are many diﬀerent ways to ﬁlter a signal, however in this case the signal is handled
with a second order digital ﬁlter. The transformation executed by the ﬁlter is based on the
rational transfer function, whose formulation is reported in Appendix C.

5.3
Method execution
The procedure used to apply and test the method starts with the deﬁnition of the ellipse
traced by rotor center on the whirling mode investigated. The ellipse is then ﬁtted on the
trace of the actual motion of the rotor center. The procedure ends up with the deﬁnition of
a ﬁtting coeﬃcient that is expected to indicate the excitation level of the backward edgewise
mode. The method is applied all along some of the simulated time series.
The steps used to deﬁne the ﬁtting coeﬃcient are here listed:
• Deﬁnition of the ellipse’s shape related to the backward edge mode
• Deﬁnition of the actual backward edge mode period
• Rotor center motion preparation
• Identiﬁcation of the scale and residual of the ﬁtted ellipse
• Determination of the ﬁtting index as a function of the scale and residual determined
in the previous step
Note that the method assumes that mode shapes and natural frequencies are known. Here
it is supposed that they are deﬁned in the design phase and/or in case the analytical model
is tuned based on experimental data.
This issue does not jeopardise the accuracy of the method because, unless severe damages
of the turbine, mode shapes and natural frequencies do not change drastically and the pro-
cedure does not have a high sensitivity respect these parameters.
As in the previous method, the method will be tested by calculating the whirling com-
ponents in a moving window all along the time series, and comparing them with the of the
ﬁtting coeﬃcients.
In the next sections it is explain in detail the content of the steps aforementioned.
5.3.1
Deﬁnition of rotor center motion in the whirling modes
The geometrical characteristics of the ellipse traced by the rotor center in the whirling modes
is obtained from the modal analysis performed in HawcStab2. Among the other functions,
HawcStab2 oﬀers the possibility to execute the aeroelastic modal analysis of the turbine,
which provides natural frequencies and mode shapes related to the selected operational
range.
The mode shapes are described in the Modal amplitudes ﬁle exported from HawcStab2. The
ﬁle provides modal amplitudes and phases of the more signiﬁcant degrees of freedom: Tower
top displacements, hub displacements and blades tip displacements.
Once the amplitudes and phases are known, the motion of the rotor center is deﬁned by
using the following formulation:

backward edgewise mode (see Figure 4.4).

natural frequency in other ways. For instance by assessing the incoming wind speed or the

In all the cases the comparison between the whirling component and the ﬁtting index sug-

In general only few peaks of the BWE component are detected. In correspondence of many

negatives, which are a serious deﬁciency in monitoring systems.
In some cases the results show also the presence of false positive, where the ﬁtting index
reaches fairly high values even though the BWE amplitude is limited.
Despite the signal has been treated with the intent to eliminate the rotor center behaviour
extraneous to the backward edgewise mode, the results brought to light that some disturbs
remained.
The results suggest that the harmonic forces that manifest with a frequency of 3P may have
a strong impact also on the revolutions of rotor center. In this case, the closeness of 3P and
ωBW would prevent completely the elimination of such disturbs.

Chapter 6
Method 3: Hub accelerations analysis
- time domain
The results of the methods developed in Chapter 4 and 5 brought to light several diﬃculties
on the detection of the rotor modes excitation. The reason is linked to the impediment to
separate the rotor modes behaviour from the harmonic excitation of the rotor due to its
rotation (3P).
The previous attempts have made clear that the behaviour of the tower top and hub are in
some extents aﬀected by the 3P eﬀects. It is then natural assume that the challenge stands
on the identiﬁcation of the structural behaviour dominated by the rotor modes.
This is why this method tries to dig into the inmost characteristics of the structural behaviour
related to the backward edgewise mode. In particular, it investigates the pattern of the
accelerations that develop at the rotor center.
A deeper understanding of the behaviour related to the modes may provide indications on
how the accelerations should be addressed in order to detect easily the rotor mode excitation.
6.1
Concept
The method deals with the analysis of the structural behaviour related to the backward
edgewise mode.
This procedure will attempt to assess the rotor mode excitation by the identiﬁcation of the
peculiarities of the rotor center accelerations in the backward edgewise mode.
The reconstruction of the BWE mode behaviour will be based on the equations that govern
the kinematics of the turbine and the modal amplitudes obtained by the modal analysis
performed in HawcStab2.
In the theory behind the method will be shown that in the backward edgewise mode,
the side side accelerations of the rotor center contain a substantial contribute due to the
rotations of the tower top.
This suggests to investigate the excitation of the BWE mode subtracting the side side
accelerations of the tower top. The operation should remove part of the 3P harmonic eﬀects
that disturb the detection of the BWE mode and, at the same time, retain the behaviour of
the BW mode in the tower top rotations.

Method 4: Hub accelerations analysis
- frequency domain
Method 4 maintains its focus on the analysis of the rotor center side side accelerations re-
spect the tower top (¨uG,rel
rc,x ).
Unlike the Method 3, the accelerations ¨uG,rel
rc,x
are addressed in frequency domain. Further-

7.1
Concept
The basic concept is the same of the method developed in Chapter 6.
Also the choice to analyse the data in frequency domain, rather than time domain, is some-
thing already applied and presented in the previous parts of this work (Chapter 4).
What is introduced in this method regards the evaluation of the reliability of the results.
All the results of the previous methods showed that the disturbs due to the 3P eﬀects
are inseparable from the eﬀects of the rotor modes excitation.
This brought to deﬁne a reliability factor able to indicate, all along the time series, the
entity of the level of the disturb, therefore the reliability of results.
The factor is built based on the rotational speed of the rotor and exploits the fact that the
whirling modes do not cause any torque into the shaft, while all the 3P eﬀects do.
7.2
Background theory
The theory behind the construction of the reliability factor is strictly related to the charac-
teristics of the whirling rotor edgewise modes.
Section 2.2.3.1 showed that in the asymmetric rotor modes the blade vibrations do not cause
any variation of torque in the shaft.
On the other hand, 3P eﬀects, which are mainly due to turbulence sampling and wind shear,
are unbalanced across the rotor. This means that their eﬀects provoke variations in the shaft
torque and then changes on the rotor speed.

• Transformation of the rotor center accelerations from local to global reference frame

The ﬁrst two steps are identical of the ones presented in the Method 3. The others are here

In each window the FFT is applied and the amplitude at the BWE natural frequency is

7.3.2

The disturbs on the detection of the BWE excitation are due to presence of the 3P eﬀects
at the BWE natural frequency. Their entity is evaluated by assessing the content of the
variations of the rotor speed, ∆Ω, at this frequency.
The execution of the FFT is applied in the same time windows used in the previous step.
In each window, if the extracted amplitude is smaller than a certain threshold, the content
of ¨uG,rel
rc,x
can be considered reliable, otherwise it does not.

Chapter 8
Conclusions
This work has presented the formulation and application of four methods conceived to oﬀer
a continuous optimisation of an existing blade edgewise vibration monitor algorithm.
The eﬀorts are focused on the improvements of the algorithm in the particular case where the
3P rotor frequency collides with the natural frequency of one of the edgewise rotor modes.
In this condition the interpretation of the structural behaviour is important as well as more
complex to evaluate.
The main challenge stands on the amount of data involved in the analysis, which are limited
to the information commonly acquired on wind turbines.
The problem has been addressed by exploiting features of the dynamics of wind turbines.
An eventual improvement of the algorithm capabilities would be reﬂected into an increment
of the turbine performances.
8.1
Summary
The Method 1 attempted to detect the edgewise vibrations by tracking the content of the
side side tower top and hub accelerations. If for some extents the method showed a relation
between the side side accelerations and the BWE mode excitation, in general the method
was not able to oﬀer a satisfying accuracy.
The reasons behind these problems are related to the fact that the tower top accelerations
are highly aﬀected from the disturbs due to turbulence and wind shear eﬀects (3P eﬀects).
The Method 2 focused on a peculiarity of the structural behaviour related to the BWE
mode.
In this mode the rotor center movement traces an ellipse in the rotor plane which develops
in the opposite versus of the rotational speed.
The characteristics of the ellipse were provided by the modal analysis executed by Hawc-
Stab2 and the simulated rotor center movement was ﬁtted in it. An index was built to
represent the similarity between the ellipse and the actual movement of the rotor center, as
well as the amplitude of this revolutions.
This should have given information about the level of excitation of the BWE mode, however
it turned out that the rotor center behaviour is not aligned with the expectations.
Also in this circumstance, the eﬀects related to the 3P rotor frequency were appointed as
the cause of the distortion of the detection.
61
==PAGE:78==
Later, the contents that build up the accelerations of the rotor center were analysed,
and Method 3 aimed to detect the excitation of the BWE mode by assessing the side side
accelerations of the rotor center respect the tower top. The assumption was that part of
these accelerations are constituted by the tower top rotations linked to the BWE mode ex-
citation.
The evaluation of this component was done in time domain.
Once again the results revealed several limits on the method eﬀectiveness.
Part of the problems are here connected to the application of a analysis in time domain.
Method 4 was based on Method 3. Unlike in the latter, in Method 4 the analysis of
the side side accelerations is done in frequency domain basis. Further, accepted that the
disturbs related to the 3P rotor excitations are inseparable, a reliability factor was introduced
to discriminate the accuracy of the outputs of the detection.
This last method showed a higher robustness, especially on its application on the Siemens
turbine simulations. Nevertheless, the procedure is still prone to errors and therefore its
application would need further improvements.
8.2
Further developments
A robust procedure to detect the blades edgewise vibrations was not reached in the time
span dedicated to this project.
The problems encountered in this work suggested that Method 2 and 3 should not be further
considered, however Method 1 and Method 4 may be worth to be improved.
A possible strategy to enhance the detection capabilities may lay on the observation of the
forward edgewise mode.
8.2.1
Forward edgewise mode assessment
If the harmonic forces related to the 3P excitation are coincident with ωBW , they aﬀect
the turbine behaviour at that frequency and therefore the detectability of the BW mode
becomes diﬃcult.
It is known that the peaks of the harmonic excitation due to the rotational speed are placed
at 3P and its multiples. On the other hand, if in the speciﬁc case ωBW is located around
3P, ωFW is located approximately at 5P.
Thus, the level of disturb of the FW mode is much smaller respect the one at the BW. The
concept is illustrated in Figure 8.1
The strategy would then attempt to read the excitation of the BW mode in the behaviour
of the FW, which, as said, is less disturbed by the harmonic forces due to the rotational
speed. However this strategy should face two eviden challenges:
• Despite in the forward mode the disturbs are lower, it is also expected that the exci-
tation of the FWE is much lower too.
• The information of the BWE mode in the response of the FWE mode are theoreti-
cally not available. However the mutual inﬂuence of these modes could be found on
measured data.
