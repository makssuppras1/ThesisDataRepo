Resume
Der er gennemført forsøg med temmelig hård til hårdt drikkevand, tappet på DTU.
Drikkevandet er passeret gennem en UF membran, LAGUR Pro system og LAGUR
med UF. Formålet med forsøget har været at undersøge eﬀekten af LAGUR induceret
elektromagnetisk behandling på drikkevand i relation til efterfølgende kalkudfældning på
ﬂader i husholdningen.
Der er målt på partikelstørrelse (PSD), overﬂadespænding (zeta-potentiale) og calcium-
carbonat udfældningspotentialet (CCPP) samt på “hvidfarvning” af sorte ﬂiser. LAGURbe-
handling af drikkevandet, tappet på DTU, ser ud til at medføre en beskeden ændring I
partikelstørrelse i retning af mindre partikler. LAGURbehandlingen ser ligeledes ud til
at ændre partiklernes overﬂadeladning (Zetapotentiale) i retning af en højere ladning,
fra neutral. Det var ikke muligt at konkluderer nogen eﬀekt af LAGURbehandlingen på
udfældningspotentialet på grundlag af de gennemførte forsøg, men en vis mindre eﬀekt
kan på den anden side heller ikke udelukkes. Visual vurdering af udfældninger på sorte
ﬂiser tyder på, at LAGUR behandlet vand kan have ledet til mindre synlig kalk på ﬂis-
erne. Med tegn på at kalken udfælder mere jævnt og “grynet” modsat ubehandlet vand,
der har tendens til at udfælde mere i kalkrander. Der er på baggrund af de fundene
resultater ikke belæg for at afvise at LAGUR kan have en eﬀekt på kalkudfældning. Der
tages ikke stilling til hvorvidt målte ændringer vil have en betydelig eﬀekt i praksis.
Filtrering af vandet gennem UF membranen ser ikke ud til at have haft nogen eﬀekt,
hverken på det ubehandlede vand eller på det LAGURbehandlede vand. Dette er en
logisk konsekvens af den registrerede tendens til, at LAGURbehandlingen medfører en
beskeden reduktion af partikelstørrelsen.
Der er behov for et væsentligt større datagrundlag for at kunne foretage en sikker vurder-
ing af eﬀekten af LAGURbehandling I forhold til udfældningsgener. Et litteraturstudie
viser, at der forskellige steder i verden er fundet forskellige og modsatrettede eﬀekter på
vandkvaliteten ved elektromagnetisk behandling af vand. Det er muligt at eﬀekten af
LAGURbehandling er væsentligt påvirket af “startkvaliteten” af vandet, og ﬂere forsøg
er nødvendige for at se om de her fundene tendenser kan genskabes. På den baggrund
må det anbefales altid at foretage test af eﬀekt på den aktuelle vandkvalitet inden der
træﬀes beslutning om fuldskalaanlæg med elektromagnetisk behandling.

Abstract
Electromagnetic ﬁeld (EMF) treatment is a non-chemical water treatment technology
that has gained traction in recent years. The goal of this project is to systematically
test the eﬀects of the LAGUR Pro equipment in combination with a SiC UF membrane.
Experiments have been carried out with relatively hard to hard tap water from DTU,
Lyngby. The tap water is respectively passed through a UF membrane, a LAGUR Pro
system, and a LAGUR Pro system followed by a UF membrane. The purpose of the
experiment was to investigate the eﬀect of LAGUR-induced electromagnetic treatment
on tap water in relation to subsequent scale precipitation on surfaces in the household.
Particle size distribution (PSD), surface potential (zeta potential), and calcium carbon-
ate precipitation potential (CCPP) were measured, as well as the “white coloration”,
described in terms of luminance, of precipitation on black tiles. LAGUR treatment of
the tap water indicated a modest change in particle size in the direction of smaller par-
ticles. The LAGUR treatment also seems to change the surface charge of the particles
(Zeta potential) in the direction of a higher charge, from neutral. It was not possible
to draw any conclusions about the eﬀect of the LAGUR treatment in relation to the
precipitation of CaCO3 on the basis of the experiments performed. On the other hand,
a certain minor eﬀect cannot be ruled out either. Visual assessment of precipitates on
tiles suggests that LAGUR treated water may have led to less visible limescale on the
tiles with signs that the lime in treated water precipitates more evenly and "gritty" as
opposed to untreated water, which tends to precipitate more in limestone ridges. Based
on the results found, there is no evidence to deny that LAGUR may have an eﬀect on
lime precipitation. No conclusions are made as to whether measured changes in water
quality will have a signiﬁcant eﬀect in practice.
Filtration of the water through the UF membrane does not appear to have had any
eﬀect, either on the untreated water or on the LAGUR-treated water. This is a logical
consequence of the registered tendency for the LAGUR treatment to lead to a modest
reduction in particle size.
There is a need for a signiﬁcantly larger database in order to be able to make a reliable
assessment of the eﬀect of LAGUR treatment in relation to precipitation genes. A liter-
ature study shows that in diﬀerent parts of the world, diﬀerent and opposite eﬀects on
water quality from the electromagnetic treatment of water have been found. It is possible
that the eﬀect of LAGUR treatment is signiﬁcantly aﬀected by the “starting quality” of
the water, and more experiments are needed to see if the trends found in this experiment
can be recreated. On that basis, it is recommended to always perform an eﬀect test on

Softening using an electromagnetic ﬁeld - Measuring an
eﬀect?
High hardness water can cause negative economic eﬀects to municipalities, consumers,
and industries [5]. High hardness levels have been empirically associated with scaling as
well as aesthetic issues [6]. Scale deposits can block the water ﬂow in pipes, and decrease
heat transfer in heat exchangers [7]. Aesthetic issues associated with hard water have
been measured via consumer or industrial employment of softening devices or averting
behaviour such as purchasing bottled water [8]. Municipalities may soften their water to
protect their distribution system, which reduces spending on maintenance and repairs.
Excessive softening however can make water corrosive potentially leading to leaching and
its associated public health burdens [9]. According to the Danish Ministry of Environ-
ment the ideal hardness to preserve the distribution system is 8 °dH (deutsche Härte)
[10]. Industries such as power plants, oil and gas, food and beverages may require or
experience improved operations with softened water as several chemical or industrial pro-
cesses are inhibited by ions and scales [11], [12], [13]. Lime scales form a time consuming
as well as economic nuisance to private households by reducing the service life of house-
hold appliances such as clothes washers or dishwashers, decreasing the eﬃcacy of soaps,
and for ancillary aesthetic reasons such as avoiding scales on kitchenware and bathroom
inventory [14].
Scale formation happens when sparingly soluble ions, also called salts, precipitate in
solution or on susceptible surfaces. Precipitation occurs when a solution is saturated
with ions, in the presence of a seed or nuclei, that forms a crystal aggregate around
which these ions can coalesce and form larger crystals. Divalent ions such as calcium
and magnesium ions are the main culprits of scale formation [15]. Once these start to
form crystals other less soluble salts, like barium and strontium may join the aggregate,
even if these would not have precipitated on their own [16], [17]. Saturation can occur
as a result of a change in chemistry, often temperature or pH, that lowers the solubility
of salts in solution [18].
Traditional methods for water softening include ion exchange, reverse osmosis (RO), nano
ﬁltration (NF), lime softening and pellet softening [19]. In ion-exchange the divalent ions
are typically exchanged for the monovalent sodium ions by passing water through a
resin bed [20]. In pellet softening precipitation is stimulated by adding a base, lowering
the ion solubility, and adding seeds [21]. As ions attach these grow into larger crystals
around 1 mm called pellets. These pellets can then be removed [5]. RO uses size and
surface chemistry to block ions with a membrane while water can pass through [22].

Theory behind EMF and methods of analysis
This section will explain some of the eﬀects of EMF. These eﬀects will be used in the
hypothesis for the expected behavior of treating tap water with the provided LAGUR
system. Methods of analysis were chosen to test if the expected eﬀect had been achieved.
A short explanation will be given for each method’s working principles and why they
were considered relevant for measuring the eﬀect of EMF treatment.
5.1
EMF eﬀect on ions dissolved in water
Supporters claim that EMF applied correctly can alleviate scaling [24], with the ﬁrst
patents involving the use of EMF issued in the 19th century [25], [26]. However, it has
remained a controversial treatment technique despite published cases documenting it as
eﬀective [27]. EMF has besides scaling control been applied to mitigate bacterial, organic,
and inorganic fouling of membranes, improve oil separation and water splitting, and as
an assisting technology in electrocoagulation and advanced oxidation processes among
others [16]. The EMF instrument is often designed as a hollow tube that water can pass
through. The tube exposes the water to a magnetic ﬁeld that varies with time, creating
an oscillating ﬁeld [1], [23], [24]. This study will focus exclusively on scaling control of
municipal water intended for human consumption.
Within the research ﬁeld of EMF there are no clear consensus about its eﬀect being pow-
erful enough to produce signiﬁcant anti-scaling eﬀects. The same goes for the mechanisms
behind EMF and their resulting eﬀects [16]. The following is a simpliﬁed description to
convey the governing principles behind EMF. For an in-depth description of the individ-
ual theories the reader is referred to the cited sources.
The literature study by Lin et al. [16], found that most theories for the scale controlling
mechanisms are based on Lorentz forces.
The Lorentz force describes the force F a
moving charged particle experiences in an electromagnetic ﬁeld [28]. The equation is
given by:
F = q E + q v × B
(1)
Where q is the particle charge, with velocity v in an electric ﬁeld E and a magnetic
ﬁeld B. The theory is that charged particles in a solution such as ions will have their
interactions altered by EMF exposure, causing coagulation by agitating them [1], [29].
Others argue that the Lorentz force is too weak to inﬂuence the precipitation [23]. Instead
it is proposed that EMF exposure accelerates the nucleation and crystallisation rates of
sparingly soluble diamagnetic salts from weak acids [30]. In the crystallisation process

5.1.1
EMF inﬂuence on precipitation type, form and size
Precipitation can happen in diﬀerent ways based on nucleation type. Further description
of nucleation can be found in Appendix A. Broadly deﬁned there are three types: homo-
geneous, heterogeneous, and secondary nucleation [17]. Secondary nucleation is induced
by adding seed crystals and/or altering the water chemistry.
This type of precipita-
tion is utilised in chemical precipitation, lime softening and pellet softening [21], [31].
Heterogeneous nucleation occurs on surfaces, leading to directly bounded scaling [17],
[15]. Homogeneous nucleation takes place in the water and leads to bulk precipitation.
A common theme in EMF studies is their focus on homogeneous contra heterogeneous
precipitation [32], [16], [15]. Where EMF is associated with increased bulk precipitation
at the expense of heterogeneous precipitation[32], [33]. Homogeneous precipitation is
assumed to result in crystals that will either be loosely deposited on surfaces, enabling
easy removal, or ﬂushed away in cases of ever moving water [32]. In some studies bulk
precipitation derived from EMF treatment is associated with a change in crystal struc-
ture, switching the preferred crystal formation from calcite to aragonite [33]. This eﬀect
is claimed by LAGUR [24]. Other studies found that the ratio remained unchanged, but
found indicators of other crystalline structures [27].
Besides precipitation type and crystal formation, EMF has also been documented to
aﬀect crystal size [16]. According to the study by Xing et al. [1] an oscillating magnetic
ﬁeld facilitated coagulation with particles growing from an average diameter of 12 µm
(no EMF for 610 min) to 43 µm (EMF exposure for 610 min). The study focused on the
formation of calcium carbonate. Figure 1 illustrates the formation of crystals. As the
crystals are heavier than the solution they can be precipitated out of the solution. This
eﬀect is rather similar to the process of pellet softening described in the introduction
[21]. The opposite eﬀect was observed by Madsen et al. [23], where calcium carbonate
crystal from a liquid not exposed to EMF had an average diameter of 8 µm with crystals
exposed to LAGUR at 4 µm. In this study it was not possible ﬁnd an explanation for what

5.4
Particle Size Distribution
A particle size distribution describes the size of granulates, powders, particles, that are
present in a liquid.
It provides a relative measure of the size distribution based on
either intensity, mass or number of particles [42]. The particle size distribution is often
quantiﬁed by its mean called the mean hydrodynamic diameter [43]. A description of
the working principle behind how a PSD is measured can be found in Appendix A.2. If
EMF treatment leads to particle agglomeration of crystals in the mm range, a diﬀerence
should be observable between a particle size distribution of untreated tap water and
LAGUR treated water. Where the LAGUR treated water should have its distribution,
and thereby mean hydrodynamic diameter moved towards higher values.
A PSD measurement is sensitive, so two criteria are used validate if the generated data
can be relied on. The ﬁrst one is the underlying model’s Baseline value. The Baseline
should ideally be equal to 1.000. If a measured baseline value deviates by more than
0.01, the measurement is unreliable according to ISO standards. A deviating baseline
can be indicative that the concentration is not high enough for reliable measurement.
The second criteria is the cumulative models Fit Error. This value is a measure of how
well a ﬁtted curve aligns with the measured data. Typically samples with narrow size
distributions will have a Fit Error below 1e-5. Broader size distributions will lead to
higher values as the magnitude of particle size is higher. Very high values (1e-2) are
however, indicative of a bad ﬁt between the measured particle movement and the ﬁtted
model. Reasons for a bad ﬁt can be multimodal particle sizes, low concentrations, and
overall particle shapes diﬀering from spherical [43].
5.5
Zeta potential and conductivity
Zeta potential and conductivity are used to respectively describe the electric charge of
particles in solution[44] and the solutions ability to to transfer current [45]. Knowing the
zeta potential can be helpful in determining how stable a particle is in a solution [15].
The more stably dissolved a particle is the less likely it is to precipitate out and form
scales. A description of what zeta potential is and how it is measured can be found in
Appendix A.3. Zeta potential is measured in volts and values are typically in the range

of +100 to -100 mV. Particles that have a zeta potential with a magnitude above 25 mV
tend to be stable whereas lower magnitudes closer to 0 leads to aggregation, coagulation,
or ﬂocculation. This is because the electrostatic repulsion is more likely to be overcome
by e.g. van der Waals forces [46]. Multiple studies on EMF have used zeta potential to
analyse the treatment’s eﬀect [34], [47]. [18] found that zeta potential increased from -10
mV to around 0 mV, even slightly positive. Parsons et al. observed an average of 16 %
drop in zeta potential. However, they do not state the actual values, so it is unknown
if the drop is in value or in magnitude. Both studies concluded that the decrease after
EMF treatment, caused the formation of CaCO3 particles leading to increasing rates of
bulk precipitation. In a previous study by Bache and Bohr, DTU 2021, [35] found that
LAGUR treatment of tap water caused the zeta potential to change from -19 mV to -31
mV after an hour of treatment. [48] found that EMF had no impact on zeta potential,
but did the measurement on particles that had been separated from solution by ultra-
centrifugation and air-dried, this would have removed any eﬀect EMF might have had
om liquid-solid interactions.
When measuring zeta potential the conductivity of the sample is measured at the same
time. It has been used in several studies [49], [50], [51] to calculate the total precipitation
in solution. As current is carried by ions, changes in conductivity are correlated with
changes in ion concentration. Similar to zeta potential, results diﬀer between studies.
In the study by Bache and Bohr a slight increase was observed for conductivity, from
1.39 to 1.44 mS/cm suggesting increased ion mobility. However, other factors such as
water temperature also aﬀect conductivity, making it less clear what factor caused the
increased conductivity.
5.6
Calcium Carbonate Precipitation Potential
Precipitation at consumers is not directly related to the water hardness, as other factors
such as temperature, pH, and alkalinity inﬂuence ionic solubility [52], [53]. Furthermore,
ions are not independent of one other and may start to co-precipitate before reaching
saturation, if another species have reached theirs [16],[17]. Measuring the actual precipi-
tation requires advanced measurement in order to provide results with high precision and
low bias [2] and exact water type will be unavailable for new systems. As an alternative
Calcium Carbonate Precipitation Potential CCPP can be used. CCPP can be calculated
from more readily available information such as source water ion concentration, target
pH and temperature [52]. CCPP is the amount of calcium carbonate, CaCO3 that needs
to precipitate or dissolve in order to reach equilibrium between its liquid and solid stage
[54]. As such, it measures the maximum precipitation that users of that water would ex-

that the water is aggressive, and if it is positive the water will be ready to precipitate

5.6.1
Alkalinity, pH and temperature dependence
Alkalinity is the water’s capacity to resist changes in pH that would make the water
more acidic, also called buﬀer capacity. It consists primarily of bicarbonate due to the
combination of a high ka value and presence in high concentrations for natural waters.
The relation between carbonate and bicarbonate in water is described by:
HCO−
3 ⇔CO2
3 + H+
(2)
Following Le Chatelier’s principle changes to alkaline water will lead to a shift in equilib-
rium to counteract the change, thereby establishing a new equilibrium. Higher temper-
ature shifts the equation to the right, slightly increasing the carbonate to bicarbonate
ratio. At the same time, the acid (H+) concentration increases slightly which causes a
slight drop in pH. This means that a warmer solution can have better buﬀering capacity
despite a lower pH.

7
Results and discussion
The literature review revealed that EMF was attributed to eﬀects on precipitation type,
form, and size.
Studies generally agreed that EMF changed precipitation type from
heterogeneous to homogeneous bulk precipitation. However, some studies claimed that
this was due to an increase in zeta potential magnitude, leading to higher electrostatic
repulsion, that kept particles stable in solution rather than reacting with each other to
form scales.
Others gave the opposite explanation, that a decrease in zeta potential
magnitude lead to particles no longer repelling each other, which caused agglomeration
to take place in the liquid rather than on surfaces.
The results on crystal form and
size also often pointed in opposite directions.
The underlying theories were likewise
ambiguous, while most studies attributed their ﬁndings to Lorentz forces forming water
clusters around the ions, altering hydration of ions, or agitating charged particles, others
claimed that the eﬀects were due to EMF exposure altering proton spin, changing the
hydrogen transfer between weak acids and water molecules. It is likely this ambiguity
in results and theories that has caused the technology to remain controversial despite
its long history and numerous published studies documenting its eﬀectiveness. However,
diﬀerences in results could be a product of studies using diﬀerent EMF systems and
testing them on diﬀerent water solutions. In lack of a theoretical framework that can
explain and predict the diﬀerent result outcomes, there is a need for though testing of
the commercially available systems such as LAGUR, on the water type intended for
treatment. In the following the results of measured PSD, zeta potential, CCPP, and tiles
precipitation for hard tap water treated with LAGUR will be presented and discussed.

Sources of error
This section will try to systematically go over sources of error within this study. The
section is divided into two segments. One on the test setup and one on the methods of
analysis.
8.1
Setup for sample taking
1. The SiC membrane was not monitored for integrity or challenge tested during use.
Small defects or breaches might have prevented the membrane from ﬁltering the
water properly.
2. Tap water (closed system) was ﬁlled into the test tank and circulated through the
pump (open system) for a minimum of one hour to achieve equilibrium with air.
This was a practical necessity removing diﬀerences between the diﬀerent treat-
ments. However, this can also have led to a number of errors:
• As the water in the tank is reaching equilibrium with air precipitation is likely
to occur. This precipitation will deposit in the tank and pipes.
• This scaling is a problem a) because signiﬁcant amounts risk becoming a source
of contamination for following experiments.
b) lowered ion concentrations
could cause sample results to be underestimated.
• LAGURs eﬀect might be observed as less eﬀective if the real eﬀect was on the
tank and pipes.
• It makes the experiment less representative of practical use cases where LAGUR
would be applied to a closed system and water is not given time to reach equi-
librium before use.
3. The experiment was not designed to account for the increased temperature due
to LAGUR treatment of the sample water. As a water temperature increase will
change precipitation, it can not be excluded that observed eﬀects from LAGUR are
correlated with the temperature increase rather than the EMF exposure.
4. Water was constantly circled through the system. However, this makes the exper-
iment less representative of use cases where one pas through LAGUR is assumed
the norm.
5. Electric interference could be caused by the electric pump. As water passes through
LAGUR multiple times it also passes through the pump. Pumps also create electric
39
==PAGE:54==

Spring 2022
pulses, not completely unlike what is carefully designed in EMF treatments. It is
a concern that this could disturb or destroy the eﬀect from LAGUR. To limit
unwanted interference water samples were taken just after LAGURs outlet.
8.2
Methods of analysis
7. PSD and zeta potential measurements are made by exposing samples to focused
light and voltage respectively. Similar to point 5 above, PSD and zeta potential
measurements might therefore compromise the eﬀect of LAGUR treatment when
trying to measure it.
8. For CCPP alkalinity is estimated by measuring the sample’s buﬀer capacity to
acid. However, these measurements were prone to either over-or undershooting
target pH. More reliable results could be obtained through direct measurement
of [HCO−
3 ]. Furthermore, CCPP takes temperature into account, as described in
point 3 above.
9. Tile precipitation measurements have a lot of the same challenges as described in
point 1 above. Water landing on the tiles will already be at equilibrium, scaling
might be deposited inside the bottle, etc. Furthermore, the bottles are ﬁlled only
once and set to spray for 50 hours during which the eﬀect from LAGUR is assumed
to greatly fade.
10. The tile precipitation tests were aﬀected by diﬀerences in water volume sprayed on
the tiles and temporal diﬀerences that prevent direct comparison.
11. Concentrations of anions and cations correlated with run, as seen in Figure 18, this
can be due to diﬀerent water on the speciﬁc day or that samples aged before being
analysed as all were analysed in one batch. The latter is assumed likely as Na, K,
Ca, S, Cl−, show a trend of lower concentrations the older the samples are.

1. Repeat experiment but where LAGUR is exposed to water in a closed system. Can
an eﬀect be measured in the closed systems on the piping and will an eﬀect last
once the water moves to an open system?

Conclusion
Experiments had been carried out with relatively hard, to hard drinking water, tapped
at DTU. The tap water was passed through a UF membrane, a LAGUR Pro system,
and LAGUR with UF. The purpose of the experiment has been to investigate the eﬀect
of LAGUR-induced electromagnetic treatment on tap water in relation to subsequent
scale precipitation.
In the conducted literature review, EMF was found to have an
eﬀect on precipitation type, form, and size. The literature study shows that within the
ﬁeld diﬀerent and opposite eﬀects on water quality have been found by electromagnetic
treatment of water. The eﬀects focused on were: precipitation type, form, and size. None
of the studied theories appeared capable of explaining the often opposing results. This
ambiguity in results and theories causes the technology to remain controversial despite
its long history and numerous published studies documenting its eﬀectiveness.
Based on the studied theory the hypothesis was formed, that LAGUR treatment would
lead to bulk precipitation. This would be detectable by crystals increasing in size. This
hypothesis was disconﬁrmed by the particle size distribution (PSD) results that showed to
increase in the average particle diameter. In fact, the few ISO complying measurements
indicated the opposite eﬀect, a decrease in the average particle size compared to tap
water samples. Without an observed diameter increase, the membrane was not expected
to aﬀect precipitation, as it was not designed to remove ions unaided.
PSD as a measurement method provided largely unreliable data according to ISO stan-
dards and Fit Error. If PSD measurements are to be continued in further studies it
is advised to either 1) harder water (higher concentration of ions) or 2) use equipment
tailored to work with the low concentration (unknown) and particle sizes in the nano
range.
Measurements of the surface charge of the particles (zeta potential) showed a slight
increase in magnitude for LAGUR treated samples. Conductivity results however did not
indicate any diﬀerence between the sample treatments. While the eﬀect from LAGUR on
average particle diameter and zeta potential is small, the two results support each other.
A higher magnitude zeta potential will decrease the likelihood of particles agglomerating,
due to the higher electrostatic repulsion between particles. This prevents them from
forming larger precipitates.
It was not possible to conclude any eﬀect of the LAGUR treatment on the calcium
carbonate precipitation potential on the basis of the experiments performed, but on the
other hand, a certain minor eﬀect cannot be ruled out either.
However, the results
disconﬁrmed the hypothesis that all three treatments would decrease the CCPP. The UF

membrane did not lower the CCPP and as LAGUR was not observed to increase the
particle size, the LAGUR with the UF did not either. It is possible that the eﬀect of
LAGUR treatment is signiﬁcantly aﬀected by the feed quality of the water, and more
experiments are needed to see if the trends found here can be recreated.
The pairs of CCPP and tile precipitation did not follow a logarithmic relation as hy-
pothesised. However, the results appear to be within the same orders of magnitude as
previous results despite the use of diﬀerent tiles between the two sets of experiments.
Visual assessment of precipitates on black tiles suggests that LAGUR treated water may
have led to less visible scaling on the tiles.
With signs that the precipitates spread
out more evenly compared to untreated water, which tends to precipitate more in scale
ridges. However, the results were uncertain due to diﬀerences in water volume spayed on
the tiles and temporal diﬀerences in initial feed water and a thorough photo analysis is
required to determine if this is a consistent trend.
Filtration of the water through the UF membrane does not appear to have had any
eﬀect, either on the untreated water or on the LAGUR-treated water. This is a logical
consequence of the registered tendency for the LAGUR treatment to lead to a modest
reduction in particle size.
Based on the results found, there is no evidence to deny that LAGUR may have an eﬀect
on lime precipitation. No conclusions were made as to whether the measured changes
will have a signiﬁcant eﬀect in practice.
There is a need for a signiﬁcantly larger data set in order to get a reliable assessment
of the eﬀect of LAGUR treatment in relation to precipitation behaviour. Against this
background, it is always recommended to perform a pilot test on the intended water
body before deciding on a full-scale system with electromagnetic treatment.

A.0.1
Hydration eﬀects
To understand how EMF treatment may eﬀect scaling behaviour of water, the concept
of hydration needs to be understood. Hydration is the process in which water molecules
arrange themselves around an ion [56]. When salts are dissolved in water both positive
and a negative ions are formed. As water is a dipole solute, the water molecules next to
the ions will arrange themselves with the electronegative oxygen facing positive charges
and in turn the positive hydrogen’s facing negative charges, as illustrated in Figure 24.
This forms a layer held in place through ion-dipole forces. The eﬀect will further stretch
out to the surrounding water molecules in the immediate vicinity through hydrogen
bonding [3]. This eﬀect will further be described in Section ?? on zeta potential.
Figure 24: Hydration shown for a cation (Na+, green) and an anion (Cl−, yellow). The water
molecules shows the electronegative oxygen in blue and hydrognes as grey. Figure is originally
from [3]
A.0.2
Nucleation
Nucleation is a key step in scaling [15], where the crystal formation starts occurring in
solution. Nucleation happens when a fraction of the present ions, atoms, or molecules in
a water solution "hits" each other in a way that allows them to connect. The connected
particles provide sites upon which additional particles are deposited letting the crystal
grow [57]. As the crystal grows it gets more and more likely to hit more particles in

the water until crystals become so large that they can precipitate out of solution [17].
Nucleation can be initiated through various approaches such as agitation and seeding.
Scale formation occurs via multiple crystallization pathways, with the two import here
being surface crystallization and bulk crystallization, which are also referred to as het-
erogeneous crystallization and homogeneous crystallization, respectively. [57], [17],[16],

A.1
EMF - further theories
The literature study by [16], found that most theories for the scale controlling mechanisms
are based on Lorentz forces. The Lorentz force describes the force a moving charged
particle experiences in an electromagnetic ﬁeld. But that the theories are divided into
two diﬀerent categories: 1) hydration eﬀects, and 2) magnetohydrodynamic phenomena
under continuous ﬂow conditions.
A.2
Working principle of PSD
Particles suspended in a liquid will undergo Brownian motion, or constant random mo-
tion. Smaller particles will at the same available energy or force move faster than larger
particles according to newtons equations. The eﬀect can be captured using dynamic light
scattering (DLS) [58]. DLS works by exposing samples with particles in liquid disper-
sions to light and recording the resulting scatted light. Repeating this process at very
small time intervals produced a continues feed recording. Comparing these snapshots to
each other is used to determine how far the particles have moved between each snap-
shot. From this the speed of the particles and in turn their size is estimated assuming a
spherical shape. The translation from image to speed to size is done using a cumulative
model. It is this resulting model that provides the average size of the particles and the
size distribution. Here the used cumulative model is done in accordance with ISO 22412
[59].
A.3
Working principle of zeta potential
Zeta potential or ζ potential describes the electrical potential of the double layer sur-
rounding particles dispersed in a solution. Figure 25 illustrates how the surface of a
charged particle in a solution will attract a thin layer of opposite charges. This liquid
layer is ﬁrmly bound and called the Stern layer. The Stern layer in turn attract an outer
layer that is more loosely bound forming the double layer [44].

D
Tile precipitation
The following is a description of the experimental method used for the tile precipitation
experiments. Two things have in this project been altered for the tile spray procedure.
This has the intend to increase the repeatability of the method.
1) spray procedure has been automated. This is done to get a constant distance to the
tile and is assumed to give a more even water exposure as each servo motor applies a
constant and equal pull on the handle. It also has the advantage to speed up to process
by running over nights and weekends.
2) A light box has been used for the tile phots to remove variance in the light exposure.
