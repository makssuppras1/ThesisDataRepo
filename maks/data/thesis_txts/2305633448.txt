ABSTRACT 
 
 
Antibiotics are essential for the control and treatment of E. coli infections in humans and animals. However, it is 
generally accepted that antimicrobial resistance is associated with the quantity of antibiotic consumption. The 
inappropriate use and misuse of antimicrobials have increased the resistance in pathogens.  The worldwide emergence 
of multidrug-resistant bacteria has dramatically limited the number of antibiotics that retain activity against these 
pathogens. This problem has been further amplified by the scarcity of novel classes of antibiotics. Therefore, 
development of novel therapeutic strategies for infectious diseases is in high demand. In response, several new 
therapies have been developed, such as phage therapy, antimicrobial peptide therapy and combinations of two or more 
antibiotics. However, there is still considerable debate over the role of combination therapy versus monotherapy for 
Gram-negative infections. Although there are numerous reasons why combination therapy may be superior to 
monotherapy, the results obtained in clinical studies are not conclusive. 
The focus of this study have been on the effect of combination therapy in the evolution of resistance, based on the 
hypothesis that the combination of tetracycline and amikacin convey collateral sensitivity towards tetracycline. 
Combination therapy are evaluated based on mutation frequency, antibiotic selection concentration, fitness, level of 
resistance and resistance profiles of mutants isolated on tetracycline and amikacin. Parallels were drawn to 
monotherapy. Resistance analysis were based on broth dilution, checkerboard and E-test and results were 
microbiologically interpreted with CLSI and EUCAST and statistically by t-test.  
I demonstrate that combination therapy using tetracycline and amikacin prevents selection of resistant strains more 
efficiently compared to monotherapy using tetracycline or amikacin. Yet, cross-resistance between tetracycline and 
amikacin is discovered and the susceptibility towards tetracycline or amikacin is decreased by combination therapy 
compared to monotherapy. The resistance profile of antibiotic combination resistant mutants reveals broad-spectrum 
resistance towards antibiotics using different mechanisms of action. A tendency towards enhanced fitness for 
combination therapy resistant mutants compared to monotherapy is discovered and the bactericidal activity of amikacin 
seems to be dominant in the combination when examining killing kinetics. The killing effect is shown to decrease using 
combination therapy compared to amikacin monotherapy even though additive interactions between tetracycline and 
amikacin is demonstrated. Both mono- and combination therapy used against E. coli MG1655 is found to encourage 
selection of multi-drug resistance, compromising the resistance prevention argument of tetracycline and amikacin 
combination therapy in this organism.

RESUME 
 
 
Antibiotika er nødvendig for at kontrollere og behandle E. coli infektioner hos mennesker og dyr. Der er consensus om 
at antibiotika  resistens er associeret med mængden af antibiotika indtagelse. Uhensigtsmæssig brug af antibiotika har 
ført til øget resistens hos patogener. Et verdensomspændende udbrud af multi-resistente bakterier har ført til et drastisk 
fald i antallet af antibiotika der udviser aktivitet mod disse patogener. Dette problem er øget yderligere på grund af 
mangel på nye klasser af antibiotika. Der er derfor et stort behov for udvikling af ny terapeutiske strategier imod 
infektions sygdomme.  Forskellige nye tiltag er under udvikling såsom bakteriofag terapi, antimikrobiel peptid terapi 
samt kombination af to eller flere antibiotika. Der er dog stadig debat omkring kombinations terapi versus monoterapi 
i forbindelse med gram-negative infektioner. Selvom der er adskillige grunde til hvorfor kombinations terapi formodes 
at være bedre end monoterapi, har foreløbige forsøg ikke resulteret i entydige svar.  
Studiets fokus har omhandlet kombinations terapis effekt på resistens udvikling, baseret på hypotesen at 
kombinationen af tetracyklin og amikacin resulterer i collateral sensitivitet imod tetracyklin. Evalueringen af 
kombinations terapi er baseret på mutations frekvens, antibiotika selektions koncentration, levedygtighed, niveau af 
resistens samt resistens profiler af mutanter isoleret på tetracyklin og amikacin. Der blev draget paralleller til 
monoterapi. Resistens analysen er baseret på bouillon fortynding, checkerboard of E-test. Resultaterne er 
mikrobiologisk fortolkede med CLSI/2014 og EUCAST samt statistisk ved hjælp af t-test.    
Jeg viser at combination terapi med tetracyklin og amikacin modvirker selektion af resistente stammer mere effektivt i 
forhold til mono terapi med tetracyklin eller amikacin. Dog ses kryds-resistens mellem tetracyklin og amikacin samt øget 
resistens imod tetracyklin eller amikacin af kombinations terapi i forhold til monoterapi.  Resistens profilen af antibiotika 
kombination resistente mutanter afslører bred-spektret resistens imod antibiotika med forskellige virkemåder. En 
tendens til øget fitness for kombinations terapi resistente mutanter i forhold til monoterapi blev set. Den bakterie 
dræbende effekt af amikacin ser ud til at dominere i kombinationen i forhold til den bakterie hæmmende effekt af 
tetracyklin når der kigges på effektiviteten. Den bakterie dræbende effekt er vist at aftage ved kombinations terapi i 
forhold til amikacin monoterapi selvom additive interaktioner mellem tetracyklin og amikacin er demonstreret.  Både 
mono og kombinations terapi mod E. coli har vist sig at føre til øget selektion af multiresistens hvilket kompromiterer 
argumentet mod resistens prevention af tetracyklin of amikacin kombinations terapi i denne organisme.

1 BACKGROUND 
1.1 INFECTIOUS DISEASES - A PROBLEM TODAY 
Worldwide, infectious diseases are still the leading cause of death of 
children and adolescents and one of the leading causes in adults, even 
though antibiotics have been around since 1929 (Ventola 2015). One 
of the reasons are antibiotic resistance. Introducing novel antibiotics 
have been followed by the evolution of resistance to that antibiotic 
in as little as a few years since the introduction of the very first 
antibiotics (Clatworthy, Pierson and Hung 2007).  
Figure 1, depicts the time line of antibiotic deployment and resistance 
up to 2015 (Ventola 2015).  
The rapid evolution of antibiotic resistance has been attributed to the 
overuse and misuse of antibiotics (Clatworthy, Pierson and Hung 
2007). There has been a lack of new drug development by the 
pharmaceutical industry due to reduced economic incentives and 
challenging regulatory requirements (Ventola 2015). According to 
The Institute of Medicine (IOM) and the Food and Drug 
Administration (FDA), only two new classes of antibiotics have been 
developed in the past 30 years, and resistance to one class emerged 
even before FDA approved the drug (IDSA 2014). 
New multidrug drug-resistant strains (MDR) are now encountered on 
a regular basis. These strains are resistant to almost all antibiotics 
(Lim et al. 2010).

1.2 THE USE OF ANTIBIOTICS  
Reducing the emergence and spread of antimicrobial resistance is a major challenge, and the use of antimicrobials in 
production animals is considered to be an important contributor to resistance development. Still, the use of 
antimicrobials is necessary in livestock production to avoid compromising animal health and welfare (Græsbøll et al. 
2014). Administration of antimicrobials to livestock increases the risk of antimicrobial resistance in commensal bacteria 
(DANMAP 2014). E. coli is a commensal bacterium found in animals, food and humans. It is able to develop or transfer 
antimicrobial resistance in response to selective pressure. Resistant bacteria from animals can be transmitted to humans 
either through direct contact with animals and their environment or through ingestion of contaminated food or other 
contaminated products (Silbergeld, Graham and Price 2008). 
Antimicrobial resistance in Denmark is monitored through the Danish Integrated Antimicrobial Resistance Monitoring 
and Research Programme (DANMAP), which has shown consistently high levels (
30% of the bacterial population in 
the past five years) of tetracycline resistant E. coli in pigs and pork (DANMAP 2014). This indicates that tetracycline 
resistant bacterial strains are endemic in the pig population. Pig production also accounts for approximately 80% of the 
veterinary use of antimicrobials in Denmark, with tetracycline being the drug most frequently used (DANMAP 2014). 
Tetracycline is also commonly used in humans, and therefore is an antimicrobial that is of interest in both the human 
and veterinary sectors (Græsbøll et al. 2014).  
In a review by Burow et al. 2013, the prevalence ratio for antimicrobial resistance in E. coli of swine, were highest for 
the use of aminoglycosides, quinolones and tetracycline.

2 AIM OF PROJECT 
Tetracycline is a widely used antibiotic in the pig industry. The emergence of resistance is a big problem for the food 
industry and also human health, due to the transfer of resistance through commensal bacteria. Combination therapy 
has been put forward as an alternative to decrease the mutation frequency and thereby the rate of resistance evolution 
compared to monotherapy. The combination of tetracycline and amikacin has been shown to increase the susceptibility 
of E. coli towards the antibiotics in use by collateral sensitivity. 
The aim of this project is to look at the effect of combination therapy in the evolution of resistance, using bacteriostatic 
tetracycline and bactericidal amikacin. 
I investigated the correlation between combination therapy and mutation frequency to see if combination therapy 
prevents selection of resistant strains more efficiently than monotherapy. Also the correlation between concentration 
of antibiotics selected for, Minimum Inhibitory Concentration (MIC) values and Mutation Frequency (MF) will be 
investigated. The resistance level of mutants selected on mono or combination therapy will be examined by MIC 
techniques including broth dilution, checkerboard and E-test and the resistance pattern looked into by examining cross 
resistance and using antibiotics targeting different resistance mechanisms. To examine the fitness cost related to the 
mutations, a growth experiment will be conducted and to investigate the bactericidal activity of amikacin in combination 
with the bacteriostatic activity of tetracycline in relation to antibiotic concentrations and exposure time, in vitro drug 
survival experiments will be performed. 
Additionally, flow cytometry will be investigated as a novel assay for fast detection of resistance development.

3 THEORY 
3.1 ANTIBIOTICS  
Antibiotics can be divided into two classes based on their mechanism of action. Bactericidal antibiotics kill bacteria and 
bacteriostatic antibiotics inhibit their growth or reproduction (Hancock 2007). One way that bactericidal antibiotics kill 
bacteria is by inhibiting cell wall synthesis, bacterial enzymes or protein translation (Hancock 2007). Bacteriostatic 
antibiotics limit the growth of bacteria by interfering with bacterial protein production, DNA replication, or other aspects 
of bacterial cellular metabolism. There is not always a precise distinction between the two modes of action and high 
concentrations of some bacteriostatic agents are also seen to be bactericidal (Ocampo et al. 2014).  
 
FIGURE 3 Antibiotic classes and their mechanism of action. Green = bacteriostatic action, Red = bactericidal action and Blue = the site of action (ihcm 
Cmap http://skat.ihmc.us/rid=1245179385682_561269208_18513/Antibiotics.cmap). 
Further classification is done on the cellular component they affect. There are five different classifications (Munck 2014): 
1. Inhibitors of cell wall synthesis. Examples: penicllins, cephalosporins, bacitracin and vancomycin. 
2. Inhibitors of cell membrane function. Examples: polymixin B and colistin. 
3. Inhibitors of protein synthesis. Examples: Aminoglycosides, macrolides, lincosamides, streptogramins, 
chloramphenicol, tetracyclines. 
4. Inhibitors of nucleic acid synthesis. Examples: quinolones, metronidazole, and rifampin. 
5. Inhibitors of other metabolic processes. Examples: both sulfonamides and trimethoprim disrupt the folic acid 
pathway, which is a necessary step for bacteria to produce precursors important for DNA synthesis.

3.2 RESISTANCE MECHANISM AND TRANSFER OF RESISTANCE 
Bacteria can be intrinsically resistant to certain antibiotics but can also acquire resistance to antibiotics via mutations in 
chromosomal genes and by horizontal gene transfer. The intrinsic resistance of a bacterial species to a particular 
antibiotic is the ability to resist the action of that antibiotic as a result of inherent structural or functional characteristics 
(Blair et al. 2015). In addition to intrinsic resistance, bacteria can acquire or develop resistance to antibiotics. This can 
happen by several mechanisms (Munck 2014). 
 
FIGURE 4 Examples of mechanisms of antibiotic resistance; drug target alterations, reduced drug uptake, increased drug efflux, circumvention of drug 
target essentiality and drug inactivation (Munck 2014). 
1. Minimizing the intracellular concentrations of the antibiotic due to reduced drug uptake into the bacterium.  
It is mainly observed in Gram-negative species and is commonly caused by reduced permeability of the outer 
membrane. Hydrophilic antibiotics cross the outer membrane by diffusing through outer-membrane porin 
proteins. In most E. coli, the major porins, such as the outer-membrane proteins OmpF and OmpC of E. coli, 
are thought to function as non-specific channels (Clatworthy, Pierson and Hung 2007).  
2. Minimizing the intracellular antibiotic concentration by increased efflux.  
This can be the result of both mutational and horizontal acquired resistance. The mutations usually result in 
up-regulation of efflux pumps or changing its substrate specificity. Some efflux pumps have narrow substrate 
specificity (for example, the Tet pumps), but many transport a wide range of structurally dissimilar substrates 
and are known as multidrug resistance (MDR) efflux pumps (Normark and Normark 2002).  
3. Modifying the antibiotic target by genetic mutation or post-translational modification of the target. 
Most antibiotics specifically bind to their targets with high affinity, thus preventing the normal activity of the 
target. Changes to the target structure that prevent efficient antibiotic binding, but still enable the target to 
carry out its normal function, can confer resistance (Normark and Normark 2002).  
4. Inactivation of the antibiotic by hydrolysis or modification.  
The addition of chemical groups to vulnerable sites on the antibiotic molecule by bacterial enzymes causes 
antibiotic resistance by preventing the antibiotic from binding to its target protein as a result of steric hindrance 
(Normark and Normark 2002). 
Aminoglycoside antibiotics are particularly susceptible to modification as they tend to be large molecules with 
many exposed hydroxyl and amide groups. Aminoglycoside-modifying enzymes confer high levels of resistance 
to the antibiotic (or antibiotics) that they modify (Mingeot-Leclercq, Glupczynski and Tulkens 1999).  
5. Circumvention of drug target essentiality leaving the cell unaffected by the inhibitory effect of the antibiotic 
(Munck 2014)

Multiple resistance mechanisms can also act together to increase the spectrum of resistance (Munck 2014).  
Bacteria can acquire the resistance mechanisms vertically and horizontally between the same or different bacteria 
species (Clatworthy, Pierson and Hung 2007). Horizontal gene transfer can happen by conjugation, transformation and 
transduction. Resistance can also arise through mutations in the chromosome by resistance evolution. Resistance 
through mutations occurs at different frequencies depending on the mechanisms of resistance (Normark and Normark 
2002).  
3.3 EVOLUTION OF ANTIBIOTIC RESISTANCE 
The evolution of resistant mutant strains is a natural occurring phenomenon when microorganisms replicate 
erroneously, or when resistant traits are exchanged between them (Genereux and Bergstrom 2004). Increased mutation 
rates in asexual organisms can give an advantage in populations that needs to adapt to a changing environment 
(Buckling et al. 2009). A larger number of beneficial mutations are then generated to offset the cost of deleterious 
mutations. Especially small populations would benefit from increased mutation rates, since they would otherwise spend 
longer time than a large population waiting for the next beneficial mutation (Buckling et al. 2009). Using experimental 
evolution to determine how microorganisms respond to selective pressures in their natural environments could allow 
their evolution to be anticipated (Buckling et al. 2009).  
In any bacterial population, emergence, spread and stability of antibiotic resistance is determined by several factors 
including the drug use, the rate of formation of resistant mutants, the biological cost of resistance and the rate and 
extent of genetic compensation of the fitness cost (Normark and Normark 2002). The rate of formation of resistant 
mutants is dependent on the selection pressure and the level of already resistant mutants in a population before 
antibiotic treatment. This level depends on the mutation frequency (zur Wiesch et al. 2011). The mutation rate is 
frequently defined as the in vitro frequency at which detectable mutants arise in a bacterial population in the presence 
of a given antibiotic concentration and the mutation frequency is a cross section of the bacterial population at a given 
time and reflects not only the mutation rate but also the history of the population before selection is applied (Baquero 
and Martinez 2000). The spontaneous mutation frequency for antibiotic resistance in E. coli is on the order of about 10-
8 - 10-9 (Denamur et al. 2005). 
Adaptation by natural selection occurs through the spread and substitution of mutations that improve the performance 
and reproductivity of an organism (Santiago and Lenski 2003). The selection pressure by the use and misuse of 
antimicrobial drugs accelerates the emergence of drug-resistant strains (Read and Woods 2014). For example, a drug –
resistance mutation would impose a fitness cost on the cell in a drug free environment and thereby fail to increase in 
frequency. If the drug were present, the cell bearing the mutation would be able to reproduce and the drug-sensitive 
cells would either fail to reproduce or die (Genereux and Bergstrom 2004). After the emergence of a drug-resistance 
mutation, other mutations can appear that further increase the fitness of their carrier under drug-selection pressure. 
These are additional drug-resistance mutations and compensatory mutations (zur Wiesch et al 2011). Multiple 
mutations are often required to acquire high levels of resistance to a specific drug whereas a single mutation can cause 
phenotypic changes that change the resistance and susceptibility to various drugs simultaneously (Lázár et al. 2013). 
The biological responses towards antibiotics are concentration dependent as seen in figure 5 (Bernier and Surette 2013). 
Bacterial interactions mediated by antibiotics induce biological responses in bacteria in a dose-dependent manner. From 
high to low concentrations, antibiotics act as either toxins, stress inducers, or as cues/coercions, respectively. At high 
antibiotic concentrations, bacterial death or inhibition is the result. At lower concentrations (subinhibitory), antibiotics 
can act as stress induces, coercions or be sensed as cues. The induced mechanisms can lead to antibiotic resistance or 
tolerance (Bernier and Surette 2013).

FIGURE 5 The concentration dependent biological responses towards antibiotics (Bernier and Surette 2013). 
The probability of a population containing a resistant member is proportional to the number of mutations required for 
resistance and the size of the population. If resistance against a specific class of antibiotics requires a specific set of 
mutations, then more mutations are required to achieve multi-drug resistance. This reasoning has led to the 
simultaneous use of multiple antibiotics, to reduce the risk of resistance emerging (Munck 2014). 
 
3.4 ANTIBIOTIC RESISTANCE PREVENTION  
One approach in the protection of the existing antimicrobial agents has been to lower the amount of antimicrobial 
agents used overall. Another approach is to optimize treatment strategies to lower the resistance evolution. Third, 
development of new antimicrobials to which current strains are not resistant (Bergstrom and Feldgarden 2007). 
For protection of existing antibiotics, the mutant selection window (MSW) and the mutant prevention concentration 
(MPC) had been proposed (Blondeau 2009). Mutation prevention concentration (MPC) defines the lowest drug 
concentration required to block the growth of the least susceptible cell present in high density bacterial populations. 
The mutant selection window (MSW) is defined as the “danger zone” for therapeutic drug concentrations (Drlica 2003). 
The MSW is bordered by the MIC and MPC values and the drug concentration range between the measured MIC and 
MPC values defines the MSW. Minimizing the length of time, the drug concentration remains in the MSW may reduce 
the frequency of resistance during therapy. MPC values, when considered with drug pharmacology may allow prediction 
on the probability of resistance selection when bacteria are exposed to antimicrobial agents during therapy (Blondeau 
2009). 
==PAGE:16==
Page 16 of 66 
 
 
FIGURE 6 Mutant selection window and mutant prevention concentration (Roberts et al. 2008). 
Figure 6 visualises the reduction in bacterial CFU with increased antibiotic exposure for a concentration-dependent 
antibiotic. As the exposure increases, a large reduction in CFU is observed. For bacterial colonies to survive the first 
drop-off at the bacteria's minimum inhibitory concentration (MIC), a first step mutation is required. For bacterial 
colonies to survive the second drop-off, a second step mutation is required. Selective antibiotic growth may occur when 
antibiotic concentrations are in the mutant selection window. The mutant prevention concentration requires at least a 
second-step mutation for bacterial survival, making antibiotic resistance evolution less likely (Roberts et al. 2008).  
Antibiotics that are susceptible to the single-step mutation development of resistance are those which interact with a 
single gene product (Silver 2007). Conversely, drugs that have a low likelihood for the development of resistance are 
those that interact with multiple molecular sites, the structures of which are determined by multiple genes. This favours 
the development of multi-target over single-target compounds for systemic monotherapy and is known as the multi-
target hypothesis (Silver 2007). 
Combining antibiotics could also fulfil the multi-target hypothesis, if there is no cross-resistance between the them, as 
two concurrent mutations would be needed in the evolution of resistance to both antibiotics (Fischbach 2011). For 
example, for a bacteria population of 1010 cells, resistance frequency to drug A equals 10-6 and drug B equals 10-8, two 
independent mutations would arise with a frequency of 10-14 making resistance development unlikely (Drlica 2003). 
 
3.4.1 COMBINATION THERAPY  
To slow the emergence of resistance, drug combination therapies have been demonstrated to reduce resistance in 
several disease areas (Fischbach 2011). The rate of evolution in combination treatment is typically slower than in single 
drug treatment because mutations giving resistance to only one drug may not give a large advantage in the multidrug 
environment (Kim et al. 2014).  
The effect of combination therapy can be evaluated by MIC measurements of the individual drugs and their 
combination. The antibiotic interactions are then divided into synergy, additive, indifferent or antagonistic effects based 
on the Fractional Inhibitory Concentration Index (FIC Index) (Hollander, Mouton and Verbrugh 1998). Table 8, lists FIC 
Index values based on the E-test Application Sheet, © AB BIODISK, Sweden.  
The combination of antibiotics does not depend on whether they are bacteriostatic or bactericidal. It depends on how 
they act together (antagonistic, synergistic or additive action). What may happen is that bactericidal antibiotics kill 
==PAGE:17==
Page 17 of 66 
 
better proliferating cells (actively growing), and the inclusion of a bacteriostatic antibiotic can arrest the growth and 
prevent the killing by the bactericidal one (Ocampo et al. 2014). 
Synergy between drugs means that their combined effect exceeds the sum of their individual effects. In figure 7, the 
synergistic effect is seen between two drugs in a time-kill assay. The drug combination A+B kills the cells much more 
efficient than the individual drugs. Additive effect or indifference between drugs is also seen as both drugs working 
individually, the number of viable cells/mL are approximately equal to drug C.  Antagonism are the opposite, where 
drug D hinders the effect of drug A (Cottarel and Wierzbowski 2007). 
 
FIGURE 7 Time kill curves for assessment of antibiotic combinations. Interpretation of results, an antibiotic A with no activity against the tested strain 
is combined with antibiotic B, C or D. By comparison with the activity of the drugs alone, synergism, indifference or antagonism is concluded for the 
combination (Vitale, Afeltra and Dannaoui 2005). 
Synergy have been the preferred effect in drug combinations due to the increased spectrum of activity, more efficient 
clearance of infections and clearance at lower drug concentrations (Ocampo et al. 2014). It can however, have 
conflicting effects on resistance. It limits the time window available for resistant mutations to arise but it also increases 
the selective advantage of single drug resistant mutants. When competition for resources is strong, antagonistic drug 
combinations have been shown to limit evolution of resistance. This is due to a reduced fitness gain, when bacteria 
develop resistance to antagonistic combinations compared to developing resistance to synergistic combinations (Munck 
et al. 2014).

A clinical effect of combination therapy compared to mono or sequential therapy for treating bacterial infections have 
been difficult to prove (Suzuki, Horinouchi and Furusawa 2014).  Also the complexity of multidrug treatment is a 
challenge since the drugs need to be administered simultaneously and reach the right concentration at the site of 
infection.  To describe the multidrug treatment complexity, pharmacokinetics tell about the drug concentration-time 
courses in body fluids resulting from administration of a certain drug dose and pharmacodynamics about the observed 
effect resulting from a certain drug concentration (Meibohm and Derendorf 1997). 
A study by Vestergaard et al. 2015 propose a different view on combination therapy. They suggest that combination 
therapy consistently selects for mutants with enhanced fitness expressing broad-spectrum resistance mechanisms and 
monotherapy for narrow-spectrum resistance. This theory is explained by a proposed model by which bacterial 
population acquire broad spectrum resistance during combination therapy.

Resistance to antibiotics ‘a’ and ‘b’ is conferred by the resistance mutations ‘A’ and ‘B’, either through the acquisition 
of first A and then B, or first B and then A. The hypothesis they propose, is that in the presence of both drugs, a mutation 
or state that results in a general resistance phenotype (‘G’) towards a and b simultaneously confers a selective 
advantage compared with the specific resistance mutations (A or B) when the two drugs are present simultaneously. 
The G state will have a fitness advantage compared with the A or B single mutants when both drugs are present. The G 
state will also have the advantage of requiring fewer additional mutations to reach full resistance and will therefore 
change the resistance evolution trajectory by potentiating the evolution of novel resistance traits, either acquisition of 
the specific resistance mutations a and b, which requires multiple mutations, or acquisition of an additional 
compensatory mutation ‘c’ that enhances G to levels of resistance comparable with resistance provided by A and B 
(Vestergaard et al. 2015). 
Analysis of antibiotic combinations is necessary in order to choose combination therapies that do not increase the risk 
of selecting for broad-spectrum resistance mechanisms (Vestergaard et al. 2015).  
 
3.4.2 COLLATERAL SENSITIVITY 
A phenomenon in drug-resistant cells identified during in vitro studies, is that the development of resistance to one 
agent can confer greater sensitivity to an alternate agent than seen in the original (parental) line (Hall, Handley and 
Gottesman 2009). The term collateral sensitivity (CS), was first used in a study by Szybalski and Bryson in 1952 to 
describe the hypersensitivity of drug resistant E. coli to other unrelated agents (Szybalski and Bryson 1952). 
==PAGE:19==
Page 19 of 66 
 
 
FIGURE 9 Collateral sensitivity defined schematically. (a) Representative dose–response curve of a parental cell line (the solid line in the center). 
Development of resistance to a drug, and concomitant cross-resistance to various other drugs (dotted line to the right). Cross-resistant cells can be 
re-sensitized to levels approaching that of the original parental cells. A small number of drugs have been demonstrated to sensitize multidrug-resistant 
cells to a greater degree than the original parental cells (dashed line to the left). This property is termed collateral sensitivity (Hall, Handley and 
Gottesman 2009).  
Interestingly, mutations were discovered that causes multidrug resistance in bacteria and simultaneously enhances 
sensitivity to many other unrelated drugs (Pál, Papp and Lázár 2015). This theory has been investigated in several studies 
for both gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria and different classes of antibiotics (Zamorano et al. 2011, Hou et al. 
2007, Vornhagen et al. 2015 and Munck et al. 2014).  These studies have explored the extent to which microbial adaptive 
evolution in the presence of a single antibiotic increases the susceptibility to other drugs.

3.5.1 TETRACYCLINE: MECHANISM OF ACTION AND RESISTANCE MECHANISM 
Tetracyclines are primarily bacteriostatic antibiotics. They enter gram negative bacteria by passive diffusion through the 
porin channels and gram positive bacteria and other organisms by energy-dependent active transport (Zakari and 
Wright 2008). After entering the cell, tetracyclines bind reversibly to the 30S subunit of the bacterial ribosome, blocking 
the binding of aminoacyl-tRNA to the acceptor site on the mRNA-ribosome complex. This inhibits addition of amino 
acids to the growing peptide (Zakari and Wright 2008). 
 
FIGURE 11 Tetracycline mechanism of action (Wilson 2014). 
The tetracycline’s are broad-spectrum antibiotics with a wide range of activity against both Gram-positive and Gram-
negative bacteria (Bradford and Jones 2012). Most bacteria possess an active transport system for tetracycline that will 
allow intracellular accumulation of the antibiotic at concentrations 50 times as great as that in the medium. This greatly 
enhances its antibacterial effectiveness and accounts for its specificity of action, since an effective concentration cannot 
==PAGE:21==
Page 21 of 66 
 
be accumulated in animal cells. Thus a blood level of tetracycline which is harmless to animal tissues can halt protein 
synthesis in invading bacteria (Nelson and Levy 2011).  
The combination of their broad spectrum, low toxicity and low cost has led to their overuse and misuse in the 
prophylaxis and therapy of human and animal infections and also at subtherapeutic levels in animal feed as growth 
promoter and the wide-spread development of resistance has reduced their effectiveness (Alexander et al. 2013). 
Tetracycline resistance now occurs in an increasing number of pathogenic, opportunistic, and commensal bacteria 
(Nguyen et al. 2014). 
Resistance to tetracycline can occur due to any of these mechanisms (Alexander et al. 2013): 
1. Enhanced efflux or diminished influx of tetracycline by an active transport protein pump;  
2. Ribosome protection due to formation of proteins that hinders tetracycline binding to the ribosome 
3. Modification of tetracycline that leads to drug degradation 
4. Ribosome mutations within the 16s rRNA conferring resistance to tetracycline 
Also the lipopolysaccharide containing outer membrane layer in gram-negative bacteria makes it naturally more 
resistant to tetracycline (Nguyen et al 2014). 
Formation of an efflux pump and ribosomal protection are the most significant mechanisms of tetracycline resistance 
(Lázár et al. 2013). Gram-negative species expressing Tet(AE) efflux pump show resistance to tetracycline. Also 
resistance due to a ribosomal protection mechanism is seen to yield cross-resistance to doxycycline and minocycline as 
the target site protected is the same for all tetracycline’s (Bradford and Jones 2012).

3.6 FLOW CYTOMETRY 
Flow cytometry is a technique in which single cells in a fluid suspension are analysed with respect to their intrinsic light 
scattering properties and are simultaneously evaluated for one or more extrinsic properties using fluorescent dyes 
(Roberts 2003). Because the flow rate of cells within the flow cytometer is rapid, thousands of cells can be analysed 
individually in seconds (DiGiuseppe 2005). Several studies have included flow cytometry as way to perform microbial 
analysis at single-cell level (Shapiro 2000; Joux and Lebaron 2000; Stepanauskas et al. 2005; Maurice, Haiser and 
Turnbaugh 2013). Also the need for real-time monitoring methods for viability assessment is met by flow cytometry by 
providing a clear discrimination between intact cells, injured or damaged cells, and dead cells (Ben-Amor et al. 2005). 
 
Accurate determination of live, dead and total bacteria is important in many microbiology applications (DiGuiseppe 
2005). Traditionally, viability is synonymous with the ability to form colonies on solid growth medium and to proliferate 
in liquid nutrient broth. These traditional, culture-based tests are time-consuming and can work poorly with slow-
growing or viable but non-cultivable organisms (DiGuiseppe 2005). They do not provide real-time results or timely 
information that is needed in applications such as industrial manufacturing. Flow cytometry can be readily applied to 
the enumeration of viable bacteria in a sample (BD Biosciences 2002). 
Live cells have intact membranes and are impermeable to dyes such as propidium iodide (PI), which only leaks into cells 
with compromised membranes. CYTO® 9 green fluorescent nucleic acid stain is cell membrane permeable and stains 
both live and dead gram-positive and gram-negative bacteria. Thus a combination of these two dyes provides a rapid 
and reliable method for discriminating live and dead bacteria (Molecular Probes®Handbook 2015).  
==PAGE:23==
Page 23 of 66 
 
Enumeration of the bacteria can be done by CountbrightTM Absolute Counting Beads (Molecular ProbesTM, Inc, UK). It is 
a flow cytometry bead standard that can be used to accurately quantify the number of live, dead and total bacteria in a 
sample (Molecular ProbesTM, Inc, 2005). In figure 13, the beads are visualised in the upper right corner.

Complexities in sample handling (many controls needed), instrument setup and data analysis (gating, colour

5.4.1 FITNESS OF ANTIBIOTIC RESISTANT MUTANTS 
To further investigate the possible resistance mechanisms, the fitness of the resistant mutants isolated on mono and 
combination therapy was determined using doubling times in LB medium. Growth curves were obtained using microtiter 
plates measured in a spectrophotometer every 10 min for 24 hours. The growth curves are shown in appendix. Based 
on the shapes of the growth curves (appendix), there seemed to be large differences in the exponential phases reflecting 
large differences in the calculated doubling times. The OD600 range used to calculate doubling times were mostly 
different for all mutants. After 24 hours of growth, OD600 was approximately 0.7, which is relatively low and probably 
due to low aeration of cultures in the well. Doubling times are presented in figure 22. E. coli MG1655 was included as a 
reference strain and shown as the first strain to the left in each figure. Statistical significant differences were calculated 
using the students t-test and the degree of significance was marked according to section 4.14 with stars above each 
mutant.  
The calculated doubling times in minutes of mutants isolated on tetracycline are visualised in figure 22A. A high degree 
of significant difference was seen for mutants isolated on 1 µg/mL tetracycline (1xMIC) compared to E. coli MG1655. 
The mutants isolated on 2, 4 and 8 µg/mL tetracycline showed either no significant difference compared to E. coli 
MG1655 or a difference of less than 30 min, indicating a low fitness cost involved. 
The calculated doubling times in minutes of mutants isolated on amikacin are visualised in figure 22B. A high degree of 
significant difference was seen for all mutants compared to E. coli MG1655. Doubling times were increased from 
approximately 1 hour for E. coli MG1655 to around 5 hours for the slowest growing amikacin mutants. This indicates a 
high fitness cost involved in the evolution of amikacin resistance.

cytometry.  To use flow cytometry for drug survival studies requires initial control studies to set up a reliable and

24, the plate count as %viable cells were correlated to %viable cells counted by flow cytometry.  A regression line was 
used to represent the data and the coefficient of determination is a measure of how well the regression line fits the 
data from -1 < r2 > 1. This correlation is r2 = 0.93, indicating that the obtained data correlate well.

6 DISCUSSION 
The emerging and sustained resistance to antibiotics is creating a major health issue worldwide. Bacterial pathogens 
are increasingly becoming resistant even to the most recently approved antibiotics (Cottarel and Wierzbowski  2007). 
In vitro and in vivo studies show that high mutation frequencies can significantly contribute to the appearance of 
multiresistant bacteria (Giraud et al. 2002). Among the several factors that affect the appearance and spread of acquired 
antibiotic resistance, the mutation frequency and the biological cost of resistance are of special importance (Björkholm 
et al. 2001).  
In this study, the mutation frequency of E. coli MG1655 towards tetracycline, amikacin and their combination was 
investigated. The frequency of mutations conferring resistance to two drugs simultaneously was statistically significant 
lower than the frequency of mutations after exposure to a single drug, indicating that combination therapy prevented 
selection of resistant strains more efficiently than monotherapy. The decrease seen in MF of combination therapy 
compared to monotherapy is most likely due to the fact that resistance is associated with an acquisition of several 
mutations to evolve resistance towards both antibiotics. The likeliness of survival for these mutations depends on the 
importance of the gene, whether it is a mutation in an essential or nonessential gene for the cell. The observed 
decreased MF of combination therapy could be obtained using a lower concentration of antibiotics compared to 
monotherapy. For the combination, no mutations were seen above 6 µg/mL which gives a narrower MSW and may 
reduce the likelihood for resistance selection during therapy.  
It was noted that the obtained MF were very high compared to data found in other articles regarding MF for antibiotic 
resistance in E. coli (Denamur et al. 2005). The most likely reason for the very high MF obtained compared to the usually 
seen MF for antibiotic resistance in E. coli of about 10-8- 10-9, is due to the different mechanisms of resistance which 
increases the possibility of developing resistance by several different mutations. E. coli can use a variety of resistance 
mechanisms to survive despite the excessive antibiotic pressure (figure 4). 
An issue affecting the potential use of tetracycline and amikacin as single agents concerns the apparent high 
spontaneous MF for development of resistance in E. coli. Thus the use of combination therapy by tetracycline and 
amkacin is preferable due to lower MF and thereby a lower risk of resistance mutations emerging. However not all 
antibiotics have been investigated, meaning there is a possibility of cross-resistance or sensitivity towards antibiotics 
not tested. The MF results revealed that when using lower concentrations of antibiotics, the probability of resistant 
mutant selection increases compared to high antibiotic concentrations. This indicate that MF seems to be concentration 
dependent. Low antibiotic concentrations result in high MF, indicating that the evolution of resistance is more likely, 
since spontaneous mutations happen more frequently due to the low selection pressure.               
To explore how the resistance acquisition to one drug (Tet, Amk or the combination) changes the resistance and 
susceptibility to other drugs (Tet, Amk, ME, CIP, CAZ), MIC values were measured. The spectra of MIC values were 
different among mutant’s resistant to the given drug, suggesting that independently evolved resistant mutants reached 
different phenotypes. Mutants isolated on high antibiotic concentrations resulted in high MIC values as expected and 
combination resistant mutants showed a much higher level of resistance towards monotherapy compared to the 
mutants evolved on single antibiotics. These increased MIC values for the drug combination have previously been 
reported by Munck et al. 2014.  All antibiotic resistant mutants revealed resistance towards the antibiotics tested 
compared to the reference strain.  
In a study by Ocampo et al. 2014, using E. coli as parent strain, the combination of tetracycline and amikacin revealed 
no interaction between the pair. This was examined using the Loewe additivity model on a dose response curve.  The 
same result was also seen in this study, where additive interactions between tetracycline and amikacin were discovered 
using FIC Index and single concentration points.   
==PAGE:47==
Page 47 of 66 
 
Determining the impact of combination therapy on antibiotic resistance evolution and bacterial fitness compared to the 
effect of monotherapy is important from both an evolutionary and a medical perspective. Combination therapy has 
been put forward as an alternative to decrease the rate of resistance evolution (Cottarel and Wierzbowski 2007). Also 
with the goal to enhance antibacterial activity of antibiotics, it has been hypothesised to not only enhance the activity 
of known antibiotics but also to support the clinical development of agents previously found to be very effective but 
too toxic for the host. Another advantage is that this approach might lead to shorter and/or lower dosing regimens, 
which has the potential to reduce the rate of acquirement of resistance in pathogens (Ocampo et al. 2014). Combination 
therapy can also provide the opportunity to expand the therapeutic use of the approved drug (Cottarel and Wierzbowski 
2007). Only a few studies have however experimentally investigated combination therapy and the data on the beneficial 
effects on combination therapy are conflicting (Vestergaard et al. 2013). 
Previous studies have suggested that the susceptibility of E. coli towards tetracycline is increased by combining 
tetracycline and amikacin. Collateral sensitivity between the two have been observed by Imamovic and Sommer 2013 
as well as Munck et al. 2014. Collateral sensitivity and resistance occurs when mutations conferring resistance to one 
drug increase or decrease sensitivity to another drug. If resistance to one drug gives collateral resistance to another 
drug, a combination of the two will not be very effective in reducing the evolution of resistance.  
In a study by Suzuki, Horinouchi and Furusawa 2014, general cross-resistance to drugs with the same mechanism of 
action was hypothesised, indicating that aminoglycosides and tetracyclines, which both inhibits protein synthesis would 
exhibit cross-resistance. Based on their results, this was not the case since aminoglycoside drugs revealed collateral 
sensitivity to many classes of antibiotics with the same or different mechanism of action including tetracycline (figure 
10). They showed that when E. coli cells acquired resistance to aminoglycoside drugs, the resistant strains generally 
became more susceptible to other drugs than the parent strain and vice versa. These results are also seen in a study by 
Lázár et al. 2014, where they analysed cross-resistance networks using E. coli. In a study by Munck et al. 2014, they 
predicted resistance development against drug combinations by collateral responses to component drugs. Using 
adaptive evolution, they studied the resistance response in E. coli towards mono and combination therapy. They found 
that the degree to which a combination can reduce the evolution of resistance depends on the degree of collateral 
resistance and collateral sensitivity between its component drugs (Munck et al. 2014). In another study by Imamovic 
and Sommer 2013, they constructed collateral sensitivity profiles of drug-resistant E. coli strains. The susceptibility 
profiles were defined on the basis of MIC inhibition curves and strains resistant to amikacin revealed collateral sensitivity 
towards tetracycline in a 2x reduction of MIC compared to the reference strain. Strains resistant to tetracycline revealed 
no change in susceptility towards amikacin compared to the reference strain. When they sequenced the resistant 
strains, they found that tetracycline selected for mutations that lead to an increased efflux and decreased membrane 
permeability, whereas amikacin selected for an entirely different set of mutations specifically involved in 
aminoglycoside-induced membrane stress. They then suggested that the reason for collateral sensitivity between the 
drug pair tetracycline and amikacin, is that they select for two subsets of mutations, where one of them counteract the 
effect of the other (Imamovic and Sommer 2013).  
When investigating the resistance pattern of mutants evolved on tetracycline and/or amikacin the hypothesis was then, 
based on the previously mentioned studies, that mutants evolved on aminoglycosides would be expected to show 
increased susceptibility towards tetracycline. This was however not the case as mutants isolated on amikacin obtained 
MIC values two and four times higher compared to the MIC value of E. coli MG1655 towards tetracycline and mutants 
evolved on tetracycline obtained MIC values two, four and eight times higher compared to E. coli MG1655. This 
indication of cross-resistance was also seen in a decreased susceptibility towards ME and CIP in figure 21A and 21B for 
the mutants isolated on monotherapy. Mutants resistant to the combination of antibiotics revealed a decreased 
susceptibility towards the antibiotic combination and single antibiotics. Also a much higher resistance towards 
monotherapy compared to the mutants evolved on single antibiotics were discovered.  
==PAGE:48==
Page 48 of 66 
 
A possible explanation for the different outcome of results in this study compared to Imamovic and Sommer; Munck; 
Lázár et al. and Suzuki, Horinouchi and Furusawa is in the experimental setup and methods used. Selection of antibiotic 
resistant mutants were performed using adaptive evolution of the parent strain in LB broth, where the strains were 
evolved towards single drugs until they reached EUCAST clinical resistance breakpoint. Selection of antibiotic resistant 
mutants in this study were conducted using LB agar plates containing different concentrations of antibiotics according 
to CLSI guidelines. Antibiotic activity was measured as the amount of drug required to inhibit bacterial growth by 90% 
relative to a control containing no drug after a fixed incubation time (IC90). Meaning that they measure at which 
concentrations 10% of the population can grow. In my study, antibiotic activity was investigated by measuring at which 
concentrations zero percent of the population can grow. It was however not completely zero percent, owing to the cut-
off when measuring MIC values. Also, in my study, I most likely selected for mutants with a single mutation, whereas in 
the work by Imamovic and Sommer, they accumulated mutations by serial transfer. Due to these differences in 
experimental setup, comparing results can be problematic. 
Another possible explanation for the different results acquired, could be mutations leading to increased expression of 
efflux genes, which can contribute to increased resistance towards antibiotics. The multiple drug resistance (MDR) 
phenotype is often associated in bacteria with efflux pumps in the cytoplasmic membrane (Elkins and Nikaido 
2002). There are more than seven efflux systems in E. coli that can export structurally unrelated antibiotics, these MDR 
pump systems contribute to intrinsic resistance for antibiotics (Sulavik et al. 2001).  
Mutations leading to overexpression of MDR pump genes show resistance towards many antibiotics but more work is 
needed to identify the contributions of the MDR pumps for susceptibility to both aminoglycosides and also to other 
antibiotics (Sulavik et al. 2001). The AcrAB system is the major MDR mechanism in E. coli (figure 26). They confer 
resistance in E. coli to a variety of lipophilic and amphiphilic drugs, dyes, and detergent molecules that include 
tetracycline, chloramphenicol, fluoroquinolones, β-lactams and erythromycin. Not all MDR efflux pumps export 
exclusively lipophilic and amphiphilic substrates. AcrD is responsible for resistance to a variety of hydrophilic 
aminoglycosides. In a study by Elkins and Nikaido 2002, the have showed that AcrD functions with AcrA, at least for 
some substrates. To further elucidate where the mutations have occurred and how the genetic adaptation to a single 
antibiotic increase or decrease the sensitivity to others, sequencing will be necessary to obtain the underlying molecular 
mechanism.

Another target for possible cross-resistance evolution towards tetracycline and amikacin is ribosomal mutations. This

One explanation for the high susceptibility of amikacin resistant mutant and combination resistant mutants towards 
amikacin could be in the transport system into the cell. This could be impaired by an anaerobic environment or if the 
ionized amikacin is repelled by increased concentration of divalent cations (Scholar 2011). 
Using antibiotics targeting different resistance mechanisms, the mutant resistance profiles was further investigated to 
reveal cross resistance between tetracycline, amikacin and their combination towards ME and CIP. Cross resistance was 
also seen towards CAZ for mutants isolated on combination therapy, whereas mutants isolated on monotherapy did 
not reveal a clear picture of either decreased or increased susceptibility towards CAZ. These results indicated that there 
is a complex interplay between collateral susceptibility changes, changes in permeability across the cell membrane and 
drug interactions which drives the evolution of resistance in drug combinations.  
At least three main mechanisms can confer resistance to carbapenems (ME) in E. coli. Acquired carbapenemases (β-
lactamases with activity against carbapenems), mutational alteration of penicillin binding proteins and reduced 
permeability of the outer membrane by loss of porins in combination with overexpression of an AmpC or an ESBL 
enzyme (Adler et al. 2013). Cephalosporins resistance (CAZ), also a beta-lactam like ME, is often attributed to PBP-
modification or protection by beta-lactamases (Livermore 1987). Resistance to fluoroquinolones (CIP), typically arises 
as a result of alterations in the target enzymes (DNA gyrase and topoisomerase IV) and of changes in drug entry and 
efflux (jacoby 2005). 
The broad spectrum antibiotic resistance exhibited by the isolated mutants in this study could be due to several specific 
individual mutations towards each antibiotic tested or it could be due to mutations in an efflux pump gene conferring 
increased efflux activity. The efflux pump would have to be an MDR pump, exhibiting a broad range of substrate 
specificity.   
Resistance mutations is expected to yield a fitness cost because they target important biological functions in the cell 
(Melnyk, Wong and Kassen 2014). In this study, the fitness of the resistant mutants isolated on mono and combination 
therapy was visualised by doubling times. As expected almost all mutants showed an increase in doubling times and 
thereby reduced fitness compared to E. coli MG1655. This result is not surprising as many antibiotics target important 
cellular processes and resistance to them either disrupts those processes or imposes large energetic burdens that 
reduce competitive ability against sensitive strains (Melnyk, Wong and Kassen 2014). The biological cost is a key 
parameter in determining the stability and potential reversibility of resistance (Andersson 2003). This means that the 
resistance mutations could be out-competed by E. coli MG1655 in the absence of antibiotic selection and the frequency 
of resistant bacteria would decline in the population. However, it rarely succeeds in eliminating them altogether (Enne 
2010, Johnsen et al. 2011).  
One of the questions raised in this study is whether there is a correlation between the magnitude of the fitness cost and 
the level of resistance conferred by a given mutation? According to Andersson 2003, if higher levels of resistance require 
a cell to devote more resources to detoxifying or eliminating a drug or involve mutations of greater phenotypic effect, 
we might expect a negative relationship between MIC and fitness. The mutants selected on tetracycline exhibited very 
low fitness cost compared to E. coli MG1655 possibly indicating that the mutation involved in tetracycline resistance do 
not interfere much with the basic cellular functions. One exception was mutants selected on the lowest concentration 
of tetracycline (1 µg/mL, 1xMIC) which showed higher fitness cost. Interestingly, these mutants with a higher fitness 
cost also exhibited eight times higher MIC values compared to E. coli MG1655 towards tetracycline. They also exhibited 
an increase in MIC values compared to the other tetracycline resistant mutants. These findings provide a strong 
indication of different mutations evolved in the different mutants.  Most of the amikacin resistant mutants exhibited 
very high doubling times compared to E. coli MG1655. From approximately 1 hour for E. coli MG1655 to around 5 hours 
for the slowest growing amikacin mutants. Indicating a high fitness cost involved for the high level of amikacin 
resistance. This seem to fit with the results obtained by Springer et al. 2001 and Holberger and Hayes 2009 where 
resistance to aminoglycosides altered the structure of the ribosome and thereby interfered with basic cellular functions. 
==PAGE:50==
Page 50 of 66 
 
Also over-expression of multidrug efflux pumps due to regulatory mutations may cause metabolic imbalances that result 
in a reduced growth rate (Melnyk, Wong and Kassen 2014). 
The mutants selected on combination therapy exhibited a significant increase in doubling times but there seems to be 
a tendency of enhanced fitness in mutants selected on combination therapy compared to mutants selected on mono-
therapy. Combined with the increased MIC values towards mono-therapy compared with mutants selected on single 
antibiotics, these results could have implications for combination therapy as a clinical tool for resistance management 
since enhanced fitness will provide the antibiotic combination resistant mutants with an advantage in both a mono and 
single drug environment. 
The fitness cost of resistant mutants did not seem to correlate with the level of resistance or antibiotic selection 
concentration. A reason for this is that the fitness cost conferred by resistance mutations can be partly or fully restored 
by compensatory mutations without loss of resistance (Andersson 2003). These are second-site mutations during the 
evolution of the resistant bacteria (Bjorkman et al. 2000). Compensatory evolution is usually more common than 
reversion as the mutation rate for compensation is higher (because of the larger mutational target) than that for 
reversion (Andersson 2003). In a large population the most fit mutants will be selected whereas if population 
bottlenecks are also introduced compensated mutants with lower fitness can become fixed if they are formed at a 
higher rate than the high- fitness susceptible revertants (Andersson 2003). 
To further investigate fitness cost of the antibiotic resistant mutants, competition experiments between susceptible and 
resistant strains would be preferred because it is an integrated measure involving all phases of the growth cycle and can 
capture aspects of competition such as toxin production that may not be reflected in pure culture assays (Melnyk, Wong 
and Kassen 2014). 
How well growth rates measured in laboratory settings correlate to fitness in real life is unclear, but it is reasonable to 
assume that if a mutation is associated with a cost under favorable in vitro conditions there is also a cost under more 
stressful in vivo conditions (Gustafsson, Cars and Andersson 2003). The evolution of fitness costs and its connection to 
clinical treatment is a very complex issue. Factors that can modulate fitness costs are epistasis between the resistance 
mutation and genetic background or other resistance mutations, environment, compensatory mutations or genetic 
linkage between resistance mutation and other mutations under selection (Melnyk, Wong and Kassen 2014).  
Combination therapy has lower MF than monotherapy and it also results in higher MIC values than monotherapy. This 
indicates that emergence of resistance is less likely compared to monotherapy but that the emerging combination 
resistant mutants are displaying broad-spectrum resistance and are less susceptible towards antibiotics compared to 
monotherapy. There also seems to be a tendency towards enhanced fitness for combination therapy resistant mutants 
compared to monotherapy. In addition, the killing kinetics of the combination therapy seems to follow the bactericidal 
activity of amikacin, except that the killing effect seems to be less for combination therapy compared to amikacin 
monotherapy. 
Based on the results obtained in this study, the only benefit of combination therapy by tetracycline and amikacin is the 
decreased antibiotic resistance evolution as measured by MF. Both mono and combination therapy used against E. coli 
MG1655 encouraged the selection of multi-drug resistance, compromising the resistance prevention argument of 
combination therapy in this organism. It might however, be plausible in a clinical strain and the selection processes 
could be further elucidated in vivo to include the dynamic environment in resistance evolution.

7 CONCLUSION 
The experiments conducted during this thesis work reveal several important aspects concerning the evolution of 
resistance through combination therapy. I have demonstrated that combination therapy using tetracycline and amikacin 
prevented selection of resistant strains more efficiently compared to monotherapy using tetracycline or amikacin. 
However, the susceptibility towards tetracycline or amikacin was decreased by combination therapy compared to 
monotherapy. The resistance profile of antibiotic combination resistant mutants displayed broad-spectrum resistance 
towards antibiotics using different mechanisms of action. A tendency towards enhanced fitness for combination therapy 
resistant mutants compared to monotherapy were discovered and the bactericidal activity of amikacin seems to be 
dominant in the combination when examining killing kinetics. The killing effect was decreased using combination 
therapy compared to amikacin monotherapy even though additive interactions between tetracycline and amikacin were 
demonstrated.  
It was speculated that the broad spectrum antibiotic resistance exhibited by the isolated mutants in this study could be 
due to several specific individual mutations towards each antibiotic tested or it could be due to mutations in an efflux 
pump gene conferring increased efflux activity.  
A very interesting thing about this study is how previously discovered results, like how the combination therapy by 
tetracycline and amikacin convey collateral sensitivity towards tetracycline in E. coli, can drastically change due to 
differences in experimental methods. Since tetracycline and amikacin have the same mechanism of action, the discovery 
of cross resistance between tetracycline and amikacin for mono therapy resistant mutants was not surprising from a 
theoretical point of view but it opposed previously obtained results by Suzuki, Horinouchi and Furusawa and Lázár et al. 
2014. 
Elucidating on the consequences of combination therapy on antibiotic evolution gives rise to many new questions and 
prompts the need for further research into this complex interplay between collateral susceptibility changes, changes in 
permeability across the cell membrane and drug interactions which seems to drive the evolution of resistance in drug 
combinations.

8 FUTURE PERSPECTIVES 
The assays used to determine MF and MIC values should be repeated in order to achieve higher statistical assurance. A 
wider concentration range within the MSW could also have been used in an attempt to further investigate the influence 
of antibiotic concentration on selection. 
Resistance mechanism of the isolated mutants need to be elucidated. Instead of sequencing the whole genome, it might 
be enough to do ribosomal sequencing to identify the mutations. An up-regulation of efflux pump systems could be 
measured phenotypically by qPCR to determine if the mutation lies within an efflux pump.  Resistance profiles of 
mutants towards a broader range of antibiotics would also further the results regarding broad-spectrum antibiotic 
resistance selection for mono and combination therapy by tetracycline and amikacin.  
All experiments have been performed exclusively with E. coli MG1655, which is a laboratory strain. Conducting the 
experiments made in this study in a clinical E. coli strain would be interesting. The ability of combination therapy using 
tetracycline and amikacin to select for mutants with decreased susceptibility towards a broad range of antibiotics could 
be further investigated in vivo to get a more accurate simulation of the therapeutic effect. 
Since the rate of selection of antibiotic-resistant mutants depends upon the concentration of the selecting agent, a full 
risk assessment for emergence of resistance to tetracycline and amikacin should include quantification of mutation 
frequencies at therapeutically achievable drug concentrations.    
An interesting perspective on this study would be to conduct experiments using time-kill assays. For example, using 
different antibiotic concentrations (1, 2, 4 and 8xMIC) to investigate the killing kinetics based on antibiotic concentration 
or to elucidate on the bactericidal/bacteriostatic activity of amikacin and tetracycline and which one is the dominant 
antibiotic by adding the antibiotics at different time intervals. These experiments could be optimised using flow 
cytometry to be able to enumerate cell concentration instantly instead of plate counting.
