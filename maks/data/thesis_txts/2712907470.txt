Abstract
Fermentation of cassava is a process widely used which aims at achieving food
security and safety. However, the end of fermentation is evaluated empirically, and
the process occurs without standardization, which results in products of inconsistent
quality, and safety. This project will focus on developing a starter culture suitable to
preserve cassava by the reduction of the pH, detoxify from cyanogenic glucosides and
deactivate pathogens. In addition, cassava softening at the end of fermentation is a
desired feature for processing further into local food products.
Three different lactic acid bacteria strains (Limosilactobacillus fermentum DK1420,
Lactipantibacillus plantarum DK1403 and Lactobacillus delbrueckii DK777) as well
as two strains of yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae (Y448 and Y1518) were tested as
potential starter cultures both individually and mixed.
All strains adapted to cassava substrate within 24 hours of fermentation. L. del-
brueckii DK777 grew more in the presence of yeast showing a synergistic behavior.
The pH was inversely correlated to growth of bacteria and yeast, with L. plantarum
DK1403 and its yeasts combination dropping the pH below 4 within 24 h. Bacillus
cereus growth was predicted to exceed the safe limits during spontaneous fermentation
in contrast to the inoculated sample with the strains of highest acidification rate L.
plantarum DK1403+S. cerevisiae Y448.
Cyanogenic glucosides were decreased by the activity of bacteria and yeast. The
presence of yeast S. cerevisiae Y448 showed better detoxification effect both combined
with L. plantarum DK1403 and L. delbrueckii DK777. However, due to the low validity
of the data, it cannot show the effect of starter cultures in detoxification of cassava.
Finally, cassava obtained from fermentation with L. delbrueckii DK777 + S. cerevisiae
Y448 gave the most desired texture for processing further which is attributed to the
inter-relationship between these two strains.
The use of starter culture resulted in a controlled fermentation process compared
to spontaneous. The final starter culture from this project is suggested to be a com-
bination with both L. delbrueckii DK777 and L. plantarum DK1403 along with the
yeast S. cerevisiae Y448, as both LAB gave promising results and their combina-
tion might give the fulfilling performance requirements. All strains tested are free of
legal constraints and can be studied further for their implementation in developing
countries.

1
Introduction
Cassava (Manihot esculenta Crantz) ranks fourth in the list of the most important food
crops in developing countries after rice, wheat and corn.
More than 500 million peo-
ple depend on cassava as their main source of energy, around 200 million of whom live
in sub-Saharan Africa [1]. Cassava roots are processed into different food forms includ-
ing fermented and un-fermented products. Fermentation is the most popular processing
method of cassava that improves the quality of the product by the formation of flavor and
taste compounds, preservation, and detoxification of the root [2].
Some of the well known fermented cassava products are fermented mashes, including
”fufu”, fermented flours, as ”lafun”, roasted grits, like ”gari” and steamed grits, as ”at-
tieke”. The fermentation process varies from place to place resulting many times in dif-
ferent names for the same product [3].
Cassava fermentation process can be broadly
categorized as solid-state fermentation as in gari production, where cassava is grated or
sliced prior to fermentation while exposed to the natural atmosphere or pressed in a bag,
and ”submerged” (retting) process as in fufu or lafun production, where the tubers are
cut into chunks and soaked in water for the duration of the fermentation [4].
The production process of submerged cassava fermented commodities incorporates com-
mon processing steps including peeling, chopping, fermenting by soaking in water con-
tained in a fermenting pot, de-watering, and sieving to remove fibers [1]. The majority of
fermented cassava products in Africa are produced via spontaneous fermentation process,
as a low-cost method for food preservation in a home-based environment. The fermen-
tation process takes place by microorganisms naturally present in the raw material, pro-
cessing equipment, and environment and the final product is the result of the competitive
activities of a variety of contaminating microorganisms [5]. The uncontrolled processing
conditions incorporated in traditional processes arise many challenges. There is a large
variation in the quality of the end product from batch to batch and many times failure of
the fermentation process can create health risks related to the survival pathogenic bacteria
[6]. Despite the limited epidemiological data for foodborne diseases, diarrheal diseases has
been for years a major health hazard in Africa. Some of the bacteria responsible for such
symptoms are Escherichia coli, Shigella spp., Salmonella e.t.c. resulted from contaminated
food products. Considering the wide spectrum of fermented products in developing coun-
tries and the accompanied challenges of this process, it is dictated to adopt better options
for food fermentation [7].
This research project is initiated from the cooperation between WFP Centre of Excellence
against Hunger Brazil and DTU Skylab FoodLab. The goal of the joint forces is to fight
against hunger and malnutrition by combining WFP’s long-term experience in assisting
countries with their food security programs and DTU’s expertise in translating research
into technical solutions for real world problems. Starting point was the improvement of
safety for Mbala Pinda, a cassava-based snack composed of fermented cassava mash and
6
==PAGE:7==
peanut butter wrapped in banana leaves, that is produced traditionally in the Republic of
Congo and distributed as school meals and in health centers to vulnerable people of the
area. The Mbala Pinda project from the World Food Programme (WFP) works towards
supporting women groups producing Mbala Pinda snack, which helps children to receive
a nutritional boost but also encourage local economy by generating income [8].
The goal of this project is to develop an appropriate starter culture for cassava fermen-
tation that can find application in Republic of Congo for Mbala Pinda preparation to
improve safety of the product. However, as submerged fermentation process is the key
step in the preparation of various cassava-based products in Africa, the aim is to pro-
vide a starter culture with expanded application in cassava fermentation for low-income
countries in Africa and South America. Therefore, there is an opportunity to standardise
the method for production of fermented cassava of high consistent quality which is not
currently available.

2.1
Overview of cassava plant
Cassava plant is a 1–2 m high woody shrub with tuberous roots see Fig. 1, that belongs to
Euphorbiaceae family of plants and is grown as an annual crop, in tropical and subtropical
areas. The crop is cultivated mostly in various countries of Africa, Asia and Latin America
with Africa being the biggest cassava producer from the rest of the world and Nigeria
together with Demographic Republic of Congo being the top two producing nations. It is
regarded as food security crop in areas prone to drought and famine, due to its relevant
resistance to drought and tolerance of poor soil, pests and diseases [9].
Figure 1: Cassava plant [10]
Cassava’s edible starchy roots are nutty flavored com-
posed of 80–90% carbohydrates on dry weight basis,
outreaching potatoes carbohydrate content. As a low-
cost carbohydrate, it is the major source of dietary
energy for low-income consumers in many parts of
Africa. Starch accounts for 80% of the carbohydrates,
followed by small quantities of sucrose, glucose, and
maltose. Cassava roots are poor source of protein (7-
2% protein), rich in arginine but low content of es-
sential amino acids (lysine, tryptophan, phenylalanine
and tyrosine) especially sulfur-containing amino acids
(methionine and cysteine). They contain significant
amounts of calcium (50 mg/100 g), phosphorus (40
mg/100 g), and vitamin C (25 mg/100 g). However, nutritional composition is dependent
to different parameters of variety and age of the harvested crop, nature of soil, climatic
7
==PAGE:8==
conditions and other environmental factors during cultivation [11].
One of the major limitation of cassava’s use as a food it’s the presence of cyanogenic
glucosides, compounds that render its consumption toxic to humans. Varieties with high
concentration of toxic compounds are bitter and cultivated in higher extend than the less
toxic sweet varieties due to their resistance to drought and higher production yields. In
addition to that, fresh cassava roots are highly perishable, that cannot stand up to 2 days,
because they dry soon after harvest, unless they remain stored underground attached to
the main stem that will conserve the root for several months. This storage method is used
extensively from farmers to secure food sources in cases of food shortage [1]. Cassava’s
toxicity and high perishable nature have been the main obstacles to cassava consumption
and make it necessary to process the roots into more stable and safer products. Following
to that, cassava with low levels of proteins and micro-nutrients have led to limited economic
development. Improved quality of processed cassava products without significant increase
in processing costs is of high importance to the expansion to various markets local and
foreign [12].
Cassava is processed in several food commodities including fermented, unfermented foods
and drinks and they vary in their mode of processing (boiled, steamed, dried, roasted
e.t.c.). Some of the most known fermented cassava products are depicted in Figure 2.

2.2
Small scale cassava fermentation in African Countries
Most traditional fermented products result from natural fermentation carried out under
non-sterile conditions. The benefits arise from natural fermentation is the uniqueness of
flavors that fermentation with the use of starter cultures under aseptic conditions is hard
to imitate. However, natural fermentation processes tend to be difficult to control, due to
the presence of unspecified microorganisms [7]. The process is described from a usually
long initiation period 24-48h during the lag phase of microbial growth that gives space to
contaminated microbes to grow and compete for nutrients and the end of the process is
generally based on sensory attributes e.g. texture or odour due to the lack of scientific
expertise [14], [15]. Variations in the quality of the final product, can render the final
product hazardous for human consumption. The main challenges associated with spon-
taneous cassava fermentation are the delayed acidification and insufficient detoxification
of the root. The contribution of fermentation to chemical food safety by reducing toxic
compounds is described later in Section 2.3.1.
Cassava substrate is favorable for the growth of pathogenic and spoilage bacteria. The fer-
mentation enhances microbiological safety of cassava product by producing antimicrobial
9
==PAGE:10==
factors such as lactic acid, bacteriocins, carbon dioxide, hydrogen peroxide and ethanol.
The contaminated microorganisms originate from the cassava itself in the form of vegeta-
tive pathogens from the soil, the processing environment, and the poor personal hygiene
of food handlers [16].
2.2.1
Microorganisms in cassava fermentation
Fermentation of cassava is described by a mixed microbial flora of bacteria, yeast, and
molds.
The number and the type of microorganisms involved in cassava fermentation
depends on several parameters including type of fermentation; solid-state or submerged
fermentation, the environment where the process takes place as well as the variety of cas-
sava and its intrinsic characteristics such as the nutrition composition [17]. Microbiological
studies on fermenting cassava samples have shown that bacterial strains involved in sub-
merged cassava fermentation belong to microbial groups of Lactobacillus spp., Leuconostoc
spp., Bacillus spp. and Corynebacterium spp. [17]. Cassava fermentation is dominated
by the fermentative activities of lactic acid bacteria (LAB) thoughout the whole process,
and most of them belong to the L.plantarum-group [18]. The acidic conditions created by
the production of organic acids from LAB makes the environment selective against acid
less tolerant microorganisms, whereas L.plantarum that dominates the process has the
ability to maintain a low pH [19]. Bacillus spp. are highly associated with the textural
modifications during cassava fermentation due to their ability for cellulase production.
However, the involvement of this genera in cassava fermentation causes concerns for the
safety of the final product, thus B.cereus strains can be responsible for food poisoning by
producing pathogenic toxins [20].
The involvement of molds in cassava fermentation is related to solid-state processes, thus
the low oxygen tension during submerged fermentation does not allow their growth [21].
Oyewole et al. (1988) [17] reported the implication of Geotrichum sp. and Aspergillus sp.
in the early stage of fermentation originated from the cassava tubers, that disappear after
36 hours of fermentation.
Yeast are the second predominant group of microorganisms involved in cassava fermenta-
tion after LAB. Reported yeasts in solid state fermentation are Candida krusei, C.tropicalis
and Zygosaccharomyces bailii as the most prominent yeasts. Yeast strains isolated in sub-
merged cassava fermentation are C. krusei, C. tropicalis, Pichia saitoi, Saccharomyces
cerevisiae, P. anomala and Z. baili. Yeast strains present in cassava fermentation have
amylolytic activities and play an important role in the fermentation process by breaking
down starch to simple sugars that are converted into organic acids form the lactic acid
bacteria [19].

2.2.2
Safety of fermented cassava
Several studies on cassava fermented products have shown the presence of Bacillus cereus
and Staphylococcus aureus [22], [23], [24]. Although there are not reported cases of food
poisoning from cassava consumption due to lack of consumption patterns, epidemiological
data, and appropriate surveillance programs, the isolation of pathogenic microorganisms
from cassava products calls concerns for the safety of these products [25].
Bacillus cereus is associated with food poisoning that can result from the ingestion of
the emetic toxins produced in food matrix or from ingestion of the bacterial strains that
produce diarrheal toxins in the small intestine of the host. The diarrheal infection cause
diarrhea and abdominal pain 7-16 h after ingestion, while the emetic infection causes
nausea and vomiting within 1–5 h of ingestion. Bacillus spp. are found throughout the
environment, and is a frequent inhabitant of soils, fresh and marine water environments
[26]. These type of food poisoning is under reported due to its self-limiting nature, how-
ever the morbidity is higher for the vulnerable consumers in developing countries due to
malnutrition and decrease immunity [27]. S.aureus cause food poisoning from the con-
sumption of its toxins produced in food that cause nausea, violent vomiting, abdominal
cramping with 2–8 h of ingestion [28]. Both toxins from B. cereus and S. aureus are heat
stable, and post processing of fermented cassava usually involves mild to moderate heat
treatment and therefore gives rise to a potential risk to consumers.
2.3
Cassava detoxification during fermentation
2.3.1
Cyanide
An important factor influencing the use of cassava as a food is its toxicity. Cassava is
a crop that can be the source of one of the most potent poisons: hydrogen cyanide that
results from their high concentration of cyanogenic glucosides [29]. Cyanogenic glucosides
are a group of nitrile-containing secondary plant compounds and natural plant toxins that
are present in several plants, most of them are consumed by humans [30]. The most known
cyanogenic glucosides in plants include amygdalin, linamarin, prunasin, dhurrin, lotaus-
tralin, and taxiphyllin. Despite of structural diversity in cyanogenic glucosides, almost all
of them are derived from only six different amino acids L-valine (Val-), L-isoleucine (Ile-),
L-leucine (Leu-), L-phenylalanine, or L-tyrosine, and cyclopentenyl-glycine (a nonprotein
amino acid) [31].
Cassava in all parts of the plant (pulp and peel of the tuber and leaves of the plant)
contain the Val- and Ile-derived cyanogenic glucosides, linamarin and lotaustralin, in an
approximately 97:3 ratio and they are primarily synthesized in the shoot apex and trans-
ported to the tubers [31]. B-glucosidase (e.g linamarase), an endogenous enzyme able

the distribution of cyanide in the different parts of cassava plant. In addition, cassava is
generally classified into sweet and bitter varieties according to the cyanogenic glucoside
contents of their roots. The level of cyanogenic glucosides produced is dependent upon
the age and the variety of the plant, as well as environmental factors [31].
The toxicity of cyanogenic glucosides and their derivatives is dependent on the release
of hydrogen cyanide. Toxicity may result in acute cyanide poisoning and has also been
implicated in the etiology of several chronic diseases [30]. Cyanide inhibits cellular respira-

Cassava tubers are generally processed by different methods which can result in decreasing
the cyanogenic content and convert the root into stable and safe product. These methods
include, peeling, washing, cutting or grating, cooking, soaking, fermentation and drying.
All these methods have been reported to reduce cyanide, which also in different combi-
nations of them show different losses [36], [37], [4]. Most processing techniques, along
with root cellular disruption, trigger the enzymatic hydrolysis of linamarin by the endoge-
nous enzyme linamarase to the corresponding ketone (acetone cyanohydrin) and glucose.
Cyanohydrin is able to break down non-enzymatically to hydrogen cyanide and acetone
dependent on pH and temperature, being stable while pH values are getting more acidic
[37]. In neutral or alkaline conditions, it hydrolyzes spontaneously into HCN.This enzy-

During fermentation, microbial flora (physically present or inoculated) is increased and
contributes to cyanogens reduction by initially softening the cassava roots which helps
the contact between endogenous linamarin and linamarase. Linamarin and linamarase are
physically separated in cassava tissues [33]. The linamarase produced by the cassava lactic
acid bacteria, accompanied with the endogenous linamarase contribute to the process of
detoxification [38]. HCN, being heat unstable, either escapes in the air or dissolves in
water, leaving small amounts of bound cyanide. It is noted that it is impossible to remove
all the cyanide through processing, but it can reach insignificant amounts. The resid-
ual cyanide can be in the three different forms of glucoside, cyanohydrin or free cyanide.
Unfortunately, careful processing of cassava tubers to remove the cyanide-generating con-
stituents may result in loss of proteins, vitamins, and minerals and therefore the nutritional
value is reduced.

2.3.2
Exposure
Human exposure to cyanide by dietary intake poses a major significant problem for cassava-
consuming populations, especially in Africa. Ingestion of incompletely processed cassava-
derived products in combination with an unbalanced diet deficient in sulfur amino acids
may give rise to chronic cyanide intoxication, as sulfur amino acids are required for cyanide
detoxification. In extreme cases, this may result in severe diseases such as tropical ataxic
neuropathy and Konzo, a spastic paraparesis.
While exposure to cyanide has been crudely estimated to be 15–50 mg/day in endemic
areas in some cases, owing to the limitations of data on exposure and potential impact of
confounders such as malnutrition, low protein content of the diet, vitamin deficiencies, and
iodine status, the available data do not provide meaningful information on dose–response
for cyanide. [31].
2.4
Organoleptic changes during cassava fermentation
Food fermentation processes also result in organoleptic changes, like texture, flavor and
aroma formation. Lactic acid bacteria influence the flavor of the fermented food by the
production of lactic acid and subsequently lowering the pH which results in an increase in
sourness [39]. During fermentation votalite compounds are produced that affect the final
aroma of the product. Butanoic acid, propanoic acid and acetic acid are some examples
of compounds responsible for cassava fermented products [40].
Change in cassava’s texture is one of the desired effects of fermentation to produce Mbala
Pinda, including other products such as gari, fufu, or lafun [41]. In particular, in the
case of Mpala pinda texture is an important quality attribute, where at the end of the
fermentation, the firm tube should be soft enough that it can easily be mashed by hands
in spherical shaped portions.
The softening of cassava roots has been linked to the structural framework of starch
molecules. Cassava roots have on average 35–40% of total solids of which starch is the
major component [42]. Starch macro molecules are packed in granules and located within
the parenchyma cells [43]. The cell wall has a cellulose structured composition bind with
pectins and surrounded by hemicellulosic polymers [44].
During fermentation hydrolytic enzymes produced by microorganisms break down the
compact cell wall structure and release the trapped starch granules [43]. Enzymes that
have been reported to take place during cassava retting are cellulases, pectinolytic enzymes,
pectin methyl esterases and polygalacturonases [41].
Factors that influence the texture of cassava roots genotype, time of harvest, and envi-
ronmental conditions during the growing season. Additionally, the size of cassava pieces
is a parameter of importance for the extent of softening during fermentation, thus smaller
14
==PAGE:15==
pieces provide greater available area/volume for the enzymes to diffuse into the piece and
degrade [42], [44].
2.5
Starter cultures
Introducing starter cultures in cassava fermentation can offer high degree of control over
the fermentation process and standardisation of the end product. A starter culture as
defined by Medina-Pradas from The Microbiological Quality of Food (2017) is ” a prepa-
ration of living microorganisms, which are deliberately used to assist the beginning of
fermentation, producing specific changes in the chemical composition and the sensorial
properties of the substrate to obtain a more homogeneous product” [45]. In other words,
starter cultures are live microbial cultures whose metabolic activity has a desirable effect
on the fermentation process to produce a fermented food. Starter cultures result from the
growth of microorganisms, which are usually isolated from fermented products, and added
to the fermented substrate to speed up and improve the fermentation process.
Introduction of starter culture, where selected suitable single or mixed strains or/and
species are inoculated into the fermentation substrate can improve process efficiency by
providing controlled and predictable fermentation [15].
Starter cultures based on the microorganism employed are bacteria, yeasts and fungi.
There are three types of starter cultures based on their complexity and their number and
they are classified as single strain (contains only one strain of same species), multi-strains
(three or more single strains of defined mixture), and mixed strains (contains unknown
strains). Based on growth temperature, they are divided into mesophilic (optimum growth
20-30°C) and thermophilic (optimum growth 40-45°C) starter cultures [46].
The use of starter culture result to [47]:
• Better control of fermentation process
• Improvement of product’s organoleptic characteristics
• Product protection from undesired microorganisms and spoilage
• Enhancement of product’s stability and extension of shelf life
It is essential to make the right choice of the strains which will be used as starter cultures.
The selection of unsuitable strains can lead to diversion of fermentation and the creation of
unwanted metabolic products. At the same time, the presence of inhibitors (e.g. phenolic
substances) or the presence of inappropriate conditions during fermentation (e.g.
low
temperature) may inhibit the growth and the action of selected strains.
Starter cultures must be [15]:
1. safe (not pathogens),
15
==PAGE:16==
2. reduce pH through metabolic pathways (production of specific organic acid, like
lactic acid),
3. develop under specific conditions of fermentation (low pH, temperature e.t.c.),
4. hydrolyze toxic compounds (e.g. cyanogenic glucosides via the enzyme β-glucosidase),
5. produce desirable aroma and taste through the production of votalite compounds
6. provide enzymes which contribute to the final product.
In addition, Bonatsou et al. (2017) has report that their ability to grow and dominate
over endogenous microflora is a necessary property for selecting suitable strains [48].
2.5.1
Production of starter cultures
Firstly, a starter culture can be isolated from nature or from another source, or can
be obtained commercially. Some of the fermented food products already provide their
own cultures through nature, while others need commercial cultures developed in the
labs, provided in a liquid, frozen (as pellets) or lyophilized form [49]. The advantage of
lyophilized form over liquid or frozen is their tolerance to ambient temperature where no
means of cooling are available [50].
Secondly, the starter culture should be in an active form.
Propagation of the strains
requires the right nutrient and under specific conditions of temperature, pH, aeration
etc., so it can give growing cells to form the product.
A starter culture needs media
carbon, nitrogen sources, vitamins, minerals, antioxidants, phage inhibitory substances
and neutralizers. Then, the viable cells are inoculated in a sterile growth medium where a
protective agent is added prior to freezing and packaging. The cryo-protective agents are
usually glycerol, sodium citrate and sodium β-glycerophosphate etc. [51].
Initially, strains used to be prepared in sterilized milk as a medium (by addition of calcium
carbonate as a buffer to adjust the pH). However, this kind of medium gave short shelf-life
due to loss of cell viability and fermented activity. Frozen and freeze-dried cultures are
now common in industries, especially in dairy products. One of the advantages of dried
cultures is that they can be stored for a long time, and it also makes the transportation
easier and without any loss in their activity [51].
Back slopping is a method of inoculation which has been widely used for fermented prod-
ucts, especially in dairy and meat products. It is basically a practice that a sample of
a successful fermentation is used for the subsequent fermentation [50]. Spontaneous and
back-slopping methods are commonly used for traditional fermented foods in Africa, how-
ever they have some constrains. As Kimaryo et al. (1999) has reported, the outcome of
fermentation, either spontaneous or back-slopping, relies on a chance inoculation or re-use
16
==PAGE:17==
of utensils and the end product might give some variations in the quality, nutritional value
and short life [52].
Developing the ideal starter culture, it must be the one giving the desirable effect dur-
ing process both during fermentation (e.g. rapid acidification) and in the end product
(organoleptic characteristics). There are many cultures selected so far giving desirable
food products. Lactic acid bacteria are widely used as starter culture in the fermentation
process, and they are the predominant species in cassava fermentation.
2.5.2
Lactic acid bacteria as starter cultures
Lactic acid bacteria have been used for food fermentation from the ancient times with
some great example being products such as yogurt and cheese. Acidfication of food sub-
strates via the production of lactic acid finds application as starter cultures in a variety
of fermented dairy vegetable meat and cereal products[53].
Lactic acid bacteria (LAB) constitute a diverse group of Gram-positive, non-spore forming,
microaerophilic bacteria, producing lactic acid as the main end-product of carbohydrate
fermentation. It is a heterogenous group of bacteria comprised of the genera Lactobacil-
lus, Leuconostoc, Pediococcus, Bifidobacterium and Streptococcus that share metabolic,
physiological and morphological features. They are widely distributed in the environment
and can be found in plant microbiota, flora of animals and humans. Based on their mor-
phology they are classified as cocci (Streptococcus, Pediococcus and Leuconostoc) or rods
(Lactobacillus, Bifidobacterium) and based on the end product of fermentation, LAB are
classified as obligate homofermentative, facultative heterofermentative, and obligate het-
erofermentative. Homofermentative LAB produces lactic acid as the major product and
often they are found in meat and dairy fermentations[54].On the other hand, heterofer-
mentative LAB except from lactic acid they produce other products such as CO2, ethanol
and/or acetic acid.
There are three major pathways for the metabolism of hexoses that differ from each other
in the method of dividing the carbon skeleton and give different end-products. The two
mechanisms for heterolactic and one for homolactic fermentation of hexose are schemat-
ically depicted in Figure 5. During homolactic fermentation of hexoses, occurring in the
genera Streptococcus and Pediococcus and homofermentative lactobacilli, that possess the
aldolase enzyme, lactic acid is produced as the major product. In the case of heterolactic
fermentation that takes place in the genera Leuconostoc besides lactate other products
are produced including carbon dioxide, acetate or ethanol. The two different pathways
in heterofermentation of hexose differ in the ratio of the end products acetate/lactate.
Ethanol is produced via 6P-gluconate pathway and is dependent on the presence of hy-
drogen acceptors, where for example in the presence of O2 , ethanol is not formed and
the products are H202 or H20. However, the presence of other fermented compounds e.g.
pentoses that are metabolized via different pathways give a mixture of lactate, acetate and

acid bacteria. [55]
CO2 in different amounts and depending on the substrate composition, the fermentation
conditions and the particular organism several other products are formed [55].

Experimental design
The aim of the experimental part of this project was to develop a starter culture for sub-
merged cassava fermentation that is composed of the most promising strains of yeasts and
lactic acid bacteria among the collected ones. The selection of strains is based on earlier
findings where traditional processing of cassava submerged fermentation is dominated by

The criteria for the selection of the strains are the effect on acidification, texture modifi-
cation and cyanide detoxification. In both stages of experiments a control non-inoculated
preparation of cassava with water was prepared serving as spontaneous fermentation for

3.2
Cassava fermentation
Cassava tubers were purchased from a local shop in Copenhagen, Denmark imported

3.6
Cyanide - enzymatic analysis
The procedure for the determination of cyanide content through time during fermentation
of cassava is an improved method based on Cooke (1978) [63] by Kimaryo et al. (2000)

and the non glucosidic cyanogens (acetone cyanohydrin).
3.6.1
Extraction from cassava
Cassava samples were kept at -20◦C after 24, 48 and 72 hours of fermentation. The ex-
traction method was done as described by Kimaryo (2000) [52]. The defrosted samples
were cut and 30g were homogenized with a blender (Bamix of Switzerland) at high speed
for 3 minutes with 50 mL of extracting media (25% absolute ethanol (VWR), 75% 0.1M

tracting media in order to obtain all the extract and minimize losses and then the extract
was centrifuged at 10000g for 15 minutes (Heraeus Multifuge 3SR Plus, Thermo Scien-
tific). After centrifugation, 10mL of the supernatant was collected in 10mL tubes and
stored at 4◦C for further analysis.

Discussion
The quality of fermented cassava products is variable, mainly because of the spontaneous
character of submerged cassava fermentation. Up to now, no commercial starter culture
has yet been developed for the production of well-fermented cassava products. Although
there have been many attempts for production of starter cultures for various fermented
cassava products such as lafun, fufu or attieke et.al., non of these has been efficiently trans-
ferred and adapted in small scale level. This is due to lack of appropriate technologies
for maintenance of the starter culture, a lacking inoculation procedure for small-scale fer-
mentation processes, as well as the costs involved and logistics in (sub)-tropical regions in
general [15]. As cassava fermentation can be classified in two major types; submerged and
solid state fermentation, a universal starter culture for submerged cassava fermentation
can be produced that can broadly used for fermenting cassava.
The purpose of this experimental work was to examine the efficacy of the selected strains
in cassava fermentation to produce a starter culture for application in Africa. To find the
most desired combination among the individual strains, a number of lactic acid bacteria
of L. plantarum DK1403 L. fermentum DK1420 L.delbrueckii DK777 and yeast strains
S. cerevisiae Y1518 and Y488, was examined individually and in combinations for their
effect on acidification, texture changes, and cyanide reduction. The selection of strains is
based on earlier findings where traditional processing of cassava submerged fermentation
is dominated by the fermentative activities of LAB and yeasts [17].
The microbiological studies have shown that all of the strains adapted to the cassava
substrate and grew both alone and together.
The presence of yeasts strains together
with the LAB contributed to enhanced growth of LAB while there was not significant
contribution to pH reduction. Oyewole (2000) reported that the presence of yeast enhances
the growth of LAB by supplying them with nutrients needed from them to grow. This
concludes to the fact that yeast plays an important role in the activities of lactic acid
bacteria during fermentation [69]. The population of yeast increased during fermentation
suggesting their tolerance under low acidic conditions. This comes in contrast to O.B.
Oyewole et al.(2000) [69] who reported the disappearance of S. cerevisiae after 36 h of
fermentation attributed to the highly acidic environment where yeast was unable to grow.
The final pH values were correlated to the bacterial growth where L. plantarum DK1403
with S. cerevisiae Y1518 or with S. cerevisiae Y448 showed the lowest pH 3,6 and 3,7 re-
spectively and higher bacterial growth 9,5 and 9,6 log10CFUg−1 respectively while spon-
taneous fermentation had the higher pH 5,4 and lowest bacterial growth 6,8 log10CFUg−1
within 72 h. This shows that the addition of the starter culture in high numbers (107
log10CFUg−1) ensured the dominance of this strain over wild bacteria population.
Rapid acidification is a priority for development of starter cultures for fermented cassava
products to ensure the hygiene control of fermentation process. Generally, the rate of
41
==PAGE:42==
acidification varied among the isolates tested, with L.plantarum DK1403 with S.cerevisiae
Y448 showing the highest rate, reaching pH below 4 within 24 h. These results generally
agree with earlier experimental data. Wakil, S. M.,et.al (2015) reported a rapid decrease
within 24 h to 3.4 using a combination of L.plantarum and yeast Candida famata [70].
Detoxification of cassava roots during fermentation is performed by the endogenous enzy-
matic activity, present in casssava roots and is enhanced from the exogenous enzymatic
activities, produced by microorganisms. The safe limit of cyanide content in cassava has
been set to <10 mg/ kg dw [31]. Our samples have shown values that range between the
limits of the sweet varieties (>50 mg/kg dw). Cutting of cassava roots in pieces contributes
in endogenous enzyme release with a resulting cyanide reduction that might be the reason
of the low cyanide content of the raw material (CNp=22 and ngCN= 40,1 mg/kg dw).
This hypothesis is in agreement with the results of Giraud, E., et al.,(1993) who reported
the complete hydrolysis of linamarin within 5h after grating [71]. Although, it is observed
a decrease pattern in cyanogenic and non cyanogenic conpounds with the passage of time
with the combinations of both L. plantarum DK1403 and L. delbrueckii DK777 with S.
cerevisiae Y448 showing greater performance compared to the other samples. Although
this decrease could be attributed to the presence of yeast producing linamarase enzyme,
the low validity of the data cannot show the effect of starter cultures in detoxification of
cassava. In literature has been found that mostly lactic acid bacteria, especially strains of
L. plantarum have been reported to be limanarase-producing microorganisms [52], [72],
however yeast strains of Candida krusei, Hansenula anomala was also found to be associ-
ated with production of linamarase [69], [73].
Fermentation is carried out also to soften the texture of cassava. The most promising
inoculant was the combination of L. delbrueckii DK777 with S. cerevisiae Y448, as it gave
sufficient soft texture after 72h of fermentation. The inter-relationship between these two
strains can be interpreted by the growth studies. It is observed that when yeast Y448 is
combined with DK777, it starts growing and exceeding inoculation volume (7 log10 CFU
g−1) within 24h and at the end of fermentation it reaches 8.6 log10 CFU g−1. On the
other hand, when yeast Y448 is inoculated with DK1403, it takes more than 48h for yeast
to reach 7log10 CFU g−1 and doesn’t grow much more (7.9 log10 CFU g−1 at 72h) (Table
4). This interaction between DK777 and Y448 seems to be synergistic and might enhance
the production of pectinolytic or amylolytic enzymes from S. cerevisiae and hydrolyze
cassava tissue resulting in better softening. This comes in agreement with Padonou et
al. (2010) [58], that Saccharomyces cerevisiae contributes to cassava root softening during
fermentation.
Although, many of the desirable effects of fermentation were also associated with sponta-
neous fermentation, i.e. sufficient softening after 72 h in trial 1 and cyanide reduction in
trial 3, the outcome was not every time the same, confirming the constrain of spontaneous
fermentation being predictable and controllable. Initiation of a spontaneous process takes
a relatively long time. The microbiological studies in spontaneous fermented samples in
42
==PAGE:43==
trial 2 showed detection of yeast and LAB population after 24 h whereas inoculated sam-
ples with mixed cultures shown in Table 5 and 4 start to grow within 6 h. Additionally,
pH changes start after 24 h for spontaneous fermentation while the inoculated samples
start to get acidified within 6 h.

Future perspectives
When assessing genetic resources, there are obligations that need to be follow upon sharing
the benefits arise from their utilization. These requirement have been set after the adoption
of Nagoya Protocol on Access to Genetic Resources and the Fair and Equitable Sharing
of Benefits Arising from their Utilization that entered into force on 12 October 2014.
However, the requirements differ from country to country depending on whether they
are parties to this Protocol. The strains that have been used in this project originate
from US and Denmark. US is one of the few countries that is not party to the CBD as
well as the Nagoya Protocol and Denmark [74], although being a Party to this Protocol
according to the Danish legislation, no provisions have been introduced on the access to
genetic resources within its national jurisdiction [75]. In this case there is no need for
documentation of PIC (Prior informed consent) and MAT ( Mutually agreed terms) and
only further negotiations for commercial use are needed. However, working under the EU
Regulation Article 4, users shall seek, keep and transfer to subsequent users documentation
for future accountability including information on when and where they were accessed; the
source and the user of the material as well as any rights and obligations[76].
Next steps suggested after this study for the implementation of the starter culture for
cassava fermentation are the development of inoculum in the final form and pilot scale
studies.
As explained in Section 2.5.1, the final form of starter culture can be in liquid, frozen or
lyophilized form. The latter gives the convenience of storage, given the level of technology
in most of the small scale processors, that does not require to be kept in the fridge or
freezer. Carriers of the strains should be from locally produced crops as performed in the
study of Okafor, N., et. al.,(1999) [77] where sufficient survival of LAB and yeast strains
reported that are associated with gari production for up to six weeks, including among
other starchy substrates, the cassava itself.
Cooperation of institutes, entrepreneurs and organizations can facilitate in the implemen-
tation of the technology. Institutions can facilitate the process of technology transfer by
providing the facilities needed in the production. The link between small-scale processors,
scientists and researchers can be entrepreneurs for the development of both business and
product marketing plans. Organizations can assist in dissemination of the technology to
contribute in educating the food handlers for the right use of the inoculum and make them
aware of the benefits arise [78].
43
==PAGE:44==
It is necessary for the production of starter culture to perform a pilot study, where the
selected strains will be examined under the actual environmental conditions. Oyewole
et.al. (1992) reported influence on the microbial succession during cassava fermentation
under temperature fluctuations during the day and night and over season alteration [4].
6
Conclusion
The results above demonstrate a scientific investigation for the development of a potential
starter culture in cassava fermentation. The aim is to improve the toxicological safety
as well as the quality of the processed cassava. This work confirms that the activity of
lactic acid bacteria along with the yeast S. cerevisiae showed to be of great importance
for acidification, detoxification and softening of cassava through fermentation.
L. plantarum DK1403 showed the greatest acidification rate which shows an important
inhibitory factor through B. cereus growth. However, reaching a pH value of 3,55 arises
concerns for the acceptance of the final taste (sour). L. delbrueckii DK777 combined with
S. cerevisiae Y448 not only gave promising results both in detoxification and softening of
cassava, but also showed a good acidification profile reaching pH 3,95. Thus, attention
should be focused on the combination from both LAB strains DK1403 and DK777 together
with Y448 to ensure the desired texture and taste, while it also reduces cyanide at an
acceptable safe level and lower the pH in a good acidification rate.
