Abstract

Abstract 
Formation of impurities are crucial for the safety and efficacy of pharmaceutical tablets. For this 
reason, the identification, quantification, and control of unwanted impurities are requirements for 
the drug development. The aim of this master’s thesis is to develop a reliable analytical method 
for the determination of formaldehyde in pharmaceutical tablets from the year of 2010 to 2017. 
Formaldehyde is an organic impurity that is very reactive with amines causing degradation of 
pharmaceutical tablets. However, formaldehyde is difficult to detect, due to lack of intrinsic 
chromophore, volatility and activity. Based on that fact, a sample preparation was developed using 
derivatisation of formaldehyde as shown in below figure. The derivatisation reagent 2,4-
dinitrophenylhydrazine was employed on a RP-HPLC system to achieve an absorbance at 356 nm.

The derivatisation was shown solid and implemented as part of the conducted method validation, 
which was proved sensitive and consistent. The observed peak area and the concentration range 
(0.001 to 10 μg/mL) of formaldehyde showed a linear relationship, with a coefficient of

Determined formaldehyde from sealed samples was found influenced by oxidation (including air) 
over time. For such conclusion, the film-coating provided on pharmaceutical tablets is suggested 
for further investigation.

Introduction 
This chapter introduces the scope of this thesis, first by a brief presentation of impurities in 
pharmaceutical tablets, then a presentation of the project aim and conducted work.  
1.1 Impurities in drug products 
Formation of impurities may influence on the safety and efficacy of drug substances and drug 
products. A drug substance is the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) in a drug product such as 
a pharmaceutical tablet. Impurities are unwanted components that are neither the drug substance 
nor the excipients in the drug product, but may be derived from them. Unwanted impurities are 
crucial for the pharmaceutical industry, which is why impurity identification, quantification, and 
control are requirements for the drug development [1–3]. Because of this, an international 
cooperation between authorities and the pharmaceutical industries has been established [4]. 
1.1.1 The International Council for Harmonisation 
The International Council for Harmonisation of Technical Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for 
Human Use (ICH) has formulated unified guidelines (quality, efficacy, safety, and multidisciplinary) 
for the handling of impurities in drug products. The ICH quality (Q) guideline includes topics such 
as stability (Q1), analytical validation (Q2), impurities (Q3), pharmacopoeias (Q4), and 
specifications (Q6) [4]. These topics provide guidelines to develop a qualitative evaluation of 
impurities. Thus, revealing the scope of impurity profiling of a given drug product [5].

1.1.2 Source of impurities 
The origin of impurities in pharmaceutical tablets as the solid dosage form may be generated from 
various sources as presented in figure 1.1 [1].  
 
 
Figure 1.1. Formation of impurities from different sources. 
 
According to ICH Q3A, the impurities are classified as organic impurities, inorganic impurities and 
residual solvents. Organic impurities may arise from raw materials, by-products, synthetic 
intermediates and degradation products. The number of inorganic impurities (e.g. reagents, salts, 
heavy metals, catalysts) and residual solvents are limited and easily identified. This is due to their 
known physiological effects and toxicity, which are neither contributing to the toxicity nor the side 
effects of the drug substances. This situation is different with organic impurities [6].  
Organic impurities may originate from raw materials such as the drug substance or the excipients. 
Such impurities may occur from the instability of the drug substance itself or from reactions 
between the drug substance and the excipients. In addition, toxicity of these organic impurities is 
unknown or not easily predictable [3,7,8]. 
1.1.3 Degradation pathways 
Manufacturing processes and storage conditions (e.g. temperature, light, humidity) of 
pharmaceutical tablets causes different degradation pathways. The most common are hydrolysis, 
photolysis, oxidation, and polymerisation depended on the functional groups (e.g. aldehyde, 
alcohol) presented in the formulation [9–12].

Water is omnipresent promoting instability of the pharmaceutical tablet by the cleavage of 
covalent bonds [13,14]. Functional groups such as amides and esters are liable to undergo such 
hydrolysis often catalysed by acids or bases [15,16]. Photochemical processes are strongly 
influenced by the relative humidity, and typically initiated by absorption of a photon from the 
exposure of sunlight or indoor lighting sources (e.g. metal halide and ﬂuorescent lamps). Such 
excitation is potential to undergo discolouration [17], which has been reported for pharmaceutical 
film-coated tablets containing amines and lactose [18], such as famotidine [19,20]. Additionally, 
light can initiate oxidation reactions [21]. In order to improve the photostability of pharmaceutical 
tablets, an incorporation of light-absorbing pigments (e.g. iron oxide) or a UV protecting coating 
layer might be applied [16]. However, the physical and chemical characteristics of the coating layer 
is able to undergo oxidation driven by the formation of radicals [16,22]. Because of this, the choice 
of package material (e.g. plastic, glass) is crucial and should be selected to ensure neutral 
environmental conditions. One consideration is that permeation of oxygen is more prone in plastic 
rather than glass, but glass is more fragile and expensive [5,23]. 
1.1.4 Kinetic solid-state degradation 
A detailed discussion of solid state kinetic is beyond the scope of this project. For this reason, the 
kinetic will only be used to support the understanding of degradations. The most common 
degradation pattern of a pharmaceutical system is rapid initiated via first-order kinetic, followed 
by a deceleration in the degradation rate. Such degradation is accumulated with time, but strongly 
depending on the number of reactants, whose concentration influences on the reaction rate [3,24]. 
In addition, stress conditions (e.g. thermal, humidity, light) may accelerate the degradation of the 
pharmaceutical tablet as described in ICH Q1A(R2) [25]. Since degradation is often lead by more 
than one impurity, the reaction pathways may differ. Regarding pharmaceutical tablets, a zero-
order degradation of aspirin in suspension has been reported to form salicylic acid depended on 
temperature [26,27].

The goal for this chapter is to emphasise the formation and some of the greater reactions with

2.1 Properties of formaldehyde 
Formaldehyde (HCHO) also known as methanal is a naturally occurring organic compound first 
reported in 1859 by A. M. Butlerov under his attempt to synthesise methylene glycol. Nonetheless, 
formaldehyde was conclusively identified in 1869 by A. W. von Hofmann as a highly reactive low 
weight molecule consisting of a polar carbonyl group attached to two hydrogen atoms as shown in 
figure 2.1 [37]. Because of this, formaldehyde is abnormal compared to other aldehydes (e.g. 
acetaldehyde, furfuraldehyde), which consist of a neighbouring carbon atom and one hydrogen 
atom to its carbonyl. 
 
 
Figure 2.1. Chemical structure of HCHO. 
 
For this reason, formaldehyde is not adapted by the characteristic keto-enol tautomerisation. 
Instead, formaldehyde provide unusually reactivities. This is reflected by the fact that although 
normal aldehydes are handled in their pure monomeric form, formaldehyde is rarely. Despite of

2.1.1 Monomeric form 
The monomeric form of formaldehyde is a colourless gas at room temperature which condenses 
on cooling to give a liquid that boils at -19 °C and freezes to a crystalline solid at -118 °C. Gaseous 
formaldehyde is strongly depended on its purity, where even a trace of water is able to provoke 
rapid polymerisation. Temperatures above 150 °C is leading to combustion of formaldehyde 
forming formic acid, whence formic acid may undergo a competing decomposition between a 
decarboxylation and a decarbonylation [37–39]. 
2.1.2 Associations 
Formaldehyde can undergo a number of self-association reactions and associate with water 
forming various physical phases, such as anhydrous liquids, aqueous solutions or polymers (see 
Appendix 1 – Properties of formaldehyde). All hydrates, polymers and derivatives are reversible 
and react as formaldehyde, differing only in the readiness with which they split up or hydrolyse to 
yield formaldehyde or its reactive solvates [37]. In water, formaldehyde is rapidly hydrated forming 
a gem-diol (methylene glycol). In such equilibrium, methylene glycol is more dominating than the 
anhydrous form of formaldehyde (less than 0.04 %) as shown in figure 2.2 [40,41].

2.2.1 Excipients 
Formation of formaldehyde can occur at any stage during manufacturing processes, use, storage, 
transport, or disposal of products with residual formaldehyde. However, the pharmaceutical tablet 
is prone to form formaldehyde itself, due to its formulation and surrounding conditions [42]. 
Excipients are substances formulated alongside with the active pharmaceutical ingredient (API), 
which together constitute a unique pharmaceutical tablet. Each excipient provide chemical 
properties to the formulation, such as specifying size, colour, weight, or as part of a protective 
coating [43,44]. Nonetheless, excipients have also been reported to promote formaldehyde during 
oxidation under various conditions as exemplified in table 2.1 [12,13,23,45–47].

2.2.2 Polyethylene glycol 
Polyethylene glycols (PEG) as presented in figure 2.3 are water-soluble polymers of ethylene oxide. 
PEG can be graded from viscous (e.g. 200 Da) to more solid (e.g. 10000 Da) polymers at ambient 
temperature. At low molecular weight, PEG is a plasticiser used as a mainstream part of film-
coating layers to protect against moisture. Low weight PEG is added to reduce the glass transition 
temperature (about 40 to 50 °C) of film-forming polymers occurred during film-coating processes 
[43,44,56–58]. 
 
 
Figure 2.3. General structure of PEG, where n represent the number of repeating units. 
 
However, the presence of peroxides (allyl- and hydroperoxides) has been shown (nanomoles per 
PEG gram) to affect the oxidative instability of PEG (300 to 3350) in film-coated tablets. 
Hydroperoxides (ROOH) is omnipresent and has been reported to catalytically be degraded by 
transition metal ions (e.g. Fe(III)) forming equimolar amounts of peroxy (ROO•) and alkoxy (RO•) 
radicals as shown in figure 2.4  [5,59,60].

2.4 Derivatisation of formaldehyde 
Chromatography techniques has become a manifest regarding detection of formaldehyde either

activity. This is compensated by derivatisation, which transform formaldehyde into a similar 
chemical structure (derivate) with new chemical properties (e.g. reactivity). Among many, the most

shown to produce a strong UV absorption characteristic with sufficient response at 360 nm [84].

other aldehydes). The two nitro groups (NO2) in DNPH stabilises the benzene by resonance, which

3.1 Chromatography 
The International Union of Pure and Applied Chemistry (IUPAC) defines chromatography as “a 
physical method of separation, in which the components to be separated are distributed between 
two phases, one of which is stationary (stationary phase) whilst the other phase (mobile phase) 
moves in a definite direction” [85].  
Chromatography was revolutionised in 1901 by M. S. Tsvet for his separation of plant pigments 
(e.g. chlorophyll and carotene) [86,87]. The evolution of chromatography has since been 
developed into several methods such as adsorption, partition, ion exchange and size exclusion 
chromatography. Adsorption is a chromatographic type where a mobile phase is absorbed onto 
the surface of a stationary solid phase. Separation in adsorption chromatography is achieved in 
equilibrium between the mobile and stationary phase. In partition chromatography, a solute is 
dissolved in a mobile liquid phase and placed on a coated surface of a solid inert support. Ion 
exchange chromatography is driven by anionic and cationic with respect to the mobile phase, 
stationary phase, and changes of pH and salt concentrations. Size exclusion chromatography is 
based on molecular size and shape, where smaller molecules are able to penetrate into porous of 
the stationary solid phase, while larger molecules elute first along with a mobile phase [88,89].

6.3 Formaldehyde integrity in pharmaceutical tablets 
Packaging modifications is able to improve the stability of the pharmaceutical tablets by acting as 
a barrier against the exposure from light, moisture, and oxygen. Because of this, the primary 
packaging material that envelopes and in directly contact with the pharmaceutical tablets is crucial. 
The most common for package container are blister cards and plastic bottle. Blister card consist of 
two layers, one aluminium foil and one plastic part. Plastic bottles are described as containers with 
screw caps. The first observed difference is the material, next the use of them. Tablets sealed in 
blister cards are only opened per used tablet, while plastic bottles contain all tablets. Plastic bottles 
opened per each use were assumed to be more exposed to such as oxidation and contamination. 
Such reflection could be tested in a long-term study.

Pharmaceutical tablets are covered by a film-coat in order to prevent permeability against oxygen 
and water vapour transfer. A film-coat is typically formulated with film-forming polymers (e.g. 
hydroxypropyl methylcellulose), plasticisers (e.g. PEG), and a colourant (e.g. iron oxide).  
PEG provides a plastic behaviour with the capacity to alter the permeability characteristics of a 
film-coat. The water solubility decreases with the grade of PEG. The higher grade of PEG, the lower 
polarity of PEG is achieved. Thus PEG-200 has higher water solubility than PEG-6000, since PEG-
6000 melt at room temperature, whilst PEG-200 is a liquid. Increase of PEG grade does not seem 
to have a significant in fluence on the temperature [106]. However, it has been shown that the gas 
permeabilities (i.e. O2, CO2) of cellulose films were increased as the concentration of PEG were 
increased. Thus, during the storage of film-coated tablets, storage temperature does not have to 
be higher than the film melting temperature to avoid possible changes in the film morphology, 
which could lead to film-coating defects (see Appendix 13 – Film-coating defects). 
To minimise the risk of formaldehyde, the composition of film-coating should be evaluated 
regarding storage conditions. Further, not to underestimate the packaging material. An aluminium 
foil to foil blister sealed under a nitrogen atmosphere would provide the most protective packaging 
without taken the expensive into account. An alternative would be the use of oxygen scavenging 
packets, which could be added to a plastic bottle.

The preparations of tablets were designed based on their nature and size in solution during several

8. 
Conclusion 
In conclusion, formaldehyde is possible to determine in pharmaceutical tablets by RP-HPLC. This 
master’s thesis has developed and documented a reliable analytical method for its intended 
purpose – the determination of formaldehyde in pharmaceutical tablets. The developed sample 
preparation with the presence of DNPH was demonstrated solid and implemented as part of the 
validated RP-HPLC method. The applied RP-HPLC method was proved sensitive and consistent in 
its production of acceptable results. Determined formaldehyde from sealed samples were shown 
influenced by oxidation (including air) over time. 
8.1 Perspectivation 
In reflection, the presented study can further be investigated over a longer timeframe not only for 
formaldehyde, but for organic impurities (e.g. formic acid, acetaldehyde) in general, which are 
compatible to the DNPH derivatisation. This will however require a new validation. In addition, a 
correlation study could be conducted between the validated RP-HPLC method and a developed GC 
method [33]. Since oxidation during manufacturing processes and storage conditions is evident, a 
compatibility study of excipients is suggested, especially grades of PEG are relevant  [13,51,52]. 
Such study could be constructed (see Appendix 15 – Perspectivation) based on ICH Q1 to settle the 
importance of climate conditions (ICH Q1F) [112], and be designed with the ability to confirm the 
relation between formaldehyde and formic acid. Nonetheless, involving manufacturing processes 
of tablets should be included to support the full understanding of the degradation pathway.

Appendix 13 – Film-coating defects 
Appendix  xix. Deliberated film-coating defects on pharmaceutical tablets. Modified from [113,114]. 
Term of defect 
Cause of defect 
Picture of defect 
Blistering is a local detachment of film from the substrate 
forming blister 
Effect of temperature on the strength, 
elasticity and adhesion of the film 
Orange peel effect is a large surface defect resulting in 
the film being rough and non-glossy. Appearance is 
similar to that of an orange. 
Rapid drying, high solution viscosity 
 
Picking and sticking are defects where isolated small 
areas of film are pulled away from the surface when the 
tablet sticks together or to the pan, and then detached 
from one another or from the pan 
Inefficient drying, higher rate of application of 
coating solution 
 
Blooming is a defect where coating becomes dull 
immediately or after prolonged storage at high 
temperatures 
High concentration and low molecular weight 
of plasticiser 
 
Pitting is a defect whereby pit occur in the surface of a 
tablet core without any visible disruption of the film-
coating 
Inappropriate drying (inlet air) temperature 
 
Colour variation is a defect which involves variation in 
colour of the film 
Improper mixing, uneven spray pattern, 
insufficient coating, migration of soluble dyes-
plasticisers and other additives during drying. 
 
Cratering is a defect of film-coating whereby volcanic-like 
crater appears exposing the tablet surface 
Inefficient drying, higher rate of application of 
coating solution 
Bridging occur when the film-coating fills in the lettering 
or logo on the tablet 
High spray rate, high viscosity of solution, high 
percentage of solids in the solution, improper 
atomisation pressure 
 
Cracking or splitting is a defect in which the film either 
cracks across the crown of the tablet (cracking) or splits 
around the edges of the tablet (splitting) 
Use of higher molecular weight polymers or 
polymeric blends
