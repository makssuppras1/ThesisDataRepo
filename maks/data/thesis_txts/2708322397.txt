Abstract
Through bioprocessing it is possible to use bacteria as the catalyst for producing chemical
compounds with a renewable feedstock. One of the feedstocks has to contain carbon. One of
the most abundant sources of carbon is CO2 from the air. However, CO2 is highly oxidised
and therefore only some bacteria are capable of growing with this carbon source. Moorella
thermoacetica is an anaerobic, thermophilic, acetogen and is thus capable of converting CO2
and CO into acetate using the Wood-Ljungdahl pathway. To get another product than ac-
etate, Parageobacillus thermoglucosidasius is used. P. thermoglucosidasius is a thermophilic,
facultative anaerobe. The strain used in this study has been genetically engineered by Ivan
Pogrebnyakov to produce acetone from acetate. This study aims to test the co-cultivation of
M. thermoacetica and P. thermoglucosidasius. During the co-cultivation, M. thermoacetica
will be fed with carbon-rich gases, which it will convert to acetate. P. thermoglucosidasius
should then convert the acetate to acetone. However, this approach presents a number of
issues. Firstly, acetate can inhibit growth in high concentrations. Secondly, P. thermoglu-
cosidasius cannot grow on acetate as the only carbon source in an anaerobic environment.
To solve the ﬁrst issue, an acetate tolerance assessment of P. thermoglucosidasius was made.
This revealed that aerobically P. thermoglucosidasius can grow in 7.5 g/L acetate and anaer-
obically it can grow in 12.5 g/L acetate when supplied with glucose. To try to increase the
tolerance, adaptive laboratory evolution was used. Unfortunately, the tested population did
not reveal an increase in tolerance.
To solve the second issue, a liquid feed was added to the envisioned design, to provide
P. thermoglucosidasius with another carbon source. Therefore a screening was performed,
testing the growth of P. thermoglucosidasius in seven carbon sources. This indicated that
dissachrides like maltose, cellobiose and sucrose were the best substrates for P. thermoglu-
cosidasius based on the growth rate and consumption both aerobically and anaerobically.
To evaluate the co-cultivation, two sequential cultivations were performed.
The ﬁrst se-
quential cultivation showed that P. thermoglucosidasius can turn an aerobic environment
anaerobic.
The second sequential cultivation showed that M. thermoacetica do not pro-
duce any toxic compound anaerobically then grown on fructose preventing P. thermoglu-
cosidasius. Neither of the two sequential cultivations indicated that the co-cultivation of M.
thermoacetica and P. thermoglucosidasius would not work.

Dansk sammenfatning
Ved brug af bakterier som katalysatorer er det muligt at producere kemiske forbindelser ved
brug af vedvarende råmaterialer. Et af disse råmaterialer skal indeholde kulstof. Den mest
tilgængelige kilde til kulstof er CO2 fra luften. CO2 er normalt et rest produkt og kræver en
særlig gruppe af bakterier, hvis man skal bruge det som råmateriale til vækst og produktion.
Moorella thermoacetica kan omdanne CO2 og CO til acetat ved brug af Wood-Ljungdahl
pathway under anaerobe forhold. Det primære produkt af M. thermoacetica er acetat og M.
thermoacetica er desværre svær at modﬁcere til at produce høje yields af andre produkter.
For at få et andet produkt, bruges Parageobacillus thermoglucosidasius. P. thermoglucosi-
dadiud er en termoﬁl, fakultativ anaerob bakterie. Stammen brugt i denne afhandling er af
Ivan Pogrobnyakov genetisk modiﬁceret til at producere acetone fra acetat.
Formålet med denne afhandling er at teste produktionen af acetone ved co-kultivering af
M. thermoacetica og P. thermoglucosidasius. Undervejs i produktionen vil M. thermoacetica
blive fodret med gasser rige på kulstof. Kulstoﬀet vil blive omdannet til acetat. P. ther-
moglucosidsius konverterer derefter acetaten til acetone. Med denne tilgang følger dog en
række udfordringer. For det første kan acetat være inhiberende for væksten i for høje kon-
centrationer. For det andet kan P. thermoglucosidsius ikke gro på acetat i anaerobe forhold.
Til at løse det første problem blev acetat tolerancen for P. thermoglucosidsius vurderet.
Dette viste at P. thermoglucosidsius kan gro i 7.5 g/L acetat under aerobe forhold og 12.5
g/L i et anaerob miljø ved at tilføje glukose. Dernæst blev det forsøgt at øge tolerancen af
acetat for P. thermoglucosidsius ved brug af adaptiv laboratorie-evolution. Dog viste de test-
ede populationer og stammer ikke nogen øget tolerance efter brugen af adaptiv laboratorie-
evolution. For at løse det andet problem, blev designet for produktionen revurderet således
at P. thermoglucosidsius vil blive fodret med en anden kulstof kilde. Derfor blev der udført
en screening for at teste væksten af P. thermoglucosidsius i 7 forskellige kulstof kilder. Dette
viste at disaccharider, såsom maltose, saccharose og cellobiose, var de bedste kandidater som
kulstof kilde både under aerobe og anaerobe forhold.
For at teste selve produktionen hvor M. thermoacetica og P. thermoglucosidasius vil blive
co-kultiveret, blev to sekventiel kultiveringer udført. Den første sekventielle kultivering viste
at P. thermoglucosidasius konverterer et aerobisk miljø til et anaerobisk. Den anden sekven-
tielle kultivering viste at M. thermoacetica ikke producerer giftige stoﬀer som kunne forhindre
væksten af P. thermoglucosidasius, når M. thermoacetica er groet på fructose.Derudover viste
ingen af de to sekventielle kultiveringer indikationer på at co-kultivering af M. thermoacetica
og P. thermoglucosidasius ikke ville virke.

Introduction
The increasing population results in a growing demand on consumables, draining the Earth
of its resources [1], all the while green house gasses are being pumped into the atmosphere.
Therefore better production methods are important to ensure the needs of future generations.
Bioprocessing oﬀers a more sustainable solution [2]. Unlike the traditional petro-chemical
industry, bioprocessing uses renewable resources as building blocks for production in a pro-
cess called fermentation [2].
In the fermentation process, bacteria are used as the catalyst to convert substrates to prod-
ucts through their metabolism. One of the substrates is a carbon source used as both building
blocks in the product and for biomass formation. Further, part of the carbon source is oxi-
dized to provide the bacteria with energy for growth, maintenance and production [3]. Most
bacteria use organic molecules as their carbon source and either oxidize it completely to
carbon-dioxide (CO2) in aerobic respiration or partly in anaerobic fermentation to gain en-
ergy [3]. However, there are groups of bacteria capable of ﬁxating and reducing CO2 and/or
carbon-monoxide (CO) to organic compounds and, in the process, gaining the building blocks
for growth [4]. Thus, by using these bacteria in a fermentation setup, it is possible to use
carbon rich gasses for production of organic compounds [5]. The organic compounds can
then be sold as a product or converted by a more conventional bacterium to create other
products such as chemical solvents or high value products. This production could either be a
sequential cultivation, where the organic compound is ﬁrst produced in one fermentation set-
up and afterwards used in another with the more conventional bacterium. Another option
would be to co-cultivate the two bacteria. Using the co-cultivation, only one fermentation is
needed to get from CO2 and/or CO to the ﬁnal product and therefore lowering the overall
price of the process. This would result in a more sustainable production using a renewable
carbon source and a more environmentally friendly production while lowering the overall
emission of CO2.
However, the fermentation process is only one unit operation in the bioprocess [6]. Additional
steps such as pre-cultivation in seed tanks, up- and down-stream processing, sterilization,
etc. all add to the overall production cost [6]. Therefore, it is important to keep the cost
down on each unit operation, making the overall process as ﬁne tuned as possible, in order
to compete with the petro-chemical industry.
High-temperature bioprocessing has a number of advantages to reduce the overall cost of the
process. This includes reduced risk of contamination compared to conventional mesophilic

post production, thus reducing the capital cost needed to buy the equipment and minimizes

Furthermore, the high

reduce the overall cost of the bioprocess.

Aim of this study
This study aims to test and optimize the co-cultivation of two thermophilic microorgan-
isms for a fermentation system that involves carbon ﬁxations and production of a volatile
compound. This study involves small-scale cultivation, characterization of tolerance, sub-
strate screening, sequential cultivation and tolerance improvement using adaptive laboratory
evolution.

Theoretical background
2.1
Carbon ﬁxation
In the microbial world, autotrophic microorganisms can capture and use inorganic carbon,
such as CO and CO2, as their main source of carbon.
There are diﬀerent systems and
pathways used to capture and reduce the inorganic carbon to organic carbon, which leads
to diﬀerent categories of microbes [4]. The categories includes photosynthetic bacteria and
algae, hydrogen oxidizers, methanogens, denitriﬁers, sulfur/sulfate reducers, iron reducers
and acetogens [4].
The most wide-known autotrophic mechanism is the Calvin-Benson cycle used by photosyn-
thetic bacteria and algae. The Calvin-Benson cycle gains redox potential from photons, in
the form of light, to ﬁxate CO2 and convert it to organic sugars. These sugars can then enter
the cellular carbon metabolism or be degraded by respiration to yield energy in the form
of ATP [4]. However, as the Calvin-Benson cycle requires light, which travels poorly in a
fermentation broth, specialised equipment is needed for fermentation with these organisms.
This leads to increased capital costs but also energy cost during fermentation [10].
For a fermentation purpose, it would be more desirable to use an autotroph that gains redox
potential chemically. Chemicals are more easily mixed within the broth and are accessible
even when up-scaling to a large tank. Further, more standardized equipment can be used,
reducing the capital cost of the fermentation [10]. For such fermentations, acetogens are
possible candidates. Acetogenic bacteria use the Wood-Ljungdahl (WL) pathway to convert
CO2 and/ or CO to acetyl-CoA [11]. Acetogens can oxidize H2 into H+ to gain redox poten-
tial and to reduce and ﬁxate CO2. Alternatively, they can oxidize CO to CO2, which is then
incorporated into acetyl-CoA, using CO as both its carbon source and electron donor [12].
2.1.1
Wood-Ljungdahl pathway
The Wood-Ljungdahl (WL), also called the reductive acetyl-CoA, pathway is used by aceto-
genic bacteria to convert CO and CO2 to acetyl-CoA. It is the most wide-spread anaerobic
carbon ﬁxation pathway [13]. However, some of the key enzymes are highly sensitive to oxy-
gen and will be aﬀected, or even inhibited, in low concentrations of oxygen [12]. Although
acetogenic species could potentially be facultative anaerobes, the acetogenic metabolism is
strictly anaerobic [12].

The WL-pathway is divided into two parts; the Methyl, or the "Eastern", branch and the
Carbonyl, or the "Western", branch (see ﬁgure 1, grey box) [11]. In the Methyl branch,
one molecule of CO2 is reduced to form a protein-bound methyl group. While in the Car-
bonyl branch, one molecule of CO2 is converted to CO. The methyl group and CO are
then combined with Coenzyme A (CoA) to produce acetyl-CoA [11]. CO can also enter the
WL pathway directly into the Carbonyl branch or be oxidized to CO2 before entering the
Methyl branch [14]. Growth on CO is called carboxydotrophic growth, while growth on CO2
is called homoacetogenic growth and requires a proton donor. Most commonly H2 is used as
the proton donor [15]. The overall stoichiometries can be seen in the equations below (equ.
1 and 2) [11]:
2 CO2 + 4 H2 + HSCoA ⇀
↽CH3CO-SCoA + 3 H2O
(1)
4 CO + 4 H2O + HSCoA ⇀
↽CH3CO-SCoA + 2 CO2 + 3 H2O
(2)
Reducing CO2 to a methyl group costs 6 protons, while the conversion of CO2 to CO only
uses 2 protons. The protons comes from the oxidation of intermediates such as NAD(P)H
and ferredoxion. The oxidized NAD(P) can then be reduced again by oxidation of CO to
CO2 by reducing water or from cleavage of hydrogen by a hydrogenase [14]. The reaction
can be seen in ﬁgure 1 in the top of the grey box.
The product of the WL pathway is acetyl-CoA. The acetyl-CoA can be incorporated through
the core carbon metabolism to form various biomass constituents, or can be converted to
diﬀerent end products, depending on the species and its environment. The most common
product is acetate, but ethanol, butanol or other small organic compounds can also be
formed. The formation of these products allows the acetogens to restore the energy balance
of the WLP (see ﬁgure 1) [14].
The conversion from inorganic carbon to an end product like acetate through the WL-
pathway yields no net ATP generation on substrate level. For producing acetyl-CoA, one
ATP is used in the methyl branch. In the product formation of acetate from acetyl-CoA, one
ATP is produced. To gain energy, acetogens also use either a proton or sodium pump to gen-
erate ATP. During the WL pathway, protons or sodium ions are moved across a membrane
creating a gradient. An ATPase is then used to transport the proton or sodium back across
the membrane catalysing the formation of a phosphate bond in the process (see appendix A
for illustration) [16] [17].

2.1.2
Moorella thermoacetica
One of the most studied acetogenic bacteria is Moorella thermoacetica, previously called
Clostridium thermoacetica, that converts carbon from CO and CO2 to acetyl-CoA using
the WL pathway. The acetyl-CoA is then converted to acetate as the main product of M.
thermoacetica (see ﬁgure 1, blue box) [15].
M. thermoacetica is thought to be the most metabolically diverse acetogen characterized
by 2004 [15]. It is a sporeforming, thermophilic, strict anaerobe. Its temperature range is
between 45 and 65 ◦C with an optimum between 55 to 60 ◦C. It grows near neutral pH at
range between 5.7 to 7.7 with an optimum at around 6.8 [15]. M. thermoacetica can use
a wide range of electron donors and acceptors and can grow both autotrophically and het-
erotrophically on diverse substrates ranging from organic sugars, carbon containing gases and
aromatic compounds [15]. The preferred electron acceptor for M. thermoacetica is nitrate,
which it can oxidize to nitrite and then further to ammonia [18]. However, when nitrate is
present, the acetyl-CoA pathway is blocked [19]. This means that M. thermoacetica does not
use the WL pathway when nitrate or nitrite is present and will therefore not ﬁxate CO2. This
might be due to the fact that nitrate reducing to ammonium is energetically more favorable
than acetogenesis [19].
M. thermoacetica is found across the world mostly in the soil. To survive in such a redox-
unstable environment, where exposure to oxygen may occur, M. thermoacetica has developed
three known survival strategies to be able to survive exposure to minimal levels of oxygen
[15]. The ﬁrst is a mechanism to reduce oxygen and its toxic byproducts using electrons.
Here oxygen can be reduced to H2O [15]. The second is by switching to an alternative elec-
tron donor, such as nitrate, when available. This inhibits the WL-pathway however, and
therefore also requires that a diﬀerent carbon source is available [18]. The third survival
strategy involves the formation of a symbiotic relationship with another microbe in the en-
vironment. Here the other microbe would be a facultative aerobe, which will consume all
the oxygen in its near surroundings, creating anaerobic micro-environments where M. ther-
moacetica can grow [15]. If all methods fails, M. thermoacetica can form resistant spores
that can germinate when the conditions are favorable again [18].

2.2
Acetone production
Figure 3: Acetone
Acetone is a clear, liquid solvent with a distinct smell and
taste. It is a small, 3 carbon, polar, aprotic solvent com-
posed of a keton group surround by methyl groups (see
ﬁgure 3) [22]. Due to the properties, acetone is used to
make many chemicals and compounds including, but not
limited to, fats, oils, plastics and ﬁbers [23]. Furthermore
acetone is used for many industrial and domestic appli-
cations, such as nail polish remover, paint and varnish
remover, solvent, or cleaning products [23]. The price of
acetone is not considerably higher than that of acetate, but it has potential to be used as a
building block for more complex and high-value products [22].
Today acetone is primarily produced chemically using the Cumene route. This method oxi-
dizes propene and benzene to acetone and phenol [22]. Propene and benzene, the feedstocks
in the process, are currently both non-renewable products produced in the petro-chemical
industry [24, 25]. Bioprocessing presents itself as a more sustainable production method
using renewable feedstocks.
With a boiling point of 56.05 ◦C at 1 atm, acetone is a volatile compound, making it ideal for
product recovery by evaporation [22]. This makes it an ideal product for thermopilic fermen-
tation with continuous product removal through gas stripping. Gas stripping is a separation
technique used in the chemical industry to remove a compound from a liquid stream by a gas
stream. The technique is, among others, used for separation in the ABE (Acetone, butanol
ethanol) process [26]. The advantages of gas stripping include that the process does not
7
==PAGE:16==

Henriette G. R. Nissen, s163713@dtu.dk
require expensive equipment and that it only removes the volatile end product and therefore
has no inﬂuence on intermediates or nutrients supplied. Further, continuous removal of the
volatile end product will also reduce product toxicity and inhibition and does not have a
negative aﬀect on the fermentation [26].
Bioconversion of acetate to acetone uses two molecules of acetate to create one molecule
of acetone and CO2 [27]. The stoichiometries can be seen in equation 3.
2 Acetate + ATP ⇀
↽Acetone + CO2 + ADP + Pi
(3)
In the reaction, acetate is ﬁrst converted to acetyl-CoA. Afterwards, two acetyl-CoA are
joined and converted to acetone in three enzymatic steps. In the overall process, one ATP
is used [27].
For production of acetone from acetate, one can either use a natural producer or a metabolic
engineered strain.
M. thermoacetica is not a natural producer, but the pathway can be
heterologously expressed in M. thermoacetica [28]. However, metabolic engineering in M.
thermoacetica is challenging and resulted in low yields [28]. Therefore the conversion from
acetate to acetone is more desirable in another organism.
2.2.1
Parageobacillus thermoglucosidasius
Parageobacillus thermoglucosidasius, previously called Geobacillus thermoglucosidasius is a
gram-positive, thermophilic, spore-forming bacteria [29]. It is a facultative anaerobe and
grows in a temperature range of 45 to 70 ◦C. It is found in a wide variety of environments,
including compost, hot springs and geothermal sites [29]. However, spores from this organism
can also be found in more moderated and even low temperature environments such as soil,
deep sea water and some mountains where the temperature rarely exceeds 30 ◦C [29]. In an
anaerobic fermentation, P. thermoglucosidasius produces lactate, formate, acetate, ethanol,
succinate and R,R-butanediol with lactate as the primary pathway for regeneration of NAD.
In aerobic condition, strains of P. thermoglucosidasuis have been reported to have a growth
rate nearing 2 h1 at 65 ◦C in rich media [29].
P. thermoglucosodasius can use a wide range of substrates including both pentose and hexose
sugars. Therefore it is being considered in relation to ethanol production on sugar derived
from hydrolysis of cellulose and hemicellulose [30]. However, P. thermoglucosidasius only

2.3
Industrial gas fermentation
In a production set-up, M. thermoacetica will be fed with a carbon-rich gas. The gas that
will be used will most likely be synthetic (syn)- or ﬂue gas. Syngas is produced in the chem-
ical industry and primarily composed of hydrogen, CO and some CO2. Furthermore there
are also small amounts of impurities in the form of diﬀerent nitrogen and sulfur connections
[31]. The exact percentage depends on the process in which the syngas was produced. One
of the largest production methods of syngas is in the synthesis of ammonia for fertilizer and
it can by produced by gasiﬁcation of biomass [31]. Flue gas is gas coming from a ﬂue, which
is a pipe or channel conveying exhaust gases. Flue gas typically consists of large amount of
nitrogen and some vapour and CO2 [32]. Furthermore there is typically also small percentage
oxygen and impurities such as heavy metals [32].
Even though carbon-rich gases are a cheap and diverse substrate which is highly renew-
able, not all waste gasses are equally ﬁtting as substrate for a microbial gas fermentation
[5].
To be used as a substrate there are demands on continuity, composition as well as

Experimental design and theory
To design a sustainable production process, one could take advantage of M. thermoacetica’s
ability to use carbon from the gases as the carbon source and produce acetate. Afterwards,
the acetate is converted into acetone by P. thermoglucosidasius (see ﬁgure 4). This is both
a sustainable and cheap solution where co-cultivation of the two organisms can lead to
production of chemicals, using a cheap substrate that is generally considered a waste product
actively causing climate changes [1].
Figure 4: Moorella thermoacetica can ﬁxate the carbon from the air and convert the carbon
to acetate via the Wood-Ljungdahl (WL) pathway [15]. Parageobacillus thermoglucosidasius
can then convert the acetate to acetone. As the cultivation is under anaerobic conditions,
P. thermoglucosidasius needs another carbon source for growth.
3.1
Co-cultivation
In a co-cultivation P. thermoglucosidasius and M. thermoacetica grow together in a mixed
fermentation. The process should be optimized so that the two organisms will have a sym-
biotic relationship, where they can co-exist and interact without one outgrowing the other.
In the co-cultivation, both organisms should rely on the combined metabolism, so that the
environment that might not be favorable for the individual organisms is favorable for the
symbiosis.
The envisioned gas fermentation is an anaerobic gas fermentation similar to the design pro-
posed in ﬁgure 5 by Redl et. al for a syngas fermentation of M. thermoglucosidasius [33]. In
the design, there will be a gas feed into a large bubble column. The oﬀ-gas will be conden-
sated, so that the product stream can be distilled and any leftover gaseous substrates can be
recycled. Besides a gas feed, there should also be a liquid feed for P. thermoglucosidasuis.
11
==PAGE:20==
Master thesis, fall 2021
Henriette G. R. Nissen, s163713@dtu.dk
P. thermoglucosidasuis will be fed an organic carbon source, as it cannot grow on acetate
alone anaerobically. The organic carbon source for P. thermoglucosidasius needs to be one
that M. thermoacetica cannot use, to ensure that there is no substrate competition between
the two organisms. Moreover, the liquid feed can then be used to control the growth of P.
thermoglucosidasius and ensure that, even though it grows faster than M. thermoacetica, it
does not outgrow M. thermoacetica during production.
Depending on the source of the gas, traces of oxygen can be present in the gas feed. The
oxygen will disturb the anaerobic environment needed for M. thermoacetica’s production.
Therefore it is important that P. thermoglucosidasius uses all oxygen available to keep the
anaerobic condition steady [14].
This it to avoid the expensive process of depleting the
oxygen from the gas feed before use [14]. Since both organisms are thermophiles, the fer-
mentation is performed at 60 ◦C. At this temperature, the produced acetone evaporates due
to a low boiling point of 56 ◦C [22].

3.2
Production of acetone in P. thermoglucosidasius
For this study, a generically engineered strain of P. thermoglucosidasius was used. This
strain was engineered to produce acetone from acetate by Ivan Pogrebnyakov, which in-
volves inserting three genes not native to P. glucosidasius [34]. The three inserted genes
comes from Clostridium acetobutylicum [27] and have been inserted into the strain in front
of a constituent promoter while the gene ldh has been deleted to yield higher production.
The pathway together with the genetic engineering can be seen in ﬁgure 6. The deleted
gene, ldh, codes for the lactate dehydrogenase (LDH). LDH converts pyruvate to lactate and
is used to oxidize NADH during anaerobic growth [29]. By removing this step, pyruvate is
pushed towards acetyl-CoA and a competing pathway for acetone is eliminated. However,
the conversion of acetyl-CoA to acetone does not oxidize NADH. Aerobic NADH is oxidized

using oxidative phosphorylation, however in anaerobic environments other pathway(s) are
used to regenerate NAD+. This could be the ethanol pathway which also uses acetyl-CoA
[3]. Therefore, one can not avoid the formation of byproducts with anaerobic growth. The
three genes that have been inserted is thl, which codes for Acetyl-CoA C-acetyltransferase
(ACAT), ctfAB, which codes for CoA transferase (CT), and adc, which codes for acetoac-
etate decarboxylase (ADC).
In the process, acetate is converted to acetyl-CoA by the reverse reaction of acetate ki-
nase (ACK) and phosphate acetyltransferase (PTA). Both these enzymes are native to P.
thermoglucosidasius. This uses 1 ATP per acetyl-CoA created [3]. The ACAT then converts
2 acetyl-CoA into one acetoacetyl-CoA. Acetoacetyl-CoA is then converted to acetoacetate
by CT, which is ﬁnally converted to acetone by ADC. In the conversion of acetoacetyl-CoA to
acetoacetate by CT, one acetate is also converted to acetyl-CoA [26, 27]. Therefore the over-
all reaction only uses 1 ATP even though 2 molecules of acetate are converted to acetyl-CoA.

3.3
Adaptive laboratory evolution
High concentration of fermentation products, such as acetate, inhibits growth [21]. Accu-
mulation of acetate produced by M. thermoacetica would limit production and growth of
P. thermoglucosidasius during the co-cultivation. To avoid inhibition, the tolerance of P.
thermoglucosidasius needs to be increased. Increasing tolerance can be done using targeted
genetic engineering or random mutagenesis and selection. However, to do target genetic
engineering one would have to know the tolerance mechanism. As this is not the case with
acetate tolerance in P. thermoglucosidasius, random mutagenesis and selection in the form
of Adaptive Laboratory Engineering (ALE) was used. ALE is a powerful method for increas-
ing tolerance without knowing the underlying mechanisms [35]. With the tolerance ALE,
P. thermoglucosidasius experiences selective pressure, resulting in several random mutations
[36]. The mutations that allows the strain to grow in the selected environment, in this case
higher acetate concentration, has a selective advantage, whereby the strain outgrows the
other competing strains in the culture. From these adapted populations, a well performing
strain can be isolated [35]. There are diﬀerent adaption strategies. One of these strate-
gies could involve converting the acetate to acetone, thus increasing the ﬁnal product titter.
Conversion of acetate to acetone uses two ATP but also generates one ATP leading to a net
energy consumption of one ATP as mentioned earlier. This could be an energy-intensive
process for the cells, however it will also lead to the complete removal of the acetate, as
acetone is volatile and will evaporate from the broth. This means that the conversion will
lead to the removal of the acetate and over time reduce the pressure on the cell.

ODity
For visualization of growth in larger volumes, the ODity was used[43].
The ODity uses
back scatter to measure the turbidity of the liquid without disrupting the culture. Lasers
at diﬀerent angles sequentially shine through the culture. When hitting the cells, the light
scatters in diﬀerent directions, of which the desired angle is captured with a sensor [43].
Once the culture starts growing, the turbidity will increase and therefore the amount of light
captured by the sensor increases, giving a quantitative proxy for cell concentration [43].
However, as the ODity measures the light that scatters back into the sensor, it is sensitive to
the morphology of the bacteria [43]. Therefore the technique is most useful in the exponential
growth phase where the morphology of the culture can be assumed to be constant.

5.1
Acetate tolerance assay
To determine a starting point for the ALE, an Acetate tolerance assessment was made. Here,
it was hypothesised that the tolerance was low, using data obtained from Daniel Bavnhøj’s
thesis [52]. Therefore concentrations ranging from 0 to 4 g/L acetate were tested both aero-
bically and anaerobically in the initial screening for acetate tolerance. In anaerobic screening
a plate reader was used to continuously measure the absorbance for 24 hours, while in the
aerobic screening the absorbance was measured manually every 2 hours for 10 hours using
a plate reader. Under both conditions the TMM was supplemented with 5 g/L YE and 10
g/L glucose besides the acetate.
The experiment proved the hypothesis wrong, as growth was observed at all acetate concen-
trations in both conditions for both strains (see appendix B.1 ﬁgure 20 for the raw data). It
could be theorised that the reason for the growth was that the acetate tolerance estimated
in Daniel Bavnhøj’s thesis was with acetate as the only carbon source, while the tolerance
estimated here was on both glucose and acetate. In the experiment the glucose was added,
as it is not metabolically possible for P. thermoglucosidasius to grow on acetate alone in an
anaerobic condition.
When grown under aerobic conditions, it was not possible to determine the growth rate for
sVM29 even though it grew in all acetate concentrations. It appears that it started to grow
after the 10 hours of measurements. For sVM62 it was possible to determine the growth rate
(see ﬁgure 8b). However, the growth rate of sVM62 increased with the acetate concentration
and thus a toxic concentration of acetate was not reached. As this was aerobic, it indicates
that the acetate was consumed and used to increase the growth.

Discussion
The overall objective of this study was to test and optimize the co-cultivation of P. thermoglu-
cosidasius and M. thermoacetica for the production of acetone. This included determining
and increasing the acetate tolerance of P. thermoglucosidasius, a potential substrate for P.
thermoglucosidasius for the liquid feed and test the possibility of co-cultivation by sequential
cultivation of both strains.
The ﬁrst part of the study involved increasing the acetate tolerance of P. thermogluosida-
sius. However, before increasing the tolerance, one needs to determine the natural tolerance
level. Subsequently, it is important to characterize the population for any mutant with an
increased tolerance.
Comparing the growth rate determined by both the Acetate tolerance assessment and of
the populations and single colonies obtained after the ALE, it appears that the general tol-
erance has decreased from before the ALE. As shown in ﬁgure 10, both strains grew at 7.5
g/L acetate with 3 g/L YE in an aerobic setting. However, ﬁgure 14 shows that most popu-
lations did not grow above 6 g/L acetate with 3 g/L YE in an aerobic setting. Furthermore,
anaerobic growth was determined up to 12.5 g/L for sVM62 and 15 g/L for sVM29 with 5
g/L YE. But the evolved populations only grew to 4 g/L anaerobically. While for the single
colonies characterized, only a few replicates grew beyond 0 g/L acetate and none grew above
5 g/L acetate. The decrease in growth after the ALE could indicate that the environment in
the plate reader introduces a stress that the evolved population cannot grow in. The ALE
itself was performed in falcon tubes ﬁlled to 10% of its volume compared to maximum volume
with a surface area of 956 mm2. In the 96-well plate the working volume was approximately
50% of the total volume and the surface area was approximately 32 mm2. This gives a large
space for diﬀusion of oxygen from the air into the liquid in the falcon tube and therefore also
yields a higher dissolved oxygen concentration in the falcon tubes.
Besides the volume and surface area, the stirring is also diﬀerent between the ALE and the
characterisation in the plate reader. The diﬀerence in stirring would aﬀect the dissolved
oxygen concentration in the liquid and therefore also the oxygen available for the cells.
As the population was pushed to adapt to the condition in the falcon tubes, it would be
expected that these adaptations might be less beneﬁcial in other conditions.
To solve this issue, one would have to test conditions closer to the ones used in the ALE.

However, this introduces some practical issues as one would have to manually measure the

Another issue is comparing the growth from the Acetate tolerance assessment and of the
populations obtained after the ALE with the controls. As controls, the parental strains and
an unevolved strain of both the WT and the engineered strain, called the original strains, was
tested and compared to the evolved populations. The parental strains have only grown in 3
g/L YE, while the unevolved strains have not been part of the ALE. This means that both
the parental and the unevolved strains had not experienced any acetate pressure. However,
in the characterisation after the ALE, these strains do not grow in acetate concentrations
higher than 5 g/L. This is less than observed in the Acetate tolerance assessment.
The unevolved strains were taken as a single colony from the same plate as for the Ac-

However, the strain was ﬁrst re-grown in the rich SPY medium before inoculation, mak-

For
both experiments, the aerobic media contained TMM supplied with 3 g/L YE and diﬀerent

glucose 3 or 5 g/L YE and diﬀerent acetate concentrations (see section 4.4 for the exact
composition). However, as the growth experiment showed that having 3 or 5 g/L YE did
not aﬀect the growth signiﬁcantly, it is unlikely that the diﬀerence in YE concentration re-
sulted in the diﬀerence in growth. Therefore, if it is the media that is slightly diﬀerent, it is
a diﬀerence in the TMM, acetate or glucose stock. TMM is the most complex stock mixture,
making it susceptible to batch diﬀerences. However, most of it was made at the same time.
It is thus unlikely that batch diﬀerences of TMM caused the observed growth diﬀerences.
This could be conﬁrmed by repeating the experiment with a new stock of TMM. If it is the
TMM that is the diﬀerence, it shows that the general robustness of P. thermoglucosidasius

is fairly low and that the repeatability with small batch changes is low. This is generally not
a preferred feature of a production strain.
As it does not appear that any of the characterized populations had an increase in tolerance
compared to the acetate tolerance assessment, it could mean that the selective pressure in
the ALE selected for a diﬀerent ﬁtness than intended. During the ALE, the strains were
transferred during late exponential or stationary phase, meaning they could have sporulated.
This means that at the next transfer there was both a mix of spores and slow growing veg-
etative cells and the pressure of an increased acetate concentration. As the spores consume
energy transforming back to vegetative cells, the adaption could have resulted in selecting
for slow growing cells. As there will then be two selective pressures, the strain will adapt to
a mixture and therefore might not reach the full potential of either.
The fact that the strains might have been pressured not to sporulate is not necessarily
a negative eﬀect. Having sporulating organisms gives more variation in terms of growth and
also leads to a loss of production. The cells have to use energy to create the spores but
the spores have no metabolic activity. This means that spores do not form any products.
Therefore producing spores is seen as a waste of energy from a production point of view. In
terms of growth, sporulation leads to lower absorbance. This makes it hard to quantify the
growth and compare diﬀerent scenarios as a low ﬁnal OD can mean that the growth was slow
or fast. Sporulation is also a problem when comparing replicates, as they do not sporulate at
the same time. Therefore it can create high deviation between replicates. This means that
for the production set-up, it would be desirable to have a strain of P. thermoglucosidasius
that does not sporulate
The second part of the study was to study the potential co-cultivation and test for fea-
sibility. To do so, a substrate screening for P. thermoglucosidasius was performed alongside
two sequential cultivations.
From the substrate assay there was a clear diﬀerence between anaerobic and aerobic growth.
Aerobically it grew on acetate while it did not anaerobically. Instead it was the disaccharides
like maltose, sucrose and cellobiose that resulted in the highest growth rate and best carbon
consumption. The growth rate and carbon consumption for maltose, sucrose and cellobiose
was also high aerobically.
The ﬁrst sequential cultivation aimed to test whether P. thermoglucosidasius could deplete

all oxygen in the media, creating an anaerobic environment where M. thermoacetica could

The second sequential cultivation was to test the potential production set-up. This showed
that M. thermoacetica did not produce any toxic compounds when grown on fructose that
could inhibit growth of P. thermoglucosidasius.
However, in the experiment, M. ther-
moacetica was grown on fructose and not on carbon-rich gases, as would be the case in
the potential production set-up. Therefore the byproduct formation for M. thermoacetica
could be diﬀerent and one would have to test whether this is the case. Furthermore, it was
not possible to detect production of acetone. As acetone has a boiling temperature of 56
◦C, the majority of acetone produced during the cultivation at 60 ◦C would be in gas form.
It is possible to cool down the culture afterwards to try to dissolve some of the acetone in
the liquid. This was done by placing the culture in the cold room directly after cultivation
and before any samples are taken. However, when measuring the acetone in the HPLC, one
would not get all acetone produced. Therefore, it is not possible to determine the exact
amount produced using a HPLC but only getting an indication if the strain has produced
acetone.
For both sequential cultivations, the amount of glucose and fructose in the broth after each
round of growth was quantiﬁed. It was not possible to determine the amount of acetate
in the broth for either of the cultures. This was because the measurement of acetate given
by the HPLC was above the detection limit of the standard curve. For the HPLC, it was
calculated that the concentration of glucose and fructose added would be just below 5 g/L.
Therefore the standards for glucose, fructose and acetate started at approximately 5 g/L
and halved 4 times to approximately 0.3125. However, one mole of glucose or fructose can
be converted to 2 or 3 molecules of acetate [15]. This yields a potential of 30 g/L acetate.
Furthermore the media was ﬂushed with N2 and CO2 to provide anaerobic conditions. The
CO2 has the potential to be converted to acetate, however as no H2 was added it would be
a minimal amount. This yields an even higher potential acetate concentration. Therefore
it was not possible to determine the amount of acetate in the broth, but one can conclude

that P. thermoglucosidasius was not inhibited by the acetate concentration at the second
sequential cultivation, meaning toxic concentrations were not reached. Therefore it might
not be necessary to increase the acetate tolerance of P. thermoglucosidasius further.
Comparing the carbon consumption in the two sequential cultivations, the main diﬀerence
for P. thermoglucosidasius in the two sequential cultivations was the oxygen availability. In
the ﬁrst sequential cultivation the environment started aerobic and was turned anaerobic,
while in the second cultivation the environment was anaerobic from the start. This will aﬀect
the growth and the carbon usage. Also in the second sequential cultivation there was a small
concentration of fructose that M. thermoacetica did not consume. As the culture was not
autoclaved between the ﬁnished growth phase of M. thermoacetica and the inoculation of P.
thermoglucosidasius, the fructose would not have been caramelization or in any other way
disrupted. As the fructose does not show up on the HPLC results after the growth phase
of P. thermoglucosidasius, it was probably consumed. Therefore, if P. thermoglucosidasius
used the YE and the fructose before starting to use the glucose, it could be the reason the
relative high amount of glucose was detected in the broth after cultivation.
For the ﬁrst sequential cultivation, the cultures was autoclaved before inoculation with the
new organims. This was because P. thermoglucosidasius has a much higher growth rate than
M. thermoacetica. So to make sure that it was M. thermoacetica that grew and not the
spores from P. thermoglucosidasius, it was necessary to kill the spores of P. thermoglucosi-
dasius, for which the autoclave was used. However, for the second sequential cultivation, the
autoclavation step was not deemed necessary, as the spores from M. thermoacetica need to
be heat-activated before they can germinate to vegetative cells. The heat activation usually
involves boiling the spore solution in water for ﬁve minutes. As P. thermoglucosidasius was
grown at 60 ◦C, there was little chance that the spores from M. thermoacetica would grow.
Furthermore, P. thermoglucosidasius was given glucose for growth instead of fructose.
Based on the ﬁndings of both sequential cultivations, it can be concluded that it is possible
to co-cultivate P. thermoglucosidasius and M. thermoacetica. The ﬁrst sequential cultivation
showed that P. thermoglucosidasius can convert an aerobic environment to an anaerobic one.
However, this was only tested in a batch environment, whether P. thermoglucosidasius is able
to keep a broth anaerobic during a continuous gas feed remains to be proven. The second
sequential cultivation showed that M. thermoacetica does not produce inhibiting byproducts
when grown on fructose and that the acetate concentration reached was not toxic for P.
thermoglucosidasius. However, here M. thermoacetica was grown on fructose and not gasses.

Conclusion
Throughout this study, a number of experiments have been conducted in order to test and
optimize the co-cultivation of P. thermoglucosidasius and M. thermoacetica. Firstly, ALE
was used to try to increase the tolerance to acetate in P. thermoglucosidasius. Secondly, the
gas fermentation setup was examined using sequential cultivation and by determining the
substrate for the liquid feed for P. thermoglucosidasius.
It was not possible to determine whether the ALE have resulted in an increase in acetate
tolerance for P. thermoglucosidasius with the tested populations or strains. This could either
be due to issues with the ALE itself or with the growth assays used to determine the growth
rate for the evolved populations. Issues with the ALE itself could be caused by sporulation,
while issues with the growth assay could be due to the media. However, this will need to be
conﬁrmed by re-performing some of the growth assays using a new batch of media and using
a diﬀerent set-up closer to the set-up used in the ALE.
In relation to the envisioned production set-up, the substrate screening of P. thermoglucosi-
dasius showed that dissaccharides like maltose, succrose and cellobiose are suitable substrates
for P. thermoglucosidasius to be added in the liquid feed in both an aerobic and anaerobic
setting. While the dissaccharide melibiose is not suitable in any setting and glycerol and
acetate are candidates for growth in an aerobic setting but is not consumed in an anaerobic
environment.
To test the co-cultivation, two sequential cultivations was performed. In the ﬁrst sequential
cultivation P. thermoglucosidasius grew using all the glucose. Thereafter, M. thermoacetica
grew in the presence of fructose even though most was not used. As M. thermoacetica is
a strict anaerobe, the growth implies that P. thermoglucosidasius consumed all the oxygen.
The second sequential cultivation showed that P. thermoglucosidasius grew on spent me-
dia from M. thermoacetica. This means that M. thermoacetica did not produce any toxic
byproduct that would inhibit growth for P. thermoglucosidasius. Unfortunately, it was not
possible to verify or quantify the acetone production by sVM62.
Therefore, this study can conclude that the experiments performed do not disprove the
co-cultivation.

Future work
For future work, one would have to validate some of the ﬁndings made in this study to
continue towards a co-cultivation.
In relation to the ALE, one would have to conﬁrm if the evolved populations have an in-
creased tolerance to acetate compared to the strain before the ALE. This could be done by
testing the media or during a growth test in falcon tubes where the growth rate is not mea-
sured but only the ﬁnal OD is used. The ﬁnal OD has some complications for comparison
due to sporulation. However it can be used to conﬁrm whether they have grown. It could
also be useful to see if there are other interesting single colonies and thus doing some more
characterization.
To determine what mutations have happened during the ALE, the genomes of the evolved
populations need to be sequenced. Afterwards, the genome alignments can be used to query
through variant calling to ﬁnd mutations. Here one can try to identify mutations that might
lead to an increased acetate tolerance.
To conﬁrm the eﬀect of the mutations, one can
reverse-engineer interesting mutations into the WT strain and compare the eﬀect on acetate
tolerance.
In relation to the testing of the co-cultivation, it would be desirable to redo the second se-
quential cultivation using cellobiose and/or maltose, as was identiﬁed in the substrate assay
as potential new carbon sources for P. thermoglucosidasius. Also, one can consider redoing
the second sequential cultivation without adding a new carbon source to see if P. thermoglu-
cosidasius can grow. Furthermore, one should also repeat the second sequential cultivation
using carbon rich gases as carbon source for M. thermoacetica to see if this changes the
growth of P. thermoglucosidasius. Here, one should see if one can use GC-MS to quantify
the amount of acetone produced by P. thermoglucosidasius.
Lastly there is the co-cultivation itself. Here, one needs to determine a correct inoculation
ratio between M. thermoacetica and P. thermoglucosidasius to ensure that neither outgrows
the other. One could do a screening experiment where diﬀerent ratios of inoculation of M.
thermoacetica and P. thermoglucosidasius could be tested in order to determine when the
growth is symbiotic. However, this requires that the media is optimized sequentially, as the
growth rate of both M. thermoacetica and P. thermoglucosidasius will be eﬀected by the
media composition. Also, it is important to determine the gas- and liquid feed rate and
composition in order to control the growth rate of both organisms. This is only possible in
a fed batch set-up where continuous feeding is possible.
