Abstract
The massive expansion of renewable energy systems (RES) requires significant invest­
ments in flexibility and energy storage to prevent extreme price fluctuations and maintain
grid stability. Yet, such investments are limited as market revenues remain uncertain and
exposed to price volatility. This thesis focuses on optimal bidding strategies for storage
operators in 100%­RES markets using a multiperiod game­theoretic model. Assuming
oligopolistic competition among key players, the model aims to identify Nash equilibria
and dominant strategies under different market conditions. In addition, complementary
policy analyses explore how market­based mechanisms could reduce price risks and en­
hance incentives for flexibility investments. Together, the modeling and policy insights
provide a coherent framework to better understand strategic storage behavior and inform
future market design in fully renewable electricity systems.

Introduction
The global energy system is undergoing a profound transformation to meet the challenges
of climate change, fossil fuel dependency and sustainable development. At the heart of
this transition is the urgent need to reduce greenhouse gas emissions and align energy
pathways with the Paris Agreement goals, which aim to limit global warming to well below
2°C above pre­industrial levels [1, 2]. Achieving this requires a rapid decarbonization of
electricity systems and a shift toward renewable energy sources, improved efficiency and
deep electrification of other sectors [3, 4].
Among European nations, Denmark stands out as a frontrunner in this transition. It has
set ambitious national climate and energy goals, targeting a fully renewable electricity sys­
tem by 2030 and a climate­neutral society by 2050, as defined in the Danish Climate Act
[5]. This vision rests heavily on the expansion of wind and solar power, sector coupling
(especially with heating and transport) and flexible system design [6]. Recent national
planning documents, such as the Danish Energy Agency’s 2022–2024 technology out­
looks [7] and Energinet’s long­term system plans [8], envision a power system dominated
by variable renewable energy (VRE) with deep interdependence between infrastructure,
policy and market design.
In this context, the role of storage technologies becomes increasingly important. As the
share of VRE grows, so does the need for flexibility to balance generation and demand in
real time, ensure system reliability and avoid curtailment or inefficiencies [9]. This study
models a simplified version of a future electricity market based on Denmark’s 2030 pro­
jections: a fully renewable system where storage technologies actively participate.
Storage can provide arbitrage opportunities, reduce price volatility and improve the in­
tegration of renewables. However, the exact nature of storage participation, especially
under strategic behavior, raises questions about market dynamics and welfare outcomes.
Unlike traditional generators, storage operators are inherently flexible and can shift supply
or demand across time. This intertemporal capability opens the possibility of exercising
market power, especially when competition is limited or when policies do not account for
their price­setting potential [10].
The role of storage as a potential price­maker has gained increasing attention in aca­
demic research. Storage operators can act strategically by withholding capacity or timing
their actions to exploit market imbalances, similar to traditional oligopolistic generators.
These behaviors may lead to price manipulations, system inefficiencies or unequal im­
pacts across participants if not properly addressed by market design [11]. Moreover, the
variability of renewable production and demand, together with the high cost and scale of
storage investments, makes the market environment particularly complex. As shown by
Barbour et al. [12], this complexity means that the number of market participants and how
storage assets are configured can significantly influence price formation, market power
dynamics and overall system efficiency.
From a policy perspective, there is growing interest in designing mechanisms that guide
storage participation toward socially optimal outcomes. These may include access rules,
which determine how storage units connect and participate in markets, revenue incen­
tives, market regulations or ownership structures that define who can own and operate
storage assets. Effective policies need to balance encouraging investment with prevent­
ing market abuses that could harm consumers or overall system efficiency [13]. As coun­

tries like Denmark move closer to high­RES systems with active storage participation,

This thesis investigates how strategic storage operation affects spot market outcomes un­
der different levels of competition and how policy and design choices can mitigate market
power. Our modelling framework combines a simplified representation of a 100% re­
newable electricity system with endogenous storage bidding behavior and Cournot com­
petition. By exploring a range of market configurations and actor strategies, this study
assesses the potential impacts on prices, flexibility and renewable integration.
The following research questions guide the analysis:
1. How does strategic operation of storage influence electricity prices and re­
newable integration in a 100% renewable system?
2. How does the level of competition among storage operators affect market ef­
ficiency?
3. Which policy and design interventions can mitigate market power and ensure
efficient storage participation?
By answering these questions, the study aims to provide insights into the evolving role
of storage in future electricity systems and contribute to the policy debate on designing
competitive and efficient markets in a decarbonized context.
To address these questions, the thesis is structured as follows. First, it presents the mod­
elling framework, detailing the market system representation, storage bidding strategies
and market competition formulation. Then, it analyzes the baseline market outcomes
under different competition levels, followed by a comprehensive evaluation of policy mea­
sures designed to mitigate market power. Finally, the thesis discusses key insights, limi­
tations, and avenues for future research.

Literature Review
Energy storage has been extensively studied as a flexibility resource for renewable­based
power systems. Early work primarily assessed its arbitrage value or grid support functions
under the assumption of price­taking behavior, where storage operation does not affect
market outcomes [10, 11, 14]. These models, often used for welfare assessments or
long­term investment analyses, provided useful insights into the economic potential of
storage but ignored market feedback and strategic incentives. As a result, they could not
capture how storage deployment itself might reshape price formation, which is central to
understanding those market outcomes and investment signals.
More recent studies treat storage as an active market participant, explicitly accounting
for its influence on prices. Strategic modeling approaches range from single price­maker
formulations [15, 16] to multi­player game­theoretic models [17, 18, 19]. These works
highlight that strategic bidding can reduce storage dispatch efficiency, distort price signals
and create distributional effects between participants, in which case some storages or
market actors capture higher profits at the expense of others or overall welfare. However,
the trade­off is clear: modeling competition between strategic storages often requires
simplifying assumptions, such as perfect foresight or simplified market price functions,
which can limit how broadly the results apply.
Different formulations capture strategic behavior in distinct ways. Bilevel optimization is
frequently used for single­player price­maker problems [20, 21], while Cournot or Stack­
elberg games are applied for multi­actor interactions [17, 18, 22]. While bilevel models
are accurate for single­player market power studies, their complexity grows quickly with
multiple players. Cournot­based approaches provide an alternative, easier to implement
and solve, but usually rely on stronger assumptions rather than capturing the full com­
plexity of market­clearing processes. Some works extend these simplified formulations
with stochastic or robust programming to address price and operational uncertainty [16,
23, 24], showing how price volatility or variable renewable conditions affect storage prof­
itability and bidding strategies.
Market representation also varies substantially. Some studies adopt simplified single­
node markets [25], while others include nodal pricing and network constraints [26]. Sim­
ilarly, temporal scope ranges from short­term bidding analyses with detailed operational
constraints [15, 16] to long­term investment models with aggregated temporal resolu­
tion [11, 27]. Few models combine both dimensions, as balancing short­term operational
realism with planning horizons is computationally demanding. Yet, doing so is crucial
to capture how market participation strategies influence future storage deployment and
profitability.
These differences are summarized in Table 2.1, which groups the most relevant refer­
ences by their core focus. The table shows that most studies either emphasize (i) oper­
ational modeling without strategic interaction or (ii) strategic models with simplified mar­
ket assumptions. Few explicitly examine how competition between multiple storage op­
erators affects prices, profits and market efficiency, despite its importance in designing
renewable­based markets.
This thesis addresses this gap by modeling a Cournot game between multiple strategic
storage units in a centralized market. Unlike prior work that focuses on a single dominant
player [15, 16] or uses simplified price­demand functions [17, 18], it explicitly captures

3
Methodology and Formulation
This section presents the modelling framework developed to simulate the strategic bidding
behaviour of storage operators in a 100% renewable electricity market. The model relies
on real market data to build representative generation and demand price quota curves
(GPQCs, DPQCs), which are used to determine the hourly market­clearing price. Stor­
age operators are modelled as strategic players in a Cournot game, each choosing their
charge and discharge quantities to maximize profit while accounting for the influence of
their decisions on market outcomes.
The optimization model and Cournot competition framework were implemented in Python.
The complete implementation, including data processing scripts and results, is publicly
available in a Git repository [28].
3.1
Electricity Market Database Processing
Day­Ahead Market Context
The Danish electricity market operates as part of the Nord Pool day­ahead market, which
functions as a uniform­price auction with hourly intervals [29, 25]. Participants submit
stepwise supply and demand bids that define the quantities they are willing to sell or
purchase at different price levels. The market­clearing mechanism matches aggregated
supply and demand curves to determine a single clearing price for each hour.
Supply­side bids from renewable producers are typically placed at zero or very low prices,
reflecting the near­zero marginal cost of wind and solar generation [25]. In this model,
renewable generation is assumed to bid at zero price. On the demand side, bids are
structured as decreasing step functions representing the maximum willingness to pay per
volume segment. All supply and demand curves are treated as exogenous inputs in the
market­clearing problem and are assumed to be perfectly known by the storage players.
In the model, storage operators submit only quantity bids i.e., they choose how much to
charge or discharge, but do not set prices. Prices result endogenously from the market­
clearing outcome.
Construction of the Generation Price Quota Curves (GPQCs)
Historical data from the Danish transmission system operator, Energinet, covering the
years 2021­2024, provide the basis for the renewable supply inputs [30, 31]. This dataset
includes production from onshore wind, offshore wind and solar PV, along with their re­
spective installed capacities on a daily basis.
For each renewable source, hourly capacity factors are computed as the ratio between
observed generation and installed capacity. This yields a normalized time series xd =
(xd,1, . . . , xd,24) for each day d, where xd,t ∈[0, 1] denotes the capacity factor at hour t.
Figure 3.1 displays the hourly capacity factors for each RES over the year 2022, showcas­
ing seasonal and short­term trends, that are similar for the others modeled years. Solar
output shows a smooth seasonal pattern, increasing toward a summer peak and declining
afterward, with occasional short peaks during the winter months. Offshore and onshore
wind exhibit high variability throughout the year, with frequent shared drops in output,
particularly during the summer and generally higher capacity factors during the winter.

To capture representative daily patterns, a clustering procedure is applied to the set
{xd}D
d=1, where D is the number of available days in the historical dataset. Among several
possible clustering methods, the k­medoids algorithm is chosen due to its robustness to
outliers and interpretability. This algorithm partitions the dataset into k disjoint clusters,
each represented by one observed element called the medoid. The medoid is the most
central day within the cluster, meaning the day whose profile is, on average, the closest to
all others in the group. Clustering is performed by minimizing the total dissimilarity within
each cluster, using the Euclidean distance between daily capacity factor profiles.
Unlike k­means, which uses the centroid (a potentially non­real vector), k­medoids guar­
antees that each cluster is represented by a real observed day. This is particularly suitable
for renewable production data, where the timing and shape of generation, such as mid­
day solar peaks or wind ramps, can vary significantly. Using real days as representatives
ensures that the selected profiles capture realistic fluctuations and daily patterns linked
to actual weather conditions.
This method is applied separately for each renewable source and season. Specifically,
two seasonal ensembles are considered:
• Winter ensemble: all days between November and February
• Summer ensemble: all days between May and August
For each RES and seasonal ensemble, clustering is performed with k = 1 and k = 2,
allowing for the distinction between average, high and low generation days. Those com­
puted medoids profiles are illustrated in Figure 3.2, grouped by season and RES type.
Among them, one profile is selected for each RES and season to serve as the repre­
sentative daily production profile used in the model. In the base case, this results in two
scenarios: one typical summer day and one typical winter day.

Bioenergy Simplification Assumption.
Bioenergy is not modelled as part of the supply
mix in this study. Although it is projected to contribute approximately 20% of Denmark’s
electricity production in 2030 [33], its operational characteristics differ significantly from
variable RES. It is considered a dispatchable generator with moderate marginal costs due
to fuel use (such as biogas or organic matter) and operational constraints [34]. These
characteristics make it likely to act as a price­maker in the electricity market, especially
when providing flexible generation in periods of low wind and solar output. Modeling this
behavior realistically would require treating it as a separate market participant forecast­
ing residual demand and submitting strategic bids, similar to a thermal generator. This
would substantially increase the model’s complexity and introduce additional strategic in­
teractions. Given that the primary focus of this study is storage competition under high
RES penetration, excluding bioenergy simplifies the model while still preserving the main
insights.

Construction of the Demand Price Quota Curves (DPQCs)
On the demand side, the model relies on actual bidding data from the Nord Pool day­
ahead market [35] to construct hourly DPQCs. These serve as fixed inputs to the market­
clearing model and reflect, for each quantity level, the marginal price consumers are willing
to pay ­ capturing their heterogeneous price sensitivity and consumption priorities.
Bidding data were available for the years 2021­2024, including both purchase and sale
sides and selected for the DK2 bidding zone in Denmark. For this study, three represen­
tative days in 2024 were selected in advance and provided for use:
• 27 November 2024 (Wednesday, winter weekday)
• 10 July 2024 (Wednesday, summer weekday)
• 19 May 2024 (Whitsun Sunday, a public holiday with typically low demand)
The selected demand days are matched with the representative renewable scenarios de­
scribed earlier, ensuring seasonal consistency across simulations (e.g., summer demand
curves are used with summer RES production).
Each daily dataset contains detailed hourly demand­side bidding data from the day­ahead
market. For each hour, all individual demand bids, each represented as (volume, price)
pairs, are aggregated and sorted in decreasing price to form a stepwise demand curve
reflecting the cumulative volume at each price level.
Unlike many studies where demand curves are either synthetically generated by market
simulations or estimated from historical consumption data using forecasting procedures
[36], this approach uses real market data. It allows to capture the heterogeneity and
elasticity of demand. This also avoids the need to estimate demand functions from con­
sumption data, which can introduce bias or overly smooth approximations.
Two main preprocessing steps are applied to prepare the curves for integration into the
model:
(1) Removal of negative price bids.
Negative price bids in electricity markets typically
occur when supply significantly exceeds demand, leading producers to pay consumers
to take excess electricity. This often happens in systems with large amounts of inflexible
generation, such as nuclear or coal plants, which cannot reduce output easily because of
limited ramp­down capabilities.
This study focuses on an idealized electricity system fully supplied by renewable energy.
Because renewable generators like wind and solar PV are generally flexible and can cur­
tail output when prices are low or negative, persistent negative price bids are less likely.
Most importantly, the study concentrates on strategic bidding behavior under normal mar­
ket conditions, deliberately leaving out extreme events or market anomalies that cause
negative prices. This approach simplifies the analysis and focuses on typical market be­
havior in a high­penetration renewable system.
(2) Standardization of DPQCs Granularity.
To reduce dimensionality and facilitate im­
plementation, each hourly demand curve is approximated by a stepwise function with a
fixed number of representative price­volume pairs. This is achieved using k­means clus­
tering on the original bid data, which groups the many closely spaced bidding blocks into
D = 20 aggregated steps. Bids corresponding to inflexible demand, submitted at the
market price cap of 4000€/MWh, are excluded from this process and treated separately

Energy Storage Self­Scheduling Formulation
An energy storage plant can help balance renewable energy variability by charging when
electricity prices are low and discharging when prices are high, performing energy arbi­

influence market­clearing prices in their favor, effectively exercising market power. This
section presents a quantity­based, price­maker self­scheduling formulation of a storage
unit, following the approaches developed by de la Torre et al. (2002) [21] and Shafiee et
al. (2016) [15].

Cournot Modelling for Strategic Game Optimization
Game­Theoretic Framework
In a competitive electricity market with multiple strategic storage actors, each player’s
decision affects not only its own profit but also market outcomes, including the market­
clearing price. The Cournot framework provides an algorithm to solve these interdepen­
dent decisions. In this setup, each player optimizes its own MILP problem while treating
the actions of the others as fixed inputs.
Formally, the Cournot game consists of N players, where each player s ∈{1, ..., N} seeks
to solve:
max
ψs Πs (ψs, ψ−s) ,
s.t. ψs ∈Fs
(3.19)
Here, ψs denotes the strategy (charging/discharging schedule) of player s, ψ−s represents
the fixed strategies of all other players and Fs is the feasible set defined by the opera­
tional and market constraints. This formulation naturally leads to the concept of a Nash
equilibrium: a stable outcome where each player’s strategy is optimal given the strategies
of others, and no player has an incentive to unilaterally deviate.
Iterative Best Response Algorithm
The Nash equilibrium is approximated using an iterative best­response algorithm. At each
iteration, players solve their optimization problem in sequence, updating their strategies
based on the current decisions of others. The method assumes that at each iteration, the
residual demand faced by a player is computed based on the fixed actions of all others.

The iterative best response algorithm converges to a Nash equilibrium if the feasible re­
gions are compact. Discretizing the power levels limits the solution space and ensures
that a Nash equilibrium can be found in a finite number of steps. In practice, the algorithm
usually converges within a few iterations when initialized with neutral strategies such as
zero or proportional dispatch.
This decentralized approach is computationally efficient, as each player’s MILP can be
solved independently. It captures strategic behavior without requiring complex reformu­
lations, such as KKT conditions, which are not compatible with the discrete nature of the
MILP problem. This setup also allows us to quantify the market impact of competition
between storage units. Results are presented in section 4.2, which follows.

4.1
Base Case Results
The base case simulation involves two asymmetric storage players participating strate­
gically in the electricity market. Their behavior is examined across three representative
profiles: Winter, Summer and Low Load (a sub­profile of Summer). The analysis focuses
first on the Winter scenario, followed by a comparative discussion of the other two sce­
narios.
Scenario Analysis: Winter
The Winter profile is characterized by relatively high and stable both demand and RES
production throughout the day (Figure 4.1). This results in a market environment where
prices rarely reach zero and where storage operation must be more selective and strategic
to generate value.
In this setup, both storage players display strategic restrained behavior. They charge
most of the available energy ahead of critical hours but not to their full capacity extent.
When discharging, they consistently leave part of the residual demand uncleared. This
prevents prices from decreasing to zero if all the demand is satisfied, but mostly allow the
storages to manipulate market prices. Their strategic impact is especially pronounced
when residual demand is high: instead of fully satisfying it, they discharge partially and
keep wider margins, thereby keeping prices to high values, for their benefit. Conversely,
when residual demand is already low, they meet most of it, still keeping a small margin,
and prices drop to values below 10€/MWh (Figure 4.3).
This pattern creates a clear relationship between the system’s residual demand (i.e., the
difference between total market demand and renewable generation inputs) and the un­
cleared demand, respectively denoted as uncleared demand before and after the storages
bidding and operation in Figure 4.2. The uncleared demand follows the same trend but at
reduced magnitudes. Below a certain residual demand threshold, storage satisfies most
of the requirement and smooths out variations. Above this threshold, however, withhold­
ing behavior dominates, limiting discharge and amplifying storage’s price­shaping role.
Despite these similarities, Players A and B exhibit asymmetric strategies (Figure 4.4),
relative to their asymmetric characteristics (Table 4.1). Player A, with the lower capacity,
charges to its maximum before critical hours but discharges conservatively, by withholding
energy even when further charging opportunities exist later in the day. In contrast, Player
B, with higher capacity, does not charge up to capacity saturation, leaving some available
energy unexploited, but discharges almost completely during critical hours, reaching its
minimum SoC ­ lower to its discrete power step limit.
These opposite strategies translate into uneven profit outcomes (Table 4.1). While a Nash
equilibrium would suggest equalized profit per unit of installed capacity, here Player A
earns a significant higher profit ratio than Player B (10.78€/MWh vs 7.71€/MWh). This is
even more unexpected as player B, benefiting from a larger installed capacity and max­
imum power rate, could exploit arbitrage opportunities more fully relative to its size and
exercise more market power, in a realistic setting. This highlights modeling limitations as
it may be explained by player A’s smaller power­step discretization, which enables more
price­sensitive operation in a highly constrained market environment, whereas Player B’s
larger operating profile is less responsive to fine price signals.

Comparison with Summer and Low Load Scenarios
The summer and low load scenarios offer additional insights into how storage players
adapt their strategies to different market conditions. While the general behaviors observed
in winter, such as strategic withholding energy and price­responsive discharging, remain
visible, several specific differences emerge.
Summer Scenario
In the summer scenario, renewable production is high and residual demand remains close
to zero for most of the day (Figure 4.5 – 4.6). This creates a long stretch of hours with
zero market prices, lasting until the late afternoon. During this time, both storage play­
ers opportunistically charge from the surplus, gradually increasing their state of charge
(Figure 4.8). For instance, player 2’s SoC rises from 3840MWh at the start of the day to
nearly 7430MWh by hour 16. It can be noted that because of its power rate granularity
(Table 4.2), player B could not have charged more. This observation is similar for player
A, reflecting an optimal absorption of curtailed energy in this scenario.
Later in the day, market prices start climbing around hour 17 and peak above 20€/MWh by
hour 19 (Figure 4.7). Storage units respond by selectively discharging during these hours,
illustrating again strategic withholding of energy to maximize revenue through arbitrage.
This smooths variations in residual demand by evening out market prices (though still
extremes).
A noteworthy difference from winter is that, since peak hours occur at the end of the day,
storage units are constrained by the model to limit their discharge. Specifically, they must
end the day with a half full state of charge, which prevents them from releasing all available
energy. In this case, the uncleared demand is not a result of strategic withholding but
rather a consequence of the final SoC constraint, as they have charged to their maximum
limit earlier in the day.
It can also be noted that in this case, profits ratio are comparable between both players
(Table 4.2), allowing to observe the expected behavior of the Cournot model, ensuring
that each player reaches their individual optimum (proportional to their potential).

Summary of Strategic Effects
Across all profiles, the storage units act as active, price­making participants, strategically
timing their charging and discharging to influence market prices. This behavior improves
system efficiency by reducing price volatility, curtailment and uncleared demand. It is
especially the case in scenarios with high renewable output and volatile demand, where
arbitrage opportunities are clearest and the potential for market power is higher.
However, the persistence of uncleared demand despite available energy highlights a key
trade­off: while storage adds flexibility and reduces curtailment, its strategic behavior
under limited competition does not guarantee reliable supply.
This observation directly motivates our research question: whether increasing storage
competition could mitigate market power while enhancing overall market performance.

5
Policy Results
5.1
Rationale for Policy Modeling
Achieving a 100% renewable electricity system requires flexible resources that can absorb
production variability and balance supply and demand. Energy storage is central to this
flexibility, but its profitability depends strongly on market prices, which are highly volatile.
This volatility increases the risk of investment returns, particularly for large­scale projects.
Policies are therefore needed both to incentivize storage deployment and to guide its
operation in ways that support system needs.
In practice, a wide range of policy instruments have been discussed or implemented to
support storage and renewable integration [39]. These include operational incentives
such as reserve or ancillary service remuneration, and investment­related incentives such
as co­location with renewable plants, capital subsidies, tax credits or feed­in remunera­
tion mechanisms. The latter focus on long­term deployment, such that they were not
modeled here as they are not directly compatible with our short­term operational formula­
tion. Instead, our focus was on operational and quasi­operational mechanisms that could
be naturally integrated in our framework.
5.2
Policy Formulation Choices
Three types of policies are implemented, chosen because they act directly on short­term
storage operation, which aligns with the temporal scope of our framework. Each policy
represents a mechanism that can be observed in practice or has been proposed in the
context of high­renewable systems.
Availability obligation policy
An availability obligation policy was implemented, in which storage units receive remu­
neration for guaranteeing discharge capacity during selected peak hours. If they fail to
meet this commitment, a penalty cost applies. This policy is designed to secure market
operational flexibility and reliability in critical periods. By providing an additional revenue
stream linked to availability rather than energy delivery, this enables policymakers to im­
prove storage profitability without changing investment conditions.
Such mechanisms are already applied in practice through flexibility tenders and availability­
based capacity payments, where assets are contracted to be available during predefined
stress periods.
The approach used here is conceptually similar to Strategic Reserve
schemes but differs in a key aspect: contracted storage remains active in the market and
continues to submit strategic bids, instead of being withdrawn from normal market opera­
tion. An extension was considered to include explicit reserve activation, where contracted
capacity would be required to discharge during selected hours. This was not retained, as
storages in this model already discharge during peak periods due to their daily cycling
and reliance on arbitrage revenues.
This design preserves strategic operation of storage in the market while ensuring that
contracted flexibility can be mobilized when most needed, supporting peak­hour reliability.
Grid tariffs
Grid tariffs represent fees charged for charging or discharging through the grid. They are
not strictly a policy instrument but act as price signals that influence operational decisions,
serving a similar purpose in this context. Three structures were modeled:

==PAGE:37==
• Flat tariffs: constant rates applied to all charging and discharging quantities. They
are simple and predictable but do not reflect temporal grid conditions.
• Hourly tariffs: fixed scheduled fees, where charging tariffs are higher during peak
periods, while discharging tariffs are lower. This structure aim to shift load but rely
on predefined profiles, which may not align with actual system needs.
• Dynamic tariffs: prices that vary with residual demand, capturing real­time condi­
tions. Charging tariffs decrease when residual demand is low (surplus renewables),
while discharging tariffs decrease when residual demand is high (shortages). This
provides strong incentives for an efficient system operation but require continuous
monitoring and accurate supply and demand signals.
Unlike the availability obligation policy, tariffs do not constrain storage behavior directly.
Instead, they add a cost term to the profit maximization. For each bid placed on the
market (charge or discharge), the relevant tariff is subtracted from the resulting revenue.
This introduces an additional optimization layer: storages must adapt their operation to
minimize tariff exposure while maximizing arbitrage value, resulting in significant shifts in
their charging and discharging patterns.
Co­location incentives
Co­location incentives exempt storages connected directly to renewable plants from pay­
ing charging tariffs. This reflects reduced grid usage and promotes integrated storage­
renewable projects. Different configurations were tested: no co­location, single storage
co­located and both co­located. These cases help explore how locational incentives in­
teract with tariff structures and affect market outcomes.
Scope and focus
These policies target operational aspects rather than investment signals. However, they
still manage to influence investment decisions indirectly, such as locational investment
through co­location, or profitability guarantee through availability policy. Parameter levels
are inspired by realistic ranges but are not scaled for comparability. The main aim is to
test how such policies affect storage behavior and market dynamics under our modeling
framework, rather than to derive definitive policy recommendations.

Specifically, dynamic tariffs prove very effective at reducing system stress by lowering

Then, an aggregated scoring framework is applied to compare policies across all met­
rics in a single measure. The metrics are combined using a dominance­based scoring
approach (see Table 5.4), where higher scores indicate that a policy outperforms others
across a larger number of metrics. This method counts, for each metric and policy, how
many other policies it dominates and sums these counts across all metrics. It is partic­
ularly suited to this context, where metrics are not normalized and vary significantly in
scale.
For comparison, two alternative aggregation methods were tested. The first is a normal­
ized sum method, which rescales metrics to a [0,1] range and sums them with directional
adjustments. While simple, it is sensitive to outliers and to metric scaling differences,
such as the large storage revenue gains observed under the reserve policy. The second
is a rank­based method, which ranks policies per metric and sums the ranks. Although it
avoids scaling issues, it is highly sensitive to small rank changes and ignores the magni­
tude of performance differences.
Given these trade­offs and the fact that policy parameters are not scaled for comparability,
it is important to use a scoring approach that is robust to differences in metric ranges.
Therefore, the dominance­based method was chosen, as it avoids scaling issues and
provides an intuitive measure of how often a policy outperforms others across all metrics.
After scoring each policy, the best performer from each season is selected to facilitate a
clearer final comparison. Policy impacts vary significantly across seasons due to differ­
ences in demand and renewable generation patterns. Additionally, no single season con­
sistently produced the highest performing policies. Focusing on only one season would
therefore have yielded a different overall ranking, underlining the importance of a multi­
season assessment.

pared to the flat tariff case alone, while providing moderate reductions in curtailment.
By contrast, hourly tariffs, which are predefined and less aligned with modeled market
conditions, tend to increase price volatility and, in some cases, worsen system balance.
These tariffs rank much lower (e.g., Baseline + hourly tariff: 161) and provide limited
improvements in storage incentives or system performance.
Finally, the baseline scenario without policies ranks near the middle (193), indicating
that while several policies clearly enhance both system and storage outcomes, poorly
designed mechanisms can yield neutral or even adverse effects.

Overall Insights (Linked to Policy Ranking)
The policy ranking consolidates multiple performance metrics into a clear comparative
framework. It confirms that availability­based reserve remuneration is the most effec­
tive policy for enhancing storage profitability and stabilizing market prices, although its
scoring remains sensitive to the chosen remuneration level.
Tariff­based policies, particularly dynamic tariffs, provide demand­side flexibility by in­
centivizing charging during low­demand periods and discharging during peaks. Their im­
pact is reinforced when combined with co­location incentives, which reduce grid costs
and improve operational efficiency.
Co­location with flat or dynamic tariffs also perform well, increasing utilization and
revenues while moderating market stress. Co­location incentives are most effective when
targeted to a single storage unit, as extending them system­wide yields smaller marginal
benefits and diminishing returns.
By contrast, hourly tariffs show limited benefits and sometimes increase volatility due to
their static and scenario­mismatched design. While restrictive in this modeling context,
this would be less limiting in real­world settings, where tariff patterns are better aligned
with market conditions.
Overall, the findings indicate that no single policy fully meets all objectives. Instead, com­
bining reserve remuneration, tariff design, and location­based incentives provides com­
plementary benefits that improve both system performance and storage profitability.
These insights highlight the importance of integrated policy design, where combining com­
plementary mechanisms can simultaneously enhance system reliability, support renew­
able integration, and strengthen storage business cases in high­renewable electricity mar­
kets.

6
Conclusion, Limitations and Future
Work
This thesis explored the strategic operation of energy storage within a 100% renewable
electricity system, focusing on the influence of storage bidding behavior on electricity
prices, renewable integration and overall market efficiency. The analysis revealed that
storage players significantly impact price formation and system outcomes. They strategi­
cally withhold energy to manipulate market prices and optimize their revenue. However,
compared to a system without any storage operation, market adequacy and flexibility
are drastically improved, even in a monopolistic setting. To weaken their price­shaping
power, competition is increased by adding more storage players. The most substantial
improvement occurs when shifting from a monopoly to limited competition with two stor­
age operators. Additional increases in the number of competitors bring smaller gains,
suggesting that even limited competition can effectively mitigate market power.
The Cournot framework performed well in modeling storage competition but required dis­
cretizing power rates for the storage players. This introduced some effects on the out­
comes, particularly when the granularity of discretization was similar in scale to market
signals. Overall, these effects were limited, but future research could explore asymmetric
players with different discretization schemes to better represent the diversity of storage
technologies and market participation strategies. In particular, using a common discretiza­
tion granularity across all storages could be an interesting extension, with the side effect
that larger units would have more power levels to operate from.
The modelling approach employed representative days for both supply and demand,
which was essential for capturing realistic market dynamics. However, the results proved
highly dependent on the scenario considered, pointing to the need to incorporate a broader
set of representative days. These could include extreme conditions such as no sun, no
wind, varying renewable availability and their combinations. Moreover, while the dataset
used was empirical and based on real market data, it would be valuable to analyze cor­
relations between the production of different renewable sources and demand patterns to
ensure the statistical validity of the representative day approach.
Building on this, incorporating uncertainty, such as through multiple data scenarios, to for­
mulate a stochastic storage operation model would provide a more realistic representation
of the variability in renewable­based power systems. Weather­driven forecasting of re­
newable supply and demand could provide realistic scenarios, enabling a more accurate
representation of storage operation strategies.
A key limitation of this study is the single­day time horizon, which restricts the ability to fully
capture the dynamics of storage state­of­charge, critical for realistic operation. Extending
the model to cover multi­day horizons, such as a full week, would improve the realism of
operational decisions but would also substantially increase computational complexity.
An additional contribution of this thesis is the integration and evaluation of operational poli­
cies that influence storage behavior within the market framework. Three main policy types
were modeled: availability­based reserve remuneration, differentiated grid tariffs and co­
location incentives. The policy modeling allowed for assessing how these mechanisms
impact storage profitability, market prices, system stress and renewable integration. The

==PAGE:45==
results demonstrated that reserve remuneration most effectively stabilizes prices and im­
proves revenues, while tariff structures and co­location incentives offer complementary
benefits by encouraging flexible operation and reducing grid costs. This policy analy­
sis highlights the importance of combined and carefully designed incentives to balance
system reliability and flexibility, storage profitability, and renewable integration in future
electricity markets.
Overall, this thesis builds an integrated framework for modelling strategic storage bidding
under Cournot competition, supported by empirical market data and novel policy anal­
ysis. It provides insights into the importance of competition and market design in high­
renewable systems, while pointing to several promising avenues for further research. Fu­
ture work should focus on extending the temporal scope, reformulating the model stochas­
tically to represent uncertainties, and incorporating a nodal network model to support more
robust policy and operational recommendations.
