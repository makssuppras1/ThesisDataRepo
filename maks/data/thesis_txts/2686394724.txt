Abstract
The dual-fuel (DF) combustion concept is one of the promising strategies for modern
automotive and marine engines to achieve lower emission levels. A premixed DF engine
operates by having a pilot diesel injection to ignite the lean premixed natural gas in the
engine. Experimental studies on premixed DF combustion with micro pilot diesel fuel are
limited. Hence, the underlying mechanism are still not well-understood. The current thesis
contains a three dimensional (3D) computational ﬂuid dynamic (CFD) study using the
Simcenter STAR CCM+ software to investigate the ignition characteristics of the premixed
DF combustion. Large eddy simulation (LES) is used for the turbulence modelling in this
study. Initially, the spray and combustion models were validated using the Sandia Spray
A case by means of the experimental spray and combustion characteristics. The obtained
numerical combustion and spray model were then implemented for the DF simulation by
modelling the injection of pilot diesel fuel into a constant volume bomb with a premixed
methane-air mixture. The adaptive mesh reﬁnement (AMR) is implemented in order to
increase the computational eﬃciency using a minimum cell size of 0.25mm. N-Dodecane fuel
is used as a pilot diesel surrogate fuel here. Two reduced n-dodecane chemical mechanisms
are being tested. Model validation is carried out by comparing the liquid penetration length
(LPL) of the inert diesel spray to the measured data. The predicted ignition delay time
(IDT) in the pure diesel case and the DF case are also validated against the experimental
data. A reasonable agreement between the CFD results and measurements for the LPL and
IDT is achieved for the validation case Spray A, hence showing robust numerical modelling
setup. The LPL for the pure diesel case in the actual case study showed only to deviate with
6% from the experimental data. Ignition happened both mechanisms for the pure diesel
case. The results obtained for the DF case using methane in the ambient gas composition
showed to delay the IDT compared to pure diesel case which is in agreement with the
literature.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The topic of reducing emissions and greenhouse gases are still on the top list as a global
problem in our world today. One of the major contributors to the emissions is the transport
sector which accounts for nearly 1/3 of the total energy consumption. Within the transport
sector the source of the energy consumption is mainly related to oil as presented on
Figure 1.1. Therefore, it is of great interest to ﬁnd alternative fuels to our current means of
transport [1]. A solution to the emissions from the light-duty vehicles has been achieved by
the electriﬁcation and hybridization, though the same does not go for the marine sector
and heavy-duty vehicles as the energy density is limited and for now cannot supply the
long shipping trips and the power that is required. For that reason it is of interest to
investigate in alternative fuels for the marine engines that can live up to the stringent
emission reduction requirements. It is of interest to ﬁnd an alternative fuel which can supply
the the same amount of energy as the conventional fuels used today such as diesel, heavy
fuel oil and natural gas. It is predicted that the internal combustion engines will still power
60% of light-duty vehicles and for the heavy-duty market it will most likely be powered by
engines for the foreseeable future [2]. The compression ignition (CI) engine which mainly is
powered by diesel is by the recent emission regulation aﬀected by the stringent requirements.
The NOx emissions from these type of engines are mainly solved by Selective Catalytic
Reduction (SCR), however in terms of other pollutants such as unburned hydrocarbons
(UHC), carbon monoxide (CO) and particulate matter is still of concern. As a response
to the global strategy, there is a need for high eﬃcient and less pollutant engines. The
marine sector does therefore seek towards a dual-fuel conﬁguration which proves a promising
solution to the emission regulations. The reason for this is that a conversion of an existing
engine to a dual-fuel operation is a promising strategy. For this procedure a lot of literature
can be found studying the eﬀect of parameters such as pilot amount, intake temperature,
load and compression ratio [3, 4, 5]. However, these studies conﬁrms the general trends of
a dual-fuel engine and not the spray and combustion characteristics. This thesis addresses
the numerical modelling aspect of premixed dual fuel combustion using natural gas as
primary fuel and diesel as the combustion promoter through micro-pilot injection, which

1.1
Motivation
The dual-fuel conﬁguration studied in this thesis is using methane in a CI engine making
it possible to introduce a pilot-fuel with a high cetane number. The introduced pilot-fuel
initiates the combustion and ignites the methane. This combustion method of ignition and
regulation of combustion in internal combustion engines using dual-fuel was presented back
in 1898 by Rudolf Diesel himself. The method was patented in 1901 and after more than a
century it is ﬂourished again. In the patent Rudolf Diesel elaborates the basics behind the
dual-fuel conﬁguration as follows:”... if a given mixture is compressed to a degree below its
igniting-point, but higher than the igniting-point of a second or auxiliary combustible, then
injecting this latter into the ﬁrst compressed mixture will induce immediate ignition of the
secondary fuel and gradual combustion of the ﬁrst mixture, the combustion after ignition
depending on the injection of the igniting or secondary combustible” [6]. This conﬁguration
can be carried out in two ways. The ﬁrst concept is by using a direct injection which
involves a special injector that can handle both the pilot-fuel and the methane. In this
concept a small pilot amount of fuel with a high cetane number is injected ahead of the main
2
==PAGE:18==
1.1. MOTIVATION
methane injection to increase the temperature and thereby allow the ignition of methane
[7]. The injected methane will burn in the diﬀusion ﬂame similar to a diesel combustion.
However, this concept is complex and therefore it is not possible to run the engine on diesel
only. Thereby the ﬂexibility of the engine is lost within this concept. The second concept
is the port injection, whereas the methane is injected into a manifold. In this concept the
methane is premixed with the air during the induction and compression. Right before Top
Dead Center (TDC) the pilot-fuel with a high cetane number is injected as a combustion
promoter and the combustion will take place. This concept is much more attractive since
it makes the conversion of a existing CI engine simple and still can operate at pure diesel
conditions in case of methane is unavailable [8]. The dual-fuel concept in this thesis focuses
solely on the port injection meaning that the methane and air is premixed before the
pilot-fuel injection. Diﬀerent test facilities can be used in order to replicate this concept
and study the spray and combustion characteristics. The importance of the test facilities
in order to examine diﬀerent combustion phenomena are crucial. They yield evidence for
what ever tested and provides a good fundamental understanding for further development
within the tested area. The Rapid Compression Machine (RCM) is used to simulate a single
compression stroke of an internal combustion engine without any cycle-to-cycle variation,
swirl and other complications associated with an engine. It is typically used to measure
ignition delay time (IDT) with conditions such as the temperature, pressure and oxygen
ratio [9]. Another example is the Cooperative Fuel Research (CFR) engine which is used for
testing and research in the performance of fuels, compression ratios and engine knock. The
studied spray and combustion vessel has the advantage of optical accessibility compared
to the other devices mentioned and may provide a wider test option e.g. temperature,
density and gas composition. However, what all these test facilities have in common is a
limitation of parameter sweeps. If a test setup would like to be expanded or applied to
do another experiment, all or some components of the setup must be adapted accordingly
to some extend. However, this is not an issue within a numerical simulation by means of
Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) as the only limit is the time and computational cost
since very good models have been developed for diﬀerent purposes or applications. One can
also capture measurements that might be diﬃcult within a real experiment. In a numerical
simulation it is possible to have an idea of the possible outcome for that experiment. It is
worth mentioning that in the experiment by Yang et al. [10] which is one of the experiments
replicated in this thesis, has performed a CFD analysis on the methane Mixing Index (MI)
in order to determine how well the mixture is inside the chamber before injecting the pilot
diesel. This could not be measured properly experimentally and that is one thing among
many that proves the importance of CFD.

High performance computing
The simulations conducted in this thesis consists of millions of cells and would take
ridiculously long time on a desktop computer. In order to speed up to computational
time tremendously it is necessary to use high performance computing (HPC). The HPC
supercomputer used during this thesis is the Niﬂheim Linux cluster installed at DTU. The
cluster contains several node partitions consisting of diﬀerent amount of cores. It is possible
to use several nodes, however this will lead to a longer queue time based on how much time
that is requested for the job. A slurm script has to be written in order to submit jobs to
the batch queue which are submitted through Secure Shell Client. The ﬁle transfer between
the local PC and the cluster has been conducted by a secure ﬁle transfer protocol (SFTP)
where each student has a certain amount of space [11].
1.3
Aims and objectives
The aim of this thesis is to perform a study on premixed dual fuel combustion by replicating
the experiment performed by Yang et al. [10] numerically. This has been done by using
n-dodecane as surrogate fuel since the cetane number is close to that of diesel. The authors
of the experiment concluded that the addition of methane delayed the IDT which they have
compared with the IDT obtained from combustion of pure diesel. It is of aim to validate the
experimental IDT for a pure diesel and a dual fuel case by means of numerical simulation.
Once ignition is obtained, the combustion characteristics and the species formation is
investigated. Initially the Sandia spray A experiment is modelled to make sure that the
model conﬁgurations are well deﬁned. The project has followed the three major stages:
• Non-reacting and reacting Spray A validation of the liquid- and vapor penetration
length and IDT at an ambient temperature of 900 K and 750 K
• Non-reacting validation of the liquid penetration length of a micro-pilot diesel injection
consisting of 8 holes
• Reacting validation of the IDT for pure diesel pilot injection and premixed dual fuel

aspects relevant for this study is presented. In Chapter 2 the governing equations and

Chapter 2
Governing equations and models
The aim of this thesis is to perform numerical studies of premixed dual fuel combustion.
This is carried out in the CFD software Simcenter STAR CCM+ which is developed by
Siemens Digital Industries Software [12]. The software is based on the ﬁnite volume method
that uses unstructured mesh and diﬀerent solvers for the purpose of solving the governing
equations for diﬀerent ﬂuid ﬂow problems. Throughout this chapter the reader will be
provided with the theory understanding the numerical methodology used in this thesis,
i.e. turbulence-, multiphase ﬂow modelling by means of the Lagrangian-Eulerian approach,
spray-, injection- and combustion modelling and chemical kinetics.
2.1
Introduction to numerical modelling
STAR CCM+ is a commercial CFD software that was started by CD-adapco which later on
got acquired and further developed by Siemens [13]. The software enables modelling a
range of engineering problems regarding ﬂuid ﬂows by using numerical algorithms that
solves a system of equations which is constructed from the particular chosen models and
the boundary conditions of these [12]. Through assumptions from continuum mechanics
one can describe the ﬂuid ﬂow problems by applying the fundamental physical laws of
conservation of mass, momentum and energy - also named the governing principles of ﬂuid
mechanics. An additional equation for conservation of species is needed for cases dealing
with multiphase ﬂows. STAR CCM+ solves the conservation equations for both compressible
and incompressible ﬂuid ﬂows. It subdivides the solution domain into a ﬁnite number of
small cells, deﬁning the computational grid. Discrete versions of the conservation equations
in the integral form are then applied to each cell and forms a set of linear algebraic equations
with a total number of unknowns in each system of equations that corresponds to the
number of cells in the computational grid. Thereby the non-linear governing equations are
discretized in time and space and a setup of linear equations is made which are solved by
an algebraic multigrid solver [12].

2.3
Multiphase ﬂow modelling
Multiphase ﬂow is the ﬂow interaction for at least two diﬀerent phases. An example of a
ﬁeld where multiphase ﬂow takes place is e.g. for diesel combustion in a constant volume
chamber/bomb. First the chamber is ﬁlled with gas and secondly the liquid fuel is sprayed
into the chamber which interacts with the gas and at a certain point leads to combustion.
One can also explain this as introducing particles in dispersed phase and let it interact with
the continuous phase [20]. Diﬀerent modelling approaches exist for modeling the multiphase
ﬂuid ﬂow. The Eulerian approach of describing a ﬂuid motion is considered as identifying a
ﬁxed location in the ﬂow ﬁeld and then follow the change in a property of the ﬂuid e.g. the
mass of particles at that location. The Lagrangian approach can be considered as following
10
==PAGE:26==
2.3. MULTIPHASE FLOW MODELLING
a ﬂuid particle throughout its travel in the ﬂow ﬁeld and tracking certain properties of the
particle e.g. density and velocity. For a better understanding one can think of the Eulerian
system as a ﬁxed weather station that records data and the Lagrangian system as a weather
balloon that follows the wind and records data [20]. One of the methods is to model both
the liquid and the gas as Eulerian. This is called the Eulerian-Eulerian approach since
the liquid jet is treated as Eulerian as well as the gas in the domain. Another approach is
to treat the liquid particles as Lagrangian and the domain consisting of gas as Eulerian -
called the Lagrangian-Eulerian approach. The Lagrangian-Eulerian approach is applied
for this thesis as the other approach is too computational demanding [21]. Within the
Lagrangian-Eulerian approach the term parcels are introduced to describe a certain number
of particles in the dispersed phase that is assumed to have the same properties such as
mass, volume, temperature etc. The induced parcels is tracked instead of all the particles
and makes it less computational costly. Throughout this section the modelling approach
of the ﬂuid particles and the gas phase, together with the coupling of the two phases by
means of source terms in the transport equation will be described.

2.3.2
The dispersed liquid phase
For the Lagrangian-Eulerian approach to describing the ﬂuid ﬂow one can treat the liquid
particles as Lagrangian particles in two ways, either as massless particles or material
particles. If the mass of the particle does not change, then it means that no force acts
on it and the velocity of the particles can be treated as equal to the grid velocity [12].
When the mass of the particles does change - a mass transfer rate occurs either due to
evaporation or condensation - then one has to consider the forces that acts on the particles.
12
==PAGE:28==
2.3. MULTIPHASE FLOW MODELLING
Regarding a diesel spray the mass of the liquid particles changes with time as an eﬀect
of the surrounding conditions which results in evaporation meaning that the mass of the
liquid particles decreases and changes form to vapor. The particles are therefore treated
as material particles which can be considered by means of one-way or two-way coupling.
One-way coupling means that the ﬂow ﬁeld will aﬀect the particles, but not the other way
around. By contrast, two-way coupling means that the ﬂow ﬁeld will aﬀect the particles
and the particles will aﬀect the ﬂow ﬁeld as well. Since the case study of this thesis
contains injection of liquid fuel which causes the gas phase to increase and the liquid
particle mass to decrease, the two-way coupling is considered. The inﬂuence of the two
phases due to interaction is accounted for in two-way coupling by including source terms
in the governing equations for the continuous phase, as one can see in Equations (2.15),
(2.16), (2.18) and (2.19). Moreover, the liquid particles do vary in size as well, either
due to evaporation or through spray breakup caused by the hydrodynamics which will be
described in a separate section. The following governing equations refer to the particles
of the Lagrangian phase modeling and the term droplet will be used instead of particle in
order to distinguish between terms for the dispersed phase and continuous phase.

2.4
Spray modelling
Apart from the size diﬀerence of the droplets caused by evaporation, there is also a diﬀerence
in the size of the droplets due to hydrodynamic eﬀect, which is a phenomena called spray
breakup. In this section one will be led through the spray and its breakup modelling. The
spray breakup happens in a certain order and is usually divided in two incidents, namely the
primary and secondary breakup. In this section the two breakup models will be described
together with the deﬁnition of key parameters within a spray breakup. During the breakup
the mass of the particles are conserved yet the particle size does change.
17
==PAGE:33==
2.4. SPRAY MODELLING
2.4.1
Liquid spray and parameters
The evolution of the spray that occurs due to fuel injection into a continuous gas phase
can be described by the regions illustrated on Figure 2.1. A liquid core is formed close to
the nozzle tip whereas the liquid core breakup into droplets through primary atomization
or primary breakup [12]. At the disperse region the droplets breakup into even smaller
droplets due to the entrance of the surrounding gas and at this region small droplets may
even evaporate due to the high temperature of the surrounding gas. Furthermore, collision
of the droplets can take place at this region which is when two droplets merge into a larger
droplet or splits due to the collision [22]. This region is called the secondary breakup and
the modelling of this region will be described further in Section 2.4.2. At the evaporated
region all the droplets has evaporated and the surrounding gas does still enter into the
spray and usually at this region the combustion takes place.
Regarding the internal nozzle ﬂow which is shown with a close up illustration on
Figure 2.1, the most common phenomenons are depicted. The phenomenons within the
internal nozzle ﬂow is somewhat complex and sometimes they have an impact on the
spray especially the primary breakup and the eﬀective nozzle area. Due to the large
velocity gradient at the nozzle hole, a ﬂow separation occurs creating gas bubbles caused
by the pressure approaching the saturation pressure of the ﬂuid. This phenomena is called
cavitation and is usually taken into account by means of the discharge coeﬃcient [22].
Furthermore, as the ﬂow is turbulent vortex structures does occur close to the nozzle hole
and all these phenomenons combined is part of the internal nozzle ﬂow and can have an
eﬀect on the droplet size and thereby also the evaporation. However, in this thesis the
internal nozzle ﬂow is not considered, yet the ﬂow just right after the nozzle exit is modelled.
This will be further elaborated at the end of this section.
An important parameter within spray modelling is the spray angle θ, which deﬁnes the
size of the spray. In STAR CCM+ the spray angle deﬁnes the outer cone angle of the cone
about the central injection axis. This angle typically varies for diﬀerent injector nozzles
and refers to the angle that the injected media is spread with. Within a spray an unbroken
liquid can be evident which also is illustrated on Figure 2.1. However, the existence of
this liquid core close to the nozzle hole for a diesel spray is questionable. This has been
concluded by diﬀerent studies and it is believed that a diesel spray has undergone complete
atomization near the nozzle [22, 23, 24]. So the further axial distance from the nozzle
hole to the end of the liquid phase i.e. the dispersed region, the liquid penetration length
(LPL) can be measured. This length is typically measured within diﬀerent methods but
the deﬁnition is mostly the same. In other words the LPL is the maximum penetration
of the liquid phase at which the liquid length reaches a quasi-steady state. The liquid
penetration length can be aﬀected by many parameters and by simple correlation one can

2.6. COMBUSTION MODELLING
2.6
Combustion modelling
Combustion is a well known process used in many diﬀerent industries such as internal
combustion engines for transportation, furnaces for producing heat, electricity and for
metals-reﬁning. However, the type of combustion can be classiﬁed in diﬀerent types. The
combustion which is evident in a conventional compression ignition engine is called a
turbulent non-premixed combustion. In short this means that the fuel and air is not
premixed as it is in a spark ignition engine. The studied cases in this thesis are as described
earlier somewhat similar to a compression ignition engine, however they are classiﬁed as a
constant volume chamber/bomb. In other words the studied cases will also be classiﬁed
as a turbulent non-premixed combustion. In a real engine simulation the spray will be
aﬀected by diﬀerent processes such as the piston motion and the increased pressure and
temperature with time. By ﬁxing these parameters and not considering any motion, the
eﬀect of these interactions are neutralized and only the spray breakup and combustion is
isolated and analyzed.
A liquid fuel spray without combustion is called an inert or non-reacting spray. In a
real experimental case this is achieved by not including oxygen (O2) into the combustion
chamber, which is the oxidizer for the combustion process. The surrounding gas will in
these cases mainly be dominated by nitrogen (N2) which is an inert gas. These type of
experiments are typically used to study the breakup processes and spray characteristics
such as the LPL and VPL. For a CFD perspective these experiments are used to validate
certain simulation models due to the lower computational time compared to a reacting
case. The reason for why it is less time consuming is due to the non-reacting simulation
which does not take any chemical reactions into account. In STAR CCM+ this is basically
done by disabling the complex chemistry node. Within STAR CCM+ one has to select the
reacting model in order to select the complex chemistry, but besides that two diﬀerent
categories of reacting ﬂow models is available. As mentioned earlier the cases in this
thesis is somewhat similar to a compression ignition engine and STAR CCM+ recommends
to use the reacting species transport model instead of the ﬂamelet model for compression
ignition simulations. The reacting species transport model solves the transport equations
for all species and applies a separate mechanism for predicting the chemistry [12]. The
mechanism for predicting chemistry is imported within the complex chemistry node and
will be further described in Section 2.7. Furthermore, in order to consider the turbulence
eﬀect on combustion the Laminar Flame Concept (LFC) has been chosen. This model
considers the turbulence eﬀects on combustion implicitly through the turbulent diﬀusivity
within the turbulence model [12]. Turbulence-chemistry interactions are when the average
reaction rate is aﬀected by the turbulence and chemistry. However, the LFC model does
26
==PAGE:42==

not consider any turbulence-chemistry interactions but is still an acceptable model used by
other studies within the combustion ﬁeld [30, 31].
For a reacting case whereas the combustion may occur, the spray characteristic will
be the ignition delay time (IDT). The IDT is the time from start of injection to start of
ignition, and is mainly dependent on the cetane number of the fuel [32]. Combustion will
not happen as soon as the liquid fuel is injected into the chamber, however the liquid fuel
will breakup into smaller droplets and then further evaporate to vapor. Once the vaporized
fuel is mixed with the surrounding gases, ﬁrst there it will ignite. In order to determine the
IDT several methods/deﬁnitions has been used. These methods will be further explained
in Section 3.5.3.
2.7
Chemical kinetics
The mechanism for predicting chemistry typically consist of three diﬀerent input ﬁles.
The ﬁrst input is the chemical input ﬁle ”chem.inp”, which contains the species that
react chemically and the rate in which these reactions take place. The second ﬁle is the
thermodynamic input ﬁle ”therm.dat”, which contains the thermodynamic properties for
the species within the mechanism. The main property is the speciﬁc heat capacities which
is changed to a temperature varying method instead of a constant value as most of the
properties which will be described in Section 2.8. The last ﬁle is the transport input ﬁle
”tran.dat”, which contains the transport properties of the species. However, this ﬁle is
not given for most mechanisms as the key parameters is included in the ﬁrst two ﬁles.
The three mentioned ﬁles are diﬀerent for each mechanism and what makes them diﬀerent
are mainly the purpose it was made for. Each mechanism consist of a speciﬁc amount of
elements e.g. H, O, N etc. and a certain amount of species. Then the number of reactions
are based on the species and can vary for the speciﬁc purpose. What matters regarding
the computational time is how many species and reactions a mechanism consist of. More
species and reactions implies a longer and more expensive computational cost. Chemical
mechanisms can be speciﬁed within diﬀerent categories. A very simple mechanism consisting
of only one reaction and ﬁve species is considered as a global mechanism. An example
of the global mechanism is illustrated in Appendix A. The global mechanism is used for
the non-reacting cases as it is not necessary to include any chemistry. When the complex
chemistry is disabled STAR CCM+ will still consider the reactions within the mechanism and
a global mechanism with just one reaction is preferred due to the computational time. This
does not mean that the reactions are happening, but just that the mechanism is needed in
order to specify the vapor component in the multiphase node. When the amount of species

2.8.1
Dynamic viscosity
The dynamic viscosity can either be expressed through Newton’s law of friction or as related
to the kinematic viscosity and density. It is an important property for liquid and gasses
as it determines a ﬂuids behaviour when it comes in contact with boundaries. It is also a
measure for the ﬂuids resistance to deformation by shear stress, which is the tangential

2.8.3
Surface tension
The surface tension material property is only a factor for the liquid phase and is not a
property of the gas phase. The liquid fuel injected into an engine or chamber forms a mist
of spherical droplets which is ﬁlled with liquid whereas on the surface a liquid ﬁlm is formed
under tension. The cause of the droplet formation is due to the surface tension as the
molecules inside the droplet surrounded by the ﬁlm is attracted in all directions, while the
molecules on the surface will feel forces inwards and along the surface edges and no forces
outwards the normal of the surface. The surface tension does also have a big contribution
to the spray break up as for a particle with a high surface tension the break up for that
speciﬁc particle will be more diﬃcult compared to a particle with a low surface tension. So
the method for specifying the surface tension in STAR CCM+ can be done in several ways.

2.8.4
Latent heat of vaporization
The latent heat of vaporization also known as enthalpy of vaporization is deﬁned as the
amount of enthalpy (heat energy) which is required in order to vaporize a liquid. The latent
heat of vaporization is also a function of temperature and pressure as less energy is needed
to vaporize a liquid at high temperatures. At higher temperatures the molecular kinetic
energy increases and they become more likely to separate from liquid to vapor. However,
at temperatures above the critical temperature for a liquid the latent heat of vaporization
vanishes as the substance neither can be deﬁned as a liquid or vapor but instead deﬁned as
a supercritical ﬂuid [39]. So this particular property is only valid for the liquid phase and
can be deﬁned in STAR CCM+ by means of diﬀerent methods. One of the methods is based
on the same NSRDS function as the surface tension in Equation (2.71). However, as this is
a diﬀerent property the coeﬃcients a-d will be diﬀerent. Using the NSRDS function yields

material property method is available when the Quasi-Steady Evaporation model is chosen

Validation case
The Engine Combustion Network (ECN) by Sandia National Laboratories (SNL) is an
international organization which provides well documented combustion related experimental
data. The experiments are used by many research units and are appropriate for CFD model
validation and the understanding of combustion at diﬀerent engine conditions [45]. The
outcome of the experiments can be used as benchmark results and typically performed in
several conditions. So in order to create a ﬂow simulation of the Michigan case, a validation
case has been simulated based on an SNL experiment, namely Spray A. The reason for
choosing this case is due to the well documented results, which can be replicated by means
of CFD. In this chapter an optimum setup of the Spray A case has been studied, which later
will be the initial setup for the Michigan case. The validation of the Spray A is separated
into the following two tasks.
• Non-reacting validation by means of liquid- and vapor penetration lengths
• Reacting validation by means of IDT with diﬀerent chemical mechanisms
Once these tasks are performed and validated, the Michigan case will have a solid background
and the ﬂow simulation model should not have any doubts in terms of validity. For the
non-reacting task SNL has provided some benchmark results on the liquid- and vapor
penetration lengths, whereas for the reacting task the IDT will be validated with diﬀerent
mechanisms. The aim of this is to see which mechanism that predicts the optimum IDT
compared to the experiment. The ﬂow simulation will be conducted with two diﬀerent
ambient temperatures, 900 K and 750 K. The reason for this is that the actual experiment
is conducted at 900 K and once the model is validated in these conditions, the 750 K case
will be simulated which corresponds to the same ambient temperature as in the Michigan
case. However, SNL has no data available for the 750 K experiment which therefore will
be validated using the study by Pickett et al. [46]. From this study a scaling law for the
liquid penetration length is available in diﬀerent ambient densities, where one of the scaling
matches the validating case which will be further described in Section 3.1.

As illustrated on the cross sectional view this reﬁnement could basically also be made with
the volumetric control option. However, this has been experimented and it is observed
that mixing AMR and volumetric control results in a very odd mesh. Once the AMR
starts to reﬁne the mesh out of the volumetric control, it creates non-cubic shaped cells and
instead mixes the cells by polyhedrals and triangles. This is the main reason for not using
volumetric control and AMR together and it is recommended to do it in this way instead.
Besides the initial reﬁnement criteria two additional criteria has been made based on
two diﬀerent scalars. The ﬁrst one is deﬁned for the velocity magnitude with two value
thresholds which deﬁnes the adaption. The velocity range is speciﬁed to [5, 20] m/s which
is the threshold values for the min and max [min, max] respectively. In other words this
means that if the chosen scalar value is above the maximum limit within a cell, the mesh
will be reﬁned. For scalar values below the minimum limit the mesh will be coarsened. The
last criteria is based on the same method, however the scalar value is chosen to be the
mass fraction of n-dodecane (C12H26) with the threshold range of [1e-9, 1e-8]. With these
three criteria the AMR has showed good results for the non-reacting cases. However, for
the reacting cases one additional criteria has to be made.
This criteria is a mesh adaption for reacting ﬂows, which is an inbuilt criteria that can
be chosen for reacting ﬂows. This particular criteria reﬁnes and coarsens cells based on
the secondary gradients of the selected combustion variables, which in this case is chosen
as the mass fraction. The chosen variables consisted of three diﬀerent species: OH, CO2
and H2O. The reason for choosing these species is that they are the typical products of a
diesel combustion. Especially CO2 and H2O is typically a product at the ﬂame front and
at the outer layer of the combustion, namely the diﬀusion ﬂame. For these three species
an Error Indicator (EI) are speciﬁed with a threshold range of [1e-10, 1e-8]. The range
speciﬁes whether the mesh should coarsen, reﬁne of keep based on the threshold range as
for the previous two criteria. However, the error indicator is not based on the scalar value
but instead calculates the error indicator as the output of the secondary gradient of the
selected variable [12]. Lastly, as the several criteria are speciﬁed within the AMR it can
obviously create some trouble as the criteria can contradict to each other. But in STAR
CCM+, their relative inﬂuence on the AMR is prioritized as follows:
• Reﬁne and Coarsen →Reﬁne
• Reﬁne and Keep →Reﬁne
• Keep and Coarsen →Keep
This order of priority ensures that the criteria are complied with and makes it feasible to add
several criteria. The AMR has shown to decrease the computational time drastically and

Species formation
The reaction with the oxygen available in the chamber starts after the injected liquid fuel
has evaporated and mixed with the hot ambient gas mixture. When the earlier mentioned
low-temperature reactions occurs i.e. the ﬁrst-stage ignition (τ1), the production of various
intermediate species and radicals takes form e.g. C12H25O2, CH2O and H2O2. These
intermediate species starts to be consumed after a speciﬁc induction time and leads to the
occurrence of local high-temperature pockets that includes species such as OH, CO and
H2O which are related to the high-temperature oxidation process. The time at which this
production of species takes place is referred to as the second-stage ignition (τ2). For this

Chapter 4
Investigation of the dual fuel study
In the previous chapter a comprehensive and detailed validation model in STAR CCM+ has
been setup and described for the Spray A experiment performed by SNL. This CFD setup
will now be used as the base model for the actual case study in this thesis, namely the study
of a dual fuel combustion in a constant volume combustion vessel performed by Yang et al.
[10]. Throughout this thesis this particular study [10] is referred to as the Michigan case.
The experiment is held in an optically accessible constant volume spray and combustion
vessel, which is placed in Michigan Technological University (MTU). The authors studied
the process of a dual fuel engine utilizing gaseous fuel at conditions near TDC, which
they achieved by utilizing pre-burn mixture procedure. Once the chamber had the TDC
conditions of a CI-engine, the premixed methane was injected to the system, which after
suﬃcient induction and mixing time was ignited with diesel by using micro-pilot injection.
The chronology of the simulation tasks for this thesis is based on a setup of a non-reacting
LES case for pure diesel injection where the chamber does not contain methane. Once the
non-reacting case simulation for pure diesel fuel is validated and accepted in terms of the
obtained results for liquid- and vapor penetration lengths, secondly a reacting case will be
activated and results for combustion, hereby ignition delay time, will be proceeded for the
pure diesel case. Lastly a LES simulation will be performed in order to validate the IDT of
the dual-fuel case.

4.3.2
Reacting results and discussion
The reacting simulations for the Michigan case has been conducted by enabling combustion
under complex chemistry and the results for the pure diesel and the dual fuel case has been
achieved. The chemical mechanisms that has been considered are the Yao- and Lapointe
mechanism. After some time of running the simulations, it was considered necessary to
speed up the simulations as there was a prospect of too long simulation time due to the
chemical mechanisms. To fulﬁll the desire for shorter simulation time, a sensitivity analysis
was performed on the time step where the results can be seen in Appendix I. So far the
simulations have run with a time step of 25 ns. From the maximum Courant number and
the maximum temperature presented on Figures I.1 and I.2 respectively, it is seen that
even an increase up to 200 ns did not aﬀect the maximum temperature signiﬁcantly beyond
ﬂattening the oscillations and the maximum Courant number is still below 0.1. Therefore,
it has been decided to run with a time step of 200 ns, which the presented results have been
achieved with. The resulting IDTs obtained from the numerical simulations of the pure
diesel and the dual fuel cases using the two diﬀerent mechanisms are presented together
with the experimental data for the Michigan case on Table 4.6. The deﬁnition used for
determining the IDT in the numerical simulations are performed in same way as for the
Spray A validation case i.e. the time when the greatest rise in the maximum temperature
gradient is observed.
First the results of the IDT for the pure diesel cases are considered. Ignition happens
in the simulations using both mechanisms which are the reason for the green colored ﬁelds
on the table. The Yao mechanism ignited later than Lapointe which can be explained
through the properties of the mechanisms at lower temperatures, as described under the
Spray A validation case. Next, the result is considered for the dual fuel case. No ignition
happened for the dual fuel case simulations which is the reason for the red colored ﬁelds
on Table 4.6. The simulations was ran for 6.95 ms and 9.15 ms for the Yao- and Lapointe
mechanism respectively, but even the long simulation time did not help on it. However,
some diﬀerence occurred with respect to the species formation for the dual fuel cases using
the two mechanisms, but this will be discussed later. A deviation is observed between the
numerical IDTs and the experimental IDT for the pure diesel cases. One of the reasons
for the large discrepancy between the numerical IDT and the experimental IDT might be
due to how the IDT is determined and also the chemical mechanism used for the numerical
simulations. Yang et al. [10] determined the IDT by using Schlieren images i.e. when they
encounter changes in the density of the liquid spray in the chamber. The density change
is created in the ambient gas as the vaporized diesel spray cools the ambient. This may
mean that the start of combustion that has been determined in this study by Yang et al.
might include early stage pre-ignition cool combustion, which is happening in the ﬁrst-stage
104
==PAGE:120==

ignition and hence a reaction for the low-temperature chemistry. The deviation between
the numerical and experimental IDT is for that reason very common to see. Another
comparison can be made with the results by ECN for the experimental pure diesel IDT.
Depending on the nozzle type they achieved IDTs of 1.79-2.53 ms using the conditions of
15% O2 at 750 K and 22.8 kg/m3 [54]. Furthermore, the IDT from the numerical validation

4.3.3
Conclusion: Michigan case
The non-reacting Michigan case shows satisfying results for the LPL which only deviated
with 6% from the experiment. The spray characteristic also shows to follow well the trend,
hereby the 8 plumes, the included angle of 150 degrees and also the particle distribution
shows to follow the Rosin-Rammler distribution during the start of the injection. More than
that, the physics are also well resolved within an acceptable maximum Courant number
and by means of the maximum volume fraction which do not exceed the critical limits.
Furthermore, the reacting cases for the pure diesel and the dual fuel case was conducted
and results was obtained. The IDT for the reacting pure diesel ignited at 3.3 ms and 2.9
ms for the Yao- and Lapointe mechanism, respectively. The species RO2, CH2O and H2O2
was formed in the ﬁrst-stage ignition, whereas OH and CH4 was formed in the second-stage
ignition which is for high-temperature reactions. Moreover, a challenge has arisen with
respect to the dual-fuel case as no ignition occurred when using the chemical mechanisms
for the dual-fuel simulation cases, namely Yao53 and Lapointe65. While the simulation
using the Lapointe mechanism showed low-temperature reactions meaning formation of
CH2O and H2O2 when the temperature increased to 900 K, the Yao mechanism kept being
at 750 K and did not form any of the ﬁrst-stage species. Moreover, the temperature for the
Lapointe dual fuel case hit the max at 900 K and decreased down to 850 K afterwards.
Therefore, no second-stage ignition happened. The simulation for the dual-fuel Lapointe-
and Yao case did run for 9.15 ms and 6.95 ms, respectively. When comparing the pure diesel
case with the dual-fuel case, the only change that has been implemented in the simulation
is the addition of methane to the chamber composition. The IDT are for sure delayed by
this change and this means that methane does delay the IDT. The density is still 13 kg/m3
and for that reason it is noticeable that the temperature has hard time increasing when
the methane is present in the chamber while using the same mechanisms as used for the
pure diesel cases. The outcome results from the dual-fuel cases somehow indicates that
the mechanisms are well for pure diesel cases and do even predict ignition both when the
density is 13 kg/m3 and using 750 K and also for the Spray A case where the temperature
is 750 K and the density is 22 kg/m3. Though, weaknesses are shown for the dual-fuel
case. It is seen from other studies of the numerical IDTs that a chemical mechanism which
performed well at higher temperatures do not necessarily do as well at lower temperatures
[59]. With respect to the dual-fuel case, the addition of methane showed not to react in
similar way as done for the Michigan pure diesel case by means of species formation using
same chemical mechanisms.

Conclusion
In this thesis a numerical study has been performed of a premixed dual-fuel conﬁguration
in a spray and combustion vessel. The study is performed using the Simcenter STAR CCM+
software to investigate the spray and combustion characteristics. Initially the main model
parameters were tested and validated against the Sandia Spray A experiment by ECN.
Diﬀerent mesh and physics conﬁgurations has been experimented over several cases. The
trimmed cell mesher consisting of square cells has proven to be the obvious choice due
to the cubical shaped domains. The adaptive mesh reﬁnement has proven to accelerate
the CPU time and is recommended for future works within spray and combustion. The
turbulence modelling, thermodynamic- and transport properties has showed to have an
signiﬁcant eﬀect on the spray characteristics especially the LPL. The turbulence modelling
by means of the URANS and the standard k-epsilon model has shown its weakness as the
LPL did not react to the change in the ambient temperature. On the other hand, the LES
proved to predict reasonable results. The stripping breakup constant Cs2 and the maximum
diameter used within the Rosin-Rammler method has proved to have a major impact on
the LPL. However, it is shown that keeping the default value for the breakup constant
and using the Dmax
2
method has proven to show good spray and combustion characteristics.
However, the thermodynamic and transport properties has to be speciﬁed accordingly. The
spray A validation cases showed reasonable results for the VPL and LPL at two diﬀerent
ambient temperatures. For the reacting spray A case the achieved IDTs was in agreement
with the experiment with a slightly deviation. Two skeletal chemical mechanisms has been
tested and the Lapointe mechanism consisting of 65 species and 365 reactions proved to be
the best mechanism in terms of determining the IDT at two diﬀerent ambient temperatures.
Once the optimum model setup and conﬁgurations was determined for the Spray A
case, the Michigan case has been conducted based on the chosen conﬁgurations. The chosen
models and conﬁgurations showed excellent results for the numerical LPL with a very
small deviation of 6% from the experiment. For the reacting cases two diﬀerent cases was
conducted. A pure diesel case and a dual-fuel case with premixed methane. The aim was
to show that including methane would delay the IDT. The pure diesel cases using the two

5.1
Recommendations and future work
Sensitivity/convergence study on the amount of parcel streams: The analogy of
the parcel streams and the mesh size is somewhat similar. Small grid sizes will in general
result in more accurate and computational costly simulation. The same goes for the parcel
streams i.e. increasing the parcel streams will yield to more accurate result and also more
computational cost. Moreover, the amount of parcel streams could be studied by doing a
convergence analysis and thereby chose the most suitable amount of parcels. However, this
would be a trade-oﬀbetween accuracy and CPU time.
Adaptive time-step: Due to the large computational time within some of the simulations
in this thesis, adaptive time-step would be a good way to reduce it. For the Michigan case
the fuel injection stops at t = 0.20 ms and the velocity of the ﬂow does thereby decrease.
This means that the Courant number and the volume fraction of the dispersed phase does
decrease as the induced parcels starts to evaporate and no fuel is injected consisting of
large droplets. Once the injection stops the adaptive time step would be preferable as it
would adapt the time step accordingly. In Appendix I it is illustrated that increasing the
time step resulted in a insigniﬁcant change in the results. Within the adaptive time step
a maximum target Courant number and a mean Courant number can be speciﬁed and
thereby allow the solver to increase and decrease the time step accordingly.
Flame lift-oﬀlength: Another combustion spray characteristic that could be investigated
is the ﬂame lift-oﬀlength. As described earlier when the liquid fuel is injected, the liquid
jet will propagate and form vapor and once fuel vapor and surrounding gas mixes it will
then ignite. The axial distance from the nozzle to the point of ignition is called the ﬂame
lift-oﬀlength. Usually the ﬂame will try to propagate towards the nozzle, however as
the region mainly consist of liquid droplets this will be quite diﬃcult and it will thereby
propagate in both directions. The lift-oﬀlength will therefore oscillate initially and later
reach a quasi-steady state. Since most of the reacting simulations in this thesis are mainly
simulated until ignition, the lift-oﬀlength is not analyzed. Furthermore, according to Pang
et al. [53] if one would like to measure the lift-oﬀlength, one could deﬁne it as the axial
distance from the nozzle at which the computed Favre-averaged mean OH mass fraction
reaches a value that is 2% of its maximum value for that operation condition.

Secondary breakup models: The Reitz-Diwakar secondary breakup model does only
consider bag and stripping breakup. However, there are more regimes than these which is
illustrated on Table 5.1. At low Weber number approximately around 12, a droplet will
breakup into two new droplets of approximately same size namely vibrational breakup.
For Weber numbers between 20 and 50 the so called bag/streamer breakup will happen,
whereas the bag shaped droplet will form a streamer shaped interior. Lastly the catastrophic
breakup is evident at large Weber number greater than 100. In this regime a large ﬂatten
droplet occurs with small droplet originated from it. Then with a rapid deformation the
droplet break into multiple small droplets.

Some of the regimes can be considered using diﬀerent models, however currently there are
only 3 models available besides the Reitz-Diwakar. The Taylor analogy breakup model can
be used for the vibrational breakup, however this model is mainly applicable at low Weber
numbers and hollow-cone injectors [12]. The KHRT model which is a combination of two
sub-models based on Kelvin-Helmholtz (KH) theory and Rayleigh-Taylor (RT) theory can
be used for the stripping and catastrophic breakup respectively. However, for this model
the coeﬃcients has to be tuned to the considered application. The KHRT model does also
create child parcels which can be more costly and is a more advanced model capturing the
same information as the Reitz-Diwakar breakup model. The Stochastic Secondary Droplet
(SSD) breakup model is a model where the breakup is unrelated to the original droplet size,
but instead creates child parcels whereas the diameter follows a log-normal distribution.
This model does consider the breakup as catastrophic. A study on Spray A with diﬀerent
breakup models would be interesting to see if it would predict the VPL and LPL even
better.

Appendix E
Determining the amount of parcels
The amount of parcels are determined on the following interpretation. If one wants to
achieve a certain amount of parcels by a given time. One can calculate how many parcels
that is needed to be injected at each time step.
Parcels=ceil(P*${TimeStep}/t)
Where P is the amount of parcels one need/wants to achieve and t is at what physical time
this is required/desired.
E.1
Determining the parcel streams
So for the optimum LES case 6 million parcels was desired when t = 0.6 ms. This means
that at the ﬁrst time step 42 parcels was required. This has been plugged in to the value of
the parcels streams node for the injector within STAR-CCM+.
Parcels=ceil(1e6*${TimeStep}/0.6e-3)

Appendix F
Mesh convergence study
This appendix consist of a mesh convergence study for the Spray A case with the AMR.
The study consist of two simulations with the same base size however, with two diﬀerent
minimum cell sizes namely 0.125 mm and 0.250 mm. The conﬁgurations for both simulations
are consistent and the only change is the minimum cell size. The physics conditions are
the optimum LES conﬁguration and the simulations are performed for the non-reacting
900 K case. After initialization the cell counts are diﬀerent for the two cases as due to the
minimum cell size. On Figure F.1a the VPL and LPL is illustrated for the two simulations.
From the ﬁgure it is observed that the case with a larger minimum cell size does overpredict
both the VPL and LPL. However, the diﬀerence between the VPL are minimal except at
early times before ∼0.7 ms whereas the largest diﬀerence occurs. Regarding the LPL it
is observed that the 0.250 mm case does have much larger peak to peak amplitudes and
reaches steady steady much later compared with the 0.125 mm case. With the larger cell
size more eddies are solved by the sub-grid scale model which might be the reason for the
larger ﬂuctuations.
On Figure F.1b the cell count is illustrated for both cases. After initialization the
cell count for the cases are 176420 and 301580 for the 0.250 mm and 0.125 mm cases
respectively. When the spray starts to propagate and the AMR adapts accordingly the
cell counts start to increase. At the end of simulation the 0.250 mm case consists of
661968 cells whereas the 0.125 case consists of 3548740 cells which approximately is 5 times
more. Furthermore, it is observed that the maximum volume fraction (Figure F.2a) and
the convective Courant number (Figure F.2b) decreases when the minimum cell size is
increased which is as expected. However, it can be seen that decreasing the minimum
cell size even further i.e. below 0.125 would yield to much higher volume fractions and
the solver/simulation would be unstable even though that the Courant number is at an
acceptable range. In the end it is concluded that the 0.125 mm case is a reasonable cell
size as it also is consistent with the study by Ong et al. [35] which is performed based on
the Sandia Spray A. Morover, ECN suggest to use a minimum cell size of 0.250 mm with a
time step of 5E-7 s for RANS Lagrangian simulation [60]. However, since this is based on

Appendix I
Sensitivity study on the time step for
the reacting Michigan case
This appendix contains results from a sensitivity analysis performed on the reacting
pure diesel Michigan case using Lapointe65 mechanism for the purpose of decreasing the
simulation time while keeping an acceptable accuracy. Four diﬀerent time steps has been
studied. The maximum temperature of the region can be seen on Figure I.1. The simulations
has always run with the time step of 25 ns until this test. It can be seen that the maximum
temperature is unaﬀected by the increase in time step by the factor of 8, meaning the
increase from 25 ns to 200 ns. The only diﬀerence between the smallest time step and
the largest is the ﬂuctuations. Another check is performed for the maximum convective
Courant number using the diﬀerent time steps which can be seen on Figure I.2. It is to
observe that the Courant number is still very low even when increasing the time step to
200 ns. This small test ensures that the results are consistent and do also demonstrate that
it is possible to use a large time step.
