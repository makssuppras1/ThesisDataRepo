III 
Abstract 
 
From 2010 onward, the well-established Cubic Plus Association (CPA) equation of 
state (EOS) was extended to also account for electrolytes without losing the backward 
compatibility. The resulting electrolyte EOS (eCPA) is competitive in terms of 
representing thermodynamic properties with other comparable electrolyte approaches. 
Nevertheless, a generally predictive behavior for such thermodynamic models is 
desired. An electrolyte EOS has to be able to effectively reproduce properties over 
wide temperature and pressure ranges and also include phase equilibria. This 
requirement has not been met yet. 
 
From a literature survey, it became clear that most approaches only concentrate on 
representing few thermodynamic properties. Although eCPA has already been applied 
to a variety of properties, phase equilibria, except solid-liquid equilibria (SLE), have not 
been widely represented. As a consequence, it was decided for the present thesis to 
focus on further investigation of the liquid-liquid (LLE) and vapor-liquid (VLE) phase 
equilibria and additionally to compare different parameter sets presented in the past. 
The comparison of different parameter sets was carried out for the original parameters 
obtained during the development of eCPA, a set where solubility data was also used 
for the adjustment of the parameters and parameters based on refitted ion diameters 
in contrast to the originally used literature diameters. 
The eCPA EOS was previously used to fairly predict and correlate a LLE for the system 
water, propanol and NaCl. This system, and systems with varying alcohols (propanol 
and the isomers of butanol) and salts (NaCl, KCl, NaBr, KBr, LiCl, KI) are further 
investigated for an overview of the general behavior of eCPA and to gain insight into 
the impact of interaction parameters on the correlation. It was shown that the prediction 
of the alcohol fraction in the aqueous phase and the salt fraction in the organic phase 
was not simultaneously possible. 
Until now, the prediction of VLEs for electrolyte solutions has not been carried out with 
eCPA. During the studies for the thesis, an issue was found and it was not possible to 
calculate VLEs. The problem was located in the calculation of the vapor phase fugacity 
coefficient and a routine was established to successfully calculate VLEs. The routine 
was tested for different salts (LiCl, NaF, NaBr, KCl) in a mixture of water and methanol 
for variable concentrations (0 - 4 mol/kg).

IV 
Zusammenfassung 
 
Die etablierte Zustandsgleichung (EOS) Cubic Plus Association (CPA) wird seit dem 
Jahr 2010 um einen Term zur Berechnung von Elektrolyten erweitert, ohne jedoch die 
Rückwärtskompatibilität zu verlieren. Die so erhaltene Elektrolyt EOS (eCPA) ist 
konkurrenzfähig bei der Berechnung thermodynamischer Eigenschaften.  
Generell sollte eine solche Zustandsgleichung das Verhalten eines Systems effizient 
und vor allem prädiktiv über einen weiten Temperatur- sowie Druckbereiche 
berechnen sowie die Darstellung von Phasengleichgewichten ermöglichen. Diese 
Vorgaben konnten, wie in der Literaturübersicht gezeigt, bislang nicht umgesetzt 
werden. Viele Modelle werden nur auf wenige thermodynamische Eigenschaften 
angewendet.  
Obwohl eCPA schon für die Vorhersage vieler Eigenschaften angewendet wurde, sind 
Phasengleichgewichte, mit Ausnahme von fest-flüssig Gleichgewichten (SLE), nicht 
ausreichend getestet worden. Darum wurde der Fokus der vorliegenden Arbeit auf die 
Berechnung der flüssig-flüssig (LLE) sowie gas-flüssig (VLE) Gleichgewichte gelegt. 
Zusätzlich wurde ein Vergleich der drei bisher entwickelten Parametersätze erstellt. 
Als Erstes wurden die während der Entwicklung von eCPA erhaltenen Parameter 
herangezogen. Zusätzlich ist ein Satz mit Schwerpunkt Löslichkeit entwickelt worden 
sowie als Drittes ein Satz, basierend auf angepassten Ionendurchmessern. 
Als bisher einziges LLE wurde das System Wasser, Propanol und NaCl mit eCPA 
untersucht. Die Vorhersage sowie eine Korrelation konnten das Verhalten jedoch nur 
unzufriedenstellend darstellen. Daher wurden in dieser Arbeit weitere Systeme mit 
unterschiedlichen Lösungsmitteln (Propanol sowie die Isomere des Butanols) und 
verschiedene Salze (NaCl, KCl, NaBr, KBr, LiCl, KI) weiter untersucht. Es wurde 
gezeigt, dass die Vorhersage des Alkoholanteils in der flüssigen Phase sowie der 
Salzgehalt in der organischen Phase nicht gleichzeitig dargestellt werden kann. 
Bislang wurden mit eCPA keine Vorhersage für VLEs mit Salzen durchgeführt. Die 
Darstellung war anfangs nicht möglich, da ein Problem bei der Berechnung auftrat. 
Dieses wurde bei Berechnung der Fugazitätskoeffizienten der Gasphase lokalisiert, 
woraufhin eine Routine entwickelt wurde, die die erfolgreiche Vorhersage der VLEs 
ermöglicht. Diese wurde für verschiedene Salze (LiCl, NaF, NaBr, KCl) in einer 
Wasser-Methanol Mischung für verschiedene Konzentrationen (0 - 4 mol/kg) getestet.

Introduction 
___________________________________________________________________ 
___________________________________________________________________ 
1 
 
1. Introduction 
 
Electrolytes play an important role not only in our everyday life, but are also 
encountered in various fields in the chemical industry. The applications range from the 
oil industry, where especially the interaction of oil, water and the two most common 
salts NaCl and KCl is of interest, to purification processes where salts can have a huge 
impact on process variables, to pharmaceutical concerns with voluminous and 
complex electrolytes.  
 
Although the need for a theoretical approach to electrolyte systems is evident, it was 
unacknowledged for a long time. Consequently, electrolyte models are far behind 
compared to state-of-the-art models for non-electrolytes, which is also reflected in the 
lack of experimental data for fundamental as well as elaborate thermodynamic 
properties [40]. 
 
In the last 20 years however, the number of electrolyte models including equations of 
state (EOS) has risen. With the upcoming of computational potential, associating EOSs 
like the Statistical Association Fluid Theory, short SAFT [8], and modifications became 
famous and were, because of their overall success, also applied to represent the 
physical part in electrolyte EOSs. Next to these various approaches, the association 
theory was also coupled with van-der-Waals-like cubic EOS. The electrolyte approach 
investigated in the present thesis is based on the Cubic Plus Association (CPA) EOS 
[35], which combines association with the Soave-Redlich-Kwong EOS, and is therefore 
called eCPA.  
Up to now, even though more effort was put into representing electrolyte systems, an 
overall predictive model, which can be applied to estimate multiple thermodynamic 
properties, was not developed.  
For all above mentioned branches of the chemical industry, among the most important 
thermodynamic properties are phase equilibria. For pharmaceuticals, the bioavailability 
is closely related to the solid-liquid equilibrium (SLE) since it represents the absorption 
in the human body. Vapor-liquid (VLE) as well as liquid-liquid equilibria (LLE) are 
fundamentals to many purification processes e.g. for cracking in the petroleum industry 
or to design waste water treatment.

Introduction

The structure of the present thesis is as follows. First, an introduction to the topic will 
be given in a general overview about basic and advanced electrolyte theories. Based 
on this, the utilized electrolyte EOS eCPA will be discussed and its characteristics are 
presented. Thereafter, a literature review is presented and critically analyzed in terms 
of range of application, system and salt complexity as well as temperature and 
pressure range. Derived from limitations found in the literature and bearing in mind the 
already published properties represented with eCPA, the scope of the thesis is 
selected. The focus lies in comparing existing parameter sets and extending 
knowledge about phase equilibria. The results are shown in  
- 
Chapter 5: A comparison of different parameter sets for salts 
- 
Chapter 6: Further investigation of LLEs containing salts 
- 
Chapter 7: Further investigation of VLEs containing salts. 
At the end, a conclusion will be drawn and the prospects for future work are presented.

2. Theoretical Background 
 
In general, to model thermodynamic properties of solutions containing salts, additional 
contributions have to be considered describing the effect of electrolytes. An overview 
of these contributions is given in FIGURE 1. It is illustrated that models are designed 
based on non-electrolyte, short-range templates with an additional term for the long-
range interaction of the electrolytes.  
 
In principle, the non-electrolyte basis is formed on the well-established models of Gibbs 
excess energy, so called gE-models, or equations of state, which utilize the Helmholtz 
free energy. The long-range interactions are commonly provided either by the Debye-
Hückel (DH) or the Mean Spherical Approximation (MSA). In addition, the Born term is 
used within various publications. Different researchers stated that next to this kind of 
fundamental parts, interactions of the electrolytes may have to be extended to include 
more physical phenomena as shown in the right column of FIGURE 1.

The theories and their numerous forms are discussed in this chapter. Special emphasis 
will be placed on the use of the Born term because of its contradictory viewing in the

parameter for the calculation of mixtures and salts, the static permittivity will be 
discussed.

2.1. 
Non-electrolyte Framework 
 
As shown in FIGURE 1, electrolyte solutions are determined by a framework of a non-
electrolyte or physical part coupled with one dealing with the interactions of the ions 
themselves. First, the non-electrolyte factor will be discussed.  
 
Universally speaking, two approaches for the non-electrolyte contribution are available, 
one derived from the Gibbs free excess energy, activity coefficient- or gE-models, and 
Helmholtz free energy models, also known as equations of state. Since both have been 
developed and successfully improved for a long time to be the fundamentals of 
producing thermodynamic property data, they are therefore seen as ideal matrices for 
electrolyte models. 
 
2.1.1. Activity Coefficient Models 
 
The first approach for non-electrolyte models discussed is the application of activity 
coefficients, also known as gE-models. These methods are well-established in the 
chemical world with plenty of simulation applications to be found, with prominent 
representatives being the Non-Random-Two-Liquid (NRTL) theory by Prausnitz [54] 
and the Universal Quasichemical activity model (UNIQUAC) [1]. 
 
Activity coefficient models have been and still are the preferred methods in the 
chemical industry. One is able to perform calculations in a wide temperature range. 
However, drawbacks have to be discussed. 
A major problem concerning the gE-models is the number of parameters needed to 
describe the thermodynamic properties properly. Another crucial drawback is the lack 
of the ability to calculate densities, e.g. for mixtures, which is an important property 
when it comes to process design. Furthermore, the calculation of pressure-dependent 
activity coefficients is not directly possible.  
Although models based on the Gibbs energy are widely used, the equation of states 
have become more popular in the past three decades. They are presented in the next 
section.

2.1.2. Equations of State 
 
Another option to deal with the prediction of thermodynamic properties is with the use 
of equations of state, with the simplest being the ideal gas equation. Van der Waals 
derived the first equation being considered an EOS from which many famous 
derivatives were deduced. It is also the first equation named “cubic”, since it provides 
two parameters.  
Many derivatives have been developed thereafter. A well-known enhancement of the 
van-der-Waals-EOS was performed by Peng and Robinson [52]. Redlich and Kwong 
and later Soave evolved the Soave-Redlich-Kwong (SRK) equation of state [62]. 
With the upcoming of computational potential and thereby statistical mathematics, a 
widely-used application was invented based on the associating theory of Wertheim 
[67]. The statistical associating fluid theory or short SAFT [8], first introduced by 
Chapman has become one of the most utilized methods. As for the other EOSs, SAFT 
was numerously refined to deal with new scopes of application, leading e.g. to PC-
SAFT [18] or SAFT-VR [17; 12].  
Kontogeorgis [35] extended the previously mentioned SRK EOS also utilizing the 
Wertheim associating theory and presented the Cubic Plus Association (CPA) EOS, 
which is largely used in petrochemical research [10; 41–45; 60]. 
All the above-mentioned formalisms have been successfully applied in calculating and 
predicting thermodynamic properties for systems lacking salts. However, after the non-
electrolytes have been covered effectively, particular attention was drawn to predicting 
solutions containing ions.

similar. Not only are the two methods linked through the chemical potential, which can 
be represented with either the Helmholtz or the Gibbs free energy, but also account for 
the electrolytes by an additional term. In electrolyte gE-models, different contributions

the adopted mathematical approach and thus on the time required to calculate the

2.2.1. General Discussion on Electrolyte Solutions 
 
As already mentioned, the electrolyte models can be categorized based on the choice 
of mathematical approach to the physical behavior. Many researchers tend to use 
approaches beginning with the simpler, the primitive model. For such a model the 
electrolytes are assumed to be surrounded by the solvent treated as a continuum. It is

furthermore assumed that the static permittivity is equal to that of the solvent. 
 
In non-primitive models, the static permittivity is not accounted for. Instead, the solvent 
is treated as its own species, composed of molecules and the coupling of electrostatic 
interactions between solvent molecules and the electrolytes is used. The benefit of 
non-primitive models is the omission of the static permittivity as the fundament of the 
dielectric continuum. 
 
The most advanced is the civilized model, which is not yet introduced to EOSs and, in 
addition to the non-primitive model, accounts for steric effects or ion-ion interaction. 
An additional differentiation for the models regards the electrolytes themselves. In 
restricted models, it can be assumed that all ions are represented with a specific 
diameter while for non-restricted models each ion has an individual diameter. 
 
Furthermore, only a few authors [25] have modeled the solvent as a species and, as 
pointed out by Maribo-Mogensen et al. [41], state of the art EOSs are not compatible 
with such approaches yet. Additionally, there is no crucial difference in the results 
comparing both approaches. 
 
Hence, researchers nowadays prefer to use primitive models for the long-range 
interactions. The two widely used models will be presented in the following sections. 
One major difference between the non-electrolyte and the electrolyte approaches is 
the framework they are associated with, which will be discussed in a separate revision 
unit (section 2.2.5). 
A general validity when dealing with solutions containing electrolytes is the 
electroneutrality, as it is for example a requirement for the Debye-Hückel approach. 
The principle is that, when introducing ions to a system, the overall charge is zero. It is 
thus not possible to have unequal proportions of ions of different charges.  
When dealing with salts, it is appropriate to choose the molality base (mol/kg solvent 
or molal), rather than the commonly used molar concentration (mol/L). This comes with 
the advantage that the concentration is calculated for one solvent only and additional 
components can be introduced without changing the molality. Hence, it is possible to 
represent the effect of an additive with the same scale. 
 
In the following sections, two contributions to the long-range electrostatic interaction 
are presented, followed by the optional third contributory Born term.

Next to the full DH theory, the DH limiting law is often used. Although this approach is 
commonly employed when describing electrolytes, it is valid for diluted solutions with 
only small amounts of salt.  
 
A disadvantage of this approach was shown by Onsager [49], who stated that for 
asymmetrical ions the requirements of equal radial distribution functions cannot be 
fulfilled. 
In contrast to the point charge in the DH theory, the subsequently presented approach 
treats all ions as charged hard spheres, likewise surrounded by a solvent regarded as 
a continuum. 
 
2.2.3. The Mean Spherical Approximation 
 
The additional term to change either the Helmholtz or the Gibbs energy due to the 
interaction of the electrolytes with the solvent can also be calculated with the so-called 
Mean Spherical Approximation. The MSA is a closure to the solution of the Ornstein-
Zernike (OZ) equation, which, derived from statistical mechanics, describes the 
interaction between two electrolytes. 
The MSA can either be used for a fluid of charged spheres of equal size (non-primitive 
model) [5] or where the solvent is considered as a dielectric continuum [66], which 
leads to a primitive model.  
For instance, Waisman and Lebowitz [66] obtained the first analytical expression for a 
restricted primitive model. Onward, Blum [5] presented a solution for primitive models. 
While this closure allows ions of arbitrary size, a simpler, explicit expression where 
ions have the same diameter was found by Harvey et al. [19]. 
For the sake of completeness, other solutions to the OZ equation like the hypernetted 
chain closure [9] exist, which are not commonly used for electrolyte solutions and will 
thus not be presented here. 
 
Recently, the two formalisms, the full DH and the MSA, were compared in terms of 
predicting properties with the same non-electrolyte model to guarantee feasible 
reproducibility. It was found that both approaches perform similarly and with a small

2.2.4. The Born Term 
 
The Born term is a very controversially debated part in electrolyte thermodynamics. 
About half of the published models do not utilize the Born term.  
 
As it is presented by Born in 1920 [7], the term accounts for the so-called self-potential 
to the Gibbs energy. It is the change in the free energy from transferring the electrolyte 
from a vacuum or ideal gas to a real medium accounted for as a continuum with a 
specific relative dielectric constant. The Born cycle is visualized in FIGURE 2.

However, it is often omitted since it does not provide a contribution to the excess 
chemical potential, but insures the ions stay in the polar phase [68]. When calculating 
VLEs, electrolytes will stay in the liquid phase due to the typically much higher 
permittivity compared to the permittivity of the vapor phase.

In contrast, for example in ePC-SAFT [21], the ions are artificially constrained within 
the liquid phase by setting their vapor fraction to zero (𝑦𝑠𝑎𝑙𝑡= 0). As this provides the 
same effect as the Born term, it is omitted. 
 
Harvey and Prausnitz [19] emphasized that the Born term is not negligible due to the 
effect on the fugacity of the solvent. On the contrary, Zuo et al. [73; 72] pointed out, 
that the Born term will not contribute to the solvents fugacity coefficient and will not 
affect activity coefficients or VLE calculations. It will, however, contribute to salt 
solubility and LLEs. 
One could argue whether artificially constraining ions to the liquid phase, e.g. by setting 
their vapor fraction to zero, provides a loss of consistency in thermodynamic 
calculations. Since the Born term is utilized in the calculations in eCPA, this possibility 
is circumvented. 
 
2.2.5. Distinction between McMillan and Lewis-Randall Frameworks 
 
Representing both solvents and electrolytes in one model can also cause 
inconsistencies. If the solvent is treated explicitly, i.e. as its own species, in the physical 
part of an electrolyte EOS, the underlying framework is Lewis-Randall (LE). In contrast, 
the McMillan-Mayer framework (MC) describes the solvent as a continuum. This is 
valid for most of the DH and MSA models, which are generally expressed as primitive 
models.  
 
Although many researchers tend to leave the conversion between both frameworks 
undiscussed, it is indispensable to avoid thermodynamic inconsistencies [57]. 
However, as stated by Myers et al [47], the difference for the total activity coefficient 
between the MC and the LE framework is less than 3% and seen as too small to 
consider. They further assumed the difference to be covered by several empirical 
parameters. As a result, Myers did neglect the conversion.   
Other authors don’t agree and assert that the conversion is required. Nonetheless, 
different conversions from the MC to the LE framework and the other way around can 
be found in the literature [20].

2.2.6. Salt and Ion Parameters 
 
In general, approaches dealing with electrolyte solutions have been developed utilizing 
two different sets of parameters, salt and ion specific, respectively. The first represents 
the salt as a component of its own and the latter divides the salt into two ions, which 
are treated independently. 
The first approaches to calculate electrolyte solutions were made with salt specific 
parameters [25; 45; 19]. They are available for many systems in literature and, what is 
rare for ion specific parameters, they are available for solvents others than water. 
Furthermore, thermodynamic properties, e.g. the experimental activity coefficient, are 
specific to salt rather than a property of the ions and therefore only the salt specific 
parameters can be adjusted [47]. 
However, numerous publications have been released utilizing ion specific parameters. 
The first benefit of ion specific parameters is easily identifiable. If a set of parameters 
is adjusted for the ion only, the set is multivalent and can therefore be applied to 
different counter ions for building other salts [29; 27; 28; 30]. If, for example, the 
parameter set is known for sodium it is possible to connect all types of anions and their 
parameter sets to form salts; with chloride, NaCl is parametrized, with bromide, it is 
NaBr, and so on.   
However, developed methods for measuring properties of ions are scarce [61]. Vera 
et al. [32] have presented a method with which they measured ion properties. They 
used ion-selective electrodes that allow one to distinguish between the different ions 
and their contribution to the electromotive force.  
 
A frequently used option to fit ion parameters lies in tables and correlations, e.g. 
Pauling or Stokes radii or crystal diameters, respectively. Yet, researchers discuss 
whether there are ion specific properties. 
Hence, most researchers fitting ion specific parameters do this by adjusting ion 
parameters for many salts containing the specific ion at a time. This procedure is 
comparable to group contribution methods like Joback and Reid [31] and comes with 
the same drawbacks [63].  
Tan et al. [64] compared the property data of mixed solvents with swapping ion pairs.

3.2. 
Range of Applicability 
 
At first glance, the range of applicability for all presented approaches reaches from 
environmental conditions to far in excess of critical states with temperature and 
pressure above 700 K and 1500 bar, respectively, and in one publication even to 
molalities of up to 80 molal [60]. However, these extrapolated conditions often come at 
the expense of additional parameters and are accordingly only semi-predictive.  
To illustrate, in 1989, Harvey et al. [19] used the Baker-Henderson perturbation theory 
coupled with a primitive MSA and the Born term to investigate high pressure aqueous 
systems with satisfactory success. Here, a temperature-dependent interaction 
parameter was used.  
 
Ji et al. [27–29] used their approach to investigate properties at up to 473 K and 
therefore introduced eight new parameters, although they were able to predict 
pressures up to 1000 bar without further adjustment.  
The extension of eCPA to predict solid-liquid equilibria by Schlaikjer et al. [60] included 
temperature-dependent parameters to predict in a temperature range of 200 K. 
Most publications use concentrations up to six molal, which is in accordance with Das 
et al. [11; 12] who stated that the assumption of strong electrolytes is valid up to this 
concentration. As stated by Lee et al., insufficiencies of models can occur when higher 
concentrations are examined [37]. 
 
3.3. 
Systems Containing Multiple Salts or Solvents 
 
Investigations of mixed components were mostly carried out for the solubility of CO2, 
water-alcohol mixtures and various salts to test e.g. the salting-out effect or to examine 
gas solubility. The solubility of CO2 in single and mixed electrolyte solutions was, for 
instance, tested by Jiang et al. A test for their model (SAFT-VRE) to both, mixed 
solvents and mixed salts, was presented by Schreckenberg et al. [61], who effectively 
reproduced the behavior of VLEs and LLEs by introducing an additional Born term. 
However, they admitted only semi-quantitative agreement.

Zou et al. [73] published two methods to successfully apply to LLEs. They used the 
Gibbs energy of transfer for both methods, where the salt is transferred from the first 
solvent (water) into the mixture. They used the same electrolyte EOS as Fürst and

Recently, an approach was published where the parametrization was performed with

liquid equilibria. Therefore, it was extended to predict SLE data, without losing 
accuracy for calculating the other properties. It has to be mentioned that SLEs cannot 
be calculated by EOSs [60] and a conversion has to be introduced. Schlaikjer et al. 
[60] were able to incorporate solubility data with a new parameterization. 
 
3.4. 
Complexity of Salts 
 
Two different types of salts are discussed in this section. First, because the majority of 
the literature deals with strong electrolytes, the problems of weak electrolytes will be 
presented and solutions from literature are displayed. Second, the challenge of more 
complex salts is presented and debated. 
As opposed to weak electrolytes, the treatment of fully dissolved and therefore 
dissociated salts is easier because they are seen as independent ions. Hence, models 
are often based on this assumption, which is valid for low concentrations or salts with 
high solubility, like KBr. However, for weak electrolytes, which do not fully dissociate, 
or if the concentration is high enough, clusters or ion pairing may occur. Only a small 
number of publications made the effort to generate a model to describe the behavior 
of weak electrolytes [23].  
One approach is to consider ion pairing as a reaction and develop a model based on 
the equilibrium constant to cover the amount of ion pairing [24; 72]. For higher ion

concentrations, Das et al. [6] covered the deviation in the degree of dissociation via 
inclusion of ion association in the Gibbs free energy, which was proposed by Rozmus 
et al. [57] for concentrations of up to six molal.  
Multivalent salts are asymmetrical and consist of either two or more anions or cations 
(anion-cation ratio: 1:2, 2:1 and 2:2). Such salts, for instance Mg2SO4, are modeled by

simplifications in the underlying method. These references indicate that more 
emphasis needs to be placed on the modeling of complex ions to generate respective 
parameters. Thereafter the model can be simplified to deal with the smaller ions. 
 
3.5. 
Scope of this Thesis 
 
There are different possibilities to further investigate the behavior of the eCPA equation 
of state. Many recently developed approaches for electrolyte solutions are still only 
valid for moderate temperature and pressure ranges. eCPA is designed to cover wider 
temperature and pressure ranges. As presented in [40], e.g. the solubility of CO2 over 
a wide temperature range was already successfully calculated with the eCPA EOS. 
However, other properties and additional systems have so far not been investigated. 
The second possibility is to deepen the knowledge about phase equilibria. For LLE 
systems, only one has been presented which gave unsatisfying results on the phase 
behavior (see chapter 6.1). Furthermore, the correlation performed to give information 
about the ability of eCPA to represent the LLE did not succeed to full extent.  
 
In addition to this, only salt free VLEs have been presented in publications and no 
influence of salt on the envelope has been calculated with eCPA. VLEs are a very 
important and yet easy to solve task for an equation of state.  
 
SLE calculations are currently being investigated within the research group [60].  
Comparing these two options with the publications, discussed in the last section, both 
are valuable for further investigation. Neither the representation of phase equilibria, nor 
elaborated conditions are found in these very frequently.

4.2. 
Vapor-Liquid Equilibrium 
 
Salt can have a great impact on binary solvent mixtures. Not only does it affect e.g. the 
solvents boiling point elevation due to the change in the static permittivity, but also 
influences the VLE.  
Many binary mixtures undergo azeotropic behavior; a well-known example is the 
water-ethanol mixture. Such azeotropes can cause significant problems or increase 
cost in, for instance, distillation processes.  
 
Salts however can, even in very small concentrations, induce or circumvent 
azeotropes. The main reason is the salting-out effect on one of the solvents involved.

For VLEs an often-utilized method is the Two-Phase Flash. It is presented in the next 
sections together with alternatives and a model for the iteration procedure. 
4.2.1. Two-Phase Flash 
 
In the present work, the investigated phase equilibrium is expected to only consist of a 
maximum of two phases. No miscibility gap or precipitation due to exceeding of 
solubility are designated.  
Therefore, the isothermal Two-Phase Flash can be used to calculate the vapor-liquid 
equilibrium. The calculation is performed for a specific temperature and pressure at a 
fixed system composition inside a loop which changes either T or p after successfully 
predicting the equilibrium.  
 
4.2.2. Bubble and Dew Point Calculation 
 
On the one hand, it is possible to calculate the VLE using the flash procedure or, on 
the other hand, treat the liquid and the vapor phase individually through calculation of 
the bubble and the dew point. This possibility will later be of great importance for the 
here given approach to circumvent the VLE calculation. 
4.2.3. The Successive Substitution Method 
 
The successive substitution method (SSM) is used multiple times for the phase 
calculations in this work. In general, the procedure starts with the estimation of the 𝐾-
factors for all components in two assumed equilibrium phases. From the total 
composition of the system, with these guesses, the theoretical phase compositions in 
the vapor and liquid and in the first and second liquid phase, respectively, are 
calculated. Thereafter, the fugacity coefficients and new 𝐾-factors can be calculated. 
As long as the values for the system composition do not agree, the composition in both 
phases will be re-calculated and the process is repeated.

4.3. 
Liquid-Liquid Equilibrium 
 
As for vapor-liquid equilibria, introducing salts does change the overall behavior of the 
system. In otherwise fully mixable systems, salts can induce a phase split and lead to 
miscibility gaps. As shown in [40], the otherwise miscible water-1-propanol mixture will 
undergo a liquid-liquid split after introducing salt to the system. The amount of salt can 
therefore also change the extend of such a miscibility gap. After the addition of a certain 
amount of salt, the solubility limit is reached and the salt will precipitate. At this point 
the liquid-liquid equilibrium is converted to a liquid-liquid-solid equilibrium (LLSE).

4.3.1. Comments on the Calculation of Liquid-Liquid Equilibria 
 
In general, the above introduced Two-Phase Flash to calculate VLEs is also valid for 
liquid-liquid equilibria and, good initial guesses provided, the procedure may calculate 
the equilibrium well. However, it makes sense to derive a method which verifies the 
stability by tangent plane analysis. Due to nearly critical conditions and no natural 
𝐾-factor, the calculation of LLEs resembles the characteristics of a multiphase flash. It 
is much more difficult to guarantee the convergence with the successive substitution 
than for a normal flash. 
 
In section 6.5, the problem of stability is discussed and it is shown to be essential to 
the optimization process of the interaction parameters.

5.3. 
Discussion 
 
For simpler salts, like NaCl, the overall difference in the three parameter sets is very 
small. To illustrate, for NaCl, the difference between MM and AS is only 0.74% and for 
MM and SM 13.1%. This leads to the overall small deviations for the properties 
investigated in this thesis. 
The parameter set developed by Schlaikjer was introduced with emphasis on solubility 
data in the fitting procedure and is therefore supposed to have advantages over the 
other sets when this property is estimated. However, the overall experience suggests 
otherwise. Although this is in general valid for the comparison with the set by SM and 
for the simpler systems (e.g. FIGURE 6 and FIGURE 8), where the results are similar or 
better compared to the original parameter set, the more difficult SLE is better 
represented with the original parameter set. 
The parameters derived from the refitted diameters result in smaller values for the 
interaction parameter between water and the salt and thus provokes lower solubility of 
the investigated salts in all cases, except for FIGURE 9. The results for the SLE of NaCl 
and Na2SO4 could not be reproduced for pure water and nor small methanol 
concentrations.  
It can be derived from FIGURE 7 and FIGURE 10 that alkanes, in this case the solubility 
of pentane, toluene and benzene in a mixture of water and NaCl and the solubility of 
methane over a wide pressure range, respectively, are mostly unaffected by the 
different parameter sets. Alkanes do not have an active or associating functional group 
nor do they build dipoles and are therefore not strongly susceptible to the different 
parameter sets for the electrolytes. In addition, the aforementioned small deviation 
between the electrolyte sets accounts for the difference.

6.5. 
Discussion 
 
The optimization routine to find the best IP set is affected by the numerical issues which 
can occur in the LLE calculations with the used procedure.  
The initial guess for the system composition is started from an equimolar binary 
solution containing both solvents. Thereafter, the molality of the salt is raised until the 
LLSE limit is reached. With this procedure, a straight line towards the pure salt will 
secure the composition to meet the binodal curve. Within the binodal curve, the phase 
split will take place along the tie lines up to the LLSE limit.  
Within the calculation routine, it is checked, if the system is in the single-phase region 
or a phase split is performed and if the solubility limit is reached. This seems to be a 
simple procedure, however an arbitrary point will possibly not be calculated to undergo 
a phase split, although salt molalities before and after this point can be in the phase 
split region. This problem is due to computational uncertainties and leads to difficulties 
in the optimization routine.  
 
In particular, within the optimization routine, the initial guesses are chosen such, that 
the experimental data (the limits of the two-phase region) are matched when the phase 
split is performed. When the interaction parameters are changed to better represent 
==PAGE:89==
 
 
Liquid-Liquid Equilibria Containing Salts 
____________________________________________________________________ 
___________________________________________________________________ 
61 
the experimental data, e.g. via reducing the residue between experimental and 
calculated data points, it is likely that the initial guesses will no longer provide valid 
starting points and the phase split is not performed. As a result, the derivation is no 
longer consistent, as the number of points investigated is not equal. 
 
In the worst case, this results in the loss of all starting guesses. Even though, this case 
seems unlikely, it appeared whenever more than one parameter was optimized with 
the lsqnonlin at a time. The optimization was hence carried out with the second method 
fminsearch.  
To solve this issue, the starting points are recalculated after every optimization step. 
This makes the optimization very time consuming, since the recalculation step has to 
be started by hand and it takes time to find new best guesses. Moreover, this process 
will not consequently result in optimal solutions. 
 
If the routine fails, a manual optimization for the alcohol in the aqueous phase is 
performed (see previous section). Exemplarily, the results for the graphical

Another issue with the method to calculate LLEs is that for some systems, the range 
of salt concentration covered by available experimental data cannot be represented. 
This can again be related to initial guesses. A more accurate answer to this problem 
is, that the model and the underlying parameter set do underestimate phase split 
regions. Conversely, the solubility limit, the LLSE tie line, is overestimated. Both issues 
lead to poorly estimated phase behavior. 
eCPA is, with the correlation of IPs, capable of successfully calculating liquid-liquid 
equilibria. Some approaches accounting for the deviation to experimental data have 
already been presented by Maribo-Mogensen. However, these suggestions have not 
yet been investigated.  
The results of this chapter can be matched to the results in other literature. To 
represent the phase behavior of LLEs, Held et al. [22] introduced an IP 𝑙𝑖𝑗 to account 
for the correction of the diameter of the solvated ion. They adjusted this parameter to 
experimental LLE data to successfully represent the LLE of the system water, butanol 
and NH4Cl. The utilization of an IP for the ion solvation underlines the suggestions 
mentioned above for the extension of eCPA. In comparison with the procedure to find 
the optimal parameters for eCPA, this parameter is assimilable to the IP between the 
salt and the alcohol (layer 1). Additionally, Held et al. [22] adjusted the solvent IP to 
binary salt-free LLE data.  
Since NH4Cl is not available in the parameter sets of eCPA, a comparison is not directly 
possible. However, the need for adjustable parameters shows that the predictive 
capability limit of ePC-SAFT is reached. In summary, after adjusting the interaction 
parameters, the representation of the alcohol distribution in both phases can be 
effectively correlated with both eCPA and ePC-SAFT.  
 
Likewise, Schreckenberg et al. [61] optimized an ion-alcohol unlike dispersion 
parameter directly to the fluid phase equilibrium experimental data. By utilizing this IP, 
they were able to represent the alcohol fraction in the aqueous phase and, like for 
eCPA, did not reasonably correlate the salt fraction simultaneously.  
In summary, although different physical terms (SAFT and CPA) and electrolyte 
contributions (MSA, DH and Born) have been used, the prediction is not possible for 
the models in the first place.

7. Vapor-Liquid Equilibria Containing Salts 
 
As already presented in the introduction, the representation of vapor-liquid equilibria is 
an important application for an electrolyte EOS. Since this property has not been 
investigated for eCPA so far, they are accounted for in this section.  
 
However, even though salt free VLEs are predictable by the model, a major issue was 
experienced when first trying to calculate VLEs including salt which resulted in an 
interruption of the process. Therefore, a major objective was to detect the source of 
defect in the underlying code. This chapter is dedicated to the process of solving the 
problem. 
At the end, a generally working calculation procedure is presented which solves the 
aforementioned inconsistency in the model’s code. It is then applied to different 
systems and molalities and is capable of representing VLEs for all available 
experimental data.

For the successful procedure of calculating VLEs containing salts, the starting point is 
again the equilibrium condition (Equation (7.1)), this time in logarithmic scale due to 
the nature of how the fugacity coefficients are calculated within eCPA.

First, the left side of the equation is solved. This is carried out by solving the bubble 
point calculation for an arbitrary composition of the system. From the previous section, 
it is reasonable to assume a working bubble point calculation. This leads to the 
following five intermediate results: 
1. The temperature where the first bubble appears 
2. The composition of the liquid phase 
With both parts known, the fugacity coefficients of the components in the liquid phase 
can be calculated.  
3. Fugacity coefficients of the liquid phase 
The left side of the equilibrium equation is thus solved and will be set for the next steps. 
In the next step the right side of the equation will be solved. Again, an arbitrary 
composition of the vapor phase is needed. It is important for solving the following 
iteration procedure, that the vapor phase contains salt. In this case the salt vapor 
fraction is set to 𝑦𝑠𝑎𝑙𝑡= 10−15.

When calculating the vapor composition in this way, the first step of an iteration process 
is carried out. Next, for the new vapor composition the fugacity coefficients are 
calculated. The left side of Equation (7.1) is again matched with the right side. If they 
are equal (or an acceptable minimum is reached) the iterative process is terminated,

It is likely that, with a good initial guess, the left and right side of Equation (7.1) are 
almost similar for the solvents. However, a greater deviation is expected for the salt. 
This is the reason why there must be a small amount of salt in the vapor phase.  
There is arguably a rather large amount of salt in the vapor phase when considering 
e.g. the almost neglectable vapor pressure of salts, yet very small concentrations can 
lead to numerical problems. Furthermore, it was tested whether smaller concentrations 
can benefit the calculation and it was found, that 𝑦𝑠𝑎𝑙𝑡= 10−15 is a reasonable initial 
guess for the iteration. 
The arbitrary guesses for either the system composition or the vapor composition can

7.6. 
Discussion 
 
As shown in chapter 7.1 the calculation of VLEs without salts, performed by CPA 
without calling an initialization involving salt, can be performed without problems. 
However, if a salt is initialized the calculation with eCPA cannot be carried out. It was 
shown in the subchapters thereafter, that the problem is found in the calculation of the 
dew points, or more precisely in the vapor phase fugacity coefficient of the salt.  
The trivial solution can appear for EOSs when the initial estimates for the equilibrium 
are chosen poorly or when the system is near the critical point. As a consequence, the 
results of the calculated vapor and liquid phase will be of identical composition and 
properties (Equation (7.3)).

As shown in FIGURE 32, the bubble point can be calculated with the available routine 
of eCPA. However, numerical issues are also a problem for this calculation. When the 
mole fraction of the alcohol becomes small, the prediction does not give reasonable 
results. 
 
This can be overcome with the initial guess for the temperature. There is a sweet spot 
around 50 K above the estimated temperature for the bubble point. Thus, if the initial 
guess for the bubble point temperature is overestimated, the prediction runs smoothly. 
This again leads to the assumption of a numerical issue within the underlying Fortran 
code.  
For systems with higher molalities of salt (>2 mol/kg), the same assumption as already 
applied for the bubble point calculation has to be made. The estimated initial guess for 
the temperature has to be much higher than the real temperature. When 
overestimating the initial temperature by 200 K, molalities greater than 4 molal can be 
handled with the procedure shown in chapter 7.5.1.

8. Conclusion and Future Work 
 
8.1. 
Conclusion 
 
In the present thesis, three different aspects for the further development of eCPA have 
been investigated to generate a better understanding of the physical and mathematical 
behavior of the model. 
 
First, the three available parameter sets for salts have been compared with different 
thermodynamic properties. The difference between these sets is almost negligible for 
simpler systems, but has a major influence when the system becomes more complex. 
It was shown that alkanes are not highly affected by the deviation of the parameters. 
The second parameter set, based on including solubility data, shows overall similar or 
better behavior for simpler systems, but lags behind the original set for the investigated 
SLE system of water, methanol, NaCl and Na2SO4. The third parameter set overall 
estimates a lower solubility due to smaller interaction parameters, since the refitted ion 
diameters met the negative artificial boarder of -10%. The results shown here provide 
an indication that adjusting the ion diameters does not improve the estimation in 
general and that assuming crystal radii from literature to also account for ions is 
sufficient. 
Second, the calculation of LLEs was extended to five other salts and four solvents and 
the impact of interaction parameters has been investigated. It was shown that 
representing the solubility of salt in the organic phase and the alcohol fraction in the 
aqueous phase at once is not possible, but for the individual property an optimal 
interaction parameter set is available.   
Third, the per se easily predictable behavior of VLEs was not reproducible for systems 
containing salts. However, after retracing the steps to solve the issue, a flash 
calculation routine based on the fugacity coefficients of the liquid phase was introduced 
to successfully represent VLEs with salts. The method is capable of predicting 
concentrations of up to at least 4 mol/kg. Results for four salts in a binary mixture of 
water and methanol of altered molality are in good acceptance with experimental data.

8.2. 
Future Work 
 
Within this work, it was shown that the capability of eCPA also comprises the very 
important VLE and LLE phase equilibria in general. However, the representation of 
LLEs is still not satisfactory. The shown properties, the distribution of the alcohol in the 
aqueous phase and of the salt in the organic phase, could not be reproduced 
simultaneously by the model as it stands now. Consequently, it is suggested to 
investigate whether the introduction of additional models for physical phenomena, like 
the proposed ion solvation or ion-ion interaction, should be implemented to account for 
representing both properties simultaneously. As a drawback, the implementation would 
come at the expense of additional parameters. 
The further work on VLEs includes establishing a method to predict salt effects up to 
the solubility limit of the respective salt (𝑚𝑠𝑎𝑡). Besides, the method should be tested 
for more complex salts and mixed solvents. 
For both VLEs and LLEs, an in-depth comparison between the eCPA here used and 
other approaches, e.g. ePC-SAFT or SAFT-VRE, would benefit the decision which 
additional interactions should be incorporated and gives hints about the importance of 
the Born term and the other contributions to predict elaborate systems in the future. 
Numerical issues have been experienced continuously during the calculations for both 
LLEs and VLEs. It is therefore a major part of future work to stabilize the underlying 
routines, like the calculation of fugacity coefficients. In addition, the methods for both 
phase equilibria are written in MATLAB. Including the routines into the underlying 
Fortran code and introducing a new MATLAB formalism similar to e.g. the 
TwoPhaseFlash will simplify future tasks. Additionally, the initialization of a system with 
or without salts is prone to errors since many inputs have to be implemented manually 
in MATLAB. Thus, to avoid false calculations, a simple interface for the import of 
components is preferable.  
The systems investigated to compare the parameter sets are limited to simple binary 
and ternary systems and only one elaborate SLE system has been utilized. Also, the 
salts were limited to simple, symmetric salts like NaCl. It was shown that for these 
simpler systems, the deviation between the sets is insignificant. Thus, especially the

solubility data. Bearing in mind the focus of this work, also phase equilibria should be
