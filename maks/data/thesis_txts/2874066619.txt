Abstract
Heat pumps are a key technology for decarbonizing building heating and cooling.
However, field studies show that 20–50% of installed systems operate below their
rated efficiency due to undetected faults. Common malfunctions such as heat
exchanger fouling, refrigerant leakage, and compressor valve degradation often
develop gradually and remain unnoticed until comfort complaints or equipment
failures occur. This gap between laboratory­rated performance and real­world
operation reduces both the environmental and economic benefits of heat pump
deployment, which is of particular concern as Europe aims to install 60 million
units by 2030.
This thesis presents the design, development, and validation of a laboratory heat
pump platform engineered for controlled fault emulation. It is designed at residen­
tial scale, enabling realistic operating conditions while allowing faults to be intro­
duced under controlled and repeatable circumstances. This platform addresses
the need for systematic fault studies without the risks or costs of inducing failures
in operational systems.
Four fault emulation methods were developed:liquid line restriction, evaporator
fouling, condenser fouling, and compressor valve leakage, and three were vali­
dated through thermodynamic modeling: evaporator fouling, condenser fouling,
and compressor valve leakage. Fouling was simulated by reducing the overall
heat transfer coefficient of the heat exchangers, while valve leakage was mod­
eled using a refrigerant bypass approach. The modeling framework incorporated
manufacturer data to ensure that component behavior remained realistic and rep­
resentative of residential heat pumps.
The validation showed that the emulated faults produced system responses con­
sistent with those reported in experimental studies. Evaporator fouling led to re­
duced cooling capacity and lower evaporation temperature, while condenser foul­
ing produced stronger effects in cooling than heating mode. Compressor valve
leakage caused simultaneous declines in heating and cooling capacity, distin­
guishing it from heat exchanger faults where impacts move in opposite directions.
These results confirm that the fault emulation methods provide realistic system
behavior and can support the development of diagnostic tools.
While the steady­state model proved effective for validation, it has clear limitations.
Future work should extend the approach into a digital twin by coupling the model
with the physical laboratory heat pump once constructed. A dynamic twin would
allow transient analysis, capture control system interactions, and generate rich
datasets for predictive maintenance and machine learning.
This research provides a validated framework for studying heat pump faults under
safe and controlled conditions. It demonstrates that low­charge propane systems
can combine sustainability with safety, and that systematic fault emulation can
replicate real­world degradation patterns. The platform offers a basis for develop­
ing diagnostic algorithms, predictive maintenance strategies, and optimized sen­
sor layouts, ultimately supporting more reliable and efficient heat pump operation
as deployment scales across Europe.

Introduction
1.1
Background and context
Heat pumps have emerged as essential technology for building decarbonization.
These systems move heat rather than generate it, achieving efficiencies of 300­
400% compared to 95% for gas boilers. The International Energy Agency (IEA)
reports that heat pump installations grew by 11% in 2022, with over 190 million
units operating worldwide. Europe leads this transition, with heat pump sales dou­
bling between 2019 and 2022 as countries phase out fossil fuel heating systems
[1].
The European Union targets a 55% reduction in greenhouse gas emissions by
2030 and climate neutrality by 2050 [2]. Meeting these goals requires installing
60 million heat pumps by 2030, up from 20 million today [1]. Denmark is a pioneer
in this shift, over 40% of Danish homes now use heat pumps for space heating,
and the government banned oil boilers in new buildings in 2013 [3]. This rapid
deployment makes system reliability and efficiency critical for maintaining public
confidence and achieving climate targets.
Field studies reveal a concerning gap between laboratory performance and real­
world operation. Research shows that 20­50% of installed heat pumps operate
at 70% efficiency or lower due to undetected faults [4]. Common problems in­
clude refrigerant leakage, heat exchanger fouling, and compressor valve degra­
dation. A fouled condenser can reduce system efficiency by 20­30% [5], while
refrigerant undercharge of 25%, increases energy consumption by up to 20% [6].
These faults develop gradually, making them difficult to detect through conven­
tional maintenance approaches.
The economic impact extends beyond energy waste. Undetected faults shorten
equipment lifespan, increase repair costs, and cause comfort complaints that
damage consumer confidence [7]. A residential heat pump costs around 50,000­
100,000 DKK to replace, and unexpected failures during peak heating or cooling
periods can cause significant disruption. Commercial systems face even higher
stakes, a failed heat pump in a data center or hospital can trigger cascading fail­
ures worth millions in damages and lost productivity [8].
Current fault detection methods rely on periodic maintenance checks and user
complaints. Technicians visit sites on fixed schedules, often finding no problems
while missing developing issues. When users notice problems, significant dam­
age has often already occurred. This reactive approach wastes resources and
fails to prevent the performance degradation that undermines energy savings.
Modern heat pumps need diagnostic capabilities that detect faults before they
cause noticeable problems [9].
Predictive maintenance offers a solution by monitoring system parameters to iden­
tify degradation patterns before failures occur. Temperature sensors track heat

exchanger performance, pressure measurements reveal refrigerant issues, and
power monitoring detects compressor problems [8]. However, developing effec­
tive diagnostic algorithms requires understanding how faults manifest in system
behavior. Researchers need controlled environments where they can introduce
specific faults and observe their effects without damaging expensive equipment
or disrupting building operations.
Laboratory fault emulation provides this controlled environment. Rather than wait­
ing for components to fail naturally, researchers can simulate degradation through
parameter adjustments. Reducing heat transfer coefficients mimics fouling, by­
passing refrigerant flow replicates valve leakage, and adding artificial pressure
drop in the liquid line simulates blockage [10]. These methods generate the data
needed to train diagnostic algorithms while maintaining safe operating conditions.
The transition to natural refrigerants adds complexity to this challenge. Tradi­
tional refrigerants like R410A face phase­out due to high global warming poten­
tial. Propane (R290) offers excellent thermodynamic properties and negligible cli­
mate impact, but requires careful handling due to flammability [11]. Systems must
minimize charge quantities while maintaining performance, and fault detection be­
comes critical for identifying leaks before they create safety hazards. Laboratory
testing with low­charge propane systems helps develop safe operational practices
for this sustainable refrigerant [12].
The Digital Heat Pump Laboratory at DTU provides an ideal platform for advancing
fault detection research. This facility combines physical test systems with digital
modeling capabilities, enabling researchers to validate fault emulation methods
against real equipment responses. The laboratory heat pump designed in this
thesis will serve as a reference system for developing diagnostic algorithms ap­
plicable to residential installations.
This project addresses the gap between theoretical fault studies and practical
diagnostic implementation. By creating validated fault emulation methods for a
low­charge propane heat pump, the thesis enables systematic development of
detection algorithms without the costs and risks of inducing actual component fail­
ures. The resulting diagnostic capabilities will help maintain heat pump efficiency
throughout their operational life, ensuring these systems deliver their promised
environmental and economic benefits.
1.2
Aim of the thesis
The aim of the thesis was to design and develop a laboratory heat pump for
fault emulation methods, using a sustainable refrigerant, while staying under 150
grams of total refrigerant charge volume. The thesis addresses the need for con­
trolled fault reproduction in controlled environments to advance fault detection and
diagnosis capabilities in residential heat pump applications.
The main objective was to design a system that accurately represents the char­
acteristics and operational behavior of the most common residential heat pump
configurations. This system was then altered to emulate the most common faults
occurring in such systems, in controlled conditions while maintaining realistic sys­

To validate the effectiveness of the proposed emulation approaches, thermody­
namic models of the following fault types were developed using Engineering Equa­
tion Solver (EES): evaporator fouling, condenser fouling and compressor valve
leakage. The modeling approach examines whether the theoretical fault emula­
tion methods produce system responses that accurately replicate the effects of
actual component degradation.
The validation method consisted of comparing the modeled fault emulation results
with existing data from fault studies documented through literature. This compari­
son will verify that the proposed emulation methods generate fault behavior in line
with observed behavior in other studies. The validation approach ensures that the
laboratory­implemented fault emulation techniques will provide meaningful and
representative data for fault detection and diagnosis algorithm development.
Through achieving these objectives, the thesis contributes to the advancement of
fault detection and diagnosis methodologies by providing validated approaches
for controlled fault generation in laboratory environments. The research enables
more effective development and testing of diagnostic algorithms while reducing
the risks and costs associated with inducing actual faults in experimental sys­
tems. The validated emulation methods support the broader goal of improving
heat pump system reliability and maintenance practices through enhanced fault
detection capabilities.

1.4.1
Most common faults in heat pump systems
Heat pump systems are complex thermodynamic systems consisting of multiple
components that must operate in harmony to achieve optimal performance. Over
their operational period, various faults can develop that significantly impact sys­
tem efficiency, capacity, and reliability. Understanding these faults, their causes,
and their effects on system performance is essential for developing effective fault
detection and diagnosis strategies.

Refrigerant overcharge
Refrigerant overcharge occurs when the system contains more refrigerant than
specified by the manufacturer, typically resulting from improper commissioning
procedures or maintenance errors. This fault can increase subcooling values sig­
nificantly and degrade the coefficient of performance (COP) [13]. In some cases,
for fault levels below 20%, overcharge may actually increase the heating or cool­
ing capacity of the heat pump, though this depends on the specific characteristics
of each system [13].
The primary effect of refrigerant overcharge is the accumulation of liquid refriger­
ant in the condenser, which reduces the effective heat transfer area available for
condensation. This leads to increased condensing pressure and temperature, as
more refrigerant occupies space that should be used for heat rejection [10]. The
elevated condensing pressure forces the compressor to work against a higher
pressure differential, increasing power consumption and reducing overall system
efficiency.In variable­speed heat pump systems, the effects of overcharge can be
more drastic. Studies have shown that overcharged systems may experience un­
stable operation at part­load conditions, with the compressor speed control strug­
gling to maintain setpoints [14]. The excess refrigerant can cause liquid slugging
in the compressor during transient operations, potentially leading to mechanical
damage if severe enough.
Detection of refrigerant overcharge typically relies on monitoring subcooling tem­
peratures, which increase proportionally with the degree of overcharge. However,
in systems equipped with liquid receivers, the effects of moderate overcharge may
be masked until the receiver capacity is exceeded [10]. This makes early detec­
tion challenging without dedicated monitoring strategies.
The long­term consequences of operating with refrigerant overcharge include in­
creased wear on compressor components due to higher operating pressures,
reduced heat exchanger effectiveness, and elevated energy consumption.
In
heat pumps using natural refrigerants like propane, which have smaller charge
quantities, the system may be more sensitive to overcharge conditions, with stud­
ies showing that overcharge levels 20% the nominal charge volume can prevent
proper system operation entirely [14].
Refrigerant undercharge
Refrigerant undercharge, often resulting from system leakage or improper initial
charging, is identified as the most frequently occurring fault in heat pump sys­
tems [15]. This fault has significant environmental implications beyond perfor­
mance degradation, as refrigerant leakage contributes to greenhouse gas emis­
sions when using HFC refrigerants, or poses safety risks with natural refrigerants
like ammonia or propane [16].
The performance impact of refrigerant undercharge is characterized by a reduc­
tion in both heating/cooling capacity and COP. Studies have shown that a 10%
reduction in refrigerant charge volume can lead to approximately 3% reduction
in COP, while a 40% reduction of nominal volume can decrease COP by up to
45% [16]. The relationship between charge level and performance is non­linear,
with more severe degradation occurring as the undercharge level increases. Un­

dercharge primarily affects the evaporator’s operation, where insufficient refrig­
erant leads to incomplete utilization of the heat transfer surface. This results in
increased superheat at the evaporator outlet, reduced evaporating temperature,
and decreased refrigerant mass flow rate [10]. For systems with thermostatic
expansion valves (TXV), the valve attempts to maintain superheat setpoint by re­
ducing its opening, further intensifying the capacity reduction.
The subcooling at the condenser outlet serves as a primary indicator of refrigerant
charge level. As charge decreases, subcooling approaches zero, eventually re­
sulting in two­phase refrigerant entering the expansion device [10]. This condition
severely impairs the expansion valve’s ability to control refrigerant flow leading to
hunting behavior and system instability.
Variable­speed heat pumps show unique responses when it comes to under­
charge conditions. The compressor speed increases in an attempt to maintain
capacity, but this compensation has limits. At severe undercharge levels, the in­
creased speed cannot overcome the thermodynamic penalties, and the system
may cycle frequently or fail to meet load requirements [14]. The variable­speed
operation can sometimes mask moderate undercharge conditions, making detec­
tion more challenging than in fixed­speed systems.
Early detection of refrigerant leakage is crucial for maintaining system perfor­
mance and preventing environmental damage. Modern approaches utilize re­
frigerant charge sensors that estimate charge level based on multiple system
measurements, enabling continuous monitoring without requiring direct charge
measurement [13].
Liquid line restriction
Liquid line restriction typically occurs due to the filter dryer clogging, moisture
freezing, or debris accumulation in the liquid line between the condenser and the
expansion device. This fault creates an additional pressure drop that affects the
refrigerant flow and system pressures [14]. The restriction acts as a pre­expansion
of the refrigerant, reducing the effective capacity of the expansion device.
The immediate effect of liquid line restriction is a reduction in refrigerant mass flow
rate, as the combined resistance of the restriction and the expansion valve limits
flow. This leads to refrigerant accumulation in the condenser, increasing con­
densing pressure and subcooling [17]. The liquid line temperature drops across
the restriction point due to the partial expansion, which can be detected through
temperature measurements along the liquid line.
In systems with electronic expansion valves (EEV), the control algorithm attempts
to compensate by opening the valve further. However, once the valve reaches
its maximum opening, no further compensation is possible, and system capacity
degrades rapidly [14]. Studies have shown that even moderate restrictions, which
result in an additional pressure drop of 20% can reduce COP by 5­10%, with more
severe restrictions causing proportionally greater degradation.
The fault’s impact varies with operating conditions and load. At lower loads, the
reduced refrigerant flow may still meet demand, masking the fault’s presence.
However, at higher loads, the restriction prevents the system from delivering re­

quired capacity, leading to extended run times and increased energy consumption
[10]. Variable­speed systems may increase compressor speed to maintain capac­
ity, but this compensation increases power consumption disproportionately to the
capacity gain, firther worsening the COP values.
Diagnosis of liquid line restriction requires monitoring pressure drop across the
liquid line or measuring the change of temperature, which would indicate partial
expansion through the liquid line. Advanced FDD systems use model­based ap­
proaches to estimate expected pressure drops and alert at deviations [18]. The
challenge lies in distinguishing liquid line restriction from other faults that cause
similar symptoms, such as undercharge or expansion valve malfunction.
Long­term operation with liquid line restriction can lead to compressor overheating
due to reduced refrigerant flow, increased wear from extended operation at high
pressure ratios, and potential compressor failure if liquid refrigerant fails to reach
the expansion device during low­load conditions.
Compressor valve leakage
Compressor valve leakage represents internal bypass of refrigerant from the dis­
charge to suction side within the compressor, essentially reducing the overall re­
frigerant mass flow circulating in the refrigerant cycle. This fault can occur due to
worn valve plates, damaged valve seats, or in the case of systems with reversing
valves, leakage through the four­way valve [13]. The consequence is a reduction
in the net refrigerant flow delivered to the system while maintaining or increasing
power consumption.
The primary symptom of valve leakage is increased suction pressure and temper­
ature, as high­pressure gas mixes with the suction gas. This reduces the pressure
ratio across the compressor and decreases the temperature difference available
for heat transfer in both the evaporator and the condenser [10]. The mixing of dis­
charge gas with suction gas also increases the specific volume at the compressor
inlet, further reducing mass flow capacity.
Studies have demonstrated that valve leakage has one of the most severe impacts
on the system’s COP among common faults. Even moderate leakage levels of 10­
20% can reduce COP by 15­25%, with severe leakage potentially reducing COP
by over 50% [14]. The degradation is particularly pronounced in variable­speed
systems, where the compressor speed increases to compensate for lost capacity,
but the additional power consumption exceeds the benefit of increased speed.
The fault will show different symptoms depending on system design and operating
conditions. In systems with hot gas bypass for capacity control, distinguishing be­

flow and repeated compression of leaked gas can trigger safety limits, particularly
in variable­speed compressors, where speed increase worsens the temperature
rise.

between compressor power consumption and delivered capacity, as well as track­

Evaporator fouling
Evaporator fouling includes problems that reduce heat transfer effectiveness, in­
cluding dust accumulation on air­side surfaces, biological growth, scale formation
on water­side surfaces, or ice formation in low­temperature applications. This fault
gradually degrades heat transfer by adding thermal resistance and restricting flow
passages [13].
The fouling process typically begins with particles depositing on the leading edges
of heat exchanger surfaces, gradually spreading to cover larger areas. In air­to­
refrigerant evaporators, fouling primarily affects the first rows of tubes, creating
non­uniform air flow distribution that further complicates the problem [19]. The
accumulated fouling layer acts as insulation, requiring larger temperature differ­
ences to maintain heat transfer rates.
Performance degradation due to evaporator fouling shows as reduced evaporat­
ing temperature and pressure, as the system attempts to maintain capacity with
degraded heat transfer [10]. This lower evaporating temperature increases the
compression ratio, requiring more compressor work per unit of cooling capacity.
Studies indicate that moderate fouling, reducing heat transfer area by 20­30%,
can decrease COP by 10­15%, although the impact on capacity may be less se­
vere than on efficiency.
Variable­speed systems respond to evaporator fouling by increasing compressor
speed to maintain setpoint temperatures. This compensation masks the fault’s
early stages but results in gradually increasing energy consumption [14]. The
fouling’s impact is particularly severe during high­load conditions when the evap­
orator operates near its maximum capacity.
Water­side fouling in water­source heat pumps presents additional challenges,
as biofilm formation can occur rapidly under set conditions. The combination of
organic nutrients, moderate temperatures, and water flow creates ideal conditions
for biological growth, which can severely degrade performance within weeks if
untreated [16].
Detection methods for evaporator fouling include monitoring the temperature dif­
ference between inlet and outlet streams, tracking the overall heat transfer coef­
ficient (UA value), or measuring pressure drop across the heat exchanger.
Advanced approaches use model­based estimation of expected performance and
alert deviations indicative of fouling [20]. Regular maintenance and cleaning are
essential preventive measures, but the optimal cleaning frequency depends on
environmental conditions and fouling severity.

Condenser fouling
Condenser fouling shares similar mechanisms with evaporator fouling but occurs
on the heat rejection side of the system. Common causes include dust and debris
accumulation on air­cooled condensers, scale formation in water­cooled systems,
and oil accumulation on refrigerant­side surfaces [13]. The fouling can reduce
heat transfer effectiveness and may restrict flow passages, leading to elevated
condensing pressures and temperatures.
The performance impact of condenser fouling is characterized by increased con­
densing pressure as the system struggles to reject heat through the fouled sur­
faces. This elevated pressure increases compressor power consumption and re­
duces the temperature lift capability of the system [10]. Unlike evaporator fouling,
which mainly affects capacity, condenser fouling significantly impacts both capac­
ity and efficiency.
In air­cooled condensers, fouling often occurs non­uniformly, with higher depo­
sition rates in areas of low air velocity or where the air flow creates recircula­
tion zones. This non­uniform fouling can create hot spots that further elevate lo­
cal condensing temperatures [19]. The progressive nature of fouling means that
performance degradation accelerates over time as the fouling layer thickness in­
creases. Studies have shown that condenser fouling, which reduces the effective
heat transfer area by 25%, can increase power consumption by 20­30% while re­
ducing capacity by 10­15% [20]. The impact is particularly severe in high ambient
temperature conditions when the condenser already operates near its design lim­
its. Variable­speed systems may reach compressor speed limits trying to maintain
capacity, leading to unmet load conditions.
Water­cooled condensers face unique fouling challenges from scale formation due
to dissolved minerals in the cooling water. The elevated temperatures on the
heat transfer surface promote precipitation of minerals, creating a hard scale that
is difficult to remove [16]. Chemical water treatment and regular cleaning are
essential for maintaining performance in these systems.
Detection of condenser fouling relies on monitoring condensing temperature and
pressure trends relative to heat rejection load and ambient conditions. Approaches
comparing actual performance to expected performance based on manufacturer
data or physical models can identify degradation before it severely impacts oper­
ation [18]. Preventive maintenance schedules should account for local environ­
mental conditions that affect fouling rates.
Other common faults
Beyond the primary faults discussed above, heat pump systems experience vari­
ous other failures that impact performance and reliability. Non­condensable gases
in the refrigerant circuit, resulting from improper evacuation during installation or
air ingress through leaks, can significantly degrade performance by increasing
condensing pressure and reducing heat transfer effectiveness [13]. Studies indi­
cate this fault can have the largest impact on system performance among common
faults, yet it often goes undetected without specific monitoring.
Expansion valve malfunctions, including stuck valves, hunting behavior, or incor­
rect superheat control, disrupt the refrigerant flow control, which is essential for

efficient operation. These faults may result from mechanical wear, contamination,
or control system errors [18]. The symptoms vary depending on the failure mode
but typically include unstable operation, inability to maintain superheat setpoint,
or complete loss of flow control.
Secondary system faults affecting pumps, fans, and control systems represent a
significant category of failures in heat pump installations. Circulation pump fail­
ures in water­source systems immediately impact heat transfer, while fan motor
failures in air­source systems create similar effects [13]. Variable­speed drives
for these components add complexity but also enable detection through power
consumption monitoring.
Sensor faults, including bias, drift, and complete failure, can cause improper con­

parameters, or mismatched components. These issues may not show up as dis­

[16]. Proper commissioning procedures and performance verification are essen­

The interaction between multiple simultaneous faults complicates diagnosis and
impact assessment. For example, refrigerant undercharge combined with evapo­
rator fouling creates compounding effects that exceed the sum of individual fault
impacts. Advanced FDD systems should account for these interactions to provide
accurate diagnosis and appropriate maintenance recommendations.

Engineering utilization of fault emulation
Fault emulation provides a systematic approach to understand how heat pump
and refrigeration systems behave under degraded conditions. It is done by using
controlled fault introduction to develop fault detection and diagnosis algorithms
and optimize control strategies for enhanced system stability. This methodology
is essential for advancing the reliability and performance of vapor compression
systems.
Performance degradation analysis
Performance degradation analysis through fault emulation reveals how compo­
nent malfunctions affect overall system efficiency, quantifying the impact of vari­
ous fault conditions on critical performance indicators such as COP, cooling ca­
pacity, and energy consumption.
Experimental investigations of variable­speed vapor compression systems have
revealed distinct performance characteristics under fault conditions [10]. Through
artificial implementation of compressor faults, condenser faults, evaporator faults,
and refrigerant leakage, studies demonstrate that variable­speed systems expe­
rience more severe COP degradation compared to constant­speed counterparts.
These findings establish correlations between fault severity levels and perfor­
mance indicators, enabling us to optimize system designs with appropriate safety
margins.
Recent advances in soft fault analysis for residential heat pumps highlight the
potential of IoT technologies for continuous performance monitoring [19]. Field
measurements reveal that simultaneous faults can induce performance degrada­
tion exceeding 5%, with fault identification achieved through thermodynamic cycle
analysis. The relationship between measurement uncertainty and fault detectabil­
ity is a crucial factor for practical implementation, particularly when multiple soft
faults interact within the system.
Quantitative studies establish specific degradation patterns for common fault types.
Heat exchanger fouling typically reduces COP by 10­13%, while severe refriger­
ant leakage can reduce cooling capacity by up to 30% [20]. These experimen­
tal relationships serve multiple engineering purposes: establishing performance­
based fault detection thresholds, determining appropriate design margins for ex­
pected degradation, developing predictive maintenance schedules based on per­
formance trends, and validating simulation models through experimental correla­
tion.
Analysis of large­scale heat pump systems identifies critical failure points in com­
pressors, evaporators, and source side heat exchangers [16]. Fouling and re­
frigerant leakage emerge as the most common issues affecting long­term perfor­
mance, with degradation patterns varying significantly based on system configu­
ration and operating conditions. Understanding these patterns through controlled
fault emulation enables engineers to design more resilient systems capable of
maintaining acceptable performance despite component degradation.

Development and validation of fault detection and diagnosis (FDD)
algorithms
The development of FDD algorithms relies fundamentally on comprehensive fault
emulation to generate training and validation datasets. Controlled fault scenarios
provide the necessary data diversity for algorithm optimization while maintaining
known ground truth conditions for accuracy assessment.
Advanced diagnosis systems show the effectiveness of systematic fault emulation
in algorithm development [18]. Through extensive test bench experiments, clus­
ters can be established, enabling accurate classification of single, gradual, and
simultaneous faults. These systems achieve high diagnostic accuracy using min­
imal sensor configurations, demonstrating the value of well­designed diagnosis
protocols.
FDD methods follow three primary categories, each requiring specific fault emula­
tion approaches [13]. Quantitative model­based methods utilize detailed physical
models validated against experimental fault data. These approaches require fault
emulation to verify model predictions across the full operating range. Polynomial
reference models tested against multiple fault types with varying severity levels
illustrate this method [17].
Qualitative model­based approaches, including rule­based and expert systems,
depend on pattern recognition from systematic fault emulation. Statistical FDD
methods utilizing temperature measurements have shown effective fault detec­
tion through residual analysis [20]. Directional residual patterns obtained from
controlled experiments form the base for rule­based diagnostics.
Process history­based methods require extensive fault datasets for training black­
box and grey­box models. Data­driven approaches achieve high accuracy only
when trained on comprehensive fault scenarios spanning various operating con­
ditions [13]. The quality and diversity of emulated fault data directly influence the
robustness of resulting FDD algorithms.
Validation metrics for FDD algorithms include detection accuracy, false positive
rates, false negative rates, diagnostic accuracy for specific fault identification,
and response time between fault occurrence and detection. Modern validation
frameworks incorporate both physical fault emulation and virtual fault modeling to
ensure comprehensive algorithm testing [13].
The distinction between deterministic and stochastic fault simulation approaches
has gained recognition in recent research. While deterministic emulation provides
repeatability for algorithm development, stochastic approaches better represent
real­world fault progression patterns [13]. Combining both methodologies leads
to more robust FDD systems capable of handling the uncertainty in actual field
conditions.
Standardization efforts in fault emulation procedures facilitate meaningful com­
parison between different FDD approaches. Addressing inconsistencies in fault
level definitions, especially for fouling conditions where laboratory studies often
exceed realistic field scenarios, remains an ongoing challenge.

2.3
Overview of heat pump technology
Heat pumps are devices that transfer thermal energy from a heat source to a
heat sink using a refrigeration cycle, which has become one of the most efficient
technologies for heating and cooling applications [18]. The fundamental principle
relies on the vapor compression cycle, where a working fluid, in the case of heat
pumps a refrigerant, undergoes phase changes to absorb and release heat at
different temperature levels [21].
Throughout this process, heat pumps remove heat from the ambient environment,
a low­temperature heat source and elevate it to a useful temperature, where it is
supplied through the heat sink.
The refrigerant flows through the cycle and changes phase as it moves through
each component. To undergo a phase change, energy must be added or removed.
When this happens at constant pressure, the refrigerant absorbs or releases heat
at a constant temperature until it is fully evaporated or condensed. This energy
is referred to as the latent heat of evaporation or condensation. In the evapora­
tor, the refrigerant absorbs heat from the source as it evaporates, including any
heat required for superheating. Once fully evaporated, it enters the compres­
sor, where its pressure and temperature are increased by mechanical work. The

==PAGE:27==
refrigerant then passes through the condenser, releasing heat to the sink. This
includes the latent heat of condensation as well as any heat removed through de­
superheating and subcooling of the fluidum. After condensation, the refrigerant
expands through the expansion valve at constant enthalpy, reducing its pressure
to match that of the evaporator, and the cycle repeats.[20].
Modern heat pumps can be classified based on their heat source and sink combi­
nations, including air­to­air, air­to­water, water­to­water, and brine­to­water (ground
source) systems. Ground source heat pumps, which use brine as a heat transfer
fluid in ground loops, are particularly attractive due to their stable operating con­
ditions and high efficiency throughout the year [20]. These systems have gained
significant market share in Europe, and predictions pointing to an increase greater
than 200% by 2030 [15].

The state points
The red dots in Figure 2.1 represent the thermodynamic state points of the cy­

subcooling, there are four main state points. This part of the thesis is merely for
explanation and it will focus on the reasoning used to identify and understand each
point in the cycle, not the exact properties. To define a thermodynamic state, two
independent properties must be specified. In the two­phase region, pressure and
temperature cannot serve as independent variables because they are interdepen­
dent. Instead pressure and enthalpy or pressure and vapor quality can be used,
as these pairs are independent and sufficient to define the state.
State 1
The first state point is located at the evaporator outlet, just before the refrigerant
enters the compressor. The evaporation temperature is selected to ensure an
adequate temperature difference between the heat source and the refrigerant.

evaporation temperature, expressed as P1 = Psat(Tevap). If the refrigerant is not
superheated, the state is defined by setting the vapor quality to x1 = 1, indicating
saturated vapor. However, if superheating occurs, the temperature at this point is
adjusted to account for the superheat, such that T1 = Tevap + ∆TSH

determine the enthalpy at state point 2.

2.3.9
Environmental and regulatory aspects
Propane offers significant environmental advantages over conventional synthetic
refrigerants. It has zero ozone depletion potential (ODP) and an extremely low
GWP of 3 kg CO2 equivalent per kilogram, compared to many currently used
refrigerants with GWP values exceeding 1000. This low environmental impact
positions propane as an excellent alternative in the context of EU Regulation No
517/2014 on fluorinated greenhouse gases, which introduces a program to reduce
emissions by 2030 and imposes a drastic phase­down of HFCs starting from 2015
[23].
The regulatory framework for propane use in heat pump applications is governed
by several key standards and safety considerations. The primary safety concern
relates to propane’s flammability classification as an A3 refrigerant according to
EN378­1­2008. Flammability and toxicity represent the two main safety parame­
ters during fluid handling. Propane exhibits a lower flammable limit (LFL) of 2.2%
by volume and an upper flammable limit (UFL) of 10% by volume, with an auto­
ignition temperature of 470°C [23].
EN378­1­2008 classifies buildings into three categories based on occupation con­
ditions, with refrigerant charge limits varying by building type and safety require­
ments. For direct systems in occupied spaces, the refrigerant charge is limited to
1.5 kg for general occupation categories (residential, hotels, schools) and 2.5 kg
for supervised occupancy premises such as office buildings. These charge limita­
tions significantly influence system design and component optimization strategies
[24].
International Electrotechnical Commission (IEC) standards provide specific guide­
lines for propane use in household appliances. IEC 60335­2­24 and IEC 60335­
2­89 allow hydrocarbon use if the refrigerant charge remains below 150 g for
hermetically­sealed systems where every connection is soldered or brazed, in this
project the refrigerant charge will be maximized at this level to adhere to these
regulations. This rule enables hydrocarbon use in very low capacity refrigerat­
ing devices or heat pumps, while middle­capacity systems require more complex
safety considerations [25] [23].
The choice of heat exchanger technology affects a heat pump system’s need for
refrigerant charge. The total amount of refrigerant needed to fill the system is
directly affected by the internal volumes and flow geometries of various designs,
such as finned­tube, microchannel, and brazed­plate heat exchangers. Reduc­
ing the refrigerant charge is a crucial safety and legal factor for systems that are
charged with flammable refrigerants, like the one in the project. New generations
of heat exchangers with better heat transfer performance and smaller internal vol­
umes are being developed to address this. Microchannel heat exchangers and
brazed­plate heat exchangers are two examples. Low­charge heat pumps allow
for a reduction in refrigerant volume without sacrificing efficiency, which is made
possible by these technologies.

Design and modeling process
This section outlines the methodology used for the design and modeling process.
It begins with the specifications and assumptions that defined the starting point
of the project. It then proceeds through the different models, beginning with the
design model, continuing with the specification of system components, and con­
cluding with the off­design model, which incorporates fault emulations.
2.4.1
System specifications
The design process began with defining the system size and selecting the refrig­
erant, as discussed earlier. The original objective was to design a heat pump with
a heating capacity of about 8 kW, reflecting the typical size for residential appli­
cations. Due to the safety concerns associated with propane, the target capacity
was reduced to approximately 4 kW, when selecting the compressor, but thanks
to the actual running conditions the final capacity finalized in roughly 6,5 kW. This
capacity remains representative of smaller residential heat pumps and of systems
operating under part­load conditions, ensuring the design remains relevant to the
intended application.
Next, the water­side inlet and outlet temperatures were set for both the condenser
and evaporator. These values reflect typical operating conditions of a water­to­
water heat pump, where the heat source may be geothermal brine or wastewater,
and the outlet temperature corresponds to a domestic hot water supply.
To establish the design conditions, assumptions were made regarding the nominal
compressor speed, volumetric efficiency, and isentropic efficiency. Fixed subcool­
ing and superheating values were also introduced, which allowed the evaporation
and condensation temperatures to be defined for the design model.
2.4.2
Design model
The design model establishes the baseline of the heat pump system under nom­
inal conditions. Its purpose is to provide a consistent reference for the selection
of components for the system. With the design model, key parameters such as
cylinder displacement, evaporation temperature, and condensation temperature
were determined. By defining the steady­state operation of each component in the
vapor compression cycle, the model ensures, that the component selections were
correct for the project. At the system level, the model uses the same methodology

The selection of the heat pump components provided the basis for incorporating

of isentropic efficiency, volumetric efficiency, mass flow rate, and system capacity
to be based on real component performance rather than assumed values. With
these refinements, the model transitioned from an idealized design framework to
an off­design representation that captures the behavior of the actual components
under realistic operating conditions.
2.4.5
Fault emulation
After completing the design model, the next step was to determine how the se­
lected faults could be represented within the EES framework. One limitation of
the software is that it does not allow direct modeling of refrigerant charge mass,
which meant that undercharge and overcharge faults could not be implemented.
However, fault scenarios such as evaporator fouling, condenser fouling, and com­
pressor valve leakage were successfully incorporated into the model through pa­
rameter adjustments and by adding additional state points. The implementation
meant that the fault emulation methods could be validated against real fault data.
Fault selection and explanation
The fault selection process aimed to identify the most relevant malfunctions to
include in the heat pump design. Faults were selected based on emulation feasi­
bility and their frequency of occurrence in field installations.
The selected faults include both hard faults causing immediate system perfor­
mance degradation and soft faults resulting in gradual performance degradation.
Refrigerant charge faults (both overcharge and undercharge) were included due
to their frequent occurrence and significant performance impact. Heat exchanger
fouling represents a common degradation mechanism affecting long­term system
efficiency. Liquid line restriction simulates blockages from moisture, debris, or

filter clogging. Compressor valve leakage addresses internal component wear
affecting overall system performance.
Each fault type was characterized by specific implementation parameters. Refrig­
erant charge faults are quantified as a percentage deviation from nominal charge.
Fouling faults are defined by heat transfer area reduction. Liquid line restriction is
characterized by additional pressure drop relative to normal operation. Compres­

Evaporator and Condenser fouling
Evaporator and condenser fouling were emulated by reducing the effective UA
value on the water side of the heat exchangers. In practical terms, this will be
achieved by introducing a restriction, a piston, into the water side to lower the heat
transfer area, therefore replicate the effects of scaling or surface degradation of
the heat exchanger. In EES, this was modeled by directly reducing the UA values
of the heat exchangers. The resulting changes in system performance were then
analyzed to evaluate the impact of fouling under different operating conditions.
Compressor valve leakage
Compressor valve leakage was emulated by introducing a bypass line around
the compressor. In practice, this bypass is equipped with a safety ball valve and
handle, allowing the bypass ratio to be manually adjusted. The uncompressed
refrigerant passing through this line replicates the effect of internal valve leak­
age, where part of the gas flow avoids compression. This approach captures the
symptom of the fault: reduced effective compression due to leakage across the
valve.
In EES, the fault was represented by adding four additional thermodynamic states:
two located before the bypass and compressor, and two after. This structure en­
abled the division of mass flow between the main cycle and the bypass line. An
additional energy balance was introduced to ensure conservation of mass and
energy across the two flow paths. By adjusting the bypass ratio, the severity of
the simulated leakage could be varied, allowing the model to reproduce different
levels of compressor degradation.

Fault interaction considerations
Real­world heat pump systems often experience multiple simultaneous faults,
making consideration of fault interactions necessary. The methods address both
independent fault effects and interactions between faults that may amplify or mask
individual fault symptoms.
Fault interaction considers temporal aspects, distinguishing between simultane­
ous fault occurrence and sequential fault development. Simultaneous faults might
occur during improper commissioning, such as incorrect refrigerant charge com­
bined with restricted flow. Sequential faults develop over time, such as refrigerant
leakage leading to increased compressor wear due to inadequate cooling. The
design enables the investigation of fault interactions systematically.
Interaction effects should be quantified through performance metric deviations
exceeding the sum of individual fault impacts. Mutual interactions amplify per­
formance degradation, while negative interactions may partially compensate for
individual fault effects.

Monitoring hardware
The measurement hardware aimed to monitor all essential system parameters

ficiency, and heat exchanger effectiveness. Additionally, the selected sensor ar­
rangement provides the necessary data inputs for fault detection algorithms.

3.3
Fault emulation strategy (how faults are
integrated into the design)
3.3.1
Liquid line restriction emulation
As shown in the PID diagram, the liquid line restriction emulation is implemented
through the placement of a ball valve positioned downstream of the thermostatic
expansion valve. This configuration allows for controlled simulation of liquid line
blockage conditions that commonly occur in real­world heat pump installations
due to debris accumulation, ice formation, or component degradation.
In the fully open position, the ball valve presents minimal flow resistance, allowing
the system to operate under nominal design conditions with unrestricted refriger­
ant flow through the liquid line. The fault emulation is achieved by incrementally
closing the ball valve, which progressively increases the flow restriction in the liq­
uid line. This controlled restriction mimics the development of blockages that typi­
cally occur in field installations, where partial obstructions develop over time rather
than sudden complete blockages. The incremental adjustment capability enables
systematic study of how varying degrees of liquid line restriction affect system
performance metrics such as capacity, efficiency, and operating pressures.
The positioning of the valve is important, it needs to be placed 10 pipe diameters
before the expansion valve and after the filter dryer. This way it emulates the
fault closest to the real life scenario, when the filter dryer is blocked or degraded
overtime.
A close up of the solution can be seen in Figure 3.13.
Figure 3.13: Liquid line restriction emulation
3.3.2
Compressor valve leakage emulation
As introduced in the PID diagram too, the compressor valve leakage fault is em­
ulated through the implementation of a dedicated bypass line equipped with a
ball valve. This bypass configuration connects the high­pressure discharge line
Developing a laboratory heat pump for emulation of faults
49
==PAGE:57==
directly to the low­pressure suction line, creating a controlled path for refrigerant
to bypass the normal compression process. The bypass line diameter must be
the same as the suction line in order to prevent unnecessary restriction of the
diameter.
When the ball valve is fully closed, the bypass line remains inactive, ensuring that
all refrigerant flow passes through the compressor’s normal compression cycle
without any leakage paths. This closed position represents the baseline operating
condition where the compressor valves are functioning properly and maintaining
complete separation between the high and low­pressure sides of the system.
The fault emulation is achieved by gradually opening the ball valve, which allows
increasing amounts of high­pressure refrigerant to flow directly from the discharge
line to the suction line circulating the compressor. This controlled bypass flow sim­
ulates the effect of compressor valve leakage, where damaged or worn discharge
valves, suction valves, or internal compressor components allow hot refrigerant
to leak into the low­pressure side of the system.
This emulation technique replicates the thermodynamic effects of real compressor
valve leakage, including decreased system capacity and COP. The progressive
nature of the valve opening allows for systematic investigation of how varying
degrees of internal leakage affect overall system performance. The bypass ar­
rangement provides a safe and reversible method to study this fault type without
causing actual damage to the compressor, while generating the characteristic per­
formance signatures that would be observed in systems with genuine compressor
valve degradation.

3.3.3
Heat exchanger effective area reduction
The heat exchanger UA value degradation will be emulated through the implemen­
tation of adjustable pistons positioned on the water side of both the condenser and
evaporator heat exchangers. This approach will provide precise control over the
effective heat transfer area, enabling accurate simulation of fouling conditions that
commonly occur in real­world heat pump installations.
When the pistons are fully retracted to their maximum withdrawn position, the wa­
ter flow remains essentially unobstructed, allowing full utilization of the designed
heat transfer surface area. In this configuration, the heat exchangers operate at
their nominal UA values representing normal conditions that correspond to optimal
system performance and maximum heat transfer effectiveness.
The fault emulation is achieved by progressively advancing the pistons into differ­
ent positions within the heat exchanger water passages. As the piston position
changes, the effective heat transfer area available for thermal exchange between
the refrigerant and water streams is systematically reduced. This controlled area
reduction directly impacts the overall UA value of the heat exchanger. The pis­
ton’s position will be held in place by a Swagelok tube fitting and a proprietary
plastic piece, so the system does not leak at the adjustment point.
This emulation technique accurately replicates the thermodynamic effects of var­
ious fouling mechanisms including scale buildup, biological growth, particulate
accumulation, and corrosion deposits that naturally occur on heat exchanger sur­
faces over time. The adjustability of the piston allows for precise investigation of
how different severities of fouling affect system performance parameters such as
heat transfer rates, temperature approaches, and overall system efficiency.

Fault modeling in EES
After establishing the operational model and presenting the results, this model
served as the baseline for fault emulation in EES. The operational model was
then gradually modified to incorporate various fault conditions, and the resulting
fault model outputs were compared against the baseline model results to quantify
the impact of each fault type.
This approach enables clear identification of performance deviations caused by
fault conditions. The baseline model provides the reference point representing
normal system operation, while the fault­modified models demonstrate how in­
dividual malfunctions affect key performance parameters such as capacity, effi­
ciency, and operating conditions.

Fault severity introduction
The compressor valve leakage fault is emulated through a bypass valve config­

fault severity parameter ’y’ represents the fraction of the total mass flow (msum)

that worsens system performance. For instance, at y = 0,05, 5% of the refriger­

(mcycle = 0, 01767kg/s) participates in the actual heat transfer process. The to­

but the effective refrigerant flow available for the thermodynamic cycle decreases
proportionally with increasing fault severity.

5
Discussion
This chapter discusses the results of the modeling and fault emulation process
in relation to existing research. The focus is on interpreting the outcomes of the
simulated faults, highlighting both agreements and discrepancies with previous
studies.
5.1
Comparative analysis of different fault impacts
with existing studies
The following subsections present a comparative analysis of the emulated fault
impacts against findings reported in literature. Each type of fault is examined
individually. The discussion emphasizes how the results of this thesis align with or
diverge from established research and what factors may explain these differences.
5.1.1
Evaporator fouling emulation impact comparison
Firstly, the evaporator fouling results show similar results to previous research
while revealing some notable distinctions. The observed cooling capacity reduc­
tion of 32.4% at severe fouling (UA = 152.2W/K) corresponds closely with find­
ings by Kim and Kim, who reported capacity degradations ranging from 30­38%
under similar fouling conditions[10]. However, Jung et al. observed more mod­
erate impacts, with COP drops within 8% at every evaporator fouling level. This
could be because of their oversized evaporator’s buffer effects[29].
Moreover, the evaporation temperature drop of 10.9 K observed in this study
aligns with the trends reported by Bellanco et al., where they noted that reduced
evaporator flow decreases saturation temperatures at the evaporator exit [13].
The increase in evaporator LMTD from 4.62°C to 19.52°C demonstrates the sys­
tem’s compensation for reduced heat transfer capability through increased tem­
perature differences. While specific LMTD comparisons are limited in literature,
the general principle is that heat exchangers operate at larger temperature gradi­
ents under fouled conditions.
Finally, the refrigerant mass flow reduction of 29.2% found through the analysis
exceeds the reported mass flow reductions by Mehrabi and Yuill , who observed
reductions of 15­20% for comparable fouling levels. This discrepancy may be
attributed to the modeling of the expansion valve, because in the thesis the ex­
pansion valve is modeled as an isenthalpic state, without a control strategy [30].

15­20% capacity degradation under similar conditions [31]. The condensation
temperature rise of 26.5 K significantly exceeds the 10­15 K increases reported

Comparative performance metrics

5.2
Strengths and limitations of the chosen
emulation approach
5.2.1
Strengths of the thermodynamic modeling approach
The use of EES with thermodynamic equations provides a physics­based frame­
work that ensures energy and mass balance consistency throughout the fault em­
ulation process. In contrast, data­driven methods may not always respect these
physical laws, especially under extreme conditions.
The parametric variation method for fault emulation allowed for precise control
over fault severity, enabling systematic investigation of performance degradation
patterns. This approach is more consistent than experimental setups, where phys­
ical changes such as blocking heat exchangers or adjusting valves can introduce
measurement errors and reduce repeatability.
Finally, the steady­state framework used here is consistent with how manufac­
turers and field studies evaluate performance. This makes the results easier to
compare with existing data and more useful for developing fault detection and
diagnosis methods.
5.2.2
Comparison with alternative modeling approaches
Researchers have used different modeling strategies for fault analysis, each with
strengths and weaknesses.
Data­driven black­box models, such as those by
Chen and Braun and more recently Eom et al. with neural networks, can pre­
dict faults with high accuracy in specific systems but are difficult to generalize to
other setups. The semi­empirical approach used in this study aims to balance
accuracy with broader applicability [38] [39].
Physical test­based approaches, as shown by Kim et al. and Payne et al., provide
direct validation under real conditions. However, they allow only limited control
over fault severity and are costly to carry out [6][40]. The computational approach
applied here avoids these issues while staying consistent with thermodynamic
principles.

Hybrid models that combine physical equations with empirical correlations, such
as those proposed by Pelella et al., are really good for capturing complex behav­
iors [19]. Still, the purely thermodynamic approach adopted in this thesis provides
great clarity in showing how faults affect the refrigeration cycle.
5.2.3
Limitations
Polynomial dependency on set conditions
A fundamental limitation of the current approach lies in the compressor perfor­
mance characterization through manufacturer­provided polynomials. These poly­
nomials are only representative of a specific evaporation and condensation tem­
perature. When fault conditions drive operating temperatures outside these ranges,
the polynomials may introduce errors.
Furthermore, the polynomials assume fixed superheat (8 K) and subcooling (5 K)
values. Under fault conditions, these parameters may vary. The inability to dy­
namically update polynomials for actual operating conditions can introduce errors
that compound with fault severity.
Limited expansion valve model
The lack of explicit thermostatic expansion valve (TXV) modeling represents a
significant limitation for fault analysis. Real TXVs respond to superheat changes
by modulating refrigerant flow, creating complex feedback dynamics not captured
in the current model. The fixed enthalpy assumption across the expansion pro­
cess, while thermodynamically correct, neglects the control aspects that influence
system response to faults.
Without TXV opening degree as a variable, the model cannot represent the valve’s
hunting behavior under fault conditions or its inability to maintain superheat un­
der severe fouling conditions. This limitation explains why liquid line restriction
impacts could not be comprehensively analyzed in this study, despite being a
common fault in field installations.
Fixed water temperature boundary conditions
The model’s use of fixed inlet and outlet water temperatures, with variable mass
flow rates to maintain these conditions, diverges from typical field installations
where pumps deliver constant flow rates. This approach, while simplifying the en­
ergy balance calculations, masks important fault indicators such as supply tem­
perature variations that are used for fault detection. Instead in the model used,
these indicators show as mass flow deviations from nominal mass flow rates.
5.3
Potential improvements in modeling
In the following subsections potential modeling improvements are discussed.
5.3.1
Fixed mass flow rate implementation
An important improvement would be to move from fixed water temperatures to
fixed mass flow rates on both the evaporator and condenser sides. This would
better reflect how heat pumps operate in practice, where circulation pumps usually
run at constant speeds. With fixed flow rates, the model would predict changes
in supply and return temperatures under fault conditions. These variations would
provide diagnostic signals that match with what is observed in real systems.
Developing a laboratory heat pump for emulation of faults
82
==PAGE:90==
This change would also capture temperature glide effects during fouling, as lower
heat transfer coefficients lead to smaller temperature differences across the heat
exchangers. The resulting temperature profiles would create more realistic bound­
ary conditions for the refrigerant cycle and could reveal secondary effects that the
current fixed­temperature approach does not show.
5.3.2
Real­time polynomial adaptation
Updating compressor polynomials dynamically based on actual operating condi­
tions would make the model more accurate. This could be done in several ways:
• Interpolation schemes: Build a database of polynomial coefficients for dif­
ferent operating conditions, and interpolate between them in real time.
• Correction factors: Apply correction terms derived from experiments to ad­
just the base polynomials when conditions deviate from nominal values.
• Machine learning integration: Train neural networks to predict compres­
sor behavior over a wider operating range and link these predictions to the
model.
These methods would allow the model to give reliable performance predictions
even under severe fault conditions, when operating points move far outside the
design range.
5.3.3
Transition to dynamic modeling
A major improvement would be to move from steady­state to dynamic modeling,
allowing faults to be studied as they develop over time. A dynamic model would
capture:
• Transient responses: How system parameters change after a fault begins,
giving insight into detection delays and system adaptation.
• Control system interactions: How expansion valves, variable­speed com­
pressors, and defrost cycles respond to faults, showing possible control­
based mitigation strategies.
• Thermal mass effects: The influence of component heat storage on the
speed of fault progression, especially for fouling in heat exchangers.
Dynamic modeling would also allow virtual testing of fault detection algorithms
under realistic conditions, including start­up, shutdown, and load changes. This
would make it possible to generate training data for machine learning without re­
lying only on experiments.
More advanced models could include stochastic elements to represent uncertainty
in fault development, supporting probabilistic fault prediction and estimates of re­
maining useful life. Linking the heat pump model to building models would also
show how faults affect overall system performance and comfort.
Although this approach would require more computing power and validation, it
would create a high­fidelity digital twin of the designed system. Such a model
could provide deeper insight into fault behavior and support predictive mainte­
nance strategies that reduce both energy use and equipment wear.

6
Conclusions
This chapter summarizes the main achievements of the thesis and reflects on
how the research objectives were met. It also outlines potential developments
and future directions for the project.
6.1
Achievement of the thesis objectives
This thesis aimed to design and develop a laboratory heat pump for fault emulation
methods using a sustainable refrigerant while maintaining a charge below 150
grams. The project aimed to create a system that could reproduce common faults
under controlled conditions to advance fault detection and diagnosis capabilities.
The project achieved these objectives through systematic development of thermo­
dynamic models in EES. The design integrated manufacturer data for compressor
polynomials and heat exchanger specifications from Danfoss, as well as standard
refrigerant properties for R290. Four fault emulation methods were implemented:
evaporator fouling through UA value reduction, condenser fouling through similar
heat transfer degradation, simultaneous fouling of the condenser and the evapo­
rator through the same method and compressor valve leakage through refrigerant
bypass modeling.
The modeling results demonstrate that the fault emulation methods produce sys­
tem responses consistent with published experimental studies. Evaporator fouling
caused cooling capacity reductions of 32.4% at severe fault levels, matching the
30­38% range reported in literature. Condenser fouling generated heating capac­
ity losses of 18% and cooling COP degradations of 48%, aligning with documented
fault impacts. Compressor valve leakage showed performance degradation with
fault severity, producing parallel LMTD decreases that distinguish this fault from
heat exchanger fouling. These correlations validate that the proposed emulation
methods generate representative fault behaviors suitable for diagnostic algorithm
development.
The design successfully met the 150­gram charge constraint while maintaining
realistic residential heat pump characteristics. The 6,6 kW heating capacity at
nominal conditions represents typical residential applications. The use of propane
as the working fluid addresses sustainability requirements while the low charge
volume ensure that the safety requirements for laboratory implementation are met.

6.2.1
Model framework refinement and digital twin
development
The current steady state model provided validation for the designed fault emula­
tion methods. This validation ensured that the experimental system could serve
as the foundation for a dynamic modeling development. Future work could imple­
ment time­dependent equations to capture transient fault progression and system

pansion valves and compressors respond to developing faults. When the physical
system is constructed at the DTU Digital Heat Pump Laboratory, real­time data
exchange between the model and hardware will enable digital twin creation. This
digital twin could predict fault evolution, optimize maintenance schedules, and test
control strategies without risking physical equipment.
Dynamic modeling would require incorporating component thermal masses, re­
frigerant dynamics, and control loop interactions. The model should predict how
quickly faults develop from initial degradation to failure. These predictions will in­

6.2.2
Experimental validation and machine learning
integration
Construction of the physical heat pump system will enable direct validation of the
fault emulation methods. Controlled experiments could compare modeled predic­
tions with measured system responses across the full range of fault severities.
Temperature, pressure, and flow measurements at key system points will verify
that emulated faults produce the intended degradation patterns.
The validated system will generate training data for machine learning algorithms.
Neural networks can learn fault signatures from the controlled degradation pat­
terns. The laboratory setting allows systematic variation of fault combinations,
operating conditions, and severity levels that would be impractical in field stud­
ies. This comprehensive dataset will improve diagnostic accuracy beyond what
single­fault training can achieve.
Pattern recognition algorithms trained on the laboratory data should detect faults
earlier than threshold­based methods. The controlled environment enables label­
ing of subtle fault indicators that field data might miss. Transfer learning tech­
niques can adapt laboratory­trained models to specific field installations with min­
imal additional training.
6.2.3
Industrial application and low­charge system design
The fault emulation methods support development of practical diagnostic systems
for residential heat pumps. Manufacturers can use the validated fault signatures
to design embedded diagnostics that detect problems before they cause comfort
complaints or equipment damage. Service technicians can use the fault patterns
to distinguish between different failure modes without extensive troubleshooting.
The design demonstrates that sustainable refrigerants can meet residential heat­
ing needs within strict refrigerant charge weight limits. This approach will help
predict how low charge volumes affects fault sensitivity. Systems with minimal re­
frigerant inventory may respond differently to leaks or heat exchanger fouling than
traditional designs. Understanding these differences will guide the development
of charge­optimized systems that maintain performance while enhancing safety.
Future commercial implementations should focus on cost­effective sensor place­
ment based on the fault signatures identified in this work.
