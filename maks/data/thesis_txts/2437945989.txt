Abstract
The European Unions renewable energy directive (2009/28/EC) sets the goal that 20% of the
energy consumed within the European Unions should be renewable by 2020. This includes
the transport sector, where one method of reducing carbon dioxide is to add bio-ethanol in
the fuel to cars and trucks. In order to fulﬁll this, a large scale production of bio-ethanol is
needed where one such projected production facility is Maabjerg Energy Center (MEC) in
Holstebro, Denmark.
This master thesis focuses on analyzing and developing concepts for the straw supply and
processing for a large scale bio-ethanol production. It builds upon investigations done for
the Maabjerg Energy Center project, by the consultancy company COWI. The work done
by COWI is analyzed, described and evaluated as way to build a foundation of background
knowledge to build the concepts upon. The current research within the ﬁeld is investigated
through a literature study which leads into a description of supply methods of straw. Supply
methods of straw bales, straw briquettes and straw pellets are investigated and described
which then leads into a conceptual phase.
In the conceptual phase the conceptual model ”exploration loop” is used to analyze the
needs of MEC in order to state what constitutes a good solution for them. Then creative
techniques and systematic thinking is used to create a framework to build the concepts upon,
and from this six concepts are developed.
Through interview and conversations two of these concepts are chosen and further detailed
and evaluated, which end out in a rough quantiﬁcation and a piping and instrument diagram.
These are then evaluated against each other and COWI’s solution, where the most promising,
seen from a general perspective, is found to be a concept which focuses on straw pellets.
From this thesis it is concluded that it was indeed possible to use conceptualization as a
mean to develop concepts or straw sorting for large scale bio-ethanol production. It further
showed that due to market forces, it may be better to focus on a straw pellet supply, rather
than a straw bale supply.

1 — Introduction
The increasing energy demand and global warming calls for an ever increased focus on
green energy technologies and exploration of new green energy sources. As this problem
has gained more awareness within the last decade, ambitious political incentives1 on both
regional and country levels, has pushed Europe’s energy from fossil based to more CO2
friendly energy sources. One of the goals is to have 10% renewable energy in the transport
sector, which has been one of the incentives that has pushed the development of second
energy bioethanol production.
Producing second generation bioethanol in large scale is something which is very new, an
almost untried. There are therefore plenty of diﬃculties yet to overcome before the tech-
nology is fully developed. One of these is the straw handling and sorting before the ethanol
production.
The purpose of this thesis is therefore to use conceptualization as a mean to develop concepts
and evaluate straw sorting facilities for large scale bioethanol production.
For this thesis, Maabjerg Energy Center (MEC) is used as a base case, where COWi as
a consultant for MEC did initial investigations of straw processing and handling. These
investigations form the basis for this thesis, and is the background for a conceptualization
process where straw-sorting solutions are conceptualized and evaluated. The idea is by use
of conceptual design theory, based on a literature study of the ﬁeld, can give new ideas about
the options available for the straw sorting and supply and explore new concepts of doing
this. Further more it can give insight to what is relevant to focus on when designing a straw
sorting facility for large scale bioethanol.
The thesis therefore starts with a literature study split in to two parts. The ﬁrst investigates
COWIs written material and ﬁndings when designing MEC which is then, supplied with
external knowledge in forms of research articles, other written and oral sources. COWI’s
investigations ended in a rough concept design suggestion for MEC which included rough

investigated the future of the straw supply, which as it turns out, has a big inﬂuence on the

With background in several product development, mechanical, bio-energy courses and con-
ceptualization at DTU, the background knowledge within these ﬁelds can give a unique
insight into the process of straw supply for MEC and other large scale bioethanol plants,
which may have been unknown or not thought of by COWI. With conceptualization as a
tool for open, creative thinking, which is not limited by experience with previous solutions,
this will be the main theory and ﬁeld applied to this thesis.
MEC in itself, as the time of writing probably have little chance of being realized, due to
political reasons but it is assumed that the investigations, still apply to other large scale
bioethanol plants. It is therefore still relevant to analyze the approach and ﬁndings used
in the hope that some things can be learned in retrospect. The focus will be on the two
supply methods investigated by COWI and a third suggested by Jesper Ahrenfeldt where
the supply is based on straw pellets.

1.1.1
Maabjerg energy center
Maabjerg Energy center (MEC) see ﬁgure 1.1, is a large-scale 2nd generation bioethanol
plant. COWI was chosen as the main consultant for the plant and calculated that of 50
tonnes of wheat straw is needed processed per hour to make the plant economically feasible.
COWI has compared two scenarios of straw sources for the plant - the ﬁrst being straw
coming from heston bales, with a 11 hour continuously delivered to the facility 5 days a
week, with a truck being unloaded by autonomous system every sixth minute - a total of
2750 tonnes a week. The second scenario investigated is straw briquettes as an alternative
method, with a less in-depth analysis which suggests the straw should be compressed into
briquettes from separate smaller facilities, and thereafter transported to the ethanol plant.
These two scenarios are investigated in chapter three and four respectively. These two supply

2.1
Theory: Exploration model
It is important to note, that this thesis focuses on the straw sorting facility, which has some
set parameters, rather than on the ethanol production itself. That is why the straw sorting
should be applicable to other large scale ethanol production facilities, also using straw as
feed stock.
Straw sorting relates to the processing of the straw used in the bioethanol plant. It is in this
thesis used as a term to describe everything from harvest, to supply, to the in-feed to the
bio ethanol plant as illustrated in 2.1.

The governing theory of this thesis will be exploration model for conceptualization [2]
as can be seen from ﬁgure 2.2. It will be used as the structure for the work through the
conceptualization phase and will also be used for the previous and later investigations and
evaluations.
As the name suggests the exploration model illustrates an idealized view of exploration work
for conceptualization. The model, as seen in ﬁgure 2.3 gives ﬁve starting points, so called
feed chains, for the concept synthesis. These feed chains can each be seen as a possible
initiation point for conceptualization and clariﬁcation. This theory can help with creating
the right concepts for MEC, by the diﬀerent reasons for the concept to be feasible. By
analyzing this, the hope is to get a better understanding of what constitutes a good concept
and solution, which can then be further evaluated after the ﬁrst round of conceptualization.
In this thesis the main focus will be on the technology part of the exploration loop.
Figure 2.2: The exploration loop model. [2]
2.1.1
Exploration model: Need interpretation
The ﬁrst input to the model is the need interpretation. As stated in Andreasen et Al. [2],
this is the designers interpretation of the users need. In this case it is COWI’s interpretation
of MEC’s need which are somewhat speciﬁed by the requirements set by INBICON, for the
13
==PAGE:17==
straw sorting facility. However, since INBICON and MEC are not the designers of the straw
facility, COWI has had to make decisions based of what they think MEC need for their ﬁnal
plant. This interpretation can include everything from running costs to the general layout
of the plant and machinery, wherefore it is hard to give a more speciﬁc description of the
need. It can somewhat be argued that COWI’s interpretation is everything else than what
is speciﬁed by the client. An illustration of a need situation can be seen in the ﬁgure 2.3.
As seen from this ﬁgure, the need constitutes ideas, problems, technologies and so on, which
are also some of feed chains in the exploration model which is why they are interconnected.
The more speciﬁc need parameters both interpreted by COWI, and which are found in this
thesis, will be described in chapter 6, where the conceptualization phase is described.
Figure 2.3: Illustration of parameters for need situation [2]
2.1.2
Exploration model: Perceptions of preferences
The perception of preference is what constitutes a good solution, which should be agreed on
by all stakeholders. This exact thing is the main reason startup meetings are held in order to
match expectation of all stakeholders. It is particularly important for a consultant like COWI
since this part can hold physical goals as well as more subjective goals and wishes, the client
has. It can mostly be said that the good solutions live up to all the demands or wishes set
by the client as best as possible. This will be discussed in chapter six, the conceptualization
phase, but will also be touched upon when describing COWIs investigations, chapter three
and four.

2.1.3
Exploration model: Problem statement
The problem statement, focuses on the problems which is obstructing the creation of a
solution. By focusing on these problems and how to solve them, a solution can be found. It
was part of COWI’s job to investigate these problems and ﬁnd solutions for them in order
to create a good solution for MEC.

Transportation
The transportation of straw bales, is as mentioned not really possible on anything else than

3.4
Cleaning of impurities
It is a known issue that straw bales comes with all sorts of impurities - being everything
from bicycle parts to beer cans. There is also a relatively large amount of sand and rocks
in the straw bales - COWI estimates a total of 0.8 to 2 percent of impurities in straw bales
[7]. This really depends on where the straw comes from and what kind of soil it is planted
in. In the personal conversation with Mikael Madsen [see appendix 2], he mentions that
straw from Zealand has a less content of than sand from Jutland. This is because of the
soil, where clay does not put alot of sand on the straw, and more sandy soil clings to the
straw which increases the sand content in the bales. Sand content also varies how much it
33
==PAGE:37==
has been raining the year it is harvested, how windy it has been when the straw was lying
in the ﬁeld for drying and how much has been washed oﬀby rain.
It is essential for the ethanol plant to get the straw as clean a straw supply as possible, as
just 1% impurities will add up to 12 tonnes a day inside the facility. The largest particles can
be caught with a stone trap system and the metals can be sorted out with electromagnets.
Figure 3.5: Sketch of principle of a stone trap. It shows straw falling through a shaft. It is
then caught by an airﬂow and led into another shaft. Heavier particles are sorted out this
way and they fall into a container.
Stone trap systems are simple systems that utilizes the heavier density of i.e. pebbles. It
works often vertically, where the straw and impurities falls down a shaft and airﬂow sucks
the straw into another channel. It does however require an airﬂow which can carry the straw
but not the heavier particles and can therefore not catch smaller particles like sand and mud.
One way to separate these smaller particles would be a vibrating sieve which can separate
the loose particles from the straw while preventing the straw from falling through. COWI
explains that a supplier of such system, Cormall, suggests several steps of such sieve systems,
for example after the straw has been further divided down into ﬁner straw lengths. It is
however hard to estimate a eﬀectiveness of such a system, and it will still not be able to sort
out the mud and sand clinging to the straw.

Fire safety
Since the shredding and cutting throws dust up into the air, and can classify the whole area
as an ATEX zone, which means the atmosphere is explosive3. In order to limit the amount
of dust, all transportation on conveyors takes place in closed tubes, and all processing needs
to have air suction and cleaning facilities. Although extensive measures are taken in order to
heighten their safety, there is a signiﬁcant risk of ﬁre in the straw processing facility. COWI
haven’t yet addressed this problem fully, but straw dust is a well known issue on regular
3Atmosphere explosible (ATEX) directive 2014/34/EU

power plants. It is normally solved by having large suction systems which cleans the air.
Such a system will most likely ﬁt this facility also.
3.6
Summary straw bales: Pro’s, Con’s & Conclusion
Advantages
• Straw bales are easy to store and stack.
• They are already made by the farmers, which means no special machinery or special
supply line is needed for the delivery of bales.
• They are used all over the world, which means the processing of these are very well
known, and there are plenty of suppliers of most equipment.
Disadvantages
• Straw bales have a relatively low mass density, and are therefore costly to transport,
and can only be transported by trucks, which limits the range for which its feasible to
transport them.
• They can contain large amounts of impurities which calls for extensive cleaning of the
straw.
• Large demand for straw bales in western Europe which can push up the price and
threaten the economy of a bioethanol plant.
Conclusion & Discussion Straw bales are a well documented and tried solutions which as
an isolated case is the best ﬁt for the MEC for the supply. However since the straw demand

5.1
Straw pellet supply
Straw pellets have a mass density three to four times higher than straw bales with a density
of 590-700 kg/m3 [9]. They are made from ﬁne cut or shredded straw, which is pressed

into pellet at high pressure. Pellets comes in diﬀerent sizes varying from 6 to 25mm in
diameter[18] and are normally produced from straw bales and are therefore more expensive
than bales per kg. Seen from an ethanol perspective there are reasons to believe that straw
pellet might produce more ethanol than actual straw from bales, since the surface area of
the particles is greater, thus giving the enzymes more surface area to work on. On the other
hand, the heat generated at the pressing gives the straw pellets a polished surface, where it is

inﬂuence since the pellet area crushed into powder before use according to co-supervisor

As they are still more expensive to produce than straw bales, the economics is a problem

from bales at stationary facilities.
Premos 5000
Krones machine ”Premos 5000”, see ﬁgure 5.1, can produce up to 5000 kg pellets per hour,
hence the name, which was the same production output as COWI’s straw briquette case. It

produces Ø16mm pellets from straw, hay or lucerne, with a requirement of the moisture con-

In the winters, where there is no straw on the ﬁeld, a stationary module can be bought to the
machine, which enables a stationary production of pellets, and it just functions as a normal
pellet presser. As the output is the same as in the briquetting case, MEC should be able to
make due with 11 of these machines, if MEC buys them themselves.
Producing pellets with this machine will automatically lower the production costs, since
the step of producing straw bales and collecting them are skipped. Lowering the costs of the
straw pellets to an amount close to that of straw bales, will make the pellets more feasible
as fuel on power plants and for the production of ethanol.

example of a country in eastern Europe, which could produce straw pellets and export them
to Western Europe for energy production.

Logistics
The logistics of straw pellets are much like that of briquettes, with it being stored in large
”pits”, where it is craned into the shredding and cleaning. The only exception is that it
could be advantageous to store the pellets in silos, since an screw conveyor can then be

a crane, since it is a more continuous process and automatic cranes are expensive and can
break down. Thereby one link is taken out of the equation, making the supply less risky.
More vertical storage in silos also takes up less surface area which makes the storage more
eﬃcient and a less area is needed for storage capacity.

If the pellets are produced from straw bales, a similar procedure as the one for the straw
briquettes is needed for the production. However shorter straw lengths are needed for the
pellet machine, which means a more ﬁne cutting of the straw is needed. If the pellets are
produced by Krones machine, the pellets are produced from the uptake from the ﬁeld, and
no cutting is needed for the straw.
The pellets needs to be crushed for the pellets to be able to be processed which can
be done with drum rollers, which crushes the pellets between to rotating cylinders. As the
pellets are normally quite brittle this will produce a powder like substance which can then
be fed into the ethanol plant.
One disadvantageous of this is that the straw lengths suggested by Inbicon cannot be upheld
since the pellets are smaller than the straw lengths speciﬁed. However it might be still be
possible to feed it into the plug screw feeder and therefore still use it.

Summary straw pellets: Pro’s, Con’s & Conclusion
Straw pellets have much of the same advantages as straw briquettes, but solves some of the

Advantages
• Just as briquettes, the density of straw pellets is three to four times higher than bales.
This means easier transport and less truckloads for MEC.
• The supply security is heightened since the pellets can be transported from further
away without large amounts of extra costs. This is mainly because it can be unloaded
to i.e. ships and trains. This further makes MEC less dependant on the straw harvest
and weather in the local areal, generally ensuring a more stable ethanol production.
This means that a large scale production has to happen somewhere else.
• Due to the high density of the pellets the storage volume on MEC can be expanded,
ensuring a longer period of production without external supply. Storage can happen
58
==PAGE:62==
in silos which enables easier handling of the pellets. Large storage capacity might also
help MEC, since they can buy pellets in cheap periods and store them for longer.
• Less risk of ﬁre, due to the shredding and cutting of bales happening elsewhere, which
minimizes the dust. Important to note that there is still a risk of ﬁre. The screw
conveyor generates much less dust.
• Production can happen on the ﬁelds with Krone’s Premos 5000, which ensures cheaper
production costs. The machine can be used stationary in the winter.
• Contrary to briquettes, straw pellets is an established technology, with many suppliers.
Krone’s is the sole supplier of Premos 5000 at the moment, but since they are such a
large company, this has less of a relevance, than with briquettes. This is due to MEC
only needing around 11 of these machine which are few compared to Krone’s expected
production of these.
• Farmers are expected to carry out the briquetting themselves, wherefore MEC doesn’t
have to invest in these machine, and thereby not pay for the salary of the farmers.
Whether the farmers takes care of the transport to MEC, is still unknown.
• The moisture content speciﬁed by Inbicon can be uphold, since the Premos 5000 cannot
produce pellets if the moisture content is higher than 16%.
• No separate stations are needed for the production of pellets.
Disadvantages
• The total operation is most likely higher with pellets than regular straw bales, since
producing pellets is most likely more expensive than producing bales.
• With Pellets being more expensive to produce a bioethanol facility will most likely
have a smaller margin of earning when selling the bioethanol.
• Since the pellets might be produced directly at the ﬁeld, the cleanliness of the pellets
might be inferior to that of bales and briquettes. This means a cleaning of the straw
powder might be needed before the in-feed to the ethanol production.

• If pellets are shipped in from diﬀerent areas, it may be hard to ensure a uniform

Conclusion & Discussion
The conclusion is that this technology solves some of reasons why straw Briquettes were
disqualiﬁed by COWI. That said, it does not automatically make it a better solution than
straw bales, but with the disadvantages out of the way, pellets shows potential as a substitute
for straw bales.

Main takeaways and answers to prepared questions As it turned out Jesper had

However he explained how the economy in the straw market was more important, since a
”sellers market” would be critical to a bioethanol facility. The below takeaways summarizes
the interview:
• Straw demand in western Europe has peaked and is in decline since power plants
converts to wood pellets and chips, rather than straw.
• Sand was not mentioned as a large concern of the design of straw handeling. This
could be because the ethanol processes have changed since INBICONs process was
developed.
• Straw lengths of the infeed has little to no aﬀect of the production of bioethanol. This

that every time a straw length is halved, the price is doubled.
• Jesper has heard of Krones Premos 5000 machine and mentions it as a possible supply

Waste products
Sand and pebbles
With a low estimate of 3% sand content in the straw the total amount of sand each year
will be 10,500 tonnes. This is a very signiﬁcant amount and it is therefore necessary to
address this. The sand and pebbles will be produced from the stone trap and sieve and since

could therefore be an idea to have a small ﬂuidized-bed incinerator system, in order to take
advantage of the waste straw-sand mixture. The heat generated from this incinerator can
then be used as process heat for the bioethanol process, a method which was suggested by
Jesper Ahrenfeldt. Afterwards the sand can then be loaded into containers and depending
on the state of this, either be spread on ﬁelds or be stored at a depot.
With a daily consumption of 890 tonnes of straw, this means 27 tonnes of waste sand will
be produced which is approximately one full truck load per day. Assuming a truck will pick
up the sand a day, this adds to the traﬃc load in the area.

Metal waste will come from two sources the ﬁrst being the small screws and bolts which can
be inside the straw bales and are sorted out in the stone trap and magnet. It is hard to
estimate a quantity of this, but setting a high estimate of 0.2% of the weight of the bale to
be metal, generates 700 tonnes of metal each year. This has value as scrap metal which is

Secondly, worn out parts also generates quite an amount of metal which can come from all
the wearable parts in the straw sorting system. As already addressed, most of this metal
will likely come from the hammer mills knives.

7.1.7
Fire hazards
As with every facilities, it is important to address the hazard surrounding the facility. As
straw is such a ﬂammable material, the ﬁre hazards is a serious problem which needs to be
solved. It is also important to put out ﬁres as quickly as possible as the ﬁre can spread very
quickly with so much straw and dust.
It is therefore important to have sprinkler systems all over the process facility, which can be
started manually or automatically if smoke is detected. The water sprinkler system should
be able to contain small ﬁres but if a large ﬁre breaks out in the storage this might not be
enough. It might therefore be a good idea to use gas extinguishing, as this puts out the ﬁre
by removing oxygen around it. However the storage’s are so large, that an absurd amount of
gas would be needed. It is therefore important to separate the four straw storage rooms by
ﬁre walls so that only one fourth the amount of gas will be needed to smother the ﬁre. The
storage can also be equipped with sprinkler systems but the inert gas and sprinkler system
should not be used at the same time since the water can prevent the gas from working. At
the same time it is necessary to clean the air and areas in the facility in order to prevent
ﬁres from occuring.
89
==PAGE:93==
Dust
Fire can occur from a large number of sources and it is impossible to think of them all.
However the best way of preventing ﬁre is to have a clean area without straw dust that can
be ignited. Straw dust can be explosive when mixed with the right amount of air and it is
therefore important to clean the atmosphere in the facility buildings to reduce the risk of
ﬁre. A way of doing this is to have large ventilation systems which cleans hte air through
a ﬁne ﬁlter. These suction system needs to have intakes around the machinery since this is
where most dust is generated. Especially around the shredder, hammer mills and sieves, is
where it is important to have large suction intakes.
Another way of prevention ﬁre is to have a slight under-pressure within the building, so that
the dust is contained within this and airborne dust cannot get outside.
Fire starters
If the hammer mills hit a pebble or metal with their hammer blades, it can generate a spark
which can ignite the straw. With the large amount of air being used within the hammer
mills, this can be quite dangerous as the air can make the ﬁre spread quickly. Inert gas
could be used to ﬂush the hammer mills in case of ﬁre. Extra ﬁre hoses could also be placed
around the machinery for manual ﬁre ﬁghting.
A more unconventional way a ﬁre can be created is the straw pellets ignites by them selves
in the silo. Jesper Ahrenfeldt has explained that it is a frequently occurring problem that
large amounts of straw pellets in storage can spontaneous ignite without any ﬁre source,
and it is still unknown exactly why this happens. This should be accounted for by having a
gas-extinguishing system ready to ﬂush a storage silo if smoke is detected.
Water source
It is important to have a large water source with pumps close by when designing ﬁre ﬁghting
systems. As the ethanol process also uses large amounts of water it would be logical to have
the ﬁre-water connected to this, if there is no close-by lake or ocean to take the water from,
which is the case on MEC.

Supply of straw
One way to improve the concept indirectly is to look at the supply of the straw. In order to
do this some thoughts about the business case needs to be done.
As shown on ﬁgure 7.5 and described in the earlier chapters, is to either have a Krone Premos
5000 create pellets from the straw uptake or to produce pellets from straw bales. With the
Premos 5000 method it is assumed that the pellets Premos 5000 is of equal quality than that
produced conventionally in regard to ethanol production.
Seen from MEC’s perspective there are several ways they can go with this concept. The ﬁrst
is to ﬁnd partners in i.e. Ukraine and collaborate with them to buy machinery to produce
pellets, and thereby set up their supply chain for the facility. A third option could be to
own a one or two pellet mills in Ukraine which buys up straw bales.
Partner collaboration
Ukraine is used as a base case, for reasons explained earlier, but most of the arguments can
be applied to other countries in East Europe A partner collaboration has several advantages
and can be done in many diﬀerent ways. Having a partner in Ukraine means that MEC
can focus on producing bioethanol, and optimizing the process. As with most bio-processes
production require a lot of ﬁne-tuning and monitoring, wherefore it would be beneﬁcial if
MEC didn’t have to focus on business in Ukraine.
By ﬁnding a partner MEC can co-invest in a at least 12 Premos 5000 machines, a large scale
supply chain of straw pellets can be established. Each machine has an estimated price of
190.000 Eur3. By having co-invested in the supply they can ensure a low straw cost and a
development within the market, and have a partner who knows the local market. It may
also be a good idea, since owning these machines can make the straw even cheaper since the
farmers does not need to collect the straw after harvesting. This way the farmer saves times
and can focus on his other chores. There may therefore be even more economic incentive to
invest in these machines, however more thorough investigations are needed before anything
can be concluded.

Fire and Dust
As explained in the previous concept, there is a risk of ﬁre occurring in the silos with straw
pellets. Large amounts of pellet have a risk of spontaneously combust when stored in large
amounts. It is therefore necessary to have gas-ﬁre-extinguishing in these, so a ﬁre can be
extinguish quickly. It may be a good idea to use more silos, rather than just three. This
way, the straw pellets has a smaller risk of combustion, while the potential damage caused
by a ﬁre is lower. The screw conveyors also need gas-extinguishing since they are closed and
ﬁrewater therefore is of no use.
Another factor ﬁre causing factor is dust. As the pellets are only being crushed and not
shredded, the generated amount of dust is substantially lower than if they were chopped into
smaller bits. However some amount is still generated, wherefore some kind of dust cleaning
is needed. As with the previous concept, this cleaning should be done by air suction with
ﬁlters, so that the explosive atmosphere created by the drum rollers, is kept clean at all times.

Tried solution
As described earlier the tried solutions are scored as high on straw bale concept. This is
because all of the equipment in the system is known from current large scale production
facilities. This means earlier experience with the machinery can be gathered and all the
problems are expected to be solved easily.
The pellet based concept has two major machinery parts which is new to large scale pro-
duction. This machinery is the Premos 5000 pellet presser and the sand-cleaning in the
prehydrolysis tank. Both are based on current machinery organs (machinery parts), but
need to be tested in that speciﬁc environment and combination. More trial and testing of
these is needed before a large scale solution can be rolled out.
Compared to the COWI base case, which was already, no real improvement compared to
this has been done. As described in chapter 6, this was also the parameter which COWI
had focused on, and it therefore already scored high. It can be argued that it is in the
consultants best interest to make a solution as robust and well-tried as possible, so that less
time is used on researching and trial, since the costs for this can quickly add up. Another
reason could be that the time-schedule has been short, and that there was no time for trial
and testing . This may have been what has happened in COWI’s case and another reason
why their solution looked as it did. On the other hand, looking from COWI’s perspective
in 2011 when they started their investigations, the demand for straw was much lower, and
Lisbjerg Powerplant was not planned for it’s conversion. Looking at the solution from this
perspective, it seems like the best solution.
Costs
The costs of the straw bale concept seem to be high or even very high. It is more or less
the COWI base case, but with an added production line to ensure the straw supply. It is
therefore more expensive in construction than the COWI solution. If there is a reason to
disregard the straw bale concept, this parameter will deﬁnitely be the reason.
The pellet based system has a very low construction cost compared with the others, but has
a relative high running cost compared to its construction cost. However as explained earlier
it is expected to take many years before the straw bale concept is cheaper - years where a
lot within bioethanol market and production can happen. The cost parameter is therefore

generally a strength for this concept. Using this view, it can be argued that it would be
better to make something which has a lower payback time than something large expensive
where it is assumed that the business case will hold for the next 10-20 years.
Another argument can then also be to just build a smaller plant, with a smaller production
rate, and thereby save costs.
Fulﬁlled requirements
As fulﬁlled requirements were deemed the most important parameter in chapter 5 this is
where the main eﬀort was put. As it turned out that the straw supply to the facility was
threatened, a lot of eﬀort was put into solving this. In the end it turned out to be two
parameters, the ﬁrst being the cleaning and the second being the insurance of the straw
supply. Both of the concepts focused on fulﬁlling these without at the same time adding
large amount of cost to the concept. The straw bale concept focused on cleaning the straw as
best as possible with current known solutions. The pellet supply line was added as a way to
improve the straw supply, making the concept less reliant on local supply. The pellet based
concept was designed so it could import straw regionally with a ﬂexible pellet production.
The cleaning was expected to be part of the production of the pellets and to happen in the
prehydrolysis tank. This way of combining the ethanol process and straw sorting/cleaning
was suggested by Jesper Ahrenfeldt.

8.3
Assumptions
The improvements were based on two assumptions. The ﬁrst was that the cleaner the straw,
the better. It is assumed that it has a direct inﬂuence on the bioethanol production and the
cleaning of the straw therefore is critical for the plant. In the interview, Jesper Dohrup told
that it was not as important than what was initially assumed in this thesis, which is why
the straw supply had a greater focus in the concept reﬁnement. It is however still assumed
to be an important factor for the function of the facility due to the wear which sand create
on components.
The second assumption was that the west-European straw market was getting more reliant
on good harvest due to increased demand with as seen with the prices increase within the
last year. Especially places with limited supply like around MEC where there was a risk
that there weren’t enough straw for the plant to uphold production. It can also be argued
that the needs of MEC, which was assumed to be general to bioethanol plants, was analyzed
the wrong way, as no direct contact with MEC was had.
If the above two assumptions does not hold, COWI’s base case may be the most optimal,
as the main reason for the other concepts is gone. However thereby not said that the two
other should be disregarded completely.
Especially the pellet based case, may have potential if a large market of straw pellets is
created. It could then be used for a cheap straw sorting facility for a bioethanol plant, as
the investments for the pellet production is gone. If the bioethanol price is high enough it
may therefore be feasible for this kind of facility to exist.

Project structure
The usage of the exploration loop was based a lot on the what could be read from COWI’s
report and from bit and pieces of information gathered from quick conversations with em-
ployees at COWI. It can be argued that it would have been beneﬁcial to more thoroughly
investigate the need parameters of MEC or another bioethanol plant owner, in order to com-
pletely map what constituted a good solution for them. However as the focus on this thesis

have given some other perspectives if more interviews had been conducted and maybe ended
out in some other concepts.
8.5
Final thoughts
Finally, it can be argued whether it is a good idea to construct a facility like MEC in a region
where there aren’t a lot of excess straw. As this is the case with in northern Jutland, where
almost all of the straw is used for something, there isn’t a great incentive to construct a
plant with such and immense straw consumption here. A better place would be to construct
bioethanol plants in for example east Europe, where the straw potential is huge, and the
straw price can therefore be kept low for many years.
A counter argument for this was stated by Jesper Ahrenfeldt, who argues that a high-
technology plant like a bioethanol plant, should be placed close to a knowledge hub with
experience within that ﬁeld - i.e. a university who researches in it. What makes Denmark
so great is that a lot of research is happening within the ﬁeld, and that Novozymes which
are the main deliverer of enzymes for the ethanol production is placed in Denmark. Col-
laborative eﬀort between universities, Novozymes and the bioethanol plant can therefore be
used to optimize the process, as this is still a new technology. It is however most likely
something which could also be achieved by collaborating with a university or a co-investor
in east Europe.
Lastly the function of MEC as a base case gave a lot of insight into the problematic of straw
sorting, and most of the conclusions gathered from this, can be applied when building a new
bioethanol elsewhere plant. MEC would also have served as a learning experience in large
scale bioethanol production, which could have beneﬁted the industry, had it only been built.

9 — Conclusion
Through this report conceptualization as a method for developing straw sorting for large
scale bioethanol production, has been investigated. More speciﬁcally the exploration model,
was used in order to analyze what constituted a good straw sorting solution for a bioethanol
plant. In order to start the thesis a literature study was done, focusing on COWI’s inves-
tigations for a projected ethanol plant in northern Jutland called Maabjerg Energy Center,
MEC. This plant was used as a base case with the assumption, that results gained from this
thesis can be applied to other large scale bioethanol plants.
The exploration model was used as the governing model to explore the technology and the
needs for this bioethanol plant. This meant that the fundamental literature, provided by
COWI, was investigated and evaluated. This gave a unique look into the argumentation and
technology behind MEC, which created the foundation to build the conceptualization on.
When COWI’s reports had been thoroughly investigated, further literature was searched
through DTU’s article search engine, DTU-ﬁndit. Some information was gathered from this
and used, but the overall conclusion was that only little study within this ﬁeld had been
done.
To build upon the foundation which the previous work had created, a more critical look at
the MEC-project was used where it turned out that the quantities of available straw within
the area of the plant, was insuﬃcient to insure a continuous supply. A way to solve this was
to transport straw from further away and two ways of doing this was investigated, where
one had already been investigated by COWI. The ﬁrst method was straw briquettes which
COWI investigated and disregarded due to a lack of suppliers the cost for this solution.
The second method was straw pellets which turned out to potentially be able to replace
straw bales at a reasonable. This was partly due to the new developed machine from Krone
which allowed for pellet-pressing directly from the straw uptake on the ﬁeld thereby skipping
the step of pressing bales ﬁrst. This method makes the economy of pellets look a lot better
than before thus beating the briquetting method.
When the background investigations were done, an analysis of the need situation was done
in order to start the conceptualization phase. This showed that the focus should be on three
110
==PAGE:114==
parameters for the bioethanol plant: ”Tried solution”, ”Cost” and ”Fulﬁlled requirements”
which later turned out to represent the straw supply and cleaning.
The next step was to carry out a brainstorm, looking speciﬁcally at supply, cleaning meth-
ods, cutting methods and transport. A framework was then developed to have a system to
build the concepts upon. Filling this framework out with ideas created in the brainstorming
section, enabled a systematic conceptualization approach. The concepts were furthermore
developed with parameters need parameters in mind. In total six concepts were created, two
for each supply method and these concepts were presented in a concept catalogue together
with some rough dimensioning and quantiﬁcation for the machinery involved in each con-
cept.
As the scope of this thesis is limited, a couple of the concepts needed to be chosen for further
work and evaluation. The choosing process was done by interviewing an expert within the
ﬁeld, Jesper Dohrup, who also gave a lot of insight and conﬁrmed some of the assumptions
made throughout this thesis. Secondly co-supervisor Jesper Ahrenfeldt advised on choosing
two concepts, where one was to focus in a straw bale supply and the other to focus on straw
pellets. From the conversation concept 1 and 5 was chosen for further work.
The two chosen concepts were developed and reﬁned partly based on the interview with
Jesper Dohrup and conversation with Jesper Ahrenfeldt. The results were rough process de-
scriptions of each concept which needed further work. They were then evaluated against each
other, their pro’s and con’s discussed, which concluded that the straw pellet case, seemed
the most promising concept to do further work with. In the end a discussion argued that
it might be more proﬁtable to place a large scale bioethanol plant where the straw poten-
tial is largest, rather than in Denmark, however the knowledge and technology also needed
science-companies and universities to be close by for the technology to be developed.
Overall conceptualization worked great as a method of analyzing the work done on MEC
and as a guideline for further conceptual work. The exploration model serves as a method
of systematically analyzing COWI’s work and MEC’s needs and ended out in two concepts,
where one may have potential to be worked further on.

Project Reﬂection
Overall the project went well but with a few challenges. One was that it did take a long time
until it was clear where the concept was going and what the goal was. The ﬁnal goal seemed
very unclear in the start which meant that it was hard to plan more than a couple of weeks
ahead. It can then be argued that if the project was to carried out again, this time with a
clear goal the conceptualization process would have gone further than what was currently
the case.
Another challenge was being a single author of this thesis rather than a group. Conceptual-
ization is a iteration method with lots of thinking process where the path right path needs
to be searched for. Because of this it would have helped if there had been another person
to spar and generate ideas with. This would have enabled a much quicker conceptualization
process which would have enabled for a more detailed reﬁnement process.
It was a clear beneﬁt to work in the company which the thesis was about, since a lot of
answers and suggestions was gained this way. This would have been much harder, had this
not been the case.

had from Jesper Ahrenfeldt heard that Vattenfall had operated a pellet factory in Køge,

factory. The conversation was carried out openly, where Mikael told the story about the
origin of the pellet factory and what happened to it. The duration was around 30 minutes.
Below, the most important conclusions are summarized. No structure or interview guide
was prepared, since I had no real idea about his involvement.
Main takeaways
• There was a great political incentive to establish the pellet factory, in order to supply
Amagerværkets block 1 with biomass pellets.
• Amagerværkets block 1 was rather worn down and was primarily running on coal.
Since there was plans to sell oﬀAmagerværket from Copenhagen municipality, and
the power plant was rather worn down it was decided to do a small reconstruction of
block 1, in order to get a better price. This reconstruction was together with a political
wish to start using biomass, done so Amagerværket could run on biomass pellets, since
they are crushed the same way as coal is.
• There was only the incinerator and boiler which had to be reworked and the in-feed
could be kept. At the same time there was a factory in Køge producing parquet ﬂoors
which had a great deal of waste wood and it was planned that they should produce
wood pellets from this waste wood.

• Amagerværket was sold to Vattenfall and they took over the operation of the pow-
erplant and straw part of the pellet factory. However, it only ran for a short time
before closing down, since it was found that there were too many complication in using
biomasss in a coal boiler on Amagerværket.
