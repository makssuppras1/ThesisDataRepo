at the Technical University of Denmark, DTU, between 3 January and 17 June 2017, under the

Abstract
In this project, the creation of carbon dioxide utilization process is investigated. First, a search
for possible products is conducted. Through this investigation, a list of possible products is
found, and these products are analyzed by reaction synthesis to the product from carbon dioxide.
The most optimal products were found to be ethylene glycol and dimethyl carbonate with an
intermediate of ethylene carbonate.
A superstructure with 10 intervals and 3 possible processing paths is created. The three feasible
processing paths are: a two-step reaction path where ethylene carbonate (EC) is ﬁrst made and
then using it as an intermediate reactant ethylene glycol (EG) and dimethyl carbonate (DMC) are
made; a direct DMC synthesis path from CO2 and methanol; a one-pot process combining the two
reactions, producing EC, DMC and EG, in one reactor. This superstructure-based optimization
using Super-O shows that with the current prices of products, the two-step processing path is
the best solution. Results of price sensitivity study showing that this processing path remains
optimal as long as the price of EG does not decrease to less than 1.39$/kg, and the raw material
price of ethylene oxide (EO) does not increase above 2$/kg.
With the process known the process is rigorously simulated and the energy used is calculated
and changed into how much carbon dioxide is released by the process using natural gas as the
fuel source is -7542.19kg CO2/h or a total of -0.03kg CO2/kg DMC

Introduction
With global warming being a major concern in today’s society, there is an increasing interest in
ﬁnding ways to reduce the emission of greenhouse gases, of which, one of the biggest contributors
is carbon dioxide (CO2). Three approaches are usually considered for reducing the amount of
CO2 that is released into the atmosphere: improve the efﬁciency of processes that actively or
passively create CO2; capture and sequestration of CO2; and capture and utilization of CO2.
None of them, however, is able to reduce all emissions alone. Because of this, a process for
using carbon dioxide is of interest, as it can make use of emissions from other processes, while
providing an economical beneﬁt in the chemical products.
3.1
Scope
The designed process needs to be sustainable as well as give a net negative emission of carbon
dioxide together with economic feasibility. To achieve this, a 3-stage method for sustainable
synthesis-design has been used. In the ﬁrst stage, process synthesis, the selection of a processing
route for converting captured CO2 into selected products, is made. In order to know all the
possible reactions and products using CO2 as a raw material, reaction path synthesis is performed.
In this work, Ethylene Glycol (EG) and Dimethyl Carbonate (DMC) have been selected as the
most promising products. Subsequently, a superstructure is created to represent a network of
processes that can convert CO2 to these products and an optimal processing route is determined.
In the second stage, process design, the optimal processing route is further designed in a rigorous
simulation to obtain a feasible base case process alternative that is then analyzed.
The third stage, innovation, further improves the base case process design in terms of sustainabil-
ity, net CO2 emission reduction and economic potential by coming up with unique and integrated
design alternatives.
3.2
Carbon Capture
Of all the ways humanity is emitting CO2, the burning of fossil fuel is the main contributor,
producing up to 87% of the total emissions; the other big contributors are clearing land and
industrial processes. Of the three fossil fuels (coal, oil, and natural gas), coal is the biggest
contributor. For this reason this project will focus primarily on the emission of CO2 released
from coal power plants.

Coal Power plant

Coal power plants have multiple ways to capture the CO2 that is released in the ﬂue gas.
• Pre-combustion
This method converts the fuel into syngas that can then be used in a water-gas-shift reaction
to make a Hydrogen and carbon dioxide stream that after combustion will make a waste
stream of water and carbon dioxide
• Oxygen fuel combustion
Burning fossil fuel in a pure oxygen environment instead of air will make the ﬂue gas
created only contain water and carbon dioxide
• Post-combustion
After the combustion, there will be a separation step to separate the carbon dioxide from
the ﬂue gas
Pre-combustion is an expensive preparatory step before combustion which makes this a capture
method that is not interesting to pursue further. Oxygen fuel is a great method as the ﬂue gas
is easy to separate but the cost of pure oxygen is a huge impediment, and for these reasons the
capture method that will be considered for CO2 utilization will be the post combustion method.
3.3.1
Post Combustion
The Post Combustion Carbon Capture method is also known as PCC; it is an end-of-pipe tech-
nology intended to be used at the end of existing carbon dioxide emitting facilities to decrease
their carbon footprint as well as for new plants.
There have been various approaches for capturing the carbon dioxide, including membrane
separation, but the process used for carbon capture in this project will be a solvent based
capture and release technology that has been used industrially for decades in the sweetening
process for natural gas. This process is done by an absorption using a mixture of an Amine
(monoethanolamine, MEA) and water. After the absorption, the solvent, now rich in carbon
dioxide, needs to be regenerated. This is done by heating the solvent to reverse the previous
reaction, which occurred in the absorption column. The heating in the regeneration stage is the
largest energy use in the process but it has been shown in a recent simulation that the energy can
be decreased if the required purity of carbon dioxide is decreased from 99% to 97%.[2] For this
project a minimum purity of the carbon dioxide is 90 % so a study of the simulation has been
done to see if a further decrease in purity can make the energy consumption decrease as well.
This has shown that energy consumption is already at a minimum and a decrease in purity will
not improve the process any further.

Methodology and Tools
This chapter describes the methods and tools utilized during this project to complete the 3-stage
approach.
For the ﬁrst step, process synthesis, ProCAMD was used to create a base line of all possible
products. This list is then decreased to only the commercially viable products. Then a reaction
synthesis tool is used to make the reaction synthesis for the possible products; from these
reactions the product with the most viable reaction is chosen. With a single product. SuperO is
used to create a superstructure of possible reaction pathways for the product, SuperO then ﬁnds
the most optimal process pathway, which is going to be used in step two, process design.
The process design step is done with the help of Pro/II. Here the process pathway is rigorously
simulated and optimized; this creates a base case process that can be analyzed with ECON. This
analysis will show where most of the energy is used and how the process affects the environment.
The last step, innovation, uses no new tools, and instead Pro/II and ECON will be used in an
innovative fashion in order, to make changes to the existing process that will decrease the amount
of energy used or the subsequent effect on the environment.
4.1
ProCAMD
ProCAMD is a program that can create chemical compounds and give the physical attributes
of the compound, often used to make screening for possible solvents. This is useful when you
are searching for a compound that can fulﬁll certain speciﬁcations. The user inputs criteria for
the compound that PROCAMD can make, how many and what groups, what molecules are
allowed and if aromatic compounds are allowed. Peter M. Harper, Martin Hostrup and R. Gani
are credited with designing ProCAMD.
4.2
Reaction Synthesis Tool
The reaction synthesis tool was developed by Stefan Cignitti. With this tool, the user inserts data
for possible reactants and/or products, and then the tool ﬁnds what needs to be added for the
reaction to be stoichiometric feasible. When used the user speciﬁes the restrictions on what raw
materials must be used and what possible products can be made, and ﬁnally if the tool can add
and change additional raw materials and products.
4.3
Pro/II
The simulation tool Pro/II is produced by SimSci and is used to simulate steady state processes.
The simulation uses different chemical unit operations, connected by streams as the process
being simulated. By using thermodynamic models, the rigorous mass and energy balance for the

Pro/II can create distillation columns with the number of theoretical stages and diameter.
4.4
Super-O
SuperO is a tool created to display a superstructure and ﬁnd the most optimal path through it, and
is developed by Lei Zhang and Raﬁqul Gani at the Department of Chemical and Biochemical
Engineering, DTU. Super-O is designed to create a workﬂow to show each step in the longest
processing path; under each step an interval can be created if the speciﬁc path has any operation
going on in that step. The intervals have the potential of having chemicals added in ration,
separating chemicals in two streams, having a faction of chemicals become waste and adding
utilities before, during or after the operation. For the most compact superstructure it’s possible to
have mixing, reactor and recycle in a single interval but for clarity it can be separated into more
steps. Each time a separation is done it has its own step. Super-O is also capable of factoring
in transportation as a factor if one of the processes has a signiﬁcant amount of transportation
needed between two steps.
4.5
ECON
Econ is a tool developed by the Department of chemical engineering at DTU. This tool is used to
evaluate the cost involved in construction and operation of a chemical plant. The only required
inputs on the user’s part are: input and output ﬂow of the process and prices for chemicals, the
units used in the process and their sizing. It is important to note that piping is not being factored
into the cost of construction and maintenance.
With this information given, ECON then gives a breakdown of the costs of building and the rate
of return, and a breakdown of the running cost from utilities.

Results part
5.1
Finding the product
The creation of possible products has been done using ProCAMD, to create possible structure
from a list of requirements put onto the product.
• Molecules in the product
The product is required only to include carbon, hydrogen and oxygen molecules, this
requirement is made so the reactions required will be simple and have a low energy usage.
• Size of product
To make the product small and manageable without great energy usage in a reaction, a
limit must be set on how many groups the product can be made up of. The total amount of
groups cannot be above four and of these, a maximum of two can be functional groups.
• Aromatic compound
Making a cyclic compound is more demanding than making an Acyclic compound; for
this reason the product is chosen to be Acyclic.
With the restrictions, the groups used for the creation of the product are all regular carbon groups
and Alcohols, Ketones, Aldehydes and Ethers. Using the restrictions and groups in ProCAMD, a
total amount of 53 possible products are formed.
The next requirement for the product is that it needs to be commercially viable. If the utilization
of CO2 is going to be interesting industrially it needs to be able to at least pay for itself so the
removal of CO2 is not an expense for the company using this method.
After a search for prices of the available products, 20 of the products are found commercially
viable. One of the available products, ethylene glycol, has a known intermediate ethylene car-
bonate when CO2 reacts with ethylene oxide. The 20 available products and the one intermediate
will be used in the reaction ﬁnding tool to ﬁnd what possible reactant with CO2 will give the
product. With this information, a product for the utilization process will be chosen.
5.1.1
Reaction Synthesis
Using the reaction synthesis tool, reactions are ﬁrst made for CO2 and a unknown second reactant
to create one of the 20 possible products and water as a byproduct. This gives 20 reactions
with two of them not creating any excess water, though most of these reactions are hydration
reactions. The program is run twice more, once with methane as the byproduct and once with
carbon monoxide. Even though carbon mono oxide is not a desired byproduct, it is used during

8 reactions are found, 4 of these with no byproducts, and for carbon monoxide 10 reactions
are found, 4 with no byproduct. The four reactions with no byproduct are the same for these
two, and the two from the ﬁrst run of the program is among them as well. As the program does
not give a SMILE, the molecules needed for the reaction to be stoichiometrically correct. For
this reason all reactions that have the possibility of having different smiles will be split into
multiple reactions before the ﬁnal reaction list is available. A ﬁnal reaction list of 52 reactions is
created, and for this list the equilibrium constant is found using Aspen and the Gibbs free energy
calculated from those. During this process chemicals not in Aspen have been removed from
the list and all reactions with the same base molecules and same Gibbs free energy have been
collapsed into a single reaction.
With the Gibbs free energy found there is a total of 31 reactions, most of these reactions have
a positive ∆G and so require energy to create and will not be looked at further. Most of the
reactions with negative ∆G are hydranisations; because of the high price of hydrogen these will
not be considered. Finally there are two reactions which create Ethylene Carbonate.
Ethylene Carbonate is the product found as a result of this search and is a known reactant to
create ethylene glycol and dimethyl carbonate.[3] With this dimethyl carbonate and ethylene
glycol is chosen as the ﬁnal product with an intermediate of ethylene carbonate. As the other
option for creating Ethylen glycol have CO2 as a by product, this is not a productive end result in
terms of developing a sustainable process.

5.1.2
Processing Paths
Following the selection of a product, a search for historical research and documentation took
place in order to ﬁnd information on the chosen reaction from CO2 to DMC and EG. In addition,
to make sure that if other reactions exist for carbon dioxide reacting into one of the products,
considerations are made to ensure that the most optimal processing route is found and utilized.
From the literature search, three processing paths have been found.
• Direct DMC synthesis[4]
This processing path is for carbon dioxide to react with methanol to create DMC, with the
byproduct of water. The conversion of carbon dioxide into DMC is at the most optimal
condition 50% over 72h; this is done with the help of a membrane reactor to remove water
during the reaction.
Figure 1: Direct DMC synthesis reaction
• Two-step reaction[5]
The two-step processing path uses the reactions found during reaction synthesis, and
separate the two reactions into two reactors. This gives the possibility of each reaction
happening under the most favorable conditions. A literature study shows that the ﬁrst
reaction gives a 96% conversion using a 201x7 resin catalyst and a 5 hour reaction time.
The second reactor gives a 86% conversion using the same 201x7 resin catalyst but with a
6 hour reaction time.

5.2
Creating a Superstructure
5.2.1
Creating Superstructure[1]
A Superstructure has been created with the 3 feasible processing paths. The structure is divided
into 10 steps, and these steps can be divided into 3 main groups.
• Materials
This group is the material leaving or entering the super structure. As such the ﬁrst step in
the superstructure is part of this group as this is the Raw materials that are needed for the
different reactions, MeOH, CO2 and EO. The other step that is part of this group is the
Product step, the ﬁnal step of the superstructure.
• Reactions
The reaction group is divided into two different steps. The ﬁrst reaction step consists of:
– Direct reaction of CO2 into DMC with MeOH
– First reaction in two-step reaction CO2 into EC with EO
– One pot where CO2 is reacted into DMC and EC with MeOH and EO
The second step that is in the reaction group is the step where EC is reacted into DMC and
EG, and the third is the EG purity reaction where EC hydrolyzes into EG with a byproduct
of CO2
• Separation
This group is all the separation steps, including the recycle step that happens after the ﬁrst
reaction step. The recycled step ensures that any unused raw materials are returned to the
reactors to reduce waste. The recycle for the two-step processing path also provides the
option to remove EC and to sell instead of sending it to the second reactor. The separation
steps contains the puriﬁcation of the products, separation SS1-1 is a membrane to remove
the water from DMC. Separation step 3 is the separation of MeOH and DMC with the use
of pressure as there is an azeotrope. The rest of the separations are distillation.

Rigorous Simulation
• Reactors physical properties
For the rigorous simulation, all the physical properties of the reactions chosen need to be
fulﬁlled. This means that controllers are added to make sure that the reactors have the
correct equilibrium of raw materials, for the ﬁrst reactor ethylene oxide needs to be in a
1.25 relation to carbon dioxide while the second reactor needs six times the methanol as
ethelyne carbonate, and the third reaction needs the same amount of water as ethylene
carbonate. With the amount of raw materials required in this project, the total reactor size
for each of the reactions is huge and will have to be separated into a parallel reactor setup.
• Temperature and pressure regulation
Heat exchangers are added for temperature regulation while pumps, compressors and
values are added for pressure regulation.
For the reactors a heat exchanger is added before and after the reactor to increase the
ﬂow to its reaction temperature, and being able to siphon the heat after the reactor to heat
integration. Outside of the reactors all products are going to be sold at 300°K or 25°C so
heat exchangers are necessary at the end of production for pressure outside of reactors.
The distillation column for methanol and DMC are going to be under pressure so pumps,
compressors and valves for that are added as well.

Sustainability Analysis
With the rigorous ﬂow sheet done and all data for equipment that need outside energy have been
calculated, this information can be used to perform an Environmental and Sustainability analysis
for the project as a whole.
5.4.1
Carbon dioxide emission
Choosing a fuel source for the project is important as different fuels have a different amount of
CO2 emitted per GJ that it creates. Hard coal releases 94.6 Kg CO2 per GJ created, where using
natural gas only creates 56.1 Kg of CO2 per GJ. As the project considers using CO2 from a coal
power plant, the heat needed for the project will ﬁrst be considered to be coming from the same
source, but the cleaner natural gas will also be considered as if the process needs to create its
own boiling station to create steam. As a result, it it is not subject to using coal.
Calculations must be made in order to determine for how much CO2is released per kilogram of
product produced. To calculate how much CO2 that is released by the produced as a result of the
process. The equation: NetCO2 = Passivelyproduced + activelyproduced −consumed
The amount passively produced is all CO2 produced when considering electricity and steam
production. The US average CO2 produced is 0.618 kg/CO2/kWh. The amount actively produced
is how much CO2 the process releases on its own the process releases CO2 from its two purges.
Using coal as the fuel source the equation then becomes:
NetCO2 = 156871.256 + 6414.41 −132508.8 = 30776.866kgCO2/h
As the NetCO2 is not negative the same calculation is done using natural gas as fuel source
instead:
NetCO2 = 118552.2006 + 6414.41 −132508.8 = −7542.1894kgCO2/h
The analysis shows that using coal as the fuel source of the process does not fulﬁll the require-
ments that have been set for the process. On the other hand, if natural gas is used instead then
the process gives a CO2 negative result. With a negative process it can be calculated how much
CO2 is removed per kg of product.
kgCO2/h/kgDMC/h = −7542.1894/251690.4 = −0.03kgCO2/kgDMC
It is important to note that these calculations are done from the rigorous design where all pressure
changes are done by a single piece of equipment where in reality multiple compressors can be
signiﬁcantly more effective in lowering CO2 contributions from electricity.

6
Innovation
Looking at hot spots in the process design revealed by the environmental and sustainability
analyses shows that the compressors and the reboilers of the process are the places where most
energy is being used. The amount of energy can possibly be reduced by chancing the compressor
into multiple small compressors as small jumps in pressure will in total require less energy and
the reboilers duty can be reduced by heat integration. Those options are commonly used and
will produce results but for this project, some more theoretical option will be analyzed. As the
biggest reboiler duty in the process is in the MeOH/DMC separation, two changes to the process
will be analyzed: Decreasing the excess methanol, and decreasing the pressure of the distillation
column.
6.1
Changing Equilibrium
Currently the reaction used from literature needs six times more methanol then ethylene carbonate
to push the equilibrium to the product side but stoichiometrically only 2 times is needed, so as
another way of pushing the equilibrium, a better catalyst or reactive distillation seems the most
effective and reasonable option. If the current design is used with only 2 times the methanol but
the same conversion is retained, then the amount of energy need for the reboiler will be decreased
by more than a factor of 10, from 1365 GJ/hr to 98.28 GJ/hr while maintaining the product.
6.2
Less recovery
Changing the recovery so that no pressure change is done instead of opening up the pressure
sensitive azeotrope under pressure, ﬁrst of all eliminates the need of compressor C-3 removing
the work required from there. At the same time there is a reduction in the energy required at
the reboiler from 1365 GJ/h to 363.3 GJ/h. Even with the recovery reduced, the same amount
of product is obtained at a steady state but the recycle stream is increased and as such so is the
purge.

Discussion
While ﬁnding the product, multiple restrictions were placed on the search to limit the amount of
possible products found. These assumptions and decisions could have removed possible viable
products from the search, which could suggest that the assumption that ethylene glycol and
ethylene carbonate was the best possible products might be wrong. Because of this the process
found in this project may not be the optimal way of utilizing captured CO2, and the product may
have more usages then the current products, making a bigger supply of the product easier to sell.
However ethylene glycol and dimethyl carbonate are small compounds and as such are low in
the energy required to create and are subsequently good candidates for the process.
When calculating on proﬁts, it was assumed that all product can be sold at current market value.
However, if this process starts getting used at multiple coal power plants, the market value of the
products will likely fall, as the supply rapidly increases but the demand will not. As such, there
is a distinct possibility that the ﬁrst plants to implement the process attached will be proﬁtable,
but later ones might not be economically feasible.
When calculating sustainability for the project, only the process in question have had its envi-
ronmental impact calculated. As such it is still be possible that if the coal carbon capture and
possible the coal power plant are added into the calculation, that the process as a whole is not
going to be sustainable even if the ﬁnal process utilizing the CO2 is.
As the resident time of the reactors are long, it has been assumed that multiple smaller parallel
tanks are used. This gives the possibility of creating smaller amounts of product, but it will still
require the same distillation columns to run, and as such a smaller product stream may not give
the same amount of proﬁt or NetCO2 as the process achieves at full capacity.
The innovation step was designed under the assumption that regular optimizations, such as
multiple compressors and heat integration, will be done at later date. As the process currently
shows the promise of a CO2 utilizer as the NetCO2 is already negative, if natural gas is used as
the fuel source. For this reason, the innovation steps focus have been on a possible hot spot, the
distillation between DMC and methanol. This was done to try and help guide future studies on
the process, on possible ways the hot spot may be lessened and the total NetCO2 reduced even
further, before the process is taken further than this theoretical approach..

Conclusion
The ﬁrst objective of the project was to ﬁnd a product that was possible to create from carbon
dioxide and had a commercial usage. With the use of ProCAMD and a reaction synthesis tool,
it’s found that ethylene carbonate, used as an intermediate to create ethylene glycol and dimethyl
carbonate, is the best product. However, this result has been found under the assumption that
using hydrogen as a raw material is not possible. As such, it can be concluded that under
the decisions made during the project the most optimal product has been found using these
restrictions but possible reactions that can rival the current product may be available if a bigger
product search is done.
Through literature studies, possible process routes have been found from carbon dioxide to the
desired products. With Super-O the most optimal route though the superstructure is created
out of possible process routes without utilities. From this it is shown that without utilities the
two-stage process is the most optimal, within a price range of EG not decreasing to less than
1.39$/kg, and the raw material price of ethylene oxide (EO) not increasing above 2$/kg. As such,
it can be concluded that the most optimal process is the two-stage process both with and without
utilities.
Through simulation the mass and energy balance of the process have become known, with the
simulations mass balance only being 0.0005% off the simulation is deemed valid. With this the
energy from the process, has been used to calculate the NetCO2 emission done by the process.
Using coal as fuel a total of 30 Tons carbon dioxide is released per hour. If natural gas is used -7.5
Tons is released. This gives a total kilogram carbon dioxide released per kilogram of dimethyl
ether produced to be -0.03kg, when natural gas is used as fuel. With this and having negative
or zero values in all WAR categories, it can be concluded that this process is not only Carbon
neutral, but carbon consuming, so the project of creating a CO2 utilization processing route that
is sustainable is successful.

Future Work
This project demonstrates that it is possible to make a CO2utilizing process, but before this can
be used in practice there is still a lot of work and investigation to be done.
• EG hydrolusis synthesis Comparison
Currently the most common way to produce ethylene glycol is with ethylene carbonate and
water as is used in this project to purify the ethylene glycol product stream. This process
only creates ethylene glycol as a sellable product and as a byproduct carbon oxide. As
such, it is of interest to compare this ethylene glycol production scheme with the process
found in this project. As with this process ethylene carbonate is also used, but because of
the methanol used in the second reaction, dimethyl carbonate is a secondary product. A
project to prove what process is better at producing ethylene glycol can remove the fear of
over-saturating the market as it will be the market stable process.
• DMC/methaol separation
As the biggest hot spot of the process, studying more about the azeotrope between dimethyl
carbonate and methanol, and how to make the separation require less power, will be
valuable for the process.
• EG/EC separation
As the current process uses a reactor to purify the ethylene carbonate, it makes it impossible
to separate the ethylene carbonate out, and either recycle it or sell it as its own product.
Because of this a project on ways to separate ethylene carbonate and ethylene glycol is of
interest.
• Investigate reactions and catalysts for the two main reactions
In the innovation part of this project, it was shown that if it is possible to reduce the excess
amount of methanol, a lot of energy could be saved. In addition, the long residence time
from literature makes the vessels needed a hindrance, and as such a project looking into
better catalysts to give a more full conversion and lower residence time is of interest.
• Look at more possible products
In this project a total of 53 products was considered. If the restrictions were relaxed, a
larger number of possible products would be available, and it is possible that one of these
will be have a better carbon dioxide utilizing process. From that, logically an investigation
with softer requirements can yield a new product.
