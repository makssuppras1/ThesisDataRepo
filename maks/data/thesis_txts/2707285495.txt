Abstract
This thesis aims to prototype a solution that will automate the extraction of the build plate
for vat­polymerization based 3D printers, like DLP and SLA. Solving this problem is a first
step in making the printing process of resin­based 3D printers less labour­intensive by
replacing the need for human intervention with an automated solution. This project was
approached as a product development problem in which the goal was to take the solution
from idea to a proof­of­concept stage. The project follows the product design process
from the development stages of decomposing and defining the problem, conceptualizing
and prototyping. Then lastly, validating and documenting the solution. The process be­
gan with establishing the customer needs and product specifications. These were then
used as the requirements in the development of the proof­of­concept. The development
of the proof­of­concept was the product of both mechanical design and software develop­
ment. The product of the mechanical design phase was a prototype, that through testing,
proved it fulfilled the stated mechanical requirements. Likewise the results of the software
development was a system that could fulfill the customer needs. The final result of the
thesis was a proof­of­concept consisting of a specialized robot tool that allows a UR5e
robot to interact with a DLP 3D printer and an image­based visual servo controller that en­
ables a UR5e to locate the DLP 3D printer. By providing a fully functioning prototype that
could complete the extraction process, the project proved that automating the extraction
process was possible.

1
Introduction
3D printing is an inexpensive and high­speed manufacturing method suited for creating
prototypes and low volume parts. One of 3D printings most significant advantages is its
low initial process time, where a part can be quickly made after being designed. For these
reasons, many manufacturing companies are starting to invest in 3D printing technology.
Researchers at DTU Mechanical Engineering have made recent advancements in DLP
3D printing technology. They have developed a method for 3D printing soft tooling made
of polymer, which can be directly used in injection molding. Manufacturing companies
who actively use injection molding will therefore be able to 3D print their molds instead of
going through the expensive machining processes of making conventional steel tooling.
Therefore, the method significantly reduces labour cost compared to conventional tooling
processes [1]. Using soft tooling allows companies greater product flexibility because it
eases changing the product design. It also enables companies that use injection molding
in manufacturing a straightforward way to offer more bespoke products. However, the
soft tooling process described by the researchers at DTU is still very labour­intensive,
making it less suited for mass production. To extend the possibilities of soft­tooling to
high volume production automating the process would be advantageous. The entire soft­
tooling process involves several post­processing steps, so numerous challenges have to
be solved to automate the whole process. The first issue is extracting or removing the
build plate from the DLP 3D printer. If it is impossible to automate the removal of the build
plate from the DLP 3D printer, it would be difficult to truly automate the soft­tooling process.
Systems to automate the removal of the build plate don’t exist commercially, either for the
most common FDM 3D printers or the 3D printers based on vat photopolymerization like
a DLP 3D printer.
1.1
Objective
The objective of this master thesis is to prototype a solution to automate the extraction of
the build plate from a DLP 3D printer. The result of the project will be a proof­of­concept
that demonstrates one way of automatically extracting the build plate. PhD Macarena
Mendez Ribo is the creator and lead user of the DLP 3D printer developed at DTU Me­
chanical Engineering. Macarena helped in defining the requirements for the automation
concept. The project will only explore the solutions that fit the requirements she defined
at the project start. The central requirement is to focus solely on extracting the build plate
from the DLP 3D printer. Therefore, reinserting the build plate will not be considered a
part of this project. A Universal Robots UR5e 6­DOF manipulator is at disposal for the
project. The project will therefore not consider or explore the use of alternative manipu­
lators.
This project does not represent an entire product development cycle but just the process
of developing and testing a proof­of­concept. The proof­of­concept could then be the ba­
sis for future development. The future development would be improving the concept itself
and automating the additional steps associated with resin­based 3D printer.

Literature review
The research area dedicated to automating additive manufacturing processes spans
mechanical­, chemical­ and electrical engineering. Within these fields there are many dif­
ferent challenges related to automating the printing process of a 3D printer [2],[3],[4], [5],
[6], [7]. Furthermore, the different 3D printing technologies like FDM [8] or DLP [9] each
has their own unique challenges associated with automating them. Modern 3D print­
ing technologies and the advantages of 3D printing have caught the attention of large
manufacturing companies. Within the last ten years, 3D printers have become relatively
inexpensive [10]. Today, many different companies offer 3D printers suited for consumer
and industrial use. However, the state­of­the­art of commercial 3D printing solutions are
still unsuited for high volume production [11]. The DLP printer soft­tooling method devel­
oped by researchers from DTU solves many of the challenges [1]. However, the method
doesn’t solve any of the issues related to automating the printing process itself. To contin­
uously print on a DLP printer, it is necessary to remove the build plate and detach the part
between every print. Solutions to automate the detachment of the part exist [4], [5], [6],
[12], [13]. Many of the documented solutions work like an ejector. The part is forcefully
detached from the build plate inside the printer using a mechanical scraper, pressurized
air or a flexible build plate surface. In essence, solutions to detach the part from the build
plate exist commercially. However, either very few solutions to automate the extraction
of the build plate exist or the companies that have developed them don’t intend to share
them [14]. Therefore, this thesis is focused on creating a solution to automatically detach
the build plate because that was a necessary step in automating a DLP 3D printer.

8.2.6.1
Repeatability test
Purpose
The purpose of the repeatability test was to verify that separating the build plate from
the 3D printer would not disturb the alignment with more than five μm. The basis for the
test was the Hasco K20 mold plates. If these mold plates could not comply with the tilt
requirement other concepts would need to be explored.
Theory
ISO­1101 is an ISO standard defined for geometrical product specifications, and ISO­
1101 defines the standard for measuring the parallelity between two planes. In ISO­1101,
parallelity is the measurement of whether two planes are parallel. The parallelity mea­
sured between two planes using one as a datum is the difference between the highest
measured value and the lowest measured value [17]. Therefore determining the paral­
lelity between two planes is the same as finding the maximum tilt between them. With
the concept based on mold plates, it is possible to illustrate the build plate parallelity. In
fig. 8.5 the illustration shows how the parallelity is defined for two mold plate halves. The
parallelity on fig. 8.5 is greatly exaggerated, but with the mold plates assembled face to
face, this is a simplification of what would be measured. Figure 8.5 shows a 2D example,
but the tilt can occur around any axis on a plane in 3D space.

discussing the results, the uncertainties of the test became apparent. The potential un­
certainties were compiled into the following list:

No temperature control.

CMM was set up in LAB 2, Building 427, but the lab was a ”workshop” environment.
4.
Imperfect positioning of the mold plates after each series of measurements.
The DeMeet 200 Combo calibration report can be found in A.5.1. The report states that,
within the range of 100 mm, the measured error is approximately one μm. This is a
considerable error when the measurand itself is approximated to be 1.1 μm. Making

8.2.7.4
Force Test
Purpose
The purpose of the force test was to investigate the effect the air gap had on the rated
holding force of the permanent electromagnet. In the product specifications chapter 5 it
was stated that the solution needed to hold 800 N in tension. The available magnet was
only rated for 500 N, but this test would show how significant the force reduction would
be.
Theory
Modelling the force of a magnet is not a trivial task. It depends on numerous variables,
both for the magnet and the object the magnetic force interacts with. Instead of theoret­
ically modelling or describing the magnetic force, a purely practical approach was used.
Permanent electromagnets are available in sizes from 30 N ­ 2 kN. To generate the hold­
ing force the magnet is rated for it requires metallic contact. However, the concept relied
on introducing an air gap between the magnet and the opposing mold plate. On fig. 8.15
an illustration shows what is meant by an air gap. Using the smallest possible air gap the
corresponding reduction of the magnet force could be measured using a tensile tester.
The results from this test would then be used to determine the appropriate magnet size
to achieve a 800 N holding force.
Procedure
The test was conducted using a MultiTest­i single­column tensile tester. The test setup
used in the tensile tester can be seen in fig. 8.16. The mold plate assembly was mounted
using brackets clamped between the jaws used for plastic tensile bars. The force tester

mount the magnetic force. The peak tensile force would be compared to the rated holding

Purpose

geometry printed on the DLP 3D printer, the first printed layers are the most important for
the adherence of the part to the build plate. If the detachment of the build plate disturbs
the alignment, the part risks delamination or failure to adhere to the build plate. Failure
to adhere would make it impossible to print using the prototype. To get an idea of how
prototype 1.2 printed compared to the current fixed setup, circular discs were chosen as
a test geometry.

circles. With the extractable prototype The parallelity would change when detached, which

8.2.9
Phase 1 discussion
The Phase 1 prototype is one of the most critical parts of the automation setup. For this
reason, there was a significant focus on testing the Phase 1 prototype. If it had not been
possible to develop a prototype that could fulfil the requirements and be used for printing,
creating the proof­of­concept would not have been possible. In total, three requirements
were tested. The prototype only failed to fulfil the build plate tilt requirement because of the
build plate and not the prototype itself. The 5 μm tilt requirement was based on the lead
user’s belief that the current build plate setup was very accurate in itself. However, the
repeatability test seemed to prove otherwise when it was found that the build plate had an

average flatness of 278 μm. This discovery meant it would be impossible to fulfil the five
μm tilt requirement. To resolve this, either a new build plate, with a better surface would
have to be made, or the 5 μm requirement needed to be reevaluated. The printing test
was meant to prove that the detachable build plate could be used for printing. However, it
was sensible to try and use the test to compare the prototype’s capabilities with the current

With a new build plate, it would be appropriate to redo the repeatability and printing test
and observe the results from these tests.

In conclusion, creating a detachable and manually extractable build plate is possible. This

13 different concepts, in which each was evaluated based on a set of scoring criteria. Only
the highest­scoring concept was selected. Through concept testing, the selected concept

9.3
Visual Servoing
This section will begin with an introduction to the basics of visual servoing. It will be
explained how a 2D camera and fiducial markers can be used to estimate the location
of an object in 3D space. It is then shown how the visual servoing controller can be
implemented in ROS and used in conjunction with a ROS UR robot driver to control the
UR5e.
9.3.1
What is visual servoing
Visual servoing is a vision­based control method based on a closed­loop feedback sys­
tem. The control method uses visual information extracted from an image sensor and
can control the motion of a robot. Visual servoing is an active research topic that spans
multiple disciples including image processing, kinematics and control theory [19]. Visual
servoing (VS) is divided into two main types Image­based VS (IBVS), as illustrated on
fig. 9.4, and Pose­based VS (PBVS). Among these two approaches are several hybrid
approaches, but these will not be mentioned in this report. In this project, an implemen­
tation of IBVS was done and therefore PBVS will not be explained in detail.
9.3.2
Visual servoing theory
9.3.2.1
Error definition
The goal of VS, like most other feedback­control techniques, is to minimise an error. For
IBVS the error is based on a set of pre­selected image features. Each feature has an
image measurement in (x, y) coordinates. An example of how the error for an IBVS could

9.3.2.2
Velocity control
The straightforward way to use visual servoing is as a velocity controller [20]. Most modern
industrial robots already have an interface for receiving cartesian velocity commands, and
that simplifies the steps needed to go from simulating to testing on physical robot hardware
[19]. The objective of the velocity controller is to control the velocities of the robot end­
effector based on visual feedback. This is a well­known robotics problem, and the solution
is to find the image jacobian. The image jacobian is a single matrix that relates 3D world
coordinates to 2D image coordinates and then from 2D coordinates to the spatial velocity
of the camera. Therefore, the image jacobian has no references to the robot geometry or
dynamic constraints [21].

9.3.2.4
Fiducial markers for estimating 3D parameters
To calculate the image jacobian the 3D parameters of the chosen image features had to
be estimated. There are generally multiple ways to approach this problem, but since the
project was limited to using a 2D camera the options were limited. It was decided to use
fiducial markers to calculate the image jacobian. The decision to use a fiducial marker
was based on several reasons:
1.
Most fiducial marker libraries designed for pose estimation
2.
Marker types that are reliable with varying distance and rotation
3.
Four marker corners can be used as image features
4.
Easy to implement open source libraries
Fiducial markers can be used to estimate the 3D parameters of a point in 3D space be­
cause the geometry of the marker is known. When observing a known geometry with a 2D
camera, the distance to the object can be estimated using a perspective transformation
and basic trigonometry.
Fiducial marker choice ­ ArUco
There exist many different fiducial markers designed for various vision problems. Fiducial
markers are not created equal. Some markers are not as suited for tracking and pose
estimation. The following criteria were considered important for the marker choice:
1.
Low computational cost
2.
Robust detection with varying orientations and distances
3.
Fast detection rate
4.
Suitable for tracking scenarios
A study has been conducted to test the capabilities of some of the more popular fiducial
markers. These include ArUco, AprilTag and ARTag. One paper consisted of testing

the markers at various orientations, distances, varying lighting conditions and at various
translation and rotation speeds [24]. In the study, the ArUco marker was found to have

study ArUco markers were identified as the marker type that best suited the requirements
for this particular vision problem.

9.3.3.2
Robot Operating System
ROS is an open­source middleware or framework designed to facilitate the prototyping
and development of robotics solutions. The goal of ROS is to build a development en­
vironment that allows robotics research to collaborate on a global level [28]. The key
features of ROS are modularity and reusability. ROS is designed to allow the reusability
of code for other robotics projects.
9.3.3.2.1
ROS communication infrastructure
ROS is based on the principle of dis­
tributed computing. Most robots consist of multiple systems, each with separate tasks
that need to communicate effectively to solve a more complex problem. ROS achieves
this using a peer­to­peer network that is based on the ROS communication infrastructure.
The ROS master is the ROS server that controls all the processes and the communication
between them. The ROS infrastructure is built using various software abstractions. The
important abstractions are nodes, topics, messages. The relationship between nodes is
controlled using a subscriber and publisher model.
50
Automating the extraction of a build plate from a DLP 3D printer
==PAGE:66==
9.3.3.2.2
ROS Nodes, Topics & Messages
A ROS node generally represents a single
computational process with input and output. It can be thought of as a single Python or C
script that takes an input from one or multiple other nodes and provides some output.
The node either requests the input or sends the output in the form of ROS messages.
The content and format of a ROS message is strictly defined. An example of a ROS
message type would be a pose message, which contains data about the position and
orientation of a specific frame relative to another frame [29]. Hundreds of message types
exist and the use of standardised messages is one of the ways ROS promotes modularity
and reusability of code. The input and output of a node is organised using topics. Topics
can be considered a data channel, which is either updated by a node or a node can listen
to a specific topic. Generally, only one message type is transmitted on a topic.
9.3.3.2.3
Publisher and subscriber model
The premise of the publisher and sub­
scriber model is simple. Subscribers are nodes that requests to receive the messages
published on a specific topic. Publishers are nodes that publish a message to a specific
topic. The publisher is also responsible for creating the topics that other nodes subscribe
to. A node is commonly both a subscriber and a publisher as it subscribes to a topic with
one message type and publishes a new type of message to a new topic.
An example of a ROS graph showing the relationship between nodes, messages and
topics can be seen on fig. 9.10. In the figure, the VS script node subscribes to an image
topic called Camera/image. From the image topic it receives a type of image message and
publishes a twist message. A twist message contains a velocity screw that is published
to a Twist_controller topic.
Figure 9.10: Example of a ROS graph
9.3.3.2.4
ROS Packages
A ROS package is the basic building block of any robotics
system developed in ROS. A ROS application is generally built on a package basis. A
ROS package contains all nodes, topics and the configuration files that define its pur­
pose. A ROS package can be considered an individual plug n’ play solution to a specific
problem. An example of a ROS package is the UR_ROS_Driver. The UR_ROS_Driver is
a ROS package developed by Universal Robots to allow the UR Robot to solve a larger
variety of robotics tasks. The UR ROS Driver package provides various topics that enable
starting, stopping, and controlling the robot using standard ROS messages.

contains elaborations on the processes performed by the UR5e controller.
1. Inverse kinematics
(a) Converts end effector desired velocity to joint speeds using the inverse manip­

2. UR Robot controller
(a) Uses error based on angular velocity of each joint to obtain velocity desired by
VS script
(b) The type of controller used by the UR robot is unclear. However, reviewing the
URSim documentation a PID controller is likely used [30].
3. UR Robot
(a) Represents the physical movement of the UR robot resulting in an achieved
velocity
4. Forward kinematics
(a) Calculates pose of end­effector relative to the base dependent on each of the
joint positions

interface is that all the safety mechanisms of the UR robot related to the constraints are
maintained. The cascading control design also allows for a simple control design of the
visual servoing velocity controller. As hinted at in section 9.3.2.2 in eq. (9.7) the control
law was used with a proportional controller, which has flaws compared to more complex

9.4.7.1.1
Purpose
This test is a qualitative test which purpose is to derive initial values of the gain matrix λ,

9.4.7.1.2
Theory
The 6x6 matrix λ is the gain of the Visual Servoing control loop, as shown in fig. 9.4. The
gain matrix λ is a diagonal matrix, and its relationship with the velocity screw vc can be
seen in eq. (9.7).
9.4.7.1.3
Procedure
Instead of choosing different gain values for each axis in the velocity screw, the same gain
value was used for all axes. Various values were tried but not documented.
9.4.7.1.4
Result
After different values of λ were tried, two challenges were identified. Starting the VS
controller with a λ to high would immediately stop the URSim robot because the safety
limits would be exceeded. Second, starting the VS controller with a low λ not to violate the
safety limits resulted in VS controller using more than 2 minutes to converge on the goal
position. The solution was to make the gain λ dependable of the distance Z between the
camera and the ArUco marker and divide this with a Kp = 0.05, which made the gain λ a
non­linear function. The gain function is shown in eq. (9.17). This control regime resulted

itself but with the build plate surface, which invalidated the test. In order to test the true
repeatability of the Phase 1 prototype, a new build plate would be needed, but designing a
new one was beyond this project’s scope. Phase 2 consisted of developing a UR tool and
the continuation of the detachable build plate. Phase 2 prototyping was done parallel with
the VS controller development. This meant both the tool and the build plate system were
designed while having no notion of the VS controller’s performance. Ultimately, the VS
controller and UR5e robot could achieve the necessary accuracy. However, when the VS
controller repeatability test was concluded, it would have been ideal to go back to Phase
2 and reevaluate some of the Phase 2 design decisions. In the development of the VS
controller, it was decided to implement the most straightforward visual servoing control
strategy and implement it with the simplest controller design, which was a P­controller.
The choices were motivated by the fact that the goal was to get a working prototype and
not necessarily something that worked perfectly. There were no requirements to speed or
accuracy in the customer needs, which are parameters usually considered in control de­
sign. The P­controller had a decent performance for the limited testing, but the controller
is arguably a critical part of the system. The P­controller was simple to implement and
tune but had considerable shortcomings. During testing, these shortcomings were also
quite noticeable. Better performance would probably have been achieved with a more
advanced controller. Many decisions for the VS controller were motivated by a shortage
of time, and ultimately the focus was to get a solution that would work, not necessarily a
perfect one. In testing the VS­controller, trajectory testing was done both in the simula­
tion and with the actual UR robot. These tests did not represent exhaustive testing but
were qualitative tests. The trajectory test’s purpose was to show that the controller could
converge from a fixed point to some arbitrary goals. There was no investigation into what
poses could result in failure to converge. However, having shown that the VS controller
worked with the UR5e robot and had the necessary accuracy it was proven that the VS
controller could fulfill the requirements.

Conclusion
This thesis sought to answer the problem statement:
How do you automate the extraction of the build plate from a DLP 3D printer?
The goal of this thesis was to design a working prototype that would serve as a proof­of­
concept. The first sub­problem of the thesis was defining the customer needs and target
specifications. The purpose of this was to define requirements that served as the basis for
testing throughout the development process. The customer needs and target specifica­
tions were defined through interviews and a survey completed by the lead user of the DLP
3D printer. A House of quality was created based on the survey to assess the importance
of the different requirements. In conceptualizing the proof­of­concept, it was defined that
a solution would be a combination of both mechanics and software. The customer needs,
and the metrics were critical in guiding the development of the mechanical parts. The
mechanical development was split into two phases. Phase 1 was creating a manually
detachable build plate system. Phase 2 would be automating the solution proposed in
Phase 1. Having these two phases meant that Phase 1 was focused on designing a pro­
totype that fulfilled the mechanical requirements. Phase 2 was then focused on how a
UR5 robot could be used to automate the Phase 1 prototype. The conclusion to Phase
1 and 2 was a manually and automatically detachable build plate that could fulfil many
of the essential requirements. A visual servoing (VS) controller was used to control the
UR5e robot. The VS controller used an image­based visual servoing strategy. Only a 2D
camera was available at the project start. Therefore, it was decided to employ an IBVS
approach that utilized fiducial markers. The VS controller was implemented as a ROS
package. There were many advantages to that, but foremost it simplified the control of
the UR5e robot. In testing the VS controller, a visual servoing simulator was created using
Unity 3D. The simulator allowed for testing and tuning of the VS controller before it was
implemented on the actual UR5e robot. The simulator testing proved that the VS con­
troller could converge on a fiducial marker positioned in various poses. After testing and
tuning the VS controller on the simulator, testing moved on to the actual UR5e robot. It
was proven that the VS controller and the UR5e robot could be used together by perform­
ing two separate tests. The first test was proving that the VS controller and UR5e robot
could achieve the necessary accuracy to detach the build plate. The second test was a
trajectory test proving that the VS controller and the UR5 robot was able to converge on
a fiducial marker in various poses. The two tests proved that the VS controller and robot
could fulfil the requirements related to automating the extraction process. A video of the
the proof­of­concept was recorded as proof that it could automate the extraction process.
The video can be found using the link: https://youtu.be/Itdho8e_2wk. In conclusion, this
thesis proved that it is possible to automate the extraction process and documented the
solution. This project was not intended to represent an entire product development cycle
but the process of developing and testing a proof of concept, which this project achieved.
