ABSTRACT 
This is the report of the final Master’s project for the Technical University of Denmark 
(DTU). It is a study of Ball bearing fault progression in Wind Turbines and it is a col-
laboration between DTU and Brüel &Kjaer Vibro. 
 
Due to the fact that Wind Energy sector is continuously growing, it is of significant in-
terest to be able to operate the wind farms for as long as possible in order to harvest as 
much wind power as possible. This makes great sense should one consider the intermit-
tent nature of this energy resource as well as the large investments that such plants en-
tail. Optimal maintenance strategies are a sine qua non to this end and Condition Moni-
toring is implemented in order to achieve them. 
 
Condition Monitoring helps to gain insight of the health state of a machine component, 
in our case ball bearings in different parts of a Wind Turbine. Monitoring of ball bear-
ings is deemed needed because these components appear to be apt to failure due to their 
operating under difficult conditions, thus seriously affecting the machine’s functionality 
and possibly threatening its general well being. 
 
So, the main goal of this report is to estimate the ball bearing fault progression so as to 
be able to predict the component’s remaining lifetime and help decide the suggested 
maintenance action and make the most out of it. To achieve that, real life measurements 
from Brüel & Kjaer Vibro’s database were used adding an extra challenge to the prob-
lem but also enhancing its application value. 
 
The approach followed was purely computational. Different prediction methods were 
developed and the achieved results were compared to evaluate each method and deter-
mine its limitations.

INTRODUCTION 
 
1.1 
Background 
 
Wind Energy has been continuously expanding during the past decades, having reached 
369.6 GW of global capacity by the end of 2014. During the last decade it’s been having 
an annual growth rate of about 30%, rendering it the fastest growing renewable energy 
field [1], [2]. This increasing tendency is expected to continue given that the European 
energy and climate policy aims at 20% energy share from renewable sources by 2020, 
the 12% of which by wind turbines [3]. The availability and reliability of wind power 
systems are the defining parameters for their competitiveness and can be considered as 
the basic challenges compared to conventional, non-intermittent energy sources. 
Amongst their most often occurring failures are bearing related issues [4].  
 
Bearings (Figure 1) are used to support thrust and axial loads and reduce rotational fric-
tion. Their operating under tough environments due to moisture, contamination, corro-
sion, overheating and improper handling can lead to premature failure, thus impacting 
the functionality and safety of the machine they are a part of [5], [6], [7]. Other reasons 
causing bearing defects include fatigue, wear, poor installation, improper lubrication 
and manufacturing faults. [8] 
 
 
==PAGE:10==

Figure 1 MRC Hybrid Ball Bearings [9] 
 
Given the frequency and possibly dire consequences of bearing defects, it is needed to 
continuously monitor their state in order to estimate the remaining useful lifetime 
(RUL) and recommend when relevant maintenance action should be taken for cata-
strophic events to be avoided. 
 
As a result, condition monitoring (CM) has been gaining continuous attention in the 
wind energy sector over the past decade. Increasing capacity of modern wind turbines 
and high demands for time or energy availability has led to integration of several widely 
used Condition-Based Maintenance (CBM) strategies. An increase of 0.5% in the avail-
ability of a park may not be appealing to certain original equipment manufacturers, 
owners or operators from a return on investment standpoint. Yet, it brings benefits such 
as up-tower repairs, resulting to minimum crane and crew mobilization, lead time to 
inspection, spare parts management, root cause analysis and fewer secondary failures. 
[10] 
 
Non Destructive Techniques (NDT) such as vibration, temperature and oil debris analy-
sis, optical fibre monitoring and acoustic emission have been extensively applied for the 
mitigation of unexpected operation and maintenance expenses focussing on the drive 
train components. [11]

1.2 
Purpose of the thesis 
 
The main purpose of this thesis is to estimate the progression of faults in wind turbine 
ball bearings. The formulation of an accurate prognostic method is attempted in order to 
predict the trends of the signals of interest as well as the RUL of the respective ball 
bearing. 
 
This problem is a truly challenging one from a research as well as an industry perspec-
tive. Hence, apart from accuracy, computational efficiency is aimed at. The latter is 
needed so as to boost the application value of the suggested methods with respect to 
their being incorporated in a company’s online monitoring system.  
 
Estimating the progression of these faults can significantly help in the formulation of an 
optimal maintenance strategy resulting in the increase of the power production without 
marring the overall health of a machine. This shows the importance of condition moni-
toring and health management and highlights the economic advantages of prognosis. 
 
A basic statistical analysis of the collected data is included in order to investigate the 
underlying distributions that describe them. Thus, the importance of an appropriate dis-
tribution choice depending on the case of interest, as well as the influence of the data 
type, are demonstrated. This type of analysis can also pave the path to data driven ap-
proaches of fault progression assessment, since the resulting distributions could be used 
for sampling in simulations and the development of data-driven methods.

THEORY AND BASIC CONCEPTS 
 
2.1 
Ball Bearings and Fault Types 
 
Ball bearings commonly consist of a number of rolling elements, one inner and one out-
er ring. Both rings have grooves or raceways to guide the rolling elements, while the 
latter are separated from each other with a cage (Figure 5). Oil lubrication reduces fric-
tion and wear in the rolling contacts. [14] 
 
 
Figure 5 Basic Parts of Ball Bearings [15] 
 
 
Bearing faults can be divided into two categories: a) Rotor dynamic failures and b) 
Bearing subcomponent defects. In the first category, some typical failure modes are: 
rotational and structural looseness, tilted inner race on the shaft or tilted outer race on 
the bearing housing. In this project our focus lies in the second category, in which we 
have inner race (Figure 6), outer race (Figure 7), ball (Figure 8) and cage defects, such 
as pitting, spalling or flaking  or fluting that can be of varying severity [4].

2.2 
Condition Monitoring 
 
CM and CBM are very important technologies with a critical role in military and com-
mercial maintenance fields, like aircraft/spacecraft systems, shipboard systems, manu-
facturing processes and nuclear plants. They are interdisciplinary fields with a notably 
rapid evolution and a wide range of applications, which explains the relevant intense 
research interest. [17] 
 
The very term CBM refers to the use of machinery run-time data from sensor measure-
ments that are indicative of the health condition of a complex engineering system and 
can be used to schedule required repair and maintenance prior to breakdown. Thus, 
maintenance costs can be significantly decreased by reducing the number of unneces-
sary relevant actions and the system functionality can be fully taken advantage of. [18] 
 
CM of wind turbines is a field of continuous research and development as new turbine 
configurations enter the market and new failure modes appear. Systems utilizing well 
established techniques from the energy and industry sector, such as vibration analysis, 
are commercially available and perform successfully in fixed and variable speed tur-
bines. [4] 
 
CM has been gaining continuous attention in the wind energy sector over the past dec-
ade. The increasing capacity of modern wind turbines and high demands for time or 
energy availability has resulted in integration of several widely used CBM strategies. 
[10] 
 
==PAGE:18==
 
Theory and basic concepts 
16 
The basic steps of CBM regardless of the specific CM technique are: 1) data acquisi-
tion, 2) feature extraction, 3) diagnostics, 4) prognostics and 5) planning of maintenance 
activities. [1] 
 
Data acquisition refers to the choice of sensor measurements needed as well as the most 
appropriate method to collect them. A balance between the right amount of data and the 
subsequent cost must be achieved. Acceleration measurements will be used as the basis 
of our analysis and a more detailed description of the measurement techniques that were 
implemented to get them will be given in the following sections of this chapter. In many 
cases, the existence of a fault can be visually inspected in certain measurements (Figure 
9) and these are the data we are mostly interested in.  
 
Feature extraction is a crucial part of CBM. It involves preliminary processing of sensor 
measurements to obtain suitable parameters or indicators revealing whether an interest-
ing pattern is emerging. The extracted features should represent the raw data in a com-
pact form and indicate a potential fault in case of progression or high levels. Features in 
time, frequency and wavelet domains are extracted and used in diagnosis and prognosis 
[17]. Such features like the amplitude of spectral components associated to the opera-
tion of the monitored equipment, like tooth mesh frequencies in gearboxes or frequency 
bands are used to describe its overall status [1].  
 
Diagnosis comprises of two sub-parts, namely fault detection and fault isolation. The 
former indicates whether there is a fault, while the latter determines the location of the 
fault. [19] 
 
Prognosis refers to the RUL prediction of a failing component based on its current diag-
nostic results and operational data. [17] It is strongly connected with the fifth step of 
CBM, since it can contribute to the formulation of an optimal maintenance strategy. 
 
Tracking of speed related spectral components describing the shaft dynamics, like the 
first and higher order running speeds is implemented in condition monitoring systems 
together with broadband measurements in various frequency ranges for overall vibration 
evaluation and early stage bearing defects. [4] 
 
Alternatively, an envelope can be set over the healthy vibration signature under 
consideration so that an alarm is triggered when a frequency component exceeds the 
predefined threshold. [4] 
 
A successful CMS should have the capability of consistent alarming based on the level 
or progression of the related signals. This implies the lowest possible rate of false 
alarms and can also provide an automated preliminary diagnosis of the fault and estima-
tion of its severity [1]

2.3 
Vibration Analysis 
 
Vibration analysis is the most wide-spread condition monitoring technique applied on 
wind turbine generators. The measurement approach for our case involves employing 
accelerometers (Figures 11 and 12) installed radially at the load zone. [4] 
 
 
 
Figure 11 Positioning of accelerometers on generator bearings (side view) [4] 
 
 
Figure 12 Positioning of accelerometers on generator bearings (axial view) [4] 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:21==
 
Theory and basic concepts 
19 
The term load (or loaded) zone (Figure 13) can be perceived as the area, within which 
the rolling elements are still in contact with the raceway [14]. In Figure 13, the balls of 
the bearings or any type of rolling contacts are modelled as nonlinear springs. 
 
 
Figure 13 The loaded zone of bearings [14] 
 
 
Accelerometers installed in the axial direction could also be part of the monitoring 
solution, although it is not common in wind turbines up to few megawatts due to 
economic reasons as well as our taking sufficient indications from the radially installed 
ones [10]. It is not uncommon that ten or more accelerometers are installed in a wind 
turbine (Figure 2) [20]. 
 
What we need to decipher from vibration analysis is how the condition of the machine 
is changing, i.e. how the internal forces in the machine are changing with time and 
accelerating the breakdown of the compoonents (Figure 14). Changes in these forces are 
either caused by changes in the work process or by changes in the properties of machine 
elements. [12] 
 
The amount of vibration generated by the forces passing through a machine structure is 
given by the product of the time varying Force times Mobility, where mobility is a 
measure of the willingness of the structure to be set into motion and is given by the 
inverse of the mechanical impedance. [12]

2.4 
Data Types and Characteristic Frequencies 
 
There is an abundance of different types of data that can be collected from the available 
monitoring systems. Yet, according to engineering experience the most indicative 
measurements of generator bearing degradation and the defect progress for different 
frequency ranges are the overall vibration RMS level, ISO RMS and the high frequency 
band pass (HFBP) which will be described later in this section. Depending on the failure 
mode or types of fault, additional or different measurements could be needed. [20] 
 
The data used to monitor and evaluate the condition of an engineering system are com-
monly referred to in the literature as Condition Indicators (CI’s), Health Indicators 
(HI’s) or simply descriptors and apart from the abovementioned ones, amplitude of run-
ning speed harmonics or statistical features in the time or frequency domains, as well as 
features in the time-frequency domain using wavelet analysis, Wigner distribution or 
Short Time Fourier Transform have been suggested. [10]

Due to varying operating conditions like loading, wind speed, manufacturing imperfec-
tions of the bearing, etc. the degradation signals that we get can exhibit a wide range of 
trends. Roughly speaking, the following categorization can help in terms of generally 
describing the various degradation phases: Good condition (Figure 15), gradual wear 
(Figure 16) and accelerated wear (Figure 17). [21] 
 
Good condition corresponds to a stationary trend, gradual wear to a first degree, linearly 
increasing trend and accelerated wear can be approximated as a second degree polyno-
mial or an exponential function [21]. It should be noted that as will be demonstrated in a 
later chapter, degradation signals do not necessarily move from stationary to gradual 
and then to accelerated wear but can stochastically change behavior. So, it is not un-
common for example to witness signals demonstrating a stationary trend after having 
been through an accelerated wear phase.

A common industry practice is that each individual measurement is trended over time to 
detect certain failure modes. When the trend from a specific measurement crosses a pre-
defined threshold, an alarm or warning will be triggered. Due to the large number of 
turbines and measurements collected from each one of them, it would be impractical to 
set the thresholds manually and hence their setting is usually based on statistics. The 
research of their distribution is an active field. These thresholds are often a tradeoff be-
tween missing real alarms and having false alarms. A block diagram of the threshold 
setting process can be viewed in Figure 18 [20].

3.1 
Data collection 
 
A crucial part of this project was the data collection and classification. As foretold, em-
phasis was placed on acceleration data. However, in order to fully capture the progres-
sion of the fault it was needed to additionally keep record of the turbine type, gearbox 
type, generator type, bearing type, severity, commissioning date, fault type, alarm report 
date, CIs and power bins in the reports, value and type of the crossed threshold as well 
as suggested maintenance activities. These data may be a part of future work for differ-
ent types of analysis and were also used to spot machines sharing some common fea-
tures. 
 
It is needed to compile homogeneous populations from machines with similar character-
istics so as to yield reliable prognostic models. Clearly, if bearings from different ma-
chine parts, machines of several power ratings and/or with different gearbox types were 
considered as a single population, reliable results could not be guaranteed unless the 
proper hypothesis tests were realized and showed otherwise [23].  
 
In this chapter, statistical analyses of single turbines as well as collective analyses for 
wind turbines of the same type are presented. Thus, the different behaviors can be de-
scribed in terms of the resulting distributions. These results can be used to construct the 
proper cumulative density functions from which one could sample and produce simulat-
ed data with a practical value as well as for training sets in data driven approach-
es.Moreover, an assessment of the correspondence between the condition monitoring 
system and the actual fault progression can possibly be done by comparing when a 
higher severity is reported and the dates when the respective thresholds are crossed. 
This can be also used by the company to evaluate and improve its services.

ISOA measurements from the DE sensor were investigated. First of all, they were in-

PROGNOSIS APPROACHES 
4.1 
Literature Review 
 
In this chapter, a general literature review with emphasis on prognostic methods will be 
presented. Thus, the general research and industry trends in the field will be outlined 
and some basic relevant ideas will be given. These methods provided the motivation for 
the algorithms presented later in this chapter and therefore a description of their charac-
teristics will provide a general understanding of the reasoning behind using them. 
 
Roughly speaking, Remaining Useful Life (RUL) prediction methods can be divided 
into data-driven and model driven methods. Data-driven methods describe the degrada-
tion process most often using machine learning techniques like neural networks and 
fuzzy logic, while model-based methods formulate mathematical or physical models to 
describe the degradation process and estimate model parameters from measured data 
[7], [24]. 
 
One basic disadvantage of data-driven approaches is that the algorithms require a rather 
large amount of training data to learn the different health states or the degradation mod-
el. Secondly, they assume a discrete-state model for the degradation of the bearing, 
which is unrealistic and in most cases arbitrary and thirdly despite their high accuracy, 
hardly do they contribute to the intuitive understanding of the process of failure. Their 
advantage lies in their being less complex than the model based methods due to their not 
requiring prior knowledge of material properties, construction or failure mechanisms 
and not needing to develop detailed models of the system. [25]   
 
Model-driven methods on the other hand can enlighten more intuitively the problem but 
in general tend to be computationally intensive and are consequently not considered 
suitable for real time monitoring. [25] Moreover, in some real life problems, it is barely 
possible to construct a model that accurately depicts the system behavior and takes into 
account all the different phenomena contributing to it [26]. 
 
A data-driven approach example can be seen in [26]. In this paper, the next degradation 
state of the bearing is investigated combining an unsupervised classification technique 
==PAGE:46==
 
Prognosis approaches 
44 
called artificial ant clustering and Hidden Markov models (HMM), predicting the immi-
nence of the next degradation state.  Finally, a neuro-fuzzy inference system (ANFIS) is 
used to estimate the remaining time before the next degradation state. This led to a reli-
able prognostic method, its main drawback being that an abundance of historical data is 
a prerequisite for its successful application. 
 
Another data-based approach is followed at [24]. In this paper, the Extended Kalman 
Filter (EKF) parameters are determined from an analytical function approximating the 
degradation, which is determined from curve-fitting. As can be observed, the RUL es-
timation accuracy depends on the estimators used. Also, RUL predictions at the begin-
ning are very erroneous and converge to the true value only after several operating 
hours have elapsed. An important contribution of this paper was the confidence inter-
vals addition at the RUL estimation. However, the fact that only one cause of failure is 
investigated should be considered as a limitation, given that one of the main challenges 
in ball bearing prognostics is the significant amount of potential fault causes. 
 
Similarly, in [18] a technique combining Unscented Kalman Filter (UKF) and Particle 
Filter (PF) is implemented, aiming at a higher processing efficiency. Actually, the UKF 
is calculating an optimal estimate of the degradation states, while PF is used for a pre-
diction of one-step ahead. In this case, apart from the health indices provided by accel-
eration vibration data, the oil debris mass is taken into account for a more holistic ap-
proach. State estimation of UKF and PF was found equally accurate under comparable 
conditions, but UKF was found to be more computationally efficient. Therefore, it was 
possible to provide a state estimation with a smaller processing time. 
 
A model-based approach is followed in [7], in which the exponential model for RUL 
prediction first developed in [27] is improved with an adaptive First Predictive Time 
(FPT) selection and random errors of the stochastic process are reduced with the use of 
PF. After testing this approach with simulated data, its performance can be considered 
very satisfactory. It should be observed though that the complex exponential model with 
the geometrical Brownian motion used is not very computationally efficient, which 
could limit the method’s usefulness for research purposes.  
 
A PF approach is also used at [28], where an on-line framework for failure prognosis is 
developed and applied on a Planetary Carrier plate in order to estimate the crack length 
development. After comparing the predicted results with the actual measured ones, the 
method is proven to be very accurate. 
 
A combination of Bayesian updating and Expectation Maximization algorithm is fol-
lowed at [29], so as to update the model parameters and RUL distribution. A linear and 
an exponential degradation model were used and an exact, closed-form RUL distribu-
==PAGE:47==
 
Prognosis approaches 
45 
tion is obtained in both cases. 
 
A quite different data-driven approach is followed at [30], where the aim is to determine 
an advanced feature that correlates clearly with the fault progression via genetic algo-
rithms. This approach was shown to be pretty accurate through a machine spindle test 
and a set of bearing run to failure data. 
 
On the whole, the degradation process has stochastic characteristics even under tightly 
controlled experimental conditions. A Markov chain representation of fault dynamics is 
presented in [31] under the assumption that modeling of fault growth physics and future 
environmental stresses can be represented by two independent stochastic process mod-
els. Here, the fault growth physics is modeled by a uniformly distributed dice throwing 
process, while a discrete random walk represents uncertain modeling of applied load. 
This approach has more of a theoretical interest. 
 
In [32], a fault detector is proposed so that early detection of faults, prediction of their 
evolution and the estimation of RUL can be accurately estimated without an unaccepta-
ble number of false alarms, which are often encountered in early fault detection. An 
optimization algorithm is applied in order to determine the parameters C and m of the 
Paris’ law, whose true values are not available. As can be seen, the PF method can ap-
proximate the actual feature vector pretty closely and the proposed model seems very 
promising. Further evidence though will be provided when the actual bearing in ques-
tion is tested and more historical data are available. 
 
The possibility of preventive replacement of multiple components of a complex system 
is investigated in [33]. This is an economically appealing idea that could be inspirational 
for offshore wind parks, where accessibility is often limited. The model used to depict 
the health state is a Weibull baseline hazard function, collectively considering the co-
variates influencing the time to failure, while an optimal replacement policy is suggest-
ed by precisely evaluating the cost of the multi-component CBM policy. However, the 
algorithm suggested can become computationally expensive for a large number of com-
ponents. 
 
A comparison between replacement decisions obtained from the Weibull survival analy-
sis model and the fit of the EKF is presented in [34]. As highlighted above, the propor-
tional hazard modeling is computationally expensive, while the EKF enables simultane-
ous inference for a large number of components and can incorporate multi-dimensional 
sources of CM data. Both methods yield similar RUL predictions in spite of the EKF’s 
using a linearization of the state equation. 
 
HMMs are used in [35] in order to estimate the RUL of drill bits. Here again, a data-
==PAGE:48==

driven method is used with historical data of thrust and torque collected from run to 
failure tests. Different HMMs are trained according to the different state the drill bit is 
considered to be. The suggested model performs satisfactorily. However, the different 
states of each HMM are determined via cross-validation which makes the whole method 
seem a little counter-intuitive regarding the actual condition of the drill. 
 
A model based approach is followed in [36], in which the parameters of the degradation 
model are recursively evaluated through implementation of the EKF. This approach 
performs significantly better compared to the weighted mean slope approach followed 
in [37] for the same problem. This becomes even clearer by observing the RUL graph 
comparison between the two methods.  
 
A different filter approach can be seen in [21] in which it is possible to combine differ-
ent models with weights according to whether the condition is considered good, gradual 
wear or accelerated wear. This approach offers an increased flexibility regarding differ-
ent degradation trends, which can be very useful in real life data that might be hard to 
capture with only one model. On the whole, this method can perform well but fails to 
predict when degradation rate becomes negative, which is not rare with real life noisy 
data.

4.2 
Prediction using Kalman Filtering 
 
4.2.1 
Introduction 
 
As can be observed in the literature review, filtering approaches are amongst the most 
commonly used in prognostics. Both particle filtering (PF) and variations of the Kalman 
filtering (KF) have been applied with great success in the field [7], [8], [24], [25]. It was 
decided to implement the KF as a first prediction approach, since it is a rather straight-
forward and computationally efficient algorithm. All predictions in this chapter are for 
thirty days. 
 
This algorithm aims at estimating the system state based on its dynamics given some 
noisy measurements [38]. It is considered by and large a very effective estimator, mini-
mizing the mean-squared estimation error for a linear stochastic system using noisy lin-
ear sensors [39]. 
	  
4.2.2

The model and different parameters used in KF can be clearly seen in the System (4.1.1) 
and Observation Equations (4.1.2), both of which are written in a discrete form. The 
System or State Equation expresses how the state of the system is expected to evolve 
according to a physical law (e.g. Newton’s law of motion in GPS) or the empirical for-
mulas (e.g. Paris’ law for crack growth)  that describe its dynamics. The Observation or 
Measurement Equation gives the measurement as a function of state. It can be perceived 
as the equation expressing how our measurement equipment is functioning. Both equa-
tions are assumed to be linear, while the system and observation noises are assumed to 
be Gaussian and independent of one another [39].

KF can be viewed as a Bayesian tracking method in which the equations of the predict 
step give an a priori state estimation, while the update step yields an a posteriori estima-
tion.  It can be conceptually perceived as a Hidden Markov Model (HMM), in which 
both the hidden states (𝑥!) and the measurements (𝑦!) are assumed to follow a normal 
distribution.  
 
Three types of inference may be realized: 1) filtering, in which the probability of state 
𝑥! at time t given the measurements up to the same point in time (𝑃(𝑥!|𝑦!, 𝑦!, … 𝑦!)) are 
computed, 2) prediction, in which the probability of a future state 𝑥!!! given the current 
state 𝑥! (𝑃(𝑥!!!|𝑥!)) is calculated and 3) smoothing, in which future observations up to 
time t+k are used to compute the probability of the state at time t (𝑃(𝑥!|𝑦!, 𝑦!, … 𝑦!!!)). 
[21] 
 
The P matrix is a prediction of the estimation error, R is the variance of the observation 
model while the Kalman gain and is a weighting factor representing the relative im-
portance of the measurement error with respect to the model estimate. Hence, if our 
measurements are accurate, the posterior state estimation depends more on them, while 
if they are not, the prior state prediction is assigned a larger weight. 
 
To give an intuitive and informal explanation of the Kalman gain, suffice it to look at 
the predict step of the algorithm. From this, it can become clear that a large value of R, 
i.e. our measurements not being believed to be accurate, leads to a small value of Kal-
man gain. Therefore, a smaller weight is placed on the measurement when calculating 
the posterior estimate of the state.

measurements, then 𝐶𝑃!𝐶!is smaller than R, so the prediction will rely more on the a

Exponential fits and first degree polynomials were able to capture a general increasing 
trend in all cases, the former giving larger values than the latter (Figures 46-48). On the 
other hand, second and third degree polynomials are observed to perform similarly to 
each other and appear to be prone to over-fitting in some cases at times even leading to 
a decreasing trend like in Figure 47 (third degree), 48 (both second and third degree) 
and 49 (third degree).  
 
Higher degree polynomials can also be potentially used. Alternatively, there can be 
picked more points to base our approximation on. Yet, a higher degree can increase the 
risk of over-fitting, in which case the polynomial will be more strongly influenced by 
the idiosyncrasies of our data instead of their general behavior [47]. On the other hand, 
picking up more data does not guarantee a more accurate trend description, since it 
would inevitably include past data not representative of more recent behavior. This is 
commonly known as the “blur of history” problem [44]. 
 
Some more specific issues regarding prediction errors for every different case can be 
seen in Figures 50, 51, 52 and 53. Similarly as in KF, increasing the number of days 
ahead that we predict generally harms the prediction accuracy, while the opposite hap-
pens with the training set days. In all cases, larger errors than the ones in KF are ob-
served.   Especially, the very large errors observed in second and third degree polyno-
mial fits (Figures 51 and 52) could provide evidence of the issues of over-fitting 
touched upon in the previous paragraph. These errors show a lack of robustness in this 
approach.

4.4 
Extended Kalman Filter 
 
4.4.1 
Introduction 
 
In order to formulate a more flexible prognostic model and expand the possibilities of-
fered by the KF, the Extended Kalman Fiter (EKF) approach was attempted.  The EKF 
is an approximate nonlinear Bayesian filter that provides us with a toolbox for non-
linear models by using the Taylor series expansion. Only the first term of the series is 
utilized to approximate nonlinear state and/or measurement equations [38]. However, 
the resulting complexity has rendered this approach rather unpopular [49].  
 
The use of a nonlinear filter aspires to give a more accurate representation of the non-
linear behavior of our data compared to the purely linear KF.

The EKF is considered as a popular alternative to the simple KF, when we are faced 
with non-linearities. It is conceptually straightforward if one is familiarized with the KF 
and is generally considered less computationally expensive than the rest of non-linear 
filter alternatives. 
 
In cases when the degradation signal follows a continuously increasing trend and its 
behavior resembles an exponential function, the EKF seems to follow the degradation 
signal closely (Figures 60, 61 and 62). Again, it is crucial to perform new predictions 
when new measurements are obtained. This is obvious from the fact that the predictions 
come increasingly closer to the actual signal in the aforementioned Figures.

creases, the prediction gets less accurate. The interesting thing to observe is that we

diction of 30 days. 
 
This can be due to the model’s changing behavior for example from accelerated to 
gradual wear, a stationary trend or to an abrupt increase of the slope because some sub-
component breaks. In general, the EKF may diverge rapidly due to wrong modeling of 
the process because of its linearization and this is one of the main drawbacks of this 
method [50].

4.5.1 
Introduction 
 
EKF is sometimes difficult to tune and its estimates are unreliable in case of severe sys-
tem nonlinearities. This is due to its first-order accuracy of linearization. A popular al-
ternative could be Unscented Kalman Filter, but this also diverges in case of highly non-
linear systems. 
 
So, to further improve this model driven approach, the exponential function parameters 
were attempted to be estimated by the particle filter, also known as Sequential Monte 
Carlo Method (SMC), one of the most utilized non-linear filtering methods in a variety 
of fields [7], [28], [51]. A brief description of our model, the reason why the Particle 
Filter method should be applied as well a presentation of the algorithm follows [38].

4.6.1 
Introduction 
As can be witnessed from all the previous sections of this chapter, the degradation sig-
nal is in practice not following an increasing trend with a constant slope. Thus, counter-
intuitive though it might seem, accelerated wear can be followed by linear or even sta-
tionary trend. To get a picture of how these trends look like, see Figures 15-17. 
 
 
 
Figure 74 daily averages from wind turbine #02 (same as Figure 38) 
Figure 74 is a good example of how unpredictably a degradation signal can evolve with 
time. It is the same example as in Figure 38 but repeated here for the sake of conven-

have accelerated trend, from t = 17 to approximately t = 339 days we have stationary 
trend, from t = 339 days to t = 741 days we have linear trend and then accelerated trend 
until the bearing’s replacement.

applied. This has also been demonstrated in Figures 44 and 65. The detrimental effect of

prediction follows the single state equation assumed in the state estimation part of the 
filters.

5.2 
Perspectives 
 
The results of this thesis show the importance of Conditioning Monitoring Systems in 
fault prognosis. The models developed could be used to improve the prediction accura-
cy (Chapter 4) and automatic threshold settings (Chapter 3) of such systems and can be 
applied to machine faults regardless of their location and nature. Emphasis was given on 
both accuracy and computational cost and hence, said models could be applied on on-
line, without further burdening the large computational systems most often used in the 
industry. 
 
Moreover, the methods presented could be applied on different kinds of data and per-
form macroeconomic [56] and weather predictions [46]. In that case, appropriate types 
of training sets should be used so as to determine the initial state and covariance estima-
tions. 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:111==
 
Conclusion 
109 
5.3 
Future work 
 
There is a very big potential in the field of prognostics and therefore there are vast pos-
sibilities that could be further explored. Another possibility to improve the accuracy of 
the prognostic methods presented in the fourth chapter could be to add an input describ-
ing the fault growth with time. Thus, the model will be further refined and more well 
informed and will be able to detect patterns previously thought to be noise [44]. This 
could be estimated with a function similar to the Paris’ law but due to unavailability of 
measurements, a least squares approach with a forgetting factor should be implemented 
[32].  
 
The PF method could yield more accurate predictions by applying the improved expo-
nential model as in [7]. As can be seen in this paper, a larger number of parameters is 
used to describe the degradation and this is expected to increase the computational cost. 
 
As far as new methods that can be applied, HMM methods using the Forward Backward 
(FB) and Expectation Maximization (EM) algorithms can effectively estimate the hid-
den state [35]. Therefore, both fault diagnostics and prognostics could be implemented. 
The same datasets and the resulting distributions as in chapter 3 could be used for the 
training sets and the prior probability estimation. The main challenge of this approach is 
the discrete hidden states of the ball bearing that should be defined. The latter will be 
arbitrary to an extent and their choice will greatly influence the accuracy of the method. 
 
Furthermore, the aforementioned datasets and distributions can be applied on the train-
ing of Neural Networks. The large number of data could hinder the computational effi-
ciency, in which case the method will mainly have a research interest. 
 
Classification algorithms such as decision trees and k-Nearest Neighbors algorithms 
could be implemented in order to estimate the ball bearing state and make predictions. 
In that case, a bigger amount of data than in filtering approaches could be needed for the 
classification, which would again make this approach computationally expensive.  
 
Apart from ISOA that was used as the CI in this project, many other such signals are 
available in the company database. The latter can be combined in the form of an analyt-
ical expression and result in a CI with a potential to give an accurate prediction for a 
longer spell or from earlier on. In [30] this was achieved with the use of genetic algo-
rithms but also such a formula could be extracted through the application of wavelet 
transformations on signals of interest.
