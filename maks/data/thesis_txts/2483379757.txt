Abstract 
Perfluoro- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFASs) have been detected ubiquitously 
throughout the biotic and abiotic environment. Perfluorooctanesulfonic acid (PFOS) and 
fluorotelomer substances (FTSs) have been used in aqueous firefighting foams (AFFFs) at 
training sites in Svea, a former coal mine in Svalbard. Samples taken from the area found 
extremely high concentrations of 7200 µg/kgdw at a landfill (Avfallsfylling) and the 
fluorotelomer, 6:2-FTS was detected up to concentrations of 240 µg/kgdw at Lundbakken. 
The samples were found to have a high organic carbon content (foc) in the surface layers (up 
to 11%) for soil contaminated with PFASs due to the high coal content. The average particle 
size in the soil was classified as very coarse grained (1 - 2 mm) or granule sized (2 – 4 mm). 
Transport of FTSs and PFOS away from the identified hot spots was found to be occurring, 
despite the high foc, indicating that the PFASs are desorbing from the soil particles during the 
snowmelt or that the fine particles are able to percolate through the coarser stones.  
The potential for two phytoremediation treatment options, extraction and stabilization, was 
explored for the native polar willow (Salix Polaris). Willow toxicity testing found that soil from 
Lundbakken containing FTSs enhanced transpiration by 17.8% relative to the control, whilst 
the soil from Avfallsfylling containing PFOS reduced transpiration by 32.6%. Root elongation 
testing found no negative effects on root growth due to the PFAS content. The phytotoxicity 
results suggest that willow growth is possible in Svea. 
Mass balance calculations for the potential of phytoextraction and stabilisation showed long 
remediation times (up to ca. 45,000 years) for PFOS from Avfallsfylling and times of 1.5-5000 
years for 6:2-FTS from Lundbakken depending on the bioconcentration factor (BCF) used for 
substance. It was concluded that phytostabilisation is far more applicable to the Arctic, given 
the long remediation times and higher proportion of plant biomass in the roots. Daily intake of 
PFOS in the willow for two endemic herbivores, the Svalbard reindeer (3.6-10.3 µg/kgbw/day) 
and ptarmigan (3.5 µg/kgbw/day) indicates exposure will not have adverse effects on the 
animals.  
Finally, soil amendment materials were suggested to be used in application with 
phytostabilisation. There is an abundance of fine coal particles in the vicinity of the PFOS 
contamination that could be used to enhance the sorption of PFASs to the soil. This 
combination of treatment options, chemophytostabilisation, will further restrict transport of 
PFASs in the soil. The costs associated with the revegetation and monitoring of the area were 
estimated to be cheaper (US$170,000) than just the landfilling of the PFOS hotspot alone 
(US$750,000).  
Further information of the practicalities of revegetation in the Arctic, survival of the polar 
willow in the contaminated soil and the ability of local coal as a sorbent material should be 
investigated to make a fully informed decision on the remediation of PFASs in Svea.

1. Introduction 
Persistent organic pollutants (POPs) are organic compounds that are almost entirely synthetic 
and found in a wide range of products. These are chemical substances that are persistent in 
the environment, capable of being transported long distances, tend to bio-accumulate through 
the food-web and pose a risk of causing adverse effects to human health and animals (Beach, 
et al., 2006). The Stockholm Convention on Persistent Organic Pollutants is an international 
treaty that was signed in 2001 and became effective in 2004, with the intention of eliminating 
or restricting the production and use of POPs. Per- and polyfluoroalkyl substances (PFASs) 
are an example of such substances and have been detected ubiquitously in all environmental 
compartments around the world (Butt, et al., 2010; Giesy & Kannan, 2001).  
Perfluorooctanesulfonic acid (PFOS) and its precursor compounds were added to the 
Stockholm Convention listing of POPs in 2009 (Wang, et al., 2009). PFOS has been found 
ubiquitously in the abiotic and biotic environment, including in the Arctic. It has been utilised 
for its properties as a surfactant in various applications such as in carpets and in food 
packaging. It was also widely used in aqueous firefighting foams (AFFFs) to combat fires at 
military facilities, airports and industrial sites.  
The coal mining town Svea, located in Svalbard in the Arctic was closed down in 2017 after 
nearly 100 years of operation. During its operations various sites have been utilised for 
firefighting drills and PFOS, as well as fluorotelomer substances (FTSs) have been detected 
in high concentrations. The current plan of the mining company Store Norske (SNSK) is to 
delineate the full extent of the contamination and “dig and haul” the soil to mainland Norway 
for disposal.  
1.1 Objectives 
The objectives of this study were two-fold. The focus of the first sections is to understand the 
environmental fate of PFOS and FTSs in Svea through analysis of samples from the 
contaminated sites and bearing in mind the physiochemical properties of the substances and 
the unique aspects of the Arctic region. The majority of background information into the 
environmental fate and ecotoxicology throughout the study will focus on PFOS due to it posing 
the highest risk in Svea and the abundance of literature on this substance in comparison to 
other PFASs.  
The second focus of this study is to evaluate the applicability of phytoremediation in Svea. 
The toxicity and possibility of plant growth was investigated using the willow toxicity and root 
elongation test. Mass balance calculations were performed to determine the time-frame for 
phytoextraction and the effectiveness of phytostabilisation. Bioaccumulation of PFOS, due to 
plant uptake of PFASs, in the terrestrial food-chain was also considered. Finally, further 
remediation methods to apply in conjunction with phytoremediation were also explored. 
The tested hypotheses in this study were: (i) that willow toxicity and root elongation tests will 
indicate low levels of toxicity supporting the applicability of phytoremediation in Svea, (ii) that 
phytostabilisation will be more appropriate in Svea than phytoextraction and (iii) that risk to 
the terrestrial food chain and humans will be minimal.

2. PFASs  
PFASs entail more than 3000 synthetic chemicals that are used in a wide range of applications 
in industry as surfactants and lubricants and in consumer products such as food packaging 
(OECD, 2002). The compounds resist heat and chemical attack because of the strong carbon-
fluorine bond (Krafft & Riess, 2015), making these molecules resistant to hydrolysis, 
photolysis, microbial degradation and metabolism by animals (Giesy & Kannan, 2001) and 
have been dubbed “forever chemicals”, due to their persistence in the environment (Butt, et 
al., 2010). Perfluoroalkyl acids (PFAAs) are a group of chemicals that are a part of the 
perfluoroalkyl substances family (Katarzynska, et al., 2019). PFAAs are a fully fluorinated 
carbon chain and are more persistent in the environment than polyfluoroalkyl compounds, 
which consist of some C-H bonds (Katarzynska, et al., 2019). PFAAs consist of a polar head 
that is hydrophilic and a hydrophobic alkyl chain. This hydro- and lipophobic nature makes the 
substance unique and ideal for use in products such as food packaging as it repels both water 
and grease/oil (Kucharzyk, et al., 2017). The most well studied PFAAs are perfluorinated 
carboxylic acids (PFCAs) and perfluorinated sulfonic acids (PFSAs), due to their ubiquitous 
presence in the abiotic and biotic environment (Butt, et al., 2010; Wang, et al., 2013).  
Long-chain PFCAs and PFSAs are of the most concern due to their ability to bioaccumulate 
(Wang, et al., 2013). For the sake of consistency, the Organisation for Economic Co-operation 
and Development (OECD) defined “long” chain PFASs as having 6 or more perfluorinated 
carbons and PFACs with 7 or more perfluorinated carbons (OECD, 2011). The most well 
researched long-chain PFASs are perflurooctanesulfonic acid (PFOS) and perfluorooctanoic 
acid (PFOA), both of which consist of a long hydrophobic atom chain, fully saturated with 
fluorine atoms and a hydrophilic polar functional group (Kucharzyk, et al., 2017). Figure 1 
shows the structure of these two compounds. PFSAs and PFCAs have a different number of 
C atoms to be classified as “long-chain” because PFSAs with the same number of C atoms 
have an extra C-F moiety, as the carboxylic acid contains a carbon atom and the sulfonic 
functional group does not. This can be seen with PFOS and PFOA, as both contain eight 
carbon atoms, but PFOS has 8 C-F moieties and PFOA only has 7 of these moieties.  
 
Figure 1. The structure of the two most well-known PFAAs. PFOA possessing a carboxylic acid functional group 
(PFCA) and PFOS possessing a sulfonic acid functional group (PFSA) (Ahrens, et al., 2011).

2.1 PFOS production and use 
PFOS was in widespread production and use until the beginning of the century. Although 
PFOS is often the most abundant PFAS detected in the environment, perfluorooctane sulfonyl 
fluoride (POSF) was produced in far greater quantities. PFOS is a result of the degradation of 
precursors chemicals such as POSF, and in total, 96 precursor chemicals have been identified 
that contain the PFOS moiety (C8F17SO3-). The 3M company was responsible for a 
significant proportion of worldwide production of POSF since the 1950s (Butt, et al., 2010; 
Oliaei, et al., 2013). Annual production of the PFOS precursors by 3M in 2000 and in other 
countries (e.g. Germany) was estimated to be 4650 tons (Paul, et al., 2010). Since then, 
production of PFOS and its relevant precursors has been phased out by 3M in between 2000 
and 2002. An overview of POSF production between 1970 and 2004 can be seen in Figure 2. 
As of 2013, PFOS related compounds were produced in Germany, Italy and China (Oliaei, et 
al., 2013). 
PFASs have been used in a wide range of commercial household items such as Scotchgard®, 
Teflon®, and Gore-Tex®. PFOS specifically has been used as a grease and water repellent 
on carpets and textiles, paper and cardboard. Its application in carpets was estimated to 
account for 14-48% of use, paper and packaging 15-28% and the use in aqueous film 
firefighting foams (AFFFs) as 6-16%. One of the major reasons for the increase in production 
in the 1970s (see Figure 2) was the widespread adoption of PFOS-based firefighting foams 
(Paul, et al., 2010). 
 
Figure 2. The estimated global production of POSF between 1970-2002. The different coloured lines representing 
different estimation methods and values. Figure taken from Paul, et al., (2010).  
2.1.1 Aqueous Film Forming Foams (AFFFs) 
Aqueous Film Forming Foams (AFFFs) are foam mixtures that are used in combating liquid 
hydrocarbon fires (Kissa, 2001). The use of PFASs in AFFFs, gives it the ability to lower the 
surface-tension of the burning liquid and thereby rapidly spread across the surface of fuel. 
Fluoro-based surfactants are unique among typical hydrocarbon surfactants in that the 
hydrophobic chain is also oleophobic so that the compound is able to form a film at the 
interface between hydrocarbons and water (Kissa, 2001). The film thereby resists the diffusion 
of flammable vapours and in this way provides a high degree of sealability, which limits the 
contact of the fuel source with oxygen (Schaefer, et al., 2008). AFFFs containing PFOS 
precursors were preferred because of its effectiveness, long shelf life and ease of use (Moody 
& Field, 2000; Moody, et al., 2003). Since the increased regulation of PFOS, AFFF production

is increasingly based on fluorotelomer substances (FTSs) such as 6:2 fluorotelomer (6:2-
FTS), which have been detected after the use of AFFFs (Karrman, et al., 2011).  
The use of Aqueous Film Forming Foams (AFFFs) has been identified as a major polluter of 
PFOS in the environment, despite only about 5% of the total production being used in AFFFs 
(Paul, et al., 2010). PFOS-based AFFFs were used extensively in Firefighting Training Sites 
(FFTSs) at military bases, airports and industrial areas and released vast quantities of PFASs 
into the environment in short durations.  
2.2 Environmental fate and transport 
Decades of production and use has led to PFOS being found ubiquitously in the natural 
environment. The POSF precursor is transformed into PFOS in the environment due to 
hydrolysis (Martin, et al., 2010). PFOS has been detected in the entirety of the Nordic region 
and high concentrations were found particularly in landfill effluents (108 ng/L) and sewage 
sludge (18.3 ng/L) that are regarded as a primary source of the substances in the environment 
(Kallenborn, 2004). As stated above, AFFFs are also now considered a major point PFASs in 
the environment with soil concentrations in excess of 1000 µg/kgdw being detected at airports 
in the Nordic region. Its fate in soil will be the focus, given the nature of the contamination in 
Svea. The physiochemical properties of PFOS have dictate its fate in the environment have

The leaching of PFOS into groundwater resources has been shown to occur in a number of 
agricultural sites in Europe in which concentrations of PFOS were detected up to 150 μg/L 
(Wilhelm, et al., 2008). It is regarded as very slightly soluble at a temperature of 25°C in fresh 
water (3M, 2001), but once the PFOS has dissolved in water, migration potential drastically 
increases and will not be broken down biotically or abiotically in the water.  
The soil compartment is regarded as a significant sink for PFOS. Losses from the soil into the 
atmosphere due not occur due to a low Henrys law constant (KH), indicating that it is essentially 
non-volatile (OECD, 2002). PFOS has also been shown to have high sorption capabilities (i.e. 
Kd 35.3 L/kg in sandy loam) predominantly due to hydrophobic bonding with organic matter in 
the soil (Beach, et al., 2006). The hydrophobic attraction existing between the long CF tail of 
PFOS and organic matter in the soil. Lower pH has also been found to increase the sorption 
of PFOS (Wang & Shih, 2011). The compound has a negative log of the acid dissociation 
constant (pKa) value is -3.3, indicating that it is a strong acid and is present in the environment 
completely in the ionised form (Brooke, et al., 2004). Thus, electrostatic attraction plays a role 
in the adsorption of PFOS and is elevated at a lower pH. Higher concentrations of Ca+2 have 
been shown to increase sorption as the positive ions reduce the negative charge of organic 
matter, decreasing the repulsion experienced by the anionic head of PFOS (Higgins & Luthy, 
2006).  
The persistence of PFOS in the soil is concerning for the bioaccumulation of it in the food 
chain. Brignole, et al., (2003) determined bioconcentrations factors (BCFs) for seven plant 
species exposed to soil concentrations ranging between 3.63-278 mg/kg. BCFs for the 
vegetative tissue of the plants was found in the range of 2-4.3. PFOS has been found to 
concentrate more in the roots of plants than the aboveground vegetation (Gredejl, et al., 
submitted). BCFs for earthworms (Eisenia fetida) of 2.6 were found to be between 1.23 to 13.9 
for soil of varying PFOS concentrations after AFFF application (Das, et al., 2015).  
2.4 Ecotoxicology  
PFOS is unique among POPs in that the compound is known to bind to blood proteins and 
largely accumulate in the liver, kidneys and bile secretions (Jones, et al., 2003b) as opposed 
to most POPs, which tend to accumulate in lipid rich tissues. Exposure to PFOS has been 
found to lead to developmental toxicity, immunotoxicity endocrine disruption and carcinogenic 
(DeWitt, 2015). There are relatively limited studies on the ecotoxicity of PFOS in soil (Zhao, et 
al., 2011). The toxicity of PFOS has been determined for earthworms (Eisenia fetida) and 
plants. The toxicity to earthworms is not as relevant to Svalbard as they do not habituate the 
region. Brignole, et al., (2003) found the concentration of PFOS in which the survival was 
inhibited by 50% (EC50) for seven plant species varied from 57 to over 100 mg/kg. The no 
observed effect concentration (NOEC) for the growth of the plants ranged from 3.91 to 
62.5 mg/kg. The root elongation of three different species was determined to be a more 
sensitive endpoint than seed germination (Li, 2009).  
2.5 Limit values 
Guideline values provided by the Danish EPA, (2015) for soil concentrations are scarce, being 
provided from only two countries, Norway and the Netherlands. The Norwegian Environmental 
Agency has a limit value of 100 μg PFOS/kg soil, this being based on the studies of 
earthworms (Stubberud, 2006). It is expected the limit value in soil will be lowered to 2 µg/kg 
(Pedersen & Evenset, 2018). The Netherlands have limit values of 2.3 μg PFOS/kg in soil 
(RIVM, 2011).

3. PFASs in the Arctic  
Concern regarding contaminants in the Arctic began in the early 1970s and POPs and heavy 
metals were subsequently found in surprisingly high levels in the late 1970s and early 1980s 
(Ottar, 1981). The Arctic Monitoring and Assessment Programme (AMAP) was established to 
address such issues in 1991 by the circumpolar countries: Canada, Denmark/Greenland, 
Iceland, Norway, Sweden, the Soviet Union and the USA (Wit & Muir, 2010). The pollution of 
POPs, heavy metals, petroleum hydrocarbons and stratospheric ozone depletion were among 
the environmental concerns of the AMAP (AMAP, 1998). Developments in analytical 
techniques and technology (e.g. LC-MS/MS) has contributed to the detection of a concerning 
degree of contamination present in the Arctic, including PFASs (Giesy and Kannan 2001). 
Since 2001, a large body of research has been established on the levels of PFASs in the 
environment and the potential transport pathways into the Arctic (Butt, et al., 2010).  
 
The focus of PFASs in the Arctic environment has largely been on the long-range transport 
(LRT) of the chemicals, which occurs via two pathways – primarily via direct oceanic transport 
of ionic PFASs and to a lesser extent, the LRT in the atmosphere of volatile precursor 
compounds – see Figure 3 (Skaar, 2016; Muir & Wit, 2010). North America and Western 
Europe are the major sources of these substances to the Arctic and it is estimated that only 
1 percent of the total PFOS production has, as of yet, reached the ocean (Muir & Wit, 2010; 
Paul et al. 2010). A number of monitoring studies have been conducted in the Canadian Arctic 
throughout the early 2000s that have detected PFOS in the abiotic environment (e.g. snow 
caps - 12-147 pg/L) and in the liver of polar bears and arctic foxes at concentrations of 3100 
and 250 µg/kgww, respectively (Martin, et al., 2004; Young, et al., 2007). The contribution of 
local sources of PFASs in the Canadian Arctic were also identified by Stock, et al., (2007) due 
to sewage and wastewater discharge. Relatively few studies have been conducted in the 
European Arctic, but more recently PFASs have been detected ubiquitously in the Svalbard 
environment (Ahrens, et al., 2016; Butt, et al., 2010; Routti, et al., 2016) and local 
contamination sources of PFOS have been identified (Kwok, et al., 2013; Skaar, et al., 2019).

3.1 Svalbard 
Svalbard is an archipelago located in the Arctic, about halfway between Norway and the North 
Pole, see Figure 4. The mean annual air temperature is -6 oC, ranging from -15 oC in February 
to 6.2 oC in July and about 60% of the islands are covered by glaciers (Ingolfsson, 2004). 
Deposition and accumulation of pollutants via LRT is a concern for the entire Arctic region; 
however, the relatively short distance of Svalbard from continental Europe (800 km north of 
the Scandinavian Peninsula) makes it particularly susceptible to atmospheric emissions from 
industry, forest fires etc. (Kallenborn, et al., 2012; Kozak, et al., 2013). Furthermore, the region 
is characterised by northward flowing oceanic currents that come from the Americas and 
Europe, transporting POPs.

The background concentration of ∑PFASs in seawater, freshwater and soil in Svalbard were 
found to be low to ultra-low (several nanograms per litre) and up to 14 pg/L in ice cores of a 
glacier due to atmospheric LRT (Kwok, et al., 2013; Skaar, et al., 2019). The concentration of 
PFOS in reindeer, liver, fat and muscle from Svalbard was found to be 0.45 µg/kgww (Carlsson, 
et al., 2012). Much higher concentrations of PFOS have been recorded in plasma samples of 
polar bears on Svalbard (671 µg/kgww) and in the arctic fox liver (1299 µg/kgww). The high 
concentration of PFOS in Arctic apex predators illustrates the ability of the substance to 
bioaccumulate and Routti, et al., (2017) has shown that top predators in Svalbard contain 
higher quantities of PFASs (15-35%) when they have a higher proportion of marine food in 
their diet. Bioaccumulation of PFOS in the terrestrial environment still needs to be considered, 
with a study in the Canadian Arctic on lichen, reindeer and wolves, determining 
biomagnification factors (BMFs) up to 9.1 for reindeer grazing on vegetation (Müller, et al., 
2011). Svalbard is home all year-round to only two herbivores, the Svalbard reindeer (Rangifer 
tarandus platyrhyncus) and the Svalbard rock ptarmigan (Lagopus muta hyperborea). 
Local sources of pollution of PFASs have been identified in Svalbard close to Longyearbyen, 
in which concentrations of PFOS were found to increase from 50 to 200 pg/L downstream of 
the glacier, due to local sources in the town such as skiing activities (Freberg, et al., 2010; 
Kwok, et al., 2013). Landfill leachate, FFTSs and waste water were all identified as local point 
sources on Svalbard, leading to concentrations 100-fold higher than background 
concentrations (Ahrens, et al., 2016; Skaar, et al., 2019). The highest ΣPFAS concentrations 
were detected in soil from a FFTS (1600 µg/kgdw) and in a nearby pond (~2750 ng/L) (Ahrens, 
et al., 2016). FFTSs that adopt AFFF usage have been identified as a major source of PFASs 
for Longyearbyen and Ny Ålseund. Svea is another settlement on the island that is a former 
Norwegian coal mine, being closed in 2017 after nearly 100 years of activity in which AFFFs 
were applied at FFTSs.

Svea is located on central Spitsbergen, approximately 45 km southeast from Longyearbyen, 
in the heart of Van Mijenfjord, see

leaving behind a landscape of steep mountain slopes and periglacial features including 
permafrost up to 500 m thick and irregular topography due moraine debris (Ingolfsson, 2008).  
The region is characterised by sedimentary mountains that rise up to approximately 1000 m 
and valley glaciers. The mine site is owned by Store Norske (SNSG) and has no connection 
via roads, being only accessible via water or air in the summer and via snow mobile in the

housed approximately 225 people (Pedersen & Evenset, 2018). In the beginning of the century 
it was the most productive coal mine in Svalbard, with 4 million tonnes being produced

3.2.1 Avfallsfylling

was utilised as a FFTS in the years of 1996-2004 and AFFFs containing PFOS used 
(Pedersen & Evenset, 2018). In 2018, 22 soil samples were analysed from 14 various points 
depicted in Figure 8. PFASs were detected in all 22 of the soil samples, in which the highest 
concentration of ∑PFASs was 3905 µg/kgdw. PFOS was the most abundant compound and 
detected in all 22 of the samples, in which five were over the current limit value of 100 µg/kgdw

also running southwards on the west side of the dump, exiting at KA into the fjord. The ability 
of these water masses to distribute PFOS was demonstrated, with a concentration of 2500 ng 
PFOS/L detected 1 km downstream from the landfill site at KA.

3.3 Remediation in the Arctic  
The Arctic region is characterised by isolated regions subject to a harsh climate, that make 
traditional remediation strategies typically adopted in lower latitudes infeasible and costly 
(Poland, et al., 2003). The remoteness is one of the major barriers to the development of 
remediation technology in the Arctic as well as the cold temperatures and permafrost. The 
region is characterised by restricted working periods of only several months during the 
summer. Thus, traditional soil remediation strategies are often reliant on “dig and haul” 
methods in which the contaminated soil is excavated and treated elsewhere (Poland, et al., 
2003.  
Remediation can be broken down into the primary and secondary impacts. The primary being 
the local risk of the contamination and the secondary being the impacts generated by the 
remediation treatment (Lemming, et al., 2012). The isolation of Svea and the lack of human 
activity in the area will make it difficult to determine or conceptualise the primary impacts of 
the contamination on the local environment. It is essential that the environmental impact of the 
remediation is minimised so that it does not exceed the primary impacts of the contamination 
being left in Svea. In this regard, remote locations such as Svea are well suited to the use of 
in-situ treatment. This can be applied on site and is ideal for the Arctic because it minimises 
the transport of materials and lower environmental impacts on the local (i.e. ecologically) and 
global scale (i.e. emissions) (Camenzuli, et al., 2015; Martin & Ruby, 2004). An example of 
such a form of remediation is the landfarming of petroleum hydrocarbons which has proven 
an effective treatment option (Filler, et al., 2006; McCarthy, et al., 2004). It can be considered 
under the bioremediation branch of options that exist in the Arctic; however, these options are 
not applicable to PFASs because of the substance’s resistance to biodegradation.   
Remediation methods for PFASs are still in their infancy and there is a lack of commercially 
applicable remediation techniques for soil (Sorengård, et al., 2019; Yao, et al., 2015). Several 
of the proposed treatment methods for PFASs in a review by Yao, et al., (2015) such as 
groundwater pump-and-treat remediation is not relevant for the Arctic and the incineration of 
the soil is not feasible due to the large quantity and lack of such a facility in Svalbard. 
Phytoremediation and soil amendment were identified as in-situ treatment options to consider 
in the context of the contamination in Svea.  
3.3.1 Svea  
Phytoremediation is a broad concept that utilises plants as a means to treat contaminated air, 
soil, surface- or groundwater. It is a promising strategy to remediate contaminated sites as it 
is a low impact in-situ method that is cost-efficient and applicable for a range of contaminants 
(Chirakkara, et al., 2016; Gobelius, et al., 2017). Phytoremediation is also well accepted by 
the general public and has the ability to enhance the ecology of the area in comparison to 
physical and/or chemical remediation options (Trapp & Karlson, 2001). It has been trialled and 
proven to be an effective method of remediation with a number of different contaminants 
including: metals, petroleum hydrocarbons and PAHs (Gerhardt, et al., 2017; Trapp & Karlson, 
2001). High BCFs of PFASs have been found (Gredejl, et al., submitted) and it was concluded 
by Gobelius, et al., (2017) that phytoremediation is the most sustainable option available for 
PFAS contamination at a FFTS in Sweden. Promising uptake values of PFASs along with the 
benefits of phytoremediation at Svea; low initial and ongoing costs, suitability to large areas, 
minimal equipment and energy demands and acceptance by the general public make this 
method a highly appropriate option (Camenzuli, et al., 2013; Trapp & Karlson, 2001). 
Downsides of phytoremediation are the long treatment times (Clausen, et al., 2017). 
==PAGE:24==
J. Crowe, DTU Master’s Thesis: PFAS contamination in Svea, Svalbard 
15 
 
Furthermore, apart from one study, cases of phytoremediation do not exist in the Arctic and 
the availability of suitable plant species needs to be taken into consideration for 
phytoremediation in Svea. 
Immobilisation of PFASs in the soil via the use of soil amendments is an in-situ remediation 
strategy that has been adopted for a range of pollutants. It involves mixing an amendment 
material (i.e. clay) with the contaminated soil by use of conventional earth-moving equipment 
(Martin & Ruby, 2004). This has the effect of reducing the water solubility by promoting 
precipitation, change of oxidation state and adsorption (Kumplene, et al., 2008) and thereby 
limiting the transport of the pollutants from the site. Soil amendment is regarded as more cost 
effective and sustainable than “dig and haul” options and appropriate for large-scale 
remediation projects (Harbottle, et al., 2007). The movement of PFOS is largely dictated by 
adsorption processes due to hydrophobic interaction with organic carbon in the soil (Higgins 
& Luthy, 2006) and various studies have investigated the sorption of PFASs to a range of 
materials; clay, activated carbon, a modified commercial clay adsorbent and a number of 
mineral materials for use as an amendment material (3M, 2001; Das, et al., 2013; Hale, et al., 
2017; Sorengård, et al., 2019). An extremely high Kd value of 16,940 L/kg was found for 
activated carbon amended soil and reductions in leaching of 99.9% in comparison to untreated 
soil were found in batch tests (Hale, et al., 2017). Limited cases of the use of soil amendment 
remediation in the field were found for polar regions. One case of heavy metal stabilisation via 
phosphate fixation could be found in Antarctica (Hafsteindottir, et al., 2014). One of the major 
downsides of this technology is achieving a homogenous mix, particularly when the polluted 
soil is highly heterogeneous and pockets of contamination exist (Martin & Ruby, 2004). The 
cost and environmental impact of the treatment is highly dependent on the availability and cost 
of materials for amendment. In the case of Svea, an abundance of coal is available for this 
purpose, which would limit the environmental impact and cost of the remediation.  
The two possible remediation options have a number of advantages for the contamination in 
Svea. Soil amendment is a promising remediation option for PFASs in the lab setting and the 
applicability of coal from Svea should be investigated as an amendment material. 
Phytoremediation is a relatively unproven technology for PFASs and particularly in the Arctic. 
A number of unknowns exist for this treatment option; suitability of plant species, phytotoxicity 
of the soil and uptake capability of plants in a real-world context. The unknowns regarding the 
feasibility of phytoremediation are more obtainable within the scope of this study and explored 
further. Soil amendment will complement the discussion of remediation scenarios.  
3.4 Phytoremediation 
The flow chart in Figure 11 from Trapp, et al., (2014) that is adapted from earlier decision trees 
(ITRC, 1999; Trapp, 2000) was utilised to provide guidance in assessing the applicability of 
phytoremediation in Svea. 1) Plant growth was not observed in AF due to human activity; 
however, this will not continue and plants were observed in many areas in Svea such as LB. 
2) One of the major disadvantages of phytoremediation is that it takes at least several growing 
seasons before any change can be detected and therefore requires a long time period for 
treatment (Khan et al. 2004). The owners of contaminated land, typically demand or expect 
fast remediation times (less than 1-2 years) due to the value of the land and desire to 
redevelop and/or environment and human health issues the contamination is causing (Song 
et al., 2018). This is not an issue in Svea, due to the isolation and lack of human contact, 
remediation time of decades will not affect human populations and this land will not be utilised.

3) Phytotoxicity data of PFASs and PFOS on plants is available; however, predominantly for

Phytoremediation can be classified into 5 main types: phytoextraction, phytodegradation, 
rhizofiltration, phytostabilisation and phytovolatilization (ARS, 2000). Phytodegradation and 
phytovolatilization are not relevant for the remediation of PFASs due to the recalcitrant nature 
of the compounds to degradation and low volatilisation. Rhizofiltration is also not relevant 
given that this method utilises the roots of plants to remove pollutants from water. 
Phytoextraction relies on the plants to accumulate pollutants in the harvestable parts, so that 
the compounds can be removed from the environment (Salt, et al., 1998). Phytostabilization 
is a method of the establishment of a vegetative cover on contaminated sites, aims to reduce 
the mobility of pollutants within the root zone through immobilization within the rhizosphere or 
==PAGE:26==
J. Crowe, DTU Master’s Thesis: PFAS contamination in Svea, Svalbard 
17 
 
within living root biomass (Bolan et al., 2011). The use of plant covers is regarded as one of 
the most effective means in reducing erosion (Zuazo & Pleguezuelo, 2009). Plant cover means 
that water runoff is reduced and the infiltration of water into the soil compartment is enhanced. 
Soil is also fixed within the roots and reduces the energy of droplets on the soil. Several factors 
such as the depth of the contamination, uptake of pollutants need to be taken into 
consideration for the viability of these two options. 
Phytoremediation is not suitable for contaminant plumes greater than the depth of roots 
(typically 3 m) as it is essential that the plants are in reach of the contaminants (Canadell et 
al., 1996). The depth of the contamination is often one of the limiting factors for the application 
of phytoremediation (Canadell et al., 1996; Ghosh and Singh 2005). The Arctic is somewhat 
ideal in that the permafrost acts as a barrier to the downwards migration of pollution and roots 
often grow down to the permafrost (Wal, et al., 2000). The highest concentrations of 
contamination in Svea are generally located in the upper profile of the soil (0-0.5 m).  
The uptake of PFASs has been demonstrated for a number of agricultural crops, grasses and 
common trees (e.g. birch).  The BCF, ratio of pollutant concentration in the plant to that in the 
soil, is often used to determine the potential of a substance for its phytoextraction potential. 
Plants that have a BCF greater than 1 are generally regarded as a hyperaccumulator (in the 
case of metal uptake) (Yoon, et al., 2006) and are generally considered suitable for 
phytoextraction. BCFs greater than 1 were found for PFOS by Gredejl, et al., (submitted). The 
translocation factor (TF), is also often used to assess the feasibility of a plant species for 
phytoextraction. The TF is less than 1 for PFOS in plants, given the BCF for the shoots is 1.37 
and 5.18 in the roots, indicating that the plant tends to accumulate PFOS in the roots. This 
makes PFOS more suited to phytostabilisation, particularly given the lower primary 
productivity in the Arctic region and the much higher biomass in the roots compared with 
aboveground biomass (i.e. stems and foliage) (Jackson, et al., 1996). Phytoextraction will still 
be considered, given BCFs are greater than 1 and because of potentially promising values 
obtained for FTSs in birch trees (Gobelius, et al., 2017). Thus, phytoremediation throughout 
the text will refer to phytoextraction and/or phytostabilisation and the mechanisms of these 
two options in an Arctic context can be seen in Figure 12 below.

3.4.1 Polar willow 
Two possible methods for the use of willows in phytoremediation have been proposed by 
Punshon, et al., (1996): (i) using the willows for the purpose of phytostabilisation in limiting the 
spreading of pollutants in the environment, with minimal uptake of the contaminants into the 
environment; and (ii) the accumulation of high quantities of pollutants in the willows for the 
purpose of phytoextraction. Several species of willow reside on Svalbard, the most prevelant 
being Salix polaris (polar willow). It occupies many substrates within the subarctic tundra at 
high-altitudes and the arctic (Hrynkiewicz, et al., 2009). It is found throughout the Svalbard 
archipelago and exhibits one of the greatest tolerances to adverse environmental conditions 
(Dormann, 2003). S. polaris is regarded as a typical pioneer coloniser of Arctic substrates and 
as a dominant species in well-developed ecosystems, often being the only vascular plant 
species in various habitats (Muraoka, et al., 2002).  
It is a dwarf shrub that is deciduous and has creeping woody stems that can form a dense mat 
over 1 m² large - see Figure 13 (Dormann, 2003; Opala-Owczarek, et al., 2018). Typical of 
Arctic species, the root mass fraction constitutes highly to the total biomass of the plant 
(Mokany et al. 2006). The annual ring width of S. polaris is the largest at a depth between 0-
10 cm (Buchwal, et al., 2013), maximum depth of the roots being limited by the permafrost 
(Wal, et al., 2000). The species is said to have an essential role in carbon sequestration in 
Svalbard and is also one of a few ectomycorrhizal plants on Svalbard (Murako, et al., 2002). 
 
Figure 13. The salix polaris plant. A - the plant spreads out like a mat. B - the species in flower 
S. polaris has been suggested as a bioindicator of metals and as a species for the 
phytoremediation of specific metals (Cd and Zn) on the islands of Svalbard. BCFs up to 60 for 
Cd and 8.6 for Zn were determined by Krajcarova, et al., (2016). Thus, given its range of 
habitats and known relevance to phytoremediation, it was further investigated for applicability 
to Svea (Wojtuń, et al., 2013; Wojtuń, et al., 2019;). The species also has a circumpolar 
distribution; thus, insights obtained from this study will benefit phytoremediation opportunities 
across the Arctic region.

4.3 Willow tree toxicity test

developed by associated professor Stefan Trapp at DTU were utilised – see Trapp, et al.,

transpire more. Thus, by measuring the amount of transpiration of various trees with and 
without exposure to contaminants, the health of the plants can be determined and thereby the 
toxicity of the soil. The benefit of using transpiration as a means to determine the soil toxicity 
instead of growth or root length, is that the test is much faster, typically indicating changes in

The foc values were similar for the coal (32.2%) and two samples from FT (27.9 and 21.3%). 
The samples from FT, visually appeared to be have the highest coal content (i.e. were the 
darkest), which is supported by the higher foc. The foc was lower at LB (10.0%) and AF18.1 
(11.0%). The lowest foc was from the second profile at AF18 (2.81%), which was taken from 
the second profile at AF18 (0.7-1 m), suggesting the coal content is only present in the surface 
layer. Soil on Svalbard is generally characterised by a low foc (i.e. 0.2%) due to a lower density 
of biomass in comparison to lower latitudes (Kotas, et al., 2018). Coal has been used on the 
surface as a backfill or for the construction of a pad at LB, AF and FT. Foc content as high as 
8.1% could be found for natural soil in Svalbard; however, this value could also be due to a 
high coal content as it is predominant substance throughout the archipelago (Kotas et al., 
2018).  
The fate of PFOS in the soil is strongly dictated by the organic content of the soil. Such high 
foc values imply that PFOS will be strongly bound within the soil compartment. Gellrich, et al., 
(2012) conducted flow-through column experiments and found that after 70 weeks, PFOS was 
eluted in soil with an organic content of 2%. However, in tests with an organic content of 7% 
and 14%, elution of PFOS after 2 years was not recorded. Kupryianchyk, et al., (2016) found 
that the Kd increased from 3.16 to 27.7 L/kg for three different soils with a foc of 0.2, 9.5 and 
34%. The foc of the surface soil at AF and LB is higher than 10% and it would be expected the 
soil acts as a significant sink for the PFOS. The lower foc of the second layer (2.81%), would 
imply that the PFOS moves more freely through this soil.  
The Kd values that were calculated, using the log koc (2.57 - 3.1) and determined foc, were 
expected to be high given the high carbon content, which they were not. Far higher values 
(35.3) have been found in sandy loam soils (Beach, et al., 2006). Calculating the Kd value from 
the Koc is problematic with PFASs, due to their hydrophobic and lipophobic nature which is 
exemplified in the determined values in Table 11.  
The soil samples were very coarse grained (VCG - 1 to 2 mm) or granules (2 to 4 mm). The 
coarsest sediment was found at LB which consisted of 64% gravel (> 2mm), 33% sand (0.063 
to 2 mm) and 3% silt and clay fines (< 0.063 mm) and the finest (not including the coal) was 
from AF18.2 which was 41% gravel (> 2mm), 57% sand (0.063 to 2 mm) and 2% silt and clay 
fines (< 0.063 mm). Besides for the coal, the percentage of silt and clay fines were less than 
5% for all of the samples. It is expected that the permeability is high through the soil because

of the coarse sized particles. The particle size analysis data and graphs can be seen in

The pH for the majority of samples was slightly basic, with values of approximately 8. The 
sample from LB was the only exception with a mildly acidic pH of 5.09. The pH values of the 
samples were all slightly basic besides for LB. The samples that had a pH of approximately 8 
is in line with results obtained in Kotas, et al., (2018). The low pH of soil is not typical of the 
area and could be due to acid-mine drainage as there is a large deposition of waste rock 
immediately above the site. Acid mine drainage has been documented on Svalbard due to the 
high sulphide mineral content in the coal (Banks, et al., 2002). In the early summer, the surface 
snow melts and drains down through LB. The pH was found to be as low as 5.2 in water 
samples taken further downstream from LB (Pedersen & Evenset, 2018). 
Phosphorous (P) content of the soil was low for all of the samples. Arctic soils are known to 
be relatively devoid of nutrients; thus, the low P was expected. The range of P content, varied 
from 292 mg/kgdw at LB to 999 mg/kgdw at AF18.1, is similar to typical sediment concentrations 
in Svalbard (Ziołek & Melke, 2014). The concentration of P was substantially lower at LB than 
the other sites. This could be due to the increased erosion in the area because of the slope 
that LB lies on as increases in erosion are known to deplete soils of N and P in Svalbard 
(Sendstad, 1980). Microbial biomass has been shown to account for 6-49% of the total soil P, 
which was not determined in this case (Hrynkiewicz, et al., 2009). 
The results of the soil characterisation will support the discussion on the contamination of 
PFASs in Svea, the environmental fate of PFASs in the area and potential of remediation 
options.

present in the three samples; LB32.1, AF18.1 and AF18.2. The PFASs in these samples will 
be analysed and also the wider contamination at the whole site. The contamination of PBDE 
in FT36.1 and 37.1 can be seen in Appendix B.

that were found in the highest concentration were 6:2 FTS (240 µg/kgdw) and 8:2 (140 µg/kgdw).

FTSs constituted 76% of the ∑PFASs at LB, indicating the use of post-PFOS products at the

Svalbard due to 6:2-FTS making up 2% of the ∑PFAS contamination. Thus, 76% of the

been used in AFFFs as a substitute to PFOS based AFFFs since the beginning of the century 
due to a ban of AFFFs containing PFOS (2007 in Norway) and are generally regarded as less 
harmful to the environment (Hoke et al., 2015).  
The concentration of FTSs is the biggest environmental concern at LB. 6:2-FTS has attracted 
considerable attention in Norway since the ban of PFOS containing AFFFs in 2007, as FTSs 
(or short chain PFASs) have been used as an alternative. An oil refinery reported 
concentrations as high as 2365 µg 6:2-FTS/kgdw in Norway, in which it was only detected in 
the top 10 cm (Amundsen, et al., 2008). The sediment at the oil refinery had a high foc of 29-
50% and fine sediment size, which may be responsible for the lack of downwards migration. 
6:2-FTS was found at an airport in Norway at concentrations of 2155 µg/kgdw in the top 5cm 
layer and at a concentration of 1241 µg/kgdw at a depth of 30-50 cm. foc was much lower (2%) 
in this soil and 90% of the fraction was sand, potentially explaining the greater downwards 
migration.  
Samples were also taken for the second and third profile at LB32.  It is evident that FTSs were 
detected down to a depth of 2 m, so essentially to the bottom of the active layer of permafrost 
- see Table 12. The soil at LB had a high foc of 10.0%, which would suggest that the substances 
are strongly bound to the soil. The vertical migration of the contaminants may be explained by 
the strong affinity of PFASs with organic carbon and the unique annual freezing and thawing 
that occurs in the Arctic. The annual freezing and thawing leads to the accumulation of fine 
soil particles in the deeper layers and a higher content of large particles on the surface (Wolfe 
and Thieme, 1964). Thus, FTSs that are bound strongly to the finer particles on the surface 
will migrate vertically over time. The granule sediment size of the soil in LB (3.13 mm) will also 
increase permeability of water and most likely fine particles through the pores.

A second hotspot of contamination was identified in the western side of LB (circled in Figure

µg/kgdw (not shown on map). It is evident that the FTSs migrate downstream towards the coast. 
As previously mentioned, the soil in Svea is dry for the majority of the year and most of the 
migration will occur during the melting season (i.e. in early summer). Greater losses of 
contaminants have been reported during the snow melt at sites with steeper topography and 
coarse materials, both of which are the case at LB (Lung & Young 2005).  
FTSs were introduced into AFFFs as environmentally friendly version of PFOS; however, there 
is generally a limited knowledge on the fate of fluorotelomers in the biotic and abiotic 
environment, particularly in the terrestrial environment. The majority of the publications on 
FTSs are in relation to 6:2-FTS. 6:2 FTS has been shown to persist in soil and water for 
decades (Schultz, et al., 2004). It is considered less bioaccumulative and has a lower aquatic 
toxicity than PFSAs of a similar length (PFHxS, PFOS etc.). The lower accumulation being 
due to the ability of fish being able to eliminate the substance. Hoke, et al., (2015) concluded 
that 6:2-FTS is not bioaccumulative and is classified as not hazardous in the aquatic 
environment. A more recent study in the Norwegian Arctic, detected 6:2-FTS in marine 
invertebrates (up to 56.8 μg/kg in the great spider crab), suggesting bioaccumulation does 
occur (Langberg, et al., 2019) and concluded that risk assessments of sites with FTSs should 
include invertebrates as they are food sources for fish, birds, mammals and humans.  
The only predicted no effect concentration (PNEC) determined for 6:2-FTS in soil is 
210 µg/kgdw; in which the concentration of 6:2-FTS exceeds this in five of the samples at LB 
(see Appendix C). The highest concentration of 6:2-FTS exceeds the PNEC by 30 μg/kg; 
however, if the 8:2-FTS is also taken into consideration, the ∑FTSs is 1.8 times greater than 
the PNEC. The concentrations recorded at LB do not seem to pose a major risk to the 
terrestrial environment; however, remediation options will be considered given the spreading 
of the contamination towards the fjord and the current knowledge gaps in regards to the 
ecotoxicology of FTSs.

greater ability to migrate out of the soil in AF18.2 due to the lower foc (2.81%).

5.3.3 Comparison 
One of the requirements of conducting the willow toxicity test in soil is the Kd value to determine 
the concentration of the PFASs in the water. The Kd value of PFOS varies considerably based 
on the foc, among other factors. A Kd for PFOS of 10 L/kg was determined with an organic 
content of 9.5% (Kupryianchyk, et al., 2016); however, a similar Kd value was also determined 
with an organic content of 2% (Baline, et al., 2014). Therefore, a reported range of 2.8-35.3 
L/kg from a review was deemed more suitable for the calculation (Beach, et al., 2006). For 
AF18.1, PFOS concentrations of 200-2,600 µg/L were determined and 50-700 µg/L for 
AF18.2. Of course, ∑PFAS concentration would be higher, but Kd values are not known for 
the other PFASs (i.e. PFHxS). Partitioning coefficients of FTSs are not available, so LB32.1 
was not considered. 
This is the first willow toxicity test that is known of for PFASs. Thus, it is not possible to 
compare this test with others of the same kind. Clausen & Trapp, (2017) conducted a review 
of the EC50 values of various substances in the willow toxicity test. EC50 values as low as 
400 µg/L have been reported for the heavy metal, copper and 450 µg/L for the phenol, 2,3,5,6-
Tetrachlorophenol in 3-day tests (Boeck, 2004; Clausen & Jensen, 2017). EC50 values were 
not determined for this study, as the greatest reduction in transpiration was approximately 
30%; however, these results indicate that it is possible for such a reduction in transpiration to 
occur for the calculated concentrations of PFOS in tests (i.e. 200-2,600 µg/L). The decrease 
in transpiration of plants exposed to PFOS in AF soil could be due to the effects on roots.  
It is known that translocation from roots to the upper compartments of the plant decreases 
with an increase in the length of the carbon chain (Gredejl, et al., submitted). PFOS content 
within the root’s accounts for 55-89% of all the substance in the total plant (Wen, et al., 2016). 
Qu, et al., (2010) found that water supplied to wheat seedlings in concentrations up to 1,000 
µg/L increased the biomass of roots and leaves, whilst concentrations of 10,000 µg/L exerted 
inhibitory effects on the biomass of roots and leaves. The maximum concentration of PFOS in 
AF18.1 (2,600 µg/L) lies between these two values, so it is possible that this is the cause of 
the change in root phenology. Brignole, et al., (2003) reported a NOEC based on the growth 
of seedlings of 3.91-62.5 mg PFOS/kgdw. These results also indicate that the soil 
concentrations in AF soil have negative effects on plant growth.

5.3.4 Root elongation test 1 
Of the 120 seeds used for the testing, only six did not germinate, which were due to errors 
such as seeds growing onto the lid. Thus, the relative germination percentage (RGP) was 
100% for all of the soil types and no signs of toxicity were present due to the germination of 
L. sativum L.  
The relative radicle growth (RRG) and their corresponding 95% confidence interval are 
presented in Figure 30. In the first test, RRG was highest for the fine coal sediment, X̄  130 
(25), and lowest for the sediment from LB, X̄  86 (18). The root elongation for the two samples 
from FT and AF18.1 were very similar to the coal, whilst the sample from AF18.2, X̄  32.5 (4.1) 
was slightly lower than the coal and others. The coal was significantly higher and LB 
significantly lower (p˂0.05) than the control (two-sided t test).

a relatively narrow pH range between 6 and 7, so this could be the reason for the shorter root 
length (Monshausen & Sievers, 2002). The RRG of AF18.2 was slightly lower than the two FT 
samples, AF18.1 and the coal. This could be due to the lower foc (2.81%TS) which reduces 
moisture retention or enabling greater desorption of the PFOS.

5.3.6 Comparison 
Seeds of the pak choi (Brassica chinensis) species were placed in six different types of soil 
and had 50% inhibition (EC50) values ranging from 95 to 4200 mg/kg for PFOS (Zhao, et al., 
2011). These concentrations are at least 10-fold higher than the maximum concentration of 
PFOS in the samples from Svea. It therefore supports the conclusion that the discrepancies 
in root length were a result of soil parameters (i.e. particle size) as opposed to concentration 
of PFASs. EC10 concentrations in Zhao, et al., (2011) were as low as 40 mg/kg for PFOS 
which is also above the highest concentration in AF18.1 (7.2 mg/kg). 
Zhao, et al., (2011) concluded that the amount of organic matter was the most significant factor 
affecting the toxicity of PFOS B. chinensis. Organic matter explained 95% of the variation for 
soils with PFOS. These results indicate that the sorption of PFOS to soil has a great effect on 
the toxicity to plants. The high foc the coal has potentially contributed to the lack of toxicity to 
the seeds, by increasing the sorption of PFOS. 
5.3.7 Implications for Svea 
The toxicity testing conducted showed that the willow plants will be able to grow in the 
contaminated soil in Svea. The willow species in the toxicity testing (S. viminalis) is not the 
same as the one found in Svalbard (S. polaris); however, toxicity data is generally regarded 
as comparable for species of the Salicaceae family (Clausen & Trapp, 2017). One factor that 
needs to be taken into consideration for the application of the results to Svea, is the 
temperature. An increase in temperature is known to increase the toxicity of the substance 
due to higher chemical activity (Cairns, et al., 1975). Thus, lower temperatures in Svalbard, 
will lower the activity of the chemicals and in this way toxicity. However, the harsh conditions 
of the Arctic mean that the plants are under stress due to cold weather and most likely 
availability of water. Plants that are closer to their physical tolerance and subject to more than 
one stress simultaneously are more vulnerable and it is therefore predicted that effects of 
transpiration on the polar willow will be greater (Mittler, 2006).

6.3 Phytoextraction  
The phytoextraction scenario was promising for the “high” uptake scenario of 6:2-FTS at LB. 
This was attributed to the high uptake BCF and relatively low concentration of 6:2-FTS. A 
number of other issues exist for phytoextraction. The harvesting of plants would be 
problematic for the S. polaris due to its small size and also require personnel to travel to Svea 
to harvest the plants. This would add to logistics and costs of the remediation due to the 
difficulties in accessing the site after closure of the town. It is also questionable whether the 
extent of the contamination warrants extraction, as the presence of the contamination at the 
site is not posing a major environmental risk, rather the migration down to the fjord is ost 
concerning. Phytostabilisation is more feasible logistically and will mitigate transport 
downstream. 
Phytoextraction is not feasible due to long remediation times of up to ca. 45,000 years at the 
AF. Clausen, et al., (2017) identified phytoremediation as being a more efficient process for 
substances that are degraded in the rhizosphere. This has not been taken into consideration 
in the calculations, as PFOS is known to be recalcitrant to microbial degradation (Giesy & 
Kannan, 2001). It has been identified by Trapp, et al., (2014) that one of the main limitations 
of phytoremediation via phytoextraction is the long remediation times with time periods of up 
to 12 million years being determined for other contaminants.  
The role of the low NPP in the Arctic on the feasibility of phytoextraction was investigated by 
comparing it to other environments with a higher NPP. The boreal forest was considered as 
an “intermediate” region of NPP and the tropical rainforest was considered as the region with 
the greatest NPP (Huston & Wolverton, 2009). See Appendix E for a table of the results. 
Remediation time of 343 years was calculated for the tropical rainforest, indicating that it is 
not necessarily the low NPP that is responsible for phytoextraction not being feasible in the 
Arctic.  
The phytoextraction of PFOS at the AF and most likely the rest of the Arctic is not a feasible 
option for sites contaminated with PFOS due to extremely long remediation times. The general 
notion, that plants with a BCF above 1 are suitable for phytoremediation is not confirmed in 
this study (Fitz & Wenzel, 2002). The concentration of PFOS at FFTSs is frequently 
documented above 1000 μg/kgdw (Ahrens, et al., 2016; Filipovic, et al., 2015; Hale, et al., 2017 
Høisæter, et al., 2019). Thus, phytoextraction is an infeasible option for FFTSs in the Arctic 
and globally. Phytoextraction being regarded as appropriate in areas of low to moderate levels 
of contamination with high BCFs was confirmed by the results at LB with a high BCF (Kikuchi, 
et al., 2006). However, the high uptake scenario in which the BCF is 653 is highly unlikely. 
6:2-FTS is said to behave similarly in the environment to short chain PFASs such as PFBS 
(Amundsen, et al., 2008). Highest reported BCFs for PFBA in vegetative tissue is 64.8 in maize 
(Blaine, et al., 2013) and 75.25 in the leaves red chicory plants (Gredejl, et al., submitted). 
Thus, a BCF value of 653 should be investigated further. Values of 191.5 were reported for 
the roots of the red chicory plant (Gredejl, et al., submitted); however, values as high as 653 
could not be found.  
6.4 Phytostabilisation  
Phytostabilisation is a more feasible scenario for Svea. Limited quantities of total PFOS will 
be bound to the roots at AF (0.23%); however, the major benefit of the remediation will be on 
the stabilisation and immobilisation of the pollution rather than its extraction. The willow 
plants are also better suited to this method, given the much higher biomass of roots to 
==PAGE:58==
J. Crowe, DTU Master’s Thesis: PFAS contamination in Svea, Svalbard 
49 
 
shoots (up to 3 times higher) (Grier & Ballard, 1981) and the five times higher BCF for roots 
than shoots. The use of plants to prevent erosion has also been demonstrated in Ny-
Ålesund, Svalbard and concern has arisen in some areas of Svalbard losing vegetation 
cover, leading to erosion (Klook & Rønning, 1987; West & Maxted. 2000). The relationship 
between plant cover and erosion is usually represented by a negative exponential curve, see 
Figure 31. It is known that the vegetation coverage of the polar willow in the wild can be up 
to 100% which will be crucial for the prevention of erosion (Cooper & Nilsen, 2004). Plant 
cover reduces erosion by limiting the water runoff along the surface of the soil and by 
increasing water infiltration into the soil matrix (Zuazo & Pleguezuelo, 2009). The movement 
of water in Svalbard is unique, due to the snowmelt occurring over a 15-20-day period. It 
would be interesting to see if the plants are as effective as limiting runoff due to the presence 
of the snow pack and the large quantities of water in a relatively short period.

Phytostabilisation is also an ideal option for Svea as an in-situ method that will have minimal 
secondary impacts. The primary impacts of the contamination in Svea are hard to quantify, 
given the isolation of Svalbard and the lack of human access to the area. Thus, minimising 
the secondary impacts of the remediation option is ideal and phytostabilisation fits this 
criterion.  
One of the negatives of phytostabilisation that needs to be considered is that the contaminants 
remain in the area and the total amount is not reduced (Dickinson, et al., 2009). This may 
entail the need to monitor the site over a long time period and it also poses a risk of 
bioaccumulation. This will be addressed in the following subsection.  
6.5. Bioaccumulation  
Bioaccumulation into the terrestrial food chain needs to be taken into consideration if 
phytoremediation is used as a treatment option. The terrestrial food web is relatively simple in 
Svalbard and is home to only two endemic herbivore species, the Svalbard reindeer and 
Svalbard ptarmigan, of which both are hunted and eaten by humans (Descamps, et al., 2017). 
The most concerning substance for bioaccumulation is PFOS and its accumulation in edible 
tissues has been identified (Lupton, et al., 2014). Limited ecotoxicity data exists for FTSs and 
will not be considered. The bioaccumulation of PFOS was considered only for the hotspot area

6.5.2 Svalbard ptarmigan 
The Svalbard ptarmigan is the only bird species that lives on Svalbard all year round. The 
polar willow makes up a large part of the diet throughout the winter and spring (Unander, et 
al., 1985). The intake of the bird was determined to be 3.5 µg/kgbw/day based on summer 
biomass. NOAEL with a PFOS intake of 990 µg/kgbw was determined for the male mallard 
(Anas platyrhynchos) and 3,600 µg/kgbw for females. The calculated value is 100-1000 times 
lower than the NOAEL; thus, any effects are not expected.   
BMFs were not found for ptarmigan or bird species, so the bioaccumulation in the species was 
not calculated. Dauwe, et al., (2007) reported one of the highest found concentrations in a wild 
animal, great tits (Parus major), of 11,400 µg/kg in the liver and 1625 ng/ml in the blood. These 
birds were sampled in Antwerp, Belgium, a former 3M plant. Thus, PFOS is able to accumulate 
at high concentrations in birds.  
The proposed threshold by the European Food Safety Authority (EFSA) for human 
consumption of 13 ng kgbw-1week-1 (EFSA, 2018). It is difficult to calculate the concentration of 
PFOS in the reindeer and ptarmigan due to a lack of data regarding biomagnification 
mechanism in animals (Müller, et al., 2011). In the event of high uptake of plants, the 
bioaccumulation in the reindeer and ptarmigan should be considered, given the bag-limit per 
hunter is 10 birds and reindeer burgers are available in Longyearbyen (Pedersen et al. 2005).  
6.6 Limitations 
One factor that needs to be taken into consideration for plant uptake and further 
bioaccumulation is the bioavailability of the substances. The BCF of 1.37 used for the foliage 
and stems and 5.18 for the roots were determined in loamy agricultural soil with an unknown 
organic carbon content. The high foc (11%) will increase the Kd at AF and therefore affect the 
bioavailability of the substance for plant uptake. Lower accumulation in plants in soil with a 
high organic carbon content was demonstrated in Blaine, et al., (2014). The BCF of PFOS in 
lettuce leaves was determined to be 6.49, 4.81 and 0.759 for soils with a foc of 0.4, 2 and 6% 
respectively (Blaine, et al., 2014). This decrease in BCF for PFOS can be attributed to the 
increase of Kd from 6.48 to 32.81 L/kg. Thus, it is essential that the Kd value for the individual 
soils is determined to better predict better estimate plant uptake and the subsequent 
implications on phytoremediation and bioaccumulation. The calculations of daily intake also 
assumed 100% of the willows consumed contained the same concentration of PFOS. More 
information should be acquired on dietary behaviour to more accurately determine daily 
consumption of PFOS in willows.   
6.6.1 Revegetation of the polar willow  
Another limitation of this chapter that needs to be addressed is the plant growth of the polar 
willow. Whilst “revegetation” projects have been documented on Svalbard, the difficulties in 
cultivating plants in a harsh climate such as the Arctic need to be taken into consideration. 
Difficulties in seed production and the establishment of seedlings have been attributed to the 
slow colonisation of the polar willow in recently deglaciated areas (Nakatsubo, et al., 2010). 
==PAGE:61==
J. Crowe, DTU Master’s Thesis: PFAS contamination in Svea, Svalbard 
52 
 
Factors for the slow colonisation include; the seeds of the willow being produced in low 
numbers and/or suffering a high rate of mortality, slow initial growth rate and the absence of 
mycorrhizal fungi in the soil (Cooper, et al., 2004: Nakatsubo, et al., 2010). The success of 
phytoremediation in Svea will be highly dependent on the ability of the plants to establish 
themselves within several years. The limiting factors described could be enhanced through 
human intervention.  
It is well known on Svalbard that the biomass is relatively luxuriant in the vicinity of bird cliffs 
due to the abundance of nutrients from the birds (Klokk & Rønning, 1987). The ability to 
enhance the growth on Svalbard via the use of fertiliser (40 g/m²) has been demonstrated on 
S. polaris, and the NPP of a native grass species (Deschampsia alpine) increased by 20-30 
times after 5 years of fertiliser application (Klokk & Rønning, 1987; Robinson, et al., 1998). 
The effect of adding fertiliser also has long-lasting effects, which is promising as it will reduce 
the need to return to Svea after an initial application period (i.e. 5 years). The application of 
fertiliser to the ground will assist in the establishment of a richer microbial community in the 
soil and the abundance of nutrients will decrease the dependency on the plants to form 
ectomycorrhizas. The unsuccessful germination rate of seeds can be avoided by the taking 
shoots of existing plants and placing them in prepared soil (Nakatsubo, et al., 2010). Grazing 
has also been shown to affect plant growth, in which it was enhanced two-fold due to the 
exclusion of reindeer (Dormann & Skarpe, 2002). This could be considered in the first five 
years, whilst personnel are returning to fertilise the area. This will also mitigate any 
bioaccumulation concerns in the initial phase.  
Moisture availability is one of the main factors that commonly defines the Arctic vegetation 
and the presence and/or absence of S. polaris (Slupianek, et al., 2019). Microhabitats 
consisting of a moist mineral material (i.e. clay) surrounded by dry stones provide an ideal 
habitat for the species (Slupianek, et al., 2019). The moisture content has been shown to affect 
the size of the xylem in the willow. Thus, soil with a high moisture content and perhaps the 
application of water in the early stages would not only enhance the survival of the plants, but 
also enhance the size of the xylem and thereby transpiration and uptake of PFASs (Slupianek, 
et al., 2019). The addition of fine coal particles and/or clay will have the benefit of retain 
moisture in the soil, but also acting as a sorbent material to retain the PFASs.

7. Remediation 
7.1 Soil amendment 
The remediation of soil contaminated with PFOS has been identified as a potential remediation 
strategy (Das, et al., 2013; Hale, et al., 2017). The use of sorbent materials can immobilise 
the pollutants, limit the leaching of the compounds the uptake by organisms (Hale, et al., 
2017). Hale, et al., (2017) reported an extremely high Kd value of 16,940 L/kg for activated 
carbon amended soil and reductions in leaching of 99.9% in comparison to untreated soil. This 
cannot be relied upon at the AF, as the concentration of the PFOS in the “contaminated” soil 
was 100-fold lower (54.5 µg/kgdw). Das, et al., (2013) reported releases of PFOS-spiked soil 
(100 mg/kgdw) in untreated soil of 13.20-19.33% that was reduced to 0.07-0.57% with the 
addition of a modified clay adsorbent at 25 oC (MatCARETM, patent number 2009905953). This 
promising for remediation at the AF, particularly due to the extremely high concentration of 
PFOS used in the spiked soil. Furthermore, increases in the effectiveness of the amendment 
were reported for a decrease in temperature from 37 to 25 oC. Thus, it is assumed leaching is 
further reduced at 0-10 oC.  
There is an abundance of coal fine particles available in Svea at kapp amsterdam that would 
most likely make an appropriate amendment material. pH and clay content are also soil 
properties that have significant effect on sorption of PFOS (Li, et al., 2018). There is also a 
high clay content in the Svea area which would enhance sorption (Rowan, et al. 1982). The 
growing season efficiency of the polar willow is primarily determined by the persistence of 
moisture in the soil which would benefit due to the ability of clay and the fine coal particles to 
retain moisture (Slupianek, et al., 2019).  
The effectiveness of the soil amendment treatment will be highly dependent on the longevity 
of the material at the applied site. This in turn, is dependent on the amount of precipitation in 
Svea (approximately 250 mm) and losses of soil particles as a result of erosion, the majority 
occurring when the snow melts during a 15-20-day period (Kristensen, et al., 2008). High 
sorption of the PFOS to the amendment material is a positive; however, the runoff of finer 
sediments will hinder its effectiveness. To minimise losses due to erosion soil amendments 
will

chemophytostabilisation. It is one of the simplest and cost-effective methods for large barren

A ratio of 2-4% by mass of amendment to soil has been shown to be efficient and economical 
in the treatment of organic pollutants (Zimmerman, et al., 2005). With this ratio, the total 
amount of coal and/or clay to remediate the hotspot area would be 100-200 tonnes and 600-
1200 tonnes for the entire AF site. The amount of coal available in the area should be 
determined and higher ratios could be used if it enhances adsorption as it will not impact the 
costs given the accessibility of the material.  
7.2 Impact of the remediation  
The currently proposed method to excavate, transport and treat the waste on mainland 
Norway has associated costs and environmental impacts such as landfilling fees and leaching 
of PFASs into groundwater. The soil removing process in Svea and its transport is relatively 
simple; however, a large quantity of soil (i.e. 28,900 tonnes at AF) needs treatment and/or 
storage. It was identified by Hale, et al., (2017) that new alternatives need to be investigated 
in leu of the high costs of landfilling contaminated soils and the lack of proper handling and 
==PAGE:63==
J. Crowe, DTU Master’s Thesis: PFAS contamination in Svea, Svalbard 
54 
 
disposal facilities that can receive soil contaminated with PFASs (Hale, et al., 2017). 
Goldenman, et al., (2019) identified 10 landfills in Norway that can take PFAS contaminated 
soil. Landfilling of PFAS contaminated soil is also not ideal as it may lead to leaching into the 
surrounding environment. Previous studies have reported significant quantities of PFASs in 
effluent from landfills (297 ng/L) in the Nordic countries (Kallenborn, R., 2004).  
The current estimate for the cost of disposing the PFAS contaminated waste in Norway is 
1250 NOK (US$136) per tonne (Kvennås, et al., draft). Thus, the disposal of the hot spot area 
at AF with an area of 1875 m², 1.7 m depth and density of 1700 kg/m³ would cost 
approximately US$750,000. Landfilling of the entire AF area (≈10,000 m²) would cost 
approximately US$4 million. This value is subject to change depending on the level and type 
of contamination. In OECD countries, the disposal of hazardous waste can be up to US$2000 
per ton (Lipman, 2002). This would lead to a maximum cost of US$12 million for the hotspot 
and US$64 million for the entire AF area.  
Alternatively, Goldenman, et al., (2019) estimated the cost of remediation by the quantity of 
PFASs - US$300,000 for the excavation and treatment of 1 kg of PFAS. The total PFOS was 
estimated to be 3.6 kg in the hotspot area and assuming a ∑PFAS content of 5 kg, this would 
lead to a cost of US$1.5 million. The estimations were for contaminated sites and treatment 
in Norway and would not take into consideration the transport of waste from Svalbard. This is 
lower than the estimated cost of excavation and treatment per site (US$5.5 million).  
One incineration facility that can take PFAS contaminated waste exists in Norway 
(Goldenman, et al., 2019). Incineration of PFASs is also not a simple or inexpensive treatment 
option. Complete destruction of PFOS requires temperatures greater than 1000 oC which is 
higher than most municipal incineration plants (ca. 800 oC) (Schultz, et al., 2003). If 
incineration is considered as the treatment option in Norway, costs would rise drastically given 
the cost of incinerating hazardous waste is approximately US$10,000 per ton (Lipman, 2002). 
This would mean a cost of approximately US$60 million for the hotspot and US$320 million 
for the entire site. Thus, incineration is not a current treatment option considered by SNSK.  
The excavation and transport to the mainland will also contribute to the cost and environmental 
impacts of the dig and haul method. It is expected that these factors are greater than that of 
the proposed scenario in Svea (i.e. chemophytostabilisation). However, this is difficult to 
quantify due to the presence of large-scale machinery such as excavators already in Svea 
and particularly due to the landscaping requirements which will entail restoring the entire area 
to resemble the “natural” topography of the region (i.e. undulating). Thus, a compacted levelled 
site such as the AF would needs to be excavated to some degree for both treatment options. 
Thus, the costs of this will not be estimated to distinguish between the two options.  
Another distinct difference between the current plan and the proposed option is the transport 
of soil via truck to the port. This would require 110 trips via truck for the transport of the hotspot 
material to the port, assuming a load of 50 tonnes (Sahoo, et al., 2010). 578 trips would be 
required for the entire AF area. The position of the site in relation to the port is somewhat ideal, 
being only 1km away and connected via road. Thus, 220 km would need to be driven for the 
hotspot and 1156 for the entire site. Average fuel consumption is approximately 26.35 kg per 
hour, assuming a material handling of 260 tonnes per hour (Sahoo, et al., 2010). This would 
lead to a cost of approximately US$1,150 for removal of the hotspot and US$6,050 for the 
entire site. These are insignificant costs in relation to the entire operation.  All of the equipment 
and a lot of various waste materials in Svea need to be shipped to Norway, so the 
==PAGE:64==
J. Crowe, DTU Master’s Thesis: PFAS contamination in Svea, Svalbard 
55 
 
environmental impacts and costs were not considered for this aspect of the dig and haul 
scenario.   
One of the major benefits of phytoremediation is that the implementation costs are low. It is 
estimated that it would cost US$15,500 to establish the plants at a site of 1 ha assuming one 
tree/m2 using willows (Clausen, 2017). The costs over a ten-year period would equate to 
94,000 or 170,000 US$ over a 20-year period, taking into consideration monitoring systems 
for groundwater, which are costs that would not be required in this case. There are additional 
costs for this method in Svea due to the remoteness and lack of commercially available 
willows. Phytoremediation of the entire AF area is approximately 1 ha, so it would cost 
approximately US$170,000 over a 20-year period, significantly lower than only the costs 
associated with landfilling the contaminated hotspot (US$750,000) and entire area (US$4 
million).   
 
The benefit of soil amendment treatment in terms of cost and environmental impact is that 
materials are available in Svea. This will limit the cost of purchasing a product such as 
MatCARETM (26 US$/kg) and the associated environmental impact of importing it to Svea (e.g. 
CO2 emissions) (Das, et al., 2013). If a ratio of 2-4% was used for a product such as 
MatCARETM, the cost of amendment would be US$2.6-5.2 million for the treatment of the 
hotspot. Thus, the availability of fine coal particles for potential remediation is a huge benefit. 
Furthermore, the soil will need to be excavated with or without remediation for landscaping 
purposes which will provide an opportunity to mix the coal with the soil and limit the 
costs/environmental impacts.  
In summary, logistically, the transport of the contaminated soil from the AF to the mainland is 
not a substantial barrier (i.e. in terms of cost) despite the remoteness of Svea. It will be the 
cost of treating the contaminated soil in Norway (750,000 to 22 million US$ for the hotspot) 
that makes the alternative in-situ options far more appealing and logical in Svea. Costs of 
US$136 per tonne for the soil referred to by Kvennås, et al., (draft) for a total cost of 
US$750,000 is a reasonable “quick fix” option in lieu of the current losses of PFOS into the 
fjord ecosystem and the time it will take to establish effective in-situ treatment options (at least 
several years). Chemophytostabilistion is a promising treatment option for the remaining soil 
in AF (and LB) given the savings in cost (US$170,000 as opposed to US$4 million).  
 
7.3 Scenarios 
The spreading of FTSs towards the fjord at LB is the biggest concern and it is recommended 
that chemophytostabilisation is adopted. If the soil did not need to be dug up for landscaping 
purposes, only phytostabilisation would be recommended to minimise the secondary impacts 
of the remediation. However, given the fact that the soil will need to be dug up, coal and/or 
clay can be mixed through the soil as an amendment material. This will increase the sorption 
of FTSs to the soil and more importantly, create ideal conditions for plant growth in terms of 
soil moisture. The willow toxicity test has demonstrated that plant growth will not be negatively 
affected by the presence of FTSs at the site.  
Two scenarios have been identified for the remediation at the AF. The use of 
chemophytostabilisation throughout the entire area (i.e. 10,000 m²) or shipping the hotspot 
(1875 m²) to mainland Norway and applying in-situ remediation to the remaining area. As 
identified in section 5.2.2, high concentrations of PFOS are currently entering the fjord 
ecosystem (130 ng/L). Willows were shown to survive in such contaminated soil; however, the

effectiveness of the chemophytostabilisation for such high concentrations of PFOS under 
Arctic conditions is unknown. The required research and establishment of the 
chemophytostabilisation method will require at least several years. Furthermore, other forms 
of contamination (PBDE), along with metal and waste materials were also found throughout 
the hotspot. Thus, removal of the hotspot and the treatment of the remaining soil would be the 
most conservative option. The costs calculated above also indicate that the landfilling of just 
the hotspot would be approximately US$750,000, which is reasonable. Further research into 
the effectiveness of the local coal as an amendment material and the practical issues 
associated with the revegetation in the Arctic needs to be carried out before a decision can be 
made regarding the hotspot at AF. 
The treatment at LB and AF would require continue monitoring of the establishment of the 
plants and the performance of the amendment, to ensure that the contaminants have been 
immobilised. Reapplication of amendment material and the planting of more willows should 
be considered in the logistics of the remediation.

8. Recommendations 
Future studies should be carried out with S. polaris in the willow toxicity testing. The stems of 
the species are thinner than that advised by Trapp, et al., (2017), but easily obtainable from 
Svalbard and cuttings have been used in the past in experiments (Nakatsubo, et al., 2010). 
The cuttings will be shorter (several cm) than that recommended in Trapp, et al., (2017), so 
the experiment will have to be adapted somewhat.  Photos of the roots should also be taken 
before and after, to understand the change in root morphology due to the PFOS. The tests 
should be performed with contaminated soil combined with coal to see if there are any 
changes to the toxicity to the plant. Kd values of the soil should also be determined so that the 
concentration in the water can be determined or alternatively, samples of the water could be 
taken for PFAS analysis.  
The toxicity of PFASs on seed germination and initial root formation seems to be minimal 
based on the results of these tests. Further studies should be carried out under Arctic like 
conditions (i.e. 0-5 oC) with seeds and cuttings of S. polaris and common grass species of 
Svalbard. This will assist in determining the likelihood and rate of natural succession of native 
species in the area. The tests should also be carried out in filtered sediment of a uniform size 
(i.e. < 0.25 mm) given the higher affinity of PFASs to finer grains and the discrepancies that 
different grain sizes caused in root length in this study (Zhao, et al., 2012). Further soil 
characterisation experiments on the available N and P in the soil for plants should be 
investigated, as well as the potential of fertiliser in the area and potential limitations (i.e. legal 
issues).  
BCFs of PFOS and 6:2-FTS for the polar willow should also be determined to shed light on 
the effectiveness of phytoremediation and the potential for bioaccumulation in the region. S. 
polaris was identified as an ideal species for phytoremediation purposes for factors such as 
playing a crucial role in carbon sequestration and its known capacity as an hyperaccumulator 
of heavy metals. However, in the event of further investigations, species such as Saxifraga 
oppositifolia should be considered due to its superior ability to naturally colonise new terrain. 
Native grass species should also be considered (Nakatsubo, et al., 2010). 
Various batch-tests should be performed on the potential amendment materials in the region. 
The fine coal particles and clay should be investigated under cold climate conditions, in which 
snow melt is simulated as in Høisæter, et al., (2019). Various ratios of approximately 2-4% 
should be trialled in order to determine the ideal ratio of amendment material to soil. 
Investigations should also be conducted to determine the exact quantities of coal available as 
an amendment material.

9. Conclusion  
The extent of the contamination in the surroundings of the former coal mine, Svea, in Svalbard 
was fully delineated in this study. Concentrations of PFOS were found to be as high as 7200 
µg/kgdw in soil at Avfallsfylling (landfill) and 6:2-FTS was found at concentrations up to 240 
µg/kgdw at Lundbakken. The two different compositions of PFASs at the two locations suggests 
the use of PFOS-based aqueous firefighting foams (AFFFs) and post PFOS-based foams 
containing FTSs. Soil samples were found to have a high organic carbon content (foc) in the 
surface layers (up to 11%) and were classified as very coarse grained (1-2 mm) or granules 
(2-4 mm).  Movement of PFASs from identified hotspot areas towards the fjord ecosystem is 
occurring, most likely due to desorption of PFASs during the snowmelt and the ability of fine 
particles to percolate through the coarser stones. The phytotoxicity testing indicated that 
germination is not affected by the PFASs and that survival of the local willow species, Salix 
polaris, is likely with a maximum decrease in transpiration of 32.6% in soil containing PFOS 
(7200 µg/kgdw).  
Mass balance calculations for the potential of phytoextraction and stabilisation showed long 
remediation times (up to ca. 45,000 years) for PFOS from Avfallsfylling and 1.5-5000 years 
for 6:2-FTSs from LB depending on the bioconcentration factor (BCF) used for substance. 
These results demonstrated that a more suitable application of the willow is phytostabilisation 
to limit losses of soil due to erosion as opposed to the active extraction of PFASs. Daily intake 
of PFOS in the willow for the two endemic herbivores, the Svalbard reindeer (3.6-10.3 
µg/kgbw/day) and ptarmigan (3.5 µg/kgbw/day) indicates exposure will be less than the NOAEL.  
The remediation scenario in the area should combine phytostabilisation with soil amendment 
materials (chemophytostabilisation), as there is an abundance of fine coal particles in the 
vicinity of the contaminated site. The costs associated with the revegetation and monitoring of 
the area were estimated to be cheaper (US$170,000) than just landfilling of the PFOS hotspot 
(US$750,000). It is difficult to conclude on the remediation option for the hotspot at 
Avfallsfylling because of the current transport of PFOS detected in water samples (130 ng/L) 
and unknowns regarding the effectiveness of chemophytostabilisation and the time-frame 
required to establish the technology.  
Future studies regarding the remediation of Svea need to take into consideration the toxicity 
of the soil to the polar willow under Artic conditions and the practicalities of revegetation in 
such as area (i.e. low productivity). Testing on the ability of the coal to increase sorption of 
PFOS is also highly recommended.
