Abstract 
 
Targeted interventions that consider transport behaviour as embedded within the context that 
shaped it are essential for promoting sustainability. Effective interventions require a deep 
understanding of city-specific differences, including infrastructure and cultural attributes that 
influence travel choices. This thesis clusters the Danish population into four distinct mobility 
styles based on existing survey data, revealing significant differences in psychological 
indicators relevant to travel behaviour, such as personal norms and attitudes towards various 
modes of transport. Additionally, Danish municipalities were clustered into four groups based 
on their representation of these mobility styles and infrastructural factors, such as public 
transport supply. These clusters highlight the existence of distinct mobility cultures across 
Denmark, offering a framework for more targeted transport interventions aimed at promoting 
greener mobility. Furthermore, this thesis empirically investigates the effect of travel 
socialization during childhood through two regression analyses on existing survey data. The 
first analysis explored whether the municipality where individuals grew up has a significant 
effect on their later travel behaviour, as measured by the number of cars they currently own; 
however, the model did not confirm a significant effect. The second analysis, based on 
individuals' responses regarding the number of cars in their household during childhood, 
revealed a significant impact of this socialization factor on current car ownership, even after 
controlling for socio-demographic variables and attitudes. 
 
 
Keywords 
Mobility culture, Cluster analysis, Travel behaviour, Travel socialization, Denmark

1. Introduction 
1.1 Motivation 
The transport sector has faced many challenges in recent years. Challenges that reflect 
broader global issues. Transport CO2 emissions reached nearly 8 gigatons in 2022, accounting 
for around 25% of global CO2 emissions (IEA,2023). Furthermore, problems like severe 
congestion in highly urbanized areas and unequal access to transportation in rural areas, 
emphasize the importance of the development of place-targeted measures based on the 
specific needs of each location. 
In this context, developing effective interventions to promote greener and more effective 
transport systems becomes a necessity. Targeted interventions that consider transport 
behaviour as embedded in the context that created it, is a promising approach for effective 
changes. Such targeted interventions require a deep understanding of city-specific differences, 
both in terms of infrastructure and in terms cultural attributes that may influence travel 
choices. 
As a student in Technical University of Denmark I decided to make an attempt in identifying 
such differences among the Danish cities. Denmark and cities like Copenhagen are renowned 
for their commitment towards sustainability. This, along with the opportunity to further 
investigate the Danish transport reality within the context of mobility cultures, served as the 
motivation for this project. 
 
1.2 Purpose and research questions 
The purpose of this project is to identify the differences in mobility behaviour among the 
Danish municipalities. The goal is to use a combined approach that utilizes both attitudinal and 
infrastructural data so that the differences in mobility can be interpreted in terms of the 
underlying motives that created them. First, survey data are analyzed to capture the subjective 
underlying factors that influence travel behaviour. Thus, different mobility styles are defined. 
Then, the Danish municipalities are clustered together based on their mobility style profiles 
and infrastructural data. Using relevant variables such as socio-demographics, the municipality 
clusters are interpreted as indicators of existing mobility cultures in Denmark. These clusters 
can be the starting point for more targeted and effective interventions in the transport sector, 
with a focus on sustainability, providing the ability to take into consideration the specific needs 
of the different municipalities. In addition, the effect of travel socialization is tested empirically. 
Survey data are used for a regression analysis to test whether the way people used to travel 
when they were younger has a significant effect on later travel behaviour. 
In this direction the study will focus on the following research questions: 
• 
RQ1: Which mobility cultures can be differentiated in Denmark? 
• 
RQ2: Does the mobility culture people grow up in effect their current mobility 
behaviour?

2. Literature review 
This chapter provides an overview of the main theoretical concepts that are used in this 
project. Section 2.1 discusses different segmentation approaches in transport research. 
Section 2.2 describes the concept of mobility culture and its use for more effective 
segmentation. Section 2.3 explores similar projects that inspired this thesis. Finally, section 2.4 
reviews the literature on the role of travel socialization in shaping mobility behaviour. 
 
2.1 Segmentation in transport analysis 
Market segmentation is a common tool in transport research to understand and influence 
travel behaviour by categorizing populations into distinct groups based on shared 
characteristics. The goal of segmentation is to tailor interventions and policies in specific 
segments to increase their effectiveness. 
Segmentation methods can generally be distinguished into two broad categories, a-priori and 
post-hoc approaches. A – priori approach involves defining the key variables and segment 
profiles before data collection. This way, each person can be clearly assigned into one of the 
predetermined segments. In contrast, post-hoc segmentation relies on empirical data to from 
segments after data collection, usually by clustering based on a set of variables (Haustein & 
Hunecke, 2013). Segmentation methods differ on the set of variables they use to group people. 
The most commonly used sets of variables used to categorize individuals are spatial variables, 
socio-demographics, travel behaviour or variables focused on attitudes. 
There is currently no universally accepted method that has been proven to be superior. Each 
method has its own pros and cons, making them better suited for different aspects of planning 
and designing mobility measures (Haustein & Hunecke, 2013). For example, (Hunecke, et al., 
2010) argue that the main limitation of behaviour-based segmentation is its inability to explain 
the reasons behind the behaviour, and that sociodemographic variables alone cannot 
adequately explain the complexity of social activities. Therefore, they perform an attitude-
based segmentation approach. They identify 5 different target groups based on “constructs 
derived from the Theory of Planned Behaviour”. Then the predicting power of the attitude-
based approach is compared to segmentations based on socio-demographic and spatial 
variables, and it is proven to be higher. 
 
2.2 Mobility culture 
In an effort to create more meaningful segments that also reflect the socio-cultural context in 
which mobility behaviour develops, the concept of "mobility culture" is introduced into 
transport research. The concept refers to segment-based approaches that focus on local 
cultural contexts. Thus, it can be a useful tool to explain why specific mobility patterns are 
observed in different places. 
That said there is no universally accepted definition of the term. Some researchers 
conceptualize Mobility culture grounded purely on immaterial characteristics and consider 
these characteristics as the key to effective change in mobility patterns. For example, 
(Bamberg, et al., 2020) defines mobility cultures as “injunctive normative beliefs on the city

district level” and develops a new measure for accessing local mobility culture. According to 
the authors objective indicators such as urban form and transport infrastructure are “too 
insensitive”. As a result, he argues that including such indicators in the analysis is not optimal, 
when the goal is research that “initiates processes of cultural change”. 
Others define the term mobility culture as a combination of objective (e.g. infrastructure) and 
subjective transport characteristics. For example, (Haustein & Nielsen, 2016), define mobility 
cultures as “specific socio-cultural settings consisting of travel patterns, the built environment, 
and mobility-related discourses”. In this context, (Klinger, et al., 2013) argues that 
concentrating on either of the two (objective, subjective) factors “is not sufficient to 
understand travel behaviour properly”. In a similar manner, (Scheiner & Holz-Rau, 2007) 
applied Structural Equation Modelling using household survey data in the city of Cologne 
Germany, to investigate the interplay between objective spatial conditions and subjective 
location attitudes. Among others he concluded that “Altogether one cannot give preference to 
'objective' or 'subjective' determinants of travel mode use”.

In an attempt to structure the existing research on mobility cultures, (Stolze, et al., 2023) 
conducted a literature review focusing on which definitions of mobility culture exist in the 
scientific discourse. The authors identify five different groups of papers based on the 
definitions of the term they define, confirming that the concept is defined and applied in 
various ways. The paper concludes among others that transport planners are recommended 
“to pay attention to the local socio-cultural contexts of the environments they operate in”, as 
well as that based on the reviewed papers approaches that focus solely on “hard factors (e.g. 
infrastructures), are unlikely to be successful”.

2.3 This project and similar attempts 
This project is an attempt to find which mobility cultures exist in Denmark, while taking into 
account both individual and spatial determinants of mobility behaviour. Considering the 
above, a combined approach was implemented. The main similar works that this attempt was 
based on, and the main differences of this project are presented. 
(Klinger, et al., 2013), in the context of mobility culture approach, used a mixed selection of 
indicators (both subjective and objective) in a cluster analysis to identify six different clusters 
among 44 German cities with more than 100000 inhabitants. These clusters differ significantly 
in mode choice, socioeconomics and spatial characteristics. One of the main limitations of this 
attempt is the minimal inclusion of subjective measures into the analysis. The author notes 
that the study would be improved by a more thorough evaluation of subjective measures, but 
more extensive data were not available in the municipality level from e.g. surveys. In the 
current project, we aimed to overcome these shortcomings by expanding the inclusion of 
objective measures of behaviour. 
Similarly, (Haustein & Nielsen, 2016), operated a two-step cluster analysis based on existing 
Eurobarometer data. First, European Union population was categorized into eight mobility 
styles. Then, the 28 EU member states were clustered based on the representation of these 
styles in each country. Due to limitations in the dataset the authors directly included 
behaviour, i.e. mode choice, as a clustering parameter. In the present work, it was attempted 
to use the most relevant psychological measures that are important predictors of travel 
behaviour, and direct use of travel behaviour was omitted from the explanatory variables.  
Attempts to construct segmentation approaches based on psychological measures that also 
have a theoretical grounding have been made in the past. These studies usually refer to the 
Theory of Planned Behaviour (TPB) (Ajzen, 1991) and Norm Activation Model (Schwartz, 1977). 
For example, (Anable, 2005), used an expanded version of TPB to construct attitude 
measurements and distinguish a population of travellers in six different groups based on their 
psychographic profiles.  
In summary, the present project aims to differentiate between various mobility styles in 
Denmark by first using selected psychological variables grounded in theory. Then, it will cluster 
Danish municipalities based on both these mobility style profiles and infrastructural 
characteristics, with the goal of identifying local mobility cultures. The specific measures used 
for the two-step cluster analysis and the rationale behind their selection are described in the 
Methodology chapter.  
 
2.4 Travel socialization 
Childhood travel experiences are believed to significantly influence travel attitudes and 
behaviours later in life. (Van Acker, et al., 2019) conducted a path analysis on survey data from 
451 respondents to examine, among other factors, the impact of childhood experiences on 
current public transport usage. The study found that having no vehicle in the household during 
childhood indirectly positively influences perceptions of public transport. Based on these 
findings, the authors suggest that policies should focus on providing children with early 
exposure to public transport to encourage positive attitudes towards its use in adulthood.

(Haustein, et al., 2008) tested the direct effects of various travel socialization factors, such as 
communication with parents about the environmental impact of travel mode choices at the 
age of 15, on social and personal norms as well as car use habits, and the indirect effects on 
car use. Among other findings, they found through equation modeling that all socialization 
factors had significant effects on either norms or car habit or both. 
Building on these findings, this thesis tests the effects of travel socialization on car ownership 
in the Danish population by examining two socialization factors in two separate regression 
models.

3 Methods 
This chapter aims to offer a detailed overview of the methodologies used to explore the 
research questions. It begins by outlining the process of formulating mobility style indicators. 
Next, it presents the clustering analysis used to categorize the population into different 
mobility styles, followed by a discussion of the second-stage clustering of municipalities. 
Finally, the chapter explains the two regression models employed to assess the impact of travel 
socialization on mobility behaviour. 
 
3.1 Data 
The primary dataset for this project was derived from the ongoing project “URGENT: Choice, 
necessity or chance? Understanding behaviouR chanGE iN Transport”. The sample consists of 
approximately 12000 people in Denmark with a representative spatial distribution. The 
extensive survey conducted for this project covers a wide range of aspects, including transport 
attitudes, socioeconomics, environmental behaviour, demographics. 
Additionally, infrastructural data at the municipal level were obtained from public entities. This 
data includes population, area and city sizes, taken from Statistics Denmark (DST), the number 
of electric vehicle plugs per 1000 inhabitants from the Ministry of Transport, and roads and 
bicycle lane length from geodenmark. The number of public transport departures for each 
municipality was taken from Trafikstyrelsen. 
 
3.2 Indicators of mobility styles 
The objective of this step is to identify mobility style indicators that capture the key 
characteristics of individuals in terms of their mobility attitudes, perceptions, and motives. 
These indicators are derived from groups of related survey questions, offering a more 
generalized measure that still retains the essential information from the specific questions in 
the questionnaire. The purpose of these indicators is to create a mobility style profile for each 
survey respondent, which will be used to group individuals with similar attitudes together. 
 
3.2.1 Subjective variables   
A critical selection of variables took place in order to keep only those that can be considered 
influential with regard to transport choices and potential indicators of existing mobility 
cultures. A description of the psychological variables that were used in the later steps of the 
analysis follows. 
Personal norm is a construct derived from Norm Activation Model (NAM) (Schwartz, 1977) and 
refers to feelings of obligation to use environmentally friendly transport modes and to protect 
the environment. Empirical studies have demonstrated its significant effect on for example 
public transport use (Bamberg, et al., 2007).  
Subjective norm describes the perceptions individuals have on the expectations and behaviour 
of people closely related to them regarding transport choices. It has been demonstrated that

it has an indirect effect on transport mode choice through its effect on intention and habit 
(Donald, et al., 2014). 
Perceived mobility necessity (PMN) describes the perceived daily mobility needs of the 
individuals. (Haustein & Hunecke, 2007) introduced the term as an extension of TPB. In 
addition, they demonstrated its negative effect on environmentally friendly transport modes 
and its moderating effect on the relationship between public transport usage and intention. 
Attitude towards car usage was measured using statements related to autonomy, privacy, and 
excitement. For biking, the measurement included statements about autonomy and 
excitement. Public transport was assessed based on statements regarding privacy and 
excitement, while walking was evaluated solely through statements about excitement. 
Values were assessed based on questions on openness to change and self-transcendence 
(Schwartz, 2012). In addition, Environmental self-identity was assessed with two items (Van 
Der Werff & Steg, 2018). 
Local mobility culture was measured by including in the analysis questions regarding the 
neighborhood of the respondent. Two groups of questions were used in the analysis, the first 
regarding neighborhood bike and cycling culture and the second regarding public 
transportation. Mobility culture variables have been indicated as important factor of travel 
behaviour. For example, (Klinger & Lanzendorf, 2016) found that urban mobility cultures have 
a strong influence on bike use of new residents. 
Answers to all groups of questions were on a 5-point Likert scale (1 = strongly disagree, 5 = 
strongly agree. Similarly, the answers to the questions regarding values were on 5-point Likert 
scale (1 = opposed to my values, 5 = extremely important). 
 
3.2.2 Mobility attitude indicators  
Hence, 40 questions from the questionnaire were chosen as the most suitable to capture 
factors that influence people’s transport behaviour. These 40 questions were afterwards 
divided into 4 different groups based on the similar nature of some of the questions. A 
complete table of the 40 selected questions is available in the Appendix (Table 10). 
Principal component analyses (PCA) with varimax rotation were performed for each of the 4 
groups of variables using the statistical software R, to identify a smaller number of factors that 
explain most of the variation in the original data (Kassambara, 2017), and to investigate the 
differentiability of the groups (Hunecke, et al., 2001). The PCA analyses indicated 10 different 
more generic indicators that can explain most of the variation in data. The resulting indicators 
are shown in Table 1. The internal consistency of the indicators, measured by calculating the 
Cronbach’s alpha, was adequate. The factor scores of the different PCA are included in the 
Appendix (Table 11).

3.3 Clustering analysis 
A two-step cluster analysis was conducted. In the first step, individuals were clustered based 
on their mean scores on the psychological indicators (Table 1) to identify distinct mobility style 
clusters. In the second step, Danish municipalities were clustered based on the representation 
of each mobility style within each municipality, along with other infrastructural variables. 
 
3.3.1 Step 1 clustering – Mobility styles 
The objective of the first step is to cluster the respondents of the survey based on their scores 
on the previously developed psychological indicators in order to identify mobility style clusters 
within the Danish population. 
For the cluster analysis a K-means algorithm was employed in statistical software R. K-means 
is one of the most commonly used clustering as it is simple and fast and can deal with large 
data-sets (Kassambara, 2017).  
Cluster solutions were tried ranging from 2-cluster solution up to 6-cluster solution. Using the 
elbow method, which is a graphical technique to determine the optimal number of clusters 
based on sum of squared error, but mainly focusing on the interpretability of the data, a 4 
clusters solution was selected. Hence, 4 mobility styles were identified. The results of this 
clustering analysis are presented in Results section. 
 
3.3.2 Step 2 Clustering – Municipality clusters 
After the initial clustering step, the goal was to use the previously identified mobility styles 
together with infrastructural data as the basis for a second cluster analysis. This second step 
clustering aims to cluster municipalities with the most similar mobility styles, while also taking 
into account infrastructural data that influence the behaviour of individuals.  
For this purpose, the proportion of the population that belongs to each mobility style was 
calculated for all the Danish municipalities. That means how much each of the 4 mobility styles 
is represented in each municipality. These proportions together with infrastructural data for 
each municipality was the basis for a second cluster analysis. Four additional infrastructural 
variables were used as clustering features. The infrastructural variables are presented. 
Population density of each municipality, i.e. the number of people that live per square 
kilometre. 
Bike infrastructure variable represents the proportion of bicycle lane kilometres to total road 
kilometres. 
Public transport departures is the public transport supply of the municipality and refers to 
the number of public transport departures per square kilometre. 
Plugs is the number of electric vehicles plugs per 1000 inhabitants.

Cluster solutions with 4 and up to 6 clusters were tested. The elbow criterion was used to 
identify the optimal number of clusters. The graph suggested that a cluster solution from 4 to 
6 cluster would be optimal in terms of sum of squared errors. Again, the statistical software R 
was used to perform the cluster analysis with the K-means algorithm. We tried to interpret 
each cluster solution based on the differences among the clusters on different socio-
demographic variables and other metrics from the survey. During the analysis a few 
municipalities were spotted in clusters that did not seem to make sense. The low number of 
respondents in these municipalities was not representative of the population. Therefore, 
municipalities with less than 30 respondents were removed from the final clustering 
algorithm. 
 In the end, the 4 clusters solution was selected as the one showing the most meaningful 
insights. The 4 municipality clusters are described in the Results section. 
 
 
3.4 Regression analysis 
Travel socialization is believed to have a significant effect on travel behaviour later in life 
(Haustein, et al., 2008) (Van Acker, et al., 2019). To test this effect empirically two regression 
analyses were conducted. Ordinal regression models were implemented in the statistical 
software R. Ordinal regression models are designed to handle dependent variables with 
ordered categories and are able to incorporate both continuous and categorical independent 
variables. 
The first model assessed whether the municipality where individuals grew up significantly 
influences the number of cars they own later in life. The second model tested whether the 
number of cars in the household during childhood affects car ownership later in life.  
Both models were implemented in three steps. First, only the independent variable of interest 
and place of residence were included. Next, socio-demographics were introduced as 
additional explanatory variables. Then, attitudinal variables were added to the models to 
evaluate their effect. 
 
3.4.1 Testing the effect of social municipality 
The number of cars each household owns was used as the dependent variable of model 1. This 
variable was chosen as an indicator for mobility behaviour, given that car ownership is a 
significant factor of personal mobility choices. 
Social municipality, i.e. the municipality where people grew up, was the independent variable 
of interest. The municipality where people currently live was included in the model to account 
for its effect, along with the interaction term between the two explanatory variables to 
investigate potential combined effects of the variables.  In the first step, income and age were 
controlled for. In the second step, attitudinal variables were also added.  
The analysis was conducted on a subset of the population. Only individuals who moved to a 
different mobility cluster later in life were included in this model, which comprises 
approximately 4,000 respondents.

3.4.2 The effect of number of cars during childhood 
An additional regression model was developed to test the effect of travel socialization, but this 
time the basis of the analysis was how people used to travel as children. Individuals were asked 
how many cars they had in the household while growing up. This was done to capture 
individual differences that the previous model could not, since it was only based on the 
municipality in general.  
Model 2 was again an ordinal regression model. This time the explanatory variable of interest 
was the number of cars people had in their household when they were children. So, the 
variable “CAR_10” was added in the explanatory variables. There are three possible answers. 
1. 1 – No cars owned 
2. 2 - One car owned 
3. 3 - Two or more cars owned 
   
The dependent variable and the rest of the explanatory variables were the same as the model 
described in 3.4.1. Model 2 was implemented to the whole sample and individuals who 
haven’t moved to a different municipality cluster were kept in the analysis. 
The results of both regression models are discussed in section 4.3.

4. Results 
In this chapter the results of the two-step cluster analysis are presented and described. First, 
the results of clustering individuals into different mobility style groups are demonstrated. 
Then, the results of clustering the municipalities and the emerging map of Denmark are 
shown. Finally, the findings of the regression analyses to test the effects of travel socialization 
in people’s travel behaviour are presented. 
 
4.1 Danish mobility styles 
The cluster solutions were evaluated based on the differences that the clusters presented in 
the 10 psychological indicators described previously (Table 1). Overall, the most significant 
indicators among the cluster solutions were the car independency, the attitude towards bike, 
and the mobility needs of the individual. 
In the 3 clusters solution, 2 car-focused and one bike-focused groups were identified. The 
differences in the 2 car-user groups were not big overall and were mainly related to the 
indicators regarding people’s perception on local mobility culture regarding public transport. 
In the 4 clusters solution, a fourth group emerged that stood somewhat in the middle between 
the cyclists and the 2 car focused groups identified before. Interestingly the clusters now 
showed significant differences in attitude towards public transport. Nevertheless, car 
independence, bike attitude and mobility needs continue to be significant determinants of 
cluster differences. Lastly, mobility norm and the variables related to neighbourhood 
presented some variation. 
After that 5 and 6 cluster solutions were tested. The main clusters remained the same as in 
the previous 4-cluster solution, and the differences in the newly formed clusters were not 
significant. Thus, it was concluded that adding more clusters would not add much in terms of 
interpretation and the 4-cluster solution was selected as the optimal one.

4.3  The effect of travel socialization 
In this section the results of the two regression models to investigate the effects of travel 
socialization are illustrated and described. 
 
4.3.1 Social Municipality 
The ordinal regression model aimed to examine the effect of social municipality on later 
transport behaviour was developed in 3 steps. Table 8 summarizes the 3 stages of model 1.  
Initially, only the variables "live in" and "grew up in," along with their interaction term, were 
included. The interaction term was found to be non-significant and did not contribute much 
additional information to the interpretation, so it was omitted. In the second stage, socio-
demographic variables were added. In the third stage attitudinal variables were also 
incorporated. 
In the first stage, the municipality where individuals grew up had a significant effect when the 
cluster of origin was 4, and a marginally non-significant effect for cluster 3. Specifically, the 
negative effect observed for cluster 3 (Copenhagen-Frederiksberg) on the number of cars 
owned suggests that individuals who grew up in the capital municipality might later own fewer 
cars, potentially due to exposure to a more sustainability-oriented mobility culture. 
Conversely, the negative coefficient for cluster 4, which consists of car-dependent, low-
population density areas, was unexpected. However, both of these effects became statistically 
insignificant when additional variables were controlled for in the subsequent stages of the 
model.  
The ordinal explanatory variables in the model are compared with the reference category, 
which is set to the value 1 by default. This means that the values presented in Table 8 are 
relative to this reference category. As shown in table 8, there is a significant effect associated 
with the cluster in which individuals live. Specifically, those living in cluster 2 and 3, which 
include the capital, its suburbs, and Aarhus, own significantly fewer cars compared to those in 
cluster 1. 
Additionally, it is noticeable that the inclusion of the attitudinal indicators car independence 
and car attitude increases the explained variance from 62% to 71%. These indicators are 
introduced in section 3.2.2.  
In conclusion, the model does not confirm the significance of the effect of the municipality 
cluster where individuals grew up once other variables are controlled for. However, the 
significance observed in the early stages may suggest some insights, and it is expected that 
some of the later added explanatory variables account for similar portions of the variance.

Model 2 explores the effect of travel socialization on an individual level. The variable “CAR_10” 
represents the number of cars owned in the household during individuals’ childhood. Model 
2 aims to test how this variable influences the number of cars individuals own later in life.

individuals who grew up in households with one or more cars are likely to own more cars today 
compared to those who grew up in households with no cars. Given that the model controls for 
income, age, place of residence, individual car dependency, and overall attitude towards cars, 
these findings empirically support that the culture around car in one’s household during 
childhood influence later travel mode choices.

5. Discussion -Conclusions 
5.1 Discussion- Policy recommendations 
5.1.1 Discussion 
Based on 10 psychological indicators, the population of Denmark was divided into four 
mobility styles. Then based on the representation of each mobility style and other 
infrastructural variables, the Danish municipalities were clustered into four municipality 
clusters. 
First, individuals were divided into four mobility styles through cluster analysis based on 
psychological indicators as for example, attitudes towards different modes of transport, 
norms, environmental values, and perceptions of local mobility culture. The analysis of 
approximately 12,000 respondents of the survey suggests that the population can be divided 
into four mobility style clusters. Car-loving drivers (25% of respondents), car-dependents 
(31%), self-determined mobiles (22%), and pro-environmental cyclists (21%). Individuals 
among groups present significant differences regarding 10 psychological indicators. 
Comparing the two car focused groups, car-loving and car-dependents, it is clear that the 
reasons behind their mobility behaviour differ. While both groups report high mobility needs 
and car dependency, car-loving drivers have low feelings of obligation to use environmentally 
friendly alternatives and have negative attitudes towards public transport and cycling. Car-
dependents, on the other hand, seem to act in contravention to their attitudes and would be 
willing to change to other modes if there was a possibility, e.g. better bike infrastructure and 
public transport supply.   
Self-determined mobiles and pro-environmental cyclists present consistency between 
attitudes and behaviour. Self-determined mobiles choose the car despite local mobility culture 
supporting the alternatives of bike and public transportation. Considering that these two 
groups usually coexist in the same areas and that they don’t present significant differences in 
age and income, it can be argued that norms and attitudes are the most contributing factors 
to their mobility behaviour. However, education could also be a factor (45% of pro-
environmental cyclists have a bachelor’s degree or higher versus 32% of self-determined 
mobiles).  
Next, Danish municipalities were clustered using additional objective measures at the 
municipal level. The interplay between the mobility styles and objective measures, together 
with the geographical distribution of the municipality clusters, help to better understand the 
underlying factors of mobility behaviour.  
Mobility behaviour in Cluster 3 (Copenhagen – Frederiksberg) seems to be almost 
independent of infrastructural factors. It is the cluster with the highest public transport supply 
and bike lane to road proportions. The majority of the individuals fall into the categories of 
pro-environmental cyclists and self-determined mobiles, which are both groups that choose 
transport mode mainly based on preferences, since they perceive their neighborhoods as 
supporting of alternative modes to car.  
==PAGE:31==
Discussion - Conclusions 
 
26 
 
Cluster 2 geographically surrounds cluster 3, creating a zone in the capital area, which together 
with the city of Aarhus has high shares of environmentally concerned travelers. The most 
represented mobility style in the cluster is self-determined mobiles, followed by pro-
environmental cyclists.  
Cluster 1 is mainly consisted of relatively low population density areas. Interestingly, cities like 
Odense are included in the cluster. In terms of population density Odense is similar to for 
example, Aarhus. The two cities show significant differences in bike and electric vehicle 
infrastructure, with Odense lacking in this respect. This suggests the existence of different local 
mobility cultures that couldn’t be realized by a segmentation based on differences in 
urbanization alone. Overall, Cluster 1 is a car focused cluster with relatively high shares of car-
dependents, car-loving drivers and self-determined mobiles.  
Cluster 4 could be characterized as the non-sustainable areas of Denmark in terms of 
transportation behaviour. Roughly 80 % of the population in these areas is dependent on a car, 
with high shares of both car-dependents and car-loving drivers, indicating that both 
infrastructure and attitudes are determinant factors of mobility behaviour in these areas.  
 
5.1.2 Policy recommendations  
To create effective policies for promoting more sustainable transport in the area clusters 2 and 
3 form, it is important to consider the mobility behavior of self-determined mobile individuals, 
which are highly represented. Given their negative attitude towards public transport, their 
perceived mobility needs, and their low sense of obligation to travel environmentally friendly, 
combined with the already well-developed bike and public transport infrastructure in the area, 
it is unlikely that further development of public transport infrastructure alone will significantly 
increase their use of such services. Instead, as suggested by (Møller, et al., 2018), measures 
that enhance car-reliant users' awareness of and acceptance of daily transport issues in 
relation to sustainability, along with initiatives giving positive feedback to cyclists, could be 
more effective. 
Municipalities within clusters 1 and 4 would require changes in both subjective and objective 
factors for effective policy making. Research by e.g. (Nielsen & Skov-Petersen, 2018) indicates 
that improving cycling infrastructure can increase cycling rates within local areas. This 
approach could be particularly effective in cluster 4, which currently has the worst bike-lane 
to car road ratio. Effective policies for mobility behaviour changes in Cluster 4 would be harder 
compared to the other clusters and considering that it is the biggest cluster with 48 
municipalities spatially distributed across the country, it would require a deeper investigation 
of the specific needs of the municipalities within the cluster. Nevertheless, it can be argued 
that both the objective (lack of bike infrastructure, low population density) and the subjective 
(car-focused mobility attitudes) are underlying factors of non-sustainable mobility behaviour 
in the municipalities included in Clusters 1 and 4. 
 
5.2 Limitations of data analysis methods 
For the purposes of this thesis survey data were utilized to segment the Danish population 
into different mobility style clusters. It was assumed that the responses of individuals are 
representative of the population in each municipality. However, a significant number of 
==PAGE:32==
Discussion - Conclusions 
 
27 
 
municipalities had low number of respondents. Even though municipalities with very few 
(below 30) respondents were excluded from the analysis, several municipalities still had less 
than 100 respondents. While this sample size might be adequate for smaller municipalities, it 
may not be sufficient for larger ones. To validate the findings, future research should aim to 
increase the sample size to ensure more representative distribution. 
Furthermore, within the scope of the thesis, psychological indicators were used to capture the 
underlying motives and reasons behind individuals’ mobility behaviour. While the municipality 
clusters were successful to some extent, revealing significant differences in mobility cultures 
that were not limited to objective measures, the data still tended to cluster based on general 
differences in urbanization, which are already known. Future research could increase the 
explanatory power of the clusters with alternative approaches, such as creating predefined 
groups. For instance, highly urbanized areas could be clustered separately to better isolate the 
influence of subjective factors. The method of predefined segmentation groups has been 
implemented in existing works, e.g. (Anable, 2005).  
 
5.3 Conclusions  
This thesis aimed to investigate whether distinct mobility cultures can be identified within 
Denmark. Through cluster analysis, four municipality clusters were identified, each 
demonstrating significant differences in both subjective factors, such as norms and attitudes, 
and objective factors, such as public transport supply and bike infrastructure. These findings 
indicate the existence of considerably different mobility cultures within the same national 
context. 
The significance of this “cluster map” of Denmark lies in its ability to describe how 
municipalities, which might be considered similar either geographically or in terms of 
urbanization, can differ substantially in their mobility cultures and thus classified into different 
clusters. This insight can be useful for future research, offering the possibility to explore 
municipalities with similar mobility cultures within the same context. 
Additionally, this thesis empirically examined the effect of travel socialization using regression 
analysis on survey data. The results suggest that car-centric culture in a household during and 
individual’s childhood can be a significant determinant of their later transportation behaviour.
