Introduction 
The Dan field 
There are 115 wells settled in the Dan field. 
The majority of Maersk Oil’s Dan wells have been completed in the Maastrictian/Tor 
formation with only one existing wells in the Danian/Ekofisk. As indicated by rock 
mechanics testing (Figure 23 & Figure 24). These formations are heterogeneous by 
nature, containing rock with particularly low tensile and compressional strength. 
Production from these tight, chalk formations has relied on acid stimulation/fractures 
and propped fractures with Maersk having pioneered many of the completion 
techniques since the 1980s. Some wells have more than a dozen propped zones, and a 
single zone may contain over a million pounds of proppant. There are currently 
approximately 30 Dan wells with frac and pack completions and, for many of these, 
resin coated proppant was placed to help retain the proppant, following the main 
treatment. The only sand control known to have been deployed in the Asset are some 
ceramic screens, integral to some of the sliding side doors (SSDs) in well DFF-40.

Haldan TOR 
 
 
[Force report MFB-6B] 
 
 
It is a general experience that corrosion problems start to escalate with the 
introduction of water injection and especially after breakthrough of the injected 
seawater into the well areas. The injected seawater is responsible for the souring of 
the North Sea oil fields and bacterial activity is a crucial factor. The injection water 
locally lowers the temperature in the underground to 30 to 50 dgr.C and with an 
abundance of nutrients and electron donors in the form of e.g. sulphate in the virtually 
anaerobic seawater the scene is set for large scale bacterial production of corrosive 
gases like hydrogen sulphite and carbon dioxide. As first the gases and subsequently 
the injected water penetrate into the well the produced fluid becomes more and more 
corrosive. As the reservoir is gradually depleted of oil the water cut increases and so 
does the corrosion problems. 
According to information from Mærsk Olie og Gas AS, the corrosion rate has been 
reduced in some systems by means of biocide injection, which could indicate that 
micro-organisms contribute significantly to the ongoing corrosion process. One of the 
challenges for the oil industry the next years will be the challenge regarding the use of 
biocides and other chemicals since they are considered as a significant threat towards 
the environment and thus will be one of the social focus areas. There is, however, little 
direct documentation of whether the microbiological activity as such is the controlling 
factor of the corrosion observed, and whether the biocides applied are really efficient 
to stop the activity in a scale/biofilm. 
[Comments FORCE Report MD-01B]

FORCE Technology therefore recommends Mærsk Olie og Gas AS to initiate a detailed 
corrosion study by means of side-stream setups facilitating studying the effect of 
different conditions and mitigation efforts. This will provide an understanding of the 
determining factors of the ongoing corrosion mechanism and thus optimize the 
amount of spent chemicals and reduce the replacement of corroded oil processing 
equipment. The corrosion study should take all corrosive factors into account such as 
possible residual oxygen (from eg. chemicals), temperature, microbiological activity, 
flow etc. FORCE Technology would like to participate in planning and performing such 
a corrosion study. The cost of this investigation would easily be covered by the cost 
savings of efficient chemicals consumption and less replacement of corroded 
equipment. Corrosion monitoring by online techniques could also in future be an 
option for obtaining safety, but the application is questionable based on the present 
understanding, as the chance of localizing the attack on a specific probe requires that 
the mechanism is identified. This study would therefore also support future better 
documentation and control.

[http://www.glossary.oilfield.slb.com/Terms/s/scale.aspx] 
A common well-intervention operation involving a wide variety of mechanical scale-
inhibition treatments and chemical options. Mechanical removal is done by means of 
a pig or by abrasive jetting that cuts scale but leaves the tubing untouched. Scale-
inhibition treatments involve squeezing a chemical inhibitor into a water-producing 
zone for subsequent commingling with produced fluids, preventing further 
scale precipitation. Chemical removal is performed with different solvents according to 
the type of scale: · Carbonate scales such as calcium carbonate or calcite [CaCO3] can 
be readily dissolved with hydrochloric acid [HCl] at temperatures less than 250oF 
[121oC]. · Sulfate scales such as gypsum [CaSO4·2H2O] or anhydrite [CaSO4] can be 
readily dissolved using ethylenediamine tetraacetic acid (EDTA). The dissolution of 
barytine [BaSO4] or strontianite [SrSO4] is much more difficult. · Chloride scales such 
as sodium chloride [NaCl] are easily dissolved with fresh water or weak acidic 
solutions, including HCl or acetic acid. · Iron scales such as iron sulfide [FeS] or iron

DFA-17 resulted in USD 51 million in deferred production revenue for 2012 
Solids that accumulate in the production system result in: 
  
Chronic loss of production due to:  
- reduced separator efficiency  
- plugged level control valves  
- extended shut down durations to clean the separators

scale. This scale is often naturally occurring radioactive material (NORM) and, when 
present, 20 specialized cleaning personnel are required for 2-3 weeks to clean a vessel. 
NORM scale requires dedicated land-storage which is becoming congested in Esbjerg.

Well analysis 
The analysis of the wells will be carried out in two sections: water injectors (WI) and oil 
and gas producers (OGP). The reason for the segregation of the well into these two 
groups is that their thermodynamic and chemical environments are very different. We 
will divide the wells depending on the status (WI or OGP) which they have at the time 
the multifinger caliper survey (MFC) is done. WI wells are usually OGP in their early 
stage of life; so this aspect will also be studied when analyzing the WI. Only the well 
completions which have had at least one MFC done throughout the well history will be 
considered for the study. These are a total of 92 well completions; 35 WI and 57 OGP.

Wells with produced water injection 
The reason for making this group is to investigate the effect of the PWI has in the 
corrosion of the injectors. It is believed that these wells will have a different behavior 
due to the PWI events. As commented previously two PWI test were carried out in 
1994 and 2010. The well MFB-2 corresponding to the first experiment in 1994 will not 
be considered to this group since it is not among the 35 studied wells with MFC. 
However we can observe the well MFB-3 which has initially a similar history. The well 
has five MFC done between 1999 and 2004 all of them show very similar maximum 
wall penetration per tubing joint (Max WP per TJ) logs, see Max WP per TJ plot for 
MFB-3 in Appendix A. The minimum internal diameter per tubing joint (Min ID per TJ) 
shows a slight growth of the corrosion products with the time, see Min ID per TJ plot 
for MFB-3 in Appendix A. Nevertheless it can be still consider a thin layer of scale 
products. The top 2,000 ft of tubing contains many sites where the wall thickness has 
been reduced by some 35% with one circumferential spot just below the subsurface 
safety valve (SSSV) at 460 ft where the wall thickness has been reduced by up to 41%. 
From 2,000 ft down to packer depth the corrosion is gradually reduced with some 
spots having a 20-25% loss of wall thickness. Although the MFC surveys did not show 
high penetration spots, a leak investigation in 2003 showed tubing to C-section 
communication at 330ft. This event led to a suspension of the well in August of 2003 
until the WO in April of 2006 [2]. The well has had several interventions around the 
SSSV which is located around 460 ft however this cannot explain the high Max WP per 
TJ pattern found through the whole interval from 2000 ft to the tubing hanger (TH). 
Therefore it is believe that this damage seen on the top part of the well is due to the 
production water injection back in 1994. (It would be nice to find scientific hypothesis

Well with workovers 
This group gathers all the wells which had tubing to C-section communication in order 
to find out which is the reason behind the failures, whether they are due to corrosion 
issues. The well MFB-3 with a WO has already been analyzed into the previous group. 
The well MFA-10 had a leak at extendable joint connection at 7491 ft detected in 2005 
by a leaking detection tool [6]. However a MFC done in near date does not show 
corrosion issues in the whole well, see Max WP per TJ plot for MFA-10 in Appendix A. 
This is likely to be due to the fact the MFC dta analysis report omit Max WP per TJ at 
the completion items like the extendable joint (EJ). It is specified that the leak is 
located in a connection [6]. According to the completion drawing the leak would be 
located between the Pup Joint (PJ) and the EJ [7]. This subassembly torque is reported 
to be done properly. Therefore it is believed that the reason could be a mechanical 
damage due to over-torque and consequent crevice corrosion propagated through the 
connection sealing as reported in the analysis done for MFA-19 well which presents a 
very similar problem and well history [9][10]. There is tubing sample analysis done by 
Force Technology Oil and Gas Department (FORCE) and Danish Technological Institute 
Oil and Gas Department (DTI). They do not analyze the subassembly which presents

The resulting average CR of the WI for the Dan field is 0.145 mm/year with a STD of 
0.076 mm/year (66% confidence). When we consider a tolerance of 12.5% of the wall 
thickness the CR is 0.07 mm/year with a STD of 0.034 mm/year. The STD values 
pretend to reflect the limits of the WO prediction model rather than reflect an actual 
uncertainty. For example if we consider the upper bound of the 95% confidence 
interval (CR = 0.3 mm/year) most of the wells would have failed already.  
As it can be seen in Figure D the CR data is not normally distributed around the mean 
but tailed to the high CR side. This is due to the fact that we are not considering the 
wall thickness tolerance. The tolerance have a greater effect in the MFC done few 
years after the completion, that is the reason why the well surveyed in a young stage 
have apparently higher CR. However the effect of the tolerance gets reduced along the 
X axis, surveys done in an older stage of the wells are not affected that much. For 
example suppose a 4.5 inch well (nominal wall thickness is 6.883 mm and wall 
thickness with 12.5% tolerance would be 6.023 mm) a 15% of Max WP in a MFC done 
one year after completion and  the same Max WP in a MFC done 10 years after well 
completion. Without considering the tolerance the CRs would be 1.03 mm/year and 
0.10 mm/year respectively. Therefore if we were considering the tolerance there 
would be a more normal distribution around the average CR and with a much lower 
average CR as already showed.  
Even though we might be overestimating the CR we choose the CR value based on the 
tubing nominal wall thickness for being a more conservative approach. Based on this 
CR the remaining well lives would be 44.6, 47.6 and 63.4 years for 3.5, 4.5 and 5.5 
inch wells respectively. Finally according to the well completion date and tubing

Conclusion for water injectors 
In general the WIs in the Dan field have had a good corrosion behavior. Some of the 
wells are still in acceptable conditions after 40 years with several years of production 
at the begging of the wells life.   
PWI can double the CR in the WI. We cannot make an accurate well life prediction for 
the wells MFB-1 and MFB-5 since we do not know how the CR has develop after the 
PWI was stopped. 
All the wells workedover in the Dan field failed in L80-13Cr or in parts in contact with 
those. One of the main reasons founded for the WI failure was over-torque in the 
completion subassemblies which initiates a mechanical damage in the connections 
leading to a propagation of corrosion to the crevices. It was difficult or impossible for 
us to find subassembly torque reports when we were investigating over-torque issues 
in the WI.  
Interventions can also damage the tubing wall as found for some well herein. This was 
already proved in similar studies for other fields [18].    
It is suspected that other important reasons could be galvanic corrosion or corrosion in 
the L80-13Cr subassemblies; however based on the data available we cannot ensure 
that these are general corrosion mechanisms for the WI in the Dan field. It is also

We could note that the sea water breakthrough can affect the other production 
parameters such as increases in H2S, increases in the water cut, and decreases in the 
well temperature. The decrease in the well temperature is due to the fact that the 
injected water is much colder than the formation water which is heated by the 
geothermal energy. As mentioned in the literature study decreasing well temperatures 
thermodynamically favors the formation of scale deposits. The H2S increase is due to 
the action of the sulfur reducing bacteria (SRB) which is brought together with the SW 
(find some reference for this statement). In some cases there was also an increase in 
the CO2 levels with the water breakthrough but this was in general not an issue. In 
general, CO2 levels remained constant throughout the wells life varying from 0.1 to 
0.2%. In terms of ions contained in produced water the SW breakthrough is 
characterized by an increase in SO4, K, Mg and a decrease in Ca, Na, and Cl. These 
changes in the ions correspond with the natural differences between sea water 
injected and formation water (See table in injected water and formation water 
section).

Table P: selected MFC surveys used to calculate the baseline CR for OGP in the Dan 
field. On the bottom of the table are the calculated nominal and tolerance CR average 
and their STD. 
The resulting average baseline nominal CR for OGP in the Dan field is 0.19 mm/year 
with a STD of 0.088 mm/year (66% confidence). When we consider a tolerance of 
12.5% of the wall thickness the CR is 0.09 mm/year with a STD of 0.036 mm/year. 
Based on the baseline nominal CR, if the wells remained at their baseline conditions, 
the remaining well lives would be 34, 36 and 48 years for 3.5, 4.5 and 5.5 inch wells 
respectively. 
The next step is to calculate the CR after the breakthrough when the SWf rises above 
30%. To this end we will select the wells which have at least one survey with bad 
corrosion status and one with good corrosion status. Thus it is possible for us to check 
how much wall thickness loss has occurred during the breakthrough period.  
As commented previously, the amount of surveys with an acceptable reliability is very 
restricted; and even more if there has to be two reliable MFC surveys showing good 
and bad corrosion status for the same well. Only 5 wells fulfilled these requirements: 
MD-7/MD7’, MD-8, MFA-2A, MFB-8B and MFB-15. We will assume that the 
interventions have affected all this wells in the same way so that their CR variations 
are just due to the chemical and thermodynamic environment.  
Each of this wells have a particular production history considering also the SWf 
evolution. For this reason each well will be analyzed independently to try to reflect a 
type of breakthrough development related to a CR. Later on we can compare these 
with the rest of the wells in the field and make a life prediction for them completing 
the WO prediction model. 
The well MD-8 is an example of direct connection with a WI where the SWf suddenly 
raise above 80%, see Figures 1, 2, 3, 4, 5 and 6. A survey done right after the SW 
connection in 2006 showed a baseline CR of 0.195 mm/year which match with the CR 
baseline previously calculated. Another survey done 4.5 years later in 2011 show 
already full wall penetration of the tubing. This shows that in an extreme case of a 
direct connection with a WI the CR would be 1.17 mm/year. 
The wells MFA-2A and MFB-8B show early SW breakthrough after completions, 
considering the age of the well at failure and the MFC surveys done in the meantime 
the wells had a CR proportional to their average SWf. The MFA-2A and MFB-8B 
average SWf are 60 and 40% respectively, with corresponding average CR of 0.53 and 
0.75 mm/year

The WO prediction model considers that the SWf will remain as it is in the last period 
selected, therefore if there are significant changes in the SWf levels the well life should 
be recalculated.   
We considered that all of the interventions were done using treated water. However

All Force reports show a very low or low MIC risk. Why keep analyzing this? At least in 
this way the results are being negative, although it could be due that these microbes 
die in contact whit the air. But maybe it would be too complex or impossible to do it 
downhole. But analysis as they are been carried out now offer nothing. 
Conclusions for oil and gas production wells 
The availability of accurate MFC data is very limited. Most of the MFC data has been 
discarded for being disturbed by the presence of scale. On the other hand there has 
not been a methodology of stablishing a CR baseline by doing MFC surveys right before 
the sea water breakthrough and some years after with well scale cleaning prior the 
survey. For this reason it has been very difficult to stablish a gradual relation between 
the sea water fraction evolution and the CR increase. However, we were able to 
calculate the CR for the case of direct communication with WI, which is the upper 
boundary of the model. 
Recommendations for oil and gas production wells 
To improve the knowledge about the relation between the CR and the SWf, it is 
recommended performing at least one MFC baseline survey when the SW 
breakthrough is observed and one some years to see the corrosion evolution. The MFC 
surveys should be done right after scale cleaning of the well to be able to measure 
corrosion. Another option even more recommendable would be to use magnetic tools 
which measure the wall thickness but also allow us to study the buildup of scale which 
could be a key to investigate mechanism such under deposited corrosion (UDC).
