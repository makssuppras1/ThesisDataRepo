Abstract
Hard water is a problem in Denmark, were households experience irritations due to the large occurrence of
precipitation among others. HOFOR (2018a) report that the household will save 500 kr annually as results
of water softening. Meanwhile, Rambøll (2017) report how the water price will rise with 0.79kr/m3 (when
softening down to 10◦dH) as consequence of the maintenance and investment costs of the water softening for
the waterworks. Literature, like the report from Naturstyrelsen (2011) and COWI (2012) estimates savings for
the household, which will justify the increased water bill for the household, however, no or limited veriﬁcation
are available of these estimates. This master thesis therefore addresses the research question: What are the
measured eﬀects of water softening for the household?
This master thesis investigates the eﬀect of the hardness of water on the performance of kettles, which is done by
development of several experimental tests. Of the invented test three tests were found interesting; the aesthetic
study, lid resistance and the image processing of precipitation coverage of the bottom of kettles. Both the aes-
thetic study and the lid resistance test indicate an improved performance for kettles when implementing water
softening. 50% of the kettles were found to be in poor state (>5class) when exposed to hard water (20◦dH),
which was lowered to 10% when exposed to softened water (10◦dH). And the improvement in the lowered lid
resistance for the kettles was found to be 78%.
The measured eﬀects of water softening were furthermore investigated by registration and interviews of house-
holds in Brøndbyvester, who experience water softening. We found that the descaler consumption are lowered
76% for kettles and 44% for coﬀee brewers. The household also reported decreasing consumption of salt for the
dishwasher, which is found to be 29% (interviews) or 26% (registration). Finally, the household revealed that
water softening leads to a 41% (interviews) or 19% (registration) decrease in detergent for washing machines.
The measured decrease in the purchase of descaler, salt and detergent are, however, not connected with the
data from the consumer survey by Statistics Denmark. The consumer survey reports the regional purchase of
kettles and coﬀee brewers, washing machines, dishwashers and tumble dryers and detergent, which was com-
pared with the regional water hardness, not to be correlated. An uncertainty is, however, present as a study on
a municipality level would be more reliable.
As mentioned the households in the greater Copenhagen area are most often irritated by the hard waters ten-
dency to cause large amount of precipitation in kettles among others. This study therefore measured if the
precipitation amount would lower when the water hardness changed. The precipitation accumulation was inves-
tigated as function of the usage, which was found to follow a logarithmic tendency. The precipitation amount
was measured to 24 mg/usage for hard water and 17 mg/usage for softened water. Indicating how household
can expect 29% decrease in the precipitation when softening 10◦dH.
The lowered need of descaling and the decrease in descaler, salt and detergent consumption are most likely
experienced by the household. But, a saving due to water softening is also the expansion of household appli-
ances lifetime. This study tried to correlate the improvement in performance of kettles with the lifetime but
found no reliable results. And through literature it was found how the estimation of lifetime are related to
large uncertainties. However, the expansion in lifetime of appliances might not be experienced by households
as it happens during a much larger time scale, were the fashion or technology development might as well be the
reason for new purchase of appliances than the state.
This study reports about measured experienced eﬀects for the household due to water softening. Which verify
the estimated savings by other reports. The measured savings hereby justify the water bill the household are
to pay for the waterworks investment and maintenance costs of water softening.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The drinking water in Denmark originates from the groundwater, which is found in subsurface groundwater
reservoirs. In these reservoirs the water encounters carbonate bearing rocks, like limestone and chalk. Contact
between drinking water and carbonate bearing rocks results in dissolution of carbonate and bicarbonate salts,
such as calcium and magnesium carbonates. These divalent metal cations are a determine factor for the hardness
of drinking water (MacAdam and Parsons, 2004). The hardness depends on the concentration of divalent metal
cations, which is related to the geological region from where the water origins (H¨ockendorf et al., 2017). The
Greater Copenhagen area is an area based on a geological region dominated by chalk and limestone resulting
in hard water, whereas the western Denmark is based on sandy aquifer resulting in more soft water, cf. ﬁgure
1.1.
Figure 1.1: Map over hardness of water in Denmark by GEUS (2017). Hardness degrees are measured in dH: Very soft
<4◦dH, Soft 4-8◦dH, Medium hard 8-12◦dH, Quite hard 12-18◦dH, Hard 18-24◦dH, Very hard 24-30◦dH and Extremely
hard >30◦dH. Figure by GEUS (2017).
Approximately 340 million (mio.) m3 water was in 2015 extracted and used in Denmark. Of these 115 mio.
m3 (34%) had a hardness of 12-18◦dH, 159 mio. m3 (47%) a hardness of 18-24◦dH and 8.53 mio. m3 (2.5%) a
hardness above 24◦dH (Rambøll, 2017). According to Naturstyrelsen (2011) central softening is recommended if
the hardness is above 15◦dH, which corresponds to 84% of the water in Denmark (284 mio. m3 water) according
to Rambøll (2017). Due to the high percentage of hard water in Denmark, the topic of softening drinking water
is highly relevant.

==PAGE:11==
Precipitation and scale formation can become an issue as a result of hard water, especially on the household
devices functioning at elevated temperatures like the kettles, washing machines, dishwashers and coﬀee brewers
(Van der Bruggen et al., 2009). At an industrial level it is estimated, that the hardness of the water leads to
fouling costs of 700-930 mio. $US for the UK and 8000-10,000 mio. $US for USA (MacAdam and Parsons,
2004). Due to these costs households and waterworks are investing in water softening with the aim of reducing
costs. (Naturstyrelsen, 2011) has estimated that the savings for the households will be approximately 6.9 kr/m3
when water is softened from a hardness of 22 to 8 ◦dH.
It is, additionally, estimated that the total savings, including the investment and maintenance costs for the
waterworks and the household’s savings for the same softening degree (from 22 to 8 ◦dH) to be 1.5 kr/m3 water
using the pellet method and 0.4 kr/m3 water using the ion-exchange method (Naturstyrelsen, 2011).
Similarly, Rambøll (2017) recently presented a report with the estimated savings of water softening on a national
scale for a 30-year time period.
Rambøll (2017) assumed, that of the 2,600 water supplying companies in
Denmark, the companies supplying more than 1 mio. m3 annually would use a pellet softener suitable for larger
plant, where companies supplying less than 1 mio. m3 annually would use an ion-exchange softening plant.
Based on these assumptions and others the net present value (NVP) after a period of 30 years was evaluated to
be 31,903 mio. kr for softening to a hardness of 6◦dH, 20,641 mio. kr for a hardness of 10◦dH or 16,552 kr for
a hardness of 14◦dH. Households were estimated to account for a NPV of 36,209 mio. kr (6◦dH), 23,635 mio.
kr (10◦dH) or 18,572 mio kr (14◦dH). It implies that the savings of the softening primarily is due to household
savings. Additionally, the costs for the investment and maintenance of the softening plants will aﬀect the price
for the water, which the household will pay. Rambøll (2017) estimates an increased water price of 1.1kr/m3
(6◦dH), 0.79kr/m3 (10◦dH) or 0.52 kr/m3 (14◦dH). It entails that the household has to pay more for drinking
water per cubic meter, however, at longer time scale users will experience savings.
The expected savings for the household are a combination of; the reduced energy consumption, reduced detergent
consumption, reduced descaler consumption or consumption of other cleaning agents, prolonged lifetime on
household devices exposed to the drinking water and the lowered salt consumption for the internal ion-exchange
softening in the dishwasher, ﬁgure 1.2 (Godskesen et al., 2012).
Figure 1.2: Illustration of the aﬀected areas within the household when central softening of the drinking water occurs, by
Godskesen et al. (2012).

HOFOR and T˚arnby forsyning are two waterworks in the Greater Copenhagen area, which in 2017 started
to soften the drinking water. HOFOR is softening their water with the pellet softening technology and has
supplied Brøndby municipality with softened water (from 22 to 10-13 ◦dH) since the 18th of September 2017
(HOFOR, 2018a) (HOFOR, 2018b). T˚arnby forsyning has used the ion-exchange softening technology in T˚arnby
municipality to reduce the hardness from 24-30 ◦dH to 19 ◦dH since the 16th of October 2017 (T˚arnby forsyning,
2018).
HOFOR has reported, that the households receiving softened water in the western part of Brøndby can expect
an annually saving of 500 kr, assuming a household of four residents. HOFOR informs the savings are due to the
decreased consumption of detergent and decalciﬁcation chemicals and due to the expanded lifetime of kettles,
coﬀee brewers, washing machines and dishwashers (HOFOR, 2018a). Similarly, COWI (2012) has reported that
much of the household savings are due to the expansion of the household appliances lifetime.
Literature, like the report from Naturstyrelsen (2011) and COWI (2012) have estimated the total costs and
savings for these softening projects. However, there are no or limited veriﬁcation of the estimated eﬀects of
softening. Sweden and the Netherlands has been central softening their water since the 70s with the pellet
softening technology.
But even though Sweden and the Netherlands have been softening for four decades,
there is no available data for the economical or environmental eﬀects of the softening. The two countries have
interviewed a part of the population, resulting in some qualitative data for the eﬀects of softening (Rambøll,
2017). But there are no measurements about hard waters eﬀects on the household appliances lifetime.
This master thesis will address the economic and environmental eﬀect of water softening and will contribute with
missing quantitative data for the eﬀects of softening and compare experimental data with some of the existing
estimates.
The experimental data is collected by following an existing project of HOFORs drinking water
softening in Brøndbyvester by DTU Environment. Due to this project, it will be possible to get measurements
from a full-scale experiment, such a study will thereby provide the quantitative data regarding the savings of
softening.
This master thesis also includes some new invented test method for testing of kettles, with the aim to assess the
kettles performance according to the degree of hardness of the drinking water. These tests can then be used to
assess the hard waters eﬀect of the household appliances’ lifetime, which is assumed to be correlated with the
performance of the kettle. The thesis furthermore includes the analysis of the precipitation change as result to
the water softening and an analysis of the statistical data for purchase of household appliances and detergent
as function of the water hardness.

1.1
Aim
The aim of this project is to quantify the eﬀects of softened drinking water for the household through experimen-
tal measurements. The investigated eﬀects are the chemical changes, detergent and time consumption by the
household before and after receiving softened water. This project follows the softening project by HOFOR at
Brøndby, where DTU Environment has gathered information regarding the performance of devices where heat
elevation occurs; kettle, washing machine, dishwasher and coﬀee brewer before the softening was conducted.
This project will compare data gathered for devices exposed to softened water with the hard water from before
HOFOR started softening.
Doing so, it is the aim to assess if the use of chemicals for decalciﬁcation, the

Research question
The research question addressed in current study is:
What are the measured eﬀects of water softening for the household?
This question is answered through the sub questions listed in bullets bellow. The questions are answered in
the same order throughout this report.
• How can the hard water’s eﬀect on kettle, in terms of performance, be measured?
• Does the household appliances need more descaling, when running on hard water than on soft?
• Does the dishwater consume more salt and dishwater detergent, when running on hard water compared

the sixth question is answered by a study of the statistical data available from Statistics Denmark about the

2.1.2
Deﬁnition of hard water
The hardness of water is measured by the sum of dissolved multivalent cations present in the water, often
magnesium and calcium. Water hardness is deﬁned in two ways; either as temporary hardness or permanent
hardness, where the sum of the two types of hardness equals the total hardness (TH). The total hardness of
water is measured as the sum of the normalities of these multivalent cations, cf. equation 2.2 (Nazaroﬀand
Alvarez-Cohen, 2001).
Total hardness (TH) = 2 · {Mg2+} + 2 · {Ca2+}
(2.2)
The temporary hardness is the carbonate hardness (CH), which precipitates when boiled and which is based
on the CO2 content present in the water. It means that the temporary hardness is caused by multivalent ions,
like magnesium and calcium, linked with bicarbonate ions (Van der Bruggen et al., 2009)(Scholz, 2016). An
example of a chemical reaction for temporary hardness is given by: 2.3.
Ca(HCO3)2 + Ca(OH)2 →2CaCO3 + 2H2O
(2.3)
The permanent hardness is the non-carbonate hardness (NCH), which is not caused by the presents of CO2 (Van
der Bruggen et al., 2009) (Valbjørn, 2017). The hardness is deﬁned as the content of calcium and magnesium
ions which are associated with non-carbonate ions like chloride and sulphate 2.4 (Scholz, 2016). An example of
a chemical reaction for permanent hardness can be expressed as:
CaSO4 + Na2CO3 →CaCO3 + 2Na2SO4
(2.4)
The deﬁnition of temporary and permanent hardness are based on the relation between the total hardness and
the normality of the carbonate species (Nc), cf. equation 2.5.
Nc = [HCO−
3 ] + 2[CO2−
3 ]
(2.5)

carbonate hardness is dominant. Which means that the total hardness equals the carbonate hardness or the
temporary hardness, cf. equation 2.6. On the other hand, if the normality of the carbonate species is smaller
than the normality of the multivalent cations, the carbonate hardness equals the normality of the carbonate
species, cf. equation 2.7 (Nazaroﬀand Alvarez-Cohen, 2001).
If Nc > TH, Then CH = TH and NCH = 0

If Nc < TH, Then CH = Nc and NCH = TH - CH

When a solution with calcium carbonate becomes supersaturated and starts to precipitate it deposits on the
surface as either calcite, aragonite, vaterite, calcium carbonate monohydrate or calcium carbonate hexahydrate,
or with the presence of magnesium, dolomite can be formed (Amjad, 1999) (Xu and Poduska, 2014). The
chemical structure of calcite (a), dolomite (b), aragonite (c) and vatertie (d) in a unit cell are illustrated on

Depending on the level of supersaturation, ﬂow rate, temperature, surface material, pH and types of impurities,
the induction time and types of polymorphs formed varies (Amjad, 1999) (MacAdam and Parsons, 2004). The
induction time describes the ﬁrst step of the precipitation. MacAdam and Parsons (2004) deﬁned the induction
time as the time it takes for the calcium carbonate precipitation to deposit on a surface. Meaning the time it
takes for the precipitate in the solution to stick onto a surface. Once the precipitation sticks to the surface it
must withstand erosion to become strong. With time the precipitate becomes solid and is strengthened by the
recrystallization and dehydration (MacAdam and Parsons, 2004).

The precipitation of calcite, dolomite, aragonite or vaterie might lead to reduced ﬂow rates in pipelines
and reduced lifetime of machines and electrical devices functioning at elevating temperatures for the house-
hold (H¨ockendorf et al., 2017). However, the correlation between the hardness of the water and its ability
to precipitate is not straight forward. A widely used theoretical parameter for determination of the calcium
carbonate precipitation is the calcium carbonate precipitation potential (CCPP). The CCPP is the calculation
of the equilibrium between calcium and carbonic acid, when accounting for the water hardness, ionic carbon
content, pH and temperature as well (H¨ockendorf et al., 2017). The CCPP is similar to the SI as it is equal to
zero, when the IAP equals the solubility constant, cf. equation 2.11. CCPP calculations are often much more
complicated. The CCPP can be calculated only for the calcite, but also for other polymorphs as aragonite,
vaterite and dolomite, which means that the CCPP needs to account for several equilibria in the calculation.
Due to the many complicated calculations required to assess the CCPP a software program is often used carry
out calculations. In this study the software program PHREEQC will be used to calculate the CCPP for the
before and after period of the water softening in Brøndby. The CCPP will be used to assess the softening
eﬃciency with respect to the precipitation. The CCPP will then be compared with the measured precipitation
weight gain and the precipitation area.

2.2
Softening technologies
The pellet reactor is the most used technology for central softening. It is used in wide extent Sweden and
the Netherlands (Rambøll, 2017). The ion-exchange softening technology however, a cheaper as investment,
why it is often chosen by smaller water supplying companies or on a decentral level (Rambøll, 2017). Other
softening technologies can be mentioned, such as the nano-ﬁltration, reverse osmosis, magnetic and ultrasound
(Naturstyrelsen, 2011), but this study will only be focusing on the two most widely applied technologies, the
ion-exchange softening technology and water softening with the pellet reactors.
Rambøll (2017) has ranked the ion-exchange method as the most optimal solution for water supplying companies
with capacities below 1 mio. m3 annually, where the pellet reactor is the most optimal solution for companies
supplying more than 1 mio. m3 water annually. These assessments are based on the economy and suitability of
the two softening technologies. Rambøll (2017) estimates the pellet reactor costs 15kr per m3 water managed,
where they assess the ion-exchange only costs 14kr per m3 water, table 2.2. A similar study was performed by
COWI (2012), who also estimates that the investment costs for the ion-exchange (0.2 kr/m3) is lower than for
the pellet reactor (0.5 kr/m3), table 2.2. Additionally, both Rambøll (2017) and COWI (2012) assess the pellet
reactor to be the most expensive regarding maintenance, which is the reason why the ion-exchange is the most
optimal device to install in smaller scaled softening systems.
It might not seem relevant to mention the water works’ costs for water softening, when the focus in this study
lies on the eﬀects for the household. However, the water price paid by the household might be aﬀected when the
corresponding water works start softening the drinking water (Van der Bruggen et al., 2009). Assuming that
all the costs for the waterworks are paid by the household, their price to pay for the water might vary much,
depending on the chosen softening technology, cf. table 2.2.
From an environmental point of view the pellet softening technology might be the most feasible of the two
technologies, as it produces less waste. The ion exchange softening technology creates waste water polluted
with calcium, chloride, sodium and magnesium ions, which needs to be cleaned before being disposed. Whereas
the waste product of the pellet reactor is primarily pellets, which can be used elsewhere, where calcium carbonate
might be needed as for instants in the agriculture (Van der Bruggen et al., 2009).

2.2.1
Ion-exchange technology
The ion exchange device soften the drinking water by absorbing the calcium and magnesium ions, through
the exchange of sodium ions. The ion exchange softening device contains resins, which typically consists of
carboxylate groups (-COO·). These resins are charged with sodium ions (Na+) by feeding a brine (NaCl) solu-
tion through the column of the device (Albrechtsen, 2016) (COWI, 2012). Thereby the carboxylate groups are
attached with sodium ions (-COONa), ﬁgure 2.5. When the column is then exposed to hard water, containing
magnesium and calcium ions, the sodium and magnesium or calcium ions are exchanged due to the electrostatic
preference, ﬁgure 2.5. When the magnesium and calcium ions are then extracted from the water, the water is
softened (Petersen, 1994) (Albrechtsen, 2016).
When most of the sodium ions are gone, as it has exchanged places with the magnesium or calcium, the resins
need to be recharged with brine. When the sodium ions in the brine solution washes through the column, the
sodium will seek an equilibrium between the water solution and the resin, leading to the washout of attached
magnesium and calcium ions. The softening device often consists of two columns, as one column can act as
water softener, while the other are recharging. The brine for the recharge of the column is located next to the
softening column and are fed with a mixture of salt and water to create the brine solution. The estimated brine
consumption is that 30-38 g of salt, NaCl, are needed for softening 1 m3 drinking water 1 ◦dH (Naturstyrelsen,
2011).
When the ion exchange softening device softens the water it removes all magnesium and calcium ions, which
leads to a water hardness of zero ◦dH. Such a state is unwanted as it acts as aggressive water, which might
cause corrosion and thereby leakage in pipelines. Therefore, the softened water is mixed with some of the hard
water, before being provided to the consumer.

2.2.2
Pellet softening
The pellet reactor softens the hard water by adjusting the pH so that the calcium carbonate crystallizes and
adsorbs to the present sand grains. The pellet reactor consists of a large tank, where hard water ﬂows through
from the bottom and up to the top, where it is softened along the way. The steady ﬂow of water lifts up the
small sand grains present in the reactor (COWI, 2012).
Chemicals like sodium hydroxide (NaOH), calcium hydroxide (Ca(OH)2) and sodium carbonate (Na2CO3) are
added as reagents to the reactor, which increases the pH to between 8.5-8.8. The increased pH aﬀects the
carbonate equilibrium and makes the calcium carbonate precipitate, see equation 2.12 and 2.13 (COWI, 2012)
(Albrechtsen, 2016).
Ca2+ + HCO−
3 + NaOH →CaCO3 + Na+ + H2O
(2.12)
Ca2+ + 2HCO−
3 + Ca(OH)2 →2CaCO3 + 2H2O
(2.13)
The precipitate attaches to the ﬁne sand grains, which increases the density of the sand grains and makes them
fall towards the bottom of the reactor. When the sand grains have grown thick in calcium carbonate, they have
reached the bottom of the reactor and are extracted from the reactor and used for other purposes elsewhere
(COWI, 2012).
The magnesium ions are not removed from the water in the pellet reactor (COWI, 2012). Even though the
pellet rector can reduce the hardness of the water down to 20 mg Ca/L (2.8◦dH) the ﬁnal hardness is only
lowered to between 3-4◦dH (based on the average magnesium present in the Danish drinking water), due to the
remaining magnesium.
As mentioned earlier NaOH, Ca(OH)2 or Na2CO3 increase the pH value of the water to 8.5-8.8, which means
it has to be lowered again before it reaches the tap of the consumers. The water is passed through a ﬁlter after
the reactor to catch the remaining precipitate, which did not adsorb to the sand grains (COWI, 2012). As
equation 2.12 and 2.13 illustrate the usage of the reagent Ca(OH)2 creates twice as much CaCO3 as the NaOH
reagent, which disposal can be costly to manage (Van der Bruggen et al., 2009). The NaOH reagent, however,
contributes to the dissolution of sodium ions in the drinking water, where the guide lines for sodium ions are
175mg/L (Miljø- og Fødevareministeriet, 2017). If the sodium concentration are high (close to the guidelines
of Na), the reagent of Ca(OH)2 would be a more beneﬁcial choice, as it would not interfere with the sodium
concentration and risking it crossing the guidelines.

2.3
Costs and savings of water softening
As addressed the hard water leads to precipitation of CaCO3, and a simple way prevent precipitation is to
apply a water softening. But what are the saved impacts, when preventing the CaCO3 precipitation through
water softening? The precipitation from hard water has a negative impact on devices, pipelines and machines
exposed to it. The impacts among others are:
• Decrease in heat transfer to water (COWI, 2012) (H¨ockendorf et al., 2017) (Godskesen et al., 2012).
• Reduced lifetime on machines and exposed devices (Godskesen et al., 2012).
• Fouling i membrane ﬁltration at water treatment facilities (H¨ockendorf et al., 2017).
• Reduced ﬂow rates in pipelines (H¨ockendorf et al., 2017).
• Descaling measures (H¨ockendorf et al., 2017).
• Large chemical consumption i.e. descaler, detergent and salt for dishwashers (Godskesen et al., 2012).
These impacts has been investigated by Rambøll (2017), COWI (2012) and Van der Bruggen et al. (2009), who
found these impacts can be lowered if the drinking water is softened. Van der Bruggen et al. (2009) reports the
savings impacts from the hard water would be 82.50 e (615 kr) annually for an average family in Flanders with
central softening of the drinking water down to 15◦F by a pellet reactor. They furthermore report the savings
from the lowered impacts of the hard water to be larger than the costs of the central softening, indicating
that the softening of drinking water is economically beneﬁcial. Similarly, Rambøll (2017) reports an average
household in Denmark would be able to save between 767-404 kr annually by central softening of the drinking
water to between 6-14◦dH. An illustration of the water softening eﬀects are illustrated by Godskesen et al.
(2012), who also illustrates the areas in the household that are expected to be eﬀected by water softening, ﬁgure
2.7.
The savings for the household are essential to assess, as they account for 65% of the water consumption in
Denmark (Naturstyrelsen, 2011). Hereby also accounting for the majorities of the saving from the softened
water. Rambøll (2017) estimates the savings for the household to be 5.81kr/m3 water, which is signiﬁcantly
higher than for companies, who savings is estimated to be 0.04kr/m3 water.
The savings are a signiﬁcant
motivation and household would probably like to pay for a softening technology, if the water price went up as a
consequence. Van der Bruggen et al. (2009) reports that 50% of the population in Flanders are willing to pay
30e more for their water, if it were to be softened. This means that the savings for the household are important
and a sensitive parameter. Because what if the savings for the household are not as high as promised, when the
household pays the higher water price? This study focuses on the savings for the household, as they are the
majority group been aﬀected by the softening of water.

y-axis similarly, the slopes for the four graphs has similar slopes, which is a decrease of 0.02 years in lifetime
per mg CaCO3/L increase in the drinking water, ﬁgure 2.8a.
The lifetime of the heat elevating household devices is thought to be shortened, when exposed to hard water.
Where Van der Bruggen et al. (2009) based their lifetime for the devices on estimations for the costs per year for
the devices. Whereas, Godskesen et al. (2012) reports about estimations based on information from plumbers
and retailers. To verify such estimations, one would need to follow the lifetime or purchase of the devices for
years and extracting data-points where the new purchase of a device was not caused by shift in fashion. Current
study does not seek to directly investigate the lifetime of the devices, but to make a comparison with the wear of
a device. The hypothesis being that the hard waters wear on a device are the reason for the shortened lifetime,
which means that the estimated decreasing slope in lifetime as function of the hardness of water will be the
same as the diﬀerent wear parameters as function of hardness of water. In current study 30 kettles, exposed
to hard water in diﬀerent degrees, were at disposal, why a performance test was developed to enlighten on this
hypothesis. The details regarding the tests are explained in section 3.1.

3.1.2
Physical wear tests
Due to the observation of the damaged state of the kettles, it was found interesting to test the wear of the
kettles in various ways. As mentioned earlier it was observed that the lid of some of the kettles was diﬃcult to
close, why we test the force needed to close the lid, and to asses if the force varies with the varying usage and
thereby precipitation degree of the kettles.
Furthermore, it was found relevant to investigate the noise level of the kettles when boiling water.
This
parameter was noted during the interviews with the participants in Brøndby, where some of the participants
answered that they experience an increase in noise level, when the kettle or coﬀee brewer needed to be descaled.
This hypothesis was found interesting and is tested in current study.
Finally, another relevant parameter that has been investigated is the surface temperature of the kettle. Some
reports state the precipitation limits the heat transfer as it coats the heating element (COWI, 2012). However,
Valbjørn (2017) reported the heating eﬃciency and electricity demand for kettles boiling water are not aﬀected
by the precipitation of calcium carbonate. However, it has not been assessed if the surface temperature of
the kettles changes, when the heating element is coated in precipitation. If the heat transfer from the heating
element to the water gets less eﬀective with an increased amount of precipitation it might be, that the kettle
would become hotter on the outside, as its release of heat is limited.
The listed hypothesises addressed in current study are that:
• The kettle makes more noise, when it is covered in precipitation.
• The lid of the kettle is more diﬃcult to close, when the hinge in covered in precipitation.
• The precipitation in the kettles makes the kettle more hot when boiling water
To address these hypothesises following four tests was conducted, which methods are elaborated in the next
sections:
1 Noise test
2 Resistance in lid when tilting it
3 Resistance in lid when closing it
4 Surface temperature
Due to the many tests and time limitations, the tests were not conducted on all two times 30 kettles at ﬁrst.
A selection of the kettles from the before period were chosen, 12 kettles, for the four tests titles, where kettles
were was chosen from category 3, 4, 5 and 6, with respect to the aesthetic categorization explained in section
3.1.1. If the ﬁndings from the tests seemed to indicate a correlation with usage the tests were performed for all
30 kettles available from the before period and all 30 from the after period.
The method for the physical tests in this master thesis are innovative and no one has tried, to the authors best
knowledge, to link the performance of kettles and the precipitation degree before. Some of the tests turned
out to be diﬃcult to replicate or with no useful outcome, and some of the tests required several iterations and
optimization. The result chapter, later in this report, will cover an evaluation of the invented test methods and
the relevance and applicability of them.

Noise test using KIMO 100
A sound level meter, KIMO 100, was used to evaluate the noise level from the boiling kettles. The device
measures the minimum and maximum noise detected within a time frame. The test was performed in a closed
noise reduced room in a basement, where few people would enter and therefore low interferes with the results.
The background noise in the room were approximately 38 dB, where the majority of the noise originated from
a ventilation in the room. The kettles were each ﬁlled with 0.8 liter of cold tap water. The tested kettles were
switched on, and the sound level meter was turned on immediately after. There was kept a close eye with the
measured dB during the boiling process, to detect if any large peaks occurred. When the kettle had clicked
oﬀ, indicating the water has reached the boiling point temperature, the sound level meter was paused as well.
The maximum dB was noted before resetting the sound level meter and running the test for the next kettle in
line. The click from the kettle was louder (>80dB) than the noise from the kettle while boiling water, why the
maximum dB noted was the value observed just before the kettle clicked oﬀ.
During the noise test, the kettles sometimes made a clicking sound after haven boiled once. This sound was
properly caused by the plastic expanding or contracting due to temperature changes. If the clicking sound made
the maximum dB measured increase, then the test was redone, as the sound is not connected to the boiling
process.
The test for the 12 kettles were repeated three times for each kettle, to assess the variance of the test method.
Here the second repetition of the noise test in general revealed a higher dB value, than the ﬁrst and it was also
noted in the test, that the kettles started to sound peculiar. In the ﬁrst test, the kettles did not seem to make as
much screaming and humming sounds, as they did on the second repetition of the test. In the third repetition,
the kettles still made sounds, as in the second repetition, but not with the same high pitch. One explanation
could be, that the ﬁrst test did not make as much sound, as the kettles had been unused for a long period
(approximately 1.5 year). Another explanation could be the noise was due to the thermal shock the kettles
got as the cold water was poured into them while they were still a bit hot. The set-up for the experiment are
illustrated on ﬁgure 3.2.
Noise test using Phone App
As mentioned it would be interesting to measure the average dB noise deriving from the kettles, why a sound-
meter app for the phone was used, see ﬁgure 3.3. The app uses the microphone on Samsung S6 to detect noise
and measure the max, min and average noise. This measuring tool was used to test the noise from 13 randomly

3.1.5
Image processing
A way to examine the precipitation of the calcium carbonate could be to analyse the bottom area of the kettles
and calculate the percentage, that is covered in precipitation. The hypothesis is that the precipitation will build
up at the bottom of the kettle, until it reaches a saturation point, after which ﬂakes of precipitation will peel
oﬀand disappear as they are poured out of the kettle. When the ﬂakes peel oﬀ, they will expose the metallic
surface of the bottom which means that the percentage area covered in precipitation would go down.
The assessment of such hypothesis was performed through image processing of pictures taken of the bottom of
the kettles. The program language Python was used to analyse the images, which would detect the transitions
between precipitation and metal bottom. The script for the image processing are available in appendix D. The
image processing consists of three steps. Initially the program imports and image and converts them into grey
scale images, see ﬁgure 3.9a.
First step apply an edge detecting ﬁlter that highlights all transitions in the image. Transition between the
metallic bottom and precipitation will in this step be detected, see ﬁgure 3.9b.
Second step apply a blurring ﬁlter that smear out all features in the image. A blurring degree parameter has
to be determined by the user, see ﬁgure 3.9c.
Third step apply a binary ﬁlter that converts the image into binary black and white image, see ﬁgure 3.9d. A
threshold parameter has to be determined by the user. The ratio between black and whiter can then be used
to estimate the area covered by precipitation.
Two parameters have to be set by the user of the program, and those parameters has been determined such
that a visual inspection of the processed images represented the reality in the best possible way. All images
have been analysed using the same parameters.

3.3
Calcium carbonate precipitation
When softening the drinking water, it is the purpose among others to lower the precipitation amount in the
household appliances. This section introduces two methods used to evaluate the precipitation amount observed
in kettles when exposed to hard water (20◦dH) and softened water (12◦dH). The ﬁrst method is by weighing the
aggregated precipitation amount and comparing it with the usage of the kettle. Another method is to visually
assess the precipitation area in the bottom of the kettle and correlate it with the usage of the kettle.
For this study two times 30 kettles were provided to the participants in the project. 30 kettles for the before
period with the hard water and 30 kettles for the after period with the softened water. The participants were
supplied with a sheet for the kettle, on which they noted every time they used the kettle. The kettles used
were OBH Nordic nemo black plastic kettles with a volume of 1 L. The kettles were therefore identical for all
participants.
3.3.1
Weighted calcium carbonate precipitation
All kettles were weighted before and after the test period. During the test period of the kettles each boiling
were noted as a mark on a sheet provided with the kettle. A correlation between the precipitation amount and
the usage of the kettle could be assessed, by knowing the weight gain of the kettle, which are assumed only to
be due to calcium carbonate precipitation, and the degree of usage for the kettle.
The kettles were dried before measuring the ﬁnal weight. This was done to eliminate the weight of the water
from the assessment of the weight of the precipitation. The kettles were dried by emptying them for water, and
afterwards leaving them to air-dry in a standard tempered room for 5-7 days.
Figure 3.12: Experimental set-up for measurement of calcium carbonate precipitation. First measuring the weight of the
unexposed kettle. Then noting the number of times, the kettle is used. Then drying the kettle, after which the amount of
precipitation can be measured.
3.3.2
CCPP calculation in PHREEQC
As mentioned in section 2.1.3 the irritating element of the hard water is its tendency to precipitate. The pre-
cipitation potential can be assessed with the calculation of the CCPP, which then can be used to compare with
the before and after period, and to indicate if the participants can expect less precipitation in their household
appliances. The concentrations of the ions; calcium, chloride, ﬂorid, iron, potassium, hydrogen carbonate, mag-
nesium, manganese, sodium, nitrite, nitrate, oxygen, phosphor and sulphur was used to calculate the CCPP in
PHREEQC, see appendix E.
The algorithm in the program calculates the saturation index between the diﬀerent ions and calculates the
concentration of the calcite crystal in the water. The carbon dioxide is set to -3.5 as an open system is as-
sumed, which is often the case for water in household appliances. The temperature for the chemical reaction is
furthermore set to 99.9◦C to simulate household appliances functioning at elevating temperatures.

4.1
Evaluation of developed tests for kettles
Each of the developed test are evaluated based on their reproducibility, variance and representation of a cor-
relation between the usage of the kettle and the performance of it. The section ends with the selection of the
tests which are classiﬁed as useful for the measurement of hard and softened waters eﬀect on kettles.
4.1.1
Noise test
The noise test required a very quiet room, where even small noises, would interfere with the results, which
means that the test is diﬃcult to perform, and interference occurs easily. Furthermore, there seems to be no
correlation between the usage of the kettles and the measured dB noise level, either with the KIMO 100 nor
with the Sound Meter app as measuring tools. It seems more as the data-points are randomly generated. It
was additionally found that the noise detected by ear was not correlated with the dB noise level. The noise test
performed with the KIMO 100 was repeated three times, and the test with the Sound Meter app was repeated
twice. The coeﬃcient of variance (CV) for the test conducted with the KIMO 100 varied between 20-140 %
and the CV for the Sound Meter app measurements varied between 40-90%. This is a large variation, which
indicates that the test is not reproducible. For this reason, the experiment was only conducted for the before
study and not for the after study. The results for the test are available in appendix B, but will not be elaborated
more in the main part of the report.
4.1.2
Surface temperature
The measurements of the surface temperature consist of the measuring tool, the FLIR C2 Compact Thermal
Imager, and the additional software program FLIR tools. The pictures were taken with the same distance for all
kettles and with the focus of the camera on the same spot of the kettle. The results of the study revealed very
similar temperature measurements for all tested kettles, and the thermal pictures look qualitatively identical.
The temperature measured varied up to 5◦C across the kettles but seems to have no correlation with usage
of the kettles. In conclusion measuring the surface temperature is a simple method, with no or insigniﬁcant
connection to the usage of kettles. The results for the test are available in appendix B, but will not be elaborated
more in the main part of the report.

4.1.3
Lid resistance tilting
The method that tested the resistance of the kettle lids, when tilting, was easily performed. A tripod was
installed, a string attached to the lid with a needle and then the resistance could be measured at the end of the
string. The test was conducted three times for each kettle, and the resistance measured, was within the same
order of magnitude (average CV on 6%). The replicates of the test were performed with the same set-up without
changes. It can be concluded that the test was reproducible. But, if the test method was to be reproduced after
tearing it down and reassembling again, the set-up needs to be noted more precisely. Parameters such as the
length of the string, distance between the tripods, angle and assembling position, position of the needle and the
friction of the pulling screwing clam. If these parameters are precisely measured and noted, it is assumed that
others would be able to conduct the same test, with the same results. An ANOVA test of the results conclude,
that there is no signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the data-sets (p>0.05). A visual study of the 12 data-points indicates
no correlation between the usage of the kettle and the resistance, when tilting the lid. Therefore, the test was
not considered interesting, with respect to the study of the precipitations eﬀect on the kettles performance, this
being the reason for no further conducted test of this type. The results for the test are available in appendix
B, but will it not be discussed further in the main part of the report.
4.1.4
Lid resistance closing
The set-up for the test is quite simple, however the challenging part was keeping the kettle ﬁxed when closing
the lid and applying the same amount of force with the screwing object. It was found that the resistance in the
lid on average increased about 30%, if the force was moved 1 cm towards the hinge of the kettle. It indicates
the test is very sensitive to the placement of the needle, caused by a change in torque. The variance of the test,
when the needle is positioned at the same spot is, however, the average CV is 8%, which is concluded to be a
suﬃciently low variance for such a test. The test furthermore reveals a correlation between usage of the kettle
and the measured resistance of the lid. By performing linear regression of the data, a convincing ﬁt (|F-value| >
F-critical), was observed for both kettles exposed to hard water and to softened. The linear regression analysis
was used to analyse lid resistance as function of the precipitation amount (g). A signiﬁcant correlation was
found for the kettles exposed to softened water, but low or no correlation were found for the kettles exposed to
hard. This indicated the precipitation amount from the hard water eﬀects the kettles in a diﬀerent way than
the precipitation from the softened water. The test provided information and was considered to be reliable.
The test was thus carried out for all kettles and accepted to be a valid test for the investigation of performance
for a kettle exposed to diﬀerent degrees of water hardness. The results of the test are presented and discussed
in section 4.4.2.

4.2.1
Descaling frequency and amount
The average amount of descaler used for both coﬀee brewers and kettles were relatively similar in all cases and
varied between 78-100 mL, cf. ﬁgure 4.1. The upper and lower quantile for the descaler amount used for the

the amount of descaler used by the participant between the before and after period. Thus, softening of the
drinking water in Brøndby has not inﬂuenced the participants amount of descaler, but what about the descaling
frequency?
Looking at the average value for both the kettles and coﬀee brewers the descaling frequency has decreased. The
participants in the project reported an average descaling frequency of 59 days for the kettles and 76 days for
the coﬀee brewers when supplied with the hard water. Whereas this frequency has lowered after they started
receiving softened water to 106 days for both the kettles and coﬀee brewers, corresponding to a reduction of
80% for the kettles and 39% for the coﬀee brewers. In the interviews with the participants from the project,
no statistically signiﬁcant diﬀerence (p>0.05) was found in the monthly descaling frequency between the before
and after period, cf. ﬁgure 4.2. The T-distribution of the data for the kettles reveals a t-value (tobs) larger than
the t-critical (tcrit), see ﬁgure 4.2, which means that the null-hypothesis must be rejected. The p-value cannot
be calculated as the |t-value| < T-critical for the equation to be applied, cf. equation 3.2. This outcome is most
likely due to the non-normal distribution of the data, which is further elaborated in appendix C.
An explanation of no signiﬁcant evidence, of a decreased descaling frequency, could be due to cleaning habits
for the participants. If they for example are used to descale the kettle every month, they might proceed with
this habit regardless of the softened water. Another explanation could be that the coﬀee brewers have been
programmed to descale in a ﬁxed interval. It would result in a descaling lamp turned on after a certain usage,
and not due to the amount of precipitation in the machine. In such case the descaling frequency would be
independent of the water hardness.

4.3
Calcium carbonate precipitation
4.3.1
Precipitation in kettles
The drinking water at the Brøndbyvester waterworks is softened from 22◦dH to 12◦dH by a pellet reactor
technology, cf. section 2.2, with the aim, among others, to lower the precipitation, which causes irritation for
the household. The weight gain was measured for 60 kettles, 30 exposed to hard water (20◦dH) and 30 exposed
to softened water (10◦dH). By plotting the weight gain as function of the water boiled (L) it is possible to
estimate the precipitation.
The precipitation amount in the kettles is observed to be lowered when the kettle is exposed to softened water
compared to hard. This is diﬃcult to tell from the graphs on ﬁgure 4.13 as the results follow a logarithmic
tendency. However, by calculation of the average value for the precipitation per usage, the precipitation is
discovered to decreases from an average of 24 mg/usage to 17 mg/usage, corresponding to a decrease of 29%
in precipitation amount. The diﬀerence is, however, not statistically signiﬁcant as the |T-value| >T-critical.
By looking at the distribution of the precipitation data for both the before and after period, it is clear that
the distribution is not normal.
Hence, the performed t.test is not reliable, see appendix C for the normal
distribution. A signiﬁcant diﬀerence in the precipitation amount, could therefore be present, although it cannot
be concluded by the statistical analysis.
The kettles exposed to softened water have more high values (>2.5 g) in precipitation weight, than the kettles
exposed to hard water. The outliers from the linear model could be due to a chemical change in the mineral
components, which changes the crystal formation of the precipitation, causing it to stick better to the metal
surface of the kettle. Another reason could be that the density of the formed minerals is diﬀerent.
The results for the precipitation amount as function of the water boiled follows a logarithmic function (see
ﬁgure 4.13a and ﬁgure 4.13b). Both correlation is found to be a good ﬁt, as the calculated F-values are much
larger than the F-critical.
A logarithmic tendency indicates a saturation point for the precipitation in the
kettle, resulting in a inclined slope. The inclined slope is assumed to be because the precipitation brakes of
in ﬂakes and gets washed out, after a saturation point has been reached. This hypothesis was also observed
by the participants, and the next section will elaborate on an image processing method, which conﬁrms the
hypothesis.
The equation for the correlation for the softened water is closer to 1 (L0.82 →L1, which indicate that the
precipitation accumulation for the softened water is more steady and does no vary as much as for the hard
water. A hypothesis could be that the equation is related to the water hardness. With reference to equation
4.1 the slope will become more linear, when the hardness of water decreases (a →1) and more logarithmic if
the hardness increases (a →0).

4.3.3
Change in dissolved ions
As the drinking water is softened from 22◦dH to 12◦dH by the pellet method the ion-balance of especially calcium
and sodium changes, see ﬁgure 2.6 in section 2.2. Table 4.3 presents some of the measured ion-concentrations
from the before and after period at the Brøndbyvester waterworks. Through a calculation in PHREEQC the
CCPP was found to decrease from 285 mg/L to 119 mg/L. This is an decrease of 58 %, which is above the
decrease in precipitation measured for the kettles which were 29% based on the average weight gain per usage.
As mentioned the types of impurities, level of supersaturation and pH eﬀects the induction time and the types
of poly-morphs, which is formed cf. section 2.1.3. As the concentration of the ions in the drinking water has
changed, and also the pH, Amjad (1999) and MacAdam and Parsons (2004) informs that we would expect a
change in the induction time and the poly-morphs found. If we assume that the induction time in our case is
the amount of precipitation weighted, when the kettle has boiled < 30 liter of water, then the diﬀerence can be
found. For the hard water the average precipitation gain is 0.6 g for the kettle used less than 30 times and 0.2
g for the softened water, which is a decrease of 62%. If this is the reduction in induction time, it conﬁrms that
it is aﬀected by the mineral concentration in the water. It is most likely that the type of crystal being formed
as precipitation has also changed as response to the water softening, but to address this a microscopic analysis
is needed for the crystals, which is not performed in current study.

4.4.2
Resistance in the lid of the kettle
The performance test showed a correlation between the usage of the kettle and the resistance in the lid. For the
kettles exposed to hard water, the resistance grows with 18 g per usage, whereas it is lowered to 4 g per usage
in the case of softened water. Another diﬀerence between the used kettles is, that for the hard water resistance
above the detection limit (5 kg) occurred. However, it was not the case with the kettles exposed to softened
water even though the usage pattern has been similar. The diﬀerence in resistance, between kettles exposed to
hard and softened water indicates that it is not just due to normal wear of the kettle, but the hardness of the
water has an impact.
An explanation for the correlation between lid resistance and amount of precipitation could be, that droplets of
water and thereby potentially precipitation reaches the lid, when the water boils. Hence, precipitation slowly
builds up on the hinge of the kettle, causing the observed resistance.
The kettles were new at the beginning of each test, which ensures that they have never been descaled not either
in the test period. Five kettles from the before period were, however, descaled before the performance test.
These kettles are marked as red dots on ﬁgure 4.19a and 4.18a. The three red dots, implies descaled kettles, on
ﬁgure 4.18a was found to have lowered lid resistance. It suggests the resistance is lowered again after descaling.
If this is the case, then the kettles performance would improve again after being descaled, and the lid might
last longer if the kettle is descaled regularly. To enlighten more upon on this matter a test should have been
performed on kettles before they were descaled.

decrease in descaling, salt and detergent consumption are noticed by the residents. Better performance of the
kettles will also be noticed by the residents. These results are strongly positive for companies as HOFOR who
are implementing water softening, as it increases the motivation from the consumers. If a consumer is aware
of these eﬀects they might experience a economical reduction in everyday life expenses, due to less cleaning
of precipitation and savings in products. With reference to section 2.2 the water softening are costly for the
waterworks, and if they were to increase the water bill as a consequence of implementation and maintenance.
Consumers might be more likely to accept this term, if they were quarantined a noticeable diﬀerence, which
this report concludes that they can.

4.6.1
Estimating appliances lifetime
An eﬀect on household lifetime due to softening is less likely to be noticed by the consumers. Based on the
interview of the participants in the project the average lifetime was found to be 4.8 years for kettles and 5.7
years for the coﬀee brewer, 8.9 years for the washing machine and 9.2 for the dishwasher, cf. table 4.7. Even
though, the expanded lifetime might not be the most noticable eﬀect due to the water softening, COWI (2012)
reports that it is the area where the household can expect the largest savings.
Based on the lid resistance test (see section 4.4.2) the expected increase of lifetime was estimated. Here it
is assumed that the improvement of the kettles performance is directly correlated with the expected kettle
lifetime. The percentage improvement of the kettles lid resistance was found to be 78%. If this improvement is
directly correlated to the lifetime for all the household appliances, it indicates lifetime is almost doubled. This
estimation is associated with a large degree of uncertainty and indicates the changed performance of the kettle
probably not is directly related to the expansion in the life. In principle there are a correlation between the
performance and the lifetime, but the calculation should include a conversion factor, which this work has not
investigated.
Other studies performed by Ragan et al. (1996) and Rygaard et al. (2010) have calculated a correlation between
hardness of the water and lifetime of household appliances. Ragan et al. (1996) makes the correlation of the
lifetime with the salinity of the water and Rygaard et al. (2010) with the TDS, cf. equation 4.2 and 4.3.

The lifetime expansion of household devices, as result of the water softening, might not be experiences by the
household. If the lifestyle reported by Hansen (2014) is still valid after 4 years, the Danes tend to replace the
household appliances before the device are broken. The expansion of lifetime for household appliances should
therefore not be the main argument for drinking water softening, as it might not be an eﬀect the consumer
experiences.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
The research question for this report is; what are the measured eﬀects of water softening for the household? To
address this question six performance tests have been developed to investigate, with the aim of investigating
how hard and softened water aﬀects kettles. Performance tests, such as an aesthetic study and a lid resistance
test can be used to evaluate the eﬀects of water softening. This report found a signiﬁcant correlation between
the usage and performance of the kettles for the two invented tests. Another observation from this study, is the
precipitation coverage at the bottom of the kettles is correlated with the usage of the kettles.
It is furthermore concluded, that the household’s consumption of descaler, detergent and salt for dishwasher
is lowered when the water hardness is decreased from 20-22◦dH to 12◦dH. This conclusion is found by an
interview study of 30 participants in Brøndby and their registrations. The measured decrease in consumption
for the descaler is 76% for kettles and 44% for coﬀee brewers. For the salt consumption of dishwasher the
measured decrease was been between 19% (results from registration) and 41% (results from interviews). The
detergent consumption for washing clothes was observed to reduce 29% (results from interviews) or 26% (results
from registration). The obtained results for salt and detergent conusmption in this study veriﬁed the results
estimated by Godskesen et al. (2012) and Rambøll (2017). The results for the descaler consumption verify the
estimated by Godskesen et al. (2012) and Van der Bruggen et al. (2009).
The precipitation of calcium carbonate decreases, when the drinking water is softened. The relation between
the precipitation occurrence and the usage of the kettle follows a logarithmic function. In addition to this
the study found a gain in precipitation move towards stagnation after approximately 50 times of usage, as the
precipitation peel oﬀas ﬂakes.
The aesthetic study and lid resistance test reveal, that the performance of kettles are improved, when exposed
to softened water rather than hard water. The correlation between the performance and the usage of the kettle
follows a linear tendency, where the acceleration for 20◦dH is 18 g/use, which is decreased to 4 g/use when
the water is softened to 12◦dH. Results, furthermore, indicate how the performance might improve as result of
descaling. The results of this study impose the wear of kettles and thereby the lifetime to be correlated with the
hardness of water. The lifetime of appliances are therefore most likely to increase as result of water softening,
but to which degree is unknown.
The correlation between the hardness of water and the diﬀerent eﬀects of hard water for the household does
not match the statistic from the consumer survey by Statistics Denmark. The regional hardness of water and
the regional purchase of kettle and coﬀee brewers, dishwasher, washing machines and tumble dryers do not
imply a correlation, mainly due to the gathering of data according to Danish regions rather than drinking water
hardness.
Finally, it can be concluded that households do experience a diﬀerence when going from hard to softened water
in savings of consumption, lowered precipitation and better performance of appliances, however, the diﬀerence
is not supported by signiﬁcant evidence as the results does not follow a normal distribution. By expanding
the project to a larger scale (>30 participants) the results might follow a normal distribution and reveal the
signiﬁcant diﬀerence (p<0.05).
