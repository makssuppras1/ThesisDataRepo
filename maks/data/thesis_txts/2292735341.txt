ABSTRACT 
 
The goal of this study is to determine the Aircon 10S wind turbine monitoring and con-
trolling capability. This study was conducted at DTU Risø Campus where one such 
wind turbine was present.  
In the first part, the thesis describes the Aircon 10S wind turbine and the location where 
it is erected. Further, it examines the wind turbine‟s graphical user interface describing 
the available options regarding monitor and control. The thesis then identifies alterna-
tive ways to observe and control the activity of the wind turbine and shows the results 
obtained.   
This study shows that the Aircon 10S is a rather compact wind turbine with limited ac-
cess regarding controllability and data collection.

INTRODUCTION 
 
In the last decade, the evolution of wind turbine typology has seen a large growth from 
the early ages of fixed-speed wind turbines equipped with induction generator to the 
variable-speed wind turbines equipped with induction or synchronous generator con-
nected to the power grid through a power converter. 
In 2011 the Risø DTU National Laboratory Intelligent Energy Systems (IES) acquired 
from the Aircon GmbH &Co.KG a small wind turbine created on the latest technology 
available. The Aircon 10S is a gearless wind turbine with a permanent magnet synchro-
nous generator directly connected to the power grid through a unique inverter technolo-
gy. The wind turbine was erected at DTU Risø Campus research institute and connected 
to the local power grid (SYSLAB).  
From the time it was erected since now, the wind turbine has been running and produc-
ing power but with limited knowledge about how to monitor and control its activity. 
The aim of this study is to investigate the capability of monitoring and controlling the 
Aircon 10S wind turbine within the physical limitations and the boundaries set by the 
manufacturer. The focus will be on: 
 measuring the output power production of the wind turbine; 
 measuring and controlling the reactive power output of the wind turbine.  
This study should provide the basis knowledge about the Aircon 10S wind turbine re-
garding the monitoring and controlling capability in order to further investigate to what 
extend it can be integrated in the SYSLAB smart grid.  
This thesis contains five chapters. Chapter 2 deals with the standard wind turbine con-
figurations followed with a description of the Aircon 10S wind turbine. Furthermore, a 
description of the SYSLAB – a smart grid facility at DTU Risø Campus is given, where 
the Aircon 10S wind turbine is erected. In Chapter 3, I present the graphical user inter-
face of the wind turbine which gives information about the wind turbine activity and 
access to the control functions. I continue with describing alternative methods to moni-
tor some parameters of the wind turbine and the tools used. In Chapter 4, I present the 
experimental execution of the tests, the results obtained and an analysis of the resulted 
data. Chapter 5 contains the conclusion of the study with recommendations for further 
work.

BACKGROUND  
 
2.1 
Wind turbine configurations 
 
The evolution of wind turbines design has seen a mix of a variety of innovative con-
cepts from horizontal-axis wind turbines that used two or three blades to vertical-axis 
wind turbines (see Figure 1). 
 
Figure 1 – Horizontal-axis and vertical-axis wind turbine concepts (Wordpress) 
Today, the most common used are the three blades horizontal-axis wind turbines that 
can be classified in four different types, whose configuration is presented in the follow-
ing sub-sections. 
2.1.1 
Type A configuration 
The first generation of wind turbines, type A, consists of an asynchronous squirrel cage 
induction generator connected directly to the grid via a transformer. The rotor of the 
generator is connected to the shaft of the wind turbine through a gearbox. A capacitor 
==PAGE:11==
Background 
9 
bank is normally used since this type of generator consumes reactive power from the 
grid. The soft-starter helps to reduce the mechanical stress on the generator and shaft 
during start-up (see Figure 2). (Ackermann, 2012)   
 
Figure 2 - Type A of wind turbine configuration: fixed speed. 
2.1.2 
Type B configuration 
The second type of wind turbine configuration, type B, is similar to type A. The differ-
ence is given by the fact that it uses a wound rotor induction generator (WRIG) with 
variable rotor resistance which gives some control of the power output of the generator 
and a better operating speed range (see Figure 3). (Ackermann, 2012) 
 
 
Figure 3 - Type B of wind turbine configuration: limited variable speed with variable 
rotor resistance. 
2.1.3 
Type C configuration 
The third type of wind turbine configuration, type C, uses a wound rotor induction gen-
erator (WRIG). The rotor circuit is connected to the grid with a partial-scale frequency 
converter while the stator is directly connected to the grid (see Figure 4). (Ackermann, 
2012)

quency converter. 
2.1.4 
Type D configuration 
The fourth type of wind turbine configuration, type D, has the generator connected to 
the grid with a full scale frequency converter which allows the use of any type of gener-
ator (either synchronous or asynchronous). The field excitation for the synchronous 
generators can be electrically (in case of the wound rotor synchronous generator 
(WRSG)) or it can be provided by magnets (for permanent magnet synchronous genera-
tor (PMSG)). In some of the cases the type D wind turbines can be built without the 
gear box (see Figure 5). (Ackermann, 2012)

2.2 
Lely Aircon wind turbine 
Aircon 10S (see Figure 6) was developed by Aircon GmbH & Co. KG which is a Ger-
man company founded in 2003. Since 2012 the company has been taken over by Lely 
Group and is now named Lely Aircon B.V. The Lely Group is a Dutch agricultural 
company founded in 1948 with more than 2000 employees active in more than 60 coun-
tries. 
 
Figure 6 - Lely Aircon 10 wind turbine.  
The Aircon 10 is a type D wind turbine based on direct drive permanent magnet syn-
chronous technology. The blades of the wind turbine are specially designed to ensure 
low noise emission while extracting the maximum power from the wind, having a rotor 
diameter of only 7.5 meters. The unique coil and permanent magnet design ensures a 
really compact generator capable of producing the rated power of 9.8 kW at the nominal 
wind speed of 11m/s. The power curve describing the electrical power output of the 
wind turbine different wind speeds can be observed in Figure 7. The detailed table with 
the measured power curve can be found in ”Summary Details for Performance, Dura-

from the wind power. The wind direction is measured with a wind vane while the wind 
speed is measured with a cup anemometer. Both wind measurement equipment are 
mounted at the back of the nacelle. The full specifications list extracted from the Lely

SYSLAB  
 
The Aircon 10S wind turbine is one of the energy sources connected to SYSLAB which 
is a flexible intelligent energy laboratory that can be used to research control strategies 
for power system with distributed control. A single-line diagram of the three-phase 
power system of SYSLAB facility can be observed in Figure 9.  
 
Figure 9 – Single-line diagram of SYSLAB (PowerLabDK, 2013) 
The SYSLAB facility consists of a 3-phase 400V grid which connects 5 sub-stations 
(marked with different color in the above diagram) a couple of energy sources, an ener-
gy storage unit and different energy loads. There are three types of energy sources 
available: 2 wind turbines (Aircon 10S - 10 kV and Gaia - 11 kW), 3 photovoltaic pow-
er plants (7 kW, 10 kW and 10 kW) and a diesel generator set (48 kW). The energy 
storage unit is a 15 kW/120 kWh vanadium redox flow battery. The energy loads is rep-
resented by two domestic houses (10 to 20 kW), different controllable energy loads (75 
kW, 3x36 kW) and the facilities in Risø Campus one of which is the NEVIC electrical 
vehicle test facility.

3.2 
Emerson Unidrive SP 
3.2.1 
Monitoring parameters 
An alternative way to monitor the wind turbine‟s activity besides using the graphical 
user interface described in the previous section was analyzed since the GUI of the wind 
turbine doesn‟t have the option to export and save any measured values. The solution 
found was to read the output values from the grid side inverter of the wind turbine (see 
Figure 14).  
 
 
Figure 14 - Three-phase schematic of the Aircon wind turbine1 
The inverter used for the Aircon 10S wind turbine is a Unidrive SP 2403 drive produced 
by Emerson. The drive can be configured in four different operating modes: open-loop, 
closed loop vector, closed-loop servo and as an AC Regenerative Unit, the latter being 
used (hereafter referred as a Regen drive). The Regen drive is a pulse width modulated 
voltage source converter (PWM VSC) that allows AC supply power to flow in both di-
rections with control over the power factor.  
The Regen drive is defined by a set of parameters (19 fixed and 40 programmable) used 
for set-up of the drive. Each parameter refers to a specific characteristic of the drive. A 
full list of the Regen drive basic parameters can be observed in Figure 15.

CTScope provides an oscilloscope display which allows the display of up to 4 channels 
simultaneously. The time division of the display can be set from 0.05 seconds/division 
up until 10 seconds/division. The scope display can be zoomed to facilitate analysis of 
small data or panned to allow examination of historical data. The channels to be dis-
played are selected by choosing the appropriate parameter of the Regen drive (e.g. 
#05.03 which corresponds to output power [kW]). The data for the channels can be 
scaled or offset in order to better display results with different magnitude. It also has the 
option to set start and stop triggers such as a specific time of the day or rising/falling 
edge of a parameter value. It has the option to save scope files with or without recorded 
data for later use.  
A great advantage of using CTScope is that the data can also be exported to text files in 
one of the two formats: comma-delimited data (*.csv) or tab-delimited data (*.txt) for 
later analysis. The software has an internal data buffer where the values are stored. 
When the buffer has reached its limit, the new data will be written over the existing data 
at the beginning of the buffer. The time length after this occurs depends on the sampling 
rate used for refreshing the graphical display 3 and the maximum buffer size4 chosen. 
Assuming the maximum buffer size is chosen the time limit varies from 5 hours for a 
sampling rate of 1 [ms] up to 231 days for 10000 [ms].

3.2.2 
Power factor correction 
The graphical user interface of the wind turbine doesn‟t give access to any control capa-
bility of the power factor but through the help of the Emerson drive this can be realized. 
This is because the Regen drive can be made to produce or consume reactive power by 
producing some current in the x axis of the reference frame. The Regen drive operates 
in a reference frame that is aligned to the voltage at the drive terminals. The reference 
frame is nearly aligned with the supply voltage since the phase shift across the inductor 
is minor. (EMERSON, 2007) 
 
Figure 17 - Power flow from supply to Regen drive (EMERSON, 2007) 
Vs – Supply voltage 
Vr – Voltage at Regen drive terminals 
Ir – Total current at Regen drive terminals  
 
The power factor can be modified by changing the reactive current reference parameter 
(#04.08) which defines the percentage of reactive current from the Regen drive rated 
current (#05.07). 
A positive reactive current will produce a current flowing from the supply to the Regen 
drive that lags the phase voltage while the negative reactive current will produce a cur-
rent that leads the phase voltage. 
Before adjusting the reactive current reference parameter (#04.08) the symmetrical cur-
rent limit (#04.07) has to be reduced below its maximum value. This is because the 
maximum current in Regen mode is limited to the maximum drive current (over current 
trip level defined as the regen drive rated current/0.45).

3.3 
Substation measured values 
 
The output values from Lely Aircon wind turbine are also measured at the sub-station 
connection point with the help of a multi instrument measurement unit type DEIF MIC-
4224 and stored at the SYSLAB database server (see Figure 18). 
 
 
Figure 18 - DEIF MIC-424 multi-instrument measurement unit (DEIF) 
The DEIF MIC-424 is a multi-instrument measuring unit capable of monitoring more 
than 50 parameters on single-phase or 3-phase energy distribution networks. The MIC 
measures true RMS values on all 3-phases. The values can be observed on the built-in 
display with 4 digit resolution for each of the measured values. The data measured can 
be transmitted to a PC through a RS485 serial output. The full data sheet can be found 
in the Appendix. 
The measured values for each second are stored at the SYSLAB server in a text docu-
ment, comma-delimited data format (*.csv) for each day for all the elements connected 
to the sub-station bus bars. The values measured from the wind turbine stored at the 
SYSLAB database server are the active power P[kW], reactive power Q [kVAr], fre-
quency f [Hz], line to line voltage U [V], and the two counters for active energy [kWh] 
and reactive energy [kVArh] (export and import).

4.2 
Controlling the nacelle position 
 
One of the few control option available from the wind turbine graphical user interface, 
besides the start and stop functions, is the control of the position of the nacelle. An ex-
periment was set to observe how the power production of the wind turbine is changing 
if the position of the nacelle of the wind turbine is rotated with an angle of 90 degrees 
from the direction of the wind and back. This was done using the wind turbine‟s manual 
function in the „Azimuth‟ sub-menu. 
The values of the active power production of the wind turbine were collected using 
CTScope software while the values for the wind speed were collected manually using 
the same process described in the previous section. The data was then processed and 
plotted with MATLAB. 
In order to better visualize the effect of controlling the position of the nacelle has on the 
power output of the wind turbine, the estimated values for the normal condition (when 
the wind turbine nacelle is tracking the wind direction) was also plotted. The values 
were estimated by means of data interpolation with MATLAB using the values present-
ed in the table of results for the measured power curve available in the “Summary De-
tails for Performance, Duration and Acoustic Measurements for the Aircon 10S Wind 
Turbine” (see Appendix). The results can be observed in Figure 29.

CONCLUSION AND RECOMMENDATIONS FOR 
FUTURE WORK 
5.1 
Conclusion 
In this report the Aircon 10S wind turbine with all accessible documentation was ana-
lyzed. A description of the internal controller graphical user interface was presented 
showing that it has rather limited access regarding controllability and no possibility to 
export data for later analysis. An alternative way to monitor the parameters of the wind 
turbine was analyzed and presented. Collecting data from the grid side inverter with the 
help of CTScope software has proved a good solution in order to measure the output 
power of the wind turbine and extract data that can be used later for analysis. However, 
the results obtained demonstrated that the values measured at the grid side inverter were 
higher than the values measured at the substation. The data collected show clearly the 
activity of the wind turbine but depending on the required data, the values measured at 
the substation might be the realistic values to consider. 
The experiment conducted when controlling the nacelle position regarding the wind 
direction revealed a possibility of limiting the output power production of the wind tur-
bine. 
5.2 
Future work 
One of the desired parts of this study was to control a fraction of the reactive power 
output of the wind turbine. Since this part couldn‟t be tested, it has to remain as a rec-
ommendation for future work. This part couldn‟t be tested during the course of this pro-
ject because communication with the technical department of the Lely Aircon Company 
has proven to be very slow. Using the steps described in this thesis, after receiving ap-
proval from the manufacturer, one could test both scenarios when the wind turbine is set 
to produce or consume reactive power.
