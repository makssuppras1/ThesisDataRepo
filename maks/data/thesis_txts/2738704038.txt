Abstract
This thesis explores the impact of different sparsity settings on the performance of
Concept Bottleneck Models (CBMs) in sentiment analysis tasks, specifically applied
to the GoEmotions dataset. The study aims to determine if varying sparsity levels
can maintain high accuracy while improving the interpretability of model predictions.
Initially, a reproduction of Sparse CBMs using the CEBaB dataset was performed
to establish a baseline. The GoEmotions dataset, containing 28 emotion concepts
with significant imbalances, was then employed to evaluate the models under various
sparsity configurations.
Through a series of experiments, including augmentation techniques and hyperpa-
rameter tuning, the research demonstrates that fine-tuned sparsity levels can enhance
model performance, even when having misclassification in concept prediction. The
evaluation of the model utilized both confusion matrices and McNemar’s test to assess
the significance of performance differences.
The findings suggest that with careful adjustment of sparsity, CBMs can achieve
a balance between interpretability and accuracy.
Future work should address the
imbalances in the dataset, improve sentiment score reliability and explore advanced
techniques for better generalization. This research contributes to the field of inter-
pretable AI by offering insights into how sparsity can be leveraged to enhance the
accuracy and transparency of sentiment analysis models.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the field of artificial intelligence (AI), the rapid pace of model development is
matched by the increasing interest in crafting models that are not only powerful,
but also interpretable and trustworthy.
The use of traditional deep learning AI
methodologies can be problematic due to their lack of transparency in explaining the
rationale behind their decisions. This inability raises concerns surrounding some im-
portant and timeless values including fairness, reliability & safety, privacy & security,
inclusiveness transparency and accountability in AI systems[Smi+18].
Many studies and surveys concluded that AI systems increasingly influence critical
areas of our daily lives, from healthcare diagnostics[Jia+17] to financial lending deci-
sions[Sar23] or autonomous vehicle control[Ma+20]. This wide range of applications
catalyzed the necessity for ”opening the black box” by enhancing transparency during
the decision-making process and hence the emergence of Explainable AI (XAI).
By peeling back the layers of complexity inherent in AI models, XAI endeavors
to provide accessible and meaningful insights into their mechanics, thus bridging the
computational predictive accuracy of machines and the cognitive understanding of hu-
mans. Such alignment is essential for building trust in AI systems, ensuring they can
be critically evaluated, validated and eventually fully relied upon. The utilization of
XAI facilitates the collaboration between machines and humans, providing a broader
oversight, a further refinement, also an extra ethical control of AI technologies.
Compared to the traditional non-concept-based XAI, concept-based methods ad-
dress one of the main obstacles in the field, namely the extent, to which a method can
explain the predictions it makes. As its name suggests, concept-based XAI (C-XAI)
operates by mapping complex model decisions, to human-understandable concepts,
thereby providing a more intuitive explanation of how models arrive at their con-
clusions. This approach is particularly effective, because it translates the abstract
and domain-specific knowledge, and the often perplexing reasoning of AI models into
terms and ideas that are both familiar and meaningful to its users.
1.1
Background
The idea of XAI is not new and could be traced back to the early days of AI itself, when
the systems were inherently more transparent, due to their simpler, rule-based nature.
These early systems, often referred to as knowledge-based system (KBS)[Hay84], were
designed to mimic the decision-making process of human experts, where explainability

was a key feature, compared to the other contemporary instances of AI. These systems,
popular in the 1970s and 1980s, could reason through bodies of knowledge, which were
represented mainly in if-then rules, making them the earliest examples of XAI.
AI nowadays is more data-driven than simply following given rules. With the rise
of machine learning (ML) and statistical models in the 90s, AI became more flexible
without naming every possibility that it might encounter. Early ML models like deci-
sion trees[Li+84] and linear regression[Net+96] were relatively interpretable, but as
models grew more complex, like support vector machines (SVMs)[Hea+98] and ensem-
ble methods, e.g., random forests[Ho95], gradient boosting[Dru+94], the interpretabil-
ity was reduced whereas their predictive power increased. As the need for XAI be-
came more apparent, XAI techniques such as partial dependence plots(PDPs)[Fri01]
and model-specific explanations, e.g., SHapley Additive exPlanations (SHAP)[LL17],
Local Interpretable Model-agnostic Explanations (LIME)[RSG16], were introduced
to explain these complex models. The more significant challenges in interpretabil-
ity were brought by deep learning (DL)[RHW86] and neural networks[MP43], where
layers of nodes neurons are interconnected and each layer processes input data to
output and then passes this to the next layer. The number of layers could result in
a high-dimensional parameter space, meanwhile, the layers could capture complex,
non-linear interactions between features, thus not only is the space hard to visual-
ize or understand, but also hardly has a straightforward relationship between input
and output. At this point, the trade-off between model accuracy and interpretability
was more valued. Techniques like layer-wise relevance propagation(LRP)[Bac+15],
saliency maps[SVZ14] and activation maximization[Yos+15] were developed to inter-
pret DL models.
Model-agnostic explainability methods like LIME, SHAP and PDPs could be ap-
plied across various types of models, both traditional machine learning and deep
learning. They do not require access to the internal working mechanism of the model,
instead, they rely solely on the input-output behavior to make interpretations or anal-
yses. Conversely, model-specific methods like LRP and saliency maps, are tailored
to the internal process of particular types of models, such as convolutional neural
networks (CNNs)[LeC+89], providing precise and detailed explanations.
The aforementioned methods are mostly non-concept-based XAI methods, namely,
they do not provide predictions that consist of human-understandable concepts. Typ-
ically, they do not rely on predefined concepts or intermediate representations from
input, but work directly with the raw input features. This brought the question of
whether AI could learn the concepts and its decisions could be mapped to a set
of human-understandable concepts.
Methods like the Concept Bottleneck Model
(CBM)[Koh+20] were developed, providing intermediate concepts that help explain
model predictions. This sort of method addresses the need for more intuitive and
meaningful explanations, bridging the gap between technical model outputs and user
understanding.

1.1.1
Concept-Based Methods
Given the black-box nature of NNs along with the advancement of more sophisticated
models, the interest of enhancing the interpretability has been revitalized. Many XAI
methods have emerged to explain predictions by explicating the internal mechanisms
of these models. Notable among these are Deep Belief Networks (DBNs)[HOT06;
Hin09]and LRP[Bac+15]. DBNs were one of the earliest models that could be adapted
to support interpretability by learning binary, structured and layer-wise representa-
tions from data. These features can be aligned with human-understandable concepts,
contributing cumulatively to the final prediction. By organizing features hierarchi-
cally, DBNs make it possible to understand how low-level patterns evolve into high-
level concepts that drive the model’s decisions. In 2015, LRP brought the concept of
layer-wise interpretability to new heights. It propagates relevance scores backward
through the layers of the model, emphasizing the contribution of each input feature
to the final prediction. This method provides a detailed map of how each part of
the input data influences the output, thereby making the decision-making process
transparent and traceable.
External XAI methods like LIME[RSG16] also play a
critical role in interpretability. It approximates complex models around individual
predictions with simpler, interpretable models, focusing on local explanations. By dis-
composing the input and observing changes in the output, LIME builds a surrogate
model that mimics the behavior of the original model in the locality of the predic-
tion. This approach provides insights into why a model made a specific decision for a
particular instance, offering a complementary perspective to methods like DBNs and
LRP that focus on global model behavior.
1.1.2
Concept Bottleneck Model
As the most significant model in concept-based machine learning, the Concept Bottle-
neck Model (CBM)[Koh+20] is a relatively recent innovation in the field, particularly
within deep learning, where the model’s predictions are mediated through a set of
interpretable concepts. While the concept of using intermediate representations isn’t
exclusive to deep learning, the development of CBMs has been heavily influenced by
the architecture and capabilities of DNNs[RHW86]. Being good at handling complex
data types and tasks, these NNs are ideal for incorporating concept bottlenecks to
improve interpretability, while they provide insights into how the model arrives at its
predictions by making the role of each concept explicit.
The CBM’s architecture is divided into two main components: a concept predictor
and a label predictor. The concept predictor maps raw input data to a predefined set
of intermediate concepts, while the label predictor maps these concepts to the final
prediction. Compared to traditional feature engineering, where meaningful features
are manually selected and transformed from raw data, the concept predictor in CBM
could automatically discover and learn the concepts through training. This discovery
of concepts often leads to better model performance as the predictor can leverage
complex, non-linear transformations of the data, whereas the suboptimal performance

caused by poorly chosen engineered features would require a significant effort to
improve iteratively.
Most of the recent studies[SN22; Oik+23; Yan+23; YWZ23; Hu+24; Sem+24]
on CBMs focus on image analysis due to the complexity and high dimensionality of
image data, where each image can consist of thousands or millions of pixels, each
contributing unique information. The application of CBMs in image analysis is par-
ticularly beneficial, because these models can break down these complex visual data
into understandable concepts, such as edges, textures, and objects, which cumula-
tively lead to the final prediction.
CBMs excel in image analysis by providing a
clear hierarchy of concepts that mirror the way humans interpret visual information.
For instance, in medical imaging[Cha+23], CBMs can identify specific anatomical
structures and abnormalities. In autonomous driving[YYZ21; Ech+23], they can rec-
ognize and categorize various objects like pedestrians, traﬀic signs and other vehicles.
This hierarchical decomposition of visual information into meaningful concepts en-
sures that each prediction step is transparent and understandable, gaining CBMs
significant traction in the field.
1.1.3
Application in Sentiment Analysis
Despite their excellent performance in image analysis, CBMs are not as prevalent
in sentiment analysis.
Due to the nature of the textual data, which differs sig-
nificantly from visual data, sentiment analysis involves understanding and inter-
preting linguistic nuances, contextual information and complex syntactic structures,
which are less straightforward to decompose into discrete, interpretable terms com-
pared to visual data.
Additionally, the intermediate concepts in sentiment analy-
sis are often abstract and context-dependent, making it challenging to define and
standardize the concepts in the same way as visual features.
Consequently, tra-
ditional text processing and sentiment analysis methods such as CNNs, Recurrent
Neural Networks (RNNs)[RHW86][MRG+87], Long Short-Term Memory networks
(LSTMs)[HS97], and transformers[Vas+17], or its variations, e.g., Bidirectional En-
coder Representations from Transformers (BERT)[Dev+18], which do not rely heavily
on explicit concept bottlenecks, remain more common in this domain. Yet, a recent
study[Tan+23a; Tan+23b] has started to apply CBM principles to pre-trained lan-
guage models (PLMs) in text classification tasks. Their method involves adding a
linear layer to a PLM to project its latent representation into a concept space, al-
lowing for the alignment of text features with human-understandable concepts such
as ”food,” ”ambiance”, ”service” and ”noise”. By introducing a bottleneck layer that
explicitly represents these concepts, this approach shows promise in filling the gap
between the complexity of textual data and the need for interpretability in more
sophisticated models on sentiment analysis.

1.1.4
Sparsity in Machine Learning
Sparsity, which is a key feature in this [Tan+23a][Tan+23b] study, has always been a
foundational component in both classical machine learning and deep learning. By set-
ting many zero or near-zero values in datasets, feature vectors, or model parameters, it
focuses on the most critical features or connections in the data[LDS89][HT06][Lee+06][Tib18].
In deep learning, sparsity can be introduced in the weights of neural networks to
improve computational eﬀiciency and reduce memory usage[Han+15][Liu+17]. Tech-
niques like dropout, weight pruning, and sparse regularization are commonly used.
Sparse models are often easier to interpret, because they involve fewer active features
or connections, allowing humans to understand which features or neurons contribute
most to predictions. In natural language processing (NLP), sparsity can not only
help improve the performance of models on diverse text corpora by focusing on the
most significant terms and structures[EAX11][YS14], but also improve interpretabil-
ity[UGK19].
1.2
Motivation
The growing complexity of NLP tasks has driven the need for more interpretable
models, where CBMs have demonstrated significant promise. However, their applica-
tion in sentiment analysis, particularly with emotion datasets, remains underexplored,
which this thesis attempts to do. To this end, incorporating sparsity into CBMs for
sentiment analysis could address three key challenges and hopefully unlock new po-
tentials:
• Providing Holistic Explanations: Current DL models often lack transparency
in their decision-making processes, making it diﬀicult for users to understand
how predictions are made. This lack of transparency can undermine trust and
limit the applicability of these models in critical areas, such as medical image.
• Enhancing Concept Representation Clarity: When using emotions as concepts
in sentiment analysis, the decision-making process can be complicated by the
number of emotions and their entangled relationships.
This complexity can
obscure how specific emotions contribute to overall sentiment predictions.
• Improving Nuanced Emotion Detection: Fine-grained emotion datasets encom-
pass subtle and varied emotional expressions that require precise interpretation.
Traditional models often struggle to capture these nuances due to noise and
irrelevant features, leading to inaccurate predictions and a lack of reliability in
emotion detection.
This paper will explore and address the three challenges by deploying sparse CBM
for sentiment analysis on a fine-grained emotion dataset. The study aims to enhance
model transparency, concept clarity and the nuanced detection of emotions, contribut-
ing to the development of more interpretable and effective sentiment analysis models.

1.3
Research Question
The research aims to address the impact of sparsity settings on the performance of
CBMs in sentiment analysis tasks, with a focus on their interpretability, accuracy,
and stability. The research question can be articulated as follows:
How do different sparsity settings impact the performance of CBMs in
sentiment analysis tasks, and can these models offer good interpretations
of their predictions while achieving high accuracy and stability on the
GoEmotions dataset?
1.4
Objective & Scope
The primary objective and scope of this research is to evaluate how different sparsity
settings impact the performance, interpretability and robustness of CBMs, in senti-
ment analysis tasks using the GoEmotions dataset. This involves investigating the
influence of varying sparsity levels on model accuracy and stability and determining
the optimal settings. The research also aims to enhance model interpretability by
analyzing how sparsity affects the identification of relevant concepts. Additionally,
the study will compare the robustness of CBMs to understand their resilience against
hyperparameter variations and explore the sensitivity of CBMs to other hyperparam-
eters such as learning rate, batch size, lambda values, etc. To validate the significance
of performance differences between models with diverse sparsity, McNemar’s test will
be employed. The research will provide comprehensive insights into the development
of more interpretable and robust C-XAI for sentiment analysis.

Structure of the thesis

2.1
Reproduction of Sparse CBM with CEBaB dataset
To validate the approach outlined by Tan et al.[Tan+23b] in their study on sparsity-
guided holistic explanations for LLMs, we have conducted a reproduction using the
Causal Estimation-Based Benchmark (CEBaB) dataset. This CEBaB dataset[Abr+22]
consists of short restaurant reviews in which aspects of the dining experience—food,
noise, ambiance and service, are marked as positive, negative or unknown. These as-
pects are used as concepts, when evaluating the effectiveness of a CBM in interpreting
and predicting sentiment through sparse representations.
Applying the default settings from their methodology, which is provided in A.1,
we replicated their experimental conditions for both the dataset and the model. The
conditions included predefined parameters and configurations that were both critical
for the original model’s performance. The Sparse CBM demonstrated robust perfor-
mance in predicting sentiment scores, achieving an accuracy of 84.251 for concepts
and 63.41 for labels. The labels are sentiment scores ranging from 1 to 5: 1 being very
negative and 5 being very positive. These results are consistent with those reported
in their paper, confirming the effectiveness of their approach.
Additional analysis was conducted, two confusion matrices for the two kinds of
predictions are shown in fig.2.1. The matrix for labels in fig.2.1(b) shows strong per-
formance in classifying higher sentiment scores, e.g., sentiment score 5, but indicates
challenges in distinguishing closely related sentiments, leading the misclassifications
being more frequent in the adjacent scores. Similarly, the concept confusion matrix
in fig.2.1(a) reveals good identification of classes, with high accuracy in predicting a
concept as ”unknown” (”unknown” is the largest class in the dataset), but highlights
diﬀiculties in differentiating ”positive” from the two others classes. These matrices
underscore both the model’s strengths and areas for improvement, particularly in
nuanced classifications.

2.2.2.3
Implementing the GoEmotions dataset
To further ensure the reliability of the predictions from the GoEmotions dataset, the
sentiment predictions were filtered based on their confidence scores. Specifically, only
those predictions with a probability exceeding 0.5 were saved, effectively implementing
a reject option for low-confidence predictions. This threshold was chosen to better
balance accuracy and reliability, ensuring that only predictions with a reasonable
level of certainty are considered, thereby reducing the risk of incorrect classifications,
which would lead to the misunderstanding of any emotion concept.
Using direct confidence scores rather than the margin of victory, which is the
difference between the highest and second-highest predicted probabilities, offers sev-
eral advantages. Confidence scores provide a clear and straightforward measure of
how likely the predicted class is to be correct, making it easier to set a meaningful
threshold.
In contrast, the margin of victory can sometimes be misleading, espe-
cially in cases where the model is unsure, leading to a small difference between top
probabilities, even if both are low.
This approach also helps in cutting down the dataset size, thereby saving computa-
tional power. By discarding low-confidence predictions, we reduce the volume of data
that needs to be processed and stored. Again, the Sparse CBM trained with the CE-
BaB dataset, was handling only 3 concepts, yet with GoEmotion dataset, this number
is increased to 28. Hence, to use resources more eﬀiciently, its particularly important
that this makes the entire process more robust and scalable.
Finally, considering
the size of the CEBaB dataset (1755 entries originally), the GoEmotion dataset was
further cut down to 1000 when training the Sparse CBM.

Augmentation experiment
To investigate the impact of various textual modifications on sentiment classifica-
tion, an augmentation experiment with 1000 rows, consisting of 200 rows from each
sentiment group was conducted.
The experiment involved three distinct augmen-
tation strategies: inserting the word ”not” randomly, inserting ”not” syntactically,
and replacing adjectives with their antonyms. Each modification strategy aims at
understand how the modifications affect sentiment analysis results and whether the
model could correctly predict the sentiment after the sentences are modified with the
different kinds of negation. These operations are detailed as follows:
• Random Insertion of ”Not”: The word ”not” was randomly inserted into the
text. The process involved calculating the length of each sample and generating
a random position within its length. The insertion did not consider the gram-
matical structure, focusing purely on disrupting the original sentiment flow.
• Syntactic Insertion of ”Not”: Utilizing the Natural Language Toolkit (nltk)
package[BKL09], the syntactic structure of each sentence was analyzed to de-
termine the most grammatically suitable place for the insertion. This method
would iterate over the sentence and insert ”not” immediately after the identified
word, which is with a verb-related POS tag, which includes verbs in different

tenses (VBD, VBN, VBG, VBP) or 3rd person singular present (VBZ), or modal
verb (MD), then it should negate the meaning of the phrase.
• Replacing Adjectives with Antonyms: This sophisticated approach replaced ad-
jectives with their antonyms to flip the sentiment of the sentence. Using the nltk
package, the adjectives within each sentence would be identified and substituted
with their antonyms. This approach relied on lexical databases WordNet[Mil95]
to find appropriate antonyms for each identified adjective, ensuring a meaning-

Comparing to other data augmentation methods such as the Easy Data Aug-

2.5.1
McNemar’s test
In our case, the predictions of two models with different sparsity levels are compared
on the same dataset and then categorized the predictions into four groups: both
models correct, both models incorrect, only the first model correct, and only the
second model correct. This helps us to focus on the instances, where the models
disagree, which is the key to understanding their relative performance.
The test

statistic for the McNemar’s test is computed using the differences in the counts of
these categories. Specifically, the statistic is calculated by
χ2 = (n10 −n01)2

where n10 represents the number of samples correctly classified by the first model,

that the two models perform differently.
Introducing McNemar’s test allows us to rigorously evaluate and compare model
performances beyond simple accuracy metrics, providing deeper insights into how

This approach is essential for sentiment analysis tasks, where understanding subtle
differences in model behavior can significantly affect the reliability and interpretability
of the results.

3.2.2
Interpretability
One of the main challenges with using sparsity in model evaluation is the lack of
a measurable underlying ground truth for natural language representations. Tradi-
tionally, evaluation has been subjective, relying on human intuition to explain the
interpretability of features based on their input set. This approach poses a significant
limitation in the field of interpretability, as it lacks objective and reproducible metrics.
Yet, with C-XAI, we sought to address this issue by extracting the activated concepts
for each input text and examining the predicted concepts through confusion matrices
on concept prediction.
The process involved analyzing the confusion matrices to identify patterns and
discrepancies in concept predictions. By comparing models with different sparsity
levels, we aimed to discern how sparsity impacts the accuracy and interpretability of
the model. The confusion matrices in fig.3.10(b), 3.11(b), 3.12(b) provided insights
into the model’s performance, highlighting the prevalence of ‘unknown’ predictions
and the misclassification of ‘positive’ concepts as ‘unknown’. The high number of
true positives for ‘unknown’ indicates that the model is biased, towards predicting
the majority class, which in this case is ‘unknown’.
For label predictions, increasing sparsity from 0.3-0.5 in fig.3.10(a) to 0.5-0.7 in
fig.3.11(a) shows improved performance. Higher sparsity levels seem to aid in main-
taining crucial weights necessary for accurate sentiment classification. This improve-
ment is particularly evident in reduced misclassifications and more balanced correct
predictions across labels. Increasing the sparsity range to 0.7-0.9 results in a more
distributed error pattern in fig.3.12(a), indicating that higher sparsity may lead to
loss of some important but latent features, affecting the model’s ability to accurately
classify certain sentiment scores.
Additionally, the results from McNemar’s test provide further insights into the

3.3
Conclusion
This thesis set out to answer the research question: ”How do different sparsity set-
tings impact the performance of CBMs in sentiment analysis tasks, and can these
models offer good interpretations of their predictions while achieving high accuracy
and stability on the GoEmotions dataset?” Through a series of experiments and anal-
yses, we have explored various sparsity levels and their effects on model performance
and interpretability.
Our findings indicate that adjusting sparsity settings significantly impacts the
accuracy of CBMs. By pruning less important weights, we can enhance the model’s
focus on key concepts. Specifically, models with sparsity levels in the range of [0.5, 0.7]
and [0.3, 0.5] demonstrated no significant difference in performance, as evidenced by
McNemar’s test. However, a noticeable performance difference was observed between
the sparsity levels [0.5, 0.7] and [0.7, 0.9], suggesting that excessively high sparsity
may detract from model performance.
Furthermore, our confusion matrix analysis revealed how different sparsity lev-
els affect the model’s ability to accurately predict sentiment labels.
Models with
moderate sparsity ([0.5, 0.7]) achieved the best performance, whereas higher sparsity
levels ([0.7, 0.9]) led to a decline in predictive performance, particularly in the con-
cept predictions. This indicates that while sparsity can enhance interpretability by
simplifying the model, there is a trade-off in terms of accuracy, when sparsity is too

high.
The augmentation experiments further highlighted the importance of diverse data
augmentation strategies in validating the model’s robustness. By employing a range of
augmentation techniques, we were able to prove the generalizability of the sentiment
analysis models, thereby ensuring their performance on the GoEmotions dataset.
In summary, our research demonstrates that CBMs can perform well on the GoE-
motion dataset, with the appropriate sparsity settings. Moderate sparsity levels offer
a sweet spot, where the model remains interpretable without sacrificing performance.
These findings underscore the potential of CBMs in sentiment analysis tasks, par-

Future work can build on these insights by exploring more nuanced sparsity levels, di-
verse augmentation strategies and hybrid interpretability methods to further enhance
the robustness and applicability of CBMs in real-world sentiment analysis.

If we take a step back and examine the syntactic approach used in both examples,
it is notable that the effectiveness of this strategy varies. In the second example, the
method correctly identifies that ”not” should follow the contraction ”’s”, resulting
in ”that’s not nice.” However, in the first example, the placement of ”not” is less
accurate, as it is inserted between the pronoun ”she” and the contraction ”’s”, leading
to an awkward and less coherent sentence structure. This shows the challenges and
inconsistencies in applying syntactic methods for text augmentation, particularly in
determining the appropriate placement of negations.
The inconsistencies in both
examples highlight the nuanced nature of text augmentation and raise some concerns
about the reliability of these methods in accurately reflecting the intended sentiment
shift.
3.4.2
Limitation of the GoEmotion dataset
Imbalance in Emotion Distribution. One significant limitation is the imbalance
in the distribution of emotions across the dataset. As shown in table 2.3, certain emo-
tions like ”admiration” and ”approval” are overrepresented in the dataset, comprising
10.6% and 11.4% of their respective categories. In contrast, emotions such as ”de-
sire” and ”relief” are significantly underrepresented, making up only 1.0% and 0.3%
respectively. There is also a second significant imbalance in emotion labels, with over
90% of emotion concepts labeled as ”unknown”. These two could present a challenge
for models trained on this dataset, as they may learn to predict the majority class
more frequently, reducing the model’s effectiveness in accurately identifying the less
represented emotions.
==PAGE:53==
3.5 Future work and Improvement
45
The second imbalance contrasts sharply with the CEBaB dataset, which shows
a more distributed set of labels across different concepts such as Food, Ambiance,
Service, and Noise. For instance, in the CEBaB dataset, ”Food” has 33% negative,
41% positive, and 26% unknown labels. In comparison, this GoEmotions dataset’s
”admiration” has 89.4% unknown, highlighting the stark difference in label distribu-
tion, which no doubt poses significant challenges for the model to learn the emotions
as concepts.
Sentiment Score Reliability. The sentiment scores, which range from 1 to 5, are
generated by a model trained on Twitter data. This can introduce biases specific
to Twitter’s linguistic style and user behavior that may not generalize well to other
contexts or text sources. Moreover, the sentiment scores are not human-annotated,
but model-generated. Even with the best effort to ensure the accuracy and reliabil-
ity of the model, the predictions could still lead to inaccuracies and inconsistencies,
particularly for ambiguous or context-dependent sentiments.
3.5
Future work and Improvement
Given the result that the Sparse CBM failed to predict the activated concepts in
sentiment prediction, some limitations are identified in this study. Several avenues
for future work and improvement can be considered to enhance the performance and
interpretability of CBMs in sentiment analysis tasks using the GoEmotions dataset.
One significant limitation of the GoEmotions dataset is the pronounced imbalance
in the distribution of emotions as addressed in the previous section. To mitigate the
issue, future work could focus on techniques such as data augmentation, oversampling
of minority classes and synthetic data generation to create a more balanced dataset.
Moreover, incorporating advanced techniques like transfer learning from more bal-
anced datasets or domain adaptation methods, such as adversarial training, can help
improve the concept layer’s ability to generalize across underrepresented emotions.
These techniques can adapt the predictor to perform well on the target domain by
learning domain-invariant features, which would be particularly appropriate in han-
dling the imbalance in the GoEmotions dataset.
Another aspect to consider is the handling of the ”unknown” class. Currently, our
approach converts binary labels (1 and 0) to ”positive” and ”unknown”. However,
it might be worth exploring whether we can better categorize the ”unknown” class.
Introducing a ”negative” emotion label class, as CEBaB has, could provide more
granularity. This, however, requires careful consideration to untangle the complex
relations among the emotions, to avoid mislabeling and to ensure that the additional
class genuinely represents negative emotions rather than noise or neutral expressions.
Accurate and consistent labeling is crucial to prevent further imbalance and potential
biases in the predictions.
