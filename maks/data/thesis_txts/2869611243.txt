Abstract
In the current digital ecosystem, science has become data-driven: there is an increasing
capability to capture, compute, and analyse great amounts of complex and very diverse
data.
In this scenario, it becomes hard for humans (and machines) to discover, understand and
evaluate all these scientific data, algorithms, and workflows.
In light of these challenges, back in 2016 the FAIR principles were published. These
are four foundational principles intended to guide organizations in maximizing the value
gained by research, by making data: Findable, Accessible, Interoperable and, ultimately,
Reusable.
This project takes the Novo Nordisk Foundation Center for Biosustainability (DTU Bio-
sustain) as the case study. DTU Biosustain is a research centre dedicated to large-scale
production of natural products, sustainable foods, and sustainable biochemicals, to leave
greener footprints on Earth.
In this context, digital maturity refers to the ability to leverage data and digital tools to
achieve trustworthy results and reproducible science. And data democratization, to mak-
ing data available across teams and foster collaboration.
Therefore, digital maturity and data democratization are understood as the degree of
FAIRness achieved through data management practices (with particular focus on data
collection and storage).
The main goals of this work are: to analyse data management practices at DTU Biosus-
tain, to provide a framework for quantitative assessment of FAIR performance (and to
assess it), and to suggest potential actions for greater alignment with the principles.

Introduction
In the current digital ecosystem, science has become data-driven: there is an increasing
capability to capture, compute, and analyse great amounts of complex and very diverse
data [1] [2].
In this scenario, it becomes hard for humans (and machines) to discover, understand and
evaluate all these scientific data, algorithms, and workflows.
In light of these challenges, various stakeholders (researchers, universities, publishers,
funders) collaborated and drafted the FAIR principles in 2014, and published them in 2016
[3] [4] [5] [6].
These are four foundational principles (Findability, Accessibility, Interoperability, Reusability,
see Figure 1.1) intended to guide organizations in maximizing the value gained by re-
search.
For instance, by ensuring that datasets are findable and accessible, organizations em-
power researchers to locate and retrieve them effortlessly. Interoperability promotes the
integration of diverse datasets across platforms and teams, and reusability maximizes the
value and impact of these data (research output) as it facilitates that they are applied in
various contexts.
Figure 1.1: FAIR guiding principles for data resources, by Sangya Pundir, CC BY-SA 4.0,
via Wikimedia Commons
It is worth highlighting that these principles are applicable to data in the broadest sense,
that is, to any kind of digital object, from the dataset to the publication.
When pursuing FAIR data, the digital maturity of an organization plays a pivotal role. It is
understood as the ability to leverage data and digital tools to change the way an organi-
zation delivers value [7].
In a research context, value is highly dependent on the data it produces and their reusabil-
ity. Trustworthy results are fundamental to motivate future research, innovate new prod-
ucts, or inform policies. In order to achieve such confidence, reproducibility (re-doing
experiments and re-analysing the data) is key.
Therefore, digital maturity relates to the extent to which an organization integrates digital
tools, automated workflows, and standardized practices to manage and utilize its data
effectively.
A digitally mature organization is better equipped to ensure that datasets are structured,
well-documented, seamlessly shared, interoperable and reused.

Another pillar of FAIRness is data democratization. It refers to making data available
across teams, breaking down silos and technical barriers, to foster collaboration.
Hence, this project construes digital maturity and data democratization as FAIR data align-
ment through data management practices (especially in data collection and storage).
Its focus is the Novo Nordisk Foundation Center for Biosustainability at DTU (here after
DTU Biosustain), whose research activities are geared toward large-scale production of
natural products, sustainable foods, and sustainable biochemicals, to leave greener foot-
prints on Earth.
This thesis aims to investigate how much DTU Biosustain aligns with the FAIR princi-
ples, to provide a framework for such an assessment, and to identify potential areas of
improvement and steps to do so.
The remainder of this document is structured as follows:
• Background
Compilation and explanation of the relevant backbone theory and literature on which
this thesis relies.
• Methodology

Background
The FAIR principles are conceived as a range of behaviours by scientific digital objects
with the goal of gradually increasing their discoverability and reusability, not only by re-
searchers but also by machines. In this regard, data should be:
• Findable, meaning that they can be found or discovered by humans and machines.
Identifiers and metadata are essential for that.
• Accessible, meaning that it is possible for the end-user (human or machine) to
technically retrieve the data, or, at least, the information on how to do so.
• Interoperable, meaning that data can be understood and integrated with other data,
workflows and applications.
• Reusable, meaning that, if allowed, data can be combined and replicated in different
settings, provided that their context is properly described.
An example of FAIRness is Rigsarkivet, the Danish National Archives. They contain all
research data (survey data, cohorts, registries, etc.) handed in to the Danish Data Archive
and Rigsarkivet since 1973 (findable, accessible).
All data and metadata are processed and enriched with metadata according to domain-
specific and international standards and contain controlled vocabularies, classifications
and keywords (interoperable, reusable).
These four foundational principles have been expanded into more detailed and measur-
able guidelines (sub-principles) [8]:
F1 (Meta)data are assigned a globally unique and persistent identifier
Such an identifier is understood as a sequence of characters that point to the entity
so that:
– When used, it always refers to this entity and it cannot be reassigned to any
other (global).
– It is long-lasting, always resolvable1 to some degree (persistent), even if data
are archived.
F2 Data are described with rich metadata
By describing the entities with extensive and comprehensive metadata (e.g., mea-
surement device, units, protocol used, author, topic), they can be more easily lo-
cated and prioritised.
F3 Metadata clearly and explicitly include the identifier of the data they describe
In cases where data and metadata are separate files, the two should be connected.
F4 (Meta)data are registered or indexed in a searchable resource
Such as Google or dedicated repositories, for instance GitHub or DTU Data.
A1 (Meta)data are retrievable by their identifier using a standardised communications
protocol
Note that this sub-principle focuses on how to retrieve the data technically, not on
who has the permissions.

A1.1 The protocol is open, free, and universally implementable
That is, anyone with a computer and an internet connection can access the
entity (or at least its metadata), since the communications protocol is available
at no cost (not proprietary). Examples of such protocols are HyperText Transfer
Protocol (HTTP) or File Transfer Protocol (FTP). The RESTful API (REST-API),
although not a protocol in itself, is a standard communications interface reliant
on HTTP that enables accessibility at machine-level.
This feature is not to be interpreted as data being free and open to everyone,
but rather that designated people can fetch the data because the procedure
employed is indeed open and free. It still abides by “as open as possible, as
closed as necessary”.
A1.2 The protocol allows for an authentication and authorisation procedure, where
necessary
The entity should contain information on the requirements to see the data. For
example, that the user creates an account and logs in.
A2 Metadata are accessible, even when the data are no longer available
Although original data may be missing or archived, it can be useful to retrieve their
metadata, namely the people, institutions or publications associated with them, es-
pecially when replicating studies.
I1 (Meta)data use a formal, accessible, shared, and broadly applicable language for
knowledge representation
Knowledge representation (KR) consists of encoding information of the world so that
a system (machine) can interpret it. In that sense, languages (syntax and grammar)
provide structure, whereas ontologies (controlled vocabularies, relationships, and
rules within a specific domain) provide semantics (meaning). Examples include:
RDF (KR), JSON-LD (KR), Dublin Core (ontology to describe generic metadata), or
Gene Ontology (ontology to describe the functions of gene products).
These specifications should be shared and accessible, so they can be learnt. As
opposed to ad-hoc, using standardised languages and vocabularies enforce com-
mon understanding, consistency and machine-operability.
To illustrate this, the statement “Elena is the author of this thesis”, in RDF it would
be represented as:
@prefix dc: <http://purl.org/dc/elements/1.1/> .
<http://example.org/thesis1> dc:creator "Elena" .
whereas in JSON-LD:
{
"@context": { "dc": "http://purl.org/dc/elements/1.1/"},
"@type": "Text",
"dc:creator": "Elena",
"@id": "http://example.org/thesis1"
}
I2 (Meta)data use vocabularies that follow FAIR principles
The vocabularies used to describe (meta)data should be FAIR themselves, namely
the vocabulary should be documented, use unique and persistent identifiers (F1),
and resolvable via standardised communication protocol (A1).

I3 (Meta)data include qualified references to other (meta)data
I.e., cross-references that explain meaningful links to other (meta)data. For exam-
ple, “Dataset X is cited by paper Y”, or “Researcher A is member of centre B.”
R1 (Meta)data are richly described with a plurality of accurate and relevant attributes
Sub-principle F2 focuses on metadata enabling the entity to be discovered, whereas
R1 is centred on having metadata rich enough to decide whether the entity is rele-
vant in a certain context. Reusable data encourage collaboration and avoid dupli-
cation of effort.
R1.1 (Meta)data are released with a clear and accessible data usage license
to clarify under which conditions the entity may be used.
R1.2 (Meta)data are associated with detailed provenance
To elaborate on the entity’s context, such as who is the author or how it has
been processed. The information should conform to community-specific stan-
dards and use cross-domain languages.
R1.3 (Meta)data meet domain-relevant community standards
It may be that certain communities have specific best practices regarding for-
mats, archiving or minimal information standards. When available, they should
be applied.
Among these, “F1: (Meta)data are assigned globally unique and persistent identifiers”
may have the biggest impact, as it lays the foundation to fully implement or promote other
sub-principles (e.g., F4, A1, I3, R1.2).
2.1
Existing tools
Originating from the life sciences but applied in other research disciplines, the FAIR prin-
ciples have become gradually and widely adopted as good management practices since
they were first published [4] [9] [10].
And so, an increasing number of tools are being developed. There exist several commu-
nity efforts to cater organisations’ need to assess themselves and boost their FAIRness
practices.
FAIRsharing is a community-driven knowledge-base aimed at fostering the use of stan-
dards, databases, policies and FAIR-enabling activities in general[11].
Regarding evaluation tools, FAIRsharing contains the resource FAIR Evaluation Services.
Developed by the promoters of FAIR principles through the FAIR Metrics Authoring Group,
it is both a public repository and a registry of maturity indicators (MIs) that measure the
FAIRness of entities. The group has developed its own MIs, and both their description
and implementation are fully open and available [12] [13]. Additionally, the site allows any
user to:
• Locate a template to submit new MIs.
• Import new MIs.
• Register a new collection of MIs, i.e., group several indicators, namely community-
oriented.
• Evaluate resources, i.e., given an entity’s identifier, run any registered collection of
MIs.

Methodology
The main goal of this project is to investigate the current data management practices by
researchers, and assess them through the lens of FAIR data principles.
When interpreting these principles, it has become clear that it is worth distinguishing be-
tween what is DTU Biosustain-bound (organisational scope) and what is published (global
scope). This directly affects the meaning and evaluation of the indicators. On that note:
• Internal datasets (organisational scope)
Refer to all datasets researchers work with (from raw data to processed), intended
only for DTU Biosustain usage.
• Published datasets (global scope)
By contrast, when researchers publish an article or paper, they may attach and make
available (some of) the data linked to it.
To illustrate this, let us consider sub-principle “F1: (Meta) data are assigned globally
unique and persistent identifiers”.
In the case of a dataset consisting of some instrument’s raw data, it is enough that the
identifier assigned is unique within DTU Biosustain’s environment. However, if an output
dataset is published, the identifier must be unique in the whole internet, to avoid any pos-
sible ambiguity.
Hence, from the point of view of findability and accessibility, as long as they are discover-
able and available for researchers at DTU Biosustain, it is considered that they fully align
with the principles.
At the same time, it is important to remember that FAIR principles are applicable to all
sorts of research digital objects, from datasets and metadata to scripts or publications [8]
[23]. For simplicity, the word entities is used hereafter to refer to all of these.

present project is limited to the following:
• Researchers
The people who conduct the research within the organization and produce data,
scripts, papers, etc.

The data (in the classic sense of the word) that stay within DTU Biosustain. As
explained above, these data are not necessarily made public, so they have their
own evaluation. The current set-up at DTU Biosustain allows researchers to store
their data locally (personal computers, DTU Drives) or in the cloud (Benchling1,
Azure Data Lake).

• It is addressed to all researchers at DTU Biosustain, and not limited only to data

terminology they are familiar with.

Discussion
On the basis of the results shown in chapter 4, below I highlight the main pain-points DTU
Biosustain faces in terms of FAIRness in their research data, as well as propose some
steps to overcome them.
As previously noted, FAIRness is not a specific set of actions but rather a continuum of
practices that ultimately yield more reusable data and reproducible research. So, the
actions are hereby recommended in light of the current circumstances, but are bound to
vary over time, as both technology and researcher’s culture evolve.
“It’s worth noting that FAIR isn’t all or nothing - a data or code output can be FAIR to a
greater or lesser degree, and any movement in the direction of FAIR is a positive one”
[37].
The suggestions are based in general FAIR data literature [38] [39] and real use cases,
in fields ranging from architecture to linguistics [9] [40].
• Researchers
Overall, these entities are aligned with the FAIR principles and have a satisfactory
score.
As previously stated, only 1 of the participants employs ResearcherID instead of
ORCID, which negatively impacts their findability. It would be advisable that all re-
searchers got, at least, an identifier at ORCID.
Regardless, interoperability could improve by making sure that all properties of
metadata are grounded in ontologies. However, DTU Biosustain can not actively
resolve this, as it is controlled by the service suppliers, i.e., ORCID or ResearcherID.
• Internal datasets
This type of entity performs poorly in all aspects measured, particularly with regards
to findability and interoperability. It is critical to address these issues (!).
The low score in findability is primarily due to the way in which datasets are stored,
that results in them not being assigned an identifier nor being indexed.
To overcome this, researchers should be encouraged to use the current LIMS (Bench-
ling) whenever relevant, and as thoroughly as possible. Other types of datasets
should be stored in Azure Data Lake or, even better, in public repositories. Should
the dataset not be shareable, the metadata record should still be created and pub-
lished.
As for interoperability, the main obstacle is that, in the occasions metadata are col-
lected, standards are barely applied.
In line with the FAIRification process [41], it would be necessary to define the seman-
tic model of the dataset and its metadata, see Figure 5.1. FAIRsharing.org gathers
a number of resources including metadata standards. The selection and usage of
metadata standards would not only improve the overall interoperability of datasets,
but also increase their findability, because they become better described and thus
more discoverable.
It could be interesting to implement automatic workflows to streamline all the above.
One could consist on having gate-checks during data collection and manipulation

With respect to data storage, a relevant one could revolve around automatically

• Papers
The score for papers is favourable, as they have (almost always) DOIs assigned,
which ensures their findability and accessibility.
In spite of that, it would be recommendable that DTU Biosustain investigates and

• Published datasets
The datasets made public, although findable to some degree, appear to be hardly
accessible or interoperable, owing to the fact that they and their metadata are not
always deposited in public repositories.
It has been shown that linking a publication and its underlying research data, besides
giving it more credibility, leads to it being more cited [43]. Furthermore, this citation
advantage is higher if the data are in a repository, versus to data presented as
supplementary material or made available on demand. That is to say, it is critical to
substantially increase F and A of these entities (!).
Aiming at that, it should be commonplace to deposit the datasets in public databases
or repositories, since these services assign DOIs, can be accessed through open
protocols and preserve the record’s metadata. Even if the publishers do not explicitly
require so, they do encourage it [44] [45] [46].
The (meta)data should be published in repositories that are suitable in terms of
data-type, discipline and size, among others. Possibilities worth exploring could be:
– Zenodo
It is an international repository, where researchers in any discipline can upload
files (up to 50 GB), and it has an integration with GitHub that allows to cite the

A solution to improve in both areas is to systematically store it in repositories such as

By doing that, code becomes highly accessible (HTTP, REST-API) and, provided the
repository is public, also searchable (should the code not be open, the repository
can be kept private).
At the same time, to ensure that they are findable, the repositories can be assigned
a DOI by means of third-party tools, like Zenodo or Figshare. This integration would
be very advisable.
When depositing the scripts, in order to provide comprehensive and rich metadata,
all possible fields should be filled and a README file always included.
The Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) successfully applied this
approach in order to boost the FAIRness in the Atlas chapter of the Sixth Assess-
ment Report [47].

6
Conclusions
The current digital system has an ever-growing capability to capture, compute and analyse
great amounts of complex and diverse data. Which poses some challenges in terms of
discovering, understanding and evaluating them.
In the context of research, it is key (and increasingly demanded) that the underlying data
are made available for others to reuse, both to ensure reproducibility as well as to find
new purposes and push innovation forward.
To attain interoperable and reusable data, it is necessary that these data are structured,
well-documented and seamlessly shared.
For this reason, digital maturity and data democratization are understood from the point
of view of data management practices, centred around the way data are collected and
stored. Since they were published in 2016, the FAIR principles have become a refer-
ence in this regard. At their core, they are guidelines to make data Findable, Accessible,
Interoperable and, ultimately, Reusable.
Thus, it is cornerstone for organizations to look into their data management practices and
seek to adopt the cited principles.
This thesis attempts to provide a framework to use for such an assessment. In order to
do so, I have:
• Read relevant literature and become acquainted with the current landscape of FAIR-
ness: institutions, tools and study cases.
• Outlined the scope of the project. The evaluation has been limited to specific digital
objects: researchers, internal datasets, papers, published datasets and scripts; and
focused on F, A, I (not R).
• Created a tailored questionnaire, targeting the referred entities and addressed to all
researchers at the centre (around 300).
• Defined the guidelines (sub-principles) to evaluate, the corresponding indicators and
the procedure to measure them.
• Analysed all answers to the form and computed a score, per entity type and principle,
see results in chapter 4
• Examined the biggest challenges for DTU Biosustain with respect to FAIRness, and
suggested some actions that could improve overall alignment. Table 5.1 encapsu-
lates these findings.
This work offers the first insights on FAIR performance at DTU Biosustain and it estab-
lishes a baseline against which to compare further on, as FAIR practices become imple-
mented at the organization.
As stated, however, the current project is circumscribed to specific entity types, and it
explores some, but not all, areas of FAIR. Thus, future work could involve an assessment
that is:
• Broader, in that it includes other entity types, such as workflows or software.
• Deeper, in that it includes all FAIR areas, especially interoperability and reusability.
This would entail examining in detail the quality of objects’ metadata.

• More refined, in that makes a more precise estimation as to the amount of data
