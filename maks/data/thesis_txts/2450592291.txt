Abstract
The aim of the slum-upgrading project in Kibugambata is to improve their standards 
of living, mainly by using the principle land readjustment to finance new housing for 
the slum dwellers. The project is complex, but the objectives of this thesis is to find 
recommendations for a physical plan suitable for Kibugambata, particularly in regards 
of using natural measures to promote future energy savings. To collect relevant data, 
a trip to Kibugambata where done, and the main methods used where site analysis, 
questionnaires and interviews. Analysis of the collected data where done particularly 
by creating three different scenarios, where each scenario had one main focus; using 
climatic measures, what the people wanted and ownership. The study showed, among 
other things that the main challenge of the households where economic stability and 
the plan should encourage for more business opportunities by mixed-use structures. 
Furthermore, the security in the settlement was low, and the layout of the settlement 
should be structured to increase the feeling and actual rate of crime, as well as encour­
age a further “social control” as seen in the settlement today. Additionally, the settle­
ment Kibugambata is located in a hot climate zone, to address future energy demands; 
the settlement should be laid out to use all natural measures to increase thermal com­
fort.

Introduction 
1.1 The rise of slums
As long as there have been cities there have been poor quarters, but it was not before 
the 16th century that the slums showed up, places that were ´squalid, overcrowded and 
wretched’. Urban population have increased immensely the last 50 years, and will con­
tinue to do so for at least 30 more years, as half the humanity -3.5 billion people- lives 
in cities today and 5 billion people are projected to live in cities by 2030. Parallel to 
the population growth and the rapid urbanization, the number of slums have increased. 
Slums have been the only large-scale solution to provide housing for low-income peo­
ple, containing the only type of housing that is affordable and accessible to the poor 
in cities where the competition for land and profits is fierce. In light of the increasing 
numbers of urban slum dwellers, governments have adopted a specific target on slums, 
UN’s development Goal 11, target 11.1, which aim to upgrade slums by 2030.
11.1 “By 2030, ensure access for all to adequate, safe and affordable housing and 
basic services and upgrade slums”  (The Challenge of Slums, 2003).
Alongside the development of informal settlements and slums, another consequence 
on the rapid urbanization and poor planning is Urban sprawl. This development has 
many drawbacks: it eats up valuable farmland, causes traffic congestion and pollu­
tion, increases the costs of providing services, increases dependency on cars and the 
need for parking, and encourages segregation into richer and poorer areas (UN-Habi­
tat, IIRR, 2004).
The total number of slum dwellers in the world were around 
924 million people in 2001, representing around one third of the 
world’s urban population. At that time, 43 percent of the urban 
populations of all development regions lived in slums, while 78.2 
per cent of the urban population in least developed countries were 
slum dwellers. In sub-Saharan Africa alone, 71.9 per cent of the 
urban population were living in slums in 2001, which is the high­
est rate in the world. It is projected that in the next 30 years the 
global number of slum dwellers will increase by about 2 million, 
if no firm and concrete action is taken. In less developed regions 
the urban population increased by 36 per cent the last decade and 
it is very unlikely that slum improvement or formal construction 
kept pace to any degree with this increase, as very few develop­
ment countries had formal residential building programs of any 
size. Most likely, the number of informal households increased in 
the same ratio as the rest of the urban population (The Challenge 
of Slums, 2003).

Introduction 
In an attempt to upgrade slums in Cape Town some people felt that the upgrading 
intervention had not significantly improved their lives, they feel poorer because they 
would now have to pay for services, which they had not expected. These difficulities 
are caused by a high rate of unemployment and lack of alternative livelihood strate­
gies. Another unintended consequence of the upgrade was a breakdown in community 
cohesion and neighbourliness. Some of the participants related this to how the houses 
where designed which they felt was constributing to individualism and lack of concern 
for one anothers well-being. 
A conclusion from the upgrading programmes in Cape Town was to find ways to sup­
port and enhance the cognizance of social networks and community cohesion within 
settlements. Taking this into account, the research supports an in situ upgrading ap­
proach that focus on improving the safety, health and well-being of residents, where 
they are located. To ensure that upgrading interventions does not intervene with the 
social support networks and informal measures of social control, which perform a vital 
function in informal settlements, care have to be taken. Another matter in the literature 
and confirmed by the research was that a one single approach to informal settlement 
upgrading is not advisable. This is because the context is markedly different across 
informal areas and certain upgrading approaches, and a need for a thorough analy­
sis and understanding of the socio-economic and governance context within informal 
settlements prior to implementing an upgrading. Community participation and active 
involvement in the upgrading project is essential (Brown-Luthango, Reyes, & Gubevu, 
2016).
The problem of slums have been dealt with differently and is the reason why the prac­
tice of slum upgrading was introduced; to improve the livelihood of slum dwellers. 
Unfortunately it is often done with little or no consultation with the people affected 
(Mureithi, 2016). 
The primary challenges in slum upgrading are achieving coherence in the community 
and finding solutions to a wide range of needs, as slums are not homogeneous, and 
many diverse vested interests exists within a slum. Additionally to the poor who are 
only looking for a decent place to live, there are criminal elements taking advantage 
of the informal space, or landlords who earn money renting out small shacks to people 
over time. All the different aspects must be properly understood and brought up into 
the planning process (Cities Alliance, n.d.). 
The literature about slum upgrading are filled with ideals based on inclusion, enable­
ment, sustainability, empowerment, capacity building, etc., and these ideals have not 
been visible. A significant gap between the development objectives and the develop­
ment realities is seen. However there have been some success cases, but a common 
methodology that can be applied everywhere have not been developed. Two main prin­
ciples can be recognized as successfull in upgrading projects: The first is that policy 
makers are recognized that there is great knowledge of what works best, and what does 
not, built into experience and actions of ordinary people. The second is to create par­
ticipation and community based upgrading approach, by increasing their options both 
trough the process and the outcomes (Calderon, 2008).

Introduction 
Definition of Slum upgrading from Cities Alliance: 
Slum upgrading is a process through which informal areas are gradually improved, formal­
ised and incorporated into the city itself, through extending land, services and citizenship to 
slum dwellers. It involves providing slum dwellers with the economic, social, institutional 
and community services available to other citizens. These services include legal (land ten­
ure), physical (infrastructure), social (crime or education, for example) or economic.
Slum upgrading is not simply about water or drainage or housing. It is about putting into 
motion the economic, social, institutional and community activities that are needed to turn 
around downward trends in an area. These activities should be undertaken cooperatively 
among all parties involved—residents, community groups, businesses as well as local and 
national authorities if applicable.
The activities tend to include the provision of basic services such as housing, streets, foot­
paths, drainage, clean water, sanitation, and sewage disposal. Often, access to education and 
health care are also part of upgrading.
In addition to basic services, one of the key elements of slum upgrading is legalising or regu­
larising properties and bringing secure land tenure to residents.
Ultimately, upgrading efforts aim to create a dynamic in the community where there is a 
sense of ownership, entitlement and inward investment in the area. (Cities Alliance, n.d.)

Introduction 
1.3 Land readjustment as a method for upgrad­
ing slums 
One method or practice that could provide an effective approach to achieving Goal 
11 of the SDGs is “land readjustment” (LR). This approach could help to address 
the challenges of improving urban conditions, in order to achieve urbanization with 
more desirable attributes and upgrading slums. Applying the LR approach to countries 
where the technique has never been used is still in an experimental stage. Pilot projects 
are being designed, but have not been fully implemented, further research is needed to 
test the validity bout this method.
Land readjustment is a method of land value capture, where the investment in infra­
structure raises the value of the nearby land. In an era of tight budgets and exploiting 
needs, cities are founding infrastructure and other public improvements trough this 
concept (Germán & Bernstein, 2018).
Despite these potential advantages of LR over conventional land assembly methods, it 
is hardly uncomplicated. The method is particularly difficult to implement in develop­
ing countries where public participation is not integrated into urban planning or where 
there is limited capacity to maintain ownership records and resolve competing land 
claims. When property owners do not recognize their obligation to pay for basic infra­
structure and services, requests to give up a portion of their land to cover the project 
costs will be strongly resisted (Hong & Brain, 2012).
A relatively new approach that resembles conventional land readjustment named PI­
LaR has been tested and promoted by UN-Habitat and the Global Land Tool Network. 
As the  Conventional land readjustment does not necessarily operate in favor of the 
poor: too often, the municipal government, working only with formal landowners, im­
poses decisions on local communities. PILaR differs from conventional land readjust­
ment in that it is participatory. It emphasizes a participatory process, rather than only 
the technical or financial results, as described in Figure 1. It involves all stakeholders 
– landowners, tenants, informal residents, the municipal authorities, land professionals 
and community organizations – in planning and making decisions. It is also inclusive, 
as it ensures that the poor and disadvantaged also benefit. It aims to achieve consensus 
among all stakeholders and avoid forcible removals or evictions.
PILaR can make a major contribution to a slum-improvement effort. It would identify 
the formal and informal rights to the land, redraw plot boundaries, allocate a propor­
tion of the land for roads, public space and municipal ownership, facilitate the shar­
ing of the increase in land value to ensure financial sustainability, and build the basic 
infrastructure. At the end of the process, it would provide clear formal land documents 
to the landholders, and clarify the tenure status of tenants. This would help create a 
market for affordable housing and reducing the creation of new slums.
A standard method of applying PILaR is not discoverd as the method is still experi­
mental. There is not enough experience to set firm recommendations, as well as each 
situation is different. However the participatory nature of the method demands that the 
approach should be adapted to suit local conditions (UN-Habitat, IIRR, 2004). 
“Participatory and inclusive land readjustment, or PILaR for short, is a way of 
relocating the land in or around a city in a more sustainable way for unified planning, 
subdivision or re-parcelling, and development” (UN-Habitat, IIRR, 2004).

1.6 The situation in Kibugambata
There have already been some development in the chosen project area before, this is 
also a reason why the location was chosen. In 2016 Jinja Municipal Council together 
with Actogether, SDI, Makerere University students and the residents of Kibugambata 
made a plan to re guide the Development in Kibugambata, because it had inorganically 
grown into a slum (Figure 4 illustrates the plan). Jinja was at one time an industrial 
harbour, where almost all industries in Uganda was located. People were coming to 
Jinja looking for jobs at that time, but in the 90’s when the industry collapsed and the 
factories closed, they had to find themselves another place to live within the city. Since 
there was no land people inhabited the ground of Kibugambata and similar areas. Illus­
tarted in Figure 3, the land was planned as low density residential by the municipality 
of Jinja in the 70’s, but the land had not been allocated by the municipality at that time, 
and they accepted that the people inhabited the area. Therefore, in 2016 the municipal­
ity demanded planning of smaller plots, since the bigger plots where for high-income 
earners. The new detailed plan produced could capture their desire for smaller plots 
suitable for the residents in Kibugambata. However, one plot is about 15 by 30 or 12 by 
30 meter and have to be for two or three households, but they are willing to be joined 
into one plot, as this is what they must do to be able to get the land titles. Today the 
next step is the registrations, so that people can get their titles (Senior physical planner 
Jinja Municiplality).
This is a great achievement for the people of Kibugambata, and something the commu­
nity have worked for since 2012. The plan created in 2016 is something the community 
is proud of, Figure 4 shows a photo where they are showing the physical plan. The plan 
is still processed as there is no one with a title in there hand today. They have never 
been closer getting their titles, and a scepticism to foreigners with a project regarding

2.1 The Importance of well planned 
settlements
When upgrading a slum and creating a new development plan, it is important to take a 
look at planning strategies to invest in how to best create a plan accepted by the people 
living there, and  make them thrive. There are several of important arguments for a 
stronger priority on focusing the planning of buildings and outdoor areas. Decisions 
regarding the design, planning and managing of built environments will affect people’s 
everyday lives, and changes could allow or reduce the sense of security, expand or re­
strict freedom or reduce opportunities for recreation, among other aspects. If planning 
is about making places better for people, the elements making places problematic for 
people must be addressed and one of the factors highest on that list is crime and fear 
for criminal actions. Evidence from criminology suggests the attributes for a more 
walkable neighbourhood may increase the incidence of crime. What criminologists 
consider the safest neighbourhoods are the ones characterised by residential housing 
and with few local shops or businesses to draw people to the neighbourhood. However 
suggestions that neighbourhoods providing sites for positive social interaction such as 
recreational centres might reduce crime (Foster, Wood, Christian, Knuiman, & Giles-
Corti, 2013).
Additionally, Jane Jacobs advocates that in areas where “mixed primary uses”, or 
homes and stores and restaurants and offices all in close proximity makes it safe. As 
different people were on the street for different reasons at different times of the day, 
contributing to the continuity of the neighbourhood and providing a continuous stream 
of “eyes on the street” to keep it safe (Tuhus-Dubrow, 2017). Safety in particularly for 
women and children comes from “eyes on the street”, the kind of involved neighbor­
hood surveillance of public space (Jacobs, 1961).
Social interaction is not only important for safety in a community, but it is also im­
portant for building social capital, increase social cohesion, a feeling of belonging and 
trust. Jane Jacobs have through her work emphasized the importance of urban neigh­
bourhood facilitating social meeting on streets, in front of stairs, entrance halls and 
balconies. Together with Jan Gehl, Jane talks about the importance of the social meet­
ing taking place close to peoples homes, since studies show that repeated meetings 
over time forms the basis for social relationships. Furthermore, Gehl have trough his 
publications highlighted the importance designing a physical environment for inviting 
social contact. Participating in or observing cultural activities, market, children play­
ing, nature makes it easier to enter conversations with strangers (Høyland, Denizou, 
Baer, Finess Evensmo, & Schanche Feragen, 2018).  
Project for public spaces have developed the idea of “The power of 10”  which creates 
a simple but profound criteria for a place, neighbourhood and city. The organization 
states that a great place needs at least 10 things to do in it or 10 reasons to be there 
(Pacheco, 2017). This could promote meetings between different people and counter 
the potential negative effects if it is dominated by one particular use or user group. Jane 
Jacobs theory concludes that the naturally occurrence of more people on one place 
creates safety, the safer we feel. The safety-enhancing measures is often featured as 
“safety in numbers” or “eyes on the street”. The feeling of safety is therefore linked to 
the experience of other people could prevent crime acts if something happens. How­
ever, it is not only about the benefits of other people being present in the urban space, 
but also that people know each other. Another way of facilitating natural surveillance 
of the space to place entrances and windows facing the streets, parks and squares, or 
by creating a territorial ownership of areas through symbolic as well as real division of 
private and public space. The locals feeling of ownership, pride and belonging to their 
neighbourhood is central to protect both the area and the people living there (Høyland, 
Denizou, Baer, Finess Evensmo, & Schanche Feragen, 2018).

2.2 Sustainable community
A sustainable community is characterized by a high level of stability, meaning a high 
amount of people living there for a longer time. A stable community, is a place where 
there are safe living conditions  and the availability for people crating trust, social 
relations and make them stronger over time. Inhabitants having an increased feeling 
of affiliation to a community results in less residential mobility, therefore it is also 
important with a wide range of housing typologies and different sizes of residential 
units in order to respond to the different changes of needs. Additionally to the different 
sized units, a financial scheme for housing and apartments is an important factor for 
providing accomodation for the different population groups (Høyland, Denizou, Baer, 
Finess Evensmo, & Schanche Feragen, 2018). Sustainable development is defined in 
Bruntlandskomission report from 1987 as; 
“Sustainable development is development that meets the needs of the present without 
compromising the ability of future generations to meet their own needs.” (United Na­
tions, 1987)

are livable requires qualities, the authors from the book The Rise of Sprawl and the 
decline of the american dream suggests six fundamental rules that have been around 
for centuries:

could get spread out and the streets could get smaller.

2.3 Climate adaptive planning
Half of the industrialized world’s population lives in urban areas today and accounts 
for half the world’s energy consumption. And only 10-20% of the population of the 
development countries live in urban areas. An enormous demand for energy resources 
could be a consequence of this. Today 83% of the global primary energy produced is 
consumed by only 25% of the world’s population.The unsustainability of this situation 
is understood when comparing together with the global trends towards urbanization. 
For many of the countries that falls within the hot climate zones such as Kibugambata, 
half or more of their urban peak load of energy consumption is used for air condition­
ing alone. For a typical air-conditioned building in a hot climate zone, 70-80% of its 
lifetime energy consumption is used just for operating these systems to achieve com­
fort. The energy consumption used to cool takes more energy than to heat, and the pres­
sure on energy resources to satisfy future demands in the hot and warm development 
countries will be great unless new measures are introduced. 
If future energy demands are to be sustainable in development countries, alternative 
approaches that promote energy efficiency need to be embraced. Through a careful de­
sign of urban environments and individual buildings potential savings could be gained. 
All naturally occurring resources should be integrated into planning and building, in 
such a way that their location, form and structure promote energy savings. 
The climate of a region is generally defined by Koch-Noelsen (2002) as the interaction 
of several different meteorological elements, as 
	
Solar radiation 
	
Air temperature
	
Humidity
	
Wind
	
Perception 
The naturally occurring resources that should be integrated when planning, would be 
features such as: 
	
Topographical Location
	
Orientation for optimal response to sun and wind
	
Urban form and external space
	
Shape and volume of the structures
	
Vegetation (Gut & Ackerknecht, 1993)

when using naturally occuring recourses in a planning process.

East and west facing walls receive the highest intensities of radiation. These walls 
should normally be kept as small as possible and contain few and small openings to 
reduce the radiation. Shading of the east and west elevations is difficult because of 
the low sun, and the south and north sides can easily be protected by an overhanging 
roof. 
The indoor temperature can hardly be kept much below the outdoor temperature in 
warm-humid zones. However, by efficient design the indoor temperature can avoid 
exceeding the outdoor temperature and inner surfaces can remain relatively cool. Simi­
lar to the sun’s radiation, existing winds should also be incorporated in the design 
concept. For the purpose of climatic design, only regular winds are relevant. Regular 
winds can be utilized for cooling, and when the temperature of the circulating air is 
below the indoor temperature, the cooling effect is obvious. When the temperature of 
the wind exceeded the temperature of the human body, a cooling effect is no longer 
possible. Efficient air circulation is one of the few possibilities for natural climatization 
in warm-humid zones. Because of the minimal temperature differences it can hardly be 
utilized to cool down the building components, but cooling is felt through the increased 
perspiration of the human body. This orientation does often not coincide with the best

2.3.4 Shape and volume
Heat exchange between the building and the environment depends on the exposed 
surfaces. A compact building would gain less heat during daytime and lose less heat 
at night, which makes the ratio to surface volume an important factor. If little heat ex­
change between the interior and the environment is desired, the ratio should be small. 
In warm-humid zones the best suitable building form have large surfaces compared to 
the volume, meaning the surface to volume ratio should be bigger. Heat exchange gets 
higher since the surface is large compared to the volume and it also favors ventilation 
and heat emission at nighttime.
The main goal is the reduction of direct heat gain by radiation through openings and 
of the internal surface temperature. The building should therefore be designed not only 
with protected openings, but also with protected walls. This task will be much easier 
if the building is kept low. In addition, the roof should extend far beyond the line of 
walls, with broad overhanging eaves and other means of shading.
The height of the buildings should not exceed 3-storeys. Higher buildings receive a 
high amount of radiant heat and give wind obstruction to neighboring buildings (Gut 
& Ackerknecht, 1993).

2.3.5 Vegetation
In tropical and subtropical regions the outdoor space is actively used. A major part of 
the social life and the daily routine work takes place there. The vegetation provides 
excellent means of improving the climatic conditions. Its surface does not heat up and 
it provides efficient shading at low cost However, it has to be arranged in a way that 
does not impede air circulation. Elements such as green covered facades would reduce 
the solar radiation heat gain since it shades the wall, as well as protect from heavy 
winds and driving rain. High trees with wide, shading crowns provide significant pro­
tection from solar radiation and should be incorporated as much as possible into any 
landscape planning. When placing trees close to the building, it is important to know 
what time of the day shade is desired as well as the suns path (Figure 45), as well as 
trees should remain open to providing access for the wind to enter the building (Gut & 
Ackerknecht, 1993). The additional vegetation can better the local climate, decrease 
noise, and even provide healthy food. Residential yards could support production of 
cultural function, through the addition of edible gardens that provide recreation as well 
as providing ecological functions. Planner and designers should consider opportuni­
ties to conserve exciting public spaces and connect these features with green public 
corridors. “Greenways” are designed to accommodate multiple functions such as bio­
diversity conservation, stormwater management, recreation, and visual quality (Lovell 
& Johnston, 2009).
2.3.6 Utilizing the cooling effect of water 
Most commonly in urban areas the road planners design drainage system to ensure 
rapid rainwater run-off. Such systems additionally to a high percentage of paved sur­
faces have the disadvantage that shortly after rainfalls the surrounding areas dries up, 
and the cooling effect of the water is lost. Furthermore, the functioning of the drainage 
systems depend to a great extent on their maintenance, as an example blocked drainage 
systems may cause dangerous flood situations if not properly maintained. 
An alternative approach would be to retain as much surface water as possible for a 
longer period, by for example keeping surfaces unpaved wherever feasible, addition­
ally to artificial lakes in areas such as parks. The increased water content in the air and 
soil improves the microclimate, supports and promotes vegetation that is an additional 
factor for a favourable microclimate (Gut & Ackerknecht, 1993). Landscape based 
stormwater management (LSM) includes systems such as green roofs, rain gardens, 
bioswales, dry and wet basins, and other green solutions for urban stormwater man­
agement. The system would also feed the ground water, which is an important factor 
regarding the water supply. In Northern European countries the development of LSM 
systems is remerging in a second wave driven by the challenge of adapting cities to cli­
mate change (Backhaus & Fryd, 2013). Instead of designing a huge drainage system, 
a system where the water is handled locally combined with a drainage system would 
only have to be designed for peak flows (Gut & Ackerknecht, 1993). The two different 
scenarios are illustrated i Figure 46.

died. This was mainly in this region of Uganda, where the minds of people changed

6.1.6 Stormwater management
Incidents of heavy rain is something that happens frequently in Kibugambata, and will probably occur more often the years to come because of the climate change. A standard 
piped drainage system could easily be clogged, as there is lack of a proper garbage collection policy. Instead of leading water away to a drainage system, the stormwater could 
be handled more sustainable. The water could be a resource and a positive element in the settlement instead of a burden. The water could be collected and used for washing of 
clothes and watering the gardens, as the water within the settlement is expensive and the water in the standpipes is clean water that could be used for drinking, and they are us­
ing that same clean water for everything. The development plan should also plan for further risks, such as dryer periods when water is scarce. A member of SDI said that there 
had been troubles with water, as the rainy season where delayed, and the people should not be dependent on the rain. Systems of collecting rainwater from the roof should be 
implemented, as it would not only give the opportunity to use the water for other purposes, but also ease the amount of water going to the pipes. 
A possible solution could be to create ponds, or places that delays the water by using landscape based stormwater management as explained in Chapter 2.3.6. The stormwater 
will be collected in different places inside the settlement as illustrated in a principle drawing in Figure 88, this would help cool down the air, keep the ground water level up, and 
help preventing flooding inside the settlement. The ponds could be used for other purposes when it is not raining. It could be a market, playground or parking spot, or it could 
be used for growing vegetables or for fish farming. The northern area being sold does not need to have as high density as the lower part; this pond could have a permanent water 
mirror and does not need to have other purposes than getting a better microclimate, as well as for recreational purposes. When the ponds cross their storage capacity, the water 
could be lead in open, or closed drainage system leading to the lake. 
All the farming activity should also be further encouraged in the new plan, and the farming would also be part of draining the surface, as the settlement is covered by a soil called 
Ferrosol, that have little permeability, a green cover would increase the permeability and also be a solution for climate resilience.

7.1 Scenario 1 - Climate adaptive planning 
Today, especially in development countries, the buildings and the houses constructed 
is often built on the background of good structures developed in western countries, and 
not designed for the particular climate zone. This factor, together with the aspect that 
the placement of the structures, is often no longer thought of, will increase the demand 
of mechanical ventilation. When planning for Kibugambata, these factors is something 
that is not too late, as well as free to take into consideration. 
The people in the settlement have expressed their discontent regarding overheated 
homes. As seen when visiting the settlement and by gathering data, they do not have 
much money and it could therefore be difficult to afford mechanical ventilation. The 
majority would most likely stay in their overheated apartments, without being able to 
do anything about it. For those getting mechanical ventilation the energy use would be 
high and expensive as well as not sustainable. If the climatic measures are taken into 
consideration and a proper computer simulation, with both a wind and a solar analysis 
is conducted, it would be important for both the inhabitants and the environment. 
The structures in scenario 1, are oriented and placed to best possible integrate all the 
naturally occurring resources. The result is a very strictly planned development, with 
little room for changes. In a close future, after the development is utilized big changes 
could occur in Kibugambata, as the government have little control on the further de­
velopment. New housing could appear, as there is much common land and the “com­
munity working spirit” have died out in this region of Uganda. In the focus group dis­
cussion with the female landowners one lady talks about the importance of the design 
of the new structures, as by tradition the parents should not live under the same roof 
as their sons. If this leads to construction of new buildings, it could interfere with the 
climate adaptive planning. The sloped terrain in the settlement could be used in such 
a way that there would be difficult to build up new structures between the houses. As 
well as the inhabitants should be informed, and maybe in an early stage could feel how 
the climate adaptive planning could affect them in a positive way. 
Today, most of the people believes that it is the municipality’s responsibility to take 
care of the public and common areas in the community, even though it have never been 
the case. If the inhabitants continue to believe that this should be the municipalities 
responsible, it is a big chance they won’t maintain these shared areas, and they would 
get neglected. If the people get a feeling of ownership of the land, the inhabitants might 
want to take care of the common areas together. It would be very important that the 
inhabitants feel that they own the project for it to be feasible. All the common areas 
between the apartment rows could be used for farming, recreational areas, as well as 
growing vegetables and keeping animals, if managed properly.
A green corridor trough the settlement would help accommodate the stormwater man­
agement as well as other functions. This would lead to a better connectivity between 
the common areas and the public structures and the connection in the settlement would 
be increased. However, the big vacant areas could in future development be used for 
new structures, this would interact with both the natural ventilation and the stormwater 
system, thus flooding’s could occur. 
It is not planned for much business by the road. The amount of mixed use by the streets 
could be increased, but that would lead to more households with decreased natural 
ventilation. To substitute the shops by the road, a market place could be set up. The 
inhabitants talked a lot about a standard market, but that is something seen in bigger 
cities in Uganda, not in a small settlements such as Kibugambata. It might take up too 
much space, as well as there would not be the same lively outside streets as today, 
if the shops where moved inside. In Chapter 2.2, point 5 in UN-Habitats principles 
for a sustainable development is to limit the amount of single-use structures, which 
also encourage the development of mixed-use structures. Instead of a standard market, 
there could be an outside area where people could gather and sell their goods. One of 
the “delay ponds” could for example be used for these purposes, as the market sellers 
would not sit outside in such an area if raining. This would create a multipurpose area,

The three scenarios are quite similar, this is because they are based on the same data 
and analyzes. However, they meet different needs and based on the needs that are most 
desirable to fulfill, the scenarios should be given different priority.
Throughout the research some important aspects have been brought up, and the differ­
ent scenarios fulfills most of the aspects, but in different degrees. The most important 
aspects will be discussed in this chapter, as well as which scenario that should be pri­
oritised to cover these aspects better. 
Keep the existing business and make room for more: This aspect have not been very 
well contained in scenario one, and scenario three should be prioritised if this need is to 
be covered as best as possible. It seems like it would be a crucial aspect that should be 
covered for the inhabitants to accept the development plan. There is still room in sce­
nario 1, more mixed-use structures could be placed at the ground floor within the set­
tlement. In the focus group discussion the businesses by the road, was the businesses 
expecting the highest income boost, and if the people have to move their business from 
the road into the settlement, they might not accept it. However, the businesses could be 
moved in some of the row houses located by the road going trough the settlement, or 
by the road facing the steel factory, since it probably will become busier than today as 
more people are moving into the settlement. 
Room for farming: This would need more ground area, and scenario one would have 
the most room and area for this purpose, but it should be held off for this purpose. As 
well as the areas already used for farming today should be kept, as there is already 
farmers having these areas today. The areas could most likely be moved if needed, by 
consultation from the inhabitants. By keeping the areas for farming, as well as getting 
more green areas used for farming, it would also help drain some of the stormwater, 
and make the area more climate resilient. Additionally to creating work places, keep­
ing their hobby, providing food and income. The other scenarios could also keep the 
interest of farming, as there is provided for many outside areas. All the private areas in 
scenario 1 would provide more people for individual farming at a small scale, and sce­
nario two have many common areas; some of these areas could be kept for farming. 
A new and proper drainage system: In all the scenarios, the drainage is prioritised 
and a similar system would apply for all. A land based stormwater management would 
be applied, and the people would be able to use the rainwater more sustainable. How­
ever, it would probably be more permeable surfaces in scenario one, even though there 
is more roads, as the roads could be permeable as well. Scenario two would be the one 
with the least permeable surfaces, but there could be more rooftops where it would be 
possible to harvest water. The ponds created, gives more opportunities than delaying 
water flows throughout the settlement, if those areas are to be neglected, or used for 
other purposes, the ponds gets blocked and the area could again get problems with 
flooding’s. A comprehension of the consequences if not maintaining the area would be 
important throughout the process. 
2-3 storey building: All the scenarios have two or three stories buildings, but scenario 
two is mostly planned for two stories, since this would better comply with what the 
inhabitants would like. It would make it easier for more people to live in the house after

Mixed development: Kibugambata is a homogenous settlement, consisting of house­

8.0 Conclusion
The three scenarios address the difficulties differently, but all the scenarios illustrate 
a set of basic requirements that the upgrade should cover. The frequent flooding of 
houses and roads is an example of such a requirement. New proper housing as well 
as a drainage system using landscape based stormwater management would  not only 
prevent flooding, but it would also be a more sustainable approach as the ground water 
will become more stable. This would also lower the amount of money used on clean 
water, as collected water could be used for washing etc. Another requirement was in 
regard to how the residents felt about the low level of security. Lack of structure within 
settlements was mentioned among other things to be a cause of the aforementioned is­
sue and could be improved with the new housing set-up leading to more “eyes on the 
street”. Other issues regarding safety was the poor housing, lack of access roads, il­
legal connection for electricity and theft. The basic requirements in the three scenarios 
as well as the new housing, solar power and access roads within the settlement would 
cover these issues. 
Depending on which scenario prioritised, the issues are addressed in different degrees. 
Scenario 1, prioritising planning by using natural measures, should be prioritised to 
prevent overheated homes, as well as to prevent future energy demands. By using 
the principles in scenario 2, prioritising what the inhabitants wish for, the economic 
situation within Kibugambata would be more ensured as there are more business op­
portunities along the streets. They would have their businesses close, and it would be 
similar as the situation is today. The inhabitants would have more private areas that 
would extend their living space, as well as it would give the households the flexibil­
ity to use their outside space as they desire. Scenario 3 prioritises a higher amount of 
shared space, have more room for recreation that were highly requested, as well as 
missed today. This approach could be the one chosen if future maintenance is to be 
prioritised, as the inhabitants would have to use these areas more if they do not have 
enough private space.
Most theories about slum upgrading concludes that the “social inclusion” is one of the 
most important criteria’s for successful projects. This also applies to what people have 
said during meetings and the data collection phase; the inhabitants should be a part 
of the project from the decisions taken in the start-up phase, as it is very important to 
create a sense of ownership, thus create interest to be a part of the project. The whole 
process could help enhance a stronger community and a stronger feeling of belonging, 
and furthermore being a factor on how the community would be able to do voluntarily 
work to maintain the area after the project is accomplished.
Increased cost of living because of the upgrade could be a cruical factor for the most 
vulnerable, thus a plan for mixed-use development and modest housing should be im­
plemented. Modest housing with room for flexibility would be important to implement 
as the economy of people changes, the households are changing in size and composi­
tion. A wide range of housing typologies would give the people opportunities to get the 
household size and standard they could afford. 
The three scenarios are laid out to suit the terrain as it is slightly sloped towards the 
lake. Some of the upper apartments would get the opportunities to enjoy the view, as 
well as the natural breeze coming from the lake. These aspects are best covered in sce­
nario 3, as the row houses are placed in a certain distance from each other to prevent 
windshield. Most of the old canopy shaped trees are preserved, as they have a great 
value for the outdoor spaces and the local microclimate. This would be in addition to 
new trees that would be placed for shading east and west facing facades, as well as 
potentially paved areas.  
The social values are important to maintain in the development and the trend within 
the settlement is to stay for a certain amount of time, as people are living there for their 
whole life. An affordable and modest housing plan with differential housing, as well as 
openness in the project, would help ensure people staying in Kibugambata. This would 
help to keep a stable community with the availability of creating trust and social rela­
tions. Kibugambata is a strong community, and there are good neighbourly relations 
were they help each other and there is a “social control” within the settlement. These 
values could be preserved by developing a physical plan where the inhabitants do not 
get isolated from each other, and do not have to move because of lack of affordable 
housing. 
For the project to succeed the plan must be accepted as well as the inhabitants should 
feel ownership over it. The different scenarios should be discussed with the inhabitants 
and the land committee group as well as the local planners. It should continue being 
an iterative process with more scenarios created until the inhabitants are ready and are 
willing to accept it.

Conclusion
8.1 Further work
A physical plan should be made for the whole area of Kibugambata to prevent a 
low-density population in the northern part, and to create a connectivity through 
the area as the different part of the settlement could benefit from each other. 
Using a self-help or CAMP approach as Kasaide Michael describes in Chapter 
3.1.4 should be further investigated, as this would decrease the construction cost 
and give the inhabitants a higher feeling of ownership. 
There should also be created a plan for how the process should be conducted when 
the project starts. How many people is there room for in the houses used for reloca­
tion, and how many houses should be constructed at once? 
A wind and sun simulation should be done on each of the scenarios to see the effect 
on the different plans. 
It is understood that they would not like to sell their land, and it is probably dif­
ficult to see the value of the project when this much land must be sold to make the 
project feasable. By building elementary housing, it could reduce cost of construc­
tion, and should therefor be further investigated.
