Abstract
Addressing climate change is a pressing concern in today’s society. Road transportation is
a major source of greenhouse gas emissions. TDC NET, an important player in Denmark’s
communication infrastructure, has set a goal to decrease car travel by 25 percent by 2025
through the use of advanced automated transport planning. The main objective of this
thesis is to use advanced artificial intelligence techniques to detect and identify outliers
and anomalies in TDC NET’s network. This will enable for the optimization of technician
routes and a decrease in environmental impact.
This thesis looks into the effectiveness of normalizing flows (NF) in detecting anomalies
in multivariate time series data. Previous research focused mainly on single-variable time
series and point anomalies frequently ignoring multivariate contexts; however, there is a
growing recognition of the importance of addressing multivariate time series and contex-
tual anomalies. This study aims to close this gap by proposing a method for identifying
anomalies in multivariate time series using NF, taking into account both point anomalies
and data context. This study is significant because it addresses two understudied areas.
The process involves utilizing NF, an advanced technique in deep generative modeling, to
create a model that effectively transforms complex distributions into simpler ones. NF per-
forms two roles through the application of learnt functions, which are usually accomplished
via deep learning techniques and its tractable functions: anomaly detection and genera-
tive modeling. The study evaluates the model’s efficacy using three increasingly complex
datasets: MNIST, GHL, and real-world data from TDC NET. MNIST is a grayscale image
dataset with labeled outliers that provides a simple first test. GHL is a synthetic multi-
variate time series dataset with labeled outliers, which enables more complex anomaly
detection scenarios. TDC NET is a real-world dataset with no labeled outliers, providing
the most difficult test of the model’s performance in practical and real-world applications.
The key findings show that the NF model can successfully identify anomalies in image
datasets, as evidenced by impressive performance metrics. Nonetheless, the results for
the GHL dataset were less favorable, highlighting the need for improved categorization
approaches. Despite the lack of labeled data, the model correctly identified probable
outliers in the TDC dataset, though benchmarking was difficult.
The importance of these findings comes from the NF model’s ability to improve the detec-
tion of abnormalities in multivariate time series, leading to more effective route planning
and significant CO2 emission reductions. The study establishes the foundation for future
improvements in the implementation of the model, which may be applied in many fields
such as medical and industrial anomaly detection.

Introduction
1.1
Problem Statement and Motivation
In contemporary society, the urgency to mitigate climate change is increasingly press-
ing. One significant contributor to greenhouse gas emissions is road transportation, with
Danish roads alone emitting approximately 4 million tons of CO2 annually. Among the
entities grappling with this challenge is TDC NET, a crucial player in Denmark’s com-
munication infrastructure, operating a fleet of 850 technician vehicles covering extensive
daily distances. Recognizing the imperative to curb emissions, TDC NET aims to reduce
its vehicle travel by 25 percent by 2025, primarily through innovative automated transport
planning.
The motivation for this thesis comes from the need to address CO2 emissions from TDC
NET technician vehicles.
Current practices necessitate a paradigm shift towards ad-
vanced automated transport planning, a transition supported by international studies demon-
strating potential cost reductions of 5 to 20 percent through such optimization.
This
presents a tangible opportunity not only to enhance operational efficiency but also to
significantly mitigate CO2 emissions. The inception of the GREENFORCE project, a col-
laborative effort between QAMPO, DTU, and TDC NET, underscores the commitment to
realizing this potential through the development of cutting-edge software solutions.
This thesis centers on the critical task of detecting outliers and anomalies within TDC
NET’s network, pivotal in optimizing technician routes and minimizing environmental im-
pact. By leveraging artificial intelligence, advanced algorithms, and comprehensive data
analysis, the objective is to enhance route planning in real time. The thesis further aims
to explore predictive analytics to anticipate network failures, empowering technicians to
proactively address issues and optimize their routes accordingly.
In the context of broadband networks, data transmission to customers occurs via a local
node known as a Coaxial Media Converter (CMC), which connects to the backbone inter-
net using fiber optic technology. The signal between the CMC and the customer typically
utilizes coaxial cables and relies on Radio Frequency (RF) signals transmitted through
insulated copper wires.
To ensure the maintenance and supervision of signal quality within the network, modems
collect a group of metrics, including power levels, signal strength, signal quality, and the
number of corrected bits, among others. These metrics are polled at regular intervals,
approximately every 15 minutes, generating multiple time series datasets. The dataset
utilized in this thesis is sourced from TDC NET’s Danish broadband network, providing a
rich source of data for the detection of faults and anomalies within the system.
In summary, this thesis proposal underscores the urgency and importance of addressing
CO2 emissions within TDC NET’s operations. By focusing on automated transport plan-
ning and predictive analytics, the goal is to not only optimize technician routes but also
contribute significantly to Denmark’s broader sustainability goals.

In order to address the challenges caused by CO2 emissions in TDC NET’s operations,

At its core, NF involves the construction of a sequence of invertible functions that pro-

1.3
Thesis Objective
The scientific questions addressed in this thesis are as follows:
1. Can NF algorithms effectively predict outliers within time series data, and to what
extent can they enhance anomaly detection capabilities?
2. How flexible and adaptable are NF algorithms across different data types, particu-
larly in the context of time series analysis?
3. To what degree can NF algorithms detect various types or clusters of outliers within
time series data, and can they provide meaningful insights and conclusions from
these distinctions?
The primary goal of this thesis is to develop a robust model, comprising a sequence of
functions denoted as fi, utilizing Normalizing Flows (NF) for modeling the distribution of
time series behaviors. This model is designed to serve two main purposes: detecting
outliers within time series sequences and clustering different types of outlier behaviors.
To achieve this goal, the scope of the thesis encompasses several key stages. Initially, the
focus lies on formulating a suitable model using NF to accurately capture the distribution
of time series behaviors. The efficacy of the model is validated through outlier detection
experiments conducted on the MNIST dataset [1], to initially test the model’s capabilities.
Then we tested the model on the GHL Dataset [2, 3] which is a simulated and simplified
synthetic multivariate time series data which was used to establish a baseline model and
assess the potential of NF for outlier detection in this kind of data environments. The
thesis evaluates the potential of NF for outlier detection, leveraging a real-world TDC Net
data. In addition, the model’s performance is benchmarked against other state-of-the-art
methods for unsupervised analysis of time series data. This comparative analysis ensures
that the proposed NF-based model is tested to the limit of its effectiveness and accuracy.

2.1
Time Series
A time series is a sequence of data points indexed sequentially over time, commonly con-
sisting of observations recorded at regular intervals [4]. Defined as a series of records
in chronological order, time series data is prevalent across various domains such as mil-
itary, economy, and scientific research, drawing significant attention from researchers.
The unique characteristics that distinguish time series from other data types are its time
and sequence attributes. Each data point in a time series is associated with a timestamp,
and the records must be arranged in chronological order [5, 6].
Time series can be divided into univariate time series and multivariate time series ac-
cording to the number of variables, the specific definition is as follows: Time Series is a
sequence records according to a chronological order S = {vi(1), vi(2), ...., vi(t), ...., vi(n)}
is defined as the time series. In which t (t = 1, 2, …, n) is defined as time, i (i = 1, 2, …., m)
is defined as a variable index. vi(t) is defined as the record of the variable i on time t.
When m = 1, S is defined as a univariate time series (UTS). When m > 1, S is defined
as a multivariate time series (MTS) [6].
As the name implies, a univariate time series (UTS) is a series of data that is based on
a single variable that changes over time. Multivariate Time Series (MTS) data consist of
a collection of time series that describes various aspects of a particular temporal phe-
nomenon. Each variable in an MTS is dependent on time and influenced by both its past
values, exhibiting temporal dependency, and other variables through their correlations [7,
8].
Time series decomposition involves breaking down a time series X into four distinct com-
ponents, each representing a specific aspect of its movement [9]. These components
are: 1. Secular Trend: This component reflects the long-term pattern in the data, which
can be an upward or downward movement over time and does not necessarily have to be
linear. For example, the gradual increase in global average temperatures over decades.
2. Seasonal Variations: These are patterns that occur at a fixed frequency, such as
monthly or weekly. For instance, retail sales often increase during the holiday season. 3.
Cyclical Fluctuations: These are rises and falls in the data without a fixed frequency,
often related to natural events or economic cycles. An example is the business cycle
in economics. 4. Irregular Variations: These are random, unpredictable changes that
remain after the other components are accounted for, such as sudden market crashes
[8].

A time series can be mathematically described by estimating these components sepa-
rately, each potentially deviating from normal behavior. The trend ( Tt), seasonal (St),
and cyclic (Ct) variations dynamically interact to form the overall time series pattern, with
the irregular component representing residual randomness (Rt). Mathematically, these
can be expressed through additive decomposition Yt = Tt + St + Ct + Rt, or multiplicative
decomposition Yt = Tt ∗St ∗Ct ∗Rt, depending on whether the components are assumed
to be independent or dependent, respectively. [5, 10, 11, 12]
The context in which data instances are observed is a crucial source of information for
detecting abnormal events in time series. This contextual information is often distinc-
tive and not explicitly measured or recorded across different applications. For anomaly
detection, the context is typically obscure, so only ubiquitous contexts are considered:
spatial context, temporal context, and spatial-temporal context. The spatial context fo-
cuses on the location where a data instance is observed, while the temporal context re-
veals the sequential information among observations. Combining these two forms the
spatial-temporal context, which has gained significant attention in recent years due to its
comprehensive approach to analyzing data instances. [5, 13]
2.2
Anomalies
Anomalies, often referred to as anomalous behavior, faults, novelties, surprise, change
point, outliers, or deviations [6], are terms used to describe data points or sequences that
deviate from the normal pattern in a dataset [5]. According to various researchers, an
anomaly in time-series data is a deviation from the general distribution, which could be
a single observation or a series of observations. These deviations do not conform to the
expected sequence distribution and are far from other points of that set of data.

There is not a harmonised definition agreed by academia for exceptions; Though, in statis-
tics, the data points which do not obey the sequence distribution and far from other objects
are defined as abnormal. In the context of regression models, anomalies are data points
that significantly diverge from the expected model output [6]. The widely accepted def-
inition by Hawkins describes anomalies as data significantly different from others in the
dataset, caused by different mechanisms rather than random errors [14]. Examples of
anomalies include outliers resulting from mechanical faults, changes in system behavior,
fraudulent activities, human errors, instrument errors, or natural deviations in populations.
Anomalies can be classified into temporal, intermetric, and temporal-intermetric cate-
gories in both univariate time series (UTS) and multivariate time series (MTS) [15]. In
a time series, temporal anomalies can be compared with either their neighbours (local)
or the whole time series (global), and they present different forms depending on their be-
havior [16]. There are several types of temporal anomalies that commonly occur in uni-
variate time series, all of which are shown in the in the Figure-2.2. Temporal anomalies in
UTS include global anomalies, which are extreme values or spikes; contextual anomalies,
which are deviations within a specific context; seasonal anomalies, which exhibit unusual
seasonality; trend anomalies, which involve permanent shifts in the mean; and shapelet
anomalies, where subsequences have different shapes or cycles. These anomalies are
identified using formulas that compare observed values to expected models and thresh-
olds. [8]
Figure 2.2: Figure from [8]. An overview of different temporal anomalies plotted from the
NeurIPS-TS dataset [16]. Global and contextual anomalies occur in a point (coloured in
blue), while other types, including seasonal, trend and shapelet, can be occurred in a
subsequence (coloured in red).
In MTS, intermetric anomalies occur when correlations between different metrics devi-
ate from the norm, indicated by a significant change in correlation coefficients within a
specified window. Intermetric-temporal anomalies violate both temporal and intermetric
dependencies, making them easier to detect. This classification framework (Figure-2.3)
helps in identifying unusual patterns and deviations in complex datasets, enhancing the
understanding of anomalous behaviors in time series data. [8]

Introduction to Artifial Intelligence
Artificial Intelligence (AI) is a multidisciplinary field that seeks to create systems capable of
performing tasks using programmed algorithms that typically require human intelligence.
Within AI, we find Machine Learning (ML), which focuses on learning from historical data
and improving performance without being explicitly programmed. Instead of following
rigid rules, Machine Learning algorithms adapt and improve with experience. Some key
concepts in ML include supervised learning, where models are trained with labeled ex-
amples, and unsupervised learning, which finds patterns in unlabeled data. Additionally,
Deep Learning is a specific technique within ML that uses artificial neural networks with
multiple layers to learn complex data representations.
An algorithm is a sequence of logical steps designed to solve a specific problem. In the
context of Machine Learning, algorithms serve as the ”recipes” that enable machines to
learn and make decisions. They provide a systematic approach to tackling problems by
breaking them down into smaller, manageable steps. Algorithms can range from simple
mathematical equations to complex decision-making processes. They are essential com-
ponents of various computational tasks, guiding computers in performing tasks efficiently
and effectively. In the field of Machine Learning, algorithms play a crucial role in train-
ing models, analyzing data, and making predictions or classifications based on learned
patterns. By following a predefined set of instructions, algorithms empower machines
to process information, adapt to changing environments, and improve performance over
time.

networks, often very large ones, to recognize patterns and make decisions. It involves
multiple layers of interconnected neurons that mimic the human brain’s structure and func-
tionality, allowing machines to learn from vast amounts of data.

cesses it through an activation function, and produces an output. Each neuron performs
a simple computation: it calculates a weighted sum of its inputs, adds a bias term, and
then applies an activation function to this sum. Mathematically, this can be represented

In a typical neural network, multiple neurons are arranged in layers. The typical architec-
ture of a neural network involves stacking multiple neurons in layers, much like stacking
Legos. This architecture typically includes an input layer, one or more hidden layers, and
an output layer. Data flows through the network starting at the input layer, where each
neuron receives a portion of the input data. The data then passes through the hidden
layers, where neurons process it and pass the results to subsequent layers. Finally, the
processed data reaches the output layer, which produces the final output of the network.
Each layer transforms the data, enabling the network to learn complex patterns and rep-
resentations.

Additionally, activation functions play a pivotal role in shaping the output of individual

Unit (ReLU), Sigmoid, and Tanh. Each activation function serves a distinct purpose in

Backpropagation stands as a main ingredient algorithm in training neural networks, en-
abling the iterative adjustment of network weights to minimize the loss function. The
concept revolves around computing the gradient of the loss function with respect to each
weight parameter by propagating error gradients backward through the network. Initially,
the network makes a forward pass to generate predictions, which are compared against
the ground truth labels to compute the loss. Subsequently, during the backward pass, er-
ror gradients are calculated and propagated from the output layer to the input layer. This
propagation of gradients allows for the systematic updating of weights using optimization
algorithms like gradient descent, gradually refining the network’s parameters to improve
its predictive accuracy. Backpropagation is instrumental in facilitating the learning pro-
cess of neural networks, enabling them to adapt and refine their internal representations
in response to the provided training data, ultimately enhancing their performance across
various tasks and domains.

2.5
Convolutional Networks
A Convolutional Neural Network, or CNN for short, is a specialized type of neural network
designed for pattern recognition, particularly good at handling spatial hierarchies in data
such as images. Additionally, CNNs are highly effective networks that excel at solving
classification problems. Unlike traditional neural networks, which treat input data as a flat
vector, CNNs leverage their unique architecture to recognize spatial patterns through con-
volutional layers that apply filters to local receptive fields, capturing spatial dependencies

Autoencoder
Autoencoders are among the simplest and oldest forms of generative models, which are
crucial for unsupervised learning tasks. An autoencoder is a specialized type of neural
network used primarily in unsupervised learning, consisting of two main components: an
encoder and a decoder. The encoder compresses input data into a simplified, lower-
dimensional representation known as the latent space, while the decoder reconstructs
the original input from this latent representation. Despite their name, autoencoders also
have a decoding capability, enabling them to generate data and thus serving a dual role
in representation learning and generation. The primary function of an autoencoder is to
learn efficient embeddings of unlabeled data by compressing it through the encoder and
ensuring the decoder accurately reconstructs the input, thereby preserving most of the
information. The true power of autoencoders lies in their non-linear nature, as incorporat-
ing nonlinear activation functions and multiple hidden layers allows them to learn complex
representations of input data with minimal information loss [22, 23, 24]. Autoencoders
have diverse applications, such as denoising images, reducing dataset dimensionality,
identifying key features in data.
The encoding process, known as Representation Learning or bottleneck, involves mod-
eling high-dimensional data into a smaller, more manageable form. Then the decoder,
which can be replaced or combined with other specialized layers for added flexibility,

2.7
Normalizing Flows
Currently, there are several prominent data generation techniques, each with distinct ap-
proaches: Diffusion models, Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs), Variational Au-
toencoders (VAEs), and Flow-based models [25, 26, 27, 28]. Diffusion models add noise
to data samples in the forward process and then reconstruct the data in the reverse pro-
cess. GANs involve a generator that creates data samples and a discriminator that eval-
uates whether these samples belong to the original dataset, effectively framing unsuper-
vised learning as a supervised minimax game. VAEs use an encoder-decoder architecture
where the encoder maps data to a latent space and the decoder reconstructs the data,
optimizing the log-likelihood via the evidence lower bound (ELBO). Despite their success
in learning complex distributions, GANs and VAEs face challenges such as exact proba-
bility distribution evaluation, training instability, model collapse, and vanishing gradients.
Normalizing flows, a flow-based model, address these issues by employing a sequence
of invertible transformations, ensuring stable training and precise probability distribution
learning without adding noise to generate samples. Unlike GANs and VAEs, flow-based

Series Data
==PAGE:25==
models explicitly learn the data distribution p(x), using a straightforward negative log-
likelihood loss function, making them a powerful alternative for generative modeling tasks.
Normalizing flows are a robust method in machine learning and statistics for modeling
complex data distributions by transforming a simple base distribution, typically a standard
Gaussian, into a more intricate one that aligns with the data of interest. This transformation
is achieved through a sequence of invertible functions, which ensure that every step in
the process can be reversed, allowing for both efficient sampling and density evaluation.
By leveraging these invertible transformations, normalizing flows enable the mapping of
simple latent space distributions to complex real-world data distributions, making them
particularly useful for generative modeling tasks where generating new samples from a
learned distribution is required. [25, 26, 27, 28]

The training process in normalizing flows involves learning a series of transformations
that map data from a complex distribution to a simple one, typically a standard Gaussian.
The goal is to ensure that the transformed data adheres to the simple distribution. This
is accomplished through a carefully designed loss function that optimizes these transfor-

The loss function in normalizing flows is designed to ensure that the model learns trans-
formations that convert complex data distributions into simple ones. It consists of two
key components: one term that encourages the transformed data to adhere to the sim-
ple distribution, and another that ensures the transformation is volume-preserving. By
minimizing this loss function, the model effectively learns transformations that accurately
represent the true data distribution. Flow-based models utilize the negative log-likelihood
as their loss function, which enables them to provide the exact likelihood of the data,
thereby ensuring precise and stable training outcomes.
Once the model has learned the reverse transformation, it can also perform the forward
transformation, turning simple distributions into complex ones. This capability allows the
model to generate new data points that resemble the original complex distribution by start-
ing from simple distributions.
2.7.3
Models with Normalizing flows
Normalizing flows encompass a variety of models designed to transform simple distri-
butions into complex ones through invertible transformations. One of the foundational
models is Real NVP (Real-valued Non-Volume Preserving), which utilizes coupling layers
to achieve these transformations efficiently [29]. Another notable model is NICE (Non-
linear Independent Components Estimation), which also employs a series of invertible
transformations but focuses on preserving volume through simpler affine transformations
[30]. Glow is a more recent model that builds on the ideas of Real NVP by introducing
additional components like 1x1 invertible convolutions to improve the expressiveness and
efficiency of the flow-based generative process [31]. These models leverage the power
of invertible transformations to generate high-quality, complex distributions from simple
latent spaces.
Autoregressive models represent another approach to flow-based generative modeling,
where the distribution of each variable is conditioned on the previous ones in a sequence.
MADE (Masked Autoencoder for Distribution Estimation) is a model that uses masking
to ensure the autoregressive property in its architecture [32]. PixelRNN and WaveNet
extend this idea to two-dimensional and sequential data, respectively, by modeling the
distribution of each pixel or time step conditioned on previous ones [33, 34]. Masked Au-
toregressive Flows (MAF) and Inverse Autoregressive Flows (IAF) enhance the flexibility
of these models by combining autoregressive models with normalizing flows, enabling
more powerful and expressive transformations[35, 36]. Additionally, VAE + Flow models
integrate variational autoencoders with normalizing flows to improve the expressiveness
of the latent space, resulting in better generative performance. [37]

plot. This classifier’s ROC curve forms a diagonal line from the bottom left to the top right,
yielding an AUC of 0.5. In contrast, a model with perfect skill is represented at the point
(0;1) on the plot, with its ROC curve extending from the bottom left to the top left and then
horizontally across the top to the top right.

performance for the specific application in question.

Anomaly detection is fundamentally a statistical method used to discern outliers from the
data, helping to identify instances that significantly deviate from the established pattern
or norm [6].
This field is crucial for applications where it is important to detect unexpected changes,
potential faults, or novel occurrences that could indicate significant events or issues and
it has been widely used across numerous application areas, including manufacturing and
healthcare. In these fields, detecting anomalies can signal novel or unexpected events
such as production faults, system defects, or heart palpitations [7, 8]. This broad applica-
bility has made anomaly detection a significant problem studied across various research
domains, especially in the analysis of multivariate time series, attracting considerable at-
tention over the recent decades [5, 42].
Anomaly detection in time series data, particularly multivariate time series, presents sev-
eral challenges. Despite advancements in system monitoring and anomaly detection tech-
nologies, high false positive rates remain a persistent issue [5]. Furthermore, while nu-
merous anomaly detection algorithms exist, ranging from domain-specific to more generic
approaches, no single algorithm consistently performs well across all domains [5]. The
challenge is compounded by the lack of a concise and operational definition of anomalies
in multivariate contexts. Multivariate time series data involve multiple variables describ-
ing different aspects of a temporal phenomenon, making the detection process complex
due to the need to consider both temporal and variable relationships simultaneously. Un-
like univariate series, where anomalies can be identified by unusual points or unexpected
subsequences, multivariate techniques must also account for the relationships among
the variables, making the detection process more intricate and less straightforward. Ad-
ditionally, most existing techniques focus on univariate time series, where only a single
variable is analyzed, but multivariate analysis requires handling the interplay between
multiple variables, significantly increasing the difficulty [7, 43, 44].
In the field of anomaly detection, various methods have been developed to identify de-
viations from normal behavior in data. This survey will explore these methods, starting
with Statistical Methods, which include Parametric Models that assume data follows a
specific distribution, and Non-Parametric Models that do not rely on such assumptions.
Additionally, it will cover Similarity-Based Methods, which are further divided into those
Based on Distance, focusing on the proximity between data points, and those Based
on Density, which examine the local data density. Next, Clustering-Based Methods is
discussed, which detect anomalies by identifying data points that do not fit well into any
cluster. Following this, Classification-Based Methods will be examined, highlighting
techniques that use labeled data to distinguish between normal and anomalous instances.
The survey will also include Transformation-Based Methods, which apply data trans-
formations to facilitate anomaly detection. Lastly, Modeling-Based Methods (Machine
Learning-Based) will be reviewed, encompassing supervised, unsupervised, and semi-
supervised approaches that leverage advanced machine learning techniques to identify
anomalies. This comprehensive overview aims to provide insights into the diverse strate-
gies employed in anomaly detection across different domains.

3.1
Statistical Methods
Statistical methods are among the earliest and most extensively studied approaches for
anomaly detection, relying on the principle that significant deviations from a given sta-
tistical distribution or model indicate anomalies [45]. These methods operate under the
assumption that normal data points follow a specific distribution pattern, and any data
point that significantly deviates from this pattern is considered an outlier. The foundation
of statistical methods is based on the idea that the difference between the observed data
and the expected statistical distribution or model, when exceeding a particular threshold,
signifies an anomaly [6, 46]. This approach includes both parametric models, which as-
sume a known distribution for the data, and non-parametric models, which do not make
such assumptions. Additionally, similarity-based methods fall under this category, lever-
aging distance and density metrics to identify anomalies by measuring the closeness or
sparsity of data points relative to each other.
3.1.1
Parametric Models
Parametric models operate under the assumption that data originates from a known dis-
tribution, with the training phase focused on estimating the model parameters using sta-
tistical methods [47]. These models are advantageous for large datasets because their
complexity remains constant regardless of data size, enabling quick evaluations during
testing. However, their effectiveness hinges on the accurate assumption of the data dis-
tribution, which requires prior knowledge. A common choice is the Gaussian distribu-
tion, applicable to both univariate and multivariate continuous data, where training in-
volves estimating the mean and covariance through Maximum Likelihood Estimation [48].
Distribution-based methods, such as normal and Poisson distributions, detect anomalies
by checking for consistency [6]. Examples of parametric models include the multinomial
distribution for categorical data [5], the Markovian model for sequential data [5, 49], and
hybrid models that combine multiple distributions to better capture the statistical char-
acteristics of complex datasets [50]. Nevertheless, parametric models often encounter
difficulties in practical applications, especially when dealing with multidimensional data
or unknown distributions, making parameter estimation challenging in high-dimensional
spaces [6].
3.1.2
Non-Parametric Models
Non-parametric models stand in contrast to parametric models as they do not assume any
specific data distributions [47]. Instead, these models adapt their complexity to fit the size
and intricacy of the data. A common characteristic of non-parametric methods is that they
are often distance-based. During the training phase, data is reorganized through trans-
formations to better reveal structural information [5]. Examples of non-parametric models
include histogram analysis and kernel density estimators (KDE). Histogram analysis relies
on counting the frequency of occurrences to maintain normal behavior patterns, proving
efficient for univariate data but struggling as the number of feature dimensions increases
[51]. KDE, on the other hand, estimates the probability density function using multiple
kernels distributed across the data space, assessing density in localized neighborhoods
[5]. Another approach, Parzen window methods, employ Gaussian kernels placed on
each data point, summing their local contributions to estimate the overall density, using a
single shared variance hyper-parameter [52]. Depth-based methods consider each data
point as a position in n-dimensional space, with anomalies identified as points with lower
depth. This method avoids the distribution fitting problems of parametric methods but is
computationally complex and best suited for low-dimensional data [53]. Despite their flex-
ibility and autonomy, non-parametric models still require meaningful prior knowledge to
effectively learn distance-based similarities, and they can be applied to both categorical

3.1.3
Similarity-Based Methods
Based on Distance
A simple and widely used technique to determine anomalies in time series is to use a
similarity measure along with a brute force algorithm [54]. In this approach, subsequences
exhibiting high differences from other subsequences are considered anomalies based on
the similarity measurement. The selection of a suitable similarity measure significantly
impacts the performance of the method and depends directly on the application purpose
[55]. Anomaly detection based on distance calculates the distance between data points in
the data space using a predefined distance function [6]. An object is considered abnormal
if there is a large distance between it and other objects. Knorr et al. first proposed this
method, defining an object o as a DB(p, d) exception if the distance between o and at
least p objects is greater than d [56]. This concept was extended by Ramaswamy et al.,
who calculated and sorted the k-nearest neighbor distances for each object, considering
those with the largest distances as anomalies [57].
Several models exemplify distance-based anomaly detection methods. Dissim distance
can be used when time series are collected at different sampling rates, as it defines a time
series using a finite set [58]. Dynamic time warping distance (DTW) is effective for unequal
time series lengths and temporal shifts in data [59, 60]. Other similarity measures like
Longest Common Subsequence (LCSS) and Edit Distance on Real sequence (EDR) are
also widely reported. LCSS finds the longest subsequence common to both sequences
without reordering, while EDR measures the minimum number of operations required to
transform one sequence into another. Sparse coding combined with Latent Semantic
Analysis (LSA) offers another approach, where features are first extracted using sparse
coding, and relationships are learned through LSA. Anomaly scores are derived from
squared reconstruction errors [61]. A linear method for anomaly detection demonstrates
low time complexity and fewer parameters but only detects the top discords k and lacks
detailed guidance on setting k [62]. Additionally, there are other methods like index-based
algorithms, nested loop algorithms, and element-based algorithms [63].
Distance-based anomaly detection methods combine ideas from distribution-based meth-
ods, overcoming some of their primary disadvantages, making them easier to realize and
comprehend, and widely studied and used. However, they have their drawbacks. These
methods often have high algorithmic complexity and do not scale well with data size and
dimensionality, with time complexity reaching O(Mn2) for some algorithms [6]. Further-
more, they struggle with data sets exhibiting internal density differences, often misclas-
sifying sparse areas as anomalies or failing to detect anomalies in dense regions [64].
Additionally, these methods are sensitive to parameter settings p and d, requiring user
expertise, which limits their practical application [6].
Based on Density
Traditional distance-based methods for anomaly detection often rely on a global distance
criterion, which may not be appropriate as anomalies are usually detected from an indi-
vidual perspective, where an anomaly point is far from its neighbor cluster [6]. To address
this issue, Breunig and other scholars proposed density-based anomaly detection algo-
rithms, which compare the density of an object to its neighbors to identify anomalies [64].
The Local Outlier Factor (LOF) method, for example, introduces a measure that consid-
ers the degree of an object’s anomaly rather than a binary classification. The higher the
LOF value, the more likely the data point is to be abnormal [6]. Another model, the Local
Sparsity Coefficient (LSC), was proposed to reduce computational complexity by using
the largest distance between the nearest k objects as the k-distance [65]. Additionally,

the Multi-Granularity Deviation Factor (MDEF) compares the number of data objects in
the r-neighborhood and their mean values to measure abnormality. This method avoids

Density-based methods align more closely with Hawkins’ definition of exceptions, as they
can detect local anomalies and reduce detection errors in datasets with varied distributions

methods also face challenges, including high time complexity and sensitivity to parameter
selection, such as the outlier factor threshold. Determining these parameters accurately
can be difficult, which limits the practical application of density-based anomaly detection

3.2
Clustering-Based Methods
Applying clustering methods to multivariate time series is another effective approach for
anomaly detection [7]. This method involves clustering data directly or indirectly using
existing clustering algorithms such as DBSCAN, ROCK, and K-Means [68]. In this context,
anomalies are defined as data points that do not fit into any cluster or are exceptions within
a class. While clustering analysis primarily aims to categorize data, anomaly detection
is considered a byproduct of this process, identifying abnormal data points that do not
conform to the identified clusters [6].
Several models exemplify clustering-based anomaly detection techniques. Typically, the
process involves two main phases [69]. In the first phase, a clustering method such as
K-Means [70] or Fuzzy C-Means (FCM) [71] is applied to generate subsequences of mul-
tivariate time series. Other clustering techniques can also be used [72, 73]. In the second
phase, an anomaly score is determined based on the fitness of subsequences to the
clusters, often using the distance between subsequences and cluster centers [54]. For
example, Fuzzy C-Means clustering can be applied to multivariate time series, where a
sliding window generates a set of multivariate subsequences, and an extended FCM re-
veals the structure within these subsequences. A reconstruction criterion is then used
to quantify the level of anomalies detected, employing Particle Swarm Optimization for
optimization [7].
Other innovative approaches include computing the weighted Euclidean distance between
observations and using an improved ant colony method for clustering [74, 75], and a novel
clustering-based compression method that reduces time complexity by using the K-Means
algorithm to convert a correlation matrix of multivariate time series to multiple clusters, es-
timating anomaly scores based on multivariate normal distributions [76]. Additionally, a
Bounded Coordinate System (BCS) approach evaluates the similarity between two multi-
variate time series, employing a modified K-Means algorithm to cluster the data and using
a two-pruning rule-based nested loop algorithm to detect the top k outlier observations
[77].
The primary advantage of clustering-based models is that they do not require prior knowl-
edge of the data. However, their performance heavily depends on the revealed cluster-
ing centers, and they often suffer from high time and space complexity. If the inherent
structure within the multivariate time series is not adequately captured, the accuracy of
anomaly detection can deteriorate [7]. Furthermore, general clustering algorithms typ-
ically lack specific optimization for anomaly detection, leading to lower efficiency. The
implicit nature of anomaly definitions and testing standards within the clustering process
further complicates the detection accuracy [6].

3.3
Classification-Based Methods
In classification-based methods, instances or subsequences of multivariate time series
are categorized into two classes: normal and abnormal [7]. The classifier is trained using
a dataset composed of normal instances, and once trained, it assigns an anomaly score
to each instance in the testing set [46]. These methods leverage supervised learning
techniques to identify anomalies based on the patterns learned from the training data.
Several models exemplify classification-based anomaly detection techniques. In one ap-
proach, the authors first use a linear regression model to determine if a data point is
abnormal and then apply a Bayesian maximum likelihood classifier based on the identi-
fied anomalies. For the testing dataset, the classifier labels each test data point. This
method also incorporates meta-features such as kurtosis, variation, oscillation, regular-
ity, square, and trend to describe the dynamics of subsequences and employs a support
vector machine to detect anomalies [78, 79]. The classic k-means clustering algorithm is
also representative in this category, working by choosing k random initial cluster centers,
computing distances between these centers and each point in the training set, and iden-
tifying the points closest to each cluster center. Variations of k-means, including fuzzy
c-means and probabilistic c-means, have been developed to enhance its performance
[80, 81, 82]. Other classification-based models include Self-Organizing Maps (SOM),
Expectation-Maximization, Find Out, CLAD, and CBLOF [83].
The primary advantage of classification-based models is their relatively higher detection
accuracy compared to unsupervised detectors in most cases. However, a significant chal-
lenge for these methods is the time-consuming process of collecting and labeling training
data required to build the classifiers [7]. Despite this, classification-based methods remain
a robust approach for anomaly detection, particularly when high accuracy is crucial.
3.4
Transformation-Based Methods
Another option for dealing with multivariate time series anomaly detection is to reduce the
multivariate time series to univariate time series, allowing the use of univariate anomaly
detection techniques to identify anomalies [7]. This approach simplifies the complexity
of the data and leverages the extensive research and established methods available for
univariate time series.
Several models exemplify transformation-based anomaly detection techniques. Time se-
ries projection and independent component analysis (ICA) are two common methods used
to reduce the dimensionality of multivariate time series, thereby speeding up computa-
tion. However, this reduction in dimensionality may lead to a loss of information, poten-
tially reducing the accuracy of anomaly detection [84, 85]. Another method involves a
novelty dissimilarity measurement for comparing multivariate time series data using prin-
cipal component analysis (PCA). This method calculates the dissimilarity by combining
distance (Euclidean or Mahalanobis distances), rotation, and variance components. To
incorporate potential domain knowledge and improve flexibility, the method allows users
to assign different weights to these components [86].
The effectiveness of transformation-based methods largely depends on how well they
retain useful information during the transformation process. While these methods can
simplify the analysis and enhance computational efficiency, there is always a risk of losing
critical information that could impact the accuracy of anomaly detection [7]. Therefore, it
is crucial to balance the dimensionality reduction with the retention of significant data
features to ensure the reliability of the detection results.

3.5
Modeling-Based Methods (Machine Learning-Based)
Modeling-Based or Machine Learning is another prominent method for anomaly detection
reported in the literature [7]. These methods leverage advanced machine learning tech-
niques to model the intricate relationships and temporal dependencies in the data. These
methods are highly flexible and can adapt to various types of anomalies, making them
suitable for complex datasets. They use algorithms that can learn from the data, making
them effective at identifying subtle and complex patterns that might indicate anomalies.
These methods often combine different techniques, such as statistical methods, neural
networks, and graphical models, to improve accuracy and robustness.
Several models exemplify machine learning-based anomaly detection techniques. One
approach involves using a Hidden Markov Model (HMM) to detect anomalies in multi-
variate time series [42]. Additionally, combining HMM with various transformation ap-
proaches, such as PCA, FCM, Sugeno Integral, and Choquet Integral, has been proposed
to enhance anomaly detection. This method first retains useful information through trans-
formations and then uses HMM to model temporal relationships. However, this approach
may struggle with data that have more complex structures due to its assumptions about
state sets [87].
Another innovative proposed model involves learning graphical models of Granger causal-
ity and using Kullback–Leibler (KL) divergence to compute anomaly scores. This method
employs a multi-scale convolutional recurrent encoder–decoder to capture temporal pat-
terns [88, 89]. Cheng et al. proposed using a weighted graph representation to model
multivariate time series, with the RBF function determining similarity between time series
pairs. In this graph, nodes represent subsequences or observations, and edges denote
similarity. Anomalies are detected based on the connectivity measure of nodes derived
from a random walk algorithm [85, 90].
Neural network models, known for their robustness and high accuracy, are extensively
used in applications such as forecasting medical time-series data and stock market prices
[91, 92]. The Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP) can learn complex time dependencies and
correlations within the data, while Recurrent Neural Networks (RNNs), including Long
Short Term Memory (LSTM) networks, are particularly effective for time-series forecast-
ing due to their ability to retain memory states over time. LSTM models have demon-
strated exceptional performance in forecasting time-series data with long-term depen-
dencies [93, 94]. Besides SVM-based classification models, other widely adopted models
include Bayesian networks and decision trees [95].
Modeling-based methods can be further classified into supervised, unsupervised, and
semi-supervised approaches. Supervised methods require labeled data to train a model
to detect outliers, using techniques such as neural networks or logistic regression. Unsu-
pervised methods, which do not require labeled data, often involve clustering or autoen-
coders to identify anomalies. Semi-supervised methods use a small amount of labeled
data along with a large amount of unlabeled data to train models, such as semi-supervised
SVMs [96]. The choice of method depends on the availability of labeled data and the spe-
cific requirements of the application.
Additionally, these methods can be further categorized based on their approach to anomaly
detection: forecasting-based or reconstruction-based.
Forecasting-based models are
trained to predict the next time stamp in a series, identifying anomalies as deviations
from these predictions. Reconstruction-based models, on the other hand, aim to capture
the embedding of time series data and identify anomalies by the differences between the
original and reconstructed data [8]. Each approach has its advantages and challenges,

drawback, necessitating substantial computational power and expertise, which can limit

Real NVP
The Linear Real Non-Volume Preserving (RNVP) model is a sophisticated framework de-
signed to transform input data through a series of invertible transformations, allowing for
the effective modeling of complex data distributions. This model combines several key
components, including linear coupling layers, permutation layers, and batch normalization
steps, each playing a crucial role in maintaining the model’s expressiveness and invert-
ibility. This model was developed in [108], inspired by the works [29] and [31].
4.4.1

two parts based on a binary mask. One part of the data is transformed using a neural

unchanged. This design ensures invertibility, as the inverse transformation can be easily
computed by reversing the scaling and translation. By stacking a sequence of these
simple bijections, the model achieves a flexible and tractable bijective function.

to invert, depending on the remainder of the input vector in a complex way. Given a D-

4.5
Outlier Prediction Methodologies
4.5.1
Methodology 1: Based in Mahalanobis Distance
In the context of outlier detection in a multidimensional space, the Mahalanobis distance is
a reliable measure for determining how far a data point deviates from a given distribution
[109]. This method uses the properties of the chi-square distribution to create a framework
for hypothesis testing. The null hypothesis H0 posits that a data point is not an outlier,
implying that its Mahalanobis distance adheres to a chi-square distribution with degrees
of freedom corresponding to the number of independent variables. Conversely, the alter-
native hypothesis H1 asserts that the data point is an outlier, indicated by a Mahalanobis
distance inconsistent with the chi-square distribution.
To put this framework into practice, the dataset’s mean vector and covariance matrix are
first calculated, which capture the central tendency and spread of the multivariate data,
respectively. The Mahalanobis distance for each data point is then calculated using the
following formula:
D2 = (x −m)T · C−1 · (x −m)
where x represents the observation vector, m is the mean vector, and C is the covariance
matrix.
The resulting Mahalanobis distance is then compared against the chi-square distribution
to derive a p-value. This p-value signifies the probability that a chi-square distributed
variable with k degrees of freedom would exceed the observed Mahalanobis distance.
A predefined threshold (initially 0.05) is used to determine statistical significance; if the
p-value falls below this threshold, the null hypothesis is rejected, and the data point is
classified as an outlier.

p-value is less than the threshold then the H0 was rejected and conclude that the data
point is an outlier. If the combined p-value is greater than or equal to the threshold then
the H0 can’t be rejected and it is conclude that the data point is not an outlier.
In simpler terms, the null hypothesis H0 states that the distributions of the attributes in the
test dataset are the same as those in the sample dataset. A low p-value indicates that it is
unlikely the observed values in test dataset come from the same distribution as those in
sample dataset, suggesting a significant difference. Conversely, a high p-value suggests
that the observed values could plausibly come from the same distribution and it is not an
anomaly, supporting the null hypothesis.
Additionally, the sample dataset is transformed using already trained Real NVP through
backpropagation to map it into an embedding space. Then the same KDE and p-value
calculation procedure is used in this embedding space. The test hypotheses in the em-
bedding space are similar: H0 posits that the transformed attributes in test dataset follow
the same distribution as those in sample dataset, suggesting no significant difference in
the embedding space. H1 suggests that the transformed attributes in the test dataset do
not follow the same distribution, indicating a significant difference. Again, a predefined
threshold is used to categorize each record. If the combined p-value in the embedding
space is less than the threshold the H0 is rejected and it is conclude that the data point
is an outlier in the embedding space. If the combined p-value is greater than or equal to
the threshold H0 is not rejected and therefore it can be conclude that the data point is not
an outlier in the embedding space.
In this methodology, some assumptions were taken. First, it was assumed that the ob-
servations within each attribute are independent of one another, which is essential for the
accuracy of p-value calculations. The reference dataset in the normal space is assumed
to follow a normal distribution, ensuring that the generated KDE accurately reflects the
true underlying distribution. When combining p-values using Fisher’s method, it was as-
sumed that the individual p-values are independent and uniformly distributed under the
null hypothesis. This independence is crucial because correlated attributes could vio-
late this assumption, leading to misleading results. Additionally, the test statistic used in
Fisher’s method was assumed to follow a chi-squared distribution with degrees of freedom
proportional to the number of combined p-values.

Outlier Prediction Methodology 1: Mahalanobis Distance Test
In this analysis, the efficacy of the first outlier detection methodology using the Maha-
lanobis distance within a multidimensional dataset is explored. First, the mean vector
and covariance matrix is calculated using the training set. Next, the Mahalanobis dis-
tance for each data point is calculated. Following this, the p-value for each data point
is computed using the chi-square distribution with k degrees of freedom, where k is 20
in this first case. The test hypotheses are defined as follows: H0 (the null hypothesis)
posits that the Mahalanobis distance D2 of the data point follows a chi-square distribution
with k degrees of freedom, suggesting the data point is not an outlier; H1 (the alternative
hypothesis) suggests that D2 does not follow a chi-square distribution with k degrees of
freedom, indicating the data point is an outlier. Finally, based on a predefined threshold
(e.g., 0.05), each record was categorized as ‘normal’ or ‘outlier’. If the p-value is less than
the threshold the H0 is rejected and it is conclude that the data point is an outlier. If the
p-value is greater than or equal to the threshold, H0 can’t be rejected and it is conclude
that the data point is not an outlier.

Outlier Prediction Methodology 1: Mahalanobis Distance Test
In this analysis, the efficacy of the first outlier detection methodology using the Maha-
lanobis distance within a multidimensional dataset is explored. First, the mean vector
and covariance matrix are calculated using the training set. Next, the Mahalanobis dis-
tance for each data point is computed. Following this, the p-value for each data point is
determined using the chi-square distribution with k degrees of freedom, where k is 50 in
this case. The test hypotheses are defined as follows: H0 (the null hypothesis) posits
that the Mahalanobis distance D2 of the data point follows a chi-square distribution with
k degrees of freedom, suggesting the data point is not an outlier; H1 (the alternative hy-
pothesis) suggests that D2 does not follow a chi-square distribution with k degrees of
freedom, indicating the data point is an outlier. Finally, based on a predefined threshold
(e.g., 0.05), each record is categorized as ‘normal’ or ‘outlier’. If the p-value is less than
the threshold, H0 is rejected, and it is concluded that the data point is an outlier. If the
p-value is greater than or equal to the threshold, H0 is not rejected, and it is concluded
that the data point is not an outlier.

Outlier Prediction Methodology 2: Density Test
The second outlier prediction methodology is applied in both the normal space and the
embedding space. In this approach, a normal sample is generated in the normal space,
and its density is compared against the test set in the normal space. Additionally, this
normal sample is passed through the inverse transformation of the real NVP model to
obtain the sample in the embedding space. The density of this transformed sample is
then compared against the test set in the embedding space. In both spaces, to compare
the distributions, the probability density functions (PDFs) was estimated for each attribute
in the reference dataset using Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) and get and individual
p-value for each dimension. Then all the p-values from each dimension were combined
into a single one using Fisher’s aggregation method. Based on a predefined threshold
(e.g., 0.05), each record was categorized as ‘normal’ or ‘outlier’. If the combined p-value
was less than the threshold,then the H0 was rejected and it was concluded that the data
point is an outlier. If the combined p-value was greater than or equal to the threshold,
then H0 was not rejected and it was concluded that the data point is not an outlier.

Outlier Prediction Methodology 1: Mahalanobis Distance Test
In this analysis, the efficacy of the first outlier detection methodology using the Maha-
lanobis distance within a multidimensional dataset is explored. First, the mean vector
and covariance matrix are calculated using the training set. Next, the Mahalanobis dis-
tance for each data point is computed. Following this, the p-value for each data point is
determined using the chi-square distribution with k degrees of freedom, where k is 20 in
this case. The test hypotheses are defined as follows: H0 (the null hypothesis) posits
that the Mahalanobis distance D2 of the data point follows a chi-square distribution with
k degrees of freedom, suggesting the data point is not an outlier; H1 (the alternative hy-
pothesis) suggests that D2 does not follow a chi-square distribution with k degrees of
freedom, indicating the data point is an outlier. Finally, based on a predefined threshold
(e.g., 0.05), each record is categorized as ‘normal’ or ‘outlier’. If the p-value is less than
the threshold, H0 is rejected, and it is concluded that the data point is an outlier. If the
p-value is greater than or equal to the threshold, H0 is not rejected, and it is concluded
that the data point is not an outlier.

more reliable method for large datasets.

of outliers, especially in large datasets.

Outlier Prediction Methodology 2: Density Test
The second outlier prediction methodology is applied in both the normal space and the
embedding space. In this approach, a normal sample is generated in the normal space,
and its density is compared against the test set in the normal space. Additionally, this
normal sample is passed through the inverse transformation of the real NVP model to
obtain the sample in the embedding space. The density of this transformed sample is
then compared against the test set in the embedding space. To compare the distributions
in both spaces, the probability density functions (PDFs) of each attribute in the reference
dataset are first estimated using Kernel Density Estimation (KDE), and an individual p-
value is obtained for each dimension. Then, all the p-values from each dimension are
combined into a single p-value using Fisher’s aggregation method. Based on the adjusted
threshold (alpha / number of samples), each record is categorized as ‘normal’ or ‘outlier’.
If the combined p-value is less than the threshold, H0 is rejected, and it is concluded
that the data point is an outlier. If the combined p-value is greater than or equal to the
threshold, H0 is not rejected, and it is concluded that the data point is not an outlier.
In the normal space (Figure-5.43), the PCA plots show a clear distribution of the datasets
(training, validation, and test) and the identified outliers. The jitter plot of p-values for
the normal space indicates a large number of data points with p-values below the ad-
justed threshold, identifying them as outliers. The PCA plots, color-coded by dataset and
prediction labels, reveal that the majority of the data points align closely with the gener-
ated normal sample, with outliers dispersed throughout. The density-based methodology
distinguishes between normal points and outliers, highlighting areas where the test set
deviates significantly from the normal distribution.

Discussion
Train Split:
In the TDC case study, three datasets were analyzed under the assumption that there
were no temporal changes and that the modems behaved consistently over time. While
this assumption seemed reasonable initially due to the minimal climate variation during
the analyzed months, practical observations revealed instances where it did not hold true.
Missing data was discovered at the beginning and end of the dataset. This emphasizes
the need for data cleaning in future studies, as the model’s output is directly proportional
to the quality of the input data.
Hyperparameters Fine Tunning:
Regarding the hyperparameter fine-tuning, a heuristic approach was employed, assuming
that the hyperparameters were independent of each other. This assumption, however, is
not entirely accurate as different combinations could result in better performance. On
the other hand, the embedding size was selected through brute force, trying different
approaches and retaining the one with the minimum mean squared error (MSE) and the
best visual performance. This strategy fails to take into account for potential data loss.
Future research should include a sensitivity analysis where a set of hyperparameters is
selected based on combined loss metrics, ensuring a more robust model.
Outlier Classification Methodology:
The second outlier classification methodology was based on certain assumptions about
data independence: 1. It was assumed that observations within each attribute are inde-
pendent, which is crucial for accurate p-value calculations. However, due to the nature
of the window creation function, where windows overlap based on the selected stride,
independence between records is not guaranteed. 2. Fisher’s approach assumes inde-
pendent and evenly distributed p-values under the null hypothesis. This assumption is
broken because to the overlapping of timestamps caused by the convolutional layers in
the encoder/decoder operations, which results in a lack of total independence between
dimensions.
Correaltion Between Modems:
The correlation between modems was not examined in this study. Investigating modems
correlations could provide deeper insights into the network’s behaviour and improve the
model’s ability to detect anomalies by considering inter-device relationships.
Comparison with Other Models:
Benchmarking and comparing our results with other models proved challenging because
other studies primarily focus on point anomalies, whereas our study concentrated on con-
text anomalies. This discrepancy in focus made direct comparisons difficult.

Conclusion
The results of this work demonstrate, on different datasets, the effectiveness and con-
straints of the real NVP model. For the MNIST dataset, it was observed distinct cluster
formations in the plot after applying the real NVP model, indicating its potential for anomaly
identification in image datasets. The performance metrics support this conclusion, with
an F1 score of 91.66%, an accuracy of 91.26%, a precision of 90.00%, and a recall of
93.33%, reflecting the model’s robust performance.
In contrast, the results for the GHL dataset were not as good as. Both prediction method-
ologies implemented after applying the model yielded suboptimal results, with an F1 score
of 33.93% for the Mahalanobis distance-based methodology and 48.15% for the density-
based approach. Despite these low scores, the PCA plots in the normal space showed
some cluster formations that differentiated from the normal points, suggesting that while
the real NVP model functioned correctly, the classification methodologies for outliers need
improvement or a change of direction in this subject.
For the TDC Net dataset, benchmarking the results was challenging due to the lack of
labeled data and unknown outlier characteristics. However, the plots indicated cluster
formations, and implementing the two classification methodologies allowed for the identifi-
cation of potential outliers. Reviewing these outliers in real space revealed some anomaly
behaviour, so it is recommended for some expert validation or comparison with TDC Net
trouble tickets to verify the anomalies. Future steps could include: 1. Checking the per-
centage of anomalies detected by the model that led to trouble tickets created by cus-
tomers. 2. Checking the percentage predicted of all network-related tickets. Additionally,
it was stated that the model in question exhibited a potential high recall, low precision,
and moderate f1 score and accuracy.
Another significant observation from the experimentation chapter is that the encoder by
itself was insufficient to identify outliers, as evidenced by the histograms and PCA com-
parisons. Consequently, the RNVP was required to accomplish this task.
In summary, the real NVP model appears to be a promising candidate for bridging the
gap in outlier detection in multivariate spaces where context is also a critical factor. This
is supported by its capacity to generate outputs that visually illustrate outlier behavior and
cluster formations. The model’s flexibility is another strength, successfully handling simple
2D distributions, complex multivariate distributions, images, 1D time series, and multivari-
ate time series. Additionally, it trains quickly, with the TDC dataset training completed in
less than a day, and it is easy to apply and understand.
Despite these strengths, there is still room for enhancement. For the TDC case, where
the model was trained with outliers, continuous learning could improve results. This could
involve classifying some outliers, removing them from the training set, and retraining the
model with this cleaned set. Another potential improvement is training the autoencoder
and real NVP together with a single loss computation. Additionally, the final method-
ologies for classifying outliers should be explored further, as current approaches were
inconclusive, and none of them are advised. For instance, one potential edge to investi-
gate is the utilization of the Normal Flow densities produced by the model during training
to examine the embedding space.
Additionally, a focused study aimed at achieving similar or better results with a smaller
