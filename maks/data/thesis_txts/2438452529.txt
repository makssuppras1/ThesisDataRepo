2 Abstract 
This work investigates the catalytic conversion of CO and H2 to C2-oxygenates over supported 
single metallic Rh catalysts. The C2-oxygenates can be reacted into ethanol, which can be used as a 
fuel additive and/or energy storage. Catalysts were prepared via wet impregnation of a Rh(NO3)3 or 
RhCl3 precursor and subsequent dispersion on SiO2 or ZrO2 as support material.  
The catalytic performance, CO conversion and product distribution was investigated on a plug flow 
reactor setup. The catalyst samples were treated in a flow of CO and H2 (ratio 1:2.4), for a pressure 
and temperature range of 2 to 30 bar and 200 to 250 ℃. The impact on product distribution and 
conversion of temperature, pressure and mass to flow ratio was analyzed. For a 1 wt.% Rh/ZrO2 
catalyst (from Rh(NO3)3) the highest obtained C2-oxygenate selectivity of 45% with a CO 
conversion of 4.4% was found at 225 ℃ and 25 bar. For a 1 wt.% Rh/SiO2 catalyst (from 
Rh(NO3)3) the highest obtained C2-oxygenate selectivity of 56% with a CO conversion of 0.4% was 
found at 250 ℃ and 30 bar. Increases in pressure decreased the selectivity of hydrocarbons and 
increased the selectivity of C1-oxygenates and esters products. Increasing the temperature increased 
the CO conversion and methane selectivity. Decreasing the mass to flow ratio decreases the CO 
conversion and shifts the ethanol/acetaldehyde ratio towards acetaldehyde. 
For a Rh catalyst treated in CO-H2 at 50 bar pressure and 275 ℃ 3 Rh-Cl/SiO2 a rapid deactivation 
of the catalyst was observed and grey precipitate was found at the reactor outlet. No Rh was found 
with EDX/EDS in the catalyst sample and the grey precipitate contained Rh. This observation is 
proposed to be related to the formation of volatile Rh carbonyl species, which was only observed 
when the catalyst was prepared an RhCl3 precursor, ascribing a significant role to the chloride ions.  
After a knowledge base had been established the reaction intermediates was investigated. 
To unravel which species were reaction intermediates in the formation of C2-oxygenates, the 
catalyst spent in the catalytic treatment of CO and H2 was subjected to a TPH. These patterns are 
compared to other TPH patterns from model systems of pre-adsorbed chemicals on the same type of 
catalyst. Acetaldehyde was indicated to be located at the surface of the spent samples. It was 
indicated that acetate could not be inserted onto the catalyst surface, but had to be formed on it.  
Co-feeding of water or methanol to the CO-H2 reaction atmosphere was investigated to see if it has 
a beneficial influence of the formation of C2-oxygenates by altering the electronic state of Rh. An 
initial liberation effect of surface species was observed for water, but there was no permanent 
change in product distribution and the CO conversion decreased. When methanol was co-fed the 
same initial liberation effect was observed, but the intensity of proposed C2-oxygenate fragments 
had a higher intensity during co-feeding of MeOH, indicating larger formation of C2-oxygenates.

Project description: 
When producing from renewable energy sources, there is a fluctuation of weather conditions. 
Therefore, the supply and demand of energy do not always match, which can be problematic for 
storing energy in conventional technology such as batteries. A solution could be to convert 
electricity into chemicals by H2O-electrolysis. Hydrogen, however, has a low volumetric energy 
density (and is therefore also difficult to store). Producing liquid chemicals such as alcohols from 
hydrogen and CO may be a more advantageous concept. 
Production of methanol is undesirable, as its miscibility with gasoline is sensitive to traces of water. 
If water is present, then 2 phases are created which can cause problems for engines. On the other 
hand, ethanol has a higher energy density, and is miscible in gasoline even if water is present. 
Producing ethanol would be advantageous as it has high energy density, high miscibility in fuel, and 
is not toxic. However, the challenge is that ethanol is difficult to produce from syngas, since the 
reaction mechanism must involve both breakage of the reactant C-O bond (like methane formation) 
and retention of the C-O bond (like methanol formation) in a perfect synchronization without 
leading to undesired byproducts.  C2-oxygenates (molecules containing 2 carbons and at least 1 
oxygen atom) can be converted to ethanol but are difficult to produce from syngas. It is possible 
however with a catalyst based on the rare and expensive metal rhodium, which has a decent 
selectively. The work at DTU-Chemical-Engineering is to investigate the mechanics of the 
rhodium-catalyst produced C2-oxygenates and to obtain a higher yield. 
The current research indicates that the yield relies on having a combination of metallic and oxidized 
rhodium sites, which depends on the particle size, choice of support, oxidative potential (such as 
CO/CO2 ratio), temperature and pressure. They all determine the distribution between the two types 
of sites. An optimal ratio leads to a higher yield. 
Therefore, the goal of the project is to obtain higher yields of the reaction, by variation of the catalyst 
preparation methods, support choice and optimization of operating condition. This may be

8 Literature study  
8.1 Introduction 
High energy densities and good storage properties of fossil fuels have made them a very important 
energy source in our society for decades. There is, however, an increasing concern for global 
climate change in addition to the depletion of the fossil fuel resources[1], both of which push the 
attention to alternative energy means and optimization of sustainable energy[2]. Renewable energy 
sources, e.g. wind and solar energy, however, have the disadvantage of fluctuating input into the 
energy grid. Depending on the demand and weather fluctuation occurring over the day, there may 
either be surplus energy or the need for energy from other, conventional energy sources.  Figure 1 
demonstrates the fluctuations of the input from renewable wind and solar energy in Germany:
 
Figure 1 Fluctuations in renewable energy (Germany) for the period of 25.06.18 to 01.07.18. [3] 
The surplus of produced energy needs to be stored. Conventional batteries are not optimal for 
storing massive quantities of energy, as Lithium-ion and lead-acid batteries have an energy density 
of less than 2 MJ/L[4]. 
The energy can be used to produce hydrogen by H2O-electrolysis[5], but has an energy density of 
0.01 MJ/L[6] (gas phase) which makes it difficult to store. Therefore, there is interest in producing 
liquid chemicals with higher energy densities such as alcohols, which can be used as fuel additives.  
Alcohols can be produced from a syngas consisting of CO, CO2, and H2. A renewable syngas can be 
obtained by gasification. Gasification is the formation of syngas from a carbonaceous material such 
as biomass[7,8] reacting with a gasifying agent like H2O[9]. The obtained composition of CO/CO2/H2 
syngas can be altered within the ratio of single components by utilizing a water-gas shift catalyst 
consisting of Fe2O3, Cr2O3 and MgO[10]. If additional H2 is needed, it can be obtained by H2O-
electrolysis.

Methanol can be produced by converting CO2 and H2 at 50 bar on a Cu-ZnO catalyst, supported by 
either Al2O3 or Cr2O3[11]. There is a production plant in Grindavik on Iceland, which produces 10 
tons methanol a day. This demonstrates the feasibility and interest in the concept of utilizing CO2 
and converting it with H2 to methanol[12]. For methanol as a fuel additive an undesirable phase 
separation in the alcohol-gasoline mixture can occur if the temperature is too low or in the presence 
of small amounts of water[13]. 
The advantage of ethanol is that it improves the miscibility of alcohol-gasoline mixtures[13] and that 
the energy density of ethanol is 21 MJ/L[6], compared to 16 MJ/L for methanol[6].  
A syngas with CO and H2 can be used to produce C2-oxygenates by utilizing a supported, single 
metallic rhodium (Rh) catalyst[14]. C2-oxygenates consist of a two-carbon skeleton and an oxygen-
containing functional group; e.g. acetaldehyde and ethanol. Other C2-oxygenate based products are 
esters, which are formed from a C2-oxygenate compound (e.g. methyl and ethyl acetate). The 
different C2-oxygenate products can be converted to ethanol as desirable product with a catalyst 
consisting of Cu and Fe and can then be used as fuel additive or for energy storage.  
The process of producing C2-oxygenates from syngas has been investigated extensively[15], with 
varying degrees of interest in this topic over the past decades [14,16,17]. Besides the single metallic Rh 
catalyst which is the focus of this work, there are three more catalyst families that produce higher 
alcohols. Among these are modified Fischer Tropsch synthesis (m-FTS) catalysts, Mo-based 
catalysts and modified methanol synthesis catalysts (m-MS).

For several types of catalysts, either high CO conversion or high selectivity of C2-oxygenates is 
achieved. A desirable catalyst would produce favorable C2-oxygenate products with a high 
selectivity and a high CO conversion at an acceptable loading of precious metals. While some of the 
other catalyst systems produce C2-oxygenates+ with a wide range of products, Rh based catalysts 
have the highest selectivity of C2-oxygenate and have a very narrow product distribution, forming 
nearly exclusively C2-oxygenate among the desired C2-oxygenates+. Besides that, the single-metal 
composition assists in reducing the complexity of the system to a minimum amount, which makes it 
a favorable system for mechanistic studies, that aims to further unravel the reaction mechanism. 
A disadvantage of utilizing Rh is that is a rare and expensive metal[18]. Furthermore, the process has 
low CO conversion and/or a low selectivity of C2-oxygenates. Therefore, the process might not be 
commercially feasible at the current stage. 
The goal of this master thesis is to investigate and aim to understand the reaction steps, to enable 
the process to be improved in the future, by improving the conversion and selectivity towards C2-
oxygenates. The thesis is a part of a PhD project by Max Schumann. Principal supervisor is 
Associate Professor Jakob Munkholt Christensen from the Department of Chemical and 
Biochemical Engineering (KT) at Technical University of Denmark (DTU).

8.2 Motivation for Rh as a catalyst 
In the pursuit of achieving a satisfying selectivity and conversion, several types of catalysts have 
been investigated such as Mo-based, Rh-based, modified Fischer-Tropsch synthesis catalyst and 
modified methanol synthesis catalyst[15]. 
A Rh catalyst is known to produce oxygen-containing hydrocarbons[14,19]. Furthermore, among the 
group VIII metals, Rh is unique in its ability to form C2-oxygenates with a high selectivity from CO 
and H2. Rh can both break and retain C-O bonds, which is important in the formation of C2-
oxygenates from CO, which presumably is an intermediate for C2-oxygenates. Ren[20] suggested 
that the dissociation of CO occurred on steps or edges for Rh. 
Supported Rh is also the most selective single metal catalyst in terms of C2-oxygenates[15]. The 
advantages of Rh compared to other catalysts is that Rh has a narrow product distribution. Due to 
this, and its single metal composition, it is also a good model catalyst simplifying the research into 
the topic. The main disadvantage of using Rh is that the availability is sparse[18], which makes it an 
expensive catalyst to use for commercial applications. 
The initial research on conversion of syngas to C2-oxygenates by a Rh catalyst was done in 1978, 
where pyrolyzed Rh carbonyl clusters were dispersed on several catalysts such as TiO2 and ZrO2[14]. 
The results showed that both the selectivity and conversion varied significantly depending on which 
type of support material was utilized. 
In the initial research it was found that activity of a Rh catalyst for conversion of syngas to C2-
oxygenates catalyst was sensitive to the reaction conditions, support material and the presence of 
promoters[14,19], as the research provided wide spread of reported catalytic data in CO conversion 
level and product selectivity.

8.4 Impact of reaction conditions 
The initial research on reaction conditions for a Rh-based catalyst was published by Bhasin in 
1978[19]. The influence of the temperature and partial pressure of CO and H2 were analyzed on a Rh 
(2.5 wt.%) catalyst on a SiO2 support.  
Increasing the temperature increases the rate of formation of all compounds, the selectivity of 
methane increases substantially. This indicates that methane has the highest activation energy of the 
compounds. The overall conversion of CO to C2-oxygenates is low. Therefore, the temperature can 
be increased to obtain an acceptable conversion but increasing the temperature will reduce the 
selectivity of C2-oxygenates. 
The influence of the of CO and H2 gas feed partial pressure were analyzed by Bhasin. Increasing the 
partial pressure of CO decreases the selectivity and rate of EtOH and methane, while an increase in 
partial pressure of H2 increases rate of all compounds, but the selectivity of especially methane. The 
ratio of CO/H2 is therefore a factor that can be used to intentionally tune the conversion and alter 
the selectivity[19].  
In the initial research it was apparent that activity of Rh to C2-oxygenates producing catalyst was 
sensitive to the reaction conditions and support material.

disperses into isolated sites and forms rhodium gem-dicarbonyl species Rh(CO)2. The Rh, which 
does not disperse, sits in a metallic cluster form of linear Rh(CO). To analyze this DRIFTS 
experiments were performed to understand the behavior of the ratio between Rh(CO)2 and Rh(CO). 
The intensity of the peaks was used to quantify the ratio, and the experimental results are shown in

8.9 Precursor 
The impact of the precursors Rh(NO3)3 and RhCl3 was investigated by X-ray adsorption 
spectroscopy (XAS). This is an analytical technique to determine electronic and geometric structure 
of a substance. The behavior of 1wt. % Rh from both precursors supported on Al2O3 was analyzed 
while treated at 1 bar pressure in pure H2 and pure CO[28]. Both catalysts, prepared from a nitrate 
and chloride precursor, were similar in size and had no significant difference in the catalyst when 
treated in H2. When the Rh(NO3)3 catalyst was treated with CO the Rh had other Rh atom 
neighbors, but the the Rh from the RhCl3 sample did not. This indicates that Rh is mobilized when 
originating from a RhCl3 compared to Rh(NO3)3 in a CO atmosphere. 
The indication that the precursor of Rh had an impact was mentioned by Ichikawa[14], as the results 
for CO conversion and C2-oxygenate selectivity were lower for RhCl3 on La2O3 than for Rh4CO12 
on La2O3.    
The catalyst from both precursors was treated in a catalytic flow of CO-H2 with a ratio of 1:1 at 250 
℃ bar and 5 bar pressure for a period of 200 hours in an article by Johnston[28]. The Rh(NO3)3 
selectivity for HC and conversion were approximately constant with time, but for RhCl3 the 
selectivity for HC declines rapidly the first 20-40 hours before reaching steady state. This may 
indicate that the Cl enables redispersion of the Rh particles during the CO hydrogenation.

8.10 Metallic and oxidized rhodium sites  
The impact on the oxidation state of Rh was investigated by Watson[29] by using a LaRhO3 catalyst 
that was treated under catalytic conditions. Rh gets incorporated into the La2RhO3 lattice and is

formation of C2-oxygenates.

investigated, which were pre-treated to become reduced (Clean, metallic Rh), preoxidised Rh or

8.11 Further investigation into reaction mechanism  
The reaction mechanism of AcH and EtOH formation was investigated by Orita[32] with DRIFTS. 
The results of the DRIFTS experiments suggested that adsorbed CH3COO- ions might be involved 
in the formation of AcH, and this was further investigated by isotopic tracer experiments. 
The active sites for formation of AcH were investigated by a catalytic reaction of CO-H2 (1:2 ratio) 
on a reduced RhCl3 catalyst supported on SiO2 at 150 ℃ and 0.22 bar. The CO had a purity of 99% 
for C18O. The AcH formed was 50% CH3CHO and 50% CH3CH18O, which indicates that the 
oxygen in AcH does not have to originate from the CO. The reaction intermediate which forms 
AcH via CO insertion onto a single carbon, could have a structure like CH3COO- where one oxygen 
is supplied by the support and the other from the CO. While the study did not find any CH3COO- on 
the catalyst surface with DRIFTS, it could be because the hydrogenation of the species is fast and/or 
the number of active sites for AcH formations is low. It is speculated that the active sites for C2-
oxygenate formation is located near the contact point of Rh metal and support. 
Similar results were found by Takeuchi[33] who investigated the reaction mechanism with the 
isotopes of 12C18O and 13C16O. A Rh catalyst supported on TiO2 was treated in CO-H2 (1:22 ratio) 
at 150 ℃ and 1 bar. The CO gas was 45% 13C16O and 52% 12C18O. The article reported all isotopic 
combinations of EtOH being formed including 12CH312CH216OH and 13CH313CH218OH and neither 
of these has an oxygen atom originating from 12C18O and 13C16O respectively. An explanation could 
be that a reaction intermediate with two oxygens is formed, such as CH3COO-.

8.12 Trapping of expected intermediates  
The species CH2CO and CH3CO- are potential reaction intermediates in the formation of C2-

of the reaction intermediates.

8.13 Differences for the CO adsorption on different Rh crystal facets  
Most recent studies have been based on the hypothesis of how the syngas conversion is based on the 
ratio of edge and terrace sites. Theoretical studies utilizing DFT calculations, reported a significant 
difference for CO reactivity when it is pre-adsorbed on different Rh crystal facets, e.g.  low energy 
density planar Rh(111) facets and high index sites, such as Rh(211) [17].  
The high index (steps/edges) has a lower coordination number, so the electron density is more 
concentrated on a single Rh atom. Therefore, the binding between the Rh and the adsorbed CO 
molecule is stronger, which means that there is a lower energy barrier for the C-O bond breakage. 
The planar sites have several CO coordination partners which means that the electron density is 
distributed across those partners, and therefore the energy barrier for C-O breakage is higher.

8.14 Hypothesis of the formation of C2-oxygenates on a Rh catalyst  
The hypothesis investigated in this thesis is based on the electronic state of Rh, and how several 
factors influence it. Rh particles are dispersed on a surface and the Rh in a metallic state (Rh0) is in 
the centre and the partially oxidized rhodium species (Rh+) is scattered around the centre.  
The electronic state of Rh and therefore the ratio of oxidized and metallic sites might be influenced 
by the particle size, CO partial pressure and type of support.  
Sachtler[16] analyzed several catalysts and found that the selectivity of C2-oxygenates was 
significantly different. This was further investigated by Kawai[31], who investigated that the support 
influenced electronic state of the Rh catalyst. 
Furthermore, Mclure[25] found that increasing CO pressure causes the ratio of Rh(CO)2 / Rh(CO) to 
increase, which means that the Rh is increasingly scattered and isolated. An increase in particle size 
can diminish the effect of an increasing in the CO partial pressure. This could be because the 
particles are larger and therefore harder to scatter and isolate. 
The current hypothesis on the conversion of syngas to C2-oxygenates with a Rh based catalyst, is 
that when CO reacts on the metallic Rhodium sites (Rh0), the C-O bond is broken, and the carbon 
species is hydrogation into CHx. When CO reacts on the oxidized rhodium sites (Rh+), it binds with 
the O to create CHO2- (formate). The oxygen can originate from either CO or the support[32]. The 
formate species is inserted onto CHx to form CH3COO- (acetate) which can be hydrogenated into 
C2-oxygenate products.

8.15 Summary  
Syngas conversion to C2-oxygenates is a well-researched topic that has renewed interested  because 
of sustainability and increase in oil prices[15]. Good results in terms of selectivity have been 
achieved with a Rh catalyst, but it has proved difficult to achieve a conversion fit for commercial 
applications.  
While the topic of syngas conversion to higher alcohols with a Rh catalyst is well-researched, 
concrete results with same reaction conditions and catalyst varies significantly. There are also 
several theories on how the reaction occurs. Therefore, creating a well-functioning system with 
acceptable selectivity and conversion is essential, to enable further development on the subject. This 
thesis investigates the topic based on the metallic and oxidizes Rh site hypothesis. 
In the literature study it was found that the following parameters influenced the conversion and C2-
oxygenate selectivity: 
• Reaction conditions 
• Support material 
• Presence of promotors and/or impurities 
• Particle size 
• CO partial pressure 
• Rhodium precursor 
The reaction mechanism was investigated by a series of catalytic experiments at elevated pressure 
for several Rh-catalyst, supported on SiO2 or ZrO2, where the conversion and production 
distribution was analyzed for various temperatures, pressure and flow. 
Catalytic experiments at ambient pressure were also performed, along with adsorption experiments 
to analyze if various species are C2-oxygenate intermediates. The impact of co-feeding H2O and 
MeOH was also investigated, to determine if the electronic state of Rh is influenced in a beneficial 
way for the formation of C2-oxygenates. 
These experiments were supplemented by TEM imaging and analysis.

9 Experimental set-ups and procedure 
9.1 Catalyst preparation 
The catalyst was prepared from either a nitrate (Rh(NO3)3) or chloride (RhCl3) precursor which was 
dispersed on a support metal oxide with a weight loading of 1 or 3 wt. %. Therefore, tested 
materials were silica (SiO2) and monoclinic zirconia (ZrO2). 
The catalyst was prepared by wet impregnation. The appropriate amount of RhCl3 ∙ xH2O (Alfa 
Aesar, 39 wt. % Rh) or Rh(NO3)3 ∙ xH2O (Sigma-Aldrich, 36 wt.% Rh) was dissolved in 
demineralized water. The solution volume was approximately the total pore volume of the support 
material. 
The support material was prepared by crushing 3 mm pellets of extrudated support material, either 
of ZrO2 (Saint-Gobain) or SiO2 (chemPUR), to a size between 150 and 300 μm. Afterwards, the 
support was calcined in air at 300 ℃ in a flow of 10% O2 in N2 for 2 h. 
The support was then put into a roundneck flask and the solution of dissolved Rh was poured on the 
support. The boiling flask was then connected to a rotary evaporator to dry the catalyst. The boiling 
flask was kept in a water bath at 40 ℃ while rotating 20 rpm. Water was removed under reduced 
pressure. 
The notation for the catalyst in this thesis is as following: 1 Rh-NO3/ZrO2, which means that the 
catalyst has a Rh weight loading of 1 wt.%, is from a Rh(NO3)3 precursor and is supported on ZrO2.

The setup is used for pilot plant testing of the rhodium catalyst in a high pressure PFR setup. The 
setup is connected to a gas chromatograph (GC), which can be used to detect a wide range of

9.3.1 Gas Chromatograph 
The gas chromatograph (GC) is used to analyze the product gas from the high pressure PFR setup. 
The analysis works with a concentration range from few ppm to 100% for organic compounds. 
Helium was used as a carrier gas.  
The GC works by having capillary columns packed with a material that retains the compounds in 
the gas, which separates them. The columns are placed in an oven. The gas is injected and moves to 
the columns where the compounds are gradually separated. Afterwards it reaches the detector at the 
outlet, where the data is logged on a PC as a function of the injection time. The GC has 2 channels 
for different purposes. It can be set to run the same program, or to alternate between the 2 programs. 
By alternating programs, the data from an outlet stream can be obtained for a broader range of 
compounds.

Hydrogen (H2), Nitrogen (N2), Oxygen (O2), Carbon monoxide (CO), Carbon dioxide (CO2) and

backflush is required as the columns are not tolerant to alcohols or other large compounds that 
might accumulate.

containing molecules. It consists of one 50 m x 0.32 mm x 10 μm Poraplot Q capillary column

experiments performed on the high pressure PFR reactor and flow cell reactor-setup, as well as the

10.3 Mechanistic studies  
To improve the understanding of the reaction mechanism of syngas conversion to C2-oxygenates on 
a Rh catalysts, further investigation is performed on the flow cell reactor-setup. The goal is to 
obtain a better understanding of the species which occupies the surface of Rh catalysts during the 
reaction of CO and H2. The species which populated the catalyst surface is intended to be obtained 
by subjecting the catalyst to a TPH sequence after catalytic treatment. 
A temperature programmed hydrogenation sequence (TPH) is a temperature ramp in a H2 flow from 
30 ℃ to 600 ℃, with an increase of 5 ℃/min. The species adsorbed on the catalyst surface after 
treatment is liberated by the influence of H2 and elevated temperature. The outlet gas containing the 
species is sent to a MS where the species defragments into several fragments which is used to 
analyze at what temperature which species leaves the surface. The analysis is qualitative as the MS 
was not calibrated for concentrations.  In Table 5 the observed fragments is shown:

11 Discussion  
The main focus was put on the investigation of the catalytic behavior of supported Rh catalysts, 
derived from Rh(NO3)3 as a precursor, supported on either ZrO2 or SiO2, for the CO-H2 reaction 
towards C2-oxygenates. Besides the evaluation of the catalytic performance, effort was spent to 
further unravel the reaction mechanism. Experimental evidence was found for a mobilization of the 
catalytically active Rh metal, when prepared from RhCl3 as precursor, under more elevated CO/H2 
reaction gas pressure conditions. 
In this thesis the highest selectivity of C2-oxygenates was 56% on Rh-NO3/SiO2 with a conversion 
of 0.4 %, compared to 45% for 1 Rh-NO3/ZrO2 with a conversion of 4.4%. There is a difference in 
conversion by a magnitude of 10 for 1 Rh-NO3/ZrO2 when reaction conditions are similar. This 
might indicate that Rh supported on ZrO2 has intrinsic properties which enables it to obtain a higher 
yield of C2-oxygenates or a higher amount of active sites.

therefore adjustments in the selectivity can be done at the cost of conversion. It is showcased in

11.3 Co-feeding of H2O and MeOH  
It was investigated if the introduction of water would oxidize the Rh and alter the ratio of Rh+/Rh0 
to shift the product distribution towards C2-oxygenates, but this did not occur. The co-feeding of 
water did not have any permanent increase in C2-oxygenate selectivity or decrease in hydrocarbon 
selectivity. The conversion decreased when water was introduced to the system. The EtOH/AcH 
ratio and ester selectivity also decreased when water was added which is related to the decrease in 
conversion. There was a significant increase in CO2 selectivity, which might be because of the 
water-gas shift reaction.  The electronic state of Rh is therefore either not altered by co-feeding 
H2O, or the effect is small. 
The reactor effluent gas has some water, but CO2 is not one of the main products when water is not 
co-fed and therefore Rh is likely not a good water gas-shift catalyst. 
The same investigation of changes in Rh+/Rh0 was performed for MeOH. The MeOH co-feeding 
was analyzed by treating a catalyst in CO-H2 for 4 hours and after approximately 2 hours the MeOH 
was introduced.   
It is unclear if the introduction of MeOH altered the production of C2-oxygenate, which would 
decompose as m/z = 43. When the MeOH is introduced there is a large liberation of m/z = 43 at 
1.84 h. The same liberation was seen for water. When MeOH is co-fed there appears to be a 
permanent increased intensity for fragments C2-oxygenates, which could indicate an increase in 
formation. Steady state was not achieved however, and therefore further investigation is required. 
11.4 Deactivation and influence of precursor  
Most of the measurements for the experiments on the high pressure PFR setup had varying time on 
stream. As seen in Figure 23 (Section 10.2.1) it can take a long time to achieve steady state in the 
setup and this is a large source of error in the experiment. 
It could be related to the deactivation of the catalyst, which could be either formation of coke on the 
Rh surface or Rh particles leaving the reactor. For the mechanistic studies, for Rh supported on 
SiO2 and ZrO2 spent 30 bar, there is a large quantity of methane liberated. This indicates that the 
formation of coke on the surface occurs.  
For Cl samples, the deactivation is influenced by Rh leaving the reactor. For a sample of 3 Rh-
Cl/SiO2 treated in CO-H2 at 50 bar and 250 ℃ no Rh particles was found in a TEM analysis. This 
was confirmed by EDX/EDS, where the elemental composition did not include Rh. Additionally, 
grey precipitate was found in the outlet of the reactor, found with EDX/EDS to consist of some Rh.

The hypothesis of Rh leaving the reactor was further enhanced by the GC data, where the 
conversion dropped rapidly to 0%. This issue was not observed for Rh-NO3 samples, which 
indicates that it is the Cl precursor that mobilizes the Rh. Johnston[28] reported the same tendency, 
comparing Rh-NO3 and Rh-Cl catalyst in a CO atmosphere. He found that while Rh was mobilized 
for both catalysts, Rh atoms still had other Rh neighbors when originated from a NO3 precursor. 
This was not the case for Cl. 
It can therefore be detrimental to the use of a chloride based Rh catalyst, as the Rh catalyst may 
deplete over time and leave the catalyst bed as volatile Rh carbonyl species.  This behavior is quite 
interesting, as this has not been clearly described in the literature which utilized RhCl3 as 
precursor[17,19]. This might be because either those research papers were better at removing the Cl or 
did the measurements of selectivity and conversion before steady state was achieved. 
11.5 Outlook and future work  
This concludes the discussion of the thesis. A base case similar to the literature for both catalysts of 
1 Rh-NO3 supported on SiO2 and ZrO2 was obtained and allowed further investigation into 
parameters such as temperature, pressure and flow. The analysis of co-feeding water could be re-
tested but did not appear to have any beneficial impact on 1 Rh-NO3/ZrO2, and the behavior of 
water co-feeding was similar for both catalysts during the mechanistic experiments. 
A few interesting issues occurred during the experimental work. The GC column to identify small 
molecules such as CO, H2 was sensitive to larger hydrocarbons and became clogged after catalytic 
treatment at of 275 ℃ and 2 bar on 1 Rh-NO3/ZrO2. A high conversion achieved by high 
temperatures should be avoided unless HC formation could be reduced. 
RhCl3 as precursor appeared to be difficult to use at elevated temperatures and pressure but should 
be investigated rigorously moving forward as the literature reports a higher selectivity and 
conversion when used as precursor.  
It could be investigated by an experiment where Rh(NO3)3 catalyst was impregnated with different 
levels of HCl, followed by catalytic treatment in CO-H2 at elevated pressure and temperature. These 
experiments would help uncover if the Cl impact is influenced by the quantity or by the reduction 
conditions, which should remove the majority of Cl as HCl. It is interesting that nobody has 
previously reported this pressure dependent behavior for the widely used RhCl3 derived catalysts.

To uncover if the large difference in conversion for Rh supported on SiO2 and ZrO2 is related to the 
number of active sites or intrinsic properties, H2-chemisoption could be used to determine the 
number of active sites. 
Additionally, it could be investigated if CO2 also had a reaction mechanism to C2-oxygenate by 
introducing a catalytic mixture of CO2/CO/H2 with variations in the CO/CO2 ratio. 
The DRIFTS experiments performed by Mclure[25] was done on a Rh/SiO2 catalyst. Rh supported 
on Zr showcased in this thesis to have a significantly higher conversion, and the structural changes 
of Rh supported ZrO2 could be investigated as a function of the CO partial pressure. 
Unfortunately, time was exhausted before co-feeding methanol could be tested on the high pressure 
PFR reactor. During the mechanistic study it was found to liberate species from the surface and it 
was unclear if the intensity of CH3CO- was increased when MeOH was co-fed (Figure 51, section 
10.3.4) or if it was just a liberation of the surface species.

12 Conclusion 
This work investigated the catalytic conversion of syngas to C2-oxygenates over rhodium based 
catalysts. The focus was for catalysts from a Rh(NO3)3 precursor supported on either SiO2 or ZrO2 
for synthesis of C2-oxygenates. 
For 1 Rh-NO3/ZrO2 a C2-oxygenate selectivity of 45% and a CO conversion of 4.4% was achieved 
at 225 ℃ and 25 bar. For 1 Rh-NO3/SiO2 a C2-oxygenate selectivity of 56% and a CO conversion 
of 0.4% was achieved at 250 ℃ and 30 bar. 
Increases in temperature increased the overall conversion and selectivity of HC, at the cost of 
selectivity of C2-oxygenates. Decreasing the mass to flow ratio by increasing the flow decreased the 
CO conversion and decreased EtOH/AcH ratio, especially for 1 Rh-NO3/SiO2. Increases in pressure 
increased the selectivity of esters and C1-oxygenates and reduced the selectivity of HC. 
A sample of 3 Rh-Cl/SiO2 was treated in a catalytic mixture at elevated temperature and pressure 
which had a rapid deactivation of the catalyst and grey precipitate was observed at the reactor 
outlet. Upon investigation it was found that no Rh was found in the catalyst sample after treatment, 
and the precipitate contained Rh which showed a depletion of the catalyst. Samples from a 
Rh(NO3)3 precursor were treated at similar conditions without observing such a deactivation which 
indicates that the Cl mobilizes the Rh.   
Model systems of catalyst pre-adsorbed AcH and EtOH were compared with catalysts spent in CO-
H2 at elevated pressure and temperature. Both experiments had CH3CO- species liberate at similar 
temperatures, indicating that CH3CO- is a reaction intermediate in the formation of C2-oxygenates.  
Co-feeding of water was investigated to have beneficial influence of the formation of C2-
oxygenates by altering the electronic state of Rh. Water liberated build up surface species but had 
no permanent improvement of C2-oxygenate selectivity.
