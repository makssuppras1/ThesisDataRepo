Abstract 
 
With growing need for clean energy, there is a constant increase in wind energy capacity each 
year. Nowadays, because of developing technology and growing sizes of turbines, offshore 
wind sites and high altitude sites tend to be the most profitable ones for placing wind farms. 
However, these regions are usually exposed to icing phenomena, which may affect power 
production as much as the safety of turbine operation. Therefore, turbines need to be 
optimized for operation in harsh, icing conditions. Understanding how ice grows and how it 
affects aerodynamic properties of a wind turbine blade is an extremely important factor for 
their operation.  
This research was a part of DTU and SNU partnership project. Its main purpose was to 
understand how ice accretes on a NACA 0015 airfoil, within different environment 
conditions. Secondly, to learn how to operate and upgrade the wind tunnel and to define the 
LWC and MVD conditions using rotating cylinder method and Cloud Droplet Probe 
measurements. Lastly, to understand the aerodynamic impacts of icing on the NACA 0015 
airfoil. 
The results of rotating cylinder measurements show a possibility of using this method for 
LWC estimation, however due to huge uncertainty coming from the measurement setup and 
tunnel conditions, could not be validated in this study. Icing measurements are showing a 
significant reduction in lift and increase in drag for almost all measured cases. Due to very 
high LWC in the CWT most of the created ice shapes were composed of mixed accretion.

1. Introduction 
 
Nowadays, renewable sources of energy have become a way to replace fossil fuels, as a 
contribution to growing need for clean energy worldwide. One that should be mentioned as 
one of the most developing is wind energy. By 2020 European Union is aiming to reduce 
greenhouse gas emissions by 20%, increase the share of renewable energy to at least 20% of 
consumption for each EU country and obtain energy savings of 20% or more. Additionally, 
each country of EU should provide a share of 10% of renewable energy in the transport sector 
[1]. Thus, it will lead to a huge growth of wind energy capacity installed in Europe in the 
following years, since each country needs to fulfil the mentioned regulation. According to 
GWEC (Global Wind  Energy Council) wind turbine market shows a general increase in 
capacity installed every year worldwide as presented in Fig 1 below. 
 
Fig 1 Annual installed capacity worldwide [2] 
 
What is worth noting that in recent years there has been a huge increase in offshore wind 
capacity with an average 28.6% rate every year. This tendency occurs, because offshore wind 
turbines have many advantages compared to onshore installations, mainly higher average 
wind speeds and more reliable wind source, having high speed levels more frequent, than 
compared to onshore sites. Moreover, noise and visual impact are less important, since there 
are no inhabitants around these areas. However, not only sea areas are advantageous to build 
wind farms. One of the best sites are the ones at high altitudes, where wind velocity grows by 
0.1 m/s per 100 m of altitude in average. Furthermore, northern areas of Europe, such as 
Norway and Finland provide approximately 10% higher available wind power due to 
increased air density, due to the low temperatures. [3] All aforementioned sites experience 
icing conditions, which may lead to many different problems, such as power losses or 
mechanical failures. These problems will be thoroughly explained in Chapter 2.3. Due to 
these issues, engineers look for solutions to optimize turbines operation in such harsh 
conditions and to ensure safety and efficiency of wind power production.  
As mentioned before, during planning wind farms in the cold climates, icing phenomena are 
of great concern, since it is known that at least for few days the power production will be 
affected by this issue. Therefore, WECO EU (Wind Energy Production in Cold Climate) has

2.2 Ice accretion 
 
Ice physics and ice accretion on structures is a topic taken into account in determining the 
resulting loads and solving many operational problems connected with these phenomena. In 
this chapter ice physics and thermodynamics are presented, with basic principles of ice type 
prediction, as well as ice growth modelling proposed by Makkonen, with an in depth analysis 
on referring parameters.   
 
2.2.1 Types of ice 
 
There are three types of atmospheric icing, which are presented in this chapter: in-cloud, 
precipitation and frost. The measurements made for this thesis were performed in the in-cloud 
conditions. Main types of atmospheric icing are as follows [3]: 
 In-cloud icing refers to a phenomenon when super-cooled water droplets are 
approaching a surface having temperature below 0°C and freeze immediately upon 
impact. Super-cooled droplets have temperature below 0°C, reaching even -30°C, but 
due to their small size they do not freeze in the air. Depending on the MVD (Median 
Volume Diameter) and LWC (Liquid Water Content), as well as the environment 
conditions (air temperature and flow velocity) different types of ice are accreted on the 
impact surface. Three main types can be distinguished: 
 
o Soft rime is defined by thin ice with needles and flakes. Occurs when the 
conditions in the cloud show low MVD and LWC. It is a very fragile type of 
ice with low adhesion, occurring mainly during light freezing fog and low 
wind speeds. It is characterized by low density value. 
 
o Hard rime appears usually at higher wind speeds and higher MVD and LWC 
conditions. It is much more difficult to remove, characterized by much higher 
adhesion and therefore larger density value. 
 
o Glaze appears in a wet growth, when a portion of droplets does not freeze 
upon impact, but creates a water film, which freezes later. Glaze ice  is 
characterized by highest adhesion and density. 
 
 Precipitation is connected with wet snow or freezing rain: 
 
o Freezing rain appears when the rain approaches a surface with a temperature 
below 0°C and freezes on it, creating glaze ice. It is characterized by high ice 
density and adhesion. 
 
o Wet snow occurs when the air temperature varies between 0°C and -3°C. 
Therefore the snow is slightly liquid and sticks to approached surface.

 Hoar frost appears when water vapor freezes directly on a surface, creating ice layer 
with low density. It usually occurs during low wind speeds.

2.2.2  Ice physics and ice growth 
 
Ice accretion type depends on many parameters, which both differ for precipitation and in-
cloud icing. To describe the first of them following parameters are the most important ones 
[9]: 
 Precipitation rate 
 Surface air temperature 
 Liquid water content 
 Wind speed 
 Wind direction 
 Air temperature 
 Relative humidity 
For in-cloud icing the parameters are as follows: 
 Liquid water content in the cloud 
 Median volume diameter 
 Air temperature 
 Wind speed 
 Wind direction 
 Relative humidity 
In in-cloud conditions two types of ice growths are distinguished: wet and dry growth. Dry 
growth is responsible for rime ice accretion and occurs when the droplets freeze immediately 
on the approached surface. Wet growth refers to glaze ice appearance and occurs when there 
is a liquid layer created between the impinging droplets and the iced layer. Approaching part 
of the droplet freezes and rest of it stays in the liquid state and freezes later. Fig 10 and 11 
show both dry and wet growth. [10]

2.3 Impact of icing on wind turbine operation 
 
For a long time cold climate has not been taken into account as a problem in planning wind 
turbines operation and power performance. However, since many wind farms started to be 
placed in harsh conditions with temperature far below freezing point, affected by freezing 
==PAGE:28==
14 
 
rains and in-cloud icing effects, wind industry started to seek for solutions for the problem of 
blades icing.  
Icing effects cause problems in wind assessment and operation of wind turbine. The main 
issues are presented below: [3] 
 Measurement errors: during the assessment of a particular wind site, anemometers, 
wind vanes and temperature sensors are highly affected by icing phenomena, causing 
up to 30% error in wind speed reading. [12] 
 
 Power losses: due to the fact that ice accreted on a wind turbine blades changes its 
shape and surface roughness the aerodynamic behavior of the blade is affected as well. 
This also leads to mistakes in yaw and power control, caused by wrong wind speed 
and direction readings from turbine measurement instruments. Annual power 
production reduction reach up to 27%, depending on icing intensity and duration. [13] 
Below in Fig  a production loss is presented after 3 days icing event: 
 
 
Fig 13 Power production comparison between normal operation and operation under icing 
conditions [14] 
  
 Mechanical failures: because of increased mass on the blades and tower, ice can 
cause an increase in loads and provide high amplitude vibrations. Due to an imbalance 
between blade masses, as well as change of particular dimensions and material 
properties of different parts of the turbine an increased fatigue loading might also be 
observed. 
 
 Electrical failures: snow infiltration in nacelle and very low temperatures can cause 
condensation in electronics. [12]

4.4 Rotating cylinder  
 
In this chapter the setup for LWC estimation using rotating cylinder method is presented. It 
based on the study made by Makkonen and Stallabrass (1987) [18], which focused on finding 
cloud droplet collision efficiencies. The method bases on Langmuir and Blodgett theory, 
which presents a relationship between the droplet drag coefficient and droplet Reynolds 
number in order to calculate the droplet trajectories, as well as the collision efficiencies by 
solving the equations of motion for droplet motion in a potential flow. In Makkonen and 
Stallabrass work, a comparison of Langmuir and Blodgett theory is presented, therefore 
experimental collision efficiency values are compared to the theoretical ones. 
The experiment performed in CWT intended to use the one made by Makkonen and Stallabras 
in order to find experimental LWC values for different wind speeds. The method bases on 
Makkonen model and Finsted empirical method [11] for determining collision efficiencies 
presented in Chapter 2.2.2.

inside had to ensure the creation of pure rime ice on the cylinder to simplify the equation used

4.4.1 Experimental setup 
 
The experiment was performed using a glass fiber cylinder of 1980 mm length and 32 mm 
diameter. It was placed in the middle of the test section, connected on both sides by two steel 
wall mounts, which included ball bearings in order to let the rotation of the cylinder. 
Moreover, in between the cylinder itself and the wall mount a clamp connection has been 
introduced in order to extend the length of the whole part to be able to apply the rotation via 
Faulhaber motor. Therefore, an additional connection shafts were introduced, since the total 
width of the test section is 2000 mm and due to harsh icing conditions inside the wind tunnel 
it was necessary to place the steel mounts and the rotating motor outside the tunnel to avoid 
corrosion. The wall mounts were introduced in order to make sure that the weight of the 
cylinder is not carried by the motor itself. 
The motor of choice was Faulhaber 3268G024BX4-32/3S with MC5005SRS motion 
controller, which provided a high continuous torque at low rotational speeds.  
The motor was controlled via Motion Manager 6, program supported by the manufacturer, 
which lets one configure the motor and program its operation.

5. Conclusions and further studies 
 
5.1 Conclusions 
 
In this research an influence of icing on aerodynamics of wind turbines on basis of NACA 
0015 wind tunnel tests has been provided, as well as a validation of rotating cylinder method 
validity. Concerning the rotating cylinder method, due to high uncertainty of the ice weight  
measurement and inability of the CWT to obtain desired conditions, one cannot prove that the 
mentioned approach is viable of estimating accurate LWC in the wind tunnel conditions. 
Moreover, there was no reliable data obtained from the Cloud Droplet Probe to validate the 
measured values, which as mentioned, already had huge uncertainty.  
During in-cloud icing tests aerodynamic influence of type of ice accreted on the NACA 
0015 airfoil has been verified. Additionally, a LWC effect on the amount of ice created has 
shown suspected tendencies in most of the cases, resulting in thicker layer for higher LWC 
values. Exceptions from this rule are coming from the nonuniformity of the spray inside the 
test section, causing different pathing of the water particles. Since, all the ice shape 
extractions were performed in the same airfoil section, the effective LWC differed. What is 
more, during the measurement campaign temperature and flow velocity effect on ice accretion 
has been observed and climatological conditions for different types of ice were verified. 
Concerning the aerodynamic performance of the airfoil, rime ice has a more significant 
influence on both drag and lift. Lift increased even 50% after 50 minutes of rime ice accretion 
and drag increase of 150% in maximum. However, not every result has shown the general 
tendency, due to huge scatter in force data from the force transducers. Therefore, the 
uncertainty of the results, with the coefficient of variation reaching 45% was significant. 
Lastly, additional uncertainty of some of the results was caused by temperature fluctuations 
during the measurements, as well as nonuniformity of the cloud, coming from the spray bar.  
5.2 Further studies 
  
In this chapter a list of future work to develop this research and its methods is presented: 
 Rotating cylinder method should be performed using a very accurate weight gauge, 
in order to omit the uncertainty. Conditions should be controlled to verify the 
accretion of pure rime ice on the cylinder. This ensures the accretion and sticking 
efficiencies to be equal to unity and therefore Makkonen model can be freely used. 
Moreover, more measurements should be performed to verify repeatability of the 
results.  
 
 LWC and MVD mapping tests with fully operational Cloud Droplet Probe need to 
be performed to know the exact conditions in each part of the test section.  
 
==PAGE:77==
63 
 
 Icing measurements should be continued with additional angle of attack analysis and 
much more tests should be performed within the same conditions in order to reduce 
the uncertainty. 
 
 Flow uniformity can be obtained by increased mixing of the cloud before the test 
section. In this research a try to eliminate this issue was conceived, however as 
mentioned, turbulence problems occurred. Additional tests should be performed, using 
both grids and turbulence generators located on the spray bar.
