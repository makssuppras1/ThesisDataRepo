Abstract
This thesis explores two contrasting approaches to machine learning deployment in cloud environ-
ments: centralized platforms, exemplified by Databricks and modular architectures, utilizing
tools like Docker, Kubernetes and Weights & Biases. The study employs a structured eval-
uation framework to analyze how these approaches address critical challenges in machine learning
operations, such as resource scalability, workflow automation and deployment management.
Machine learning pipeline was implemented to demonstrate the centralized approach in practice.
Terraform was used to provision infrastructure, while Delta Lake and the Medallion Archi-
tecture structured data management across Bronze, Silver, and Gold layers. Model training, de-
ployment, and monitoring were managed using MLflow, ensuring a streamlined and automated
MLOps lifecycle within the Databricks ecosystem.
Findings indicate that Databricks provides a highly integrated, scalable, and automated environ-
ment, making it an efficient choice for organizations prioritizing ease of use and rapid deployment.
However, it comes with higher costs and reduced flexibility compared to modular setups, which,
while more customizable and cost-effective, require greater engineering effort and operational com-
plexity.
The thesis highlights trade-offs between standardization and customization, offering practical in-
sights for organizations deciding between centralized and modular MLOps solutions. It provides
strategic recommendations based on real-world implementation experiences, helping businesses align
their infrastructure choices with technical capabilities, scalability needs and long-term goals.

Chapter 1
Introduction
This thesis investigates the strategies for deploying AI models in cloud environments, with a specific
focus on centralized platforms, namely Databricks, with the assistance of Terraform. As organiza-
tions increasingly adopt artificial intelligence, they face challenges in achieving a balance between
performance, scalability, and cost efficiency [1]. Centralized platforms address these challenges by
offering integrated solutions that streamline the machine learning lifecycle, including data prepro-
cessing, model training, deployment, and monitoring. Their appeal lies in their ability to provide
seamless automation, scalability, and enterprise-grade reliability.
Despite their advantages, centralized platforms often come with trade-offs, including higher costs
and limited flexibility compared to open-source alternatives, such as FastAPI, Docker, and Weights
and Biases.
These modular tools allow tailored workflows but require considerable expertise to
integrate and maintain. This thesis evaluates centralized platforms in real-world AI deployment
scenarios, focusing on their ability to enhance efficiency, scalability, and operational simplicity. By
leveraging the capabilities of Databricks and Terraform in the Azure ecosystem, the study provides
insights into the strengths and limitations of centralized solutions in enterprise settings.
1.1
Motivation
Centralized platforms like Databricks are transforming the field of machine learning operations(MLOps)
by providing a unified environment to manage the entire lifecycle of machine learning models.
Databricks integrates critical components, such as MLflow for experiment tracking and Delta Lake
for data reliability and governance, ensuring seamless transitions across the pipeline. Its integration
with GitHub further simplifies collaboration and version control, making it a powerful option for
enterprise-scale workflows.
Terraform complements Databricks by automating the provisioning of infrastructure, enabling scala-
bility and reproducibility. With its declarative syntax, Terraform ensures that resources are allocated
efficiently to meet the dynamic requirements of AI workflows. Together, these platforms offer an
enterprise-grade solution that simplifies the deployment, monitoring, and operational management
of machine learning systems.
This thesis underscores the growing importance of centralized platforms for organizations prioritizing
scalability, automation, and ease of use. By focusing on Databricks and Terraform, the research
demonstrates how these platforms address key challenges in MLOps, such as resource management,
reproducibility, and model monitoring, while minimizing operational complexity. The conclusions
aim to offer insight into when and why centralized platforms might be favored over modular, open-
source solutions in the deployment of machine learning models.

1.2
Objective
The objective of this thesis is to evaluate centralized platforms, specifically Databricks, in the
deployment and management of AI models in cloud environments. The study examines how these
platforms streamline MLOps processes. By leveraging Terraform for infrastructure provisioning,
the research assesses the benefits of an integrated ecosystem compared to modular, open-source
alternatives.
This thesis explores key challenges in AI model deployment, including infrastructure management,
data versioning, experiment tracking, and automation. The Primary Objectives(PO) are:
• PO1: Analyze the efficiency and scalability of centralized platforms in handling machine
learning workflows.
• PO2: Evaluate the impact of automation in Databricks and Terraform on reducing operational
complexity in AI deployments.
• PO3: Assess flexibility and adaptability, examining whether centralized solutions limit cus-
tomization for specific AI applications.
• PO4: Provide strategic recommendations on when organizations should opt for centralized
platforms over modular alternatives, aligning business objectives.
This thesis aims to provide a structured framework for organizations to make informed decisions in
selecting an MLOps approach, ensuring alignment with project requirements and expertise.

Chapter 2
Background
This chapter provides an introduction to the concept of MLOps, outlining its principles and practices
for operationalizing machine learning workflows. It also explores cloud computing as a foundation for
scalable and reproducible systems, with a focus on Microsoft Azure. Additionally, Databricks and
Terraform, tools utilized in this project, are introduced alongside their roles in building the pipeline.
Finally, the chapter discusses challenges commonly encountered in implementing such systems. This
chapter assumes that the reader has a foundational understanding of machine learning concepts,
including model training, evaluation, and deployment. Familiarity with cloud platforms and basic
DevOps practices is also beneficial for a deeper understanding of the topics discussed.
2.1
Introduction to MLOps
Machine Learning Operations applies DevOps principles to the process of delivering an ML model
to production. There are a few challenges in that regard, such as ensuring model reproducibility, de-
ployment, and managing a changing data landscape. By using automation, monitoring and iterative
improvement practices, MLOps ensures the reliablitity and efficiency of the machine learning model
throught its lifecycle[2, 3, 4]. To achieve these goals, MLOps emphasizes several key objectives:
• Reproducibility: Ensuring that models can be retrained and validated under consistent
conditions[3].
• Scalability: Supporting the growing computational and storage demands of machine learning
workflows[2].
• Automation: Reducing manual tasks such as deployment, testing, and monitoring through
automated pipelines[5].
• Monitoring and Adaptation: Providing real-time insights into model and system perfor-
mance to adapt workflows dynamically[2].
2.1.1
MLOps Lifecycle
The goal of Machine Learning Operations is to unify machine learning (ML), development (Dev),
and operations (Ops) under a single framework[2].A diagram illustrating the three distinct stages
can be seen in Figure 2.1.

That integration allows for a smooth transition between three stages of the machine learning life-
cycle: design, model development, and operations. By focusing on these stages, MLOps bridges
the gap between data science, data engineering, software development, and IT operations, creating
a streamlined workflow for building and maintaining machine learning systems[3]. This approach
ensures that every aspect of the ML pipeline, from understanding the business problem and require-
ments to a production-ready solution, is handled with efficiency and precision.
Each of the stages plays a unique role in the creation of a robust and reliable system:
• Design: serves as the foundation of any Machine Learning project.
It focuses on under-
standing the problem, identifying use cases, and establishing the requirements for a potential
solution. Teams assess the data needed for training and determine whether additional data is
required. This stage sets the direction for the entire project.
• Model Development: shifts the focus to preparing the data, selecting algorithms, and
building the model. Data preprocessing and feature engineering ensure the data is ready for
training. The model is then trained, validated, and tested to guarantee it performs well on
unseen data.
• Operations: deploys the model into production, integrating it with live systems. Automated
pipelines that handle updates, retraining, and monitoring are created. Continuous monitoring
can detect issues such as data drift or accuracy degradation, enabling dynamic adjustments.
It is important to highlight that these stages are deeply interconnected in a cycle. Decisions made
during the design phase influence how models are developed, while insights from operations might
redefine both design and development.
This connection keeps the lifecycle adaptive and helps
maintain the relevance of models over time.
2.1.2
Core Practices in MLOps
The division of production machine learning into three phases provides a high-level framework for
building and maintaining those systems. However, to effectively operationalize machine learning
workflows, MLOps incorporates several core practices that ensure consistency, efficiency, and scala-
bility across all stages of the lifecycle.
Version Control and CI/CD
In the context of MLOps, version control extends beyond traditional source code management to
include datasets, model configurations, and artifacts. Tools like Git, Data Version Control (DVC),
Mlflow, Weights and Biases enable teams to track changes in data, code, and models, ensuring

Continuous Training
Continuous training ensures that machine learning models remain accurate and relevant in situations
where data distributions or key features frequently change. This process involves automation of
retraining process of models using updated datasets, fine-tuning model parameters, and checking
performance metrics to prevent model degradation over time.
By automating these steps, continuous training reduces the need for manual work and allows systems
to quickly adapt to new data, ensuring they continue to meet business needs and provide reliable
results.
Continuous training is closely integrated with deployment workflows, enabling retrained models
to be transitioned into production environments. Rather than focusing on the deployment pipelines
themselves (covered under CI/CD), this integration ensures that updated models replace older ver-
sions with minimal disruption to live systems .
Effective integration reduces latency between retraining and production deployment, allowing orga-
nizations to react quickly to changes in data while maintaining consistent model performance and
reliability.

Monitoring and observability are integral to managing machine learning systems in production.
Monitoring focuses on tracking essential metrics such as accuracy, latency, and system uptime to

2.2
Cloud Computing
With the previously discussed MLOps concepts, such as scalability, reproducibility, and automation,
it becomes clear that modern machine learning systems demand infrastructure capable of meeting
these requirements. This is where cloud computing plays a crucial role. By providing on-demand
access to scalable resources and integrated services, eliminating the need for expensive on-premises
servers[7].
2.2.1
Resource Managment
The main factor by which cloud services excel over physical servers is their flexibility. Depending on
the current requirements of the system, the amount of resources used can be expanded or reduced.
Cloud providers offer multiple resource management models to address different workload needs
and budget considerations. These models allow users to manage expenses dynamically while still
obtaining the necessary resources for their operations. Below are the three most prevalent models,
each designed for specific situations and types of workloads:
• Pay-As-You-Go: This pricing model charges users based on their actual resource usage,
including compute time, storage, and network bandwidth. It offers flexibility by eliminating
upfront costs and is ideal for workloads with fluctuating or unpredictable demands [8].
• Reserved Instances: Reserved instances allow users to commit to a fixed amount of resources
for one or three years. This model provides substantial cost savings compared to on-demand
pricing and is well-suited for stable and predictable workloads [8].
• Spot Instances: Spot instances enable users to access unused cloud capacity at reduced
cost. These instances are best suited for interruptible workloads, such as batch processing or
machine learning tasks, where cost efficiency outweighs the risk of termination [9].
2.2.2
Microsoft Azure
Microsoft Azure is one of the leading cloud providers globally, with a significant share of the
market[10]. The platform’s extensive use can be attributed to its broad range of services, from
storage to advanced data analytics platforms, as well as its numerous compliance certifications [7].
Azure places a strong emphasis on security, ensuring that data and operations are protected at
every level. Key security features include data encryption at rest and in transit, Role-Based Ac-
cess Control (RBAC) for fine-grained access management, and compliance with major international
standards such as GDPR. These measures make Azure particularly suitable for machine learning

2.3
Databricks
Databricks joins data analytics, data engineering and data science into one unified Data Intelligence
Platform. Built on Apache Spark[15], it offers a cloud-agnostic environment, meaning it can be
used across major cloud providers such as Azure, AWS, and Google Cloud. This flexibility allows
to standardize data pipelines and machine learning workflows while avoiding dependency on any
single cloud ecosystem. Databricks simplifies the data lifecycle management through its core com-
ponents: Delta Lake, Mlflow, and Databricks Jobs. These tools provide essential capabilities for
data management, orchestration, and operationalizing machine learning workflows.
2.3.1
Delta Lake
Delta Lake is an open-source storage layer used in Databricks that enhances traditional data lakes
with advanced features for performance and governance[16]. It supports ACID transactions and
schema evolution, enabling control over data and its consistency. Additionally, the Time Travel fea-
ture provides access to historical versions of datasets, which is crucial for reproducing environments
and recalculating data in case of errors or inconsistencies. These features make Delta Lake a good
choice for MLOps workflows, as it addresses a need of reproducibility and manages changes in data
over time.
2.3.2
Databricks Jobs
Databricks Jobs feature is an orchestration tool for automating data workflows and machine learning
pipelines[17]. It allows users to define and schedule jobs that can include multiple tasks executed in
sequence or parallel, depending on requirements. The tool provides monitoring and alerting features,
giving real-time insights into workflow execution and information on resource utilization during the
run. It supports dynamic resource scaling, ensuring workflows run regardless of workload size.

2.3.3
Mlflow
Databricks provides native integration with Mlfow which is an open-source tool that manages ma-
chine learning lifecycle[18]. It enables experiment tracking by automatically logging key metrics,
parameters and artifacts during model training, making it easier for teams to compare experiments
and reproduce them. Additionally, Mlflow supports model serving, allowing trained models to be
deployed into production environments as REST APIs endpoints for real-time use or as batch work-
flows for processing large datasets.
MLflow also includes a model registry, which helps manage multiple model versions, track their
approval status, and control their transition between staging and production environments. This
ensures better governance and traceability throughout the deployment process[19].
2.4
Infrastructure as Code
Infrastructure as Code (IaC) simplifies collaboration and ensures reproducibility by allowing in-
frastructure and resources to be defined and managed through code. This approach is essential in
modern workflows, including MLOps, where consistent and scalable environments are required for
deployment.
2.4.1
Terraform
One of the most popular IaC tools available on the market is Terraform, an open-source, declar-
ative platform for automating the provisioning and management of infrastructure[20]. Terraform
allows users to define resources in configuration files and is cloud agnostic, supporting multiple
cloud providers, such as Azure, AWS, and Google Cloud. Terraform was chosen for this project
due to its support for managing Databricks-specific resources and jobs [21]. This feature allows
users to define Databricks workspaces, clusters, and even jobs directly in Terraform configurations
joining the integration between infrastructure and workflow automation. Additionally, Terraform’s
state management capabilities ensure that infrastructure changes are tracked accurately, enabling
consistent updates and reducing the risk of configuration drift[22].
2.5
Decentralized vs Centralized Platforms in MLOps
In the landscape of MLOps, two primary strategies dominate: using centralized platforms and
selecting specialized tools for each functionality to build a highly customized pipeline. Both ap-
proaches aim to operationalize machine learning workflows by addressing key principles but they do
so through fundamentally different strategies.
2.5.1
Decentralized Approach to MLOps
This approach involves combining independent, modular tools to build a fully customizable MLOps
pipeline. It is particularly suited for teams that value flexibility and wish to design customable
pipelines tailored to their needs. However, it often requires advanced technical expertise and addi-
tional effort to integrate and maintain the individual components. Examples of widely used tools
in this category include the following:
• Docker: For containerizing machine learning workflows and ensuring consistency across en-
vironments [23].

2.5.2
Centralized Platforms for MLOps
The centralized platform approach consolidates the entire machine learning lifecycle into a sin-
gle, unified environment. Platforms like Databricks, Google Vertex AI, Azure Synapse and AWS
SageMaker provide built-in tools and services to manage data, orchestrate workflows, and deploy
machine learning models. These platforms are specifically designed to reduce the complexity of in-
tegration, enabling faster iteration cycles and simplifying the operationalization of machine learning
pipelines[28, 29, 30, 31].
This approach is increasingly favored by large companies due to its strong focus on integration
and automation. Centralized platforms eliminate the need to piece together various tools manually,
offering workflows that handle everything from data preprocessing and model training to deploy-
ment and monitoring. By providing a cohesive ecosystem, they allow organizations to focus on
experimentation and optimization rather than infrastructure management[28]. Furthermore, these
platforms support collaboration by providing shared workspaces and native tools for experiment
tracking, ensuring that teams can work together efficiently.
With the growing demand for scalable and efficient machine learning solutions, centralized plat-
forms have become a cornerstone for businesses. Their focus on ease of use, resource optimization,
and built-in scalability makes them a compelling choice for organizations that want to reduce the
overhead of managing fragmented tools[3].
2.6
Challenges in MLOps Implementation
Although MLOps provides a structured approach for implementing machine learning systems, its
executions can often present numerous obstacles. These challenges come from the complexity of
integrating diverse components, into single workflow, maintaining the performance in production
environments. Below, some of the most significant obstacles encountered in MLOps are listed:
Data Management and Quality
Ensuring consistent and high-quality data across environments is one of the most persistent chal-
lenges in MLOps. Issues such as data drift, schema evolution, and incomplete datasets can sig-
nificantly impact model performance. Managing data lineage and version control for datasets are
critical but often complex tasks [32].
Tool Integration
The MLOps ecosystem is composed of a wide range of tools, each designed to address specific
stages of the machine learning lifecycle. Integrating these tools into a cohesive pipeline can require
substantial effort, especially when dealing with decentralized approaches. Mismatched interfaces
and lack of standardization often result in additional development requirements.

Scalability and Resource Management
Scaling machine learning pipelines to handle large datasets or high-velocity streaming data requires
advanced infrastructure. Balancing cost efficiency with computational performance is a constant
challenge, particularly when running models in production environments with dynamic workloads.
Monitoring and Maintenance
Machine learning models deployed in production require continuous monitoring to ensure that they
perform as expected. Detecting issues such as data drift, model degradation, or bias in predictions
is critical but challenging. Building automated systems for anomaly detection and alerting often
requires significant customization and expertise[32].
Collaboration Across Teams
MLOps workflows depend on effective collaboration between data scientists, data engineers, and
DevOps teams. If these groups do not align their processes or communicate well, it can cause delays
or even failures in deploying models to production.
Compliance and Security
Data privacy and security are paramount in machine learning workflows, particularly in industries
governed by strict regulations like GDPR or HIPAA. Implementing robust access controls, encrypt-
ing sensitive data, and maintaining audit trails for data and model changes are non-trivial challenges
that must be addressed to ensure compliance and trust.
These challenges highlight the complexity of operationalizing machine learning workflows at scale.
Addressing these obstacles requires a combination of robust tooling, best practices, and collaboration
across disciplines.

Dataset and Model Introduction

3.1.1
Dataset
AG is a collection of news articles gathered by academic search engine called ComeToMyHead[34].
The AG News dataset, curated by Xiang Zhang, was originally constructed by selecting the four

For this project, the dataset was accessed through the Hugging Face Datasets library [36].
3.1.2
Model
The distilbert-base-uncased model from Hugging Face was selected for its optimal balance of compu-
tational efficiency and strong performance. As a distilled version of BERT (Bidirectional Encoder
Representations from Transformers), it retains approximately 97% of BERT’s performance while
being 40% smaller and 60% faster [37].
Pre-trained on large English datasets such as the BookCorpus and Wikipedia, the model learns
general language representations that can be fine-tuned for specific tasks. By fine-tuning on the
AG News dataset, it was tailored to perform the specific task of text classification. This fine-tuning
demonstrated the model’s capability to efficiently perform classification, achieving an accuracy in
the range of 80–90% [38].

3.4.1
Medallion Architecture Overview
Medallion Architecture organizes data processing into three distinct layers: Bronze, Silver, and
Gold.
Each layer serves a specific purpose, ensuring that the data is incrementally refined and
prepared for downstream use. The architecture is illustrated in Figure 3.2.
Figure 3.2: Medallion Architecture storage division into three layers: Bronze, Silver, and Gold.
Bronze Layer
The Bronze layer serves as the raw data storage point in the Medallion Architecture. This layer
captures the data as it arrives from source location, in this case, the AG News dataset retrieved from
Hugging Face. The raw data is stored in its raw JSON format without any modifications, ensuring
complete data traceability. This layer ensures that even if data in downstream layers needs to be
reprocessed, the original dataset is always available as a backup.
Silver Layer
The Silver layer processes the raw data stored in the Bronze layer, transforming it into struc-
tured/tabular format. In this stage, data cleansing operations are applied, such as removing du-
plicates, handling missing values, and standardizing text fields. These transformations ensure the
data is ready for downstream processing.
In this project, the cleaned data from the Silver layer is stored in Delta format to leverage Delta
Lake’s advanced features such as time travel and incremental refreshes. Time travel allows for ver-
sioning, enabling users to audit changes and revert to previous states if needed. It also speed up
querying against the table as it leverages column statistics stored in delta log. Incremental refresh
ensures that only new or updated data is processed, optimizing resource usage.
Gold Layer
The Gold layer is the final stage of data processing in the Medallion Architecture. This layer is
designed for clean, reliable and ready to consume data, where aggregates, calculated metrics and
features are typically stored. It provides the dataset required for analytics, reporting, and machine
learning workflows. Its main purpose is improving performance of downstream tasks.
In this project, the Gold layer is used to tokenize the cleaned data from the Silver layer and prepare
a feature table. This feature table serves as the input for the BERT model during training, ensuring
that the data is in the correct format for training efficiency.

Data Processing Job
The data processing workflow is fully automated using a Databricks Job, an orchestration tool that

Figure 3.3: The data processing job structure showing tasks across the Bronze, Silver, and Gold

The job consists of five notebooks. Three of these notebooks handle the ingestion and transforma-

The job is executed using a Databricks job cluster, which is dynamically created at the start of the

The scheduling of the job can also be configured using cron expressions to match the project’s
requirements. For example, jobs can be scheduled to run hourly, daily, or triggered on demand.
Databricks provides robust monitoring capabilities for these jobs, allowing users to:
• View current and past job runs.
• Analyze logs and cluster performance metrics to optimize resource allocation.
• Debug errors by pinpointing the exact step where a job failed.
• Track execution times for better pipeline optimization.

The foundation of any MLOps workflow lies in its data pipeline. Modular approaches emphasize

4.1.1
Data Versioning
In this project, Delta Lake was chosen for data versioning, as mentioned in the previous chapter.
Delta Lake offers strong versioning capabilities at the table level, including features such as time
travel and ACID transactions, making it efficient for handling largescale data pipelines [16]. That
being said, there are other tools available that one can use for this task:
DVC
DVC is a Git-based tool designed for tracking and versioning datasets, making it straightforward
to manage data alongside code in machine learning projects [42]. It integrates seamlessly with Git
workflows, allowing users to tie specific data versions directly to experiments. DVC also supports
a variety of cloud storage solutions, such as S3 and Azure Blob Storage, enabling flexible storage
options. Its lightweight and intuitive design makes it well-suited for ensuring reproducibility and
traceability in machine learning workflows.
LakeFS
Another tool used for data versioning, designed for managing data lakes is LakeFs. It allows users
to use Git-like operations such as commits, branches, and merges for datasets stored in data lakes in
the cloud[43]. With LakeFS one can create isolated branches for testing and development, ensuring
data integrity and reproducibility. It also supports the integration of CI/CD pipelines, making it
easy to automate workflows.

tools like MLflow within Databricks for this purpose, modular approaches often rely on external
solutions. A prominent tool in this space is Weights & Biases (W&B) [25].
It is a powerful tool for tracking experiments, logging metrics, and visualizing results in machine
learning projects. It enables users to monitor hyperparameters, compare models, and collaborate
through shared dashboards. While W&B requires configuration, such as setting up API keys and
logging metrics manually, it offers flexibility and advanced visualizations.
4.3
Infrastructure Management
In the centralized setup described earlier, Terraform was used to provision and manage resources
within Azure, including Databricks-specific infrastructure such as workspaces and clusters. Databricks,
in turn, handled much of the complexity involved in managing machine learning workflows by pro-
viding an integrated environment for development, execution, and monitoring. This setup ensured
project reproducibility.
An alternative approach to managing infrastructure is using Docker for containerization. Docker
packages applications and their dependencies into containers, ensuring consistency across develop-
ment, testing, and production environments[23]. Developers can define these environments using
a Dockerfile, simplifying the development and deployment processes. Docker containers are eas-
ily portable, promoting teamwork. However, it does not offer inherent features for orchestration
or scaling. To address this limitation, Docker is often combined with Kubernetes, an open-source
orchestration platform[26]. Kubernetes automates tasks such as deployment, scaling, and load bal-
ancing of containerized applications. It ensures high availability and efficient resource allocation,

4.4
Model Deployment
In the implementation described earlier, Databricks was used to deploy and serve models, lever-
aging its integrated serving endpoints to simplify the process of making trained models accessible
for inference. In a modular approach, deploying models typically involves creating APIs to handle
inference requests. Frameworks like FastAPI and Flask are popular options for this purpose due to
their simplicity and flexibility.
FastAPI is a modern Python framework used to serve machine learning models[27]. It provides
built-in asynchronous programming capabilities, enabling efficient handling of multiple inference
requests simultaneously. FastAPI also has many user-friendly features like generating API docu-
mentation automatically and supporting features like request validation, making it a good choice for
modular deployments.Another alternative is Flask, a widely used Python framework [45]. It allows
developers to create custom endpoints to serve machine learning models with minimal overhead.
While Flask has less features than FastAPI, it remains a viable option for small to medium-scale
deployments where advanced functionalities are not required. Both tools enable modular deploy-
ment workflows but require additional effort to manage scalability and monitoring compared to the
centralized solutions offered by Databricks.
4.5
Monitoring
The final step in the machine learning lifecycle is monitoring the live model to ensure it continues
to perform well over time. This involves tracking metrics, detecting issues like data drift or model
degradation, and triggering retraining workflows when needed. Centralized platforms often provide
integrated monitoring tools, while modular setups require selecting and integrating external solu-
tions. One such tool is Evidently AI [46].
Evidently AI is an open-source framework designed specifically for monitoring machine learning
models in production.
It provides functionality for tracking key performance metrics, detecting
data drift, and identifying anomalies in model behavior. Evidently AI generates dashboards and re-
ports offering insights into issues like shifts in input data distribution or changes in model accuracy.
These features make it an excellent choice for maintaining model performance in modular workflows.

Chapter 5
Evaluation Framework
This chapter outlines the metrics and criteria utilized to assess and compare two different meth-
ods for implementing machine learning workflows: the centralized platform approach, implemented
using Databricks, and the modular approach, which utilizes open-source tools such as Docker, Ku-
bernetes, and Weights & Biases.
The comparison will focus on how these approaches address key challenges in MLOps, including scal-
ability, automation and flexibility. The goal is to provide insights into the strengths and limitations
of each approach, based on qualitative analysis.
5.1
Evaluation Metrics
The following metrics provide the foundation for comparing the centralized and modular approaches:
Scalability
Scalability measures the system’s capacity to handle increasing workloads and adapt to growing
demands. This includes how easily resources can be scaled up or down to accommodate workload
fluctuations and the ability to support multiple model deployments simultaneously.
Automation
Automation evaluates the degree to which repetitive tasks, such as infrastructure provisioning and
model deployment, are streamlined. It focuses on how effectively manual steps are replaced with
automated workflows and the seamless integration of tools and workflows for smooth end-to-end
automation.
Infrastructure Management
Infrastructure management assesses the ease of setting up and maintaining the underlying resources
for MLOps workflows. This includes the intuitiveness and straightforwardness of resource provision-
ing, as well as the effort required to manage and update the infrastructure over time.

Chapter 5 – Evaluation Framework
Cost Efficiency
Cost efficiency evaluates the financial implications of the approach in terms of resource utilization
and operational overhead. It examines how effectively compute and storage resources are used to
minimize costs and the effort required to optimize these costs while maintaining performance.
Experiment Tracking
Experiment tracking examines the ability to monitor, reproduce, and compare experiments during
model development. This includes assessing how well the approach supports tracking and reproduc-
ing experiments, as well as managing multiple model versions and configurations effectively.
Monitoring and Observability
Monitoring and observability evaluate how well the approach supports tracking workflows in pro-
duction. This includes the ability to monitor model performance and system health in real-time, as
well as the effectiveness of detecting anomalies such as data drift or model degradation.
Time to Value
Time to value assesses how quickly developers can transition from initial setup to having a func-
tional system for prototyping and production. This metric considers the learning curve required
for developers to familiarize themselves with tools and workflows, as well as the speed at which the
system allows for the development and deployment of a working prototype.
Flexibility
Flexibility measures the adaptability of the approach to meet diverse use cases and integrate new
tools. This involves evaluating how easily workflows can be modified to meet specific requirements
and how well the system supports the integration of new tools and functionalities.
Evaluation Summary
The comparison between the centralized and modular approaches will be conducted qualitatively
based on practical experience and system behavior.The practical experience involves insights gath-
ered during the implementation of each approach, while system behavior focuses on observations
of how each approach addresses challenges such as scalability, automation, and monitoring. This
framework is designed to systematically address the Primary Objectives of this study. PO1 evaluates
the efficiency and scalability of centralized platforms in handling machine learning workflows. PO2
focuses on automation and its role in reducing operational complexity, while PO3 examines whether
centralized solutions impose limitations on flexibility and adaptability. These objectives guide the
selection of evaluation metrics.
Even though this framework provides a structured approach to
comparison, it has limitations. The fact that evaluation is based solely on qualitative analysis may
introduce subjectivity. Practical insights offer valuable comparisons a more data-driven approach
such as measuring infrastructure costs, deployment latency, or system performance, could enhance
the analysis. Moreover, other factors, such as regulatory compliance, security or user experience,
could also be considered in future studies to provide a more comprehensive assessment.
Evaluation framework aims to highlight the qualitative strengths and limitations of centralized
platforms like Databricks, identify scenarios where modular approaches may outperform central-
ized solutions, and provide practical recommendations for selecting an approach based on project
requirements and organizational priorities.

Automation
Centralized platforms like Databricks simplify automation significantly by providing a range of built-
in tools that are integrated natively across different stages of the machine learning pipeline. For
instance, Databricks Jobs allows for straightforward scheduling and execution of workflows, while
MLflow integration ensures consistent experiment tracking and model deployment. This integrated
environment ensures that all steps in the pipeline work together, reducing the risk of errors and
inconsistencies.
One practical example of this is scheduling a pipeline for daily model retraining. In Databricks, a
user can configure this directly in the Jobs interface, defining tasks, dependencies, and schedules
through an intuitive UI or API. For example, a job can automatically retrieve data, preprocess it,
retrain a model, and update it in the MLflow model registry, all within a single system. This elim-
inates the need for extensive custom scripting or external integrations, making the process highly
efficient and user-friendly.
On the other hand, similar to scalability, automation in modular approaches is also achievable but
comes with additional challenges. These setups often require external tools like Apache Airflow or
GitHub Actions to orchestrate workflows. These tools can fulfill the task and are customizable.
However, they involve additional integration effort, manual configuration, and sometimes even cus-
tom scripting to achieve the same level of coordination provided by centralized platforms. This
can lead to longer setup times and greater operational complexity. While modular approaches offer
flexibility, their automation capabilities often require a higher level of expertise and effort compared
to the ease provided by Databricks.
Infrastructure Management
Databricks, provisioned and managed using Terraform, simplify infrastructure management by au-
tomating the deployment of resources such as clusters and workspaces on Azure. This approach
minimizes manual intervention, ensuring consistency across environments. Additionally, the tight in-
tegration between Terraform and Databricks ensures that infrastructure components work together,
reducing the risk of errors and simplifying operations. In modular approaches, infrastructure man-
agement becomes significantly more complex due to the need to integrate multiple different tools.
Docker, for instance, provides consistent containerized environments, but managing images and
orchestrating workloads with Kubernetes introduces challenges. These include configuring network-
ing, scaling policies, and resource allocation, all of which require domain knowledge. Furthermore,
modular setups often involve manual configuration and the integration of other tools, increasing the
risk of inconsistencies and errors.
Cost Efficiency
Using Databricks comes with higher costs due to their usage based pricing model. These expenses
can be justified for larger organizations or teams prioritizing speed and fully integrated workflows.
However, for smaller teams or organizations with limited budgets, these higher costs may be a
significant constraint. Modular setups, built using open-source tools, can be a more cost-effective
alternative. However, these setups require significant engineering effort to configure and maintain,
and the time invested in customization and integration can lead to higher personnel costs. As a
result, although modular strategies might lower tool-related costs, organizations need to evaluate the
overall expenses, which include the time and expertise necessary to effectively manage and operate
these systems.

Experiment Tracking
In our case, the main advantage lies in the fact that MLflow and experiment tracking are already
integrated within the Databricks platform. These tools are easily accessible to users through an
intuitive UI and the built-in model registry. Additionally, MLflow provides dashboards for tracking
metrics out of the box, eliminating the need for any additional configuration. This allows us to

On the other hand, tools like Weights & Biases (W&B) also offers extensive functionality for tracking
experiments and metrics, but its implementation requires additional effort. Setting up integration
with the pipeline and ensuring its maintenance can be more demanding compared to the built-in
tools provided by Databricks.
Monitoring and Observability
Databricks offers a wide range of built-in tools that significantly enhance monitoring and observabil-
ity across various aspects of the workflow. These tools go beyond monitoring endpoints and models.
For example, Databricks offers capabilities for monitoring the performance of the underlying clus-
ters where jobs are executed. This includes identifying bottlenecks, optimizing resource usage, and
ensuring that computational resources are effectively allocated.
The platform also supports the
creation of internal dashboards that aggregate metrics, logs, and insights in one location. These
dashboards simplify monitoring process, enabling users to track the entire model lifecycle, from ini-
tial data ingestion to final deployment and ongoing maintenance, in a unified manner. This all-in-one
approach reduces the complexity of managing multiple tools and interfaces, making Databricks a
strong choice for organizations prioritizing integrated observability.
On the other hand, tools like Evidently AI can be integrated into pipelines to provide advanced
capabilities for monitoring models. Evidently AI specializes in detecting data drift, model perfor-
mance degradation, and other anomalies, offering detailed insights that may not be directly available
through Databricks’ built-in tools. While this level of granularity can be valuable, particularly in
research-focused workflows, integrating such tools into modular setups requires additional effort and
expertise. In practice, the choice between the two depends on the specific use case.
Time to Value
When it comes to prioritizing time-to-value, the centralized approach emerges as the clear winner.
Databricks significantly reduces the time required not only for initial setup but also for prototyping
and deploying models. Its integrated environment includes pre-built models that can be quickly
served as batch jobs or real-time endpoints on a given dataset. This allows teams to test how these
models perform in specific scenarios and problem contexts even before fine-tuning, streamlining the
prototyping process and helping to validate ideas efficiently.
This capability to rapidly iterate and evaluate models provides a substantial advantage, particularly
in environments where quick decision-making and validation are critical.
By contrast, modular
approaches excel in delivering tailored solutions but require significantly more time at every stage
of the workflow, as outlined in the metrics above. From setup and integration of tools to building
workflows and customizing models, modular pipelines demand additional effort, making the time-
to-value noticeably slower.
While modular setups may be better suited for highly specialized or niche problems, the centralized
approach provides a more efficient pathway for teams focused on rapid prototyping, deployment,
and overall workflow efficiency.

Flexibility
In terms of flexibility, modular setups clearly outperform centralized platforms. Tools like Docker,
Airflow, and FastAPI can be customized to meet specific requirements, making them ideal for highly
specialized workflows. This level of configurability allows modular approaches to adapt to unique
challenges and innovate beyond the limitations of predefined workflows. Such setups are particularly
advantageous for startups or research projects where bespoke solutions are often necessary.
One example of this flexibility is the ability to create custom environments and runtime configura-
tions. Using Docker allow users to define specific Python and required libraries versions providing
full control over environment. This enables teams to experiment with tools and configurations that
centralized platforms may not support.
Databricks, on the other hand, prioritizes efficiency and integration, so it imposes constraints due to
its standardized workflow. It offers some flexibility within its managed environment, allowing users
to select specific Databricks Runtime versions (e.g., Databricks Runtime 15.4 LTS with Python 3.10)
and dynamically install additional libraries using %pip install. However, the inability to modify the
base Python version or install system-level dependencies makes centralized platforms less suitable
for highly tailored use cases.
While centralized platforms like Databricks excel at simplifying workflows and ensuring compatibil-
ity across the machine learning lifecycle, their standardized nature may not meet the demands of
projects requiring extensive customization. Modular setups remain the better choice for environ-
ments that require complete control and flexibility.

Chapter 7
Conclusions
This thesis has explored and compared two distinct approaches to implementing machine learning
workflows: the centralized platform approach, exemplified by Databricks, and the modular ap-
proach, which leverages open-source tools. By evaluating these approaches with metrics mentioned
in evaluation framework this work provides a comprehensive understanding of their strengths and
limitations.While the framework ensures a structured comparison, its qualitative nature may intro-
duce subjectivity. Future research could complement this analysis with empirical benchmarking,
such as performance profiling or cost evaluation in real-world deployments.
The findings highlight that centralized platforms, in our case Databricks, excel in terms of effi-
ciency, ease of use, and rapid deployment. Their integrated environment simplifies automation and
enables fast prototyping. These features make centralized platforms particularly suitable for use
cases where time-to-market is critical and standard workflows can meet project requirements. For
large enterprises, centralized platforms provide significant benefits. They offer robust scalability,
allowing organizations to process massive datasets and run multiple workflows concurrently with-
out additional complexity. Additionally, centralized platforms act as a unified workspace for data
science and data engineering teams, simplifying collaboration, data governance and resource man-
agement. However, these advantages come at a higher cost and with limited flexibility, which may
not be ideal for organizations seeking highly customized solutions. Small teams need to carefully
weigh their requirements. For instance, a small organization working on a business-focused project,
such as implementing machine learning models for customer segmentation or internal reporting,
may benefit from the simplicity and integration of centralized platforms. Databricks minimizes the
need for specialized expertise in areas like infrastructure management or orchestration, enabling
such teams to focus on solving business problems.
On the other hand, modular approaches outperform centralized platforms in flexibility and cost
efficiency, offering the ability to tailor workflows to specific requirements. These setups are par-
ticularly beneficial for small teams or research groups working on cutting-edge projects, such as
developing custom deep learning models or experimenting with new architectures. For example, a
team building a model requiring specific dependencies or hardware configurations may find modular
setups essential. However, these benefits come with trade-offs in terms of setup complexity, longer
time-to-value, and the need for specialized expertise to manage the infrastructure and workflows.
Ultimately, the choice between centralized and modular approaches depends on the specific needs
of the project and organization landscape. For large enterprises prioritizing speed and scalability,
centralized platforms provide an excellent solution. On the other hand, smaller teams, startups, or
research-focused projects may find centralized platforms less feasible due to higher costs or project-

specific requirements that cannot be easily met within standardized workflows. To make an informed
decision, organizations should consider the following key factors:
• The nature of the project: Centralized platforms are ideal for standard machine learning
workflows with a focus on rapid deployment and efficiency. Modular setups, on the other hand,
are better suited for projects requiring extensive customization.
• Team expertise: Centralized platforms reduce the need for specialized knowledge in areas
such as infrastructure management and DevOps, making them accessible to teams with limited
technical expertise. Modular setups demand higher levels of expertise across multiple domains.
• Organizational size and resources: Large enterprises benefit from the scalability and
collaboration features of centralized platforms, while smaller teams may favor modular setups
for cost efficiency, provided they have the required technical capabilities.
• Long-term goals: For organizations seeking a fast path to deployment, centralized plat-
forms are highly effective. However, modular setups may provide greater adaptability and
sustainability as project requirements evolve over time.

have their strengths and limitations. The evaluation framework enabled a structured assessment

emphasis on flexibility ensures that organizations can make choices aligned with their goals, technical
capacities, and project requirements.
By considering these factors, organizations can align their strategy with their needs. Centralized

market, while modular setups provide flexibility and control for specialized workflows.

projects, ensuring their choice aligns with project requirements, team expertise, and long-term goals.
