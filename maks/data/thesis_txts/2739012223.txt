Abstract
Despite knowing the importance of effective and efficient reading in humans’ daily lives,
the presentation of texts in reading has changed little since the invention of printing for
readers to challenge readers. The project ”Reading the Reader” is trying to address this
through a multi­modal detect­intervene system that adapts the text to the reader’s behav­
ior and state. The researchers of ”Reading the Reader” are collecting real­time data for
learning reading patterns using eye­tracking that could inform individual adaptations for
informing the training database to achieve its vision which is an ongoing adaptation to
the current reading process. In order, the project developed a text corpus of AI and non­
AI texts of different authors’ written styles with typographic variations to collect data for
learning using real­time data which necessitates a thorough understanding of AI texts’ im­
pact on reading behavior compared to human­authored (.i.e., non­AI) texts. As part of the
overall ”Reading the Reader” project, this research­based study was aimed to conduct,
to facilitate the researchers of the ”Reading the Reader” project by investigating the text
generation and presentation process of collecting real­time data for learning to build large
datasets for more robust modeling, training, and validation for the overall project. Thus,
this research­based study aimed to learn how AI and non­AI texts of different authors’
writing styles impact individuals’ reading behavior (e.g., thorough reading, skimming, or
immersive reading, varying difficulty levels). The intended outcome was analyzing the
correlation of individual texts to reading behaviors across individuals, possibly leading to
attempting to inform the presentation of text and validating the text generation technique
of ”Reading the Reader” the project for robust learning.
Therefore, to align with the aim, an experimental design study was conducted for within­
subjects, where 13 participants completed six literature­based reading comprehension
tests and questionnaires (e.g., multiple­choice questions and fill­in­the­gaps). Among the
six experiment texts, three were AI, and the other three were non­AI texts of three differ­
ent authors’ written styles (e.g., Hans Christian Andersen, Søren Kierkegaard, and Karen
Blixen) from the Reading the Reader project text corpus. The presentation of stimuli(e.g.,
AI and Non­AI texts) was characterized by one typographic style from the typographic
variations of the ”Reading the Reader” project. Additionally, interviews were conducted
with all participants to supplement the findings. However, statistical methods (i.e., de­
scriptive and inferential) were used to obtain and validate the results. The inferential
(e.g., Linear­Mixed Model (LMM)) test showed a significant impact on reading behavior
across participants’ reading AI texts’ compared to human­authored (.i.e., non­AI) texts.
This study highlighted the main and interaction effect of the author’s identity (i.e., different
author writing styles such as Hans Christian Andersen, Søren Kierkegaard, and Karen
Blixen) and the Presence of AI (i.e., AI, Non­AI) impacting individuals’ reading behavior to
unveil the reading patterns. The test results were supported by model diagnostics (e.g.,
residuals analysis), and model comparisons (e.g., AIC, BIC). The observed effects from
LMMs were validated using Bootstrapping Confidence Intervals. Also, the effect size was
measured using Cohen’s f² and Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) of the final results,
indicating a small but consistent difference favoring the impact of AI and Non­AI texts on
reading behavior, especially measured by fixation duration than saccades metrics (e.g.,
angles and distance). The results suggest that while the observed differences in reading
behavior measured by fixation duration, saccades angle, and saccades distance are sta­
tistically reliable, their practical significance may be modest. It attributes this to limitations
since the experiment’s sample size was smaller than desired.

Introduction
This chapter presents the background of using real­time eye tracking to capture reading
behavior between AI and non­AI texts of different authors. This is done by discussing the
commencement of cutting­edge technologies followed by the motivation of this study. This
chapter concludes with a presentation of the initial problem statement, with a discussion
of the significance of this study, and a brief outline of how the experiment was conducted
as a part of the overall objective.
Reading without effort is one of the most essential skills for helping people live their social,
professional, and personal lives effectively and efficiently. Effortless reading significantly
impacts an individual’s capacity to acquire knowledge in all spheres, from academic to
professional, and from navigating day­to­day living to social life, such as reading bottle la­
bels and road signboards to messaging with friends. The commencement of cutting­edge
technologies opens up new potential to improve and change the landscape of reading
experiences through real­time data analysis. Based on [1, 2], real­time data that includes
eye movements, reading speed, and text comprehension levels produces information to
support reading processes with the help of assistive devices. This flow of information
provides a detailed understanding of reading habits, leading to personalized adjustments
that enhance reading efficiency, engagement, and comprehension [3].
Moreover, cutting­edge eye­tracking technology has made it feasible to gather and ana­
lyze real­time data accurately [4]. Eye tracking technology can collect in­depth data on
where and how long a reader focuses on different parts of the text, giving researchers
a perspective, into their thinking process and areas of struggle to assess readability and
customize content in time according to the readers’ progress and comprehension level [5,
6].
1.1
Motivation: Why is this interesting
Despite knowing the importance of effective and efficient reading in humans’ daily lives,
the presentation of texts in reading has changed little since the invention of printing for
readers to challenge readers. However, new technological advancements have paved the
way for building new reading­assistive technologies by identifying individual reading pat­
terns and adjusting the reading material according to the reader, not only making reading
easier and more accessible but also for the reading impaired to improve comprehension.
The project ”Reading the Reader” is trying to address this through a multi­modal detect­
intervene system that adapts the text to the reader’s behavior and state. The researchers
of ”Reading the Reader” are collecting real­time data for learning reading patterns using
eye­tracking that could inform individual adaptations for informing the training database
to achieve its vision which is an ongoing adaptation to the current reading process. As
part of the overall project, a text corpus, and typographic variations have been developed
to collect real­time data using eye­tracking for learning reading patterns that could inform
individual adaptations and facilitate the reading experience. Thus, meticulous assessing
and understanding these components’ impacts on reading behavior is crucial since they
play vital roles in building a large dataset for more robust modeling, training, and valida­
tion to achieve the ultimate vision of the ”Reading the Reader” project which is facilitating
reading experience through a multi­modal detect­intervene system that adapts the text to
the reader’s behavior and state [7, 8], this research­based study was motivated to conduct
this study as a part of the Reading the Reader” project, to facilitate the project’s ongoing

process by informing and validating its key components such as text presentation (e.g., ty­
pographic variations), and generation techniques (e.g., text corpus of AI and Non­AI texts

data.
The reason, behind incorporating real­time data eye­tracking into reading is to find a feasi­
ble solution for the ever­lasting challenges that readers encounter in educational and daily
life circumstances which serves the main purpose of the ”Reading the Raeder” project.
According to [9], 400 million individuals (i.e., globally 216 million had moderate to se­
vere, and 188 million mild, vision impairment) are affected by vision loss and many are
challenged readers [10], leading to a global productivity loss of $410 billion annually [11].
Despite facing these challenges to date, text presentation style in reading materials has
remained unchanged since the invention of printing. In this paper [12], mentioned how tra­
ditional one­size­fits­all approaches to reading instruction often fail to meet the individual
needs of learners, resulting in varying degrees of engagement and comprehension.
Moreover, reading is one of the important keys for humans to communicate in social and
professional life. Effective and efficient reading ability not only facilitates academic suc­
cess but also opens doors to various career opportunities and enhances an individual’s
ability to participate fully in society [13]. The ability to read effectively and efficiently is
linked to better employment possibilities and higher earning potential, emphasizing the
critical role of reading skills in socioeconomic mobility [14]. Thus, improving reading out­
comes through personalized and adaptive methods can have far­reaching significance for
individual success and social equity.
However, by leveraging real­time data, researchers, educators, designers, and devel­
opers can create adaptive learning environments through assistive technologies that re­
spond to each reader’s unique pace and style, fostering a more inclusive and effective
reading experience [15, 16]. As well as, the opportunity of commoditized eye­trackers,
recent advances in deep learning in related fields, and classical methods (i.e., neural
network­based analysis of reading) set a path to overcome the current challenges in read­
ing [17, 18, 19]. Using real­time data can significantly enhance the ability to diagnose and
remediate reading difficulties. Early identification of reading issues allows for timely in­
terventions, that can prevent long­term struggles from academic to professional life [20,
21]. For instance, real­time data on eye movements can pinpoint specific points where a
reader encounters difficulties, enabling targeted support that addresses the root causes
analysis of comprehension breakdowns [1]. This proactive approach not only improves
individual reading outcomes but also contributes to broader educational and professional
goals by promoting literacy and reducing educational discrepancies.
In addition, the continuous feedback loop facilitated by real­time data supports a goal­
oriented growth­based learning process. Since readers receive immediate adjustments
and feedback based on their current performance, they are more likely to stay motivated
and engaged. This iterative cycle of assessment and adaptation encourages persistent
improvement and helps readers develop a deeper, more confident command of reading
skills [22, 23, 24, 25, 26].

different authors has been developed to collect the real­time eye tracked data for learning
to inform the training database [7, 8]. Despite the rapid advancements in AI­generated text
technologies, significant gaps remain in understanding how these texts impact readers’
behavior compared to human­authored texts. Existing studies suggest that AI­generated
texts often fail to capture the intricate patterns of natural language, and lack the coher­
ence and readability that characterize human­authored texts, potentially affecting reading
engagement and comprehension [27, 28]. This raises concerns about their effectiveness
in professional settings where strong reading comprehension is essential along with pre­
senting the texts to one’s need. Therefore, integrating AI­generated text in the Reading
the Reader project necessitates a thorough understanding of its impact on reading behav­
ior compared to human­authored texts. Hence, this study intended to bridge these gaps
as a part of the ”Reading the Reader” project by investigating the differences through
an experimental design study with participants, focusing on how different authored texts
(e.g., Hans Christian Andersen, Søren Kierkegaard, and Karen Blixen) and the presence
of AI influence reading patterns during reading, using eye­tracking technology to capture
detailed reading patterns to extract user behavior as participants read both AI­generated
and human­authored texts.
Research Objective The primary objective of this study was to create an experiment
with participants to collect real­time data using an eye tracker to analyze whether there
are any noticeable differences in the reading patterns by exploring reading behaviors
such as e.g. skimming or immersive reading, or varying levels of difficulty while reading
both AI and non­AI texts of different authors with fixed presentation styles. In particular,
the study aimed to investigate how author identity (e.g., Hans Christian Andersen, Søren
Kierkegaard, and Karen Blixen), the presence of AI, and their interactions influence read­
ing behaviors. Hence, this study was conducted through a within­subjects experimental
design by building a data collection tool, where participants read both types of content
while their eye movements were tracked. This experiment was carried out with a data
collection tool called ReadSense which was built as a part of this study to present exper­
iment stimuli and collect the participants’ data responses on the presented stimuli such
as how long participants stayed at each reading text by logging every event during the
experiment, and the answers of the presented questionnaires. By utilizing eye­tracking
tools like the Tobii Pro system which collected the eye metrics such as gaze points and the
data collection tool ReadSense this study aimed to collect real­time reading patterns data
to extract reading behavior measured by fixation duration, saccade angles, and distance
to investigate the correlation of AI and Non­AI texts influence on reading behavior. Since,
all of these provide valuable insights into the cognitive effort and efficiency [4, 2].

Research Significance
This research study is significant as it aims to provide empirical evidence on the differ­
ences in reading patterns elicited by AI and non­AI texts of different authors with fixed
presentation styles. By understanding these differences, researchers of the Reading the
Reader project can better assess the existing capabilities and limitations of its AI text gen­
eration methods for the project text corpus and text presentation style (i.e., typographic
variations), contributing to enhancing its text generation and presenting technique if AI­
generated content requires modifications to be more reader­friendly and if readers can
effectively and efficiently read the presented texts with customized typographic variations.
Additionally, this research will not only offer insights and validation into the broader im­
plications of integrating AI­generated content into different areas of work such as experi­
mentation, educational and professional contexts, where the quality and readability of text

General background
Understanding the potential of real­time data in reading enhancement requires familiarity
with the foundational technologies of eye­tracking and natural language processing (NLP).
Eye­tracking technology measures the point of gaze, capturing metrics such as fixation
duration, saccades, and regressions to study visual attention and cognitive processing
during reading [2, 5]. This data reveals how readers interact with text, indicating areas of
difficulty and cognitive load, which are critical for developing adaptive reading systems [1].
Understanding the complexities of how humans read and process text is crucial for de­
veloping technologies that can enhance reading experiences. This section dives into the
experimental design used in reading research, the eye­tracking technology employed,
statistical methods for analyzing data, the mechanics of human reading, and the spe­
cific metrics derived from eye movement data to provide a comprehensive overview to
understand this study well.
2.3.1
Experimental Design
Experimental design in reading research often employs within­subjects designs, where
the same participants are exposed to various conditions to control for individual differ­
ences. This design enhances the reliability of the findings by reducing variability due
to individual differences in reading ability and cognitive processing [33]. In the context
of eye­tracking studies, participants typically read texts presented on a computer screen
while their eye movements are recorded. Tasks can range from reading simple sentences
to comprehending complex passages, with variables such as text difficulty, font size, and
layout being manipulated to observe their effects on reading behavior.
2.3.2
Eye­Tracking Technology
Eye­tracking technology, such as the Tobii Pro system, is pivotal in capturing detailed
data on where and how long a reader looks at specific parts of a text. The Tobii Pro sys­
tem, for instance, uses infrared light to detect eye positions and movements, offering high
accuracy and precision (Tobii Pro, 2021). This technology records various metrics, in­
cluding fixation duration, saccades, and regressions, which provide insights into cognitive
processing during reading [2].
2.3.3
Statistical Methods
Analyzing eye­tracking data requires robust statistical methods to draw meaningful con­
clusions. Common approaches include mixed­effects models, which account for both
fixed effects (e.g., text difficulty) and random effects (e.g., individual differences) [34].
Time­series analysis is also used to examine patterns for reading a text. Moreover, clus­
tering and classification algorithms can identify distinct reading behaviors or strategies

7
==PAGE:18==
among participants [35]. These methods enable researchers to interpret complex data
sets and understand the underlying processes of reading.
2.3.4
How Humans Read
Human reading involves a series of rapid eye movements called saccades, interspersed
with brief periods where the eyes are relatively still, known as fixations. During fixations,
visual information is processed, and comprehension occurs [5]. The average fixation
duration is about 200­250 milliseconds, but this can vary depending on text complexity and
reader proficiency. Skilled readers typically exhibit shorter fixations, fewer regressions,
and longer saccades, indicating more efficient processing and better comprehension [1].
2.3.5
Eye Movement Data (Metrics)
Eye­tracking provides a wealth of data that can be analyzed to understand reading be­
havior. Key metrics include:
• Fixation Duration: The length of time the eye remains stationary, indicating pro­
cessing load. Longer fixations often suggest difficulty or complexity in the text [1].
• Saccades: Rapid eye movements between fixations. The length and direction of
saccades can indicate reading fluency and strategy.
• Regressions: Backward saccades to previously read text, often signaling compre­
hension difficulties or the need to re­evaluate information [5].
• Gaze Path: The sequence of fixations and saccades, which can be visualized to
show the reader’s navigation through the text [2].
These metrics are essential for developing adaptive reading technologies that respond to
real­time data, providing personalized support to enhance reading proficiency and com­
prehension.
2.3.6
Eye Movement Classification Algorithms
Eye movement classification algorithms play a crucial role in analyzing eye­tracking data
by categorizing different types of eye movements such as fixations, saccades, and re­
gressions. These algorithms use various computational techniques to process raw eye­
tracking data and classify them into meaningful categories, facilitating the understanding
of reading behaviors. The following are the most commonly use algorithms:
• Velocity­Threshold Identification (I­VT): This algorithm classifies eye movements
based on the velocity of the eye. Movements above a certain velocity threshold are
classified as saccades, while those below are considered fixations [36].
• Dispersion­Threshold Identification (I­DT): The I­DT algorithm classifies fixations
based on the spatial dispersion of eye positions. Eye positions within a certain dis­
persion threshold are grouped as a fixation, and those that fall outside are classified
as saccades [2].
• Hidden Markov Models (HMM): HMMs use probabilistic models to classify eye
movements by learning patterns from labeled data. They are particularly useful in
distinguishing between various reading states and identifying complex eye move­
ment sequences [37, 38].
• Machine Learning Approaches: Recent advancements include the use of super­
vised learning algorithms like support vector machines (SVM) and neural networks
to classify eye movements. These approaches can handle large datasets and cap­
ture intricate patterns in eye­tracking data [39, 40].

3.2.1
Design Type: Within­subjects
For this experimental study, Within­subjects were chosen as a design type. This repeated
measure experimental design type like this study; is useful especially in controlling for in­
dividual differences since each participant serves as their control, thereby reducing vari­
ability and increasing the power of the experiment. The key features of Within­subjects
design aligned with the need of this study such as the same participants across conditions
(i.e., Each participant is tested under multiple conditions while their eye movements are
tracked), reduction of variability (i.e., Having the same participants for all test conditions,
individual differences (e.g., fixation duration, saccadic movements) that could potentially
confound the results were minimized). This increased the sensitivity of the experiment
to detect actual differences due to the experimental manipulation [43], increased Statisti­
cal power (.i.e., All participants compared among themselves across different conditions,
fewer participants were generally needed to achieve the same level of statistical power as
compared to a between­subjects design [44]), and efficient use of participants (i.e., More
efficient as it required fewer participants to obtain the same amount of data [45]).
Therefore this design type opened up the opportunity to find several key insights of this
study such as consistency and reliability (i.e., Assessing consistency of reading patterns
for each text across different participants, which helped to validate text generation meth­
ods), pattern Analysis(i.e., By analyzing how reading patterns vary for different texts within
the same individuals, identifying specific features of the texts that influence reading be­
havior [1] and validation of methods (i.e., the data obtained can be used to validate the
methods and tools used for text generation of the Reading the Reader text corpus by
demonstrating predictable changes in reading patterns).
On the other hand, this design type also brought some potential challenges in this ex­
periment study such as order effects (i.e., Participants might become fatigued or learn
from earlier conditions, which could affect their performance in later conditions. Although,
counterbalancing the order of text presentation can mitigate these effects [46]), practice
Effects (i.e., Repeated testing might lead to improvements in performance simply due to
familiarity with the task. This can be managed by including practice trials and ensuring a

3.5.1
Classifying eye movements: IDT
The eye­tracking device used in this study recorded the participants’ gaze positions and
movements at high temporal resolution. The data collected included raw gaze coordi­
nates over time, which were subsequently analyzed to identify fixations and saccades.
The Identification by Dispersion Threshold (IDT) algorithm was employed to classify eye
movements into fixations and saccades. Fixations refer to the periods when the eye re­
mains relatively stationary, allowing for detailed visual processing of the text. Saccades
are rapid eye movements between fixations, representing the transition of gaze from one
point to another. They are essential for navigating through the text and capturing new
information [5]. These are crucial for understanding how readers engage with different
parts of the text [2].
IDT is a widely used method in eye­tracking research due to its robustness and reliability
Unveiling Reading Patterns
25
==PAGE:36==
[36]. The following steps were considered to classify eye movement as fixation or sac­
cades:
• Dispersion Calculation: The algorithm calculates the dispersion of gaze points
within a sliding time window. Dispersion (D) is the measure of how spread out the
gaze points are within this window and is given by the formula: The dispersion (D)
of gaze points within a sliding time window is calculated using the formula:
D =
√
(xmax −xmin)2 + (ymax −ymin)2
where (xmax, ymax) and (xmin, ymin) are the maximum and minimum gaze coordinates
within the window, respectively.
• Threshold Application: If the dispersion within the window is below a predeter­
mined threshold (T), the gaze points are classified as part of a fixation. If the dis­
persion exceeds the threshold, the points are classified as part of a saccade [36].
• Temporal Window Adjustment: The window size is adjusted dynamically to opti­
mize the classification accuracy, ensuring that fixations and saccades are accurately
identified based on their temporal and spatial characteristics [2].

3.7.2
Parametric assumptions check
After initial analysis of the data, it was observed that the data for fixation duration saccade
distance and saccade angles did not meet the assumptions of normality and equality of
variances of the parametric method which set an alternative test path for to analyze the
findings (see in A.12). However, In this experimental study parametric statistical tests
were used to analyze the experiment data, since the authors in [55] discussed scenarios
where parametric tests were performed on non­normal data and argued that, in some
cases, it may be preferable to non­parametric alternatives. Also, the works of literature in
[56, 57, 58, 59], showed that much research work has been done with parametric (e.g.,
ANOVA, two­sample t­test, Linear Mixed Models (LMM)) tests and obtained significant
results, despite not meeting the necessary assumptions.
Parametric test method: Linear Mixed Model
To choose the parametric test for this study, the following papers were reviewed that were
similar to the experiment nature­wise of this study such as within­subjects, eye­tracking
data, and use of test methods, particularly Linear Mixed Model. More specifically, the stud­
ies that employed LMMs on eye­tracking data from a within­subjects design, discussed
the application of LMMs in the context of data that do not meet all parametric assump­
tions.
The paper [59] discussed the application of Linear Mixed Models (LMM) in psycholinguis­
tic research, including eye­tracking data, and addressed the robustness of LMMs even
when some assumptions (e.g., normality of residuals) are violated. In [60] study used
LMMs to analyze eye­tracking data from a within­subjects design focused on reading and
discussed how the models account for data complexities despite potential assumption vi­
olations. And [61] examined eye­tracking data with LMMs in a within­subjects design to
explore reading behavior and noted the robustness of LMMs to deviations from normality
and other parametric assumptions. This paper [62], used LMMs to analyze eye­tracking
data in a within­subjects design for reading and highlighted how LMMs can handle viola­
tions of parametric assumptions such as non­normality and heteroscedasticity.
Moreover, The review paper [63] discussed multiple studies where LMMs were used to
analyze eye­tracking data in within­subjects designs for reading. It addressed how re­
searchers deal with non­normal data distributions and other assumption violations.
After a meticulous review, for this study, the parametric statistics method which is Linear
Mixed model effects was adopted for the within­subjects design considering effects with
2 factors and 3 levels as well as their interactions.

random effects in repeated measures data, making them ideal for studies examining the

author 2, author 3)on reading metrics while accounting for the variability among subjects.
Hence, in this study context, researchers used LMM to model the impact of author iden­
tity and the presence of AI (e.g., AI, non­AI) on various reading metrics while accounting

4.1.3
A note on outliers
outliers can significantly influence statistical analyses, and their presence often raises
concerns about data validity. In this context, outliers in fixation duration, saccade distance,
and saccade velocity might suggest unusual reading behavior or irregularities in the data
collection process.
Despite the rigorous pre­processing of the datasets, including using z­scores to identify
and remove outliers some data points still appeared as outliers, particularly in the fixation
duration and saccade angles datasets in the visual inspection using rain cloud plots in the
processed datasets. Therefore, the following points are considered to decide whether to
exclude or include these outliers in the final analysis:
• Natural Variability in Reading Behavior: Reading patterns can vary widely among
individuals due to differences in cognitive processing, familiarity with the text, and
reading strategies. Outliers might represent genuine variations in how participants
interact with the text rather than errors or noise in the data [70, 71].
• Importance of Preserving Data Integrity: Removing outliers can sometimes lead
to a loss of valuable information. In studies involving human behavior, extreme
values can provide insights into the range of responses and contribute to a more
comprehensive understanding of the phenomena being studied [72, 71].
Unveiling Reading Patterns
37
==PAGE:48==
• Robust Statistical Methods: Many modern statistical techniques are robust to the
presence of outliers. These methods can mitigate the impact of extreme values
without necessitating their removal. For example, using non­parametric tests or ap­
plying robust regression techniques can provide reliable results even in the presence
of outliers [73, 74, 75].
• Contextual Interpretation: In the context of eye­tracking data, outliers might in­
dicate moments of increased cognitive load, difficulty in processing certain parts of
the text, or other meaningful occurrences. Rather than dismissing these data points,
they should be examined within the broader context of the study’s objectives and
hypotheses [2].
Thus, considering the above points, this study opted to retain the outliers in fixation dura­
tion saccade distances, and saccade velocity datasets for the following reasons:
• To ensure a full range of reading behaviors is represented in the analysis.
• To avoid the potential loss of significant information that might be crucial for under­
standing the nuances of reading patterns across different texts.
• To leverage robust statistical methods that can handle the presence of outliers with­
out compromising the validity of our findings.
By maintaining these outliers, the researcher aimed to provide a more accurate and holis­
tic view of the reading behaviors observed in this study, thereby enhancing the reliability
and interpretability of the final results and findings.

Discussion
This section analyses the results presented in the previous section compared to the final
problem statement of this study, their implications, and their significance in a broader
context of the ”Reading the Reader” project.
5.1
Achievement
The primary objective of this study was to create an experiment with participants to collect
real­time data using an eye tracker to analyze whether there are any noticeable differ­
ences in the reading patterns by exploring reading behaviors such as e.g. skimming or
immersive reading, or varying levels of difficulty while reading both AI and non­AI texts
of different authors with fixed presentation styles. In particular, the study’s final prob­
lem statement was formulated with aiming to investigate how author identity (e.g., Hans
Christian Andersen, Søren Kierkegaard, and Karen Blixen), the presence of AI, and their
interactions influence reading behaviors, measured by fixation duration, saccade angle,
and saccade distance 2.4.
To achieve the objective of this study, a reading application called ”ReadSense” was built
to collect participant eye movements simultaneously with the Tobii Pro eye­tracker while
experimenting (i.e., reading texts on the display (ReadSense UI) and answering ques­
tions). After collecting all the data, subsequent data processing, exploratory analysis,
and statistical analysis, such as the exploratory analysis illustrated regression, re­reading,
focus points on texts (i.e., attention distribution), and reading paths, also descriptive statis­
tics exhibited the overall data distribution characteristics of the reading behavior observed
in this study. On the other hand, implementing the LMMs (i.e., the inferential statistics) the
study observed significant differences between AI­generated and human­authored texts
(i.e., non­AI).
Notes on Significant Differences Observed in the Results section 4.4 are presented below
by following up with final problem statements research questions and hypotheses:
1. By recalling the first research question’s hypothesis from the final problem state­
ments, it proposes that there would be significant differences, either in author iden­
tity (Author 1, Author 2, Author 3), the presence of AI, or in their interaction impacting
reading behavior, as measured by the fixation duration.
• This study’s findings reject the null hypothesis of the first research question
based on the results shown in Table 4.6 and conclude that there are statisti­
cally significant differences in reading behavior, indicating that the interaction
between the author and AI has a tangible impact on fixation duration.
2. The second research question’s hypothesis proposes that there would be significant
differences, either in author identity (Author 1, Author 2, Author 3), the presence of
AI, or in their interaction impacting reading behavior, as measured by the saccade
angles.

to influence users to make larger saccadic movements, especially for content

5.2
Comparing
The study in [27] was previously discussed in the background section, underscoring the
ongoing challenges in AI­generated content, whether in the context of human­authored
text or AI. They reveal that while AI technologies have made considerable advances,
there remain notable gaps in how AI­generated content compares to human­authored
texts, particularly in areas that require creativity, coherence, and ease of comprehension.
[27] has outlined in its findings that there are significant disparities between AI­generated
and human­authored texts, which also stand out in this study’s findings in general.
The study in [5] mentioned that the duration of fixations typically ranges from 200 to 250
milliseconds, with saccades taking about 20 to 40 milliseconds. However, in this study the
average fixation duration is 256.96 ms scored a bit higher than the usual range, indicating
that participants may have needed more time to process the text or that the text might have
been more complex. However, the average saccade duration is 139.30 milliseconds,
which is significantly longer than the typical range of 20 to 40 milliseconds. This could
indicate that participants had slower eye movements, possibly due to the difficulty of the
material or the participants’ reading habits.
Moreover, the study [32] which work is more relevant to this study since the study explored
fixation duration metric effect on Human­Translated text to Machine­Translated text, found
significant differences in how readers process MT texts versus HT texts and mentioned
the quality of the machine translation had a direct impact on fixating. They stated that
readers generally spent more time fixating on MT texts, which indicates that MT output
often requires more cognitive effort to comprehend. This increased cognitive load was
reflected in longer fixation durations and more frequent regressions (i.e., when readers
go back to re­read a portion of the text) which is also reflected in this study’s findings
for KarenB (AI vs. Non­AI): There is a noticeable increase in fixation duration when AI
is involved (234.27 ms) compared to when AI is not involved (230.23 ms). Additionally,
they mentioned that the higher­quality MT outputs led to fewer fixations and regressions,
making them closer to the reading patterns observed with HT texts. However, even the
best MT outputs did not fully match the ease and fluidity with which readers processed
human translations which are also observed in this study’s finding where fixation duration
decreases for SorenK (AI vs. Non­AI) from 231.14 ms (Non­AI) to 225.32 ms (AI) also
for H.C Anderson from 231.60 ms (Non­AI) to 229.19 ms (AI). This indicates that the

5.3
Limitations
Although the experiment was designed to reduce the possible limitations, there might be
limitations present in the study. There are several possible limitations when it comes to
the external validity of the findings if they are to be generalized across the population.
The findings of this study may not generalize beyond the specific AI and non­AI texts
used in this experiment. Despite measures taken to limit this during experiment design,
it is possible that different AI and non­AI texts could potentially obtain different participant
responses, and subsequently different results.
The sample of the population that was chosen for this experiment has similarities in their
perception of reading literature content. This may be a factor that influenced the results.
In addition to this, this study has a total sample size of 13 participants which is notably less
than the desired number of participants (approximately 30 participants as per the sample
size calculation). A larger sample size closer to the desired amount would provide more
reliable results and increased generalizability. Lastly, this study only considers AI­ and
non­AI texts of three different authors in the literature context, and different degrees of
writing styles were not explored, which limits the understanding of the impact of headlines
on trust.
Lastly, the fixed environment might have an impact on participants’ natural reading pat­
terns since the experiment was conducted in the lab. The fixed text presentation style
might have an impact as well on the participants’ reading behavior as it didn’t fall entirely
into their usual reading habits. These findings were elicited from the additional informal
interviews that were conducted with all participants after each experiment to supplement
the findings. (see in A.5).
5.4
Future works
In future work, additional metrics of the eye (e.g., pupil dilation) and reader data( age, sex,
reading habits, preferences) along with the reading information (e.g., reading sequence,
questionnaire answers) can be used to implement different models for inferential statistical
tests as well descriptive statistics and exploratory analysis to verify and complement the
results obtained provided the assumptions are met.
In addition, subgroup analysis can be done to investigate whether the observed differ­
ences vary across different subgroups. Specifically based on the data collected in this
experiment, it can be analyzed whether demographics (age and sex), or different rela­
tionships with speed on reading devices, domain knowledge, language proficiency, and
reading preferences on devices have an impact/effect on the results. It could also be
analyzed whether the effect is consistent across different demographic groups or even
types of AI and non­AI texts. Along gaze points, pupil diameter can be used to measure
reading behaviors that may serve valuable insights for this study. All of these can provide
insights into the generalizability of the findings, by enhancing study reliability.
Moreover, to gain additional qualitative insights into the nature of AI and non­AI texts, an
analysis of the AI and non­AI content can be performed. This will help to understand the
specific characteristics that contribute to the reading behavior that participants exhibited
while experimenting, perhaps providing more insights into the quantitative results. To gain
further insights into the participants’ perspectives, surveys can be performed to assess the
participants’ thought processes. This would provide more context when understanding

6
Conclusion
This study Uneveiling Reading Pattens: Exploring the Impact of Writing Styles
on Reading Behavior through Eye Tracking was conducted as a part of the ”Reading
the Reader” project where the project integrates AI texts of different authors for learning
reading behavior using eye­tracking data to build a large­scale dataset for more robust
modeling, training, and validation along with its other goals which aim to serve the ultimate
vision of the project to enable a new level of adaptive support for readers with impaired
vision or cognition. Since the integration of AI texts plays a crucial role in learning human
reading behavior to serve the bigger goal, thus this study intended to investigate the im­
pact of authors’s identity, the presence of AI, and their interactions on reading behavior
measured by fixation duration, saccade angles, and saccade distance so that pitfalls can
be determined and text generating as well as presenting process can be adapted accord­
ingly. In order, this study designed a within­subjects experiment, by building the reading
application (ReadSense) to collect real­time reading data from the reading app and the
eye­tracker simultaneously in a controlled lab environment and finally analyzed the data
through statistical analysis. This study’s investigation into the impact of AI and non­AI
texts of the different authors (e.g., Hans) provides evidence of a significant difference
in reading behaviors by fixation duration, saccade angles, and saccade distance. The
result from the Linear Mixed Model (LMM) effects indicated a difference in reading behav­
ior, with interaction effects between the author and AI, indicating that the impact of AI on
user attention and exploration patterns varies depending on the content’s author(e.g., AI
Karen, Human Karen). This was supported by bootstrapped confidence intervals, con­
firming the robustness of the findings. However, effect size measures, Cohen’s f², and
Intraclass Correlation Coefficient (ICC) indicate a small difference in reading behaviors
between AI and non­AI in particular fixation duration. It is important to note that while sta­
tistically significant, the observed effect may not be substantial in real­world terms. The
findings contribute to the understanding of the impacts of AI and non­AI texts on reading
behavior for the bigger purpose of the ”Reading the Reader” project. Future research may
dig deeper into language, cognition, and other factors that may influence the perception
of AI and non­AI texts and inform individual adaptations which may facilitate improving
modeling and classification of reading patterns for the project.

Appendix ­ Iteration 1: Results from informal evaluation
#1
2 Participants: ”Reading the Readar project” Researchers
An informal evaluation was done with the ”Reading the Readar project” Researchers by
showing the initial prototype See in Appendix A.6 based on the initial adaptation of this
study’s data collection requirement.
1. We want to create a book­like reading experience during the experiments, page by
page would be preferable to scrolling. It may help to stimulate the natural way of
reading patterns during the experiment. Also, scrolling might increase the intricacy
of understanding the eye data in terms of understanding where participants looked
at
2. Presenting stimuli in one column in the middle with 80 characters per line would help
to keep the eye movements stable during the experiment that may not cause the
unwanted jump of eyes which would add any meaningful observation
3. Fixed layout and presentation style (participants can’t make any changes in reading
settings while reading) might increase the validity of the findings by ensuring that the
observed results are only caused by the stimuli, not by any other factors. The color
contrast of text and the background would be good to follow minimalism as we are
interested in observing reading patterns in real­life book­like reading experiences
which are mostly in black(font) and white(background), off­whitish background, and
black(font).
4. Allowing users to back and forth from passage to the question may elicit the dif­
ference in the reading patterns which might illustrate the changes in the reading
behavior (skimming to immersive reading, engagement).
