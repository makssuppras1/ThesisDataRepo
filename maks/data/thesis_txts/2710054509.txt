Abstract
After the successful coupling of European day-ahead and intra-day markets, the integration of balanc-
ing markets is the next step in the European Commission’s vision of an integrated European electricity
market. PICASSO, the platform for shared activation of aFRR balancing energy, will be launched for
the first participants by mid-2022. Stakeholders in the electricity market benefit from understanding
what impacts can be expected from the go-live of PICASSO on the balancing markets and system
stability. This study aims to investigate the economic and technical impacts of PICASSO, using a
case study including Belgium, the Netherlands, and Germany.
The study bases itself on publicly
available data from 2021. Based on the results, PICASSO can be expected to generate an additional
economic surplus of e400 million compared to the status quo. Prices are expected to converge between
countries. Therefore, previously expensive areas will benefit more from PICASSO than cheap areas.
Finally, a technical analysis has shown that the collaboration through PICASSO has the potential of
improving system stability.

1.
Introduction to the research
Electricity generation and demand must be continuously balanced within electricity systems to main-
tain system stability. Market operators and market parties schedule their generation and demand in
advance, but unforeseen events and forecast errors often result in remaining imbalances. Transmission
system operators (TSOs) use balancing reserves to counteract any close-to-realtime imbalances.1
Due to the limited forecastability of variable renewable energy (VRE) resources, a progressive
integration of VRE sources will possibly stimulate the need for balancing reserves in Europe in the
near future [1]. However, with a collaboration project called Grid Control Cooperation (GCC), the
German and Austrian TSOs have shown that shared management of balancing reserves can increase the
efficiency of the management of balancing reserves. Indeed, the need for balancing reserves activation
was even reduced despite increasing VRE generation [2]. Other countries have joined the collaboration
project, which therefore transformed into the International Grid Control Cooperation (IGCC).
In line with the success of GCC and the successful integration of the day-ahead (DA) and intra-
day (ID) markets in Europe [3], the European Commission (EC) has issued the Electricity Balancing
Guideline (EB-GL, [4]) to further integrate the balancing markets of its Member States in 2017. The
EBGL aligns with the vision of the EC to establish an integrated energy market across Europe [5].
However, the heterogeneity of balancing markets among EU Member States makes the coupling
of balancing markets challenging. For example, balancing products can have diverse characteristics,
such as activation time, bid sizes and allowed technologies [6]. Moreover, the interaction between the
TSO and balancing service providers (BSPs) differs from area to area, especially regarding the degree
to which the TSO controls the activation of the BSPs’ balancing units [6].
In order to comply with the EB-GL, the European TSOs have been working on establishing
common European platforms for balancing reserves. The platform for the integration of automatic
frequency restoration reserve (aFRR) markets is called PICASSO2. It should be launched by mid-2022.
The focus of this study will be on PICASSO, as aFRR markets have the highest trade volumes in
most European balancing markets and form a crucial short-term ancillary service [7].3
PICASSO’s operation relies on two mechanisms: imbalance netting (IN) and cross-border activa-
tion (XBA). IN is the process where imbalances with opposite signs between areas “compensate” for
each other so that activation of reserves in opposite directions can be avoided. XBA refers to solving
domestic imbalances by activating reserves in another area.
It is important for stakeholders in European electricity markets to understand what economic
and technical impacts PICASSO might have on the balancing markets. A proper appreciation of the
potential changes that PICASSO may induce on activation volumes and prices, as well as imbalance
settlement prices, could allow BSPs, suppliers and ultimately consumers to better prepare for the
impacts these changes may have on them. Furthermore, TSOs considering joining PICASSO in the
near future benefit from a good understanding of its savings and the effect on total economic surplus,
especially since transaction costs to switch to a different market set-up can be high [8]. Moreover, the

transparency of their TSOs regarding data publication.

2.
European electricity markets
In order to keep the electricity system frequency close to its nominal value, 50 Hertz (Hz) in Europe,
electricity generation and demand must be balanced at all times within the system. As in many places
worldwide today, the electricity balance in Europe is preserved through a series of liberalised energy
and capacity markets with sequential time frames. Acknowledging that there are variations among
European countries, this chapter presents the main characteristics of European markets today.
2.1
Sequential electricity markets
Electricity supply is planned throughout different time frames to meet the electricity demand. For the
longest time frame, electricity can be traded bilaterally through future contracts, or futures. Futures
are deals between a supplier and a consumer that can be closed months or even years before delivery
and describe the electricity supply against a fixed price. Futures allow customers to hedge themselves
against the risk of high electricity prices in the uncertain future [10].
A large part of the electricity is traded in the day-ahead (DA) market one day before the deliv-
ery.5 Each morning, Balance Responsible Parties (BRPs), i.e. market parties who trade a portfolio
of energy supply or demand, submit their energy bids for every hour of the following day to the
market operator. In order of increasing (decreasing) bid price for supply (demand) bids, the mar-
ket operator aggregates the bids to a supply (demand) curve. The supply curves are also commonly
called merit-order curves. For each hour of the following day, a market-solving algorithm schedules
the generators and demand in a way that maximises economic surplus, thereby taking into account
capacity limitations of interconnectors for the electricity exchange between coupled market zones [12].
Economic surplus is maximised at the intersection between the supply and demand curve, also called
the equilibrium point. The day-ahead price then becomes equal to this “equilibrium price”, i.e. the
price of the most expensive scheduled bid within a non-congested area.
BRPs are financially responsible for maintaining their net supply at the scheduled level [13]. They
are penalised for deviating from their scheduled net electricity injection into the grid (i.e. supply
minus consumption). However, demand and generation cannot be predicted with 100% accuracy, and
usually forecasts are updated as they approach delivery time. For example, weather circumstances
(and therefore expected VRE generation) may differ from what is foreseen, or demand forecasts need
revisions. BRPs can respond to these new insights by trading in intra-day markets. The intra-day
markets allow BRPs to update their schedules based on the newly-available information regarding
demand positions and available supply. Most European intra-day markets are continuous markets:
trade takes place around the clock, in some cases up to 30 minutes before delivery [14]. Here, demand
and supply bids are matched on a first-come, first-serve basis, where best bids are scheduled first [15].
Still, small imbalances will inevitably remain. Causes for this can be, for example, unscheduled
plant outages, ramping limitations at hour changes and intra-day forecast errors [16]. Within the last
hour of delivery, Transmission System Operators (TSOs) have the responsibility to solve the remain-
ing system imbalances. This process of solving (close-to-)real-time system imbalances to maintain
frequency stability is called Load Frequency Control (LFC) [17]. Nowadays, TSOs usually do not own

2.2
Balancing markets in Europe
As balancing practices vary around Europe, this section will describe European standard balancing
products.
These ancillary services in Europe can be grouped in Frequency Containment Reserves
(FCR), Frequency Restoration Reserves (FRR) and Replacement Reserves (RR) [17].8 After a small
introduction of the balancing products, the main focus of this section will be on automatic FRR
(aFRR) markets, given the focus of this study on aFRR market integration.9.
The sequence of responses to disturbances is schematically displayed in Figure 1. When an im-
balance occurs, FCR is the first to respond. The FCR process is used to stabilise the frequency after
immediate disturbances.
FCR are fully activated within 30 seconds [17].
After 30 seconds, FRR
gradually replace the FCR. FRR consists of automatic FRR (aFRR) and FRR that are manually
or semi-automatically activated (mFRR). The function of aFRR is to bring back the frequency to
its nominal value of 50 Hz, and to restore the net power exchange with exchanging areas to their
scheduled values [17]. aFRR usually have a faster activation time than mFRR, which are activated for
longer-lasting disturbances. Finally, RR can be used to free up the upstream ancillary services after
the system has settled to a steady state and the frequency is brought back to a value close to 50 Hz
[17].

Load-Frequency Control (LFC) aims to maintain frequency stability within an area while keeping net
interchange power with neighbouring LFC areas at scheduled values. This section briefly introduces
the most basic elements of load frequency control (LFC). The explanation follows to a large extent
[26].
3.1
Load frequency control: status quo
The two LFC objectives, maintaining frequency stability while keeping net interchange power with
neighbouring LFC areas at scheduled values, are combined in a single indicator called Area Control
Error (ACE).13 The ACE is defined as:

cent change in frequency-dependent load for every % change in frequency.

3.2
Load frequency control: market integration
With the introduction of cooperation platforms such as IGCC and PICASSO (see Section 2.3), the
aFRR bids from all participating areas are sent to a central platform, together with the local aFRR
demand estimations and available cross-border line capacity estimations (Figure 4). Based on these
inputs, an Activation Optimisation Function (AOF) optimises the use of aFRR. The platform sends a
correction signal (Pcorr.) towards each LFC area to correct the ACE. The corrected ACE (ACEcorr.)
is fed into the local LFC controllers much like in the old situation (section 3.1). The AOF provides
two main contributions relative to the old, “isolated” situation.
First, imbalances can be netted between LFC areas. This means that positive imbalances in one
area can be offset against negative imbalances in another area, so that in total less aFRR will have to
be activated. For example, if area A has an upward imbalance of +20 MW and area B has a downward
imbalance of -30 MW, the old situation would require a total downward activation of -20 MW and
upward activation of +30 MW. In the new situation, however, these imbalances are netted towards a
net imbalance of -10 MW, meaning that in total only +10 MW upward activation is required.
Naturally, the reduction in aFRR activation from IN has the potential to save on activation costs.
In addition, situations could present themselves where the total system ACE is stabilised faster than in
the old situation. For example, LFC areas with opposite imbalance signs could net among themselves
directly in the new situation. In contrast, in the old situation the LFC areas might have ramped up
and down with different speeds to bring their own ACEs back to zero, thereby evoking a new error.
On the other hand, IN could lead to ACE oscillations and reduced frequency quality if sampling times
are large [27].
Secondly, the market integration allows for Cross-Border Activation (XBA) of reserves. This means
that imbalances in one area can now be solved by aFRR activation in another area. Which generators
are activated is decided on the basis of a Common Merit Order List (CMOL): the bids from all areas
together are sorted from cheap to expensive, and in this order they are activated until the aFRR

4.
Market coupling literature review
In order to provide a background to the central topic of this study, balancing market integration, this
section presents a review of the literature about market coupling in Europe. Integration of balancing
markets is new in Europe. In contrast, European countries have more experience with the integration
of day-ahead (DA) and intra-day (ID) markets (see: Section 2). As a result, the body of literature
focused on DA and ID market coupling is larger than that focused on the coupling of balancing
markets. Therefore, this review starts with a description of the literature about DA and ID market
integration (Section 4.1). Afterwards, the specific characteristics of the literature on balancing market
integration are presented (Section 4.2). This section does not intend to present an exhaustive overview
of what has been written about market coupling, but rather serves as a synopsis of the most important
research directions in the field of electricity market coupling in Europe.
4.1
Day-ahead and intra-day market coupling
Since the 1950s, European countries have been cooperating in the electricity sector through cross-
border exchanges of energy [30]. Whereas the primary purpose of cooperation consisted of fuel savings
and security of supply in the early years, throughout the past 30 years cost-efficient utilisation of
generation capacities and reduction of VRE curtailment have become increasingly important additional
objectives of the cooperation [8, 30].
Section 4.1.1 provides a theoretical background regarding the economics of electricity market cou-
pling. A selection of the most relevant studies concerning the coupling of DA and ID markets in
Europe using market simulation models under different scenarios is presented in Section 4.1.2. Sec-
tion 4.1.3 provides an overview of the ex-post empirical analyses of the effects of market coupling for
already-implemented market coupling cases.
4.1.1
Economic theory
Microeconomic theory provides an analytical framework to describe the impacts of market integration
on economic surplus. Economic surplus is described as the sum of consumer surplus and producer
surplus, with consumer surplus referring to the product of the willingness-to-pay (WtP) minus the
market price (P*) of a good and the total quantity traded in the market (Q*). Producer surplus (PS)
is defined as the difference between the total revenues of the sellers and the amount they would have
been willing to accept for the given quantity. The top-left graph in Figure 5 presents the equilibrium
market prices (PA,ref and PB,ref) in two isolated markets, A and B. The suppliers in area A (SA)
provide full demand of area A (DA) at price PA,ref) and the same takes place in area B. For the
respective areas A and B, the consumer surpluses are represented by the green and yellow areas, while
the blue and red triangles form the producer surpluses.
The top-right graph of Figure 5 shows the situation when the same markets are coupled and there
is unlimited interconnection capacity between the areas. The suppliers in the cheaper area A will sell
a quantity of QA−→B in area B at the cost of the producer surplus of B’s suppliers. As the market
settles at price P*, which is between the two “isolated” market prices, the consumers in the cheaper
area will lose part of their surplus but those in the more expensive area will gain. Overall, economic

However, available cross-border transmission capacities are often limited in real-world electricity
systems. This is illustrated in the bottom graph of Figure 5. The resulting congestion reduces economic

Simulation studies
[31–33] assess the advantages of further coupling of wholesale electricity markets in Europe.
The
studies note potential efficiency gains in the scheduling of electricity generation resulting from market
coupling, which in [31] could result in up to billions of euros of savings until 2030 in the CWE16
region. [33] show that besides cost savings, a synchronisation of the Baltic states’ power system with

the Continental Europe Network reduces redispatch17 requirements and increases system adequacy.
Moreover, these studies highlight the importance of aligning national regulatory frameworks into
a common European regulatory framework. For example, market coupling allows for a more efficient
application of security of supply measures, such as capacity and reserve mechanisms (CRMs). CRMs
are temporary subsidies to capacity resources in order to safeguard the security of supply, and are
currently used in around half of the EU Member States [35]. The more divergence in peak consumption
hours between Member States, the more capacities can be shared and, therefore, the higher the
potential of efficiency gains are. Furthermore, cross-border VRE support schemes can more efficiently
stimulate investments in VRE capacities in locations where VRE resources are optimal. A simplified
calculation in [32] shows that additional electricity worth of 740 million euros could have been generated
if solar energy support schemes between Germany and Spain had been shared in 2011 and 2012.
An interesting branch of the simulation studies looks into market integration under imperfect
market conditions. For example, [36] estimate that the losses resulting from imperfect competition
are substantially lower in a coupled market than in the uncoupled market (-550 versus -746 million
EUR/year) in the CWE region. Interestingly, they also illustrate a case in which market coupling
could lead to a welfare loss. This is the case if large producers switch from price-taking behaviour to
a Cournot strategy, due to a lower perceived risk of regulatory intervention in response to exercised
market power, for example because their overall market shares are lower in the coupled markets than
in the national market.
A third important part of the literature on DA and ID market coupling focuses on cross-border and
intra-zonal congestion management. Increasing penetration of VRE generation is likely to increase
the frequency of congestion occurrences in the European electricity network [37]. Congestion on cross-
border transmission lines between zones diminishes the possibility to cost-efficiently use generation
capacities. Thus, the effectiveness of congestion management strongly affects the benefits of market
coupling.

4.1.3
Empirical studies
Ex-post empirical analyses confirm the conclusions of the simulation studies about the benefits of DA
and ID market integration. [38] provide an example of an empirical analysis of cross-border flows
before and after coupling. They found that the DA market coupling on 14 May 2014 reduced the
average capacity not-used on the interconnector between France and Spain in hours where there was
a price difference between the countries to zero. This indicates that market coupling has made the
interconnector use more efficient. Moreover, they observed that cross-border transactions in the “anti-
economic” direction, i.e. commercial energy exchanges from the high-price zone to the low-price zone,
were no longer recorded after the DA market coupling.
As a result, [38] predict prices in France and Spain to converge in the years after the market
coupling and the commissioning of a new interconnector in 2015. Indeed, this has been the case. The
frequency of full price convergence (i.e. price difference is smaller than 1 EUR / MWh) increased
from 8.4% in 2014 to 30% in 2016 and 42% in 2020 [39, 40]. This trend of converging prices is more
generally observed throughout Europe in recent years [39]. However, [39] warn that "reaching full price
convergence is not an objective in itself, as it would require over-investment in network infrastructure."
Also the trend of diminished cross-border transactions in the anti-economic direction is observed [39].
[3] analysed the effects of the go-live of the Single Intra-Day Coupling (SIDC) in Spain in 2018 and
2019. Their main observation is that the common ID market helps market parties – VRE generators
in particular – to adjust their programs very close to realtime. [41] explore the potential benefits
and challenges of complementing the European continuous ID auction with intra-day auctions with a
central clearing for all parties. They argue that intra-day auctions with a central clearing bring benefits
in terms of liquidity, market power resilience and efficient usage of cross-zonal capacity. Finally, [41]
identify the challenge of harmonising the ID auctions with respect to economic, legal and political
developments.
In their annual market monitoring reports, the European Agency for the Cooperation of Energy
Regulators (ACER) measures (among many other things) the actual status of market integration in
Europe [39]. They confirm that both the volume and efficiency of cross-border ID exchanges have been
increasing over the past years. Moreover, they confirm the recommendation by [31] to use implicit
auctions, by observing a 79% efficiency of ID flows on border crossings that using implicit allocation
methods, compared to 55% for borders using explicit allocation methods. With these efficiencies being
substantially lower than efficiencies of cross-border flows in the DA markets, [39] estimate that more
efficient use of ID cross-zonal capacities could increase the economic surplus benefits by over e50
million annually.

4.2.1
Studies on economic implications
As explained in Section 2.1, balancing markets usually consist of reserve capacity markets and re-
serve energy markets. Studies like [42] and [43] simulate common procurement of balancing capacity
products (mFRR and aFRR) in Europe, and the implicit allocation of cross-border capacity between
the day-ahead wholesale electricity markets and balancing capacity markets. They find that common
procurement brings significant cost advantages not only in the capacity procurement stage, but also
in the wholesale electricity market, because the available cross-border transfer capacities are more
optimally allocated between the different market stages (Figure 6). In addition, they show that short-
ening the timing and length of aFRR production results in more optimal use of available generation
and transmission capacities.
[44] analyse the effects of reserving fixed transmission capacities (as a percentage of NTC) for
reserve procurement on costs in the DA electricity market and FRR procurement market.
They
observe a decrease in generation costs with low levels of transmission capacity reservation for reserve
procurement (compared to no reservation). They explain this by arguing that the need to keep reserves
in the more expensive system is reduced, which increases competition in the DA market and thereby
reduces costs. [43] show that dynamic transmission capacity allocation (i.e. based on an hourly trade-
off between DA and reserve capacity market) could result in substantial cost savings compared to the
fixed reservation.
Another branch of studies focuses on balancing energy markets. [43] explores scenarios for bal-
ancing market integration in the future northern Europe energy system. Their results suggest that
imbalance netting and activation of cheaper cross-border reserves could reduce activation costs by
30%, or e512 million per year, in the Nordic electricity system.
[45] models common balancing energy activation between multiple zones in an IEEE 30-bus test
system, using flow-based line constraints (DC-OPF). Based on 150 generated scenarios for imbalances
from wind and load forecast errors, they show that common activation reduce the total activation
and total activation costs. Moreover, they find out that flow-based balancing exchange is slightly less
expensive than balancing exchanges limited by net transfer capacities (NTCs) between areas.

Studies on technical implications
Several studies have also looked at the impacts of imbalance netting (IN) and cross-border activation
of balancing reserves on technical indicators such as frequency quality, area control error (ACE), and
reserve dimensioning. For example, [2] mark the German Grid Control Cooperation (GCC) as one of
the main explanatory factors for the “German Paradox”, which refers to the observation that balancing
reserves in Germany were reduced by 15% between 2008 and 2015 in a context of a tripling of solar

4.3
Contribution of this study to academic literature
The contribution of this study to academic literature is twofold. First, to the authors’ knowledge, this
is the first study that analyses the impacts of the new platform PICASSO on economic surplus and
system stability. Whereas most other studies (e.g. [42, 43]) about balancing market integration take
more freedom about designing rules of the sharing platform or model other platforms than PICASSO
(e.g.
[47]), this study follows the regulations and operations of the foreseen balancing platforms
(PICASSO and IGCC) as closely as possible. Furthermore, the study is unique in that it compares
the benefits of a platform combining cross-border activation and imbalance netting (PICASSO) to
those from an IN-only platform (IGCC).
Secondly, this study introduces a case study including Belgium, the Netherlands and Germany
based on real historical data. Some other studies have analysed cross-border balancing cooperation
on test systems (e.g. [27, 45, 51] or without modelling the merit-order based activation. The fact that
the secondary reserves markets were modelled using a full year of historical data, is new.

5.1.3
PICASSO: aFRR activation and implicit imbalance netting
The objective of PICASSO is to select the optimal combination of aFRR bids to be activated, while
implicitly netting the member’s imbalances. Four priorities are defined to find this optimal combina-
tion, with the next priority only becoming relevant if there are multiple solutions to fulfil the previous
priority [53]. First, aFRR demand satisfaction of individual LFC areas must be maximized. Secondly,
the total volume of selected aFRR bids must be minimized. Thirdly, economic surplus is to be maxi-
mized. Here, economic surplus is defined as the the total surplus of the participating TSOs resulting
from satisfying their aFRR demand and the total surplus of BSPs obtained from the activation of their
aFRR energy bids (i.e. the sum of the CS and PS in Figure 5). Finally, in case there are multiple
optimal solutions to fulfil the other three priorities, the solution with the lowest interchange of aFRR
volumes between LFC areas is selected.
In this study, the implicit imbalance netting and cross-border activation optimization are modelled
separately. The variables of the (now no longer implicit) imbalance netting process are fed into the
cross-border activation optimization as parameters. The decision to split these two processes up is
made in order to be able to better measure the respective contributions of imbalance netting and
cross-border activation to the changed dynamics.
Splitting the processes up in this way assumes
that imbalance netting goes at zero cost and is therefore always prioritized over cross-border activation.

5.2
Technical model: dynamic LFC controller
A dynamic LFC controller model has been used to analyse the impact of PICASSO’s imbalance
netting on the ACE stability. The model only takes into account the secondary response, i.e. the
aFRR response with the elements displayed in Figure 3. The PI controller outputs which response
is required by the BSPs. This value is the weighted sum of the proportional gain (Kp), providing
a response proportional to the current error value, and integral gain (Ki), providing a response in
proportion to the integrated error value over the past period. The integral contains an anti-windup
that reduces the integral term when it approaches an upper limit.
For the scope of this research, ramping is modelled through rate limiters for each BSP, and the
assumption is made that the BSPs ramp up and/or down to their set points linearly, within a full
activation time of 7.5 minutes. This complies with the ENTSO-e standard aFRR products, as displayed
in 2. All other parameter values for the LFC controller can be found in section 7.1.4.

Assessment criteria
The different cases are analysed and compared using a set of economic and technical indicators. This
section presents these indicators and describes how they are calculated.
Activation volumes
The analysis looks at the total upward and downward activation per country under the different sce-
narios. Activation for domestic consumption and for exports to neighbouring areas are distinguished.
Activation prices
The marginal activation price is observed for each country at each time step. A country’s marginal
price represents the price of the most expensive generator activated for domestic consumption (Figure
8). The marginal price is obtained by taking the shadow price of the demand constraints (Equation 7,
Equation 14 and Equation 28 for the reference, IGCC and PICASSO case, respectively). This follows
the logic of linear programming where the shadow price of a constraint represents by how much the
objective value would increase or decrease in case the right-hand side of the constraint were increased
by one unit [56].

For downward bids, the negative value of the shadow price is assumed as marginal price. Every
additional unit of upward demand (i.e. decrease of downward demand) increases the objective value
by the shadow price, because the downward BSP will pay less to be scheduled and so the TSO will
receive less money if the downward demand is smaller in magnitude. Therefore, the negative value of
the shadow price must be taken as marginal price for downward activation.
Figure 8 illustrates how the marginal price is set in an integrated market. In case there is no
congestion between two countries, the marginal price for both countries corresponds to the price of
the most expensive activated bid in both areas combined (i.e. bid B3 in the top-right). This means
that the price increases in the cheaper area (area B) and decreases in the more expensive area (area A).
Sometimes inter-zonal congestion prohibits optimal scheduling of the bids (in Figure 8 B3’s energy
cannot be transported to area A). In such cases, the marginal price is set by the most expensive
generator required to meet domestic demand (i.e. A3 in area A and B2 in area B). This results in a
price differentiation between the areas and is referred to as “market splitting”.
During some time steps, the shadow price is not determined by the most expensive activated
generator, but by the penalty M for remaining imbalances after aFRR activation ρ (see Section 5.1).
These situations occur when there is not enough capacity available to meet the demand, so when
ρ is non-zero. Maintaining M as marginal price would lead to extreme distortions of the marginal
price distributions. This would not reflect reality, because in reality remaining imbalances after aFRR
activation would be solved by activating mFRR. Therefore, for this study the assumption is made
that during these time steps the marginal price is defined by the most expensive activated generator
instead of the shadow price of the demand constraint.
Finally, ACER has established a price cap of 15,000 / e MWh for the balancing energy price
throughout the first four years of operation of PICASSO, i.e. until 2026 [58]. This cap is included in
the study as a price cap on the balancing price.
Imbalance settlement prices
The imbalance settlement price (IP) is the price that BRPs pay (or receive) for their imbalance
position (see Figure 2).
In contrast with the balancing price which is used to remunerate BSPs
for providing balancing services, the imbalance price is determined per Imbalance Settlement Period
(ISP) of 15 minutes and not per optimisation cycle [59]. The imbalance price calculations in this
study comply with the ENTSO-e proposal for imbalance settlement harmonisation [59] to the largest
extent possible. This proposal states a preference for single pricing and allows dual pricing only under
certain conditions. Therefore, this study uses single pricing.
However, the European regulations leave national regulatory authorities free to base the imbalance
price on the maximum/minimum of all activated balancing energy prices, or to base it on volume-

price during all optimisation cycles t within ISP p ∈ISP.23 The average prices are defined as the
total costs (from up- and downward activation) minus the total revenues (from downward activation),
divided by the net activation gnet during ISP p.

7.1.2
Merit-order data
The data for the merit-order lists are based on historic aFRR bids from 2021. Data about bid prices and
volumes were retrieved from the websites of the TSOs of the case study countries [64–66]. Voluntary
bids are excluded, because no public data was available for these bids. This means that only bids for
which also a capacity bid was offered, are taken into account. As ACER has announced to cap all
bid prices to 15,000 e / MWh [58], all historic bid prices exceeding this value are set at 15,000 e /
MWh.
For computational reasons, the maximum number of bids per LFC area was set at 126 bids per
time step. For German bids, this means that the merit order curve is sampled every 10 MW up to
1,000 MW, and then every 50 MW up to 2,400 MW (this is the maximum total bid volume in the
year). These form the bid prices. The bid volumes are either 10 or 50 MW.
Unlike the German TSOs, the Dutch TSO does not provide the complete merit-order curves.
Instead, they provide the minimum and maximum bid prices as well as the bid prices at 100 MW,
300 MW and 600 MW [66].
The prices between these sample points are estimated using linear
interpolation. The bid volumes are the same as described above for the German bids, i.e. either 10
or 50 MW.
The Belgian TSO provided the full merit order curves of all energy elements of the aFRR capacity

7.1.3
Power flow constraints
Each LFC area is represented by a single node. Power flow constraints are taken into account by

ACE stability
The dynamic LFC model shows the impact of PICASSO’s imbalance netting on the quality of the
ACE control. It is observed that the total sum of remaining ACE after aFRR activation is reduced
by 4% when imbalance netting is allowed like in the PICASSO market set-up. Moreover, Figure 17
shows that the ACE thresholds are exceeded less often for Belgium and Germany in upward direction.
The occurrences of the ACE exceeding threshold values L1, L2 and L3 is reduced in Belgium and the
Netherlands.
The improved ACE values are most likely explained by the “milder” set points in the PICASSO
set-up. As IN reduces the extremeness of the imbalances, the integral term of the PI controller builds
up less than in the reference set-up. Hence, the BSPs can adapt faster and more adequately to sudden
changes (for example sign changes) without causing substantial new imbalances.

9.
Discussion
In this section, the most important implications of the results are interpreted.
Furthermore, the
limitations of this research are indicated and suggestions for future research are given.
9.1
Interpretation of results
The economic and technical impacts of PICASSO were investigated for this Master’s thesis.
The
results indicate that the (northwest) European electricity system as a whole will benefit from the
go-live of PICASSO. The estimated annual savings from joining PICASSO for the TSOs of the mod-
elled countries sum to e1.3 billion compared to the current market set-up (IGCC) and e1.6 billion
compared to a situation without any balancing cooperation. Total producer surplus is reduced by
e950 million compared to IGCC and e1.1 billion compared to a situation without any balancing co-
operation. The total economic surplus for all countries combined (sum of consumer surplus, producer
surplus and congestion rent) increases by around e400 million compared to IGCC and around 550
million compared to the non-cooperation reference case.
Furthermore, the total aFRR activation required in the balancing market is reduced. The same
holds for the mFRR activation, as the remaining imbalances after aFRR activation are reduced. Hence,
depending on the electricity mix of the balancing service providers in the countries, PICASSO might
foster decarbonisation of the electricity sector. However, given the small quantities traded in balancing
markets, this effect is not expected to be substantial.
In addition, the results suggest that PICASSO might positively affect the system’s stability, with
reduced ACE values. Given the additional VRE integration that is expected in the near future, this
effect should be welcomed by TSOs.
Nevertheless, it should be noted that the benefits are not equally shared between the case countries.
As a result of the observed activation price convergence, prices increase in main exporting countries
and decrease in main importing countries. Therefore, producers in exporting countries benefit from
the collaboration at the cost of the producers in importing countries. In the case example, this is
illustrated by the increase of the Dutch producer surplus by e166 million and the producer surplus
reduction in Germany of e1.1 billion compared to the IGCC set-up.
The situation is the opposite for the consumers. The exports make the activation prices higher in
the main exporting areas The Netherlands and Belgium and lower the activation prices of the main
importer Germany. The higher activation prices in The Netherlands and Belgium are dampened by
the reduced aFRR demand through IN. In Germany, IN reduces the prices even further. However, the
IN effect has turned out to be smaller than the cross-border activation price effect. As a result, the
BRPs in low-price countries will have to pay higher imbalance settlement prices when they are in a
short position, whereas BRPs in BRPs in high-price countries benefit from lower imbalance settlement
prices. This will similarly affect the end-consumers in these countries as BRPs are expected to charge
these cost changes to their customers ultimately.
At the same time, given the single-pricing settlement in most European countries, the more extreme
prices in previously low-price zones may increase the potential for strategically imbalanced positions.
Suppose BRPs manage to predict the imbalance sign (for which there is evidence, see [71]) and
congestion. In that case, the more extreme prices offer an opportunity for BRPs to increase revenues
50
==PAGE:61==
by assuming an imbalanced position opposite to the system imbalance.
Finally, the analysis shows a total congestion rent of e53 million, which indicates that the system
may benefit from increased interconnector capacity.
9.2
Limitations and future research
Given the fact that this study is based on publicly available data, there were limitations to how
representative the data are. For example, the Dutch and Belgian TSOs do not publish the full bid
ladders (e.g., voluntary bids are missing from the Belgian bid ladder). The fewer bids available in these
areas might have resulted in an underestimation of the total activation of the countries in PICASSO.
Furthermore, the historic bids used in Germany are based on a Pay-as-Bid (PaB) pricing scheme.
As bidders need to include a mark-up in their bids to cover their fixed costs under PaB pricing
schemes [48], German bid prices might be lower under the new PICASSO set-up than they have
historically been. This effect and the effect of increased competition on bid prices have been studied
in the sensitivity analysis. The sensitivity analysis shows that German activation is less outsourced if
PICASSO lowers the bid prices in Germany. Moreover, it shows that such a situation lowers the total
activation costs. It would be interesting to include more bidding scenarios in future analyses and the
impact of PICASSO on bidding behaviour. These scenarios could include recent developments in the
electricity mix, with, for example, increased demand response and batteries in balancing markets.
Moreover, this research could be improved by adding scenarios for future imbalance values. Invest-
ments in new VRE and conventional generation and transmission capacities will probably affect the
system imbalance. According to [72], system imbalances are not entirely price-inelastic as commonly
assumed.
They convincingly argue that wholesale market prices and imbalance prices incentivise
strategic deviations. The increased imbalance settlement price volatility from PICASSO observed in
this study could provide more incentives for strategic deviations. It could be very interesting to include
this effect in future analyses.
Another approach for generating imbalance scenarios could be to include historic IN values from the
IGCC platform in the model, instead of modelling the imbalance netting values. IN works differently
with more countries. The IN potential may be larger, but sometimes an IN potential has to be shared
between multiple countries. Therefore, this research is limited to only modelling three countries and
not +20 countries like in real life. Using historic IN values could better isolate the contribution of
the implementation of PICASSO, compared to the status quo (which already includes IN through the
IGCC platform). However, this study did not choose this methodology as only 15-minute resolution
data was available for IGCC IN values.
Finally, future research should include interactions with other markets, such as reserve capacity
markets, wholesale electricity markets and the markets for other balancing products such as mFRR
and FCR. As mFRR and FCR activations are disregarded in this study, the results are probably an
overestimation of the aFRR activation.
Accordingly, the effects of PICASSO are most likely also
overestimated.
Including interactions with the wholesale and reserve capacity markets could look
into the effects of PICASSO on those markets. For example, lower reserve capacity reservations in
expensive areas (due to capacity reservations abroad) may free up these capacities for the day-ahead
markets and increase competition there.

10.
Conclusions
This study aimed to estimate the expected economic and technical benefits of implementing the Eu-
ropean balancing platform for secondary reserves PICASSO. The research focuses on the implications
for the participating countries’ economic surplus, activation and imbalance prices, and system stability
(ACE). A case study including Belgium, the Netherlands and Germany was used to analyse this.
The results suggest that the go-live of PICASSO may result in an additional annual economic
surplus for the three countries combined of e400 million compared to the status-quo where the case
countries participate in IGCC. Compared to a reference market set-up where the case countries would
not participate in any shared balancing program, additional benefits from PICASSO could sum to
e550 million per year. The additional economic surplus results from a more cost-optimal activation
of the balancing energy bids across countries.
However, the participants will not benefit equally from PICASSO. Based on the results, it is
expected that producers in areas with cheaper balancing bids (in the case study, these are Belgium
and the Netherlands) will see their revenues increase. In contrast, part of producers’ revenues in more
expensive areas are reduced (Germany in the case study).
With activation and imbalance settlement prices converging between participating areas, consumers
in more expensive areas can expect substantial cost reductions. Conversely, the raised prices in the
cheaper areas will result in higher costs to consumers than in the IGCC market set-up. However,
despite those price increases, total activation cost increases in these countries are limited compared to
the reference non-collaboration set-up, as many of the imbalances can be netted against imbalances
abroad. Netting reduces the requirements for activation by approximately 12%.
A sensitivity analysis on bid prices in Germany shows that savings might be even higher if PI-
CASSO successfully reduces the usage of market power in the German aFRR market. The topic of
market power reduction from increased cross-border balancing cooperation deserves to be investigated
more in future research.
Furthermore, the technical analysis has shown that the collaboration through PICASSO increases
system stability, as the total ACE is reduced. Moreover, the reduced activations may bring small
benefits in terms of carbon reductions.
Concluding, this study has shown that substantial economic and technical benefits can be expected
from the implementation of PICASSO. Based on the results in this analysis, TSOs that have not
decided yet to join PICASSO are encouraged to participate.
Depending on their location, BRPs
and BSPs can expect improved or deteriorated financial flows due to price convergence between the
participating LFC areas.
