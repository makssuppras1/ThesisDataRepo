Smart Tip for wind turbine blades
Chrysanthos Galetsas
Abstract
Nowadays, one of the main drives of the wind energy development is the target for
reducing the Cost of Energy (COE) by increasing Annual Energy Production (AEP) with
larger swept areas and taller towers, but at the same time reducing the increased costs
due to that size increase. Following that perspective, the scope of this project is to design
and evaluate an add-on morphing blade tip (Smart Tip) that will enable an increase in the
AEP while reducing the additional loads through passive or active control. For this reason
a business case framework is deﬁned, that focuses on the design of three diﬀerent Smart
Tip extensions that will vary only in length and will be attached in an already existing
blade of the 10 MW Reference Wind Turbine. The reference blade as well as the rest of the
wind turbine components are kept the same in order to enable savings in manufacturing
and transportation costs, but also focus the study on the Smart Tip device design and
evaluation. The new extended blade design is validated by checking the blade and the
wind turbine response in steady state simulations performed in HAWCStab2. Based on
these simulations the AEP and blade root moment MxBR increase due to the extensions
are calculated. The AEP increase is estimated to 0.78%, 1.54% and 2.29% for 1%, 2%
and 3% blade tip extension respectively. The corresponding increase of MxBR is equal to
1.52%, 3.34% and 5.32% for each of extensions. Moreover, several load control tip systems
are evaluated based on their potential to alleviate the increased loads and a ﬁnal concept
is chosen for evaluation by performing unsteady state simulations in the aeroelastic tool
HAWC2. The chosen concept was the Adaptive Trailing Edge Flap (ATEFlap) concept
that allowed a decrease of 1.01%, 2.29% and 3.88% for each of the extensions respectively,
based on theoretical calculations. ATEFlap model was implemented in the three extended
tip wind turbine models and a set of unsteady simulations in the aeroelastic tool HAWC2
were performed. The results of these simulations for the AEP showed a decrease compared
to the steady state computation of approximately 4% for all extensions. The design loads
results and the tower clearance (TTDis) diﬀerence caused from blade extension were
calculated in order to set a load alleviation target for the ﬁnal Smart tip design.
In
addition the three extended blade tip wind turbines were evaluated in order to choose
one the extensions that will formulate the ﬁnal Smart Tip design model used in a set of
parametric analysis with the target of obtaining the higher possible load alleviation, by
tuning the ﬂap controller in the best possible manner.

Chapter 1
Introduction
At the ﬁrst part of this report the main idea behind this research and the scope of the
project, are presented. In addition, the already existing research that formulated the basis
for this project, is analyzed. Finally, an analysis of the business case along with research
objectives and an overview of the report’s structure and contents, is conducted.
1.1
Smart Tip concept
Over the last 30 years, the wind turbine industry is focused on increasing the size of
wind turbines, by upscaling them. Increasing the size of a wind turbine rotor leads to a
larger swept area and a higher Annual Energy Production (AEP). But at the same time,
upscaling causes challenges because the mass of the wind turbine increases with the cube
of the rotor radius when linear upscaling is used. This leads to increased manufacturing
costs and increased operational and maintenance costs due to the increased loads that
the wind turbine is subjected to. Therefore, the aim of reducing Cost of Energy (COE) is
always a balance between increasing AEP and trying to keep the increased design loads
at approximately the same level as the reference wind turbine.
Nowadays, the largest wind turbines have a rated capacity of 7-8 MW and the rotor
diameter reaches the length of 170 m. As mentioned before this increasing size of the rotor
and other components of the wind turbine, creates a lot of challenges in manufacturing and
transportation. Because of that, there is an ongoing discussion in wind energy industry,
about the limitations that this continuous increase of size may impose. This discussion
generated a lot of ideas and concepts that focused on the extension of the existing blades
rather than upscaling the whole wind turbine.
The initial idea that led to the Smart Tip concept was the observation that in oﬀshore
power plants, most wind turbines are not loaded to the full load capacity, leading to losses
of annual energy production that can be critical for the viability of the wind farm [1].
In order to take advantage of this potential increase in AEP, an extension of the already
existing blades was proved to be the most eﬀective solution. Two types of extensions can
be implemented: The addition of an extender between the hub and the blade root and a
blade tip’s extension. The second option is easier to be applied since the extension can
be mounted on the blade directly without having to remove the whole blade from the
hub. A prototype set of a rotor blade tip extension has been designed , manufactured and
successfully tested in laboratory and in the ﬁeld [1, 2]. Another project that the same

principle is used with a target to increase the AEP of a wind power plant and reduce
the COE, is the “High Yield Low Loads Enlarged Rotor” (HYLLER) project [3]. At this
project, a new rotor blade is designed with ﬂexible blade length that will maintain the
manufacturing cost and the blade mass root moment at the equivalent level of a standard
single-size blade, while increasing the AEP of the wind turbine. From these two concepts
the one that is easier to be implemented is the tip extension of an already existing blade
and it will be the one analyzed and evaluated in the present research.
The geometry of this add-on blade extension can vary from a standard tip extension of
the original blade that will retain the same aerodynamic and structural characteristics, to a
winglet (out of plane) or a swept (in plane) design. Each of these blade tip geometries have
their own advantages and disadvantages in terms of how easily they can be implemented
on an existing blade geometry and their ability to alleviate the increased loading of the
wind turbine blade, either passively in the case of winglet [4, 5] and swept tip extension
[6] or actively in the case of a standard tip extension with the addition of a ”smart
rotor control” device [7]. The latest years, an on-going research has been conducted on
evaluation and testing of diﬀerent wind turbine blade tip designs, like the ones mentioned
above or tension-torsion and bend-twist [8] coupling, that will allow passive load control
based on aeroelastic tailoring but compared to active control methods, these ones are still
under investigation and they can not still be considered reliable.
1.2
State of the art in smart rotor control
For the desirable reduce of COE, the increase of AEP is not the only target because at
the same time it must be ensured that the additional loads caused from the rotor increase
will be reduced to a level close to the one that the reference wind turbine is designed.
Therefore, a lot of research on load alleviation methods has been made the latest years
[7]. Those methods are focusing in innovative blade design that will result in decrease in
loading and the general cost of the wind turbine, as rotor loads aﬀect the loading of other
components like the tower and the drive train. Principally, these methods are divided into
two main categories: active and passive control. In passive load control, the aerodynamic
and structural design are deﬁned in such way that the aeroelastic response of the blades
will adapt passively and counteract the changes in wind speed. For regulating loads or
power performance actively, the angle of attack or the lift coeﬃcient is changed, based on
input signals given by sensors.
Both methods were widely used in wind turbine industry, for the control of power
performance and aerodynamic loads of wind turbines. Especially for load control, there
is not one method that prevails over the other since they both have diﬀerent advantages
and disadvantages.
Active control methods oﬀer greater ﬂexibility and controllability
especially when dealing with unsteady ﬂow phenomena but at the same time they add
complexity to the system. On the other hand, passive control methods, are more simple
and in most cases they can be easily applied on blades but they are not so reliable and
sometimes they can be diﬃcult to maintain.
The term ’smart rotor control’ refers to the implementation of mechanical actuators
that in combination with censoring devices and controllers will provide eﬃcient load con-
trol, by controlling the aeroelastic response of the blades along the span. The research on
’smart rotor control’ has initiated from aerospace and speciﬁcally helicopter technology.

1.3
Project scope-Business Case
Based on the previous discussion on ’Smart Tip’ concepts and ’Smart rotor control’ de-
vices, the scope of this research is based on the evaluation of an add-on morphing blade
tip that will increase the AEP of an existing wind turbine by extending the rotor swept
area while having the capability of reducing additional loads through aeroelastic tailoring
(passive control) or active morphing (active control).
The rotor blade of an existing wind turbine, DTU 10 MW Reference Wind Turbine,
is used as a prototype for the design and the evaluation of the ’Smart Tip’ add-on. As
mentioned before, this concept idea derived from the need to decrease the additional
manufacturing challenges and transportation costs that a normal upscale of the wind
turbine blade, would create. Compared to an upscale of the rotor blade, the blade tip
extension oﬀers a lot of advantages in terms of ﬂexibility and cost savings. Firstly, there
is no need for the blade and the other components of the wind turbine (tower,shaft etc) to
be redesigned, leading to a lot of savings in manufacturing costs. At the same time, this
add-on can be mounted on an hanging blade, reducing transportation costs and avoiding a
lot of challenges that are caused in the transportation of a 90 m blade, especially onshore.
For these reasons, a simple extension of the wind turbine blade, without any additional
implementation of a passive or active control concept, can be used as a eﬃcient solution in
cases of AEP losses observed usually in wind farms, especially oﬀshore. The wind turbines
are not loaded to the full design capacity due to incorrect planning and installation of
them at sites that do not match the appropriate wind class [1].
The business case of the present project, focuses on the design of three diﬀerent Smart
Tip extensions that will vary in length and provide a relevant increase in AEP. In addition
diﬀerent load control tip systems are evaluated with quantiﬁcation of their load alleviation
potential. The choice of the ﬁnal concept, is based on steady state simulations where
the diﬀerent devices or structures are compared for their ability to change the lift and
drag coeﬃcients and reduce the blade root moments. This Smart Tip concept is then
implemented in HAWC2 and tested in aeroelastic load simulations of speciﬁc Design
Load Cases (DLC 1.2 and DLC 1.3). Finally, parametric analyses are performed in order
to tune the controller’s main parameters.
To summarize, the research objectives are presented below:
• Aerodynamic, structural and blade design of three diﬀerent extended blades with
the extension part equal to 1% 2% and 3% of the reference wind turbine blade,
respectively.
• Investigation of the AEP increase potential with each of the extended blades during
steady state simulations.

1.4
Overview of Thesis Content
In the last section of the introduction, the diﬀerent parts of the present study are brieﬂy
presented, in order to be easier for the reader to follow the basic structure used in the
composition of this work.
Computational tools and Design Load Basis (DLB) analysis
The aeroelastic and pre and post processing tools that are used for the various simulations,
are presented. Also the implementation of the ATEFlap model in HAWC2 is analyzed and
the main characteristics of the DTU 10 MW Reference Wind Turbine model are presented.
At the end of this chapter a brief description of the Design Load Basis framework that is
used in the HAWC2 simulations, is conducted with an analysis of the methology followed
for the computation of power production, AEP and fatigue and ultimate loads.
Presentation of the extended wind turbine blades design
In this chapter, the three diﬀerent extended blades along with their aerodynamic, struc-
tural and blade designs, are presented. In the blade design part speciﬁcally, the calculation
of the new centerline, trailing and leading edge are investigated in great detail because
it must be ensured that there will be a smooth transition between the geometry of the
reference blade and the extended part.
AEP increase potential of each extension
After the design of the three diﬀerent extended blades, an investigation of the potential

2.1
Aeroelastic tools
2.1.1
HAWCStab2
HAWCStab2 is a frequency based aeroservoelastic code that can perform steady state
computations and stability analysis of wind turbines.
One of the characteristics that
make it really useful is the ability to read the same input ﬁles as HAWC2. It is a tool
developed at the Department of Wind Energy of the Technical University of Denmark
originally developed by Morten Hartvig Hansen. The model is an analytical linearization
of a nonlinear ﬁnite beam element model and it is coupled with an unsteady blade element
momentum model of blade aerodynamics. The aerodynamic model includes shed vorticity,
dynamic stall, and dynamic inﬂow. the basic structrure as well as some examples are
provided in the user manual of the software [12]. A detailed description of the aerodynamic
model can be found in [13].
In the present study, HAWCStab2 has been used for an initial evaluation of the three
extended rotors designs (1%, 2% and 3%) on steady state computations and an estimation
of the AEP increase that they can provide in comparison with the reference wind turbine.
The output ﬁles generated from HAWCStab2 include useful information about the steady
states characteristics of the wind turbine for each of the operational points prediﬁned
from the user. This information was inserted into matlab codes in order to calculate the
AEP and Flpawise Blade Root Moment (MxBR) increase or decrease for each study case.

2.1.2
HAWC2
The acronym HAWC2 stands for Horizontal Axis Wind Turbine Code 2nd generation.
It was initially developed at Risø National Laboratory and now at DTU Wind Energy.
HAWC2 is used for calculating the response of a wind turbine in time domain and its
structural core is formulated based on multi-body dynamics. This means that the diﬀerent
objects deﬁned interact with each other at the connecting interfaces through a certain set
of constraints. For each body the stiﬀness, mass and inertia distributions can also be
deﬁned. The Blade Element Momentum (BEM) theory consists the basis of aerodynamic
part of the code but it is extended in order to handle unsteady phenomena like dynamic
inﬂow, dynamic stall, skew inﬂow, shear eﬀects on the induction and eﬀects from large
deﬂections. Additional information about the HAWC2 structure and commands can be
found in the manual [14].
In the present study, HAWC2 is used for the calcumlation of AEP and ultimate and
lifetime fatigue loads of the three extended rotors in comparison with the reference wind
turbine, for DLC 1.2 and DLC 1.3. In addition, a model with ATEFlap is formulated for
each case and parametric studies for the tuning of the ﬂap controller gains, are performed.
Finally, the load alleviation potential of ATEFlap for each of the extended wind turbines
is evaluated and special attention is given on the reduction of blade root moments and
on the increase of tip clearance.
2.1.3
Implementation of ATEFlap in HAWC2
The implementation of ATEFlap model in HAWC2 was performed by Leonardo Bergami,
as a part of his PHD project[15]. The model is developed, implemented and evaluated
from HAWC2 simulations. The diﬀerent commands needed for this implementation are
presented and explained in great detail in [14, 16].
Speciﬁcally for the implementation of ATEFlap model, a .ds ﬁle is needed as a data
input ﬁle, which contains the pre-processed aerodynamic steady input data. The ﬂap
section is deﬁned on all the blades of the rotor, as a starting and an ending point. It should
also be noted that, in the aerodynamics deﬁnition section, the dynamic stall model that
refers to the Deformable Trailing Edge Flap, is selected. Furthermore, the aerodynamic
calculation points are distributed with the same spacing as the values in the aerodynamic
layout ﬁle. Finally, a ﬂap angle controller and a servo model are inserted to the HAWC2
model with the use of DLL interfaces that enables the interaction and data interchange
between the DLL and the HAWC2 core.

2.2
DTU 10 MW Reference Wind Turbine
DTU 10 MW Reference Wind Turbine (DTU 10 MW RWT) is used as a baseline model for
the design of the extended rotors. An analytical description of the complete aerodynamic
and aeroelastic model for the DTU 10MW RWT, is included in [17]. It was initiated as

that can be publically available also to people outside of this project. As clearly stated
in the aforementioned report, the DTU 10 MW RWT was designed in order to serve as
a basis in the development of computational tools and alternative design methods. It
should also be mentioned that the design is considered to be light weight in an attempt
to be on the limit of maximum stress and strain values. As a result, several challenges
were identiﬁed for the design of large wind turbines that should be taken into account in
future designs [17].
For the scope of the present project, the DTU 10MW RWT model was also simulated in
HAWC2, in order to identify a load alleviation target for the smart rotor control concepts.
The model and the ﬁles that are needed for the simulation of the wind turbine in HAWC2
are available at the HAWC2 website [18].
2.2.1
Baseline controller
The controller used for the simulations of the baseline wind turbine and the extended
ones, is the DTU Wind Energy Controller and an analytical presentation of it along with
the source code and the block diagrams can be found in [19].The controller is programmed
in Fortran90 using the format of the type2 DLL interface for HAWC2. The controller’s
operation is based on classical proportional-integral (PI) control theory with the use of
some additional ﬁlters and its primary feedback sensor is the generator speed.
In full load operation, the torque limits are speciﬁed by the torque that derives from
the power control strategy that is selected by the user. This can be either constant power
or constant torque. The pitch reference angle is obtained from a combined PI feedback
of the generator speed, power errors and a possible diﬀerential feedback of the speed
error.The speed error refers to the diﬀerence between the second order low-pass ﬁltered
LSS generator speed and the rated speed while the power error is the diﬀerence between
the reference power and the rated power.
In partial load operation, the optimal CP tracking is based on a balance between gen-
erator and aerodynamic torques to obtain a tip speed ratio, close to optimal . For varying
the minimum pitch angle, a ﬁrst order low-pass ﬁltered (LSS) wind speed measured at
hub height is used, based on a look-up table that is provided by the user. In addition,
the power error feedback of the pitch controller ensures the pitch reference is kept at this
minimum pitch angle [19]. It should be noted that, the baseline controller was used with-
out tuning for the extended wind turbines as well. The size of the selected tip extensions
and the fact that it is attached to the tip of the blade that corresponds to 2% of the total
blade length, make this decision valid. One of the major advantages of the ’Smart Tip’
concept is that no redesigning and reconstruction of the blade is needed. This implies a
limitation at the size of the extension , but at the same time leads to a signiﬁcant savings
in manufacturing and transportation costs and enhancing the main target and scope of
this project and the wind energy industry in general, which is reducing the LCOE.

The Design Load Base (DLB) setup
International standards are provided from the International Electrotechnical Community
(IEC) in order to ensure that there will be a common ground between wind turbine
manufacturers and certiﬁcation authorities on all questions concerning standardization in
the electical and electonic ﬁels. In the present study, the relevant IEC standard used, is
the third and latest published edition of IEC 61400-1 [20] which outlines the minimum
design requirements for wind turbines.
In this IEC Standard, there is a deﬁnition of
the external conditions and speciﬁcally the wind turbine classes, in terms of wind speed
and turbulence parameters. Those classes have are intented to cover most wind turbine
applications. In Figure 2.1, the table that contains the basic parameters that deﬁne the
diﬀerent wind turbine classes, is presented. It should be noted that the parameter values
refer at the hub height. A more detailed description of the wind turbine classes can be
found in [20].

Pre-/post processing
The ﬁnal part of this chapter includes the description of the pre and post processing tools

fatigue load tables. Also, some other necessary parameters for the fatigue analysis like the
Wohler slope coeﬃcient M and the lifetime equivalent number NeqL, are deﬁned. After
the extrapolation of the statistics and the creation of the full standard report, the output
values for each channel are inserted in matlab scripts where the results from the diﬀerent
simulations are compared to each other in terms of AEP increase and load reduction
potential.

After the creation and evaluation of the extended blade tip design, three diﬀerent load
alleviation methods (articulated tip, trailing edge ﬂaps and swept tip) are presented and
examined on their ability to control the increased aerodynamic loads of lift and drag
forces, based on theoretical calculations.
An analytical description of the theory and

moment and the aerodynamic loads, is conducted. The load increase of MxBR, caused

load reduction. Finally the three methods are compared and one of them is chosen based
on its load reduction capability but also on its applicability in HAWC2.

Adaptive Trailing Edge Flap (ATEFlap) concept
The trailing edge aerodynamic devices like ﬂaps are considered to be a concept with high
potential on load alleviation. The ATEFlap aerodynamic model created in [16] contains
information about the unsteady lift, drag and moment coeﬃcients of a 2D airfoil equipped
with trailing edge ﬂap. It is integrated in the aeroelastic tool HAWC2 and it can reproduce
steady and dynamic characteristics, of an airfoil that is undergoing arbitrary motion and
ﬂap deﬂection,both in attached and separated ﬂow conditions [16].
In simple words, the model is able to interpret the deformations of the camberline for
an airfoil according to the deﬁned ﬂap angle (see ﬁgure 4.6). It can be seen like a model
that changes airfoils in a speciﬁc position. It uses as inputs the instantaneous angle of
attack, the instantaneous ﬂap angle β and the inﬂow velocity and it provides as outputs,
the lift, drag and pitching angle.

5.1.1
Presentation of the load channels
Before the presentation of the results obtained from the HAWC2 simulations, a deﬁnition
of the loads that will be investigated is necessary. In order to evaluate the response of the
examined wind turbines, the time-series response of a number of channels is investigated.
The interaction between the diﬀerent components of the wind turbine indicates the need
of investigating not only the loading of the blades but also the rest of the wind turbine
parts. The channels that are used for this design load evaluation of the extended and the
reference wind turbines are the following:
• Blade channels: The blade load analysis is the one considered as he most critical
in the present study and the main driver in the parametric analysis that will be
conducted. This is because the blade is the tool for energy extraction from the wind
and the part that the optimization process of the whole wind turbine is usually
focusing on. It should be mentioned that for simplicity reasons the blade loading
is only checked on the blade root excluding the investigation of the complex cross
sectional area for local phenomena like buckling.
The three blade root bending
moments that are investigated are:
– MxBR: refers to the ﬂapwise blade root bending moment
– MyBR: refers to the edgewise blade root bending moment
– MzBR: refers to the torsional blade root bending moment
• Tower channels: The tower channels are divided into two main categories: the Tower
Top (TT) and Tower Bottom (TB) bending moments. The TB bending moments
correspond to the ﬁrst node of the tower which is deﬁned to be ﬁxed in the ground
in the tower deﬁnition of the htc ﬁle. The TT bending moments refer to the last
node of the tower deﬁnition at which the nacelle is deﬁned to be attached. For the
TB bending moments there are two directions that are investigated :
– MxTB: refers to the fore aft bending moment
– MyTB: refers to the side side bending moment
The TT bending momens that correspond to the nacelle moments are:
– MxTT: represents the tilt moment of the nacelle
– MyTT: represents the roll moment of the nacelle
– MzTT: represents the yaw moment of the nacelle
• Main bearing: The main bearing is the one supporting the main shaft of the wind
turbine and it is included in the load evaluation since it is a critical part of the wind
turbine operation. It is a highly loaded part and as a result it needs to be carefully
designed and maintained. The main bearing bending moments are separated into
the following:
– MxMB: represents the tilt moment
– MyMB: represents the yaw moment
– MzMB: represents the torsion moment

5.2.1
Presentation of the ATEFlap controller
The controller used with the ATEFlap model is an Individual Flap (IF) single-input-single-
output Porportional integral Diﬀerential (PID) control based on MxBR input signal and
without the presence of any intermediate transformations. This type of controller has been
implemented used and validated with ATEFlap model in HAWC2, in various previous
studies for Fatigue load alleviation [26], [34]. In the present study, a rather simplistic
implementation of this controller in HAWC2 simulations is used. The controller’s target
is focused in Fatigue load reduction for wind speeds above the rated wind speed, i.e. from
12 m/s to 26 m/s since this is the range of wind speeds mainly attributing to fatigue
loading. It is considered that the controller is mainly focusing on fatigue load reduction
of MxBR but the other components will be aﬀected as well since the load reduction of the
blades will be propagated to them, especially the ones closely connected with the blade
operation and performance.
The algorithm of the IF PD controller can be seen in Figure 5.7 and it is explained
brieﬂy in diﬀerent steps below:
• The MxBR signals (MxBRbli(i)) of the three blades are passed through a High Pass
(HP) ﬁlter that centers the signal values around zero. In that way the ﬂap angle
avoids reacting to steady and low frequency variations. The HP ﬁlter is characterized
by a corner frequency fc, used in order to obtain the new ﬁltered values of MxBR,
MxBRf(i).
• An initial checking of the turbine operational status is performed. If the turbine is
not in normal operation connected to the grid, then a ﬁxed angle is assigned and
the following steps are omitted.
• If the turbine is in normal operation, the ﬁrst derivative of the ﬁltered MxBR
(MxBRf(i)) is calculated from the following equation:
dMxBRbli(i) = MxBRf(t) −MxBRf(t −1)
dt
(5.3)
• The gains of proportional Kp and derivative terms Kd are calculated, based on the
wind speed signal which is Low Pass (LP) ﬁltered, as:
Kp(Vfilt) = PKp(Vfilt)
(5.4)
Kd(Vfilt) = PKd(Vfilt)
(5.5)
• The ratio ηrat is calculated based on the threshold rthes value, which can be between
0 and 1 and the controller’s full indicator. This ratio is used in order to regulate the
switching between operational regimes and it is also limited in the range between 0
and 1. A second threshold Thres has also been added in order check the values of
ηrat and if it is higher than it then ηrat becomes 0.
• Finally, the reference ﬂap signal βref, i is calculated for each blade individually and
then it is fed up to the servo:

6.1
Conclusion
The ﬁnal chapter of this study, includes the conclusions based on general observations and
obtained results from the investigations and the evaluations performed in the diﬀerent
parts of the present project.
• Three diﬀerent extensions of 1%, 2% and 3% of the reference wind turbine blade
were designed and evaluated. These extensions resulted to an increase of the swept
area by 1.9%, 3.92% and 5.9%. At the new blade design, the chord length at tip
was kept as closer to the reference chord length as possible and the blade tip was
extended smoothly from the reference blade, according to a validation of the design
performed by checking the normal and tangential forces distributions.
• As an outcome of the eigenvalue analysis of the wind turbine, the damping ratios
and the natural frequencies were almost equal to the ones of the reference wind
turbine with a slight diﬀerence of approximately 1% for most of them. No issue
has been observed from the checking of the Campbell diagram. The 1P excitation
excitation was not crossing none of the modes while 3P is crossing the ﬁrst the tower
modes at 5 rpm which is lower than the minimum rotational speed which is set to
6rpm and does not causes a concern.
• AEP calculations based on steady state simulations at HAWCStab2 have showed
an increase of the AEP of 0.78%, 1.54% and 2.29% for 1%, 2% and 3% blade
tip extension respectively.
The MxBR increase due to the blade extension was
calculated equal to 1.52%, 3.34% and 5.32% for each of extensions respectively.
• The best results between the smart rotor concepts in comparison to their load al-
leviation potential were achieved by the articulated tip concept that reduced the
increased MxBR moment to the level of the wind turbine blade with an angle dif-
ference of approximately 6◦. The ATEFlap performed a load reduction that was
slightly lower from the targeted level of the RWT, with a ﬂap angle decrease of 6◦.
The swept angle had the worst results on terms of load alleviation mainly due to

tween the three concepts investigated the one chosen was the ATEFlap concept since
it could combine both a satisfying potential and high applicability to the existing
aeroelastic tools.
• Th AEP validation of unsteady state simulations performed in HAWC2 for DLC 1.2
and 1.3 have showed a decrease of approximately 4.5% for the three extended blade
tip wind turbine. This decrease cna be attributed to the unsteady phenomena of

• From the load validation of the extended blade tip wind turbines, it can be concluded

model.
The blades are the most inﬂuenced components from the fatigue loads
increase. Between the blade channels the most inﬂuenced is the edgewise blade root

extended blades.

6.2
Recommendations for Future Research
The structure of the present study has formulated a basic analysis and design of the
Smart tip project and can be used as a reference for future research. There are several
suggestions for further investigations and diﬀerent tools or methods that can be used.
• Regarding the geometry of the blade tip, other designs can be chosen and evaluated,
like the winglet or a hybrid design of a swept blade with a winglet that can use the
advantages of both.
• For the smart control method another active method like active twist or active
vortex generators, could be used. Also there is a range of passive methods that
could be investigated, like tension-torsion coupling or bend-twist coupling .
• Investigate the increase of the ﬂap angle operational range (±15◦) by altering ATE-
Flap aerodynamic model and expanding it to higher angles by increasing the chord
percentage that it covers.
• Perform full DlB simulations in order to investigate the Smart Tip behavior in other
DLC cases than the ones investigated in the present work (DLC 1.2 DLC 1.3).
• Investigate other control methods like the cut-oﬀcontrol approach or the blade root
moment threshold approach.
• Formulate, implement and validate the concept of articulated tip, which seemed to
have a highest load alleviation potential from the results of the theoretical calcula-
tions.
