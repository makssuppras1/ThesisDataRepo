Abstract
Alternative splicing and somatic mutation jointly shape the molecular diversity of cancer, yet most survival
analyses still treat genes as single, undivided units, collapsing everything into one value per gene.
Here, a pan-cancer, isoform-resolved, multi-omics survival framework is constructed from RNA-seq
and somatic mutation data together with mass-spectrometry-based proteomics. For each cancer type,
feature-wise Cox proportional hazards models are fitted for three expression representations: (1) gene-
level expression, (2) isoform abundance, and (3) isoform fractions, as well as for gene-aggregated
mutation features stratified by functional class. Additional models combine expression and mutation
terms and include interaction effects. Survival analysis—where time, events and uncertainty all sit at
the same table—is used as the mathematical lens that turns this molecular chaos into structured state-
ments about patient risk. Within-omic and cross-omic comparisons, together with a contribution-based
classification of genes, summarise how gene-, isoform- and mutation-level signals relate.
Across cancers, expression-based features produce the richest and most stable survival landscapes.
Isoform abundance typically marches in step with gene-level effects, indicating that total transcript or
protein output often captures the main risk signal. Mutation baseline models yield sparser, more cancer-
specific patterns, and many apparent mutation effects weaken once expression is included, suggesting
that much mutation-driven survival variation is mediated through downstream expression rather than
acting as independent genomic switches. Isoform fractions, however, repeatedly break this symmetry:
they uncover genes where risk- and protection-associated isoforms coexist and contribute large sets of
isoform-only associations that are invisible at the gene level.
Integration of transcriptomics and proteomics shows that a subset of survival-associated genes exhibit
concordant RNA and protein effects—particularly for isoform abundance—while others are confined to
a single omics layer or to specific isoform representations. A contribution-based taxonomy, distinguish-
ing gene-only, isoform-only, shared-agreement, disagreement, mixed and mutation-conditioned signals
across omics layers, turns this into a compact conceptual map of the survival landscape.
Together, these results indicate that gene-level models capture only the broad strokes of the survival
story. The plot twists live in isoforms and in how their signals propagate—or fail to propagate—across
omics layers, providing a foundation for future isoform-sensitive biomarkers and multi-omics survival
atlases.

1
Introduction
Cancer is often described as a disease of the genome, but it is just as much a disease of dysregu-
lated information flow from DNA to RNA to protein. Somatic mutations alter the genomic template,
while alternative splicing reshapes how that template is read out into distinct RNA and protein isoforms
[Hanahan & Weinberg, 2011]. Together, these processes generate a diverse and sometimes unstable
repertoire of isoforms and protein products that ultimately influence tumor behavior and patient outcome.
Alternative splicing is a fundamental mechanism in eukaryotic gene expression, enabling a single gene
to produce multiple transcript variants, or isoforms, with distinct biological functions [Zhang et al., 2021].
Between 92-94 % of multi-exonic human genes undergo alternative splicing, contributing substantially
to cellular complexity, developmental processes and tissue-specific regulation [Dam et al., 2023]. This
expands the functional capacity of the genome far beyond what gene-level analyses alone might capture
and motivates an isoform-resolved view of gene regulation.
In cancer, alternative splicing is deeply entangled with tumor phenotypes [David & Manley, 2010]. Aber-
rant splicing events contribute to tumor initiation, progression and therapy resistance, and influence
hallmark processes such as cell proliferation, apoptosis evasion, angiogenesis, immune modulation
and metastasis [Wu et al., 2023]. Tumor-specific isoforms often possess unique functional properties,
and their expression can vary not only between tumors but also among subtypes within the same cancer
type, making isoforms attractive as biomarkers and therapeutic targets [Zhang et al., 2021]. highlighting
isoform-level data as clinically relevant, particularly for patient stratification and prognosis.
Gene expression and splicing mediate overlapping yet distinct biological signals, indicating that sub-
gene-level analyses reveal aspects of regulation and function that remain invisible at the gene level
[Dam et al., 2023]. Previous isoform-switch studies have shown that cancer-associated isoform changes
can occur without accompanying gene-level differential expression, and that such switches may alter
protein domains or other functional features [Vitting-Seerup & Sandelin, 2017]. This indicates that an
isoform-centric perspective can reveal aspects of tumor biology that are not visible at the gene level.
Advances in high-throughput, mass-spectrometry–based proteomics are beginning to enable isoform-
resolved quantification at the protein level. Proteomic readouts, by directly reflecting the functional
layer of gene expression, provide a more immediate view of cellular state than transcript abundance
alone [Dam et al., 2023]. Integrating matched transcriptomic and proteomic information therefore offers
a richer picture of isoform biology and can sharpen biomarker discovery.
Isoform analysis frameworks, such as differential transcript usage (DTU) models, have demonstrated
that biologically meaningful isoform variation can be captured in bulk and single-cell data at scale
[Gilis et al., 2022]. These approaches open the door to statistically robust isoform-level investigations
across large patient cohorts and complex experimental designs.
Nevertheless, the survival landscape of isoforms remains only partially charted. Isoform-level analy-
ses of RNA-seq data have linked specific alternative splicing events or transcript variants to patient
outcome in individual cancer types, for example through splicing-defined EMT subtypes with distinct
prognoses [Jun et al., 2022]. Systematic, multi-cancer isoform survival maps, however, are still rare.
Protein isoform–level survival analysis is even less explored, despite the availability of isoform-resolved
proteomics. Existing proteogenomic efforts, such as those from the Clinical Proteomic Tumor Analy-
sis Consortium (CPTAC), typically operate at the gene or protein-group level and do not systematically
assess the prognostic relevance of individual isoforms. As a result, a clear gap remains in integrating
proteomics and transcriptomics for isoform-level survival analysis.

1.1
Cancer heterogeneity and molecular layers
Cancer is not a single disease but a family of heterogeneous conditions arising when normal cells accu-
mulate genetic and epigenetic alterations [Hanahan & Weinberg, 2011]. Even within a single histologi-
cal cancer type, tumors can differ dramatically in their genomic alterations, transcriptomic programmes,
proteomic profiles and clinical behavior. This heterogeneity is layered:
• At the genomic level, somatic mutations, copy-number changes and structural variants reshape
the DNA sequence.
• At the transcriptomic level, changes in gene expression and alternative splicing generate distinct
isoform repertoires.
• At the proteomic level, differential translation, protein degradation and post-translational modifi-
cations shape the actual pool of protein isoforms and complexes.
Survival analysis provides a natural statistical lens for linking these molecular layers to clinical outcome.
By modeling how variation in genes, isoforms or mutations associates with time-to-event endpoints
such as overall survival, it becomes possible to ask which molecular features matter most for patient
prognosis and in which cancer types, as illustrated in recent pan-cancer survival resources and analyses
[Liu et al., 2018].
1.2
Alternative splicing and isoforms in cancer biology
1.2.1
Mechanisms of alternative splicing
In eukaryotes, most protein-coding genes are organised as multiple exons separated by introns. During
pre-mRNA processing, introns are removed and exons are ligated by the spliceosome, a large ribonucle-
oprotein complex that recognises conserved splice sites and catalyses intron removal [Matlin et al., 2005].
Alternative splicing refers to the regulated use of different splice sites or exon combinations from the
same precursor transcript, allowing a single gene to give rise to multiple mRNA isoforms (Figure 1.1).
Figure 1.1: Alternative splicing overview. A single multi-exon gene is transcribed into pre-mRNA, which can be spliced in different ways to
produce distinct mRNA isoforms. Translation of these mRNAs yields alternative protein isoforms with altered domain composition.
At the exon–intron level, alternative splicing can skip exons, choose alternative splice sites, swap mu-
tually exclusive exons or retain introns [Matlin et al., 2005]. These events can change untranslated re-
gions, protein domains and reading frames, or create and remove regulatory motifs, thereby influencing
mRNA stability, localisation and protein function [Matlin et al., 2005, Wright et al., 2022].

Splicing decisions are controlled by cis-regulatory elements in the pre-mRNA and trans-acting RNA-
binding proteins, including splicing enhancers and silencers and the factors that bind them [Matlin et al., 2005].
Their combined activity is further modulated by transcriptional and chromatin context, creating a dynamic
isoform landscape that can be rewired in disease [Wright et al., 2022].
From this perspective, each gene is less a single fixed entity and more a small family of potential isoforms,
whose composition can be tuned by the cell.
1.2.2
Isoforms in normal physiology and disease
Alternative splicing is a major contributor to cell- and tissue-specific gene regulation. Many isoforms
are expressed in restricted tissues or developmental stages, where they fine-tune signalling pathways,
receptor usage and protein interaction networks [Wright et al., 2022, Dolgalev & Poverennaya, 2023].
Recent work highlights alternative splicing as a key source of phenotypic diversity across species, tis-
sues and individuals, sometimes rivaling gene-level expression changes in functional impact
[Wright et al., 2022].
At the molecular level, isoform switches frequently change protein domains, intrinsically disordered re-
gions or post-translational modification sites, thereby altering protein localisation, stability and inter-
action partners [Vitting-Seerup & Sandelin, 2017, Dolgalev & Poverennaya, 2023]. Switching isoforms
can therefore be a faster and more reversible way to alter function than mutating the gene itself, making
splicing both a flexible substrate for evolution and a sensitive readout of dysregulation.
1.2.3
Isoform changes in cancer
In cancer, alternative splicing is widely perturbed. Pan-cancer analyses of transcriptomes from thou-
sands of tumors have revealed pervasive isoform switching across many cancer types. For example, a
large TCGA-focused study reported extensive splicing changes across 8 705 patients, including tumor-
type-specific and pan-cancer recurrent events [Kahles et al., 2018]. Systematic analyses have shown
that such switches are common, recurrent and often affect domains involved in protein interactions or
regulatory functions [Vitting-Seerup & Sandelin, 2017, Dolgalev & Poverennaya, 2023].
Many isoform changes map onto core oncogenic pathways, including apoptosis, metabolism, adhe-
sion and immune evasion [Zhang et al., 2021]. Across multiple cancers, specific isoforms have been
reported as prognostic markers, and isoform-level expression can carry prognostic information beyond
gene-level summaries [Jun et al., 2022, Dam et al., 2023, Zhang et al., 2021]. This suggests that the
choice of isoform, rather than overall gene expression level, can carry critical information about tumor
state.
From a quantitative perspective, recent work emphasises not only whether an isoform is expressed,
but how expression is redistributed among isoforms of the same gene. Measures of isoform switching
and differential transcript usage can uncover cancer-relevant events that are missed by gene-level sum-
maries [Vitting-Seerup & Sandelin, 2017, Dolgalev & Poverennaya, 2023]. In this thesis, isoforms are
therefore treated as primary units of interest rather than as a secondary annotation on top of genes.
Splicing factors and splicing dysregulation in tumors
The cancer-associated isoform landscape reflects both local changes at individual genes and global al-
terations in the splicing machinery [Dvinge et al., 2016]. Somatic mutations and expression changes in
splicing factors have been reported across multiple tumor types. In a comprehensive pan-cancer anal-
ysis of 33 TCGA tumor types, Seiler and colleagues characterised the somatic mutational landscape
of splicing-factor genes and showed that recurrent mutations in components such as SF3B1, SRSF2
and U2AF1 can drive widespread, characteristic splicing alterations [Seiler et al., 2018]. These aber-
rant splicing programmes can contribute to tumor initiation and progression and, in some cases, have
motivated the development of splicing-directed therapies [Islam et al., 2023].
Together, these observations motivate an isoform-centric view of cancer biology: rather than treating

1.3
Mutations as fundamental drivers of cancer
1.3.1
Mutational processes and cancer evolution
Somatic mutations arise from a combination of endogenous processes (e.g. replication errors, enzy-
matic deamination) and exogenous exposures (e.g. ultraviolet radiation, tobacco carcinogens). Over
the lifetime of a tumor, these processes leave characteristic patterns of base substitutions and small
indels in the cancer genome. Large pan-cancer studies have decomposed these patterns into recur-
rent mutational signatures that reflect specific mutagenic processes and DNA repair defects and can be
prognostic or predictive in some contexts [Alexandrov et al., 2020].
1.3.2
Mutations impacting splicing and protein function
Somatic mutations influence splicing and protein function at multiple levels. First, mutations that occur at
canonical splice sites (the conserved GT–AG dinucleotides, branch points or polypyrimidine tracts) can
directly disrupt recognition by the spliceosome, leading to exon skipping, intron retention or activation of
cryptic splice sites. Second, mutations in exonic or intronic splicing enhancers or silencers can modulate
the binding of splicing factors, altering exon inclusion in more subtle ways [Zhang et al., 2021].
Genetic variation affecting splicing is now recognised as a major mechanism linking genetic differences
to disease risk, with splicing quantitative trait loci (sQTLs) playing an important role in human traits
[Li et al., 2016]. In cancer, recurrent mutations in splicing-factor genes can lead to systematic splicing
changes, effectively rewiring isoform expression programs across the transcriptome [Seiler et al., 2018].
In parallel, mutations that induce frameshifts or premature termination codons can trigger nonsense-
mediated decay (NMD) of specific isoforms, reducing their abundance and shifting the isoform balance
of a gene. Alternatively, truncated protein products may be produced, which can be inactive, dominant-
negative or acquire novel functions.
From the perspective of protein isoforms, both coding and regulatory mutations can therefore change
which isoforms are produced, their relative abundance and their functional properties [Zhang et al., 2021,
Islam et al., 2023]. Mutation-level and isoform-level changes are thus not independent layers, but inter-
act in ways that can reinforce or counteract each other.
1.4
Multi-omics integration for survival analysis
1.4.1
Linking isoforms, mutations and clinical outcome
The central dogma provides a natural scaffold for connecting molecular alterations to patient outcome:
DNA mutations alter the genome; these changes, together with epigenetic and regulatory inputs, shape
RNA isoform repertoires; isoform-specific translation and post-translational regulation determine the
pool of protein isoforms; and these proteins, in turn, drive cellular phenotypes such as proliferation,
apoptosis, immune evasion and metastasis that ultimately influence clinical endpoints such as overall
survival and treatment response [Zhang et al., 2021, Wright et al., 2022]. Within this framework, alterna-
tive splicing is not a peripheral modification but a key layer through which genomic variation is translated
into phenotypic diversity.
Genetic variants and somatic mutations can perturb splicing at several levels — by altering core splice
sites, disrupting cis-regulatory elements or affecting splicing factor activity — thereby generating iso-
forms with distinct or even opposing functions [Li et al., 2016, Islam et al., 2023]. Isoform switches can
create strongly oncogenic isoforms (for example by producing constitutively active receptors or remov-
ing inhibitory domains), but can also buffer the impact of mutations when isoforms that skip mutated
exons or are subject to nonsense-mediated decay are favoured [Zhang et al., 2021, Wright et al., 2022].
The net effect on tumor behavior depends on the combined pattern of mutations and isoform regulation.

Multi-omics survival studies have begun to combine genomic, transcriptomic and proteomic features, but
isoforms are rarely integrated with mutations in a systematic, cross-cancer framework. Existing efforts
often focus on gene-level summaries, rely on relatively small sets of predefined features or restrict
attention to one or a few cancer types at a time. The present thesis extends this landscape by treating
isoforms as primary features in both transcriptomic and proteomic layers and by explicitly modeling how
isoform-level and mutation-level signals relate to survival across many cancers.
1.5
Aim, research questions, and hypotheses
The thesis is framed around the following overall aim:
To investigate how isoform-level expression and gene-level mutation contribute to
cancer survival across multiple cancer types, using an integrated multi-omics frame-
work spanning transcriptomics, proteomics and somatic mutations.
This aim is addressed using public RNA-seq and mutation profiles from TCGA together with mass-
spectrometry-based proteomics from PDC, focusing on overall survival and feature-wise Cox propor-
tional hazards models.
More concretely, the work is guided by the following research questions:
• RQ1: How are survival-associated signals distributed across genes, RNA isoforms and protein
isoforms in different cancer types?
• RQ2: To what extent do isoform-level features (abundance and relative isoform usage) reproduce,
refine or contradict gene-level survival associations?
• RQ3: How do gene-level mutations and expression-based features (gene and isoform) jointly
relate to survival, and in which settings do mutations retain, lose or gain apparent prognostic
impact when modeled together with expression?
• RQ4: Where transcriptomic and proteomic data overlap, how often do RNA- and protein-based
isoform signals point in the same survival direction, and when do they diverge?
The following chapters translate these questions into a concrete computational framework, beginning
with data processing and feature engineering and culminating in an integrated survival analysis that
compares gene-level, isoform-level and mutation-level contributions across omics layers and cancer
types.

2.1
Transcriptomics and Mutation Data Processing & Feature
Engineering
All transcriptomic and mutation analyses are based on TCGA RNA-seq data and somatic mutation calls
across 33 cancer types [Liu et al., 2018, Ellrott et al., 2018]. The goal was to construct, for each can-
cer type, harmonised clinical covariates, normalised gene- and isoform-level expression matrices, and
prevalence-filtered mutation features suitable for feature-wise Cox modeling. Implementation details
(scripts, launchers, directory structure) are documented in Appendix B [Støvlbæk, 2025a].
2.1.1
Clinical harmonisation and survival endpoints
TCGA clinical tables were first harmonised across cohorts. For each cancer type, case identifiers and
barcodes were cleaned, and key variables (tumor stage, sex, and, when available, molecular subtype)
were mapped to a standardised set of categories. Overall survival (OS) time and status were derived
from baseline and follow-up information using explicit priority rules. Only primary tumor samples with
valid OS time, event status, and the basic covariates were retained. These per-cancer clinical files serve
as the common foundation for all transcriptomic and mutation-aware Cox models.
2.1.2
Expression normalisation and feature filtering
Transcriptomic features were derived at three levels:
• gene-level expression;
• isoform-level expression (absolute abundance);
• isoform fractions within each gene (relative isoform usage).
For each cancer type and expression representation, raw RNA matrices were linked to the corresponding
clinical table and processed in a uniform way:
1. Minimum expression and prevalence. Features were required to reach at least 1 TPM in at
least 10 % of samples in the cohort. This filter was applied identically to genes and isoforms.
2. Normalisation. RNA-seq expression values were obtained as transcript-per-million (TPM) mea-
surements from TCGA and transformed using log2(TPM+1) to stabilise variance and maintain an
approximate fold-change interpretation.
Isoform fraction features were treated separately from absolute expression measures. Because isoform
fractions represent relative usage proportions bounded between 0 and 1, they were not log-transformed.
Instead, values were subjected to a small ε-clipping step, truncating fractions into the open interval
[10−6, 1 −10−6]. This avoids exact 0 or 1 values that can lead to numerical instabilities in downstream
analyses. The proportion of values affected by clipping was recorded for quality control, and optional
diagnostic plots of the clipped distributions were generated during preprocessing.
2.1.3
Gene-level mutation covariates
Somatic mutation features were constructed from the TCGA MC3 dataset [Ellrott et al., 2018]. After
harmonising patient identifiers and gene annotations, non-synonymous calls were aggregated to gene-
level binary indicators per patient.
Mutation consequences were collapsed into four functional groups:
• missense/inframe: missense, in-frame deletions and insertions;
• truncating loss-of-function: frame-shift deletions and insertions, nonsense and nonstop muta-
tions;
• splice: splice-site mutations;
• rna: RNA-level mutations.

2.2
Proteomics Data Processing and Isoform-Level Integration
All proteomic analyses are based on LC–MS/MS data from CPTAC/TCGA studies accessed through
the NCI Proteomic Data Commons (PDC) [Thangudu et al., 2024]. For each selected cancer type, PDC
manifests, peptide–spectrum match (PSM) tables, mapping workbooks and clinical files were down-
loaded and processed through a dedicated, version-controlled pipeline developed as part of this thesis
[Støvlbæk, 2025b]. Isoform-level quantification itself relies on an existing Bayesian framework and is
treated here as a black-box module (Section 2.2.4). A more technical specification (directory layout,
scripts, SLURM launchers) is provided in Appendix B.1.
2.2.1
Data acquisition and clinical linkage
For each CPTAC/TCGA study, a manifest was retrieved from the PDC web portal using standard filters
(proteome analytical fraction, qualified biospecimens and cases). These manifests list all proteomics
files and serve as stable entry points into the PDC archive.
Using the PDC/CPTAC Python API together with custom wrappers [ESAC Inc., 2022], the pipeline it-
erates over each manifest, downloads the corresponding PSM tables and mapping workbooks, and
organises them into a structured directory tree keyed by study, version and acquisition type.
Clinical annotations for the same cohorts were obtained from PDC and supplemented, where needed,
with CPTAC-provided clinical tables. Case identifiers were harmonised and linked to the proteomics
sample IDs, resulting in per-study clinical tables with vital status, overall survival time and basic covari-
ates (age, sex, tumor stage). Only tumor samples with both proteomics data and valid survival endpoints
were carried forward.
2.2.2
PSM mapping and peptide-level aggregation
The core of the proteomics pipeline is the mapping and aggregation of PSMs into coherent peptide ×
sample matrices.
For each study, mapping workbooks distributed via CPTAC/PDC are parsed to connect LC–MS/MS
entities to clinical samples. For TMT/iTRAQ experiments, the mapping specifies which run folders and
TMT/iTRAQ channels correspond to which case_id; for label-free experiments, runs are linked directly
to case_id. The pipeline:
• normalises run and channel identifiers to a consistent format;
• identifies tumor, normal and reference samples per run;
• produces separate mapping tables for patient and reference samples.
Before aggregation, duplicate participants are detected by grouping the mapping table by case_id and
counting distinct runs. Cases appearing in multiple runs are logged and temporarily disambiguated by
appending the run label (creating case_id_with_run), so that technical replicates remain distinct during
low-level processing.
PSM tables are then streamed and collapsed to the peptide level by summing intensities for identi-
cal peptide–sample combinations within each run. This yields per-run peptide × sample matrices for
patients and references. Finally, study-level patient matrices are constructed by summing intensities
across runs for the same peptide–case_id, after stripping the run suffix for patients. Reference sam-
ples retain their run suffix so that batch structure remains visible during QC.
2.2.3
Quality control and cohort definition
To check technical consistency across runs, pairwise Spearman correlations are computed between ref-
erence channels within each study and acquisition type. These are summarised as correlation heatmaps
and distributions of reference–reference ρ values. In the datasets analysed here, reference channels
formed coherent clusters, so no runs were excluded on the basis of this metric alone. The QC plots
nevertheless provide a record of technical variability for each study.

Isoform-level quantification and feature matrices

Conceptually, pepQuantIsoGene uses GENCODE protein-coding annotations [Mudge et al., 2025] to

focus here is on how its outputs are integrated into the downstream survival framework.
2.2.5

Before entering survival models, gene- and isoform-level protein abundances are further normalised

between samples [Ritchie et al., 2015].
3. Features with non-zero expression in fewer than 20 % of samples are discarded; the same mask
is applied to gene and isoform matrices to keep a consistent feature set.

2.3
Survival modeling across omics layers
Survival analysis is where time, events, and uncertainty all sit at the same table. In this thesis, it is treated
as the mathematical lens through which molecular chaos (genes, isoforms, mutations) is translated into
statements about patient risk. The same Cox proportional hazards framework is applied feature-wise
across transcriptomic, mutational and proteomic data, allowing direct comparisons between gene-level
abundance, isoform-level abundance and isoform fractions in multiple cancer types.
2.3.1
What is survival data
Survival (time-to-event) data consist of
• a time variable Ti, typically measured from diagnosis, surgery or study entry to an event or last
follow-up;
• an event indicator δi (e.g. 1 = death observed, 0 = censored);
• a vector of covariates xi (here: molecular features and clinical covariates).
Right-censoring arises when the event has not occurred by the end of follow-up, so that the true event
time is only known to be later than the observed time. Classical regression methods that ignore cen-
soring are biased in this setting, motivating dedicated survival models [Kalbfleisch & Prentice, 2011,
Dalgaard, 2008].
2.3.2
Cox proportional hazards models
The Cox proportional hazards model describes how covariates modify the instantaneous risk of an event
at time t [Cox, 1972]. For an individual i with covariates xi,

2.3.4
High-dimensional setting and regularisation
High-dimensional survival analysis raises several issues:
• p ≫n: far more features than samples.
• Multiple testing: testing thousands of features inflates the chance of false positives.
• Overfitting: complex models may fit noise instead of signal, leading to optimistic performance
estimates.
A pragmatic strategy adopted here is to use feature-wise, low-dimensional Cox models combined with
multiple-testing correction based on false discovery rate (FDR) control [Benjamini & Hocberg, 1995].
This turns the problem into many small, well-defined hypothesis tests and produces ranked lists of
candidate features rather than a single “big” risk score [Harrell, 2015].
More complex regularised Cox approaches, such as lasso or elastic-net penalties applied to many molec-
ular covariates, have been proposed for high-dimensional omics data [Tibshirani, 1997, Simon et al., 2011].
These methods are not implemented in this work but represent natural extensions for future modeling
efforts.

• Proteomics (CPTAC/PDC; see Chapter 2.2): only Model 1 is fitted, because genome-wide muta-
tion calls are not systematically available for these studies.
In all cases, the expression term expr is log-transformed expression (for transcriptomics: log2(TPM+1);
for proteomics: normalised, batch-corrected intensities) and covars contains age, sex, stage and, where
available, molecular subtype. The generic form of the expression baseline model is
Surv(OS_time, OS_event) ∼expr + covars.
Comparisons between gene-level and isoform-level models are used to assess whether isoforms im-
prove prognostic performance or reveal associations that are not visible at the gene level.
For cohorts with matched somatic mutation data, additional models are fitted on the same patient sets:
Surv(OS_time, OS_event) ∼mut + covars,
Surv(OS_time, OS_event) ∼expr + mut + covars,
Surv(OS_time, OS_event) ∼expr + mut + expr : mut + covars,
corresponding to Models 2–4, respectively. Together, these transcriptomic models provide a base layer
of survival associations at the gene and isoform level, both with and without mutation information, against
which proteomic survival signals can later be compared.

2.3.7
Stability filtering of Cox results
Before Cox results are used for summary plots and cross-omics comparisons, a post hoc stability filter
is applied. For each fitted model, a 95 % confidence interval is computed for the coefficient β and trans-
formed to the log2(HR) scale. Features with very wide intervals (CI width > 10 in log2(HR) units) or with
extreme effect sizes (absolute log2(HR) > 10) are excluded. The remaining, more stable associations
form the default input for all downstream summaries and visualisations.

Model 3: Joint expression and mutation main effects
Model 3 (Equation 2.9) includes both expression and mutation terms in the same Cox model, allowing
survival associations to be interpreted conditional on both molecular layers. This configuration answers
a simple but important question: once expression is known, how much prognostic information remains
in mutation status, and vice versa?
The distribution of significant mutation features differs markedly from that observed in mutation base-
line models (Model 2). Figure 3.10 shows that, across cancer types, significant mutation terms are
dominated by splice-site and RNA-level mutations. This pattern contrasts with Model 2 (Figure 3.8),
where missense and truncating mutations account for the majority of significant features. Although the
total number of significant mutation terms varies across cancers, the composition of mutation classes
consistently shifts between the two models.
This redistribution indicates that the inclusion of expression terms does not simply eliminate mutation-
survival associations, but selectively filters them. Missense and in-frame mutations, which dominate in
mutation baseline models, are attenuated once expression is included, whereas splice-site and RNA-
level mutations preferentially retain significance. Importantly, this shift is observed across all cancer
types, suggesting a systematic effect rather than a cancer-specific anomaly.
At the same time, the absolute number of mutation terms that remain significant in Model 3 is small com-
pared with the number of significant expression features (Figure 3.8), indicating that mutations contribute
a narrower and more selective set of survival associations once expression is accounted for.
This redistribution suggests that mutation effects detectable in bulk survival analyses are largely those
that directly perturb transcriptional regulation or isoform composition. Rather than acting as indepen-
dent drivers in parallel with expression, most mutation effects appear to be mediated through, or tightly
coupled to, downstream expression changes.

3.3.3
Role of isoform representations in shared RNA–protein signals
Within the cross-omics overlap analyses, gene-level expression, isoform abundance, and isoform frac-
tion represent alternative ways of assigning survival signal to genes. Comparing these representations
reveals how the choice of expression summarisation influences which genes are identified as shared
between transcriptomics and proteomics.
When considering gene-level expression alone, the intersection between transcriptomic and proteomic
survival features is modest. Incorporating isoform abundance increases the number of shared genes,
indicating that isoform-aware abundance models more readily capture expression patterns that are de-
tectable at both RNA and protein levels. In contrast, isoform fraction models produce many transcrip-
tomic survival associations that lack clear proteomic counterparts, resulting in a smaller shared compo-
nent for this representation.
This behavior mirrors the within-omics results, where isoform abundance often tracked gene-level ex-
pression, while isoform fractions revealed additional, representation-specific signal. The limited cross-
omics overlap for isoform fractions is therefore consistent with their role as a fine-grained layer that
captures variation in relative isoform usage, much of which does not translate into proportional differ-
ences in measurable protein isoform levels. Technical constraints in protein isoform quantification likely
further contribute to this gap.
Together, these patterns show that isoform-aware modeling influences not only how many survival as-
sociations are detected, but also which of them generalise across molecular layers. Isoform abundance
strengthens cross-omics concordance by refining gene-level expression programmes that propagate to
protein, whereas isoform fractions primarily expand the transcriptomic landscape and highlight a smaller
subset of robust, biologically coherent signals that persist across RNA and protein. This supports a
layered interpretation in which transcriptomics and proteomics are complementary, and where isoform-
level resolution is central to understanding when survival associations are shared and when they remain
omic-specific.
3.4
Directional concordance between gene- and isoform-level survival
signals
The previous sections (Sections 3.2.2 and 3.2.6) showed that isoform-based models contribute a large
share of survival-associated features within each omic layer. An open question is how far these isoform-
level effects simply mirror gene-level behavior and how often they introduce genuinely different directions
of risk. This section addresses that question by examining the directional concordance between gene-
and isoform-level hazard ratios in selected cancers.
3.4.1
Selection of cancers for detailed concordance analyses
For each omic layer, cancers were first ranked by the richness and stability of their survival signal, taking
into account the number of significant features, the presence of isoform-level associations, and the
balance between gene- and isoform-derived hits (Section 3.2.2). From this ranking, three representative
cancers were chosen per omic for in-depth concordance analyses.
In the transcriptomic data, KIRC, KICH and BRCA were selected. KIRC combines a strong gene-level
signal with extensive isoform representation, providing a reference case where gene and isoform models
both perform well. KICH is more skewed towards isoform-level associations, making it informative for
situations where isoforms appear to add information beyond the gene level. BRCA shows a rich and
heterogeneous survival landscape, useful for probing concordance patterns in a biologically complex
setting. In the proteomic data, CCRCC (=KIRC), UCEC and COAD were selected using analogous
criteria. These cancers display robust protein-level survival signals together with detectable isoform
abundance and isoform fraction effects, allowing the same concordance questions to be asked at the
protein layer.

3.6
Summary of key findings
The results chapter first established the data and modeling context. Cohort overviews in Section 3.1.1
(Figures 3.1–3.2) demonstrated large differences in sample size, number of events, follow-up time and
clinical composition across cancers and omics layers. Together, these descriptive results explain why
some cohorts support dense survival landscapes while others yield only sparse associations, and they
frame the interpretation of all subsequent models.
Within each omics layer, expression baseline models (Model 1; Section 3.2.2, Figures 3.6, 3.5 and 3.7)
revealed widespread but strongly cancer-specific survival signal. In transcriptomics, thousands of sig-
nificant features were detected in several cancers, with isoform-level representations contributing the
majority of associations. Isoform abundance closely tracked gene-level expression, whereas isoform
fractions showed broader effect-size distributions and introduced additional, representation-specific sig-
nal. Proteomic Model 1 results exhibited the same qualitative structure but with fewer significant fea-
tures and a smaller set of strongly signalling cancers, reflecting both reduced coverage and cohort size.
Together, these findings address RQ1 by showing that survival-associated expression features are com-
mon, but that their density and representation balance depend strongly on cancer type and omics layer.
Mutation baseline models (Model 2; Section 3.2.3, Figures 3.8 and 3.9) identified far fewer survival-
associated features, concentrated in a subset of high-mutation-burden cancers and dominated by mis-
sense and truncating variants. When expression and mutation covariates were combined (Model 3; Sec-
tion 3.2.4, Figure 3.10), expression-derived associations largely persisted, while mutation effects were
redistributed towards splice-site and RNA-level classes. Interaction models (Model 4; Section 3.2.5,
Figure 3.11) yielded a sparse set of significant effects, primarily involving isoform fractions, consistent
with both limited statistical power and the biological specificity of mutation-conditioned isoform usage.
Together, these results address RQ3 by showing that mutations contribute selectively to survival, often
through their interaction with or modulation of expression rather than as dominant main effects.
Analyses of overlap and directional agreement then examined how survival signal is distributed across
molecular representations within each omic. Within-omics overlap summaries (Section 3.2.6, Figures 3.12–
3.13) demonstrated that many survival-associated genes are detected exclusively through isoform-
based models, while purely gene-level associations form a minority in transcriptomics and a larger
but still incomplete subset in proteomics. Directional agreement analyses (Section 3.4.2, Figure 3.16)
showed that isoform abundance generally reinforces gene-level effects, whereas isoform fractions fre-
quently introduce mixed or discordant isoform behavior within the same gene. Representative loci (Sec-
tion 3.5, Figures 3.18–3.20) illustrated these patterns at the gene level. Together, these analyses ad-
dress RQ2 by demonstrating that isoform-level features can both refine and fundamentally redistribute
gene-level survival signals.
Finally, cross-omics comparisons linked transcriptomic and proteomic survival landscapes (Section 3.3,
Figures 3.14–3.15). Most significant genes were omic-specific, reflecting biological decoupling as well
as differences in coverage and power. However, a non-trivial, cancer-specific subset of genes—most
prominently in KIRC and to a lesser extent BRCA—showed concordant survival associations at both
RNA and protein levels. These results address RQ4 by showing that proteomics acts as a complemen-
tary filter rather than a mirror of transcriptomics, highlighting which expression-derived signals propagate
to the protein layer and revealing a smaller set of protein-centric survival associations.

4
Discussion
This thesis set out to characterise how gene-level expression, isoform-resolved expression and muta-
tion features contribute to patient survival across cancers, and how these signals align within and across
transcriptomic and proteomic layers. By combining feature-wise Cox modeling with isoform-aware rep-
resentations and cross-omics integration, the results provide a structured view of how prognostic signal
is distributed across molecular levels rather than concentrated in a single dominant mechanism.
4.1
Expression versus mutation as survival drivers (RQ1, RQ3)
Across cancers and omics layers, expression-based features emerge as the dominant and most ro-
bust contributors to survival associations, directly addressing RQ1. In the baseline expression models
(Model 1), both transcriptomics and proteomics yield extensive survival landscapes in several cancers,
with isoform-level representations—particularly isoform fractions—accounting for a large share of sig-
nificant features. Importantly, these expression-derived signals largely persist when expression and
mutation are modeled jointly in Model 3, indicating that expression captures a substantial fraction of the
prognostic variation that is statistically accessible in bulk tumor cohorts.
Mutation-based associations show a markedly different pattern. In mutation baseline models (Model 2),
significant features are comparatively sparse and concentrated in a subset of cancers and mutation
classes. However, rather than uniformly disappearing in joint models, mutation signals are redistributed
when expression is included. In Model 3, splice-site and RNA-level mutation terms become more promi-
nent relative to baseline mutation models, suggesting that mutations directly affecting transcriptional
processing are more likely to retain independent or partially independent prognostic associations once
expression is taken into account. This behavior is consistent with a view in which mutations influenc-
ing RNA regulation occupy a distinct position between genomic alteration and expression phenotype,
whereas many coding mutations exert their survival impact primarily through downstream expression
programmes.
Interaction models (Model 4) further refine this relationship between genotype and expression, address-
ing RQ3. Significant expression–mutation interactions are rare and dominated by isoform-fraction terms.
This sparsity has a clear statistical component: interaction models implicitly stratify patients by mutation
status, reducing the number of events per subgroup and amplifying uncertainty, while simultaneously in-
curring a large multiple-testing burden. As a result, only relatively strong interaction effects are expected
to pass FDR control. The interactions that do emerge therefore likely represent a selective subset of
mutation-conditioned isoform-usage patterns rather than an exhaustive map of genotype–expression
dependencies.
Taken together, these results support an expression-centred view of survival modeling in this setting.
Expression features—especially when modeled at the isoform level—act as the primary carriers of prog-
nostic signal, while mutations contribute more selectively by shaping which expression programmes are
active or by modulating isoform usage in specific genetic contexts. This implies that mutation baseline
survival models are unlikely to provide a comprehensive view of risk on their own, and that integrating
mutation information into isoform-aware expression frameworks may be a more effective strategy for
capturing clinically relevant genomic effects.
4.2
Isoform-level representations and redistribution of survival signal
(RQ1, RQ2)
Within the expression landscape, isoform-resolved models reveal a clear stratification between absolute
and relative representations. Isoform abundance behaves largely as a refinement of gene-level expres-
sion: across cancers it shows substantial overlap with gene-level associations, similar effect-size scales,
37

==PAGE:50==
and predominantly concordant directions. In practice, this suggests that isoform abundance often sharp-
ens gene-level signal rather than fundamentally changing its interpretation.
Isoform fractions, by contrast, introduce a qualitatively different layer of structure.
At the transcrip-
tomic level, isoform-fraction models display broader effect-size distributions and uncover many survival-
associated genes that are weak or absent at the gene level. Directional patterns further show that
these signals frequently involve mixed or discordant isoform behavior within the same gene, implying
that relative isoform usage can redistribute survival signal across antagonistic isoforms even when total
expression provides an ambiguous summary. In this sense, isoform usage emerges as an additional
regulatory dimension through which survival-relevant variation can appear, rather than a minor pertur-
bation of gene-level effects.
At the proteomic level, effect-size distributions are more similar across representations, consistent with
smaller cohorts and fewer events increasing uncertainty and widening estimates across all feature types.
Even under these constraints, isoform-resolved proteomic models often contribute comparable numbers
of significant features to gene-level analyses. Notably, the contrast between isoform abundance and
isoform fractions remains relevant: while isoform abundance tends to align with gene-level behavior,
isoform fractions more often point to representation-specific patterns that are not fully captured by total
protein abundance.
Taken together, these findings address RQ1 and RQ2 by showing that isoform-aware expression model-
ing is not redundant with gene-level analysis. Isoform abundance primarily reinforces gene-level behav-
ior, whereas isoform fractions frequently expose within-gene heterogeneity and opposing architectures
that can be invisible—or even misleading—at the gene level. This supports a stratified biomarker view:
gene-level models remain useful as a first pass, but isoform-fraction analyses are essential for identifying
loci where survival risk is carried by isoform composition rather than total expression alone.
4.3
RNA–protein concordance and added value of multi-omics (RQ1,
RQ4)
A third dimension of this thesis concerns how far survival signals propagate from RNA to protein, and un-
der which circumstances transcriptomic and proteomic layers instead highlight distinct aspects of tumor
biology. This question is addressed directly through the cross-omics overlap analyses, which compare
for each molecular representation whether genes are significant in transcriptomics, in proteomics, or in
both (Section 3.3, Figures 3.14–3.15).
At a global level, most survival-associated genes are detected in only one omic layer (Figure 3.14).
Transcriptomics-only signals dominate, consistent with the broader feature coverage and higher statis-
tical power of the RNA-seq cohorts described in Section 3.1.1. Proteomics-only associations form a
smaller but non-negligible group. Superimposed on these omic-specific components is a shared subset
of genes that are significant in both RNA and protein. These genes define a cross-omics “core”, where
survival-associated patterns in expression or isoform usage appear robust enough to propagate to the
protein level.
The extent of this shared core depends strongly on representation. When only gene-level expression
is considered, RNA–protein overlap is modest. Including isoform abundance and isoform fractions in-
creases the size of the shared subset, particularly when all representations are combined (Figure 3.14,
“all”). This mirrors the within-omics findings: isoform-aware models not only expand the detectable
survival landscape within each layer, but also increase the likelihood that a given gene is identified as
concordant across omics. In this sense, isoform-resolved representations sharpen both within-layer and
cross-layer views of survival signal.
Per-cancer analyses add an important nuance (Figure 3.15). Only a subset of cancers shows substantial
RNA–protein concordance, while others are dominated by omic-specific signals. This heterogeneity

aligns with differences in cohort size, event counts and proteomic coverage (Section 3.1.1), and indicates
that cross-omics agreement is most readily detected when both molecular layers carry sufficient signal.
In cancers where proteomic signal is weak or sparse, survival-relevant regulation may be clearly visible
at the RNA level but fall below detection thresholds in protein.
Taken together with the within-omics analyses, these results suggest three distinct roles for proteomics
in this framework. First, proteomics confirms a subset of propagated survival signals, identifying genes
whose associations are stable from RNA to protein and therefore strong candidates for follow-up. Sec-
ond, proteomics highlights omic-specific behavior: transcriptomics-only genes point to splicing or ex-
pression changes that do not translate directly to measurable protein differences, while proteomics-only
genes suggest protein-level regulation or technical constraints not captured by RNA alone. Third, the
joint analysis structures the survival landscape into RNA–protein concordant, transcriptomics-specific
and proteomics-specific components, providing a principled way to interpret downstream isoform- and
gene-level patterns.
Viewed through the research questions, these findings address RQ4 by showing that RNA and protein
agree on survival direction for a non-trivial, isoform-enriched subset of genes, while a large fraction
of survival signal remains omic-specific. In combination with RQ1 and RQ2, this supports a division-of-
labour model: isoform-aware transcriptomics provides a broad and sensitive map of survival-associated
variation, while proteomics adds value not by duplicating this map, but by filtering for propagated signals
and revealing a smaller set of protein-centric associations that would be difficult to infer from RNA alone.
4.4
A conceptual model of survival architecture across omics
Pulling the result streams together suggests a simple but multi-layered picture of how survival signals
are organised across genes, isoforms and mutations (RQ1–RQ4). Rather than a single dominant mech-
anism, the analyses point to a structured survival architecture in which different molecular layers and
representations play recurring, complementary roles.
At a high level, this architecture is organised along three axes:
1. Molecular layer: transcriptomics versus proteomics.
2. Representation: gene-level expression, isoform abundance, isoform fractions, and gene-level
mutations.
3. Mode of contribution: main effects, omic-specific versus shared signals, and interactions.
4.4.1
Expression defines the backbone; isoforms determine whether that backbone is
coherent or heterogeneous
Across cancers, the most stable and information-rich survival signal arises from expression main effects
(Model 1; Section 3.2.2). Within this backbone, isoform abundance most often behaves as a gene-like
refinement, whereas isoform fractions introduce an additional layer of structure that can split a locus
into opposing isoform-level associations (Sections 3.2.6 and 3.4.2, Figures 3.12 and 3.16). In practical
terms, this means that the gene-level view is sometimes an adequate summary of survival-relevant reg-
ulation, but that a substantial subset of loci requires isoform-aware interpretation to avoid averaging over
discordant isoform programmes (Section 3.5, Figures 3.18–3.20). This directly addresses RQ1–RQ2:
isoform-level features can reproduce, sharpen, or qualitatively reframe gene-level survival associations.
4.4.2
Mutations act primarily as sparse context; interactions are a thin, hard-to-detect
layer
Adding mutations changes the question from “what expression patterns are prognostic?” to “when is
prognostic expression conditioned by genomic status?”. In this thesis, mutation baseline associations
are comparatively sparse (Model 2; Section 3.2.3), and joint models indicate that many mutation signals
weaken once expression is included (Model 3; Section 3.2.4). Interaction models add a final, highly
39

==PAGE:52==
selective layer (Model 4; Section 3.2.5): statistically, interactions are difficult to detect because mu-
tation strata reduce effective sample size and events, and the multiple-testing burden is large. As a
result, the detected interactions likely represent a small set of relatively strong mutation-conditioned
expression effects rather than an exhaustive catalogue of genotype–expression dependencies. Taken
together, this supports an RQ3 interpretation in which mutations often influence survival indirectly by
shaping downstream expression programmes, with a smaller set retaining independent or interaction-
driven prognostic impact.
4.4.3
Across omics, concordance is informative precisely because it is limited and
cancer-dependent
Cross-omics overlap analyses (Section 3.3, Figures 3.14–3.15) indicate that much of the survival land-
scape is omic-specific, and that the balance between transcriptomics-dominated and proteomics-dominated
signal varies across the cancers available in both layers. Importantly, the shared RNA–protein compo-
nent is not uniform across tumor types: in these data, a substantial fraction of the shared signal is driven
by a small number of cancers (most notably KIRC), while other cancers show only limited overlap across
representations. This nuance matters for RQ4 and for interpretation more generally: a lack of RNA–
protein overlap should not be read as “disagreement” in a vacuum, but as the combined consequence
of biological decoupling (post-transcriptional and post-translational regulation), proteomic coverage, and
power differences between cohorts. Conversely, when a gene is repeatedly detected in both layers, it
stands out as a higher-confidence candidate whose prognostic association is more likely to reflect a
propagated molecular effect rather than a layer-specific proxy.
4.4.4
A parsimonious model
Viewed as a whole, the results support the following conceptual model of survival architecture across
omics:
• Expression main effects (gene and isoform) constitute the primary, high-coverage carriers of
prognostic signal in bulk tumor cohorts.
• Isoform fractions form a fine-grained regulatory layer that can redistribute survival signal within
a gene and expose heterogeneity that is invisible to gene-level summaries.
• Mutations contribute more selectively, often acting as upstream context that shapes expression
programmes, with a smaller subset retaining independent or interaction-driven prognostic effects.
• Proteomics provides a complementary constraint: it does not simply duplicate the transcriptomic
landscape, but helps distinguish which signals plausibly propagate to the protein layer and which
remain layer-specific.
In terms of what this means for the field, the architecture argues for a division of labour in multi-omics
prognosis studies: isoform-aware transcriptomics is well-suited for broad discovery of survival-associated
regulation, while proteomics is most valuable as a confirmatory and disambiguating layer that identifies
which transcript-level associations are reflected at the protein level. More generally, the results em-
phasise that “overlap” and “agreement” are different questions: concordance across omics adds con-
fidence, but the absence of concordance can itself be biologically meaningful, highlighting regulatory
layers where prognostic variation may be expressed through splicing, translation, or protein stability
rather than total mRNA abundance alone.

Conclusion
Overall, this thesis demonstrates that isoform-resolved expression modelling is central to understanding
survival associations in cancer. Rather than replacing gene-level analysis, isoform-aware approaches
clarify when gene-level summaries are sufficient and when they are misleading. By integrating mutations
and proteomics as contextual layers, the thesis outlines a stratified view of survival architecture in which
expression dominates, isoform usage refines and redistributes signal, and mutations provide selective
upstream context.
The resulting picture does not claim to be exhaustive, but it outlines a coherent survival landscape: hills
where gene-level expression gives a reliable overview and isoform abundance largely follows the same
direction; valleys where isoform resolution—especially relative isoform usage—is essential because
survival signal is redistributed across risk- and protection-associated isoforms; and a small number
of bridges where associations are observed in both transcriptomics and proteomics. Mutations add
further structure by shaping patient subgroups and, in a limited number of cases, conditioning expression
effects, but they contribute fewer robust associations than expression in this setting.
For now, the main message is clear: genes tell an important part of the survival story, but isoforms—and
their position within a multi-omics context—decide many of the plot twists. This perspective has direct
implications for future biomarker discovery and multi-omics survival modelling, suggesting that incorpo-
rating isoform-level information, together with mutation status as contextual information, is essential for
capturing the full structure of prognostic variation.

6
Future work
This thesis provides a systematic, isoform-resolved view of survival associations across transcriptomic,
mutational and proteomic layers, but it also highlights several directions where additional data, alter-
native modeling choices or extended analyses could substantially deepen the biological and clinical
insight.
A first natural extension concerns richer modeling of genomic variation. In this work, mutations were
encoded as gene-level indicators grouped by broad functional classes, enabling pan-cancer compar-
isons but necessarily simplifying the underlying genomic complexity. With larger cohorts or cancer-
specific analyses, future work could incorporate variant-level annotations, mutational signatures, copy-
number alterations or pathway-level genomic scores. Such representations may better capture combi-
natorial and context-dependent mutation effects and clarify when mutations exert prognostic influence
independently of expression.
Second, the results motivate more detailed investigation of isoform-specific regulation beyond bulk
measurements. Isoform-fraction signals were particularly prominent in transcriptomics, often redis-
tributing survival signal within genes. Future studies could integrate long-read RNA sequencing, allele-
specific expression, or single-cell transcriptomic data to disentangle whether these patterns reflect cell-
type composition, tumor heterogeneity or genuine isoform switching within malignant cells. Similarly,
advances in protein isoform quantification and proteoform-resolved mass spectrometry could improve
the interpretability of isoform-fraction effects in proteomics, where current coverage remains limited.
Third, the cross-omics analyses suggest opportunities for more explicit modeling of RNA–protein
coupling. Rather than assessing overlap post hoc, future work could employ joint or hierarchical models
that explicitly link transcriptomic and proteomic features, allowing uncertainty and measurement error to
be propagated across layers. Such approaches may help distinguish truly propagated survival signals
from coincidental overlap driven by correlated noise or shared clinical covariates.
From a methodological perspective, future work could also explore regularised or multivariate sur-
vival models. The feature-wise Cox framework used here offers transparency and scalability, but it
treats each feature independently. Penalised regression, Bayesian survival models or multi-view learn-
ing approaches could capture correlated feature sets and potentially improve stability, particularly for
interaction terms and mutation-conditioned effects that were sparse in this study.
Finally, the findings invite closer connection to clinical endpoints and translational validation. Ex-
tending the framework to progression-free survival, treatment response or therapy-specific subcohorts
could reveal whether isoform-resolved signals carry predictive as well as prognostic value. In the longer
term, targeted validation in independent cohorts or functional experiments focusing on representative
isoform-switching genes could help bridge the gap between statistical association and biological mech-
anism.
Together, these directions underline that isoform-aware, multi-omics survival modeling is still an emerg-
ing area. With improved data resolution and more integrative modeling strategies, future work can refine
the survival architecture outlined in this thesis and further clarify how genomic variation, isoform usage
and protein abundance jointly shape cancer outcomes.

B.1.4
Isoform quantification: implementation details
Script
mapping_pipeline/scripts/mapagg/isoform_quant.R
Inputs
mapping_pipeline/data/results
Outputs

Isoform quantification is performed as a post-processing step on aggregated peptide matrices. GEN-
CODE protein translations and gene models are used to map peptides to compatible isoforms and genes
via an Aho–Corasick trie. All peptides, including shared peptides, are retained.
Bayesian inference is performed with IsoBayes on a per-sample basis to estimate gene-level abun-
dances, isoform abundances, and normalised isoform fractions. Outputs are written as gene.csv, iso_log.csv,
and iso_frac.csv matrices, with optional serialisation of intermediate objects.
Large-scale execution is supported via SLURM array jobs, and final CSV exports are produced for
downstream expression preprocessing and survival analysis.

B.2
Expression preprocessing and CoxPH implementation
This appendix documents the implementation of post-quantification expression preprocessing and uni-
variate Cox proportional hazards modelling used in the proteomics analyses.
B.2.1
Expression preprocessing pipeline
Scripts
02_proteomics/scripts/*.R
Outputs
02_proteomics/out
Clinical metadata are split by study and harmonised using 00_split_clinical_by_study.R. Tumour
stage strings are collapsed to coarse categories, sex labels normalised, and optional filters applied to
exclude reference samples.
Expression matrices are normalised using cyclic loess (01_cyclic_loess_normalize.R) and filtered
for sparsity (02_filter_positive_expression.R). Features are retained only if the fraction of strictly
positive values exceeds a configurable threshold (MIN_POSITIVE_FRAC, default 0.20).
Batch correction is applied with RUVIII (03_ruv_batch_correction.R). Corrected matrices and esti-
mated unwanted variation factors are written to disk, and QC plots are generated by 04_plots.R to
confirm attenuation of batch effects without loss of biological structure.
—
B.2.2
Univariate CoxPH pipeline
Script
01_univariate_coxph.R
Outputs
02_proteomics/out/*/cox_results
For each study and data type (gene, iso_log, iso_frac), the driver merges preprocessed expression
matrices with study-specific clinical data and fits univariate Cox models using survival::coxph. Model
fitting is wrapped in tryCatch to prevent numerical failures for individual features from terminating the
analysis.
For each feature, the script records effect estimates, hazard ratios, Wald statistics, p-values, Benjamini–
Hochberg FDR, sample and event counts, and direction labels. Full and FDR-significant result tables
are written per study and data type, together with textual summaries.
—
B.2.3
Stability filtering of significant results
Script
00_filter_cox_ci.R
Outputs
02_proteomics/out/*/cox_filtered
To stabilise effect estimates prior to plotting, a confidence interval filtering step removes features with
excessively wide 95 % intervals or extreme log2 hazard ratios. Retained associations are written to
mirrored directories and aggregated into a single summary table used for all downstream visualisation
and cross-data-type comparisons.
