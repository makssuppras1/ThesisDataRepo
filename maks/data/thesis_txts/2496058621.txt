Abstract
The formation of clouds in the atmosphere has recently been shown to be
dependent to a great degree on the presence of ions. The study of ion-assisted
nucleation has been carried out using a 3D particle Monte Carlo algorithm.
The original code showed very promising results, and so the goal of this thesis
was optimization of the code to allow for faster simulations, more particles,
and longer run-times. Additionally, the inclusion of ion-ion and ion-dipole
interactions. The goal is the investigation of nucleation rates in experimental
settings, with the goal of one day to simulate the nucleation of atmospheric
conditions. Simulation times have been lowered by at least a factor 40 for the
original simulation cases, by writing parts of the code in C++. Simulation
results show many cases where dipoles do not nucleate by themselves, but
the addition of ions assists greatly assist in the nucleation process. The code
changes has resulted in simulation speeds 40 times faster than the original
code, for the case of 1000 dipoles.
The inclusion of an Octree made the
overall code twice as fast for the case of 1000 dipoles, and the dipole-dipole
interaction algorithm 7 times as fast. With the inclusion of the Octree, the
new code would then be 80 times faster than the original for the case of 1000
dipoles. The new code reached a simulation time of 60s for a simulation with
1000 dipoles and 10 ions, which is a factor 75 times as long as for the original
code, and is stable with regards to the kinetic energies of ions.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Clouds are some of the most fascinating phenomena to gaze at in the sky.
They are a source of wonder with all their diﬀerent shapes and sizes, and
have since ancient times been mystifying to mankind. However, just as they
are pretty to look at, they can also bring havoc, in the form of extreme and
dangerous weather. This same ambivalent nature persists for clouds from a
scientiﬁc point of view. Although centuries of scientiﬁc study has gotten the
ﬁeld far, there are still important questions regarding clouds, about how they
are formed, and the nature of the eﬀect they have on Earth’s climate. Clouds
on a large scale, and even down to the tiny aerosol constituents, have been
found to have many diﬀerent eﬀects on the climate and even on health. This
thesis concerns the earliest stages of cloud formation, which is the nucleation
of tiny gas-phase particles. It has been shown[?] that around 54% of cloud
condensating nuclei(CCN) starts with new particle formation(NPF). The for-
mation of new particles is however still a big are of research in science. It is
thought that most of NPF comes from a comparably small number of species
in air, such as ions, compared to the large chemical range of gas particles.
The subject of ion assisted cloud formation will be investigated in this
thesis. A newly developed and novel method to study this aspect of NPF,
using a 3D particle Monte Carlo simulation of nucleation[22], shows very
promising results for sulphuric acid-water clusters. The challenge and major
focus of this thesis is the introduction of ions, and making improvements to
code that results in better faster run-times and performance. The impor-
tance of ions has been brought into the light recently[5]. Ions are produced
for most of the troposphere mainly by galactic cosmic rays(GCR) and thus
the study of how radiation from the universe aﬀects us here on earth has
become of big interest in the scientiﬁc community[2]. From a climate point
of view, the eﬀect of indirect radiative forcing from aerosols still carry great
uncertainty[19].

1.1. MOTIVATION
CHAPTER 1. INTRODUCTION
The 3D particle Monte Carlo code can be a big resource to help investi-
gate the importance of ions in nucleation, both for the atmosphere and for
other practical uses. The Monte Carlo method simulates randomness, and
has been in use ever since computers made it possible to eﬃciently generate
random numbers. The interesting dynamics happen on the timescale of days,
but the interactions between gas molecules happens on timescales orders of
magnitudes lower. This presents an interesting challenge for the computa-
tional study of nucleation in the atmosphere, and will be a big part of this
thesis.
1.1
Motivation
The motivation behind this thesis is to take scientiﬁc understanding of the
cloud formation a step further. A model and algorithm based on the Monte
Carlo method has already been made[?] and just a few key ingredients are
missing to give new insight into the nucleation of tiny particles. It is believed
that ions play an important role in the nucleation process[5], and some models
indicate ions may be the most important factor[6].
Most similar work consists of models based on averaged quantities. The
hope is that the stochastic nature of the 3D particle Monte Carlo method
can give new insight to the nucleation of particles. The developed code shows
great initial results, and the task of this thesis is to improve performance and
include electromagnetic interactions between species of particles in a stable
manner. Additionally some practical features of the new code were required.
First, due to the limitations on run-time of code on DTU High Performance
Clusters, it was required that the code should be able to continue simulations,
by loading the physical data from the particles at the last step. Second, since
there’s many cases and scenarios to simulate, another task was to make a
feature to easier load cases without having to go into the code and look for
parameters.
Overall, the major goals of this thesis consists of
1. Make the original code run faster and simulate more particles.
2. Implement ion-ion and ion-dipole interactions in a stable man-
ner.
3. Do test runs to compare nucleation rates with those obtained
from experimental setup.

2.1. AEROSOLS
CHAPTER 2. MODEL
2.1
Aerosols
The cloud cover in the sky consists of a wide range of diﬀerent particles, of
diﬀerent sizes. The smallest of these with a size of less than 1µm are called
aerosols. The nucleation of the tiniest aerosols is a scientiﬁc ﬁeld with huge
research.
In short, aerosols can be categorized based on their origin and
their eﬀects on Earth’s radiative balance. The primary aerosol types are[?]
sulphurous gases, sea-salt spray, ﬁne dust raised by winds in the deserts, and
organics. Sulphurous gases like SO2 are precursors to sulphuric acid, and one
important step is the reaction of SO2 with hydroxyl radical OH. There’s a
lot of evidence that these sulphuric acid gas-phase molecules participate in
nucleation.
Several studies also indicate that the atmospheric nucleation
rate depends on [H2SO4]n for n between 1 and 2[6]. Same authors show
a strong correlation between H2SO4 and nucleation-mode particles when
taking into account 90min time lag for the time-frame it takes for nucleation.
Sulphorous gases react with other atmospheric species, and the important
ones for nucleation are ammonia sulphate, which remain solid particles, and
liquid particles in the form of sulphuric acid.
The direct aerosol eﬀect is that some scatter sunlight, and some absorb it.
Sulphates are particularly good at scattering sunlight[?] Buildup of these in
the stratosphere helps against global warming. The indirect aerosol eﬀects
consist of their eﬀects on cloud formation.
Thus although they are tiny
particles, they have a signiﬁcant role on Earth’s climate and atmosphere.
The most important processes for nucleation in the atmosphere are thought
to be[4] Binary nucleation(BHN) of sulphuric acid and water, ternary nucle-
ation(THN) of sulphuric acid, water and ammonia, and ion-induced nucle-
ation. These three are thought to be among the most important processes for
the nucleation of tiny aerosols. In all cases, sulphuric acid is believed to play
a key role. The picture becomes even more confusing when one considers
nucleation in diﬀerent environments, e.g iodine compounds such as OIO are
assumed to be the most important for nucleation near coastal areas.
BHN can’t explain NPF in lower troposphere[20], for that we need THN,
either with ammonia or with ions.
The latest research presented shows that ions and sulphuric acid water

Nucleation theory
The nucleation of aerosols is a process that begins with individual gas molecules
grouping together to ﬁnally form stable clusters. If growth persists, they can
eventually grow large enough to become CCN, which are the precursors to
cloud drops. There are several theories that explain nucleation, from diﬀerent
perspectives. Some are classical and based on thermodynamics, while some
are based on kinetic models and yet others are based on quantum chemical
calculations. Models exist that include aspects of all of them. The ﬁrst two
will be introduced, with some variations that are important for this work.
Classical nucleation theory still forms the basis for thermodynamic inter-
pretation of nucleation. The growth of a droplet in the atmosphere can be
examined by thermodynamics and in particular Gibbs free energy[4].

The surface tension makes it diﬃcult for clusters to grow. One view of
surface tension is a force pr. length, and there’s an energy cost when trying
to do work against this force, in the way of increasing the size of the cluster.

inter-molecular attractive forces towards the bulk interior, and not from the
ambient gas-phase. Condensation increases the radius of the droplet, and in
eﬀect pushes the surface molecules further outward, doing work against the
attractive forces. As the droplet grows larger and larger, it will be less costly
to add an additional molecule to the cluster, as an additional molecule can
better ﬁt into the interior without a substantial increase in surface area.

Algorithm
The main algorithm and its components will be explained, along with some of
the considerations, regarding computational eﬃciency, that went into them.
There’s many diﬀerent algorithmic choices to be made when coding a particle
Monte Carlo method, with diﬀerent beneﬁts and drawbacks to each choice.
Generally there are three main approaches for simulation of gas-phase
molecules, biological molecules, polymers and other systems, which is the
Monte Carlo(MC) method, the Molecular Dynamics(MD) method and Brow-
nian Dynamics(BD)[7, 8]. The algorithm used for this thesis, for the ion in-
duced nucleation of sulphuric acid - water clusters, uses a combination of all
three, which is not uncommon. MC and BD share some commonalities and
depending on context and deﬁnition of MC, BD could be said to be simply
an MC method. A quick explanation of each is warranted. MC simulates
changes in state of a system with randomly generated states, with each gen-
erated state being either accepted or rejected based on likelihood ratios. MD
in particle physics attempts to capture the forces and interactions between
particles, to integrate the system forward in time using Newton’s laws. BD
uses random numbers to generate states that follow a certain distribution of
a system, and is often used in diﬀusion processes. The movement of particles
in our simulations are given by the BD method, described in [Kohn], where
as dipole and ion evaporations and recombinations follow a more traditional
MC method based on acceptance/rejection of generated states. Lastly, ion-
ion and ion-dipole interactions are implemented with the MD methods and
electromagnetic interactions between these particles will be given in detail.
The original code was written in Python2 as a modiﬁcation for a code by
K¨ohn[17]. To make the code faster, it has been written in Python3, using
Numpy, and the bottlenecks for performance have been written in C++ with
multiprocessing using OpenMP, which is called from Python using the Ctypes
module.

3.3.7
Comparison of collision algorithms
Multiple runs have been done with all the collision algorithms. Point-point
is the most eﬀective computationally, but alas do not give any collisions.
The particles are simply too small the ever be detected to collide in any
reasonable simulation time. The point-line algorithm doesn’t seem eﬀective
in producing collisions, and furthermore can’t take advantage of Newton’s
third law, so it becomes an O(n2) algorithm instead of O((n(n−1))/2). The
line-line algorithm is very eﬀective in detecting collisions, and is the most
promising. It is computationally slow due to many calculations required for
each pair of particles, but this is partly mitigated by it being symmetric and
thus has O((n(n −1))/2).

3.6
Boundary Conditions
The original code was implemented with periodic boundary conditions cond-
tions and hard wall boundary conditions. Although the model and simula-
tions are often viewed in the frame of periodic boundaries, the initial intro-
duction of ions to the algorithm showed instability with regards to unphysical
kinetic energies, and thus in this section we will explore a couple of diﬀerent
methods to stabilize the ions, including the eﬀects of boundary conditions.
Two kinds of boundary conditions will be tested for stability. These are
periodic and hard wall. Boundary conditions usually have some unintended
consequences. For simplicity, one can imagine a single particle ﬂying around.
Using periodic boundary conditions, the system will not conserve linear mo-
mentum, while for periodic boundary conditions, the angular momentum will
not be conserved. For collision algorithms, any boundary condition will give
unwanted eﬀects as previously mentioned. Several other boundary eﬀects
will be investigated in this section.

There’s two things to note about the potential energies. First that the
variance increases with time, and especially that lower potential energies are

This eﬀect is also a positive feedback loop, causing the ions to reach higher
and higher and eventually unphysical speeds. Secondly, there’s a lower limit
on the potential in the form of the domain, as the particles are kept within

Periodic boundary conditions
In periodic boundary conditions, the particles can leave a side of the domain,
but it will be symmetrically reinserted at the opposite side. Technically, pe-
riodic boundary conditions means that we imagine extending the simulation
domain inﬁnitely in all directions, i.e repeating the domain and all informa-
tion within it. This has implications for some purposes, e.g some methods
for calculating electromagnetic interactions and gravitational interactions, as
the number of terms for the sums for Coulomb forces would be inﬁnite. An
example iss Ewald summation, where Fourier series are used for the long
range interactions, under the view that the boxes(simulation domains) are
repeated in all dimensions[31], to achieve an O(NlogN) algorithm for forces
for the number of particles N. For our purposes, the main point of the bound-
ary conditions are such that the particles stay within the domain, and the
ions to be energetically stable.

3.8
Datastructure - Octree
As the number N of particles in domain increases, the algorithms take longer
and longer computational time. The force and collision algorithms intro-
duced in the earlier sections scale as O(N 2), which is a scaling order that
will end up causing computational bottlenecks as the number of particles
increase. A good alternative is to use an Octree, which is a datastructure
that inserts particles into boxes, such that the particles only notice neighbor
particles in the same box during collision detection, and long range forces can
be approximated by center of masses of the boxes. Octrees are widely used
in astrophysical simulations, where they are a central part of the Barnes-Hut
algorithm. The algorithm forms the basis for some high performance comput-
ing implementations as in[32], where a cluster of GPUs were used to simulate
562 million particles eﬃciently. The big diﬀerence is the scaling, which goes
as O(NlogN), which is a massive improvement over the traditional O(N 2)
algorithms.
There are many diﬀerent variations and implementations of Octrees for
diﬀerent purposes[33]. The focus of this implementation has been a lightweight
implementation with small memory load and useable with multi processing.
The data structure is recursive, and one can view a subnode as the be-
ginning of a completely newOctree for that sub region.

Many diﬀerent runs have been made, the code seems to work for a variety

compared to the original code.

during experimental setup.

Chapter 5
Conclusion
Ions show to be very important for many of the simulations for the nucleation
rates. The nucleation rate is a factor 10 too high for the case of 1000 dipoles
and 10 ions, but it is still closer than the original code. The selected algorithm
for collisions is the line-line algorithm, as point-point and point-line give no
collisions. The ions are part of the code in a stable manner, and the kinetic
energies do not reach unphysical scales. This will allow for further simulation
of the importance of ion-induced nucleation.
Simulations using up to 50000 dipoles and 20 ions were performed using
the setup of the reaction chamber with the Octree and 20 CPUs for the line-
line algorithm in C++. Ion speeds were stable and showed slight decrease
in time, presumably due to increased mass from ion-dipole collisions causing
less acceleration. Interestingly, the only stable growth appeared to be from
ions, as no dipole clusters reached sizes larger than that corresponding to
two dipole molecules.
Performance compared to original code
The new code appears to be 40 times faster for the case of 1000 dipoles,
and the runlength of ion-dipole case was extended by a factor 75, with the
possibility of further runlength.
Octree speedup
The total speed up of the Octree for the case of 1000 dipoles was a factor 2
for the overall code, and a factor 7 for the dipole-dipole algorithm itself.

5.1
Outlook
The challenge ahead is to get the proper nucleation rates and address collision
rates. More simulations of diﬀerent cases needs to be done, with diﬀerent
volumes. Along with checking for collision rates between dipoles, the ion-
dipole collision frequency should also be calculated.
An equation for the
ion-dipole collision frequency is given in[18], which might give some insight
to ions in terms of their collisions.
5.1.1
Inclusion of additional chemical species
As mentioned in the model section, there are some species that are presently
assumed to be more important than others. Besides ions, the inclusion of am-
monia may be of interest to research at some point. The clusters would then
be ternary sulphuric acid - ammonia - water systems. The challenging part is
that it becomes a combinatoric problem, with each species more interaction
algorithms are required. At the very least, it will be necessary to add in a
third index to the particle array, to keep track of the indices. The best way
to do this in Python and C++ is to add the index at the beginning, such that
the array containing particle information becomes particlelist[s, i, k], where
s is the species number, i is the index for particle i of species s, and k is one
of the quantities associated with particle i, e.g. mass or radius. This is to the
alternative of adding index s at the end, which would produce more cache
misses.
