Abstract
This thesis investigates the development and deployment of a real­time underwater im­
age enhancement model tailored for integration into UVision’s operational workflow. The
main research goal was to create a dehazing model capable of enhancing underwater
visuals in murky port environments while maintaining a processing delay below 500 mil­
liseconds per frame. This performance benchmark enables real­time operation (≥2 FPS)
on CPU­based hardware, reflecting the needs of UVision’s clients, who often lack access
to GPU acceleration.
A lightweight learning­based model, FUnIE­GAN, was selected as the foundation due to
its suitability for low­latency inference. The training pipeline began with a domain adapta­
tion attempt using the EUVP dataset, but results showed that training directly on UVision’s
proprietary underwater data yielded better performance. To further improve visual quality,
a custom color­balance loss function was introduced. The final model was converted to
ONNX format for compatibility with the company’s Rust­based interface and evaluated
across multiple deployment methods.
Extensive runtime testing on an Apple M2­powered device revealed that input image re­
sizing was the most effective optimization technique, allowing the model to meet the real­
time constraint consistently. Other methods, such as quantization (FP16, UINT8), GPU
offloading via CoreML and FastAPI, and ONNX graph optimizations, were evaluated but
showed limited improvements in the current setup. The final model demonstrated robust
visual enhancement and stable runtime performance when deployed in Rust, with further
gains possible in Python or GPU­based environments.
The results show that the proposed solution is well­suited for operator­side deployment
and can serve as a foundation for future upgrades, including GPU acceleration and adap­
tation to different underwater environments.

Introduction
1.1
UVision and Their Current Dehazing Pipeline
UVision is a young Danish company that specializes in underwater 3D scanning. It was
founded in February 2022 and has since developed both hardware and software solutions
for capturing and processing underwater environments.
Their main product is the UScanner, shown in Figure 1.1, a compact underwater 3D scan­
ner equipped with an NVIDIA Jetson Xavier NX for onboard processing. To operate the
scanner, UVision also offers the UController, a cross­platform control application avail­
able for Windows, Linux, and Mac. With the UController, users can view live video feeds,
monitor point clouds in real­time, start recordings, and upload data directly to the cloud
for further processing.
In addition to their products, UVision provides 3D scanning services on request. Scans
can be performed using various mounting setups, such as ROVs, telescopic poles, or
handheld by divers. They also offer training sessions, typically completed in a single day,
due to the intuitive nature of their system.
Figure 1.1: UScanner from UVision, equipped with an NVIDIA Jetson Xavier NX
UVision’s underwater 3D scanning system includes both hardware and software compo­
nents. The scanning device, the UScanner, is typically mounted on an ROV and con­
nected to the operator’s laptop via an Ethernet cable (RJ45 connector). This direct con­
nection enables real­time data transmission, including video and point cloud data.
For image enhancement, UVision currently uses the Unsupervised Colour Correction
Method (UCM) [1]. This method performs basic dehazing and color correction directly
on the ROV using onboard processing. While UCM improves visibility to some extent,
its effectiveness is limited in more challenging underwater environments, especially in
murky ports or highly turbid waters. As a result, there is room for improvement through
more advanced or data­driven dehazing techniques.
1.2
Motivation and Background
In recent years, advances in digital imaging and computer vision have enabled the devel­
opment of systems capable of operating in increasingly challenging environments, such
as underwater scenarios. For the autonomous navigation of an Remotely Operated Vehi­
cle (ROV), the robot must provide reliable visual data, both for the human operator and its

own onboard decision­making. Underwater imaging presents one of the most challenging
cases, as light behaves fundamentally differently due to the physical properties of water.
The approach to underwater imaging often depends on the specific environment in which
it is used, for example, coastal and shallow waters, coral reefs, the open ocean, deep
sea, or freshwater environments.
Applications such as marine biology, Autonomous Underwater Vehicles (AUVs), maritime
archaeology, and even underwater Augmented Reality (AR) rely heavily on clear visual

becomes severely degraded. This degradation not only limits human interpretation but
also compromises the performance of computer vision tasks like detection, segmentation,
tracking, and 3D reconstruction.
To correct these effects, various dehazing and image enhancement techniques have been
proposed, ranging from traditional image processing methods to more modern, data­
driven approaches. Enhancing underwater images is not just a visual improvement; it
is essential for enabling scientific exploration, improving human­robot interaction, and
supporting real­time decision­making in autonomous systems operating underwater [2].
An example of dehazing results can be seen in Figure 1.2, clearly illustrating how much
additional detail can be revealed once the haze is removed, details that would otherwise
remain obscured.

1.3
The Challenge of Underwater Imaging
Unlike images taken in air, underwater images suffer from unique and complex degra­
dation effects due to the interaction of light with water and suspended particles. Several
physical phenomena impact the visual quality of underwater images [4, 5]:
• Absorption and color shift: water absorbs light at different wavelengths unequally.
Red light is absorbed first, often within just a few meters, followed by green and then
blue. As a result, underwater images usually have a strong bluish or greenish tint
2

==PAGE:13==
and lack warm color information. The deeper the scene, the stronger this effect
becomes.
• Backscatter: Light is scattered by suspended particles such as sand, silt, and bub­
bles, reducing image contrast, blurring fine details, and introducing uneven illumi­
nation across the scene.
• Lighting conditions: Achieving consistent and even lighting underwater is chal­
lenging. As sunlight enters the water, it becomes scattered and attenuated. The
angle of sunlight, time of day, and water depth all affect the amount and type of light
that reaches the scene.
• Water clarity: The clarity of water depends on its particulate content, which can
vary significantly based on geography, weather, tides, and seasons. This variability
causes unpredictable haze and cloudiness that severely affect image quality.
These combined distortions result in images that are often dark, blurry, color­distorted, and
low in contrast, making both human interpretation and computer vision­based processing
significantly more difficult.
Understanding the environmental characteristics of the specific underwater context, such
as geographic location, typical visibility, water turbidity, and lighting conditions, allows for
more effective fine­tuning of enhancement models. By tailoring dehazing and enhance­
ment techniques to the known features of target environments (e.g., port areas or specific
coastal sites), it becomes possible to improve both the quality of visual output and the
robustness of downstream tasks such as detection or tracking [6, 7].
1.4
Objective and Scope
The entire project is centered around one clear goal:
• Develop an underwater dehazing model that can run in under 500ms per frame
(≥2 FPS) on the operator’s laptop.
This performance requirement, defined by UVision, shaped every design choice in the
thesis, from data preprocessing and model architecture to deployment format and infer­
ence pipeline. The ultimate aim is to provide the operator of the ROV with the clearest
possible underwater view in real time, without introducing noticeable latency.
However, to make this core objective feasible and practically valuable, the following sub­
goals will also be addressed throughout the project:
• Develop a reusable training pipeline that enables future model improvements or
adaptations as new data becomes available. This includes support for supervised
learning using synthetically generated targets from preprocessing techniques.
• Benchmark inference performance and output quality across different platforms
(CPU and GPU) to evaluate the feasibility of deploying a trained dehazing model in
real­time underwater applications.
• Explore and evaluate optimization strategies for achieving real­time performance
under deployment constraints, including techniques such as quantization, input res­
olution scaling, GPU acceleration, ONNX graph optimization, and multi­threaded
execution.
UVision’s current system operates at 8 FPS using the NVIDIA Jetson Xavier NX 16GB
compute module. While the original plan was to deploy the model directly on this em­

Challenges Addressed in This Thesis
Underwater imaging presents several unique challenges that degrade visual quality and
complicate downstream tasks such as object detection, navigation, or operator feedback.
Based on existing literature and practical observations from UVision’s system, this thesis
focuses on addressing the following key challenges:
• Murky visual conditions in port environments and lack of ground truth: Shal­
low water sites like ports often contain suspended particles, organic matter, and
poor lighting, leading to reduced visibility and intense red­brown color casts [8, 9].
• Strict runtime constraints for real­time deployment: In operator­in­the­loop sys­
tems, delays above 500ms hinder usability. Many enhancement models exceed this
threshold when deployed on CPU [10].
• Limited hardware support and platform compatibility: The system must work
without GPU acceleration and run within a Rust­based pipeline, which limits the
availability of optimized backends and tools [11].
• Bias in training data and overfitting to specific conditions: Models trained on
limited datasets may not generalize well to unseen environments, often producing
color distortions in blue water or high­visibility scenes [12].
• Difficulties in measuring image quality objectively: No ground truth images are
available in real­world underwater settings. This makes traditional full­reference
metrics unusable [13, 14].
These challenges form the core focus of this thesis and are addressed through data prepa­
ration, model fine­tuning, and a deployment­focused optimization pipeline.

2
Literature Review and Approach
This chapter provides an overview of the literature and techniques relevant to underwater
image enhancement, with a particular focus on methods that could be applied to images
captured in shallow waters, such as harbors. Although many existing approaches in the
field are developed for deep­sea environments, where visibility is limited and images tend
to have strong blue or green color casts, this use case required evaluating whether these
methods were suitable for more complex and variable shallow­water conditions.
To address this, general underwater image enhancement techniques were explored, in­
cluding dehazing methods commonly applied in deep­ocean scenarios. From there, more
recent approaches based on Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) were examined,
which have shown promise in learning to restore or enhance underwater images in a
data­driven manner.
Following the discussion on GAN­based approaches, this chapter outlines the prepro­
cessing techniques used to generate synthetic target images for training. These steps
were necessary to improve the quality and consistency of the data before training the
enhancement model. The final section explains how the effectiveness of these prepro­
cessing techniques was evaluated, both through quantitative image quality metrics and
visual inspection. Although several standard parameters were used to assess quality, the
final choice of the preprocessing method was made in collaboration with UVision, based
on the visual output that was considered the most suitable for their application.
2.1
Underwater Image Enhancement Techniques
Underwater image processing presents unique challenges due to the complex optical
properties of water. To address these, a wide range of techniques have been developed,
typically categorized as image restoration or image enhancement. Additionally, these
methods can be divided into classical (rule­based) and learning­based (data­driven) ap­
proaches. This section discusses these distinctions and explains the motivation behind
choosing a learning­based model for this project.
2.1.1
Image Restoration vs. Image Enhancement
Although often used reciprocally, image restoration and image enhancement have distinct
goals and methodologies [15]. Image restoration focuses on recovering an image that
has been degraded due to known or unknown distortions, aiming to recover the original
scene as closely as possible by reversing effects such as blur, noise, or underwater haze.
Typical restoration techniques include dehazing, denoising, and inpainting.
In contrast, image enhancement aims to improve the visual quality of an image. The
goal is not to recover the original scene, but rather to make the image more appealing or
easier to interpret. This can be done by enhancing contrast, correcting colors, or adjusting
brightness. Enhancement techniques are often used in photography, advertising, and
other fields where aesthetics or clarity are prioritized over strict accuracy.
In the context of underwater imaging, the distinction between these two categories often
blurs. Many practical techniques combine elements of both, such as reducing haze and
enhancing contrast simultaneously.

2.1.2
Classical vs. Learning­Based Approaches
Classical image processing methods rely on well­defined rules and transformations.
Techniques such as histogram equalization, white balancing, and Retinex­based mod­
els are computationally efficient, easy to interpret, and require no training data. These
methods are ideal for tasks with predictable distortions and where transparency and low
resource consumption are essential. However, they often fall short when confronted with
the complexity of real underwater environments, which may include varying lighting con­
ditions, different levels of turbidity, and inconsistent object appearances.
Learning­based approaches, particularly those using deep learning, offer a powerful al­
ternative. Convolutional neural networks (CNNs) and GANs can learn from large datasets
to model complex visual transformations. These models have shown superior perfor­
mance in tasks like image dehazing. Unlike classical methods, they do not require ex­
plicit rule definitions. Instead, they learn patterns directly from data, making them highly
capable of adjusting to diverse scenarios.
According to a white paper comparing traditional image processing and deep learning
[16], learning­based methods provide higher accuracy and flexibility, especially in visu­
ally complex tasks. They can be fine­tuned with new data and repurposed for different
objectives, making them ideal for customization.
2.1.3
Why a Learning­Based Approach Was Chosen
For this thesis, a learning­based approach was chosen, specifically a GAN model, to
develop an underwater video dehazing tool tailored to the needs of UVision. While this
path involves a steeper learning curve, greater implementation effort, and the need for
GPU­accelerated training, it offers several advantages:
• Personalization: The model can be fine­tuned to the company’s specific underwa­
ter environment and camera settings.
• Adaptability: As new underwater data becomes available or if there is a camera
system change, the model can be retrained or updated with minimal effort.
• Expandability: The resulting model can be integrated into different platforms and
expanded with additional functionality.
In the context of this thesis, a learning­based approach was selected to build a custom,
high­quality underwater image enhancement tool that combines the advantages of both
image enhancement and image restoration. The flexibility, performance, and future­proof
nature of deep learning models, particularly GANs, make them well­suited for the com­
pany’s specific goals, whilst leaving the door open for future enhancements.
2.2
State­of­the­Art GAN­Based Approaches
In recent years, Generative Adversarial Networks (GANs) [17] have become powerful
tools in image generation and enhancement. Introduced in 2014 by Ian Goodfellow, GANs
rely on a concept called adversarial training, where two neural networks, a generator and
a discriminator, compete with each other. The generator’s goal is to produce realistic im­
ages, while the discriminator tries to distinguish between real and fake ones. Through this
back­and­forth process, the generator gradually learns to create increasingly convincing
photos.
One of the key advantages of GANs is that they can learn complex mappings from data
without requiring hard­coded rules. This makes them especially valuable for enhancing
8

==PAGE:19==
underwater images, where the visual distortions are non­linear and complicated to model
using traditional techniques.
2.2.1
Image­to­Image Translation Models
A specific group of GAN models called image­to­image translation models has been
widely used for tasks like deblurring, style transfer, and even underwater image enhance­
ment. These models aim to learn a mapping from one image domain to another, such as
from distorted to clear images [18, 19].
Pix2Pix is a well­known conditional GAN model designed for supervised image­to­image
translation using paired datasets. It has shown strong results in various tasks by learning
a direct mapping from input to target images, often producing sharp and realistic out­
puts. However, Pix2Pix was not explicitly designed for underwater imagery or resource­
constrained environments.
While it can be effective, its relatively heavy architecture
and reliance on large paired datasets can limit its practicality for real­time deployment
in robotics [20, 21].
To overcome this limitation, CycleGAN was introduced as an unsupervised alternative. It
does not require paired data and instead learns to map between two domains by ensuring
that an image translated from one domain to the other can be mapped back to its original
form. While this flexibility is great, the training process is often unstable and requires
careful tuning. In practice, CycleGAN­based models can struggle with color consistency
and may introduce artifacts, which is not ideal for robotics or deployment in the field. For
the objective of this thesis, it was also decided that a supervised learning approach would
be more efficient due to its capability of better fine­tuning [22, 23].
2.2.2
FUnIE­GAN: Fast Underwater Image Enhancement GAN
In contrast to Pix2Pix, FUnIE­GAN, developed by Islam et al. [9], builds on a similar
supervised learning framework but introduces several key modifications that make it better
suited for underwater image enhancement and real­time robotic applications. In addition
to supporting both paired and unpaired training modes, it leverages a lightweight and fully
convolutional architecture optimized for speed and deployability. These design choices
make it especially suitable for embedded systems on visually guided underwater robots
such as AUVs or, in this case, an ROV.
FUnIE­GAN uses a conditional GAN framework, architecture seen in Figure 2.1, with a
generator based on a shallow U­Net­style encoder­decoder [24]. The encoder consists of
five convolutional blocks that progressively reduce spatial resolution and extract high­level
features, while the decoder mirrors this structure to reconstruct the enhanced image. Cru­
cially, the generator employs skip connections between mirrored encoder and decoder
layers: specifically between (e1, d5), (e2, d4), (e3, d3), and (e4, d2). These connec­
tions preserve fine spatial details, which are essential in underwater environments where
textures and edges may already be degraded.
The architecture is designed for efficiency:
• The generator is fully convolutional with no dense layers, reducing the number of
parameters.
• It uses 4×4 convolutional kernels and limits the number of feature maps to a max­
imum of 256.
• The input and output image sizes are fixed at 256×256×3, striking a balance be­
tween computational load and image quality.

Its discriminator is based on a PatchGAN architecture, a discriminator that classifies small

The training objective of FUnIE­GAN combines several loss components:
• The adversarial loss ensures realism.

• A global similarity loss (L1) encourages output images to be structurally close to
the ground truth.
L1(G) = EX,Y,Z[||Y −G(X, Z)||1]
• A content loss based on VGG­19 features helps preserve high­level semantics and
scene structure.
Lcon(G) = EX,Y,Z[||Φ(Y ) −Φ(G(X, Z))||2]
• The PatchGAN discriminator contributes to local texture and style consistency.

trainable model instead:
• Establishing a reusable pipeline: A trained model allows future updates using
new or better data, enabling continual improvement.

3.1.2
Preprocessing for Target Image Generation
As presented in Section 2.3, seven preprocessing techniques were considered for this
project. The Hybrid Fusion Method (HFM) was chosen due to its ability to enhance the
natural colors of the image, while preserving the essential details.
The core idea behind HFM is to combine the strengths of multiple image enhancement
strategies through a multi­branch fusion approach. The method uses three parallel en­
hancement operations on the input image:
• Color and White Balance Correction: The white balance correction in HFM con­
sists of two steps. First, the method applies the Gray World Assumption [42] to
perform initial color correction. This technique is based on the idea that the average
color of a natural scene should be neutral gray. By calculating the average intensity
values of each RGB channel and adjusting them to match a target gray level, the
method compensates for dominant color casts, particularly the loss of red in under­
water environments. After this correction, a nonlinear white balance [43] operation
is applied to reduce further color bias caused by underwater lighting. The white bal­
ance correction module is based on a simple observation. Different light sources,
like the sun or artificial lights, have different color temperatures, ranging from warm
to cool. A white object in a cool color temperature may appear blue, while in a warm
color temperature it appears yellow. The human eye is great at recognizing the true
color of objects, while cameras often struggle to capture accurate colors under vary­
ing lighting conditions. The nonlinear color mapping helps finetune the balance by
projecting the corrected image onto a color­neutral space, producing more natural
and visually pleasing results.
• Visibility Recovery: This module improves the visibility of underwater images,
Real­Time Image Enhancement in ROV Systems
25
==PAGE:36==
which often suffer from haziness due to particles and light scattering in water. The
method uses a concept called type­II fuzzy sets [31, 32], which helps estimate the
uncertain visibility levels in different parts of the image. To do this, the algorithm cal­
culates how bright and how varied the pixel values are (using the mean and standard
deviation), and then defines a lower and upper limit for how much each pixel could
be enhanced. These limits are combined using a modified Hamacher t­conorm to
create an intermediate result. Finally, a transformation­based gamma correction
[44, 45] is applied to brighten and sharpen the image.
• Contrast Enhancement: This module increases the contrast of the image to make
details more visible, especially in low­visibility scenes. It works by applying two
different curve­based transformations to the input image, one using the probability
density function of a normal distribution, and another using a soft addition function.
These two outputs are then combined using a logarithmic image processing tech­
nique. Finally, the image intensity is stretched using a gamma correction function,
which helps adjust brightness and contrast in a balanced way.
The weights for calculating the pixel intensities and the weights of the global gradient are
then combined and normalized using a specific equation.
The final enhanced image R(x) is computed as a weighted sum of the individual enhance­
ment outputs:

3.2.1
Domain Adaptation Attempt
Leon C. Megginson summed up Charles Darwin’s work by saying, ”It is not the strongest
of the species that survives, not the most intelligent that survives. It is the one that is most
adaptable to change. The same thing can also be said about technology. The workhorse
of Machine Learning (ML) and Artificial Intelligence (AI) – supervised learning has a se­
vere disadvantage in that it works well when samples for training and testing both belong
to the same distribution and are independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.). Domain
Adaptation (DA) is a special case of Transfer Learning (TL), which supports and solves
real­world (including in the wild) challenges by effectively applying the model trained on
one dataset (source) for testing on another domain (target) with different distribution.”[46]
As mentioned above, domain adaptation is a subfield of transfer learning. The idea is to
use an existing and trained (on a source dataset) model and finetune it on a target dataset.
In the scope of this project, the idea was to train the FUnIE­GAN model on a large public
underwater dataset, such as the EUVP (Enhancing Underwater Visual Perception) [41] or
the UFO­120 [47] dataset, and then finetune it on the company datasets. This approach
is commonly used to use diverse examples from large source domains, while adapting to
specific characteristics of a smaller target domain.

4.2.4
Quantization to convert the model to FP16
Quantization is a common technique used to make machine learning models smaller and
faster. By reducing the numerical precision of model weights and activations (e.g., from
32­bit floats to 16­bit or 8­bit values), models use less memory, require less storage, and
can run faster. This can also lead to lower power consumption, which is important for
deployment on edge devices. While quantization can sometimes slightly reduce model
accuracy, the trade­off is often worth it for real­time or resource­constrained applications.
In this section, the focus is on converting the model to FP16 precision. INT8 quantization
is discussed in subsection 4.2.5 [50].
To try speeding up the inference, the final ONNX model was converted from FP32 to FP16
format. This was done using a Python script with the float16 conversion tool. The Python
script was obtained from an open­source Git repository [51].
The FP16 model was tested in both Python and Rust, but unfortunately, it didn’t lead to
any improvements in processing time. In fact, in some cases, the FP16 model performed
slightly worse than the original FP32 model. Some of the results can be seen in Figure
4.8. It is visible that the models perform better when deployed in Python. This can be
explained by the fact that ONNX Runtime is more mature and better optimized for Python.

4.2.5

To make the model faster and smaller, post­training quantization was used to convert it to
8­bit integers. This replaces 32­bit floating point (FP32) values with 8­bit integers (INT8
or UINT8). It reduces memory usage and can make the model run faster, especially on
edge devices.
INT8 was the original goal, but it did not work. ONNX Runtime on macOS does not support
some operators used in INT8 models, like ConvInteger. Because of this, UINT8 was used
instead. The main difference is that INT8 supports both negative and positive numbers,
while UINT8 only supports positive values. UINT8 models are sometimes easier to run on
different systems, and are also better suited for image processing, because pixel values
range from 0 to 255 [52].

4.2.7
Parallelizing the Inference Pipeline Using Threads
While the current implementation of the real­time dehazing system processes images
sequentially, future deployment could benefit significantly from the use of multi­threading
to improve performance. Although the implementation of this functionality has not yet
been carried out on the UScanner, since the integration of the model into the final system
is handled by the company, this approach is presented alongside it.
Multi­threading refers to the use of multiple threads of execution within a single program.
Modern CPUs contain multiple cores, each capable of running a thread independently. By
distributing distinct tasks across threads, it is possible to overlap operations that would
otherwise block or delay the entire pipeline, making the whole process slower. In the case
of live video dehazing, the processing steps can be divided into three stages:
• Frame Acquisition: Capturing images from the live feed or camera (UScanner).
Real­Time Image Enhancement in ROV Systems
47
==PAGE:58==
• Inference: Running the dehazing model on the input frame.
• Output Handling: Displaying, logging, or saving the enhanced image.
In a single­threaded implementation, these steps occur in strict sequence, meaning that
inference cannot begin until the frame has been read, and output is delayed until inference
is complete. However, with multi­threading, each stage can run in its dedicated thread.
The suggestion for this setup would look something like this:
• Thread 1: A camera thread continuously acquires frames and sends them through
a channel or buffer.
• Thread 2: An inference thread reads frames from this buffer, performs model infer­
ence, and sends the output to another channel.
• Thread 3: An output thread receives the enhanced images and handles visualization
or storage.
This structure allows each component of the pipeline to operate independently and in
parallel, reducing time and potentially increasing the overall frame rate. Importantly, such
a design also provides flexibility to decouple slow or variable stages (e.g., inference la­
tency) from time­critical ones (e.g., image acquisition), which is particularly relevant in
CPU environments. Given that the ONNX model was designed with dynamic input sizes
and optimized for fast CPU inference, this kind of threaded execution could enable faster
dehazing of the UScanner’s video feed at higher and more stable frame rates.
For this approach, a simulation of a real­time scenario was implemented in Rust to test
and compare different threading strategies. The goal was to evaluate the performance
of single­threaded and multi­threaded versions of the dehazing pipeline under conditions
resembling live video input. Three versions of the program were tested:
• A single­threaded version, where frame loading, inference, and saving were exe­
cuted sequentially.
• A multi­threaded version without any frame rate control, allowing threads to process
images as fast as possible.
• A multi­threaded version with an artificial delay to simulate an 8 FPS video stream,
where each frame is sent every 125 milliseconds.
The multi­threaded implementation was written in Rust using standard libraries such as
std::thread , std::sync::Arc , and crossbeam_channel for thread­safe communication
between pipeline stages. The program was structured into three main threads, already
mentioned above. Communication between the threads was managed using message­
passing channels, enabling a clean separation of tasks and safe data transfer.
Each thread runs on a separate CPU core, leveraging the multi­core architecture of mod­
ern systems. This parallelism allows the frame acquisition to continue independently while
previous frames are still being processed or written to disk. This setup is especially ben­
eficial in CPU­only environments where inference may take a significant fraction of the
total processing time.
To replicate the behavior of a live video feed, a sleep delay of 125ms was added after each
frame acquisition in the third version. This constraint mimics the rate at which frames
would arrive from a real 8 FPS camera, allowing performance evaluation under realistic
timing conditions. The following line of code was used to simulate this delay:

provided more stable timing behavior when simulating a real frame rate. This confirms that

live pipeline, especially in cases where CPU inference performance is a limiting factor.

6
Discussion
This chapter reflects on the key decisions, constraints, and challenges that shaped the
final solution. It begins with a review of the shift in deployment strategy, from a GPU­
capable embedded system to a CPU­only operator laptop, and the impact this had on the
project’s direction. The discussion then explores how the lack of GPU support influenced
model optimization, including trade­offs between speed and image quality. Limitations
related to deploying the solution across different platforms are also considered, along with
the risk of model bias due to the specific nature of the training data. These reflections
provide context for understanding both the strengths and the boundaries of the current
implementation.
6.1
Impact of Deployment Decision Shift
An important turning point in the project occurred when it was decided that the model
would be deployed on the operator’s laptop rather than on the Jetson Xavier NX module
integrated in the UScanner. The original plan had assumed deployment on the Xavier
NX, which comes with a dedicated GPU. This hardware capability initially shaped many
early design choices, including the selection of a moderately lightweight but GPU­friendly
model, the decision to work with higher input resolutions, and the intention to explore
GPU­accelerated inference using CUDA or TensorRT.
When the deployment target changed in a later stage of the project, many of these initial
assumptions had to be reevaluated. Unlike the Xavier NX, the operator’s laptop required
the model to run efficiently on CPU, as GPU availability was no longer guaranteed. At
that point, the core model and training process were already finalized, so starting from
scratch with a new architecture explicitly optimized for CPU use was not feasible within
the project’s time frame.
As a result, the focus shifted to adjusting the existing model and inference pipeline to
work under new constraints. This included converting the model to a CPU­compatible
format, optimizing preprocessing steps, reducing input image resolution, and exploring
lightweight inference techniques such as quantization and multi­threading. The change
added complexity but also provided valuable insights into the challenges of adapting ma­
chine learning models to different deployment environments.
6.2
Why a Trainable Model Was Chosen Over Direct
Preprocessing
A key decision in this project was to use a trainable model rather than directly deploying
an existing enhancement algorithm. This choice was driven by both the limitations of
the available data and the long­term goals of the company. The image data collected by
UVision was largely unpaired and of poor quality—murky, low­contrast, and lacking clear
reference targets. To enable supervised learning, preprocessing was used to generate
synthetic “target” images. These served as stand­ins for ground truth during training.
While it may seem natural to deploy the best­performing preprocessing algorithm directly
in the final system, this approach has several drawbacks. First, it locks the enhancement
process into a fixed pipeline that is difficult to tune or extend. Second, it limits the ability
to add domain­specific constraints, such as custom loss functions, like the color­balance
loss introduced in this project. Most importantly, the company expressed a need for a
Real­Time Image Enhancement in ROV Systems
57
==PAGE:68==
retrainable and future­proof solution. By creating a trainable model, UVision gains the
ability to improve performance over time as new and better data become available. The
resulting pipeline is thus not only a solution to the current data problem but a foundation
for future iterations of underwater visual enhancement.
6.3
Why GPU Deployment was not Prioritized
Most of UVision’s clients operate on platforms that do not have dedicated GPU support. As
a result, the company prioritized deploying the image enhancement model on CPU rather
than targeting GPU acceleration. The target use case includes real­time processing on
machines without discrete graphics cards, making CPU inference the most practical and
broadly compatible solution.
The deployment was also carried out using the Rust programming language. While Rust
is efficient and well­suited for performance­critical applications, GPU support in the Rust
ONNX Runtime ecosystem is limited, especially on macOS. Although ONNX Runtime
supports various GPU execution providers like CUDA (on Windows/Linux) or CoreML (on
macOS), the CoreML backend offers only minimal performance improvements for this
model. In some cases, CoreML defaults back to CPU execution, particularly when using
models with dynamic input shapes like in this case. According to GitHub discussions
and ONNX Runtime documentation, dynamic shapes are often unsupported by CoreML,
which can lead to the fallback to CPU inference even when CoreML is enabled [54, 55].
Additionally, ONNX Runtime’s CoreML integration is still under development and does not
offer the same level of optimization as CUDA on supported hardware. The limited GPU
gains observed during testing confirmed that relying solely on CPU inference is currently
the most robust and portable approach for the target environment.
Potential GPU­related performance improvements, especially on Windows and Linux sys­
tems using CUDA or other providers, will be discussed in Section 7.3 of Chapter 7.
6.4
Reflections on CPU Constraints and the Speed–Quality
Trade­off
The decision to deploy the model on the CPU introduced several practical challenges that
influenced the overall design and optimization strategy. Many of the deployment­oriented
optimization methods discussed in Section 4.2 were driven by this constraint. While the
original plan had considered GPU­based inference, the switch to CPU meant that achiev­
ing acceptable processing speed became a key priority. FunIE­GAN was initially chosen
for its lightweight architecture, which made it a better fit for real­time performance on less
powerful hardware.
The most effective approach, when trying to achieve a faster processing time, turned out to
be reducing the input resolution. Lowering the image size led to significantly quicker pro­
cessing while still producing visually solid results. Especially with resolutions like 960x600
and 1280x800, the model stayed within the target processing time of under 500ms per
frame and maintained reasonable visual quality. These sizes offered a good balance
between clarity and performance.
This trade­off between speed and image quality is an essential part of real­time deploy­
ment. While higher resolutions (e.g., 1920x1200) gave sharper outputs, they were too
slow to be used in a live setting. On the other hand, very low resolutions may process
quickly but can lose critical visual details.

Looking forward, these parameters, especially image resolution, can be adjusted depend­
ing on the specific needs of a deployment. If speed is more important, lower resolutions
can be used. If visual clarity is the priority, the resolution can be increased at the cost of
slower performance. The current structure allows for this flexibility going forward.
6.5
Limitations of deployment across different devices
While the model performs well on the current test setup, a macOS device using the Rust­
based interface, deploying it on other platforms may introduce new challenges. Different
operating systems and hardware configurations can affect both compatibility and perfor­
mance.
For instance, ONNX Runtime behavior can vary between macOS, Windows, and Linux.
Some execution providers, such as CoreML, are only available on macOS and are not
supported on other systems. On the other hand, Windows and Linux offer better support
for CUDA, which enables high­performance inference on NVIDIA GPUs, something not
available in the current macOS test environment.
Quantized models, such as those using INT8 or UINT8, also behave differently across
platforms. Certain operators used in INT8 models (e.g., ConvInteger) are not supported in
ONNX Runtime on macOS, which caused runtime failures during testing, see subsection
4.2.5. However, community discussions and ONNX Runtime documentation suggest that
these operators have more mature support on Windows and Linux systems, particularly
on hardware with AVX2 or VNNI instruction sets [56, 57]. This means quantized models
that failed or underperformed on macOS may work well on other systems.
Overall, the current implementation is optimized for macOS with CPU inference, but future
deployments on different operating systems will require additional testing. Differences
in available backends, instruction sets, and ONNX operator support could significantly
impact performance, compatibility, and real­time feasibility.
6.6
Potential Bias
The final model was trained and fine­tuned using datasets collected from various UVision
port environments. While these datasets include a wide range of conditions, they still
represent a specific type of underwater scene, mainly murky, shallow water with reddish
or orange color casts. As a result, the model has become particularly tuned to these visual
characteristics.
This can introduce a bias when the model is applied to environments that are visually
different. For example, images captured in clearer water or scenes with strong blue or
green tones may lead to color distortions in the enhanced output. In such cases, the model
may overcorrect or shift the colors in a way that reflects the training data distribution rather
than the actual conditions in the new environment.
Some examples of this behavior are shown in Section 5.3, where the model struggles
with very clear or very bright underwater images. These results highlight the influence
of the training data and show that performance may drop when the model is used in
environments it has not seen before.
To mitigate this bias in the future, a more diverse training dataset that encompasses dif­
ferent water types, lighting conditions, and color distributions would be beneficial. This
would enable the model to generalize more effectively and perform more consistently
across various underwater environments.

7
Conclusion and Future Work
7.1
Summary of Work
The goal of this thesis was to develop an underwater image enhancement model that
can run in real time, defined as processing each frame in under 500ms on the operator’s
laptop. While the original deployment target was the Jetson Xavier NX, which includes
a dedicated GPU, the plan changed more than halfway through the project. The new
goal became a CPU­only deployment through UVision’s operator laptop interface. This
required a significant shift in focus, from GPU­accelerated inference to CPU­efficient op­
timization, forcing a rethinking of the deployment pipeline under stricter constraints. As a
result, the final model is not only capable of meeting the performance target, but is also
more adaptable, providing support for both CPU and GPU. Further, despite this shift, a
real­time dehazing solution was successfully developed, achieving the main objective and
sub­goals of this thesis.
The lightweight FUnIE­GAN model was selected and trained using the company’s internal
datasets, as described in Subsection 2.2.2. Since no clean training targets were avail­
able, several preprocessing methods were evaluated to generate suitable ”ground truth”
images for supervised training. Rather than deploying a preprocessing algorithm directly,
this allowed training a flexible and extensible model, one that UVision can retrain in the fu­
ture as better data becomes available. Various preprocessing and optimization methods
were explored, and the most effective approach for reaching the 2 FPS benchmark was
reducing the input image resolution, detailed in Subsection 4.2.2. The final model runs
within the target time limit when using input resolutions such as 960x600 or 1280x800.
To integrate the model into UVision’s existing infrastructure, the PyTorch model was con­
verted to ONNX format. A baseline Rust script provided by Andreas Wendelboe (UVision)
was used as the starting point. Building on this, several additional Rust scripts were writ­
ten to test and compare different optimization techniques, including quantization, CoreML
acceleration, and multi­threading, as discussed in Section 4.2. A parallelized version of
the pipeline was also implemented, making better use of available CPU cores, as covered
in Subsection 4.2.7.
The final model is ready to be integrated into UVision’s Rust­based interface. Since the
current frontend is in Java and the backend is in Rust, the company plans to convert the
frontend to Rust as part of the complete system integration. UVision will handle this part
of the deployment after the thesis project ends.
All source code of this thesis can be found on GitHub1.
7.2
Challenges and Solutions Summary
This thesis addressed a range of practical and technical challenges encountered when
developing a real­time underwater dehazing model for ROV operator support. These chal­
lenges arose not only from the nature of underwater imagery but also from the deployment
environment and system­level requirements. The following outlines the main challenges
and summarizes the strategies implemented to meet them:
• Murky visual conditions in port environments and lack of ground truth.

• Strict runtime constraints for real­time deployment.

recommendations for collecting more diverse training data were provided [12].

metrics (UIQM and UCIQE) were used for objective evaluation. These scores, com­

the model’s enhancement quality [13, 14].
Together, these solutions contributed to a robust, real­time capable underwater dehazing
pipeline that is portable, extendable, and ready for integration into UVision’s operational
systems. Additionally, a comprehensive and modular training workflow was developed,
encompassing data preparation, training, fine­tuning, and evaluation scripts. This en­
ables the company to retrain the model on new data, adapt to varying conditions, or test
alternative configurations without needing to start from scratch.
7.3
Suggestions for Further Improvements
While the final model achieved the target performance on CPU and is ready for deploy­
ment, several approaches exist for further improvement and future development.
• Training with more diverse data: The current model was trained on a specific set
of underwater images, mainly collected from UVision’s port environments. This in­
troduced a bias, especially toward reddish, murky water conditions. Training with
a broader dataset that includes a wider range of lighting, color conditions, and un­
derwater scenes (such as clear blue ocean water or deep­sea imagery) could help
the model generalize better and reduce color distortions in unfamiliar environments.
The company could also consider creating dedicated models for different scenarios
if needed.
Additionally, the model was trained with downscaled image resolutions and lim­
ited epochs to reduce training time due to resource constraints. Longer training
on higher­resolution images could potentially improve visual quality, especially in

fine textures and edges. Since the training pipeline is now established, the com­
pany can reuse the preprocessing and supervised training setup as soon as more
diverse data becomes available. All scripts developed for training and evaluation
will be delivered with thorough documentation, allowing the process to be repeated
or extended by the company.
• Deploying with GPU acceleration: Although this project focused on CPU­based
deployment due to current client hardware limitations, many systems could benefit
from GPU inference. ONNX Runtime provides strong support for CUDA­based GPU
inference on Windows and Linux platforms. Using the CUDA Execution Provider on
these systems enables significant performance improvements, especially for Con­
volutional neural networks (CNNs) like FUnIE­GAN [58, 59].
In future setups, GPU acceleration could be enabled either for live deployment or
for faster offline processing tasks. This could also allow for using higher­resolution
inputs without compromising frame rate. Additionally, INT8 or FP16 models could
perform better when deployed on systems that support advanced instruction sets
(e.g., AVX2 or VNNI), which are common in modern GPUs and Windows/Linux con­
figurations [56, 57].
• Deploying ONNX models in Python: All deployment in this thesis was performed
using Rust, as that is the language used by the company’s interface. However,
ONNX Runtime has significantly more mature support in Python, including full ac­
cess to optimized backends like CUDA, TensorRT, and OpenVINO [60]. The Python
API supports features such as graph optimizations, memory reuse, and dynamic
execution provider selection, which can lead to faster inference and easier exper­
imentation [61]. In future projects, Python­based deployment could be explored,
particularly for prototyping or testing performance trade­offs.
