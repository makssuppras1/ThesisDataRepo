Summary (English)
The goal of the thesis is to implement a novel method for deforming surfaces
using embedded deformation. A deformation method is an important part of
doing non-rigid surface registration. The motivation for this is to be able to
determine point correspondences across a population of heads in order to build
a statistical shape model of the human head. Examples of uses of such a model
include aiding the design of hearing aids and head phones, modelling of head
related transfer functions (HRTFs), and detection of mental illness.
The deformation method is based on work by Sumner et al. and the central idea
is to have the deformation embedded in a deformation graph. A benet of the
deformation graph is that the complexity of the deformation can easily be con-
trolled, by adjusting the resolution of the deformation graph. The deformation
method is implemented in C++ using a mesh processing library called GEL.
To use the deformation method in a registration process an optimization scheme
is needed to optimize deformation parameters based on some error measure-
ments. Two approaches are described, and the simpler approach is implemented.
Annotated landmarks are used to perform a preliminary registration between
two test heads.
The results are encouraging and with further improvements of the registration
method, including making it independent of annotated landmarks, it can be
used to build a statistical shape model of the human head.

Preface
This thesis was prepared at DTU Compute in fullment of the requirements for
acquiring an M.Sc. in Engineering.
The thesis deals with 3D modelling of human head shape. The main goal of
the project is to implement a novel method for deforming 3D head models. An
early attempt at performing surface registration of two heads is also presented.
Further, a procedure for building the statistical shape model is suggested.
This thesis consists of six chapters. Chapter 1 motivates the need for a statistical
shape model of the human head, and presents the current state of this particular
eld of research. Chapter 2 presents the dataset that will be used throughout the

constructed and implemented. Chapters 4 and 5 present and discuss the results

1.1
The concept of shape modelling
The shape of a certain object can in general be described using statistical shape
modelling. That is, a model that describes both the general shape of the object
as well as the ways in which the shape of the object can vary, using statistical
methods.
In order to build a statistical shape model, it is necessary to have a well-dened
way of describing and measuring correspondences between shapes in a popula-
tion. For any given point on a sample shape, we need to nd the corresponding
point on all other shapes in the population. This point correspondence is often
facilitated by the use of a selection of landmarks (e.g. distinct points, that are
easy to locate consistently across a shape population). Dening such landmarks
is not an easy task, as it is inherently ill-dened. For example, if we choose a
point on the rst ear in Figure 1.2, we now need to locate the corresponding
points on the remaining ears. This might be easy in some cases (e.g. the green
point), however in other cases it can be almost impossible, due to dierence in
overall shape (e.g. the red point).
Figure 1.2: The points illustrate the ambiguity, that can arise, when annotat-
ing landmarks on a shape.
Furthermore, manual annotation of landmarks is time consuming and infeasible
if the dataset is very large. Also, there is the issue of inter- and intra operator

1.2
Previous work
1.2.1
Statistical shape modelling
A vast amount of work has been done in the eld of statistical shape modelling.
Cootes et al. introduced the Active Shape Model (ASM) in 1995 [CTCG95],
which has been implemented in numerous variations. They argued that it was
necessary to introduce variability in shape models in order to recognize shapes
that did not t a rigid prototype. However, in order for the model to be robust,
this variation needed to be consistent with the natural variation within the
population of an object.
They used a training set of shapes with correctly
annotated landmarks to build these statistical shape models.
Statistical shape models have a wide range of applications. In [PLN+02] Paulsen
et al. introduce the eld of statistical shape modelling and present a method
for building a shape model of the human ear canal.
In his PHD thesis [Hut04], Hutton investigates whether modelling of the human
face can help detect certain mental illnesses.
Fagertun et al. [FHR+14] proposed to extend the Active Appearance Model
(AAM), which was introduced by Cootes et al. in 1998 and is closely related
to the ASM model. The extension of the AAM model, includes making it non-
linear, in order to better be able to capture more complex shape variations.
Applications include face labelling for use in facial tracking systems.
1.2.2
Non-rigid registration
The non-rigid registration method used in this project is based on the work of
Sumner et al. [SSP07]. They introduced the notion of a deformation graph
as a tool for deforming an object. The deformation graph is here dened as a
simplied representation of the original shape. For a mesh, it can be obtained
e.g. by sub-sampling the vertices of the original mesh. They use it to control
the (user-dened) deformation of a shape, where an ane transformation 1 is
assigned to every node in the graph. The ane transformation of each node
inuences a local neighbourhood of vertices in the underlying shape mesh.
They set up an optimization scheme with multiple energy terms that enforce

1.3
Project plan and specications
This project ts into a larger research pipeline, the goal of which is to build a
statistical shape model of the human head. Therefore, at the beginning of this
MSc project, the necessary steps for achieving this were determined and the goal
of the project was thus to get through as many steps as possible. Ultimately,
we succeeded in implementing a deformation model as well as a rst attempt at
a registration scheme. The deformation model uses embedded deformation by
use of a deformation graph, similar to the approaches in [SSP07] and [LSP08].
Two dierent implementation approaches are investigated, of which the simpler
method is fully implemented. The simpler approach was investigated due to the
complexity involved in the optimization of the more complex approach.

Model
The goal of the registration method presented in this thesis is to take a source
head and deform it to match a target head. In this way we can take an arbitrary
point on the source head and nd the corresponding point on the target head.
The registration process is divided into two steps. First we do an initial rigid
transformation to move the source head to the target head. We then apply a non-
rigid transformation that we solve for iteratively, using two dierent approaches,
until we reach convergence.

Since the source and target heads have dierent shapes, the initial rigid align-
ment has to be done in the "best possible way", so that we get the most overlap
of the two surfaces. The two heads we use for testing have had a set of landmarks
annotated manually. The landmarks are shown in Figure 3.1.

3.1.2
ICP approach
Landmarks need to be manually or semi-automatically annotated. This takes
time and is a source of error, depending on how meticulously it is done. There-
fore it can be preferable to use a fully automatic approach for doing the initial
alignment. An example of this is the iterative closest point (ICP) algorithm.
The algorithm basically does the same as described in the previous subsection.
However, since we do not have pre-dened point correspondences, it creates
these by matching points on the source mesh to the closest point on the target
mesh. It re-calculates the closest point after every rigid transformation. A k-d
tree can be used to increase computation speed when nding the closest point.
A drawback of the method is that the iterative process can lead to convergence
to local minima.

3.2
Embedded deformation
The non-rigid deformation method presented in this thesis builds on the surface
deformation model presented by Sumner et al. [SSP07].
The deformation is controlled by a deformation graph. It is dened in a general
sense as a collection of nodes, distributed across the source surface. For meshes,
it can be constructed by sub-sampling the vertices of the source mesh.
Each node in the deformation graph has an associated ane transformation,
which is applied to nearby vertices in the mesh. The deformation of a single
vertex therefore becomes a weighted average of the transformations of its closest
nodes.
Since the end goal of the registration is to obtain point correspondences, we do
not simply wish to nd the deformations that give the most overlap, when com-
paring the transformed source mesh to the target mesh. This does not necessar-
ily give the best registration, since we want the registration to be anatomically
correct as well. We also want the deformations to be as anatomically correct
as possible. This is done by ensuring that the deformation is locally as rigid as
possible.
3.2.1
Deformation graph
The deformation graph nodes are denoted gj, j = 1, . . . , m. They each have an
associated ane transformation, described by a 3 × 3 deformation matrix, Aj,
and a 3 × 1 translation vector, tj.

There are several options for creating the deformation graph. One can simply
uniformly sub-sample the vertices of the source mesh, which will result in an
even deformation across the mesh (see Figure 3.3). However, there is also the
possibility of regulating the deformation by varying the denseness of the defor-
mation graph nodes, so that some parts of the mesh can be transformed more
independently from the rest of the mesh.
Then, there is also the possibility of doing multi-level deformation, in which we
use a range of deformation graphs sampled at dierent scales. This is discussed
further in Chapter 5.
In our case, we construct the deformation graphs by mesh simplication function
provided by Bærentzen [Bae16].

Chapter 5
Discussion
The landmarks used to guide the registration presented in the previous chap-
ter require manual annotation and are not practical for this reason. However,
anatomical correspondences often require human interpretation in order to be
determined. This is due to the fact that some anatomical points are distinguish-
able more by human understanding of facial anatomy than by any specic shape
feature of the particular point. In [LSP08], Li et al. give an example of this,
when they compare their registration method to an implementation of non-rigid
ICP. Even though surface overlap is slightly lower with their method, they use
color marking of the nose and lips to show that their method aligns the points
more anatomically correct.
If we do not use landmarks we need to trust that our denition of a natural
deformation, i.e. enforcing local rigidity, will ensure that the registration is as
anatomically correct as possible.
Other approaches could be to use a type of local shape signature at selected
points on the mesh, as mentioned in Chapter 1, which could then be used to
match points on the source and target heads with similar shape signatures.
Another indirect way of ensuring that a registration is as anatomically correct
as possible, is the multi-level registration approach that was suggested in Sub-
section 4.1.1. In this way, we ensure that the coarse shape of the heads get
==PAGE:56==
44
Discussion
aligned rst, after which we can align increasingly detailed features.
The optimization method presented in [SSP07] and [LSP08] was not fully im-
plemented due to the complexity involved in the optimization.
In order to
implement it, we need to use advanced non-linear optimization as well as ex-
plicitly compute the Jacobian of our objective function. The advantage of this
method is that it solves for the ane transformation parameters of the nodes
in the deformation graph, which is the foundation of the embedded deformation
model that we use.
For the simpler optimization method, we have to backtrack the deformation
process in order to derive the transformations. When doing this we simplify
these transformations to be locally rigid, as opposed to simply ane, since we
only nd the rotation of each face, and disregard any skewing. The advantage
of the second method is that it is simpler to implement, since it uses the simple
geometric signature to describe the structure of the mesh, that we wish to
preserve, as well as solving alternately for the node positions and the geometric
signature, so that we can solve it using linear optimization.

Chapter 6
Conclusion
The work done in this thesis is part of a larger research project with the goal
of developing a statistical shape model of the human head. In order to build
this statistical model, it is necessary to have a deformation method, that can be
used to determine point correspondences across a population of heads by doing
non-rigid surface registration.
The central part of the thesis is the embedded deformation model, consisting of
the deformation graph and the weighting scheme for applying the node trans-
formations to the underlying mesh. The method was fully implemented in C++
using the GEL framework.
A registration of two test heads from the head database was carried out, using
annotated landmarks to guide the deformation. Although the achieved registra-
tion is not good enough to establish full point correspondence between the two
test heads, the results are still encouraging. The deformations are constrained
to be locally as rigid as possible, in order to keep the deformations natural,
since we expect that this will give the most correct nal registration. In order
to make the method independent of annotated landmarks, alternative ways to
measure registration error were also suggested.
It was shown that the resolution of the deformation graph controls the inuence
region of the deformations on the underlying mesh. This gives rise to the idea of

Conclusion
a multi-level registration method, where heads are registered using increasingly
denser deformation graphs. This way, the heads are rst aligned by the global
shape variations, after which we register shape variations of increasingly ner
scale. Alternatively, a non-uniform deformation graph could also be used to
control what regions should be deformed more locally than others.
The statistical shape model of the human head will be useful for manufacturers
of hearing instruments in their quest to improve the acoustic quality of their
products. Particularly it can be used in relation to modelling HRTFs, to improve
the directionality of hearing aids.
The availability of this framework for building a statistical shape model prompts
the question of other uses. It could potentially be applied in evolutionary re-
search or used to improve the design of other products tting the human head,
e.g.
helmets and masks.
Further, the framework could also be used to de-
velop statistical shape models of other shapes, such as other body parts or even
inanimate objects, perhaps to monitor the shape of a product in a production
