Abstract
This thesis investigates the effectiveness of automated natural ventilation strategies in
a residential building, with a primary focus on thermal comfort and a complementary
analysis of indoor air quality (IAQ) and energy use. Measurements were conducted in
a Danish single-family house during late winter and spring. The measurement results
were evaluated by comparison with dynamic simulations carried out in IDA ICE (Indoor
Climate and Energy) 5.1, developed by EQUA Simulation AB, to assess the annual per-
formance of various ventilation scenarios, including single-sided, cross-ventilation, and
stack ventilation using flat roof windows under both scheduled and sensor-controlled
automation.
Thermal comfort was assessed based on the applicable regulations in Denmark. Addi-
tionally, IAQ was evaluated by comparing CO2 concentrations, and an energy analysis
was conducted by comparing the electricity consumption across different scenarios.
The results indicated that stack ventilation consistently provided superior thermal com-
fort during the warmer months, particularly when controlled by sensor-based automation.
Compared to scheduled routines, sensor-driven control was more effective in maintain-
ing CO2 concentrations below the 950 ppm threshold, thereby enhancing indoor air
quality while also reducing energy consumption. During the heating season, this auto-
mated control strategy also contributed to a reduction in heating demand, though it led
to a slight increase in overheating hours due to fewer ventilation opportunities.
These findings highlight the limitations of natural ventilation in mitigating summer over-
heating. In contrast, during winter, the risk of draught increased, underscoring the need
to refine automation strategies or adopt scenarios with reduced air movement and lower
velocities to ensure both comfort and efficiency.
Keywords:
Natural ventilation, thermal comfort, automated windows, energy consumption, residen-
tial house

Background
1.1
Introduction
Ventilation has long been a fundamental component of building design, evolving from
simple passive methods in ancient times to sophisticated systems aimed at ensuring
indoor air quality (IAQ), thermal comfort, health, and performance. In ancient civiliza-
tions, open courtyards, operable windows, and cross-ventilation were used to mitigate
heat, smoke, and humidity. The Industrial Revolution introduced more enclosed struc-
tures, necessitating mechanical ventilation systems to address increased urban density,
industrial activities, and later the need to reduce energy use for air conditioning. While
integrated ventilation systems became standard practice in commercial buildings — em-
phasizing the control of temperature, humidity, and air quality — residential ventilation
systems have developed more slowly. It was not until 1997 that ASHRAE initiated the
development of a dedicated residential ventilation standard [1]. This effort led to the
publication of ASHRAE Standard 62.2, first released in 2003 and subsequently updated
on a regular basis, with the most recent edition issued in 2022.
From a building physics perspective, high humidity and low air exchange rates foster
mould growth and material degradation [2], with long-term consequences for structural
integrity and occupant well-being [3]. Ventilation have indeed a critical role in maintain-
ing IAQ and health in residential environments. Elevated CO2 levels, often resulting
from insufficient ventilation, impair cognitive performance even at levels previously con-
sidered acceptable [4], but also increase incidence of asthma and allergies in both chil-
dren and adults [5]. In addition, indoor pollutant exposure, such as cooking, smoking,
and off-gassing materials contribute to health degradation [6]. Furthermore, thermal
comfort models should consider gender, age, and socioeconomic differences. For in-
stance, children prefer lower temperatures, while women tend to feel colder due to lower
metabolic rates [7].
Beyond health concerns, the role of ventilation in ensuring thermal comfort and energy
efficiency has gained attention. Effective ventilation strategies must balance indoor cli-
mate quality with energy consumption, especially in the context of sustainability goals.
In Denmark, for example, buildings accounted for nearly 40% of total energy consump-
tion in 2021 [8], with residential buildings representing about two-thirds of that share [9].
The Danish government aims for a fossil-free energy supply by 2050 [10], and its build-
ing code BR18 [11] imposes strict energy performance requirements. These include
minimum efficiency standards for windows, ventilation systems, and building envelopes
[10], ensuring continuous improvements in existing structures.

==PAGE:10==
This thesis investigates the performance of natural ventilation strategies — particularly
stack-effect and cross-ventilation — in a residential context. A combination of short-term
in-situ measurements and year-long dynamic simulations using IDA ICE 5.1 (EQUA Sim-
ulation AB) was employed to evaluate thermal comfort, indoor air quality, and energy
use across different automated window control scenarios. The study follows Danish
regulatory standards.
1.2
Literature review
A literature study was conducted to explore natural ventilation (NV) strategies and their
influence on thermal comfort and energy efficiency in residential buildings. NV through
automated window systems has gained increasing attention as a strategy to reduce en-
ergy use while maintaining acceptable indoor climates. This review synthesizes key
studies, emphasizing residential relevance and identifying research gaps.
Regarding thermal comfort in naturally ventilated buildings, Santamouris and Kolokotsa
[12] reviewed NV strategies across climates. In Malaysia, full-scale studies on terraced
houses found night ventilation reduced peak indoor temperatures by 2.5°C and noctur-
nal temperatures by 2.0°C. In Madrid, solar chimneys in social housing helped maintain
night temperatures between 21–23°C without active cooling. A broader analysis of two
hundred fourteen air-conditioned residential buildings using night ventilation found en-
ergy savings up to 40 kWh/m²/year, showing a clear but not perfectly linear relationship
between airflow rates and savings — highlighting NV’s potential in reducing cooling
loads when designed effectively.
Omrani et al [13] compared single-sided and cross-ventilation in a high-rise Brisbane
apartment under summer conditions. Cross-ventilation provided acceptable comfort
over 70% of the time, while single-sided ventilation failed to maintain comfort throughout,
underscoring the influence of layout and window placement.
Rupp et al [14] emphasized the role of occupant behaviour, including window use [15],
in thermal comfort. Their field study [16] observed a linear link between indoor and
outdoor temperatures in NV buildings.
The Center for the Built Environment at UC Berkeley [17] found that naturally ventilated
commercial buildings had higher occupant satisfaction, despite noise concerns. Win-
dow use was driven by occupancy, while discomfort was often due to weak air move-
ment. Thermal comfort was achieved across a broader temperature range (16-30°C)
than ASHRAE Standard 55 [18] predicts. The study expanded EnergyPlus modelling to
better simulate NV, supporting smarter ventilation strategies.
Optimal design or automation for natural ventilation is a key aspect to promote thermal
comfort. Chung et al [19] evaluated automated NV using updated comfort and occu-

pancy models. They tested three modes: pure NV (21–28°C), HVAC (21.7–24.4°C),
and a hybrid system with a 1°C buffer to prevent frequent switching. Windows were con-
trolled by indoor–outdoor temperature differences and allowed manual override. The hy-
brid system achieved up to 40% energy savings with minimal comfort trade-offs, showing
how advanced control strategies can optimize performance in residential applications.
Priyadarsini et al [20] assessed passive and active stack-ventilation (described in 1.4.1)
in high-rise housing in Singapore. Passive stacks were largely ineffective, while fan-
assisted stacks substantially improved airflow and comfort, suggesting that mechanical
support may be needed in certain designs. Window automation strategies with sun
shading and fans was shown to support both comfort and energy efficiency [21].
Sacht & Lukiantchuki [22] used CFD simulations to show that increasing window size
from 10% to 25% of floor area significantly enhanced ventilation, especially with oblique
wind angles (45°). The study also found notable changes in airflow patterns, highlight-
ing the importance of detailed window design and placement.
However, ventilation can impact a building energy consumption. Given that HVAC ac-
counts for 60–70% of energy use in non-industrial buildings [23], mixed-mode ventilation
is a promising path. However, its control logic remains underdeveloped.
Wang & Greenberg [24] stressed the need to model operable windows realistically. They
demonstrated that HVAC savings between 31.7% and 59.1% could be achieved in sum-
mer with NV, but that increased winter heating demand — caused by uncoordinated
ventilation — requires refined control algorithms. In Sweden, strict energy policies led
to underheating in some homes, stressing the need for balanced comfort and efficiency
regulations [25].
Sorgato et al [26] studied residential buildings in Brazil and found that medium ther-
mal mass buildings benefit most from controlled ventilation. Scenarios with automated
control outperformed unrestricted window use in both comfort and energy metrics, un-
derlining the importance of strategic control timing and user behaviour.
Several gaps remain despite the advancements in understanding automated natural
ventilation systems. First of all, much of the existing research focuses on commercial
buildings or educational buildings. There is a real need for more studies specifically
targeting residential buildings to account for unique occupancy patterns, thermal com-
fort and air quality requirements. In addition, limited long-term empirical data exist on
the performance of automated window systems in diverse climatic conditions. Compre-
hensive field studies are necessary to validate simulation models and assess real-world
effectiveness. Further research is also needed to understand how occupants interact
with automated systems and how these interactions impact thermal comfort and energy
consumption. This includes studying user acceptance and the effectiveness of manual
overrides. Finally, the potential for integrating automated window systems with broader

smart home technologies remains under-explored. Research into seamless integration
could lead to more responsive and efficient home environments.
1.3
Objectives and research questions
This thesis focuses on evaluating the thermal comfort implications of different residential
ventilation strategies, focusing on natural ventilation approaches such as single-sided,
cross-flow, and stack-effect ventilation. Using both monitored data and simulation out-
puts, the study assesses the thermal comfort levels achieved under these different
strategies. Although thermal comfort remains the central focus, the analysis also in-
tegrates limited comparisons of indoor air quality metrics (e.g., CO2 concentration) and
electrical consumption, in order to provide a broader perspective on the trade-offs be-
tween comfort, health-related outcomes and energy efficiency. This multi-criteria eval-
uation aims to inform design and operation choices in residential settings, especially
in climates with strong seasonal variability, such as Denmark and northern European
countries.
While automated ventilation systems have been studied primarily in commercial and
educational settings, their potential in residential applications remains underexplored.
Therefore, this research aims to investigate the potential of automated natural ventila-
tion systems in enhancing thermal comfort and reducing energy consumption in a resi-
dential house.
To achieve this, the study is guided by the following key research questions:
• How do different window-opening control strategies influence indoor thermal com-
fort in residential buildings?
This question examines the extent to which natural airflow contributes to indoor
thermal comfort, considering factors such as temperature regulation, comfort cat-
egories (EN 16798-1 [27]), and the risk of overheating.
• To what extent can smart window controls enhance thermal comfort while main-
taining acceptable air quality?
By comparing indoor CO2 levels and thermal conditions under different control
modes, this question examines whether sensor-based operation improves both
comfort and indoor air quality compared to simpler ventilation routines.
• What is the impact of automated natural ventilation systems on household energy
consumption?
By analysing energy consumption of different scenarios, this research seeks to
determine to what extent natural ventilation improves overall energy efficiency.
By addressing these questions, this thesis aims to provide insights into the integration

of automated natural ventilation, ultimately contributing to the development of more sus-
tainable and comfortable residential environments.
1.4
Theoretical framework
This section outlines the theoretical principles and evaluation criteria used to assess
the performance of natural ventilation strategies in this study. Each of the following
subsections defines how natural ventilation is characterized and how its effectiveness is
measured across three key dimensions: thermal comfort, IAQ, and energy consumption.
These frameworks provide the basis for both the simulation setup and the interpretation
of measured and modelled results presented later in the thesis.
1.4.1
Natural ventilation principles
Natural ventilation refers to the process of supplying and removing air from indoor spaces
through openings such as windows, doors, and vents, without the use of mechanical
systems. It is driven primarily by natural forces — namely, wind-induced pressure dif-
ferences and buoyancy (stack) effects resulting from temperature gradients [28]. These
driving mechanisms are shaped by outdoor environmental conditions, building geome-
try, and the configuration of openings.
Natural ventilation can be classified into three main strategies based on the spatial con-
figuration of openings and the dominant driving force: single-sided ventilation, cross-
ventilation, and stack-effect ventilation. While the exact equations used in IDA ICE are
not disclosed, the empirical models presented below offer a valuable foundation for un-
derstanding the physical drivers of natural ventilation under different configurations.

1.4.2
Thermal comfort assessment
Thermal comfort, a key aspect of Indoor Environmental Quality (IEQ), is assessed us-
ing standardized classification systems. In this study, comfort conditions are evaluated
based on four categories (I to IV), as defined in EN 16798-1, representing high, medium,
moderate, and low levels of occupant satisfaction. Category II is considered the stan-
dard level of expectation for residential environments and serves as the reference thresh-
old for acceptable comfort.
When seasonal distinctions are relevant to the analysis, the classification from Danish
Standard DS 447 [35] is applied. According to this framework:
• Winter (heating season): November 1 to March 31;
• Shoulder seasons: April and October;
• Summer (cooling season): May 1 to September 30.

Methodology
3.1
Scenarios tested
To evaluate the effectiveness of two automated FRWs in ensuring indoor thermal com-
fort, a series of ventilation scenarios were tested in a typical residential layout. The
objective was to assess how various opening strategies influence indoor conditions,
particularly during warmer periods. The house is equipped with multiple operable ele-
ments, but the focus was on the performance of the FRWs in comparison with other
openings.
• To provide a baseline for comparison, scenario 1 simulated a worst-case reference
condition with all openings closed. Scenario 2 introduced only the flaps of the
other windows in open position, while keeping all other openings closed, as this
represents the typical state of the house when unoccupied.
• Scenario 3 tested the ventilation efficiency of the façade door alone — dimen-
sioned to match the combined opening area of the two FRWs, as shown later
in this section — to directly compare its single-sided ventilation effectiveness to
the one of scenario 4. Scenario 4 was representative of the normal automated
operation of the FRWs alone.
• In scenario 5, the kitchen window was opened together with the FRWs to create a
vertical path for buoyancy-driven airflow, assessing stack-effect performance. This
is a realistic condition used occasionally by the occupants, such as after cooking.
• Finally, scenario 6 compared stack-ventilation with cross-ventilation, by testing the
façade door in combination with the kitchen window.
These six configurations aimed to quantify how different ventilation strategies — single-
sided, stack, and cross-ventilation — affected the overall indoor thermal environment,
and how efficient the FRWs are in achieving acceptable comfort levels on their own or
in combination with other openings.

the respective findings. In the context of the present study, the ACH values are used

movement, which directly relates to perceived air velocity and overall comfort within the
space.

The VELUX ACTIVE control strategy was provided by VELUX and is described in detail
in Appendix A.4. VELUX ACTIVE controls the opening of the roof windows based on
air temperature, humidity and CO2.
The system operates through several automated functions designed to optimize indoor
climate conditions:
• Window opening automation features default settings that maintain indoor temper-
atures between 18-23°C, with comfort ranges adjustable from 16-35°C for differ-
ent seasons. The system automatically opens windows when indoor temperatures
exceed the set thresholds, regularly otherwise every three hours, with additional

exceeding 70% RH, and night ventilation as long as the thresholds are met and

3.2.5
Tracer gas measurement
To determine the ACH, various tracer gas methods are available — including the concen-
tration decay method, constant concentration method, and constant emission method.
For this study, the concentration decay method was selected. This approach was partic-
ularly suitable as it enables estimation of ACH over short timeframes without requiring
continuous or precisely controlled tracer gas injection.
This experiment requires:
• a gas cylinder (in this case, R-134a);
• fans to ensure complete mixing within the room;
• a gas concentration detector (in this case, INNOVA 1303).
The room was left undisturbed for a few minutes prior to the background check in order
to approximate steady-state conditions. Then, the tracer gas was supplied in front of
the two fans, for approximately 5 to 10 seconds. Measurements were then taken over
a 20-minute period, with data collected at 35-second intervals.
Concentration decay method
The concentration decay method estimates the air exchange rate by tracking the rate at
which the tracer gas concentration declines over time. It relies on several key assump-
tions to simplify the model:
• no filtration;
• no internal pollutant production;
• clean outside air supply;
• no sorption effect;
• complete mixing of air within the room.

Wind profile and pressure coefficient
The wind profile applied in the simulations uses the default urban exposure values in
IDA ICE, characterized by a reference coefficient a0=0.67 and an exponent aexp=0.25.
These parameters correspond to a heavy terrain profile as defined by Sherman and Mod-
era [44], typically used for urban, industrial, or other densely built-up areas. They adjust
the wind pressure acting on the façade openings by accounting for surface roughness in
the surrounding environment. The resulting wind speed at window height is a key input
for calculating pressure-driven airflow through natural openings, as well as for determin-
ing the building’s infiltration rate. Although the case study is a single-story residential
house located in a low-rise area, the selected urban exposure profile is considered a rea-
sonable approximation, particularly for windward/leeward pressure differentials. In IDA
ICE, these parameters influence the magnitude of wind-induced ventilation, especially
under cross and stack-ventilation scenarios, by modifying the effective wind pressure
on each façade according to building geometry and orientation.
For the pressure coefficients, the model uses the “sheltered coefficients” auto-filled by
IDA ICE. These values are designed to reflect typical wind pressure distributions on
building surfaces in sheltered or partially shielded environments, aligning with the sub-
urban character of the site. While more site-specific values could improve accuracy, the
chosen settings offer a balanced compromise between realism and simulation simplicity.

from winter and summer seasons were analysed. These periods show significant indoor

assessing the impact of ventilation strategies on indoor cooling.
• April 24: Categorised as ’winter’ under EN 16798-1 [27] due to a running mean

window, façade door, and FRWs opened simultaneously at 10 AM.
• July 2: This summer day showed the highest operative temperatures in the din-
ing room. Following macro-opening assumptions, windows were open overnight
and closed at 4 AM as outdoor temperatures began rising. At 9 AM, selected win-
dows reopened when indoor temperatures in the living room exceeded Category

tested:
– all windows reopening at 9 AM;

While thermal comfort is a key determinant of occupant satisfaction, maintaining accept-
able IAQ is equally critical — particularly in airtight, energy-efficient dwellings where
pollutant build-up can occur rapidly. Therefore, alongside comfort analysis, this study
evaluates IAQ using CO2 concentration as a primary indicator.
3.4.2
CO2 concentrations
In this study, CO2 concentration was chosen as the primary indicator of indoor air qual-
ity, rather than a broader range of indoor pollutants, due to both practical and method-
ological considerations. Most importantly, CO2 is used as the controlled variable in
the VELUX ACTIVE macro developed by VELUX, which regulates ventilation based
on CO2 thresholds while neglecting other parameters such as RH. Given this focus,
it is methodologically consistent to evaluate the effectiveness of VELUX ACTIVE by
analysing its performance in maintaining acceptable CO2 levels, especially in compari-
son to alternative control strategies based solely on thermal comfort. Moreover, CO2 is
a well-established indicator for occupancy and ventilation adequacy, making it a reliable
metric for comparative analysis. Simulations were performed throughout a whole year,
then divided into summer and winter seasons.
In addition to thermal comfort and air quality, energy consumption plays a central role in
assessing the viability of natural ventilation strategies. To provide a comprehensive per-
formance evaluation, each scenario’s heating and ventilation-related energy demand
was also quantified.
3.4.3
Energy use
Energy use was also considered due to the inherent trade-off that exists — particularly
during the heating season in naturally ventilated buildings — between thermal comfort
and air quality. While ventilation helps mitigate overheating and improve air quality,
excessive ventilation can increase heat loss and, consequently, heating demand. Sim-
ulations were performed both across the whole house for a whole year and focusing on
the dining room, living room and kitchen during the heating season only (from October
1 to April 30).
Therefore, assessing both thermal and IAQ performance in relation to energy use pro-
vides a more comprehensive understanding of the efficiency and cost-effectiveness of
different ventilation strategies. With the experimental and simulation methods defined,
the next chapter presents the results obtained from on-site measurements and simula-
tions, starting with the thermal comfort assessment.

and contributing to greater heat extraction from the living and kitchen zones.

loss during the heating season varied significantly between zones, and automated con-

5
Discussion
This chapter synthesizes the key findings from simulations and measurements, compar-
ing natural ventilation strategies across thermal comfort, IAQ, and energy use. Empha-
sis is placed on seasonal variation and trade-offs, providing insights for future residential
ventilation design. In addition, the main limitations of the study and directions for future
research are discussed.
5.1
Seasonal trade-offs in thermal comfort, IAQ and energy
The results indicate that relying solely on either flaps or FRWs is insufficient to meet
thermal comfort and IAQ requirements. However, when combined with operable win-
dows enabling stack-ventilation, FRWs significantly improve performance. Both stack
and cross-ventilation strategies proved most effective at maintaining acceptable thermal
comfort and IAQ throughout the year.
Notably, stack-ventilation with VELUX ACTIVE automation demonstrated the best ther-
mal performance during summer. However, the basic scheduled stack-ventilation min-
imized the total hours exceeding thermal comfort thresholds. The simplified VELUX
ACTIVE control prevents FRW openings outside the schedule when outdoor temper-
atures drop below 14°C, reducing cooling opportunities during nights, shoulder and
winter seasons — which increases overheating hours compared to the basic sched-
ule. This likely overestimates overheating risk, as the real algorithm includes dynamic
responses to indoor thresholds, even in cooler conditions. Differences between the
simplified macro and actual VELUX ACTIVE guidelines are summarized in Table A.3.
Refining this automation could further improve global thermal comfort, as the strategy
already shows strong promise. These results align with annual ACH simulations, where
stack-ventilation consistently achieved the highest ventilation rates (Figure A.16).
Measurements revealed no significant local discomfort under this strategy, reinforcing
the conclusion that stack-ventilation is both effective and acceptable in practice. Further-
more, occupants may find draughts tolerable — or even beneficial — when operative
temperatures exceed 25°C.
In winter, single-sided ventilation through the façade door showed the best global ther-
mal performance among the tested scenarios. However, limitations emerged: the CBE
Thermal Comfort Tool classified it as Category III, below typical residential comfort stan-
dards. This rating may reflect unusually high indoor temperatures measured shortly after
Page 61 of 107
==PAGE:70==
a lunch break, with windows closed and high occupancy leading to heat build-up. De-
spite only one door opening, significant temperature drops occurred due to direct airflow
and elevated air velocity (Figure A.8a), consistent with the relatively high ACH measured
(Table A.4) and the expectations from single-sided ventilation ACH [45]. Nevertheless,
this scenario also caused excessive draughts, exceeding thermal comfort thresholds —
likely intensified by the south-facing door and prevailing south-southeast winds.
Balancing global thermal comfort, local draught discomfort, and energy use is especially
critical in winter. Stack- and cross-ventilation emerged as the best compromises, provid-
ing good simulated thermal comfort with only moderately higher overall energy demand.
Stack-ventilation may be preferable, as cross-ventilation was not measured in winter
but would likely generate drafts similar to — or even worse than — single-sided venti-
lation. Further in-situ measurements or CFD simulations are recommended to confirm
the suitability of stack-ventilation during colder periods.
Additionally, the automation of roof windows in stack-ventilation further reduced energy
consumption compared to the scheduled alternative. In addition, incorporating wind sen-
sors into the automation logic, as the original VELUX ACTIVE automation control does,
could help mitigate the risk of draughts during winter or shoulder seasons, enhancing
comfort while maintaining ventilation efficiency.
Stack-ventilation strategy also met CO2 concentration thresholds — Category II in win-
ter and Category I during summer and on average — indicating satisfactory IAQ perfor-
mance.
Overall, this study underscores key limitations of relying solely on natural ventilation -
particularly in temperate climates like Denmark’s. These include the risk of uncomfort-
able draughts in winter and inadequate cooling during heat peaks. The findings high-
light the need for adaptive, season-specific strategies and suggest that hybrid systems
— combining natural ventilation with mechanical support — may be necessary to ensure
consistent indoor comfort and air quality year-round.
While these findings highlight both the potential and limits of natural ventilation in prac-
tice, it is important to critically reflect on the assumptions and constraints underlying the
study. The following section discusses the main limitations of both the measurements
and simulation models used.

5.2
Limitations of the study
5.2.1
Limitations of the measurements
• Short duration: Only two days of full-scale measurement limit the generalizability
of findings across seasons.
• Weather dependency: External factors such as wind direction were uncontrolled
during the measurements, introducing variability between scenarios. Additionally,
the weather data used for simulations and outdoor conditions during the experi-
ment were obtained from a coastal weather station near the case study location,
but may not accurately reflect the microclimatic conditions at the actual site, po-
tentially affecting wind-driven ventilation interpretation.
• Unusable IAQ data: No IAQ measurements were available for winter due to data
logger failure. During the summer campaign, data reliability was affected by un-
controlled variables such as solar gains, an imprecise occupancy schedule, and
unpredictable occupant behaviour (e.g., additional window openings). These fac-
tors limited the ability to draw clear on-site conclusions regarding IAQ.
5.2.2
Limitations of the simulations
• Basic opening control: The simplified control logic used in simulations may not
accurately replicate real-world occupant behaviour, which is inherently complex
and influenced by personal preferences, habits, and situational factors. This limits
the realism of modelled window operation and its impact on thermal performance
and IAQ.
• Simplified VELUX ACTIVE algorithm: Table A.3 in the Appendix outlines the key
differences between the original VELUX ACTIVE control algorithm and its simpli-
fied implementation in IDA ICE. These simplifications may have contributed to
underestimating the system’s real-world performance.
• Internal gains simplification: Activities such as cooking, appliance use, or man-
ual ventilation adjustments were not modelled, which may affect the accuracy of
heat gain and air quality predictions.
• Constant year-round simulation: Simulations were run for the entire year using
consistent control parameters, regardless of occupancy. A more realistic approach
would involve applying these parameters only during periods when occupants are
present in the dwelling.
• Idealistic window opening periods: In the simulations, windows could open
even during times when occupants were not home or at night. In practice, oc-
cupants may be reluctant to leave windows open under such conditions due to
concerns about security, privacy, or outdoor noise, which could limit the effective-
ness of natural ventilation in real use.

5.3
Considerations for future work
Based on the study’s findings and limitations, several directions for future research are
proposed.
Firstly, long-term monitoring across multiple seasons is essential to validate simulation
models and better capture the effects of climate variability and occupant behaviour over
time. Such empirical data would allow more accurate calibration of dynamic simulation
tools and increase confidence in their predictive capability.
Additionally, broader experimental studies should be conducted across a variety of res-
idential housing types, as the efficiency of natural ventilation is highly sensitive to build-
ing geometry, orientation, and site location. Such diversity is essential for developing
generalizable design recommendations and performance benchmarks.
Furthermore, more advanced airflow modelling approaches — such as integrating IDA
ICE with computational fluid dynamics — could significantly improve the accuracy of ven-
tilation predictions, particularly in scenarios involving cross-ventilation, stack-ventilation,
or complex building geometries. To better assess occupant comfort, there is also a need
to evaluate local air speed and draught rate at the occupant level, as these are critical
factors influencing perceived thermal comfort, especially in naturally ventilated environ-
ments. Parallel to this, there is a strong case for refining how smart control strategies
are simulated. Implementing control logic that more closely mirrors the actual firmware
of systems like VELUX ACTIVE would enhance the credibility of simulated outcomes,
especially in relation to IAQ and comfort performance.
Future research should also explore the integration of natural ventilation with broader
smart home ecosystems, including heating systems, shading devices, and occupancy
sensors. This could improve both the operational efficiency and user experience of
automated ventilation systems. In parallel, greater attention must be paid to occupant
interaction. Understanding how users override, adapt to, or accept automated systems
is crucial for designing strategies that perform well not only in theory but in real-world
settings.
Finally, it would be valuable to examine the resilience of natural ventilation strategies un-
der projected climate change conditions, such as more frequent and intense heatwaves.
This could help determine the long-term viability of passive and hybrid systems as part
of climate adaptation measures [41].

6
Conclusion
This thesis investigated the performance of automated natural ventilation strategies in
a residential house in an urban surrounding, with a focus on thermal comfort, indoor air
quality, and energy consumption. Through a combination of short-term in-situ measure-
ments and year-long simulations in IDA ICE, the study evaluated various control strate-
gies and ventilation configurations, including the use of stack, cross and single-sided
ventilation under both scheduled and sensor-based (VELUX ACTIVE) control strategies.
The results confirmed that natural ventilation offers clear potential for improving indoor
environmental quality, particularly when well-aligned with building geometry and sup-
ported by automation. Among the strategies tested, stack-ventilation consistently pro-
vided the best performance during warm periods. It enabled effective heat removal,
reduced overheating, and facilitated rapid temperature decay when outdoor conditions
were favourable. The version equipped with simplified VELUX ACTIVE control further
improved performance by limiting openings during colder periods, thus lowering heat
loss. However, this same control logic also reduced cooling potential during winter,
shoulder seasons and nights, slightly increasing overheating risk — a limitation linked
to the oversimplified temperature thresholds used in the simulation.
Conversely, single-sided ventilation through façade openings did not perform well in
winter. Although it achieved favourable global comfort and IAQ indicators in simulations,
measurements revealed elevated air velocities and draught rates, particularly at ankle
level, that exceeded acceptable thresholds. This highlights a key limitation of natural
ventilation: while simulations may predict acceptable conditions based on air change
rates and mean temperatures, local discomfort caused by draught can be significant
and must be considered when designing winter ventilation strategies.
IAQ across all scenarios remained within acceptable thresholds, particularly in summer,
where more frequent openings effectively controlled CO2 levels. However, during winter,
higher ACH strategies came at the cost of increased energy use and draught risk. The
trade-off between air renewal, heat loss, and local discomfort was most evident in this
season.
Annual energy consumption analysis showed relatively small differences between sce-
narios, with the highest consumption linked to configurations with sustained high air-
flow, such as stack-ventilation without smart control. Conversely, sensor-based control
strategies like VELUX ACTIVE demonstrated modest but meaningful energy savings by
limiting openings, especially during colder outdoor conditions.
Overall, this study underscores the seasonal trade-offs inherent to natural ventilation
in a temperate oceanic climate like Denmark’s. While it can provide excellent comfort
Page 65 of 107
==PAGE:74==
and IAQ during mild conditions, it does not reliably address peak summer overheating
or winter draught risk without additional measures. For year-round performance, hybrid
solutions — combining passive strategies with smart controls or mechanical support —
appear necessary, particularly during increasingly frequent heatwaves and changing cli-
mate conditions. Future designs should consider integrating refined adaptive algorithms,
occupancy-sensitive controls, and diversified airflow paths to enhance the effectiveness
and user acceptance of automated natural ventilation systems.

INNOVA Multi Gas Monitoring Instruments 
Reliable, easy-to-use multi-point sampler and doser
INNOVA 1303 
• Full remote-control from a PC
• Automatic calculation of the 
amount of tracer-gas deliv-
ered, to the dosing location
• Discontinuous dosing facility
• Self-calibrating dosing system
• Self-test function
• Pneumatic system constructed 
of AISI-316 Stainless Steel and 
PTFE tubing to minimize gas 
adsorption
An increasing number of legislative 
measures are aimed at improving 
air quality in workplace environ-
ments. These improvements require 
gas monitoring equipment with the 
flexibility to provide sensitive and 
accurate monitoring in a variety of  
environments. 
The INNOVA 1303 Multi-point 
Sampler and Doser from LumaSense 
Technologies is designed to be 
remote-controlled from a PC over its 
IEEE 488/RS-232 serial interface and 
used in conjunction with an INNOVA 
1412 Photoacoustic Field Gas-Monitor 
to provide a flexible, sensitive and 
accurate monitoring system. The 1303 
greatly increases the area monitoring 
capabilities of the 1412 by drawing air 
samples through tubing from up to six 
sampling points, up to 50m away, and 
delivering them to the 1412.
Comprehensive air exchange analyses 
and ventilation efficiency checks are 
easily performed using the 1303’s 
dosing facilities. Tracer-gas is delivered 
through tubing to “label” the air in 
up to six locations. The amount of 
tracer-gas delivered is automatically 
calculated by the 1303. The labelled 
air is then sampled by the 1303 and 
delivered to the 1412 for analysis. Up 
to six temperature transducers can be 
connected to the 1303 and positioned 
at the desired sampling/dosing points. 
The 1303’s self-calibrating and self-
checking routines allow easy verifica-
tion of the unit’s operation and ensure 
reliable functioning.
Functions 
The 1303’s pneumatic system is shown 
in Figure 1. The sampler system is 
constructed of AISI-316 stainless 
steel and poly tetrafluoroethylene 
(PTFE) tubing to minimize adsorp-
tion of samples. The system has six 
inlet channels, each with a solenoid 
valve. Each inlet channel has a 
tube-mounting stub on the 1303’s 
frontplate. Six tubes of up to 50m 
connect each channel to the respective 
sampling point. The six inlet channels 
converge into one; a three-way valve 
then directs the gas sample to the 
1412 for analysis or through the pump 
to the waste-air outlet on the 1303’s 
back-plate. A pressure transducer 
checks the efficiency of the sampling 
pump and allows checks for blocked 
airways. An air filter is attached to the 
end of each sampling tube to keep the 
samples free of particles.
Application areas:
• Air sampling in six locations and de-
livery to a 1412 Photoacoustic Field 
Gas Monitor or a 1314 Photoacoustic 
Multi-Gas Monitor.
• Delivery of tracer-gas to six  
locations for air-exchange analyses 
with the 1412 Photoacoustic Field 
Gas Monitor
The Doser System
The doser system has six outlet 
channels, each with a solenoid valve. 
Each channel has a nozzle that reduces 
the internal diameter of the channel. 
This nozzles ensure that the rate of 
flow of tracer-gas to the dosing points 
is dependent only upon the tracer-gas 
supply pressure and temperature and 
is unaffected by the pressure in the 
dosing tubes or at the dosing point 
itself.
Three different nozzle sizes are used: 
one small (nozzle 1), four medium 
(nozzle 2-5) and one large (nozzle 6). 
Each size gives a different volume flow 
rate, allowing the amount of tracer-gas 
delivered to a particular dosing point 
to be matched to the requirement at 
that point by using the outlet channel 
which has the appropriate nozzle. Each 
of the six dosing outlet channels has a tube-
mounting stub on the front plate of the 
1303. Six tubes of up to 50m connect each 
stub to the respective dosing point. The 
tracer-gas and the carrier-air inlet channels 
mounted on the backplate of the 1303 
serve the dosing channels.
The tracer-gas inlet channel is pressur-
ized by the tracer-gas supply cylinder, 
which is connected by tubing to 
the inlet on the 1303’s backplate. A 
pressure and a temperature transducer 
give information on the tracer-gas 
supply; a fine filter ensures that the 
dosing channels are particle-free. The 
main valve on the tracer-gas inlet 
channel is used to enclose a volume 
of tracer-gas between itself and the 
dosing valves. This volume is used 
when calibrating the doser system. 
The carrier-air inlet pumps extra air to 
the dosing outlets to speed delivery 
of the tracer-gas to the dosing point. 
This inlet has a coarse air filter, a pump 
and a pressure transducer for checking 
the efficiency of the pump. Delivering 
a dose of tracer-gas to a dosing point 
50m distant takes one minute.The 
dosing system can deliver  an uninter-
rupted flow of tracer-gas over a period 
of time or a discontinuous dose, in 
which the amount of tracer-gas deliv-
ered is reduced by interrupting the 
flow at regular intervals during the 
dosing period.
The doser system is configured by 
specifying a dosing time-out: the 1303 
will stop any current dosing proce-
dure after the given time has elapsed 
without instructions from the control-
ling computer. This value is specified 
from the controlling computer.
Calibrating the Doser System 
The 1303 can calibrate each dosing-
nozzle automatically, so that the 
amount of tracer-gas delivered 
during a dosing procedure can be 
accurately determined by the 1303. 
A volume of tracer-gas is enclosed 
between the main valve and the 
dosing valves; this volume is then 
released through the nozzle to be 
calibrated. As the internal volume of 
the airways are known, the effec-
tive outflow area for each nozzle is 
calculated from the rate of pressure-
decrease measured in the dosing 
airway. The amount of tracer-gas 
delivered can then be calculated 
from the effective outflow area, 
the tracer-gas supply pressure and 
temperature, and the dosing time-
period.
Temperature Measurement
To complete the information about 
the air environment at the sampling/
dosing points, the 1303 is equipped 
with six temperature transducer inputs, 
suitable for use with the MM0034 Air 
Temperature Transducer, MM0035 
Surface Temperature Transducer and 
MM0060 Operative Temperature 
Transducer. Each transducer can be 
positioned up to 50m away from the 
1303.
Reliability
Reliability is ensured by automatic self-
tests of both hardware and software. A 
check of the pneumatic system can be 
performed on request by the control-
ling  computer. The 1303’s operating 
status can be read-out at any time; if an 
error exists, the type of error is given to 
help repair.

tion in the internal air-channels.

Control of the 1303
The 1303 is fully remote-controlled from 
a PC using the IEEE/IEC interface. Control 
via the 1412 is also possible: the control-
ling computer communicates with the 
1412 over the RS-232 interface. The 
message is then transferred to the 1303 
via the IEEE/IEC interface. Commands 
and information requests are sent over 
the interface to the 1303 to control the 
sampler system; to set-up, calibrate, 
and control the dosing system; and to 
read-out data and command the perfor-
mance of self-tests.
System Use
The 1303 combined with the 1412 and 
a controlling computer offers wide-
ranging monitoring capabilities. The 
1303 makes it possible to perform 
multi-point air-exchange analyses and 
multi-point monitoring tasks in many 
different situations and environments, 
without changing the system compo-
nents.
An example air-exchange analysis 
system is shown in Fig.2. In such a 
system, the doser/sampler systems 
of the 1303 are used as follows. The 
doser system marks the supply-air of 
the room with a known amount of 
tracergas. The sampler system then 
takes a sample of the return-air from 
the room, and delivers the sample to 
the 1412 for analysis. 
While the 1412 performs one analysis, 
the 1303 takes the next sample for 
analysis from the room. As the amount 
of tracer-gas delivered to the room is 
known, and the remaining concentra-
tion of tracer-gas in the samples is 
determined by the 1412, the venti-
lation system performance can be 
calculated.
LumaSense Technologies’ INNOVA 7620 
Application Software – is available to 
give full coordination and control of all 
the dosing/sampling and monitoring 
functions of such systems. The 7620 can 
control one 1412 and up to two 1303 
units. 
A similar system set-up can be used 
for multi-point, multi-gas monitoring, 
using only the 1303’s sampler system. 
This system can be remotely controlled 
using Lumasoft Gas Multi Point 7850 
software. The sofware coordinates 
the function of the monitoring system 
comprising up to four 1303 and one 
1412 or 1314. Gas monitoring can then 
be performed in up to 24 different 
locations.

Figure A.6: Sketches showing the opening of a door and a FRW
Using Figure A.6 as reference, the degree of opening can be estimated. The blue areas
indicate the door or FRW surface when fully closed. Upon opening, additional areas are
created: for the door, these include a top triangle, a bottom triangle, and a rectangular
side area; for the FRW, two side areas and a top triangle are formed.
For simplicity, the following explanation refers only to the sketch of the door, though the
same logic applies to one of the FRW.

standard triangles, due to the small opening angles, they are approximated as right-
angled triangles for calculation purposes. Their area is calculated as:
