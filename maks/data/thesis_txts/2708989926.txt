Abstract
To avoid future problems, it is critical to be precise in the prediction of soil deformations
when building a project. Nowadays, many projects are built in challenging soils. In Den­
mark, one of the most challenging soils is the overconsolidated high plasticity clay due to
its non­linearity and stress history dependency behavior. It is not the only one; there are
other existing overconsolidated formations out there with similar properties.
The existing investigations about the Danish clay are mainly focused on the soil deforma­
tions due to loading scenarios and with less attention paid to unloading. Fewer researches
are focused on the behavior of the stress paths in the shearing phase, which is the aim
of this thesis.
Currently, the immersed Fehmanbelt Tunnel between Denmark and Germany is under
construction founded in Paleogene Clay. Providing an excellent opportunity to investigate
in detail the behavior of the Røsnæs Folded Clay. Some findings were discovered during
the analysis of the triaxial tests provided by COWI A/S from the 2009­2010 geotechnical
investigations collected in a database owned by Femern S/A. The initial state of the sam­
ple and the final state influence the stress paths of the soil. That is why is important to
classify the soil by the initial fissuring presented and the failure mode, in order to have a
better understanding of the behavior.
Then, selected tests are evaluated by simulations following the frameworks of constitutive
models, the MCC and SANICLAY. To study the influence of each aspect in the frameworks,
a conceptual assessment and calibration is first undertaken. The best performing model to
predict the behavior of the folded Røsnæs clay is the SANICLAY­B, in which is introduced
the gradual plastic hardening and rotation of the yield surface and plastic potential. Some
limitations were found in the models, but also very precise predictions of existing tests
were reproduced.

1
Introduction
High plasticity clay has challenged Danish engineers for years, still nowadays there is not
enough knowledge to understand its complex behavior. In Denmark, Paleogene clay is
one of the most complicated soil types and one of the most challenging due to its non­
linearity and stress history dependency behaviour.
Danish Paleogene clay was deposited before the Quaternary period, consequently, expe­
rienced glacial cycles. Therefore, the evolution and movement of the ice masses above
the clay highly influenced its stress history. But it is not an uniquely found in Denmark.
There are other existing overconsolidated formations that have similar characteristics, the
London Clay, Italian stiff clay, Flanders deposits and Bearpaw Shale [1]–[5] are the most
studied formations of these clays. They are generally stiff and dense with low permeability
due to the compression from the Neogene deposits and the loading and unloading of the
glaciers.
Currently, there is little research on the characterization of the behavior of the overconsoli­
dated high plasticity clay. The existing investigations mainly focus on the soil deformations
due to loading scenarios and lesser to unloading. The few research on unloading sce­
narios focus in the 1­D compression behavior but not in the stress paths in the shearing
phase.
In the latest years, the number of construction projects founded in high plasticity clays
have increased significantly. A good performance of large infrastructures requires an
accurate prediction of deformations during the project lifetime. The prediction of long­
term deformations of modern infrastructure is challenged by adverse soil conditions, such
as high plasticity Paleogene clay.
Over the past decade, the demand of global high­speed railway connections has in­
creased significantly, this challenge is linked to the strict tolerances for deformations. The
main focus in the prediction of soil deformations has been on compressive behaviour, but
many construction projects are dominated by unloading. Excavation trenches and im­
mersed tunnels are some examples.
One of the most recent projects founded in these high plasticity clay is the immersed
Fehmarnbelt Tunnel between Denmark and Germany, which gives a great opportunity to
investigate and understand deeply the clay behavior. Since the shearing phase in triaxial
tests has not been investigated in detail, this project will be focus in predict their behavior.
The current study presents a range of triaxial tests provided by COWI from the geotech­
nical investigations collected in a database owned by Femern A/S. Even though the tests
have been analyzed, in this project the data will be corrected based on the ISO Standard
guidelines, re­analyzed and interpreted in order to achieve the objective and to obtain
better description of the loading and unloading behaviour. Moreover, some of these tests
were selected to be evaluated by simulations following the constitutive models of MCC
and SANICLAY in order to understand more the Paleogene clay behavior. To conclude,
the evaluation of the model performance and results will be performed to characterize the
clay.

ity, fine grained nature, slickensides and fissures, poor bearing capacity and high volume

tively high compressibility and relatively low strength [8]. Up until now, the deformational
behaviour has been associated to smectite content and complex geological loading his­
tory.
Based on the investigations, there are strong indications that the upper, thick part of the
unit at least in parts of the area is heavily folded by ice pressure in the Quaternary period,
and the clay in the folded part of the unit seems to be weakened compared to the intact
clay. It is difficult to distinguish between folded and intact deposits by direct observations
because there is no visible layering or lamination in the clay. It is generally difficult to
get reliable information about the true thickness of the deposits because the upper part
seems to be folded in most of the area.
In the Fehmarnbelt area investigations was found several formations of the Paleogene unit
such as Holmehus Clay, Ølst Clay, Røsnæs Clay and Lillebælt Clay, from high plasticity
to very high plasticity clays. These formations consist on clay, smectite and a complex
loading history.
The sedimentation of these formations occurred when the North Sea covered the current
onshore of Denmark. Followed by a a period of large volcanic activity in the Icelandic
Hospot. The deposition of the sediments in this area, mainly clays, occurred over a pe­
riod of several million years. The mineral smectite was produced from weathering of the
volcanic ash, which made up a significant part of the clays mineral composition.

1.2
Geotechnical characteristics of Danish Paleogene clay
A very large laboratory testing was carried out in order to describe the characteristics and
behaviour of the Palaeogene Clay. In the technical reports GDR 00.1­001 Ground Inves­
tifation Report [9], GRD 01.3­002 Fehmarn Geology [7] and GRD 09.0­701 Soil Models
[8] the Danish clays are characterized and described up to the nowadays findings.
Paleogene clays are sedimentary, marine clays and have high clay contents, high plas­
ticity index and significant content of smectite. Some other characteristics described in
the reports are presented below.
• When the clay is unloaded in presence of water, the soil starts to expand/swell lead­
ing to an increase in void ratio, while the stiffness and the undrained shear strength
of the clay seem to decrease as swelling continues [8].
• Secondary consolidation has a smaller effect on the total strains than the primary
consolidation, but it is important in the long term movements.
• The permeability of the Paleogene clay seem to vary with the void ratio.
• The strength of the Paleogene clay was found to be dominated by pre­existing shear
planes within the fabric of the clay resulting from its geological history, especially
glaciar actions.
• The significant potential to deform is related to the active clay mineral, smectite.
• In the Advanced Laboratory Investigations was found lower yield stresses than ex­
pected from the geological history.
• As the soil volume increases during unloading, the void ratio increases and the
apparent pre­consolidation pressure is reduced.
• It has a lack of memory.
As mentioned before, several formations were found in the boreholes performed in the
Fehmarnbelt area (Lillebælt, Æbelo, Røsnæs, Ølst and Humelhus). The Lillebælt forma­
tion is the youngest pre­quaternary deposit. Æbelo formation seem to be silty or even
very silty clay, but the rest are high to very high plasticity.
These clays have clear, bright colours that vary a lot down through the layers and area. But
it is tendency that Lillebælt Clay is often dark greyish green, while the most of Røsnæs has
reddish to brownish colours. However, for Røsnæs the colour and the general character
changes significantly down through the layer.
The most dominating formation is the Røsnæs Clay, significantly thicker than 20 m and
is well known to be presented in other parts of Denmark. This formation is divided into
folded and intact, which describes whether the soil was subjected to disturbance from

Background Theory
In this chapter, the background theory applied to analyze the data is described.
2.1
Critical State Soil Mechanics
The aim of the project is to understand and be able to predict the response of the Pale­
ogene clays in the Shearing phase. In order to do that, some models will be applied to
be able to predict their behaviour. These models involve the mechanical properties of the
soil.
Before explaining the models some concepts will be presented: yielding and yield locus,
Critical State Line and Stress Paths. Then, the original Cam­Clay, the Modified Cam­Clay
and the SANICLAY are explained.
2.1.1
Yielding and Yield Locus
Elasticity is the property of a body that recovers its original configuration when the de­
forming forces are removed. Plasticity is the opposite, when the deforming forces are
removed the body acquires a permanent deformation. The transition between elastic and
elasto­plastic response is called yielding.
The yield point is the point in the stress­strain curve that indicates the limit of elastic be­
haviour and beginning of the plastic behavior. Below the yield point the material behaves
elastically and will return to the original state. But, once this point is passed, the body
gain some deformation and will never return to the initial state. The figure presented be­
low, Figure 2.1, represents the stress­strain curve, where the yield point is marked as
yield strength. On its left the elastic behaviour takes place and on the right the plastic
behaviour.

2.1.3
Stress paths
The need to consider stress paths in a elasto­plastic model arises because real soils
can not be treated as isotropic and elastic materials, they should be treated as inelastic,
anisotropic and history dependent materials [10].
The approach of the estimation of stress paths in the model is to predict settlements of
foundations. It requires triaxial tests as representative behaviour of the soil for stress
changes. From their response pseudo­elastic parameters can be deduced which then
are used to give numerical estimates of settlements.
In order to study the behaviour of a soil and its stress paths there are some models that
with some assumptions predict the behaviour of the material. Since the material studied
in this project is overconsolidated clay the models chosen to its analysis are: Modified
Cam Clay and SANICLAY.
2.1.4
Cam Clay
The Cam­Clay model (CC) is a elastic plastic strain hardening model that is based on
Critical State theory and the assumption of the logarithmic relationship between the mean
stress and the void ratio [11]. This model describes three important aspects of soil be­
haviour, strength and compression/expansion, and the critical state at which the soil can
recover its initial state without any changes in stress or volume.
The model is based on the assumptions of the soil being isotropic, elasto­plastic, deforms
as a continuum and it is not affected by creep. Based on this assumptions the relationship
between the specific volume and the mean stress consists on of straight virgin consolida­
tion line Equation 2.2 and swelling line Equation 2.3.
The hardening behaviour of the CC is formulated based on the CSL. The URL is used in
calculations of elastic properties.

2.3
SANICLAY
Previous models as the Cam­Clay and MCC are based on isotropic and unstructured
elastoplastic soil models. But most of the natural soft clays have a significant degree of
anisotropy. The SANICLAY model takes into account the effect of anisotropy, structure
and their evolution from soil behaviour.
In this model, anisotropy is accounted by rotational hardening, a rotation of the yield and
plastic potential surfaces. This is not the first time that rotational hardening models are
proposed. Dafalias [12] also introduced the anisotropy in the Modified Cam Clay model,
introducing the rate of plastic work expression. The resulting potential and yield surface
is a rotated and distorted ellipse. The rotation and distortion is controlled by α, a scalar
in the triaxial space. A lot of models were based on this theory but they differed mainly in
the rotational hardening law, α.
Recently Dafalias proposed the SANICLAY model based on non­associated flow rule,
allowing a simulation of softening response under undrained compression conditions [13].

Terzaghi (1944) [14] described the sensitivity of the clays as the ratio of their undisturbed
and remoulded strength. It varies from 1 for heavily over­consolidated to 100 for extra­
sensitive or quick clays. The structure of natural soils is based on the fabric and the
bonding between particles, which can be destroyed progressively during plastic straining
[2].
Destructuration is the progressive damage to bonding during plastic straining. This loss
of strength can be critical for design. It can be manifest as sudden post­yield increase in
compressibility, or a post­peak decrease in strength in shearing.
There are two types of destructuration: isotropic, which results on the softening of the
yield surface, and frictional, which reduces the critical stress ratio by the friction angle.

Special cases
There are two special cases that can be applied by using the formulation presented pre­
viously but simplifying the model a little bit.
Case 1: Two surface model with N = M.
This does not mean that the yield surface and plastic potential are the same since α ̸= β.
M and N were different in order to capture the softening without the destructuration. But
destructuration allows to present the softening even with M = N. M = N lead to Nc = Mc
and n = m.
Case 2: One surface model (associative flow rule) with N = M and xβ = xα > 1.
This is the original version of the model proposed by Dafalias (1986), characterized by an
associative flow rule and normality. This simplification gives several advantages:
• Symmetry of the elastoplastic stiffness matrix due to associate flow rule;
• Only one rotational back stress ratio needs to be updated
However, this simplifications also give some problems in the reproduction of the behaviour
of the soil in the model.
First, the plastic potential can not have a rotational back stress α that stabilizes with ηk, the
stress ratio under Ko. Because Ko must produce both volumetric and deviatoric plastic
strains, but this is not possible if α = ηk, where only volumetric plastic strain is produced.
In order to avoid this, xα > 1 in the evolution law for α, so α = ηk/xα at stabilisation of
rotation.

3
Methodology
The Danish Paleogene Clay was deposited before the Quaternary, this means that expe­
rienced glacial cycles. The evolution and movement of ice masses above the clay highly
influenced their stress history. During the interglacial periods the glaciers melted, unload­
ing the formations. On the other hand, in the glacial periods the glaciars loaded the clay,
showing in the clay’s early preconsolidation stresses.
Paleogene clay has a very high plasticity this results on high compressibility and low
strength [8]. That is why there is an interest on understanding the behavior of the soil.
Several Geotechnical Investigations were carried out in order to analyze the geology of
the area through ground investigations boring of 2009­2010, in­situ testing, advanced
laboratory on samples from the boring campaign and large scale field testing [15]. The
advanced laboratory testing were done by GEO with Deltares and NGI as subconsultants
for some tests. The results from the investigations are collected in a database owned by
Femern A/S, the access to the data was given by COWI to make possible this project. The
project focus on the stress paths in the shearing phase of the Triaxial tests. These tests
have been re­analyzed and interpreted, to obtain a better description of the loading and
unloading behaviour based on the stress paths of the shearing phase of the Paleogene
clay.
The Triaxial tests were performed in several boring of the 2009­2010 campaign. The
boreholes are: 09.A.001, 09.A.002, 09.A.003, 09.A.701, 10.A.051, 10.A.052, 10.A.053,
10.A.054, 10.A.055, 10.A.056 and 10.A.072. Where 09 and 10 are the year in which the
boring was performed, 2009 or 2010. The letter A is the type of tests, in this case Triaxial
test. The last three numbers are the core number.
In Figure 1.1 all the boreholes of the campaign are presented in grey circles in the align­
ment for the Fehmarnbelt immersed tunnel. The boreholes with triaxial testing are high­
lighted in blue circles as well as the name of the boreholes. In Figure 3.2 all boreholes and
triaxial tests are presented as well as the different geological units along the alignment of
the tunnel and level [m]. As it can be observed, the majority of the tests are found in the
German side.
The Triaxial tests were carried out according to the Danish tradition being the tests spec­
imens with a diameter to height ratio of 1 (D:H of 1:1) and with smooth lubricated ends
[15]. The Consolidation phase of the tests were performed at stress states corresponding
to effective in­situ stress.
A total of 128 tests were carried out.
• 78 Anisotropically Consolidated Undrained Compression test (CAUc);
• 17 Anisotropically Consolidated Undrained Extension test (CAUe);
• 8 Anisotropically Consolidated Drained Compression test (CADc);
• 25 Unconsolidated Undrained test (UU).
All the formations of the Palaeogene clay were found in these tests: Røsnæs, Æbelo,
Ølst, Lillebæalt and Holmehus. Also in all environments folded, intact and floe.

4.3.2
Modes of failure
In geotechnical engineering is important to know and understand what is the resistance
of a soil. Resistance of a soil is a concept that is related to failure. A soil that is subjected
to failure is not able to bear a higher load and the deformation of the soil increases even
though the load does not increase. When a soil fails volumetric deformations and shear
stresses occur. There are many failure modes, the Paleogene clay from the Fehmarnbelt
area in the triaxial tests present two types of failure mode: conchoidal and slickensides.
The failure mode of the tests is in the Table 4.13.
Conchoidal failure is defined as a fracture with smooth, curved surfaces that resemble
the interior of a seashell. Typically slightly concave. In Figure 4.31 (a) an example of
conchoidal failure in the Paleogene clay is shown.
Slickensides failure is a smoothly polished surface caused by frictional movement be­
tween rocks along the two sides of a fault or bending plane. In soils can be also defined
as natural crack surfaces produced by swelling and shrinkage. In Figure 4.31 (b) an ex­
ample of slickensides failure in the Paleogene clay is shown.
Figure 4.31: Example of (a) conchoidal failure, (b) slickensides failure.
For CAUc and CIUc tests, the conchoidal failure mode present higher friction angle than
the slickensides, while the effective cohesion is higher for the slickensides than the con­
choidal, Figure 4.32, Figure 4.33, Figure 4.34 and Figure 4.35. Moreover, it can be ap­
preciated that the friction angle in conchoidal (ϕ′ = 21.30o) and slickensides (ϕ′ = 10.81o)
for CAUc and CIUc are the same, because the CSL slope is the same. The interception
of the CSL with the vertical axis is different, which explains the different cohesion.
CAUe has higher friction angle and cohesion in the slickensides tests than the conchoidal,
Figure 4.36 and Figure 4.37. But the comparison is not complete because in the con­
choidal the three tests were considered as one point to calculate the CSL, since they
were very close to each other. While, in the slickensides the tests are more separated
and it was easier to define the CSL.
From the stress paths presented in subsection A.4.2 is appreciated that in general, the
pore pressure of the slickensides tests reach a peak and then has a sharp decrease.
While the conchoidal tests present smoother change of pore pressure.

5
Conclusion
The aim of the project was to characterize the Paleogene Clay based on triaxial testing
focusing in the shearing phase. By analyzing the triaxial tests provided by COWI A/S
the prediction of the Paleogene clay in the shearing phase for loading and unloading was
possible.
During the analysis of the data was observed that tests with the same in­situ stress be­
haved differently. Comparing these tests was found out that the main reason the stress
paths were not similar is because they presented different initial fissuring and failure mode.
As a consequence, the tests were classified by this two conditions and compare. The ma­
jority of the tests that had a conchoidal failure presented high intensity of fissures, while
the ones that failed in slickensides presented very high intensity. The slickensides are
constrained by the shear surfaces which influences the critical state failure, that is why for
the simulations the main focus was on conchoidal tests.
The deformational behavior is influenced by the fissuring, failure mode and pore pressure.
From the results, it is clear that the pore pressure has a big role in the behavior of the soil,
it influences the shape of the stress paths leading to the S shape or C shape.
Five tests were simulated in order to predict the behavior of the soil based on the consti­
tutive models: MCC and SANICLAY framework. These models do not take into account
the pore pressure in the calculations and prediction of the stress paths. The relevance of
integrating progressive plasticity while modeling the folded Røsnæs Clay is clearly demon­
strated by the simulations. Also the anisotropy is needed to match the anisotropic behavior
of the Danish clay. The SANICLAY­B provides the best fit to experimental data on high
plasticity overconsolidated clay.
Even though the SANICLAY­B presented very promising results and very accurate fit to
the triaxial testing data. The model has few limitations, one of them is when the path
in the p′:q space yields in the dry side. The eq:q stress path obtained was not very rep­
resentative failing in earlier strains compared to the triaxial tests results. Changing the
parameters from the SANICLAY­B framework no better results were obtained. This would
be interesting to analyze, to check if it is a limitation of the script or another constitutive
model should be used to predict these tests.
An interesting future approach would be to try to simulate the behavior of the soil by
taking into account the cohesion in the calculations. The critical state line would be more
representative of the data and the majority of the tests would yield in the wet side. Which
will solve the limitation that the script have.
Regardless the limitations, the three methods do the prediction very precise, but it is clear
that the SANICLAY­B is the one that resembles more to the reality. Taking into account
the anisotropy and the gradual hardening correction works well for the simulation of the
Røsnæs Clay.
