Abstract
Tilting-pad journal bearings (TPJB) are a common bearing choice in turbomachinery, with their
excellent stability properties. However, in order for modern machinery to become more eﬀective
and eﬃcient, bearing technology has to improve. One way of improving the TPJB is to make
it into a mechantronic device, by incorporating it in a control system to improve its dynamic
properties.
The bearing can be modiﬁed to allow for injection of high pressure oil directly
into the bearing clearance, which can be controlled by high performance electronically operated
servovvalves. For large scale implementation of such system, model based controllers allow for a
more direct approach of designing the actuator controller. This also allows the controller to be
considered in the design phase of the machinery, but it requires accurate mathematical models
of the system dynamics.
The objective of this project is to design and implement a model based controller for reduc-
ing rotor lateral vibrations of a coupled rotor-bearing-foundation system. This includes math-
ematical modelling of the rotor-bearing-foundation system with actuator dynamics, design and
implementation of the controller, and evaluation of its performance on a test rig corresponding
to an industrial overhung compressor.
The mathematical modelling of the individual components is covered thoroughly, as well
as the coupling of the models. The models of the individual components are primarily based
on contributions from previous authors on the test rig, but with signiﬁcant modiﬁcations to
the foundation and bearing models.
The coupled model shows very good agreements with
experimental data.
Based on the rotor-bearing-foundation model, controllers were designed and implemented
on the test rig. It was possible to design a linear quadratic Gaussian controller with very good
performance at 1,000 rpm in both horizontal and vertical directions, with almost ﬂat frequency
response amplitude from 50-250 Hz, and reduction of ﬁrst rotor bending amplitudes at 100 Hz
from 0.4 µm/N to 0.1 µm/N. The controller however suﬀers from an ampliﬁcation of the response
around 25 Hz, believed to be a consequence of the used controller design, with no penalization
of rotor conical modes.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Bearings are some of the most critical components in rotating machinery. They provide the
transfer of axial and radial loads to the supporting structure, while ensuring free rotation around
a ﬁxed axis. A wide variety of bearing designs exists, suiting diﬀerent requirements such as cost,
eﬃciency, and durability. For applications with high loads and rotational speeds, oil ﬂuid ﬁlm
bearings are usually preferred. These bearings utilize a thin layer of oil to separate moving parts.
This means they provide very low friction and potentially almost unlimited life. Unlike rolling
element bearings, oil ﬁlm bearings inherently also provide damping, thus reducing vibrations.
Fluid ﬁlm bearings can be broadly classiﬁed into two types, i.e. hydrodynamic and hydrostatic,
depending on how the pressurized ﬂuid ﬁlm is generated. Hydrostatic bearings are externally
pressurized by a pump, meaning no motion is required. Hydrodynamic bearings on the other
hand rely on the rotation of the journal to force ﬂuid into a converging gap, thus raising the
pressure of the ﬂuid. The most common type of hydrodynamic bearing is the journal bearing.
In the simplest form, a journal bearing consists of a shaft rotating in a slightly larger bore
supplied with ﬂuid. The word journal refers to the part of the shaft which is located inside the
bearing. Fluid ﬁlm bearings can however become unstable due to destabilizing cross-coupling
forces caused by the ﬂuid. More advanced bearing designs can however be utilized to negate
these hydrodynamic instabilities. One of such designs is the tilting-pad journal bearing (TPJB).
These bearings consist of separate pads that are able to pivot, enabling them to adjust their
equilibrium positions. The result of this is a decoupling of the forces acting on the journal,
greatly reducing cross-coupling of the stiﬀness. While providing excellent dynamic stability, this
characteristic of the TPJB depends heavily on the damping of the oil ﬁlm, which reduces with
the rotational speed.
During the last half of the 20th century and up until present day, the world has seen sig-
niﬁcant advances within the ﬁelds of computers and electronics. Many operations previously
performed by purely mechanical devices have either been replaced or at least complimented by
electronic implementations. Consider for example the gasoline combustion engine, which used
to rely on cams, chains, and carburettors to control valve timings, ignition, and fuel delivery.
Over the years these functions have either been replaced by or complemented by electronic ac-
tuation and control. While able to function by purely mechanical means, the implementation
of electronics simply allows for much improved ﬂexibility and eﬃciency. Bearing designs have
also beneﬁted from electronic integration to create ”active” or ”mechatronic” bearings.
For
example the magnetic bearing which relies on a controller to make it stable, while active gas
bearings rely on controllers for their damping mechanism. Oil ﬁlm bearings are also able to
beneﬁt from active control as a way to improve damping at higher rotational speeds to increase
the operational range.

1.2. Previous Work
Regarding active tilting-pad journal bearing (ATPJB), Santos [1, 2] considered an imple-
mentation with hydraulic chambers on the back of the pads. By controlling the pressure in the
chambers, the bearing clearance could actively be modiﬁed, thus changing the bearing dynamic
properties. The design however faced some practical limitations due to the high pressure re-
quired to alter the ﬂuid ﬁlm. A more direct approach to obtain an ATPJB is to directly perturb
the pressure ﬁeld in the bearing, also refereed to as the TPJB with active lubrication. This was
ﬁrst introduced by Santos [1] in 1994. The design combines the hydrodynamic and hydrostatic
eﬀects, by placing a nozzle in the bearing pad radial direction. Pressurized oil can then be
directly injected into the bearing clearance. It is this type of bearing which will be the focus of
this project.
1.2
Previous Work
Since its introduction, TPJBs with active lubrication have been investigated extensively. Santos
and Russo [3] introduced the Modiﬁed Reynolds Equation (MRE), as a method of calculating
the oil ﬁlm pressure with the inclusion of radial oil injection. Reduced dynamics of servovalves
for controlling the active oil injection were also included by means of second order diﬀerential
equations. Dynamics of the pipes from the servovalves to the injectors were neglected, as were
thermal eﬀects and pad ﬂexibility. Nonetheless, the feasibility of modifying the oil ﬁlm pressure
was demonstrated theoretically. At the Technical University of Denmark (DTU), multiple con-
tributors have worked on the theoretical and experimental investigation of TPJB with active
lubrication, based on test rigs at the university. This has resulted in a state-of-the-art program
for modelling ATPJB with radial oil injection. Kristiansen [4] optimized the geometry of the
ATPJB with respect to the bearing damping, which was shown to increase with the injection
pressure. The optimal pad oriﬁce conﬁguration was obtained using four oriﬁces located sym-
metrically around the pivot line and near the axial edges. The optimal oriﬁce diameter was
4 mm. Improvements to the ATPJB model were sought out by Mønsted [5] by including the
pivot support radial and transverse degrees of freedom, based on Hertz contact theory. It was
shown that the pivot support stiﬀness can have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the bearing dynamics.
Kurt [6] performed a theoretical investigation and optimization of the dynamic properties of
the ATPJB. It was concluded that for a 4-pad load-between-pad (LBP) conﬁguration, having
oil injected only through the two bottom pads, with the two top pads operating only hydro-
dynamically, provided the best performance.
In agreement with previous observations, high
injection pressures seemed to give the best vibration suppression. The high injection pressures
would however result in very high power consumption. It was concluded that using the right
injection pressures, it is possible to signiﬁcantly aﬀect the system damping and modeshapes.
In [7] Salazar and Santos experimentally demonstrated that the inclussion of oil injection, even
without feedback, can improve the system lateral dynamic response compared to the passive
TPJB. In agreement with previous investigations, it was also observed that injection through
the bottom pads provides superior performance. Based on a mathematical model of the AT-
PJB with the inclusion of servovalve dynamics, model based linear-quadratic Gaussian (LQG)
controllers were investigated in Salazar and Santos [8] for feedback-controlled lubrication. The
LQG controller was tested experimentally and compared with a TPJB under passive operation,
hybrid operation with constant oil injection, and active operation with PID controller. The
experimental results indicated good performance of both the LQG and PID controllers at rotor
resonance, signiﬁcantly reducing vibrations. The LQG controller however had the undesired
eﬀect of generating a new resonance at lower frequencies. This was attributed to unmodelled

dynamics of the foundation. It was concluded that inclusion of the foundation dynamics into
the mathematical model was required to improve performance of the LQG controller. As an
alternative to experimental modal analysis, operational modal analysis methods were applied to
the ATPJB test rig in [9], as a way of extracting dynamic properties without taking the system
out of service. Inaccurate results were generally obtained, which were also contributed to un-
modelled dynamics of the foundation. For future work on the test rig, the foundation dynamics
had to be included.
As an eﬀort to deal with the unmodelled foundation dynamics, Rasmussen [10] attempted to
include test rig foundation dynamics in the mathematical model, using a ﬁnite element approach
with plate elements. Additionally, bearing pedestals and stiﬀeners were also modelled using
beam and bar elements. Even when ﬁtting the model parameters, the mathematical model with
the plate element foundation did not seem to provide satisfactory results, when validated against
experimental data. In an attempt to improve the foundation model, the plate elements were
replaced by solid 3D elements by Gani [11] and validated against experimental data. Signiﬁcant
improvements were obtained, with good agreements between model and experiments. These
results however required adjustments to the stiﬀness of pedestals and stiﬀeners, reducing them
to non-physical values. It was thus suggested that the modelling of these elements and the
coupling between components should be investigated further, for optimal performance of a model
based controller. Because of the very large size of the foundation model, a reduction method
was utilized. This greatly reduced the system size, thus signiﬁcantly reducing computational
time. One of the latest contributions is by Svensson [12], investigating dissipation of energy
into heating of the oil ﬁlm. The motivation was to determine if the previous assumptions of
isothermal and isoviscous conditions could be justiﬁed.
To do this an energy equation was
solved along the MRE. The conclusion was that the thermal eﬀects had a signiﬁcant eﬀect,
especially at higher rotational speeds. The inclusion of the energy equation however resulted
in various numerical problems. When including the radial oil injection the program would have
diﬃculties with convergence. More work would have to be put into the model to increase its
scope of operation. Another contribution in [12] was the addition of an alternative approach
to determine the bearing dynamic coeﬃcients.
Previously the Allaire Perturbation Method
has been used, which would prove to struggle when including thermal eﬀects. This led to the
implementation of Perturbation Theory, where the bearing dynamic coeﬃcients are determined
from analytic expressions, rather than from numerical diﬀerentiation.
1.3
Project Scope
The main objective of this project is to combine the knowledge and work of the previous con-
tributors, with respect to modelling and control of the ATPJB with radial oil injection. This
means combining the mathematical models for the rotor, bearing, foundation, as well as actua-
tor dynamics, to create a model based controller for the ATPJB. The designed controller is to
be implemented in the overhung rotor ATPJB test rig at DTU and tested experimentally. The
structure of the report can be summarized as:
• Description of ﬂexible rotor active tilting-pad journal bearing test rig at DTU
• Mathematical modelling of Rotor-Bearing-Foundation and actuator system
• Design of controller
3
==PAGE:16==
1.3. Project Scope
• Implementation and test of controller
First a description of the ﬂexible rotor ATPJB test rig is given. The mathematical modelling
of each component will then be covered in relation to the test rig. The rotor model based on
Bernoulli-Euler beam theory used by the previous contributors working with the test rig has
proven to agree well with experimental data. Major work on the rotor model should thus not be
required. Regarding the TPJB, the isothermal model will be used, due to the various problems
encountered in [12] when including the thermal eﬀects. The implementation solves the MRE
with inclusion of radial oil injection as well as radial and transverse pivot degrees of freedom.
The model has been validated on multiple occasions in for example [4, 5, 6] against benchmarks
in the literature, for cases with no oil injection and neglecting the pivot supports. Servovalves
are to be included in the TPJB model such that the dynamic properties with the valves can
be determined. In relation to this the Perturbation Theory method of determining the bearing
dynamic coeﬃcients presented in [12] will also be utilized. Regarding the foundation model, the
model using 3D solid elements in [11] will be used. The non-physical values required for the
pedestals and stiﬀeners are however worrying. An alternative model has been designed by the
author of [11]. The new model replaces the beam and bar elements used for the pedestals and
stiﬀeners with 3D solid elements similar to the foundation. The author however did not have
time to thoroughly implement and test the new model. Work will thus be put into coupling of
the rotor-bearing-foundation using the new foundation model.
With the complete mathematical model assembled, a controller is to be designed and tested
using Simulink as a simulation tool. Finally the designed controller will be implemented on the
test rig, where tests will be conducted to evaluate the performance of the ATPJB with radial
oil injection.

Bearing Test Rig
This chapter seeks to provide an overview of the test rig located at the Technical University
of Denmark, on which the work in this thesis is based on. This includes a description of the
primary components, instrumentation, and data acquisition.
2.1
Overview
The test rig was originally designed by Østergaard [13] in 2001, to study the dynamic behaviour
of a ﬂexible rotor supported by an ATPJB. It has since then seen various modiﬁcations and
been used extensively by numerous contributors at the university. The test rig is comparable to
an industrial overhung compressor and is shown in Fig. 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Flexible rotor ATPJB test rig at the Technical University of Denmark.
5
==PAGE:18==
2.1. Overview
The ﬂexible rotor (1) is supported by the ATPJB (2) at one end, while being supported by a
ball bearing (3) at the other. The ball bearing is mounted in a steel pedestal, while the ATPJB
is located in a steel housing, mounted in an aluminium pedestal. Steel stiﬀeners have been added
to both bearing pedestals to increase horizontal rigidity. Stiﬀening of the ATPJB housing was
done in [14], as the housing dynamics aﬀected the rotor signiﬁcantly in the horizontal direction at
around 100 Hz. The eﬀect of the stiﬀeners was concluded to suppress the vibrations suﬃciently
for the housing to be assumed rigid. An active magnetic bearing (AMB) (4) can be used to
excite the rotor, but has not been utilized during this project. The rotor is driven by an AC
motor (5) with variable frequency drive, through a belt, layshaft (6), and ﬂexible coupling (7).
The coupling transfers torque to the rotor, while ensuring drive motor dynamics are decoupled
from the rotor. At the free end of the rotor after the ATPJB, discs (8) can be mounted to
replicate impellers, making it an overhung conﬁguration. In front of the discs an excitation
bearing (9) is attached. This is simply a ball bearing where the outer race is kept stationary,
such that a electromagnetic shaker (10) can be used to excite a speciﬁc direction while the rotor
is running. The rotor and drive motor are mounted on a cast iron T-slot foundation (11), which
is placed on top of rubber damping mats to decouple the test rig from the ﬂoor.
Two small gearpumps are driven by an electric motor (12) through a belt drive. One pump
(13) supplies the ATPJB with oil at a low pressure for hydrodynamic (passive) operation. The
other pump (14) is used for returning oil from the ATPJB to the reservoir (15). A rotary vane
pump (16) driven by an 11 kW motor (17) supplies the high pressure oil to the servovalves
(18)(19), for hydrostatic (hybrid/active) lubrication of the bearing. A more detailed description
of the hydraulic system is given in section 2.2.
The test rig is equipped with a rotary encoder (a), connected to the layshaft through a ﬂexible
coupling. Along the length of the rotor ﬁve horizontal and ﬁve vertical displacement sensors
(b)(c)(d)(e)(f) are located to measure lateral vibrations of the rotor. The hydraulic system is
equipped with two manometers for the high pressure line (g)(h), as well as a manometer for the
low pressure line (i). Temperature and pressure in the low pressure line is also measured with a
digital probe (j). The combined ﬂow to the servovalves is measured digitally with a ﬂowmeter in
the high pressure line (k). On the reservoir the oil level and temperature can be read manually
(l). Further details on the instrumentation and data acquisition is given in section 2.4.

element bearings of the test rig with their respective speed limits. Even though the drive motor
can run the rotor at 7,000 rpm, the maximum operational speed is limited by the rear pedestal
bearing at 4,800 rpm.

The test rig was originally mounted on a much smaller foundation, but has since then been
replaced with the current large T-slot grey cast iron foundation. It is 3,500 mm long, 1,100 mm
wide, 105 mm tall, and weighs approximately 3,000 kg. The dynamics of the foundation has
caused many problems with experimental work on the test rig. The current rubber damping
mats were added in [9], in an attempt to reduce the inﬂuence of the foundation dynamics.
The eﬀect of this eﬀort changed the dynamics of the foundation from acting like a cantilever
beam constrained at one end and moving freely at the other, to instead act like a plate in free
conditions. This created an even stronger coupling between the rotor and foundation, increasing
the importance of including foundation dynamics in future models.

Both the low and high pressure circuits pull oil from a common reservoir (1). An electric
motor (3) running at constant speed is connected to the high pressure pump (4) and cooling
fan for the heat exchanger (5). The high pressure pump has variable displacement with pump

kept fully open. The other valve is used to control the ﬂow to the ATPJB, with a delivery
pressure of 1-2 bar for the hydrodynamic lubrication, resulting in a ﬂow of about 1 L/min.
2.2.1
Active Tilting-Pad Journal Bearing
Fig. 2.5 shows pictures of the ATPJB used in the test rig, with bearing design parameters listed
in table 2.3. The bearing uses a symmetric 4-pad LBP conﬁguration, which means the pads are
oﬀset 45◦relative to the vertical center line. The pads are made of brass and are able to tilt on
steel pivots. The pad oﬀsets are 0.5, meaning the pivots are placed centrally along the length of
the pads. While this results in a lower load capacity, it also enables the rotor to run identically
in both clockwise and counterclockwise direction. By allowing the pads to tilt they are able to
change their equilibrium positions, increasing stability by reducing cross coupled forces.
Low pressure oil for hydrodynamic lubrication enters the bearing at the top of the housing.
Through internal passageways in the bearing it is injected with four nozzles placed orthogonally
in the bearing. High pressure oil is supplied to the two servovalves mounted externally on the
bearing housing. Each servovalve is connected with two pads placed opposite from each other.
The oil is injected through a single centrally placed oriﬁce in each pad. On each side of the
bearing housing in the axial direction, the injected oil is collected and returned to the reservoir.

The oil used in the hydraulic system is Mobil DTE 22 hydraulic oil, with ISO viscosity grade

then be substructed from future measurements to eliminate noise related to the rotor position.

2.4.2
Run Out Compensation
As previously mentioned the rotary encoder was primarily added to the test rig to perform run
out compensation for the displacement sensors. Run out refers to mechanical and electrical noise
in the sensor readings. The mechanical noise is caused by the machining tolerances of the shaft,
e.g. surface roughness and roundness. The electrical noise is caused by the inhomogeneous
material, aﬀecting the magnetic ﬁeld of the used inductive sensors. In addition to aﬀecting the
displacement sensors, the run out also aﬀects the force transducer. Mechanical run out causes
the excitation bearing to move, which will create tension and compression in the force transducer
as it is connected to the shaker. Fig. 2.14 shows the non-compensated signals from sensor 1 and
2, as well as the force transducer. Considering that the ﬂuid ﬁlm thickness in a hydrodynamic
bearing typically is less than 100 μm, the peak-to-peak ﬂuctuations of around 140 μm in the
displacement signals are indeed very signiﬁcant.

In order to increase the coherence of measured signals, some processing of the raw signals is
usually carried out. Common problems are aliasing, noise, and spectral leakage. Aliasing results
from analogue to digital conversion, where analogue signals are misrepresented by the digital
signal. Aliasing occurs if the sampling rate is too low, which causes high frequency components
to appear as low frequencies when sampled. In general to avoid aliasing the sampling rate should
be two times the highest frequency component. Anti aliasing ﬁlters can also be used.
Real signals will always contain some noise coming from various sources, e.g. electrical noise
from a power supply or mechanical noise from run out as discussed in section 2.4.2. A common
way to reduce noise is by ﬁltering. If the frequencies of interest are known in advance, low- and
high-pass ﬁlters can be utilized to remove frequencies outside the range of interest. To reduce
the eﬀect of random noise and increase the quality of experiments, tests should be repeated
multiple times to obtain an average of the results.
Spectral leakage arises when performing a discrete Fourier transformation on a signal which
is not periodic. This is because signals are sampled over a ﬁnite time and must be cut oﬀat some
point. This means even if the signal is perfectly periodic, it could be cut oﬀmid period making
the sampled signal non-periodic. Erroneous frequencies will then appear in the spectrum of
the Fourier transform, because the discrete Fourier transform assumes the signal to be periodic
22

within its sampled length. The eﬀect of leakage is stronger frequency components can aﬀect and
obscure weaker components. To reduce leakage a mathematical function called a window can
be used. A window function is simply a weighting function which is multiplied onto the signal
to modify its amplitudes. They are often shaped like bells, starting at a value of zero, then
increasing to a value of one, and decreasing back to zero again. As such, by applying a window
the signal is modiﬁed to make it seem periodic. A common window functions is the Hamming
window, which is a single period of a cosine function oﬀset such that it peaks at one and barely
reaches zero. The Hamming window is used throughout this project. It is common to split a
signal into blocks in the time domain and then apply windows to each block separately. This
is illustrated in Fig. 2.19, which shows the raw shaker input force signal as well as the signal
separated into blocks with applied Hamming windows.

Rotor-Bearing-Foundation Model
In this chapter modelling of the rotor, bearings, and foundation to obtain the respective linearised
dynamic matrices is described. Coupling of the components to assemble the global system will
also be covered. Validation of the individual models and the coupled model is performed based
on experimental data.
3.1
Flexible Rotor
The rotor shaft is the primary component of turbomachinery, providing the transfer of torque as
well as attachment points for various components. It also usually contributes the most signiﬁcant
dynamics of turbomachinery.
A drawing of the test rig rotor shaft is shown in Fig. 3.1. For more details, a work drawing
with dimensions is included in Appendix A. To summarize, the rotor has a total length of 1,150
mm, is made of steel, and weighs approximately 49.6 kg. It consists of multiple sections of
varying diameters to accommodate attachments. At its thickest section where the ATPJB is
located, it has a diameter of 99.6 mm. Keyseats and threaded holes are machined into each end
to allow for the attachment of bearings and ﬂexible coupling. On the right side of Fig. 3.1 at
the section with the keyseat, the rotor is threaded externally such that a sleeve pushing against
the discs can be screwed on to fasten the discs.
Figure 3.1: Drawing of rotor from ATPJB test rig.
3.1.1
Modelling
Neglecting machining tolerances, minor features like the keyseats, drilled holes, threads, and the
two thin 5 mm ﬂanges near the center, the rotor is assumed to be completely axissymmetric and
to consist of ten sections. The rotor model will be based on that of [7] using a ﬁnite element
approach based on Euler-Bernoulli beam theory with inclusion of gyroscopic eﬀects, as well as
a similar discretization with 26 two-node elements shown in Fig. 3.2. The ﬁgure also shows the
location of attachments and displacement sensors. The discretization is performed such that
each section is discretized individually in order to preserve the physical dimensions. A node is
also placed at the physical location of the attachments and sensors, which is the reason for the
irregular element lengths of some sections.

3.2
Rigid Discs
When modelling the dynamics of rotor systems, complex geometries of impellers, gears, etc.,
are often simpliﬁed by replacing them with rigid discs. In this project two diﬀerent discs are
considered: a thin 80 mm disc and a wider 100 mm disc. Illustrations of the two discs can be seen
in Fig. 3.7, while more detailed work drawings can be found in Appendix A. With the exception
of the widths, the discs are similar having the same diameters and three grooves cut along the
circumference. The grooves allow for the attachment of small masses to create unbalances. The
center hole of the discs consists of sections of varying diameters, to allow for accommodation of
axial spacer sleeves. A ring is screwed onto a threaded part of the rotor which pushes against
the discs to fasten them axially to the rotor. The discs are made of steel, have outer diameters
of 295 mm, and weigh approximately 37 and 46 kg respectively.

(b) 100 mm width
Figure 3.7: Drawings of discs from ATPJB test rig.
3.2.1
Modelling
Considering only small angles of rotation and assuming the discs are rigid and have no unbal-
ances, they are modelled as lumped masses added to the rotor. This means they only move
with the rotor, sharing the same four DOF of the respective nodes and contribute no additional
stiﬀness. Additionally, axial and torsional deformations are neglected and the discs rotate with
constant angular velocity Ω. As a simpliﬁcation of the disc geometries, the grooves are neglected
and the centre hole diameters assumed to be constant along the width. The discs can then be
described only by their width, inner diameter, and outer diameter, as illustrated in Fig. 3.8.
The ﬁgure also shows the disc coordinates, which are the same as used for the rotor shown in
Fig. 3.3. This means no transformation is required when adding the discs to the rotor.

3.3.4
Model Order Reduction
While the solid 3D elements seem capable of capturing the foundation dynamics, they result in
a very large number of DOF making it computationally demanding. To reduce this complexity,
model order reduction was applied in [11] by use of component mode synthesis (CMS), also known
as dynamic substructuring. CMS allows individual components of a model to be treated and
reduced separately. The submodels can then be combined by their common interface DOF and
analysed together. In this project the foundation, stiﬀeners, and bearing pedestals are reduced
as a single model, which is to be coupled with the bearings. If however multiple combinations
of pedestals or stiﬀeners were to be tested on the foundation, it would have been advantageous
to model all components individually and then combine them later.
To illustrate the concept of CMS, consider the system in Eq. 3.27

3.4
Active Tilting-Pad Journal Bearing
To couple the rotor with the foundation, the active tilting-pad journal bearing (ATPJB) is used
at the rotor overhung end. The ATPJB with radial oil injection is the component which sets
the test rig apart from usual industrial compressors with passive hydrodynamic bearings. The
ATPJB can function as a ”passive” bearing with only low pressure hydrodynamic lubrication,
but is also capable of combining it with high pressure hydrostatic lubrication to improve the
dynamic characteristics.
This can either be by the use of a constant injection pressure to
create ”hybrid” lubrication, or using ”active” lubrication by varying the injection pressure using
feedback control with the injectors as actuators. The use of hydrostatic lubrication brings many
advantages. At start-up or during low speed operation, hydrodynamic lubrication is not possible
due to insuﬃcient rotor speed for maintaining an oil ﬁlm. Hydrostatic lubrication can then be
used to provide the oil ﬁlm such that contact is avoided. At higher rotor speeds the oil injection
can increase the bearing damping, reducing vibrations and increasing the operational range,
illustrated in [6].
The concept of the ATPJB with radial oil injection was proposed by Santos [1].
In [3]
Santos developed a model for the ATPJB with radial oil injection by including an additional
injection term in Reynolds equation, to create the Modiﬁed Reynolds Equation (MRE). The
implementation of the model for the ATPJB used in this project was also originally created
by Santos, but over the years it has seen many improvements by contributors at the Technical
University of Denmark, e.g.
[4, 5, 6].
The latest contribution was by Svensson [12], who
added the energy equation to include heating of the oil, to remove the assumption of isothermal
conditions.
This addition has however been left out of this project, keeping the isothermal
assumption.

Obtaining the dynamic matrices for the ATPJB is more complicated than the other com-
ponents. The linearised dynamic coeﬃcients depend on the journal equilibrium position, which
is initially unknown and as such an iteration based method is utilized. The ﬂowchart in Fig.
3.20 illustrates the procedure. From an initial guess for the journal equilibrium position, pad
tilts, and pad translations, the ﬁlm thickness is ﬁrst calculated. With the ﬁlm thickness known,
the MRE can be solved for the pressure distribution on the pads. Integrating the pressure over
the pads then results in the ﬂuid forces, which should be in balance with the external loads.
Pivot ﬂexibility is also considered and the pivot displacements should also be in balance with the
load. If these balances are not obtained, a new guess for the equilibrium positions is used and
the procedure repeated. When balance is ﬁnally obtained, the linearised dynamic matrices can
be calculated for the bearing equilibrium position. The test rig uses a 4-pad load-between-pad
conﬁguration, with a drawing of a pad shown in Appendix A.

by the stiﬀness multiplied by the corresponding pad displacement. The self adjusting design of
the tilting pads means the pads will adjust such that only radial forces are present. This means
no tangential forces or moments are acting on the pad in equilibrium.
If the ﬂuid forces balance the external load, while the pivot deformation also corresponds
to the external load and the resulting tangential forces and moments are zero, equilibrium is
said to be obtained. If equilibrium is not obtained, a new guess for the equilibrium is used and
calculations repeated.

3.4.7
Determining the Linearised Bearing Dynamic Coeﬃcients
The challenge then is to determine the coeﬃcients of the matrices. The most straightforward
method is to use numerical diﬀerentiation. By introducing a perturbation on each DOF indi-
vidually, the change in the ﬂuid forces from each perturbation can be determined. Dividing the
changes in ﬂuid forces by the respective perturbation sizes then results in the dynamic coeﬃcient
for each DOF. Choosing the perturbation size can be problematic as illustrated in [12]. If the
perturbation is not small enough the coeﬃcients do not capture the linear behaviour, but if it is
too small numerical issues can occur because of the division by the small value. As an alternative
to numerical diﬀerentiation in [12], Perturbation Theory was used as an analytical method of
determining the dynamic coeﬃcients. The equations are modiﬁed to include the perturbations
and can then be solved for the change in ﬂuid ﬁlm force directly.

3.4.8
Modelled Test Rig Coeﬃcients
The TPJB model has previously been validated against data from the literature, most recently
in [6] and [12], with the program providing very good agreements. In [12] a comparison of the
dynamic coeﬃcients obtained with Perturbation Theory and numerical diﬀerentiation was also
performed. The methods provided almost identical results, except at low speeds where it was
believed that the neglected higher order ﬂuid ﬁlm terms in the Perturbation Theory method
could be signiﬁcant.
The previous authors did however not include servovalves in the bearing model and each
pad could therefore be treated individually. With the new coupled pad model, validation was
performed against the uncoupled model by removing the oﬀ-diagonal coupling elements in the
stiﬀness matrix. Both models then provide exactly the same dynamic coeﬃcients. Both numeri-
cal diﬀerentiation and Perturbation Theory has also been implemented for the coupled pads. In
Fig. 3.25 and 3.26 the journal stiﬀness and damping coeﬃcients are shown for increasing rotor
speed, using both methods for determining the dynamic coeﬃcients. The results were obtained
with two discs, i.e. vertical load of 1,440 N, an oil temperature of 60◦C and with the servo-
valves centred. As observed in [12] the two methods deviate slightly at lower rotor speeds. As
mentioned this could be due to the neglected higher order ﬂuid ﬁlm terms for the Perturbation
Theory. However, as the rotor speeds below 1,000 rpm are not of interest, the deviation is not
of concern. Perturbation Theory also seems to be slightly faster than numerical diﬀerentiation
as also observed in [12]. It was decided to use Perturbation Theory for all results throughout
this project.

When designing a control system, it is important to ensure that the control system is able
to aﬀect the desired dynamics of the system. Similarly the control system must also be able
to detect changes to the system for feedback. These properties are called controllability and
observability, with diﬀerent approaches available for evaluation. A classic approach described in
[20] is to calculate the following products

output can be made. If the product ψiTbj = 0 the mode is said to not be controllable. Similarly
if the product ckφi = 0 the mode is not observable. Performing this analysis on the reduced
order real valued continuous time system, it is determined that one mode is uncontrollable for
each input. These modes correspond to the servovalves, which makes sense as one servovalve
cannot aﬀect the other. It was not possible to assess the observability using this method, as
very small values were obtained for all modes.

4.7
Kalman Filter
In real systems both the states and measurements of the states will usually be aﬀected by some
noise, which will cause errors in the states for the controller. In this case it is desirable to ﬁnd
optimal estimates of the system states given the noisy measurements. By optimal it is meant
that some criteria is minimized or maximized. Given that the disturbance on the states v1 and
the measurements noise v2 are both uncorrelated white noise, the Kalman ﬁlter is the optimal
observer.
The basic principle of the Kalman ﬁlter is that it ﬁrst predicts the current states using a
model. When a measurement containing some noise is available, the state estimates are updated
using a weighted average between the initial prediction and the measurement. This average is
based on the observer gain, such that more weight is being given to either the prediction or
measurement, depending on which has the higher certainty.
Although the optimal Kalman ﬁlter is time dependent, a suboptimal constant solution exists.

6
Conclusion
The overall goal of this project was to create a model based controller for an active tilting-pad
journal bearing with radial oil injection, with the objective of reducing lateral vibrations of the
rotor. A mathematical model for the coupled rotor-bearing-foundation system with inclusion
of actuator dynamics was created. The model was for the most part based on models of the
individual components by previous authors working on the same test rig.
The rotor model saw no signiﬁcant changes compared to models of previous authors, as
it has proven to match very well with experimental data.
A large number of changes were
however made to the foundation model. The dimensions and placements of bearing pedestals
were modiﬁed to more accurately match the physical foundation. The initial model also had
the same material parameters for foundation, stiﬀeners, and pedestals, which was changed such
that they could all be set individually.
A convergence study was also performed to ensure
the used mesh was suﬃcient. The ﬁnal foundation model would show good agreements with
experiments. The active tilting-pad journal bearing model also saw signiﬁcant changes. The
addition of the servovalves couple the bearing pads, which means they can no longer be solved
individually, but have to be solved in pairs. With the addition of the servovalves, the model
was also modiﬁed to use injection ﬂows instead of injection pressures for control purposes. The
servovalve dynamics were included by second order diﬀerential equations for the valve ﬂows.
Finally, Perturbation Theory was implemented as a method of obtaining the bearing dynamic
coeﬃcients. The coupled rotor-bearing-foundation model was assembled and compared with
experimental data. The eigenvalues, modeshapes, and frequency responses all show very good
agreements.
With the validated rotor-bearing-foundation model, a model based controller was to be de-
signed. The mathematical model was ﬁrst manipulated in order for it to be implemented on the
test rig controller. This included a model reduction, separation of complex states, and discretiza-
tion from continuous time to discrete time. Only linear quadratic Gaussian controllers were
investigated, combining both the Kalman ﬁlter and linear quadratic regulator. The controllers
were designed using time simulations and then implemented on the test rig for performance
evaluation.
It was possible to design a controller which can reduce rotor lateral vibrations very signiﬁ-
cantly, considering both the horizontal and vertical directions. The resulting frequency response
amplitudes are almost ﬂat for the frequencies from 50-250 Hz, with a reduction at the ﬁrst rotor
bending mode from approximately 0.4 µm/N to 0.1 µm/N. The designed controller would how-
ever seem to cause ampliﬁcations around 25 Hz. The ampliﬁcations are believed to be caused
by the rotor conical modes. The ﬁnal controller was designed without weighting the conical
modes, as it resulted in overall good results at 1,000 rpm. It was however observed that using
the controller with a supply pressure lowered from 100 to 40 bar would result in good balance
of performance across the full frequency range 20-250 Hz.
107
==PAGE:120==
6.1. Future Work
While the same controller performed similarly at 2,000 rpm from 50-250 Hz, the ampliﬁcation
at lower frequencies became even more signiﬁcant. At 3,000 rpm the controller would destabilize
the system, as the dynamics of the system has been seen to change signiﬁcantly at 3,000 rpm.
The controller was also compared to a controller designed with a model without the inclusion of
the foundation. While the inclusion of foundation dynamics does seem to improve the controller
response, the controller without foundation also performs very well. It was however observed
that it would amplify the foundation modes slightly. Unfortunately, because of multiple problems
with the test rig during the project, the ﬁnal results were not obtained until the last week of the
project. This meant there was not enough time to test diﬀerent controllers more thoroughly, to
see if performance could be improved.
6.1
Future Work
In general more work should be put into the controller design. While it was possible to design a
controller which could reduce rotor vibrations signiﬁcantly, the performance at lower frequencies
needs to be improved. If this is not possible by simply tuning the controller, some additional
dynamics might have to be investigated. Similarly the design of controllers for higher rotor speeds
should also be looked into. It would seem that at 3,000 rpm, the dynamics of the system change
signiﬁcantly and more advanced control strategies could be explored, for example using adaptive
control. If higher rotor speeds are to be tested, the mathematical model could be improved by
removing the isothermal and isoviscous assumptions with the addition of the energy equation
to the bearing model. The mathematical model could also be improved by including the sensor
pedestals in the foundation model.
This would allow for a more accurate compensation of
pedestal movements in the rotor displacements.
