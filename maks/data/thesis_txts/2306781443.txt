Abstract
Characterization of heterogeneities and general bioreactor performance are highly valu-
able tools for both academic and industrial development. With recent times concerns
about electrical eﬃciency and emissions perspective have resulted in an interest in al-
ternatives processes.
However, methods for validation and characterization of these
processes are required. In this project a glucose oxidase method has been developed for
better characterization of mass transfer in bioreactors. The performance of the devel-
oped method has been investigated in a newly constructed bubble column at DTU Pilot
plant.
The glucose oxidase method was developed and compared with known methods such as
the hydrogen peroxide. The method was found to be superior on many aspects such
as the simulation of a real aerobic process and additional information of pH. The per-
formed experiments revealed major gradients and that simple assumptions about mixing
between gas and liquid are not applicable. Sophisticated simulations must implemented
to completely characterize and model such a complex process as the bubble column.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Many industrial operations within the ﬁeld of chemical and biochemical engineering
requires mass transfer between liquid and a gas phase.
Chemical processes such as
stripping and absorption are highly depending on either transfer from liquid to gas or
vice versa. Another example of a chemical process which requires mass transfer is the
Fisher-Tropsch coal liquefaction synthesis [1–4]. Within the biochemical ﬁeld, processes
such as fermentation also requires an adequate mass transfer. Especially for aerobic
fermentation processes, oxygen is one of the most important substrates for growth and
formation of products [5, 6]. Before oxygen can be utilized, it must be dissolved into the
liquid phase. However, oxygen has a low solubility in water, which is highly inﬂuenced
by parameters such as temperature, pressure and the concentration of other components
in the liquid. Especially with a high amount of biomass, which usually is desired, the
oxygen demand rate could exceed the supply, resulting in oxygen depletion. At depletion,
the metabolics will drastically decrease, resulting in reduced productivity and anaerobic
conditions [7]. Another biological process is for example the treatment of wastewater. In
nature, the process is carried out without the addition of mechanical energy. This results
in a very limited access to oxygen, reducing the biochemical reaction rate [8]. Thus,
oxygen transfer is often seen as the limiting factor and therefore an utmost important
parameter in design and scale-up of oxygen requiring processes [8–10].

Gas-liquid contactors
A broad range of reactions within the ﬁelds of chemical, petrochemical and biochemical
require multiphase reactors, or gas-liquid contactors, due to a gas to liquid transfer or
vice versa. They operate by dispersing gas into the liquid in the form of bubbles where
oxygen then gets further dissolved [8]. A drawing of some of the common gas-liquid

1.1.1
Stirred tank reactor
To enhance mixing between two phases in an industrial reactor, mechanical agitation is
the most common method [12]. One of these mechanical agitators are the stirred tank
reactor (STR). The stirred tank reactor is widely used within the chemical and biochem-
ical industry for example in production of food, minerals, polymers, pharmaceuticals etc.
[13, 14]. The STR can be operated in diﬀerent conﬁgurations such as batch, fed-batch
or continuous [12].
A STR is a cylindrical vessel typically containing one or more impellers, which is attached
to a shaft in the middle. A schematic design is presented in Figure (1.1b). The volumes
of the vessel can be from a few millimeters to hundreds of cubic meters. With aerated
operations, a sparger is required and the aspect ratio of 3 is common due to the increased
gas residence time within the vessel. To avoid vortex at turbulent ﬂow, baﬄes can be
attached to the wall of the vessel [13].
Furthermore, to accomodate the transfer of
heat either coils can be placed inside the vessel or the vessel can be surrounded by a

1.1.2
Bubble column
Within the chemical and biochemical industry bubble columns are intensively used as
gas-liquid contactors.
An examples of chemical processes where the bubble columns
are used is the the famous Fischer-Tropsch coal liquefaction synthesis. Within the bio-
chemical industry the bubble column has been used for both fermentation and biological
treatment of wastewater [3]. Many other processes where the bubble column has been
applied can be found in the literature [2].
Various types of bubble column designs are developed, but the main purpose of them
all are the contact between a gas and a liquid phase. As the name indicates, bubble
columns are a tall cylindrical vertical vessels with bubbles dispersed inside. For industrial
applications, the aspect ratio is around 5 [3]. A simple bubble column is shown in Figure
(1.1a), but with a liquid ﬂow from the bottom. The gas is usually dispersed at the bottom
by a sparger and bubbles are going through the liquid to the surface [18]. However,
other designs have been investigated such as Downﬂow bubble columns where the gas
and liquid are dispersed from the top. Bubble columns can be operated in fed-batch,
with only a ﬂow of gas, or in continuous with a ﬂow of liquid. In respect to the gas ﬂow,
the liquid can either ﬂow co- or counter current according to the bubble column design
[2]. If solid particles are present in the liquid phase, it is usually referred to as a slurry
bubble column [3].
In a bubble column, liquid and gas are mixed without any need of additional mechanical
stirring [4]. This is an essential advantages compared to other gas-liquid contactors such
as the stirred tank. Stirred tank reactors have moving parts, which must be maintained
and requires mechanical energy. Furthermore, in fermentations where sterile environment
are crucial, the cleaning procedure must take the shaft and impellers into account [19].
This is not the case for a simple bubble column, which only has a sparger at the bottom.
Since the agitation is performed by the gas ﬂow rates, the aeration is coupled within.
This coupling complicates the control of aeration if the same agitation is to be maintained
[19]. Alternative methods such as controlled air compositions can be used in order to
alternate the aeration. However, the equipment and operation for this kind of procedure
is is costly [3, 6].
Many other advantages are claimed in the literature, such as little maintenance, low

1.1.3
Gas dispersion
For both stirred tank reactors and bubble columns, the sparger is an important feature.
The sparger decides the size and the distribution of the bubbles. The smaller the bubbles
the bigger the surface area for mass transfer. Especially for the bubble column, the
sparger is a major concern at low aspect ratio. This is due to no mechanical distribution
as for the STR by impellers [20]. Even thought there is no mechanical stirring, sparging
with an injector results in a high pressure drop at the sparger nozzle, which is a high
energy consumption [2]. A sparger can be designed in many varieties and some of the
commercial used are for example rings or perforated sieves or pipes. Even the same
sparger type can have diﬀerent properties such as the amount of holes, diameter of these
holes and their orientation [4, 21].
Injector
Injectors, also referred to as ejectors, are two-phase co-current nozzles. Both liquid and
gas are mixed and dispersed into the liquid bulk simultaneous as a liquid jet [22]. A
simple design of a bubble column with an injector is shown in Figure (1.1c). Liquid
and gas comes from the bottom and gets dispersed as a jet up in the column. A cross
section of the injector is presented in Figure (1.3), where the diﬀerent inlets and outlets
are shown. Liquid is pumped through at the bottom and mixed with the gas introduced
from the side. The gas-liquid mixture is dispersed out of the chamber, resulting in a
free jet. The free jet is a result of the kinetic energy of the liquid, that makes the gas
throughput disperses into very small bubbles [23, 24].
Within this cloud of bubbles mass transfer occurs [25]. The injector generates very small
bubbles of the dispersed phase. This results in a higher mass transfer due to improved
contact between the two phases [26]. Even though the bubbles comes out in very small
sizes, coalescence can be a problem. For the STR, impellers can break up the bubbles
dispersed by the sparger, this is not the case for a bubble column with the absence of
impellers [2].

expected. From an operating cost point of view, the pressure drop must be as low as

2.1
Oxygen mass transfer
Before going into details about methods for determining the volumetric mass transfer
coeﬃcient, the basics of mass transfer will brieﬂy be described. As mentioned previously,
oxygen mass transfer from one phase to another is an important issue within many
industries. The oxygen mass balance of a well mixed vessel can be written as Equation
(2.1), whereas the accumulated oxygen concentration dissolved (dc/dt) in the liquid phase
is equal to the diﬀerence between the Oxygen Transfer Rate (OTR) and the Oxygen
Uptake Rate (OUR) [28, 29].
dc
dt = OTR −OUR
(2.1)
The OTR characterizes the rate of oxygen transfer from one phase to another, whereas
the OUR represents the uptake or utilization of dissolved oxygen. Both parameters are
aﬀected by many factors, including the hydrodynamics [29].
When a chemical reaction or microorganism requires oxygen, it utilizes the oxygen dis-
solved in the liquid. Therefore, in the oxygen mass balance, the OUR has a negative
contribution to the accumulated oxygen concentration. For chemical reactions the OUR
is expressed as the reaction rate, whereas for microorganisms, the speciﬁc uptake rate
and concentration of biomass determines the OUR. Especially since the concentration
of dissolved oxygen is a crucial parameter for the microorganisms growth, maintenance
and formation of products, oxygen must be supplied continuously. During exponential
growth, the OUR increases and more oxygen is therefore required [5]. Thus, suﬃcient
oxygen transfer is essential for maintaining the demand of oxygen uptake rate for the
microorganism or chemical reaction.

2.1.1
Oxygen transfer rate
The transfer mechanisms from gas to liquid can be divided into several steps. A gas is
dispersed into the liquid, where oxygen must transport to the gas ﬁlm layer. From here,
oxygen can diﬀuse through the gas-liquid interface and into the liquid ﬁlm surrounding

2.2
Hydrodynamics
The bubble column can be used within a wide range of applications as described in
Section (1.1.2). However, in order to predict design parameters such as the mass transfer
coeﬃcient, the complex hydrodynamic of a bubble column must be understood [1, 31,
32]. The hydrodynamics generally depend on the gas-liquid physical properties such as
viscosity and density but also the geometry and conﬁguration of the bubble column for
example properties of the sparger [33].

2.2.3
Flow regime
In a simple bubble column, the ﬂow pattern is very much determined by the gas rising
from the sparger. However, as described in Section (1.1.2), modiﬁcations such as liquid
ﬂow can change the ﬂow patterns in the column. The motions of the ﬂuid can be catego-
rized in diﬀerent ﬂow regimes, which depends on the superﬁcial gas velocity, properties
of the liquid phase and the column design [31]. Two of the most common ﬂow regimes
are homogeneous and heterogeneous.
• Homogeneous ﬂow regime: When operating at a relatively low superﬁcial gas
velocity, a homogeneously distribution and size of bubbles are observed [3]. The
mixing is relatively gentle over the entire cross sectional area of the column. This
regime is also referred to as bubbly ﬂow [18]. Within this ﬂow regime, the gas
holdup tends to follow the increase in superﬁcial gas velocity proportional [3, 31,
35].
Page 11 of 118
==PAGE:26==

• Heterogeneous ﬂow regime: With increased gas velocity, the homogeneous
ﬂow can no longer be maintained. Unsteady ﬂow patterns and channeling of larger
bubbles will then occur, altering the hydrodynamic dramatically [3, 35, 36]. Fur-
thermore, due to intense breakup and coalescence the bubble size distribution is
no longer uniform [18]. In this regime, circulations of the ﬂuid tend to go up-
ward in the center and downward near the wall. This regime is also referred to as
Churn-turbulent ﬂow [3].
Within the heterogeneous ﬂow regime, high gas velocity and small column diameter can
promote bubbles to start stabilizing on the wall. These bubbles are deﬁned as bubble
slugs or slugging ﬂow [2]. Bubble slugs can form into large pockets of air that rises up
as plugs throughout the column [3]. Therefore, slugging ﬂow is generally avoided due to
relatively low surface area resulting in a reduced volumetric mass transfer [19]. Other
classiﬁcations of diﬀerent ﬂow phenomena can be found in the literature, but only these
three are elaborated.
As described, the two diﬀerent ﬂow regimes have very diﬀerent ﬂow patterns and there-
fore inﬂuence the hydrodynamic signiﬁcantly [3, 37].
Especially parameters such as
mixing and mass transfer are sensitive to the change of ﬂow regime [18]. Thus, during
design and scale up of a bubble column, it is important to maintain a constant ﬂow
regime [2, 33, 36].
The exact transition between these regimes is very dependent on many other factors

and purity. To identify the operation area, several ﬂow regime maps has been presented

2.2.4
Bubble dynamics
Understanding about the bubble dynamics is important, to achieve the desired operation
conditions. The bubble size is an utmost important parameter regarding the performance
of a bubble column, since it determines many factors such as the rising velocity as gas
residence time, the gas holdup and interfacial area [37]. With a decreased bubble size,
the surface area of the bubbles increases. Furthermore, smaller bubble rises slower to the
surface, which prolongs the residence time and gas holdup [28, 39]. Since, the volumetric
mass transfer coeﬃcient is depending on the gas-liquid interfacial area, a decrease in
bubble size will naturally increase kLa.
Since the size of the bubbles determines many factors, especially the mass transfer coef-
ﬁcient, knowledge about the mechanisms or bubble breakup and coalescing is important
[40]. The bubble size is dependent on a balance between coalescing and bubble breakup.
The formation of bubbles is determined by the gas ﬂow, sparged into the liquid phase,
where it gets further distributed [34]. Many process variables of the bubble column,
as described in Section (1.1.2), assist the bubble distribution by for example multiphase
injectors. Fluid stress, by turbulent ﬂow ﬁelds in the liquid may lead to bubble break-up,
whereas collision between bubbles can result in coalescence [40]. Many other parame-
ters can have an eﬀect on the bubble coalescence such as organic species, surfactants or
electrolytes [37, 40].
In industrial scale operations the presence of inorganic or organic substance are not
unexpected. Especially within the biotechnology area such as fermentors, bioreactors and
waste water technologies. These salts or electrolytes are altering the physical properties
by repelling the gas liquid interphase and increasing the surface tension [41].
This
increase in surface tension results in a less coalescing system and reduces the size of
formed bubbles [39, 42]. Therefore, the presence of electrolytes is an important parameter
for the mass transfer. The transformation from coalescence to a non coalescing system
has been found to be a sharp transition [43].

Viscosity
For many biochemical processes, for example during a fermentation or processes with
high amount of solids, the viscosity can increase signiﬁcantly and have an impact on the
bubble behavior [33]. With increased viscosity bubble coalescing intensiﬁes and large
bubbles can occur near the wall in tall columns as slug ﬂow. Even with low gas velocity,
these bubble slugs can form due to the increased viscosity [2]. These bubbles rises faster
to the liquid surface and lower the retention time and gas holdup. It has been shown
that the operation area for homogeneously ﬂow regime decreases with an increase in
viscosity, due to the enhanced formation of large bubbles and channeling [19, 33].
When determing the volumetric mass transfer coeﬃcient, the conditions must simulate
the real operating conditions due to changes in hydrodynamics which inﬂuences the
mass transfer. To simulate viscous liquids, many compounds can be added for example

2.3
Methods for determination of kLa
When choosing a method for experimental determination of the volumetric oxygen trans-
fer coeﬃcient several factors needs to be taken into account. The method needs to sim-
ulate the real process as much as possible. Addition of compounds to the media can
inﬂuence the physical properties resulting in errors in the determination. Many diﬀerent
techniques for measuring the oxygen transfer have been developed such as Gas Anal-

are based on measurements of either exhaust gas and dissolved oxygen or dissolved oxy-
gen alone, respectively. The sulphite and hydrogen peroxide methods are based on a
chemical reaction simulating the oxygen mass transfer [9, 28].

Model description
In order to describe and understand the enzymatic processes in the bubble column,
compartment models of the setup are constructed. These models will be used to predict
and be compared with experimental data obtained in the bubble column. The model is
based on a simple mass balance of the system as shown in Equation (5.1).
In + Produced = Out + Accumulated + Consumed
(5.1)
The model is going to be used at steady state, therefore the accumulated term is set to
zero. The terms produced and consumed are depending on the enzymatic reaction rate.
Therefore, if the reaction is consuming, the produced terms is zero and vica versa. With
compartments, the terms in and out is depending on the ﬂow between these compart-
ments. Thus, based on this simple mass balance, the models are constructed.
5.1
Two-compartment model
By visual inspection and as seen in Figure (5.1), the bubble column can be assumed to
be divided into two compartments; top and bottom.

To simulate the compartment model, assumptions such as ﬂow pattern must be stated.
During operation, it is assumed that the incoming ﬂow goes through the injector and
only into the top compartment. An equal amount of liquid is transported to the bottom
and same amount is leaving the bottom by the recirculation.
One could argue that
the compartments could be divided into an inner and outer section, where plug ﬂow
could be assumed. However, for simplicity, both compartments are assumed to be well-
mixed. The feed is introduced to the top compartment, due to the addition before the
injector. The increase in volume, due to the feeding, is neglected within the model. For
simpliﬁcation of the model, mass transfer is only assumed to appear in the top, as no
bubbles are visible in the bottom compartment. The enzymatic reaction is occurring
in both compartments, but with regards to the diﬀerent substrate concentrations. The
model will then simulate the change in both substrate and dissolved oxygen for both
compartments by mass balances.

Discussion
In this section, the results obtained during the experiment will be further elaborated and
discussed. Furthermore, during the experiments, many aspects of the determination of
the volumetric mass transfer coeﬃcient were observed and will also be discussed.
7.1
Glucose oxidase as a method
During the development of the glucose oxidase method, many advantageous were ob-
served compared to other methods. With consumption of oxygen, no highly supersat-
urated levels of dissolved oxygen were present as for the hydrogen peroxide method.
Furthermore, limitations were found with the dynamic method which was not a prob-
lem with the GOX method.
During the experiments, the feed was controlled by a
PI-controller regulating based on the measured pH. The pH was kept relatively close to
the set point during all glucose oxidase experiments. There has been some deviation
from the set point, but it has been corrected by the controller. Even with the two nozzle
non-coalescing xanthan system, where the measured pH were ﬂuctuating, the controller
kept the pH within a reasonable range.
This extra information about the system is
a major advantages and not available to get with for example the hydrogen peroxide
method. Furthermore, the glucose oxidase simulates a real uptake of oxygen and there
is no increase in temperature during the experiments. Thus, it is clear that the glucose
oxidase method is superior to use compared with the other methods tested. However,
throughout the development, some limitations and disadvantages were found.
When determing the volumetric mass transfer coeﬃcient, a signiﬁcant change from the
saturated dissolved oxygen concentration is desired, to ensure oxygen limitation. With
the relatively low glucose oxidase reaction rate, this can become challenging with pro-
Page 77 of 118
==PAGE:92==

Chapter 7. Discussion
cesses having a high mass transfer. This problem was encountered many times during
the glucose oxidase experiments, especially with non-coalescing water system. With non-
coalescing xanthan system, the top probe started to decrease slightly indicating lower
mass transfer, if the feed is constant (indicating constant reaction rate). However, when
increasing the liquid and gas ﬂow rates, the dissolved oxygen concentration in the top
reaches the saturated point again. With a top measurement close to the saturated dis-
solved oxygen concentration, the system could be assumed to be enzymatic reaction rate
limited and not mass transfer limited. Therefore, the kLa could be underestimated due
to the limitation of the enzyme reaction.
Several factors can be changed to enhance the utilization of dissolved oxygen. Since the
enzymatic reaction rate is depending on the amount of substrate, an increase in glucose
could increase the reaction rate. However, the increase is not signiﬁcant compared to the
additional amount of glucose required [62]. The amount of enzyme could be increased
to enhance the reaction rate. This approach has been investigated and the result is
presented in Appendix (H). As shown, an increase in enzyme did decrease the dissolved
oxygen concentration in the top to a reasonable level, indicating oxygen limitations.
However, with an increased reaction rate, the pH drops drastically and the PI-controller
must be able to encounter that. This was not the case as showed in Appendix (H). The
pH dropped drastically and the controller could not feed as much as required, even with
a feeding rate at 50 L/h. The feeding concentration could be increased, however the
strong base could increase a possible pH gradient through the system.
One of the disadvantages compared to for example the hydrogen peroxide method is the
required initial glucose concentration. Especially when going up in scale, even a 0.1 M
solution requires a large amount of glucose. Without the initial glucose concentration,
the enzymatic reaction rate would be very low, which already is a limitation to the
method.
7.1.1
Oxygen limitation
To determine the volumetric mass transfer coeﬃcient, the limiting factor should be
the oxygen transfer rate. Otherwise, as discussed above, the estimated kLa could be
inﬂuenced by other limitations such as the reaction occurring. During the non-coalescing
water systems almost all experiments resulted in a top measurement near saturated level,
as presented in Table (6.1) and (6.2).
This could indicate that all the mas transfer
has occurred and the reaction at the top is lower than the mass transfer. The same
occurred when the non-coalescing xanthan experiments were operated at high gas or

liquid ﬂow rates.
This could indicate that the system were not oxygen limited but
enzymatic reaction rate limited.
It is not only the glucose oxidase method that requires a suﬃciently low dissolved oxygen
concentration to estimate kLa properly. The same goes for other methods such as the
hydrogen peroxide method. During the hydrogen peroxide method experiments, suﬃ-
cient feeding was investigated to ensure suﬃcient change in the dissolved oxygen probe
in the top. A percentages of 115 %DO was achieved which is relatively ﬁne. However, to
achieve this, the dissolved oxygen concentration reached 240 %DO in the bottom. This
highly supersaturated dissolved oxygen concentration could induce spontaneously bub-
ble formations, enhancing the mass transfer. Thus, no matter what method used for the
determination of kLa, the dissolved oxygen concentrations change during the experiment
is a signiﬁcant parameter.
The measured dissolved oxygen concentration is important since it ultimately determines
the driving force used in the estimation of kLa. However, there is many aspects in the
determination of the dissolved oxygen concentration in the whole system. Especially
with the gradients observed during the experiments.
7.2
Dissolved oxygen concentration
No matter what method is used for estimating the volumetric mass transfer coeﬃcient,
knowledge about the dissolved oxygen concentration is required. The measurement can
easily be performed by a dissolved oxygen sensor inside the reactor. However, if only one
probe is present and the reactor goes up in size, the assumption about perfectly mixed
systems might not be true. If the system is not well mixed, gradients occurs, which
can result in wrong estimations of kLa, when only using one probe. In the ﬁrst test
experiments only one dissolved oxygen sensor was available and placed in the bottom
of the bubble column.
The estimated volumetric mass transfer coeﬃcients based on
those experiments were signiﬁcantly low, even with maximum capacity on both ﬂows.
By visual inspection it could indicate that all the mass transfer occurred in the top and
therefore a gradient could be present in the system. One could for simplicity assume that
at the top of the bubble column, the dissolved oxygen concentration was near saturation
level. However, especially with increased viscosity, as seen in Figure (6.3), where the
mass transfer decreases or with increased reaction rate, the assumption about saturated
dissolved oxygen in the top would probably be incorrect. An extra dissolved oxygen
sensor was borrowed and revealed that there was a signiﬁcant gradient in the column.

7.3
Modeling of experimental results
As previously presented in the results section (6.3), the model had diﬃculties simulating
the experimentally obtained data for non-coalescing water systems and non-coalescing
xanthan systems with high ﬂow rates. This could be due to complete mass transfer
before the top, however assumptions in the model could also describe the mismatch in
simulating the experiment.
One of the assumptions in the model is a constant enzymatic reaction rate.
If the
reaction rate is lower than used in the model, the dissolved oxygen concentration would
be higher. During the dispersion from the nozzle, the ﬂuid get exposed to high shear rate
through the nozzles. However, hydrodynamic shear is rarely causing damages or loss to
the enzyme [64]. Another cause to loss in enzyme activity could be the temperature or
pH. During the experiments, the temperature has been stable within a degrees Celsius,
but the pH has been ﬂuctuating.
Especially based on the results from the multiple
pH sensor, Figure (7.2), the pH has been around 8 during some experiments. As seen
from the enzyme initial reaction rates dependence on pH, see Appendix (C), there is
a signiﬁcant decrease in initial reaction rate at pH below 7. The lowered enzymatic
reaction rate was tested in the model and the simulations were much better to predict
the experimental dissolved oxygen measurements.
Another assumption is the ﬂows through the compartments. It is assumed that all the
leaving liquid from the bottom compartment is only leaving through the recirculation
loop. No liquid is therefore leaving the bottom compartment at the interface with top
compartment, only entering. The division of the compartments are performed based
on visual inspection of the present of bubbles. Even though there is no bubbles mov-
ing, liquid could go back and forth the compartments. With increased ﬂow through the
compartments, the dissolved oxygen concentration would be more uniform. The liquid
ﬂow was slightly increased in the model for the non-coalescing xanthan experiment at
(M/M) conditions. By during this, the model start to ﬁt the experimental data signif-
icantly better. However, the actual value of liquid ﬂow through the compartments are
only guesses and a more advantages simulations, such as CFD, must be performed to
get knowledge about the ﬂows.

Further work
This project has focused on the development of a new method for determination of the
volumetric mass transfer in a newly constructed bubble column at DTU. During the
project a number of diﬀerent improvements to the method and equipment have been
observed.
8.1
Glucose oxidase method
The experiment with the glucose oxidase method performed in the bubble column could
indicate that the system was not oxygen limited but perhaps more enzymatic reaction
rate limited. This means that the system is so good at transferring oxygen
compared to the enzymes reaction rate. Many changes or improvements has been
discussed and investigated. A technique not investigated is by altering the air, which
gets sparged into the system.
By reducing the oxygen concentration in the air, the
transfer gets reduced. However, the equipment is not suitable for such operations and
modiﬁcations must be performed.
8.1.1
Enzyme kinetics
During this project, the enzyme was only characterized at diﬀerent pH, with same amount
of glucose and dissolved oxygen. A more thorough investigation of the kinetics at diﬀerent
pH would be helpful for understanding the processes in the bubble column. Furthermore,
no experiments in the stability of the enzyme at these conditions have been performed.

Nozzle
In the beginning of this project, only a one nozzle conﬁguration was available. Halfway

Multiple sensors
As a part of the project, a way to have sensors inside the bubble column needed to
be designed. In collaboration with the workshop, the solution was found to be a wire,
having adjustable sensor inlets along the length. With the constructed wire, there is
the possibility of having multiple sensors along the height of the column. The wire was
installed just a few weeks before the deadline and only a few experiments were performed
having extra sensors in the bubble column. For further characterization of the bubble
column, the wire must be used to get knowledge about the large gradients found during
the experiments.
8.3
Compartment modeling
The constructed compartment model showed some diﬃculties simulating the experimen-
tal obtained values. The assumptions in the model have been discussed and could be
investigated for optimization of the model. For a better understanding about the ﬂows
between the compartments, a more sophisticated simulation must be used such as a CFD
simulation. Furthermore, with the possibility of multiple sensors inside the bubble col-
umn, dissolved oxygen probes can be attached and the gradient of dissolved oxygen can
be mapped. This can be used for the compartment model and even extend the number
of compartments.

Chapter 9
Conclusion
The aim of this project was to use the oxidation of glucose into gluconic acid as a method
for mass transfer characterization of a bioreactor. The developed method was to be used
on a newly constructed bubble column at DTU Pilot plant. The bubble column was made
operational and a speciﬁc operating window was found for the one nozzle conﬁguration.
By a factorial design of experiments, with operational limitations, the pressure drop was
characterized by the use of Eulers number with diﬀerent viscosities and aspect ratios.
With many adjustments and failed attempts, a glucose oxidase method was developed.
All the parameters such as enzyme amount, initial glucose concentration and feeding
concentrations were evaluated and determined. The technique of having a controlled
feeding system based on the measured pH was found to work properly, since the pH was
kept relatively constant throughout all the experiments. By external HPLC analysis of
samples taken during the experiments, it was found that the system was able to maintain
a steady concentration of glucose.
With the newly developed glucose oxidase method, volumetric mass transfer coeﬃcients
of the bubble column were investigated. It was found, especially for the non-coalescing
water system, that the mass transfer was complete or close to saturation level in the top
part of the column, even with low ﬂow conditions. This indicates that the system could be
limited by other parameters such as the enzymatic reaction rate, resulting in an incorrect
estimation of kLa. In viscous systems, the mass transfer should be decreased, which were
also observed during the experiments. However, increased ﬂow rates enhanced the mass
transfer and resulted in a nearly complete transfer in the top, as for the non-coalescing
water system.
Based on the experiments performed on the bubble column, simple assumptions about
mixing between the gas and liquid phases are not applicable. Visually there were no

column. Furthermore, the non-negligible observed gradients could be characterized with
multiple sensors inside the bubble column.

Appendix B
Non-coalescing system
To ensure conditions similar to fermentation during experiments, salts were added to
the bubble column, thus the system became non-coalescing. Sodium sulphate was added
step wise to see the eﬀect during operation of the bubble column. These concentrations
of salt were chosen both below and above the transition state concentrations stated in
Lessard et al. All experiments were operated at the same ﬂow conditions and due to the
transparent column, the eﬀect can easily be seen in Figure (B.1).
Figure B.1: Non-coalescence experiment with diﬀerent concentrations of sodium sulphate all
at same operating conditions (From left: 5 g/L, 7.5 g/L, 10 g/L, 12.5 g/L and 15 g/L).
From the experiment, a clear visual of transition occurs at between 7.5 and 12.5 g/L of
sodium sulphate. According to Lessard et al., the transition state occurs at 8.66 g/L
(0.061 M). This corresponds to what is seen in this experiment, that between 7.5 and
Page 99 of 118

10 g/L the bubble size changes. Between 10 and 12.5 g/L a clear visual of the non-
coalescence shows and bubble size decreases [43].
Other salts can be used for non-coalescing systems, for example magnesium sulphate with
a transition concentration at only 0.032 M. This is almost half the concentration needed
with sodium sulphate. Thus, due to the cost and availability, magnesium sulphate were
chosen to be used for further experiment. A similar test was performed with magnesium
sulphate to ﬁnd the optimal concentration.

and presented in Figure (C.2).

Dynamic method
As for comparison of the diﬀerent methods in the bubble column, the dynamic method
has been used. The bubble column were operated at a liquid ﬂow of 100 L/min and a gas
ﬂow rate of 400 L/min, in a non-coalescing system. For deoxygenate the liquid, glucose
oxidase were added together with a small amount of glucose (10 mM). No aeration, only
liquid recirculation was turned on during deoxygenating. When the dissolved oxygen
reached 0 % and has been stable for a certain amount of time, the air was turned
on again.
The dissolved oxygen concentration were measured at the bottom by the
internal oxygen electrode and in the top by an external probe. The dynamic method for
determination of kLa were performed in triplicates and the results are shown in Table

Appendix H
Increased enzyme
Based on the preveieusly experiment with non-coalescing water system, it could indicate
that the system was limited by the enzymatic reaction rate. To investigate this, addi-
tional amount of enzyme was added systematic to see the inﬂuence of more enzyme. The
experiment was performed with one nozzle conﬁguration at (M/M) ﬂow conditions in
non-coalescing water. The experiment proﬁle is presented in Figure (H.1).
Figure H.1: Glucose oxidase experiment with increased enzyme amount. Experiment was
performed with one nozzle conﬁguration in non-coalescing system with a ﬂow condition of
(M/M). Additional enzyme were added at 22 min and 42 min. The upper dashed and lower
dashed lines represents the dissolved oxygen concentration and pH set point, respectively. The
experiment was performed at a temperature of 24.5◦C.
Page 117 of 118
==PAGE:132==
Appendix H. Increased enzyme
From the beginning until 20 min, the same amount of enzyme was added as in the
GOX experiments. After 20 min, additional 32.5 g was added and the top probe starts
to have a lower measurement at steady state. The bottom probe goes down as well,
but it seems that the pH controller is not working properly. The pH decreases drastic
and with additional of 32.5 g enzyme again, the controller can no longer maintain the
pH near 7. As a result of the increased amount of enzyme, the drop en pH makes the
feeding rate go up. At 48 min, the feeding tank needed to be changed, as seen on the
graph. At the end a feeding rate of almost 50 L/h, was observed. With a total of almost
100 g of enzyme, the top probe has reached a signiﬁcantly lowered dissolved oxygen
concentration. However, with signiﬁcantly change from pH 7, the experiment can not
be used for any determination of kLa.
Thus, this experiment shows, that with enough enzyme, the enzymatic reaction rate can
overcome the mass transfer rate in the system. However, the controller can no longer
maintain a pH near the set point.
