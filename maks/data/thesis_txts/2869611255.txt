Abstract
Climate change poses a significant global challenge, with rising greenhouse gas emissions driving tempera-
ture increases and intensifying extreme weather events. Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) has emerged
as a critical mitigation strategy, particularly for hard-to-abate sectors such as steel and cement. Denmark,
aiming for a 70 % reduction in emissions by 2030 and net-zero status by 2050, has identified CCS as a key
component of its transition. This work evaluates the performance of CO2 capture using aqueous 30 wt%
monoethanolamine (MEA) solvent with 7.5 wt % monoethylene glycol (MEG) as a vapour reduction additive
(VRA) at a pilot-scale Waste-to-Energy (WtE) facility using steady state analysis. The focus is on identifying
an optimal operating point and effective pilot operation while ensuring accurate measurements and robust
modelling of derived parameters and balances.
A systematic data processing workflow was implemented to ensure high-quality steady-state analysis. This
included segmentation of experimental periods, qualitative steady-state identification, and mass balance val-
idation to verify data reliability. The CO2 mass balance was assessed using multiple independent estimation
methods, ensuring process accuracy and robustness. The heat loss of the system was estimated and excluded
from the reboiler duty in the analysis for increased comparability to literature. This resulted in the iden-
tification of a minimum specific reboiler duty (SRD) of 3.53 MJ/kgCO2, which was achieved at a capture
efficiency of 89.7 % with a stripping intensity of 271 kJ/kg (rich solvent flow of 220 kg/h, reboiler duty of
16.6 kW).
Through operational analysis of the pilot, an optimal range of stripping intensities of 240 to 320 kJ/kg was
identified and validated in an extensive inefficiency analysis. Specifically, operational inefficiencies in the main
heat exchanger (MHX) and condenser were estimated and correlated to largely explain increases in required
SRDs at extreme stripping intensities beyond the optimal range. Stripper and absorber temperature profile
analyses identified optimal column utilisation, highlighting the trade-offs between energy efficiency, process
stability and pilot flexibility. Additionally, solvent loading behaviour was examined to maximise cyclic ca-
pacity and correlated to the identified inefficiencies. Furthermore, complete CO2 capture was investigated as
an example of inefficient and disproportionate pilot operation, which concluded that it should be avoided as
a clear limiting factor to the analytical insights of the resulting steady states.
This research contributes to the exploration of the potential in VRAs by identifying an optimal SRD value
for subsequent studies. It advances pilot-scale carbon capture optimisation and optimal operation through a
comprehensive analysis of inefficiencies. Furthermore, it contributes to creating a strong foundation for cost-
effective industrial implementation and reinforcing CCS as a viable solution in mitigating climate change.

Problem statement
Carbon Capture and Storage (CCS) is a growing field of research with the purpose of significantly reducing
CO2 emissions and potentially creating negative emissions. The core capture technology development is in-
tended for small, medium, and large industries. Here, CO2 is extracted from exhaust gas (e.g. combustion),
industrial gas (biogas, natural gas, syngas etc.) or an air type system (direct air capture, fermentation).
Following capture, the CO2 is transported to potentially be utilised in products (e.g. e-fuels) or stored per-
manently underground e.g. in depleted gas reservoirs or aquifers.
DTU Chemical Engineering, AT-CERE, took part in a project of designing, commissioning and operating
a mobile testing unit (MTU) to demonstrate carbon capture at the Waste-to-Energy (WtE) plant Amager
Bakke (ARC) at a scale of 1 tCO2/day in 2021 and 2022. The project aimed to reduce the high energy
requirements to capture the CO2 generally corresponding to approx. 25% of the lower heat value in the
waste. This was done by focusing on energy optimisations of every step of the carbon capture process and
to integrate it into the district heating production at ARC, to achieve the cheapest possible CO2 capture.
The main outcome of the project was the construction of a larger 4 tCO2/day demonstration carbon cap-
ture plant, integrated with the waste-to-energy plant and optimised for net zero energy consumption. The
demonstration plant was commissioned in 2023.
The MTU ARC project was based on conventional amine scrubbing technology. In amine scrubbing, solvent
absorbs the CO2 in the flue gas, usually resulting in 95 % reduction. The CO2 is desorbed in another column
by raising the temperature, which also results in the regeneration of the solvent to repeat the cycle. During
the project, various solvents were tested, including the potential in Vapour Reduction Additives (VRAs)
aimed at reducing the energy intensive regeneration process. One such solvent was 30 wt% monoethanol
amine (MEA) solvent with monoethylene glycol (MEG) as a VRA.
Your task will be to analyse the data obtained through experimental campaigns using 30 wt% MEA solvent
with MEG as a VRA to improve performance and lower energy consumption. In the work, you will carry
out mass and energy balance calculations, and thoroughly examine the performance by identifying optimal
operating points, evaluating limiting factors and considering potential impacts for larger scale or prolonged
operation.

Introduction
Climate change is a pressing global issue, with human activities thus far causing global surface temperatures
to rise by 1.1°C between 2011 and 2020 compared to pre-industrial levels as illustrated in Figure 1.1. This
is established by the International Panel on Climate Change (IPCC), organised under the United Nations
(UN), which recently released its AR6 Synthesis Report [1]. The 2023 report warns that this warming trend
has already intensified weather extremes, impacting 3.3 to 3.6 billion people. If current emission trajectories
continue, global temperatures could rise by 2.8 [2.1-3.4] °C by 2100, leading to significant threats to ecosystems
and human well-being, as illustrated from the intermediate emissions scenario from the IPCC report in Figure
1.1. Recent news has reported that the current goal of limiting global warming to 2 °C might already have
been breached [2]. The window of action is rapidly closing, and the choices made these decades will shape
the climate for centuries to come.
Figure 1.1: Global surface temperature change historic and forecasted until 2100 relative to 1850-1900 [1].
Denmark recognises the urgency of addressing climate change and has committed to reach a 70% emissions
reduction by 2030, compared to 1990 levels, and to become a net zero country by 2050 [3]. The Danish Coun-
cil on Climate Change [Klimarådet] advises the government on reaching these lofty goals. A recent analysis
by the Council highlights that while expanding renewable energy and reducing agricultural emissions is key,
carbon capture technologies, including Bioenergy with Carbon Capture and Storage (BECCS), play a critical
role in the transition to net-zero [3]. On a global scale, the IPCC highlights BECCS as a major mitigation
measure capable of removing more than 1 gigatonne of CO2 annually, although at a higher cost compared to
other measures in wind or solar energy production [1].

INTRODUCTION
In a Danish context, carbon capture presents an opportunity for multiple sectors. For one, the Waste-to-
Energy (WtE) sector represents a significant amount of Danish national emissions with the incineration of
residual Municipal Solid Waste (MSW). The practice exists at the intersection of waste treatment and energy
production and is very prevalent in the developed economies of northern Europe [4]. In recent years, WtE
has faced criticism from NGOs and climate activists arguing that it discourages material recycling, which
arguably should be the end-game goal for sustainable development [5, 6]. The reality is that thermal recy-
cling of MSW is not a perfect solution for waste treatment or energy production, but it is also a reality that
increasing material recycling is a complex challenge that requires comprehensive action over the next many
years. The main alternative to incineration is, in many countries, the disposal of waste in landfills, which is
much worse, generating mainly methane resulting in up to four times the CO2 equivalent emissions (CO2-eq)
relative to incineration [7, 8]. Incineration of MSW to extract both heat and power is therefore viewed by
many as an effective transitionary solution as society as a whole moves towards a more circular economy.
Section 1.1 elaborates on the WtE sector, waste incineration and provides a modern example of a WtE plant
in Copenhagen, Denmark.
Carbon capture as a technology can mitigate emissions when combined with permanent storage or sequestra-
tion of the CO2. The technology especially has potential if implemented on otherwise hard-to-abate emission
sources where few alternatives exist, e.g., in the cement industry [9].
Furthermore, as mentioned, when
implemented on biogenic emissions (BECCS), carbon capture is capable of producing negative emissions as
short-cycle carbon of biogenic origin is removed from the atmosphere and permanently stored underground.
In a Danish context, this is especially relevant as biogenic emissions account for upwards of 70 % of emissions
from the WtE sector [10]. Despite great interest in recent years and major implementations in fossil power
plants, the carbon capture market is only just developing [11]. The first large-scale carbon capture implemen-
tation in Denmark is expected in 2026 to capture more than 400,000 tons CO2 using amine-based technology
[12]. Section 1.2 elaborates on WtE with CO2 capture whilst briefly describing competing technologies and
the associated value chain. Amine-based technology is introduced in Section 1.3 and further expanded on
with relevant theory in Section 2.
1.1
Waste-to-Energy
WtE represents a way to treat MSW while extracting heat and power through waste incineration. Generally,
the process starts with the reception and collection of MSW in a major waste silo, whence it is moved to
the furnace for incineration as needed. The incineration process is divided into key thermal subprocesses:
drying, pyrolysis, gasification, and combustion [13]. These stages ensure that the waste is fully decomposed,
maximising energy recovery as listed below. Explained step-wise below, Figure 1.2 provides an illustration.

Figure 1.2: Overview of the WtE incineration process by stages of waste decomposition (black) with outline of
idealised relative temperature (gray), oxygen availability (blue) and heat development (red).
1. Drying: Initially, as waste is introduced into the furnace, it is dried as moisture evaporates due to the
high ambient temperature within the furnace. The waste retains structure and is yet to decompose.
2. Pyrolysis: After drying, the waste is subjected to pyrolysis, where it decomposes at higher tempera-
tures in the absence of oxygen. During this stage, volatile gases are released from organic matter, most
of which are rich in energy, including carbon monoxide (CO), carbon dioxide (CO2), hydrogen (H2),
methane (CH4) and water vapour (H2O).
3. Gasification: The volatile compounds generated during pyrolysis are then gasified or reduced with
limited oxygen.
This process results in partial oxidation, transforming volatiles into a mixture of
combustible gases, such as CO and H2, which provide the energy required for subsequent stages.
4. Combustion: In the final stage, the remaining solid char combusts in the presence of oxygen, releasing
a significant amount of heat. This energy is transferred to a boiler unit, where steam is generated for
power production.
Although incineration is by far the most common WtE process, the final steps can be omitted to avoid
complete oxidisation. This would result in the final step being pyrolysis or gasification with no or only little
oxidation, respectively.
Specifically, pyrolysis can produce gaseous and liquid hydrocarbon mixtures and
gasification can produce syngas. While these may be valuable products, both alternatives face barriers of e.g.
catalyst deactivation, pretreatment requirements, cleaning of product gases, and thus complete incineration
has prevailed as the preferred option for WtE treatment [14].

1.1.1
Grate furnace
A key technology used in WtE plants is the grate furnace and boiler unit, which facilitates the incineration of
heterogeneous waste streams and subsequent transfer of energy from the hot flue gas to create high-pressure
steam. A schematic of a moving grate furnace and boiler unit can be found in Figure 1.3.
Figure 1.3: Moving grate furnace and boiler unit schematic. Sourced from [15] with adaptations by [16].
As can be seen from the lower left-hand side of Figure 1.3, the waste is initially loaded onto a moving
mechanical grate, which transports it through various thermal zones. As it travels along the grate, the waste
undergoes drying, pyrolysis, gasification, and char combustion as outlined above. Air is injected from beneath
the grate to enhance the combustion process, ensuring efficient oxidation of gases and solid residues. The air
injected through the grate is stoichiometrically insufficient for complete decomposition. When the gases leave
the first combustion zone, referring to Figure 1.3, they therefore still contain a large proportion of energy-rich
volatile compounds. These are combusted in the second combustion zone upon the further addition of oxygen,
raising the temperature prior to the gas being directed to the boiler for heat extraction. The combustion
results in the production of flue gases, slag, and ash, where the latter two consist of incombustible materials
with some recoverable portion [17]. The flue gases are directed to a secondary combustion zone to ensure
complete oxidation before passing through a heat recovery boiler to generate steam for electricity or district
heating. This system is highly flexible and can handle waste with varying moisture content and calorific
values, making it widely adopted in WtE plants around the world [13].

1.1.2
A modern WtE plant: Amager Bakke
A relevant example of a modern WtE plant is Amager Bakke located in Copenhagen and operated by
the municipality-owned company Amager Ressourcecenter (ARC). The plant receives waste from 670,000
residents and 68,000 companies to supply power and municipal heating back to the city [18]. Amager Bakke
emits about 560,000 tons of CO2 (tCO2) every year with one of the most effective flue gas cleaning systems in
the world. As a general goal, they aim to capture up to 500 kt CO2 annually [10]. The emissions are upwards
of 70 % biogenic, indicating that the implementation of carbon capture technology in the plant could at least
partially constitute BECCS [10]. A cross-sectional illustration of the plant can be found in Figure 1.4 with
following step-by-step summary of the plant energy generation process [17].

Amager Bakke optimises energy production from waste while incorporating advanced emission control and
recycling to minimise environmental impact. In an effort to go further, along with the potential for BECCS
combined and the wish of the municipal owner to reduce Copenhagen’s emissions, it has prompted an ongoing
initiative to assess the feasibility of carbon capture through pilot projects. This work is based on one of these
pilots introduced in more detail in Section 1.4.
1.2
WtE and CO2 capture
WtE plays a dual role in waste management and energy production, as introduced earlier, converting MSW
into usable energy while reducing the dependence of landfills.
However, WtE processes emit significant
amounts of CO2 from both biogenic and fossil sources. The implementation of CO2 capture technology in
WtE systems presents an opportunity to effectively mitigate these emissions. Implementation prolongs the
lifetime of existing WtE assets and enable a smoother transition to a circular and renewable economy. This
section explores CO2 capture technologies suitable for WtE applications and outlines the associated carbon
capture, utilisation and storage (CCUS) value chain.

1.2.1
CO2 capture technologies
Carbon capture technologies are crucial for reducing CO2 emissions by capture prior to release into the
atmosphere. Although technologies can be categorised in many ways, as introduced in the following, the so-
called post-combustion capture (PCC) technologies are the most relevant for WtE purposes. Post-combustion
capture focuses on the capture of CO2 after the combustion process, i.e., the CO2 in the flue gas. This
configuration is particularly suited for retrofitting existing WtE plants, where the flue gas would be directed
to a capture unit that separates and purifies CO2. Various physical and chemical techniques, such as chemical
absorption, physical adsorption, membrane separation, cryogenic condensation, and more, can be employed
for this purpose [19, 20]. As alluded to, CO2 capture technologies can be classified and organised in many
ways. Figure 1.5 is an attempt to capture the main ones with some degree of commercial traction in recent
years. Although there are many promising yet novel technologies, which are included in the overview, they
are not expanded on further. With reference to Figure 1.5, the main types are explained below.
Figure 1.5: Overview of CO2 capture technologies. Adapted from [21].
• Absorption and adsorption: This method utilizes either liquid solvents (such as amines) or solid
sorbents to capture CO2. These typically require regeneration using different driving forces; pressure,
temperature, electrochemical or a combination hereof [21]. Especially for the WtE sector, amine-based
systems are the most mature and widely used technologies.
• Membranes: These technologies separate CO2 from other gases by passing the flue gas through a
membrane, which selectively allows CO2 to permeate through. However, high pressure is needed to
effectively permeate the membranes. As it requires vast energy consumption to increase pressure, the
general understanding has prevailed that membranes are more suitable for systems where the CO2

• Liquid or supercritical CO2: This typically involves cooling the flue gas to very low temperatures
to liquefy and or even form dry ice separating CO2 from other gases referred to as cryogenic separation.
Oftentimes, this step is pre-seeded by applying pressure swing adsorption (PSA) to increase the CO2

Among these technologies, amine-based chemical absorption remains the most relevant for retrofitting existing
power plants [19, 22]. In particular, it is relevant for WtE post-combustion capture due to its maturity and
efficiency in separating CO2 from the typical flue gas composition at WtE plants [19]. Amine-based systems,
for example, using aqueous alkanolamines as solvents, have been widely adopted and optimised for CO2
capture in several large-scale operations [20]. The technology is favoured for its high capture efficiency, and,
despite the high energy requirements for solvent regeneration, ongoing advances are improving its overall
economic feasibility including opportunities for low grade or waste heat utilisation. In the future, advances
in amine systems and solvent formulations are expected to further enhance energy efficiency and reduce the
cost of CO2 capture in WtE plants [23].
1.2.2
CO2 value chain
Large scale implementation of carbon capture relies heavily on the development of the associated value chain
around the produced CO2. In general, the value chain encompasses the processes of capturing, transporting,
utilising, and storing CO2 [9, 21, 24]. As CO2 capture was already introduced in the previous section, this
section focuses on the remaining elements of the value chain.
Compression and transport: Once CO2 is captured and dehydrated, it must be pressurised for feasible
transportation, with the required pressure level varying by transport mode. Lower pressures result in CO2
remaining in a gaseous state, suitable for transport through large diameter pipelines because a larger volume
capacity is needed. At higher pressures, CO2 achieves a dense or even supercritical state, which, depending
on the temperature, is more suitable for road, rail, or shipping transport and potentially for smaller-diameter
pipelines.
Regardless of the pressure level, compression is costly and significantly impacts both capital
(CAPEX) and operational expenditure (OPEX). In the early stages of value chain development, there will
likely be greater reliance on flexible transport modes such as road and shipping. However, as the value chain
matures, pipeline infrastructure is expected to expand beyond the approximately 8,000 km of CO2 pipelines
globally in 2021 [24].

Utilisation: CO2 can be used as a resource, e.g., for the production of e-fuels using renewable hydrogen
or for Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) by displacing and swelling reservoir oil to increase recovery. Although
e-fuel production is only just seeing commercial adoption, the experience with the commercial use of EOR
dates back more than 50 years [24]. Though these and other uses of CO2 can be characterised as more or
less sustainable, utilisation in general is not compatible with BECCS. For emissions to be truly negative, the
captured CO2 must be permanently stored rather than utilised, ensuring that the process results in a net
reduction in atmospheric CO2 [24].
Storage: A more sustainable and long-term solution for the captured CO2 is dedicated geological storage.
This involves injecting CO2 deep underground into stable geological formations, where it can remain se-
questered for centuries [24]. Northern Lights, an equal joint venture by the Norwegian state-owned Equinor
along with publicly traded oil supermajors Shell and TotalEnergies, is one of the key projects demonstrating
the potential of dedicated storage. As of September 2024, the site announced that it was ready to receive
up to 1.5 Mt CO2 annually making it the largest project to date [25]. The CO2 captured from industrial
emitters in northern Europe will be transported via pipelines or ships to a storage site under the North Sea
in Western Norway. Although storage of CO2 is a challenging undertaking, the efforts are based on more
than a century of experience, as highlighted by the joint ownership of the Northern Lights project.
In a Danish context, Greensand is one of the most mature storage projects having just made the final invest-
ment decision (FID) of the initial commercial phase at the end of 2024[26]. With the FID, the project is on
track to start operations of the phase at the end of 2025/early 2026 with a storage capacity of 400 kt CO2
annually. The project, led by the petrochemical company INEOS along with its partners Harbour Energy
and Nordsøfonden, seeks to expand the capacity of the INEOS-operated Nini field in the Danish North Sea
toward 2030 and beyond with the potential to store 8 Mt CO2 annually.
Maturity and challenges in the value chain: The carbon capture part of the CCUS value chain has
been demonstrated, with 45 commercial facilities capturing more than 50 Mt of CO2 annually. However, for
large-scale deployment and to reach the announced total annual project capacity of 435 Mt CO2 by 2030,
significant scaling is required, supported by adequate funding and government incentives. The transport
and storage segments remain underdeveloped, despite the mentioned Northern Lights project along with the
substantial announced capacity as of 2023 amounting to 615 Mt CO2 annually by 2030. Key challenges
include misaligned incentives from existing legislation, immature technologies, and unclear price signals,
which make it difficult for companies to form partnerships and enter into long-term contracts. In addition,
low public investment in CO2 infrastructure and the absence of comprehensive international frameworks,

1.3
Amine based capture
Amine-based carbon capture is one of the most established technologies for post-combustion CO2 capture.
The core mechanism relies on chemical absorption or scrubbing of CO2 by aqueous amine solvents, typi-
cally monoethanolamine (MEA), diethanolamine (DEA), or more advanced blends, e.g. CESAR1 containing
piperazine (PZ) and 2-amino-2-methyl-1-propanol (AMP), reacting with CO2 to form carbamates and bicar-
bonates. The specific reaction mechanism differs depending on the amine structure. Typically, amines are
grouped by structure as a primary, secondary, tertiary or sterically hindered amine. Amine reactivity and
types are detailed further in Section 2.3 on amine scrubbing and specifically in Section 2.3.1 on amine reac-
tivity. This process is integral in separating CO2 from the flue gas produced by WtE plants. Amine-based
CO2 capture is conceptualised around the Girbotol process, which was developed in the 1930s and remains
foundational in modern CO2 capture applications. The Girbotol process uses temperature swing absorption
to separate CO2 from flue gases. As illustrated in Figure 1.6, the process involves both an absorption and a
stripping column operating in a continuous cycle [23].
Figure 1.6: Amine capture process [27].
Absorption column: The flue gas, or sour gas as a more general term, enters the absorption column at the
bottom, where it comes into contact with a counter-flowing amine solvent entering at the top of the column
as can be seen from Figure 1.6. The column is filled with structured packing materials to enhance the contact

surface area between the gas and the liquid. The CO2 in the flue gas exothermically reacts with the amine
solvent to form chemical compounds, primarily carbamates and bicarbonates. The column typically operates
between 40–60 °C to maintain favourable conditions for CO2 absorption, and the resulting CO2-rich solvent
exits from the bottom of the column. The cleaned flue gas, now with reduced CO2 content, is vented from
the top of the column. To reduce amine emissions, the gas is typically cleaned in an acid and water wash
section or tower (not depicted) [23].
Stripping column: The CO2-rich solvent, or rich amine as called in Figure 1.6, is then directed to a
stripping column where it is heated, typically between 100–140 °C, in a process known as thermal swing
regeneration. The heat reduces the solvent’s affinity for CO2, causing endothermic desorption of the CO2
releasing it. A reboiler at the base of the stripping column supplies the necessary heat, while steam assists
in the desorption process by diluting the CO2 and carrying it upward through the column. The regenerated,
or lean, amine solvent is then recycled back to the absorption column after cooling, creating a continuous
capture loop [23].
Amine-based CO2 capture has been successfully implemented in several full-scale projects. One prominent
example is the Boundary Dam Carbon Capture and Storage Project in Saskatchewan, Canada. This facility,
which started in 2014, uses amine-based technology to capture CO2 from a coal-fired power plant, making it
one of the first large-scale projects of its kind [23, 28]. Another significant project is the Petra Nova Project
in Texas, USA, which began operations in 2017 but was shut down for a three-year period after the beginning
of the COVID pandemic due to plummeting oil prices, restarting in 2023 [29, 30]. The project is scaled to
capture approximately 1.4 million tons of CO2 per year from a natural gas power plant using an advanced
amine-based system [28].

2.1.2
Component balances
The main component of interest in the mass balance is CO2 as determination of the produced flow is necessary
to assess performance. Both CO2 and H2O are assumed absorbable into the aqueous 30 wt% MEA and 7.5

1. All gas streams can be regarded as ideal.
2. All gas streams are saturated with water.
3. Exclusively H2O and CO2 are absorbable in the solvent.
4. N2 and O2 are the only considered components in the flue gas stream besides H2O and CO2, and they
are regarded as completely inert.
5. Exclusively H2O and CO2 is contained in the produced gas.
6. The volatility of MEA and MEG is negligible and there is no loss of either during the short period with

2.2.2
Heat-loss considerations
This goal of this section is to discuss and introduce theory to estimate the heat loss during operation of the
MTU. The actual estimation of heat loss is carried out in Section 3.6.
The main input of heating to the system occurs through the reboiler connected to the stripping column. In
an effort to assess the heat loss, the MTU operators initially performed experiments when the MTU was set
up. The experiment centred on cycling water between the columns and analysing temperatures throughout.
Complete system enclosure was employed during experiments e.g. with no gas entering the absorber and with
complete condensation of the stripper top steam. The stream heat flows were estimated akin to the method
outlined in Section 2.2.1 for the condenser and cooling operations. The heat loss represents the difference
between these and the reboiler duty as a percentage of the heat input in the reboiler duty i.e. the percentage
of heat unaccounted for or lost. At different reboiler duties from 15 to 22 kW, the experiment was carried out
and the heat loss was calculated. The results, as can be seen from Appendix A, Figure A.1, indicate a heat
loss of 10-30 % at varying reboiler duties with the greatest heat loss at a duty of 15 kW (54 MJ/h). While
this could be applied directly, the wide-ranging relative heat loss indicates uncertainty of the results, which
would require significant assumptions to apply. An alternative method of estimating heat loss was therefore
chosen.

Amine scrubbing
This section details amine scrubbing, which is another name for the concept of amine-based chemical absorp-
tion as a PCC technology that has been introduced earlier. Specifically, the section provides an introduction
to amine reactivity with a brief overlook of the mechanisms involved for different amine types, with further
description of these as solvents in addition to the potential of additives called water-lean or Vapour Reduction

2.3.2
Amine solvents
Solvent choice impacts a multitude of process aspects. A solvent should thus ideally possess several key
properties to be relevant for thermal swing operation. While criteria can be quantified within e.g. thermody-
namics, kinetics, solvent stability and physiochemical properties [32], this section outlines more qualitative
characteristics. Warranted characteristics of an ideal solvent can be seen from the following non-exhaustive
list [23, 28, 32, 37, 39]:
1. High loading capacity indicates absorption of a significant mol CO2 per mol solvent with potential to
reduce solvent flow rate.
2. Low molecular weight results in higher molar concentration, which can translate to more CO2 binding
sites for the same solvent weight fraction.
3. Low degradation tendency and volatility limits solvent loss to formation of stable inactive degradation
products and loss of solvent in the product stream, respectively.
4. Low viscosity eases pumping and increasing heat transfer in heat exchangers due to better mixing and
thinner boundary layers.
5. High mass transfer rate stemming from fast reaction kinetics allows for reducing equipment sizing as a
lower solvent flow can be utilised. Mass transfer is related to diffusivity and thus viscosity.
6. High protonation equilibrium constant indicates high alkalinity, which related to Equation 2.21-2.23
enables high rate of carbamate formation, which is a significant contributor to overall mass transfer.
7. Large (negative) enthalpic heat of protonation indicating that alkalinity declines with temperature
allowing for efficient release of CO2 during stripping.
8. Low toxicity and environmental impact ensuring safety for operators in case of leakage and the society
and environment in case of emitted solvent.
9. Low bulk cost affecting solvent makeup cost during operation.
24
==PAGE:35==

10. Little corrosive tendencies limiting the necessity of anti-corrosion measures and prolonging equipment
lifetimes.
Generally, as outlined in Section 2.3, the amine group is responsible for reaction with CO2, but the hydroxyl
group is also central as it reduces vapour pressure and increases water solubility [37]. An optimised water
concentration is essential to the amine solutions as they otherwise have higher viscosity and are prone to pre-
cipitate. The water concentration is therefore mainly a trade-off between viscosity and the amine absorption
capacity, though it also has further implications.
This project focuses on aqueous 30 wt% MEA solvent as the base solvent, with MEG applied as VRA (the
latter part is detailed further in the following Section 2.3.3). The base solvent of aqueous 30 wt% MEA has
broadly been considered the default in literature and has become the industry standard in open source pilot
implementations [40]. Though is starting to change slowly with the increasing adaptation and research on
advanced solvent blends such as CESAR1 [41]. Solvent specifications are considered critical intellectual prop-
erty in the industry, and information on solvent composition is thus not public for the large-scale commercial
projects implemented today. To motivate the use of aqueous 30 wt% MEA solvent in this work, an assess-
ment related to the outline of characteristics for an ideal solvent and comparison with relevant alternatives
is presented:
Main strengths of MEA: High mass transfer rate as a primary amine allowing for direct reaction and
thus faster kinetics compared to e.g. tertiary amines in MDEA, which impacts equipment sizing. Further-
more, mass transfer is aided by high alkalinity increasing carbamate formation and low relative viscosity also
contributing to efficient flow and heat integration. MEA also has a large enthalpic heat of protonation for
efficient stripping. Lastly, the low bulk cost of MEA in comparison to most other viable amines contributes
to lower solvent makeup cost [23, 28].
Main weaknesses of MEA: Relatively low theoretical maximum loading capacity of only 0.50 mol CO2
per mol amine compared to secondary or tertiary amines capable of achieving significantly higher loadings.
Another main weakness of MEA is the high levels of degradation, especially thermally occurring when the
solvent is subjected to high stripping temperatures.
The degradation can be catalysed by metallic ions
potentially stemming from corrosion, from the flue gas stream, or added as corrosion inhibitors [23]. No
degradation is considered in the analysis of this work following assumption (7) as listed in Section 2.1.2.
The operating conditions of the stripping columns are therefore limited as temperatures above 120 °C have
been found to substantially increase solvent degradation. For reference, far greater operating temperatures
approaching 160 °C using piperazine (PZ) as solvent do not lead to significant degradation [23]. Additionally,

2.3.3
Vapour Reduction Additives
One of the primary drawbacks to amine technology is the high energy requirements for solvent regeneration,
as mentioned previously, primarily delivered through the reboiler. This can be reduced and optimised in
various ways, with one more recent focus being the solvent and especially replacing some or all of the water
in the aqueous amine-blends with lower volatility solvents. In literature, the complete or close to complete re-
placement of water is referred to as water-lean solvents [43]. A range of different, especially organic solvents,
have been investigated as water-lean solvents. Despite the higher relative vapour pressure and high heat
capacity of water, substantial water content offers benefits mainly in improved viscosity and very low price
comparably to organic solvent [44]. To leverage the benefits of aqueous amine-blends, partial replacement
of water with lower volatility solvents can be achieved with VRAs. With the low vapour pressure of VRAs,
it is expected that they reduce the vapour pressure of both water and amine vapours, reducing the solvent
mixture vapour pressure with the expectation of reduced reboiler duty [45]. Furthermore, most of the solvent
loss for amine-based PCC stems from water wash and emissions of amines relative to degradation or other
loss mechanisms [39], which can be expected to be reduced with a lower solvent volatility.
Several types of compounds have been explored in literature for partial or complete water replacement using
various approaches [43, 46, 47]. Specifically for VRAs, urea, triethylene glycol (TEG), imidazole, and more
have been explored [33, 46], yet this work investigates the potential in monoethylene glycol (MEG). MEG
has been explored as a water-lean solvent and characterised, e.g. in determination of CO2 solubility in MEA-
MEG solvents with complete water replacement [48, 49]. As for water-containing systems, the CO2 heat of
absorption and CO2 solubility of MEA-MEG-H2O has been characterised by a range of authors [45, 47, 50].
Though the literature on MEG as a VRA is far from extensive, these authors have outlined the potential
to achieve improved kinetics, lower reboiler duty, reduced amine emissions, etc. Further, MEG is a bulk
chemical with over 20 Mt in global production (2010) and main uses in polyester fibre manufacturing and in
antifreeze formulations [51, 52]. This is consistent with one of the major strengths of MEA in low bulk costs
as discussed in Section 2.3.2.

2.3.4.4
Discussion of assumptions
The estimation of amine solvent loading relies on several assump-
tions and approximations that simplify the complexity of the system while maintaining reasonable accuracy.
Firstly, the solvent is assumed to exhibit ideal behaviour of the system i.e. non-ideal mixing effects are
neglected. This entails not considering excess volume, meaning that the molar volumes of components com-
bine linearly without interactions influencing the overall density. The implications of neglecting non-ideal
mixing effects is that density and thus loading might be under- or overestimated depending on non-ideal be-
haviour. Secondly, it is assumed that CO2 is the only component absorbed into the solvent that contributes
to deviations from the baseline composition of 30 wt% MEA, 7.5 wt% MEG, and 62.5 wt% H2O. As it has
been assumed that H2O is absorbable in the system, the fraction of this might fluctuate from the baseline,
which is not accounted for. Thirdly, the density and temperature correlations, derived from experimental
data for a specific temperature range (e.g., up to 75–80 °C), can potentially be used at extended to higher
temperatures (up to 120 °C). While this extension introduces uncertainty, the high correlation coefficients
(R2 > 99) of the regression models suggest the relationships are robust. Furthermore, the loadings are cal-
culated based temperatures of the cold rich and lean streams, i.e. before and after the main heat exchanger
(MHX), respectively, that are generally below 50 °C. The density temperature correlations should therefore
be relatively accurate. Additionally, it is assumed that the density correlation between 0 wt% and 15 wt%

in a similar manner and had samples taken during operation. The study found that the calculated values
were in fair agreement with experimental values. Specifically, a relative difference of less than ±0.5 % was
found for MEA-H2O-CO2 systems. These results increase confidence in the methodology despite the analysis
being performed with a different solvent composition not accounting for the impact of MEG.

At the start of each step change, the system takes some time to adjust before reaching steady state. This

be a minimum of 3 hours. Depending on the actually selected step change period, ample time should be
available to reach steady state for the primarily assessed parameter mentioned previously. The steady states

1. Cyclic: Especially lean and rich solvent densities (Figure 3.4, d) exhibit cyclic behaviour indicating
variation, yet if one or more full cycles are captured, the mean should represent the values. Further,

The sensitivity analysis highlights the significant impact of content temperature and surface properties on
heat loss within the system. The stripper, due to its large surface area, is the dominant contributor, with
temperature increases leading to substantial heat loss. Ambient temperature changes have limited effect on
total energy loss due to low variance for indoor pilots, while surface emissivity and insulation material play a
critical role in radiative heat dissipation. The findings suggest that optimising column insulation, particularly
through low-emissivity coatings on the stripper, could provide meaningful energy savings, though cost-benefit
considerations should guide implementation.

3.8.4
Discussion of estimation accuracy
The estimation accuracy is completely reliant on the input from different probes supplying information on
pressure, temperature, density, flow, CO2 concentration, and more. Two probes are mentioned in the sections
above; the CO2 concentration probe Vaisala GMP 251 and the flow probe Siemens SITRANS FC430. Both
of these probes have been shown to have various faults and data quality issues as documented in other work
on the MTU pilot primarily by Randi Neerup [32]. These findings serve as the main basis for the discussion
in the following section.
Firstly, the Vaisala GMP 251 records CO2 concentration using IR technology in the in- and outlet gas streams
around the absorber as described previously. It has a range of validity of 0-20 % CO2 and for temperatures
up to 60 °C. It has been shown that these probes are sensitive to water condensate [32], which resulted in
fluctuations upwards of ± 20 % in concentration measurements. In the CO2 cross-validation Section 3.10,
values for MTU dry CO2 measurements of around 9 % were observed to be close to a third lower than
external measurements. This reduces the confidence in M1a for calculating CO2 flow where these data points
are applied. Alternative means of calculation for M1a related to the flue gas are outlined in Section 3.8.1.
Secondly, the Siemens SITRANS FC430 flow meter, which is based on the Coriolis principle, a highly re-
garded technology as it measures mass flow directly without the need to measure volumetric flow or account
for changing temperature, viscosity, or pressure. This equipment can only be considered accurate up to flows
of 140 kg gas/h [32]. All the steady states of this work have raw flue gas inlet flows of more than 140 kg/h.
This potentially is a large inaccuracy of the flow measurements. Though the flow of incoming flue gas cannot

flow. Additionally, Randi Neerup [32] concluded that the rich flow meter, of the same probe type, is im-

measurement-point to be of lower quality. Though this impacts M2, the finding was the least quantified of

from increased condenser duty required mainly to condense additional water vapour at greater stripping

requirements for the produced CO2 stream. The energy lost in the condenser can be estimated by setting up
an energy balance around the condenser. Here, three streams are considered; firstly, the stripper top stream

are performed using the energy balance theory introduced in Section 2.2.1. This allows for setting up the
energy balance in Equation 4.5. The cooling energy required in the condenser ( ˙Qcond) is calculated as the
enthalpy difference between the in- and outgoing streams to the condenser. The enthalpies account for the
full streams considering both the component temperatures and states.

4.2.6
Solvent loading
Solvent composition is key to understanding CO2 transport in the system. To quantify this, the concept
of solvent loading (α) was introduced in Section 2.3.4, representing the molar ratio of CO2 to amine in the
solvent, as defined in Equation 2.26. Generally, the goal in optimisation of solvent loadings is to maximise
the difference or delta between rich and lean loading at steady state i.e. the maximum cyclic capacity to
allow for the maximum CO2 transport. Although this may seem as straightforward as increasing rich loading
or decreasing lean loading, it carries several implications:
• Firstly, increasing rich loading is limited by the inherent loading capacity of solvents as outlined in
Section 2.3, which for MEA is α = 0.5 (at 0.1 bar CO2 and 40 °C) [23, 38]. Furthermore, as the loading
capacity provides a theoretical maximum, the optimisation of achieving and even surpassing this for
the rich solvent stream is mostly related to efficient operation of the absorber. The equilibrium loading
of MEA has been shown to increase with partial pressure of CO2 indicating that greater transport
efficiency can be achieved with greater CO2 content. Here, it should be noted that efficient absorber
operation entails a multitude of elements in e.g. enough height and packing material, efficient use of
the packing material surface area, optimal temperature conditions for CO2 absorption and more.
• Secondly, decreasing lean loading is mainly related to the stripper; specifically, supplying an adequate
amount of heat energy through the reboiler to allow for efficient CO2 desorption.
Whilst efficient
stripper operation regarding SRD and temperature has been analysed in previous sections, in the
context of loading, two factors mainly limit decreasing the lean loading; thermodynamics and solvent
degradation. As heat is the driving force for amine capture, increasing temperature theoretically results
in further CO2 desorption decreasing loading, yet the desorption of course incurs additional heating
costs. Furthermore, increasing the temperature would entail significant water evaporation if pressure is
not increased increasing inefficiency and costs further. Increasing pressure has implications beyond the
scope of this work, and will thus not be commented on. In addition, though generally also beyond the
scope of this work, it is well documented that amines degrade excessively above specific temperatures,
which for MEA is around 120 °C [23, 57]. These factors of inefficiency and solvent degradation provide
a natural limit to lean loading.

series (B2 and B3), which varied rich solvent flow in the range 153-340 kg/h and thus stripping intensity at

Case 1A, referring to the too high stripping intensity case in Figure 4.15, shows a sharp temperature bulge
at the bottom of the absorber and a low temperature for the remainder of the column. This is caused by an
abundance of free amines (unloaded with CO2) in the solvent absorbing most of the CO2 at the bottom flue
gas inlet. Due to the exothermic nature of CO2 absorption, the temperature increases at that point. Because
the reaction is occurring at the bottom of the column, most of the column is not utilised, which is inefficient.
A capture rate close to 100 % would generally be expected in this case, as there is an excess of free amines
in the solvent. This would furthermore impact the rich loading, which would to decrease due to incomplete
amine saturation [32]. Relevant for the discussion below, it should be noted that any significant reduction in
rich loading was not observed in Section 4.2.6 on solvent loading. This reduces the likelihood that this case
can be identified in the experimental temperature profiles.

Upscaling
Carbon capture pilot experimentation is all part of maturing and ultimately upscaling the technology for
full-scale applications. The WtE operator, ARC, has a goal of annually capturing 500 kt CO2 as described
in Section 1.1.2. At an assumed 95 % capture uptime, this corresponds to 1.44 kt CO2 per day.
This section presents a simple upscaling to full-scale carbon capture based on the MTU pilot and operational
performance from this work. The general design philosophy and thus scale of the MTU was to capture 1 ton

the different elements included in this simple upscaling analysis including column dimensioning, scaling of
operating parameters, adjustment of heat loss, and total cost estimation.

Finally, it is of interest to estimate the total cost of the large scale capture plant. A simple method of ballpark
estimation is to apply the S-factor on the cost price of the MTU, which excluding the extensive development
came to be approximately 4 mDKK. Upscaling this to the large scale yields a cost of 5.8 bDKK. The method
fails to account for economies of scale reducing the costs. On the other hand, it also fails to account for the
significant general inflation since the MTU was built in 2020-2021 increasing costs. The number should be
interpreted as an order of magnitude estimate based on the simple nature of the method. While the precise
estimate is of little relevance, the suggested order-of-magnitude range of 1-10 bDKK is likely in the right
ballpark for the total CAPEX. Furthermore, it would be of interest to estimate a cost of capture (DKK/tCO2)
to evaluate investment. Unfortunately, this is beyond the scope of the upscaling analysis as an operational
estimate would have to be conducted.

6
CONCLUSION
6
Conclusion
This work has presented and analysed the performance of CO2 capture on a pilot scale mobile test unit (MTU)
using an aqueous 30 wt% monoethanolamine (MEA) solvent with 7.5 wt% monoethylene glycol (MEG) from
a Waste-to-Energy (WtE) plant with a flue gas CO2 content of approximately 12 %. Using the basecase
configuration of the MTU, a minimum energy consumption (SRD) of 3.53 MJ/kgCO2 was obtained at a
capture rate of 89.7 % and a stripping intensity of 271 kJ/kg (220 kg/h, 16.6 kW). Furthermore, an optimal
range of stripping intensities of 240 to 320 kJ/kg was identified and validated in an inefficiency analysis. The
findings align with previous work using 30 wt% MEA solvent on the MTU at the WtE [16, 33].
Heat loss was estimated to account for approximately 10 % of the total energy supplied to the reboiler.
Sensitivity analysis demonstrated that stripper temperature had the most significant effect on heat loss, with
an estimated heat loss of 1.76 kW, which was excluded from the core analysis. A mass balance indicated
an overall system bias below 2 %. However, signal quality issues for several critical probes was documented
in other work [32], particularly for gas and solvent flow measurements along with CO2 content, which ne-
cessitated careful consideration of the CO2 flow estimation. Multiple estimation methods were introduced,
though an average of three methods provided results most consistent with literature values. Most of the
analysis was conducted using the different specific method (M2), where solvent loadings determine CO2 flow,
which exhibited high sensitivity to flow and density measurements.
The introduction of U-curve plots was applied to visualise trade-offs between energy consumption and op-
erational inefficiencies. The optimal operating window was identified at stripping intensities between 240
and 320 kJ/kg, where specific energy losses in both the main heat exchanger (MHX) and condenser were
minimised. Residual energy loss analysis indicated that inefficiencies in the MHX explained most of the
SRD increases at lower stripping intensities, whereas only 60-70 % inefficiencies on the higher end could be
attributed to the condenser.
Stripper temperature profiles provided further insights into column utilisation. The optimal stripping inten-
sity was associated with a balanced temperature profile, ensuring effective utilisation of the packed column
area and minimising energy losses. At high stripping intensities (>340 kJ/kg), the higher energy availability
led to significantly greater stripper column temperatures. This, in turn, resulted in greater heat loss and
excess water vapour exiting the stripper increasing condenser inefficiency. At low stripping intensities (<240
kJ/kg), ineffective CO2 desorption at the column bottom reduced solvent regeneration efficiency. While the
most balanced temperature profile and thus column utilisation was obtained at 314 kJ/kg, steady states at
far lower stripping intensities and column utilisation degrees yielded satisfactory SRD values. This implies
89
==PAGE:100==
6
CONCLUSION
that stripper bed dimensions likely could be reduced without impacting energy efficiency at certain conditions.
The solvent loading analysis supported the findings related to the stripper, with lean loading exhibiting a
sharp increase at lower stripping intensities, indicating incomplete CO2 desorption due to inadequate energy
availability. Rich loading remained relatively stable across stripping intensities, suggesting no significant
mass transfer limitations in the absorber. Further, optimal lean loading was identified between 0.21 and 0.28
mol CO2/mol amine, balancing SRD minimisation with operational constraints. High overall loading values
suggested overestimation in the chosen calculation method.
Absorber temperature profile analysis revealed that none of the examined steady states operated at an amine
surplus relative to CO2. This confirmed that the pilot was not run at excessive capture rates (>95%) limit-
ing opportunity for analytical insights. Further, it indicated that mass transfer constraints did not limit the
process, as equilibrium loading was consistently approached. Instead, the inefficiencies observed at extreme
stripping intensities were linked to energy losses in the MHX and condenser rather than absorber limitations.
An initial objective of this work was to evaluate the impact of using vapour reduction additives (VRAs),
specifically, MEG. While experimental data of solvent containing 7.5 wt% MEG was analysed, the results
obtained do, however, not provide a definitive conclusion on the effect of MEG as no baseline was established
within the work.
The minimum SRD obtained in this study (3.53 MJ/kgCO2) was slightly better than
Frederik Elbek’s [16] result (3.91 MJ/kgCO2) yet slightly worse than Vinjarapu et al. [33] (3.46 MJ/kgCO2).
Both of these studies used 30 wt% MEA without MEG on the same pilot experimental campaign. An im-
proved energy efficiency resulting from MEG can thus not be identified from these conflicting findings.
The findings of this work provide important insights for the upscaling of carbon capture technologies. With
Amager Resource Center (ARC), the WtE owner and operator, targeting an annual CO2 capture of 500 kt,
the pilot results offer a basis for scaling and a suitable optimal operating point with a SRD of 3.53 MJ/kgCO2.
It was concluded that an upscaled carbon plant would consume a minimum of 12.6 % of the heat output
from the WtE plant. In comparison with the generally expected 20-30 % range, the result was regarded as
optimistic. Lastly, the cost of an upscaled plant was estimated to be in the lower range of billions of DKK.
In conclusion, this study, overall, provided insights into the optimal operation of CO2 capture using aqueous
MEA solvents in a WtE setting. By leveraging mass balance validation, stripping intensity optimisation, and
absorber/stripper temperature profile analyses, this work has established practical guidelines for improving
pilot-scale capture performance while highlighting areas for future research and industrial application.

FUTURE WORK
7
Future work
This work examined the basecase configuration of the MTU using aqueous 30 wt% solvent with 7.5 wt% added
MEG as a VRA. Specifically, the work focused on conducting in-depth operational performance analysis of
two steady-state series with varying rich solvent flow at constant reboiler duty. Building on the findings in
this work, this section outlines perspectives on potential future work. Initially, a couple of areas close to the
scope of the work are outlined:
• Evaluation of the impact of 7.5 wt% MEG addition to the solvent by establishing a baseline using
aqueous 30 wt% MEA for comparison within the same work. Further studies should likewise study
increased MEG concentrations (e.g., 15 wt% MEG) to determine optimal VRA levels.
• Further analysis and optimisation of the CO2 flow method (M2) based on solvent loadings to account
for the seeming flow underestimation. Additionally, the method would benefit greatly from density and
compositional cross validation using samples taken during steady state.
• Variation of additional control parameters beyond rich solvent flow. For example, reboiler duty as a
primary control parameter for stripping intensity, stripper bottom pressure, inlet flue gas flow, and
reduced CO2 concentration in the inlet flue gas via recycling cleaned gas. This would provide insights
into dynamic operational flexibility and its effect on energy efficiency and capture performance.
• Investigation of alternative process configurations, such as intercooling, rich solvent split (also called
cold feed), rich recycle to the absorber, lean vapour compression, or combinations thereof. The MTU
are capable of these configurations and they have been shown to improve energy efficiency and solvent
performance [40].
In addition, several areas of interest further beyond the scope of the work could be of interest to investigate
in future work:
• Determination of the minimum necessary absorber and stripper column dimensions before performance
is significantly impacted at optimal operating points.
This would help define cost-effective scaling
strategies while maintaining high capture efficiencies.
• Exploration of advanced process control strategies for dynamic optimization of CO2 capture efficiency,
considering complex trade-offs between solvent degradation, emissions, energy efficiency, and regulatory
compliance.
• Expansion of upscaling methodologies by incorporating more detailed equipment scaling and sizing, cost
analyses, and process intensification strategies. Additionally, investigating the impact of fluctuations
in power plant operations on capture performance would provide insights into full-scale deployment.
91
==PAGE:102==
7
FUTURE WORK
• Investigation of solvent degradation, emissions, and corrosion aspects, which become increasingly rel-
evant when considering long-term pilot operations and upscaling. Understanding degradation mecha-
nisms and their mitigation strategies would be key to improving process sustainability and minimizing
environmental impacts.
Overall, future research could focus on many key aspects including refining methods and operational pa-
rameters, improving energy efficiency through process modifications, and addressing long-term operational
challenges such as solvent stability, emissions control, and industrial-scale implementation. These efforts will
be essential for advancing carbon capture technology towards practical, cost-effective deployment.
