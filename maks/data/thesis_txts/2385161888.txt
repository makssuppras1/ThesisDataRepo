Abstract
Keywords: Oﬀshore Wind Turbine, Foundation, Monopile, Installation, Impact Driving, Flange
Ring, L-ﬂange, Finite Element Modelling, Fatigue, Damage
Oﬀshore wind energy is a rapidly growing renewable energy source. The most popular deep
foundations for oﬀshore wind turbines are large diameter (up to 9m) steel monopiles. The
supersize monopiles are typically installed in the seabed by impact driving. The monopile
installation is followed by mounting the transition piece by means of grouted joint or bolted
ﬂange connection. Cost-eﬃcient solutions have lately escalated the use of bolted connections.
The L-ﬂange ring is pre-welded in the workshop, implying that the pile-driving operation, by
means of hydraulic hammer, is directly performed on the ﬂange. Highly dynamic pile-driving
forces cause fatigue damage at the ﬂange, the nature of which is still not fully understood and
hence it is in detail assessed in this study.
The problem is analysed using advanced ﬁnite element modelling and the results are validated
by comparison with one-dimensional wave equation analysis. The driving eﬀects on the ﬂange
are evaluated in terms of cumulative fatigue life damage and sensitivity analysis is performed
to improve the ﬂange design. The main highlights of the project are as follows:
• An eﬃcient and comprehensive axisymmetric ﬁnite element model, representing hammer-
ﬂange-monopile-soil interaction during impact driving was created.
• The model was successfully validated by direct comparison with one-dimensional wave
equation analysis in terms of axial force, displacement and system energy balance.
• Axial pile movement, coupled with local ﬂange bending, enabled qualitative assessment
of the fatigue damage at the most critical points.
• The largest damage was determined to occur at the ﬂange weld. Monopile wall thickness,
impact energy and contact width between hammer and the ﬂange were identiﬁed as
critical design parameters.
• Guidelines for ﬂange design were derived by means of parameter sensitivity analysis.
The pile-driving performed directly on the ﬂange was found to be problematic due to ﬂange
oscillations and uneven stress distribution across the top of the monopile wall. Thorough design
in combination with pile-driving energy control can prevent the excessive driving induced
fatigue damage.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Oﬀshore wind and monopile foundations
Global climate is evidently changing and humanity ﬁghts this challenge while altering energy
politics. Green energy is set to have a goal replacing high carbon emission fossil fuels and low-
ering the undesirable temperature rise. It is expected that over the next 5 years sustainable
energy will cover more than 60% of global power capacity [IEA, 2016] and wind will be one
of the biggest contributors. Currently, oﬀshore wind is the fastest growing sustainable energy
source.
Oﬀshore wind is being utilized because onshore wind cannot cover the needed volume in long
term without compromising land property, noise and environmental aspects. Oﬀshore wind
has more potential due to larger areas, higher wind speeds and possibility to reach an order of
magnitude similar to power stations. However, there is a number of economical and technical
issues. Currently, oﬀshore wind energy is 20-50% more expensive than both energy obtained
onshore and fossil fuel alternatives [Michael Allen, 2016]. The price is very much inﬂuenced by
transportation, erection and operation of the oﬀshore wind turbines. Technological research,
therefore, is very important to minimize the costs and increase the potential of the oﬀshore
wind turbines.
Wind turbines and foundations are the two most expensive components. The cost breakdown
shows that wind turbine can cost up to 60% of the total price and foundation takes up to
20 % [Chong and Ran, 2016]. It is believed that rising water depths and consequently larger
foundations, can increase the foundation costs up to 30%. The signiﬁcance of foundation op-
timization and cost reduction through technological development is evident.
Monopile (MP) is at the moment the most typically used foundation type for the ﬁxed-bottom
oﬀshore wind farms. Monopile is a single, large-diameter cylindrical structural element of a
deep foundation, driven or drilled vertically into the ground to support all the loads from the
structure above. 3354 monopiles were installed by the end of 2016, representing in total 80.8%
of all wind turbine substructures in European waters [Wind Europe, 2016]. Monopiles have
gained popularity due to their relatively simple concept, economical design and feasibility to
be used in larger water depths. In addition, monopiles can be installed in nearly any type of soil.
Deeper waters as well as more powerful and larger wind turbines require bigger monopiles.
Monopiles are the most suitable for water depths up to 30m whereas jacket foundations are
more suitable for larger water depths. However, simple design made monopiles being very
favorable and they are seen to be installed even in deeper waters. For example, in the end
of 2016, 67 of 1302.5 tons, 7.8m outer diameter and 82.2m long monopiles were installed in

1.2
Scope of the thesis
The aim of this thesis is, by means of numerical simulation, to investigate consequences that
impact driving has on the monopiles with pre-ﬁtted ﬂanges. Fatigue damage of critical points
at ﬂange level is a direct criteria used to measure the damage due to the installation loads. At
the moment, fatigue life reduction due to the monopile installation process is not considered
critical. However, monopile diameters and wall thicknesses are being increased signiﬁcantly,
therefore causing a possible issue in the future. The thesis goal is to determine the fatigue
damage, minimize uncertainties that prevent from using bolted ﬂange connections and provide
guidelines for a cost-eﬀective geometry, further increasing favorable aspects of the connection
and wind turbines in general.
A dynamic ﬁnite element model, representing hammer-ﬂange-monopile-soil interaction, for that
purpose is created. Computational eﬃciency of the model is ensured employing axisymmetric
geometry for the ﬂange and the monopile. The hammer is simulated using lumped masses and
intermediate springs whereas the anvil is modelled as a rigid plate. The soil is represented
using Smith’s rheological model.
The model quality is ensured by direct comparison with
second-source commercial software, speciﬁcally intended for assessment of motions and forces
in foundations during driving- Wave Equation Analysis of Pile driving (WEAP). Only a single
hammer blow is simulated using maximum hammer energy and the total fatigue damage is
assumed to be proportional to the required number of blows in order to reach the desired
monopile embedment depth.
Total fatigue life damage of the ﬂange and the weld is calculated using standard codes for
oﬀshore structures and commonly accepted S-N curves. The ﬂange is modelled with no imper-
fections to ﬁnd lower bound fatigue damage. Upper bound fatigue damage is then estimated by
including safety factors derived in other studies. Finally, input parameter sensitivity analysis
is performed to provide guidelines for safe and economical design of the ﬂange ring.
1.3
Reading guide
The reading guide is meant to provide a better overview of the report layout and contents of
each chapter. The report is divided in 10 chapters where each of them are brieﬂy explained in
the following. Note, that in case of limited reading time, chapters 1, 2, 6-10 are proposed to
be read ﬁrst because they lead to the most important conclusions in this study.
Chapter 1, Introduction gives a background overview about wind energy importance,
monopile foundations popularity and adoption of ﬂanged connections. Scope and delimita-
tion of the thesis is followed by this reading guide.
Chapter 2, State-of-the-art begins with the reasons why ﬂanged connection is favoured
instead of the grouted connection. Design guidelines and relevant oﬀshore standards are de-
scribed including example of conventional bolted ﬂange connection capacity calculation. Fi-

nally, available research regarding ﬂange response during pile-driving is summarized.
Chapter 3, Oﬀshore Pile Driving provides a description of pile driving methods and ex-
plains why impact driving is the most popular. Working principles of hydraulic jack, used for
impact driving, are described. Then, the parameters inﬂuencing the impact load are stated.
The chapter is ﬁnished with introduction to commonly accepted Wave Equation Analysis Pro-

at the ﬂange neck (ﬁllet) is calculated, followed by mesh convergence analysis.
Chapter 6, Dynamic Response of the Monopile and Flange System is one of the most
important chapter in the thesis. After the ﬂange behaviour under static loading is analysed,
dynamic impulse can be modelled. The ﬂange is extended with a MP in order to simulate
stress wave propagation in the structure. Firstly, the ﬂange-MP system is ﬁxed at the toe
and stress wave propagation is simulated using a simpliﬁed rectangular shaped impact load to
capture the essence of the stress wave. Then, ﬁxed boundary conditions are replaced with soil
conditions and rectangular impact load is superseded with hammer representation in order to
obtain a realistic impact load, pile as well as ﬂange movements. The model is validated using
GRLWEAP along the way.
Chapter 7, Fatigue Damage starts with a short introduction to fatigue mechanism and a
method used to determine fatigue damage in this study. Critical points for fatigue damage
are described and the method of fatigue damage calculation is provided for a reference model.
Upper and lower bound fatigue damage is determined.
Chapter 8, Sensitivity Analysis analyses how diﬀerent design parameters aﬀect the fa-
tigue damage at each hot-spot. After ensuring model correctness and versatility in Chapter 6,
the FEM is easily adapted to varying input parameters and the consequent eﬀects are observed.
Chapter 9, Results and Discussion summarizes the eﬀect of diﬀerent design parameters
and provides guidelines for safe and eﬃcient structure.
Chapter 10, Conclusion ﬁnishes the thesis with the most important aspects of the pile
driving modelling, expected fatigue damage limits and the most important design parameters
as well as provides suggestions for future work.

CHAPTER 2
State-of-the-art
This chapter provides an overview of what has been reported in the literature regarding bolted
ﬂange connections and direct pile driving on the ﬂange. Firstly, the tower can be mounted
on the monopile using a transition piece with grouted connection, or using bolted connections.
These two types of assemblies are brieﬂy compared. Next, available design guidelines for the
conventional bolted connection are summarized.
Finally, it is noted that only few papers,
concerning direct pile driving on the ﬂange have been found. Most of the work on the topic
has been done by German engineers and published in their native language. All the latest
knowledge about the ﬂange response during impact driving is summarised.
2.1
Monopile-tower connection alternatives
Grouted connection has been a typical way of connecting two tubular members in oil and gas
industry for more than 30 years. It is deﬁned as a structural connection, which consists of
two concentric tubular sections where the annulus between the outer and the inner tubular
or conical shaped space between the two cones is ﬁlled with grout [DNVGL-ST-0126, 2016].
It is primarily intended to transfer axial forces and force couples through frictional resistance
between steel and the grout. Oﬀshore wind turbine engineers have adopted the grouted connec-
tion design and applied it to connect the monopile with the tower. However, new application
area of the grouted connections still faces issues with this connection, mainly due to large
tower oscillations. In 2010, 600 out of 948 oﬀshore wind turbines installed in European waters
were observed to have had shifted from their initial position [Stancich, 2011]. Dissolution of
the grouting material caused the problem, thus design improvements had to be made. Conical
shaped monopiles were designed that would give larger shear resistance and would contribute
to the tower axial resistance in case of grouting failure. If case of cylindrically shaped monopile,
shear keys were proposed. Due to long experience, the solution remains popular. However,
cumbersome installation method, and failures in the past led to new solutions that are being
applied more often.
Bolted-ﬂange is an alternative way to connect monopile and the tower (or transition piece).
This connection type has been applied in oﬀshore wind turbines for a long time to connect
diﬀerent tower segments. Due to relatively simple concept, the connection has recently been
applied to connect MPs with WTG (wind turbine generator) towers. A steel ﬂange ring is
welded (pre-ﬁtted) to the head of the monopile and another steel ﬂange is welded at the
bottom of the transition piece. Two ﬂanges are bolted together on the site. This solution is
simpler than structural grouting. It allows towers to be installed shortly after the monopile
driving. However, pre-ﬁtted ﬂanges require that pile driving is performed directly on the ﬂange.
Highly dynamic loads induce large stresses in the ﬂange and thus lead to a risk of signiﬁcant

General design guidelines
Flange geometry is dependent on the bolted connection design, that is also used to connect
steel tower segments (here termed conventional bolted ring-ﬂange connection). Standards rel-
evant for design of oﬀshore structures, including conventional bolted connection are shortly
summarized here.
The most recognized international design standard for wind turbines is published by Interna-
tional Electrotechnical Commission(IEC) and the regulations IEC 61400-1 Design requirements
as well as IEC 61400-3 Design Requirements for Oﬀshore Wind Turbines are the most relevant.
This international standard is followed by classiﬁcation organizations which grant certiﬁcation
for wind turbines. Two of the biggest organizations Germanischer Lloyd from Germany and
Det Norske Veritas from Norway have merged their forces in 2012 and now they have a common
standard DNV-GL. For the purpose of the foundation design and veriﬁcation, DNV-GL-ST-
0126:Support Structures for Wind Turbines is the basic standard.
Despite of international standards, national regulations are applied, particularly in Denmark,

there, wind turbines are legally considered as buildings. All of these national guidelines match
IEC 61400-1, but also include some speciﬁc national adjustments. In addition to these, a com-
mon European standard- Eurocode 1993: Design of steel structures is generally referred to.

Flange vibrations is another important phenomenon taking place during direct pile-driving on
the ﬂange. Firstly, stress wave with constant energy dissipation is traveling down and up the
monopile, aﬀecting the displacement at the top of the monopile for ≈0.3 s as noted in [Janele
et al., 2015]. During pile-driving, MP together with the ﬂange experience a temporary set
after each blow. The temporary set is followed by a rebound due to elastic deformation of the
soil. This is named vertical pile oscillation δv inducing compression/tension stresses in the pile.
Secondly, the ﬂange oscillates around the support δb due to inertia eﬀects caused by impact
load. Higher frequency oscillations here are observed. Bending stresses in the ﬂange inner and
outer sides of the shell are consequently introduced. The stresses on either side of the shell are
highly dependent on the contact width. Width smaller than half the shell thickness t induce
higher bending stresses in the outer shell edge and in case of contact width being larger than
half the thickness, higher stresses in the inner shell edge are observed. The combination of
stresses due to vertical pile oscillation and ﬂange bending oscillation make the total stresses
contributing to the fatigue damage. The optimal solution is ﬁnding contact width that induces
equal stresses on both sides.

Concluding remarks
To sum up, there is relatively little research regarding the topic. The bolted connections are

driving factors are still present. Actual ﬂange bending oscillation contribution to the fatigue

Oﬀshore pile driving
This chapter provides background knowledge about oﬀshore pile installation by impact load.
Firstly, most common installation methods are shortly described, followed by a more detailed
explanation of the impact driving principles.
Hydraulic hammer setup during driving and
working principles are explained and commonly used tools to perform driveability analysis are
pointed out. This chapter will provide basis for impact load modelling as well as qualitative
evaluation of it’s magnitude and shape that is used in Chapter 6.
3.1
Common installation methods
There are three common methods to install the monopile: drilling, vibratory driving and im-
pact driving. Characteristics of these methods are shortly described here.
Drilling is usually used in hard soils where driving the monopiles can damage them. The soil
has to be able to self-support during drilling. A hole into seabed is drilled by using jack-up
barges. Monopile is placed in the hole and it is stabilized with concrete, ﬁlling the gap between
the the monopile and the rock. This method has for example been used in Bockstigen oﬀshore
wind farm in Sweden due to a rocky bed. Monopiles of 2.1 m diameter were grouted in 2.4 m
wide and 10 m deep borehole. A combination of drilling and driving is also possible and, for
instance, has been implemented in Barrow Oﬀshore Wind Farm. Monopiles of 4.75 m outer
diameter and length between 30.2 m and 40.7 m, were installed. Drilling, however is expensive
and therefore not a preferable method to install monopile foundations.
Vibratory driving is based on counter-rotating eccentric masses, placed on the top of the
monopile that reduce ground resistance.
Reduced ground resistance allows penetration of
the pile using only its own weight. As for example, Anholt and Riﬀgat oﬀshore wind parks
have used this method. It is proven to be almost 10 times faster than impact driving. Due to
smaller noise, this concept is also more environmentally friendly [OﬀshoreWIND.biz, 2017(a)].
However, due to uncertainty of vibratory eﬀects on the monopile and long term soil properties,
it is rarely being used.
Impact driving is currently the most popular monopile installation method. The impact process
starts with a hammer (large mass) lifted in a certain height and released to fall vertically or
sometimes also accelerated with gas pressure. The ram, with initiated potential energy, falls on
the head of the monopile and induces a huge energy impulse, making the monopile penetrate a
small distance in the bed soil. The process is repeated until the monopile reaches the required
embedment depth. Diﬀerent types of hammers can be used, but nowadays diesel and hydraulic
hammers are most common due to their high energy and high working speed. The analysis of

3.2
Hydraulic jack impact driving
Large diameter monopiles with or without pre-installed ﬂanges are most commonly driven in
the soil using impact loading by application of hydraulic hammer. Impact load is a deﬁned
as a large force applied during a short time period when two or more bodies collide. A very
simple example of impact load is a nail pounding. A number of high velocity impacts is used
in order to overcome the static friction between the nail and the base. The same phenomenon
but on a much larger scale happens during pile-driving. A striking part of the impact hammer
(termed ’ram’) is lifted to an appropriate height (termed ’stroke’) and then dropped on the pile
head certain number of times per minute (termed ’blow rate’). The monopile is then gradually
penetrating the soil by overcoming friction between the soil and the pile walls as well as the
base until an intended embedment depth is reached.
Full hydraulic hammer setup combines a solid one-piece ram with secondary accessories, see
Figure 3.1. Impacting steel pile does not require any cushion because there is no danger in
material spalling. However an intermediate adaptors between hammer and the pile are needed
to adjust for diﬀerent monopile sizes and accommodate load transfer. Solid steel anvils and
conical followers are used for that purpose. In order to ensure a suﬃcient stability for driving,
the hammer is also equipped with a pile sleeve.

Pile-driving hammers are rated according to their output/striking energy. Here two types of
hammers are present: single-acting and double-acting hammer. Single acting hammer is lifted
at the top of the stroke by use of pressurized gas creating potential energy equal to product
of ram weight and stroke. The hammer is then released and freely falls on the anvil that is
sitting on top of the pile. The potential energy is changed to kinetic energy. The cycles are
then repeated. The rated energy of the single-acting hammer is maximum potential energy.
Diﬀerently, double-acting hammer does not fall freely, but it is accelerated by pressurized gas
until maximum acceleration of 2g. The rated energy of the double-acting hammer is the sum
of the maximum potential energy and the energy induced by the pressurized gas. This type of
hammer reduces the necessary maximum stroke, increases the blow rate and allows operation
under every inclination.
The hammer rate energy is not the actual energy transferred to the monopile. Energy losses
due to friction in the hammer cylinder, back pressure losses in ﬂuid ports and diﬀerent as-
sembly element deformation occur. Therefore, each hammer type also has a related energy
eﬃciency factor η. The hammer eﬃciency describes the ratio of the transmitted energy to the
rated energy. Currently, largest hydrohammers have energy of up to 4000 kJ and the eﬃciency
factor is between 0.85 and 0.95 [IHC, 2016].
Two load phases are identiﬁed during pile-driving. The ﬁrst phase is the potential energy con-
version to kinetic energy, taking place during downwards movement of the ram. During the
second phase, the kinetic energy is converted to impact force. Right after the contact between
the ram(anvil) and the monopile, a force pulse is induced in the foundation structure. Energy
losses during the second phase occur due to ram ad anvil elasticity, ram rebound, friction
between the ram and the anvil, anvil and the pile head as well as tolerances and misalignment
in the system. The energy of the force pulse, after accounting for these energy losses (termed
’enthru’) deﬁnes amplitude and the shape of the force pulse.
The pulse acts as a dilatational (compressional) stress wave and travels down the monopile
at supersonic speed. During travel time, the stress wave loses part of the energy due to the
soil shearing occuring at the interface between pile wall and the soil media (hysteretic damp-
ing). After the stress wave reaches the monopile toe, part of the energy is transmitted to the
soil in form of heat and plastic deformation (radiation damping). The rest of the energy is
reﬂected back to the monopile due to impedance mismatch between the monopile and the soil.
A smaller stress wave travels back in the monopile until it reaches the hammer (anvil) and
part of it will be reﬂected back due to impedance mismatch between the anvil and the pile
[Pop, 2014]. Numerical analysis of driven foundation pile behaviour is called wave equation
analysis and it is further described in Section 3.4.

measure during the impact driving. Analytical methods have been derived for a simpliﬁed

is commonly used. In general, there are two ways of modelling dynamic impulse load:
1. Applying a dynamic load as a force-time function on the head of the monopile, either
from a second-source data, such as WEAP or using analytical estimation.
2. Modelling a ram with assigned mass and initial velocity that interacts with the pile
during the simulation.

3.4
Wave equation analysis
One dimensional wave equation was derived by [Smith, 1960] and is still being used to deter-
mine the number of blows for particular soil resistance and hammer parameters, required to
penetrate the pile into the ground. Wave equation allows to ﬁnd the hammer energy transferred
into the pile and stress levels at any segment of the pile. In this model it is assumed that the
eﬀective hammer’s input energy is a product of the total pile’s resistance and the permanent set.
The ram and associated equipment such as helmet, helmet cushion, anvil, anvil adapter and
the pile are represented as series of weights and weightless springs as shown in the Figure
3.4. Pile and soil interaction is modelled using simple rheological model (using elasto-plastic
springs and viscous dashpots). The ram and pile cap are represented as lumped masses with
no elasticity due to being short, rigid and heavy objects. The pile is modelled as a system
of lumped masses and elastic springs due to being heavy and simultaneously long enough to
be compressible. Impact load in the model is simulated by employing hammer velocity in the

Impact driving, performed directly on the ﬂange surface, causes a stress wave propagation
through the system of the ﬂange and the monopile.
The stress wave is dependent on the
impact magnitude, geometry and material properties of the structural system as well as inter-
action with the soil media. Central part of this research is to determine the impact load eﬀect
on the ﬂange. No analytical solution exists for this complex problem, thus numerical modelling
is required to capture the most aspects of it.
A model representing a ﬂange, a monopile, a hammer and soil media is to be created in a
computationally eﬃcient way. In order to model the physical problem accurately, a sequential
modelling approach has been adopted. Diﬀerent phenomena is analysed separately and veriﬁed
through analytical solutions before building up the ﬁnal model.
The modelling is performed using commercial FEA software ABAQUS, v.6.16 by Dassault Sys-
temes. Preprocessing is performed in CAE environment and numerical analysis is carried out
using ABAQUS/Explicit solver. The operating system is based on x64 PC with i7-4790 CPU
@ 3.6 GHz and 4 cores as well as 16 GB of RAM.
This chapter will introduce the reader to the most important numerical modelling techniques
used in order to create the ﬁnal FEM throughout the report. Underlying assumptions, possible
cause of errors, limitations and available modelling alternatives are described. In case if the
reader is familiar with the particular software methodology, this chapter may be skipped.
However, note, that references are later made to the current chapter.
4.1
Planning numerical analysis
In order to understand the nature of the problem and relate it to quantitative modelling tech-
niques, the most important aspects that need to be included in the analysis were initially
assessed.
To begin with, a preliminary analysis by analytic methods should be performed
to anticipate the computed results qualitatively, quantitatively, or preferably both. A static
model, with loads close to dynamic, is suggested to be used in the beginning as it is easier to
evaluate the results. Finally, results are also estimated in order to decide whether elasto-plastic
material deﬁnition is required or only linear material behavior is suﬃcient.
==PAGE:38==
22
4 Numerical modelling methodology
To begin modelling of the ﬂange under impact load, only one blow was decided to be simulated,
therefore analysis time would be relatively short. Brief and transient dynamic problems, such
as impact loading, have special purpose ﬁnite element formulation called explicit analysis. It
simulates the problem more eﬃciently than standard FE formulation. Moreover, stress wave
traveling across a FE mesh tends to create noise in higher-order elements, thus lower-order
elements are preferable.
FE models very often employ symmetry conditions in order to reduce the size and compu-
tational time of the model.
However, in dynamic analysis, vibration modes can be easily
overlooked. Hence, generally it is recommended to avoid symmetry conditions or perform a
separate analysis to determine mode shapes and their frequencies. Despite the fact that it is
diﬃcult to model ﬂange imperfections in 2D, axisymmetrical model is very eﬃcient for this
problem and will be used with a special care.
Finally, consistent units must be used and reliability of the geometry, boundary conditions
and loads need to be ensured. Self-weight is not to be neglected and the model is always to be
critically evaluated.
4.2
Explicit vs Implicit analysis
Abaqus has two time integration methods, namely explicit and implicit. Depending on the
problem, an appropriate method has to be chosen. In cases where both methods can be ap-
plied, the most eﬃcient method is used. Below, a short summary of these two methods is
provided according to [Dessault Systemes, 2016]
Abaqus/Implicit method applies the load incrementally and solves simultaneous system of equa-
tions at each load step. A large number of equations during one load increment have to be
solved in order to reach equilibrium, therefore the computational cost is high. Usually this type
of analysis is used for non-linear problems. The time increment is controlled automatically by
the solver, according to accuracy and convergence criteria. The time increment is much smaller
than in explicit analysis.
Abaqus/Explicit on the other hand, does not iterate but advance the kinematic state from the
end of the previous increment. It integrates the equations of motion in time using central dif-
ference method. Position, velocity and acceleration from the previous time increment are used
to determine the following time step kinematic conditions. If the problem is expected to be
linearly elastic, this approach is suitable. The load is applied in a single step, but plastiﬁcation
is not veriﬁed because no equilibrium equations are solved. The solution is conditionally stable.
When analyzing dynamic problem, in fact, both time integration methods can be used. How-
ever, explicit is chosen because of its better performance during short period load, as in the
case of impact load. Stress wave propagation is a high speed dynamic event and the explicit
method was originally developed for it.

4.3 2D axisymmetric vs 3D model

4.3
2D axisymmetric vs 3D model
Axisymmetric modelling is suitable for the problems where 3D geometry can be converted to
2D as shown in the Figure 4.1. 2D plane is revolved around axis of symmetry and is used to
model axisymmetric geometry and axisymmetric loading, such as the ﬂange ring.
Eﬃciency of using axisymmetric model rather than 3D can be described using a simple exam-
ple. Let’s assume a 360◦model created with 3D brick elements that each spans 10◦. Using
simple, 3D brick elements with 8 nodes and 6 degrees of freedom (dof) each, the total number
of degrees of freedom would be 144 times more than using rectangular axisymmetric elements
with 3 dof at each of 4 nodes. Moreover, in axisymmetric model, mesh reﬁnement by a factor
of 2 in each direction creates 4 times more elements. On the other hand, 3D model under
the same circumstances would give 8 times more elements. Thus, a signiﬁcant diﬀerence in
number of elements and computational time is present. The ﬁnal dynamic model will be based
on axisymmetric geometry.
Figure 4.1: 3D to 2D conversion using axis of symmetry.
First three dofs in 3D model elements represent translations in 3 directions and last three dofs
represent rotations about the equivalent axis. Degrees of freedom in axisymmetric elements
are exceptional. Dof 1 represents translation in radial r-direction, dof 2 represents translation
in vertical z-direction and dof 6 represents rotation in the r-z plane. Similarly stress com-
ponents in axisymmetric elements are as following: S11- radial stress, S22- axial stress, S33-
circumferential (hoop) stress, S12- shear stress. Loads are to be input as integrated around
the circumference within axisymmetric model, meaning that the total value of the input load
has to be always used.
4.4
Finite element type and mesh
Finite elements are fundamental components of FE analysis and it is vital to choose an ap-
propriate element type for each problem. Since an explicit method will be used in the present
analysis, only a subset of all the elements is available. Due to simulation of impact loading,
ﬁrst-order elements are to be used. They represent stress wave more accurately than second-
order elements due to their lumped mass. Moreover, ﬁrst-order triangles are to be avoided
because their large stiﬀness requires a dense mesh to reach accurate results.

Abaqus/Standard oﬀers full and reduced integration elements whereas Abaqus/Explicit oﬀers
reduced integration elements, modiﬁed tetrahedron, modiﬁed triangle and fully integrated ﬁrst-

time due to lower-order integration to form the stiﬀness of the elements. However, ﬁrst order,

hourglassing and can be avoided by use of built-in function hourglass-control in combinations
with reasonably ﬁne mesh [Dessault Systemes, 2016].

4.7
Computational time reduction
Explicit dynamic analysis allows reducing computational time by few methods, namely by re-
ducing number of increments required, introducing mass scaling or using selective subcycling.
By reducing number of required increments we artiﬁcially reduce the time period of the event,
T, thus we obtain the results at the points inquired before the analysis. This type of trick
is to be used with care because if the simulation time is increased too much, inertia forces
may increase and will change the predicted response. However, in case of material being rate
dependent, this speeding up method cannot be used.
Mass scaling is applied by scaling the material density and thereby reducing the ratio of the
event time for wave propagation across an element but simultaneously leaving the event time
ﬁxed.
The dilatational wave speed is dependent on Poison’s ratio, elasticity modulus and
material density, see Equation (4.8), thus increasing material density, the wave speed will be
decreased, resulting in larger time step and reduced computational time. This method cannot
be used in problems where inertia inﬂuences results, as the inertia will be also scaled.
Finally, in case if the model consists diﬀerent sizes of elements, subcycles can be made for
the larger elements to use larger time increments. Otherwise, the software chooses to use the
smallest time incrementation needed for all the model. This method, as later will be seen,
would be beneﬁcial for the dynamic models, however due to limited project time has not been
implemented.

4.8
Damping in explicit analysis
In addition to dashpots, and inelastic material behavior, general Rayleigh material damping
can be used as energy dissipation mechanism in Abaqus/Explicit. Rayleigh damping is intro-
duced using two coeﬃcients-βR proportional to stiﬀness damping and αR proportional to mass
damping.
In addition to Rayleigh damping, Abaqus introduces bulk viscosity damping in order to im-
prove the high-speed dynamic modelling. Bulk viscosity is associated with volumetric element
straining and is divided into two types, namely linear and quadratic. Linear bulk viscosity
exists in all the elements to damp the highest frequencies. Quadratic bulk viscosity is applied
on the solid continuum elements and is used in order to avoid element volume collapse in case
of high velocities. Small scale damping in form of bulk viscosity is to be kept in mind when
performing the dynamic simulations.
4.9
Contacts
Abaqus does not automatically recognize mechanical contact between two bodies, therefore
contact has to be speciﬁed by the user.
Abaqus/Explicit allows to choose between general
contacts and contact pairs, see Figure 4.3.
General contact allows to deﬁne a contact between multiple objects within the model. All
surfaces can interact with each other but only a penalty method is available. General contact is
considerably faster than contact pair. Contact pairs, on the other hand, deﬁne only interaction
between two particular surfaces.
Figure 4.3: Principle of contact modelling in Abaqus, left- general contact, right-contact pairs.
There are two diﬀerent methods to enforce contact constraints, namely kinematic contact al-
gorithm and penalty. The kinematic contact algorithm uses kinematic predictor and strictly
constraints two surfaces, preventing from any penetration. It also conserves energy momen-
tum, thus some energy is absorbed during the impact. This energy may be signiﬁcant in high
velocity problems where high mesh reﬁnement at the contact surface is advised to be used.
The penalty contact enforces penetration weaker, but is used for more general problems, such
as impact with a rigid body. That requires extra stiﬀness introduction in the model, that also

4.10
Constraints and connections
Constraints are introduced in order to partially of fully exclude degrees of freedom of node
groups or couple their motion to a single node. Constraints are useful to couple interaction
between a reference node and surface node. Standard applications are application of loads,
boundary conditions, model interaction with connectors.
Connectors are used in order to model mechanical relationship between two points in the model.
They are assigned using wire feature and can describe connection between deformable or rigid
elements. Connector elements can deﬁne what degrees of freedom are associated as for instance
allow to model a welded, hinged or planar motion only. Basic engineering elements such as
springs and dashpots are suited only for implicit analysis, thus explicit simulation requires use
of connectors. Axial connectors, shown in Figure 4.4, for instance, serve as link to provide

This inﬁnite stress at relatively small area is called stress singularity. Stress singularities are
caused for instance due to sharp corners or point loads. Luckily St.Venant’s principle states
that the eﬀect of local disturbances to uniform stress ﬁelds remain local, meaning that further

area is treated unaﬀected is equal to the disturbance size. Thus, the model can be used if
stresses at the location of disturbance are not wanted. It has to be noted, that displacements
are not inﬂuenced by the stress singularities and hence are correct everywhere.

concentration occurs. Stress concentration resembles stress singularities but stress converges

5.4
Concluding remarks
Static analysis of the ﬂange showed that it is feasible to model ﬂange using 3D or axisymmetric
geometry. The ﬂange was observed to be bending around monopile wall, hence wall thickness
is one of the critical parts in the structure. In order to observe accurate stresses in the ﬂange
ﬁllet, minimum 80 elements are needed. Global size of the element in the ﬂange should not
be smaller than 0.01m. Finally, stress concentration factor can reach a factor of 2 within the
ﬂange ﬁllet, but it is to be evaluated in each case individually.
Static analysis of the ﬂange laid a good foundation for understanding the ﬂange behaviour.
Now more complex phenomenon of pile driving with dynamic impact load can be analysed.

When the hammer rams the monopile, a dilatational (compressional) stress wave is created.
The stress wave travels down the MP and back up again. Flange oscillations are aﬀected by
stress wave movement. Due to the pile being a long, elastic structure, a signiﬁcant pile con-
traction occurs, that also inﬂuences the ﬂange movement. Therefore, there is a need to model
the monopile in addition to the ﬂange ring.

monopile. Initially the pile was assumed to be placed on a rigid seabed. No embedment is

6.1.2
Vertical ﬂange displacement
Undamped stress wave behavior in the monopile has been assessed. Now, the ﬂange displace-
ment due to rectangular impact load will be evaluated. The simulation of the axisymmetric
model was run for 0.1 s. Data for 10000 evenly distributed time points during this time period
was extracted to observe ﬂange oscillations.
Firstly, vertical displacement at two points on top of the ﬂange tip (Point D) and at ﬂange
contact with the anvil (Point E) were extracted, see Figure 6.3. Impact activates the ﬂange
that in addition to the pile displacement, has local, high frequency, bending oscillations. Pile
contracts around 12 mm whereas the ﬂange moves in the vertical direction by only about few
millimetres. Free ﬂange oscillations are observed to be decreasing. Since no material damping
has been input in the software, it is thought that this damping occurs due to linear bulk vis-
cosity used in ABAQUS to damp element ”ringing” as explained in Section 4.8 on page 27.
Furthermore, increase in vertical ﬂange movement at 24, 48, 72 ms is noticed. Flange oscil-
lations increased due to axial movement caused by undamped stress wave. Oscillations are
undesirable not only because they induce large bending stresses in the ﬂange ﬁllet, but also
because additional contact between the ﬂange and the anvil may occur. The top of the ﬂange
surface is usually designed with a predeﬁned horizontal contact width that is followed by an
inclination. The contact width is usually around half of the MP thickness and is placed at
the outermost distance of the ﬂange. Due to ﬂange movement upwards, the outermost point
D may hit the anvil and additional stresses in the structure would be induced due to bending
moment, see Section 2.3 on page 8. Therefore the ﬂange geometry is adjusted to have a gap
at the tip to allow undisturbed oscillations and prevent undesired stresses.

Firstly, pile plugging is an uncertain phenomenon that changes the distribution of the shaft/end
bearing. Unplugged soil bearing capacity would consist of only annular end bearing and both
internal and external shaft friction. Since the monopile diameter is large, it is very unlikely
that plugging would occur, thus the foundation will act primarily as friction pile. Secondly,
internal friction is diﬃcult to estimate, but usually the same eﬀective stress on the inner wall
is considered as on the outer wall.
The driving process also reduces the shaft friction. Static resistance to driving (SRD) has to
be applied for driveability analysis. Due to soil friction fatigue, partial loss of soil resistance
occurs.
The partial loss factor is determined as ratio between SRD and long term static
resistance (LTSR), fs = LTSR/SRD. However, in case if any waiting time during driving a
monopile takes place, the soil regains the strength.
The capacity reduction factor due to
waiting time is deﬁned as fRd = RUR/RUF, where RUR is capacity reduced by the action of
dynamic energy and RUF is full ultimate capacity.
Note, that soil friction fatigue can be
modelled more precisely, as for example using Heerema’s fatigue approach [GRLWEAP, 2010],
but in this case it has been implemented by simply limiting the maximum unit soil resistance.

Axial displacement was plotted for 1 s in order to determine damping in the structure. It is seen

6.5
Concluding remarks
Existing studies on the ﬂange response suggest the use of 3D models. 3D model can represent
hammer-ﬂange-monopile-soil system comprehensively. 3D model can also simulate geometry
imperfections. However, detailed analysis requires long simulation time.
Axisymmetric model was proven to yield good correlation with GRLWEAP in terms of top
pile displacement, impact enthru and system energy. Detailed hammer modelling is necessary
to achieve a correct impact load. Lumped masses and intermediate springs were shown to be
a convenient and eﬃcient way to represent the hammer.
Bed soil resistance during pile driving was represented using system of non-linear springs and
dashpots. Permanent pile set and reﬂective stress wave indicate satisfactory monopile bound-
ary conditions.
One dimensional stress wave equation analysis of monopile driving by impact was successfully
transferred to detailed FE model. FEM was enhanced with a ﬂange ring, enabling the cap-
ture of localized stress concentration and high frequency bending oscillations. Detailed fatigue
damage at the top of the monopile can now be determined.

CHAPTER 7
Fatigue damage
This chapter aims to determine fatigue life damage at the critical points of the ﬂange caused by
impact driving. Firstly, recommended practice in [DNVGL-RP-C203, 2016] is shortly described
with emphasis on the most important safety measures related to fatigue damage. A single blow
is simulated using a FE reference model with pre-selected parameters. Driveability analysis,
suitable for fatigue damage calculation is provided in order to estimate total number of blows
that ﬂange experiences. Stress time-series are extracted at 5 diﬀerent hot-spots and by use
of Rainﬂow cycle counting, Palmgren-Miner fatigue damage rules and standard oﬀshore steel
S-N curves, the upper and lower bound fatigue damage limits are evaluated. The chapter
also serves as an intermediate explanatory material before parameter sensitivity analysis is
performed.
7.1
Fatigue mechanism
Fatigue is a deﬁnition standing for material weakening caused by cyclic loading. Fatigue dam-
age is usually observed in form of localized and progressive cracking at construction details
subjected to concentrated loads such as sharp corners, bolted connections, welds, etc. Two
phases of crack propagation are distinguished, namely crack initiation and crack propagation.
Crack initiation occurs at micro-structure level between two grains of the material, usually
at the surface level, because of smaller restraints to adjoining grains in comparison with the
grains in subsurface. Due to initiation phase taking place at micro-structure level, even low
stress levels can cause local plastic deformation. The crack starts to grow when the surface
conditions are no longer governed by surface but the underlying grain layers instead.
Crack initiation and growth is governed by stress diﬀerence, also called stress range that the
structural detail is subjected to.
Crack propagation initiates when the tensile stresses are
present in the load cycle, thus making the mean stress important, see Figure 7.1. Three diﬀer-
ent possible stress ranges with diﬀerent means are shown. Particular attention is paid to the
stress range (c) where the detail during the full cycle is subjected to tensile stresses even in
the case when external compression pulsating load is applied.
One must account for combination of all stresses that the detail is subjected to in order to
predict the fatigue damage accurately. Welded details are particularly important to be as-
sessed because stress concentration due to geometry imperfections, weld defects and residual
stresses are present. Typical non-linear stress distribution at butt weld is shown in the Figure
7.2 where σm is membrane stress, σb is bending stress and σnlp is non-linear peak stress.

7.2
Recommended practice
In addition to ultimate capacity, fatigue limit state is very often governing the design of
the oﬀshore structures.
Large number and high amplitude load cycles make the structure
dependent on the fatigue resistance.
The largest monopile life damage consists of fatigue
damage during driving and operation. Other fatigue damage Di occurs during transportation,
decommissioning, etc. Thus, the total fatigue life damage can be expressed as
Dt = Ddriving + Doperation + Di

Two categories of fatigue loading are usually distinguished- low cycle fatigue, generating few

with lower stress amplitude. The loading above 104 cycles is considered high cycle fatigue that
most oﬀshore structures experience. Number of blows, required to drive the monopile into the
seabed, can easily reach 4000-5000. In case of direct pile driving on the ﬂange, each single

Generally, in addition to the thickness eﬀect, many other safety factors are to be included in
the design. Measures to account for (1) corrosion eﬀects, (2) material yielding limit, (3) mean
stress eﬀect, (4) weld shape eﬀect, (5) misalignment between two welded plates, (6) stress
concentration and (7) imperfections result in increased stress ranges. The ﬂange is analysed
in an elastic material range during pile driving with the vertical nominal stresses alternating
from tension to compression, thus (1-4) eﬀects are assumed to not inﬂuence the results. From
experience, it is known that misalignment between two plates gives a relatively small increase
in stresses, thus it will also be omitted. Since the stresses are extracted using a detailed FEM,
the SCF is already included.
Finally, manufacturing imperfections are considered, among which the ﬂatness tolerance of
the ﬂange and the anvil is one of the most important as described in Section 2.3. The main
disadvantage of the axisymmetric models is the lack of asymmetric geometry, hence another
method has to be used to account for imperfections. Here, it is referred to research [Gollub
et al., 2014] that analysed combination of diﬀerent imperfections of two rings in contact. 5.5 m
diameter rings were used in the study. Anvil imperfections were modelled with 2 mm amplitude,
and 40° sinusoidal wave length. Flange imperfections were modelled as 120° sinusoidal waves
with 0.5 mm amplitude. The resulting increase in local stress reached 46 % at outer ﬂange
ﬁber and 39 % at inner ﬂange ﬁber. 3500 kJ hammer energy in the example was applied. It is
known that the ﬂange ring would be ﬂattened during driving process, thus consequently, from
a conservative point of view, stresses will be increased by a factor of fI = 1.4 in this study
before determining the fatigue damage. This result will provide upper bound estimate of the
fatigue damage.

Fatigue damage was determined for a reference model with a single combination of parameters.
The fatigue damage has been found to be signiﬁcant, thus the ﬂange design is to be assessed
carefully.
This chapter will analyse fatigue damage sensitivity due to several parameters,
namely, impact energy, monopile wall thickness, circular ﬁllet radius and elliptical ﬁllet ratio,
ultimate soil resistance, contact width and ﬂange length. Sensitivity analysis serves as initial
step to ﬁnd out which parameters have the largest inﬂuence on the fatigue damage. Design
of experiments is then performed and ﬁnally, suﬃcient simulation time required to observe
fatigue damage is determined.
8.1
Parameter screening
Sensitivity analysis has been performed by changing one parameter at a time and keeping the
rest of them equivalent to the reference model. A simulation is performed for each model
for 1 s. It is reminded, that the damage is calculated for Nblows = 3000, DFF = 2, and using
upper bound imperfection factor fI = 1.4. The reference model parameters for convenience are
reproduced in Table 8.1. The simulation results are provided as relative fatigue damage in
relation to the damage of the reference model given in the Table 8.2. Arbitrary number of
simulations have been performed for each parameter, dependent on whether clear parameter
sensitivity was observed. Each parameter’s inﬂuence is shortly explained below.
Hammer Energy. In addition to 3000 kJ hammer, 2500 kJ and 3500 kJ hammers were analysed.
100 % hammer energy and 3000 blows were kept constant. In general, smaller hammer results
in smaller damage and vice versa. It is observed that damage at the inner ﬁllet is aﬀected the
most because smaller hammer energy results in smaller pile displacement and smaller inertial
eﬀects of the ﬂange that lead to smaller oscillations. It is noticed that outer hot-spots are not
inﬂuenced as much as the inner points.
Wall thickness. Eﬀect of the wall thickness is checked keeping initial contact width c = 30 mm
constant. First of all, a huge damage increase in simulation 3 with t = 50 mm is observed. The
ﬂange oscillates due to bending in the wall. Small wall thickness results in smaller bending
resistance and larger wall/ﬂange deﬂection. An inadequate wall thickness can give excessive
ﬂange oscillation. Moreover, it is noticed that 100 mm and 125 mm wall thickness yield nearly
the same results, the damage approaches zero for the inner ﬁllet and inner weld with increasing
wall thickness. The absolute damage at the outer ﬁllet and outer weld are smaller than 10 %.

Results and Discussion
Currently, the fatigue damage induced during pile driving is assessed using WEAP software.
This approach takes into consideration only primary and reﬂected stress wave propagation,
but does not include higher frequency bending oscillations of the ﬂange. Direct pile driving
on the ﬂange was simulated by use of FEM and resultant fatigue damage was determined in
this study. The results showed that the high-cycle oscillations caused 49 % and 70 % fatigue
damage at the inner neck ﬁllet and inner weld respectively for the reference model depicted as
simulation 0. Outer neck ﬁllet and outer weld were mostly eﬀected by low-cycle stress wave
propagation in the monopile where high-frequency oscillations contributed only 4 % and 21
% respectively. Note, that the impact load in this case was applied on the monopile with 70
mm wall thickness and 30 mm contact width between the anvil and the ﬂange. Therefore, the
initial eccentricity led to outer points being exposed to larger stress. The fatigue damage was
estimated using 3000 kJ hammer, 3000 blows and DFF=2. The maximum damage of 27.65 %
was determined at the outer weld and not the inner ﬂange ﬁllet as expected in the beginning
of this study. Sensitivity analysis showed that the fatigue damage can be signiﬁcantly reduced
by controlling ﬂange geometry and driving energy.
Sensitivity analysis
Parameter sensitivity analysis evaluated fatigue damage dependency on impact energy, MP
wall thickness, neck ﬁllet radius and shape, soil resistance to driving, contact width, MP diam-
eter, ﬂange width and ﬂange distance to weld. All the parameters were proven to be important,
among which the impact energy, MP wall thickness and contact width were shown to be the
most critical.
Smaller hammer energy reduces stress levels in the monopile. Parametric study results showed
decrease in fatigue damage up to 74 % using 2500 kJ hammer and increase in fatigue damage
up to 92 % using 3500 kJ hammer. The results were compared with 3000 kJ hammer working
in it’s full capacity, see Table 8.2 on page 74. HiLo technique is therefore suggested, meaning
that higher number of blows with lower hammer energy is preferred. However, the beneﬁts of
using lower hammer energy cannot be directly derived from these results because lower hammer
energy requires more blows to drive the monopile until the intended embedment depth and vice
versa. The resultant beneﬁt of HiLo technique will be smaller. In addition, the hammer en-
ergy is to be controlled in order to avoid local ﬂange plastiﬁcation at the contact with the anvil.
The MP wall thickness was shown to be critical parameter during pile driving. Inadequate wall
thickness of 50 mm showed large amplitude ﬂange bending that caused excessive fatigue dam-
age at the hot-spots. On the other hand, 70 mm wall thickness was determined to be suﬃcient
for the reference model. In addition, the contact width between the ﬂange and the anvil was
==PAGE:98==
82

proven to be important parameter that distributes the impact load across the monopile wall
thickness. For this matter, design of experiments showed strong correlation between the MP
wall thickness and the contact width for the outer points, see Figure 8.2 on page 78. The inner
points were mainly dependent on the high-cycle ﬂange oscillations, thus the MP wall thickness
governed the damage. The total fatigue damage is not critical ≤10% if suﬃcient MP wall
thickness and ﬁnal contact width, taking into consideration anvil deformation, are designed.
Finally, the ﬂange width and ﬁllet radius have been shown to be important parameters aﬀect-
ing the fatigue damage at the inner ﬁllet. Longer and heavier ﬂange induces larger bending
moment and causes larger amplitude stress oscillations. The smaller ﬂange width is desired,
however the minimum dimension will be governed by capacity of the bolted ﬂange connection.
The damage at the ﬂange ﬁllet, however, can be reduced using elliptical transition radius. The
elliptical shape ratio with b = 50 mm was shown not to eﬀect the results greatly, thus ratio
a/b = 0.5 (see Figure 7.5 on page 69) is proposed to provide smooth transition. Elliptical
radius is also desired in order to reduce the distance from the wall thickness until the bolt,
thereby reducing total ﬂange width.
Model Precision
This study is based purely on numerical analysis and the validation has been relied on re-
sult comparison with GRLWEAP as well as similar studies. Firstly, static numerical analysis
showed feasibility of using axisymmetric model, enabling relatively short simulation time. The
enthru shape, shown in Figure 6.10 as well as primary and secondary stress wave arrival times
at the top of the monopile matched the results with GRLWEAP very well. The impact load
magnitude was higher in numerical results by maximum 3 % for the primary and by 6 % for
reﬂected stress waves. The top displacement time-series, however, showed lack of the energy
dissipation through the soil media, that was represented as elasto-plastic soil springs and dash-
pots. The reason can lie within approximation of the springs and their levels. The embedment
depth of 30 m was subdivided in 10 equal size layers and the resultant soil springs were applied
at the middle of each layer. More uniform spring and dashpot application would naturally
yield more accurate results. Furthermore, the gravity load application could have had inﬂu-
ence to the top displacement response. Force equilibrium including gravity load in GRLWEAP
is solved before the dynamic analysis begins, however gravity in the numerical model had to be
applied as an instantaneous load at the beginning of the dynamic simulation. Finally, Abaqus
suggests to check the model adequacy by evaluating energy conservation in the model. The
energy balance was found to yield expected model behavior with total energy being constant
throughout the full simulation.
Due to very small elements in the ﬂange neck, the simulation of a single dynamic model for 1
s took up to 20 hours. Long simulation time was also caused by general contact between the
ﬂange and the rigid plate as well as double precision, that was necessary to ensure accuracy
of highly dynamic impact during very small time period. Time convergence analysis showed
that 0.6 s is necessary to account for 95 % of the fatigue damage at the inner ﬁllet and and at
the inner weld, the points where the damage is governed by the high-cycle fatigue. Hence, the
axisymmetric model here is proven to be feasible. 3D geometry would require to signiﬁcantly
lower minimum element size in the ﬂange ﬁllet in order to achieve reasonable simulation time.
On the other hand, the imperfections could not be included in the axisymmetric model, that in

Fatigue Damage
The fatigue damage was estimated using design codes and standard S-N curves. Many assump-
tions had to be made along the way in order to determine stress damage level. The damage
is dependent on parameters such as stress range, total cycle number, method of calculating
cycles, mean stress level, etc. This study tried to provide a quantitative estimation of the
fatigue damage that can be expected during the pile driving at diﬀerent hot-spots and an
independent analysis would have to be carried out each time for diﬀerent set of parameters.
High-cycle fatigue damage occurred due to varying stress levels with mean stress equal to 0.
Low-cycle induced damage was occuring due to compressive stresses only, however the mean
stress eﬀect at the welds was still not applied due to uncertain non linear residual stresses.
The stress ranges, therefore were not diﬀerentiated between tension or compression.
Number of blows were estimated using driveability analysis, employing full hammer capacity
as a conservative approach. High hammer energy would be only used during driving the last
meters of the monopile. Diﬀerent levels of hammer energy would have to be simulated in order
to ﬁnd more accurate stress levels at the hot-spots. However, the approach used here was found
to be convenient in terms of fatigue damage estimation. In addition, number of blows were cal-
culated using conservative 300 MN of the SRD. The soil friction fatigue, yielding smaller SRD
has not been included, however parametric study with 100 and 200 MN ultimate soil resistance
showed relative fatigue damage reduction of up to 31 % and 9 % respectively. Lower num-
ber of blows required to penetrate smaller resistance soil would reduce the damage even further.

Conclusion
Throughout the work of this thesis, a comprehensive study of the ﬂange ring behavior under
pile driving impact load has been completed. The study, despite the literature review, con-
sisted mainly of 4 parts: static ﬂange response, dynamic response of the ﬂange and monopile
system, fatigue damage estimation and parameter sensitivity analysis.
Static ﬂange analysis initiated numerical modelling considering only a ﬂange ring with ﬁxed
boundary conditions at the weld. The analysis estimated smallest element size required to
determine SCF at the ﬂange ﬁllet, showed axisymmetric modelling feasibility and presented
the importance of the MP wall thickness to the ﬂange response.
Dynamic analysis of the ﬂange-monopile system was initiated by applying rectangular impact
load, that showed expected stress wave propagation within the monopile. The longitudinal
stresses at the top of the MP were shown to be coupled due to axial and bending motion of the
ﬂange. Signiﬁcant ﬂange tip displacement proved the need of the ﬂange surface declination in
order to avoid additional re-strikes with the anvil.
The need to account for stress wave energy dissipation and more accurate monopile displace-
ment led to modelling soil boundary conditions. Conservative SRD was calculated and resul-
tant elasto-plastic soil springs accounting for elastic quake and permanent pile set were applied
on the MP toe and the shaft. Hysteretic soil damping during pile-driving was accounted by
use of dashpots. In addition to the soil modelling, a need for detailed hammer modelling was
observed in order to achieve correct enthru. For this reason, the hammer was represented as a
number of lumped masses that were connected with linear springs representing ram’s stiﬀness.
The bottom spring was connected with rigid plate that represented the anvil. Comparison
between the ﬁnal numerical model showed good agreement with results from GRLWEAP in
terms of pile top displacement, axial force and system energy for 0.1 s, that is enough time
to observe primary and reﬂected stress wave propagation at the top of the monopile. The
one-dimensional stress wave propagation was successfully transferred to more versatile ﬁnite
element model.
The presence of the ﬂange allowed to observe bending induced ﬂange oscillations and their con-
tribution to the total fatigue damage that was determined using standard S-N curves suitable
for oﬀshore structures. Number of stress cycles and stress ranges were counted using Rainﬂow
cycle counting method including half and full cycles. The total damage was determined using
Palmgren-Miner linear damage rule at 5 diﬀerent hot-spots of the ﬂange including points at
inner and outer ﬂange neck as well as the inner and the outer points at the weld. The results
were controlled using middle point of the ﬂange wall, capturing only primary and reﬂected
stress waves. The damage was found to be highly sensitive upon the ﬂange thickness, contact
==PAGE:102==

width and hammer energy. The largest damage is caused by a weaker detail of the weld and
combination of low and high-cycle fatigue. The sensitivity analysis showed that a good ﬂange
design with controlled contact width and hammer energy during pile-driving allows to reduce
the damage even below 10 %.
In addition, time convergence analysis showed that time period of 0.6 s is needed to be included
at the points where fatigue damage is governed by high-cycle fatigue and 0.1 s is not enough
as currently being used in the industry. According to the observations, a ﬁnal design table has
been made including upper and lower bound design parameters, level of importance related to
fatigue damage and level of diﬃculty to fabricate. The ﬂange design table is intended to be
used as a starting point to create ﬁnite element model, leading to more precise determination
of the fatigue damage at the top of the monopile.
10.1
Future Work
The present study is one of a few researches regarding the ﬂange design, hence not much infor-
mation has been published before. Information regarding the impact load, soil representation
and numerical modelling of pile driving had to be gathered that consumed a lot of time, leaving
space for additional research in terms of model improvement, result interpretation and possible
experimental analysis.
Firstly, the axisymmetric model is suggested to be improved in order to match the displace-
ment, stress and velocity at any position of the monopile with GRLWEAP results ideally for
longer time period. This could be done by implementing uniformly distributed soil boundary
conditions or using continuum elements. The model versatility using cohesive soils is also to
be veriﬁed. The hammer and the anvil must be modelled as deformable elements using detail
speciﬁcations from the hammer supplier such as IHC or Menck. In case of success, ﬁnal contact
width between the anvil and the ﬂange would be observed and more precise stress levels could
be estimated providing even better input for cumulative fatigue damage calculations. As seen
from this study, axisymmetric analysis served as a good starting point for observing ﬂange
oscillations and calculating damage, but the model is limited. One of the most important lim-
itations was infeasibility to model manufacturing tolerances and therefore a very conservative
approach has to be used to account for that. The 3D model would allow to model all sorts of
ﬂange ring imperfections starting from the ﬂange ﬂatness. The procedure that has been used
in this project is also proposed to be used in the future and it is shown as a ﬂowchart in Figure
10.1.
The best approach to validate numerical results would be to perform experimental analysis
of the ﬂange, measure the stresses at the hot-spots and estimate the damage. Due to lack
of the samples, appropriate test equipment and time constraints, validation test experiments
have not been performed in this study. Thus, even though constant validation has been used
throughout the research work and results matched the second source commercial software to
an adequate level, the ﬁnal conclusions are diﬃcult to draw without real life data.
Improved numerical model would not only provide better results, but also give a chance to
perform more detailed sensitivity analysis, leading to further optimized design. The numerical

In FLS, an exact bolt load as a function of the shell force Z is important because the depen-
dancy is non-linear and stress diﬀerences play an essential role. The typical behaviour of the
pre-stressed, L-shaped bolted ring ﬂange connection is shown in the Figure B.3. The load
dependancy on the shell force is further explained stepvise.
1. While shell forces are below zero, Z ≤0, the ﬂange is under compression and the bolt
load is equal to it’s prestressing force. Until a particular increase of the shell force Z1,
the pressure in the ﬂange starts moving towards the edge of the ﬂange. The bolt load
increases slightly as seen from the slope of the curve.
2. After further increase in the shell load Z1 ≤Z ≤Z2, the ﬂange begins to open and
therefore, the force in the bolt starts increasing more rapidly.
3. After reaching shell force Z2, the contact between the two ﬂanges remains only at the
edge, thus the bolt load Fs increases linearly as a function of Z. Limit Z3 is elasticity
limit and beyond that, the plastiﬁcation of the bolt is reached.
4. The plastiﬁcation of bolt and/or ﬂange takes place until the connection fails.
Figure B.3: Typical non-linear bolt force dependancy on the shell force, [Seidel and Schau-
mann, n.d.].

sidering ideal elastic behaviour and omitting bending stresses in the bolt. A rather accurate

APPENDIX E
Rainﬂow counting
In accordance to fatigue calculations, cycle counting has been performed with commonly used
Rainﬂow algorithm. It allows to count half and full cycles, their amplitudes and mean values.
Importance of counting half-cycles is present when the signal does not start and does not end
with either a peak (one of the maximum poins) or a valley (one of the minimum points). Rain-
ﬂow algorithm does not take take cycle occurrence or it’s period into consideration and it is
the most popular technique used in process of fatigue damage determination.
Rainﬂow counting algorithm is implemented in a Matlab toolbox described by [Niesłony, 2009].
After small adjustments for the project needs, the toolbox was used as an intermediate tool
to determine fatigue damage. In order to illustrate how the Rainﬂow cycles are counted, an
exemplary stress time series at the ﬂange ﬁllet is used, shown in Figure E.1 below.
