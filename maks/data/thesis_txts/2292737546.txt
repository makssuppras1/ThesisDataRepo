Abstract
This report deals with the challenge of solving large datasets of the university
timetabling problem using mixed integer programming. Focus is on the modelling
of the problem as well as the development of a new solution method. This model
considers the curriculum based scheduling which includes scheduling pattern pe-
nalisation. A short description of literature dealing with university timetabling is
given, to show currently known methods and solutions. The method devised in
this report is the local branching algorithm described by Fischetti and Lodi (2003).
21 datasets of the 2007 Udine timetabling problem is solved using this method.
The local branching algorithm is extended with penalty function neighbourhoods
to obtain better results. The model is solved using GAMS.
The results from the local branching algorithm gives a signi’¼ücant improvement
compared to running a standard CPLEX solver through GAMS. The improvement
vary from >700% to just an improvement in solution time for two of the datasets.
However, it does not compare to the best known upper bounds described in the
literature.
The relative di’¼Ćerence vary according to the size of the individual
problem, but for datasets with more than 40,000 variables the di’¼Ćerence is above
100%.
The extended local branching algorithm does not perform signi’¼ücantly
better.
Actually it only performs better for about half of the problems.
For
the other half it performs slightly worse. Generally the performance is not much
di’¼Ćerent from the basic local branching algorithm.
It becomes clear that the constraint penalising the curriculum compactness is
causing the large problems to become di’¼ācult to solve.
Hence, this is clearly
the part of the model which needs to be solved e’¼Ćectively. To this extend local
branching does not show to be the most bene’¼ücial solution method. However
further improvements might be made to the algorithm, enhancing the capabilities
of solving the curriculum compactness problem.
Keywords: Operations research, mixed integer programming, university timetabling,
local branching, GAMS, ITC2007.

Introduction
For most students around the world their daily life is planned around their studies.
Holidays and actives in their free time must ’¼üt in with the courses they attend
at the university. Due to this the students will have many wishes for the perfect
plan. Some might want to have all morning lectures or all evening. Others would
want to have a whole day of during the week, or the opposite where the lectures
are perfectly spread out. Usually not every wish can be accommodated for all
students. Though, some might be more important to consider than others. For
instance it can make sense that a student is capable of following all necessary
courses for his or her curricula at the same semester, e.g.
that non of these
courses are scheduled simultaneously.
Of course there are also things to consider from the universityŌĆÖs perspective, things
that might clash with the desires of the students. The university will often be
limited by resources.
This can be the sta’¼Ćor the physical facilities available.
Lecturers will obviously have some wishes or even demands on the scheduling of
the courses they are to teach. Perhaps they wish to have at least half an hour
without courses, in the middle of the day, for lunch break. Also, it will not be
a good idea to assign a lecturer to courses that run simultaneously as they will
have di’¼āculties being two places at once.
Clearly both students and lecturers can be quite demanding, even unreasonable
at times. Hence, a well made time table is of great importance. It is easy to
see, that with all these demands and possible con’¼éicting wishes from di’¼Ćerent
parties, the scheduling process becomes quite di’¼ācult. Especially when you think
about that such scheduling processes to a wide extend is still done by hand on
most universities. This is of course both costly due to the man-hours it takes to
conduct such a process. But also, the ’¼ünal result might not be the most optimal
possible given the demands for the schedule.
Some might think, ŌĆÖif only there was a way to solve this complex problem to opti-
malityŌĆÖ. Now, with the aid of mixed integer programming (MIP) this is actually
possible. Though, due to the complexity of the problem and the huge amount of
data it is not that easy to solve with standard MIP solvers.
This is the reason why it is important to develop fast and e’¼ācient algorithms to
solve the university timetabling problem (UTTP).
This report will consider the UTTP based on 21 datasets for the Udine Course
Timetabling Problem (UCTP). Firstly a short presentation of relevant literature
will be given as to give a basis for possible solution methods. This will bring
knowledge on the currently best know solutions for the UCTP. A complete math-
ematical model will be developed and described in detail and the data will be
solved with a standard CPLEX solver. Subsequently the local branching method
(LBM) will be applied to obtain better and possibly faster solutions. Di’¼Ćerent
enhancements for the local branching method will be described. Lastly, a discus-
sion of the obtained results will be given in relation to best known objectives and
possible further work.

Solution methods from the literature
The large amount of data usually needed and the extensive number of variables,
often make the problem di’¼ācult to solve within a reasonable time frame. Hence,
many di’¼Ćerent approaches have been used to solve the UTTP. Obviously this can
also be due to di’¼Ćerences in constraints and the overall objective, though, it still
provides insight on possible approaches. One of the ’¼ürst papers to exploit the use
of integer linear programming (ILP) for these type of problems were Daskalaki
and Birbas (2005a). The problem solved with ILP is close to the UTTP described
in this paper, namely the UCTP. Even though it is computational hard using ILP
some good results are shown. The fact that the compactness of courses makes
the problem NP-hard by itself is shown. The method is used on a case study
described in Daskalaki et al. (2005b).
MirHassani (2006) further explores the ILP of the UTTP using the Shahrood
University of Technology as a case. MirHassani considers room allocations and
students requirements implicitly to reduce the size of the problem. In proceeding
work on the UTTP, this seams to become the standard.
Burke et al.
(2012) uses a branch and cut algorithm to reduce the problem.
The paper deals with the same data and basic formulation as used in this thesis
namely the UCTP. The constraints used are also the same to a large extend.
The introduced cuts reduces the number of variables needed at the cost of an
increase in constraints. The cuts are based on reformulation of the event pattern
penalising (the same as curriculum compactness as used in this thesis) as well as
more compact formulations of day spread and the utilisation of implied bounds.
The method gives some good lower bounds on the data within a two hour time
frame.
The cuts associated with penalising of patterns build on the work by Burke et
al.
(2007).
These cuts are found to be the most crucial when enhancing the
performance. The enhancement is made by shifting from a feature penalisation,
where a variable for each period in a day describes whether or not a pattern
is present, to a enumeration penalisation.
The enumeration method does not
introduce new variables but use the value of the basic variable split up for each
period per day.
A larger number of solution methods are explored in MirHassani and Habibi
(2013). They ’¼ünd that in particular decomposition of the problem can give ben-
e’¼üts in solution times together with hybridizing of the heuristics methods such
as simulated annealing and taboo search. No data is presented but the solutions
cover problems dealing with twelve di’¼Ćerent objectives such as maximizing stu-
dents preferences and the minimization of the number of empty periods between
two classes for the students (which is di’¼ācult as found in other studies).
Column generation combined with a branch and price strategy for the problem
has been explored by Qualizza et al. (2005). The speci’¼üc problem is not based on
the UCTP data, but the constraints are mostly the same. A thing to note is that
the method searches for a set of patterns for each course. These patterns will then
7
==PAGE:9==
be basis for possible ways of scheduling a course. The objective is to maximize
the amount of preferred rooms and timeslots rather than minimizing unwanted
features as described earlier.
The problem is relaxed by removing the integer
constraint, and solved by column generation until optimality. Upon fractional
values, a branch and price procedure will be used. The procedure produces good
upper bounds within reasonable time, though optimality for certain problems
might be di’¼ācult to obtain due to the branch and price algorithm.
Meta heuristics are a widespread tool when solving the UTTP. Nandhine and
Kanmani (2009) describes several meta heuristics for solving UTTPs.
Among
which they describe several local search techniques such as hill climbing and vari-
able neighbourhood search. The local branching algorithm as will be applied in
this report is such a local search meta heuristic, though not described in this ar-
ticle. The article does not provide any results but gives a good overview of other
articles dealing with meta heuristics within this area of research.
Cacchiani et al. (2013) has solved the UCTP using almost the same model as
presented in this paper. Only some minor formulations are di’¼Ćerent, but results
should be the same. By the use of column generation Cacchiani et al. (2013)
is able to ’¼ünd good lower bounds. These lower bounds are tight enough as to
prove optimality or near optimality for previous solutions to the UCTP. The
method splits the objective function in two integer linear programs and solve
them separately. By adding the optimal values for the two problems, a global
lower bound for the entire problem is found.
Other methods such as Benders decomposition has been described in Sarin et al.
(2010). In which also di’¼Ćerent formulations of the problem is referred with a large
number of references for relevant literature.

Introducing the model
The problem considered in this report is the University Timetabling Problem.
The problem considers, as the name implies, scheduling of curses for universities.
An other part of University timetabling is the scheduling of exams. This problem
is not considered in this study.
Several constraints and parameters can be taken into account when scheduling
university courses. These di’¼Ćers from study to study based on the nature of the
problem or the desires of the given university. To give a better understanding of
the UTTP, some of the most common constraints will be listed.
ŌĆó The capacity of a room can not be exceeded
ŌĆó A lecturer can only teach one course at a time
ŌĆó Each room can only accommodate one course at a time
ŌĆó Courses belonging to the same curricula can not be taught simultaneously
ŌĆó Courses belonging to the same curricula should be planed together to avoid
gaps
ŌĆó There must be a gap some point during the day for lunch
ŌĆó Each course should be taught in the same room for every occurrence
ŌĆó Each course must be taught a pre-speci’¼üed number of times each week and
each semester
ŌĆó Some courses must be scheduled in special rooms such as laboratories
This list is just an overview of some of the usual constraints but many more
exist. Some are hard constraints and some are soft. The hard constraints must
be satis’¼üed, whereas the soft constraints can be violated at a prede’¼üned cost.
In the mathematical model the hard constraints will be formulated as actual
constraints where as the soft constraints will be introduced with di’¼Ćerent kinds
of slack variables. These variables can then be penalised by having a cost in the
objective function. The relative cost of these violations must be chosen with care
as it will obviously in’¼éuence the ’¼ünal solution, unless such a solution exist where
no constraints are violated.
4.1
The mathematical formulation
This section will describe the UTTP considered throughout this report. A walk
through of the constraints and variables will be presented. The model presented
here is the basic model without any enhancements. The data for the model is from
9
==PAGE:11==
the Second International Timetabling Competition (ITC2007)1 . And contains
21 di’¼Ćerent data instances/datasets, with di’¼Ćerent amounts of data e.g. curses,
lectures and rooms. All instances are based on real data from various universities.
The constraints for the model are chosen on the basis of the available data. In
other words a constraint ensuring lunch breaks are not included as data for this
is not in the instances from the ITC2007.
Some assumptions in the construction of the model must be made.
For the
scheduling process to be applicable in real life, all data must be at hand before
the semester start. This means that the students must sign up for the courses
in due time, changes to the enrolments might completely change the output of
the program. Also, it is of course assumed that the data given is adequate. This
means that if the schedule is normally made manually, the person undertaking the
task may make some additional decisions based on the emerging schedule. This is
not feasible for a mathematical model. Here all constraints and other factors in-
’¼éuencing the decision must be given explicitly. It is not necessarily assumed that
students sign up for speci’¼üc curricula, but if they do not, they can not expect to
have compact scheduled days.
Hereby follows a list of the hard and soft constraints chosen for the model used in
this paper. The mathematical formulation will be presented later, together with
an explanation of the constraints.
Hard constraints
ŌĆó Lectures: Each course has a predetermined amount of lectures. Each lecture
must be scheduled in distinct timeslots and the total number of lectures
cannot be exceeded. Also, a lecture can only be scheduled in one room at a
time.
ŌĆó Room occupancy: Two lectures cannot take place in the same room at the
same time.
ŌĆó Con’¼éicts: Lectures of courses in the same curriculum or taught by the same
lecturer can not be scheduled in the same time slots.
ŌĆó Availabilities: Some courses cannot be scheduled at speci’¼üc timeslots.
Soft constraints
ŌĆó Unscheduled: The constraint penalizing less lectures scheduled than de-
manded. The penalisation cost will be 10. This should obviously have a
high cost as it is important to give the desired number of lectures. On the
other hand, one might be able to live with a few unscheduled lectures in
special cases.

ŌĆó Room capacity: Having more students in a room than the capacity pro-
scribes is allowed. Though, a penalty cost of 1 is incurred for every student
exceeding the maximum capacity. Of course, having more student in a room
than the capacity allows is not optimal, hence the penalty. But if there is
not enough room on campus, it will most likely be more important to teach
the lecture than cancelling it due to too small rooms.
ŌĆó Minimum working days: Each course has a minimum amount of days over
which the lectures of that course must be divided. This could be to give the
students and lecturers time to prepare for each lecture which is of course
important. The penalty is 5 for each day below the minimum.
ŌĆó Room stability: It is desired that all lectures of a course is scheduled in the
same room. This makes it easier for students and lecturers alike. Shifting
rooms could cause misunderstandings, and the timetable will probably not
be considered well planned. For each room, more than one, that a course is
scheduled in, a penalty of 1 is given.
ŌĆó Curriculum compactness: It is important to ensure good timetables for stu-
dents following a particular curriculum. This can be done by ensuring that
the courses belonging to a curriculum are scheduled adjacent to each other.
This will prohibit gaps in the timetables for the students. Such gaps are
often not desired as planning a school day becomes increasingly di’¼ācult. If
a course is not scheduled adjacent to another course belonging to the same
curriculum a penalty of 2 is given.
This model will lay weight on having compact curricula as described above in
the soft constraints. This is not always the case, but may often be of importance.
The data for the UTTP accommodates this as it is known which courses belong to
which curricula. Hence, the model is curriculum based. Furthermore the following
is given in the datasets:
ŌĆó Which courses are taught by which lecturers
ŌĆó How many lectures must be scheduled for each course
ŌĆó How many days must each course be spread out over
ŌĆó How many students attend each course
ŌĆó The capacity of each room
ŌĆó In which periods is it not possible to teach a speci’¼üc course
The data given is enough to construct the necessary soft and hard constraints. The
model can be solved within minutes for smaller datasets. The model is a mixed
integer program (MIP) model. This means that a combination of continuous and
integer variables are needed. Hence, the solver must accommodate MIP programs.

This section will collect and compare the results obtained throughout the report.
Based on this a discussion of possible conclusions will be given. Also, a more

It was clear that the standard CPLEX solver did not perform very well due to the

Conclusion
This section will present the ’¼ünal conclusion on the work described throughout
the report. The section will both focus on the results from the di’¼Ćerent methods,
as well as the overall work conducted.
The model formulation seems to yield correct and useful results in relation to
the demands for scheduling based on curricula. Some of the constraints might be
handled di’¼Ćerently, but the model presented in this paper appears to be the most
compact formulation.
Throughout the report three di’¼Ćerent sets of results was calculated.
For the
standard CPLEX solver, the basic LBM and the extended LBM. These results
was compared with best known upper bounds. Non of the methods compared with
the known upper bounds, only the two smallest datasets which could be solved
to optimality within a few minutes.
It is important to notice that the upper
bounds currently known are a collection of the best results from several di’¼Ćerent
reports. Hence, obtaining these are not the work of one method. It would not be
expected that the local branching method could improve on all the upper bounds.
Improvements for some of the datasets was hoped fore. Even though it must be
stated that, as this is a new method within the area of UTTP the ’¼ünal outcome
was unclear from the beginning.
In relation to evaluating the results, a note on the actual meaning of these must
be made. As the formulation of the model shows, the ’¼ünal objective value will
be a collection of the penalties which could not be avoided for that particular
solution. As these are penalised the solution is obviously not optimal in respect
to the desired outcome for the university. Though, the outcome without penalties
will in most cases not be possible which can be seen from the lower bounds.
Hence, a university will have to make do with some undesired scheduling. This
puts the solutions values in perspective. Because when the actual decisions are
made, does it make a big di’¼Ćerence for the university if the overall penalty is
200 or 250? Therefore, even though the solutions obtained in this report does not
compare with best known solutions, they might actually for several of the datasets
be practically su’¼ācient.
So to answer the main question of this report, Is it possible through the local
branching algorithm to obtain good results for the university timetabling problem,
the short answer is no.
Despite the lack of improvement, the local branching method did serve some
use.
The ’¼ürst goal was to be able to obtain better feasible results than the
standard CPLEX solver. In that respect the local branching algorithm improved
the objective signi’¼ücantly. Both in respect to ’¼ünal objective, and solution time
for the di’¼Ćerent datasets. As the local branching algorithm is relatively easy to
implement, it can be used as a good starting point, still receiving relatively good
solutions for many of the datasets.
Another important fact can be drawn from the calculations made in this report.
As discussed in the previous section it has become clear that the curriculum com-
40
==PAGE:42==
pactness posses the biggest di’¼āculties related to solving the problem. When this
penalty function was removed from the objective, all datasets could be solved
within reasonable time. This is not surprising as the function consider all neigh-
bouring timeslots for every variable. As the number of variables grow, the amount
of neighbouring timeslots obviously also grow to extreme heights. When choosing
a proper solution method for these UTTPs, the highest emphasis must be laid on
how to deal with the curriculum compactness. Can this part be solved e’¼āciently,
the problems will be solved e’¼āciently.
This is exactly what studies as Burke et al. (2012) deals with. In that study the
problem is decomposed and the part considering the scheduling pattern is solved
separately. This could be one way to go. It might be possible to enhance the
local branching algorithm in such a way that it would deal with the curriculum
compactness problem.
In practise di’¼Ćerent universities might demand di’¼Ćerent things of the scheduling.
Hence, it might not be the case that curriculum compactness is important. As
a matter of fact this is more a convenience for the students ensuring less gaps
in their timetables.
This means that in many cases the solution presented in
this report might su’¼āce in obtaining optimal results. This should be an issue
to consider when formulating demands for the timetable, if a university want it
solved by linear programming including curriculum compactness. At least until
better solution methods have been found for cases of large scale data.
