Abstract 
 
Infectious diseases caused by multi-resistant bacteria is a still-growing problem. Shigella is an 
intracellular bacterium that causes bloody diarrhea by attacking the intestinal epithelium cells. 
Butyrate has shown high efficiency in helping host cells fighting bacterial infection. It is however 
still unclear how butyrate affects host cell metabolism. The purpose of this project was to 
investigate how butyrate affects the metabolism of four different cell lines. Two of them (HeLa and 
Caco-2) were of cancerous nature. The Two others (HUVEC and HIEC-6) were non-cancerous 
cells.  The cell metabolism was determined by 1H NMR after the cells were incubated for 6 hours 
without butyrate and with 1,10 and 20 mM NaBu. Both cancerous cell lines produced a large 
amount of lactate. The HIEC-6 cell line consumed 23.7 % of the accessible butyrate significantly 
more butyrate at 20 mM NaBu concentration compared to both HeLa and Caco-2. HeLa consumed 
significantly more glucose than the two non-cancerous cell lines (HUVEC and HIEC-6). HeLa 
consumed 25.47 % glucose, HUVEC consumed 3.03 % and HIEC-6 consumed 6.14 %. NAD+ 
concentrations were determined in order to gain further insight into the energy metabolism of 
intestinal cells as a function of butyrate concentration. The normal intestinal cell line HIEC-6 and 
the cancerous Caco-2 were used. The concentration of NAD+ in Caco-2 went down from 2.46± 0.37 
to 1.79± 0.31 µM (treated with 20 mM NaBu compared to no butyrate treatment). In HIEC-6 cells 
NAD went up from 0.67 to 1.79 µM. This difference indicates a difference in the mechanism in 
which butyrate regulate host cell metabolism in cancerous cell lines versus non-cancerous cell lines.

1 Introduction 
According to the World Health Organization (WHO) diseases costs by pathogenic bacteria are 
among the greatest causes of death worldwide [1]. Some pathogenic bacteria are evolutionary 
evolved to multiply inside a host cell and survive on the available nutrition’s. One example is the 
pathogenic intracellular bacterium Shigella flexneri, which causes bloody diarrhea. To fight these 
pathogens, a better understanding of the metabolism of both the host cells and pathogens are 
needed.  
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) has been used in numerous studies to investigate the 
metabolic fingerprint of pathogen/host interactions. Kentner et.al has studied the metabolism of 
Hela cells infected with the bacterium Shigella flexneri [2].  
This study revealed Shigella's ability to grow on the nutrients of the host cell. This enables Shigella 
to proliferate at a doubling time of 37 min.  Like this study most infection models are based on 
cancer cells. 
1.1 Shigella flexneri 
  
Shigella flexneri is a gram-negative bacterium that infects the epithelium cells of mammalian 
intestinal cells.  In order to gain access to the intestinal cells Shigella invades microfold cells and is 
transported across the epithelial layer [35]. When transported over the epithelial layer Shigella is 
released in the intraepithelial pocket. Here Shigella survives by causing apoptosis in attacking 
macrophages [7]. When released from the dying macrophages shigella attack epithelium cells from 
the basolateral side and replicate in the cytoplasm. Shigella is dependent on mxi-spa [30]. This 
operon encodes a type 3 secretion apparatus which enables the bacterium to secreted proteins into 
the cytosol of the host cell. [3]. Inside the host cell these proteins alter the gene regulation of the 
host cell to modulate the innate inflammatory response of the host cell which allows shigella to 
replicate without getting killed by the immune system of the host cell. 
1.2 The intestinal barrier 
  
An important defense against pathogen invasion is the intestinal barrier which ensures that the 
epithelial cells are not permeable towards pathogenic bacteria. The intestinal barrier contains a 
single layer of epithelial cells joined together by tight junctions [4]. The epithelial cells of the 
==PAGE:10==
 
10 
 
intestinal barrier use short chain fatty acids (SCFA) as an energy source. SCFA is produced by a 
number of bacteria including Eubacterium, and Roseburia species [34] which produces SCFA by 
anaerobic fermentation of non-digestible carbohydrates. A schematic overview of the production of 
butyrate can be seen as figure 17 in the appendix [32]. SCFA is of importance for maintaining 
normal cell physiology [5]. In particular butyrate was shown to be an important energy source for 
epithelium cells as 70 % of the energy used in epithelium cells comes from oxidation of butyrate 
[6]. Butyrate is oxidized in the mitochondria to form acetyl co enzyme A (Acetyl-CoA) which 
enters the tricarboxylic acid cycle (TCA) to generate adenosine triphosphate (ATP) [7]. This 
reaction is shown in equation 1[8].  
Equation 1: 
AcetylCoA+ 3 NAD+ + FAD+ ADP + Pi+ H2O→ 2 CO2+ 3 NADH + FADH2 + ATP +2 H+ + CoA 
This energy allows the cells to differentiate and proliferate fast enough to maintain a first line of 
defense against invading pathogens by forming a physical barrier to stop the invading bacteria. 
Butyrate decreases the permeability of epithelium cells by increasing the AMP-activated protein 
kinase (AMPK) which increases the number of tight junctions between the cells [9]. This makes it 
more difficult for the pathogen to enter the host cell. 
1.3 Butyrate transport  
 
Butyrate is transported across the cells through both passive and active transport. Butyrate diffuses 
passively in its undissociated form. The dissociated form is actively transported over the cell 
membrane by SCFA transporters. Because of the week acid nature of butyrate (Pka =4.8) [10] the 
majority of butyrate exist mainly in its undissociated state in the gut where the pH is about 5.5 - 6.7. 
Active transport is therefore necessary for the uptake of butyrate. Two examples of SCFA 
transporters have been described. The first is the monocarboxylate transporter (MCT) the second is 
the sodium-coupled monocarboxylate transporter (SMCT) [11]. 
The MCT transporter is coupled to an H+ gradient and is expressed on the apical membrane and the 
basolateral membrane of epithelium cells and induces influx of butyrate and other SCFA from the 
lumen and efflux of SCFA to the blood [12].  MCT facilitate butyrate transport by physically 
transporting it over the plasma membrane together with one proton [29]. SMCT transporters are 
similar but are only expressed in on the apical membrane and is a sodium coupled transporter 
instead of hydrogen [12].

1.4 Butyrate as an HDAC inhibitor 
 
Butyrate has been reported as the most potent inhibitor of histone deacetylase (HDAC) among the 
SCFAs (IC50 0.09 mM) [13]. HDAC is a class of enzymes that functions by deacetylation of lysine 
residues on histones which remove the positive charge on lysine [14]. The inhibition of these 
enzymes results in a positive charge on   the DNA molecule which results in transcription of the 
apoptosis genes BAX, BAK and FAS [15]. This will prevent the cells from proliferation. In humans 
there has been found 18 HDACs [16]. These 18 HDACs are divided into 4 classes called I, II, III 
and IV [17]. Butyrate effects class I and II. Class I HDAC are located in the nucleus of the cells and 
class II in both nucleus and cytoplasm [16]. The exact mechanism in which butyrate acts as an 
HDAC inhibitor is not completely understood. A model was proposed by Davie 2003 suggesting 
that butyrate inhibits the transcription factors sp1 and sp3 which inhibits the recruitment of HDAC 
enzymes [18]. This led to histone hyperacetylation and transcription of the p21 gene. This gene

inhibits cyclin-dependent kinase 2 activity [18]. Cyclin-dependent kinase 2 is an enzyme which is 
necessary for the cell to be apple to go into s phase [19]. 
1.5 The Butyrate paradox 
 
Even though butyrate helps the growth of non-cancer cells like the intestinal epithelium cells by 
acting as a source of energy, it shows different effects in cancer cells. Butyrate has been reported to 
inhibit the proliferation of cancer cell lines. A study from 2007 showed that butyrate inhibited cell 
proliferation in Caco-2 cells at 8 mM butyrate after 24 hours incubation [20]. This opposite effect of 
butyrate in cancer cells compared to non-cancer cells are known as the butyrate paradox. Cancer 
cells undergo a phenomenon known as the Warburg effect [21]. This means that the cancer cell 
switches metabolism from primary using butyrate as an energy source to mainly anaerobically 
oxidize glucose into pyruvate and further into lactate. This helps the cell to divided faster. Butyrate 
may act as an energy source in non-cancer cells but accumulate in cancer cells which undergoes the 
Warburg effect and thereby acts as an HDAC inhibitor.    
1.6 PKM2 
 
An alternative mechanism of the antiproliferation property of butyrate in cancer cells is that it binds 
to the pyruvate kinase M2 enzyme (PKM2). PKM2 is the enzyme that catalyzes the transformation 
of phosphoenolpyruvic acid to pyruvate. A study by Li et al (2018)[22] suggested that butyrate by 
binding to the PKM2 enzymes can inhibit the production of lactate from pyruvate and instead 
letting the cell use aerobic oxidation of butyrate to gain energy consumption of colon cancer cells 
(HCT116, HT29 and LoVo cells) [22] and thereby inhibit proliferation. The reason why this is not 
seen in normal intestinal cell might be that they do not produce large amount of lactate and instead 
is metabolizing pyruvate by aerobic oxidation.

2. Background 
 
2.1 Glycolysis  
 
Glycolysis is a biochemical redox process where glucose is anaerobically oxidized into pyruvate 
and NAD+ is reduced to NADH. This is shown in equation 2. When pyruvate is produced it can be 
further degraded anaerobically to lactate or it can be oxidized to acetyl co enzyme A. The glycolysis 
consists of in total 10 reaction that transform mainly glucose into pyruvate. The overall reaction of 
the glycolysis is shown in equation 2 
Equation 2: Glucose + 2Pi + 2ADP + 2NAD+→ 2 Pyruvate + 2ATP + 2NADH + 2H+ + 2H2O  
This pyruvate can either be further transformed anaerobically to form lactate the reaction is shown 
below: 
Pyruvate + NADH + H+ → Lactate + NAD+ 
 or aerobically to form CO2. See section 2.2 for reaction. 
The glycolysis can be divided into two stages. The purpose of the first stage is to transform 6 
carbon monosaccharides into two three carbon units. This stage consists of 5 reactions. The purpose 
of the second step is to generate NADH and consist of 5 reaction. The 10 reaction will be explained 
in the following. In the first reaction A phosphate group is transferred from ATP to glucose to form 
glucose-6-phosphate. The purpose of this reaction is to trap the glucose inside the cell since the cell 
do not have transporters for this compound. The second reaction is the isomerization of glucose-6-
phoshate to fructose-6-pohsphate. This reaction is reversable. Next the fructose-6-pohsphate is 
transformed to Fructose 1,6-bisphosphate. This reaction is depended on ATP and is therefore an 
important regulator of the glycolysis. Fructose 1,6-bisphosphate is divided into the two three carbon 
molecules Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate and Dihydroxyacetone phosphate. Only the first is further 
used in stage two of the glycolysis [23].   
The second stage of the glycolysis begins with the oxidation of Glyceraldehyde 3-phosphate to 1,3-
Bisphosphoglycerate (1,3BPG) and one molecule of NAD+ is reduced to NADH. In the next step 
1,3BPG is oxidized to 3-Phosphoglycerate (3PG) and ADP is reduced to ATP.  3GP is isomerized 
into 2-Phosphoglycerate (2GP), and then 2GP is converted into Phosphoenolpyruvate (PEP). 
Finally, PEP is transformed to pyruvate and one molecule of ATP is produced.

2.2 TCA cycle  
 
The citric acid cycle is the final pathway to gain energy for carbon sources such as carbohydrates, 
amino acids and fatty acids. The TCA cycle takes in eukaryotic cells place in the mitochondria. 
Where the glycolysis is an anerobic process the TCA cycle does depend on oxygen as electron 
acceptor and is therefore an aerobic process. The TCA cycle is linked to the glycolysis though the 
enzyme complex pyruvate dehydrogenase complex which consists of three different enzymes. As 
described in section 2.1 xx carbohydrates are oxidized to pyruvate which under anerobic conditions 
can be further oxidized into lactate. Under aerobic conditions pyruvate is actively transported into 
the microconidia of the cell where it is oxidized to acetyl CoA in the following reaction:  
Equation 3: Pyruvate + CoA + NAD+ → AcetylCoA + CO2+ NADH + H+ 
This reaction is catalyzed by the pyruvate dehydrogenase complex which is known as the link 
between the glycolysis and the TCA cycle. Beside the three different enzymes that makes the 
pyruvate dehydrogenase complex five different coenzymes are needed for the reaction to take place.

enters the TCA cycle [8]. 
2.3 NAD+ and NADH 
  
NAD+ plays an impotent role in redox reaction insight living cells. In humans NAD can either be 
synthesized from the amino acid tryptophan or by breakdown of the molecule niacin and is mostly 
found in the cytocol and in the mitochondria [25]. The main function of NAD+ or NADH in its 
reduced form is to participate in biochemical reactions either as reducing agent (NADH) or an 
oxidizing agent. The ratio of NAD+/NADH Is important biomarker in the regulation of energy 
metabolism since it controls the preference of reduction and oxidation insight cells. A high NAD+ 
concentration will mean a high level of oxidation and a high concentration of NADH will mean a 
high level of reduction.

NMR is an analytical method based on the quantum mechanical phenomenon of spin. Atom nuclei 
is charged and have spins. The spin gives the nucleic a small magnetic moment making them 
behave like a bar magnet.   
The magnetic moment is proportional to the spin quantum number times the gyromagnetic ratio of 
the nucleus.  
Equation 4:  µ=s*γ 
In equation 4 µ is the magnetic moment, s is the spin and γ is the gyromagnetic moment. 
Every nucleic with a spin quantum number of 0 will therefore not have a magnetic moment and will 
not be detected in NMR. The nuclei with 0 spin are the ones with an even number of protons as well 
as an even number of neutrons. These include 12carbon and 18oxygen. 
2.4.1 The NMR instrument   
 
An NMR instrument contains a magnet which apply a magnetic field with strength B0 along the z-
axis. If a nucleus with non-zero spin is placed in a magnetic field, it will orientate itself either with 
the applied field or against the applied field. The state in which the nucleus is orientated with the 
magnetic field is of lower energy compared to the one in which the nucleus orientates itself against 
the magnetic field. The number of spin states depends on the spin quantum number I in the 
following way 2I+1 where I is the spin quantum number. The Hydrogen atom has spin quantum 
number of 1/2 meaning that it can have 2 different spin states + 1/2 and - 1/2. The population of the 
different states is detriment by the Boltzmann distribution.

 2.4.2 Resonance 
 
When subjected to a radio wave a nucleus or rather group of nuclei may change spin form high to 
low and from low to high. The absorbed energy by the spin change produces a voltage which can be 
measured by a coil of wire and the signal will be displayed as a free induced decay (FID). 
2.4.3 Chemical shift 
 
The frequency at which a nucleus resonance is depended on the chemical environment. The protons 
in the nucleus are shielded from the magnetic field by the electrons surrounding it. The reason for 
the shielding is that electrons also have spins and therefore creates a magnetic field that lowers the 
magnetic field of the magnet. 
 2.4.4 Relaxation 
 
If 2.000.000 protons are placed in a magnetic field of 18.8 Tesla at 298.15 k there will be an excess 
of approximately 125 protons in the lower energy state at thermal equilibrium. When the sample is 
radiated with radio wave the system will end being out of thermal equilibrium. This means that the 
distribution of spin will change resulting in the excess of lower energy spin states will be < 125. 
When the system is saturated there will be an even distribution of spin states. The time it takes for 
63% of the protons to regain thermal equilibrium is known as T1 or the spin-lattice relaxation time. 
2.4.5 Spin-spin 
 
Hydrogen atoms spin is affected by other nearby hydrogens. This phenomenon is known as spin-
spin splitting.  As previously described Hydrogen nuclei can have either spin 1/2 or spin - 1/2. This 
means that the chemical shift of one set of chemical equivalent protons can feel the spin of another 
set of protons. This makes the protons resonate at a slightly different frequency and will split the 
signal in n+1 peaks where n is the number of neighbor hydrogens.

For metabolomics this is very useful as it makes it possible not only to determine metabolites but

groups bound to the silicon atom. When the signal of interest is integrated relative to the standard

5 Discussion 
 
In this project, 4 different cell lines have been investigated using MTT assay, NMR spectroscopy 
and Abnova NAD+/NADH assay kit. The purpose of the project was to evaluate the cells suitability 
as host cell for infection models to investigate butyrate ability to lower infection in these cell lines. 
Therefore, it was necessary to investigate butyrate’s effect on the cell lines viability by MTT assay. 
The MTT data showed no significant change in the viability of the Caco-2, HUVEC, and HIEC-6 
cell lines when treated with 20 mM NaBu for 6 hours compared to untreated cells. The lack of any 
significant change in cell viability might be due to the short incubation time. Other studies have 
shown a significant decrease in cell viability for Caco-2 cell with as little as 8 mM NaBu with 24 
hours of incubation [20]. This could indicate that the use of cancerous cells as infection model is not 
optimal for long term studies as butyrate introduces cell apoptosis in Caco-2 cells.  
 
5.1 Expectations 
 
Based on the theory explained in the background section the two cancerous cell lines HeLa and 
Caco-2 were expected to consume glucose and produce a large amount of lactate. Furthermore, it 
was assumed that the non-cancerous cell lines would consume butyrate. 
 
5.2 Substrate preference  
 
In this study we observed that the two non-cancer cell line HUCEC and HIEC-6 consumes 
significantly more butyrate than the cancer cell lines Caco-2 and HeLa. Furthermore, we observed 
that the cancer cell line HeLa consumes significantly more glucose than both of the non-cancer cells 
HIEC-6 and HUVEC. This was expected since HeLa cells are cancer cells and undergoes the 
Warburg effect. In agreement with this finding the Hela Cells produced significantly more lactate 
than both the other cancer cell line Caco-2 and the non-cancer cells HUVEC and HIEC-6. 
Surprisingly Caco-2 consumes more butyrate than glucose. The non-cancer cells produced 
significantly more butyrate than the cancer cells. Of all the cell lines HIEC consumes most butyrate. 
This makes sense considering that HIEC-6 is normal intestinal cells and is therefore expected to get 
70 % of its energy from butyrate [6]. Caco-2 consumed more butyrate than HeLa. The reason may 
==PAGE:40==
 
40 
 
be that Caco-2 is an intestinal cell so even though it is affected by the Warburg effect it still 
consumes butyrate like normal intestinal cells. The fact that HeLa consumes more glucose and 
produces more lactate than Caco-2 might suggest that it is more effected by the Warburg effect than 
the Caco-2 cell line. This could explain way Caco-2 consumes so much more butyrate than HeLa 
does.   
5.3 Pyruvate 
  
 
For the HeLa cell line, the   pyruvate signal went down as a function of butyrate. For Caco-2 cell 
line pyruvate concentration went significantly up at 20 mM butyrate compared to no butyrate 
(p<0,05).  
This result from the Caco-2 is in agreement with the data from Li et al [22]. The pyruvate signal 
was still lower for all concentrations of butyrate compared to the pyruvate signal in the medium. 
This indicates that Caco-2 is still affected by the Warburg effect, but the butyrate inhibits the 
Warburg effect and thereby changes the metabolism of the Caco-2 cells. This could also explain 
way Caco-2 consumes more butyrate than glucose. The study by Li et al [22] was done in HCT116 
cells which like Caco-2 cells comes from the human colon. HeLa cells, on the other hand, are 
isolated from a cervical adenocarcinoma [33]. The difference in the response of these different cell 
lines suggests that butyrate effects different cancer cell lines differently. The pyruvate concentration 
was not affected by the butyrate treatment in the two non-cancerous cell lines suggesting that 
butyrate does not affect the glycolysis of healthy cells. Since lactate is produced from pyruvate it 
was assumed that lactate would behave opposite to pyruvate. Meaning that the lactate concentration 
will go up when the pyruvate concentration goes down and vice versa. The change in lactate as a 
function of butyrate is not significant for any of the four cell lines except for HIEC-6 where lactate 
went significantly down at 20 mM butyrate compared to no butyrate. Pyruvate did not change 
significantly for the HIEC-6 cell line. It can therefore not be concluded if pyruvate and lactate did 
behave different of each other as there was no significantly change in both signals for both of these 
metabolites for any of the four cell lines. 
 
==PAGE:41==
 
41 
 
 
 
 
5.4 NAD+/NADH 
 
The NAD+/NADH ratio for the HIEC-6 cells went up at butyrate treatment. A study from Donohoe 
et al. [6] Showed that the NADH/NAD+ is significantly higher for normal mice compared to germ 
free mice lacking butyrate producing bacteria. This indicate the opposite of the finding of this report 
that NAD+/NADH ratio should go up instead of the NAD+/NADH ratio.  
According to Christensen et al. [36] an increase in NAD/NADH ratio is associated with the limited 
reduction of NAD+ to NADH in the glycolysis.  This could indicate that the HIEC-6 cells are 
relying on the metabolism of butyrate instead of the glycolysis. This is in accidence accordance 
with to the fact that HIEC-6 uses much more butyrate than glucose.

6 Conclusion 
 
The four different cell line was successfully analyzed with respect to cell viability redox state and 
metabolic activity. The MTT data showed that HeLa was the only cell line that showed a lower 
viability when treated with butyrate. The pyruvate signal was analyzed for each cell line showing 
different trends. For HeLa cells pyruvate went down at 20 mM butyrate compared to no butyrate. 
The Caco-2 cell showed the opposite trend that pyruvate went up 20 mM butyrate compared to no 
butyrate. For the two non-cancerous cell lines HUVEC and HIEC-6 there was no significant change 
in the pyruvate signal. The Lactate signal did not change significantly for any of the two cancer cell 
lines nor for the non-cancerous cell line HUVEC. It did however went significantly down for the 
HIEC-6 cell line. A decrease in lactate concentration is likely due to the fact that HIEC-6 prefers to 
use butyrate as an energy source and therefor do not reduce pyruvate to lactate in a high amount. 
Since the lactate/pyruvate ratio is inversely proportional with the NAD+NADH ratio, a decrease in 
lactate level my result in an increase in NAD/NADH ratio. This is in accordance with the result of 
the NAD experiment for the HIEC-6 cell line. However, the pyruvate level did not went 
significantly down for the HIEC-6 cell line, so this cannot concluded with absolute certainty.
