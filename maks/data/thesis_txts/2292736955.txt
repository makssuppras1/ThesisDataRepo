Abstract 
 
Global warming has evolved the climate of Denmark, which now has a more diverse weather 
throughout the country. The new updated Danish reference year published in 2013 (DRY13), 
comprises the evolution of the weather conditions, and separates the country in a series of clima-
tological zones. Designers now have the possibility to either design building systems with weath-
er data from the actual location of the building, or use a building permit dataset (BP13) present-
ing the weather conditions of Denmark generally.  
 
The target of the thesis is to examine how the implementation of the new DRY13 data, influences 
the energy frame and requirements for heating, ventilation and air conditioning (HVAC) systems 
in building simulations, of office buildings in Denmark. Building simulation models using the 
formerly used DRY data from 1995 (DRY95), the new BP13 data, as well as weather data from six 
chosen locations of Denmark. The resulting energy demands for heating, cooling and ventilation 
have been compared together with the required ventilation rate, found in order to sustain an 
acceptable indoor environment. 
 
The study showed that BP13 have a warmer climate than DRY95, and it sets higher requirements 
to the HVAC system demanding a higher ventilation rate and more cooling. It results in a higher 
energy frame and difficulties complying with the requirements set for low energy building class 
2015 and building class 2020.  
 
It was also evaluated that building projects located on the west coast of Jutland, would benefit 
from using the location based weather data, compared with the BP13 dataset.  
 
Three scenarios with different combinations of occupancy utilisation and diversity factors for 
electronic equipment was also analysed through building simulations. It was evaluated that using 
a design scenario with heat loads from occupants and electronic equipment between the maxi-
mum design load, and the average load found in offices, would lower the requirements for the 
HVAC system substantially. 
 
Night ventilation and utilization of thermal mass in an office building was also assessed through 
building simulations. The assessment illustrated that the requirements for HVAC was decreased 
when applied, resulting in a lower energy frame. By using night ventilation and thermal mass in 
the office building, was it possible to get the office building using the BP13 weather data to a low 
energy building class 2020.

Abstrakt 
 
Global opvarmning har ændret det danske klima, som nu har mere varierende vejrforhold hen-
over landet. Den nyeste opdatering af det danske reference år (DRY) udgivet I 2013, tager højde 
for denne udvikling ved at opdele landet I en række klimazoner. Bygnings designere har nu mu-
ligheden for enten at designe bygningssystemer med vejrdata fra den aktuelle lokation, eller 
benytte sig af et “Building permit” dataset (BP13), der indeholder et generaliseret vejrdata for 
Danmark.  
Formålet med specialet er at undersøge hvordan indførelsen af det nye reference år påvirker 
energirammen og kravene til varme, ventilation og køle systemer I bygning simuleringer af kon-
torbyggerier  i Danmark. Analysen er udført ved brug a bygnings simuleringer med modeller der 
bruger det forrige benyttede reference år DRY95, den nylig anvendte BP13 og vejrdata for seks 
udvalgte lokationer af Danmark. Energibehov for opvarmning, afkøling of ventilation er sammen-
lignet imellem moddellerne sammen med den nødvendige ventilations rate der skal til, for at 
opretholde et acceptabelt indeklima. 
 
Undersøgelsen viste at den nye BP13 vejrdata har et varmere klima end DRY95, og det resultere i 
højere krav til ventilations raten og kølebehovet. Dette medfører at energirammen er lavere og 
der er flere komplikationer i at overholder kravene sat til lav-energi bygningsklasse 2015 og 
bygningsklasse 2020. 
 
Det var også vurderet at byggeprojekter på vestkysten af Danmark vil have gavn af at bruge de 
lokation baserede vejrdata fremfor den generaliserede BP13 vejrdata fil. 
 
Brug af personbelastnings udnyttelsesfaktorer og diversitet af elektronisk udstyr i et kontorbyg-
geri blev undersøgt med bygnings simuleringer. Det viste at ved at opstille et design scenarie 
imellem at have fuld belastning, og den gennemsnitligebelastning fundet I kontorer, blev energy 
forbrugene til opvarmning, afkøling og ventilation, sænket betydeligt. 
 
Nattekøling og udnyttelse af termiske masse i et kontorbyggeri blev også undersøgt ved brug af 
bygningssimuleringer. Undersøgelsen viste at ved anvendelse ville kravene til bygningssyste-
merne blive nedsat betydeligt, og at det sænkede energirammen. Ved brug af nattekøling og ud-
nyttelse af den termiske masse, var det muligt at få kontorbygningen i bygningsklasse 2020, når 
der blev anvendt vejrdata for BP13.

1. Introduction 
Climate change is a challenge that needs to be taken seriously, both on a global and local level. 
The Danish government’s climate policy targets to reduce the greenhouse gas emissions by 40% 
by 2020, compared with the levels of 1990. The long-term energy-policy is that the entire energy 
supply for electricity, heating, industry and transport is to be covered by renewable energy by 
2050 [ENS, 2013]. 
 
Energy savings and efficiency are two important factors that need to be applied within all areas, 
in order to be able to fulfil the goals for the climate and energy policies. The building department 
of Denmark especially plays a significant role in the goal, with 40% of the overall Danish energy 
consumption used for heating, ventilation and lighting of buildings [KEBMIN,2015]. 
 
The Danish building regulation (BR10) is a performance-based code that sets limits for the man-
datory energy frame calculations, in order to establish a maximum energy use for the buildings of 
Denmark. Two building classes are presented in BR10 low energy class 2015 and building class 
2020. The highest requirements are set for building class 2020 which have a tightened the ener-
gy frame with a reduction of 75% compared to the energy frame of 2006. Besides setting limits 
for the energy demand of the buildings, are the requirements for the quality of the indoor envi-
ronment also covered in BR10. The building class 2020 requires documentation of the indoor 
thermal environment in the summer months of the most critical rooms of the building.  
[Energistyrelsen, 2011] 
 
Building simulation programs are used to evaluate the indoor environment and energy perfor-
mance of a building. They are used as an integrated tool in the building design process to create 
concepts and design strategies, and assessing their performance [Attia, 2010].  
 
The building simulation tools are necessary in order to quantify possible energy saving, and ac-
curate weather data play an important role in the process. There are thee main types of weather 
data: “Typical” weather data representing a larger region, “Actual” weather data, representing 
the climate conditions at a specific location and “future” weather data which is used for adaptive 
control of a building. [Bhandari, 2012]. 
 
The Test Reference Year (TRY) was a dataset used from 1976, containing hourly measurements 
of climate conditions over a period of 1948-1975 [Crawley 1998]. Since has global warming 
caused an increase of the external temperatures, and it has been necessary to update the refer-
ence dataset. The Danish Ministry of Climate made an update in 1995, in which measurements 
from 1975-1989 where gathered in a dataset called Danish Design Reference Year (DRY95). The 
purpose was to produce a more recent dataset designed specifically to be used in the energy and 
construction sector, as input data for building simulations.  
 
Again in 2013 was the dataset updated and published in the report [Wang,2013] made by the 
Danish Meterological Institute (DMI). The DRY13 data was collected from measurements made at 
13 weather stations throughout Denmark, in the period from 2001-2010. 12 months where cho-
sen form the measurements to represent a yearly climatological variation, at various locations of 
Denmark. 
 
 
Ten climatological parameters are presented in the new DRY. They individually sectionalise 
Denmark in 5-6 zones, each zone approximately containing the same variations of the given pa-
rameter, on a daily and yearly basis. It gives the possibility to create weather data files for specif-

==PAGE:14==
ic locations of Denmark, by defining the zone representing each parameter at the geographical 
location of the building, and combine the data for the defined zones in a weather file.  
 
As for the formerly used DRY95 has DMI chosen a climatological zone for each parameter and 
made a building permit dataset that represent the weather fluctuations of Denmark generally. It 
is combined from 3 of the 13 weather stations and can be used as input with a simpler approach 
in building simulations for in any given location of Denmark. [Wang 2013].  
 
From [SBi a, 2015] was it informed that designers can use the specific location based DRY data, in 
building simulations to optimize the building systems, but it is a legal claim that the building 
permit data is used for calculations of the Energy frame. The Danish building regulation imple-
ments that DRY13 is mandatory to use in building simulations from 2015 and onwards [Ener-
gistyrelsen a, 2015]. The building permit dataset has been converted to a DRY file and is available 
for download at [SBi b, 2015]. 
 
The scope of the thesis is to evaluate how the new DRY13 data influences the requirements for 
HVAC and the energy frame, in a time where energy savings are at highest priority. The new 
building permit dataset describing the “Typical” weather data of Denmark will be evaluated and 
compared with the DRY95. Building simulations of an office building will be carried out with each 
of the weather files, to validate and compare the requirements for HVAC.  
 
The energy frame of the office building will be estimated using both DRY95 and BP13, and their 
compliances with the low energy building class 2015 and 2020 will be specified. 
 
Weather data for specific locations of Denmark will also be evaluated in order to estimate how 
the new possibilities of using the more untraditional “Actual” weather data, can influence on the 
requirements for HVAC.  
 
Three questions concerning the implementation of DRY13 data will be answered in the analysis: 
 
1) What are the resulting differences between using DRY95 and the building permit data 
set of DRY13, in building simulations and estimations of the energy frame? 
 
2) How will the results of building simulations differentiate, between using the building 
permit dataset of DRY13, or weather data created for a specific location? 
 
3) Would it be beneficial to use the specific created weather data compared to the building 
permit dataset? 
 
A examination will also be carried out, investigating possible solutions of how to lower the re-
quirements for the HVAC systems, through optimisation of the heat load scenarios, and by inves-
tigating possibilities for night ventilation and utilisation of thermal mass in an office building.

2 Global climate conditions 
Svante Arrenius suggested for more than 100 years ago that the emission of CO2 from burning of 
fossil fuels could result in a warmer atmosphere of the earth. This global warming is caused by 
the “Green house effect” that occurs because of heat exchange between the earth, its atmosphere 
and space. The heat balance of the earth is influenced by the shortwave solar radiation that 
warms its surface, and the cooling long wave radiation emitted towards space. The radiation 
towards space can be absorbed by the human created infrared-trapping Greenhouse Gases 
(GHG), keeping the temperature up in the layer of the atmosphere, called the troposphere. 
 
Figure 1 A; indicates predictions of the future CO2 stabilization in the atmosphere. It is varying 
from 350ppm-750ppm and the business as usual curve, indicates the CO2 concentration if no 
stabilization occurs. It is estimated that a doubling in the CO2 level causes a global average tem-
perature rise of 1,5-4,5°C [Hoffert, 2004].  
 
 
Figure 1. A) Atmospheric CO2 concentrations (left) and radiative forcing (right). Curves prior 2000 are 
made by historical measurements and from 2000 onwards they are BAU predictions.    B) Effect of radia-
tion trapping green house gasses in the atmosphere that makes the CO2 radiate from a higher level. Found 
from [Hoffert, 2004]. 
The graphs of Figure 1 A, show measured data before the year 2000, and predictions onwards. 
When comparing them to the measured CO2 concentration that in the year 2013 reached a global 
annual mean abundance of 396ppm, can it be validated that the trend in atmospheric CO2 con-
centrations are increasing as predicted. [WMO, 2014]. 
 
Figure 1 B shows that because global warming increases the temperature in the troposphere, will 
the maintenance of the planetary heat balance, cause that the CO2 radiates from a higher colder 
altitude, reducing the infrared cooling. It also causes that the stratosphere gets warmer [Hoffert, 
2004].

and the European Union have set future goals of a energy saving of 20% by 2020, and a long term 
goal of reducing the GHG emission of 80-95% by 2050 [BPIE, 2011].  
 
The year 2013 was one of the 10 warmest years since global records began in 1850. It had a 
global land and ocean surface temperature that was 0.48°C higher than the 1961-1990 average 
[WMO, 2013].

Offices in Denmark has gone through a more drastic change in energy use with a building stock

There is a connection between the heating demand of a building and the electrical use. All electri-
cal appliances emit a part of their consumption as a heat gain that contributes to the heating of 
the building. Earlier was this source of heat seen as an advantage for the winter situation that 
needs heating, but since the temperatures are rising due to global warming, could it end up con-
tributing to the overheating in the summer or an excessive need for cooling [Marsh, 2008]. 
 
2.2 Future climate case studies 
Investigations of future climatic conditions in Switzerland has shown that a decrease in heating 
demand of 33-44% for residential buildings in the period 2050-2100. For offices it was found 
that the cooling demand would rise with 223-1050% while the heating demand would decrease 
with 36-58%. [Frank T., 2005]. 
 
Also in The UK is it indicated that climate changes will increase challenges when designing a 
sustainable built environment over the next decade. It is estimated that the peak summer tem-
peratures could be 7°C higher than they are today by 2080. In such warmer summers can the 
high temperatures lead to heat stress, which can be fatal, especially for the elderly. Heat stress 
occurs when the body is in able to maintain the core temperature of 37°C. The danger line for 
Heat stress is set at 35°C with a relative humidity of 50%. 2003 was the hottest summer of Eu-
rope in the last 500 years. 20.000 people, particularly elderly, lost their lives because of heat 
stress, and it was estimated that it was one of the worst natural disasters in 50 years. The case 
study of UK showed that it is possible to achieve an acceptable indoor thermal environment 
when projecting warmer future climates to a building with passive cooling, modern building 
materials and design [Hacker, 2005]. 
 
Also in the colder parts of china has a study of energy demand of commercial buildings in the 
future shown a significant decrease in the heating load and increase in cooling load. The study 
was made from 2 scenarios, one with low forcing of climate change and one with medium forcing 
of climate changes. The result of the study showed that the rise in cooling demand would be larg-
er than the fall in heating demand resulting in a total energy consumption of 8,29% higher in the 
period from 2011-2100 compared to 1971-2010. [Mingcai 2014]

3.2 Climate data comparisons 
It is important to understand the changes that are happening in the updated dataset, in order to 
compare the climates. The new possibilities for designing buildings with weather data for specif-
ic location will not have a direct influence on the energy frame, since it is a requirement that 
BP13 is used in this correlation. Although designers of the building systems, can use the location 
based weather data in their dimensioning and optimizations of the system. If lower requirements 
for ventilation are found through the use of the location based weather data, will it be used as a 
input data in the energy frame estimation, and affect it indirectly. The climate parameters tem-
perature and global radiation are compared for the locations listed in Table 2, plus DRY95.

4. HVAC requirements 
HVAC systems are used to provide an acceptable climate in the space being ventilated. Foremost 
is it a requirement to the air quality of a building, that the health risks of breathing are negligible, 
but it is also important that it is fresh and pleasant. The two important factors that must be con-
sidered when designing a ventilation system are the thermal indoor environment and the indoor 
air quality.  
 
Thermal comfort 
Fanger developed a fundamental approach for assessing the thermal environment. He made it on 
the principle that a person senses his/her own temperatures and not those of the environment. 
By the use of the principle and physical assessment and tests of the body heat exchange with the 
environment, he made an equation called the thermal comfort equation with three requirements 
for achieving thermal comfort.  
 
1. The body must be in thermal equilibrium with the environment. This implies that the 
rate of heat production must be equivalent the rate of heat loss to the environment.   
 
2. Thermal sensation is perceived on the basis of skin temperature, therefore should the 
mean skin temperature be at an appropriate level. 
 
3. The rate of sweating should be at a level preferred by the person, for example would a 
sitting person not prefer to sweat. 
 
Indoor air quality 
A well-known phrase in the building industry is the `Sick building syndrome´ (SBS). It is synonym 
with poor indoor air quality and affects people in 3 areas. 
 
• 
Comfort: the environment could be stuffy or odorous. 
• 
Acute health effects: Burning eyes, chest symptoms, transmission of airborne diseases.  
• 
Chronic (delayed) health effects: may take decades to appear 
 
If a building has a direct affect on peoples health it is called a `Sick building´. Lack of fresh air and 
poor ventilation systems are large contributors to this problem. The sources of pollution inside a 
building could be the building materials, furnishing, occupants, equipment etc. The indoor air can 
also be affected by external factors such as pollution from vehicles, exhaust ventilation, boiler 
flues etc. that gets carried inside by the ventilation air.  
Although the level of pollutants in most cases is below the threshold level values, that has an 
immediate effect on a person, are the most commonly found pollutants in indoor air: odour, car-
bon dioxide, formaldehyde, volatile organic compounds, tobacco smoke, ozone, radon, nitrogen 
oxides and aerosols. The level found is for the majority below the threshold limit values. [Awbi 
2003].

5. Heat load determination 
As equation (1) implies is the thermal load of the building an important parameter when estimat-
ing the requirements for the ventilation system. Ventilation systems are not designed to handle 
events that are statistically rare, and have unusually hot or cold weather. As mentioned is it suffi-
cient if the heating and cooling capacities comply with the requirements to the environment 95% 
of the time of a reference year, since it will be to expansive in terms of initial investment if it was 
to meet the 100% compliance.  If the heat loads are determined with worst-case scenarios and 
wrong assumptions could the steady-state calculation of equation (1) result in an oversizing of 
the system.  The heat contributed to the building on a warm day in the summer, mainly come 
from the heat transferred through windows, and the heat emitted by occupants, lighting and 
electrical devices. If the heat load calculations are based on data for old electrical equipment and 
lighting, or solar radiation are used without taking a shading device into account, can the ventila-
tion system end up being over dimensioned [Melikov, 2004].  
 
It is also important to take into consideration how big a portion of the lighting that will be func-
tioning when the sun is up, and if the building has a control system to adjust its level [SBI, 2000]. 
 
Especially in relation to the warmer climates of DRY13 is it important that the cooling demand of 
the building is lowered in order to minimise energy consumption and maintain thermal comfort. 
So internal heat gains from occupants, artificial lighting and electrical devices are pursued to be 
calculated with precision to lower the cooling demand.  
 
Different methods of how to estimate these loads have been used in the analysis further in the 
report. The cooling load factor (CLF) method used to calculate heat gain from occupants, NB 
method used to calculate the lighting power density and a tables used to determine the heat gain 
from solar radiation, are described and illustrated in appendix B. The occupant utilisation and 
electronic equipment usage and diversity, also influence the heat, and will be described in the 
following. 
 
5.1 Occupier densities & utilisation 
The occupier densities describe the relation between the floor space used per working area. A 
comprehensive study of 381 properties and 1331 individual floor, compared to an IDP database 
of 823 properties from the private sector, concluded that the mean density was one workplace 
per 10.9m2. The study describes 3 types of densities made to measures the appropriate popula-
tion of a building.  
 
The work place density is found from dividing the net internal area (NIA) with the number of 
workplaces contained in the property [m2/workplace]. The workplace density is the most used 
measure when estimating requirements for building services that needs a maximum number of 
people that a building can accommodate at any one time.  
 
The population density is found from dividing the NIA with the number of people that the prop-
erty. The population density can be higher than the work place density if the building functions 
on the basis of flexible working styles and support more people than the number of workplaces it 
contain.

Estimations of the occupancy density in the design phase, influences the heat load used to deter-
mine the requirements for the HVAC system. The “designed density” is high above the “effective 
density” found from measurements in offices. Though it would not be sufficient to use the “effec-
tive density” directly in the determination of the heat loads, since the capacity of the HVAC sys-
tem would be limited. It would result in a low quality of the thermal environment on days that 
deviates from the average. On the other hand would a system designed with the “designed densi-
ty”, rarely reach full capacity. Use of the “workplace density” in the heat load determination 
would be a solution in between the two scenarios. The bottom up model is a method of defining 
the usage profiles of the electronic equipment in the building, and setting up high-end and low-
end scenarios for the diversity factors.  
 
By setting up high and low end criteria both for the occupancy utilisation and the diversity fac-
tors, is it possible to determine the maximum and average loads in the office. The requirements 
for the HVAC system can then be found with a solution in between the two, securing a high quali-
ty of the thermal environment, and lower the sizing and costs of the system.

6. Thermal mass  
The required ventilation rate for thermal comfort calculated using the equation (1), is based on 
the flow rate the ventilation system needs to deliver to remove the energy of the internal heat 
gains. When doing so, is the heat balance of a room at a specific time of the day at a specific 
month estimated. It implies that all the energy is removed by the HVAC system and does not take 
the accumulation of heat in the building materials into account. The accumulation of heat has a 
large impact on the indoor air temperature variation throughout the day. It is the ability of ther-
mal mass to absorb and store the heat in warm period, by convection and radiation, and release it 
again when it gets colder that makes the difference. Thermal autonomy of a building is its ability 
to meet the constraints of the operative temperature while the ambient temperature is varying, 
without use of HVAC systems. A design of a thermal autonomous building cannot be made by the 
use of the heat balance method [Wang et. al, 2014] 
 
6.1 Applying thermal mass 
Thermal mass can be applied to a building as exterior envelope mass (eEM), as for example the 
exterior walls which functions as a storage medium and heat transfer medium. It can also be 
applied as interior thermal mass (iIT), which goes for the internal walls, ceiling, and floor slabs 
that predominantly functions as a storage medium.  
 
The study of [Wang et al., 2014] have shown that the effect on the daily operative temperature 
variations (∆𝑻𝑻𝑜𝑝) is not as significant for use of eEM as for iIT. It also showed that the thickness 
of the concrete used in slabs and walls have a large influence on the ∆𝑻𝑻𝑜𝑝. It was tested by analys-
ing on ∆𝑻𝑻𝑜𝑝 when concrete thickness of the exterior wall, ceiling and floor slabs where varied, 
and the building was located in climates having daily outdoor temperature variations (∆𝑻𝑻𝑜𝑢𝑡) 
ranging from 2-30 °C.  
The study recommends a thickness of 250mm for ceiling and floor slabs, while 100mm was suffi-
cient for external walls. Although the results of the study show that it had no effect on the ∆𝑻𝑻𝑜𝑝 to 
increase the slap thickness beyond 250mm, is it also visible that the differences are insignificant 
when comparing the results of a slap thickness of 200 and 250mm.  
 
European concrete platform explains that the heat penetration depth of a floor slab is 100mm for 
a 24-hour heating and cooling cycle. Although larger sizes can be advantageous if a hot period is 
extended, avoiding the concrete becomes saturated with heat. [European concrete platform, 
2009].

6.1.2 Thermal mass in office buildings 
Thermal mass in office buildings is a widely used approach to passive cooling. The thermal mass 
can provide a heat sink that counters the high internal heat gains from occupants, lighting and 
electrical devices. In addition to the reducing the peak temperature by absorbing the internal 
heat gains, it is also delayed by up to 6 hours. This is large benefit in an office building since the 
peak temperature then occurs late afternoon or evening when the occupants are not present. The 
drop in external temperatures at this point also makes night ventilation and effective mean of 
removing the accumulated heat  Although thermal mass is highly beneficial for office buildings, it 
does also have its obstacles. Reorganisations and rebuild of the office plan are desired in modern 
offices, giving them a high degree of adaptability and the possibility to meet different require-
ments. This desire does not comply with a building having heavy materials in partition walls. It 
makes the external walls, floors and ceilings the most common use for exposure of thermal mass. 
[European concrete platform, 2009]. 
 
It is also common to use suspended ceilings in offices giving a problematic approach to utilizing 
the thermal mass of the slab. The sutdy [Høseggen et. Al, 2008] made in Norway, aimed to meas-
ure how having an exposed concrete ceiling would affect the thermal comfort and the energy 
demand of an office building using night ventilation. The experiment was measured over a year 
in six office rooms, 3 with suspended ceilings (SC2), and 3 in which the suspended ceiling was 
removed (EC2). The experiment showed the following observations: 
 
• 
“For all periods, both the mean temperatures and the maximum temperatures are higher for Room 
SC2 than Room EC2.” 
 
• 
“To obtain the thermal conditions, Room SC2 also requires considerably higher ventilation airflow 
rate, especially during the summer and spring periods.” 
 
• 
“In addition, Room EC2 is according to the occupancy data used more intensively than Room SC2. The 
ratio between occupancy without delay and with a 20 min delay is higher for Room EC2, which indi-
cates that the room is used more continuously than Room SC2.” [Høseggen et. Al, 2008] 
Ventilation in offices, utilising thermal mass, can be applied with different strategies. Exposing 
both the slab and the ductwork is one solution, but it is also possible to deliver the ventilation air 
through a hollow core slab. The exposed hollow core slab is connected with the mechanical venti-
lation system, and supplies air via its cores as illustrated in Figure 17 a. The heat transfer be-
tween the air and the concrete gives it a high performance for both heating and cooling. Another 
possibility is to use an exposed slab with a mechanical under floor ventilation system. A raised 
floor used for routing of small power and communications in offices, can also be a useful means 
for ventilation. The under floor system also reduces the ductwork in the office. It is illustrated in 
Figure 17 b [European concrete platform, 2009].

6.2 Night ventilation 
Another aspect that is not take into account when estimating the required ventilation rate from a 
steady state situation, is the possibility of cooling the thermal mass of the building during night. 
When the outdoor temperature is low enough can natural or mechanical ventilation be used to 
remove large amounts of heat in a short time, providing a heat sink the following day. Natural 
ventilation might be the most energy saving solution, though it is not the easiest to control com-
pared to mechanical ventilation. A control strategy is necessary to achieve the most efficient 
night ventilation and using it at times where the benefits of night cooling are optimal [Wang et al. 
2009]. 
 
Whether it is efficient to run the night ventilation depends on the temperature difference be-
tween the building and the external air. If the difference is to small will the energy consumption 
of operating the ventilation system in night, be larger than the energy removed by the ventilation 
system and cooling demand saved the following day. This also depends on the specific fan power 
(SFP) of the aggregate that determines how much energy the fan uses to move 1m3/s of air. This 
is also described in the DS 13790 that defines night cooling as following: 
 
“Night cooling: the flow rate of external air is set to its maximum during the unoccupied period, 
provided 
a)  the internal temperature is above the set-point for the comfort period,  
b)  the difference between the internal temperature and the external temperature is above a given 
limit. “ [Dansk standard, 2008]. 
 
The quotation indicates that it is important to have the ventilation running at maximum capacity, 
if the criterias a & b are met, and that it should run until the internal temperature meets the set-
point for comfort in the period. 
The lowest threshold that the temperature difference between the building and the outdoor air 
should not drop below when operating the night ventilation, is found when the energy used by 
the fan is equal to the energy removed from the building and saved in cooling demand the follow-

6.3 Summary 
The accumulation of heat in thermal mass influences the thermal environment of a building ab-
sorbing the heat during the day, and releasing it during the night. It evens the temperature oscil-
lations in the building, and thereby also lowers the requirements for the HVAC system, since the 
peak temperature is reduced. By designing the building with sufficient amounts of high-density 
surfaces and thicknesses, is it possible to utilize the effect of the thermal mass. Though office 
buildings tend to hide the ductwork of the ventilation system behind a suspended ceiling, reduc-
ing the heat exchange between the ceiling slab and the room air. Using under floor ventilation, 
hollow core slabs or having an exposed slab and ventilation system, are three possible solutions 
of how to utilise the thermal mass of the floor slab in an office building. 
 
Combining a design with high quantities of thermal mass, with a night ventilation strategy have 
high potential in energy savings for office buildings. The building is cooled with use of the low-
ered outdoor air temperature at night, and since the occupants are not present, is possible to 
have a lowered inlet temperature and supply cold air, without use of electricity.  
 
The ΔTcrit configuration of the night ventilation defines the minimum difference between the 
indoor and outdoor air, for which the ventilation system will be active. The higher the tempera-
ture difference, the more heat will be removed, so it affects the efficiency of the night ventilation. 
The activity of the night ventilation is thereby also influenced by the climate. A way to evaluate 
the interaction between the ΔTcrit and a climate is to evaluate the quantities of the CCP.  
 
The CCP was evaluated for the 6 temperature zones of DRY13. The results illustrated that the 
zones 4, 5 and 6 have smaller possibilities for having night ventilation with a high ΔTcrit. The 
more detailed results for temperature Zone 5 (BP13) and DRY95 showed that using a ΔTcrit of 6°C 
could result in days of which night ventilation was not applicable. How large an impact that the 
climate data will have on the efficiency of the night ventilation, will have to be evaluated in more 
detail using building simulations.

8.1.3 Ventilation system type and principle 
The ventilation system will be designed in accordance with the requirements of indoor environ-
ment class II of DS 15251, having a normal level of expectation. A description of how the air de-
livery system, chilled water system and condenser water system of a mechanical ventilation sys-
tem functions, is described in Appendix C, together with the ventilation principles of mixing and 
displacement ventilation. Due to higher energy efficiency and better control of the comfort, is a 
centralised ventilation system chosen for the building. The AHU is positioned in a plant room on 
the roof of the building and will serve fresh air to each floor through riser ducts. Cooling of the air 
is done by the use of a cooling coil that has chilled water circulating in a vapour compression 
cycle, which rejects the heat to the outdoors through a cooling tower outside of the plant room.  
 
The system will be VAV controlled to lower the energy demand of the building and deliver the 
required amount of fresh air to the given zone. Even though the open plan office does not have 
internal partition walls, will the size of the floor result in a varying operative temperature de-
pending on the heat load in the given zone of the. The zones in the office floor are illustrated in 
Figure 29. 
 
Zone 1 is a perimeter zone with a façade facing south. Although active solar shading will be in-
stalled, it will have a higher heat gain from solar radiation compared to other zones. Zone 2 is a 
centre zone that will have a lack of daylight, contributing to a higher heat gain from artificial 
lighting. Zone 3 is a perimeter zone with a facade-facing north, which will have a high transmis-
sion from windows and low solar gain.

maximum temperatures. It has been used to evaluate the set points for the heating and cooling 
season, and the period in between in which both warm and cold climates occur. These periods 
will be mentioned as the mid season and found during the months of April - May & September - 
October. The min, max and average temperatures of these months are shown in Figure 36.  
 
 
Figure 36. Minimum maximum and average temperatures of the months April, May & October. 
 
It shows that DRY95 reaches high and low temperatures in all the months while the temperature 
zones of DRY13 does not reach as high temperatures in October or as low in September. Also the 
average and minimum temperatures of September are significantly higher for temperature Zone

October for DRY13 and in mid September for DRY95, but since it would require different models,

are listed below.  
 
• 
Heating season from the 1st January - 31th march & 1st November – 31st December 
• 
Mid season from the 1st April – 15th May & from the 1st of October – 31th October  
•

window openings describes the temperature setpoints at which the proportional controller has

Heating and cooling seasons 
The heating and cooling seasons for a regional area can be determined by using [Dansk standard, 
2008]. It presents three methods, which calculates the energy performance of a building and the 
cooling and heating season. Two of the methods presented describes calculation methods using 
the daily heat balance of a building and the set point for heating/cooling, and compare it with the 
average outdoor temperature to determine if the day is within the heating or cooling season. The 
third method is called “The dynamic simulation method” and it uses a building simulation pro-
gram to determine the requirements for cooling and heating. When the requirements are esti-
mated is it possible to evaluate the heating and cooling season by using the “Simple hourly meth-
od”. The method evaluates the heating and cooling demand each hour throughout the year, and

8.2.6 Mechanical ventilation 
The analysis is done using two air handling unit´s (AHU) each having different control-strategies 
for daytime and night time ventilation. One AHU will have timer and supply air temperature con-
trol during daytime. It is used to analyse the energy demand when the air conditioning in the 
cooling season is done by the use of a cooling coil connected to the chiller. The other AHU will 
have a control system for night ventilation, and will besides the daytime ventilation, use another 
fan operation for night time controlled by a set of thermostats on the supply and exterior air. 
Both AHU is set to be VAV temperature controlled, and adjusts the ventilation rate in accordance 
with the temperature in the room and the setpoint for heating  
 
Timer & supply air temperature control 
The layout of the timer and supply air temperature controlled AHU is illustrated in Figure 41. 
 
 
Figure 41. Components of the timer and supply air temperature controlled AHU  
 
The supply and return fans are controlled by the fan operation schedule, and will run from 8 in 
the morning until 17 in the afternoon during weekdays. The set point for the inlet temperature is 
controlled by a schedule, to deliver a inlet air of 18°C in the heating season, and 16°C in the mid 
and cooling season. Throughout the heating season will the heat exchanger first heat up the sup-
ply air, and after will the water based heating coil secure that the temperature of the supply air is 
kept at the set point. The heating coil will adjust the temperature, by changing the pumping fre-
quency of the hot water that runs through its pipes. The pipes from the heating coils are connect-
ed to the boiler in the plant room, where hot water will be supplied from district heating. The 
electricity use of the pumps is included in the energy demand for “Ventilation” in the results of 
IDA ICE.  
 
The cooling coil will secure that the inlet air is kept at the set point in the summer period by 
pumping cold water through its pipes, just as for the heating coil. The pipes of the cooling coil are 
connected to the chillier located in the plant room. The electrical cooling of the water is done 
through the vapour compression cycle, explained in Appendix C. The electricity that the com-
pressor of the chiller uses to cool the water for the cooling coils, has its own energy demand illus-
trated as “Electrical cooling” in the results of IDA ICE.

Normal fan operation is running in weekdays throughout the year. Night vent operation is run-

The parameter “The outdoor temp limit” of Figure 43 uses a thermostat to measure the outdoor 
air temperature and secures that night ventilation is not used in the colder periods of the mid 
season where the cooling demand is eliminated. It stops the night ventilation if the inlet air gets 
below 12°C. The efficiency of the night ventilation is as described earlier also dependent on the

ing coils, and the heat exchanger are not operating and using energy. This can either be done by 
control of the boiler/chiller or as chosen for this project, by controlling the set points for heating 
and cooling. The setpoint for the heating coil and heat exchanger is lowered by -10°C by the 
“heating temp setpoint” parameter, and the setpoint for the cooling coil is raised by 20°C by the 
parameter “Setpoint shift for cooling”. This secures that the coils and heat exchanger will not be 
functioning during night ventilation.

9. Results and discussion 
The results are shown as the required ventilation rate that the models use in order to fulfil the 
requirements set in the Danish standards, and their energy demands for heating, ventilation, 
cooling and artificial lighting. The energy demands are shown as primary energy including the 
multiplication of energy factors. A energy factor of 2.5 has been used for energy consumptions 
from electricity, and 1 for district heating. 
The criteria’s set for the indoor environment in a building class II where earlier found from DS 
15251 to be: 
  
1. CO2 concentration < 900ppm 
2. 100 hours > 26°C 
3. 25 hours > 27°C 
 
The required ventilation rates in order to fulfil these criteria’s are found with a trial and error 
approach. Through a number of simulations are the ventilation rates fine-tuned by being in-
creased or decreased between each simulation, until the criteria’s are just met and the energy 
demand is optimized.  
 
During the heating season when the ventilation system runs on a minimum throughout a day, 
will the CO2 concentration build-up in the office reach maximum level. The minimum ventilation 
rate for the VAV system will therefore influence the maximum CO2 concentration level and is 
adjusted for each model until criteria 1 is fulfilled. The outdoor CO2 concentration is fixed in all 
the models, and changing the weather data only has minor influence the maximum CO2 concen-
tration from the infiltration affected by the wind speed of the location. Changing the room size or 
the amount of occupants have a larger influence on the CO2 concentrations, and the minimum

9.4 Summary 
Building simulations of the open plan office have shown that the implementation of the new DRY 
data will influence the requirements for HVAC systems and the demand for cooling in an office 
building. The combination of a warmer climate data and higher requirements for the energy 
frame, favor building systems that fulfils the requirements for the thermal environment, with low 
energy demands.  
 
Optimised estimations of the occupancy utilisation and electronic equipment diversity factors 
have shown to lower the requirements for the HVAC systems substantially. Three models where 
set up. One having full load, another having the average load found in offices and a third having a 
solution in between. It illustrated that it is possible to lower the requirements by making realistic 
design assumptions of the heat load. Since the internal heat gains have such a big part in the heat 
balance of a building, could it be an idea to make legislations and standardised methods of how 
they are evaluated the best way. The bottom up model makes precise assumptions of the usage of 
the building. It could be discussed if the heat load determination should be taken to the level 
where building systems are designed for the built application only, and not for a flexible uses. 
Predictions of the future climate also indicate that it will get warmer, so in a larger perspective, 
would it also be beneficial to dimension systems that can handle the climates in 50 years.  
 
It was possible to get the open plan office using mechanical ventilation with electrical cooling and 
BP13 weather data, to a low energy building class 2015, but not 2020 class. It implies that future 
building projects will have to apply alternative solutions in order to fulfil the requirements of the 
energy frame. It was found that utilising thermal mass and night ventilation, was one possible 
solution to do so, since it lowered the required ventilation rates and the energy frame radically.  
 
Though it should be pointed out that the night ventilation was only efficient for the models hav-
ing an exposed concrete slab in the ceiling. The models using a suspended ceiling had higher 
demands for cooling, ventilation and heating, and it would not be an efficient solution to use 
night ventilation in such cases. The exposed concrete slab could be combined with different 
methods to supply the ventilation. Underfloor and hollow core slabs are two possibilities that 
utilise thermal mass without having visible ventilation ducts in the office. 
 
The optimization of the night ventilation configurations only showed minor improvements in 
some parts of Denmark, so overall would it not be wrong to use a ΔTcrit of 3°C. It would also se-
cure that the ventilation system is not starting and stopping every 5 min, which was seen for the 
simulations with higher values.  
 
An unexpected result found through the analysis of the open plan office, was that even though 
Copenhagen was evaluated to have the warmest climate, was the demands for ventilation and 
cooling similar, or even lower than for the model using BP13 weather data. It was then discov-
ered that Copenhagen being located in a coastal area having higher wind speeds, utilised the 
natural ventilation airflows better than BP13.  
 
Building projects of the coastal areas seems to be able to take advantage of the higher wind 
speeds, and have better possibilities of making building designs based exclusively on natural 
ventilation. In this relation would it be interesting to examine the possibilities of cooling a build-
ing down with night cooling from natural ventilation, and compare it between buildings located 
on the coastal areas, and buildings located on the inland. 
 
The analysis of the open plan office also illustrated large differences between required ventila-
tion rates of models using BP13, and weather data of Esbjerg located in temperature and wind

10. Conclusion 
The thesis was aimed to examine how the New Danish Reference year influences the require-
ments for HVAC systems and the energy frame, of office buildings in Denmark. The new DRY data 
was published in 2013 (DRY13) and have from 2015 been mandatory for use in building projects 
of Denmark. The New DRY data consist of a building permit data set (BP13), which is a chosen 
climatological year presenting the climate of Denmark generally, and data presenting the climate 
at various locations of Denmark.  
 
It gives designer’s new possibilities to create and use specific location based weather data in 
building simulation programs, to design and dimension building systems. Although when esti-
mating the energy frame, is it a criterion that the BP13 data is used. The DRY13 data sectionalises 
the Danish climate in up to six zones depending on the climatological parameter viewed.   
 
The analyses of DRY13 was carried out by creating weather files for six chosen locations, includ-
ing locations the 5 largest cities of Denmark, and use them together with the BP13 and the for-
merly used Danish reference year from 1995 (DRY95), in building simulations of IDA CIE, for a 
large open plan office and a smaller two person office. The six chosen location each represented 
one of the six climatological temperature zones of DRY13. 
 
 
Climate data simulations 
The consequence of implementing the BP13 and the location based weather data, on the re-
quirements for HVAC and the energy frame was evaluated through answering of three questions 
through the analysis. 
 
1. What are the resulting differences between using DRY95 and the building permit data set of 
DRY13, in building simulations and estimations of the energy frame? 
 
The climate of DRY95 and BP13 was compared, and it was evaluated that BP13 had a high in-
creases of the average temperatures in the months of June-September, and in the yearly amount 
of hours with temperatures < 20°C. The global radiation of BP13 data was increased in the 
months March, April, September and October. 
 
IDA ICE was used to make dynamic simulations of an open plan office building using mechanical 
ventilation with electrical cooling, either having DRY95 or BP13 as weather data. The simulations 
showed that there are more aspects to the comparison, than first thought. The warmer climate of 
BP13 affects the required ventilation rate and the cooling demand in order to sustain an accepta-
ble indoor thermal environment. But besides having a warmer climate, is the climatological wind 
speed zone of BP13, located on the inland, having lowered wind speeds compared to DRY95. It 
was found that the model using BP13 had a lower removal of energy from natural ventilation, in 
the warmest months, compared to DRY95, and it had a significant effect on the requirements for 
HVAC. The required ventilation rate found in order to achieve an acceptable thermal environ-
ment was 44% higher for the model using BP13 than for the model using DRY95. Due to the 
warmer climate did the results of the dynamic simulations also show a slight decrease in the 
heating demand of the BP13 models. 
 
The building was then modelled in Be10 with DRY95 and BP13 weather data. It illustrated that 
the model using BP13 weather data had due to the increased required ventilation rate, higher 
energy consumption for fans, but due to the higher global radiation levels, was the energy de-

==PAGE:95==
mand for lighting lowered. Overall was the resulting energy frame of the model using BP13 ap-
proximately 6% higher than the model using DRY95.  
 
The model using a mechanical ventilation system with electrical cooling, complied with the re-
quirements for low energy building class 2015 for both DRY95 and BP13, but none of them for 
the 2020 requirements.  
 
An alternative solution utilising the thermal mass of an exposed concrete ceiling slab, and night 
ventilation was examined, and it lowered the required ventilation rate substantially and thereby 
also the energy frame of the building. It ended up complying with the requirements for low ener-
gy building class 2020. 
 
2. How are the results of building simulations differentiating, between using the building permit 
dataset of DRY13, or weather data created for a specific location? 
 
When looking at the overall energy consumption for ventilation, cooling heating and artificial 
lighting, was the result from the open plan office model using BP13 similar with the results for 
the models located in temperature zones 4-6, but higher than for the models located in the tem-
perature zone 1-3.  
 
When evaluating the required ventilation rates found to achieve an acceptable indoor environ-
ment in the open plan office, was the only deviation found between the model using BP13 weath-
er data, seen for the model located in Esbejrg (temperature zone 1). Esbjerg located on the east 
coast of Jutland with a combination of low temperatures and high wind speeds, increasing the 
natural ventilation airflows and reducing the requirements for ventilation substantially. The 
required ventilation rate of BP13 was found to be 50% higher for the model located in Esbjerg. 
 
Although the other zones had similar required ventilation rates, was it evaluated that the models 
located in the coastal areas in Copenhagen and Bornholm (temperature zone 4 and 6 respective-
ly), had a significant increase of heat removed from natural ventilation through window open-

system, was it mainly due to the reason that the models using weather data of Copenhagen and 
Bornholm had extra cooling from natural ventilation. If the models were configured to have the 
same airflows through window openings, would the models located in Copenhagen and Born-
holm have higher requirements for ventilation.  
 
The two person offices also illustrated that the south oriented office overall had the lowest ener-
gy consumption for ventilation, cooling heating and artificial lighting, and that the north oriented 
models, had the highest, although they had the highest requirement for the ventilation rate.

The analysis of the open plan office illustrated that the only location that had substantial lower

would be of great benefit in this correlation to estimate the requirements for the HVAC system 
using the location based weather data. The lower requirement for the HVAC system would influ-
ence the energy frame as well. Taking into account that Bornholm and Copenhagen generally 
have warmer climates than BP13 data, would it also be a good idea to evaluate how the thermal 
environment of the building would respond to the local climates in these regions.

Although it is also evaluated, that since the climate data can influence the results in ways that are 
not always predictable, will it in most cases be beneficial to test different possibilities in order to 
get a larger scope an choose the right solution. It could result in a solution that had not been 
though of possible. 
 
Occupancy utilisation and diversity factors 
The analysis also estimated how the occupancy utilisation and use of electronic equipment diver-
sity factors influenced the requirements for HVAC systems. Through building simulations for 
each of the weather data, was it evaluated that when using a design scenario with 90% occupan-
cy utilisation and 75% diversity for laptops, and 60% for screens, was the requirement for the 
mechanical ventilation rate lowered with 16-20%, and the total energy used for ventilation and 
cooling lowered with16-18%. That the ventilation rate is lowered with such a magnitude has a 
beneficial effect on the energy frame. 
 
Thermal mass and night ventilation in offices 
Another focus point from the analysis was the utilisation of thermal mass and night ventilation in 
office building. It illustrated that using night ventilation in an open plan office with low-density 
material in floor and ceiling, was highly inefficient and increased the energy demands for ventila-
tion, heating and cooling. On the other hand were the results when having an exposed concrete 
ceiling slab in combination with night ventilation, showing great results. Both the required venti-
lation rate and the energy demand for heating and cooling was lowered, in comparison with the 
model using electrical cooling to condition the office space.

11. Future studies 
If there had been more time would it be interesting to evaluate how the new DRY data could be 
utilized in the design process. It have been evaluated that due to a warmer climate are the re-
quirements for the HVAC system increasing for the majority of Denmark. The next step would be 
to evaluate how the higher requirements are handled. Go into detail with each of the locations 
used in the thesis, and evaluate what design criteria that would suit specific locations of Den-
mark. Some regions had higher solar radiation, and it could be interesting to evaluate the most 
optimal orientation, shading control and use of PW panels in these regions, compared to others. 
 
For the coastal areas having higher wind speeds, would it be a interesting to estimate the influ-
ence of the orientation in relation to the wind direction and natural airflows in the buildings. To 
evaluate what direction the winds primarily are coming from in the different locations, and how 
the natural airflows are utilized the best way. In this relation, would a research about night venti-
lation driven by natural forces be very relevant.  
 
For most of the coincidences was the coastal areas having both warm climates and high wind 
speeds, so a combination of using natural ventilation as a source of cooling, and having a low 
energy demand for heating due to the warm climate, would result in a building with a overall low 
energy demand.

C.1 Central air conditioning system 
A central air conditioning system uses a plant from which it can serve multiple spaces and floors. 
The cooling for the system is generated with chilled water in a chillier at the plant location, and is 
transferred to the air that is distributed in the building through ducts. The advantage of a central 
system is better control of the comfort conditions, higher energy efficiency and a larger potential 
to manage the heat loads in the zone. Centralized systems are mostly used in mid and high-rise 
buildings with 5 storeys or above [Parlour, 2003]. 
 
A typical chilled water central system is shown in Figure 63. Overall can it be separated into 3 
major subsystems: The air delivery system, the chilled water system and the condenser water

C.1.1 Air delivery system 
Figure 63 shows a typical air conditioning system with a air delivery system using an air-
handling unit (AHU). A fan forces the outside air through the AHU consisting of a range of parts 
that conditions the air before it enters the indoor space. The conditioning is done by the use of a 
heat exchanger that transmits energy between the return air and supply air, heating and cooling 
coils to control the temperature of the supply air, and filters to remove particles and dust, all 
within an insulated metal casing. Humidifiers can also be installed to secure an acceptable level 
of humidity during the dry winter months, by evaporation of water droplets or injection of steam 
directly into the air stream.  
Once the air has been conditioned by the AHU, will it be circulated through the building via a 
ductwork and on to a VV terminal that  released to the space from an a 
The air is then exhausted from the space via a return air system that circulates the air back to the 
heat exchanger before being discharged to the outside. There are mainly two types of air deliver-
ing systems in the central plant system, constant air volume and Variable air volume [Parlour, 
2003]. 
 
Constant air volume (CAV) 
Constant air volume systems supply a constant volume of conditioned air to the zones of the 
building with a varying temperature to control the zone temperature. The temperature is regu-
lated by modulating the water valve on the cooling/heating coil or by controlling the compressor 
of the direct expansion system. This will be explained in detail in the chilled water system part. 
CAV is an all- air system that can be used to maintain a comfortable thermal environment in 
spaces with uniform heating and cooling requirements. 
 
An advantage of the CAV system is that there is no need for damper control in the ducts making 
the installation and balancing easy. Disadvantage of the CAV system is that it has a high-energy 
consumption of fans that operates at a constant high level. It is not possible to have an individual 
control of temperatures in a multizone system and the ducts and units are relatively large in a 
CAV system. [Awbi, 2003] 
Variable air volume (VAV) 
 
117 
==PAGE:118==
Variable air volume systems use a terminal valve box, which has a damper controlled by a zone 
temperature sensor. The supply fan speed is controlled to sustain a constant pressure in the 
ductwork. When the dampers in the valve boxes open/closes due to temperature change, will the 
fan run slower/faster due to pressure changes in the ductwork.  
 
The fan power for VAV systems is optimized by having it maximized when cooling and heating 
loads are high, and minimized when they are low. The flow rate is controlled according to the 
number of occupants and solar gains. VAV systems are also all-air systems that are used in build-
ings with multiple zones. Disadvantages with a VAV system is that the initial costs are about 10 % 
higher than for a CAV system, and the control of the fans and flow rate are more complicated 
[Bhatia, 2014]. 
C.1.2 Chilled water system 
The chilled water system consists of a chiller that supplies chilled water to the cooling coils of the 
AHU´s. It consists of two separate water tube circulations, one that absorbs the heat from the air 
going through the AHU, and another that rejects the heat to the outside. They are connected 
through a vapour compression cycle that transmits the heat from one tube to another. There are 
3 main components in the vapour compression cycle, the evaporator, compressor and condenser. 
The following describes the components in an example using water and the refrigerant R12. 
 
Evaporator 
Figure 64 shows a section through the evaporator, which cools the water for the cooling coils. 
The evaporator consists of a tank with a liquid refrigerant that is kept at its boiling point. Water 
with a temperature above the boiling point flows through a tube in the evaporator. The liquid 
refrigerant in the tank extracts heat form the water in the tubes and evaporates into a gas. The 
water returns to the cooling coil colder and absorbs the heat in the inlet air before it is pumped 
back to the evaporator.  
 
Figure 64. Section through the evaporator showing the tube with water and the tank with liquid refrigerant. 
Found from [Parlour, 2003] 
Compressor 
When the gas has evaportated is it pumped into the compressor. The compressor pressurises the 
gas to a higher pressure level than in the evaporator, raising its boiling point. 
 
Condenser 
When the gas Is pressurised is it pumpåed into the condenser. Figure 65 shows a section of the 
condenser tank. A tube is looped inside the condenser circulating water with a temperature be-
low the boiling point of the evaporated gas. The water in the tube extracts heat from the vapour 
gas, condensing it to a liquid. This raises the temperature of the water that is  circulated to the 
condenser water system.

C.2 Ventilation principles 
Two of the most common ventilation principles in commercial buildings are mixing and dis-
placement ventilation. 
 
Mixing ventilation 
Mixing ventilation is the most widely used ventilation principle. With a proper design can it be 
used for heating and cooling as well as ventilation, and it cope with much larger heat loads than 
displacement ventilation. The principle of mixing is shown in Figure 67. It dilutes the concentra-
tion of contaminants in the room by supplying fresh air usually from high levels in the room. The 
principle requires that the supply air and room air is mixed to acceptable levels by the monu-
ment of the supply jet and the buoyancy. Due to a supply of air above the occupied zone are the 
contaminant concentrations always higher within the zone.  
[Awbi, 2003] 
 
119 
==PAGE:120==
 
 
Figure 67. Example of a mixing ventilation principle. Taken from [Awbi, 2003] page 88. 
 
Displacement ventilation 
This principle functions by displacement of room air with fresh outdoor air. It is used in large 
buildings with high internal gains, and mainly where cooling is needed. Momentum is small, but 
buoyancy is the dominant force of displacement ventilation and the airflow can be either up-
wards or downwards. Upwards airflow system is the most widely used system, since it gives the 
best indoor air quality. An example of such a system is shown in Figure 68 where fresh outdoor 
air is supplied at low velocity close to the floor, and rises throughout the room by a combination 
of buoyancy and momentum forces. The presence of people and warm surfaces creates tempera-
ture and concentration stratification with cool clean air in the lower part of the room, and con-
taminated warm air above the occupied zone [Awbi 2003]. 
 
 
Figure 68. Example of a displacement ventilation principle. Found from [Awbi, 2003] page 87. 
 
Choice of mixing or displacement ventilation 
Two important factors when choosing between displacement and mixing ventilation are the cool-
ing load and ventilation rate. The supply air of displacement ventilation is delivered directly to 
the comfort zone, and with a high cooling load or ventilation rate is there a higher risk for caus-
ing draft at floor level. The temperature and concentration stratification can also cause discom-
fort since the temperature gradient between the occupant’s feet and head can get too high [Awbi 
2003]

Appendix G – Natural ventilation 
 
Natural ventilation can be supplied using the following three principles: 
1. Single-side ventilation, supplying air from one side of the building. 
2. Cross-ventilation supplying natural ventilation between 2 or more outer walls. 
3. Buoyancy ventilation, used in rooms with high ceiling height. 
 
It is recommended that the room depth do not exceed 2,5 times the room height, when using 
single side ventilation, since the air the enters the room needs to flow out through the same 
opening, making it less efficient than cross ventilation. For cross-ventilation is it allowed to have 
a room depth up to 5 times the room height. When air flows through a window is, is it only flow-
ing through a part of the geometrical area of the window. It is taken into account by multiplying 
the geometrical area with a contraction factor. Another parameter that influences the ventilation 
rate is the friction of the window opening that affects the air speed. This is also taken into ac-
count by multiplying the air velocity with a speed factor. The ventilation rate through a window 
is calculated using equation (13) [Jensen, 2005].

Appendix I – Night ventilation tool for TCD 
The tool looks at a typical day of a given month and calculates difference between the hourly 
temperature variations of the building and outdoor air. With data of the SFP is the threshold for 
𝚫𝑇𝑚𝑖𝑛 estimated and used together with the possibility to increase it to define the 𝚫𝑇𝑐𝑟𝑖𝑡. The 
temperature difference found throughout the day is then hourly compared with the 𝚫𝑇𝑐𝑟𝑖𝑡 to 
evaluate the timespan for which night ventilation will be active, and calculates the average tem-
perature during the hours of operation. These values are then used in the TCD program as a 
source of cooling. The yellow coloured cells in the spread sheet are for input data and the green 
coloured cells are the results. The cells with a red marks has notes that describes its parameter 
 
Data of the daily mean temperature, its amplitude defining the minimum and maximum and the 
time of day it has its peak, is typed into the yellow cells for both the building and outdoor. They 
are illustrated in Figure 75.

evaluate the 𝚫𝑇𝑚𝑖𝑛, and with an added value is the 𝚫𝑇𝑐𝑟𝑖𝑡 evaluated. The cells used are illustrated 
in Figure 76. 
 
 
Figure 76. Input data cells, for SEL value and El factor and the result of 𝚫𝑻𝑻𝒄𝒓𝒊𝒐𝒐.
