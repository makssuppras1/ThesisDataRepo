I.
ABSTRACT
The characterisation of fast ions in a background plasma is a key challenge for understanding the
influence fast alpha particles generate through the fusion process. These fast ions are characterised
by 5D distribution functions. In order to measure these functions, a good combination of diagnostics
is required. In general, more diagnostic lines of sight (LOS) will improve the reconstruction of the
distribution function from measurements. When installing new diagnostics, the most information
is revealed if these cover a part of phase space which is not already detected by existing LOS. It
is of crucial importance to understand how fast ions behave in a background plasma as we move
into the era of burning plasma physics where fast alpha particles in magnetic confinement devices
heat the plasma. Today, fast ions are generated by neutral beam injection (NBI) and ion cyclotron
resonance heating (ICRH). For each discharge in a stellerator or tokamak, these fast ions can be
characterised by slowing-down distribution functions.
One of the diagnostics for measuring fast
ions in Wendelstein 7-X (W7-X), fast-ion D-alpha (FIDA) spectroscopy, is investigated in order to
estimate the complementary nature of the different diagnostic views in the system. Each diagnostic
is characterised by a weight function which describes how well this diagnostic measures different
regions of phase space. The FIDA diagnostic at W7-X has three different fans of sight lines, one
toroidal, and two mixed (neither poloidal nor toroidal), which gives rise to two significantly different
weight functions which are able to observe different regions of velocity space. Neoclassical collision-
free gyro-centre equations are solved using the Beams3D code in order to see how different physical
volumes couple different regions of velocity space. It is seen that certain areas of velocity space
are coupled for different physical volumes such that if the volumes have different magnetic field
strengths, the pitch will change due to adiabatic invariance of the magnetic moment. Furthermore,
if the starting region permits trapped particles, these will be observed with pitches of either sign.
It is demonstrated that at least for two volumes at W7-X, the velocity-space regions couple in a
similar way for five different magnetic configurations, the standard, low-iota, high-iota, low-mirror,
and high-mirror. For the investigated volumes, the velocity-space coupling is fairly insensitive to
the magnetic configuration such that only analyses using the standard configuration is presented for
the remainder of the project.
The coupling between FIDA LOS is investigated by starting a particle distribution evenly spaced
in energy and pitch covering all relevant values for particles injected by the NBI system of W7-X
in each LOS. All particles are tracked when they enter a LOS. The nested flux surfaces are clearly
visible, and it is seen that the particles stay in the vicinity of these. The weight function of each
LOS is taken into account which shows a relatively larger correlation between LOS of different flux
surfaces compared to the correlation between those on the same flux surfaces. Due to the large
difference between the weight functions from the toroidally viewing fan and from the two others,
there are differences in, which particles will be observed by redshifted or blueshifted light respectively
for these fans. Thus the relative magnitude of the redshifted signal in one fan compared to the other
will reveal asymmetries in the fast ion distribution function. Furthermore, the differences in the
weight functions also affect the ability of each fan to detect trapped particles, such that the trapped
particles can be identified by using a measurement from the different fans. Based on the analysis, it
is concluded that the physical volume covered by the currently installed FIDA system is well covered
in velocity-space. This means that if every flux surface is covered by a FIDA measurement volume,
ideally all passing particles are detected. However, there may still be trapped particles which are
not detected. Furthermore, the bulk ions, the beam emission and impurity lines cover part of the
FIDA spectra and have much higher intensities, such that the FIDA radiation is not observable in
all wavelength ranges, and hence real world FIDA systems do not observe the entire velocity space.
Here we focus on the Doppler shift, which is the most important effect of the spectrum formation.
It is demonstrated that the error on the reconstruction of the 5D distribution function for an NBI-
injection discharge increases relatively more when removing lines of sight with small correlation to

based on these principles it is proposed that when installing new fast ion diagnostics for W7-X, it

Synthetic measurements of the slowing-down distribution function for a single slice of an NBI at

The distribution function of a single segment is characterised by the peak at the injection energy
and a tail. The tail is too short for typical slowing-down distribution functions which is explained by
an unknown bug in the simulation. Due to time constraint, this bug was not located or solved. Only
a narrow set of sight lines actually measures a non-zero signal for the beam segment in question,
indicating that the particles stay near magnetic flux surfaces. Furthermore, the toroidal sight lines
observe a much higher signal than the perpendicular as a consequence of the shape of the weight
function which is highly dependent on the angle between the magnetic field and the viewing direction
of the LOS.
II.
INTRODUCTION
Fusion energy is one of the most promising forms of green energy. It utilizes the strong nuclear force to
merge two lighter element and create a heavier element while releasing energy in the process. This is due
to the decrease in binding energy pr. nucleon for elements with lower atomic number than iron through
the famous formula, E = mc2, by Einstein.[1] Therefore, the fusion processes D(T, n)He4, D(D, n)He3 and
D(D, p)T produce respectively 17.6 MeV, 4 MeV and 3.3 MeV of energy[2][3] due to the mass difference
of the reactants and the products. This means that 1 g of D, T fuel can produce an equivalent amount of
energy as burning around 1 t of coal.[4]
In order to make the light elements fuse, the particles need to reach very high speeds. When considering
thermal fusion, you need temperatures above 5 · 107 ◦C to get a significant amount of fusion due to the
energy dependence of the reaction. Thus a way of confining the plasma without having it touching any
material is needed. The most widely used confinement type is magnetic confinement as used in stellerators
and tokamaks.[5–8] In both cases a magnetic field is contained in some kind of torus shape. However, in
order to avoid that the particles gyrating around the magnetic field lines drift and eventually are lost, the
field lines have to curve around the torus.[8, 9] This can be accomplished in three ways. By applying a
plasma current in the toroidal direction, by making the magnetic axis not lying in a plane, or by rotating
the poloidal cross section of flux surfaces when going toroidally around the torus.[8, 9] The tokamak takes
advantage of the first method while the stellerator uses a combination of the two latter.
By considering simple energy balancing for any fusion device, using a D −T plasma, it is possible to set
up a criterion known as the Lawson criterion,
neτET ≳5 × 1021 m−3keVs,
(1)
where neτET is known as the Lawson triple product. ne is the electron density, τE is the energy confinement
time and T is the temperature.[5] Thus in order to increase the efficiency of a fusion device, one can either
increase the energy confinement time which is typically done by scaling up the device in order to minimize
the energy loss (scales with surface) compared to the stored energy (scales with volume). Otherwise, one
can increase the temperature. This will however make it more difficult to confine the ions due to their
increased velocities. In addition this will decrease the energy confinement time since the energy difference
relative to the surroundings increases. Finally, one can increase the density. There are, however, different
limitations on the density caused by instabilities of different kinds.[5]

For almost all plasma discharges, the distribution function for some ions are not Maxwellian. This is
for example the case for plasmas heated by ion cyclotron resonance heating (ICRH) and neutral beam
injection (NBI) which are the two often used heating schemes for proper high performance tokamaks or
stellarators. Therefore, in order to understand the state of the plasma, eventually it is a goal to make
tomographic 5D reconstructions of the distribution function. Such reconstructions are nowadays possible
in 2D[10–18] and in tokamaks in 3D[19] but they turn out to be extremely difficult in 5D since the number
of sight lines are restricted by the limited space around the device and the many diagnostics and other
devices such as the cooling system for the superconducting coils taking up this space. Thus there often is
an underdetermined system with large noise levels which makes the conversion of measured signal to the
distribution function a notoriously difficult inverse problem.[20, 21]
A.
Plasma tomography
When doing fusion experiments, it is important to know the state of the plasma at all times. By going to the
continuum limit, all species in the plasma can be defined by distribution functions. For each species, α, one
can define a distribution function, fα, where fα(v, x)d3vd3x describes the number of particles in a small
position-space volume d3x around a position x with a velocity in a small velocity-space volume element d3v
around a velocity v. Since particles will gyrate quickly around magnetic field lines due to the Lorentz force,
it is efficient and customary to split the velocity into one parallel part, v∥, in direction of the magnetic field
B and two perpendicular, (v⊥1, v⊥2). Since the particles gyrate, the perpendicular direction is converted
to polar coordinates, (γ, v⊥), (v⊥1, v⊥2) = (v⊥cos γ, v⊥sin γ) where the gyrating angle, γ, is treated as a
random variable, which has no impact on the distribution function, due to the large cyclotron frequency
for ions and electrons respectively, ωgi = |qi| B/mi ∼

III.
WENDELSTEIN 7-X
Since this project concerns the specific diagnostics at Wendelstein 7-X (W7-X), the device is introduced
in this section. W7-X is the world’s largest stellarator located at the Max Planck Institute for Plasma
Physics in Greifswald, Germany. It has 5-fold symmetry which means that the magnetic configuration
repeats itself after a toroidal turn of 360/5 ◦= 72 ◦. The device is among other diagnostics equipped
with a FIDA spectroscopy system and a Collective Thomson Scattering (CTS) system. See section IV
for details about these diagnostics. The stellarator has an average major radius of 5.5 m and an average
minor radius of 0.53 m which gives a total plasma volume of 30 m3. The superconducting magnets give
a maximum magnetic field strength of 3 T. The device is designed to be operated with both helium,
hydrogen and deuterium plasmas.[25] The device has mainly been operated with hydrogen plasmas in the
previous campaigns.
W7-X is quasi-isodynamic.[26] This means that W7-X is omnigenous and the contours of constant magnetic
field strengths are poloidally but not toroidally closed.[8] That the magnetic configuration is omnigenous
means that all time averaged drifts in the radial direction are zero. This means that on average, a particle
stays on the same magnetic surfaces since the poloidal flux coordinate stays constant. Among other effects,
it implies that the radial displacement of trapped ions vanishes at low collisionality.[8] Since almost all
discharges to date have been done with hydrogen plasmas, hydrogen is used as the species for background-
and fast-ions in this project.
IV.
DIAGNOSTICS
In this section, some common diagnostics used for measuring fast ions and their weight functions will be
introduced. A lot of emphasis is put on the weight functions of the given diagnostics since the weight
function is an indication of, which part of phase space is accessible for a given diagnostics. The weight
function is also key to inverting a measurement signal and hence successfully recover the 5D distribution
function of a discharge.
A.
Neutron detector
Due to the fusion processes, D(T, n)He4 and D(D, n)He3, neutrons will be emitted from a D- or DT-
plasma. Since the fusion process is stochastic, the emission of neutrons will be roughly isotropic in space.
Neutron detectors measure the energy of an incoming neutron, from which the speed can be derived. From
energy and momentum conservation, for each fusion process the energy of the neutrons can be calculated,
∼14.0MeV for D(T, n)He4 and ∼2.45MeV for D(D, n)He3.[27] Using energy and momentum conservation
and the measured velocity of the neutron, we can deduce the velocities of the reactants, D or T, undergoing
the fusion process.[28] This means, that we use this data to reconstruct the D or T distribution function

C.
Other diagnostics
For completion, two other common diagnostics for fusion plasmas are introduced here. The weight functions
consist of the same components as introduced above but the measurements are made in a different manner.
Among other diagnostics for the reconstruction of the fast-ion distribution functions are collective Thomson
scattering (CTS). CTS relies on the detection of scattered electromagnetic waves in the millimetre range.
This diagnostic also relies on measuring a Doppler shift such that the projected velocity is related to the
change in frequency, νδ, and wave number, kδ, by νδ = ukδ/2π. When u is known, the weight function is
completely determined by the Doppler effect and therefore given by equation (5).[18, 35]
Another diagnostic used for reconstruction of the fast-ion distribution function is gamma-ray spectroscopy.
Like with neutron detectors, the diagnostic depends upon detecting a product of a fusion reaction. In this
case either through an one-step reaction, e.g. D(D, γ)4He or T(D, γ)5He, where γ is a gamma-photon, or
through a two-step reaction with impurities, e.g.
9Be(α, n)12C∗where the 12C is in an exited state and
will at some point emit a photon with a characteristic energy of 4.44MeV. Similar to the FIDA diagnostic,
a red- or blueshift is measured for the photon and the velocity of the fast-ion reactant is inferred from this
measurement.[18]
V.
CORRELATION BETWEEN MEASUREMENTS IN DIFFERENT SPATIAL VOLUMES
In order to maximize the information content of measurements taken by a system of diagnostics, it is of

For a collisionless orbit without any electric field, it is expected that energy is conserved, and thus only
changes in the pitch are expected when a particle moves from V1 to V2. If an electric field is present, the
energy can change by converting potential electrostatic energy into kinetic energy. In order to investigate
how different volumes are connected, the particle orbits are split into passing orbits, where the orbit visits
every possible toroidal angle, ϕ, and trapped orbits, where the particle bounces back an forth between two
values of the toroidal angle, ϕ, ϕ1 and ϕ2.

B.
Numerical results
In the case of Wendelstein 7-X (W7-X), it is investigated which part of physical space is also connected
in velocity space. This will enable the determination of the importance of each diagnostic LOS. Except
if explicitly mentioned, the magnetic configuration used for the simulation is the standard configuration,
EIM, since it is in the middle of the configuration space for the W7-X.

2.
Testing different magnetic configurations
It is of interest to investigate how the phase space correlation between volumes depend on the magnetic
configuration. Therefore a similar simulation as above is started for the low iota (DBM), high iota (FTM),
low mirror (AIM), and high mirror (KJM).[37]
A similar analysis as in section V B 1 is presented here for the four different magnetic configurations; low
iota (DBM), high iota (FTM), low mirror (AIM), and high mirror (KJM) using the volumes V1 as starting
volume and V2 as detection volume. The simulation with a densely populated region of interest is shown
here to illustrate how little effect the magnetic configuration has on the correlation between the phase
space of the two regions in question, V1 and V2. The starting distribution is approximately similar to
figure (4a) when neglecting noise due to the varying magnetic field inside the finite volume.
See figure
5 for the relative difference of the distribution of particles entering V2 compared to those entering the
volume in the standard configuration and figure 6 for the same distribution of particles in V1. Only small
variations on the order of 2% are observed. The largest variations are seen in the region with large signal
indicating a non-systematic derivation which can be attributed to noise. It is seen that the qualitative
behaviour is similar in all cases. In order to restrict the extend of the project, it is therefore decided only
to use the standard configuration for the rest of the project.

For each FIDA LOS, 10 000 hydrogen particles evenly distributed, with 100 steps each for p ∈[−0.9; 0.9]
and E ∈[10 keV; 55 keV], are started randomly distributed in the two cylinders defining the intersection
of the two NBI’s with the given LOS. A non-collisional simulation is started using Beams3D, following
all particles for 1.50 ms sampling the position of each particle every 40 ns. This means that the fastest
particle travels a maximum of ∼13cm each step and thus a trajectory with sufficient spatial resolution can
be achieved and interpolation is later used to get a finer spatial resolution. The total simulation time is
chosen such that it is on the order of 10 collision times. That is, the total simulation time is approximately
10τcol = 10/νii where νii is the ion-ion collision rate and the collision time is given by equation (24). Even
though the simulation is run collisionless and therefore only a runtime of approximately 1 collision time
is justified, this method will give a representative picture of the correlation between different measuring

being detected also in any other LOS. The reconstruction method rely on Tikhonov regularisation.[20, 21]

reconstruction is to be attempted in the future.
There is a very small falling trend when removing the LOS with maximum correlation. This should not

reconstruction.

VII.
PROPOSING NEW DIAGNOSTICS FOR W7-X
Although figure 12 gives a lot of information about coupling between the different LOS for the FIDA
diagnostics of W7-X, it does not contain any information about, whether certain regions of phase space
are not covered by the current diagnostics. In order to increase the amount of information and avoid data,
which is already accessible for existing diagnostics, one would either place the new measurement volume
so it detects particles on a magnetic surface not already covered by the existing diagnostic measurements
or one would have to pick a diagnostic with a significantly different weight function placed on the same
location. In the case of FIDA, this can either be done by varying the angle between the LOS and the
magnetic field or pick a diagnostic with an entirely different type of weight function. The first method
would give more information about the spatial dimensions for the 5D distribution function and the second
method would give more information about the velocity dimensions.
Figure 16 supports the conclusion that the given spatial region is well covered by at least five LOS where
two are toroidally viewing with ϕ ∼10 ◦, AEA21 (blue) and three are neither poloidally nor toroidally
viewing, AEM21 (green) (two fans) and AET21 (yellow) (one fan), all three with ϕ ∼136◦. Using figure 10
in combination with figure 16, it is seen that the entire velocity space is covered in the spatial region which
is covered by the FIDA-diagnostics. Figure 17, 18, 19, and 20 show that the difference in the blueshifted
and redshifted signal between the different fans give information about trapped particles. Furthermore,
asymmetries in pitch will also be detected by this difference. Therefore, a lot of information is available if
at least two differently viewing fans are used, one from the AEA21 (blue) fan and one from the AEM21
(green) or the AET21 (yellow) fan.
Currently two angles with the magnetic field is covered to a good extend in the combined measurement
volume. The centre region of the measuring volume is covered a lot more than the outer regions so unless
only the investigation of the central flux tubes are of interest, the LOS farther from the centre are more
critical for the reconstruction.
For a new diagnostic to detect highly trapped particles, the measurement volume has to be located near
a minimum of the magnetic field. Since the magnetic configuration of W7-X has 5-fold symmetry with
the maximum magnetic field strength at ϕ = n72 ◦,
n = [0, 1, ..., 4], it would be beneficial to place the
new diagnostic somewhere in the middle of each segment, ϕ = n72 + 36 ◦,
n = [0, 1, ..., 4]. Since the
current FIDA measuring volumes are located at around ϕ ∼88 ◦, some but not all trapped particles will
be detected by the diagnostic. On the other hand, if one is only concerned with detecting the fast ions
coming from the seventh or eighth NBI-beam, every particle will be detected as long as the entire beam is
covered by the FIDA LOS.
In principle, in order to find the optimal location for a new diagnostic, all volumes must be connected to
the current measurement volumes using a similar approach as in section V B 1 keeping the volumes small
enough so that the magnetic configuration is homogenous. However, when sufficient resolution in velocity
space is sought, the computations turn out to be extremely costly and therefore unfeasible. If only the
passing particles are of interest, it also should be sufficient to cover all flux surfaces for some cross section
of W7-X. Some simple principles will tell you to cover all flux surfaces and if this is done at almost the same
toroidal angle, all passing particles will be detected by the diagnostics. Furthermore, if a diagnostic relying
on measuring a Doppler shift is used, one toroidally viewing fan and one poloidally or mixed viewing fan is
required in order to give information about trapped particles. Furthermore, if feasible the new diagnostic
should be placed at a minimum of magnetic field strength such that all trapped particles of this well are
detected. This minimum should also be in the segment of largest interest with respect to the fast ions,
since the trapped particles remain in the same segment of the machine.

VIII.
SYNTHETIC MEASUREMENTS
In order to aid the reconstruction of slowing down distribution functions or deposition profiles, synthetic
FIDA measurements are simulated for a Beams3D slowing down run. One of the advantages of working
with synthetic data is that you can create unphysical distribution functions through simulations. This
could be utilised in order to split the deposition profile of a single beam into a number of segments and
giving each segment of the beam its own distribution function. Thus a synthetic measurement of only the
particles stemming from a specific part of the beam can be conducted. This will then aid in reconstructing
the deposition profile through an expansion of the distribution function into the bases stemming from the
different beam parts. An example of the distribution function and all measurements of a single segment of
the beam is shown in figure 22 for a slowing down run taking 0.1 s. The injection peak for the distribution
function is clearly seen. Furthermore, the distribution function is located around a pitch of p = −0.5
indicating the injection direction of the beam.
As expected, a tail is observed but it is shorter than
expected which is usually an indication that the slowing-down simulation was ended prematurely. It is
expected that the tail would extend down to very small energy, E ≲10 keV, for a proper slowing-down
simulation. Since the simulation is running for approximately 700 slowing down times, there must be a
==PAGE:35==
31
bug somewhere in the code. Due to time constraints, this bug was not detected and corrected. For the
measurement, it is seen that only a few LOS actually detect this beam. This stems from, that the particles
from the given segments are close to the same flux surfaces and thus only LOS scanning these flux surfaces
will detect a significant signal. Furthermore, the parallel sight lines have a much higher signal than the
perpendicular. This stems from the fact that as seen from figure 10, the weight function is larger for the
parallel than perpendicular sight line. For a perpendicular sight line, u can take any value from −v⊥to
v⊥while for a parallel sight line, u = v∥. This causes the weight function to be more narrow for a more
parallel sight line which again, since the weight function integrates to unity, causes the weight function
to be larger for the toroidal sight lines. Therefore, the measurements are also larger for the toroidal fan.
Although, it would be expected that collisions would be able to transport particles across field lines, it is
seen that this effect is minor although just observable. This is again very uncharacteristic for slowing-down
simulations and are most likely also an artefact from the failed simulation. As expected, the toroidal (blue)
fan detects negative u and the first mixed fan (green) detects a much weaker blueshifted signal with u > 0,
since p is negative for the entire non-zero part of the distribution function. This follows from the weight
functions in figure 10. The reason that no significant signal is observed in the third fan (yellow) could be
that the LOS are much more sparse such that the particles miss these.
IX.
CONCLUSION AND OUTLOOK
A gyro centre code, Beams3D, has been used in order to simulate collisionless particle trajectories and
see how different physical volumes connect different volumes of velocity space in the 5D-phase space.
According to neoclassical theory, changes in pitch are observed when the magnetic field strength changes
between the two physical volumes. Furthermore, when the particles are initialized at a location not with
maximum magnetic field strength, trapped particles are observed.
The weight function of the FIDA LOS at W7-X is investigated and great differences are observed between
the toroidally viewing fan, AEA21 (blue) and the first mixed-viewing fan, AEM21 (green), and between
the toroidal fan and the second mixed-viewing fan, AET21 (yellow). This means that the two non-toroidal
fans, AEM21 (green) and AET21 (yellow), can provide crucial information about the fast ions since they
can accurately determine the difference of the velocities of the given particles. The magnetic flux surfaces
are clearly visible in the correlation between the FIDA LOS indicating that particles stay near the magnetic
flux surfaces. Furthermore, it is seen that there is a relatively larger cross talk between measurements
of particles drifting across magnetic flux surfaces compared to those staying on the same, indicating that
it is easier to detect particles drifting slightly off the magnetic surfaces. Finally, it is demonstrated that
the information of particles detected by blueshifted or redshifted light in the toroidal fan, AEA21 (blue),
compared to those detected by blueshifted or redshifted light in the two other fans, AEM21 (green)
and AET21 (yellow), give information about the trapped particles and asymmetries in the pitch for the
distribution function.
It is concluded that the centre LOS for all fans contain the most correlated information since these flux
lines have a relatively better coverage of information than those lying further away from the centre.
Furthermore, due to the LOS’ different angles with the magnetic field, the entire velocity space is well
covered in the observed spatial region which covers all flux surfaces to a certain extend. Thus the bulk
is very well characterised by the FIDA system. This analysis should help prioritise which LOS is most
critical for reconstructing the fast-ion distribution function of a discharge.
A reconstruction through synthetic measurements is conducted and it is demonstrated that by removing the
LOS with minimum correlation one by one, the reconstruction error slowly increases while when removing
==PAGE:36==
32
the LOS with maximum correlation, the error remains constant or drops until the majority of the LOS
have been removed. Thus it is possible through the earlier analysis to create a prioritised list going from
most important to least important LOS. It is however worth mentioning that different LOS have different

a slice of an NBI at W7-X are conducted. The distribution function shows some slowing down behaviour
although the tail of the distribution function is shorter than expected. This discrepancy is most likely a
consequence of a programming bug. The measurements of the distribution are only non-zero for a narrow
band indicating that the particles stay near the magnetic flux surface of birth. Further work should be
conducted in generating correct synthetic measurements and using these to do a reconstruction of the
distribution function or deposition profile and when inversion of synthetic data is demonstrated, the same
exercise should be done using experimental data.

The need for a twisted magnetic field in a torus shaped magnetic confinement device becomes apparent
when studying the drift velocities (A42). Due to B ∝1/R in such devices, ∇B points in the negative
R-direction. Since the magnetic field is in the toroidal direction, ˆb = ˆϕ, electrons will drift in the −z-
direction and ions in the z-direction. This will then create a strong electric field in the −z-direction which
then causes the electrons and ions to drift in the R-direction and eventually be lost at the vessel wall.
