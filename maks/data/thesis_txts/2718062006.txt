Abstract
English
This thesis aims to study fire spreading from a burning container to adjacent containers in
a cargo compartment aboard a container ship. Historical fire scenarios aboard container
ships have proven extremely dangerous and complicated. In the past decades, container
transport has increased drastically; therefore, container ships’ capacity has more than
tripled since 1996. Fire incidents are increasing, especially in the greater vessels, and
indications are that the fire extinguishing and fire detection systems have not kept in
phase with the vessel’s enlargements. Therefore, knowledge of the fire-spreading mech-
anism is one of many essential parts in increasing safety aboard container ships. This
study consists of experimental fire studies of parts from a container and computational
fluid dynamic modeling using the computer program Fire Dynamic Simulator FDS. The
objectives were to study heat transfer from a burning container to adjacent containers
and predict possible fire-spreading mechanisms using the experimental data and findings
to support the numerical simulation of container fire. The results indicates that exposing
temperatures of a burning container with certain heat release rate can spread to adjacent
containers, however ignition inside the adjacent containers will depend on the material
being transported and the condition of the container.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Since containerization started in 1956 the global demand for transported goods has
dramatically increased, especially in the last 25 years where the annual global trade
has risen from 40 millions TEU’s1 to almost 160 million in 2018. To take advantage of
this economy of scale, the container line companies have heavily increased their vessel
capacities, e.g., the world’s biggest container ship in 1996 was Regina Maersk with 6400
TEU’s compared to 2COSCO SHIPPING TAURUS built in 2018 having 20.119 TEU’s.
The numbers are still growing, and in 2020 HMM3 launched the two world’s largest
container ships with a capacity of 23.964 TEU’s. Historically fires aboard container
vessels have proven to be complicated. The increased capacity leads to increased risk
of fire and even more complex fire scenarios, like the accident of the Maersk Honam
proved in the Arabian Sea 2018. Among involved members and stakeholders in the
shipping freight industry, concerns are raised, and according to Gard Cargo Claim team
determined that on average a fire aboard container vessels have occurred weekly during
the first half of 2019 [18]. Fire aboard container vessels are more frequent due to large
quantities and variation of cargo being transported. As stated in a report by the Nordic
Association of Marine Insurance, there is an increasing number of fire in cargo aboard
container vessels over 100,000 gross tons [10].
Containers aboard container vessels can be stored on deck under free air and inside
the hull in a cargo compartments. Previous fire scenarios aboard container ships where
the ignition can be attributed to the cargo inside a container may occur both on deck and
in the cargo compartment. Both those scenarios are extremely dangerous and complex,
and can cause long firefighting with increased risk to human life and of total loss of vessels.
However, there are differences between these two events. This difference mainly consists
in firefighting strategies, fire fighting equipment and fire detection equipment. When
fire occurs on deck there are more possibilities to use water as a extinguisher directly
to the fire with out increasing the risk factor of loosing stability and ship strength, for
fires in cargo compartment the direct water usage inside the ships hull can increase those
risk factors. Most common fire detection and extinguishing systems inside cargo holds
consists of sampling pipe system which sometimes are called sniffers. The sampling pipes
are located at the top of the pillar bulkhead inside all cargo holds and each hold has an
individual sampling pipe line from the ships fire station that usually is normally located

The variety of cargo being transported leads different fire scenarios aboard container

pointed out that the main fire causes in 50 different cases being studied were the following:
self-heating cargo covered by 5IMDG Code, lithium-ion batteries, cargo reacting with
water, faulty reefer units, welding, sun exposure and ship collisions.
In 1977 the U.S. Coast Guard did four full-scale fire tests to examine the potential
fire development inside 20-ft shipping containers. According to Randal Eberly [17],
all four tests produced similar results where the fire became oxygen regulated and
smothered before flame could spread to adjacent containers. Results of similar tests
were published in the report "fire investigation in containers" by Ould El Mochar et
al. [13], where series of 20-ft containers were tested to assess the fire mechanism of
unprotected steel construction and to provide data for the validation of numerical sim-
ulation. The test was carried out both for containers with intact flooring and with
openings on the flooring. The results showed that for intact container, fire became oxygen
controlled and extinguish before temperature at the unexposed sides became critically
high, but in tests with openings, the temperature at the unexposed side of the roof
panel became high enough indicating possible vertical fire spreading to adjacent container.
1.2
Project objectives and research question
This project is a part of the "CONTAIN" project which is a pilot project that was carried
out by DBI - The Danish Institute of Fire and Security Technology in cooperation with
the Danish Maritime Fund [15]. In this project, a fire spread between containers was
examined by studying the heat transfer mechanism from a burning container to adjacent
containers in a stack of containers. The container stack was assumed to be onboard a
container ship and located under the deck in the ship’s cargo compartment. All containers
involved were considered to be one TEU sea-going shipping container to simplify and
induce uniformity in the project. In general combustion process in a container depends
on various factors that can influence fire development, including factors such as different
types of materials transported in containers and ventilation and condition of the container.
The circumstances of fire ignition in goods transported in a container can vary depending

on the materials and material properties, as some materials can be more sensitive to
temperature changes. Containers can have different types of ventilation depending on

Earlier literature and experiments of container fires indicate that the combustion
process inside a container would most likely self-extinguish rather than develop in a way
that the adjacent containers would be assumed threatened by heat transfer. However, the
facts is that fires in containers on board container ships are on the rise, and therefore it
can be assumed that this sequence of events can occur in containers. Still, the goods in the
container likely have a significant influence on the development of the combustion process.
With regard to the projects objectives to examine heat transfer between containers, it
was assumed that a burning container could develop as a well ventilated fire with a
certain energy release. Therefor the project does not include any influence of different
materials of transported goods on the burning process. With the respect to burning
container in a stack of containers the projects research question is therefore stated as:
• Can maximum fuel-controlled fire inside a container with a specific heat release
rate spread to adjacent containers?
and as a consequence of that which adjacent container or containers in the stack are
most likely to be ignited first.
1.3
Methods
Container ships are generally large and the transport capacity is high, which leads to
a variety of fire scenarios. Therefor various experimental approaches and methods are
possible to answer the question of whether a fire in container can spread to adjacent
containers. Because fire experiments can be very expensive depending on the experimental
scale and resources, methods and experiments needs to be selected accordingly. A full
scale fire tests is most likely to give the best results. One approach could be to perform
a full scale fire test on container stack and measure relevant parameters on the adjacent
containers, or even go further and perform full scale test aboard container ship. Such
experiments will though require large costs due to resources and time in preparation and
implementation.
This project consists of a bottom up approach with a combination of experimental
and numerical simulations to determine the spread of fire between containers with the
assumption mentioned earlier. The objectives were to analyse behavior and collect data
for different parts of a container when the parts were exposed to heat. Knowledge and
data found in the experiments was used in numerical modeling, using the computer
program Fire Dynamic Simulator FDS. Due to large cots in fire experiments this approach
was found to be preferable and useful as precursor for further experiments.
Experiment was carried out using a cone calorimeter for the plywood flooring of the
container. Total 42 number of samples were collected from the container flooring and

CHAPTER 2
Theory
The combustion process inside a container depends greatly on the materials burning
inside the container and the amount of available oxygen for the fire to develop. Sea-going
container are water tight to protect the goods being transported, and therefore containers
are usually relatively airtight, except for ventilated areas. Therefor general fires inside
a container will likely follow the principles of underventilated fires often described as
ventilation controlled fires. To study the spread af fire between containers it was found
meaningful by assuming scenario with maximum fuel controlled fire at a steady heat
release rate such that conditions inside the container would not reach flashover. To
describe the burning process of a ventilated fire inside the container the principles of fire
dynamics in enclosure was followed.
The fundamental elements of combustion are well described in figure 2.1 of the fire
triangle. For most fires to ignite and maintain all of the three elements shown in the
figure are required.
Figure 2.1: The fire triangle, including the three fundamental elements for most combus-
tion.

Compartment fires are described as fires in enclosed spaces. Enclosed spaces is usually
used in the context of rooms in buildings, but may include other spaces such as in
transportation, ships air plains, trains and the like. Generally compartment fires go
through several stages and are therefor often discussed in the following stages: ignition,
growth, flashover, fully-developed and decay. Those stages can be seen graphically in
figure 2.2 forming the fire curve.

The growth stage following ignition is the stage when fire growths as function of the
fuel and continues growing with right mixture of fuel and oxygen. during the growth
stage influence from the compartments to the combustion is negligible.
Flashover is the transaction stage from growth stage to fully developed stage at this
stage all combustible item inside the compartment will be involved in the fire. At the
stage of flashover gas temperatures are usually around 500-600 [◦C].
During the stage of fully developed fire heat release rate is at its maximum and tempera-
tures reaches the maximum. During fully developed stage the fire is greatly influenced
by the compartments structural material, size of openings and distribution of fuel in the
compartment. Frequently during the fully developed stage more fuel is available than
oxygen inside the compartment and therefor hot smoke gashes traveling out can ignite
outside the compartment when the right mixture with oxygen is reached. After the fully
developed stage the fire reaches the post fleshover and the decay stage where all fuel
becomes consumed and the fire slowly decreases.[20]

CHAPTER 3
Experiments and simulations of
the container flooring
Shipping cargo containers are built and designed to meet the international standards
published by the International Convention for Safe Containers (CSC) to ensure uniformity
in quality and design. New containers are tested for water tightening and can have the
highest 5 years of CSC validity and containers in periodic examination schemes can
have up to 3 years of validity. For new containers, water tightness testing is performed
on the conveyor belt in the production factory by pumping water onto the container
and inspecting for interior leakages. containers in used condition are inspected by an
inspection body, the inspection body examines aspects of the container for seaworthiness
such as watertightness, condition of the structure and general condition of the container.
The main material used in containers is steel, structural steel that is used for primary
bearing parts and hot rolled plates and profiles that are used for sides, ceiling and
other parts. The container bottom flooring is made out of approximately 30 [mm] thick
plywood [14].Most standard containers are equipped with ventilation openings located at
the long sides, the openings consist of twelve drilled 8 [mm] holes in rectangular pattern
with the total area of 603 [mm2] as shown in figure 3.2(a). On the exterior side the
vent openings are concealed with plastic shield to hinder water and insects to enter the
container as can be seen in figure 3.2(b). The number of ventilation openings can vary
from 2 to 10 units located on the long sides about 15 [cm] below the ceiling. Figure 3.1
shows a exploded schematic sketch of a container.

Cone colorimeter experiment
The mass loss cone calorimeter is a bench scale fire behavior test device that is used to
measure material flammability based on heat release rate HRR. Cone calorimmeter is
one of the most significant methods used in fire engineering because it measures real fire
properties of the material being tested. The principle of the HRR measurement is based
on the measurements of decreasing oxygen consumed during the combustion to a chosen
radiant heat levels generally in the range of 10 to 100 [kW]. The cone calorimeter is a
subject of the international standards 2ISO 5660. In order to measure the develop of mass
loss during the experiment a sample with the area of 0.01 [m2] and certain thickness is
placed into a tray on a load cell directly under a conical radiant electrical heating device.
To emit a correct radiating heat flux on to the sample the conical hater is heated up to a
calibrated temperature. A constantly flashing sparking igniter is positioned above the
test sample to trigger the combustion, at which point the time to ignition is recorded.
During the experimental process gases pass through the heating cone into a exhaust
hood and through exhaust pipe that is added with centrifugal fan where measurements
for the oxygen concentration of time are taken and also including the measurements for
gas flow, CO, CO2 and smoke density.

In this section modeling and simulation of a full scale containers will be described. Cargo
compartments in a container ship are formed by subdivision of the hull by transverse bulk-
heads and watertight transverse bulkheads where one cargo hold consists of at least one
40 foot container length but mostly of two 40 foot lengths. Figure 4.1 shows a schematic
figure for part of a container compartment. Positioning of containers aboard container
ship are addressed by so called BAY-ROW-TYRE stowing plan often known as bay plan.
The plan helps with determining of the correct stowage positions of the containers for the
terminals loading and unloading plans. Bay plan plays also a vital part in the ship emer-
gency planing as for example the positioning of containers that contain cargo that falls
under the IMDG code1[5]. The numbering of the bay plan follows the ship coordination
where bays are the long ship positions and start counting in bay 01 from bow up to stern.
The rows are the container positions across the ships width with evenly numbering of
rows on the port side starting in row 0.2, and with odd numbering at the starboard side
starting in row 0.1, if container is located at the center line it will be in the row number 00.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
The thesis experiments and studies show that a fire spreading between containers for a
ventilated fire at a specific heat release rate can occur. Through the experiments, the
plywood has been shown to ignite at a similar temperature as the literature citations
indicate. The mobile furnace experiment shows that the plywood´s thermal resistance
leads to relatively slow heat conduction through the material and therefor slow tempera-
ture rise up to the boiling temperature of water. The experiment also shows that the
leakage in the joints of the container flooring can lead to convective heat transfer by hot
gashes into the container, which can occur before the plywood is burnt through. This
indicates that either or both can be the caution, the tightness of the flooring due to the
conditions of the container and/or the insulation rubber being burnt away. From the
simulation part its shown that the calculations of the heat transfer trough the plywood
replicates results from the mobile furnace test acceptably. Simulations show that the
heat transfer through the thin steel of the container sides gives a negligible temperature
difference between the exposed and unexposed sides.
According to above, in order to estimate which container in the stack will ignite first
depends strongly on the material being transposed and conditions of the container.
Assuming that the flooring in the container above the burning container has no leakages
and also that all three exposed containers were carrying the same kind of heat sensitive
goods it may be assumed that the interior of container in slot BRT121 would ignite first.
This leafs questions and gives rise for further studies.
