Abstract  
Background: With the increasing workload of the endoscopic units and the need for more skilled endoscopists, 
it has been acknowledged that training and evaluation of endoscopists must be based on robust and objective 
methods to ensure all endoscopists have reached a define level of competency before getting hands-on expe-
rience with patients. Learning endoscopic skill require extensive training and useful feedback, but no simula-
tion-based training assess or provide this regarding important parameters, such as how the endoscopist is 
standing (stance) or managing the endoscope (scope handling). A potential solution, presented in this thesis, 
is the motion analysis technologies: Microsoft Kinect, accelerometers and gyroscopes, which only few studies 
have used to distinguish between endoscopic skills before. Objective: The purpose of this thesis was to quan-
tify the variation between the body postures and scope handling of experienced endoscopists and novices, 
during a gastroscopy, both in the clinical- and training setting. Methods: The data acquisition was carried out 
in 1) a pilot study where 2 experienced and 8 novices were recorded during different scope handling maneu-
vers 2) a training setting where 15 experienced and 12 novices were recorded with a phantom model and 3) 
a clinical setting, where 11 experienced were recorded with patients. The data analysis consisted of Procrustes 
Analysis, Principle Components Analysis and Archetype Analysis. Additionally, an acceleration- and rotation 
score were defined and analyzed by two sample t-test, Levene’s test and Spearman Correlation. Results: Ex-
perienced had a significantly higher rotation score (mean=28.87, range(18.96-40.99)) compared to novices 
(mean=23.08, range(11.99-36.32)), and significantly lower variance in the acceleration score (p-value=0,01). 
The scope handling in the training setting was characterized by significantly more rotation (mean=28.87, 
range(18.9-40.9)) and acceleration (mean=0.03, range(0.02-0.04)) compared to the clinical setting 
(mean=19.43, range(13.43-28.55))(mean=0.02, range(0.01-0.03)). A clear distinction between the body pos-
tures was found, where experienced showed considerable larger variation of their body postures before pas-
sage of esophagus, stomach and duodenum compared to novices who kept similar pose during the procedure. 
Five archetypes were identified among the experienced endoscopists, indicating that more appropriate tech-
niques exists in performing a gastroscopy. Conclusion: Microsoft Kinect, accelerometers and gyroscopes can 
be used to quantify the variation between the body postures and scope handling of experienced endoscopists 
and novices during a gastroscopy. Experienced are using different and dynamic approaches characterized 
with more rotation, where novices are static and apply inconsistent scope handling. This thesis demonstrates 
that using motion analysis technologies as a part of the simulation-based training can be an important step 
closer towards efficient training and robust assessment of skills in endoscopy. This will ensure higher quality 
and safety for the increasing number of endoscopic procedures that are required in the coming years. Further 
studies are needed to quantify the dynamic techniques in detail, and assess which postures and scope han-
dling should be considered as most appropriate and ergonomically correct. Additionally, the postures from 
the clinical setting was not possible to include within this project, and should be studied in further investiga-
tions.

1. Introduction  
Endoscopy is the standard procedure for evaluation of many disorders, such as pulmonary-, colorectal- and 
gastric cancers. Like many other countries, such as the United Kingdom and the United States, the National 
Board of Health in Denmark has recently decided to introduce screening programs for colorectal cancer for 
citizens in the age group 50-74 (April 2014) (1, p. 57-58),(2, p. 3) and special treatment plans for esophagus- 
and stomach cancer (June 2012) (3, p. 20-21). Such initiatives will lead to an increased workload of the en-
doscopic units having to perform more procedures. At the same time are also required to instruct and super-
vise more future endoscopists to ensure that they approach endoscopic procedures in a safe and efficient 
way.  
Since more patients will be exposed to the complication risks associated with the procedures, concerns for 
patient safety has driven up standards for endoscopists and questioned whether the quality and amount of 
endoscopic training is adequate to ensure qualified endoscopists(4). Traditionally, like in many other surgical 
specialties, training in endoscopy has previously been based on supervised hands-on training, requiring nov-
ices to perform a number of procedures on patients under supervision of a highly experienced endoscopist. 
However, this method requires expensive resources and exposes patient safety. Furthermore, the method 
lacks standardization of training to repeat certain procedures, due to patient’s anatomic differences, and is 
prone to subjective bias. The development of more advanced Virtual Reality (VR) simulators and phantom 
models has therefore gradually been replacing this apprentice method and allowed for a safer and more 
appropriate way for novices to gain competency before they get hands-on experience with patients(5, p. 5-
6),(6, p. 663),(7-9)   
Several studies have demonstrated simulators as being useful in training novices (7, 8) and that it is a safe 
and efficient method for novices with little or no endoscopic experience to achieve competency(9). However, 
despite the advantages of simulation-based training, teaching and evaluation of endoscopic skills have shown 
lack of useful feedback and objective assessment of novices, influenced by measures with limited relation to 
how well the endoscopist has performed the procedure. Therefore, to guarantee high-quality and safety in 
the endoscopic health care system, unbiased and validated training and evaluation of future endoscopists is 
needed to ensure a defined level of competency before practicing on patients (4, p. 3),(10, p. 194),(11, p. 
xxi).  
1.1. Endoscopy  
The equipment used in an endoscopy procedure, called an endoscope, is approximately a one-meter long 
flexible tube, containing a micro camera and a light at the tip, which is inserted into natural openings of the 
body by an endoscopist. There exists several types of procedures and endoscopes dependent on which part 
of the body is examined(12). For instance, an upper gastrointestinal (GI) endoscopy/gastroscopy examines 
the upper GI tract (esophagus, stomach, and duodenum) by a gastroscope (Figure 1).

doscopy the endoscopist needs to learn and develop certain motor skills, such as manual dexterity, scope

1.2. Clinical education in endoscopy 
While in most other professions it is acceptable that a young professional does not master all aspects of 
his/her job, this is not the case for medical professionals. Even a minor mistake can have a significant impact 
on a patient. The problem is that novices need practical experience in endoscopy, but letting a student with 
improper knowledge and experience train on patients is not acceptable nor ethical. Therefore, training and 
certification in simulation-based training are becoming more popular as a way of providing novices with an 
opportunity to train basic skills in a risk-free environment, ensuring all trainees have reached a basic level of 
competency before practicing on patients(5, p. 5-6),(6, p.663). However, the challenge now is how to tell 
whether novices, after a specific among of training, are competent to practice on patients or not in a reliable 
way. Clear standards for this part of the education or specific requirements for the certification are not de-
scribed. There are no agreed standards on how to run these programs, which equipment to use, which length 
of training to apply, or whether or not there should be supervisors present during training (18). Moreover, 
the VR simulators and phantoms models that are available today (Figure 4) provide very limited feedback 
related to endoscopic skills, such as how the physician is standing (stance) or managing the instrument (scope 
handling). Instead, the current feedback, which is reported at the end of each procedure, focuses only on the 
endoscopic view and outcome goals, based on assessment metrics, such as time to complete the procedure. 
However, such metric can easily be tricked(19, p.1229) and do not provide constructive feedback in guiding 
the novices in what to do differently, nor provide useful information about the novices’ technical skill.

Certainly, it is likely that an experienced endoscopist will be faster than a novice in performing an endoscopy, 
but it is important to acknowledge that speed does not tell anything about how well the procedure actually 
has been performed. Information about inappropriate handling or the endoscope (scope handling) or ergo-
nomically awkward postures (stance) are neither revealed by an assessment metric based on time(22, p. 
150). Therefore, the metrics should not be considered as a valid or reliable way of training and evaluating the 
competency level of novices (23, p.6). These issues are critical since feedback is key in learning endoscopy 
and a practical skill is best acquired when the trainee receives feedback on the procedural part, not just on 
outcome goals, such as time taken (24, p. 512),(25, p. 534). One study has in fact demonstrated that trainees 
failed to improve their skills, when no feedback were giving on their performance during simulation-based 
training (19).  
To compensate for this, some educational programs uses experienced endoscopist as a supervisor to train, 
evaluate and give proper feedback on the performance of the novices. However, this method is not optimal 
either, as it contribute in some of the same problems found in the apprentice model; subjective bias and 
resource requirements(23). Even though there has been developed several guidelines and tools for assess-
ment of competency based on direct observation of an expert, such as Assessment of Competency in Endos-
copy (ACE) recommended by The American Society for Gastrointestinal Endoscopy (ASGE) (26) or Direct Ob-
servation of Procedural Skills (DOPS) (27, p. 595), these methods are not adequate for reliable and valid 
training and evaluation for endoscopic skills due to two main issues. Firstly, they are developed for the clinical 
setting, and should not be generalized to a simulation-based environment (23, p. 1-2). Secondly, even among 
experts there are significant variations in what is considered to be the “correct way” of performing endosco-
pies, such as manipulation of the control wheels, method of insertion and how the instrument should be 
managed (28, p. 572). This indicate that subjective assessment is not suitable to provide a consistent and 
reliable evaluation of novices, since very different opinions exist. Furthermore, the number of performed 
endoscopies has also been used as an indicating of competencies and whether the physician should be cer-
tified to perform procedures on patients alone, however, this metric is not reliable either. The specific num-
ber required for achieving competency has long been discussed, and vary significantly between countries and

organizations. Some studies advocate a minimum of 130 upper GI endoscopies, while others claim that it is 
necessary to perform over 500 procedures to achieve competency(29, p. 224). These differences emphasize 
that skills should not be based on the number of performed procedures, since it is very individual how fast 
skill is achieved (23, p.1),(28, p. 571). Also the number does not indicate how the procedures has been per-
formed in terms of accuracy, comfort and safety for the patient (5, p. 5),(15, p. 316),(26, p. 1).    
Correct scope handling and stance of the endoscopist does not only have an association with the level of 
technical skill, but also affects the health of endoscopists. Studies have shown that musculoskeletal pain and 
overuse injuries, such as tendonitis, are commonly reported among endoscopist, due to incorrect repetitive 
scope handling and awkward body postures. As no feedback or assessment are focusing on correct handling 
or posture during training, the physicians are not properly educated in the ergonomic principles of endoscopy 
and risks of having overuse injuries later, and the possibility of a shorten career in endoscopy, is likely (30, 
31). 
These issues emphasize a critical need to improve training and assessment of future endoscopists in a more 
validated, automatic and objective way. This thesis presents how motion analysis technologies, such as Mi-
crosoft Kinect (Kinect), accelerometers and gyroscopes have the potential to improve training and assess-
ment of endoscopic skills in an unbiased and reliable way. Motion analysis technologies have increasingly 
been applied in the field of medical research to quantify human motion patterns related to e.g. specific dis-
eases or clinical skills (32-34). However, only few studies have been conducted in establishing objective 
measures to discriminate between competency level in the endoscopic setting (10, 15, 16) and no simulation-
based training are currently using motion technology to provide feedback or assessment related to the en-
doscopist body posture or scope handling. Therefore, the purpose of this thesis is to quantify the variation 
between the body postures and scope handling of experienced endoscopists and novices to evaluate how 
these variation is characterized and which parameters can be used to discriminate between endoscopic com-
petency levels. It is especially in interest to quantify the variation shortly before and after passage of ana-
tomic landmarks, like entering esophagus, stomach and duodenum, to evaluate if specific parts of the pro-
cedure are associated with certain endoscopic techniques. The variation among the experienced endoscopist 
will also be evaluated to study if more than one good way of performing a gastroscopy exists. Furthermore, 
the postures and scope handling in the real clinical setting will be analyzed, which has not yet been studied 
before. The goal is that the results achieved in this project can be used in developing an interactive feedback 
system to train and evaluate the competency level of future endoscopist in terms of the body postures and 
scope handling depending on specific parts of the procedure.  
Two research questions will be the main focus through the thesis: 
1) Do there exists any significant variation in body postures and scope handling between experienced 
endoscopist and novices, depending on specific parts of the procedure? If so, how are the variation 
characterized and which parameters can be used for discrimination? 
2) Do there exists any significant variation in body postures and scope handling between the experi-
enced endoscopists? If so, how are the variation characterized and do they apply different tech-
niques?  
The questions will be answered by a data acquisition from three sources: 1) the Kinect tracking the endosco-
pists body movements during a gastroscopy, 2) the sensors (accelerometer, gyroscope), attached to the top

of the endoscope to describe scope handling and 3) the endoscopy video from the micro camera at the tip 
of the instrument, to determine the anatomic location of the endoscope tip during a procedure. The data 
acquisition consists of three settings 1) a pilot study, 2) test in a training setting and 3) test in a clinical 
setting. This means that physicians will be recorded when performing a gastroscopy on a phantom model, 
and during a gastroscopy on patients. The reason why a phantom model is used and not a VR simulator is 
because studies have demonstrated that a physical model provides a more realistic feel of the anatomy of 
the GI tract and endoscope behavior(23, p. 6),(35, p. 145). The purpose of the pilot study is to get an initial 
idea of how the motion patterns from the accelerometer and gyroscope, during a gastroscopy are carried

and scope handling of only experienced in a realistic setting where patient’s anatomic differences will be 
included. The data will be analyzed by different statistically analysis methods, such as Procrustes Analysis, 
Principle Components Analysis (PCA) and Archetype Analysis to identify the variation between the postures. 
An acceleration- and rotation score will also be defined, revealing the motion patterns of the scope handling 
during a full procedure. These scores will be analyzed by a two sample t-test and Levene’s test. Furthermore, 
it was in interest to study if participant’s anatomic differences have an association with scope handling, which 
was analyzed with Spearman Correlation.  
This thesis can be considered as the first part out of a much greater research project in improving the clinical 
education endoscopy. The research project can be divided into three main parts; first part (this thesis) will 
focus on the data acquisition. This will take place on the Department of Surgical Gastroenterology (Afdeling 
C) and Centre for Clinical Education (CEKU) (Rigshospitalet, Denmark). It is desired that the results achieved 
from this thesis can be used in the second part of the research project and be the motivation of the develop-
ment of an interactive feedback system, which may have the potential to train and evaluate endoscopists 
competencies in a more valid and reliable way. Ideally a statistical classification algorithm can be developed 
to identify when the physician provide inappropriate body postures or scope handling during training, with 
a corresponding graphical interface to intuitively guide and correct the novices into better postures or scope 
handling. Third and last part includes test of clinical effect from using the system.  
This thesis presents many new aspects and methods which have not been conducted or validated in the 
endoscopic setting before, such as tracking the movements of endoscopist in the clinical setting, which will 
include many challenges compare to the fixed conditions in the training setting. Therefore, an important 
point to take from this project will be whether it is possible to gather useful information in the clinical setting 
with the motion analysis technologies. Additionally, it should be emphasized that several new information 
will be gathered during the data acquisition, and it is not expected that all will be analyzed within this project. 
This thesis presents one way of how the variation between body postures and scope handling could be ana-
lyzed, well-knowing that other methods could be applied.

2.1.2. Scope handling  
Different techniques in scope handling exist but most skilled physicians favor the single-handed approach, 
where the endoscopist manages the controls wheels and valves with one hand and inserts the shaft with the 
other(Figure 6a)(11, p. 106). The tip of the thumb should rests on the up/down control wheel (large wheel). 
This grip allows the index finger to activate the air-water and suction valves, and the middle finger assists the 
thumb as a “helper” during major movements of the up/down angulation. The right hand is then free to push 
and pull the instrument, and to control service channels. Some endoscopists are able to manage the left/right 
controls (small wheel) with the left thumb (Figure 6b). This maneuver is challenging because the grip of a 
standard endoscope is developed for large hands, and many endoscopists are therefore assisting with their 
right hand to steer the left/right control wheel, called the two-handed technique. Use of two-handed steering 
is completely appropriate, but if the right hand is used too often for left/right control, and the right hand is 
away from the shaft for too long, shaft control and efficient steering will be lost. (11, p. 34, 106-107). An 
alternative in using the left/right control wheel, is rotating the control head with the left hand or twisting the 
shaft with the right hand (torque) (Figure 6c)(36, p. 18). This will provide great movements of the endoscope, 
however, the torque and rotation technique will only works when the instrument is straight, otherwise the 
forces applied will be lost or not appear as accurate at the tip as intended(11, p. 109).

wheel, the index finger controls the air-water and suction valves, middle finger assists the thumb of the angulation controls and the

the thumb.  c) Torque steering applied by the right hand to the shaft. Twisting the shaft when a slight angulation in the bending 
section is giving, results in a great movement to the tip of the endoscope, without adding unnecessary forces or pain to the pa-

2.1.3. Maneuvering the endoscope  
The endoscopy technique is complex, and maneuvering cannot be learned sufficiently from theoretical 
knowledge, because it is important to know the behavior of the endoscope in practice. For instance, push 
and pull maneuvers are more complicated than just advance and withdrawal. When the shaft is relatively 
straight with no loop formation, pushing the instrument will result in advance of the tip (Figure 7a), however, 
when the endoscope is formed into a large loop, which usually occurs when the tip of the endoscope is just 
before entering duodenum and the shaft is in the stomach, pulling back the instrument will not result in 
withdrawal, but instead straighten the shaft (loop), which initially causes the endoscope tip to advance by 
several centimeters (Figure 7b). When the loop is straightened, further pulling will then result in withdrawal

of the endoscope tip(Figure 7c)(37, p. 17-18). This means that it is always necessary for the physician to be 
aware of how the shaft is looped in order to perform the correct maneuvers.

Another important aspect for novices to master includes the ability to perform the retroflexion maneuver 
when examining the fundus and cardia of the stomach (Figure 8). These regions are often best seen from 
upside down, meaning that the endoscope tip should be angled up 180°. A combination of angulation, slowly 
withdrawal and rotating of the control head will result in a good view of the cardia and fundus from different 
perspective. During this maneuver, it is also important to keep the shaft straight, since it results in more 
precise movements of the tip of the endoscope (11, p. 42).

2.1.4. Stance  
In literature, only few details are described about the correct stance of the physician during an endoscopy. 
Cotton and Williams describe the optimal stance as “the endoscopist should stand comfortably facing the

patient, with a confident and balanced stance with a straight instrument, gently handled” (11, p. 34)(Figure 
2). In order to keep the instrument straight it depends on the endoscopist stance and how far his/her hands 
are apart. Sedlack emphasizes the importance of having an appropriate posture, body positioning, and 
hand/limb mechanics to minimize the risk for long-term overuse injuries (38, p. 383-384). As mentioned ear-
lier, novices often stand in a cramped position, creating strain in the endoscope. An inappropriate stance will 
not only result in a less efficient and fluent procedure, but also expose physicians for overuse injuries. Upper 
GI endoscopy involves many risk factors that are associated with overuse injuries, such as repeated pinch-
ing/gripping of the endoscope, pushing, pulling and torqueing of the shaft. Basic ergonomic principles should 
therefore be incorporated in training novices to minimize the risk for endoscopy-related injuries. Incorporat-
ing ergonomic principles in training will not only have the potential to reduce the risk of work related injuries, 
but also result in better and more comfortable procedure for the patient (30, p. 148). It is therefore in interest 
to study the different techniques applied by experienced endoscopists which in further studies can be eval-
uated as the most appropriate.  
2.2. Microsoft Kinect 
Microsoft Kinect is an inexpensive and non-invasive motion sensor (Figure 9), originally developed for the 
Xbox 360 console, but has opened up for several new opportunities in medicine and health care as well. The 
Kinect consists of a RGB camera, infrared (IR) emitter, IR depth sensor and a multi-array microphone, ena-
bling to detect and track the position and movements of the human body. The IR emitter projects a speckle 
pattern of infrared light, which allows Kinect to create a depth map of the light reflected back from the ob-
jects in the camera’s field of view by measuring the distance between the objects and the motion sensor. 
Using the depth map and a highly trained and advance randomized decision forest classifier (39), Kinect is 
capable of specifying the position of 20 body joints providing skeleton tracking of the person in front of the 
Kinect (40). The accuracy of the Kinect is estimated to be 1-4 cm at a range of 1-4 m, which is considered as 
sufficiently accurate and reliable in clinical use (41, p. 351). Details about the software to record and extract 
skeleton tracking will be described later in section 3.3. Kinect for Windows Developer Toolkit v1.8.  
 
Figure 9. Microsoft Kinect motion sensor(42) 
Increased attention in using Kinect in health care and medical research has been drawn in the recent years. 
Especially applications in rehabilitation has been developed, such as SeeMe(43) or VirtualRehab (44) with the 
purpose of treating patients with e.g. motion disorders or balance problems. Kinect allows an easy and quick 
way of tracking the movements of a patient while an interactive feedback system is guiding the patient in 
performing correct movements, during specific physical exercises (41, p. 344). Kinect has also shown to be 
beneficial in tracking and analyzing different motions patterns, such as detecting possible motor disabilities 
in infants(45) or a home-based assessment tool of movements in Parkinson’s patients(46).

Kinect has also shown great potential as being an objective and automatic assessment tool for endoscopy

during the procedures, which provide great motivation for this thesis since it is clear that focus on the stance 
of the endoscopist during training of novices is needed in endoscopy.   
2.3. Accelerometer and gyroscope  
The Kinect sensor’s ability to track smaller movements, such as motion of the hands is not as accurate as of 
the bigger skeleton joints (46, p. 1067). As scope handling is considered as an important part of technical 
skill, this project will use a combined accelerometer and gyroscope (PhidgetSpatial Precision 3/3/3 High Res-
olution(48)) to quantify the motion and rotation applied to the endoscope control head by the endoscopists’ 
left hand. The gyroscope will detect when the endoscopist is using the rotation technique by measuring the 
angular velocity (49) whereas the accelerometer detects the motion of the control head by measuring the 
proper acceleration (50). Technical details about the sensors will be described later in section 3.4. Accelerom-
eter and Gyroscope.    
Both sensors have been applied in the field of medical research before, however no investigations in the 
endoscopic setting has been conducted. Gyroscopes have shown to provide highly relevant information for 
e.g. balance motions patterns, where one study demonstrated that is was possible to discriminate between 
different balance disorders (33). Accelerometers have been used to track and record Parkinson’s patients 
hand tremors, which have shown considerable degree of accuracy and reliability (32, p. 66). A recent study 
has also used an accelerometer as an assessments tool for surgical skill in laparoscopy. The study developed 
an accelerometer-based motion tool, which demonstrated the validation of accelerometers ability to distin-
guish between novices and experts in laparoscopic skill. The study showed that experts had a more steady 
and controlled approach compared to novices, who frequently had more sudden movements (51). These 
results demonstrate the possibility of adapting accelerometers and gyroscopes to the endoscopic setting as 
a valid and objective measure to discriminate between the scope handling of experienced endoscopist and 
novices.

2.4. Motion analysis in medicine 
Studying the shapes and motion of the human body has been in great interest for researches for a long time. 
With the development of new and advanced technologies, such as Kinect, and motion sensors like accel-
erometers and gyroscopes, it has opened up for new opportunities in motion analysis of human shapes and 
movements (52, 53). Studies involving accelerometers and gyroscopes have been characterized by finding 
the magnitude of the motion, and comparison of the mean and variation to identify any significant difference 
between groups (novices vs. experts, patients vs. healthy subjects) (52). This project will therefore define an 
acceleration- and rotation score based on the magnitude of the motion and rotation applied to the endo-
scope head, and compare the scores between the two groups by two-sample t-test, Levene’s test and Spear-
man Correlation. The body postures from Kinect will be analyzed by the shape analysis Procrustes Analysis 
to align and compare the postures of the physicians. Procrustes Analysis is a widely used method for shape 
analysis and has previously been applied in relation to Kinect data, such as aligning and discriminating be-
tween facial expressions (54). Additionally, PCA will be used in this project as identifying the variation of the 
postures applied by experienced endoscopist and novices, which has also been used with Kinect skeleton 
data before (55). Lastly, Archetype Analysis will be conducted on the data from experienced endoscopist to 
evaluate if any variation exists in the techniques performed by highly trained endoscopist and the possibility 
of more than one good way of performing a gastroscopy exists. Archetype Analysis has been used to study 
archetypes of human shapes(56) but no investigations has been found in studying human poses with Arche-
type Analysis. Details about the data analysis will be described later in section 3.7. Data Analysis.

3.4. Accelerometer and Gyroscope  
A combined accelerometer and gyroscope (1044_0 - PhidgetSpatial Precision 3/3/3 High Resolution(48)) was 
attached to the endoscope control head to quantify the endoscopists scope handling and evaluate the sen-
sors discriminative ability in endoscopic competency.  
Accelerometers measure the proper acceleration, and not the acceleration due to changes in velocity. This 
mean that if the accelerometer is placed on a surface, not moving/accelerating, the accelerometer will still 
experience 1g of acceleration upwards due to the earth’s gravity force(1g=9, 81 m/s2), even though the object 
is not moving. In other words, accelerometers measure the proper acceleration in a given direction through 
the force that is applied to it, where these forces can either be static, like the constant force of gravity of the 
earth, or dynamic forces, when the object is moving. The accelerometer used in this project measures the 
acceleration in a three-dimensional axes, labeled x, y and z, reported in terms of g-force, which corresponds 
to the direction in which the acceleration occurs (Figure 12) (50).  
 
Figure 12. Illustrates the directions (x, y, z) in which the prober acceleration is measured by the accelerometer 
As described in section 2.1. The endoscopic equipment and techniques the endoscopist is maneuvering the 
instrument by using the control wheels or rotating the scope head. Therefore, the goal of attaching an accel-
erometer on the control head is to define an acceleration score, which corresponds to how intense or steady 
the endoscopist have moved the control head through the full procedure.  
Gyroscopes have the ability to distinguish between the orientations of the three axes to detect when the 
endoscopists are rotating the control head. A gyroscope determines the orientation by measuring the angular 
velocity (angular change that a rotating object undergoes per time unit). The gyroscope in this project 
measures the orientation in three-dimensional axes, labeled x, y and z, reported in terms of degrees per 
second (°/s), which corresponds to the angular velocity that the control head undergoes in one of the given 
directions, see Figure 13.

3.7.2. Procrustes Analysis  
In order to compare the physician’s postures the annotated skeleton data needs to be comparable by mini-
mizing the variability between them. This is done by Procrustes Analysis, which is a statistically shape analysis 
used to align the shapes of objects. The alignment, called Procrustes superimposition (PS), includes translat-
ing, rotation and scaling the shapes. However, scaling was not needed in this project. The objects are trans-
lated so the mean of all its coordinates (centroid) lies at the origin and rotated until the sum of squared 
distances between the corresponding coordinates are minimized. Performing PS will ensure that the differ-
ences between the postures are minimized, so they are comparable and PCA can be applied on the data. 
Procrustes Analysis can either be classified as Ordinary Procrustes Analysis (OPA) or Generalized Procrustes 
Analysis (GPA). When aligning more than two objects and it is desired to compare the shapes to a determined 
“mean shape” rather than a randomly selected shape, the GPA is utilized. Therefore GPA is used in the data 
analysis. The GPA is an iterative process to find the true mean shape for the objects. The evaluation of the 
differences between the shapes are determined by the measure Procrustes distance, which is the sum of 
squared distances between corresponding points. The basic steps in GPA is described below(69, p. 3-4, 70): 
1. Select one reference shape to be the mean shape (the first shape in the set)  
2. Superimpose (PS)  all the shapes to the current reference mean shape  
a. Calculate the centroid of each shape and translate their centroid to the origin  
b. Rotate each shapes to align with the current reference mean shape  
3. Calculate a new mean shape from the current set of superimposed shapes  
==PAGE:29==
Mathilde Dyrholm Jensen 
Master’s thesis in Health Informatics  
 
 
3D measurement of endoscopic procedures for training and evaluation  
 
28 
 
 
4. If the Procrustes distance between the new – and reference mean shape is above a certain threshold 
(too different), return to step 2 and repeat the protocol until the Procrustes distance reaches the 
desired level, and the true mean shape of the set is found.   
It is expected that the variation between the postures of experienced endoscopists will be lower compared 
to the postures of novices as their motor skills are not fully developed and therefore not making use of a 
consistent stance. Experienced endoscopists possess more extensive practice and familiarization with the 
instrument and techniques, therefore lower variation is expected(22),(71, p. 397-398).  
3.7.3. Principal Components Analysis (PCA) 
PCA is a well-known technique used to identify patterns in data to highlight the similarities and explain the 
variation with only few components. The technique can reveal relatively simple patterns within a complex 
set of variables to discover if the observed variables can be explained largely or entirely in terms of a much 
smaller number of variables, called principal components. The basic idea of PCA is to minimize the dimensions 
of the data, while retaining as much information as possible. It uses an orthogonal transformation to convert 
a set of observations of possibly correlated variables into a set of values of linearly uncorrelated variables 
(principal components). The orthogonal transformation is defined as the first principle component (eigen-
vector) that has the largest possible variance (eigenvalue) and thus accounts for as much of the variability in 
the data as possible. Each of the following components will account for as much of the remaining variation 
as possible under the constraint that it will be orthogonal (uncorrelated) with the former component(72, 
p.12-20).  
The reason for using PCA on the skeleton data is because the method provides a relatively easy way of quan-
tifying the variation between the postures. However, PCA will only be meaningful after performing Procrustes 
Analysis, otherwise the data will not be comparable and the first principal component (largest variation in 
poses) would likely be due to anatomic differences and not necessarily an important variation between the 
postures.  
3.7.4. Archetype Analysis 
The variation between the poses of experienced endoscopists will be analyzed by Archetype Analysis to eval-
uate if different approaches exists among them and how these are characterized. Normally, the outliers in a 
classification analysis are seen as special or random cases, which do not fit in the average – but in this relation 
the extremities might indicate that there exists more than one good way to perform an optimal gastroscopy. 
It is likely that different approaches will be identified, since up until now, literature has not been able to 
define exactly how the best stance should be and has been highly debated among experts (28, p. 572). 
Many classification analysis describe patterns in the data using the average, whereas Archetype Analysis uses 
the extreme data points to describe the patterns of the data. An average good posture could be identified 
among the experienced, but it is important to acknowledge that it is only one way of understanding how 
good poses should be. With Archetypal Analysis several “pure” postures might be identified where the aver-
age pose might be considered as a mixtures of these pure types (56).  
The statistical method was first introduced by Cutler and Breiman in 1994(73) and has been used in different 
areas from marketing(74) to studying human shapes(56). The basic idea of the method is to find a few points 
(archetypes) in the data set so all the data can be well represented as convex combinations of the archetypes.

The two scores express the average amount of acceleration and rotation applied through the entire proce-

any differences between the scores in the two groups, a two sample t-test will be used. Two sample t-test is 
used to compare the mean of the two scores between the two groups. The classic two sample t-test is only 
valid under the assumption that the data is normally distributed or have a sample size over 15, and the vari-
ance in the compared groups are equal. If this is not the case, a Welch t-test should be used instead. Data 
will therefore initially be tested for variance homogeneity with Levene’s test. Levene’s test is used since it 
provides more accurate results when it is suspected that the samples come from non-normal distributions 
compared to methods like Barletts test(75),(76, p. 66-67). Levene’s test is also used to reveal if the two groups

The Coefficient of Variation (CV) for the magnitude of acceleration and rotation will be calculated as well. 
CV is the ratio of the standard deviation to the mean, which is useful to determine the size of standard devi-
ation in relation to the size of mean. The CV will be used to determine how each participants vary according 
to their own mean/overall approach (1.1-1.2), which will reveal how consistently the physicians’ acceleration 
and rotation patterns are through the full procedure. For instance, if a physician has used a similar approach 
through the full procedure characterized as e.g. using the rotation technique, the CV will be low (under 1), 
since the magnitudes are closely spread around the mean. Conversely, if a physician is not making use of a 
consistent approach with e.g. sudden movements, the CV will be high (over 1) since the magnitudes are 
spread far away from the mean (76, p. 37).  
Based on literature it is expected that experienced endoscopists will have a lower variation in the acceleration 
patterns compared to novices, since they are more familiarized with the instrument and the techniques, and 
are not assumed to make sudden or uncontrolled movements (10, 11, 16, 77). This assumption also comply 
with the well-known theory by Fitts and Posner of learning surgical motor skills. According to Fitts and Posner, 
acquisition of technical skills occurs in three stages; the first stage involves the cognitive aspect, where the 
learner tries to understand the procedure and the mechanics of the technical skill and the different steps 
involved. In the second stage, integrative, the learner translates the knowledge of the steps into action where 
the movements are characterized as clumsy and inconsistent. In the third stage, autonomous, the learner 
through practice and appropriate feedback is able to perform specific tasks more fluidly with fewer interrup-
tions. Theoretically, the second stage could correspond to the competency level of the novices recruited in 
this project, whereas it is most likely that the third stage relates to the motor skills of the experienced en-
doscopist, which will be more accurate and automatic in their approach(22),(25, p. 532-534),(71, p. 397-398). 
Therefore, it is assumed that the variation in the motion (acceleration) will appear higher for novices com-
pared to the variation of the experienced endoscopist. For the rotation score it is assumed that the score will 
appear higher for experienced compared to novices, since they have more knowledge in how rotation tech-
nique benefits in their scope handling compared to novices who might not master this dynamic technique 
yet(11).

3.7.5.1. Discriminate between scope handling maneuvers 
Studies have previously been conducted using Kinect in the endoscopic setting (10, 16). However, an accel-
erometer and gyroscope have not yet been used in the endoscopic setting before. It was therefore decided 
to do a pilot study before the actual data collection, only focusing on the data from the sensors alone. The 
purpose was to study whether it was possible to distinguish when the endoscopist is using the control wheel 
(finger activity) or rotating the control head (rotation). This is an important distinction since it revels great 
knowledge about the scope handling applied by the endoscopist. To evaluate how the different scope handle 
maneuvers (finger activity and rotation) are characterized the magnitude of acceleration (1.1) and rotation 
(1.2) will be calculated and visualized for the different scope handle maneuvers to see if any characteristics 
apply. If a specific pattern exists a threshold can be defined, which can give an idea of how many times the 
physician rotates his/her hand through an entire procedure.  
3.7.5.2. Impact of anatomic differences  
It is expected that the experience level accounts for most of the variation in scope handling. However, since 
the thumb constitute in a large part in scope handling, it was also in interest to evaluate if anatomic differ-
ences, such as length of thumb has an association with skills in scope handling. This will be evaluated by 
Spearman Correlation. Spearman Correlation is used to assess how well the relationship between two vari-
ables are, which in this case will be the association between the length of thumb and scope handling (accel-
eration- and rotation score). The analysis provides a correlation coefficient with a number between -1 and 
+1, which tells how strong the two variables are correlated, where 0 indicates no correlation and -1 and +1 
indicate strong correlation. The reason for using Spearman Correlation instead of e.g. Pearson correlation is 
because the method is suitable for non-normal distributed samples and is not under the assumption that the 
association should be linear(76, p. 94, 349). It is assumed that the scores might have a negatively association 
between length of thumb, since participants with longer thumb might have easier in performing the single-
handed technique in reaching and managing the control wheel.

the endoscopist was influenced. The pictures (Figure 18) show situations during the procedure where good

very inconvenient for the workflow of the staff if placed inappropriately. Placing the Kinect in front of the 
endoscopist would have been the optimal choice. However, since the nurse usually stands on the other side 
opposite of the endoscopist to take care of the patient, it was not appropriate to place the Kinect there, as it 
would interfere with her work. Her job is to comfort the patient, but also assists the endoscopist on the other 
side, meaning that she will need to walk between the patient and endoscopist from time to time, and if the

5. Discussion   
It has been acknowledged that training and evaluation of endoscopic skills needs to be based on objective, 
automatic and validated metrics to ensure that all future endoscopists have reached a define level of com-
petency before practicing on patients. Remarkably, no simulation-based training has yet been focusing on 
how the physician is standing or managing the endoscope. They only provide assessment metrics based on 
the endoscopic view, which are easily tricked (19, p.1229) and do not provide informative feedback in teach-
ing novices what to do differently to improve their skills. A potential and promising solution, presented in 
this thesis, is motion analysis technology. Direct measurement of the postures and scope handling of the 
endoscopist cannot easily be tricked or manipulated, but tells directly how the physician is managing the 
endoscope. Using motion technology as a part of the simulation-based training could be an important step 
closer towards a less biased training and assessment of skills in endoscopy. Only few studies have investi-
gated in Kinect’s ability to distinguish between novices and experienced endoscopist, (10, 16, 47), and no 
studies have used an accelerometer or gyroscope to quantify scope handling. The purpose of this thesis was 
to quantify the variation between experienced endoscopist and novices during an upper GI endoscopy 
through these motion analysis technologies. The goal was to evaluate how the variation could be character-
ized and which parameters could be used to discriminate between competencies, depending on different 
==PAGE:58==
Mathilde Dyrholm Jensen 
Master’s thesis in Health Informatics  
 
 
3D measurement of endoscopic procedures for training and evaluation  
 
57 
 
 
parts of the procedure. Additionally, one of the main challenges in the current assessment method in endos-
copy is that it constitute in subjective bias, since there exists several opinions about how the “correct way” 
of endoscopy should be performed. Therefore, another goal of the thesis was to evaluate the variation of the 
experienced endoscopists to quantify if they are using different techniques and how these are characterized. 
No previous motion tracking has either been conducted in the clinical setting, which this project has included, 
to account for patients anatomic differences and the real clinical environment.   
5.1. Scope handling  
It was demonstrated that experienced had a significant higher mean of the rotation score compared to nov-
ices. This indicates that the scope handling of experienced are especially characterized by using the rotation 
technique as a large part of their endoscopic approach, compared to novices, where rotation do not consti-
tute as such a large part of the procedure. This was to be expected since experienced have more practice in 
how this technique benefits in their scope handling compared to novices, who do not master this dynamic 
technique yet(11). However, this distinction has not been quantified before. The results from the pilot study 
confirm this assumption that more rotation has been applied through the procedure by experienced endos-
copist compared to novices. The goal of the pilot study was to distinguish between scope handle maneuvers 
(finger activity and rotation), which was demonstrated in this project. A clear difference in the gyroscope 
during a finger activity and rotation tasks was identified and used to define a threshold to detect when en-
doscopists are rotating the scope head. The two sample t-test showed significance in how much rotation 
constituted for the full procedure between the two groups, which support the assumption that novices do 
not make use of the rotation technique, as a part of their general approach, but probably using the controls 
wheels to navigate instead. This is also confirmed in the CV results for the two groups. The mean of CV of 
rotation was significant lower for the experienced endoscopists compared to the novices, suggesting that the 
scope handling for each experienced endoscopists are using a consistent approach, characterized by rotation 
through the full procedure.  
It was found that experienced had significantly lower variance in the acceleration score and CV of accelera-
tion compared to novices. Since the acceleration score express the motion of the endoscope trough the full 
procedure and CV of acceleration express if the physician has a consistent approach, this corresponds well 
to Fitts and Posners theory of motor skill acquisition. The low variance for experienced endoscopists indicate 
that they have a more accurate and automatic approach, where the scope handling of novices is more incon-
sistent, perhaps with uncontrolled and sudden movements(22),(71, p. 397-398). It is not unlikely that nov-
ices, who have very limited experience, do not possess a accurate approach, since they are still in the second 
stage of motor skill learning, where they are trying to figure out how to translate theoretical knowledge into 
actions. They have not reached the third stage where the motor skills become more automatic and accurate. 
No significant difference in the mean of the acceleration score was identified, suggesting that the average 
motion applied does not differ between the two groups. This might be explained by endoscopists in general 
are not moving the scope head that much (up/down, left/right, forward/backward), since it does not give 
any significant effect of maneuvering the scope compared to rotating it, which was clearly demonstrated 
with the gyroscope. Perhaps, a difference in the acceleration score would have been identified depending on 
the specific part of the procedure or if the directions were studied separately. 
No association between participant’s length of thumb and the scope handling was revealed. When testing 
correlation between two variables it is important to remember that the results cannot be used to tell about 
==PAGE:59==
Mathilde Dyrholm Jensen 
Master’s thesis in Health Informatics  
 
 
3D measurement of endoscopic procedures for training and evaluation  
 
58 
 
 
cause and effect, only if the variables are related. Therefore, the main point to take from these results is that 
physicians anatomic differences, such as length of thumb, are not necessarily a constraint in novices’ poten-
tial to achieve good technical skills in scope handling, but more likely depends on extensive training and 
practical skill, like it would be expected. Further studies are needed to include how other anatomic differ-
ences might be related to the individual styles of scope handling, such as left- or right handed physicians.   
When comparing scope handling between the clinical- and training setting, thereby including real clinical 
environment, a strong distinction was demonstrated. The endoscopists are rotating and moving the endo-
scope more in the training setting compared to the clinical setting. A higher CV of rotation is seen for the 
clinical setting, meaning that the endoscopists are not using the rotation technique through the full proce-
dure. This could be explained by endoscopists are using very different movements in the clinical setting as 
well, caused by examination, biopsies or resistance from the patient, compared to the fixed phantom model, 
which contain little realism or challenges. However, a lower CV of acceleration is seen for the clinical setting, 
meaning that the endoscopists overall motion applied trough the full procedure is consistent with less sud-
den movements. This might be explained by the fact that the procedure is performed on an awake patient, 
who feels discomfort when sudden or more severe movements are applied, and therefore more caution is 
needed (less acceleration- and rotation score, and less CV of acceleration) compared to a phantom model, 
where a less gentle approach might be performed. This highlights that the scope handling patterns identified 
as appropriate in the training setting might not be fully corresponding to the scope handling required in the 
clinical setting, since several other factors are influencing the movements. It might also indicate that simula-
tions-based training only have the ability to provide the novices in achieving certain amount of skills, and 
highly expertise can first be achieved in the real clinical setting. There might be a need for a more gradually 
transition between the training- and clinical setting to make sure their competencies achieved in the training 
setting is corresponding to the clinical setting. However, the purpose of simulation-based training is to ac-
quire core endoscopic skill and familiarization with the endoscope, to learn how a fluent and safe procedure 
should be carried out, and to be prepared for the real and more complicated environment in the clinical 
setting. Therefore, the identified pattern for scope handling in the training setting might be more relevant to 
interpret and use as a guideline for novices to work towards during simulation-based training, meanwhile 
keeping in mind that scope handling in the clinical setting might be different with less motion and rotation. 
Though, to get an idea of how patient’s anatomic differences and specific procedures exactly have an impact 
on the scope handling, it would involve several challenges. Firstly, it will require a much larger sample size to 
be able to divide the patients into categorizes in how severe their abnormalities are and what examination 
and samples are performed. Secondly, it is important to remember that it is not a fixed phantom model, 
which do not make any resistance or sudden movements, it is a living human being, who inevitably will lead 
to unpredicted movements, making it even more challenging to specifically quantify one way of appropriate 
scope handling in the clinical setting.  
One main goal of this thesis was to evaluate whether an accelerometer and gyroscope could be used as a 
valid tool for assessment of endoscopic skills. The results indicate that the sensors can be used to discriminate 
between competency levels in relation to scope handling and therefore have the potential to be used in 
training and assessment in endoscopy. One key advantage of these sensors is that they are directly measuring 
the motion of the scope head and therefore give a true indication of how the motion of the instrument is 
applied by the endoscopist. The gyroscope has shown considerable degree of discriminating ability in terms 
of how frequently and consistently the endoscopist are rotating the scope head, suggesting that rotation 
==PAGE:60==
Mathilde Dyrholm Jensen 
Master’s thesis in Health Informatics  
 
 
3D measurement of endoscopic procedures for training and evaluation  
 
59 
 
 
through the full procedure is associated with good technical skills. This corresponds well to the recommen-
dations described in literature about good scope handling(11, p. 109), but has not been quantified before. 
The accelerometer has shown to be able to distinct between competencies depending on the variation of 
motion applied through a full procedure, meaning that consistent motion of the scope head is related to 
technical skills, which also corresponds to the learning theory of Fitts and Posner. Further studies and testing 
are however needed, such as defining a more robust threshold for detecting rotations. Even though a thresh-
old set to 95 (°/s) showed discriminatory ability between experienced and novices, it was very individual of 
how the rotation peak appeared in the full procedure; some had a peak around 300 (°/s) where others had 
around 90-100 (°/s). The challenge is to find the balance between a high enough threshold so finger activity 
is not included, meanwhile accounting for those physicians who only have a rotation peak around 90 (°/s). 
The Kinect video showed clearly that those physicians who only had a rotation peak around 90 (°/s) were 
rotating the scope. This means that to define a robust threshold to detect when the operator truly is rotating 
the scope, a calibration is needed to account for the endoscopist individual style of rotation and finger activ-
ity. It also raises the question if rotation and finger activity are applied in the same time and whether this 
would be possible to identify. Additionally, as literature states, the rotation technique only works when the 
shaft is straight, otherwise, will the forces applied be lost or not appear as accurate at the tip as intended(11, 
p. 109). Therefore, it is important that the rotation score will be combined with information about the exten-
sion of the instrument, else, should the amount of rotation not be associated directly with good technical 
skills.  
5.2. Body postures  
When aligning the poses of all the participants with Procrustes Analysis, the largest variation between the 
participants are seen in the hands and elbows, where the joints are consistently more spread out, compared 
to the other skeleton joints. This indicates that most of the endoscopic techniques and maneuvering are 
carried out by the hands, which corresponds well to how endoscopy is described in literature(11, p. 34-43) 
(see section 2.1. The endoscopic equipment and techniques).   
Like it was demonstrated in previous studies (10, 16) the distance between hands for the experienced en-
doscopist appeared to be wider apart and scope hand held higher compared to novices. These differences 
were identified in the data analysis as well, when performing Procrustes Analysis and PCA on the skeleton 
data. The results showed that experienced have their hands wider apart and scope hand held higher, when 
entering the esophagus and stomach, where the distance between hands eventually comes closer when en-
tering duodenum. However, experienced endoscopist still seems to keep their scope hand higher compared 
to novices during this sequence. This common stance is an important indication of endoscopic skills, since 
keeping the hands wider apart and scope hand high, results in a straighter shaft and thereby more precise 
and fluent maneuvering. Even though no association between  length of thumb and scope handling was iden-
tified, further studies about the impact of distance between hands on the body postures is needed, because 
keeping the hands wide apart will most likely not be the same for a short and a tall physician. 
The Archetype Analysis showed that five different techniques were performed by the experienced endosco-
pists, when entering esophagus and duodenum. They were characterized as having their left elbow flexed 
upright or stretched to the side (Figure 25b, 25c, 25d and Figure 26b, 26c). These archetypes are likely an 
expression of different ways of providing a straight shaft. However, ergonomically it would be interesting to 
study which of these approaches would be best for the endoscopist and whether anatomic differences might

affect this. Another identified archetype was having the right hand lifted in the esophagus and leaning his/her 
upper body to the side (Figure 25e). Having the right hand lifted could be an indication of the two-handed 
technique or the possibility that noise in the skeleton tracking has occurred. Few times during the tests, the 
skeleton tracking was interfered with the endoscopic equipment, where Kinect identified the endoscope as 
an arm. However, using the two-handed technique during insertion in esophagus is not unlikely and the pose 
could be an illustration of that. The pose is leaning to the side, which is not uncommon either when entering 
esophagus. Most endoscopist are looking directly into the patient’s mouth during insertion of the endoscope, 
and therefore are bending their upper body down. When the instrument is inserted, the endoscopist is slowly 
rising his/her body to look at the video monitor. The archetype could be an indication of that and the endos-
copists has not raised his/her upper body fully up yet. Another option is that the endoscopist is leaning to-
wards the video monitor to get a better view, which would be interestingly to evaluate whether this is ergo-
nomic appropriate. Identification of how endoscopists insert the endoscope could provide useful feedback 
to novices in learning the insertion technique, as it is considered as a more difficult part of the procedure(11, 
p. 34-36).  
The archetype which had the hands close together, forming a curved shape when entering duodenum (Figure 
26e) is most likely an indication of the endoscopist is rotating the control heed, causing the left hand to come 
closer to the right hand. The rotating technique is commonly used for entering duodenum and this stance 
supports this. This is also confirmed when evaluating the archetypes after entering duodenum, where the 
stance is not seen anymore. This means that the rotation technique is used to enter duodenum, where the 
physician is then putting the left hand back in “normal” position after entering duodenum. The archetype 
which had the hands stretched down, when entering duodenum (Figure 26d), could be an indication that the 
endoscopist is using the withdrawal technique, which causes the shaft to advance several centimeters into 
the duodenum, due to loop formation in the stomach. The archetype identified in both sequences where the 
hands unrealistically are crossing each other and the shoulders are not in place (Figure 25a and Figure 26a), 
is most likely caused by noise in the Kinect skeleton tracking and should not be considered as a representation 
of experienced approaches. Further investigation to exclude those poses, biased by incorrect skeleton track-
ing is needed.  
It was expected that the variation in the poses of novices would contain more variation compared to experi-
enced endoscopists, since they would more likely perform uncontrolled and sudden movements. However, 
the results from Procrustes Analysis and PCA indicated that it is the experienced who vary more in their 
poses, especially in the hands and elbows for the passage into the esophagus and stomach, and shoulders 
when entering duodenum. This could be an indication that experienced endoscopists are using different ap-
proaches, like seen in the archetype analysis. However, it could also be the possibility that the experienced 
are using a dynamic approach though the full procedure, whereas novices are more static and do not change 
their stance considerably during the procedure, thus, the variability between them are not as large. This 
assumption is also supported when performing the Archetype Analysis before and after entering duodenum 
for both experienced and novices, where it was clear that experienced did change their positions during the 
few seconds of passage (different identified archetypes), where novices did not change their poses (almost 
exactly same archetypes). The motion of endoscopists are not captured or quantified in this thesis, and 
thereby no certain conclusion can be made about this. However, in combination with the results from the 
accelerometer and gyroscope, where a significant difference in amount of rotation used by experienced, was 
identified, it is most likely that some of the variation found in the body postures of experienced is due to

Mathilde Dyrholm Jensen

that. Additionally, literature recommends endoscopists to make use of the rotation maneuver when entering 
the duodenum, which might explain the variation in the shoulders for the experienced, as they are rotating 
their body to the side (11, p. 41-42). Therefore, the larger variation identified in the experienced endoscopists 
are more likely an indication that they are applying different and dynamic techniques to get a fluent and 
efficient maneuvering. Novices are not familiarized with the endoscopic techniques yet, and it is therefore 
not unlikely that they are using a more cautious and static approach to complete the procedure. Experienced 
endoscopists are changing their poses depending on the specific part of the procedure, where novices seems 
to keep a similar pose during to full procedure. This raises the question whether body postures are the best 
way to quantify endoscopists’ skills, or whether motion might capture the specific techniques more clearly. 
Studying the postures will give an idea of the mean pose for a specific moment in the procedure, which truly 
is useful to provide feedback about an appropriate stance. However, if the endoscopist is making a dynamic 
technique, it will not appear clearly in one pose. The postures could perhaps be visualized over a sequence 
(e.g. entering the duodenum) so the full movement is captured. This might give a better understanding of 
which techniques experienced are making use of which novices are not. This information combined with the 
accelerometer and gyroscope could potentially provide a strong quantification of the dynamic techniques 
applied in a gastroscopy.  
When only aligning the center of the shoulders important variation in the poses were identified, which did 
not appear as clearly when aligning all the skeleton joints. This means that when using Procrustes Analysis 
caution must be taken when evaluating the variations between poses and considerations about which skel-
eton joints should be aligned to capture the most relevant variation, is needed. For instance, it was possible 
to detect what appeared to be the two-handed technique, when only aligning the center of the shoulders. 
This is an important identification, since it reveals if the endoscopist is having the right hand for too long on 
the scope head, which is not considered as appropriate (11, p. 34, 106-107).  
5.3. Interactive feedback system for training and evaluation  
There has been identified a clear distinction between the body postures and scope handing for experienced 
and novices depending on specific parts of the procedure. This difference could potentially be used in an 
interactive feedback system for training and evaluation of endoscopic skills. The goal of integrating such 
feedback system into simulation-based training is to ensure that all new endoscopists are trained and as-
sessed in important parameters, related to what an efficient and safe procedure depends on, such as how 
the endoscopist is standing (stance) and managing the endoscope (scope handling). This could guarantee 
higher quality and safety for the increasing number of endoscopic procedures that are needed in the coming 
years. Currently, as no objective assessment or clear standards for endoscopic training are described, the 
competency level for newly endoscopists differ significantly(18), which is the reason why it so important that 
reliable and objective methods are integrated into training to ensure a clear defined level of competency is 
fulfilled before practicing on patients.  
The system could provide useful feedback in guiding the novices in what to do differently, such as keeping 
the hands wide apart, scope hand held high or apply beneficial techniques when entering specific anatomic 
landmarks. However, a single metric of the body posture or scope handling should not be the only criteria in 
training and competency assessment. The metrics do provide important information of how the physician is 
managing the endoscope, but cannot provide information about the physician’s ability to make good deci-
sions, use of extensive force or progression of the procedure. Assessment of skills should therefore be a 
==PAGE:63==
Mathilde Dyrholm Jensen 
Master’s thesis in Health Informatics  
 
 
3D measurement of endoscopic procedures for training and evaluation  
 
62 
 
 
combination of different aspects related to endoscopy. One study have used the MEI technology (magnetic 
endoscopic imaging) to track and record the route of the colonoscope, during a procedure to define a valid 
and objective score, that reveals the endoscopists skill in terms of progression of the procedure. They devel-
oped a graphical map of progression, showing exactly where the endoscopist had trouble advancing the co-
lonoscope (78). This assessment tool could be adapted for gastroscopy procedures as well, and benefit in the 
desired interactive feedback system used in combination of the posture and scope handling metrics pre-
sented in this project. Many of the novices in this project were stuck when advancing through the stomach 
or entering duodenum, likely caused by loop formation in the stomach, and because they were not making 
use of the rotation- or withdrawal technique, but maneuvering with the control wheels instead. With infor-
mation about progression it would give novices a quick overview of the location of the problem and combined 
with information about the posture and scope handling, feedback on what should be applied instead to get 
a more continuous progression, could ideally be given. If such tools and feedback could be integrated into 
the simulation-based training, it would have the potential to improve the learning process considerably.   
Like it was demonstrated with Archetype Analysis, different approaches between the experienced endosco-
pists in performing a gastroscopy exists. This means that the feedback system needs to include a certain 
ability to recognize or adapt to these different individual approaches. Otherwise there might be a risk of 
some trainees, who have a slightly different approach or is affected by individual physical tremor, is evaluated 
as having poor skills. Ideally, the system could eventually learn the novices’ personal style and account for it 
through the training, so the novice will not be corrected or forced to do unnatural movements in relation to 
their individual approach. However, developing such system that supports and adapts to the specific individ-
ual, will be challenging. Firstly, it needs to be determined which approaches are appropriate and which are 
not. Secondly, it will require a larger sample size to determine all the different approaches and techniques 
which might exists (79, p. 2-5).  
Another aspect needed to be considered when developing the interactive feedback system is how the feed-
back should be carried out to teach the novices in a most educational way, while still keeping it simple and 
intuitive. Developing better educational simulation-systems that provide more information will also require 
more intuitive and better ways to present this information. The feedback system has to understand what the 
novice is doing (e.g. recognizing and distinguishing between poses and scope handling depending on the 
anatomic location), adapt to level of skill (e.g. history of performance, or individual style), and provide useful 
and intuitive feedback (what to do differently, when and why). The novices should not be overloaded with 
information and corrections about what they should do. This would likely distract them from the main focus 
of getting familiar with the instrument and the techniques. However, simple features such as shapes, colors 
or intuitive sounds could be available to let the novice be aware of when something is wrong, like different 
postures or if less/more motion should be applied. Current VR simulators provide human sounds like cough-
ing or moaning when the “patient” feels discomfort(6, p. 663). Feedback regarding correct poses could be 
similar to that, by providing subtle warning sounds, indicating when the physician is in an inappropriate pose. 
The feedback could also show a simple avatar of the novices’ skeleton in relation to how the ideal posture 
should be, which could turn red or green, if the novice is far from what is considered as a good pose. This 
could be displayed in the corner of the video monitor, which novices are looking at anyway for the endoscopic 
view. It will probably not even be necessary to provide feedback through the full procedure, but just specific 
parts associated with certain techniques or stance. For instance having a good initial stance before insertion 
will most likely influence how the stance is carried out for the rest of the procedure. Subtle “remainders” 
==PAGE:64==

could then be giving if the novice is moving towards a bad pose, such as having the hand too close to each 
other or the left hand is held too low. This should off course be according to the height of the novice. Fur-
thermore, allowing novices to turn off the graphical or audio feedback should be considered since skills are 
very differently achieved and the feedback might not be needed in the same amount for all novices with 
strong skills or fast learners(23, p.1),(79, p.1, 32-33).   
 
5.4. Strength and limitations  
An important strength of this study is that the data acquisition is carried out by Kinect, accelerometer and 
gyroscope, meaning that the variation of poses and scope handling are analyzed objectively. Subjective bias 
is eliminated, which contribute in some of the main issues in assessment of endoscopic skills. Therefore, the 
results identified in this project have the potential to be used in improving simulation-based training based 
on reliable metrics. Additionally, the accelerometer and gyroscope are direct measures of the motion of the 
endoscope, as it is attached to the actual instrument, which gives a true correspondence of how the motion 
of the scope head is.  
However, there are some limitations which need to be emphasized and considered in further work. It is im-
portant to acknowledge that the Kinect skeleton data gathered in the project is vulnerable for measurement 
bias, since two different rooms and set-ups during the training setting were used. Even though there was put 
much attention and effort in ensuring that the two set-ups were similar, it was not possible to place the 
objects in the exact same position. The participants were also moving during the procedure, causing different 
distances between the Kinect and phantom model. Therefore, the variations identified in Procrustes Analysis 
and PCA might come from these issues. However, telling the participants not to move during the procedure 
would have created more bias in the data, since it would affect how novices think they should stand, or 
forcing experienced endoscopists in performing a gastroscopy differently, than they are used to. If the varia-
tion identified is due to the physician is moving during the procedure, it just emphasizes that experienced 
have a more dynamic approach, like is was indicated in the data analysis. However, using two rooms for the 
training setting were considered as a reasonable compromise, since they only differed slightly and are un-
likely to have a significant impact on the motion patterns identified, compared to the benefits it gained in 
recruiting more experienced endoscopist in the limited time range of the project. Additionally, both set-ups 
were adjusted according to the recommendations from Microsoft on how to achieve best skeleton track-
ing(61). Another limitation, which might have caused bias in the results, is the method in how the EndoAn-
notations was conducted. Since it was a person, who annotated the videos, it was not exact in every video. 
Even though, it might only concern few seconds in difference, it could still have an impact on the mean poses 
that were identified. To make more objective and consistent annotations it could be done by a sensor on the 
phantom model, which registers precisely where the endoscope tip is anatomically, e.g. by MEI technology. 
There are gathered a lot of new information in this project and it was not expected to analyze all of it within 
this project range. For instance, it was not possible to include PoseAnnotations to evaluate the variation of 
good and bad poses between novices, but is certainly in interest to do in further studies. However, a consid-
erable limitation of how the PoseAnnotations were conducted, is that only one person has annotated the 
recordings, which is the same person who has collected the data. Therefore, the annotations might contain 
subjective bias, because the determination of the quality of the poses is only based on one person’s opinion. 
To compensate for this a protocol based on literature of good stance, were followed, to ensure that the 
==PAGE:65==
Mathilde Dyrholm Jensen 
Master’s thesis in Health Informatics  
 
 
3D measurement of endoscopic procedures for training and evaluation  
 
64 
 
 
physician were annotated consistently and minimizing subjective bias. However, in future work, it is ex-
tremely important that the videos will be annotated by several experienced endoscopists independently. 
Similar approach were used in a study, where endoscopists were videotaped during a colonoscopy, and eval-
uated by three experienced colonoscopists, unaware of the identity or level of experience of the endoscopist 
who had performed the procedures (28). This method could be used in this relation as well, where different 
experienced endoscopist could independently annotate when they believe that the physician is in a good or 
bad pose. By comparing these annotations a consensus of the quality of the postures could be identified. The 
assessments should be done anonymously in case of the observer personally knows the endoscopist. The 
approach would still contain subjective bias, however, if the observers make their annotations based on a 
structured protocol or specific criteria related to important part of the procedure, like in this project, the 
method could become more reliable and consistent. One challenge though could be the possibility of various 
opinions of how these criteria should be weighted (28). Another challenge when annotating good and bad 
poses is that novices are not using the dynamic techniques to provide fluent maneuvering, but are instead 
standing in almost the same position during the procedure. Standing still is not necessarily a bad pose, or 
ergonomically inappropriate, but it is causing in a less optimal procedure and should therefore be associated 
with endoscopic skill. This again raises the question whether poses are the best way to quantify endoscopic 
skills or whether motion might capture the techniques more clearly. 
One strength of the project is that the data is gathered in a real clinical setting, meaning that the motion 
analyzed for the scope handling truly correlates to the reality of the motion patterns of experienced endos-
copist, in the real clinical environment. If motions only were acquired in the training setting, direct clinical 
correlation could not be proven, since simulators cannot replace the exact realistic setting or same “feel” of 
the true human anatomy (15, p. 319). Additionally, more challenges are applied in the clinical setting, such 
as patient discomfort and a more stressed environment, requiring different movements, which was demon-
strated with the scope handling. Unfortunately it was not possible to include analyzing the body postures in 
the clinical setting, since the data acquisition was delayed and extracting the skeleton data was more chal-
lenging, due to more people were being tracked. However, it is expected that the body posture would differ 
from the training setting as well, which should be included in further studies.  
Another strength of recording in the clinical setting is that some of the endoscopists might not have been 
possible to recruit in the training setting, because of limited time or willingness to use time outside work to 
participate. Recording in the clinical setting meant that the endoscopists were not asked to take time out of 
the work or do anything differently; they just had to perform the scheduled gastroscopies, like they were 
supposed to do anyway. However, the data in the pilot study, and in the training- and clinical setting are still 
vulnerable to volunteer bias, since the participants volunteered for the study and gave consent to be rec-
orded, which may limit the ability to generalize the variation between experienced endoscopist and novices 
(80).     
Even though a sample size of 36 is considered reasonable in a research project, it is small in absolute terms 
(81). The experienced endoscopists are also recruited from the same hospital department, which might affect 
their endoscopic approach. Many of the physicians are trained by some of the same senior endoscopists, 
which only constitute in a few subjective opinions of how the correct way should be. This could easily have 
an impact on how newly endoscopists recognize what is considered and accepted as best practice on the 
specific department. There might be applied other techniques depending on the hospital department, which

when interpreting the results, and feedback and assessment scores should be based on a larger sample size

few years of experience, which should be accounted for when comparing their performance. However, due 
to the relatively small sample size, this was not reasonable to do in this project.  
5.5. Perspective 
Only upper GI endoscopy has been examined in this project, but could certainly be used for other endoscopy 
procedures, such as colonoscopy or bronchoscopy. Additionally, other surgical specialties in which the sur-
geon’s movements constitute for a large part of the procedure, such as laparoscopy, could also be studied. 
The potential of adapting these inexpensive and convenient motion technologies to other medical areas for 
training and evaluation should be considered, to increase the quality of clinical education in the health care 
system. However, the set-up for using the Kinect in an endoscopic setting is considered relatively simple 
compared to other surgical procedures, where the postures might be more complicated, such as in a seated 
position or only using small hand movements. In gastroscopy the endoscopist is standing upright, facing the 
Kinect with hands apart, which has been demonstrated in this project as suitable to track, though with few 
exceptions. The endoscopic equipment affected the skeleton tracking, and the endoscopists are moving dur-
ing the procedure, causing different distance between the Kinect and phantom model. Another challenge 
when using Kinect is that it is affected by factors, such as the lighting and dark or fluffy clothes worn by the 
person being tracked, or if more people are present in the room(42). Therefore, if Kinect were to be used in 
other surgical specialties consisting of more sophisticated movements or equipment, the same set-up, used 
in this project, will not be able to work and same accuracy of the skeleton tracking might not be possible. 
Procedures with bigger movements or assessment of the progression of motion related diseases might be 
easier to use Kinect’s motion technology. If Kinect were to be applied in simulation-based training it will need 
a fixed and dedicated room with minimal distracting factors and acknowledge that the surgical/endoscopic 
equipment and different position of the physician, needs to be accounted for during motion tracking.  
Sensors, such as accelerometers and gyroscopes, do not have the same limitations as Kinect, and can be used 
in many medical fields, where it is desired for a simple and objective method for assessment of motion (32, 
51, 82). Recent technological development has made these sensors an ideal choice for evaluating variability 
of motions in a non-invasive and portable way, which has great potentials to be integrated in clinical practice 
or home-environment. The sensors have the advantage that they are small, inexpensive and can be attached 
to many different objects and applied in several settings without interfering with the patient or physician, 
meanwhile capturing important and reliable information of the movements. However, the sensors do carry 
some limitations, which need to be accounted for. For instance, many of the devices will require calibration 
to adjust for individual physiological tremors, which might require technical support during training (34, p. 
1475). Additionally, the accuracy of the sensors might be affected by environmental factors, such as temper-
ature, causing noise in the measurement. However, sensors with high resolution with low noise rate, as the 
one used in the project, is considered as accurate and reliable for clinical use (33, 82). The sensors might

6. Further investigation  
This thesis has worked with many new aspects, which has not been conducted or validated in the endoscopic 
setting before. The data acquisition includes several new information, and it is therefore important to em-
phasize that this thesis only presents some of the aspects that could have been analyzed. This study should 
only be considered as the initial part of a much greater research project, based on many years of investiga-
tions to improve the clinical education in endoscopy. There are several other parameters and analysis meth-
ods that should be tested, to study the variation of the body postures and scope handling completely. For 
instance, statistically analysis are needed to evaluate if the identified variation in the body postures is signif-
icantly different, which could be done by e.g. ANOVA.  
An important goal of this thesis was to evaluate if it was possible to gather useful information in the clinical 
setting, which was demonstrated with the scope handling, but unfortunately not possible to conduct for the 
Kinect skeleton data within this project range. It was learned that recording in the clinical setting consists of 
many challenges, which require longer time for the data analysis, since Kinect tracking was often challenged. 
Additionally, the data acquisition was delayed because only one endoscopist could be recorded per day, since 
the same physician was scheduled for the whole day in the examination room, where the recording took 
place. Further investigation for the body postures in the clinical setting will therefore be needed to quantify 
the variation from the body postures found in the training setting.  
Additionally, only postures for passage of esophagus, stomach and duodenum was studied in this project. 
Other anatomic sequences such as the retroflextion maneuver or during a biopsy, could be studied in further 
details. It could also be in interest to record the same endoscopist several times and evaluate if his/her ap-
proach differ significantly according to the patient anatomy, or depending on which setting the procedure is 
carried out (training or clinical).  
Since different body postures have been identified, the challenge will now be to determine which should be 
considered as the “correct way” novices should learn. One way to do this is to let the identified poses be 
rated by several experienced endoscopist, like it could be done for the PoseAnnotations. However, this would 
likely contain subjective bias and differ depending on the individual style of the specific endoscopist who 
rates the poses. Another way, would be to ergonomically study which of these different techniques would 
be best for the endoscopist, to integrate ergonomic principles and help the physician prevent overuse inju-
ries. Consultants with ergo therapists are needed in further studies to make sure that the body postures and 
feedback are based on valid and reliable knowledge about ergonomic principles. In addition, the optimal pose 
for each physician might also vary depending on anatomic differences. Scope handling did not show any 
association between lengths of thumb, however, there might be a stronger association regarding the distance 
between hands and the body postures, since keeping the hands wide apart will most likely not be the same 
for short and tall physicians. A third option is to acknowledge that there exists more appropriate endoscopic 
approaches which should be supported in the feedback system.  
Moreover, important parameters not measured in this study is the force used to insert and torque the shaft 
applied by the right hand. The torque maneuver constitute in a great part of endoscopic maneuvering and

use of excessive force increases patient discomfort and the risk of perforation(83, p. 147) and should there-
fore be included in further studies. Few investigations have used sensors to discriminate between the force 
applied by experienced and novices during a colonoscopy, where experienced showed significantly lower 
force peak than novices (15, 35). Use of sensors that monitor the force during the procedure could provide 
useful information about the endoscopist skill, where feedback would help novices to adjust and learn be-
tween appropriate and excessive force and thereby prevent complications for the patient and overuse inju-
ries for endoscopist.  
It was discovered that rotation constitute in a large part of endoscopic skills, but also that it differed between 
the physicians how the rotation appeared in the gyroscope. A calibration is therefore needed to account for 
how rotation and finger activity are carried out. Additionally, it is important that the rotation score will be 
combined with information about the extension of the instrument, otherwise, will the score not be a reliable 
metric in assessment of scope handling. Further studies regarding whether motion and rotation are associ-
ated with specific parts of the procedure will also be needed, which unfortunately was not able to include 
within this project. It is likely that there are parts of the procedure which require more motion or rotation 
than others, probably those sequences that are considered more difficult, such as insertion or entering duo-
denum. Further investigation should also focus on a sequence of the postures to capture the dynamic tech-
niques. Combined with information from the accelerometer and gyroscope this could provide a strong quan-
tification of the dynamic techniques applied in a gastroscopy. 
The third part of the larger research project includes evaluating of the clinical effect of learning by the desired 
interactive feedback system. This could be done as an intervention study comparing training and learning 
curves when using VR simulators with and without motion tracking metrics focusing on correct posture and 
scope handling. Studying whether the complication rate for newly endoscopist, who have used VR simulators 
with motion tracking metrics will also be in great interest. However, the complication rates are low for upper 
GI endoscopies(0.1%)(84), and therefore a large sample size would be required to show significant differ-
ences in patient clinical outcomes, which would be challenging to accomplish (23, p. 7).  
7. Conclusion 
With the increasing number of endoscopy procedures, it has been acknowledged that training and evaluation 
of future endoscopists must be based on robust training and assessments methods with objective, automatic 
and reliable metrics, to ensure all endoscopists have reached a define level of competency before getting 
hands-on experience with patients. Learning endoscopic skills require extensive training and useful feedback. 
However, no simulators have yet been focusing on how the physician is standing or managing the instrument, 
or providing informative feedback in teaching the novices in what should be done differently. 
This project has presented how the motion analysis technologies; Microsoft Kinect, accelerometers and gy-
roscopes have the potential to improve training and assessment of endoscopic skills in an unbiased and reli-
able way. The goal of the thesis was to study the variation between the body postures and scope handling of 
experienced endoscopists and novices during the performance of an upper GI endoscopy while being rec-
orded by these motion analysis technologies. Only few studies have previously used Kinect to discriminate 
between endoscopic skills in a training setting and no studies have used an accelerometer and gyroscope to

evaluate endoscopic skills. This thesis has studied the variation of body postures and scope handling depend-
ing on specific parts of the procedure both in a training- and clinical setting. Through the statistically shape- 
and classification methods Procrustes Analysis and Principal Components Analysis, a clear distinction be-
tween experienced and novices was found, where experienced showed considerable larger variation of their 
postures before passage of esophagus, stomach and duodenum. Experienced endoscopist are changing their 
poses depending on the specific part of the procedure, whereas novices are more static and seems to keep 
a similar pose during the full procedure. The larger variation identified are likely an indication that experi-
enced are applying different and dynamic techniques to get a fluent and efficient maneuvering. Based on 
these findings questions whether body postures are the best way to quantify endoscopic skills or whether 
motion might capture the specific techniques more clearly, should be considered. However, different tech-
niques among the experienced endoscopist have been recognized by Archetype Analysis, indicating that dif-
ferent approaches in how to enter esophagus and duodenum, exists. These individual approaches need to 
be evaluated in further studies by several experience endoscopists and ergo therapists to determine, which 
of these approaches should be considered as most appropriate and ergonomically correct.  
Validation of using accelerometers and gyroscopes to discriminate between competency levels in relation to 
scope handling has been shown. The rotation technique constituted in a significantly larger part of the pro-
cedure compared to novices. Additionally, experienced were providing a more consistent approach with less 
variation in the motion applied, indication that novices provide more uncontrolled and sudden movements. 
Furthermore, it was demonstrated that the scope handling differed significantly between the training- and 
clinical setting, indicating that other movements are required in the real endoscopic setting. Due to more 
challenges in recording endoscopists in the clinical setting, the body postures were not possible to include 
within this project range, but should certainly be studied in further investigations. Additionally, it was iden-
tified that anatomic differences, such as length of thumb, are not necessarily a constraint in novices’ potential 
to achieve good technical skills in scope handling, but more likely depends on extensive training and practical 
skill.  
This project emphasizes that using motion analysis technologies, as a part of simulation-based training could 
be an important step closer towards efficient and robust training and assessment of skills in endoscopy. This 
could ensure that all newly endoscopists have reached a defined level of competency before practicing on 
patients, and thereby guarantee higher quality and safety for the increasing number of endoscopic proce-
dures, that are required in the coming years. However, further studies are needed and it is important that 
training assessment of endoscopy competency will be combined with more metrics such as information 
about the extension of the instrument, the physician’s ability to make good decision and use of extensive 
force.

Risk Analysis  
In the plan, the risks are classified using a scale from 1 (no risk) to 5 (high risk). The risks are described as the 
chance of the activity being delayed.  
Connecting the Kinect, sensors and endoscopy video to record simultaneously is done in cooperation with 
Ph.D. student Gudmundur and Ph.D. student Morten Bo Søndergaard Svendsen that will be assisting with 
the programming part. This step is considered as an intermediate risk because it depends on Gudmundurs 
and Mortens time and availability, how well the three devices can synchronize, and the issue with the simu-
lator being occupied by physicians and trainees. Meanwhile the technical set-up in the training setting would 
be tested to find the best place for e.g. Kinect skeleton tracking. Additionally, a pilot study is conducted to 
get an idea of how the data from the accelerometer and gyroscope can be used to discriminate between 
scope handling maneuvers. This is seen as an intermediate risk as well, since it requires recruiting partici-
pants. When the recording-software is developed and the technical set up with Kinect, sensors and endos-
copy video is in place, test in training setting is the next step. This is considered as a high risk of delay, due to 
recruiting participants in the study. It is assumed that the main challenge would be to recruit experience 
endoscopist, rather than medical novices since physicians usually have a busy schedule.  
In the same period, observation of gastroscopies on Afdeling C will be conducted to get a better understand-
ing of how the procedures are performed by experienced endoscopist and plan how the test in the clinical 
setting should be carried out. Test in the clinical setting has a high risk of delay because it depends on the 
number of gastroscopies that are performed on the hospital department by different endoscopists. It was 
expected that the amount of participants in this project are limited due to the project range. Based on num-
ber of participants the two studies, using the Kinect in endoscopy setting, recruited (10), (16), the goal in this 
thesis is to recruit at least 10 novices and 10 experienced endoscopist in the period of March 2015 to May 
2015. Additionally, 10-15 gastroscopies should be recorded in the clinical setting as well. This amount is esti-
mated as the minimum of how many procedures should be recorded in order to do a reasonable data analy-
sis.  After the data acquisition is complete the data analysis can be conducted and the last weeks are used to 
finish the report.

Appendix B: Data flow of the recordings 
Firstly, start.py allows typing the participant’s name/number, which will be the name of the folder containing 
the participant’s recorded data. Start.py calls Gastrostart.py and LoadKinect() will open Kinect Explorer. The 
Seated Mode should be manually selected in Kinect Explorer, and ENTER in the shell can afterwards be 
pressed. This will open Kinect Studio, while the folder where the UNICDOC and sensor data temporarily will 
be saved in, awaits a file (“Monitor dir C:\unicdoc for file”). When the button connected to UNICDOC is 
pressed, Accelerometer.py will be called, starting to save the data from the sensors to the temporary folder 
along with the UNICDOC video. Kinect Studio records two seconds after the button is pressed. When all the 
sources are recording, a stop button is created on the screen. To stop the recordings, first, the physical button 
needs to be pressed; this will stop the recordings for UNICDOC and the sensors. Immediately after the stop 
button on the screen will be pressed. This will stop the Kinect Studio, while the UNICDOC and sensor data 
will be moved from the temporary folder to the participant name/number folder. The Kinect Studio recording 
is saved manually in the same folder. The information collected from the participant will also be saved in the 
same folder.  
This means that for each participants a folder is created containing the following:  
- 
Kinect Studio recording (.xed) 
- 
Data from accelerometer and gyroscope (.txt) 
- 
Video from UNICDOC (.avi)

Formål med forsøget 
Studier har vist, at ved brug af Microsoft Kinect motion analysis teknologi, kan skelne mellem øvede og 
uøvede endoskopøre på baggrund af forskellig bevægelsesmønstre og posture. Formålet med dette studie er 
at undersøge og identificere disse bevægelser nærmere for at beskrive hvordan den mest hensigtsmæssige 
postur af endoskopøren bør være i forhold til udførelsen af en optimal gastroskopi. Dette gøres ved at 
foretage en objektive dataindsamling af endoskopørens bevægelser under udførelsen af gastroskopi foretaget 
på simulationsmodel. Den kliniske dataindsamling vil ligge til grund for et interaktivt trænings-og evaluerings 
feedback system, som har potentiale til at blive anvendt i træningsøjemed og forbedre endoskopørernes 
kompetencer. 
Fremgangsmåde: Forsøgspersonen vil blive bedt om at udføre en gastroskopi af simulationsmodellen. Under 
udførelsen vil endoskopørens bevægelser blive optaget via Microsoft Kinect, samt videoen fra 
endoskopkameraet. Derudover vil et accelerometer være fastsat på endoskophåndtaget, uden at være til 
gene for endskopøren. Accelrometeret måler når forsøgspersonen roterer eller bevæger skophåndtaget. 
Det skal understreges at der ikke vil blive pålagt ekstra udstyr som vil være til gene for endskopøren, eller som 
kan ændre den normale fremgangsmåde for udførelsen af en gastroskopi. Det er udelukkende endoskopørens 
bevægelser der vil være i fokus. Endoskopørens overkrop samt ansigt vil være synlig under optagelserne. 
Forskningsprojektet har dog ingen interesse i at bruge ansigtet som materiale og vil dermed heller ikke blive 
offentliggjort. Optagelserne vil være anonyme.  
Der vil både indgå uøvede og uøvede endoskopister i projektet.   
Plan for forsøget: Det forventes at forsøgets varighed vil være 10-15 minutter afhængig af lægens erfaring og 
hvor hurtig han/hun er til at udføre gastroskopien. 
Bivirkninger, risici, komplikationer og ulemper: der er ingen risiko ved forsøget. Kinect er en trådløs og non-
invasiv teknologi.

Appendix G: PoseAnnotations  
- 
Poses should only be annotated as bad if present for longer than 2 -3 seconds 
- 
Distance between hands should be kept wide apart (0, 4 m(10, 16)) and scope hand held high for the 
first part of the procedure (from mouth to pylorus), so the instrument runs in a gently curve to the 
patient’s mouth(11, p. 34)  
- 
Right hand should not leave the shaft for more longer than 2-3 seconds and not more than 5 times 
within 30 seconds(11, p. 106-107)   
- 
Physician should stand in a comfortable way with a confident and balanced stance, not too close to 
the patient(11, p. 34)  
- 
The physician should stand directly in front of the video monitor, which should be adjusted to eye 
level to avoid rotation and flexion of the spine or straining the neck(30, p. 149)  
- 
The physician should use fluent and efficient movements, not sudden, uncontrolled or forceful move-
ments  
- 
The physician should not hold the shaft to close to the patients/phantoms mouth during insertion, 
holding the instrument at the 30 cm mark is recommend(11, p. 35)

Appendix H: Pilot study protocol  
The participants in the pilot study were asked to perform 5 simple scope handle maneuvers, where task 1-4 
included finger activity, task 5 included rotation and task 6 was to perform a full gastroscopy 
1. Steer the large wheel downward until it stops – hold the position  
2. Steer the large wheel forward until it stops  
3. Steer the small wheel downward until it stops – hold the position  
4. Steer the small wheel forward until it stops  
Rotate the scope head clockwise and then counter-clockwise  
5. Perform a gastroscopy on simulator – stop when reach endpoint in duodenum    
The following information was collected from the participants as well: 
- 
Age  
- 
Sex 
- 
Experience level: number of performed endoscopies (sum of gastroscopy, colonoscopy and simula-
tion training) 
- 
Length of the thumb (cm)
