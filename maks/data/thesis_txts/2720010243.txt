Abstract
Sewage sludge ash (SSA) is the byproduct generated from wastewater treatment processes. Sewage
sludge ash is mainly disposed of by landﬁlling, which is not considered a sustainable solution for
waste management. By utilizing the ash in brick material, it would no longer be a threat of pollution
as landﬁll disposals. Producing bricks requires a huge amount of energy, and the properties of the
SSA could reduce the energy needed for ﬁring and save clay material, thereby conserving natural
resources. This thesis investigated the inﬂuence of implementing SSA, treated with electrodialytical
separation (ED), on ﬁred brick specimens.
The SSA used in this project originated from Avedøre wastewater treatment facility. The SSA was
treated with ED prior to this investigation, in order to extract phosphorous for the purpose of reuse,
and heavy metals to reduce the risk of leaching out. Clay brick manufacturer Egernsund Wienerberger
A/S provided two types of clay that are commercially used for brick production, yellow clay with 15
wt.% sand +1.29 wt.% Mn for color, and red clay with 6-7% sand. Clay without additives was also
provided for this project.
The physical properties of the SSA-ED and the clay were investigated. SSA-ED consisted mainly of
silt-sized particles and indicated potential as ﬁller material in clay. Clay with sand had slightly larger
particles than clay without sand. The chemical composition of the SSA-ED was investigated and
the bulk mineralogy for SSA-ED and clay, to evaluate the potential of SSA-ED as a supplement to
brick material, and to investigate quantities of heavy metals in the ash. The content of Cu and Zn
leaching from the raw ash could be of concern in relation to polluting the surrounding environment.
The mineralogy showed high contents of clay minerals, quartz, feldspar, and hematite.
Investigation of 30 wt.% replacement of clay (dry weight) with SSA-ED was carried out, on a
laboratory scale. Inﬂuence on shrinkage, density, porosity, and water absorption was investigated
on miniature brick discs (d = 2mm, h = 3mm), ﬁred at di↵erent temperatures (950, 975, 1000,
1025, and 1050 ◦C), with 4 di↵erent clay types. Inﬂuence on appearance during drying (9 days) and
after ﬁring (950 and 1050◦C), and the compressive strength was investigated and tested on brick
cylinders (d = 60 mm, h = 6 mm).
Firing at 950, 975, and 1000 ◦C resulted in approximately the same properties for discs with SSA-
ED and reference discs. Firing at 1025-1050◦C resulted in a remarkably bettering of investigated
properties for brick specimens. Bricks with SSA-ED achieved compressive strength more than twice
as high as reference bricks when ﬁred at 1050◦C. The highest compressive strength of 17.83 MPa
was reached with SSA-ED utilized in yellow clay and ﬁred at 1050◦C. When ﬁred at 950◦C the clay
bricks with SSA-ED obtained slightly higher strength compared to reference bricks.
Bricks with SSA-ED experienced a higher level of vitriﬁcation during ﬁring, which resulted in a
stronger binding between particles.

Introduction
Each year, signiﬁcant quantities of ash produced from burning sewage sludge are generated all over
the world. Some of this ash is recycled or used for beneﬁcial purposes, but a signiﬁcant portion is still
disposed of in landﬁlls. With limited landﬁll space and shifts in waste management strategies, this
will not be a sustainable solution in the future. In a circular economy, landﬁlling is not the ideal way
to manage waste. However, the presence of metal-containing and potentially hazardous substances
in the ash may pose challenges to increasing recycling or utilization e↵orts (Hjelmar et al., 2022).
Sewage sludge is generated during wastewater treatment processes. The largest wastewater treat-
ment facility in Denmark, BIOFOS A/S, stated in their Environmental report from 2021 that a total
of 22578 tons of sewage sludge was ﬁred, producing 6548 tons of ash (“Miljøberetning, Environ-
mental data 2021”, 2021).
Sewage sludge ash (SSA) is a ﬁne-grained material with particles in the size range of ﬁne sand
and silt. The ash contains high content of Ca, Al, Si, and Fe, which are also considered the main
components in clay (Bubalo et al., 2021); (Ottosen et al., 2020); (Lynn et al., 2016); (Anderson
et al., 2003). The SSA contains a considerable amount of hematite (Fe2O3); hematite has been
reported beneﬁcial to the ﬂuxing properties and could potentially lower the melting temperature of
a clay mix with SSA as substitute (Bubalo et al., 2021); (Lynn et al., 2016). SSA contains relatively
high amounts of phosphorous (P), 6-12 % (Hjelmar et al., 2022). 11 % is comparable to a medium
to high-grade phosphate ore (Aydin et al., 2010). P is an important plant nutrient that is used in
fertilizer, and it is placed on the EU list of critical raw materials (COM 474 On the list of Critical
Raw Materials for the EU, 2020). There is growing interested in ﬁnding techniques for recovering P
from SSA. One method, electrodialytic separation, has been developed at DTU and makes it possible
to extract and separate phosphorous and heavy metals from the SSA (Ottosen et al., 2015).
Ottosen et al. (2020) investigated the properties of brick materials with replacement 0-50 % of clay
with untreated SSA and SSA treated with electrodialytical remediation. It was found that brick
specimens with 30 % replacement were satisfactory in relation to color properties, porosity, and
strength.
The objective of this thesis is to investigate the inﬂuence of sewage sludge ash, treated with electro-
dialytical separation, on brick discs ﬁred at ﬁve di↵erent temperatures, 950, 975, 1000, 1025, and
1050 ◦C. Four di↵erent mixes will be tested, a yellow- and red-clay mix, with and without sand,
added. The aim is to determine the optimal ﬁring temperature with a 30 % supplement of ash
and to investigate the potential inﬂuence of using di↵erent clay types. The physical and chemical
properties of the sewage sludge ash will be investigated. The sewage sludge ash originates from
Avedøre wastewater treatment facility and has been treated with electrodialytical remediation prior
to this investigation. The four clay types have been provided by clay brick manufacturer Egernsund

Sustainability
The 2030 Agenda for Sustainable Development was adopted by all United Nations member states in
2015. With this program, 17 Sustainable Development Goals (SDGs) were introduced as an urgent
call for action by all countries (“Program UND. Sustainable Development Goals.”, 2022).
The reasoning and investigation of this thesis target speciﬁc sub-goals of SDG 9 and 12. Sub-goal
9.4 strives to better infrastructure by increasing resource-use eﬃciency, and it advises all countries
to take action in adopting clean and environmentally sound technologies and industrial processes.
Utilizing sewage sludge ash as clay replacement has great potential in reaching sub-goal 9.4. The
amount of pure clay needed can be reduced, thereby conserving natural resources. Furthermore,
the sewage sludge ash could have a positive inﬂuence on the brick production itself and the ﬁnal
product of the production.
Subgoal 12.2 strives to achieve sustainable management and eﬃcient use of natural resources by
2030. Sub-goal 12.4 strives to achieve sound management of wastes throughout its life cycle and
reduce the release of waste into the air, water, and soil (“Transforming our world: the 2030 Agendea
for Sustainable Development”, 2022). As stated earlier, a signiﬁcant amount of ash is disposed of
in landﬁlls as it has reached its end of life. By utilizing the ash as brick material, it resumes its
life cycle, and it would no longer be a threat as a pollutant in landﬁll disposals. By extracting
natural resources such as heavy metals and phosphorous, the ash is better suited as replacement
material in brick production due to a decreased risk of leaching of these components. Furthermore,
the extracted material can be utilized in other reuse purposes.

Figure 2 shows the ﬂow diagram from clay pit to ﬁnished brick. Clay, used for brick manufacturing,
can be found in many parts of Denmark and the clay brick factories are often located near the clay
pits. Egernsund Wienerberger A/S has factories near clay pits in South and North Jutland and on
Fyn. The clay is extracted and transported from the pits to the factory with heavy machinery. At
the factory, the clay is kneaded in a pan mill that crushes larger rocks. In the pan mill, additives are
added, such as water, dry clay, or sand, to change the plasticity of the clay, and barium carbonate for
binding sulfates or manganese is added for coloring purposes (Holmboe, 2001). After the pan mill,
the clay goes through a rolling mill that ensures a particle size of max 0.8 mm (EPD:MD-21061-EN,
2021). Then the clay is molded by one of three processes. The clay can be extruded and cut with
metal wires to create machine stones with a smooth surface. Another method is hand-cut facing

After forming, the wet stones have a moisture content of approximately 20 % (Vissing, 2022b). The
drying process begins with placing the stones on pallets that will drive through a long drying tunnel
(Holmboe, 2001). The drying process takes between four to ﬁve days, the drying is a well-monitored

and regulated process, and it consists of three main phases (Vissing, 2022b). Initially, the stones
are kept for 2 days at temperatures from 35/40 up to 50 ◦C with air humidity above 90 % and low
air exchange. The aim is to increase the temperature of the brick without losing too much water. If
water starts to evaporate, it will most likely be from the surface of the stone, and the core will stay
moist. The consequence is that the dried surface will act as a cover for any evaporation of the water
in the core, and this will lead to cracks in the stone. After this state, the shrinkage of the stones
is initiated by increasing the temperature and the air exchange and reducing the relative humidity.
When the shrinkage is complete, the last drying phase begins. The shrinkage takes approximately
one day. The last phase takes 1-1.5 days. The temperature increases to 80 ◦C, and the aim is to
achieve a moisture content of 2 % in the stone. If the stones were completely dried free of water,
cracks from re-absorption could occur when the stones were moved from the drying oven to the
ﬁring (Vissing, 2022b).
When the drying is complete, the stones will be moved to a tunnel oven for ﬁring. The ﬁring of
clay bricks takes 2-3 days (Vissing, 2022b). During the ﬁring of clay, minerals decompose and melt
phases in the clay, and the sintering of the particles begins. At a temperature range of 200-300◦C,
water bound in the pore structure of the clay evaporates. At 500◦C, water bound in the clay minerals
evaporates. Figure 3 shows the burning diagrams for yellow and red clay. Before the ﬁring begins,
the chemical composition matches the one listed in table 1. The red clay contains more than 50
% quartz and 10 % feldspar, and the rest consists of di↵erent clay minerals. Yellow clay contains
20 % calcite. As it can be seen in ﬁgure 3, the decomposition of calcite begins at 550 ◦C, and
then the crystal structure of the clay minerals (kaolin, mica, illite, and smectite) starts to break
down, 500-900 ◦C. It is when the clay minerals decompose, melt phases are created instead, and the
sintering of the particles begins. Hematite (Fe2O3) is formed in red clay from this crystal breakdown.
Hematite gives the bricks their red color. The large content of calcite (CaCO3) in the yellow clay
prevents hematite from being formed. Other crystalline phases are formed when the crystal structure
of calcite breaks down at 750 ◦C but not hematite (E. Jensen, 1989) (Holmboe, 2001). From the
broken-down minerals, an amorphous glass phase evolves. Danish brick usually contains 50% glassy
phase and 5 to 15% minerals when ﬁred. It is the glass phase that makes the coarse grains of quartz
and feldspar stick together, and this strong binding that determines the porosity and strength of the
ﬁred clay brick. The porosity and strength of the brick decrease and increase respectively and reach
a maximum at 1050 ◦C. After this point, amalgamation can occur, and the stone will lose its shape
(Holmboe, 2001).

Sewage Sludge Ash and Possibilities for Utilization
Sewage sludge ash (SSA) is the byproduct produced when sewage sludge, from municipal wastew-
ater treatment plants, is incinerated. In Denmark one-third of the wastewater treatment sludge
is incinerated (Hjelmar et al., 2022). The largest wastewater treatment facility in Denmark, BIO-
FOS A/S, stated in their Environmental report from 2021, that a total of 22578 tons of sewage
sludge was ﬁred, producing 6548 tons of ash (“Miljøberetning, Environmental data 2021”, 2021).
Figure 7 shows a ﬂow diagram for treating wastewater. The sewage sludge is separated from the
wastewater and then it is incinerated. Incineration of sewage sludge in Denmark takes place in ﬂuid
bed combustors. Fluidized bed combustion is a technology that heats and blows a sand bed up
around the sludge. Temperatures reach 850-900 ◦C and the sand breaks down the sewage sludge
particle resulting in an almost immediate combustion of the sewage sludge. After the incineration
has occurred, the ash constitutes 40-45 wt. % of the burned sludge (“DAKOFA”, 2022).

After combustion, the SSA is a ﬁne-grained material with particles in the size range of ﬁne sand
and silt. The SSA has high contents of phosphorous, 6-12 wt. % of the dry matter (Hjelmar et al.,
2022). The wastewater treatment plant strives to retract as much phosphorous as possible prior to
combustion. The wastewater is treated with iron sulphate, iron chloride, or aluminum chloride to
precipitate phosphorous. BIOFOS uses iron chloride, as a result, the SSA contains large quantities of
Fe (BIOFOS, 2023). The high content of Fe gives the ash a bright dark red color because hematite
(Fe2O3) is formed. Other main components found in the SSA is Si, Ca, Fe, Mg, and Al. The ash
also contains traces of heavy metals (Hjelmar et al., 2022).
2.3
Utilization of Sewage Sludge Ash in Brick Manufacturing
SSA is a ﬁne-grained material with particles in the size range of ﬁne sand and silt. It has a high
content of ceramic materials (Bubalo et al., 2021); (Ottosen et al., 2020); (Lynn et al., 2016);
(Anderson et al., 2003). When ﬁred at high temperatures, 1000 ◦C, the formation of hematite
(Fe2O3) occurs in the SSA. Hematite has been reported beneﬁcial to the ﬂuxing properties and
could potentially lower the melting temperature of a clay mix with SSA as substitute (Bubalo et al.,
2021); (Lynn et al., 2016).
The utilization of sewage sludge ash in brick manufacturing has been investigated in previous work.
An overview of selected studies, that focus on utilizing sewage sludge ash in brick material is
presented in the following.
Use of Sewage Sludge Ash in the Production of Innovative Bricks - An Example of a
Circular Economy (Bubalo et al., 2021)

Investigation of properties of seven di↵erent types of clay bricks with 0, 5, 10, and 20 wt. % replaced
with SSA. The SSA came from either of two di↵erent wastewater treatment plants in Croatia. The
clay bricks were ﬁred at 900 ◦C. The testing showed that bricks with 5 to 10 wt. % supplement
was of better or equal quality compared to control bricks. There was no notable di↵erence between

The oxide content for both SSAs and the clay was very similar, with a
high content of SiO2, AlO2, and Fe2O3. The SSAs also contained a high content of CaO. The

was a linear increase in water absorption with increasing SSA content. It was concluded that the
SSA had ﬂuxing properties that promoted lower ﬁring temperatures of the bricks.
Recovery of Phosphorus from Sewage Sludge Ash Prior to Utilization as Secondary Re-

Three SSAs from di↵erent sewage sludge mono-incineration plants were investigated. Two from
Denmark and one from Germany. The ashes were treated with electrodialytic separation to separate
phosphorous and heavy metals from the SSA. 65-80% P was recovered. Di↵erences in the raw ash
a↵ected the ashes after electrodialytic separation. The recovery of phosphorous was successful for
two of the ashes, with 81-82 % recovered. 65 % was recovered for the third. The investigation
indicated that electrodialytic separation could be used to separate SSA into two resources: a resource
for P and a material to utilize in construction materials. However, the method needed optimization.
Sewage sludge ash as a resource for phosphorous and material for clay manufacturing
(Ottosen et al., 2020)
In this investigation, SSA from a Danish wastewater treatment plant was treated with electrodialytic
separation. Brick discs, 2 cm in diameter were made with either untreated SSA or treated ash, SSA-
EDS, as a supplement for clay, with 0, 10, 30, and 50 % replacement. The discs were ﬁred at
either 950, 1000, or 1050 ◦C. The investigation found that all brick discs ﬁred at 1000 ◦C, obtained
densities and open porosity within acceptable ranges of bricks used in the industry. Discs with SSA-
EDS had an increased open porosity compared to discs with untreated SSA. Discs with SSA-EDS
had a higher content of hematite formation, which gave the discs a reddish color. The porosity of the
discs increased with increasing SSA-EDS when ﬁred at 950 ◦C but reversed between 1000 and 1050
◦C. The hematite content coursed the formation of a glassy phase when the discs with SSA-EDS
were ﬁred at 1050 ◦C and resulted in a lower porosity. When ﬁred at 1050 ◦C, the open porosity
was very low, 12.5 and 13.6 % with 50 and 30 % clay replacement with SSA-EDS, respectively.

The purpose of this study was to critically examine and evaluate literature from 30 di↵erent databases

amount of SSA increased, and water absorption properties were inversely related to the density and
compressive strength.

SSA was collected from a sewage sludge incinerator in England over six weeks.
The variability
in the consistency of SSA produced over time was investigated, furthermore, the SSAs ceramic

were mixed and formed in a hydraulic press in a 38 mm diameter cylindrical die. The discs were

water absorption, and tensile strength. Large ﬁring contraction gave low water absorption and large
tensile strength. This correlation was considered a direct response to the particle size distribution
and packing of the particle. Smaller particles resulted in a higher cumulative surface area and a
better particle-to-particle contact area. It was reported that when the SSA was added to a raw
ceramic body, the SSA enabled the constituents to fuse more readily and at a lower temperature.

The incorporation of sewage sludge ash in the manufacturing of bricks was investigated. 0, 10,

Electrodialytic Separation of Heavy Metals and Phosphorous from
Sewage Sludge Ash
Sewage sludge ash can be treated with eletrodialytic separation (EDS), in order to separate phospho-
rous and heavy metals from the ash. The separation occurs in a two-compartment electrochemical
cell. Compartment I contains the anode, compartment II contains the cathode, and the dissolution
with the ash. Figure 8 shows the two-compartment eletrodialytic remediation setup. A cation ex-
change membrane (Ionics CR67 HUY N12116B) separates the two compartments. An electric ﬁeld
is applied to the dissolution and acidiﬁcation of the anolyte begins, due to electrolysis of H2O at the
anode. This will lead to the migration of cations from the anolyte toward the cathode, through the
membrane. Cations, heavy metals, will go to the catholyte, and anions, including phosphorous, will
remain in compartment I as negatively charged ions or neutral molecules. After the electrolysis is
complete, 7-14 days, the ash suspension is ﬁltered and dried (Ebbers et al., 2015); (Ottosen et al.,
2015)
The loss of mass after EDS is around 40 wt. %, but can vary (Ottosen et al., 2020); (Ottosen et al.,
2021). Up to 96 % separation of phosphorous from the ash is achieved. 87-91 % separation of Cd,
61-77 % Cu, 38-53 % Zn (Ebbers et al., 2015);(Kappel et al., 2018).

3.3.4
Production Procedure for Brick Cylinders
The mixing procedure for brick cylinders follows the principles for mixing brick discs but on a larger
scale. For mixing ash with clay and water, ToniMix Basic Model 6213 standardized mortar mixer
was used. First, clay was mixed with half of the water for 1 minute. Then ash was added, and the
rest of the water was mixed for 1 minute. If the consistency was too dry, more water was added until
the consistency matched the reference mixture’s consistency. References were mixed with water for
1 minute. The clay mix was left for one day in a closed box so that the clay mix could absorb the
water.
Figure 38: Clay, water and ash is mixed until right consistency is achieved
It was desired to produce brick specimens based on the production principle for soft stone press
production, used in the industry. The way of production is explained in section 2.1. This was a
challenge as the production of bricks in the industry uses heavy machinery for forming and well-
monitored drying chambers. One challenge was to form stones with low water- and air-content in the
stone without applying a huge amount of force. Another issue was very simple but challenging: how
to easily release the molds. And lastly, how to achieve satisfactory drying, as explained in section
2.1, this process is constantly monitored and regulated. It was investigated if the traditional way
of brick stone production could be implemented. Still, the experience needed for casting stones by
hand was not realistic to achieve, the amount of material needed was not available and the precision
and monitoring that is desired when conducting a scientiﬁc investigation of material behavior was
not achievable. L¨utzhøft (2022) investigated best practices for producing stamped clay cylinders to
achieve greater compressive strength and high dry density. The method used the Instron-6022 with
a cylinder form and stamp, much like the production of brick discs in this project. This method
applied a load of 2 kN on a cylinder surface with a diameter of 60 mm, corresponding to a load of
0,71 MPa. This load was applied 5 times at 800mm/sec speed. This method was adopted for this
project.

The brick cylinders were formed with Instron-6022. Figure 39-40 shows the procedure of forming

3.4
Testing of Brick Discs and Brick Cylinders
The porosity of the ﬁred brick discs was measured after 24 h of saturation. The porosity of the
material indicates how fragile the material is. High porosity means that the material absorbs water
easily. The porosity was found for three discs of each disc type, as the relation between the density
of the dry disc and the density of the disc in a fully saturated state.
Figure 42 to 44 shows the procedure for ﬁnding the porosity of the discs. Three discs of each mix
were placed in nylon socks, they were kept separated, and the location of each disc was noted. The
discs were then placed in a desiccator, and a ceramic plate was placed above to keep the discs from
ﬂoating. The desiccator was sealed completely. All the air was then sucked out of the desiccator
and the discs with a vacuum pump over 3 hours. After 3 hours, water was led into the desiccator.
The water had been boiled to get rid of air. The discs were fully saturated for one hour, then air
was led to the desiccator again, and the discs stayed saturated for 24 hours. After 24 hours, the
discs were weight one by one, ﬁrst underneath the water and then above. Before weighing above
water, excess water was carefully removed from the disc with a wet cloth.
The mass of the disc-elements was noted, and the volume, density, water-dry mass relation, and
porosity were calculated. Table 9 lists the relevant measurements and how the density and porosity
are then determined. Water absorption was calculated as described in DS/EN:772-21 (2011).

3.4.1
Testing of Brick Cylinders
The compressive strength of the brick cylinders was found by pressing one cylinder at a time in a
Matest servo-hydraulic testing machine, E161PN173, with a loading rate of 0.74 kN/s. The cylinders
had a slightly uneven surface and were therefore placed in between two cardboard discs to activate
the entire surface.
The loading rate was found in table 2 in DS/EN:772-1 (2015). Initially, a test cylinder with yellow
clay was compressed at a loading rate of 0.3 (N/mm2)/s; table 2 listed the expected compressive
strength of 21-40 N/mm2 from this rate, and states that it should be reached within one minute.
The maximum compressive strength was found within the range within one minute, and therefore
the load rate was set to 0.3 (N/mm2)/s. This corresponds to 0.74 kN/s for a cylinder with a
compressive area of 2463 mm2.

Element Content
Table 16 lists the element content found from SSA-EDS-c. The element composition of SSA2 from
Lynn et al. (2016) and SSA1 and SSA-ED1 from Ottosen et al. (2021) is also listed in the table.
The major elements found in SSA-ED-c are Ca ≥Fe ≥P ≥Al. Lynn et al. (2016) compares
168 di↵erent articles, and as can be seen in table 16 the variation in the element content of the
SSA2 can vary a lot. But the four most signiﬁcant element contents are generally found as they
were for SSA-ED-c, Ca, Fe, P, and Al. The phosphorous content was very low for the SSA-ED-c,
approximately the same that was found in SSA-ED1 (Ottosen et al., 2021). This was expected,
as the ED treatment should result in low phosphorous content. The Fe content in SSA-ED-c was
the lowest compared to what has been found in the literature. The SSA1 and SSA-ED1 in table
16 stem from the same wastewater treatment plant as the SSA-ED-c. As stated in section 2.2,
the wastewater treatment facility precipitates P with Fe. Based on this, the Fe content would be
expected to be higher for SSA-ED-c. Fe could have been removed during ED treatment. The Fe
content could also potentially be higher. The digestion method from the DS:259 (2003) standard
does not give the total concentrations for Fe, only the content soluble in acid. Ottosen et al. (2013)
found from an XRF analysis that the content of Fe in SSA was twice as high as the concentration
found from acid digestion. A high level of calcium and iron in the SSA-ED-c could contribute to
glass formation during ﬁring and thereby contribute to better ﬂuxing properties when mixed and
ﬁred with clay brick material (Bubalo et al., 2021), (Anderson et al., 2003).

Mineralogy - Crystalline Phases
Figure 50 shows the X-ray di↵ractograms for the SSA-ED-c with the main compounds marked.
Figure 51 and table 18 show the XRD for SSA-ED-c and the bulk mineralogy for Yc, YSc, Rc, and
RSc. The quantity of each mineral is also listed. There could be a number of minerals that could
not be identiﬁed with suﬃcient certainty, so the actual mineral distribution could look di↵erent.
It can be seen that the SSA-ED-c contains mainly quartz(SiO2), large quantities of hematite (Fe2O3),
and feldspar (Na(AlSi3O8)) and a smaller amount of whitlockite (Ca7Mg2P6O24). The Fe content
in the SSA-ED-c was found to be low with acid digestion, but the content of hematite in SSA-ED-c
suggests that there was a substantial amount of Fe present in the ash. Yc and YSc contain the same
minerals and the same quantities of these minerals, quartz, feldspar, and calcite (CaCO3). Rc and
RSc also contain almost the same quantities of the same minerals. RSc contains a larger amount of
quartz and feldspar compared to Yc and YSc, and a very low amount of calcite. This corresponds
to what was stated for typical clay types in Denmark in section 2.1.
Quarts, calcite, and hematite were found in SSA by Anderson et al. (2003), Lynn et al. (2016),
and Bubalo et al. (2021), and in SSA-ED by Ottosen et al. (2020). Whitlockite has been reported
in SSA-ED and SSA before (Ottosen et al., 2020); (Cheeseman et al., 2003). As was stated in
section 2.1, hematite was created during the ﬁring of red clay material and was the reason for the
red color of the bricks. The SSA-ED-c will contribute to the creation of hematite when mixed with
both yellow and red clay and have an impact on the color properties. Ottosen et al. (2020) stated
that the high content of hematite would contribute to the formation of a glassy phase. Figure 3
showed that the clay minerals kaolinite, montmorillonite, mica, illite, and partly feldspar would start
to decompose when ﬁring temperatures reached 550◦C, and from these broken-down clay minerals a
glass melt-phase would form and bind the quarts and remaining feldspar together. The clay minerals
are composed of varying amounts of Al, Ca, Si, Fe, Mg, and K. Most of these elements were also
found from the XRD analysis in the minerals in SSA-ED-c. From table 16 it was seen that the SSA-
ED-c contained substantial quantities of K also, which was not identiﬁed in the XRD-analysis. This
indicates that there are compounds present in the SSA-ED-c that will contribute to the formation
of the glass phase when mixed and ﬁred with clay.

4.2.1
Change in Volume and Mass During drying and ﬁring of brick discs
Figure 55 shows the ﬁring mass loss of the discs after they were ﬁred at 950, 975, 1000, 1025, and
1050 ◦C. The loss of mass was not dependent on increased ﬁring temperature. It depended on the
clay type and whether the disc contained SSA-ED-c or not. Discs with yellow clay lost more mass,
both with and without SSA-ED-c. In section 2.1 it was stated that yellow clay contained 20 % calcite
(CaCO3), which decomposes around 550◦C and releases CO2 in the process is a major contributor
to mass loss (E. Jensen, 1989) (Ottosen et al., 2020). It was found from the XRD analysis that
Yc and YSc contained approximately 16-17 % CaCO3, which was much more than what was found
in Rc and RSc. This explains the di↵erence in the mass loss for discs with yellow or red clay. YSc
contained 15% sand compared to Yc; this decreased the loss of mass because the quantity of CaCO3
also decreased. The SSA-ED-c contributed to a lower ﬁring mass; this was seen for all four mix
types. Both Yc, YSc, Rc, and RSc contained quantities of CaCO3, and adding SSA-ED-c would
reduce this content quantity for all four clay-mix designs. The SSA-ED-c contained high quantities
of feldspar and quartz; these two minerals decompose to a very low extent when ﬁred (E. Jensen,
1989). An increased amount of quartz and feldspar in the clay mix would contribute to a lower ﬁring
mass loss for the discs.

CONCLUSION
5
Conclusion
The inﬂuence of sewage sludge ash, treated with electrodialytical separation (SSA-ED), on brick
material was investigated. Brick discs and brick cylinders with 30% clay (dry weight) replaced by
SSA-ED, were produced. Two types of clay were investigated, with and without sand as additives,
and the brick properties and ﬁring behavior from ﬁve di↵erent ﬁring temperatures were evaluated.
From the experimental work that was carried out in this study, the following can be concluded:
• The main minerals found in SSA-ED were quarts, feldspar, hematite and whitlockite. The
components of the minerals in the SSA-ED contributed to the formation of a melt phase
during the ﬁring of the brick and resulted in strong binding between particles in the ﬁred brick.
• The implementation of SSA-ED thereby resulted in higher shrinkage, increased apparent den-
sity, and a decrease in porosity and water absorption for ﬁring temperatures 1025-1050◦C.
When ﬁred at 950-1000◦C the brick properties were similar for specimens with SSA-ED and
corresponding references.
• Fired brick cylinders with yellow clay obtained a rough appearance with rounded edges and
small bulges, whereas the red clay resulted in a more smooth surface. When SSA-ED was
added it enabled the shrinkage of the clay material for both yellow and red clay. Reference
bricks did not shrink during ﬁring, instead, they obtained large cracks and a porous structure.
• Hematite in SSA-ED inﬂuenced the color properties of the bricks. Red bricks turned a darker
shade of red, whereas yellow clay obtained a brown color. This is an aesthetic property that
could have an inﬂuence on whether to supplement SSA-ED in yellow or red clay material.
• The optimum ﬁring temperature was 1050◦C when SSA-ED was utilized. A radical change
in the structure of the brick material would occur somewhere between 1000 and 1050◦C and
initiate a melt phase, created from broken-down clay minerals that would draw the particles
together.
• The compressive strength did not reach the expected strength of 20-40 MPa, deﬁned in
DS/EN:772-1 (2015), for any of the cylinders. But brick specimens with SSA-ED contributed
to substantially higher compressive strength, than references. The highest strength of 17.83
MPa was reached when utilizing SSA-ED as 30 wt.% supplement for yellow clay and ﬁred at
1050◦C.
The production method used for producing brick cylinders resulted in very poor-quality for reference
cylinders, and the desired break-down and development of clay mineral did not occur as intended,
but there is no doubt that bricks with SSA-ED experienced a higher level of vitriﬁcation, which
resulted in a stronger binding between particles.

RECOMMENDATIONS FOR FUTURE STUDIES
6
Recommendations for future studies
The recommendations for future studies are divided into two. One is related to understanding the
chemical changes when utilizing sewage sludge ash in clay brick material, and the other focuses on
the optimization of brick production on a laboratory scale, with SSA-ED.
Future studies related to understanding the chemical changes when utilizing sewage sludge ash in
clay brick material.
• Investigation of mineralogy for clay bricks with SSA-ED in the temperature range 1000 to
1050◦C.
• Investigation of changes in mineralogy in the temperature range 500-900◦C, for clay materials
with 30% SSA-ED as supplemented.
Future studies related to the optimization of brick production on a laboratory scale, with SSA-ED.
• Method development for producing brick specimens with SSA-ED, with a focus on initial water
content and forming method.
• Method development for producing brick specimens with SSA-ED, with a focus on drying and
ﬁring time.
