Abstract
This thesis deals with topology optimization for acoustic design problems.
Investigations in how topology optimization can be applied to an acoustical
design in order to reduce the noise emitted from certain sources, are carried
out.
The goal of the optimization is to minimize the sound pressure at a
certain place in a ﬁxed domain for a set of predeﬁned frequencies, by either
placing reﬂecting materials within the design domain, or by placing absorbing
material on the boundary of the domain. The sound ﬁeld is governed by the
scalar Helmholtz equation, and is used for both homogeneous and inhomoge-
neous mediums. For inhomogeneous mediums and inhomogeneous material on
boundaries, suitable material interpolation functions are introduced in order
to be able to solve these kind of problems.
The ﬁrst part of this thesis concerns a simple Matlab model, where the
ﬁnite element method together with topology optimization is applied to per-
form optimization of an acoustic ﬁeld in a ﬁxed domain by applying reﬂecting
material in the design domain. The sensitivity is calculated using the adjoint
variable method and chain rule. The second part is to extend the methodology
by using COMSOL to apply absorbing materials at the walls of a ﬁxed do-
main in order to minimize the noise in a certain place. The last part concerns
optimization with simple ﬂow-acoustic interaction.

Introduction
The aim of this thesis is to apply topology optimization to acoustic design
problems where there is a need to reduce noise in human surroundings. Usu-
ally, ceilings are used as design domain where reﬂecting materials are put or
absorbing materials are distributed along the walls of the acoustic domain.
The idea is about an acoustic domain that contains the acoustic medium and
a sinusoidal sound waves emitting input source, where the goal is to optimize
the topology in the domain or at the boundaries, in order to reduce sound
pressure level in a certain place for a chosen frequency or a frequency interval.
The acoustic ﬁeld is governed by the scalar Helmholtz equation, where the
ﬁnite element method can be used to solve the discretized problem.
Topology optimization of acoustic designs to reduce noise can be useful in
many applications. These applications can vary from rooms and halls, roads,
railways, airports, noisy factories, noisy machinery, engine noise in almost all
means of transport, sound barriers, small devices like phones, microphones,
headphones and hearing aid. Noise reduction by optimized distribution of ma-
terials, whether these materials are reﬂective or absorbing, is called a passive
method of noise control, while other methods like electroacoustic techniques
[1], are called active methods, which can also be used to control noise.
Topology optimization is a method to determine an optimal structural
layout for a given problem subject to design constraints, by the material dis-
tribution in the design domain. Topology optimization is based on numerical
methods such as Finite Element Modeling together with classical mathemat-
ical optimization techniques and can be used to design mechanical structures
systems in a large range of engineering applications. The method was ﬁrst in-
troduced in structural mechanics by Bendsoe and Kikuchi in 1988 , and since
then it has been successfully applied to other engineering ﬁelds including ﬂuids
and wave propagation problems. The topology optimization of acoustic design
problems is not very common in the literature, and has so far only been applied
to a few problems. In [2], topology optimization method is applied for scalar
wave propagation problems modeled by Helmholtz equation and results for an
inverse acoustic horn are presented. In [3] topology optimization is applied
for an acoustic horn. Topology optimization of acoustic–structure interaction
problems are discussed in [4, 5]. Radiation and scattering of sound from thin-
bodies is optimized by genetic algorithms in [6] and rigid body modeling issues
in acoustical topology optimization are treated in [7]. Application of topology
optimization to noise barrier design is presented in [8]. A brief overview of
applied topology optimization of vibro-acoustic hearing instrument models is
presented in [9]. In [10], a method to design acoustic devices with topology
optimization is presented and and exempliﬁed by the design of a reﬂection
chamber that minimizes the transmission of acoustic waves in a speciﬁed fre-
quency range. The last-mentioned paper together with [11] are used as the
basis for the acoustic model in this thesis. The mentioned literature references
are the fundamental and the very ﬁrst of their kind in this topic. A few more
1
==PAGE:8==
papers and articles discuss topology optimized acoustic designs, but are ex-
cluded either due to, treatment of a very narrow and speciﬁc problem, or due
to their use of earlier mentioned papers as base for their work.
This thesis takes the topology optimization for acoustic design a step fur-
ther moving into the ﬁeld of acoustic-ﬂow interaction where to my knowledge
no literature exist at the time of writing. This thesis starts with pure acoustic
topology optimization done in a simple Matlab model and veriﬁed in COM-
SOL, where the methodology is then extended to include more options. The
last part of this work investigates the acoustic-ﬂow interaction with the ap-
plication of topology optimization, where Stokes ﬂow is considered. Diﬀerent
aspects and features of topology optimization are investigated along the way
of this thesis, where general rules and ideas in acoustic topology optimized
designs are presented as well. Last but not least, some of the concepts are
tried out on a simpliﬁed design of a silencer for an exhaust system for large
ships.

In this chapter a brief description of the fundamentals of acoustics theory based
on conservation laws, and derivation of Helmholtz equation, are presented.
1.1
Fundamentals of Acoustics
Sound waves in ﬂuids are oscillatory, compressional disturbances that prop-
agate in a media, and can be generated e.g. by a vibrating surface. These
disturbances are characterized by pressure ﬂuctuations, which a human ear
can hear, also by perturbations of the temperature and the density, and by
the vibrations of particles. The various acoustical quantities mentioned are
connected by some basic laws that will be covered here. These basic laws allow
us to set up a general diﬀerential equation that governs the sound propagation,
which will be used in this thesis. In this part, we are dealing with a static
system without a ﬂow.
Firstly, the needed variables of state to describe acoustic pressure waves,
are deﬁned as:

To derive Helmholtz equation following assumptions are made. First, it is
assumed that the perturbations are small, so that: ua ≪us, ρa ≪ρs, Ta ≪Ts
and pa ≪ps. Next, adiabatic compression is assumed which means there is no
heat exchange, and it is further assumed that the relation between pressure
and density is linear. Furthermore, it is assumed a non-dissipative system and
large geometries.

Modelling
In this chapter, the modelling of the ﬁnite element method for acoustics is
presented. The chapter begins with an introduction to FEM and the problem
deﬁnition. Next, diﬀerent scenarios with diﬀerent considerations are presented
with descriptions and derivations for each case. Finally, diﬀerent aspects of
damping and absorption are proposed and discussed.
2.1
Finite Element Method for Acoustics
The ﬁnite element method is a widely used numerical technique for solving
diﬀerent engineering problems.
By FEM it is possible to solve partial dif-
ferential equations, i.e. ﬁnding approximate distributions of one or several
ﬁeld variables in a given domain, with given boundary and initial conditions.
The numerical solutions to the diﬀerential equations are obtained by discrete
approximation of the continuous derivatives. The ﬁnite element method basi-
cally is comprised of two steps. The ﬁrst step is to construct a weak form of
the original PDE, and secondly, to discretize the domain into ﬁnite elements
using locally approximating functions known as shape functions [16].
In an acoustic problem, one is interested in ﬁnding a solution for the
Helmholtz diﬀerential equation (1.19), by applying the ﬁnite element method
to obtain a numerical approximation of the pressure.

2.1.4
Damping and impedance
Damping in the system can be imposed in two diﬀerent physical meanings.
The ﬁrst method is where damping is imposed in the acoustic medium and the
reﬂecting materials. This is useful in order to avoid inﬁnite amplitudes which
gives numerical calculation problems as singularities in matrices when hitting
a natural frequency. The damping eﬀect can be taken into account in several
ways. One way is to use a complex bulk modulus such that κd = κ + iακκ.
The second way is by using a mass proportional damping C = αmM, which is
analogue to damping in stiﬀness problems in structural mechanics. Damping
coeﬃcients ακ and αm are typically between 0.01-0.001. The last-mentioned
damping can not be imposed in COMSOL directly in an acoustic medium,
as done in Matlab. See appendix B.3 for more information about how this
can be implemented in COMSOL. These methods are useful in cases where
non-aborbing materials are placed in the design domain.
The second method is with damping in the boundaries, which is achieved
by placing absorbing materials to absorb the sound waves.
In an acoustic
absorption process, the material takes in sound energy when sound waves are
encountered, instead of reﬂecting it. Part of the absorbed energy is trans-
formed into heat and the rest is transmitted through the absorbing body or
partly reﬂected. The fraction of sound absorbed is governed by the acoustic
impedances of both media, such that using the same material as boundary, say

3
Topology Optimization
This chapter begins with a short introduction to general topology optimiza-
tion, where the methodology for solving the topology optimization problems
is described, and the approach used for the material distribution is presented
along with the derivation of adjoint sensitivities. Finally, topology optimiza-
tion for speciﬁc acoustic problems are presented.
3.1
General topology optimization
Topology optimization is a method that determines optimized distributions
of material within a predeﬁned design domain, to achieve the best possible
system response in terms of a predeﬁned objective and possible constraints.
For a typical topology optimization problem, one should predeﬁne the design
domain and the volume fraction which is the allowable fraction of the design
domain to be solid material.
In this thesis, the density approach is used,
where material properties are assumed constant for each element in the design
domain. The optimization step includes a gradient-based sensitivity analysis,
where the gradient of the objective and constraint equations, with respect to
the design variables are calculated, and the design is updated based on these.
More detailed procedure of the topology optimization can be found in C.1.
In the following, a general constrained topology optimization problem for
a complex linear dynamic system with harmonic excitation, is considered.
Let the state variables be the real and imaginary parts of the sound pressure
amplitude p. The problem can be solved independently from design variables,
where the design variables ξ are the only optimization variable. The problem
can then be written as follows:

use ﬁnite diﬀerence methods to do a so called sensitivity check on few design
elements after a couple of optimization iterations, where the adjoint method

3.1.4
The Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA)
For optimization, the Method of Moving Asymptotes by Svanberg [19] is ap-
plied. MMA is an optimization algorithm for solving general nonlinear con-
strained problems, and is very popular in the topology optimization commu-
nity since it can easily handle problems with a large number of design variables,
subjected to few constraint functions.
3.1.5
Filtering
Filtering technique in density-based topology optimization schemes is usually
used to ensure manufacturability and to create mesh-independent solutions.
If no ﬁltering is applied, several issues might appear [20]. The ﬁrst problem
is the formation of checkerboard patterns which originates from discretizing
the problem and using ﬁnite element approximation to solve it. The second
problem is the mesh-dependent solutions since normally using a ﬁner mesh
results in ﬁner truss-like solutions or ﬁner distribution of material with more
holes.
A simple ﬁltering technique is the sensitivity ﬁlter suggested in [21, 22],
where the ﬁlter modiﬁes the design sensitivity of a speciﬁc element based on
a weighted average of the element sensitivities in a ﬁxed neighborhood. The
ﬁltered sensitivities are then used instead of the original sensitivities in the
optimization routine. This type of ﬁlter has been implemented in the Matlab
model, however this was diﬃcult to implement in COMSOL. Consequently,
21
==PAGE:28==
a density ﬁlter was implemented in Matlab based on the method provided
in [23], whereas an equivalent PDE ﬁlter [24] was implemented in COMSOL.
One can choose between the two ﬁlters in Matlab, but in order to compare
the results with COMSOL, density ﬁlter is used as default.
The density ﬁlters work by modifying the element density, such the ﬁltered
value of a design variable at a given cell is obtained by averaging over the
neighbor cells[24]. In the Matlab model the ﬁlter works by a deﬁned ﬁlter
radius based on element length, such that if rmin = 1.5, the ﬁlter radius is
1.5 × element length. Whereas in COMSOL it uses the normal length scale.

Part II
Results and Discussion
Acoustic topology optimization with non-absorbing materials has been imple-
mented in a 2D Matlab model. In this chapter the results from this Matlab
model are presented and compared to COMSOL results. The goal is to verify
the Matlab model and show that the topology optimization can be applied for
acoustic designs for both models. Afterwards the study is taken a step further
where a model with absorbing materials is made in COMSOL and the results
are also presented. Using the implemented absorbing boundary condition in
topology optimization of acoustic designs in presence of mean ﬂow, have been
investigated and results are presented in the last section.

Mean ﬂow
A mean ﬂow over time is considered in the following study, in order to sim-
ulate a simpliﬁed real-world problem. The type of problems investigated is
about ﬁnding the optimal distribution of the absorbing material in a silencer
in an exhaust system. Initially, the numerical solution obtained in COMSOL
is compared to the one-dimensional analytical solution. Afterwards, optimiza-
tion of the boundaries with the presence of mean ﬂow in a simpliﬁed silencer
design is studied. Last but not least, several complicated cases are tested and
results are presented and discussed.

6.3
Initial guess
In general, a topology optimizing algorithm does not require a qualiﬁed ini-
tial guess in order to obtain the optimal design, which makes it easier for the
designer with small or no knowledge about the best design. However, in some
cases the optimization algorithm can easily be captured in a local minimum,
which may not result in a 0-1 design as shown in ﬁgure 6.9, or a poor optimiza-
tion performance as the above-mentioned case. One of the problems could be
a highly sensitive initial design which can be solved by using a non-uniform
initial design as the initial guess.
In the following, three diﬀerent randomly chosen initial guesses have been
investigated by utilizing them in the optimization of the Uf = 20 m/s, and
the results are presented in ﬁgures 6.10-6.12. It is observed that in 8 of these
9 cases, the obtained design lies close to the solution in ﬁgure 6.7 where a
uniform initial design was used. Furthermore, a couple of these initial guesses,
have successfully resulted in lower objective values than the optimization using
uniform initial design which was 106.10 dB. An important thing to mention
is the lower objective value of some of the initial designs, which are caused
by overuse of the amount of absorbing materials, due to unevenly manual
distribution between absorbing and reﬂecting walls.

Part III
Conclusion
In this thesis, topology optimization to acoustic design problems have been
investigated. In the beginning, a simple Matlab topology optimization tool,
which uses FEM to solve the Helmholtz equation, and the MMA as optimiza-
tion algorithm, have been built. The tool can ﬁnd the optimal distribution of
reﬂecting materials in a predeﬁned design domain and by a set of predeﬁned
constraints, with the objective of reducing the sound for a speciﬁc frequency in
a certain location in the acoustic domain. In order to be able to distribute two
type of materials to be considered as the acoustic medium, it was necessary
to deﬁne suitable material interpolation functions between the two materials,
due to the inhomogeneous medium created. The model have been success-
fully veriﬁed by doing a convergence analysis and by comparing it to another
model implemented in COMSOL, which showed good accordance between the
implementations. Diﬀerent types of damping have been studied, and it was
concluded that damping mostly aﬀects high resonances. Furthermore, a very
short study on ﬁlters, where the density ﬁlter of the Matlab model is compared
to a PDE ﬁlter implemented in COMSOL, have been carried out and where
consistency was achieved. It was found that in the case of non-absorbing mate-
rials, the reﬂecting materials placed are mostly forming some type of cavities,
in such a way that the nodal lines go through them.
The COMSOL model was further developed to include absorbing bound-
ary conditions. The boundaries in this case was considered the new design
domain, where the goal was to ﬁnd the optimal distribution of the absorbent
on the boundaries, in order to reduce the objective for a chosen frequency or a
frequency interval. The concept of impedance have been investigated and an
appropriate interpolation function between absorbing and reﬂecting materials
have been made. The latter takes into account the need of obtaining good 0-1
designs, avoiding intermediate densities appearing in the optimized topology,
by using a suitable penalization factor, found to be between 1.5 and 2 in that
particular case. It was further observed that the reduction in the objective
is constant regardless of the chosen acoustic input velocity. Optimizing for a
single frequency places the absorbent at the high pressure locations seen in the
SPL plot corresponding to that particular frequency. Optimizing for several
natural frequencies results in a design with tendency to place the absorbent
based on the majority of the modeshapes associated to the frequencies.
In the last part, the presence of mean ﬂow in the system has been con-
sidered. A one-dimensional simple problem of sound waves propagation with
a mean ﬂow, has been solved in COMSOL and compared to the analytical
solution, which showed good accordance. Diﬀerent optimization cases in the
presence of mean ﬂow with diﬀerent velocities have been presented and dis-
cussed. A cross check was carried out in order to look for any kind of sys-
tematical observations, but the results were unexpected. It have been found
55
==PAGE:62==
that the results where highly sensitive to the initial designs, which could point
towards local minima issues. Accordingly, several initial guesses have been
tested and the maximum allowable inner iterations were reduced. Hence, bet-
ter results were obtained and convergence to minimums have been avoided.
Unfortunately, none of these values have outperformed the cross check results
in table 6.2, which raises suspicions of an improper way of how acoustic prob-
lems with ﬂows have been implemented in the model. Another essential part
of this story, is the method by which the problems have been treated and
results have been compared, since changing the ﬂow velocity had signiﬁcant
inﬂuence on the frequency response by moving the placements of the high
response peaks dramatically.
7
Future work
Due to the lack of time, and that the project has deﬁnitely not been without
diﬃculties on all levels, the work did not reach much further than the most ba-
sics of acoustic-ﬂow interaction. Therefore, following suggestions are outlined
here for further research and investigation:
-More complex ﬂows: Navier Stokes, rotational, compressible, turbulent.
-Realistic absorbing materials
-More investigation of ﬁltering techniques in COMSOL.
-3D models

C.1
Optimization procedure
Figure C.1: Topology optimization procedure ﬂowchart
The topology optimization procedure presented as a ﬂowchart is shown in
ﬁgure C.1. Initially, material properties, dimensions, discretization, boundary
conditions and input source are deﬁned. The allowed volume fraction should
also be deﬁned prior to the optimization, which restricts the allowed material
to be distributed, and also often used as initial guess where the material is
evenly distributed in the design domain. Then, ﬁlter radius and ﬁlter type
might be chosen.
Using the initial guess, the ﬁnite element analysis constructs the global
matrices, and then solves the problem Sp = F. The obtained result, which is
the sound pressure, is used to calculate the objective, the constraints and the
sensitivities. Filtering can be applied for either sensitivities or densities. Using
the found objective, constraint values and the sensitivities in the optimization
algorithm, e.g. MMA, results in a new optimized material distribution. Fi-
nally, this new material distribution is compared to the previous one. If the
64
==PAGE:71==
diﬀerence is signiﬁcant, another iteration is taken by using the found mate-
rial distribution as initial guess in the FEA. This procedure is repeated until
the change is smaller than the deﬁned tolerance, where then the optimization
stops and the optimized design is obtained.
