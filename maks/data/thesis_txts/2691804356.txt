Abstract
In human immune responses induced by allergens, peptides in allergens are taken up by
antigen-presenting cells (APCs) and presented to T helper cells by the major histocom-
patibility complex (MHC) class II molecules. Inspired by the process, we introduced
this as novel features for prediction of allergens by calculating the peptides presenta-
tion to MHC class II molecules. We developed machine learning-based classifiers with
additional features including physiochemical properties, structural information, compo-
sitions, and evolutionary information of allergens. To learn from data closer to reality,
models were trained on a dataset consisting of 688 allergens and 3440 non-allergens.
The entire dataset was split into independent partitions with low similarity to ensure
non-redundancy among partitions. Under a nested cross-validation schema, logistic re-
gression, support vector machine and random forest were trained and evaluated respec-
tively. Compared among different models and features, the peptides binding to MHC
class II have been verified to be the most discriminative than other features. The final
best SVM model achieved an AUC of 0.960, and an AUC of 0.890 on an independent
validation dataset. In comparison with the current state-of-the-art model, our model
can be further improved and developed in the future.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Introduction to allergies
Allergy is a hypersensitivity disorder in the human immune system. In general, hyper-
sensitivity reactions are classified into 4 types by Gell and Coombs based on different
mechanisms [1]. The rules of classification have been expanded by more complete mech-
anisms as well. The Type I or immunoglobulin E (IgE) mediated hypersensitivities can
be further divided into two diseases, atopic and allergic [2, 3]. The atopic disorder is
an exaggerated lgE-mediated immune response, for instance, allergic rhinitis, atopic der-
matitis, allergic asthma, etc. However, the allergic disorder is an exaggerated immune
response induced by foreign substances regardless of mechanisms, e.g., urticaria, anaphy-
laxis, some food allergies, etc. Although the hypersensitivities have been divided into
many types or subtypes, our research focuses on the Type I hypersensitivities and the
allergenicity prediction.
Allergens are the substances that lead to Type I hypersensitivities. These substances
typically are harmfulless. Humans can be exposed to allergens through ingestion, in-
halation or direct contact. Most food allergies are induced by the ingestion of food (e.g.
peanut, seafood, milk, egg, etc.). However, inhalation can trigger food allergic reactions
as well [2]. The allergen (e.g. pollens, fungal spores, animal dander, dust mites, etc.)
that lead to allergic reactions by inhalation may have aerodynamic properties. They
can be attached to airborne particles due to their small particle size and low molecular
weight [4]. Moreover, allergies can be induced by direct contact with some allergens (e.g.
latex, cosmetics, mango peel, etc.)
Allergies are associated with various diseases, like food allergy, allergic rhinitis,
asthma, atopic dermatitis, anaphylaxis, etc. Worldwide, there are approximately 6-8%
of the population suffering from food allergies [5]. The prevalence of asthma is approxi-
mately 3.5% [6]. In the United States, the prevalence of allergic rhinitis is around 10-20%.
The prevalence of allergic diseases has increased in the past decades, and it is likely to
rise in the future. In addition, allergies produce some common symptoms (e.g. itching,
wheezing, nasal congestion, hives, etc.), as well as severe and life-threatening signs (e.g.
stridor, hypertension) [2]. Therefore, we would like to develop an allergen classifier to
assist in the prevention of allergic disorders and aid the development of allergy therapies.

1.2 Immune response to allergens
2
1.2
Immune response to allergens
Allergic response typically involves two stages, sensitization and re-exposure (Figure 1.1)
[7]. During the sensitization stage, allergic reactions do not develop when an individual
firstly gets in contact with allergens. Antigen-presenting cells (APCs) uptake and digest
allergens into small peptides with specific amino acid sequences. These peptides can
be loaded into the major histocompatibility complex (MHC) class II molecules, and the
peptide-MHC complexes are subsequently recognized by T cells. The activated T cells
release cytokines to stimulate B cells. Finally, allergen-specific human immunoglobulin
E (IgE) antibodies are produced by abnormally activated B cells and recognized by mast
cells. Allergic responses occur when the sensitized immune system is exposed to allergens
again. In the re-exposure, except for the common processes in sensitization, allergens
build cross-links between IgE bound on mast cells with themselves. Subsequently, the
mast cells are activated and secrete histamine and other mediators, which trigger acute
allergic reactions within minutes. Hours later, the mediated immune cells (basophils,
eosinophils, etc.) produce mediators, which provoke chronic allergic reactions. Consider-
ing the essential role played by the MHC class II molecules in allergic reactions, it would
be reasonable to regard the peptides binding to MHC class II molecules as features to
classify allergens.

1.3 Previous studies
3
1.3
Previous studies
The FAO/WHO has published a guideline for evaluation of allergenicity [8]. By com-
paring with allergen databases with evidences, the predicted protein sequences are con-
sidered to be homologs to allergens, if the sequences have over 35% similarity with a
window size of 80 or have at least 6 contiguous identical amino acids. Allermatch [9]
was developed based on the FAO/WHO guidelines. The prediction accuracy is highly
dependent on the quantity and quality of proteins in the database.
Homologous se-
quences can be well-predicted. However, in the case of low similarity sequences, it may
be less applicable. Later motif-based approaches discovered the motifs in allergens and
searched the query sequences against motifs. This approach improved the precision than
approaches based on the FAO/WHO guidelines [10].
In addition to sequence similarity- or motif-based algorithms, machine learning-based
methods recently have been widely applied to predict allergenicity. Moreover, different
features to classify allergens and non-allergens were used in previous studies.
First,
based on support vector machine, AlgPred was developed as a hybrid predictor [11]. It
combined IgE epitopes, motifs and a SVM with amino acids and dipeptide compositions
as features. Afterwards, another SVM with iterative pairwise sequence similarity as fea-
ture vectors was employed on AllerHunter [12]. Allerdictor predicted the allergenicity
with k-mer frequencies [13]. Except for the support vector machine, classifiers based
on k-nearest neighbors (kNN) were developed as well. AllerTOP and AllerTOP v2 ap-
plied the auto- and cross-covariance of Z-descriptors and E-descriptors as features to
predict allergens respectively [14, 15]. AllergenFP was developed based on the descrip-
tor fingerprints [16]. Last but not least, random forest is another algorithm that has
good performance in allergenicity prediction. AllerCatPro incorporated Q-repeats, 3D
structural similarities, and sequential similarities into the decision tree [17]. AlgPred 2
integrated random forest, BLAST, and motif methods to produce hybrid scores [18].
In studies about the mechanisms of allergy, the crucial role of major histocompati-
bility complex class II molecules in allergy has been revealed. Therefore, in the present
study, we investigated the peptide presentation on MHC class II molecules which had
not been considered as features for allergenicity predictions. Additionally, physiochem-
ical properties, structural information, and evolutionary information were vectorized in
distinct representations. Based on these investigations, we developed machine learning-
based (logistic regression, support vector machine, and random forest) predictors with
other informative features. In order to learn from data that was closer to reality, an
imbalanced dataset was collected and used to develop the classifier.

Methods and Materials
2.1
Dataset
Curated allergens were retrieved from the AllergenOnline public database (http://
www.allergenonline.org/databasebrowse.shtml) on 25 September 2020 [19]. The
database contains various protein sequences from different species (e.g., ragweed, salmon,
birch, cat, etc.). The majority of allergens were selected from accessible protein sequences
of 50-1000 amino acids in length. Redundant sequences in the dataset affect the learn-
ing and predictive performance of the machine learning models. Thus, the Hobohm
1 algorithm was applied to remove the redundancy in the allergens dataset [20]. The
Hobolm 1 algorithm generated a similarity by sequence alignment, and created unique
clusters that contain sequences with a similarity below 90%. The positive dataset has
688 allergic proteins after removing the sequence redundancy.
The number of non-allergenic proteins is greater compared to allergenic proteins.
Therefore, for each allergen, we collected 5 corresponding non-allergens from the same
species. The sequences in the negative dataset were downloaded from the NCBI pro-
tein database. We searched the non-allergic proteins by species names excluding results
with other keywords (allergen, hypothetical, putative, uncertain, predicted, probable).
Furthermore, we selected the non-allergens with a 20% range of the length of the cor-
responding allergens from the candidate results. The negative dataset consists of 3440
non-allergic protein sequences.
Furthermore, an independent validation set was collected from AlgPred 2 validation
datasets.
To ensure the independence of our data and the validation data, BLAST
search was performed on the validation dataset against our dataset. The threshold of
identity was set to 50%. We obtained a non-redundant validation dataset containing
2303 sequences and a redundant validation dataset with 1727 sequences.
2.1.1
Data partitioning
To ensure that the models can learn from the pattern of every allergen and its counterpart
of 5 non-allergens, we divided these every 6 protein sequences into separate subsets.
Thus, the entire dataset is composed of 688 subsets. Each subset has one allergic protein
as well as 5 non-allergic proteins.
Since the test data is supposed to be not redundant to training data, the entire
dataset was split into partitions by similarities.
We generated 688 allergen BLAST
databases using the leave-out-out method for allergic protein sequences. We used BLAST

2.2
Feature space
2.2.1
Physiochemical properties
The feature subspace of physiochemical properties comprised the average residue weight,
average charge, isoelectric point, hydrophobicity and hydrophilicity. The residue weights
of amino acids range from 57 to 186 Da, and the weighted average residue weight is
approximated to 110 Da. Hence, the average residue weight, to a certain extent, reflects
the composition of amino acids in a protein. In terms of the average charge and isoelectric
point, they represent the charge carried by a protein and the pH at which a protein
presents with no net charge. The charged residues result in electrostatic interactions
between protein and protein.
The electrostatic effects are essential for the stability,
recognition, folding, and binding of proteins [21]. A study has suggested that significant
differences exist in both isoelectric point and net charge for allergens and non-allergens
[22]. Besides the above features, the hydrophobic or hydrophilic effects have influences
==PAGE:12==
2.2 Feature space
6
on protein folding. Hydrophobic residues are more likely to be buried inside a protein,
while hydrophilic residues compose the accessible surface. Thus, we used the hydropathy
index to measure the proteins.
The statistical results of the average residue weight, average charge, and isoelectric
point were obtained from the Pepstats API in the EMBL-EBI tools [23]. We used the
widely used Kyte-Doolittle scale to calculate the hydropathy indices [24]. In the Kyte-
Doolittle scale, positive scores indicate the corresponding amino acid is hydrophilic, and
the negative scores are for hydrophobic amino acids. Position-specific hydropathy indices
were determined by equally weighted amino acids within a window size of 9. To obtain
an overall hydropathy score for a protein from the sequential indices, we counted the
number of hydrophobic or hydrophilic residues with a sliding window of 5. In a window,
if all the consecutive residues had the same hydrophobic or hydrophilic tendencies, we
would add 1 to the overall score. The final overall scores of proteins were normalized by
the sequence length to exclude the influence of length.
2.2.2
Secondary structure information
Compared with charge and hydropathy index, secondary structures intuitively repre-
sent the results of protein folding. Allergenicity of proteins is likely related to protein
structures [25]. The size, solubility, stability that are decided by the protein structures
probably to be the determinants of allergenicity. In addition, the flexibility and solvent
accessibility of proteins play important roles in protein allergenicity [26, 27]. Confor-
mational flexibility of allergen isoforms may be related to the proteolytic susceptibility
which is regarded as a factor for allergenicity [28, 29].
Hence, the α-helix, β-sheet,
average relative solvent accessibility, and disorders were added to be four other features.
These secondary structure features were predicted using NetSurfP 2.0 [30].
The
NetSurfP produces position-specific secondary structure prediction as well. First, to
summarize the α-helices and β-sheets for a protein, we counted the number of occur-
rences of 5 predicted α-helices and 3 predicted β-sheets within a sliding window of length
7. Second, we calculated the average value of relative solvent accessibility in a window
and compared it to a threshold of 0.3. If the threshold was exceeded, the average relative
solvent accessibility was increased cumulatively by 1. Likewise, the existence of at least
3 residues with disorder greater than 0.5 was regarded as a contribution to the overall
disorders. At last, these 4 features were normalized by the sequence length.
2.2.3
Peptides binding to MHC II
Several immunogenetic studies have suggested that the HLA-DR isotypes of MHC class
II are associated with different allergies [31, 32, 33, 34]. The binding peptides analysis of
MHC class II molecules was performed on the NetMHCIIpan 4.0 [35] with 7 different alle-
les. These alleles (DRB1_0301, DRB1_0701, DRB1_1501, DRB3_0101, DRB3_0202,
DRB4_0101, DRB5_0101) include the majority of allele frequencies in geographical re-
gions throughout the world [36]. The NetMHCIIpan identified the binding strength for

peptides in a sliding window of length 15 and generated the number of strong and weak
binders for a protein. For the final binding scores of a protein, the numbers of binders
from different allele settings were summed and then normalized by sequence length.
2.2.4
Compositions
The amino acid compositions (AAC) are primary features that determine protein struc-
ture and functionality. In the past, the analysis of amino acid composition was used
to characterize allergens. Furthermore, they have been widely applied to predict pro-
tein activity, protein-protein interactions, allergenicity, etc. Additionally, summarized
compositions by physical properties are likely to be informative features. On the basis
of amino acid compositions, amino acids were classified into tiny, small, aliphatic, aro-
matic, non-polar, polar, charged, basic, and acidic groups by size, structure, charge, etc.
Each group gave a composition by property. These compositions were obtained from
the results of Pepstats when we calculated the isoelectric points and residue weights.
Other than single amino acid compositions, dipeptide compositions (DPC) were related
to protein properties as well. Stability, solubility, types of proteins are correlative to the
dipeptide compositions [37, 38, 39]. The dipeptides is the Cartesian product of 20 amino
acids with itself, which produces 400 new features. Eventually, to investigate the effect
of compositions on allergenicity prediction, we extended the features space by 9 compo-
sitions by properties, 20 amino acid compositions, and 400 dipeptide compositions.
2.2.5
Evolutionary information
Evolutionary information contained in protein sequences has proven to be discriminative
features in binding, interactions, and structures of proteins [40, 41, 42]. Position-specific
scoring matrix (PSSM) represents evolutionary information by the amino acid substi-
tutions at a specific position. The elements in PSSM are log-odds scores, i.e., the log
transformation of the substitution probabilities. After the sequence alignment, if the
occurrences of amino acid substitutions are more than the background, the score would
be positive.
Otherwise, lower substitutions lead to negative scores.
The PSSM has
the shape of query sequence length by 20 amino acids. To vectorize the PSSM for a
protein, we applied the autocovariance (AC) transformation. The autocovariance trans-
formed PSSM has discriminative power on protein structure and bioactivity predictions
[43, 44, 45].
We performed the PSI-BLAST from the NCBI-BLAST 2.11.0 tool packages to pro-
duce the position-specific scoring matrix [46].
To search query sequences with PSI-
BLAST, we first constructed protein databases using our training dataset. To prevent
data leakage, PSSMs were obtained by searching sequences in the training and test
dataset on the constructed BLAST database. The E-value threshold for PSI-BLAST
was set to be 0.001. Due to the limit of the database, the PSSMs of query sequences
with no hits were replaced by the values in the default scoring matrix BLOSUM62. The

2.3.2
Feature reduction
In general, the curse of dimensionality caused by high dimensional data is another prob-
lem in machine learning. The model performances usually could be improved by intro-
ducing more discriminative features. However, when the data have numerous features,
the data in feature spaces become extremely sparse. The sparse data is not suﬀicient
to estimate the model. parameters in high dimensional space. In this case, the number
of data is required to exponentially increase to achieve equal performance. Moreover, a
classifier in high dimensional space is easy to find a hyperplane that well separates the
sparse data. Thus, decisions under sparse feature space are likely to be overfitting and
have poor performance in prediction.
In feature engineering, feature selection has been widely used to select important
features and reduce the dimensionality. Feature selection has three different methods:
filter, wrapper, and embedded. The filter methods are based on feature variances and
correlations between features and targets. Features with low variances and low correla-
tions are considered to be less informative. We used a t-test to investigate the statistical
differences of features for allergens and non-allergens and used boxplots to visualize the
differences.
In addition, the discriminative powers of numerical features to categori-
cal targets were evaluated by area under the curve (AUC). For the wrapper method,
the algorithm evaluates performances in different subspaces, and selects the subspace in
which models have good performance. We split the feature spaces into several subgroups
(e.g. structural information, compositions, etc.), and used a simple algorithm similar to
forward/backward feature selection to find a good subspace. Unlike the filter method,
the last embedded methods generate feature weights or feature importances by learning
models. For instance, regressions with regularization and decision trees can provide the
contributions to predictions from every feature. In our work, integrated filter, wrapper,
and embedded approaches were performed to study and select features. Likewise, the
evaluations of features were carried out on training datasets that are independent of test
datasets.

2.3.4
Cross-validation
Cross-validation is a widely used method to prevent overfitting and generalize predictive
models. In our work, feature selection and hyperparameter tuning were performed under
a nested cross validation schema (Figure 2.3). We divided the entire dataset into 10 folds
using the previous data partitioning method to make sure that partitions were indepen-
dent to each other. Based on the data partitions, the outer layer of cross-validation has
10 folds. To ensure the independence of sub datasets, the inner layers have 9 folds. In
this case, training, validation, and test datasets were mutually independent. First, the
training sets were used for feature selection and hyperparameter tuning. Second, model
evaluations with different hyperparameters were carried out on the validation sets. At
last, selected optimal models produced predictions on the outer test sets. The predictions
were ensemble results, which calculated the average predicted values provided by the
inner 9 optimal models. We obtained the final prediction results for the entire dataset
by concatenating the predictions for the 10 outer test sets.

2.3.7

2.3.7.1
Baseline model
A baseline model was created to compare whether the performances of different ma-
chine learning models exceed the BLAST performance. The BLAST is a simple ap-
proach to identify allergic or non-allergic proteins by searching allergen databases. A
query sequence with a low E-value indicates high similarity with allergen sequences. On
the contrary, high E-value suggests that the query sequence can be predicted as non-
allergens. Therefore, we constructed allergen sequences in training datasets as BLAST
databases. Sequences in the outer test sets were searched on the corresponding indepen-
dent databases as query data. First hit E-values of test sequences were extracted as the
predictions of BLAST. The overall performance of BLAST was evaluated by AUC.

A novel method for prediction of allergens is presented in this work. The method includes
a series of novel physiochemical features as well as features associated with MHC antigen
presentation of local protein structure. The feature selection and model construction is
performed on a large data set of known allergens using nested cross-validation to ensure
proper performance estimations and avoid information leakage in the feature selection.
The final method is compared to a current state-of-the-art predictor.

3.6
Model comparison
All the models we developed outperformed the BLAST baseline model (AUC=0.715).
The previous best SVM model with 60 features had a few improvements on the basis of
840 features (Figure 3.16). However, there is insuﬀicient evidence to determine whether
the good performance was due to chance. Thus, in order to determine the final model
and investigate the model robustness, bootstrapping was performed to obtain the distri-
bution of performance. New predictions were randomly sampled from the concatenated
results with replacement, and then a new AUC was obtained. We repeated this progress
10,000 times and calculated the p-value. The p-value was obtained by the occurrences
of SVM_840F AUC being greater than SVM_60F and divided by 10,000. The p-value
for AUC and AUC 0.1 were 0.407 and 0.640 respectively. It indicated there was no
significant difference between SVM_60F and SVM_840F.
In addition to comparison of our models, the SVM models were compared to the state-
of-the-art predictor as well. The validation dataset of AlgPred 2 was split into redundant
and non-redundant datasets to ensure the data independence. Both the SVM_60F and
SVM_840F were evaluated on the the redundant and non-redundant datasets respec-
tively. SVM_60 had AUC of 0.964 and 0.858 on redundant and non-redudant datasets,
while the values of SVM_840F were 0.975 and 0.890. The performance might be higher
if adding informative features to SVM_60F or removing non-informative features from
the SVM_840F. Additionally, the AlgPred 2 predicted on these two datasets as well,
and both AUC were 0.999. It outperformed our models.

CHAPTER 4
Discussions
In this work, we explored the feature space and verified the assumption that the number
of peptides binding to MHC class II molecules were representative features for allergens.
These novel features have not yet been incorporated into allergenicity prediction. In
addition to the MHC II binders, the feature exploration revealed other informative fea-
tures. In the sets of physiochemical properties, the average residue weight was more
significant than other features in both logistic regression and random forest. It might
explain that some allergens have small size and are easily carried by airborne molecules.
Other features (e.g. average charge, isoelectric) also showed importance to predictions.
However, the commonly used hydrophobicity and hydrophilicity had the lowest impor-
tance in the random forest. Among the structural information features, beta-sheet and
average relative solvent accessibility had more contribution to predictions. The disor-
der was always the least important feature in both algorithms. The compositions by
properties were significant but lower than some single amino acid compositions (e.g. Q,
R, C, etc.) both in logistic regression and random forest. It is worth noting that the
amino acid composition of Q and some dipeptide compositions containing Q (e.g., QQ,
EQ, QS, QC, etc.) were likely to have positive coeﬀicients and more importance. On
the contrary, compositions related to R (e.g., R, LR, RK, RP, IR) tended to have signif-
icantly negative coeﬀicients in the logistic regression. In addition to Q, the coeﬀicients
of compositions including C tended to be positive as well. That indicated allergens have
more glutamine, more cystine, and less arginine than non-allergens in general. In terms
of evolutionary information, some autocovariance transformations of PSSM (e.g. AC_Q,
AC_V, etc.) were discriminative to classification. In PSSM, higher amino acid scores at
a position suggested that it was likely to observe the amino acid at that position. Auto-
covariance represented the average covariance between the sequence and itself. Hence,
the coeﬀicients of AC_Q indicated that glutamine might be present in a large num-
ber of repetitions in the sequence. The information suggested by AC_Q and AAC_Q
was similar to the Q-repeats features used in AllerCatPro. Moreover, the features of
PSSM crosscovariance exhibited importances (e.g., CC_EQ, etc.) The crosscovariance
reflected how two amino acids correlated in a protein sequence. For instance, within a
sequence fragment, high crosscovariance between amino acid E and Q indicated that ob-
serving E at a position was highly correlated to observing Q at the other position. Thus,
we supposed that the crosscovariance might reflect conservative, functional residues, or
motifs. On the basis of these findings, we developed a SVM-based allergen classifier
with an AUC of 0.960. Based on the AUC on test data, the SVM_60F and SVM_840F
had no significant difference. The further evaluation on redundant and non-redundant
datasets indicated the SVM_840F outperform SVM_60F on independent dataset. The

SVM_840F might contain more informative features than SVM_60F. These features
could be further incorporated into the SVM_60F. However, compared to AlgPred 2,
our models still need to be improved. Moreover, the prefect performance might indi-
cated overlapping exists in the training and validation dataset. Thus, newly published
independent dataset were required for further comparison as well.
In the pre-processing of features, some features could be fully identified, like the
average residue weight, average charges, compositions, MHC II binders, etc. The rest
features, including hydrophobicity, structural information, and evolutionary information
were extracted from position-specific scores. The window sizes, thresholds, and lag were
empirical parameters. Different parameters generated different feature values. Thus, the
current features might not be optimal for allergenicity classification. However, these fea-
tures had shown good discrimination on both allergens and non-allergens under current
parameter settings. Optimizing the features is supposed to improve the classification
performance. On the other hand, the feature subspaces could be further subdivided into
smaller features spaces, thereby only extracting informative features from the entire fea-
ture subspaces. In feature explorations, some significant features could be well-explained,
and it would be interesting to further investigate the biological implications behind these
features.
When dealing with imbalanced data, the machine learning predictor usually classifies
test data as the dominant class.
Therefore, we mainly used the AUC as evaluation
metrics rather than the accuracy.
Additionally, when developing the models on the
imbalanced dataset, we resampled the training datasets to construct a balanced training
dataset (1:1) at the beginning. The trained models were evaluated on the imbalanced
test dataset (1:5). However, the AUC was lower than the corresponding model trained on
the imbalanced dataset. That might be due to the fact that the undersampling generates
small training datasets, which results in information loss. As a consequence, we trained
on the original training datasets but used the threshold-moving approach to solve the
problem. In this case, 0.5 was no longer the threshold for classification. The threshold
varied around 0.2 depending on the algorithms and datasets. In addition to threshold-
moving, adjusting the class weights is also a possible way to improve the performance
on imbalanced datasets in future work. Speaking of model training, although Support
vector machines produced the best predictive model, the training sometimes was time-
consuming, especially for hyperparameter tuning. Hence, it is worth simplifying models
or exploring more eﬀicient algorithms to reach the same or better performance.

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
In this work, peptides in allergens binding to the MHC class II have been verified to be
discriminative features. In particular, the number of weak binder is the most informa-
tive for allergenicity prediction than other features. Based on these findings, an allergic
protein SVM-based classifier was developed for the imbalance dataset. The model pre-
sented good performances in classification. Compared with state-of-the-art predictor,
our models still have room for further improvements.
