Abstract
To optimize the effective depth by placing the bending reinforcement as close to the edge
as possible an alternative orientation of the shear reinforcement is explored. The stirrups
are positioned along the bending reinforcement as opposed to encircling it, leading to
imperfect anchorage conditions. This raises concerns regarding the shear capacity and
the effectiveness of the shear reinforcement. This thesis presents a mechanical model
to estimate the shear capacity based on the upper­bound theorem and observed failure
mechanisms of reinforced concrete beams. A crack pattern characterized by smeared
cracks is observed with a primary yield line consisting of a bi­linear and horizontal yield
line. The model takes into account the effect of dowel action, stirrups activated due to
dowel action, and the height of the shear reinforcement. The estimated capacity of the
model is in good agreement with experimental tests for greater shear­ and compressive
reinforcement degrees. An average reduction of 17% in capacity was found when com­
pared to a traditional orientation of the stirrups.

1
Introduction
To increase the effectiveness of the bending reinforcement it is desired to place the rein­
forcement as close to the edge as possible and thereby increase the effective height and
optimize crack spacing calculations due to the influence of the effective tension area. A re­
inforcement configuration where the stirrups are placed along the bending reinforcement
instead of encircling it is therefore explored shown in Figure 1.1. The stirrups are placed
on the inside of the longitudinal reinforcement effectively reducing the stirrup height, while
maintaining the concrete cover. The primary concern revolves around the imperfect an­
chorage conditions and the effectiveness of the reinforcement, which subsequently affects
the shear capacity. Due to ongoing discussions regarding reinforcement effectiveness,
a decision is usually made to increase the amount of reinforcement, surpassing the cal­
culated requirements for a traditional cross­section significantly. Both the Eurocode [1]
and other well­known methods such as the lower bound solution [2, 3] cannot estimate
the capacity for this orientation of the stirrups as the bending reinforcement is no longer
encircled and anchored by stirrups [4].
Figure 1.1: (A) Stirrups placed along the bending reinforcement, (B) Traditional orientation
of the reinforcement
Experiment studies [5, 6] focusing on different reinforcement types with varying anchorage
conditions have been examined to identify the shear failure mechanisms, crack patterns,
and reinforcement stress. A smeared cracking pattern is observed for normal closely
spaced shear­reinforced beams, characterized by a more controlled development of the
primary crack [7]. The stirrups allow for a distribution of the crack resulting in new cracks
formed from the primary yield line with smaller spacing and opening. The reduced crack
opening enhances the ability to transfer forces through aggregate interlocking or the resid­
ual tensile strength of the concrete [7]. The primary yield line is characterized by an incline
yield line going from the tensile reinforcement to the compression zone and from the tip
of this crack to the applied load. A horizontal yield line develops along the reinforcement
located within the tension zone and connects with the incline yield line. Studies propose
various models capturing different effects to asses the shear capacity for traditional re­
inforced beams [7, 8] or non­shear­reinforced beams [9]. The author has not been able
to collect any research papers or experimental studies with an identical representation of
the reinforcement configuration, even though UK standard [10] showcases a cross­section
similar to the one of interest.
The scope of this report is to determine the shear capacity of a reinforced concrete cross­
section where the stirrups are placed along the bending reinforcement instead of encir­
cling it. The hypothesis is that the capacity can be determined based on observed crack

patterns of normal­shear reinforced beams by utilizing the upper bound theorem. A me­
chanical model is proposed utilizing several optimization parameters to capture the com­
plexities regarding the reinforcement configuration and the observed yield line.
When the stirrups are placed along the bending reinforcement, the bending reinforcement
is usually prescribed as welded together with the transverse bar located in the corner of
the stirrups. The influence of dowel action, therefore, plays a significant role as a defor­
mation of the bending reinforcement will result in activation of the stirrups located along
the horizontal yield line. The effect of dowel action is usually treated for un­reinforced
cross­sections [9, 11, 12], but when shear reinforcement is present the dissipated energy
is greater as the stirrups also restrict the deformation of the dowel. As the displacement
vector starts deviating from vertical, bending stresses will gradually be accommodated
with tensile stress in the rebar and introduce a dissipation of energy due to tension. This
effect is taken into account by utilizing the second­order plasticity model in [11]. A bi­linear
yield line is introduced to effectively represent the observed yield line and the influence of
dowel action in the compressive zone. Yielding of all stirrups and small deformations are
assumed, as well as a rigid plastic behavior at failure, where the total energy dissipated
must be in balance with the external force according to the work equation.
The model is validated based on experimental test [13, 14, 15] and a parametric study
was conducted to assess the influence of what has been deemed the governing design
parameters on the accuracy of the model. Lastly, a comparison is made between the ca­
pacity of a typical bridge deck, reinforced with different orientations to asses the capacity
between the two. The thesis is limited to beams subjected by points loads in one­way
shear and beam considerations.

The theory of Plasticity
This chapter contains the basis for the theory of plasticity and introduces important con­
cepts such as yield conditions, failure of concrete, and normality conditions described in
[16, 17] used through the report. The theory is developed based on the idealized behavior
of a rigid­plastic material, which exhibits no elastic strains and allows for infinitely large
plastic deformations. Although such a material does not exist in reality, the theory of plas­
ticity can be applied with good approximation for materials experiencing significant plastic
strains compared to elastic.
Limit analysis is used in combination with the theory of plasticity to determine the load­
bearing capacity of a structure with this material property.

Failure Criteria for concrete
The state required for a material to fail and thereby lose its ability to withstand the stress
leading to failure is defined by the failure criterion. Failure of concrete is treated as a
modified Coulomb failure criterion, where failure occurs to either sliding­ or separation
failure. Separation failure occurs when the separation strength, fa, or tensile strength, ft,
is exceeded. Sliding failure occurs when the shear stress, τ, along a plane is reached
and depends on the cohesion, c, friction angle, ϕ, and normal stress, σ. An illustration for
such material is presented in Figure 2.2.

3
Existing Models
The following chapter will provide an overview of the lower bound solution and the Eu­
rocode approach to determine the shear capacity. Both approaches have many similari­
ties but are unfortunately not usable for the shear capacity of beams where the bending
reinforcement is not encircled and anchored by closed stirrups, as this results in a violation
of key assumptions. Since the stirrups are no longer encircling the longitudinal reinforce­
ment yielding of the reinforcement cannot be guaranteed. Stirrups must be placed around
the bending reinforcement in the compression­ and tension zone also described within the
guidelines [4].
Although these methods are not directly usable, they still offer valuable insights into the
capacity of a given cross­section. The capacity cannot be greater than the one found by
the lower bound solution and design should comply with the Eurocode. These methods
are essentially used to assess the results of the mechanical model.
Within the standard [10] a cross­section with links placed around the transverse longitudi­
nal reinforcement is presented, illustrated in Figure 3.1. This suggests that it is acceptable
to encircle only the inner bar. However, no underlying research papers or experimental
tests have been found to support this orientation by the UK standard.
Figure 3.1: Shear reinforcement detailing in Standard Method of Detailing Structural Con­
crete [10]
3.1
Lower bound solution
The lower bound solution also known as the variable strut inclination method or diagonal
compression field theory will be presented in the following, described in [2, 3, 16]. The
method determines the shear strength of a reinforced cross­section based on the plastic
lower bound theorem. It assumes a concrete tensile strength equal to zero, uses a plane
strain failure criterion, and considers the stirrups to be closely spaced. As the tensile
strength of concrete is neglected, the shear stresses can only be resisted as an inclined
compression chord.
The reinforcement is represented as compressive­ and tensile stringers and in between
lies the shear zones illustrated in Figure 3.2, where the loads are transferred to the shear
zones through some imaginary diaphragms.

4
Analysis of experimental data
To get a true understanding of the effectiveness of the reinforcement configuration, specifi­
cally, stirrups placed along the longitudinal reinforcement experimental testing is required.
To the knowledge of the author, no papers or experimental testing for this orientation of
reinforcement have been published. However, to get an insight into the influence of non­
optimal anchorage conditions on the ultimate load­bearing capacity the study [5, 6] is
examined. The experimental studies investigate how various shapes and placements of
shear reinforcement impact the shear failure and crack patterns of reinforced concrete
beams. The study involves beams with closed stirrups, u­stirrups, shear studs, shear
studs with transverse bars, and beams reinforced with a combination of stirrups and shear
studs. This report will only be dealing with the cross­sections presented on Figure 4.1 with
uniform shear reinforcement. While the reinforcement configuration in the experiments
may differ from this study, valuable comparative data and insight are obtained.
Figure 4.1: Cross sections of experiments. (1) Closed stirrups; (2) U­stirrups; (3) Shear
studs placed center; (4) Shear studs placed near the top; (5) Shear studs placed near the
bottom; (6) Shear studs with transverse bars.
Through strain gauge measurements mounted on the shear reinforcement, the study has
been able to determine whether and when yielding has occurred. Additionally, a three­
dimensional crack pattern at failure was drawn up by the usage of a combination of digital
image correlation and multiple cutouts of the beam. The DIC analysis estimates the sur­
face crack width in the longitudinal direction, while the cut­outs reveal the cracks within
each cross­section.

4.2
Digital Image Correlation Analysis
In the following, experimental observations obtained with the use of 2D digital image cor­
relation (DIC) are presented for the beams reinforced with closed stirrups and shear studs,
cases 1 and 3 respectively. Through the extraction of crack width data, a relation with the
strain measurements at a given load is obtained for these experiments. The analysis
was performed by the use of the software ZEISS INSPECT Correlate. All of the data is
presented within Appendix A as animations.
The images are considered to be of high quality, with a resolution of 4496 x 3000 pixels.
To establish a relation between measured strain and crack opening a number of measure­
ment points are required. This becomes somewhat difficult due to the unclear placement
of the strain gauges on the reinforcement. It is therefore assumed that a strain gauge is
placed at the center of the reinforcement and the two others are positioned 100 mm above
and below this point. This placement of the strain gauges is deemed reasonable due to
the height of the reinforcement being 350 mm. The placement is indicated on Figure 4.6
and Figure 4.7.
From the DIC analysis and Figure 4.4, a smeared cracking pattern of the reinforced beams
is observed, where multiple cracks form in parallel with the primary crack. Additionally,
small minor cracks emerge from the primary crack, and a short horizontal yield line be­
comes visible running along the bending reinforcement and extending to the support.

5
Mechanical model
In this chapter, a mechanical model for the shear capacity of reinforced concrete beams is
presented. The model is established using the upper bound theorem and observed shear
failure mechanisms of normal­reinforced beams with close stirrups presented in chapter 4
and other studies [7]. The failure mechanism is characterized by an incline yield line going
from the tensile reinforcement to the compression zone and from the tip of this crack to
the applied load. Additionally, a horizontal yield line develops along the reinforcement
located within the tension zone and connects with the incline yield line.
The model utilizes an optimization algorithm with several optimization parameters and
determines the magnitude of the optimization parameters resulting in the lowest required
dissipation of energy to achieve failure. Discrete changes in the capacity take place due
to the crossing of stirrups as the horizontal projection increases. To minimize the risk of
identifying local minima caused by discrete changes, the capacity is calculated along the
entire shear span by iterating over the horizontal projection of the inclined yield line to
define the critical yield line.
The chapter showcases various stages of the model, each version building on the previous
one and progressively increasing in complexity by incorporating additional effects. Two
examples are shown in Appendix B to illustrate the effect of dowel action and dowel­
activated stirrups as well as the final model.
The first iteration of the model considers a yield line idealized as a straight inclined and
horizontal yield. Dowel action is implemented, as this is deemed one of the governing
effects for the reinforcement configuration of interest. When the shear reinforcement is
placed along the longitudinal reinforcement instead of encircling, the connection between
the transverse and longitudinal bars is typically specified as welded together. By mobiliz­
ing the longitudinal reinforcement an activation of the stirrups occurs even though they are
not crossed by the inclined yield line. The contribution from dowel action and the shear
reinforcement activated due to dowel action is explored.
The displacement vector is introduced, which allows the two rigid bodies to move diago­
nally away from each other. Furthermore, the orientation of the displacement vector also
influences the stress state of the longitudinal reinforcement as it can now be in tension.
Lastly, a segmentation of the inclined yield line is proposed creating a bi­linear yield line.
The influence of the shear reinforcement height now becomes evident, as the yield line
can transverse over the stirrups effectively negating their contribution. This highlights an­
other main difference between traditional reinforcement placement and the configuration
of interest as the stirrups are shorter and placed on the inside of the bending reinforce­
ment. Dowel action within the compression zone is implemented based on the geometry
of the yield line and the influence of flexural cracks is taken into account to reduce the
dissipation of energy from concrete inspired by the crack sliding theory.

5.1
Failure mechanism
The upper bound theorem rests upon a geometrically possible failure mechanism that sat­
isfies the boundary conditions. The yield line is expected to develop through the weakest
regions of the element, i.e. the yield line must be associated with a geometry that requires
the least amount of work to achieve failure. The observed failure mechanisms reveal a
critical shear crack characterized by an inclined and horizontal yield line. Both the hori­
zontal projection of the inclined yield line and the length of the horizontal yield line vary
among observed failure patterns.
The critical yield line is defined as the geometry of the incline­ and horizontal yield line
resulting in the lowest required dissipation of energy to achieve failure. The contribution
from the inclined yield line is determined by the dissipation in concrete and the number
of crossed stirrup legs, while the contribution of the horizontal yield line is governed by
dowel action.
It is especially from the contribution of dowel action that a significant difference is seen
concerning whether the longitudinal reinforcement is enclosed by stirrups or not. If the
stirrups are placed along the reinforcement a failure mechanism similar to what is shown
in Figure 5.1 is assumed, where the longitudinal reinforcement is mobilized based on the
length of the horizontal yield line.
Figure 5.1: Failure mechanism with no connection between transverse­ and longitudinal
reinforcement.
However, the longitudinal and transverse reinforcement are typically prescribed as welded
together. The deformation of the reinforcement is therefore restricted by the stirrups as
shown in Figure 5.2 resulting in multiple plastic hinges along the dowel. This situation is
therefore associated with activation of the shear reinforcement by mobilizing the dowel,
which significantly increases the capacity. The capacity is both increased due to an acti­
vation of the stirrups, but the dowel action contribution it self is also amplified as it depends

in section 3.1 and section 3.2, respectively.

the concrete cover. Other geometric properties such as height, h, and slenderness, a/d,

6.1
Accuracy of model
The proposed model is applied to experimental results collected within a database for
shear tests on slender reinforced beams with stirrups [13] and the experimental studies
performed by Cladera [14] and López [15]. Some tests from the database have been
excluded, as access to some of the original research papers was not possible and would
require a number of assumptions to perform the analysis. Furthermore, the database also
contains tests of T­beams all of which have been removed from the analysis. In total 71
beams are tested from the database.
Cladera investigated the influence of concrete compressive strength for un­reinforced,
minimum­reinforced, and beams with a high amount of shear reinforcement. López per­
formed a similar study but with varying amounts of longitudinal reinforcement in the com­
pressive and tension zones. By adding the normal shear­reinforced beams to the analysis
a total of 85 beams are tested. All of the beams are reinforced with stirrups encircling the
reinforcement.

8
Conclusion & Future Work
This thesis aimed to determine the one­way shear capacity of shear­reinforced bridge
slabs, where the reinforcement is placed along the bending reinforcement instead of en­
circling it, due to ongoing discussion regarding the effectiveness of the reinforcement.
A mechanical model has been presented resting upon observed shear failure mecha­
nisms for normally reinforced beams characterized by a smeared cracking pattern. The
observed failure mechanisms were utilized to define a yield line consisting of an incline
bi­linear yield line and a horizontal yield line. By utilizing the upper­bound theorem within
the theory of plasticity, the shear capacity was established, consisting of contributions due
to dissipated energy in concrete, from stirrups and the longitudinal reinforcement. An ef­
fectiveness factor from the crack sliding theory excluding the term associated with dowel
action was proposed to model the dissipation in concrete. The model adopts optimization
parameters to determine the effect of dowel­activated stirrups and accounts for an inclined
displacement vector and its influence on the stress state of the longitudinal reinforcement.
The effect of dowel­activated stirrups and the shear reinforcement height were considered
the governing factors, distinguishing the reinforcement configuration from a traditional ori­
entation of the stirrups. To validate the model a comparison between the measured failure
loads for several experimental tests and corresponding results of the model was made.
The model tends to underestimate the capacity with a mean value of 1.21 for the normal
reinforced beams (1.45 if including the lightly shear reinforced) and through a parametric
study, it was concluded that good agreement between the model and measured failure
loads was achieved for greater shear­ and compression reinforcement degrees. By us­
ing the model as a benchmark, a comparative analysis was conducted to determine the
difference in capacity for a traditional orientation of the stirrups against the reinforcement
configuration of interest. The analysis was based on a typical slab design with adjust­
ments of specific parameter resulting in a total of 47 imaginary slabs for each orientation.
From this analysis, an average reduction of 17% in capacity was identified when opting to
place the stirrups along the bending reinforcement.
Although a good agreement was observed between experimental failure loads for greater
shear­ and compression reinforcement degrees, this study is purely analytical, and ex­
perimental testing is required to effectively determine the shear capacity and the effec­
tiveness of the reinforcement. Furthermore, the model builds upon the assumption, that
the weld between the transverse reinforcement located in the corner of the stirrup and the
longitudinal reinforcement can fully activate the stirrup due to deformation of the longitu­
dinal reinforcement. Based on Eurocode guidelines an investigation should be made to
determine if the stirrup can be activated fully or possibly what is required to achieve full
activation of the stirrup. In some cases, no deformation of some plastic hinges occurs, but
they still restrict the propagation of the yield line. For this scenario, it could be interesting
to investigate if a weld stronger than the stirrup is required.

B.1
Example 1: Influence of Dowel action and dowel
activated stirrups
The effect of dowel action, dowel­activated stirrups, and the returned displacements from
the optimization algorithm are displayed in the following. Beams with similar properties
and cross­sections to the beams from the experiments in chapter 4 are utilized for the
following examples.
In Figure B.1 the dissipated energy for each contribution is displayed as well as the corre­
sponding displacements as a function of the horizontal projection of the inclined yield line.
A transition from dowel action to stirrup activation is observed when the distance when the
incline yield line approaches a stirrup. When this transition point is met a gradual increase
of displacement takes place until the incline yield line intersects the activated stirrup. In
the figure, a sudden drop of the relative displacement is shown when the yield line sur­
passes a stirrup. This is because it essentially transitions to the contribution associated
with energy dissipated due to the yield line crossing the stirrups.
If the stirrups are placed closer together or the diameter of the stirrups was reduced, multi­
ple stirrups could be activated at once. On the contrary, if the dimension of the longitudinal
reinforcement was reduced, the transition would occur later.
