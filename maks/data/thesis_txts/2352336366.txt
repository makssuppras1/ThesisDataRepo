Summary
The DTU Wind Energy department is involved in ”Single Tree Experiment” whose
main goal is to investigate on methods to include contributions of single trees and
smaller forest patches into the current state-of-art wind flow modeling tools. As part
of the experiment, the wind field around a single tree in DTU Risø Campus will be
monitored in detail. For numerical simulation purposes, a 3-dimensional model of the
tree is required. A laser scan of the tree was performed from multiple angles producing
a point cloud dataset of 90000 samples. This project investigates the possibility of
setting up a processing pipeline for extracting an accurate 3-dimensional tree model
from the acquired point cloud.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Wind Energy is becoming a significant part of energy production scene of our society;
More and more wind farm projects are being deployed. The productivity of these
projects is directly affected by the profile of the local landscape. It is known than
single trees can significantly reduce wind speed, however the exact reduction given a
certain tree structure has not yet been quantified. The DTU Wind Energy department
is involved in the ”Single Tree Experiment” whose main goal is to investigate on
methods to include contributions of single trees and smaller forest patches into the
current state-of-art wind flow modeling tools.
As part of that experiment the wind field around a single tree in DTU Risø
Campus will be monitored in detail. Afterwards wind models of different complexity
will be investigated with the goal to reproduce the observed data. In order to do so,
a 3-dimensional (3D) model of the single tree under investigation is required.
1.2
Project Objective
The DTU Wind Energy department is in the fortunate position to possess a total
station (an integration of an electronic theodolite and a laser rangefinder). A laser
scan of the tree (Figure 1.1) was performed from multiple angles. The outcome was
a point cloud dataset of 90000 surface samples in a local 3D Cartesian coordinate
system.
The main objective of this project is to extract an accurate 3D shape representa-
tion of the tree from the acquired point cloud. The produced 3D model will be used
in Computational Fluid Dynamics (CFD) experiments that will try to reproduce the
observed wind field data.
The intent of the project is to compare different techniques for extracting an
accurate 3D representation of the tree shape. The main approach will be the following:
The point cloud will be pre-processed and an effort will be made to extract a skeletal
structure of the tree. This structure will be hopefully used as a guide for guiding the
shape reconstruction.
As a baseline for evaluating the acquired results, a 3-dimensional tree model will be
extracted using the well-known method of Poisson Surface Reconstruction [KBH06].

CHAPTER 2
Basic Processing
Pipeline
2.1
Data acquisition
The initial point cloud dataset was collected using a Leica TotalStation. The spe-
cific survey instrument combines a electronic theodolite and an Electronic Distance
Measurement.
Prior to the actual data acquisition, four field points were marked with poles at
a distance from the scanned tree. Using the theodolite sub-component of the instru-
ment, a frame of reference was established. During the initial calibration, the position
of the instrument was set as the origin. By calculating the horizontal angle, vertical
angle and distance to the initial position of the instrument, cartesian coordinates
could be assigned to the control points on the poles. As a consequence, whenever the
instrument was moved to a different observing position, by measuring the angles and
distances to the control points, the instrument could re-calibrate itself and become
aware of its new position. This made possible the automatic application of translation
transformations on the next scans with respect to the fist one. A total of five scans
were performed from different viewing angles. Three of them were performed from a
distance of 20 meters to have a good overview of the target tree and the remaining
two were done close to the base of the trunk to capture the structure of the internal
branches.
2.2
Preprocessing
2.2.1
Cropping & Outlier removal
Before applying any method for shape reconstruction, a basic pre-processing of the
raw scan was required. The scan contained additional features such as ground surfaces,
background objects. These had to be removed manually using point cloud processing
software.
As a second step, a outlier removal filter was applied. The applied filter is part

of the Computational Geometry Algorithms Library (CGAL) and its principle of

2.2.2
Surface Normal Estimation
A number of surface reconstruction techniques require estimates of the surface normal
on the sampled points. One of the most known estimation techniques is to perform
a Principal Component Analysis (PCA) on the neighborhood of each point for which
an estimate of the surface normal is required. Since the neighborhood of the point
represent a surface, most variance will be across two dimensions. The direction of
least variance (corresponding to the eigenvector of the smallest eigenvalue) will be
the surface normal.
A drawback of this method is that the surface normals are not consistently ori-
ented. In [Hop+92], a method is proposed for consistently orienting normals based
on a Riemannian graph representing proximity relations between points. The edge
between point i and j of the graph is weighted according to 1 −| ˆni · ˆnj| where ˆni, ˆnj
are the respective normals. As a result, points that lie on planes that are more or less
aligned, they will be closer on the graph. The direction of the normal is then prop-
agated through the Minimum Spanning Tree of the Riemannian graph. Whenever
normals are disoriented more than 90 degrees ( ˆni · ˆnj < 0), the normal orientation is
flipped.

Skeleton Extraction
As part of this project, a major pre-processing step was the extraction of the skeletal
structure of the point cloud.
The target was to extract a tree structure (in the
mathematical sense) to use as a guide for reconstructing the 3-dimensional shape of
the tree. This step will be further analyzed in Chapter 4.

3.1
Method Overview
The Poisson surface reconstruction method [KBH06] tries to produce an indicator
function Φ that has a value of 0 on the surface and 1 outside of it. Its main observation
is that the gradient of the indicator function Φ tracks the surface normal. The gradient
is zero everywhere except the surface points, where it becomes unbounded. Because
the gradient in general is not a reversible operator, solving the variational problem
minΦ∥∇Φ−⃗N∥is of no practical use. The problem is solved in a least square sense by
equating the divergence of the gradient field to the divergence of the surface normal
which formulates the following Poisson problem:
∇2Φ −∇· ⃗N = 0
(3.1)
Having a solution for Φ, an isovalue defining the surface has to be selected. The
authors of the of the method [KBH06] select the average value of Φ across the initial
surface samples.
3.2
Sensitivity to the Normal Estimation Method
As apparent from the previous section, the main input of the method are estimates
of the surface normal at the sample points. These were not available in the original
scan and had to be estimated using the PCA-based method described in Section 2.2.2.
The single control parameter is the size of the neighborhood used in the PCA. It is
expected that as the neighborhood size increases, more smoothing will be introduced
during the reconstruction process. Using MeshLab, the surface normal estimation
was performed and the result was fed into the Poisson reconstruction routine. The
results are presented in Figures 3.1(a) to 3.1(c).

CHAPTER 4
Skeleton Extraction
4.1
Motivation
The Poisson surface reconstruction method (in the context of the point cloud un-
der consideration) exhibits some limitations in its ability to reconstruct features of
fine detail. Although the general structure and the main branches of the tree are
successfully represented, the smaller final branches are not captured.
The acquired results motivate a backtrack in the reconstruction pipeline. Instead
of directly reconstruct the underlying tree volume, it makes sense to try to extract a
simpler skeletal structure that could used as a guide for a finer reconstruction. The
main inspirational sources for for this reconstruction pipeline stage are the methods
described in [Hua+09] and [Hua+13].
4.2
WLOP operator
In [Hua+09], the authors describe the Weighted Locally Optimal Projection (WLOP)
operator. It is a improved version of the Locally Optimal Projection (LOP) operator
proposed by [Lip+07]. The final method re-projects the original data points towards
the local L1 median (as defined by a support radius) but an additional repulsion
force is modeled to avoid projected points getting clustered into a local centroid. The
authors of [Hua+09] modified the original LOP operator to use a linear repulsion
factor (with respect to distance between points) and augment it with an adaptive
weighting scheme based on local point density.
The WLOP operator was applied on the dataset and the outcome is shown in
Figures 4.1(a) to 4.1(c). The operator manages to capture the center lines of medium
and small branches, but the contraction in the trunk area is not enough to capture
the center line. One could increase the radius of the supporting neighborhood but
then there is the risk that smaller branches would get merged together. An example
is given in Figures 4.2(a) and 4.2(b) where the support radius was increased by a
factor of two. Although the center line of the trunk is now clearly captured, branches
got merged.

4.3
L1-Medial skeleton
Some of the authors of [Hua+09], developed an incremental algorithm [Hua+13] to
extract a skeleton based on the WLOP operator. The presented algorithm resembles
to an evolutionary algorithm. After a WLOP contraction, the algorithm tries to trace
branches using a co-linearity criterion for selecting points. Points that are already
branch members are considered skeletal points. When no more points can be added
to branches, the algorithm will perform a WLOP projection of the remaining points
with a larger support radius. The remaining non skeletal points will be contracted
and possibly more branches can now be formed. As the algorithm progresses, smaller
neighboring branches are getting connected into larger structures that will eventually
form a skeletal graph.
The method has been applied to the tree point cloud with mixed results. The
algorithm manages to track features of different scale, but not the thin final branches.
The graph is not strongly connected and the algorithm has problems separating two
parallel branches leading to the formation of loops (as depicted in Figures 4.3(a)
to 4.3(c)).

4.4
Approximating Geodesics
The previous methods rely on local neighborhoods defined by the euclidean distance,
therefore no distinction can be made between points belonging to different features.
Points that belong to different features could potentially skew the WLOP reprojection
of the skeletal points towards a region that is unrelated to the intrinsic feature they
belong to. In the specific case of a tree point cloud, one possibility to eliminate such
effects is to restrict the neighbor search using the local tree branch radius.
As suggested by Mr J.A. Baertzen, given a tree point p, the perimeter of the branch
it belongs to could be estimated by performing a Dijkstra shortest path search and
tracking the number of vertices being discovered (added in the priority queue). A
Dijkstra search with p as a seed point will start expand a ”disc-like” area around p
containing known vertices. The vertices that are inserted in the queue have just been
discovered and lie in the perimeter of the disc. Their distance from the source p is
approximately the radius of the ”expanding disc”. The queue length is the size of the
disc perimeter given as a number of vertices. This ”Dijkstra front” is growing as the
search advances along the branch surface. However as the disc starts wrapping the
branch, it will overlap itself and start forming a cylinder. At this moment, the size of
the front will have a small dip and it is indication that the tree branch got wrapped.

4.5
Skeleton estimation using K-means clustering
An alternative approach on extracting the skeleton was also tried that was based on
the K-Means clustering algorithm. The extraction pipeline was heavily inspired on
the ideas presented in [Yan+09].
The authors applied the ideas presented in [CAD04] for segmenting triangular
meshes to the case of point clouds. The main difference is that they replaced the
mesh connectivity information by the neighborhood information of the point-cloud
discovered by a Kd-Tree structure. The authors modified the classic K-means iter-
ation scheme, by reversing in a sense the proximity search when assigning cluster
identifiers. Whereas in the classic K-means algorithm each point gets assigned to
the closest cluster seed, in this modified version the iteration is happening over the
seeds. The neighbors of each seed are tagged with the seed’s identifier and pushed
in a priority queue based on the distance from the seed that discovered them. As
the tagged points are getting popped from the queue, their cluster identification is
finalized and in their turn are tagging and pushing their closest neighbors. This pro-
cedure is continued until the queue becomes empty. The iterative refinement of the
clusters is performed similarly to the classic K-Means scheme with the difference that
the new seed points are not the clusters centroids. For each computed centroid, the

K-Means combined with Simulated Annealing
Using a ”brute-force approach”, a Simulated Annealing scheme was implemented
based on the branching strategy presented in Section 4.5.
In a Simulated Annealing scheme, an artificial temperature T and an energy func-
tion E of a system are defined. As the scheme starts to iterate from a system state and
a high temperature T = 1, neighboring states are tested for less energy. For each dis-
covered neighboring state an acceptance probability is defined as Pacc = e−(En−Ec)/T
where En is the energy of the neighboring state, Ec is the current state’s energy and
T is the temperature. The acceptance probability has the property that is always
larger than 1.0 when the neighbor state’s energy is less than the current one, so a
better state is always accepted. A worse state can be accepted with probability Pacc,
but this probability decreases as the system virtually cools down on every iteration
(Tk+1 = Tk(1 −ac) where ac is a cooling factor). This allows the system while still

the non-empty bins defines the ”isotropy” of the cluster.
The more isotropic a cluster is, the more chances it has to get split than merged.

CHAPTER 5
Shape Reconstruction
5.1
The SQM inverse skeletonization method
The goal for this stage of the pipeline was to use the Skeleton to Quad-dominant
polygonal Mesh (SQM) method described in [BMW12].
The described algorithm
receives as input a skeletal graph of a 3D object where each node represents a articu-
lation sphere of different radius. The method then drapes the skeletal structure using
polar and annular patches in order to create a 3D object. A subdivision scheme is
then applied to smooth the generated mesh.
5.2
Collapse of the proposed reconstruction pipeline
The target of the proposed pipeline was to feed the SQM method with a skeletal
representation of the tree (produced by one of the methods examined in Chapter 4)
combined with the radius estimates produced by the method described in Section 4.4.
The produced skeletons had severe flows (as shown in Figure 5.1) and proceeding
with a reconstruction was not realistic1. In the WLOP-based skeleton, the skeletal
points form a small cloud and no articulation points are clearly defined. In the case of
the L1-medial skeleton, features are missing and a artificial loop is introduced. The
skeleton based on flooding K-means clusters captures the small branches, but fails
to follow the curvature of the trunk. The skeleton based on simple K-means clusters
even after a lot of post-processing can not adequately trace the baseline of the trunk.

CHAPTER 6
Results & Future work
6.1
Outcome
The desired outcome of the project was a reconstruction pipeline with 3 stages:
1. A pre-processing stage for cleaning up and estimating characteristics of the raw
input cloud.
2. A skeleton extraction stage.
3. A shape reconstruction stage.
The first stage used well-known techniques and was successfully implemented. The
stage removed outliers and generated estimates of the surface normal and the branch
radii. The implementation of the second stage failed as it didn’t provide usable results.
An implementation of the third stage was not attempted, since no usable inputs were
available.
6.2
On branch radius estimation
The method for estimating the branch radius described in Section 4.4, had some good
relative results. The generated estimates were concise with the scale of the branches.
The method however is not robust on junction areas where it overestimated the local
branch radius. As it is based on detecting the first subtle peak in a noisy dataset, it
has some accuracy issues. Given its performance, the method can only be used as an
indicator for the relative scale of branches downstream in the processing pipeline.
6.3
On skeletonzation
The main challenge was the multi-scale nature of trees. All the tested approaches for
this stage were dependent either directly or indirectly to some clustering or proxim-
ity search scheme. The goal was to group points belonging to the same branch into
clusters that would resemble to cross-cuts or cylindrical sections. The associated cen-
troids could then trace the baselines of the different branches. The multi-scale nature
of the tree shape, expressed mainly through the variance of the branch radius, posed
==PAGE:44==
34
6 Results & Future work
challenges to the skeletonization methods. Although some of them succeeded to cap-
ture correctly the structure of smaller branches, they failed to track the trunk baseline.
The underlying clustering schemes were forming patches on the trunk surface and not
cross-sections. The proximity searches were done using Nearest-Neighbors queries on
Kd-Tree structures. In the case of branches with small radius, given a a query point
p, the points across a diameter and the points in the surface patch around p are
in similar distances and associations are made leading to the creation of cylindrical
clusters. However in the case of the trunk, the points across a diameter are at a
greater distance than those of the local surface patch. As a consequence, the clusters
take the form of surface patches, whose centroids are not getting pulled towards the
center-line.
An alternative approach could be to use distance based proximity searches where
the threshold could be adapted based on the radius estimates provided by the method
described in Section 4.4, but new challenges are introduced. Given that the initial seed
points are located on the surface itself, the distance threshold should be twice the local
branch radius in order to catch points across the diameter. However this means that
points from neighboring branches can be potentially discovered. A possible solution
could be to use the technique described in [Hop+92] to consistently orientate the
normals giving a inwards/outwards direction allowing to filter out points that lie
outside the branch.
The technique of flooding proposed in [Yan+09] and tested in Section 4.5, tried to
inverse the direction of proximity searches. By providing an initial guess, each seed
was searching for its neighbors and was getting refined. The method seemed to be
able to create consistent cylindrical clusters but failed to detect progressive changes in
feature scale. For example in Figure 4.10(a), starting at the root of the tree the trunk
is nicely clustered, but as we move towards the crown, the algorithm fails to detect the
reduction of radius and slight change of direction. This leads to the secondary major
branches being classified as part of the trunk. The idea of inverting the neighbor
search is interesting but in some degree a circular dependency is created since a good
initial guess of the skeleton is needed. A possible bootstrapping approach could be the
following: Initially, a inwards radius search (based on the oriented surface normal) is
performed and reflected back (Figure 6.1). The collected points would form roughly
a cylindrical section. The associated centroid could be used as an initial seed point.
In Section 4.5.2, a Simulated Annealing scheme was tested for clustering branches.
Different setups were tested, but none of them succeeded to create a reasonable cluster
configuration. The search space was explored by slitting and merging clusters. The
energy under minimization was the mean ”cylindricity” as defined in Figure 6.1. The
intent was that the scheme would use a top-down approach. It would start with a
small number of clusters (containing multiple branches) that were to be split until
each cluster contained a single branch.
The merging functionality, controlled by
the isotropy measure, would fix cases where the splitting went too far. During the
executions, it became apparent that the ”cylindricity” as an energy measurement was
not adequate. The compactness of the clusters was not taken under consideration and
resulted in configurations where the clusters were macroscopically cylindrical but not

6.4
On Surface Reconstruction
Given the failed skeletonization results, the SQM method could not be assessed. The
Poisson reconstruction method, gave reasonable results, but could not resolve small
final branches, which was the motivation for the skeletonization approach. As already
mentioned, the method requires a high density point dataset. This was the was the
case for the trunk whose scan was dense and done from overlapping viewpoints. On
the other hand, the crown scan was not complete due to existing foliage at the time
of the scan and the fact that a top-down scan was not possible.
6.5
Further Work & Alternative approaches
For the case of the Simulated Annealing scheme, a stricter definition of the system en-
ergy function has to be defined. As mentioned previously, the compactness of clusters
is not taken into consideration. Currently during cluster merging, cylindrical but not
continuous clusters that span multiple branches are accepted. These configurations
should be penalized.
==PAGE:46==
36
6 Results & Future work
A possible extension in the meta-heuristic based approach is to switch to a Genetic
Algorithm scheme where multiple solutions (in the form of cluster configurations) are
mutated and recombined to a better set of solutions. However, a efficient energy/fit-
ness function is still needed.
In [TZC09], a method is described using planar cross-cuts to estimate skeletal
points that could be investigated. The method uses surface normal information to
compute planar cuts that are perpendicular to the underlying skeleton and a Maha-
lanobis distance (based on distance and surface normal orientation) to filter relevant
points within the planar cut. A skeletal point is then computed that minimizes the
sum of squared distances to the line extensions of the surface normal on the points
included in the cross-cut.
6.6
Final thoughts
As apparent in multiple stages of this project, the prominent challenge was the multi-
scale nature of the point cloud dataset. It generated significant adaptability require-
ments toward the applied algorithms that were not satisfied during this project. Al-
though the dataset has a clearly visible structure, its complexity makes it challenging
to exploit.
For the discrete optimization problem of splitting into clusters the different branches,
no optimal solution could be clearly defined which lead to the use of meta-heuristics.
The main assumption that a branch has a cylindrical shape was weakly material-
ized in the ”cylindricity” and ”isotropy” metrics and failed to guide efficiently the
exploration of the search space.
Retrospectively assessing the project flow, a incremental approach could have been
a better choice. The work-flow of the project was focused around the specific dataset.
The properties of dataset was generally unknown and the feedback given from the ap-
plied methods were generally interpreted based on common sense. A better approach
could be to work fist on small synthetic sub-cases with known characteristics. Uti-
lizing the setup described in [BMW12], a artificial tree structure could be generated
using L-systems ([PL97]) and meshed using the SQM method. The generated mesh
could then be point-sampled. The acquired point clouds would have predefined char-
acteristics and a better quantification of the reconstruction results would be possible.
The pipeline could be tested in cases of increasing complexity and when results were
satisfactory, an application on the real dataset could be performed.
In conclusion, the project had multiple challenging aspects that allow experimenta-
tion with different approaches. Although the project did not meet its goals, hopefully
its partial results would be useful to the ”Single Tree Experiment”.
