Project Formulation 
Silicone elastomers have been found to be a promising material for dielectric elastomer 
actuators (DEAs) due to their favorable viscoelastic properties, good weatherability, fast 
response rate and biocompatibility. These lightweight flexible actuators could find numerous 
applications in the biomedical or consumer electronics fields, but while commercially available 
silicones exhibit numerous favorable properties, high driving voltages and limited achievable 
strain still restrain commercial viability.  
This project involves addressing the limited achievable strain by increasing the electrical 
breakdown strength of the silicone elastomers through the incorporation of covalently bonded 
aromatic voltage stabilizers while striving to minimize any loss of desirable mechanical 
properties. The undesired phase separation of the aromatic moieties that counteracts the voltage 
stabilizing effect could be minimized by a wider distribution of the aromatic groups which 
would be achieved through the synthetical route chosen to covalently incorporate the aromatic 
moieties, that is by copolymerizing single functionalized polysiloxane units with 
polydimethylsiloxane spacers creating an alternating copolymer where each functional group 
is separated by an equal distance from each other within the polymer chain.  
Additionally, the possibility to offset the characteristic stiffening effect of aromatic side-groups 
by increasing the distance between the functional aromatic side-groups and the polysiloxane 
backbone chain with flexible alkyl chains would be explored.  
These functional copolymers would then be cross-linked into elastomeric films and 
characterized by rheological, tensile, electrical breakdown, and dielectric testing to evaluate 
the effect of the different distributions of aromatic voltage stabilizers.

Abstract 
Functionalized polysiloxane copolymers with covalently bonded aromatic groups were 
synthesized by utilizing the Piers-Rubinsztajn reaction to induce the polycondensation of 
phenyl functional silanes and polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) spacers to study the voltage 
stabilizing effect of aromatic functional groups within silicone elastomers. A well-defined 
spatial distribution of phenyl functionalities was achieved within the copolymers and 
aromatic compound concentrations of 7.2 and 12.3 mol g-1 were attained by altering the 
length of the PDMS spacers. The molecular weight of the copolymers was controlled by 
altering the stoichiometry of the precursors with a weight average molecular weight of 
around 25 000 g mol-1 being chosen to produce elastomeric films. Both phenyl and 3-
phenylpropyl functional copolymers were produced to explore the effect of flexible alkyl 
chains between the aromatic functionalities and polysiloxane backbone chains. 
The produced copolymers were end-capped with allyldimethylsilane (with a conversion of > 
90%) to facilitate platinum catalyzed cross-linking with an 8-functional hydride functional 
cross-linker. Rheological analysis of the produced elastomeric films confirmed that sufficient 
cross-linking had occurred. No discernable increase in electrical breakdown strength was 
observed for the cross-linked films which is assumed to be caused by defects imparted to the 
films during processing. Inconsistencies between the film samples due to the presence of air 
bubbles and rough grooves on the film surfaces also caused difficulties in comparing the 
stiffness of phenyl and 3-phenylpropyl functional films meaning that the effect of the alkyl 
chains could not be properly assessed. Further optimization of the film casting process is 
required to draw more concrete conclusions. 
The samples showed increased stiffness when compared to the reference film indicating that 
the desirable plasticizing effect was not obtained. The relative permittivity of the functional 
films ranged from 3.2 to 4.1 compared to the 2.7 of the reference film thus demonstrating a 
moderate improvement. Furthermore, the films were all non-conductive.

1.  Introduction 
Dielectric elastomer actuators (DEA-s) have shown promise as flexible, light-weight linear 
actuators for numerous novel applications where conventional electrical motors that create 
rotational movement have not been suitable. These applications can range from artificial 
muscles in the biomedical field to flat speakers and miniature actuators for camera lenses in 
electronics.[1, 2] Elastomers are known for their rubber-like property to endure great strains 
without permanent deformation and DEA-s utilize that ability along with the electroactive 
properties of dielectric elastomeric materials, that is the ability to transduce electrical energy 
into actuation.  
Dielectric materials do not conduct electricity but can be polarized when placed in an electric 
field. This polarization causes positive charges to be displaced in the direction of the electric 
field and negative charges in the opposite direction. Subjecting a dielectric elastomer to voltage 
causes it to contract in the direction of the created electrical field due to the migration of 
charges, which in turn results in the material expanding in the transverse direction due to the 
incompressible nature of elastomeric materials as seen in Figure 1. This transverse expansion 
is the basis of the actuation of these modules.  
 
Figure 1 Operating principle of a DEA. A) dielectric elastomer sandwiched between two compliant 
electrodes. B) In the presence of an electric field the dielectric material is polarized which in turn causes 
the material to contract in the direction of the field and expand in the transverse direction.  
The linear actuating properties of these materials have drawn increased interest amongst 
researchers, but high driving voltages and limited achievable actuation strain have currently 
constrained the commercial viability for devices based on this technology. It is generally 
assumed that the maximum actuation strain before failure is dictated by electrical breakdown 
which occurs at a lower strain than the mechanical breakdown.[3] Thus, an increased electrical 
breakdown strength is necessary to increase the maximum achievable actuation strain.  
Previous research has shown that the inclusion of aromatic groups within silicone elastomers 
as chemically bonded side-groups on the polysiloxane chains can increase the electrical 
==PAGE:11==
3 
 
breakdown strength by stabilizing the charges forming within material under working 
conditions.[3] The voltage stabilization is achieved by the electron trapping effect of the 
aromatic rings. When the colliding electrons disturb the π-electron clouds of the aromatic rings 
they form a more stable anion radicals mitigating the accumulation of negative charges that 
can lead to electrical failure.[4] This increase in electrical breakdown strength is however 
counteracted by the phase separation occurring between immiscible phenyl rich and non-
functionalized PDMS domains when concentrations of aromatic functional groups are 
increased. A larger degree of control over the distribution of the aromatic functional groups 
could be a possible solution to minimize the occurrence of phase separation at a given 
concentration of aromatic groups. This can be achieved by copolymerizing single 
functionalized siloxane units with PDMS spacers to create an even distribution of aromatic 
side-groups. 
Given that aromatic side-groups are known to inhibit the flexibility of polymeric chains thus 
resulting in stiffer materials, it can be stipulated that the stiffening effect could be minimized 
by increasing the length between the polymeric backbone and aromatic functional groups by 
introducing the functionalities as longer side-groups connected with flexible alkyl chains as 
seen in Figure 2. Furthermore, the longer phenyl-functional branching points could create more 
free volume between the polymeric chains therefore producing a softening effect. 
 
Figure 2 Aromatic side-groups directly connected to the backbone polysiloxane chain (left) and longer 
side-groups with flexible alkyl chains (right) 
In summary, the goal of this project is to explore the impact that the distribution of the 
functional groups and the distance between chemically bonded aromatic groups and 
polysiloxane backbone has on the dielectric and mechanical properties of the final elastomeric 
material by producing novel phenyl-functional copolymers with a varying alkyl separator 
length (0 vs. 3 units). The general experimental route can be seen in Figure 3. A more detailed 
discussion of this pathway will be presented in subsequent sections.

2. Background 
2.1. Dielectric Elastomer Transducers 
Dielectric elastomers can be suitable materials for numerous applications due to their 
electroactive properties as either lightweight flexible sensors, linear actuators, or even electric 
generators. While the general term dielectric elastomer transducer (DET) encompasses all three 
of the aforementioned application groups, it is due to the increased interest in the actuating 
properties that the more specific term, dielectric elastomer actuator (DEA), is commonly used. 
A simple representation of an actuator can be seen in Figure 4 where it is depicted as a dielectric 
elastomer film sandwiched between two compliant electrodes.  
 
Figure 4 The operating principle of a DEA module[5] 
The actuating properties of elastomeric materials were first reported in 1880 in a paper by 
Wilhelm Roentgen where he noted the change in shape of a natural rubber film when a large 
electric field was applied.[6] When an external voltage is applied on a dielectric elastomer, 
compression occurs in the direction of the electrical field produced. This is a result of the 
Maxwell stress which originates from the Coulomb interaction between the two electrodes used 
to apply the voltage on the material.[6] The expression for the Maxwell stress can be seen in 
Equation 1 where 𝜀𝑟 is the relative permittivity of the material, 𝜀0 is vacuum permittivity and

This simplified model, however, does not consider numerous other mechanical and electrical

softening and hardening, response speed, creep, conductivity, and current leakage that are also 
relevant for DEAs.  
Typical driving voltages for 10 to 100 µm dielectric elastomer films range from 500 to 10 000 
V.[6] It is due to these high operating voltages that commercial viability of DEA-s has been 
limited. A high dielectric breakdown strength is also important when higher actuation strains 
are desirable. There is thus a need for tailored dielectric elastomers that exhibit favorable 
electric properties while not compromising in the mechanical properties.

2.2. Choice of dielectric elastomers 
Numerous elastomers have been used for producing DEAs such as polyurethanes, acrylic 
polymers (such as commercial VHB produced by 3M), natural rubbers, thermoplastic 
elastomers (such as SEBS[9, 10]) and silicones. Silicone elastomers containing cross-linked 
polysiloxane chains (most commonly polydimethylsiloxane - PDMS) have multiple 
advantageous properties such as low viscoelastic losses and electrical leakage, good resilience 
and good temperature and humidity tolerance that make them a perspective material candidate 
for actuation.[11] 
Silicones have lower viscoelasticity than acrylic films and can therefore work at higher 
frequencies with lower mechanical losses.[6] Creep and stress relaxation are low for silicones, 
but can be significant for acrylic elastomers.[12] Then again silicones have a relatively low 
dielectric constant and are capable of lower strain than acrylic films.[6]  
The temperature range suitable for operation is intrinsically higher for silicones (-100 to 260 
°C compared to the -10 to 90 °C range for acrylics[12]) which can be important considering that 
a rise in the glass transition temperature has been reported for some permittivity increasing 
chemical modifications.[13, 14] Silicones are resistant to oxygen, ozone and sunlight irradiation 
meaning that they have good weathering properties and self-healing properties have also been 
reported on recently tailored silicone elastomers.[14, 15] All of these aspects reflect positively in 
the approximation of the lifetime of the modules made with silicone materials. Silicones are 
also known to be highly biocompatible which offers the possibility for the use of silicone DEAs 
for various implants.[16] 
For these numerous favorable intrinsic properties polysiloxane elastomers have become 
garnered great interest amongst researchers as the perspective material for improved DEAs.  
2.3. Synthesis of silicone elastomers  
The most common precursor for PDMS is dichlorodimethylsilane, Me2SiCl2, which is 
produced industrially by a copper-catalyzed reaction involving methyl chloride and elemental 
silicon in what is called the “Direct Process”.[17]  This organosilicon species is then taken 
through a two-step process where the first step consists of a hydrolytic polycondensation which 
leads to a mixture of linear and cyclic oligosiloxanes and the second step entails the 
==PAGE:16==
8 
 
transformation of the oligomers into high molecular weight polymers through either acid 
catalyzed polycondensation or cationic ring opening polymerization as seen in Scheme 1.[18]  
 
Scheme 1 General route for linear polysiloxanes 
Since polysiloxane synthesis is quite a mature field, most DET research doesn’t focus on the 
polymerization of PDMS and the use commercially available siloxane polymers is common. 
Some research has been conducted on functionalizing cyclic oligosiloxanes before 
polymerization, but the side groups have been shown to influence the equilibrium between the 
linear product and the low molecular weight cyclic species resulting in a significantly lowered 
polymerization yield.[13] Thus, post-polymerization modification has become the main route 
for functionalization.  
In order for the polysiloxane material to have elastomeric properties, it needs to be cross-linked. 
Cross-linking creates covalently attached linking points between the polymeric chains locking 
in the stress and allowing the material to return to its original dimensions even after very large 
strains. These covalent links also add stiffness and strength to the material since they are 
stronger than the weaker physical cohesive powers (entanglements, Van der Waals binding). 
PDMS elastomers are usually prepared by hydrosilylation, radical, or condensation reactions 
to form the cross-linked network. Hydrosilylation is the most commonly used method which 
utilizes the catalyzed reaction between vinyl groups and silicon bonded hydrogens. [5]  Thus, a 
multifunctional hydride-functional silicon cross-linker can be used to create an elastomeric 
network between vinyl-terminated PDMS chains. Hydrosilylation has advantages against the 
slower condensation reactions as it does not form a low molecular weight by-product which 
can form voids within the synthesized elastomers. These voids contribute negatively to both 
the mechanical and electrical properties of the material.[5] The platinum catalyst is quite 
expensive however and unreacted hydrosilyl groups can cause unwanted gelation over time.[19]  
2.4. Progress in improved silicone elastomers 
A wide range of research has been conducted to improve the material properties of silicone 
elastomers for actuating purposes. As previously mentioned high dielectric permittivity, high 
==PAGE:17==
9 
 
electrical breakdown strength and low Young’s modulus are desirable for perspective DEA 
materials since these are the main properties that should be tackled in order to lower the high 
voltages needed to drive the actuation and increase the maximum actuation strain. A lowered 
elastic modulus does not however increase the actuation stress that the transducer would output 
which is an important parameter for many of the proposed applications for DEA devices and 
can increase the occurrence of electromechanical instability which is the main cause for 
premature electrical failure. Furthermore, lowering the modulus can affect numerous other 
important viscoelastic properties such as network integrity, hysteresis, viscous loss, and tear 
strength. It is thus increased permittivity and electrical breakdown strength that have become 
the main focal points for the field. The possible methods for improving silicone elastomers for 
dielectric actuation include tailored formulations, compounding with fillers and oils, blending, 
creating complex network structures such as interpenetrating networks, and chemical 
modification. 
It is not that common to focus on changing the formulation of silicon elastomers in regard to 
mixing ratios. While altering the stoichiometry could be used to decrease the elastic modulus 
by lowering the amount of cross-linker used, it would also result in a higher degree of unreacted 
silicone chains that would contribute to negative mechanical properties such as viscous loss. It 
is thus more sensible to alter the cross-linking density by modifying the length of the 
polysiloxane chains between the cross-linking points. An average molecular weight between 
cross-links (Mc) of 20 000 to 30 000 g mol-1 has shown to produce desirable mechanical 
properties.[20] 
The use of high permittivity fillers such as TiO2, BaTiO3, Al2O3 is one of the more investigated 
solutions. The inclusion of silica as a reinforcing agent is already a staple for commercial 
silicones. A common issue for high permittivity fillers however is that the increase in 
permittivity at higher loadings also include a considerable stiffening effect. Thus, there needs 
to be a compromise between increasing permittivity while avoiding any concession in regard 
to elastic modulus. The effect of the fillers is strongly dependent to the degree of dispersion 
and since there is a wide range of methods applied by different researchers, it is difficult to 
directly compare the effectiveness of different fillers. Another issue is that quite often the 
means of which the dispersion is carried out such as in situ formation of particles, various 
laboratory mixers, ball milling, and ultra-sonication in multiple solvents are not up-scalable.[5] 
As with most additives the interface between the filler particles and polysiloxane matrix 
influences the effect of the filler. Poor compatibility can cause the formation of voids which 
==PAGE:18==
10 
 
considerably lower the electrical breakdown strength. Additionally, leaching of the fillers can 
occur over the lifetime of the material. 
Blending highly polarizable conjugated polymers with polysiloxane precursors before curing 
has been another method to increase permittivity.[21] An increased dielectric permittivity can 
be achieved at relatively low concentration of conjugated polymers (1-6 wt%) without 
compromising the dielectric loss and reduction of Young’s modulus is also present with this 
method which means that both the electrical and mechanical properties improve 
simultaneously. However, the effectiveness of the added polymers depends on the miscibility 
of the polymers within the PDMS matrix.  
Altering the network structure is another approach to improve silicone elastomers. This could 
be done by creating a network of two or more polymers that are cross-linked in a manner where 
they form interpenetrating networks (IPN). This could be done by cross-linking the components 
simultaneously or in a stepwise faction where the first homo-network is cured at the beginning 
and then swollen with the precursors and initiators of the second component to create an 
interlaced network as seen in Figure 5. These IPN-s can be used to combine the favorable 
properties of two polymers or to lock in pre-strain.[5]  
 
Figure 5 methodology of creating a step-wise cross-linked IPN[22] 
All of the aforementioned methods utilize multicomponent systems, but it is alternatively also 
possible to introduce moieties with favorable properties as a part of the polysiloxane chains 
themselves through chemical functionalization. By covalently linking the functional groups to 
the polymeric structure, issues like leaching or poor interfacial interaction can be avoided.

2.4.1. Chemical functionalization 
There have been numerous approaches to chemically functionalize polysiloxane elastomers. 
Using functionalized cross-linkers was one of the earliest methods explored, but it entailed 
limiting the degree of functionalization with the cross-linking density.[23, 24] It is thus the 
polysiloxane polymers themselves that need to be modified to reach higher degree of 
functionalization. This can be done by either grafting the desired moieties as side-groups or by 
copolymerizing with commercially available functionalized silicones such as PPMS that 
contains a phenyl side groups within its repeating unit.[3, 25, 26]  
The addition of N-allyl-N-methyl-p-nitroaniline dipoles by means of hydrosilylation during 
curing was explored by Risse et al as seen in Figure 6.[24] Since the dipoles are bound to the 
cross-linking points, only a moderate increase of permittivity was observed along with a 
decrease of Young’s modulus and dielectric breakdown strength confirming limitations of this 
method. 
 
Figure 6 The introduction of permativity increasing dipoles as functional groups connected to the multi-
functional cross-linker[24] 
Böse et al. investigated the use of commercially available fluorinated polysiloxanes precursors 
for producing dielectric elastomer films.[27] The increased viscosity caused by the 
trifluoropropyl side-groups made it necessary to incorporate silicon oil before cross-linking as 
a softening agent. The vinyl terminated polymers were cured by means of hydrosilylation.  The 
effect of the different silicon oil loadings on the electromechanical properties of the dielectric 
elastomer were investigated while the degree of functionalization was kept constant. The 
silicon oil did not influence the permittivity of the produced films which showed a relative 
permittivity of 5.4 compared to the PDMS reference of 2.9, but it did lower the elastic modulus 
and increase the maximum strain by a factor of five at the highest loadings of 40-45% compared 
==PAGE:20==
12 
 
to the PDMS reference films. The incorporation of silicon oil does however have ramifications 
on the life-cycle of the material since leaching of the silicon would be expected to occur. 
A click-chemistry based method of modification involving thiol-ene addition has been 
proposed by Dünki et al.[13, 14, 19, 28] which involves reacting thiol containing functional groups 
to vinylated polysiloxane chains. This approach however requires an alternative means of 
cross-linking since the sulfur containing moieties are detrimental for the efficiency for 
hydrosilylation and polycondensation reactions as mentioned in previous sections. Thus, a f-
functional thiol cross-linker is used along with the thiol containing functional groups, vinylated 
polysiloxane, and a UV activated catalyst for a one-pot solution. The degree of 
functionalization can be further modified by means of adding “dummy” butyl mercaptan 
groups that extinguish leftover vinyl groups without affecting the material properties. Further 
research has been conducted by Perju et al. on improving this process by conducting the thiole-
ene addition in a so-called reverse fashion by introducing thiol functionality on the 
polysiloxane chains rather than on the functional groups.[29] This method solves the problem 
of poor miscibility between non-polar polysiloxane chains and polar thiolated functional 
groups. 
Nitrile groups were first introduced by this method resulting in an increased permittivity of 
18.4 from 4.7 at a frequency of 1 kHz.[19] An increase of Tg was also noted since an increase of 
Tg is inevitable due to the introduced dipole-dipole interactions. Further research was 
conducted with the addition of sulfonyl groups which resulted in an impressive increase of 
permittivity from 4.9 to 22.7 at 1 kHz.[13] An even larger effect on Tg was observed with the 
highest transition temperature reaching 9.3 °C. While the functionalization increased the 
conductivity of the materials, they were still in the range for insulators at 10-10 S cm-1. 
Mechanical testing has not yet been conducted on these materials. 
Copper-catalyzed azide-alkyne 1,3-dipolar cycloaddition (CuAAC) has been another click-
chemistry based method applied by Madsen et al. to introduce high dielectric permittivity 
molecules to the elastomeric network.[30-32] The degree of functionalization was controlled by 
copolymerizing the azido functional polysiloxane chains with unmodified PDMS chains that 
acted as “spacers” which also resulted in a well-defined distribution of the functionalities. The 
use of dipolar 1-ethynyl-4-nitrobenzene molecules as the permittivity increasing groups 
resulted in a 70% increase in dielectric constant at 100 Hz and a 25% increase in dielectric 
breakdown strength without compromising the viscoelastic properties.[32]  
==PAGE:21==
13 
 
 
Recent research has found promising click-chemistry methods to functionalize the 
polysiloxane chains post-polymerization. Azide-alkyne method has been used by Madsen et al. 
while thiol-ene addition has been practiced by Dünki et al. It should be noted that the 
functionalization method can also affect the subsequent cross-linking method. Hydrosilylation 
for example is not suitable for thiol-ene functionalization, since the platinum catalyst can be 
poisoned by the sulfuric compounds.[33] Polycondensation has also shown to have a much lower 
yield in the presence of thiols.[14] 
The electron-trapping effect of phenyl side-groups that had been previously investigated for 
polyethylene insulation cables was explored by Razak et al. for increasing the dielectric 
breakdown strength of silicon elastomers.[3] The π-electron clouds within the aromatic rings 
are disrupted when voltage is applied and electron accepting radicals are formed. By creating 
negative space charges within the elastomer, the electrical field strength is decreased causing 
a delay in electrical breakdown. The aromatic functionalities were introduced by creating 
polyphenylmethylsiloxane (PPMS) and polydimethylsiloxane (PDMS) block copolymers as 
seen in Scheme 2. 
 
Scheme 2 Production of PPMS-PDMS copolymers through hydrosilylation[3] 
 An increase of 36% from the reference was observed in the best sample and an optimal 
aromatic ring concentration was determined at approximately 8.4 x 104 mol g-1. The drop-off 
in electrical breakdown strength at higher concentrations was due to occurrence of microscopic 
phase separation between the phenyl rich PPMS and PDMS regions.  
It can thus be proposed that this issue could be side-stepped by a broader distribution of 
functional groups. This could be achieved by the method applied by the method applied by

2.4.2. Copolymerization through Piers-Rubinzstajn polymerization  
The Piers-Rubinsztajn (PR) reaction has been utilized for the preparation of well-defined 
copolymers as a Lewis acid catalyzed polycondensation reaction. Tris(pentafluorophenyl)- 
borane is the preferred catalyst for this type of reaction due to the high stability and a 
remarkable strength that is comparable to BF3.[34] Furthermore, it does not undergo 
decomposition in the presence of water like more traditional Lewis acids such as the 
aforementioned to BF3.[16] While the reaction occurs under mild conditions at room temperature 
and with relatively low concentrations (0.5% or less) it does however form complexes with 
water resulting in lower reaction rates due to the removal of active catalyst from the reaction.[16] 
Thus, high concentrations of water might lead to no reaction occurring at all. The proposed 
complexes can be seen in Scheme 3. [16]

4.2. Instrumentation 
4.2.1. Nuclear magnetic spectroscopy (NMR) 
NMR spectroscopy was used to characterize the reaction products. The aromatic concentration 
of the copolymers was determined by quantifying the hydrogens associated with the functional 
groups and the methyl side-groups of the spacer units. The conversion of the end-groups after 
the end-capping reactions was determined by quantifying the hydrogens connected with the 
residual methoxy end-groups and comparing them with the introduced vinyl or allyl end-groups 
that were quantified in a similar fashion. Proton NMR was performed on a Bruker 300 MHZ 
spectrometer. A sample concentration of approximately 50 mg per ml of deuterated chloroform 
was used for each NMR analysis.  
4.2.2. Size exclusion chromatography (SEC) 
Size exclusion chromatography was used to determine the size distribution and average 
molecular weight of the synthesized products. This is achieved by passing the polymeric 
sample in a solution through a porous column which separates the different fractions of the 
sample based on hydrodynamic volume with the smaller fractions being retained in the column 
for longer due to their ability to diffuse into the pores of the stationary phase of the column. 
The emergence of the different fractions from the column is measured by a refractive index 
detector and the speed at which the fractions pass through the column is determined by the 
==PAGE:28==
20 
 
volume of the eluent pumped into the column between the injection of the sample and the 
emergence of the fraction. This retention volume is then compared with a set of calibration 
standards made with model compounds with a known molecular weight distribution.  
The molecular weight distribution of the sample is thus determined in an indirect manner based 
on hydrodynamic volume and the accuracy of this method is dependent on the degree of 
similarity between the sample and the standards. The average molecular weight is then 
calculated either as a number average (𝑀̅𝑛) where a simple arithmetic mean is taken or a weight 
average (𝑀̅𝑤) where the average is weighted by a statistical method where the larger molecules 
have a larger contribution to the calculated average. The dispersity of the sample (ƉM, formerly 
abbreviated as PDI for polydispersity index) is then defined as the ratio between the weight 
and number average molecular weight. 
The chromatography was performed with Shimadzu instrumentation (SIL-10AD autoinjector, 
LC-10AD liquid chromatograph, RID-10A refractive index detector) with a toluene eluent flow 
rate of 1.000 mL/min at room temperature through a Millipore/Waters Ultrastyragel 105 Å 
column. The calculations were conducted on the OmniSEC software (461 build number) based 
on PDMS calibration standards (87 400 to 237 g mol-1).  
4.2.3.  Dielectric relaxation spectroscopy (DRS) 
DRS was performed on a Novocontrol Alpha-A high performance frequency analyser at room 
temperature with a frequency range of 10-1 to 107 Hz. A sample diameter of 25 mm with a 
thickness of 0.5 to 1 mm was used. DRS was utilized to determine the relative dielectric 
permittivity of the samples, conductivity, and dielectric loss (tan δ) of the samples and their 
frequency dependency. 
4.2.4. Electrical breakdown tests 
The electrical breakdown tests were performed on an in-house developed device based on 
international standards (IEC 60243-1 (1998) and IEC 60243-2 (2001)). The cross-sectional 
thickness of the sample films was first determined through microscopy at three different points. 
The sample was placed between two spherical electrodes which were lowered to indent the 
sample (around 1% sample thickness) to ensure contact with the sample. A stepwise increasing 
voltage was applied (50-100 V per step at a rate of 0.5-1 steps per second) until breakdown. 
Each sample was measured at 12 different points. 
==PAGE:29==
21 
 
4.2.5. Linear viscoelastic analysis (LVE) 
LVE was performed with a TA Discovery HR-1 rheometer at a controlled strain setting of 1% 
with a frequency of 100 to 0.01 Hz at room temperature. A 20 mm diameter parallel-plate 
geometry (Code: 106669) was used. The axial pressure applied to the sample was set at 7 N. 
The rheological data obtained from these measurements was then used to explore the network 
integrity, stiffness, and mechanical loss of the samples. 
4.2.6. Extensional rheology 
Stress-strain relationships and Young’s modulus was investigated by means of extensional 
rheological testing performed with an ARES-G-2 rheometer from TA instruments. An SER2 
testing platform consisting of two rotating 10.3 mm drums that were offset by 12.7 mm from 
their axes was used. Thin film samples were cut into 6 mm wide strips using a special 
punchcutting tool and fixed on the drums. The thickness of the film was determined through 
microscopy at three different points. A Hencky rate (𝑟𝐻) of 0.001 s-1 was used for determining 
the Young’s modulus at 5% strain and a rate of 0.01 s-1 was used for investigating stress-strain 
curves, tensile strength, and strain at break.

5.5. Tensile Properties 
The tensile properties of the samples were investigated by means of extensional rheology. All 
three films showed strain hardening behavior meaning that their mechanical strength increased 
after a certain strain was achieved. This strain hardening behavior is beneficial for DEAs since 
it suppresses the electromechanical instability (EMI) of the actuators which is considered one 
of the most significant failure modes of DEAs.[37] This type of failure occurs when the thickness 
of the dielectric elastomer falls below a certain threshold at which the lowered thickness allows 
a higher electric field to be obtained at a given voltage which in turn compresses the elastomer 
to be even thinner. This creates a feedback loop that finally ends with the electrical breakdown 
of the elastomer. Strain hardening however causes the elastomeric material to stiffen when 
strained past a certain limit allowing to inhibit EMI. The stress-strain relationship of the 
produced dielectric elastomer films can be seen in Figure 16.

displacement of charges at the interfaces between different material phases.[40] The

the air bubbles, but it is also possible that it is an indication of mesoscopic phase separation 
within the material itself. It is however not possible to determine this with a sufficient degree 
of confidence. The relative permittivity of the rest of the samples showed no frequency

important to note the interplay between the different material properties for enhancing 
actuation. Slight increases in both dielectric permittivity and electrical breakdown strength can 
be counteracted by an increased Young’s modulus as seen with the highly functionalized 3-

standpoint to avert compromises in mechanical properties when incorporating voltage

6. Conclusion 
No significant increase in electrical breakdown strength was determined in the produced 
dielectric elastomer films, but a large degree of uncertainty was present in the analysis due to 
the considerable defects incorporated during the processing of the films. While the electrical 
breakdown tests were rendered inconclusive due to the inconsistent nature of the samples it 
was possible to observe a stiffening effect of the functionalization when analyzing the stress-
strain curves obtained from extensional rheology. Since the film defects in question do not 
contribute to the Young’s modulus of the material, it can be assumed that any increase in 
stiffness is due to the intrinsic properties of the copolymers themselves. Thus, it can be 
concluded that the desired softening effect was not obtained by the inclusion of propyl chains 
between the aromatic functional groups and the polysiloxane backbone chains. It should be 
thus reconsidered if it is in fact the influence of the hindered rotational freedom caused by the 
phenyl side-groups that significantly increases the Young’s modulus in phenyl functional 
polysiloxanes. 
Further research would be required to properly study the effect of the distribution of aromatic 
groups on the dielectric and mechanical properties of the elastomeric films in regard to phase 
separation.  
6.1. Outlook and continuation of the present work 
Due to the time constraints of the current project it was not possible to conduct the project work 
to the degree necessary for more concrete conclusions. Optimization of the film processing 
practices during cross-linking steps would be required to remove inconsistencies between 
samples and to produce comparable films necessary for a more in-depth material 
characterization. More thorough drying of the end-capped copolymers to remove any residual 
solvent with a possible inclusion of an additional drying step would be necessary to avoid the 
formation of voids during curing. The area of the thin films should be increased to achieve a 
consistent thickness for the samples prepared for tensile and electrical breakdown tests. Since 
it is also imperative that the bottom surface of the film remains unaltered, the films should be 
peeled off the glass substrate without the aid of any cutting tools. 
A larger focus could be directed towards studying the effect that the distribution of the aromatic 
groups within the copolymers has on the morphology of the final elastomer with the 
==PAGE:50==
42 
 
determination of phase separation being of interest. Scanning electron microscopy could be 
utilized to investigate the occurrence of difference phases on the surface of the material and 
mesoscopic phase separation could be identified with dielectric spectroscopy. The inclusion of 
PPMS-PDMS block-copolymer with similar aromatic concentrations and molecular weights as 
references would also be necessary.  
A more rigorous computational study would be required to determine the effect of the alkyl 
connector between the aromatic functional groups and the polysiloxane backbone chain 
because the influence of the distance between the phenyl groups from the backbone was not 
evident from the current experiments. While it could be possible that longer alkyl connectors 
would further minimize the stiffening effect of the phenyl side-groups, current results indicate 
that a higher rotational freedom was not achieved by the use of a propyl connector. 
Furthermore, it could be stipulated that due to the nature of the siloxane units of the polymer, 
that is the almost freely rotating oxygens between each silicon, the steric effects of the side-
groups are already diminished.
