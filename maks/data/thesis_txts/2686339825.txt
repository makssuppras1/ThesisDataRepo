Abstract
Three recently discovered subglacial lakes in Greenland were monitored by sev-
eral remote sensing methods including altimetry, InSAR derived velocities, optic
imagery and satellite derived DEMs. Furthermore basal ﬂux data was also used to
investigate how the lakes could be connected to a larger network of underground
rivers. The results of this study suggest that the subglacial lake in West Greenland
recharged in the period 2011-2020 after a draining event in the summer period of
2011. The subglacial lake was believed not to have recharged fully in 2020. The
associated drainage basin on top of the Greenland Ice Sheet (GrIS) was observed
to drift substantially in a western direction in the period 2011-2020. A supraglacial
lake east of the basin was believed to be connected with a large basal ﬂux under the
GrIS, most likely providing melt water to the subglacial lake. The results of the two
subglacial lakes in South-West Greenland showed a recharge in the period of 2012-
2017 and a western drift of the basins. The year of both draining events are still
unknown, but the most southern basin was observed in 2008 by optic imagery. The
most northern basin was the smallest and had no large ﬂux nearby, but the southern
basin showed a large ﬂux structure that was connected to an outlet glacier.

Introduction
With the state of the art remote technology the Greenlandic Ice Sheet (GrIS) has
been monitored like never before. Height and velocity models that cover the whole
GrIS have provided fundamental knowledge of how the ice sheet behaves and evolve
during the heating climate of present day, giving evidence of an accelerating loss
of mass[1]. Recent studies have shown that 50 percent of the mass loss is due to
melt water from the surface that run oﬀinto the ocean[2]. The transportation of
melt water on the surface of the GrIS is well studied, and it is accessible by mod-
ern remote sensing. But the transportation of melt water under the GrIS near the
bedrock has been diﬃcult to monitor, leading to insuﬃcient knowledge of how much
it contributes to the mass loss and how this intricate network of subglacial rivers
behave.
New studies have shown that along the subglacial rivers local saddle points in the
topography creates lakes under the ice. These subglacial lakes have been observed
to drain, increasing the run oﬀof melt water to the ocean. The subglacial lakes have
been detected in Antarctica for some time, but only a few have been identiﬁed in
Greenland[3]. This study will investigate 3 identiﬁed subglacial lakes on the GrIS
by the means of several remote sensing methods and modelled basal melt water
ﬂux. The aim is to explore how they can be monitored, what the limitations of the
methods are, and how the draining of the lakes behave.
The remote sensing methods include CryoSat-2 and IceSat-2 altimetry data, Landsat-
8 optic imagery, and InSAR velocity data. Furthermore the remote sensing derived
digital elevation model ArcicDEM and basal ﬂux data provided by The Geological
Survey of Denmark and Greenland (GEUS).
With the increasing global temperatures having a great impact on the Arctic
region, it is paramount to fully understand how the melt of the GrIS develop. The
role of the recent found subglacial lakes in the dynamics of the GrIS is therefore
pivotal to understand in the present climate.

Background
3.1
Current State of The Greenlandic Ice Sheet
Since the introduction of remote sensing methods as altimetry, gravimetry and Syn-
thetic Aperture Radar (SAR) from satellite missions, it has been possible to de-
termine the changing mass balance of the Greenlandic ice sheet (GrIS) on a wider
scale and without the troublesome acquisition of InSitu data. Results have shown
that the GrIS has lost substantial mass the last 20 years [4], with 2012 being a
severe melt year. The mass loss has been observed to decelerate from 2012-2018,
nonetheless the GrIS is still experiencing a net loss, due to extensive melting. The
mass loss have been correlated with the increasing global temperatures caused by
global warming, which is most extreme at the poles due to polar ampliﬁcation[5].
The state of the GrIS has shown to be highly volatile, having severe ramiﬁcations
on the global sea level, salinity of the Atlantic ocean, and local ecosystems.
3.2
Surface Mass Balance of The GrIS
The focus of most recent studies has been to investigate the surface mass balance
(SMB) of the GrIS. The SMB is computed by adding and subtracting the diﬀer-
ent elements of the mass contributions and losses to the surface of the GrIS, thus
neglecting the discharge at the terminus of the outlet glaciers. The main positive
contribution is precipitation in the form of snow, where the negative parts are sub-
limation, which has been increasing due to global warming, run oﬀ, which is melted
snow or rain that travels to the terminus, and snow that the wind will blow oﬀof
the surface. This can be equated into:
SMB = Precipitation −Sublimation/Evaporation −Runoff −Blowing Snow
(1)
Since precipitation often occurs in the colder and elevated interior of Greenland,
the SMB is only negative at the edge of Greenland, where sublimation and run oﬀ
dominates. To compute the overall mass balance of the GrIS the discharge at the
terminus of the Greenlandic outlet glaciers has to be quantiﬁed and subtracted from
the SMB. The discharge consists mainly of melt at the top, calving and basal melt
through subglacial channels at the terminus of the outlets.

The melt and calving can be monitored by optical remote sensing and other
methods, where as the basal melt is a more diﬃcult element to observe and estimate.
The ﬂow of basal melt water will be concealed underneath the glacier, originating
from descending water from the GrIS surface through crevasses and holes called
moulins. To better understand the contribution of basal melt in the Runoﬀin (1), a
basal ﬂux model based on bed topography and ﬂuid dynamics was made by GEUS
among others. It was estimated that basal melt at least contributes 22 Gt per year
in the mass loss in (1), and that it will only increase with the warming climate[6].
3.3
Subglacial Lakes
As ﬁgure 1 entails down at the bedrock local depressions are formed, these can be
ﬁlled with melt water through crevasses and moulins, creating a network of subglacial
lakes and rivers. It has been observed in Antarctica that these subglacial lakes tend
to drain, thus changing the hydrostatic equilibrium at a particularly local point,
creating a basin on the top of the ice sheet, this will be referred to as a Draining
Event[7]. The subglacial lake will consequently recover some of its volume from
melt water, leading to the ice sheet regaining some of its lost height, termed as a
Recharge.
Subglacial lakes have been observed and studied in Antarctica for some time.
With the more unstable dynamics of the GrIS, it has been the subject of recent
studies to ascertain if they are present in Greenland and how they behave.

temperatures all year [8]. A possible draining event is easier to spot, if the rest of the
ice sheet is relatively inactive, naturally a velocity gradient is still present, but that

3.3.2
Subglacial Lakes in Greenland
Draining events in Greenland have ostensibly been harder to observe, the dynamics
of the GrIS suggest that a sudden change of height could easily be concealed by other
factors, such as supraglacial lakes or extensive melting of the ice sheet. Bowling et
al. argues that the size and the topography of these lakes are distant in nature
to their Antarctic counterpart. The Antarctic lakes are mostly close to ice divides
under a thick layer of ice, laying at large depression in the interior, where the ice is
warm due to thermal activity. Where as the Greenlandic lakes are closer to the ice
margin, absent from the middle of the GrIS where the ice is frozen to the bedrock. A
large event at a big subglacial lake in the interior of GrIS is hypothesised to happen
every 103 years. Making it unlikely to observe with remote sensing methods that
only have been realised the last 20 years.
The frequency of events close to the
equilibrium line are believed to happen every decade, which have been seen already.
Subglacial lakes close to the terminus of glaciers may drain seasonally, but due to
the complex dynamics of a glacier, it is hard to single out the event from other
changes[3]. The distribution of subglacial lakes, and the probability of it being a
lake, were performed by Bowling et al. (ﬁg. 4), bringing together the research so
far, to give an overview of the current situation.

3.3.3
Event at Flade Isblink
Willis et al.
showed in 2015 how a subglacial lake at the Flade Isblink ice cap
in North-East Greenland drained into the sea, leaving a ”Mitten” shaped collapse
basin. The volume of the subglacial lake was computed to be ≈0.4 km3. The draining
was observed to occur between the 16th of August and the 6th of September 2011
[10]. This was detected by optic imagery, and after almost a year in May 2012 a
Digital Elevation Model (DEM) gave height data from the collapse event, which
consisted of two basins; a thumb basin and a main basin, hence the name Mitten.
Figure 5:
Satellite image of the mitten shaped collapse basin after a
draining event at Flade Isblink ice cap in North-East Greenland. Con-
sisting of a main and a side (thumb) basin. To the south small crevasses
were observed, where surface melt water could supply the subglacial lake
The study further showed how the basin subsequently regained some of its lost
height gaining ≈40 meters in 2013, which was believed to be the result of a recharge
of the subglacial lake. The scale of the event at Flade Isblink has not been observed
in Greenland since then. The Flade Isblink ice cap is situated on a secluded penin-
sular, which is not a direct part of the GrIS.

Remote Sensing Methods
Remote sensing of the cryosphere has accelerated since the launch of the ﬁrst
satellite, giving vital data of the GrIS. The models of the arctic climate were previ-
ously based on in situ data, which were time consuming and expensive, due to both
the hostile climate and the vast area of the arctic region. Remote sensing utilise
antennas to transmit and receive electromagnetic waves to both actively and pas-
sively observe our planet. Since satellites operate in space, the atmosphere absorb
all signals that are not in the microwave and near-visible light spectrum (ﬁg. 9).
Figure 9: Atmospheric absorption of electromagnetic waves[12]
Earth observing missions are therefore limited to transmit and receive signals in
the visible light and microwave spectrum. This project will focus on missions that
observe the height, the visible state and the velocity of the GrIS.
4.1
Altimetry
Altimetry satellites transmit electromagnetic waves within a certain frequency range
in a quantiﬁed amount called a pulse, to monitor the height of the terrain and ocean.
The satellites receives the transmitted signal, and then by applying the constant
speed of light and the known transmission time, the range from the antenna to the
surface of the terrain can be computed. The height of the terrain is evaluated, by
subtracting the range and the reference ellipsoid from the altitude (ﬁgure 10).
Height = Altitude −Range −Geoid
(2)
The altitude is always known from the orbital position of the satellite, and the
reference ellipsoid is a model that approximates the true surface of the earth, also
referred to as the geoid. [13]

Fig. 10 illustrates that an altimetry satellite will always point the antenna in a
straight nadir direction, thus receiving the most possible amount of the transmitted
signal .
The frequency of the transmission and the size of the antenna determines the
footprint size of the signal, i.e. the area which the pulse will be reﬂected on the
surface (ﬁg. 11).
X = λ · h
L
(3)
Where X is the radius of the footprint, h is the altitude of the satellite, L is the
width of the beam determined by the antenna size and λ is the wavelength of the
transmitted signal which equals to:
λ = c
f
(4)
Where c is the speed of light and f is the frequency of the transmitted signal.
A small footprint will give a good resolution in the horizontal plane, giving more
data points per area. A small footprint is always preferable, but increasing the size
of the antenna is expensive and not convenient when building satellites. Therefore
the frequency is the key decider in how well the resolution will be.
As mentioned before only the microwave and visible light spectrum are available.
An altimetry satellite transmitting in the visible spectrum is called a LIDAR, an
abbreviation of ’light detection and ranging’. And an altimetry satellite working in
the microwave spectrum it is called a Radar Altimeter. Seeing that visible light
has a higher frequency than microwaves, the LIDAR footprint will be smaller than

the Radar Altimeter, thus giving a better resolution, but the latter has the advantage
of working through clouds. Clouds will scatter all visible light, but microwaves will
penetrate them and reﬂect on the surface unhindered [15].
Figure 11: A visual representation of a transmitted pulse, and how it
interacts with the Earth surface. The 4 plots are waveforms representing
the power received as function of range. [16]
When the transmitted signal reﬂects on the ground and is received by the satel-
lite, the obtained signal will be the power as a function of range called a waveform.
As an eﬀect of electromagnetic waves following the inverse square law, the waveform
will visually resemble ﬁg. 11. If the ground is close to level, the ﬁrst power received
will be the surface directly under the satellite. The ensuing decrease in power re-
ceived will be increasingly far away from the point directly under the antenna.

4.3
Inferometry Synthetic Aperture Radar
Inferometry Synthetic Aperture Radar (InSAR) is a method to detect displacement
of the terrain in all directions in a standard Cartesian coordinate system, where as
the altimeter only gives data in the the z-direction, InSAR will also give information
in the x and y-direction.
An InSAR is based on comparing the phase of the electromagnetic signals re-
ceived from a Synthetic Aperture Radar (SAR) at diﬀerent points in time. A SAR
is a radar that always look in a oﬀ-nadir direction, sending pulses and receiving
simultaneously along its ﬂight path (ﬁg 13). The oﬀ-nadir angle utilise that objects
will scatter the signal diﬀerently, therefore diﬀerentiating between individual objects
by looking at the received signal. Furthermore the SAR use the Doppler shift of the
received signal, since the satellite is always moving, to observe the same target from
multiple pulses, this increases the spatial resolution[17].
Figure 13
An electromagnetic signal transmitted from an antenna is almost a perfect sinu-
soidal function where ω = f · 2π. The phase (φ) of an electromagnetic signal is then
the angle from 0 in the unit circle (ﬁgure 14)

ﬁeld of the signal. If φ is π

more importantly the velocity can be evaluated. This is especially used to infer the
velocity of the GrIS.

Tikhonov Regularised Interpolation
Due to the scattered and noisy nature of some altimetry data, it would be prefer-
able to construct a model that can interpolate the data on a well deﬁned spaced
grid, that handles gaps and noise. In geophysics, inverse problems is a well used
and investigated subject, which in this project can be used to estimate heights of
the GrIS. In classical science a model is expressed analytically and then tested by
observed data to verify or reject the model. If the model is incorrect, then a new
model has to be constructed. In inverse problems the model is determined by the
observed data, thus reversing the traditional method, also referred to the forward
problem.
Figure 15
The forward problem can be expressed as a linear matrix vector product
dobs = Gm

6.1
ArcticDEM
A DEM is an approximation of the surface of the earth from observed data. Data
are usually scattered and noisy, thus creating a model that estimates the height of
a whole area can be a great advantage. The ArcticDEM is a elevation model of
the arctic region (ﬁg. 17) using multiple data sources, mainly from satellites, to give
the best resolution possible. The spatial resolution depends on the available data
in the speciﬁc region that the user investigates, yet it can be as good as 5 m. One
key feature is that the model is time variant, allowing the opportunity to observe
change over time, which spans from 2011-2017.

The ArcticDEM was downloaded from the The ArcticDEM Explorer website,
due to the accessible temporal data in TIFF format. QGIS was used visualise the
TIFF data, and to export proﬁle lines that was plotted in Python. The proﬁle lines
consisted of two lines that crossed the drainage basins, resembling the methodology
in ﬁg. 8 used by Palmer et al.
6.2
CryoSat-2
Cryosat-2 is a radar altimetry satellite launched in April 2010, with the objective of
monitoring the cryosphere, giving crucial data on the changing climate in the arctic
region. The satellite have three operational modes, Low Resolution Mode (LRM),
Synthetic Aperture Radar Mode (SARM) and SAR Interferometric (SARIn). The
LRM is a simple standard radar altimeter, having a large footprint of 15 km op-
erating in the microwave spectrum. The LRM is mainly used over areas that are
homogeneous in height, like ﬂat planes or the sea. The SARM use the principles of
SAR to receive multiply signals from the same target, thus enhancing the spatial
resolution 10 times. The SARIn is a support mode that gather information about
the phase of the signal[19].
The SARIn has recently been the study of enhancing the spatial resolution of
Cryosat-2. This was explored by Gourmelen et al. 2017, showing that the phase
diﬀerence between diﬀerent channels could greatly enhance the localisation of each
signal, in other words unwrapping the signal. In order for this to work the terrain has
to have a small slope angle, since the signals will come from an oﬀ-nadir direction.
==PAGE:26==
Data and Methods
Page 21
This resulted in a spatial resolution of 30 meters, which is unprecedented in radar
altimetry[20]. The newly found data is called CryoSat-2 Swath data.
The Swath data was downloaded directly from the ESA FTP server[21]. The raw
data was divided into areas of 10,000 km2, containing observed data points for one
month each, exported in the Network Common Data Form (NetCDF) data format.
Python was used to import and ﬁlter the data, compiling the desired attributes into
a CSV ﬁle. The attributes were the x and y components in Polar Stereographic
Coordinates, and the elevation expressed by the z component. QGIS was used to
map the imported data, because the location changed from month to month, it was
important to see where the Swath processing had obtained data. DEMs were also
made on the CryoSat-2 data, implementing a Tikhonov Regularised Interpolation in
Python. The kernel matrix G was chosen to be a Radial Basis Function (RBF) that
used weighting between the data points to interpolate new points on a well deﬁned
grid, this was exported as a TIFF ﬁle with a spatial resolution of 20m. The theory
of using RBF on height data can be further investigated in the Smolik et al.[22] and
Xiao[23] papers. The speciﬁc RBF used was the Gaussian function.
φ(r) = e−(ϵ·r)2
(15)
Where r was the distance in meters between the data points, e was the euler con-
stant and was the shape parameter. controls how far away a data point weighs on
the interpolated data. Both the shape parameter and the regularisation parameter
α were set using trial and error, arriving at 4.2 · 10−4 and 10−5 respectively. The
distances between the data points were computed by utilising the Distance Matrix
function from the Scipy Spatial library in Python.
6.3
IceSat-2
IceSat-1 was a LIDAR satellite launched in January 2003, with the purpose of under-
standing the dynamics of the polar regions, being the ﬁrst LIDAR for that function.
Its Geoscience Laser Altimeter System (GLAS) gave a spatial resolution of 15 cm,
far better than its Radar counterpart, unfortunately it only works during cloud-free
days. IceSat-1 was decommissioned in August 2010 when it crashed into the Barents
Sea. The mission was a success and a follow-up was quickly manufactured, result-
ing in IceSat-2 launching in September 2018. The on-board ATLAS laser emits six
beams in three pairs, the distance between beams in a pair is 90 meter and 3 km
between the pairs (ﬁgure 18).
==PAGE:27==
Data and Methods
Page 22
Figure 18: The 6 beams of the IceSat-2 GLAS instrument. There is 3 km
between the beam pairs and 90 meters between the beams in a pair[24].
The distance between individual samples, i.e. the footprint, is 70 cm[24]. And
with a polar orbit the data was observed in a N-S or S-N direction.
The data was downloaded through the EarthData website governed by NASA.
The product was ATLAS/ICESat-2 L2A Global Geolocated Photon Data (ATL03),
it gave information of each individual photon, to save memory and time, any point
that was not located near the subglacial lake was removed. The parameters saved
and ﬁltered from the ATL03 product were the Lon, Lat coordinates, the height of
the photon and the conﬁdence level. The conﬁdence of the photon is the likeliness
of it being a signal or background noise. The level goes from 0-4, 0 being noise, 1
being a buﬀer but considered as noise, 2 is a low conﬁdence signal, 3 is a medium
conﬁdence signal and 4 is a high conﬁdence signal. Only photons with a conﬁdence
level of 3 and 4 were saved, if all of the data had a conﬁdence level of 2 or less, the
weather was most likely cloudy and another day was tried.
6.4
Landsat-8
The Landsat missions are organised by NASA, having continuously providing optic
data since 1972, frequently launching updated satellites to provide the best service.
The latest mission is the Landsat-8 launched in February 2013, carrying the Op-
erational Land Imager (OLI) with a resolution of 15-30 m divided into 9 spectral
bands. As a result of observing in the visible light spectrum, natural factors like
clouds and darkness of the night, limits the output of useful data.
Optic imagery from Landsat-8 were downloaded from the Living Atlas website
through the ArcticDEM Explorer in three spectral bands (2, 3 and 4). These bands
are the natural colours blue, green and red, with a spatial resolution of 30 m. QGIS
was used to map the images imported in a TIFF format, all three spectral bands
were used to give colourised images.
6.5
Ice Velocities
The aim of the Greenland Ice sheet Mapping Project (GIMP), is to create high
resolution data sets of the whole GrIS, primarily to use as a reference to detect

change of the ice sheet[25]. This includes ice sheet velocities from InSAR satellite
such as RADARSAT[26]. The ice velocity data consisted of the annual displacement
of the whole ice sheet in meters in an eastern direction, downloaded from the GIMP
website in a spatial resolution of 200 meters. QGIS was used to map the velocities
near the 3 diﬀerent sites.
6.6
Basal Flux
GEUS provided the basal water ﬂux of the whole GrIS, the resolution varied from
5 km to 250 m.
The basal ﬂux was computed by using the Bedmachine model of the Green-
landic topography, the geothermal ﬂux and ice sheet velocities from InSAR[6].
The drainage basins investigated in this project varied in size but the largest was
1000x500 meters. This prompted the decision that only the resolution of 250 m was
usable. A poorer resolution would make it impossible to ascertain a correlation with
the draining event. The 250 m resolution data was based on very loose constriction
on the computation, the precise ﬂux values were by that result not trustworthy.
But a correlation between a high ﬂux and an event could be explored, this is further
discussed in section chapter 8.
To get a continuous and easy to interpret model, QGIS was used to implement
a simple weighted interpolation (IDW) and a gaussian ﬁlter to smooth the data.
6.7
Alignment of Data sets
With height data from 3 diﬀerent sources, ArcticDEM, IceSat-2 and CryoSat-2,
there will be an oﬀset, which can be caused by using diﬀerent reference ellipsoids or
processing methods. Since only the height changes are of interest, and not the exact
height above sea level, a correction had to be made. It was arbitrarily chosen that
the ArcticDEM was the reference height. The CryoSat-2 data had an oﬀset of +38.0
meters, and IceSat-2 of +30.9 meters. The alignment will be further explained in
segment 8.1.
Besides a spatial correction of the data sets, a temporal correction was also
needed. The moving GrIS caused the drainage basins to drift over time, observed
by Landsat-8 and the ArcticDEM. In order to obtain height proﬁles of the same
feature on the ice at diﬀerent times, the proﬁle lines were moved accordingly to
the drift. The distance the basin was displaced over time was inferred by feature
tracking of the optic imagery and the ArcicDEMs. GIMP velocity data was included
to support the correction. The drift will be further explained in segment 8.3. All of
the results have both corrections implemented.

8.4
Possible Recharge of The Subglacial Lakes
The drainage of the subglacaial lake that formed the basin in West Greenland is
believed to have happened in June/July 2011. Former studies show that the event
transpired in a month or less, creating a negative height change of 60 meters at an
area of ≈0.5 km2 [11]. As observed in Antarctica the subglacial lakes will be ﬁlled
with water, and consequently lift the formed basin ﬂoor[7]. The results of Line A of
the Western basin (ﬁg. 30a) showed a clear elevation change just after the drainage
with an uplift of ≈25 meters from 2011 to 2013, which was understood as a recharge
of ≈0.025 km3 of water. The rate slowed down from 2013-2015 to an uplift of ≈10
meters, to less than 5 meters in 2015-2016 and 2016-2017. The southern slope of
the basin was observed getting ﬂatter than the steeper northern slope, this was
hypothesised to be an eﬀect of the sun melting the ice on the northern slope more
eﬀectively, because Greenland is in the Northern Hemisphere. The results of Line B
were the same as in Line A, but with IceSat-2 data from the 2nd of November 2020,
it was ascertained that after an uplift of ≈10 meters in 2017-2020, the basin was still
there. The southern slope was almost ﬂat, indicating that the subglacial lake was
almost recharged nine years after the event. It was established that with the total
uplift of ≈55 meters in the period 2011-2020 almost half of it happened in the ﬁrst
two years, revealing that the recharge rate will be fastest just after an event. With
the current recharge rate it is likely that the subglacial lake has reached its maximum
around 2022-2023, if it stays active and drains or turns dormant is unknown.
The year when the northern subglacial lake in South-West Greenland drained
is still uncertain, but Bowling et al. hypothesise that the event happened at least
5 years before 2012, where the ﬁrst ArcticDEM was available[3].
The area was
observed to be ≈0.06 km2 in 2012. The results of Line A (ﬁg. 39a) illustrated an
uplift of ≈13 meters in 2012-2015, including a side basin experiencing an uplift of ≈3
meters. The negligible change in elevation from 2015-2016 would suggest that the
lake had recharged fully. But a negative height change of ≈3 meters in 2016-2017
imply that the recharge was not done yet. As seen in ﬁg. 72 in Appendix B, the ﬂat

Basal Connection of The Subglacial Lakes
The water source of the subglacial lakes as mentioned earlier, stems from surface
melt water that travels through the ice sheet via moulins and crevasses into an
integrate net of subglacial rivers. Some of theses rivers reach a local minimum in
the topography which can create a lake. The provided basal ﬂux model could give
an idea of where these lakes are formed by investigating the three existing lakes.

inferred by Palmer et al.
the nearby supraglacial lake drained right before the
event in the summer of 2011 [11]. And by placing a Landsat-8 image from August
2013 underneath and lowering the opacity of the basal ﬂux model, it was clear to
see that the large ﬂux was directly under the supraglacial lake (ﬁg.

ﬂux and ice sheet velocities. The 250 resolution set some very loose restrictions on
how the water could ﬂow near the bedrock modelled by Bedmachine, reaching the
limit of how small ﬂux variations the model could estimate. It was inconclusive if
a more comprehensive topography model of the Greenlandic Bedrock would have
given a diﬀerent result, but it does put into perspective that these Greenlandic
subglacial lakes are on the verge of being undetectable with our current technology
and knowledge. It is most likely that the subglacial lake is connected to the outlet
glacier to the west, recognised by the large ﬂux structure with the two distinct ﬂux
inputs from the east (ﬁg. 31b). But determining how it is connected was diﬃcult
to aﬃrm by the current models and was therefore uncertain.

lake with melt water that prompted both the drainage and the subsequent recharge.
The ﬂux continues to the south-west ending in an outlet glacier (ﬁg. 40b). Recalling
that the basin was substantial bigger in 2008 (ﬁg. 55), for this reason the size of the
subglacial lake is arguably larger than the other two, thus explaining why a large
ﬂux was observed.
The absence of a notable ﬂux nearby the northern event in South-West Greenland
and the event in West Greenland, was an indication of the limitations of our models
of the topography, but also that the events were small in size thus having a limited
impact on total basal ﬂux. It was believed that the southern event in South-West
Greenland was the largest of the three, even though the ensuing basin was only the
second largest in 2012. The evidence of a recharge transpiring for 12 years, a large
basin detected by MODIS in 2008, and a considerable ﬂux input supports the notion
of a large subglacial lake.

Future Work
Page 56
9
Future Work
The satellite methods used to monitor the subglacial lakes in this project proved
to infer how much they recharged and for how long. But CryoSat-2 Swath data
lacked the ability to observe local depressions like basins formed after draining
events. The data was imported directly from the ESA FTP server, and was not
processed further. The Swath could be improved by investigating the tail of the
waveform adding new height data, similar to the study made by Smith et al. at
Thwaites glacier [9].
In the GIMP InSAR derived velocity data, only the horizontal displacement in
a west-east direction was investigated. InSAR can determine the displacement in
all directions in a Cartesian coordinate system. The z-direction, i.e. the height of
the ice sheet, would be interesting to compute. If the modern InSAR techniques
could observe the height change related to a draining event, the identiﬁcation and
examination of a subglacial lake would become substantial easier.
The IceSat-2 satellite has only been operational since 2018, it will provide a great
amount of data in the years to come. The satellite will most likely have data over
unidentiﬁed draining events. And considering the drifting nature of the basins, a
recharge is still possible to observe even though the tracks does not intersect the
original location of the basin.

Conclusions
Page 57
10
Conclusions
The three subglacial lakes proved possible to monitor using remote sensing meth-
ods, but the newly established methods of CryoSat-2 Swath processing was not ad-
equate in detecting local depressions. The Swath data was either absent over the
basins, leaving gaps in the measurements, or the data was noisy due to a geoloctaion
error. The other methods revealed a fast changing nature of the subglacial lakes and
the rising ice sheet above.
Landsat-8 and ArcticDEM observed the basin formed by the draining of the
subglacial lake in West Greenland to drift over time. The basin drifted ≈710 meters
in the period 2011-2020 in a western direction towards the sea.
GIMP velocity
data supported that a drift was transpiring in the region, with an observed annual
displacement of ≈55 meters in a Western direction. The drift prompted a correction
of the data sets, so the height data of the ice sheet was from the depression and not
a drifted ﬂat surface. The drainage event was hypothesised to have transpired in
June or July 2011. The ArcticDEM detected the newly formed basin ﬁrst in October
2011 having a depth of 60 meters and the dimensions 1000x500 m. The following
ArcticDEM of July 2013 showed an uplift of ≈30 meters of the basin, meaning the
subglacial recharged in that period. The recharge slowed down in 2013 to 2015 with
an observed uplift of ≈5 meters.
The gradual uplift continued with less than 5
meters in the period 2015 to 2016 and to the last ArcticDEM of 2017. The basin
eventually drifted into an IceSat-2 track, where a ﬂyby in November 2020 gave the
result of an uplift of 10 meters in the period 2017-2020. The subglacial lake had not
recharged fully yet, since the surface in the IceSat-2 results was not completely ﬂat
and a small hint of a basin was still visible. With the current recharging rate the
subglacial lake will probably ﬁnish its recharge by 2022-2023. The modelled basal
ﬂux showed no large ﬂux under the basin, only a small structure east of the basin
was observed. The basal ﬂux was directly under the supraglacial lake which has been
hypothesised to be a melt water source to the subglacial lake through crevasses and
moulins. The results conﬁrmed the supragracial lake had a connection with the
basal ﬂux of Greenland.
The Northern basin in South-West Greenland was also observed to drift, under-
going a displacement of 90 m in a western direction in the period 2012-2017. The
year which the drainage event happened is still uncertain, but the basin was observed
by the ArcticDEM in July 2012 with a depth of ≈15 meters and the size 250x250
m. The ArcticDEM of June 2015 showed an uplift of ≈13 meters since 2012, but
Landsat-8 imagery identiﬁed it as the water surface of a formed supraglacial lake,
and not the result of a recharge. The next ArcticDEM height measurement from
May 2016 had the same height as in 2015, Landsat-8 conﬁrmed that it was the
surface of the frozen supraglacial lake. The supraglacial lake drained during the
summer period of 2016, leaving a small basin behind. The ArcticDEM of July 2017
therefore gave a result of negative height change of ≈3 meters. In total the subglacial
lake recharged in the period 2012-2017 with a measured uplift of ≈10 meters of the
basin. Landsat-8 imagery observed a close to ﬂat surface in 2020, indicating that

Conclusions

the subglacial had recharged fully. No large basal ﬂux was near the event, only a
small structure to the south that was connected to sea. The small ﬂux was believed
to be caused by the small size of the event, supported by the narrow dimensions of
the basin in 2012.
The southern event in South-West Greenland drifted a total of 175 meters in the
period 2012-2018 in a western direction, lack of noticeable features made tracking
diﬃcult after 2018. A low resolution MODIS image from April 2013 was compared
to a high resolution Landsat-8 image from the same month and year, to create a
baseline that was used to identify the drainage basin in a MODIS image from April
2008. The ﬁrst height data was the ArcticDEM from May 2012, the basin had a
depth of ≈30 meters with the dimensions 750x750 m. The basin was observed to
be larger in the MODIS image from April 2008, but due to the low resolution it
was diﬃcult to ascertain the size. 3 years later in May 2015 the subglacial lake had
recharged lifting the bottom of the basin ≈10 meters. Landsat-8 imagery detected
a frozen supraglacial lake in October 2015 in the basin, which had not drained yet
by June 2016. The ArcticDEM from June 2016 observed the ﬂat surface of the lake,
giving the wrong result of a recharge. The supraglacial lake subsequently drained
during the melt season in 2016 observed by the Landsat-8 satellite. The ArcicDEM
measured the true height of the bottom of the basin in July 2017, giving the result
of a ≈5 uplift since 2015. The basin got steadily more ﬂat in the period 2017-2020,
seen in the Landsat-8 images. It was believed that the subglacial lake had fully
recharged in 2020-2021. A substantial larger ﬂux was observed under the southern
event compared to the other two. A large horizontal structure to the north and
south of the event connected with a vertical ﬂux, was deemed to be the source of
the melt water to the subglacial lake. Considering that the basin was larger in 2008
and the large input of basal water, it was believed that this event was largest of the
three.
