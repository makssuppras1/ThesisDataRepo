1 Abstract 
A hot new topic within space missions is formation flying spacecraft. Several small spacecraft that work 
together have the ability to far outperform conventional large crafts. They allow simpler designs, and make 
it possible to observe things from different positions and times. 
This report explores the possibilities of a vision based system for determining relative pose and position 
between two spacecraft. The aim of the system is to achieve high precision and robustness, so the system 
can be used for future formation flying spacecraft missions. As a basis for the work the upcoming PROBA-3 
mission is used. As the “physical” camera the VBS system developed by DTU is used to obtain images. The 
system is cooperative, so the target spacecraft is equipped with an LED pattern to help with the pose 
determination. 
The problem is how to obtain the pose and position of a target spacecraft, using only 2D measured LED 
positions (from images taken by VBS), and comparing them to a known 3D model of the target (The PnP 
problem). A number of different algorithms are tested for this purpose to find the most optimal ones for 
the PROBA-3 application. 
A proposed system is designed and simulated that successfully achieves pose and position accuracies 
significantly higher than the original VBS system. The system is also designed to be quite stable to missing 
or fake measured points. An analysis of the full range spectrum of the formation is also explored, and an 
acquisition algorithm is proposed to find the target from a far distance.

3 Introduction 
Ever since the beginning of manned space missions, the relative positions of spacecraft have been a subject 
of much study. At that time the challenge was for two spacecraft to rendezvous and dock, which was a 
critical and very dangerous part of the mission. In modern times, determining the relative positions have 
become only increasingly important, as the concept of formation flying spacecraft has been put into 
practice. Formation flying simply means that a number of separate spacecraft autonomously interact and 
cooperate in order to achieve some common goal. [1] 
 The idea behind formation flying spacecraft is that several small satellites, that work together, by far can 
outperform a single large craft.  The advantages of formation flying spacecraft are among other things: 
simpler design, cheaper replacements, and higher redundancy. It also makes it possible to observe a target 
or an area from multiple angles, positions, and/or multiple times. 
Several missions already take advantage (or plan to) of some of these possibilities. Recently SWARM was 
launched by ESA on a mission to measure the magnetic field of the Earth. The mission consists of 3 nearly 
identical spacecraft that fly in a unique constellation separated by about 80 km. This allows SWARM to 
identify the sources of the magnetic fields in a way that no single spacecraft would be able to. However the 
constellation of the satellites is maintained by careful orbit planning, and maintenance from the ground. So 
it cannot be said to be a true formation flying mission if the above definition is used. It does demonstrate 
the advantage of spatially separated spacecraft though. [2]  
 
Figure 1: SWARM mission [2] 
 
Figure 2: MMS mission [3] 
Even more recently the Magnetospheric Multiscale mission (MMS) was launched by NASA to observe the 
magnetic reconnection effects thought to happen in the magnetosphere of the Earth. This mission consists 
of no less than 4 individual spacecraft flying in a tetrahedral constellation. However this mission also relies 
on external sources to maintain its formation, in this case signals from the GPS system. [3] 
Both these missions demonstrate the advantage of having several formation flying spacecraft. It also 
indicates that a demand for an autonomous positioning system exists. Autonomous systems could make 
the missions both cheaper, and more robust, in the sense that they no longer would need to rely on ground 
stations or other satellites to complete their objectives. For future missions there is also a need for much 
more accurate determinations of the relative pose and positions. For example the proposed Terrestrial 
==PAGE:7==
6 
 
Planet Finder mission consists of instruments distributed over a number of small spacecraft (see Figure 3). 
The science objective of the mission would range from locating exo-Earths to imaging of the event horizon 
of a black hole. However for that to work the satellites must maintain an extremely precise constellation, 
with a relative position control of nanometers, and pose control of less than 0.01 milliarc seconds. This kind 
of accuracy would be impossible by GPS or by orbit planning. [4] 
 
Figure 3: Terrestial Planet Finder formation. Credit: JPL 
So to satisfy the requirements for future missions, the technology of formation flying spacecraft is in rapid 
development. The next major step in this field is the PROBA-3 mission, which is a mission intended to test 
and demonstrate the technologies of formation flying spacecraft. In this book PROBA-3 is used as a baseline 
to examine the possible methods behind acquiring pose and position information of another cooperative 
spacecraft.  This is done with only a vision based system (a camera). The goal is to obtain the relative pose 
and position information with maximum accuracy in a fast and also robust way. [5] 
The situation examined is for cooperative spacecraft. This simply means that the individual satellites are 
designed to work together. So there are certain methods in place designed to help with the determination. 
In the PROBA-3 case it means that there is an LED pattern on the target that can be used to determine the 
relative pose and position.

4 Problem formulation 
The main goal of the project is to design a system that can determine the pose and position of a spacecraft 
relative to another spacecraft (an observer). This is done visually by having a camera on the observer that 
takes images of the target object. But how is this done? 
Imagine a 3-dimensional object, for example a book, and then visualize how it looks head on. Then think 
about how it looks if it is rotated by some angle around a horizontal axis (see Figure 4). This is quite easy for 
the human eye to detect, and for the brain to visualize. Even if the target is 2-dimensional (for example the 
cover of the book), it is easy to see, because the lower part of the cover looks smaller than the top. The 
range to things can also be approximated quite easily, because the apparent size of things changes with the 
distance. But how can a computer be designed to see this? 
 
 
Figure 4: A book. Left: Head on. Right: Rotated. 
This is a field that has seen a lot of interest and research for a number of different applications. Everything 
from Virtual Reality [6] to odometry for robots [7] are fields with an interest in this. So many different 
algorithms to solve the problem have already been designed. The goal for this project is then to examine 
and test a good amount of these algorithms on a space application, to find out what the optimal solution is.  
Since every space mission is different, and can have extremely varying goals and requirements, the task is 
delimited to mainly focus on the upcoming PROBA-3 mission. This is a demonstration mission with the 
purpose of testing formation flying technology. Therefore most of the parameters chosen for the tests are 
from the design of this mission. [5] 
In order to have a vision based system there must naturally be a camera on the observer. Cameras can also 
vary greatly depending on their objectives. So for this project it is assumed that a DTU VBS system is in 
place on the observer. The VBS system is a rendezvous and docking sensor developed by DTU, and used 
previously on the PRISMA mission [8]. The VBS system will in this project be used as the “physical” camera, 
but the software algorithms is what will be analyzed and designed. 
So the aim of this thesis is to determine optimal algorithms for acquisition and tracking of pose and position 
of a target spacecraft operating in cooperative formation flight mode. The algorithms shall be designed to 
deliver robust operations over the full distance ranges as specified for the PROBA-3 mission.

5 The PROBA-3 Mission 
PROBA-3 is an abbreviation for Project for On-Board Autonomy-3, and it is the third mission in a series of 
demonstration missions aimed at demonstrating and testing developed satellite technology. For this 
mission the main goal is to demonstrate the technology required for formation flying spacecraft. This 
requires in-orbit validation of formation flying techniques through a series of precision maneuvers, such as 
formation acquisition, reorientation, maintenance, resizing, and more. 
In addition to this the mission also has a scientific payload. This will be a large coronagraph instrument that 
will take images of the inner solar corona with observational parameters never achieved before. The 
coronagraph will have a length of about 150 meters, which is a size that would be very challenging with a 
conventional single spacecraft. However in this mission the instrument will be distributed over 2 small 
formation flying spacecraft, making it possible to achieve such a size.  
 
Figure 5: Occulter Spacecraft (OSC). [5] 
 
Figure 6: Coronograph spacecraft (CSC). [5] 
The two spacecraft will be the Occulter Spacecraft (OSC) and the Coronograph Spacecraft (CSC) as seen in 
Figure 5 and Figure 6. The coronograph spacecraft will contain the actual camera that will take images of 
the solar corona, while the occulter spacecraft will be a platform for the occulter disc that will block out the 
direct light from the surface of the sun. This will serve as a good test for the formation flying system, as a 
very high accuracy in the relative positions of the two spacecraft will be required for the coronograph to 
work. 
The satellites will fly in a highly elliptical orbit (HEO) as seen in Figure 7. It will have a perigee of around 600 
km, and an apogee of more than 60000 km. At the perigee pass both spacecraft will be inactive. As they 
move away from the earth towards the apogee they will acquire the required formation, and perform their 
science and demonstration operations far away from the Earth. The orbital period is almost 20 hours, and 
the mission is expected to last for approximately 2.5 years.  
==PAGE:10==
9 
 
 
Figure 7: PROBA-3 Orbit. [5] 
To determine and control the relative distance and orientation of the spacecraft two steps are used. First 
GPS is used to move the spacecraft into approximate positions. At this point the position uncertainty will be 
<10 meters for both the lateral and longitudinal directions.  Then a Coarse Lateral Sensor (CLS) instrument 
is used. This instrument works by transmitting a laser beam that is reflected by a corner cube on the 
companion satellite. The reflection is then detected and a lateral shift will be seen as an image 
displacement. The instrument is expected to have an accuracy in the lateral direction of less than 100 𝜇𝑚 
(standard deviation) at the 150 meter range. The required accuracy for the system to work is 1 mm. [5] 
As mentioned in the problem formulation, the goal of this book is to design a vision based system for 
position and pose determination based on the VBS system. So the developed system will be an alternative 
to the GPS/CLS system currently planned. For use of the vision based system the CSC spacecraft is outfitted 
with a pattern of LEDs. The main concept of the vision based system is to take images of these LEDs, and 
then compare the measured positions with the known relative positions of the pattern (the “model”). This 
will allow the system to calculate the pose and position of the target spacecraft. So the VBS camera will be 
located on the OSC looking at the LEDs on the CSC. 
 
Figure 8: LED numbering on the coronagraph spacecraft. 
The LEDs on the CSC is located as seen on Figure 8. In the image each LED is marked by a red dot. They have 
also been labeled with a number that will be used in future references.

6 System design 
In this section the overall design of the system is described. A very broad overview can be seen in Figure 9. 
As can be seen in the figure the system must consist of a camera that is observing the target spacecraft 
(CSC). On the target there is a pattern of LEDs that is visible on the image. The digital image is then 
processed in a micro controller unit (MCU).  
Within the MCU the first thing that must be done, is that the image must be processed and the locations of 
the LEDs must be found in the image. This processing along with the actual MCU and camera is assumed to 
come from the DTU VBS system, so it is simply assumed to be working in this project. The output of this 
part is then a number of 2-dimensional points that the VBS system believes correspond to the locations of 
the LEDs in the image. The real challenge of this project is then to use these points, along with the known 
“model” of the LEDs, to calculate the pose and position of the target.  
 
Figure 9: System overview. 
Concerning the LEDs there are a number of questions to consider. How can the design and choice of LEDs 
ensure that the LEDs can be seen clearly in sunlight? How accurately is the position of the LEDs known? 
(how good is the “model”?).  These questions will be addressed in section 6.2. 
It is trivial to realize that there are many different situations for which the system must work, so it is very 
unlikely there is a “one-algorithm-fits-it-all” solution that can be done. First of all the available information 
obtained from the target vary greatly with the range. Therefore the system will have several different 
“range modes”, all with different algorithms. So the first thing that must be done after the image 
processing, is to detect the range of the object. Hereafter the system can proceed with the appropriate 
range mode.  
==PAGE:12==
11 
 
How many range modes there should be, and their precise definitions, will be discussed later in section 11. 
However it is clear that in some of these modes it will not be possible to determine both the pose and 
position of the target. If the spacecraft is too far away it may be possible to determine a position and range, 
but finding the pose will be impossible unless the target can be seen clearly. Therefore there will be a 
special emphasis in this thesis on the “close range” situation, in which it will be possible to determine the 
pose. This is because this must be the most important mode, in which the scientific and demonstration 
mission objectives will be done.  
Even within this close range mode, there will be several different algorithms at work. This can be seen on 
Figure 10. The idea is that there are two different situations for determining the pose: 
 
“Lost in Space”: Nothing is a priori known about the pose and position of the target. Basically a 
situation where the object must be found from a completely unknown position. 
 
“Tracking”:  There exists a previous position and pose of the target from some time earlier. This 
information can be used to find the current pose in a faster and maybe also more accurate way.  
 
Figure 10: Close range mode overview. 
The first situation is the most challenging one. This situation will happen at least every time the spacecraft 
moves away from the perigee at the Earth, and starts to re-acquire their formation. When they do this their 
relative pose and positions will be completely unknown. So looking at the figure the algorithm will follow 
the “Yes” branch and go the full pose and position estimation algorithm. But once the pose has been 
calculated the first time, this information can be used for each following calculations. This is seen in the 
figure as following the “No” branch and then executing the “Tracking” algorithm. However at some point 
the tracking may be lost, which will return the algorithm to the full determination method again. 
The purpose of the “Tracking” algorithm is to calculate the pose and position in a faster or more accurate 
way than the full pose determination method. It will be shown later in the theory sections that obtaining 
the pose is actually much easier if a good guess for it already exists. There will be much more about this in 
the following sections.

6.1 VBS system 
The physical hardware setup for the entire system is based on the VBS system. The VBS (Visual Based 
System) is an optical rendezvous and docking sensor developed by DTU for the PRISMA mission in 2010. 
This system is again based on the 𝜇ASC platform (micro Advanced Stellar Compass), which is also developed 
by DTU and is already used by more than 20 satellites worldwide. The 𝜇ASC is an instrument designed for 
attitude determination of a spacecraft. It works by taking an image of the sky, and then detecting all 
luminous objects in the image. All these objects are then compared to an onboard star catalogue, and 
based on this the attitude is determined. However there are usually a number of luminous objects in the 
image that are not stars, and therefore do not appear in the catalogue (this could be planets, other 
satellites, etc). The 𝜇ASC will in that case also return a list of non-stellar objects, and this is a feature that 
can be used to detect the position of the LEDs on the target spacecraft. More on this in section 11. 
The 𝜇ASC platform consists of a micro-processor and up to 4 camera head units (CHU). In the case of the 
VBS system, 2 CHUs are used. An image of this system is seen on Figure 11.  
 
Figure 11: The 𝝁ASC with two camera units [8]. 
There are many reasons why using this system as a baseline is a good idea: 
 
The physical components are heritage components. This means that they have already been flown 
on many missions and have proven their reliability. This lowers the required amount of testing and 
the overall risk of the mission.  
 
The system is already able to detect non-stellar objects, which means it will be able to detect the 
target spacecraft or the LEDs against the stars in the sky. 
 
Even with the 𝜇ASC software running, the micro-processor still has plenty of processing power 
available for the system to use.

The resolution of the images is 752 times 580 pixels. Each pixel has a size on the image plane of 8.6 𝜇𝑚 
times 8.3 𝜇𝑚. So that is the actual physical size of the pixels on the CCD of the camera. The focal length of 
one camera is 2 cm, and for the other camera it is 10 cm. A larger focal length will give a larger 
magnification, therefore the first camera is referred to as the short range camera, and the second one the 
far range camera. The VBS uses an update frequency of 4 Hz. This sets the limitation for the timing in the 
system. The algorithms must be able to complete at least 4 times pr. second for a result to be ready for 
each update refresh. 
The noise represents the uncertainty of the determined position of the object in the image. Since it is a 
digital image the accuracy is limited by the resolution of the image, in addition to noise in the system.  
However the system uses centroiding to improve it further. Centroiding means that the image taken is 
deliberately out of focus. This causes the luminous objects to appear as larger circles rather than small 
bright spots. It is then possible to locate the center of the circle as the position of the object, which makes it 
possible to have an accuracy that is better than one pixel. What the uncertainty is exactly, is hard to say. It 
depends on many factors such as the range to the target. But in the following sections a typical value of 0.2 
pixels is often used. [8] [9] 
6.2 LED design 
A very important part of the design of the cooperative spacecraft system is the choice of LEDs. If the system 
is not able to detect the LEDs, it will not be able to perform any of the desired maneuvers. As mentioned 
before this book will focus mostly on the algorithms for determining the pose and position, and not so 
much on the physical setup. Despite this it is important to include at least a brief discussion on the choice 
of LEDs. There are many parameters to consider when choosing LEDs: 
1. They must be robust. It will not be possible to replace broken LEDs, so it is very important that they 
do not break down. This also means they should be radiation hardened to withstand the hostile 
environment of space. 
2. The placement of the LEDs must be done very accurately. The pose and position of the target is 
estimated from the measured positions of the LEDs, compared to the known model of the LEDs. So 
if they are not placed accurately the model will not be correct and this will lead to errors. This is a 
quite challenging problem, but it will be left for further work. 
3. They must be visible in the space environment. LEDs are not extremely bright, and they are 
competing with another strong light source: The Sun.

7.3 Pose determination 
The system must determine the pose and position of the target w.r.t the observer. When the system first 
starts running it is immediately met with the most challenging problem: The “Lost in Space” problem. In 
this situation no information is known except for 2 things: 
 
A model of what the object looks like in 3D space is known. This is represented as M number of 3D 
points in the world frame. 
 
The camera obtains measurements of the positions of the LEDs on the target. These points are the 
projections of the locations of the LEDs in the world frame to the 2D image frame. These 
measurements are represented as N 2D points. 
The goal is then to use the N 2D points to find the pose and position of the target with the a priori 
knowledge of the 3D model. This problem basically consists of 2 sub problems: 
 
The assignment determination problem. This problem is how to determine which point in the 2D 
image corresponds to which 3D point in the model.  
 
The perspective-n-point problem (PnP). This is the problem of how to determine pose and position 
from n given correspondences between a 2D image and a 3D world model. 
Both of these problems can be solved relatively easily if the other problem is known. If the pose and 
position is already known, then the correspondences (point assignments) could be found by rotating and 
projecting the world model to the 2D image. When this is done the 2 sets of 2D points should be very 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
similar (perfectly similar if there is no noise and the pose and position is known perfectly), and therefore 
the correspondences could be found as the configuration where the inter-point distance between matches 
is minimum. However without a known pose, the image points could be a very distorted version of the 
model, because of the unknown rotations and the projection. Similarly if the correspondences were known 
the PnP problem could be solved with several methods as will be described later in section 9.2. 
In the following the basic theory behind solving the 2 problems separately is described. It is then followed 
by a section on how to determine them simultaneously, as is needed for this part of the algorithm.

situation could look something like on Figure 16.

7.4 Tracking 
Once the pose and position of the target has been determined, the system is no longer considered “Lost in 
Space”. The system must update with 4 Hz, so it must of course continue to determine poses with this 
interval. But it now has a good initial guess for the pose and position of the target. This is very 
advantageous as will be explained in the following sections. 
7.4.1 Determining the pose and assignments separately 
Calculating the pose and point correspondences simultaneously is as mentioned a complicated and time 
consuming process. But now that an initial pose is known, it is possible to solve each problem (pose and 
assignment) separately. This means that the process can be much faster. An overview of how the system 
could work is seen on Figure 21. 
 
Figure 21: Determining pose and point assignments after "Lost in Space". 
The first block is where the problems are solved simultaneously either by the SoftPOSIT algorithm or by the 
geometry implementation. As indicated on the figure this will take one refresh of the system to do (that is 
1/4 second). But then when the system wants to update the pose for the following refresh it starts on the 
“triangle” implementation instead. From the previous refresh, an initial rotation and translation vector is 
known (Ri and Ti), and these are used as parameters for the new calculations.  
The first thing that happens is that the initial rotation and translation parameters are used to re-determine 
the point correspondences in the image. For this the softassign method can be used independently of 
POSIT. This should work if it is assumed that the target has not moved too much, so that Ri and Ti are still 
mostly valid. If this assignment fails (the number of matched points is below a certain threshold), the 
system will go back to the full simultaneous algorithm, as the target is then again “Lost in Space”. But if it is 
a success it can continue to the next part. Here the newly found point correspondences are used to 
calculate a fresh pose and position for the target. Since the point correspondences are known this is 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
reduced to the PnP problem and it can be solved by a number of very fast algorithms (see section 9.2). 
Then following this the result can be further improved by an iterative algorithm or filtering. These 3 steps 
should be done every refresh, so that next time the algorithm starts at “find assignments” with the 
previous pose as the initial conditions again. 
In theory the system could continue in the “triangle” indefinitely as long as the assignment doesn’t fail. But 
it may be wise to enforce a “reset” with a certain interval. At the very least it will be done when the two 
spacecraft becomes inactive for a duration when they pass by the Earth in their orbits. 
7.4.2 Pose ambiguities 
When the PnP problem is solved in a case where all the model points are coplanar, a problem known as 
pose ambiguity appears. This ambiguity is a form of optical illusion that can be illustrated as seen on Figure 
22. It is simply a case where there are more 3-dimensional solutions to the observed 2-dimensional image. 
 
Figure 22: Pose ambiguity illustration. [27] 
It can be seen on the image that the two objects with different poses will have the same projected 
“silhouette” on the 2D image plane. Ambiguities appear in the pose and position estimation methods as 
false minima in the cost-function. The problem can also be seen by looking at Figure 23. 
 
Figure 23: Pose ambiguity planes. [23] 
On this figure it can be seen that if all the target points are in a plane (for example object plane 1), then the 
same image could have been formed if the points were in object plane 2. It can also be seen that if the 
target is rotated a certain amount relative to the parallel plane to the image plane, then the false 
ambiguous pose will have the same rotation only with the opposite sign. 
==PAGE:31==
30 
 
One thing that may make it possible to distinguish the two poses is the foreshortening that appears of the 
object on the image. When the target is rotated one side of it will be closer to the observer than the other. 
This means that the side further away will appear to be shorter than the close one. This is illustrated on 
Figure 24 and Figure 25, where the target is rotated 45° around the x-axis. In the close range situation the 
target is 1 meter away, and it can be seen that the side length of the close and far side is very different. This 
means that there is only one feasible solution to the rotation! In the long range though (this is at 100 
meters) the difference between the side lengths are much smaller. At some range the difference will be so 
small compared to the noise in the image, that it will not be possible to use this foreshortening effect to 
determine the correct pose. 
 
Figure 24: Foreshorteing close range. 
 
Figure 25: Foreshortening far range.

target itself, the projection can be said to be orthographic. [12] That means that all points will be projected 
by nearly parallel lines into the image plane, and no foreshortening will appear. In this situation there is no 
simple way to determine the correct pose from the false one.

7.4.3 Iterative solutions 
The point of this part is to improve the results from the pose determination even further by applying an 
iterative algorithm. Iterative algorithms have been used to solve least squares problems for many years. In 
fact it is how it was done originally [28]. The downside of the iterative algorithms has always been speed. It 
may take many iterations to reach the minimum, and that may be computationally expensive. Another 
downside is that these minimization techniques are not guaranteed to reach the global minimum of the 
cost function; it may move down to a local minimum and thus reach a wrong result [29]. 
However if it is assumed that a very good approximation of the pose and position already exist from a PnP 
algorithm, it must be possible to use this initial knowledge to make sure the iterative algorithm converges 
quickly and stably to the global minimum. Two iterative methods are interesting for this. They are the 
“simple” Gauss-Newton method, and the more complex Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm.

7.4.4 Filtering 
All the images taken by the system will be a number of measurements taken over an amount of time. These 
measurements will contain the positions of the LEDs, but they will also contain noise. Filtering is a very 
common approach to improve noisy measurements to get rid of the random inaccuracies caused by the 
noise. The idea is that it is possible to make a better estimate of an unknown variable with a series of 
measurements, than with a single measurement alone. This means that filtering can help getting more 
accurate pose and position estimations, but it can also help with the pose ambiguity problem! 
As described in section 7.4.2 the system will suffer from pose ambiguities if the target is further away than 
around 70 meters. If the target is further away than 70 meters, the obtained pose will sometimes be the 
==PAGE:35==
34 
 
wrong one, because the foreshortening effect becomes smaller than the noise. But if it is not too far away, 
it will still obtain the correct pose more often than the wrong one. This can be used to create a filter, or a 
sort of voting-system, to determine the correct pose over some time. 
As long as the right pose is chosen more often than the wrong one, the correct one can be found by 
measuring for a long time and then simply counting the number of times each pose was determined. Then 
the pose with the highest number should be the correct one. But this kind of voting system has the obvious 
disadvantage that it takes a long time, and it doesn’t work if the target is in motion. If the pose is constantly 
changing it will be impossible to implement it.  
Instead a common approach is to use a Kalman filter. A Kalman filter is a filter that takes into account a 
motion model of the target. So it is often used in tracking applications of planes and spacecraft. The Kalman 
filter has the before mentioned purpose of improving the accuracy by taking into account several 
measurements, but as suggested in [27] it can also be extended to help with the pose ambiguity problem.

8.1 SoftPOSIT 
8.1.1 Implementation 
This method and how to implement it is described in [20]. A Matlab implementation is made to test the 
method on modeled data. The implementation is based on an implementation by D. DeMenthon in 2003 
[36]. However the method has been modified quite a bit in order to alter the method to work in the planar 
case, as described in section 7.3.3.2. These alterations are done as described in [23]. 
==PAGE:38==
37 
 
In the implementation it is necessary to choose a number of variables and initial values that have a high 
impact on the speed and general performance of the method. Choosing the variables too strictly and the 
algorithm may outright fail in situations where it should work, but choosing them too loosely will make it 
run even slower than is necessary.  
One of these variables is 𝛼. This is the “threshold” variable that controls how far apart two points can be 
before they become unmatchable. In [20] it is suggested to set this parameter as: 
𝛼= 9.21 ∙𝜎2 
In this equation 𝜎 is the standard deviation of the noise in the position of the points. Choosing this value 
will prevent point matches being discarded wrongly with a probability of 99%. However choosing a smaller 
value will make the algorithm discard matches faster, and therefore speed up the method significantly. This 
will of course come at the cost of having more wrongly discarded matches, but it may be something that 
can be tweaked for optimal performance. For this implementation the suggested value above is chosen 
though. 
Other variables to determine are the deterministic annealing parameters 𝛽0, 𝛽𝑢𝑝𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑒, and 𝛽𝑓𝑖𝑛𝑎𝑙. In section 
7.3.3 the variable 𝛽 was introduced in the deterministic annealing process. The role of the parameter is to 
start out small (which makes the algorithm not immediately converge to a possible wrong solution) and 
then slowly grow with every iteration until it reaches a final value. This is controlled by the three 
parameters mentioned above. 𝛽0 is the small starting value of 𝛽, 𝛽𝑢𝑝𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑒 controls how quickly 𝛽 grows 
(every iteration 𝛽 is multiplied by 𝛽𝑢𝑝𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑒), and finally the process is stopped when 𝛽 reaches the value 
𝛽𝑓𝑖𝑛𝑎𝑙. 
For these variables [22] suggests the following values: 
𝛽0 = 0.01/𝑆 
𝛽𝑢𝑝𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑒= 1.053 
𝛽𝑓𝑖𝑛𝑎𝑙= 100/𝑆 
Here S is defined as the squared mean distance between points in the image frame. In this implementation 
experiments have shown that this value for 𝛽0 is not so good. Instead 𝛽0 = 0.1/𝑆 is used. The same value 
for 𝛽𝑢𝑝𝑑𝑎𝑡𝑒 is used though, and for 𝛽𝑓𝑖𝑛𝑎𝑙 the value 𝛽𝑓𝑖𝑛𝑎𝑙= 10000 ∙𝛽0 is used, which is similar to how it is 
suggested above. 
One of the challenges with the SoftPOSIT algorithm is that it requires an initial guess for the pose and 
position of the target. This was described in more details in sections 7.3.3.2 and 7.4.2. So every time this 
method is run a pose and position must be chosen as the initial parameters. If the system is truly “Lost in 
Space” then no such initial information is available. Therefore there is no other option than to run the 
algorithm several times with different initial parameters and then find out which one give the best result. 
This is not necessarily a problem, but it could take a long time. Therefore the initial poses must be chosen 
carefully, in order to make sure that at least one of them will converge to the right pose. In other words the 
initial poses must cover all possible rotations and translations of the target. At the same time there should 
==PAGE:39==
38 
 
be as few initial conditions as possible to improve the speed. Then when SoftPOSIT has been run with all 
the initial conditions, the result with the smallest residual error can be chosen as the correct result.  
How to choose the initial conditions depend a lot on how the results of the algorithm depend on these 
initial guesses. That means: how close must the initial guess be for the method to converge to the correct 
result? This will be examined in the following test section.

Pose ambiguity 
One of the major challenges in this whole project is the pose ambiguity problem. So it is interesting to see 
how this method performs in that regard. As mentioned the algorithm is actually implemented to return

8.2 Geometry method 
8.2.1 Implementation 
In the theory section it was shown that trying out all possible point assignments would give 40320 
possibilities. To reduce this, the first thing that can be done is to split the points into two groups. The first 
group is the “cluster points” which consist of the 5 points in the corner of the model. The other group is the 
“outer points” which is the remaining 3. It is relatively easy to separate these points because there is an 
obvious difference between them: The cluster points have a lower mean distance to all the points in the 
model than the outer points. The two groups are illustrated on Figure 37. 
 
Figure 37: The points are sorted into the cluster group or the outer group. 
So this sorting is implemented by calculating the distance from every point to every other point, and then 
taking the mean of these distances for each point. This mean distance is then compared to a threshold 
value to see if the point should belong to the cluster group or the outer group. The threshold used is the 
mean of the mean of the distances. That is, each point has a mean distance to all other points, and the 
threshold is then the mean of these distances for all points. Through experimentation it is found that the 
method works better if this threshold is further multiplied by 0.8. 
==PAGE:48==
47 
 
Doing this grouping immediately reduces the number of possible assignments, because it is known that the 
outer points must correspond to LED number 1, 2, and 3. Likewise the cluster points must correspond to 4, 
5, 6, 7, and 8. This means that the number of possibilities now is: 
3! ∙5! = 6 ∙120 = 720 
But the algorithm can be improved much more. To help make a unique determination of the 3 outer points, 
a helping point is constructed as the mean of all the cluster points. This is seen on Figure 38 as the “mean 
cluster point”. The vectors from the mean cluster point to each of the 3 outer points are then constructed. 
A random vector is then chosen as the baseline, and the angle between it and the other 2 vectors are 
found. Since the angles will be positive in the counter-clockwise rotation, the LED with the number 1 is then 
known to correspond to the point with the lowest angle (the blue vector), LED 2 to the intermediate angle, 
and LED 3 to the largest angle. This works no matter which vector is chosen as the baseline. For example if 
the blue vector is baseline, the angle to itself is 0°, to the orange it is some positive angle, and to the yellow 
it is a larger positive angle. So the blue vector will have the smallest angle. If on the other hand the orange 
vector is chosen as baseline, then the blue vector will have a negative angle, the orange will have 0°, and 
the yellow will have a positive angle. So once again the blue vector will have the minimum angle. 
 
Figure 38: Outer LEDs are determined by the angles of the vectors from the mean cluster point to them. 
This means that the 3 outer points can be determined without any need for guessing. A similar thing can be 
done for the cluster points. But first a reference point must be used again. This time the point 
corresponding to LED 4 is found by finding the point that has the largest mean distance to the 3 outer 
points. This point is labeled the corner point as seen on Figure 39. Then just as before the vectors from this 
point to the other points are drawn. The correspondences can then be found once again by calculating the 
angles between a random vector as baseline and the other vectors. This time there will be two vectors with 
intermediate angles though. In the figure they are overlapping, but they might not do that in a real 
situation because of noise. In any case the two intermediate vectors can be distinguished by their length. 
The vector with the smallest norm is known to point to LED 8, and the longer one points to LED 7.   
==PAGE:49==
48 
 
 
Figure 39: Determining the correspondences of the cluster points. 
With this done all the correspondences have been determined without any need to run through several 
thousand guesses. So now one of the simple PnP methods can be used to determine the pose and position 
of the target. 
There is a major issue with the above implementation though. It is not robust if the system should detect 
false points, or if some LEDs fails to be detected. For example look again at Figure 39, if there is 1 random 
point added to the image, the whole method of making vectors and looking at the angles will fail. 
This can be solved by going back to the method of trying out all possibilities. If for example there is one 
extra point detected as an outer point, then there are 4 choose 3 possibilities for choosing the 3 points out 
of the 4 outer ones. Similarly if there is 1 extra point detected as a cluster point, there will be 6 choose 5 
possibilities for those. So all in all the number of possibilities will be: 
(4
3) ∙(6
5) = 4 ∙6 = 24 
Remember that right now it is not attempted to guess which point correspond to which LED exactly. It is 
only attempted to guess which 3 out of 4 outer points are the “real” ones, and which 5 out of 6 cluster 
points are the “real” ones. This is the reason for the massive reduction in possibilities. Then for each of 
these possibilities the whole geometry method is used, and the one with the best result is chosen as the 
right one. 24 possibilities are not very many, so it should easily be possible to calculate them all in time.  
The numbers of possibilities do grow exponentially though. For example if there are two fake outer- and 
cluster-points, the number of possibilities will be: 
(5
3) ∙(7
5) = 10 ∙21 = 210 
So it is still very desirable to have as few fake points as possible. 
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
If there is a missing points the problem is different. If for example there are only 2 outer points detected, 
the geometry method with the angles becomes impossible to use. However in that case there will only be 3 
choose 2 possibilities of choosing the 2 right LEDs which equals 3. So it will probably be possible to simply 
go through all correspondence possibilities. The real headache is if there are both fake and missing points. 
If 3 outer points are detected, but in fact the 3rd point is actually fake, and one of the real points is missing, 
then the algorithm will get a wrong result.  
Therefore this is a strong need for a way to tell the right points from the fake ones. One way to do this is to 
have the LEDs blink with a certain interval. That would be a good way to make them easily distinguishable 
from other objects in the image.

9.2 Perspective-n-Point Problem 
Once the correspondences have been found with the softassign or geometry method, the pose and 
position can be recalculated for the new refresh iteration. The pose and position determination problem 
with known correspondences is known as the Perspective-n-Point Problem and is usually abbreviated PnP. 
There is a large body of work on this specific problem, so choosing the best method for this is not trivial. 
Therefore several methods are tested and compared in the following sections. 
9.2.1 DLS method 
This method is originally proposed in the paper “A Direct Least-Squares (DLS) Method for PnP” by Hersch 
and Roumeliotis. [15] The method is a way to solve the minimization problem described in section 7.3.1 
without requiring iterations or initial guesses. 
The method works by relaxing the optimization problem, and then manipulating the equations to reduce 
the number of unknowns. This makes is possible to solve the minimization problem analytically, and to find 
all pose solutions in cases where the number of points is greater than 3. The math will not be explained 
here, but can be studied in greater detail in the paper. 
In short the main advantages/disadvantages of the method are as follows: 
 
It computes all pose solutions in the case of 𝑛≥3 points. This means that in case of pose 
ambiguity it will return both possible poses. 
 
The method is scalable. The modified minimization problem is not dependent on the number of 
points. 
 
The method does not require initialization or iterations. Which means it is much faster than 
iterative methods. 
The test is done using the implementation supplied by the creators of the method. [15] 
9.2.1.1 Rotation accuracy test

9.2.2 RPnP method 
In this section the RPnP method is tested. The method is proposed in the paper “A Robust O(n) Solution to 
the Perspective-n-Point Problem” by Li et al. [31] 
The method uses a slightly different way to set up the minimization problem. It divides the reference points 
into 3-point subsets in order to achieve a series of fourth order polynomials. Furthermore it uses an 
intermediate orthogonal coordinate frame, which allows the cost problem to be reduced to a linear 
equation system, reducing the computational complexity. The details of this can be found in the paper. 
The cost function may still have several solutions, but this method simply chooses the camera pose that has 
the least projection residual error as the correct one (that is the least error in the cost function). This means 
that the algorithm always return only one solution. 
The method should work well in both planar and non-planar conditions, and with any number of points 𝑛≥
4. Because it is non-iterative it should also be quite fast, and the speed is only linearly proportional to the 
number of points.  
The test is done with an implementation supplied by the creators of the method. [31]

9.2.3 EPnP method 
The EPnP method was proposed in the paper “EPnP: An Accurate O(n) Solution to the PnP Problem” by 
Lepetit et al. [32] This paper also proposes a method called “EPnP+GN”. This method is actually the same 
one, where EPnP is used to find an initial guess, and then a Gauss-Newton (GN) scheme is used to improve 
accuracy further. But this algorithm only has an effect if the target points are not coplanar, and in this case 
they are, so this will be omitted. 
This method also takes another approach to the PnP problem. It expresses the n 3-dimensional points as a 
weighted sum of 4 control points. This then reduces the problem to estimating the position of these control 
points in the camera frame. The advantage of this is that it can be done in a time that is only linearly 
dependent on the number of points. The details of this method can be found in the paper. 
The method should work for any number of points 𝑛≥4, and it should be quite fast since the complexity 
only increases linearly with the number of points as mentioned. It also originally did not work for planar 
configurations, but a separate implementation (supplied by the creators of the method) exists where it 
does. It is this implementation that is used for the test. [32]

9.2.4 LUT method 
This method was presented in the paper “Efficient lookup table based camera pose estimation for 
augmented reality” by Li and Xu. [6] 
This method is quite different from the other tested ones. This method is designed to estimate the pose 
and position of a square target only. That means that the number of points n is always 4, and they must be 
placed in a square with an equal side-lengths. The idea of the method is to exploit the additional 
information that the target is a square by creating a lookup table and using this for very fast computations.  
The details of this can be found in the paper.  
This may not seem very applicable to a space mission, but it can give a good indication of how good results 
can be achieved if only the simplest setup is available, that is 4 points in a square. It should also be the 
fastest method yet, and time is definitely critical in many space applications. 
Generating the lookup table takes time of course, but this needs only be done once. So the only major 
drawback is the additional storage space required for the table. [6]

9.3 Iterative improvement 
Now that a quite accurate pose and position has been found by solving the PnP problem, it must be tested 
whether the result can be improved further by an additional iterative algorithm. Two methods are tested: 
Gauss-Newton and Levenberg-Marqaurdt. 
9.3.1 Gauss-Newton 
9.3.1.1 Implementation  
The implementation of the method follows an implementation done by Lowe [33]. For full details examine 
his paper. But a quick explanation is presented in the following. 
The first thing to do in order to implement the Gauss-Newton method is to determine the Jacobian for the 
system. This is however not necessarily an easy thing to do. As mentioned earlier the measured values of 
the system are the locations of the 2D points in the image plane. These points are then related by a 
==PAGE:74==
73 
 
rotation and translation to the known 3D model points. This rotation and translation is unknown, and it is 
the goal of it all to find them.

Jacobian and start another iteration.

This state is entered when the corrections are considered too small to really improve the result. Once this is 
reached the algorithm is done and the final result is presented.

9.3.2 Levenberg-Marquardt 
From the results of the Gauss-Newton algorithm it has become apparent that the Levenberg-Marquardt 
method is not really suitable for this situation. Recall from section 7.4.3.2 that when the algorithm is far 
from the optimal solution, it uses a trust-region approach, but when it gets closer it actually becomes like a 
normal Gauss-Newton method (𝜆 becomes small). This means that Levenberg-Marquardt cannot be 
expected to have a better/more accurate convergence than GN. The advantage of the method is supposed 
to be its convergence speed and ability to avoid false minima, not its accuracy. So the accuracies seen in 
Figure 67 and Figure 68 should be exactly the same for this method.  
Then when looking at the computation speed of GN (Table 30), it can be seen that it normally only needs 1 
iteration to converge to the result. Therefore it does not seem like an advantage to start with the trust-
region approach at all since it already converges so quickly. It only makes the algorithm more complicated 
than it needs to be. 
So to conclude the Levenberg-Marquardt method is not suitable in this situation, because very good initial 
pose and position estimations already exist from other methods like SoftPOSIT or RPnP. This makes the 
entire trust-region functionality redundant. So the method is not implemented and tested.

11 Range modes 
The measurement system has the main goal of determining the pose and position of the target spacecraft. 
But it is clear that this will not be possible at all ranges. In fact the pose and position estimation is only 
possible at a relatively small range interval. When the target is outside of this range, the task of the system 
is then to realize that it is out of range (or too close), and then obtain whatever information that still is 
available at the given range. There are many different situations that will require the system to operate in 
certain ways: 
 
Far range: When the target is so far away that it will not be possible to see or distinguish the 
individual LEDs. In this case no pose information can be obtained. It will be possible to estimate a 
range based on the visible area of the target, or the radiance, but no accurate distance can be 
found. Information about the lateral position can be found though, but since the distance is 
uncertain it is only known as a general direction. 
 
Intermediate range: This range is defined as a distance where it is possible to distinguish at least 
the 4 corner LEDs. This makes it possible to estimate the pose and range of the target, but not very 
accurately as only 4 LEDs are used. 
 
Close range A: This is the normal operating mode of the system. Here all LEDs are visible and the 
pose and translation can be determined in both the lateral and depth directions. High accuracy is 
expected. 
 
Close range B: The system will enter this mode when the target gets so close the LEDs will 
disappear out of the field of vision (FoV) of the camera. In this mode the close range CHU will take 
over from the far range one. Meaning that the focal length will be less. 
 
Docking range: In this range the target is so close that parts of it disappear out of the field of vision 
even with the close range CHU. In this mode the cluster square must still be visible (LEDs 4, 5, 6, 7, 
and 8). It will function as a sort of “docking pattern”. 
How the system should operate under all these conditions is analyzed in more detail in the following 
sections. 
11.1 Far range

target will appear as a single luminous object. This could either be because the projected area of the

11.7 Mode switching and acquisition algorithm. 
In the previous section a number of different range modes were introduced. They all vary in procedures 
and operating ranges, so how can the system move from range mode to range mode in a robust way? And 
how can the system determine which mode it should be in at certain times? Most problematic is as usual 
the “Lost in Space” situation. Here there is no knowledge about the position of the target, so it is unknown 
what to look for exactly. 
Concerning the “Lost in Space” situation, it is clear that the system cannot function completely 
independently in this situation. In order for the vision based system to work at all, the target must at least 
be within the field of view of the camera. Therefore some outside navigation method must be used to get a 
very rough estimate of the relative position. An obvious way to do this is to use the GPS system, as is 
==PAGE:96==
95 
 
already planned for the PROBA-3 mission in the first place. The “Lost in Space” problem will typically occur 
as the two spacecraft starts moving away from the perigee of their orbits towards their apogee. At this time 
they are both close to Earth so the GPS signal should be available. While at the perigee the spacecraft will 
be inactive, so when they start moving away they will need to re-acquire their relative positions every time. 
The crafts can then use the GPS information to form an approximate formation that should be a line 
pointing towards the sun (as seen in Figure 7). Since the GPS positioning is not that accurate far away from 
the Earth (± 10 𝑚), the two spacecraft should be at a safe distance to avoid any risk of crashing. Also with 
that accuracy to make sure the target is indeed within the field of view the distance must be at least: 
tan(2.76°) = 10 𝑚

↔𝑟= 207 𝑚 
This means that with the GPS information the crafts are able to position themselves in a formation where 
the CSC is guaranteed to be within field of view, as long as the distance is larger than 207 meters (if the OSC 
is pointing in the right direction that is!). So from this it is known that the system must start out in the far 
range mode to acquire the position of the target spacecraft. To be on the safe side it is probably best to aim 
for a starting separation of around 300 meters.  
In far range it is also necessary for the OSC to point towards the CSC in order to actually have it inside the 
field of view. Later in the closer ranges the CSC must also point towards the OSC in order for it to detect the 
LEDs. How can this be achieved? Since they have the formation as seen on Figure 7, one way to do it could 
be to detect the Sun. The Sun is very bright compared to everything else, so it should be very easy to detect 
it uniquely. So the CSC could simply look for the sun and then point towards it. OSC could then look for the 
sun and then rotate to look directly away from it. This should ensure that the crafts are looking more or less 
towards each other. If this is done the pointing accuracy might not be very high, so it will probably be best 
to go for an even further range to ensure the target is within field of view still. 
Another method could be to use the μASC functionality (see section 6.1) to find the attitude of each 
spacecraft. That is not the relative pose, but the pose in a common reference system. This information 
could then be used to determine the rotations necessary to fix the field of view.  
In any case it should be possible to establish this coarse low accuracy formation with the help of GPS. This 
situation will then be the starting point of the range modes. Since the crafts must be separated quite far to 
begin with, the system will start out in “Far range”-mode. Depending on the mission, the desired formation 
will then be acquired as summarized in Figure 82. Each part will be explained in a bit more detail below.

Target acquisition 
This is where the algorithm starts. Because of the GPS positioning it is assumed that the CSC is within the 
field of view (FoV) of the OSC. The target will only be a single bright dot at this range, and there may be 
many unidentified bright objects seen by the camera. But from the GPS data it should be able approximate 
which object would be the target. The system itself can also determine direction and approximate range at 
this point, so this data can be cross-checked with the GPS data to make a robust target acquisition. Once 
the target is detected the crafts will move closer together, and move to the next stage of the acquisition 
algorithm. 
(Intermediate range) 
In the range mode section (11) the intermediate range was introduced, but it may not really be needed. 
The idea of it is that some pose and position information can be determined before “close range A” mode 
goes into effect. This is in a range interval of about 150 meters to 2km. But according to the calculations it 
might not be possible to actually distinguish the LEDs at this distance. Also it is not certain what this mode 
will be useful for in the mission. So it is omitted in this acquisition algorithm. 
Pose and position acquisition  
Once the OSC and CSC are around 200 meters apart the mode switches to “Close range A”. Now all the 
LEDs can be detected separately, and therefore the PnP and point correspondence algorithms can be used 
to find the pose and position of the spacecraft with high accuracy. One problem is that the pose cannot be 
determined uniquely at this range. This can be solved by moving closer to the target until it can be resolved 
(70 meters). Then once this pose is known it can be used as an initial condition to track the pose as the 
target moves further away again, for example by use of a Kalman filter (section 7.4.4). 
==PAGE:98==
97 
 
What will happen next depends on what the mission objectives are. If the system should perform its 
scientific mission it should go to the “Coronograph” mode. Otherwise if it wants to do formation flying 
maneuvers, it will proceed to “Docking” or some other procedure mode. 
Docking 
In this mode the spacecraft will attempts a sort of docking maneuver, which just means that they will fly 
very close to each other and maintain an accurate relative formation. To do this the craft will move closer 
and switch to “Close range B” mode at 8 meters distance. This will allow it to move even closer and switch 
to “Docking” mode at 2 meters. Finally in this mode it will be able to get as close as 25 cm. 
Coronograph 
This is the mode where the scientific mission will be performed. That means that the OSC and CSC will 
maintain an accurate relative position with a distance of 150 meters, to function as a single coronograph 
instrument. To do this the algorithm will stay in “Close range A” mode. It can first move closer to resolve 
the pose ambiguity, and then go back to the 150 meters range. But maybe in this mode the pose ambiguity 
is not so important, as the desired relative pose is probably always 0° in all rotations. When there is no 
rotation there is no ambiguity anyway. 
 
(Other maneuvers) 
The mission has a goal to perform many other formation flying tests, but it is not within the scope of this 
project to go through them all. However this algorithm can quite easily be extended to perform several 
other forms of maneuvers when in the “Close range A” mode.  
 
Some final thoughts about the acquisition algorithm are concerning robustness if the target is for some 
reason lost. If for example the pose and position of the target is tracked at 150 meters and the pose is 
suddenly lost. Then the system must simply go back a step and move the target to 70 meters to re-acquire 
the pose and the move back. If the whole target is lost at some point, it must go back to the GPS 
measurements to re-acquire the target. As a worst case scenario the system must wait for the orbit to 
return close to earth to regain the GPS measurements, and such a whole orbit could be lost. But this should 
be worst case scenario. 
12 Proposed system and final tests 
Many different algorithms for many things have been analyzed and tested in this book. In this section the 
most promising ones will be combined into a suggested system for PROBA-3 mission. First this system will 
be described, and then after that some tests will be done to finally determine expected accuracies and 
robustness of the system. 
12.1 Proposed system 
Most of the algorithms described are concerned with the close range modes. The challenge of locating the 
correct object in the image at far range is also very interesting, but this is not covered in this book. 
Therefore the focus here will be to go back to Figure 10, and examine what algorithms can perform those 
tasks in the best way. 
 
==PAGE:99==

12.1.1 Full pose/position estimation system 
For this part there are two possibilities: 
 
SoftPOSIT can be used to determine the PnP and point correspondence problem simultaneously. As 
tested this is quite robust to fake or missing points, and will work for pretty much all rotations. 
However it is very slow, and it also requires an initial position that may require the algorithm to be 
run several times in order to cover all possible poses/positions. 
 
The correspondence problem can be solved with the discussed geometric solution, and then the 
PnP problem can be solved by any of the much faster PnP algorithms such as RPnP. This is much 
faster than SoftPOSIT, but it less robust to fake or missing points. It also requires that the rotation 
of the target is not too extreme.  
From the discussion in section 11.7, it is revealed that an initial pose might actually be available if the 
attitude of the crafts is set either by finding the sun, or from using the 𝜇𝐴𝑆𝐶. This can have high or low 
accuracy (𝜇𝐴𝑆𝐶 could potentially give very high accuracy), but either way it should be good enough to form 
a good initial position for SoftPOSIT. It is also an advantage for the geometry solution though, as the 
relative pose can then be set to be within the approximately −60° to +60° rotation around both the x- and 
y-axis that this method requires.  
Considering this it seems like the geometry solution with RPnP is the best solution to the problem. 
SoftPOSIT is simply too slow (at least 100 times slower), and the geometry method seem to be a much 
smarter solution. In case of fake points the algorithm must run through several possibilities, which can 
increase the computation speed, but there is still a long way up to SoftPOSIT. 
To reduce the problem of fake and extra points it is also recommended to implement some cooperative 
way to detect the LEDs easier, for example they could be blinking with a certain interval. 
In section 11.7 it was also discussed that there is no way to resolve the pose ambiguity if the target is 
further away than 70 meters (not without using some outside information at least). So this part of the 
algorithm will simply leave the pose as ambiguous if the range is too far. This must then be solved by 
following the pose and position acquisition algorithm as seen in Figure 82.

12.1.2 Tracking 
The tracking part of the system was discussed in section 7.4 (see Figure 21). There are also a number of 
possibilities for the different parts here. They will all be described in the following. 
Correspondence problem 
For the point correspondence part the two possibilities are: 
 
The geometry solution. The same method for the “Lost in space” situation could simply be 
repeated. It has again the drawback of not dealing too well with fake points, and it also doesn’t 
work for high rotations. 
 
Softasssign. The method that was half of the SoftPOSIT algorithm could be used independently to 
find the point correspondences. This is quite slow compared to the geometry solution, but it 
generally performs better and is more robust.  
Which one should be chosen depend a lot on how many fake objects is expected. But without this 
knowledge the best solution here is chosen to be Softassign. This is because it may be desirable to have the 
relative pose be very high at certain points. For the “Lost in Space” situation it is fine that the pose cannot 
be too extreme, since it is only used once to acquire the pose. For tracking however it would be nice if the 
target can be allowed to rotate more without losing the track, so Softassign is considered better here.

Perspective-n-Point problem 
As examined in section 9.2 there are a number of methods to solve this problem. It was also discussed in 
that section that the RPnP method seems to have the edge over the other methods. It is not the fastest 
method, but it is one of the faster ones, and it has high accuracy and high robustness. Therefore this part is 
simply to apply the RPnP method on the point correspondences found in the previous step. 
Filtering/Iterative improvement 
In section 7.4.3 two methods to iteratively improve the pose and position results was discussed. Those 
were the Gauss-Newton and Levenberg-Marquardt methods. But as was already concluded in section 9.3.2 
the Levenberg-Marquardt method did not really add anything compared to Gauss-Newton. So clearly the 
Gauss-Newton iterative algorithm is the best one considered. The tests showed that the iterative algorithm 
did improve the accuracy significantly, but at the price of computation speed. The added speed was only 
about 0.3 ms, so it is still quite fast. All in all there does not seem to be any good reason to not improve the 
result with this iterative algorithm.  
For the filtering the Kalman filter was considered and tested in section 9.4. This filter proved quite effective 
at robustly determining the pose. But it has drawbacks such as slow reaction time to pose changes, and 
added computation speed. If the filter could improve the accuracy (and by how much) was never truly 
tested, so that cannot speak either for or against the implementation. It is clear that if the pose must be 
known unambiguously at distances higher than 70 meters, the implementation of at least a similar filter 
must be done. Therefore it is finally recommended to implement the filter, so it can at least be used for the 
pose and position acquisition phase as seen in Figure 82.

13 Future works 
All the work done in this project is about exploring the possibilities of a vision based pose and position 
estimation system. Many algorithms have been tested, and an analysis of the most optimal system has 
been made. But to actually implement a final system there is a long way to go. In the following a number of 
challenges are mentioned that must be solved on the path to a fully functional formation flying system 
based on VBS. 
To fully prove the concept some real physical tests must be made. That means that the algorithms must be 
implemented on a micro-processor (must likely the 𝜇ASC platform), and some tests must be run on real 
images. This is a task that should be relatively straightforward, although time consuming. 
In section 6.2 the overall design of the LEDs for the system was briefly discussed. But a much more in depth 
analysis of this is needed to ensure the right LEDs are used for the mission. Another challenge is to 
determine the exact “model” of the LEDs used in the algorithms. The positions of the LEDs are measured 
with 𝜇𝑚 precision, but how do we know the actual relative position of the LEDs in the model with such high 
accuracy? It is not easy to place the LEDs so precisely, and after that the distance will vary with things like 
thermal expansion. The apparent position of the LEDs may also change if they are rotated etc.  
It has been mentioned a few times in the book that acquiring the LEDs can be challenging if there is a lot of 
fake and/or missing points in the image. It has been shown that this can both cause a reduction in accuracy 
or in worse cases a completely wrong pose determination. So it would be a good addition to the system if 
there was some way to distinguish the real LEDs from other points. As suggested a few times one way to do 
==PAGE:105==
104 
 
this could be to have the LEDs blink with some interval, but how exactly the details of this should be is an 
open question. There may also be other methods to this that should be examined. For example one 
spacecraft could tell the other which LED is which by having them blink in a certain order. 
The possibilities of using a Kalman filter (or similar) was shortly explored, and it proved quite promising. But 
a true implementation and test was never done. If the accuracy must be improved this could be one way to 
do it. It also showed great potential in solving the pose ambiguity problem. 
Finally there are many choices in the system that depends on what the exact mission is. PROBA-3 is a 
demonstration mission so it does not have a lot of strict requirements for precision and control. But for a 
mission that would have this, the methods may have to be changed accordingly. For example better speed 
might be required, but lower accuracy needed, or vice versa. It may also be necessary to change the 
physical properties of the camera like the focal length if higher accuracy is needed at a certain range.   
14 Conclusion 
In this book the prospect of creating a vision based system for pose and position estimation for formation 
flying spacecraft was examined. Since the main focus of the task was to evaluate different algorithms, it 
was all done as Matlab simulations. Any physical implementations or tests were therefore considered out 
of the scope of the project. It was found that the pose can indeed be determined by measuring the 
positions of LEDs with a camera, and then comparing these 2D points with the known 3D model of the 
target. 
Solving this problem consisted of two parts:  
1. The point correspondence determination problem, which was about finding which point on the 2D 
image corresponded to which point in the model. 
2. The perspective-n-point problem, which used the found correspondences to determine the 
rotation and translation vector that best fit the 3D model with the measured 2D points. 
Solving each of the problems separately was relatively easy, and many algorithms already exist to do this. 
But when they must be solved simultaneously it can be challenging and computationally heavy. The 
SoftPOSIT method was implemented and tested for this and it proved robust, yet slow. Another 
implementation was designed that exploited the geometry of the LED pattern to determine the 
correspondences much faster. This method was very fast but less robust to fake/missing points. Calculating 
the pose and position for the first time was named the “Lost in Space” situation, and it is a problem that 
must be solved at least once for every orbit of the PROBA-3 mission.  
Once the pose has been found one time, it was shown that calculating consecutive poses was much easier 
as this initial pose can be used to solve problem 1 and 2 separately. This was exploited to design a much 
faster “Tracking” algorithm. In this algorithm the correspondence problem was solved by the Softassign 
method. Many algorithms for solving the perspective-n-point problem was tested, but in the end the RPnP 
method was chosen as the best one. In the “Tracking” part the system was also extended to include an 
iterative algorithm to further improve the accuracy. The Gauss-Newton method was implemented and 
tested to do this. 
==PAGE:106==
105 
 
In the project the challenge of pose ambiguities was also demonstrated. It was found that if the spacecraft 
are less than 70 meters apart the pose ambiguity could be resolved by looking at the foreshortening effect. 
However when further away it was not possible. In that situation a Kalman filter was tested to ensure 
stable pose determination, and it showed promising results, although it was never fully implemented. 
The proposed system was intended to be an improvement over the previously developed VBS system. This 
was a success as the mean translation error of the proposed system at 5 meters distance was around 
39 𝜇𝑚 in the lateral directions and 0.4 𝑚𝑚 in the longitudinal. The original VBS system has a typical 
accuracy of 100 𝜇𝑚 lateral and 0.5 𝑚𝑚 longitudinal. However at 150 meters distance the system was not 
able to obtain high enough accuracy for the coronagraph part of the PROBA-3 mission to work. The mean 
accuracy error at that range was 2.4 𝑚𝑚 lateral and 0.39 𝑚 longitudinal, and the requirements were 1 𝑚𝑚 
lateral. The mean pose error at this far range was measured to be 1.05°. 
Speed is also a very important parameter for the algorithms, so it has also been tested thoroughly. The 
proposed system had a computation speed of 89 ms for the tracking part. But if the geometry solution 
would be used instead of Softassign it could be reduced to below 5 ms. It is not known how long any of 
these algorithms will really take on a micro-processor implementation though, so they are only rough 
estimates.  
A number of different “range modes” were analyzed and established to determine how the system will 
work at different ranges. The main focus was on the close range modes though. A target acquisition 
algorithm was also proposed to show how the target can be acquired first in the far range, and then later 
the LEDs can be detected in the close range.
