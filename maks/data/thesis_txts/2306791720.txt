Summary
This report describes the topology optimization method applied to the design of elec-
trothermomechanical actuators, speciﬁcally intended for the use in microelectrome-
chanical systems (MEMS) operated in a vacuum. The theory and implementation
of adding the electrothermal problem to an existing ﬁnite element method (FEM)
code is covered, in addition to applying the adjoint sensitivity analysis in order to
create the gradients for the optimization problem. The method of moving asymptotes
(MMA) has then been used for solving the optimization problem. A ﬁlter has been
added onto the topology optimized shape, which results in the ﬁnal design being more
robust and thus more realistic for production and physical function. Finally, several
examples have been computed and the results have been discussed in order to verify
their validity and the inﬂuence of design variables.
Resumé
Denne rapport beskriver topologioptimeringsmetoden benytted til at designe elek-
trotermomekaniske aktuatore med det speciﬁkke formål at benyttes inden for mikroelek-
tromekaniske systemer (MEMS), der arbejder i et vakum. Teorien og implementerin-
gen af at tilføje det elektrotermiske problem til en eksisterende ﬁnite element method
(FEM) kode dækkes, samt påførelsen af ”adjoint sensitivity” analysen, der benyttes
til at ﬁnde gradienterne for det optimerede problem. ”Method of moving asymptotes”
(MMA) benyttes til at løse optimeringsproblemet. Et ﬁlter er blevet tilføjet til den
topologi optimerede form, hvilket resultere i at det endelige design er mere robust og
er derfor mere realistisk med hensyn til produktion og fysisk udfoldelse. Endeligt er
samtlige eksempler på topologi optimerede designs blevet fremført og disse resultater
diskuteres for at afgøre deres validitet, samt eﬀekten af design variable.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The motivation behind the project was largely to combine the unique properties of
materials at the microscopic scale, with the eﬃciency of the topology (shape) opti-
mization process. In particular the large impact of volume dependant properties such
as thermal expansion and the diminished impact of surface-area related properties
such as heat loss. This allows heat to be used as an eﬀective force in micro mechani-
cal systems (MEMS), instead of using conventional motors as would be the norm on
a normal scale.
It was decided to make a topology optimized electrothermomechanical micro actua-
tor; eﬀectively an arm in microscale, driven by heat expansion, which is created by
an electric current passing through the structure as demonstrated in ﬁgure 1.1.
The purpose of this micro actuator is to eﬃciently move an atomic force microscope
(AFM) inside a micro chip; scanning the surface. The DTU nano-tech project, also
known as ”Lab on a chip”, inspired this actuator as their microscopic lab is enclosed
in a vacuum. They wanted a way to monitor the lab without having to open the lab
and have an external actuator move the AFM around on the chip. By implementing
a micro actuator inside the chip, they would be able to constantly monitor the micro-
scopic lab, to ensure that it is working as intended. Scanning with the AFM requires
the actuator to move in three axes. This report will only cover an actuator designed
for moving in one axis, but through further work, movement in three axes can be
achieved. The actuator will use its electrothermomechanical properties to move in
the X-Y plane, and could use a piezo-electric material to bend this plane, moving it
in the Z-axis.
When designing MEMS, the structure usually has to be two-dimensional due to the

Topology optimization has been around for a while and has been used for various

not work if the structure was manufactured and used in the physical world.
Since then a robust topology optimization formulation based on eroded, intermediate
and dilated projections of the design has been proposed [10]. This method solves the
topology optimization of the desired structure along with ensuring that a structure
which is in a slightly eroded or dilated state from the original are also optimized as
seen in ﬁgure 1.3.

During the topology optimization a work space is deﬁned, consisting of numerous dif-
ferent elements as per the FEM. The amount of elements used in this method can vary.
A larger number of elements is desired to ensure a converging and correct solution,
but as the number of elements increase, so does the computation time substantially.
The elements of this work space is designated with an initially evenly distributed
material density throughout the structure, along with its boundary conditions.
The design variable for solving the problem then becomes the material density of each
element. Designing the structure is thus a question of distributing the material den-
sity. This is done in regards to optimizing the objective function, which in this case is
the maximum displacement in a single direction. In order to optimize this objective
function it is then needed to calculate the gradient of this objective function with re-
gard to an elements density, for each element. These gradients will show whether an
increase or decrease in density for each individual element will improve the objective
function. These derivatives are calculated based on the adjoint method and they are
solved in an iterative process using the method of moving asymptotes (MMA)[9]. In
order to implement the robust design solution[10], it will be necessary to calculate the
objective function and their derivatives of three diﬀerent cases (eroded, intermediate
and dilated) as displayed in ﬁgure 1.3 before optimizing the design through the MMA
solver.

2.4
Optimization
Topology optimization of a structures design is generally done by redistributing mate-
rial among the elements in order to minimize the objective function. This distribution
of material is done by varying the relative density of each element ρe coinciding with
the FEA.
This density can vary between the value of 0, meaning no material; and 1, meaning
solid material. Everything in between is a theoretical ”spunge-like” material, which
is partly solid and thus only partly contributes with the stiﬀness, heat and electri-
cal conductivity. In order to solve the problem, there has to be these non-binary
in-between values such that the solver can converge on the optimal design. Unfor-
tunately, this way the optimal design will always contain elements which have these
non-binary values. To avoid converging on results that rely heavily on non-binary
densities a material interpolation scheme is made. To do this the density of each
element is raised to the power of a ”penalty” factor p (ρp
e). This factor is selected in
accordance to the power-law approach, which is also called the SIMP (Solid Isotropic
Microstructure with Penalization) method.

The optimization is done using the MMA solver [9] and this solver will try to min-
imize the objective problem. For an actuator the objective is however to maximize
displacement. This is a non-issue as minimizing the displacement will simply causes
a ”maximum” displacement in the negative direction. By deﬁning the negative direc-
tion as the one where displacement is required everything works as intended albeit
with negative displacement values.
However, as one can imagine, if there absolutely no resistance on this point, the op-
timal design would simply be an inﬁnitely thin and complicated structure that when
expanded would unfold like a spring and push the point inﬁnitely far away. Therefore
when optimizing an actuator it must always be optimized in relation to a resistance
at the actuation point. This means the optimal design will diﬀer greatly depending
on the case it is used for. From a spring we know that displacing it a certain dis-
tance requires a certain force F = U × R. Thus the largest displacement will depend
on force and resistance. If the resistance is large a greater force is required, which
means the structure has reduce its ability to stretch in favour of creating a more solid
structure. Vice versa, the structure can be optimized to stretch more when a smaller
force is required. Ultimately low resistances create complicated thin designs, which
result in a relative large displacement. Larger resistances call for thicker and more
solid structures with a relatively low displacement. If the thin complicated structure
was to displace the large resistance it would hardly move, because it can not provide
the force required to move it. Simultaneously, the thick and solid design would easily
provide the force needed to move the small resistance, but would lack the ﬂexibility
to displace for enough to take advantage of it.
To summarize; for electrothermomechanical actuators both electrical, thermal and
structural aspects must be accounted for, since material distribution aﬀects them all.
In topology optimization of actuators the objective function is to minimize negative
displacement of a certain node in a particular direction, while unwanted movement
directions can be restricted.
Material distribution is restricted with a constraint
function and the design variable ρ is restricted with upper and lower bounds.

Implementation
The optimization scheme was programmed using Fortran 90 language and build on
top of a FEM-program developed in the course FEM-Heavy - 41525 at DTU. In that
course, the program was developed to minimize compliance of a structure under one
load case. The additions to the program were:
• Enabling to solve for multiple load cases.
• Ability to handle thermal and electrical loads.
• Changing the objective function from minimizing compliance of the entire struc-
ture, to eﬀectively maximizing deﬂection of a single point.
• Adding a density ﬁlter and a heaviside function, which removes infeasible links
and structures, consisting of single elements.
• Creating robust solutions by optimizing for both an eroded, intermediate and
dilated objective function.
In this chapter the Fortran program will be explained step by step, with an em-
phasis on the changes in the list above. The ordering of the sections will be in the
same order as the routine run by the program. A ﬂowchart of this routine can be
seen in ﬁgure 3.1.
The program starts by initializing variables and element values which are read
from the input ﬁle.
First the design variable, element’s relative density ρ, is dis-
tributed evenly throughout the design space according to the volume constraint, ex-
cept for the elements which are part of the boundary conditions, which are ﬁxed to the
density of 1 or 0. After this the program enters the topology optimization procedure
which is done iteratively.

3.1
Density Filter and Heaviside
An inherent problem when running topology optimization based on the FEM is that
two corners of two separate elements have an unrealistically large structural strength.
This causes the topology optimization to produce links and beams, which only con-
nect through the corners of two elements, or through a single element, which would
not be feasible in practice. Therefore, the ﬁrst step in the iterative topology proce-
dure is imposing a density ﬁlter followed by a Heaviside function, which creates a
smoother transition between the elements in the structure.

3.3
Robust Optimization
When topology optimizing designs for actuators, the design will usually be right on
the design limit, such that if the structure was any thinner or thicker, the design
would not work. This problem can be alleviated by optimizing for three sets of design
variables at the same time: One that is slightly eroded (¯˜ρe), the normal intermediate
design (¯˜ρi) and a design that is slightly dilated (¯˜ρd). As mentioned in section 3.1, the
η factor can be altered above or below the value of 0.5, for an eroded or dilated design,
respectively. As displayed in the ﬂowchart in ﬁgure 3.1; three diﬀerent routines are
run between adding the density ﬁlter and running the MMA optimization. These
routines are run with three diﬀerent η values: ∆η, 0.5 and 1-∆η. After each routine

This min/max problem allows the MMA solver to redistribute the material, before
continuing to the next iteration. There are many possible optimization solvers that
could be used, for example the optimality criteria, the dual algorithms, CONLIN and
the Method of Moving Asymptotes (MMA). In our code the MMA solver [9] was used.
According to O. Sigmund [8] this solver is stable, eﬃcient and well suited for handling
topology optimization problems with multiple constraints.
The redistribution of material will depend of the weakest of the three designs. For
an actuator the weakest designs will usually be the eroded and the dilated designs.
This results in the intermediate design having an overall lower performance regarding
the value of the objective function, compared to the case where only the intermediate
design would be optimized for. However, these robust designs display a much larger
objective function value in the case of small changes to the thickness of the design,
which is unavoidable when the designs are manufactured and used in reality.
From a computational stand point this unfortunately almost triples the computation
time required, since the majority of the computation time is within the ﬁnite element

CHAPTER 5
Conclusion
A program that is capable performing the FEA on a multi-physics problem and
designing robust electrothermomechanical topology optimized actuators was success-
fully created. The implementation of the robust design ﬁlter created actuators which
were far less sensitive to potential variances in design thickness by removing small
hinges as seen in previous studies [8]. Despite the actuators being limited in their
design in order to allow for this variance in design thickness, the actuators still per-
formed well.
The designs created do however, show a very large temperature generation due to
short path between the two electric terminals.
This creates a very large electric
current intensity which results in large temperatures and relatively high power re-
quirements. It would therefore be ideal to investigate the design of the actuators
with an applied constraint on electric resistance in further work.
