Abstract
The thesis is based on a predefined mixed­integer linear model (MILP) for a smart home
energy model system (SHEMS). The SHEMS is constructed with a battery storage sys­
tem, a air­sourced heat pump, photovoltaic (PV) system, and a thermal storage systems
for floor heating and hot­water supply. It was developed in a previous research project to
identify optimal operation strategy in a residential building, while consider user comfort.
The objective of this thesis is to understand how to include an electrical vehicle in the
predefined MILP for a SHEMS. To build upon on this, the model will be utilized to inves­
tigate new potential benefits from the integration of electric vehicle. The area of interest
is to analyze different charging strategies for EVs based on natural charging pattern, the
availability of renewable energy, and the carbon intensity of the power grid. The last
two mentioned key indicators are grid­based signals and are derived in cooperation with
Electricity Map.
The first part of the thesis focuses on determining the technical assumptions and creating
input data necessary for the model. The study spans four cases: base case with static
prices (case 1), dynamic pricing (case 2), grid signal violation related to carbon intensity
(case 3) and grid signal violation related to renewable energy percentages in the grid
(case 4). Notably, Cases 3 and 4 maintain static pricing while considering the grid signal
impacts.
Key findings reveal that static pricing schemes result in lower overall profits compared to
dynamic pricing due to the lack of incentives for off­peak consumption. Dynamic pricing
increases overall profits and improves comfort levels.
When grid violations are included in the model, they introduce larger comfort violations.
This suggests that grid­friendly charging often compromises other household demands.
Rather than using grid resources to fulfill EV charging needs, resources are redirected
from addressing household demands to ensuring that the EV is charged with locally gen­
erated energy.
The analysis shows that Denmark, with a high share of renewable energy, achieves the
highest self­sufficiency rates and the lowest carbon intensity violation. In contrast, Ger­
many and Belgium, with higher reliance on fossil fuels and nuclear energy outage, face
challenges in achieving similar outcomes.
Overall, this project illustrates the integration of EV into a SHEMS, with both financial and
environmental calculations performed to evaluate the systems efficiency.

Introduction
1.1
Background
Single or multi­residence households are major energy consumers and contributors to
carbon emissions, with considerable potential for optimizing energy efficiency. The resi­
dential sector represents 31 % of the total energy consumption in Denmark [1]. Denmark
has declared its intention to mitigate climate change and to achieve the goal, a reformu­
lation of construction standards for new buildings has been made to further decrease the
countries CO2 emissions [2]. It implies that the country shall be free of fossil fuels in 2050,
and the construction of new buildings should document its environmental impact over a
time period of 50 years [3].
To further improve energy efficiency and reduce carbon emissions, the implementation of
an energy management system (EMS) in buildings can be crucial to achieving this goal.
Originally developed for the industrial sector, these systems optimize daily operations by
monitoring, controlling, and conserving energy [4]. They take advantage of low energy
prices or lower CO2 emissions, allowing companies to reduce their energy consumption
and reschedule production to times when energy is more economically attractive or envi­
ronmentally friendly.
EMS technology is also considered in normal households. The smart home energy man­
agement system (SHEMS) typically includes components such as photovoltaic (PV) sys­
tems, battery storage, and heat pumps. With the increasing electrification of various sec­
tors, including the transport industry, installing an electric vehicle (EV) along with the
electric vehicle supply equipment (EVSE) is becoming more common and is a part of the
future home.
The integration of grid signals, such as carbon emissions and the share of renewables in
electricity production, into SHEMS can enhance their effectiveness. By aligning energy
consumption with periods of low carbon intensity or high renewable energy availability,
households could significantly reduce their carbon footprint. Denmark’s electricity grid
is increasingly supplied by renewable sources, particularly wind and solar power [5]. In
2020, 84% of the electricity production was generated from renewable sources, with wind
power being the largest contributor, accounting for 54% of the total electricity generation
[1]. This variability in renewable energy generation underscores the importance of smart
energy management systems that can adapt to these fluctuations.
Today’s charge point operator (CPO) offers services for charging the EV. But those ser­
vices can be seamlessly integrated into SHEMS by managing individual charging strate­
gies according to the house owner’s specific needs. For example, the system could man­
age when to charge the EV using solar power or the battery system. In addition, there
is significant potential to incorporate the EV to meet internal demands, both electric and
thermal. This approach could reduce the dependency on the external grid.
The market for EVs has increased drastically in the past few decades, mainly driven by
the EU’s goal of reducing greenhouse gas emissions (GHG) by 55 % in 2030 compared to
1990 [6]. This transition from fossil to electrically driven cars will lead to approximately 30
mio. EVs in Europe by 2030 [7]. This estimate could be conservative considering the EU
bans all sales of CO2 emitting vehicles in 2035 [8]. This upcoming increase in EVs hides
an unseen potential for homeowners. Residents will be able to be more self­sufficient, as

the battery car can be used as an additional energy storage system (ESS) with a higher
energy capacity than the normal household battery. Higher energy capacity is the result
of further developments in lithium­ion battery technology [9]. These types of batteries are
used mainly in modern EVs, which has led to a higher range and decreased the price of
cars.
Another challenge to point out is the imbalance between electricity demand and sup­
ply. During off­peak hours, a significant amount of energy goes unused because there
aren’t enough storage facilities. However, during peak hours, there is not enough power
available, resulting in some demands not being met and loads being dropped. These
challenges highlight the importance of further development into the SHEMS.
Two major advantages could be gained by letting the EV being integrated into the power
system. A fleet of EV could be an energy storage large enough to balance the fluctuation
of power production. Thus, energy could be saved for later use and transferred back when
the supply is below demand (V2G). On the other hand, which is pointed towards the end
consumer, utilize the EV capability to cover some of the household energy demand (V2H)
[10][11].
1.2
Previous work
This project will build on a previous research project carried out by Lissy Langer and
Thomas Volling [12]. They successfully modeled the energy flow for a single residential
building in Germany and investigated various scenarios implemented in the SHEMS. One
of the goals was to determine a cost­optimal energy flow and heat pump operation during
a specific time span. The problem was formulated as a mixed integer linear problem
(MILP) and later translated into the mathematical optimization language JuMP. A more
detailed description of the model will be given later in Chapter 3.
The results showed that the PV and ESS system are an essential parts of improving self­
sufficiency. The house relies on its PV system to generate energy during daylight hours,
making it largely self­sufficient during that time. However, during the late afternoon and
night, the energy generated by the PV system is insufficient to meet the house’s energy
demands, which highlight the importance of including the EV to cover these periods.
Luo and Chen [13] conducted an SHEMS study that analyzed the operation of the system
using V2H technology. They successfully implemented how to schedule the operation
of household appliances together with the EV in a high PV power penetrated environ­
ment. Their findings were that the system could reduce home energy costs and improve
demand­side efficiency. Due to various uncertainties, they assumed that the state of
charge (SOC) of the EV and the plug­in time were precisely known. However, their re­
sults did not account for fluctuations in PV generation and household demand in different
seasons during the year. Additionally, their simulations were conducted over only one
day, using static pricing for both selling and buying energy.
Dynamic pricing schemes were included in a study by Chandra and Chanana [14]. They
showed that the EV battery can be charged during low periods of energy demand and
could be fed into the home as well as into the external grid during peak load hours. They
concluded that it is possible to meet the entire demand of a residential consumer together
with the charging demand for the EV, based on their defined parameters.
The following analysis of this report should incorporate seasonal fluctuations in PV gener­
ation and differentiate between thermal and electrical demand throughout the year, which
were not considered in the last two mentioned studies.

Another area of interest could be examining how the model behaves when dealing with
different grid signals such carbon emission or shares of renewables in the electricty pro­

1.3
Problem statement
With the above information, the objective of this thesis is to understand how to include
EV in a predefined mixed integer linear model for a smart home energy management
system. The area of interest is to analyze different charging strategies for certain signals
such as natural charging patterns by the EV owner, the availability of renewable energy,
and the carbon intensity of the power grid. After having successfully included the EV in
the SHEMS model, a comparison of how changes in the signals will affect the strategies
for charging in different EU countries will be analyzed. Within this analysis, an estimation
of environmental and financial benefits will be given.
Research Question
To clarify the purpose of the thesis, the following research questions are used as an overall
foundation of the work:
• How can electric vehicles (EV) be integrated into a Smart Home Energy Manage­
ment System (SHEMS) to optimize energy usage in residential buildings?
• What are the optimal charging strategies for electric vehicles considering natural
charging patterns by the EV owner, renewable energy availability, and grid carbon
intensity?
• How do the charging strategies differ from other EU countries based on KPI?
1.3.1
Report structure
The next section provides an outline of the thesis structure. Chapter 2 includes a descrip­
tion of the modified model, including all technological assumptions and parameters. This
applies for all components in the model.
Additionally, all input data, such as energy prices, CI, and RE values, will be presented
and explained. A comparison of the last two grid signals for selected EU countries will
also be provided.
Chapter 3 offers a detailed description of the mathematical model, incorporating the EV
and the necessary modifications to the constraint and objective function. In addition, grid
violation terms will be introduced to evaluate charging strategies for different grid signals.
At the end of this section, the goal is to improve insight into the various cases applied in
the analysis. This will subsequently be utilized to execute the optimization model with a
clearly defined objective.
Chapter 4 will assess the results for various defined cases, which lead to a discussion
and finally to the conclusion of this thesis.

2.1
Overview of the system
The house is designed as a four­person bungalow containing three bedrooms and a total
floor area of 104 m2. This is larger than the average dwelling in Copenhagen, which is
82 m2 and smaller than the average size of a house in Denmark, which is 112.6 m2 for
2024 [15]. Terraced houses or blocks of flats are typically smaller, but will not be consid­
ered, as it is assumed that the house is located in the greater area of Copenhagen. An
illustration of the updated residential building is given in Figure 2.1. The house comprises
a solar system, a heat pump, and an electric vehicle as main components. Additionally,
the external power grid together with the general electricity demand is shown with the two
circles crossed and the appliances symbols. The demand for hot water and floor heating
is represented by the heat pump itself. In addition, the red dotted lines with the arrows
indicate the direction of the energy flows.

The appliances in the household can be classified into two categories: controllable and
non­controllable appliances. Some devices in the house, such as dryers, televisions, and

whose operation cannot be disrupted, such as refrigerators, ventilation systems, and heat

of when to use energy and when to save it. Especially when considering the high cost of
electricity in the last couple of years due to the energy crisis. However, it was decided not

to include controllable appliances due to their relatively small energy consumption com­

The single­family house is considered to be connected to the 0.4 kV power grid, and it is

such as the dryer or kitchen stove, are independently connected in series with a fuse rated
at 16A. It is also presumed that EVSE is similarly connected in an isolated manner.
2.2
Technical assumptions of the SHEMS components
The following will introduce the technical assumptions of the house. Some background in­
formation about the components will be provided to offer a comprehensive understanding
of the technical specifications that will later be used for modelling the house later.
2.2.1
Photovoltaic system
The installed capacity of solar energy production is set to 10 kW peak, which above the
average for installed capacity in Denmark. The installed capacity has historically been
around 6 kW peak for residential in Denmark from before 2020 [16]. The 10 kWp for
residential PV installations, allowing significant energy generation, which can be used to
cover the household demands or the opportunity to transmit it back to the grid.
The overall technology is well­known as solar radiation is converted into electrical direct
current (DC) energy is stored in the battery energy storage (BES). Most panel manu­
facturers provide a performance warranty of 90 % after 10 years and 80 % after 20­25
years. The exemption is on the materials such as frame, glass, and cell encapsulation is
generally only for 5 to 10 years [17].
For real­life PV systems, several inefficiencies are typically present. PV systems generally
have a Temperature Coefficient of Power (TCP) of approximately ­0.4%°/C, aging losses
of less than 1% per year, and wire and inverter losses [17]. All of these inefficiencies will
not be considered separately in the SHEMS model. Instead, a 5% total loss is assumed
for simplicity.
2.2.2
Battery energy storage system
The model uses technical specifications from the Tesla Powerwall 2 battery. The data
sheet can be seen in Appendix A. The capacity is set at 13.5 kWh with an efficiency of
95 %, and the maximal discharge/charge power is 3.3 kW. The battery can be integrated
with the solar system and, due to the rechargeable functionality, provides power supply to
the remaining components of the house. The battery allows for the storage of excess pro­
duction to be used later. Often, solar panels generate more electricity than the household
consumes during the middle of the day, while at other times, the panels may not cover
the household’s electricity needs. Power from the battery or PV is used behind the meter,
saving money on electricity purchases, tariffs, and taxes. This helps minimize the amount
of solar curtailment during the day and reduce the dependency on the external grid.
2.2.3
Heat pump
The heat pump are assumed to operate with variable speed modulation, allowing for con­
tinuous adjustment of its operation rather than turning it on and off. The air­to­water heat
pump has two separate storage tanks: one for floor heating and one for hot water. The

storage volume is determined by the thermal mass of the floor heating. The coefficient
of performance (COP) of the heat pump determines the ratio of the electrical power input
to the thermal power output. The COP value is often set to a value of 3.5, but in SHEMS
it is calculated considering the temperature difference between the indoor and outdoor
temperature and the compressor speed given from the vendor’s datasheet. The equation
used to calculate the COP per hour was taken from Dengiz [18] and will be described in
Section 2.3.2.
2.2.4
Electric vehicle and supply equipment
The EV can be seen as an additional battery and can act as a flexible load, where the
EVSE can control both the discharging power and the charging power. This can have
the potential to be more self­sufficient over a longer period than has been investigated
until now. When looking at the data analysis performed by the charging point operator
(CPO) Spirii [19], the average kWh consumed per day on the AC charger was around 28
kWh, regardless of the type of location (parking lot, public road, or residential). It was not
explicitly stated that the same car charged this amount every day. It is assumed that it
represents an average value for all EV charged with the AC charger in the portfolio of the
CPO.
There are two types of EVSE that can be used for charging: AC and DC. AC powered
chargers are often used in residential buildings and can power the car within 1.38 kW on
single phase up to 11 kW 3 phases. They are limited by the rate of current through the
cables, which is often set to 16 A. DC chargers are often used in public spaces with a
much higher power rating, ranging from 50kW to 350 kW. Noticeable, the efficiency when
charging below 3.8 kW marks, so 16A single phase, is extreme low. Above the mark, the
average efficacy was determined to 80% [20].
The charging capability is limited by the onboard charger located inside the EV. It is due
to the onboard charger that the EV has different charging capabilities, as the onboard
charger is responsible to converting the in going AC to DC current. A higher charging rate
will naturally decrease the duration of charging. The charging session can vary from cus­
tomer to customer, but on average the duration for fully charged battery was determined
to 282 min. or 4.7 hours using AC chargers.
Determination of SOC for the EV
A 50 kWh battery capacity was selected for the EV. For example, this specification cor­

Due to technical simulation issues, it was not decided to have a lower limit. But in the real­
life it is given with 1.38 kW single phase. This is stated in IEC 61851 technical regulation
[22].

the supply of energy exceeds the demand by having charged the storage systems and

Dynamic electricity prices
The Danish Distribution System Operator (DSO) Cerius defines a normal household as
a C customer [28]. The DSO is responsible for transporting electricity from the location
where it was produced to the end consumer. The seasonal tariff, fixed fees, and VAT will
be chosen from this category. They will be added on top of the price of electricity.
The electricity prices are plotted in Figure 2.4 monthly throughout the year. The Danish
electricity grid is divided into two pricing zones: east Denmark (DK2) and vest Denmark
(DK1), where DK2 is part of the power grid in the Nordic countries and DK1 is connected
to continental Europe. The price of pure electricity is based on the marginal cost of pro­
duction and is given by the intersection between supply and demand. This is called spot
price or clearing price. This process uses the merit­order principle. The producer offers
quantities and their prices (marginal cost). The bids are ordered in ascending order, with
the cheapest offer placed on the left. The resulting curve is defined as the merit order
curve. The responsible for clearing the market is Nordpool, a European power exchange.

In order to evaluate the model’s performance, we initially implemented a scenario with

2.3.5
Carbon intensity and share of renewable
Electricity Maps include short­ and long­term carbon intensity, both measured of CO2
per kWh associated with electricity generation. Short­term marginal carbon intensity is
the change in CO2 emissions related to a unit change in electricity. For instance, the
emissions produced from a person charging their EV.
The long­term marginal intensity reflects the variation in CO2 emissions associated with a
continuous, system­wide unit rise in electricity usage, potentially leading to the installation
or shutdowns of power plants. For instance, when multiple individuals charge their EVs
every day over a period of several months.
Electricity Maps determines the CI value by using different emission factors for different
technologies of electricity production. By multiplying the power consumption from each
source by the corresponding emission factors, the CI is calculated. These emission fac­
tors depend on different parameters such as the production source, the region, and the
amount of energy produced from each source. The CI factors are listed in the Table 2.2
for each energy source and category.
In this thesis, the mean carbon intensity was chosen as the grid signal for carbon emis­
sions from Electricty Maps. They are described as the carbon intensity that covers the
entire electricity mix currently accessible on the grid, including both the electricity gener­
ated and the electricity traded with neighboring zones [26].

2.4
Scope and limitation
Compared to Chandra and Chanana [14], the investigation of SHEMS will be analyzed
during a time horizon of one year. An annual evaluation is conducted to capture seasonal
variations in energy production and consumption, ensuring a better understanding of the
system’s performance over seasonal variations.
The geographical context is centered on a typical family house in Denmark, aligning the
study with local climate conditions, energy consumption patterns, and policy frameworks.
The SOC in the EV battery and the time connected to the EVSE will be derived from
previous research projects based [36][23] in collaboration with DTU Wind and Energy.
This approach ensures that the outcome of the analysis will be based on up­to­date and
realistic inputs.
The study assumes that the EV travels an annual distance of 1614.05 km, based on data
from a Danish survey institution [23]. The EV is assumed to be used daily from 07:00 to
16:00, excluding holidays and weekends. The daily charging requirement for the EV is
set at 8.94 kWh, corresponding to the average distance driven. The analysis does not
account for variations in EV owner behavior during holidays and weekends, which could
affect the accuracy of the usage pattern assumptions.
Static pricing schemes are based on the average day­ahead spot prices for selling elec­
tricity and the wholesale electricity prices from Radius for purchasing. The criteria for
green charging are defined according to the standards of the EU Commission for the pro­
duction of green and renewable hydrogen [37]. Grid signals, including data on carbon
intensity and renewable energy share, are sourced from Electricity Maps, with necessary
adjustments made for incomplete datasets.

For the grid signals provided for Denmark, a new cost factor parameter must be deter­

with respect to the CI values. When increasing ϕ will reduce the charging cost and the
carbon intensities.
The analysis presented in section 4.3 indicates that the peak charging activities occurred
during the winter and fall seasons. Consequently, the specification of the cost factor will
be concentrated within this time span. A thorough and comprehensive analysis for the
obtained findings will be given in subsequently section.
For the numerical determination of the costfactor (ϕ), a new test condition was installed.

impact on the penalty factor compared to the previous settings of 8.9 kWh.

Self­sufficiency (SS) refers to the system’s ability to operate independently and efficiently
by generating its own energy. Optimizing energy usage leads to a reduction in reliance on
external sources. It is defined as the share of local demand satisfied by local production
[12]. The areas that will be calculated are the local demand for electricity, electric vehicle,
heating and hot water. Finally, total self­sufficiency will be visualized for each month. The
formula used to calculate this is given by Equation 4.1.
σ = 1 −Eimp

(4.1)
The Eimp represent the energy imports to cover the demand from the grid and Elocal local
production to cover the demand. The house is completely self­sufficient, as previously
demonstrated by edge summer case 1. However, the primary focus is to observe the
system’s performance during the other seasons throughout the year.
Figure 4.9 shows the calculated self­efficiencies for case 2 with variable prices. The figure
displays the mean KPIs for each day in each month (dot), 50 % confidence interval (light­
olored areas) and the median values. When looking at the self­suffiencies given in Table

The self­sufficiency for EV demand shows more fluctuations compared to electrical and

(2) is 0.37%, which is significantly lower than the 96.33% and 88.52% for electrical and
thermal demands, respectively. This could be justified by the increased energy require­
ments for heating during winter, which limits the availability of local energy sources for EV
charging. It can be assumed that the EV is used to cover potential energy sources from
the grid to improve the self­sufficiency for household energy demands.

In the following a detailed analysis of the optimal flow of energy based on the CI grid

tioned grid signal. A new simulation was conducted including the term for grid violation
for Denmark, Germany and Belgium was inserted.
The outcome is shown in Figure 4.11. The dotted blue line in the last row illustrates the
limit for environmentally friendly charging set by the EU­commission (limit 68 gram of CO2
per kWh). Energy is sourced from the grid (Xgr→ev) when it falls below the predefined
threshold. If the CI values are higher than the acceptable limit, the optimization process
considers a set of the smallest possible values to minimize grid violation.
As seen in Figure 4.11, the CI values fluctuate significantly during the day and are mainly
above the acceptable limit, especially during peak hours. This will induce grid violation,
and the charging strategies aims to minimize this under the optimization process. On
the 20th and 21st of February, energy is sourced from the grid before departure time,
which are under the predefined CI limit. This is desired, and the optimization operates as
expected. As grid violations are penalized, this will result in that the model prefer to used
locally generated energy instead. Consequently, the demands of the house will be more
dependent on the grid.
When analyzing all charging sessions for only sourcing from the grid, it occurs that high
amount of energy is charged consistently during the day in the winter month. To gain
further insight, the amount of charging occurring during the evening and night was inves­
tigated. The evening period are defined from 17.00 to 23.00 and the night period from
00.00 to 06.00. The results showed that 16.61% of the charging occurred during the
evening, and 85.58% at night for the whole simulation.
Compared to the the other cases, where no grid charges were observed in the evening,
this was a new behavior of the optimization model. The higher proportion of night­time
charging can be justified with lower electricity demand and consequently lower carbon
emissions during these hours. This aligns with the model’s objective to minimize carbon

Discussion
This chapter will give a detailed discussion of some of the methods, assumptions, and
results of the thesis.
Technical assumptions
Considering the lifespan of a SHEMS, particularly for the battery and PV system, which
is around 15 years [17]. A limited lifespan could result in economic issues, as the entire
system model would require replacement and reassessment after this duration. The daily
operational inefficiencies in the battery, EV, and PV system must be considered over time,
as they can decrease the system’s energy efficiency. More studies are needed to evaluate
whether the entire system is economically feasible or not.
Another assumption made in the model is that the EV and household battery could dis­
charge down to a SOC of 0%. In reality, maintaining a minimum SOC above 0% is crucial
for limiting cell degradation and secure battery health. This discrepancy means that the
minimum usable capacity of the batteries could have been misrepresented in the model,
affecting the overall performance of the energy management system. Adjusting the model
to reflect realistic SOC limits would provide a more accurate representation of battery.
Regarding efficiency, the model assumes a constant efficiency for the PV and the battery
system. However, real­life PV systems are subjected to various inefficiencies. PV system
do have aging losses of less than 1% per year, and wire and inverter losses [17]. These
factors collectively impact the overall efficiency of the system. In the SHEMS model,
instead of accounting for each inefficiency separately, a simplified assumption of a 5% total
loss is used. Although this simplification makes the model more suitable for theoretical
studies, it may not reflect the actual performance systems under varying conditions.
Charging pattern
For the EV it had been difficult to find a representative charging pattern, which correlated
with the average distance driven in Denmark. The model assumed a daily energy charge
of 9 kWh. This value is relative low and the PV and battery system could fully charge the
EV without sourcing from the grid.
It is unreasonable to assume that the EV owner charges the EV every day. This is maybe
reasonable on an average level, but for given a clear conclusion for individual charging
strategies specific charging demand need to be considered. This was not considered in
the thesis. The demand for daily charges was also considered for the selected countries,
leading to potential overlooks of differences in the driving pattern.
Variations in CI values
Fluctuation in the CI values can arise due to maintenance activities or outages, and could
influence potential charging strategies when charging based on minimizing carbon emis­
sion. In particular, the use of nuclear power plants can result in higher CI values to com­
pensate for the outage, as seen by the CI data for Belgium at the beginning of the year
2023.
Moreover, the generation of renewable energy from wind and solar power is strongly
weather dependent. Such fluctuations can affect the reliability of the CI values over time.
Also the progression to a more sustainable energy infrastructure could lead to fluctuation
in the CI values.

Grid violations
The approach to penalizing grid violations can be further optimized. Rather than using
a constant factor to penalize grid signals, employing a range of varying factors could
enhance the models dynamics.
The cost factor (ϕ) was only numerically evaluated for Denmark. The grid signals from
the selected countries could potentially result in different outcomes when identifying the
Pareto optimal solution.
Additionally, the violation term for grid signals only considers instances when the EV is
charged from the grid. This could potentially overlooks the other energy flows from the
grid to the household. Expanding the violation term to include all household energy flows
from the grid could provide a better view of grid interactions.
Thus, in real­life applications, CPOs often prioritize charging the EV when energy is green
or cheap, without considering the broader energy needs of the household. For example,
while the EV might be charged using green energy, other household demands might si­
multaneously draw power from the grid during high carbon intensity periods.

Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to explore possible EV charging strategies within a pre­
defined MILP optimization model for SHEMS. The integration of EV into the SHEMS was
successfully modeled and evaluated. The results demonstrated that EV could signifi­
cantly contribute to meeting household energy demands. During the winter months, the
EV contributed to meet heating demands, thereby reducing dependency on the external
grid.
Under a fixed pricing scheme (Case 1), the charging sessions were not observed during
the summer months but rather at the year’s beginning and end. This was justified to
higher thermal demands and lower PV generation. The most substantial energy charging
occurred at in the mornings short before departure time in December and January. The
self­sufficiency rate was assessed to be 77%, the self­consumption rate 52% and overall
profits of around ­1,693 DKK.
The study compared fixed and variable pricing schemes. The variable pricing scenario
(Case 2) illustrated the potential for more flexible and responsive energy management,
achieving the same self­sufficiency rate of 77% but reducing overall comfort violations with
around 42%. The net profit was found to be ­1,230 DKK, around 28% increase compared
to Case 1.
Cases 3 and 4 incorporated the grid signals: carbon intensity and the share of renew­
ables in the grid. The results indicated that optimizing for grid signals could enhance the
green friendly charging of EV by ensuring that EVs were charged during periods of high
renewable energy availability. For Case 3, the self­sufficiency in meeting both electrical
and thermal demands notably decreased during the EV’s charging period.
This suggests that grid­friendly charging often compromises the household’s other de­
mands. Rather than using grid resources to fulfill EV charging needs, they are redirected
to address household demands. By doing so, it can be garanteed that the EV is charged
environmentally friendly, as the energy locally generated is transferred to the EV.
The study included an analysis of Denmark, Germany, and Belgium to understand how
charging strategies differ across these EU countries. With a high share of renewable
energy in its grid mix, Denmark achieved the highest self­sufficiency rates and the lowest
carbon intensity. The SHEMS model effectively utilized Denmark’s renewable energy
resources, optimizing energy usage and reducing carbon emissions used for EV charging.
In contrast, Germany’s energy mix, which includes a significant proportion of coal­based
power, resulted in higher carbon intensity and lower self­sufficiency rates were calculated.
The SHEMS model for Germany highlighted the challenges of optimizing energy usage
in regions with higher carbon emissions, which often exceed the EU Commission’s stan­
dards for producing green hydrogen (carbon intensity lower than 64 grams CO2/kWh).
Similar to Germany, Belgium’s energy mix posed challenges for achieving high self­sufficiency
rates and low carbon emissions. During the analysis for Belgium it was found that increase
in CI values can arise due to maintenance activities or outages, significantly influencing
the results of the SHEMS model.
Overall, this project illustrates the integration of EV into a SHEMS, with both financial and
environmental calculations performed to evaluate the system’s efficiency. The results

argument for investment in electrification and advanced optimization system.
6.1
Further work
Unidirectional and bidirectional charging
Unidirectional and bidirectional charging were excluded in the setup of the EV from the
model. Furthermore, in this study, the role of unidirectional charging for the stability and
frequency regulation of the power grid was not addressed. These factors should be inte­
grated into future SHEMS models, especially since CPOs currently offer these function­
alities. Incorporating such a feature could generate an additional revenue stream.
Cooling Demand
An additional area of interest could be the extension of heating demand to include cool­
ing, which would provide a better understanding of energy management across different
climates. This would allow regions in the southern hemisphere to be more accurately
represented, which would make the SHEMS model more efficient.
Sensitivity analysis
Conducting sensitivity analysis by excluding various components could provide deeper
insights into the model’s performance. This approach would help identify the most critical
factors influencing the SHEMS effectiveness and guide future improvements. The sen­
sitivity analysis could involve varying the parameters of key constraints and objectives to
observe their impact on the outcomes. For instance, adjusting the the amount needed for
charging the EV could reveal the robustness of the model and its response to changes in
assumptions. Additionally, sensitivity analysis could explore the effects of different sce­
narios of renewable energy availability, grid carbon intensity, and different user behavior
for charging, thereby enhancing the models adaptability to real­world conditions.
Urban residential integration
In urban areas, people often live in residential condominiums. Thus, it would be interesting
to examine the efficient integration of a model like the SHEMS within such an environment.
One potential benefit could be, the feasibility aspects of additional EVs. Not just in terms
of individual charging strategies, but the potential to use a fleet of EV to compensate the
energy demands of the residential condominium.
Feasibility study
Finally, a feasibility study should be conducted to evaluate the practical implementation of
SHEMS. This study should help evaluate the upfront costs and long­term financial benefits
of the system, as well as calculate the average cost per unit of energy over the lifetime of
the system. Such a study would contribute to a better understanding of the financial and
practical viability of implementing SHEMS in various residential settings.
