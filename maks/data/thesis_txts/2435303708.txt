Abstract 
This Master of Science thesis estimates the potential of oil production in the South Arne field, 
which is located in the Danish sector of the North Sea and operated by Hess Denmark. 
A literature review was made, that gives an extensive look into the usage of CO2 flooding as a 
tertiary production method. The use of waterflooding was only briefly mentioned due to the 
widespread implementation and relatively simple nature. 
A further look into the South Arne field and specifically the SA-01C well was made. Reservoir 
dimensions and well layout were researched, along with properties of the reservoir rock and fluid 
properties. This was then used to construct a model of reservoir at as realistic conditions as 
possible. 
Oil from the South Arne field was lumped into a 10 component fluid and a phase envelope was 
constructed. It was concluded this way, that the CO2 injection was of a miscible nature. The phase 
envelope was calculated using SPECS v5.70. 
Production data was supplied from water- and CO2 flooding experiments. Two sets of relative 
permeability curves were calculated into to complete a history match of the aforementioned 
experiments. In order to get better understand of the influence of the parameters used for the 
relative permeability curves, an influence analysis was carried out. A grid size a analysis was also 
carried out in order to estimate the grid size required to estimate accurate production profiles. 
The experimental data was then scaled up, in order to give an estimate of potential production 
profiles of different areas within the SA-01C well by both water- and CO2 flooding.

Introduction 
It is clearly evident that the global consumption of energy has been increasing steadily over the 
past many years. Although the consumption of the green energy is increasing, so is the 
consumption of fossil fuels, specifically oil. In fact oil remains one of the biggest energy sources 
on the planet. 
Even though new fields are found every year, these fields are usually more inaccessible and/or 
smaller in size, thus providing smaller quantities of additional commercial reserves, than in the 
past. Therefore, in order to keep up with the demand of the global market, it is increasingly 
important for oil producers to further develop older assets. As a result various methods of 
Improved Oil Recovery (IOR) have been widely used, in both the secondary and tertiary 
production phases. 
 
Figure 1 - Overview of production phases and viable techniques of IOR/EOR. (Source: Shiaukat and Sigalas 2013) 
==PAGE:13==
 
2 
 
After supporting oil production through waterflooding for many years, many oil fields across the 
planet are entering the tertiary production phase. Therefore, more producers have undertaken 
Enhanced Oil Recovery (EOR) projects. The most widely used categories of techniques are 
thermal recovery, miscible/immiscible gas injection and chemical injection. 
The present study focuses on the use of water flooding and gas injection. Although immiscible 
gas injection does exist, it is mostly considered to be the exception to the norm. This is because 
the most widespread form of gas injection is miscible gas injection. The concept is based on 
injection of a gas, which at reservoir conditions can be dissolved in the oil phase. The oil and the 
injected gas will mix in the reservoir, and thus make it easier to mobilize oil in the reservoir. 
However, miscibility cannot be achieved in all cases. . In order for a reservoir to be a candidate 
for miscible gas injection, the reservoir pressure must be above the so-called Minimum Miscibility 
Pressure (MMP). At a given temperature MMP is the pressure at which miscibility can be 
achieved. The two most widely injection gasses used in miscible gas injection are lean gas or CO2. 
These two are used mainly because of they are most likely to be affordable and more importantly, 
miscible at lower pressures. 
The critical point of CO2 is 74 𝑏𝑎𝑟 and 31 ℃. As the critical point parameters of CO2 are below 
both reservoir pressure and reservoir temperature of many reservoirs across the planet, CO2 is 
the most common injection gas for miscible gas injection projects. This is because, when 
operating at super-critical conditions CO2 attains a gas-like viscosity and liquid-like density. 
Therefore, the mixture of gas will have increased mobility, due to low viscosity, and ability to 
sweep lower into the oil zone due to the higher density.2 
As of 2016 approximately 10% the EOR projects are done using miscible CO2 injection. EOR 
projects using CO2 have lead to an incremental increase in production of 10% of Oil Initially In 
Place (OIIP). However studies using simulations, have found that up to 20% OIIP could be 
ascertained in low permeability reservoirs. Experience has shown that such low permeability 
reservoirs very common to be found within North Sea sector.  
==PAGE:14==
 
3 
 
The quantities of CO2 required for large-scale CO2 injection projects must be found and captured 
as bi-products from industrial plants or be obtained otherwise, and then to be transported to an 
EOR site. In the USA, CO2 injection has already been implemented in developments of several oil 
fields and is supporting/enhancing their oil production for many years by now. However, the 
same is not the case in the Danish part of the North Sea. This is in spite of the fact that, as 
mentioned earlier, reservoirs in the North Sea would usually be considered as good candidates 
for CO2 injection projects, due to their low permeability, relatively high pressure, as well their as 
relatively high temperature.  In most cases this would ensure miscibility and act in favor of CO2 
injection. However, due to the far-offshore location of many potential candidate fields in the 
North Sea logistics costs increase significantly. This is in considerable contrast to the on-shore 
CO2 injection schemes conducted in places like Texas and Oklahoma. 
The fossil fuel industry has long been under pressure from environmental agencies around the 
world, looking to decrease the amount of greenhouse gasses emitted to the atmosphere. Here 
CO2 capture and injection for enhancing oil production can assist in reducing or even diminishing 
such emissions. Over the past hundred years, the planet has endured a large increase in 
temperature. This has likely resulted in highly worrying climate changes. As CO2 emissions are 
the prime contributor to the increase in temperature, stifling CO2 emissions could prove crucial 
in the efforts to stop further temperature rise. It is therefore beneficial to capture and inject CO2 
into the reservoir and store it there after oil production has seized. In this way, large amounts of 
greenhouse gasses will never enter the atmosphere. This is what is known as Carbon Capture 
Utilization & Storage (CCUS). The International Energy Agency (IEA) aims to lower temperature 
rise by 2-6 ℃ by limiting global CO2 emissions, amongst others, by increasing the number of CCUS 
projects. IEA estimates that 13 %  of the reduction in CO2 emissions required to limit 
temperature rise of this century to 2 ℃, can be achieved by using CCUS projects.3 
This study provides an in-depth experimental view of the possible effects and potential benefits 
of implementing a miscible CO2 injection projects for enhanced oil recovery at the South Arne 
field. The work includes history matching of experimental results obtained and provided by GEUS 
and published in SPE131516-MS-P22,30. These data were to provide relative permeability curves

Literature Review 
The global oil industry has been facing new challenges in recent years. The number of new 
discoveries is in decline in both size and accessibility, and more and more developed assets are 
maturing. In order to meet the energy demand of the global marked, more and more oil 
producers are turning to enhanced oil recovery techniques.4 
The life of an oil field can have three phases, primary, secondary and tertiary. The primary 
production phase is completely driven by reservoir forces. These forces include properties of the 
reservoir rock, fluid properties in the reservoir and initial reservoir pressure. In other words, the 
primary production phase commences can run without interference from the surface except for 
what can be done with a producer well (gas lift, pumping etc.). After some time, production rates 
will drop to a level where it is no longer optimal financially. The secondary production phase is 
when production decline is stifled by the application of pressure maintenance. Pressure 
maintenance can be done with either water injection or gas injection to maintain reservoir 
pressure, which in turn encourage oil displacement towards the producer well. After the 
secondary production phase there are still large amounts of oil left in the reservoir, some of which 
may be recovered in the tertiary production phase. 
Base (2012)5 describes how significant amounts of oil can be left in reservoir rock due to many 
factors. Factors such a heterogeneity resulting in low sweep efficiency, capillary forces, interfacial 
forces and bad physical connection between injector and producer wells, all negatively impact 
the overall oil recovery. Thereby, leaving a high macroscopic residual oil saturation. Depending 
on residual oil saturation, reservoir fluid properties and reservoir rock properties, tertiary 
production may commence using chemical injection, injection of steam (more commonly known 
as thermal injection) or gas injection.  
The International Energy Agency reports that as of 2015 the global oil production breached 97 
million barrels per day. This is also with the assumption that the global oil demand will break 100 
million barrels within a 5 year period.6  
==PAGE:17==
 
6 
 
Kokal et al. (2010)7 reports that, at the time of writing, the global oil production, in fields with 
implemented EOR production techniques, was more than 3 million barrels per day. This mean 
that around 3% of the global oil supply is provided by the use of EOR projects. EOR projects have 
mainly been undertaken in North America where a large portion of the oil production is done 
from very mature on-shore fields. This is true for both conventional and unconventional oil fields. 
Both EOR production and conventional production are reported to be in decline. The production 
from EOR projects is predicted to peak once global oil production goes into decline. 
 
Water Injection 
Water injection projects are numerous and commonplace in the North Sea. Most, if not all, fields 
support secondary projection by water injection. Specific production mechanisms of various 
methods are briefly discussed in this paragraph. 
Zhang et al. (2007)31 describe the idea of multicomponent ion exchange (MIE). In a carbonate 
reservoir the pore wall is initially positively charged. When negative functional groups of the oil 
phase will adhere to the positively charged calcium ions on the pore well. As calcium is a divalent 
cation, it can hold the negatively charged oil to the pore wall, but with the injection of a new fluid 
monovalent or divalent cations of a different electronegativity can break calcium-to-oil bond and 
carry the oil off the pore wall. This mechanism also causes wetting alteration, meaning the 
reservoir rock goes from oil wet to water wet. 
Emadi and Sohrabi (1999)32 describe the ability to use micro-dispersions and micro-emulsions as 
production mechanisms. When water is injected into a reservoir, reducing interfacial tension 
between the water and oil phase was found beneficial. It was found that oil and water, under 
certain conditions can create micro-dispersions, and thereby produce oil captured in dead-end 
pores. The produced oil is usually produced in the form of micro-emulsions. 
Austad et al. (2010)33 report the effects of acid-base reactions as a production mechanism. The 
authors propose the use of absorption of acidic and basic components of the oil phase onto clay 
particles as a mean to increase oil recovery. The mechanism works by locally increase pH values 
==PAGE:18==
 
7 
 
in the reservoir, by injection of selective salinity. The use of this mechanism will also result in 
some degree of fines migration. 
Tang and Morrow (1999)34 describe the use of fines migration. Fine particles may become 
dislodged inside a reservoir due to properties of the injection fluid, or in some cases injection 
rates. The particles flow through the pores of the reservoir and are either produced in a 
production well or settles at another part of the reservoir. This is called fines migration. Fine 
migration can, if controlled properly, increase macroscopic recovery by increasing permeability 
of a area where sweep efficiency is already high. This will then divert injection fluid through areas 
they do not experience the same sweep efficiency and thereby increase oil recovery. 
Lingthelm et al. (2009)35 report on the use of a mechanism called double layer expansion. Double 
layer expansion occurs when a water wet pore wall is covered by a only thin layer of water and 
then an oil layer. The oil layer can experience some attraction of the pore wall due to the low 
thickness of the water layer separating them. Thereby holding oil in place. Injected water of a 
similar composition of that of the water layer can be injected and from underneath the oil phase 
lift it further away from the pore wall until oil can be mobilized. This mechanism is mostly seen 
in low-salinity injection. 
Hermansen et al. (2000)36 describe smart waterflooding. Authors describe how a well-designed 
composition of injection fluids can help increasing oil recovery. When accurately designing 
salinity composition of the injected fluid, oil recovery can be increased significantly. On a case by 
case basis, different cations and anions in the injection fluid can either increase or inhibit 
production. Ions such as sulphates can increase the potential of MIE, but also increase the rate 
of scaling up of wells if barium is present in reservoir water. On a microscopic scale this may not 
necessarily lower the residual oil saturation, but shorten the required to achieve it. Injection of 
surfactants can also be utilized, and often can decrease residual oil saturation.

Gas Injection 
Kokal et al. (2010)7 describes gas injection as one of the most common practices for tertiary 
production. It is reported that gas injection, at the time of writing, provided an incremental oil 
production of 600𝑚𝑏𝑏𝑙−700𝑚𝑏𝑏𝑙 per day. It is also reported that over 100 CO2 injection 
projects and around 25 lean gas injection projects were operating globally.  
Stosur (2003)8 and Kokal et al (2010) both report that in coming years the application of gas 
injection will gain popularity due to its relative simplicity and potential for high recovery factors. 
 
Figure 2 - Overview of Global EOR projects and resulting production. (Source: Kokal et al 2010) 
The production increase is a result of multiple production mechanisms. When injecting gas into 
an oil reservoir the interfacial tension (IFT) is reduced to zero if the reservoir pressure is above 
MMP. Furthermore, oil swells when in contact with CO2, as well as, the viscosity of the mixed 
(oil/gas) phase is lower than that of oil. All these mechanisms increase mobility and thereby oil 
recovery. 
Firoozabadi et al. (1986)9, Hudgins et al. (1990)10, and Dindoruk et al. (1995)11 describe the 
importance of having a full understanding of oil composition, injection gas properties, reservoir 
conditions and MMP, when conducting gas injection. The minimum miscibility pressure (MMP) 
is the pressure at which the injection gas becomes miscible with the oil phase at reservoir 
==PAGE:20==
 
9 
 
temperature. The MMP is found by conducting slim-tube experiments, by multiple contact 
experiments, by using the rising bubble technique, or by use of phase calculation software such 
as SPECS v5.70. 
According to Green et al. (1988)12, Fayers et al. (1981)13, and Johns et al. (1981)2 there are three 
types of gas injection. There is the immiscible displacement, the first contact miscible (FCM) 
displacement, and the multiple contact miscible (MCM) displacement. Immiscible displacement 
is when reservoir pressure is well below the MMP. In immiscible displacement, the production 
mechanism is viscosity decrease, permeability increase, as well as oil swelling. If MMP is lower 
than operating reservoir, pressure the displacement is miscible displacement. In the case of 
miscible gas injection a transition zone is developed. At the start of the transition zone, the 
injection gas contains no oil, while at the end the reservoir oil contains no injection gas. FCM is 
when this transition zone is non-existent. However in practice this is a very rare occurrence at 
first injection. In the case of MCM there are three types of driving mechanisms; vaporizing gas 
drive (VGD), condensing gas drive (CGD) and vaporizing/condensing gas drive (sometimes known 
as combined gas drive)(VCGD). VGD is when the light components of the oil phase transfer into 
the gas phase. This happens when the oil phase is on the extension of critical tie lines. CGD is 
when the gas phase is on the tie line extension. This will cause some oil components to condense 
into the gas phase. VCGD is when these two types of gas drive can occur in unison. This was the 
case in the injection by CO2 of Green et al. (1988). 
Gozalpour et al. (2005)14 reports that the two types of gas mainly used are CO2 and natural gas 
(mostly C1-4). There are pros and cons for the use of both types of gas. Natural gas can be in a 
large byproduct when producing from the reservoir. If infrastructure is not in place to sell 
produced gas and it is not needed as a power source, reinjection is a viable option. Any injected 
gas, can also potentially be produced again at a later point and sold. The negative side is that it 
is rather expensive compared to CO2. The pros for CO2 is in the simplicity of injection, cheap 
operating cost given availability from nearby sources, and high potential of incremental recovery. 
The downside include potential of increased corrosion issues. CO2 injection has been 
implemented in large scale for almost 50 years. According to Kokal (2010)7 the injection of CO2 
==PAGE:21==
 
10 
 
has resulted in an incremental recovery of 10% of OIIP. Figure 2Fejl! Henvisningskilde ikke 
fundet. shows that CO2 injection represent approximately 10% of the global production from EOR 
production. The majority of implemented cases are in the Permian basin, which is an on-shore 
area in Texas and Oklahoma. This area is favorable to CO2 injection as injection gas is readily 
available from neighboring fields, and because reservoir pressure is usually above MMP. The 
largest CO2 injection operation in the World is the Weyburn field in Saskatchewan, Canada. Here 
the CO2 is captured by industrial plants nearby, and then transported by pipelines to the injection 
sites. The Weyburn field was discovered in 1954, production started in 1964, CO2 injection began 
in 2000, and today produces close to 18000 barrels per day from a sandstone reservoir. The size 
of the field is 180 km2.15  
Orr (2004)16 reports that injection of CO2 has increased in recent years, due to CO2 emissions 
being a major benefactor to the greenhouse effect and climate changes. As combustion of oil and 
gas is the main source of CO2 globally, the disposal of greenhouse gasses would be ideal. Awan 
et al. (2008)17 therefore suggests development of EOR projects which include CO2 injection even 
for oil reservoirs with light oil, such as the North Sea reservoirs. It is also reported that it should 
be feasible to store large amounts of greenhouse gasses in reservoir rocks. This is because the 
ability for the reservoir seal to contain hydrocarbons for millions of years indicate their ability to 
contain CO2 as well. However, it must confirmed that the reservoir seal was not damaged during 
production, as CO2 leakage from the reservoir could have catastrophic environmental 
repercussions. 
Johns et al. (2013)2 reports that after a successful gas flood, an average of 55% of OIIP are still in 
the reservoir. This emphasizes out the extreme importance of proper reservoir engineering and 
prevention of early breakthrough, ahead of a gas flood.  
Ferguson et al. (2010) reports that CO2 gas injection results in an average of 10% OIIP (±5% 
points) of incremental recovery. However, in lab results, such as the source of this study (ref 22.), 
almost complete recovery is achieved. The reason for this is that in lab work, cores are very small 
and almost perfect sweep efficiency and displacement is achieved. In field scale this is simply not 
possible. Heterogeneity and fractures in reservoir rocks result in oil phase and gas phase having 
==PAGE:22==
 
11 
 
highly difference mobility. Therefore, the effects of gravitational forces and viscous fingering 
occurs start to show, resulting in poor sweep efficiency. As the density of supercritical is CO2 still 
lower than that of most reservoir oils, CO2 can override the oil phase, resulting in communication 
forming between injector and producer. Ferguson et al. also points out that other reasons for 
poor performance can originate in unstable CO2 supply (either logistically or economically), 
unstable reservoir conditions, incomplete understanding of reservoir characteristics or simply 
poor data collection or project surveillance. To combat these failures, Ferguson et al. suggests a 
variety of solutions. New considerations to well placements are suggested to combat low sweep 
efficiencies due to heterogeneities. In order to avoid reservoir pressure dropping below MMP, it 
is suggested to inject light components already present in reservoir oil. This is done to create a 
reservoir oil with a lower MMP, so miscible gas injection can run at lower pressure. It is also 
suggested to inject an additional 1.5 hydrocarbon pore volume (PV) of CO2, to increase the 
contact surface between reservoir fluids and injected CO2. According to the author, this would 
result in an incremental recovery of 20% OIIP. Ferguson et al. dubbed these steps as Next Gen 
CO2 EOR. 
It is suggested by Dipietro et al. (2014)19, that the Next Gen CO2 EOR could prove to be key to 
implement financially viable offshore CO2 injection projects. This is because for offshore 
operations, injection CO2 is proving logistically and financially more difficult than on-shore 
operations. 
 
Economics and Logistics in Offshore CO2 Injection 
Johns et al. (1981)2 discusses the feasibility of CO2 injection. The authors describe how the CO2 
for injection is produced in nearby fields and industrial plants for projects implemented in the 
Permian basin. As these fields are on-shore, logistics are significantly easier and more economical 
than for potential projects in the North Sea.  
Suicmez (2015)20 reports that not only is CO2 rather expensive in the North Sea, but in most cases 
there is no proper infrastructure in place in order to take care of CO2 logistics efficiently. 
==PAGE:23==
 
12 
 
Gray (2014)21 writes that the production and transportation expenses for CO2 must be brought 
down, for CO2 injection to be viable. As there has been no infrastructure expansion announced, 
this does appear unlikely. In an attempt to increase the feasibility of CO2 injection in the North 
Sea, the author put three suggestions forward. (A) Construct CO2 supply for assets with no CO2 
injection capabilities. (B) the supply chain from CO2 producer to injection well must be supported 
by technical integration, and lastly, it is suggested that CO2 is procured from power suppliers

In cases where injection gas overrides hydrocarbons and thereby result in low sweep efficiency,

Theory 
In the Theory chapter, certain reservoir parameters, concepts and equations will be discussed. A 
good understanding of these terms is of vital importance for carrying out successfully the 
necessary modelling work for this study. 
 
Porosity 
Any porous medium has a certain percentage of void. This degree of void, given usually in bulk 
fraction or percentage is known as porosity. This volume is filled with water and hydrocarbons in 
oil reservoirs. There are two kinds of porosities; absolute and effective porosity. Absolute 
porosity is the percentage of void in a solid, while effective porosity is the interconnected 
porosity in a solid. 
In simulation of reservoir behavior, porosity is a very important factor, as it is required for 
modelling of flow of fluids through a reservoir formation. The total void in the reservoir rock, 
makes up the combined volume of all the reservoir fluids. 
 
Permeability 
 Permeability is a characteristic of the reservoir rock that indicates the resistance the rock exerts 
in flow through a porous medium.

Miscibility 
When utilizing CO2 as injection fluid, the recovery can be increased as the injection fluids are 
operating at supercritical conditions. This means that, while in a liquid-like state the injected CO2 
is not only miscible with the oil, but also has a super low viscosity, in the magnitude of 0.01𝑐𝑃, 
while maintaining densities around those of the oil, approximately 700-800 𝑘𝑔/𝑚3.  
In the Literature Survey two types of miscibility mechanisms were briefly described (FCM and 
MCM), in this chapter these descriptions are elaborated upon further. Furthermore, the driving 
mechanisms of MCM (VGD, CGD and VCGD) are also described in greater detail, including 
graphical explanations. 
In a FCM injection, the injected fluids mix with the reservoir instantaneously, and therefore 
produce no transition zone, and only one phase. At a given temperature and pressure, a ternary 
diagram can be used to describe a FCM injection scheme. 
==PAGE:39==
 
28 
 
 
Figure 11 - Ternary diagram of a system capable of first contact miscibility. (Source: Greene and Willhite 1988) 
In Figure 11, an oil of composition “A” is being produced by gas injection, with gas composition 
“a”. As long as a tie line can be drawn between point “A” and point “a”, these two phases are 
capable of FCM. Therefore, any injection gas to the right of point “a”, would be capable of FCM. 
These conditions hold for any given temperature or pressure, as long as the two-phase region is 
depicted according to the operating conditions. Injection with a gas with composition “b”, would 
not be a FCM candidate, as any tie line drawn to point “A” would go through the two-phase 
region. In a field-sized case, all gas composition would be able of FCM, given the right condition. 
However, the pressure requirement may be too high for practical use. Temperature could also 
be controlled in a field, but due to the large scale this would be done at, controlling pressure 
alone would prove to be much easier. As a side note, it could be mentioned that oil producers 
would be interested in choosing an injection gas composition as close to point “a” as possible for 
economic reasons. 
Vaporizing Gas Drive (VGD) happens when the fluid injected into a reservoir, results in light 
components from the oil to be vaporized into the injected fluid. At one point the injected fluid 
achieves a composition that, at reservoir conditions, is miscible with the reservoir oil. If the 
==PAGE:40==
 
29 
 
reservoir oil does not contain enough light components capable of vaporizing, a two-phase 
system occurs. Therefore, the injection is immiscible, as two carbon phases are present at all 
points between injector and producer well.  
As the case of FCM, it is possible to explain VGD using a ternary diagram.

and Willhite 1988) 
A graphical example of VGD is depicted in Figure 12. The composition of the reservoir oil is 
marked by the point “C”, and the composition of injection gas in marked by point “A”.  When 
injection gas “A” comes into contact with oil “C” they form a mixture of composition “a”, which 
in turn then is separated into two phases; 𝑉1 and 𝐿1. The liquid phase is miscible with “C”, while 
the vapor with composition 𝑉1 creates a mixture of composition “b”, which in turn separates into 
phases 𝑉2 and 𝐿2. This mechanism continues until all 𝑉𝑛 is miscible with oil “C”. The point 𝑉𝑛 is 
known as the point of the critical tie line. VGD is possible in all cases where the extended critical 
tie line either intersects, or is on the left side of the point “C”, the oil composition. 
In Condensing Gas Drive (CGD) the oil composition “C” and the injection gas composition “A” 
swap sides comparatively to the extended critical tie line as shown in Figure 13. 
==PAGE:41==

Figure 13 - Ternary diagram of a system capable of multiple contact miscibility through condensing gas drive. (Source: Greene 
and Willhite 1988) 
When the injection gas “A” comes into contact with oil “C”, a mixture of composition “a” is 
created, like the case of VGD. The liquid phase of this mixture is directly miscible with oil “C”. 
Because of the increased mobility of  the vapor 𝑉1, 𝐿1 is left behind and creates mixture “b”, 
which in turn is split into two phases. This process continues until the critical tie line is reached. 
Again, the composition at the critical tie 𝑉𝑛 is miscible with injection gas “A”. Hence, if injection 
gas of composition “A” is to the right of the extended critical tie line, and oil of composition “C” 
is on the left of the extended critical tie line, the CGD occurs. Oil “C” and injection gas “A” are 
both to the left of the extended critical tie line, injection is immiscible.

In most real cases, large oil assets do not only experience either VGD or CGD, but both at the 
same time. This process is called VCGD, sometimes known as combined gas drive. In such case 
some intermediate components in the injected gas condense into the oil phase. Thereby, the oil 
becomes lighter and more miscible with injection gas. At the same time, some lighter 
intermediate components may vaporize from the oil into the gas phase. This then results in 
alternation of the ternary diagram over the life of an asset. A Transition zone is also established 
this way, as shown in Figure 14.

Model Validation 
Eclipse 100 and Eclipse 300 is state of the art in within simulation software. Therefore, a 
simulation in this software was used to attempt to estimate the potentials of water flooding as 
pressure maintenance for the secondary production phase. Thereafter the potentials of tertiary 
CO2 injection were also estimated. Eclipse is widely used for simulations all around the world, in 
hundreds of operations. Only depending on accurate input, the simulators have accurately 
predicted production rates and reservoir behavior for a long number of years. Thusly, it is safe to 
conclude that it is also applicable for the simulations conducted for this study. 
The experimental data was the results from work conducted on core samples taken from core 
plugs in SA-01C, therefore upscaling was done around this well. For simulation of experimental 
work two 1D models were made, while the upscaling was done in both 2D and 3D. The 3D models 
were used to estimate potential production, while 2D models were used to who flow patterns 
inside the reservoir. 
 
Model Description 
The objective of this study was to study the influence on oil recovery during water flooding and 
a miscible CO2 flooding scheme. This was done by modelling two 1D experiments, one of water 
flooding on a virgin state reservoir and one of CO2 flooding on a heavily water flooded reservoir 
state. These two 1D models were then used for upscaling on to well-size. The rock properties 
required for the 1D models were provided by Olsen (2010)30 and Olsen (2011)22. These properties 
in combinations with values shown in Figure 7, Figure 8 and Figure 9 were also used to estimate 
the Tor rock properties of the upscaling. The South Arne oil data was lumped into an oil with 10 
components, which in interest of saving computational time, was used for simulation purposes.

Model Gridding 
For the base case of the 1D grids, it was chosen to divide the first five core samples into 100 cells, 
but due to its smaller size, the last core sample was divided into 70 cells (1x570x1). The rock 
properties and dimensions were chosen as shown in the theory. No incline or rock layering was 
considered.  
In the case of the 2D and 3D models, SA-01C runs through the Tor formation for approximately 
1800m, with 14 production zones. Leaving 50m between entry into Tor and first production zone, 
every production zone is positioned with spacing of 125m. As described in the Modelling Input, 
the height of the oil zone is 60m. To minimize injector response, the average distance between 
injector and producer is 250m. Therefore, a rectangular box with measurements of 250x125x60m 
was constructed, to imitate a single production zone. In the base case every cell is of the 
dimension of 5x5x2.5m. According to Figure 5 the well runs down a decline on the western flank 
of the South Arne field. This incline is 25% in the direction across from the direction of flow.

History Matching 
A history match was made for both the black oil model of the waterflooding and the 
compositional model of the CO2 flooding. An additional black oil history match was made for the 
CO2 flooding in order to simplify the upscaling, in order to combat extremely long computation 
times.  
The history match was made by an iterative process. The arbitrarily chosen set of parameters 
from the previous chapter (𝑎𝑤= 2, 𝑎𝑜= 1.5, 𝑘𝑟𝑤𝑜𝑟= 0.7, 𝑘𝑟𝑜𝑤𝑖= 0.8) and the knowledge 
gained from the influence analysis was used, until at satisfactory production profile was 
simulated.

models. The black oil model only shows two produced phases; W phase, and O/G phase. 
Therefore, the O/G was split into an oil fraction and a gas fraction in accordance with the 
compositional model as a function of PVI.

Assumptions 
Two main assumptions were made in relation to the upscaling and creation of the six scenarios. 
The first assumption is that there is no flow through the edges of the model. As production zones 
are laid out side by side and end to end this a fair assumption to make. Because all zones are of 
the same porosity and permeability and injection rate, the flow that would move to the edge of 
one production zone, would be exactly countered by an equal flow of the neighboring zone. 
The other assumption that was made is regarding the rate of oil production of CO2 injections. As 
computation times were extremely long the CO2 injection scenarios were simplified to Eclipse 
300 black oil model, where the rate of water production was the same as in the compositional 
model. The production of gas and oil is added together in the compositional model and a gas to 
oil of the production stream was calculated. A graph of the gas fraction is shown in Figure 39. 
These calculations were from a 1D model, where permeability was the same everywhere. 
Therefore, applying this assumption to the 3D models is not ideal, as not all of the areas across 
the production zone are swept at the same time or have the same pore velocity. This means 
composition of oil mixed with gas is not the same at all points in the cross section.

Implementation Remarks 
In the past the South Arne operator, Hess Denmark, has looked into the implementation of EOR. 
While it is also the believed by the operations department that oil recovery could be increase 
with the use of CO2 injection, this method has so far not been proven profitable. The logistics 
required to undertake a major CO2 injection is not possible with the current infrastructure. 
Whereas a look into smart waterflooding could be a cheaper and more feasible way of increasing 
production. Additionally, due to a high degree of heterogeneity of the reservoir, combatting low 
sweep efficiencies could prove to be a more effective way of increasing production.  
However, Hess Denmark is currently up for sale, and therefore no investments are made into the 
asset at this time.

Conclusion 
Lumping of a 10 component oil was made and a phase envelope was created using Peng-
Robinson EoS. The phase envelope of the lumped oil and the real oil were shown to be mostly 
similar, and more importantly both oils were at sub-critical temperature and pressure at reservoir 
conditions.  
Two flooding experiments were given as data, one waterflooding of Tor cores from the South 
Arne field and one CO2 flooding on the same core. Production was matched in both cases by 
construction of relative permeability curves. In the case of the CO2 injection experiment, it was 
also required to change initial water saturation slightly from 25% to 27% in order to match the 
oil production more precisely.

the reservoir was 2968𝑚𝑏𝑏𝑙 to 2985𝑚𝑏𝑏𝑙 by waterflooding. The potential production of a 
production zone in the bottom of the reservoir was 1578𝑚𝑏𝑏𝑙 to 1602𝑚𝑏𝑏𝑙. In both cases the 
==PAGE:105==
 
94 
 
production depend on the ratio between permeability in the x- and y-direction. Upscaled models 
were made injection 0.1𝑃𝑉/𝑦𝑒𝑎𝑟 for just over 15 years. 
In a reservoir depleted by waterflooding, and additional 976𝑚𝑏𝑏𝑙 to 991𝑚𝑏𝑏𝑙 of oil could be 
recovered by the use of tertiary CO2 injection. Assumptions were made in the upscaling of CO2 
injection that make the production somewhat unreliable.

Future Work 
There are many areas in which this study could be expanded. Hess Denmark was not involved 
directly in this study and therefore proprietary information could not be handled. If Hess was 
taken in, and significantly more computing power was acquired, the presence of actual fractures 
and heterogeneities in the matrix could be brought into consideration. Also matching data from 
upscalings to actual production data could be carried out. Also if additional computing power 
was available compositional models could be carried out for all models, rather than just the 
matching of injection of CO2 in the lab. 
An additional look into flow paths and injection rates could also be carried out to extend the look 
into production optimization.
