Abstract 
_______________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________ 
Generic drug development is a complex process that requires involvement of various 
stakeholders. In this thesis the process is presented from the perspective of the following  
areas: Commercial potential, Regulatory Affairs, Patent Landscape and R&D. The aim of this thesis 
is to identify and highlight challenges behind development of generic antibiotics. Moreover, the 
impact of a chosen regulatory pathway (ANDA and 505(b)(2)) on the process complexity is 
investigated.  
 
During this thesis data was gathered by interviewing experts from various departments of Xellia 
Pharmaceuticals what was further backed up by information from literature, reports and 
company documentation. 
 
As a result, the conducted research showed that commercial potential perspectives together with 
IP landscape are the main decisive aspects while deciding about development of a drug product. 
The analysis of two regulatory pathways indicates that they do not unequivocally affect the 
complexity of generic antibiotic development process. The choice of regulatory pathway does not 
impact the work intensity under patent landscape and commercial investigations. However, 
choosing 505(b)(2) regulatory pathway departments such as R&D and Regulatory Affairs showed 
a higher workload. Finally, it was noticed that close collaboration with a proper communication 
flow between departments is needed to develop a generic drug product and successfully bring it 
to the market.  
 
The thesis covers generic antibiotics, however, investigations and findings related to commercial 
perspectives, IP landscape and Regulatory Affairs can be also applied to generic drugs in general.   
 
This thesis will be used in Xellia Pharmaceuticals as a document to improve communication 
between departments by expanding knowledge of other departments and improving 
understanding of the whole process of generic antibiotic development. Moreover, it will be helpful 
for new employees, who have diversified educational backgrounds, to provide comprehensive 
overview of generic antibiotics development process.

Project aim 
_______________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________ 
The aim of this thesis is to identify and highlight challenges behind development of generic 
antibiotics in departments that are involved in this process. Moreover, the impact of a chosen 
regulatory pathway (ANDA and 505(b)(2)) on the process complexity is investigated. As a result, 
the process of generic antibiotic development is presented from the perspective of the following 
areas: Commercial potential, Regulatory Affairs, Patent Landscape and R&D, describing specific 
challenges particular department might have. These areas were identified, during initial 
discussions, to be the key fields to analyze generic antibiotic development process throughout 
project nomination to market launch. Production part and Quality Assurance aspects are not 
included in the thesis report. 
 
The thesis aims to improve communication between Xellia departments by expanding knowledge 
of other departments and improving understanding of the whole process. In addition, it will be 
beneficial for new employees of Xellia Pharmaceuticals, who have diversified educational 
backgrounds, to understand the process of generic antibiotics development. 
 
To achieve the above aims, analysis of individual contribution of various departments in the 
generic drug development process was conducted. It was done by interviewing experts from 
various departments of Xellia Pharmaceuticals backed up by information from literature, reports 
and company documentation.

1.2 Generic drugs 
The World Health Organization (WHO) defines a generic drug as ‘a pharmaceutical product usually 
intended to be interchangeable with the originator brand product, manufactured without a license 
from the originator manufacturer and marketed after the expiry of patent or other exclusivity rights’ 
[2]. 
 
Generic medicines have a significant impact on a pharmaceutical market. They help to improve 
patient access to medicines and generate savings, as they are less expensive than branded drugs. 
According to The U.S. Food and Drug Administration (FDA) when the first generic enters the 
market its price is approximately 31-39% lower than the branded product. Moreover, when on 
the market are already four competing generic products their prices are 73-79% lower comparing 
to the branded product. This shows that, as the number of competitors in the market increases, 
the price of generic medicines decreases [3, 4, 5]. Based on the report “Generic Drug Access & 
Savings in the U.S.“ published by Association for Accessible Medicines from 2017 the U.S. 
healthcare system has saved US$1.67 trillion in the last decade due to the availability of low-cost 
generics [111]. Moreover, the U.S. generic drug market in 2018 reached a value of US$ 103.8 
Billion and is expected to grow in upcoming years to reach US$ 190.4 Billion by 2024 according 
to the report “Generic Drugs Market: Global Industry Trends, Share, Size, Growth, Opportunity and 
Forecast 2019-2024” created by IMARC Group [6]. These statistics show the scale and importance 
of the generic medicines market.   
 
This project is focused on the United States regulations that is why the U.S. Food and Drug 
Administration guidelines are followed. There are various possible regulatory pathways to 
register drugs in the U.S. under submission of: New Drug Application (NDA) which is further 
divided into 505(b)(1) and 505(b)(2) sections, Investigational New Drug Application (IND), 
Biologic License Application (BLA) and Abbreviated New Drug Application (ANDA).

Generic medicines can be registered under ANDA, when they are ‘pure generics’ and are ‘identical’ 
to originator, or under 505(b)(2) section, when they are modified version of generic drugs [62]. 
505(b)(2) pathway is a type of hybrid between a ‘pure generic’ ANDA and a novel drug 505(b)(1). 
505(b)(2) allows making changes to the composition of the medicine relative to the original drug 
product and to submit studies which were not conducted by an applicant. Both sections ANDA 
and 505(b)(2) were added by the Hatch-Waxman Act in 1984 year which main aim was to 
promote greater access to generic drugs while maintaining their quality [7, 8]. An important 
section of Hatch-Waxman Act is a patent certification that promotes challenging patents that 
cover branded drug products [9]. When filing a generic drug application a pharmaceutical 
company must state if there are any patents listed in the Orange Book (the FDA website) that 
protect the branded drug and what is the correlation between patents and proposed drug product. 
There are four patent certifications that can be made and the applicant must certify to each patent 
respectively one of the following statement [7]: 
 
 
Paragraph I: no listed patents; 
 
Paragraph II: listed patents has expired; 
 
Paragraph III: listed patent will expire on the particular date; 
 
Paragraph IV: listed patent is invalid or will not be infringed.  
 
1.2.1 ANDA pathway  
Drugs that are submitted under Abbreviated New Drug Application (505(j) section) are duplicates 
of previously approved drug products. That is why they do not require to have performed pre-
clinical and clinical tests, as they rely on the studies which have been approved previously for  
a reference listed drug (RLD). That fact significantly lower development costs. ANDA must contain 
information to prove that the proposed generic product is the same as the RLD by providing 
bioequivalence (BE) studies. In other words, it has to be shown that the product acts in the same 
way as the original drug. It is done by the measurement of time which is needed to reach the 
bloodstream of healthy people. This provides information about the rate of absorption or 
bioavailability which is further compared with RLD. According to the FDA requirements, the 
medicine to be approved must deliver the same amount of active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) 
into a patient’s bloodstream in the same amount of time as the branded drug. Other requirements 
for generic drugs are to provide the same strength as originator, to have the same route of 
administration (such as oral, intravenous, intrathecal, subcutaneous or topical), they have to be 
manufactured under the same standards, belong the same type, and have the same indication as 
the brand-name drug [10, 11].   
 
However, for parenteral drugs as well as for eye and ear products bioequivalence studies are not 
necessarily required and a pharmaceutical company can request a waiver of BE under the FDA 
regulation. That is why parenteral drugs generally must contain the same inactive ingredients and 
in the same concentrations as the RLD. The only exceptions in which generic parenteral drug may 
differ from a branded product are buffers, preservatives and antioxidants but it must be shown to 
have no effect on how the medicine functions and its safety [7, 10, 11, 12, 13].

1.2.2 505(b)(2) pathway 
505(b)(2) pathway, has common features shared between both 505(b)(1), which is dedicated to 
novel drugs with full clinical studies, and ANDA. It is applied for modified version of a previously 
approved drug that requires additional non-clinical or clinical data. However, 505(b)(2) allows to 
get approval based on studies which were not conducted by the applicant and to which the 
applicant has not obtained a right of reference. They may be publicly available studies about safety 
and effectiveness or already approved applications by the FDA [7]. Drugs registered under 
505(b)(2) may differ from the original product in formulation, dosage form (for example tablets, 
syrups, creams) or a method of delivery. When compared with 505(b)(1), the 505(B)(2) pathway 
reduces costs of development as clinical trials studies do not have to be conducted by an applicant, 
is faster and reduces risk associated with drug development and its registration. However, 
505(b)(2) pathway requires careful consideration and planning. Pharmaceutical company has to 
analyze the amount and quality of supporting information available from reference products or 
the literature [14]. 
 
1.3 Antibiotics 
There are many definitions of the term ‘antibiotic’. According to the World Health Organization 
(WHO) antibiotics are defined as “medicines used to prevent and treat bacterial infections” [15]. 
 
In this project two drugs are used as examples daptomycin and voriconazole. Daptomycin is an 
antibacterial drug which represents ANDA registration pathway. Voriconazole is an antifungal 
agent used in the treatment of severe fungal infections and included in this thesis as an example 
of how the 505(b)(2) pathway was explored to gain early access to the U.S. market. Even though 
the antifungal drug was used as an example, the regulatory 505(b)(2) pathway can be applicable 
to any antibiotic or other drug.  
 
Nowadays, antibiotics are widely discussed due to antimicrobial resistance (AMR) crisis that 
poses a serious threat to global public health. Currently, at least 700,000 people die each year due 
to drug-resistant diseases. AMR is occurring naturally over time, however extensive overuse of 
antimicrobials in humans and animals is speeding up the process. WHO released a report in 2019 
calling for urgent reaction to prevent the emerging AMR crisis. According to the World Health 
Organization report, if no action is taken, drug-resistant diseases could cause more than 10 million 
deaths every year by 2050 [16]. 
1.3.1 Historical aspect of antibiotics 
The term ‘antibiotic’ was introduced by microbiologist Selman A. Waksman in 1947. However, the 
first observation that one microbe can inhibit other microbes was in 1877 when Louis Pasteur 
noticed that Bacillus anthracis in the presence of some aerobic organisms lost its virulence.  
A breakthrough in the history of antibiotics was the discovery of penicillin in 1928 by Scottish 
bacteriologist Alexander Fleming. Fleming noticed that Staphylococcus colonies which were close 
to contamination from Penicillium notatum dissolved. Moreover, Fleming found that mold’s 
product, penicillin, is able to kill a wide range of harmful bacteria, such as Streptococcus, 
Meningococcus and the Diphtheria Bacillus. The large scale production of penicillin was initiated 
in 1939 by biochemist Ernst Chain, and pathologist Howard Florey who worked on penicillin

Walter Florey received the Nobel Prize in 1945 "for the discovery of penicillin and its curative effect

1.3.2 Antibiotics classification 
Antibiotics can be classified into different types based on different molecular classes such as: 
aminoglycosides, amphenicols, ansamycins, beta-lactams, lipopeptides, diaminopyrimidines, 
fosfomycins, 
imidazoles, 
macrolides, 
oxazolidinones, 
streptogramins, 
polymyxins, 
sulphonamides, glycopeptides, quinolones and tetracyclines. Antibiotics that belong to the same 
class often show similar effectiveness, toxicity, propensity for antibiotic resistance development 
and potential allergic side effects [19].  
 
Antibiotics may also be classified by their mechanism of action. Antibiotics that are able to kill 
other microorganisms, are termed microbicidal (bactericidal). One of the mechanisms to kill 
bacteria is inhibition of cell wall synthesis which is done, inter alia, by beta-lactams. Moreover, 
bactericidal antibiotics may cause inhibition of bacterial enzymes or protein translation. On the 
other hand, there are also antibiotics that cause bacterial growth inhibition (named 
bacteriostatic). They are working by inhibiting bacterial protein synthesis pathways for instance 
by reversible inhibition of the 30S bacterial ribosomal subunit (as it is done by tetracycline 
antibiotics) or the larger bacterial ribosomal subunit 50S (by macrolides) [19, 20].  
 
Antibiotics can be administered to people, animals, and crops by many routes such as injectable 
(intravenous, intramuscular), oral, topical or spray. Intravenous (IV) antibiotics are often used for 
intensive care unit patients with serious infections, administered under oversee of healthcare 
professionals through a needle or catheter placed in a vein. Intramuscular administration is the 
most helpful when antibiotics cannot be given orally. The antibiotic is delivered into the body 
through injection or a shot into a muscle. Oral administration is done by antibiotics in tablet or 
liquid form often used in outpatient health care. Topical antibiotics are applied directly to the skin 
in the form of creams, ointments or liquids. Spraying method is mainly applied to crops and can 
be delivered by trucks or airplanes over crop land [21]. Products in a finished dosage form that 
are manufactured by Xellia Pharmaceuticals are solely intended for injection or infusion. 
1.3.3 Daptomycin 
Daptomycin is a cyclic lipopeptide antibiotic. It consists of a 13-member amino acid cyclic 
lipopeptide with a decanoyl side-chain (Fig. 1.2) and the molecular weight of 1620.7 g/mol. It is 
obtained from the fermentation broth of Streptomyces roseosporu and has a broad spectrum of 
activity against Gram-positive bacteria, both aerobic and anaerobic. As written in Chapter 1.3.2, 
antibiotics can be divided into those that kill bacteria (bactericidals) and those that inhibit 
bacteria’s growth (bacteriostatics). Daptomycin belongs to the group of bactericidals. The activity 
of daptomycin is calcium-dependent and limited to Gram-positive bacteria, however, the 
mechanism of action is not fully understood yet [22]. It seems to be based on the insertion of the 
lipophilic tail into the bacterial cell membrane, which causes a rapid depolarization of the 
membrane and a potassium ion efflux. This causes inhibition of DNA, RNA, and protein synthesis 
resulting in bacterial cell death [23].

Daptomycin according to WHO has been classified to the group of so-called ‘last-resort’ 
antibiotics, which are used as a last chance against serious infections when all other antibiotics 
fail due to its ability to act against antibiotic-resistant bacteria [30]. That is why it should be 
prescribed only in the most severe circumstances for example for life-threatening infections 
caused by multidrug-resistant bacteria. Last-resort antibiotics should not be used to treat simple 
infections to prevent the development of resistance to these antibiotics [24].  
 
Daptomycin together with linezolid, vancomycin and teicoplanin are the main antibiotics against 
methicillin-resistant Staphylococcus aureus (MRSA). Moreover, daptomycin shows effectiveness 
against other bacterial strains such as glycopepetide-intermediate Staphylococcus aureus (GISA), 
vancomycin-resistant enterococci (VRE), coagulase-negative staphylococci (CoNS), and penicillin-
resistant Streptococcus pneumoniae (PRSP). Due to the ability to effectively work against resistant 
strains daptomycin is an essential drug in the context of the world-wide antibiotic crisis [23, 31].  
1.3.4 Voriconazole 
Voriconazole is a synthetic derivate of fluconazole with molecular weight of 349.31 g/mol and has 
antifungal activity [32]. Antifungals are classified into five major classes: azoles, which inhibit 
ergosterol synthesis (the main fungal sterol) by inhibition of the lanosterol 14-alfa-demethylase 
enzyme; polyenes, that bind to ergosterol and create pores in the fungal cell membrane; 
allylamines, which inhibit squalene epoxidase that synthesize precursors to ergosterol; 
echinocandins that inhibit synthesis of beta-(1,3)-D-glucan that is a compound of cell membrane; 
and other agents, including griseofulvin and flucytosine. [108].  
 
Voriconazole belongs to the azole class. The drug was developed by Pfizer Pharmaceuticals under 
the brand name Vfend® and approved for medical use in the United States in 2002.  
 
Fig.1.4: Voriconazole structure [33].  
 
It is commercially available in 3 different dosage forms (Fig. 1.5): as a tablet, as a powder for 
suspension and as an injection. Voriconazole is indicated for the treatment of fungal infections 
including Invasive Aspergillosis, Esophageal Candidiasis and Serious Fungal Infections caused by 
Scedosporium apiospermum and Candidemia in non-neutropenic Patients. The drug is suitable for 
patients, who have 12 years or more.

Voriconazole has a wide-spectrum of activity against fungi such as Aspergillus and Candida spp, 
Scedosporium apiospermum, Fusarium spp.. Moreover, voriconazole has several-fold higher 
activity against Candida spp. than fluconazole and belongs to the list of Essential Medicines 
created by WHO. This list presents all the most effective drugs that satisfy the priority health care 
needs of the population and is updated by WHO every two years [36].   
 
The mechanism of action of voriconazole is based on inhibition of cytochrome P450– dependent 
14α-lanosterol demethylation, which is a vital step synthesis of ergosterol in the cell membrane. 
Ergosterol is the major sterol component of fungal plasma membranes. As a result of lack of 
ergosterol and accumulation of 14-α-methylated precursors both the fluidity of the membrane 
and the activity of several membrane-bound enzymes (for example chitin synthase) is altered. The 
major effect is an inhibition of fungal growth and replication. There are also observed other effects 
such as toxic effects on membrane phospholipids, toxic effects on membrane phospholipids and 
decreased fungal adherence [37].  
 
Voriconazole is in general well tolerated medicine. The most common side effect that occurs in 
approximately 30% of patients is a reversible disturbance of vision (photopsia). However, the 
symptoms usually tail off or disappear after the first week of the therapy. The second most 
common side effect is a skin rash. That is why patients should be informed to avoid direct sunlight 
during therapy. Another adverse effect that may occur while taking the medicine is hepatitis, 
which clinically apparent is unknown [38]. However, it may be related to the ability of 
voriconazole to alter human sterol synthesis. To control a liver function patients should be tested 
for liver performance before the therapy, within the first 2 weeks of therapy and then every 2-4 
weeks during treatment [38, 39].

The aim of this chapter is to identify and highlight complexity and challenges behind generic 
antibiotics development. Understanding work specificity of selected departments and their 
impact on the development of generic antibiotics is part of this Master's thesis and the knowledge 
presented in this chapter is the result of conducted studies. The following areas were investigated: 
Commercial potential, Regulatory Affairs, Patent Landscape and R&D. These fields are selected to

development phase.

represents ANDA regulatory pathway, voriconazole represents 505(b)(2) section. These

3.1 Commercial potential 
For pharmaceutical companies, the commercial potential of a new product is crucial while 
deciding about starting a new project. A favorable business case can decide whether a company 
will continue development or abandoned a project. That is why before a project will be nominated 
and selected for further development commercial perspectives have to be evaluated. This chapter 
contains information about how Xellia performs a commercial assessment pointing out 
parameters that should be taken into consideration during the development of a generic drug. 
Moreover, Xellia has established a process for assessment, nomination and approval of new 
projects known as the Portfolio Review Process, which is described in the 3.3.2 subchapter. As 
this master thesis project is conducted under the Technical University of Denmark the aim of the 
chapter is to give an overview of the commercial principles behind developing generic drugs 
instead of providing advanced business analysis.  
 
3.1.1 Commercial and financial aspects  
There are many factors that have to be considered during the development of generic drug 
including product identification, analyzing the patent landscape, choosing the most appropriate 
regulatory pathway and providing studies necessary to gain approval.   
 
In the early stage of drug development, it is necessary to evaluate both the market landscape and 
the company’s technical capabilities. The following aspects of the market landscape should be 
considered: the total size of the market; competitive environment; the number of brands of the 
same molecule in the market; acceptance of the molecule amongst the medical practitioners; and 
the future direction of the market for a drug product [40, 41]. 
 
Market landscape can be analyzed by using several databases such as Global Data, IQVIA (former 
IMS), Orange Book and IPD analytics. Global data may be useful while looking for data about the 
country situation, an overview of the pharmaceutical market in a particular country, 
reimbursement process or regulatory landscape. IQVIA is a database where sales data of drugs 
are available. It allows evaluating the market size, growth rates, market shares and average selling 
prices. It is a useful tool while preparing a market forecast. Orange Book is the FDA website where 
are listed all approved drugs in the United States. Moreover, in the Orange Book the following 
information are available: approved NDA drugs and their generic versions, patent information, 
regulatory exclusivity and therapeutic equivalence ratings [42]. The last of mentioned databases 
is IPD Analytics that provides data about drug life-cycle, forecast about brand and generic market 
and reimbursement information. 
 
Once the market landscape has been evaluated, the company’s technical capabilities have to be 
considered. At this point, it has to be analyzed whether the company has a manufacturing capacity 
to make the product once it gets approval. Moreover, it has to be clarified if this product requires 
additional investment in new facilities, equipment or human resources. All of these aspects are 
connected with costs which a company has to cover while deciding about starting a new project. 
They can be divided into operational expenditures (OPEX) and capital expenditures (CAPEX). 
 
==PAGE:26==
CONFIDENTIAL 
23 
 
OPEX means expenses related to maintaining a product, business or system. It covers inventory 
costs, utilities, marketing, payroll, insurance, business travel expenses. Operating expenses also 
consists of research and development expenses and the cost of goods sold (COGS). COGS is the 
total cost that a company has to cover while producing an exact product. It includes the costs of 
materials and labor directly used to create the goods. 
  
Capital expenditure covers investments that have to be done in order to develop a product. 
Moreover, expenditures may be for new equipment for instance additions to or replacement of 
existing equipment that affects an increase in capacity [43].  
 
All of these costs have to be compared to the anticipated profits from sales. For the project to be 
continued, the amount received must be profitable for the company. While evaluating forecasted 
sales, in Xellia, the commercial team assumes the best and the worst case in terms of market size, 
market share, net sales, revenue, and total project cost. In addition, the sales team has to estimate 
quantities the company can sell from launch up to 5 or 6 years in terms of volume and value as 
well as anticipate price over the years [40]. The additional cost that should be considered by a 
generic pharmaceuticals company is a cost of litigation. Most of the applications that certificate 
Paragraph IV ends up in a court. The average cost for Paragraph IV litigations is between 1-4 
million USD depending on the complexity of the case [44, 45]. This topic and types of Paragraphs 
that are used by generic pharmaceutical companies is further explained in chapter 3.3.  
 
3.1.2 Portfolio Review Meeting  
To nominate a new project or to monitor the development of ongoing projects Xellia conducts 
internal meetings called Portfolio Review Meetings where executive managers from different 
departments discuss and evaluate projects. These meetings are on a monthly basis and usually 
cover several projects. During the meeting, the updates from various departments like R&D, Legal, 
Regulatory Affairs and Sales & Marketing are given. Moreover, the executive members decide 
about project nomination or discontinuation. Normally, each project in Xellia is analyzed under 
the below topics:  
  
1. Feasibility phase, which covers value proposition of the project, analysis of IP landscape, 
definition of a target segment (market for the product). 
 
2. TPP – target product profile, contains all necessary data about the product such as: 
indication, dosage form, strength, RLD, package type, storage conditions, and shelf life. 
 
3. R&D potential strategy - scientists from API and FDF teams are involved in defining 
development strategy considering the FDA requirements with the support from the 
Regulatory Affairs department. 
 
4. Clinical/non-clinical program covers studies that have to be done for some 505(b)(2) 
projects. Their scope is defining individually depending on the requirements that have to 
be fulfilled.

5. Manufacturing strategy – estimation of internal production capabilities, deciding about 
the most efficient way of manufacturing.  
 
6. Regulatory strategy – defining the most appropriate regulatory strategy for a project (the 
topic is further explained in Chapter 3.2). 
 
7. Timeline, budget and R&D resources analysis.   
 
8. Risk description where potential risk is identified and its impact on a project. Usually, the 
risk matrix is done. 
 
9. Market assessment with the sales forecast, market evaluation, market price analysis, and 
participated market share.  
 
10. Financial business case evaluation with key assumptions provides data on potential 
earnings which the company can gain on the project after deducting investment costs.  
 
3.1.3 Challenges behind commercial perspectives  
The challenge identified from the commercial point of view is that market perspectives that were 
forecasted can change during developing a new drug. For instance, new competitors can appear, 
or prices can significantly drop which may not have been included in earlier assumptions. That is 
why the constant monitoring of the market has to be ensured to lower the risk.  
 
While comparing projects developed under ANDA regulatory pathway with 505(b)(2), ANDA 
projects, in general, are less complex. All of the above described topics of Portfolio Review 
meetings need to be analyzed both for ANDA and 505(b)(2) regulatory pathways except the 
clinical and non-clinical studies which are not required for ANDA (point 4) [46].

According to the FDA guidelines “Determining Whether to Submit an ANDA or a 505(b)(2) 
Application Guidance for Industry“ a sponsor should consider submitting a 505(b)(2) application 
instead of ANDA dossier when the proposed drug product contains changes to its formulation that 
are not permissible in an ANDA. For instance, Xellia Pharmaceuticals changed a cyclodextrin in 
voriconazole composition and that led to choose the 505(b)(2) pathway.  
 
Another situation when the 505(b)(2) pathway should be chosen is when a proposed drug 
contains novel excipient that has not been used before in products accepted by the FDA. Then the 
safety studies are required with clinical testing and it is advised to submit a 505(b)(2) application 
instead of ANDA. Moreover, certain differences in labelling will determine if a product should be 
registered as ANDA or 505(b)(2). The FDA compare the labelling of the proposed drug in 
accordance with the RLD. It is checked whether, there are any differences in the expiration date, 
formulation, bioavailability, or pharmacokinetics. In addition, when an applicant proposes a new 
dosing regimen or a new indication then 505(b)(2) pathway should be chosen.  
 
Another difference between ANDA and 505(b)(2) is stability study - more specifically, stability 
data which needs to be available at the time of application submission to the Authority. Exhibit 
batches for stabilities studies are done under GMP conditions during production phase. For ANDA 
6 months of stability data is required, but for 505(b)(2) - 12 months stability data. This is the 
factor which impacts the cost and time of development that is why it should be evaluated when 
deciding about regulatory pathway.  
 
While comparing the cost of application review by the FDA it differs depending on the application 
type. New Drug Application fee in 2020 is approximately 3.3M $ for application that covers full 
clinical data. When clinical studies are not required the cost is approximately 1.8M $. NDA fee 
applies to both 505(b)(1) and 505(b)(2). The cost for reviewing ANDA application is significantly 
lower and it is approximately 0.2M $ in 2020 [50, 51]. As described, the price differs between 
types of applications and therefore may have an impact on decision about development strategy. 
According to the FDA, the review period for these three application types is 10 months. However, 
it is a theoretical timeframe provided by FDA and in many cases this period is prolonged. It can be 
observed in the daptomycin and voriconazole example which are described below [47]. 
 
Summing up, the 505(b)(2) may be an appropriate pathway when the listed drug product is the 
same as a previously approved product but differs in dosage form, dosage strength, route of 
administration, or has a slightly altered active ingredient. However, the FDA will refuse to file  
a 505(b)(2) application for a drug that is a duplicate of a reference listed drug. Before deciding 
about the drug product development strategy all aspects from Table 3.1 should be taken into 
consideration by a pharmaceutical company. When an applicant needs a support from the FDA 
during development of a drug product it can request for assistance from the FDA. For ANDA it is 
possible to submit controlled correspondence to the Office of Generic Drugs (OGD) or request  
a pre-ANDA meeting with OGD. If a request applies to 505(b)(2) application then the applicant 
should contact the appropriate Office of New Drugs review division for assistance and ask for  
pre-IND or pre-NDA meeting [52].

3.2.2. Dossier preparation 
Dossier preparation chapter is divided into Drug Master File (DMF) that covers API dossier and 
The Common Technical Document (CTD) that is applicable to the medicines in their finished 
form. 
3.2.2.1. Drug Master File 
For a pharmaceutical company that is looking to obtain an ANDA or a 505(b)(2) approval, it is 
highly important to collaborate with a reliable API supplier in order to avoid any unwanted delays. 
That is why it is preferable to cooperate with a supplier who has fulfilled Drug Master File. This 
document, which specifically for API is DMF type II, compiles all the information that is required 
to demonstrate the quality of the drug substance. Moreover, the fact that the FDA will not start 
reviewing an ANDA or a 505(b)(2) application until DMF reviewing process is done, stress the 
importance of choosing a supplier with DMF. Especially, it may be crucial while seeking for  
180-day exclusivity period for first to file ANDA and any delays related to DMF dossier can prevent 
implementing this strategy, which can be associated with significant financial losses [47, 53].   
 
Although it is possible to submit a combined application containing both ANDA and DMF, Xellia’s 
general strategy is to submit them separately as it allows to make changes respectively to each 
application. Moreover, this strategy is useful when a company plans to sell API products to other 
customers, because when both API and FDF part are in the same application, there is no possibility 
to give the right of reference to DMF to other customers [47, 53, 54, 55].  
 
3.2.2.2 The Common Technical Document 
The CTD is a format set by The International Council for Harmonisation of Technical 
Requirements for Pharmaceuticals for Human Use (ICH), which was agreed by the Regulatory 
Agencies of Europe, Japan and the U.S. The document was designed to avoid any duplication and 
to make submission easier by using single application to more than one country. Electronic 
version is also available and it is called the electronic Common Technical Document (eCTD). CTD 
is organized into five modules, however, module 1 may differ from country to country and is not 
part of the CTD. Figure 3.1 presents all five modules with their classifications [57].

The different modules are as follows: 
 
 
Module 1: This module is specific to each region and is usually not part of the CTD. It 
contains all the administrative information and prescribing information including 
labelling, packaging and patent information.  
 
 
Module 2: It consists of the overviews and summary of the remaining three modules.  
Quality overall summaries, clinical and non-clinical overview are included in this module.  
 
 
Module 3: Quality – It covers the complete pharmaceutical and technical aspects that can 
affect the quality of the drug product. The chemistry, manufacturing, and controls reports 
are included.  
 
 
Module 4: Non-clinical study reports – It covers the completed pharmacological and 
toxicological study reports.  
 
 
Module 5: Clinical study reports – The results of various clinical trials that were done on 
volunteers and the reports that list the desired effect of the drug product. This part proves 
the efficacy data of the drug that has to be provided to the regulatory authorities. 
 
 
For ANDA it is required to provide information for the three first modules and partially for  
module 5, to demonstrate that the generic is bioequivalent to the RLD. However, parenteral drugs

3.2.3 Daptomycin development from RA perspective 
Xellia Pharmaceuticals submitted ANDA dossier for daptomycin to the FDA in 2013 and received 
tentative approval in March 2016. The application was submitted with Paragraph III certification, 
which means that the company would wait to launch the product until all patents listed in the 
Orange Book for branded daptomycin expired. In accordance with the judicial invalidation of the 
originator (Cubist) patent, Xellia decided to change its certification to Paragraph IV (the legal 
aspect is further discussed in chapter 3.3.2). The company subsequently received several 
deficiencies concerning labelling and CMC, which had to be clarified. Final approval was obtained 
on the 15th of June 2016. After launching a drug product Xellia has to submit an annual report with 
all changes done during the year as well as sales information. It is applicable both to ANDA and 
NDA [47, 58].    
 
3.2.4 Voriconazole development from RA perspective 
Xellia’s primary strategy was to submit an ANDA for voriconazole, however, due to patent 
protection that covered the excipient used in the branded version of voriconazole and difficulties 
with finding a supplier for this excipient the company decided to change the drug formulation. 
That is why, Xellia had to change drug formulation and therefore, change the regulatory strategy 
and submit the application under 505(b)(2) pathway.  
 
Using the right to ask the FDA for assistance before dossier submission, Xellia requested for  
a pre-IND meeting to clarify some aspects of the development of voriconazole. The meeting took 
place in the end of 2014. However, there were more issues that Xellia needed to consult with the 
FDA. That is why the company submitted a request for a pre-NDA meeting to understand FDA’s 
expectation regarding the application.  
 
After filing a dossier the FDA has 60 days to decide whether to accept the application so it can be 
further reviewed or decline the application. Xellia’s application for voriconazole was approved for 
review after 2.5 month. In the beginning of the review process Xellia got few Information Requests 
==PAGE:33==
CONFIDENTIAL 
30 
 
and Easily Correctable Deficiency Letter from the FDA about labelling and patent certifications. In 
May 2016 the company received information about tentative approval. It was due to the fact that 
one patent was certificated with Paragraph III. That is why Xellia had to wait until patent expiry 
to get the final approval. Subsequently, Xellia had to answer a few more Information Requests 
regarding chemistry, manufacturing, and controls (CMC). In March 2017 the company received 
final approval from the FDA [47, 59].   
 
3.2.5 Challenges behind Regulatory Affairs  
The Regulatory Affairs team has to closely collaborate with other departments in order to provide 
high-quality dossiers to the authorities to finally obtain approval. The identified challenge for the 
Regulatory Affairs team is related to the quality of the received documents. Usually, documents 
that are enclosed to the final application are composed by employees who are experts in specific 
fields. Documents are prepared on a high level, however advanced technical details may not be 
clear or well known to a person reviewing the application in the FDA. This results in receiving 
questions from the FDA.      
 
In addition, squeezed timelines, when documents and data come last-minute means that usually 
there is no enough time for action, which can further lead to mistakes. Moreover, in this highly 
regulated area, it is very important to stick to the deadlines and any delays could have a decisive 
negative impact on a dossier and may lead to delays in getting an approval. 
 
Comparing ANDA pathway with 505(b)(2) the identified difference from the RA point of view is 
in the complexity of dossiers. Most of Xellia products are parenterals for which ANDA requires 
only the three first modules of CTD, however, for drug registered under 505(b)(2) more 
comprehensive dossier is needed that covers all five modules [47].

3.3 IP landscape 
IP landscape has a crucial impact on generic drug development. Based on the current IP landscape 
a pharmaceutical company decides about medicine components, manufacturing methods and  
a regulatory strategy. As the patent landscape is constantly changing the Legal department is 
involved all the way from a drug nomination until the product launch. That is why the Legal team 
has to strictly cooperate with departments such as R&D, Regulatory Affairs, Global Portfolio and 
Sales & Marketing. 
 
In this section, the voriconazole and daptomycin patent situation is analyzed while Xellia started 
to work on these products. The current IP landscape of daptomycin and voriconazole is not 
included. In subchapter 3.3.1, the key knowledge about patenting of generic pharmaceuticals is 
described. The selected subjects are strictly related to the thesis and are necessary to understand 
the following sections. 
 
3.3.1 Patenting in generic pharmaceuticals  
Pharmaceutical patents can be divided into three categories in terms of type of claims [60]:  
 
 
Compound patents; 
 
Medical use; 
 
Formulation patents. 
 
The compound patents give the highest level of protection as they protect a drug no matter how 
it is formulated, produced, or indicated as far as it contains the patented compound. That is why 
generic pharmaceutical companies rarely try to invalidate patent on a compound [61]. This type 
of patents usually claims the API and its esters, salts and hydrates. To prevent competitors from 
producing similar products, often, it covers a broad range of similar compounds. Medical use 
claims provide intermediate protection among these three types of claims. They aim to protect 
the first, second or subsequent medical usage of a drug product. Formulation patents can 
effectively protect invention especially from generic companies that seems to reproduce branded 
drugs [62].  
 
Patent Certifications 
As described in the introduction, chapter 1.2, there are four patent certifications that can be 
made and the applicant must certify to each patent respectively one of the following statement: 
 
 
Paragraph I: no listed patents; 
 
Paragraph II: listed patents has expired; 
 
Paragraph III: listed patent will expire on the particular date; 
 
Paragraph IV: listed patent is invalid or will not be infringed.  
 
In ANDA applications, Xellia mainly refers to paragraph III or IV of which the most desirable is 
paragraph IV as it may give the possibility of 180 days exclusivity, which will be described further 
in this subchapter. Both, Paragraph I which is reserved for old drug products that were not 
protected by patents or Paragraph II that state that all patents expired, are related with products 
==PAGE:35==
CONFIDENTIAL 
32 
 
that are on the market for long time with usually with high number of competitors. That results 
with lower prices and therefore lower income for a generic pharmaceutical company [7, 42].  
 
While applying a generic drug approval using the Paragraph I or II certification, the FDA may 
accept the application immediately. With Paragraph III certification the FDA may approve an 
application any time after the patent’s expiration date [3]. The procedure to apply for ANDA or 
505(b)(2) paragraph IV certification is more complicated and is explained below [61]:  
 
1. Application filed to the FDA. 
2. After filing the application, FDA has 60 days to accept for further reviewing.  
3. Within 20 days after obtaining acceptance by the FDA for application review, the patent 
holder has to be informed about filed application by the applicant. 
4. If the patent holder files infringement suit against ANDA/505(b)(2) applicant within 45 
days then litigation starts.  
5. Then the FDA puts 30 months stay to approve the application. If the case is not taken to 
court, the FDA has the right to make decisions about approval or disapproval the 
application. 
6. If the court’s decision supports the applicant, then he gets the 180 days exclusivity to be 
on the market and no other can market generic drug during these 180 days. However, it is 
only applicable for the first filer. 
7. If the court gives decision in favor of the patent holder, FDA will not approve the ANDA. 
Then the available option for the applicant will be to do certificate II or III. 
8. When the 30 months period has elapsed, FDA can accept the application, if it fulfill all 
regulatory requirements, even though litigation is ongoing until the decision would be 
given by the court.  
 
The certification is required for both generic registration pathways ANDA and 505(b)(2). 
However, filing a 505(b)(2) does not give the right to the 180 days exclusivity, but may potentially 
give right to other exclusivities primarily reserved for branded products [9, 64].  
 
Exclusivity for generics 
According to the FDA the term ‘exclusivity’ describes a period when a brand-name drug is 
protected from competitors. Its aim is to promote a balance between new drug innovation and 
generic drug. There are seven regulatory exclusivities for branded drugs available under U.S. law 
[63]:  
 
 
New chemical entity – 5 years; 
 
New condition of use – 3 years;  
 
New indication – 3 years;  
 
New formulation – 3 years;  
 
Orphan drug – 7 years;  
 
Pediatric – 6 months and can be added to any other term of exclusivity;  
 
Generating Antibiotic Incentives Now (GAIN) – 5 years.  
 
For antibiotics it is also a possibility to obtain a QIDP (Qualified Infectious Disease Product) 
designation, which was designed to promote treatment for pathogens posing a serious threat to 
public health. However, it is not an exclusivity in itself. A drug having a QIDP designation may 
==PAGE:36==
CONFIDENTIAL 
33 
 
receive priority review and fast track status at the FDA. Furthermore, it may receive additionally 
5 years of regulatory exclusivity under GAIN. GAIN offers incentives for the development of 
antibacterial and antifungal drugs for human use to treat serious or life-threatening infections. 
The primary incentive is a 5-year exclusivity for drug products that have been designated as QIDP. 
A drug must be designated by the FDA as a QIDP in order to qualify for the GAIN incentive. The 5-
years GAIN exclusivity may be added to any exclusivity that the product has already. Accordingly, 
if the company develops a NCE that is also a QIDP, they may get a total of 10 years exclusivity [64].  
 
Moreover, there is also an exclusivity which may be applicable only to ANDA drugs. According to 
U.S. law, the pharmaceutical company that is the first to file a generic drug (ANDA) application in 
the Orange Book and also challenges at least one listed patent for that drug (by filing Paragraph 
IV certification) can gain a 180-day exclusivity period. It was identified as one of the most 
important goal for a generic company as usually the first generic on the market obtains the 
majority of the market share and has higher prices than following generic drugs. Furthermore, the 
profit generated during the 180-day exclusivity period often exceeds the profit made for the rest 
of the life of the generic drug [65].   
 
Authorized generics 
As the generic drug market is growing, brand-name companies are increasingly more determined 
in the effort to maintain a monopoly on their products. Two strategies, which originator can do to 
delay or thwart generic drugs are:  
 
 
Authorized generics arrangements;  
 
Citizen petitions to the FDA. 
 
 
According to the FDA’s definition, an Authorized generic is an approved brand name drug that is 
marketed without the brand name on its label. This may be marketed by the brand name drug 
company, or another company with the brand company’s permission.  
 
In many cases, brand name companies decide to launch authorized generics to grab the 
competitive advantage of being the first generic on the market. In addition, authorized generics 
are an option for generic manufacturers which would avoid drug development and pending patent 
challenges. Authorized generics can be marketed under originator application therefore they are 
not listed in the Orange Book, as both the brand drug and the authorized generic are produced on 
the same manufacturing line. The difference is that one is packed under a brand name and the 
authorized generic is packed under a different brand and sell as a generic with lower price. 
However, the FDA requires that the NDA holder will notify the FDA about launching an authorized 
generic. The NDA holder is allowed to launch both the authorized generic and the brand-name 
product at the same time. In addition, NDA holder can market authorized generics before or after 
180 days of exclusivity applied for ANDAs. It significantly lowers the income of the first generic 
on the market even up to 30% of its revenue [67, 68, 69].   
 
The citizen petition concept was designed to give an average citizen concerns about drug safety 
straight to the Food and Drug Administration. However, it is mainly used by pharmaceutical 
companies to block or delay approval for generic drugs. According to the Robin Feldman  
“A Citizen’s Pathways Gone Astray – Delaying Companies from Generic Drugs“ delaying generic drug 
competitors for a few months can result in hundreds of millions USD in revenue.  
==PAGE:37==
CONFIDENTIAL 
34 
 
Commonly, branded companies file a citizen petition in final dates before the FDA approves  
a generic product, although, they could file earlier in the process. Moreover, many petitions ask 
the FDA to wait with approval of generic drug until an applicant conducts the test which is already 
required to get approval. That shows that often the main aim for filing a citizen petition by 
pharmaceutical companies is to delay the approval process of competitors’ drug [70].  
3.3.2 Daptomycin 
Xellia started working on the daptomycin project in 2006 and the patent landscape was closely 
monitored until the product was launched due to constant changes. The company decided about 
starting daptomycin project as the drug fit into Xellia’s strategy to produce fermentation-based 
drug products. Moreover, the patent landscape and business case were favorable. 
 
The primary strategy was to collaborate with another pharmaceutical company Teva. Under the 
proposed agreement, Xellia was supposed to produce the API and Teva was going to manufacture 
the FDF. During that time Xellia Pharmaceuticals was mainly focused on production of API that is 
why the company decided about collaboration with Teva. The goal was to launch the product in 
2011. However, meanwhile Teva settled with the originator and decided to launch an authorized 
generic daptomycin. That means that Teva bought the drug from Cubist (the originator) and was 
selling it under its own brand. As a result, Xellia decided to apply for its own ANDA with Paragraph 
III certification. The reason behind choosing Paragraph III instead of Paragraph IV was that the 
company wanted to have the application on file and monitor the litigation situation, which was 
very dynamic at that time. Moreover, Xellia had the possibility to change the certification from 
Paragraph III to Paragraph IV later on, which can be done by sending a letter to the FDA. In 
addition, this strategy allows not to share to the public information that Xellia was working on  
a generic version of daptomycin due to the fact that filing ANDA with Paragraph III is not 
published anywhere until the FDA gives approval or tentative approval.  
 
During that time there were 6 active patents in the Orange Book:  
 








The patent of the number US RE39,071 has claims covering drug composition, both patents US

Between 2009 and 2014 few companies including Teva, Hospira, Fresenius Kabi filed ANDA with

3.3.3 Voriconazole 
The voriconazole project started in 2011 in Xellia due to favorable business case. The primary 
development strategy for the U.S. market was to find a sales partner and to file an ANDA with 
Paragraph III and paragraph IV certifications. The need behind certification of two paragraphs 
was due to the fact that the planned launch in the U.S. was during Q3 2016 and in the Orange Book 
there were the two active patents at that time:  
 
 
US 5,567,817  - expired in May 24, 2016;  
 
US 6,632,803  - expired in June 2, 2018. 
 
Paragraph III referred to patent number US 5,567,817 and meant that Xellia would wait until it

Paragraph IV the company stated that the listed patent was invalid or would not be infringed by 
Xellia and thus the company could enter the market with a generic version of voriconazole before 
the patent expires. Paragraph IV certification was referring to the patent US 6,632,803, which 
claims a pharmaceutical formulation comprising voriconazole and a cyclodextrin derivative [72]. 
 
The estimated challenges during that time were that Xellia would be late in the market compared 
to competitors as Sandoz that had already filed an ANDA with Paragraph IV. Pfizer (the originator) 
sued Sandoz in January 2011 on two U.S. patents: US 5,364,938 (expired in Nov 2011) and US 
6,632,803. Sandoz received the FDA approval on May 30, 2012 and launched the product the same

day. However, the only impact it had for Xellia was that there would be at least one other generic 
player in the market and that it could influence the company’s business case by lowering the

Due to the difficulties with getting access to the sulfobutyl ether beta-cyclodextrin sodium (an

3.3.4 Challenges behind IP landscape  
The key challenges during a generic drug development process from the IP perspectives have 
been identified. First of all, finding all relevant patents, not only through the Orange Book. For 
example, identify process patents as they are not listed in the Orange Book. It is needed to have 
the whole overview of the patent landscape about the product a company wants to develop. 
 
Moreover, the challenge is to understand patent claims, their scope and putting them together 
with R&D capabilities. To obtain this, tight communication between these two departments is 
needed. For instance, during development of the purification process of daptomycin, close 
collaboration between R&D and the Legal department was important as there were patents claims 
related to daptomycin purification and the Legal department had to guide the R&D to avoid 
infringing patents.  
 
The lesson learned from the daptomycin and voriconazole projects is that patent landscape, 
litigation, market space and competitors should be monitored by the company all the time. It has 
been 10 years since the daptomycin project started until the product was launched. Constant 
monitoring during these 10 years of the litigation situation was the key to success with 
daptomycin project and to finally enter the market as the first generic. That is why the company 
knew when to change the certificate from Paragraph III to Paragraph IV and was ready to launch 
the product just after the FDA approval. Moreover, to be successful with launching generic drugs 
good communication between various departments is needed. The legal department is 
responsible to inform other departments about litigation processes and changes in the patents 
landscape. This information sharing allows them to adjust to the current situation [44, 61].

3.4 Research and Development 
This subchapter has been prepared on the basis of interviews and internal documents of Xellia 
Pharmaceuticals and presents the R&D process during generic antibiotic development. The 
technical details of R&D development are not covered as the aim of this chapter is to give a general 
understanding of the process with a focus on the complexity and challenges behind it. Moreover, 
differences in the development between ANDA and 505(b)(2) drugs have been identified and will 
be highlighted.  
 
Both daptomycin and voriconazole are lyophilized, parenteral products. Parenteral drugs (para 
enteron - beside the intestine) are non-oral drugs that are directly administrated into body tissue. 
This type of therapy is preferred during serious infections because high therapeutic 
concentrations are achieved reliably and rapidly. Moreover, parenteral drugs characterize with 
high bioavailability, zero enzymatic degradation in the gastrointestinal tract and could be 
administered to unconscious patients.  
 
On the other hand, there are also some disadvantages behind parenteral dosage form. First of all, 
it has to be administered by trained personnel and requires strict adherence to aseptic 
procedures. Also, an injection is associated with pain or is any other unpleasant sensation. In 
addition, with this type of administration it is not possible or difficult to reverse its physiological 
effect. For example, in case of an adverse reaction, the drug cannot be removed from circulation, 
what for oral administration can be done by inducing vomiting. In addition, parenteral have to 
comply with rigorous requirements about sterility. That is why their manufacturing and 
packaging process is more demanding in comparison to other dosage forms [74].   
 
Typical routes of administration of a parenteral dosage form include subcutaneous, 
intramuscular, and intravenous delivery. Less common but also possible routes of administration 
are: intrathecal, intracisternal, intraarterial, intraspinal, intraepidural, and intradermal routes to 
achieve local or systemic effects [75].  
 
Parenteral medicines have strict requirements about their purity and must be free from physical, 
chemical, and biological contaminants. As they bypass the body’s natural defense against 
microorganisms, parenteral drugs must be sterile throughout their shelf life, free from endotoxins 
and visible particulate matter as well as compatible with intravenous (IV) diluents, delivery 
systems, and other co-administered drug products. To make the product as safe as possible, 
companies in the pharmaceutical industry must practice current good manufacturing practices 
(cGMPs) in the production of parenteral dosage forms and preferably follow quality by design 
approach during product development. According to the FDA quality by design is an approach 
that aims to ensure the quality of medicines by employing statistical, analytical and risk-
management methodology in the design, development and manufacturing of medicines [76]. 
 
Parenteral drugs can be divided into five categories based on their dosage forms:  
 
Solutions ready for injection; 
 
Solutions ready for dilution prior to administration;  
 
Dry products (powder-fill or freeze-dried products);  
 
Suspensions ready for injection; 
 
Emulsions ready for injection. 
==PAGE:41==
CONFIDENTIAL 
38 
 
 
Solutions ready for injection are liquid preparations that are drug substances or solutions thereof 
and do not require any other preparation before administration. They are applicable for drugs 
that are stable in solutions. Another dosage form, solution ready for dilution prior to 
administration, is a drug in the form of liquid concentrates and required further dilution in an 
appropriate solvent. Dry powder form which could be both powder-fill products and freeze-dried 
products is applicable mainly for drugs unstable in solutions. The powder form is ready to be 
combined with suitable solvent before administration by dissolving the powder or re-suspending 
it. If it is required further dilution should be done. Suspension ready for injection form consists of 
a liquid preparation of solid suspended in a suitable liquid medium. Emulsion ready for injection 
is a drug substances which was dissolved or dispersed in a suitable emulsion medium. This dosage 
form is applicable for drugs which have limited solubility in water [74, 77].   
 
Generic pharmaceutical R&D development process can be divided into two parts: development of 
active pharmaceutical ingredient (API) – the drug component that is responsible for the 
pharmacological and therapeutic activities in the body; and finished dosage form (FDF) 
development, which is a preparation of the product in its final form that is appropriate and 
approved to enter the market. Choosing a strategy for a final formulation of a parenteral drug 
depends on the route of administration of the product, pharmacokinetics of the drug, drug 
stability, drug solubility, and type of packaging.

3.4.1 Active pharmaceutical ingredient development 
In this subchapter development process of lyophilized daptomycin was used as an example to 
show active pharmaceutical ingredient development process. The Xellia’s antibiotics are 
produced based on a fermentation process. During fermentation, organisms produce the 
antibiotic material, which can then be isolated to use as a drug [78].  
 
API development process can be divided into 9 phases, presented in Figure 3.2. The individual 
phases will be presented in the following sections. 
 
 
 
Fig. 3.2: API process development [79]. 
 
3.4.1.1 Literature search – patents understanding 
The aim of this phase is to search for any available literature, which can provide a better 
understanding of API molecule and its production process. It is an ongoing process through the 
development phase as new relevant information can appear. All needed data and information 
about the molecule, its physical and chemical properties as well as process information such as 
molecule purification have to be collected in this phase in order to ensure to correctly plan the 
next stages of drug development. An important issue of the initial phase is to design and develop 
a process which does not infringe on any patent. To ensure this, the R&D department collaborates 
with the Legal department using available web pages; https://www.epo.org/ and 
http://patft.uspto.gov/. The first website is The European Patent Office (EPO) which examines 
European patent applications, the second is a database of The United States Patent and Trademark 
Office (USPTO) where U.S. granted patents and patent applications can be found. Furthermore, 
relevant literature is identified via the database https://www.rightfind.com. The information 
given in patents is used to not infringe any patents but also it is used in order to understand what 
kind of purifications steps (filtration, extraction, chromatography, precipitation etc.) will be 
needed and to estimate the complexity of the process [78, 80].

3.4.1.2 Fermentation 
The next phase, fermentation development, is run in parallel with the bacterial strain 
development and purification development. During this phase a scientist team do modifications 
to the media composition, vary amount and type of different nitrogen and carbon sources, vary 
operating parameters such as temperature, pH, stirring rate, fermentation time, follow oxygen 
consumption, actually adjust all parameters that can be adjusted in order to find the optimal 
conditions that give the highest yield.  
 
Both the strain development and the fermentation development is time demanding as there are 
many parameters to vary and the R&D team needs to look into the combination of all these, which 
leads to the complexity of this step. Moreover, the fermentation process consists of several steps: 
one or two seed steps before main fermentation, and optimization of each step [79, 80]. 
 
 
Fig. 3.3: Fermentation process development for daptomycin [80].  
 
Bacterial strains, that can produce the desired antibiotic, are commercially available. However, 
they produce a very low amount of antibiotics (often less than 0.1 g/L). That is why the scientists 
in the company have to make such modifications that the bacteria secrete more compounds. 
Usually, it is based on classical mutagenesis by introducing mutagens such as ultraviolet radiation, 
x-rays, or certain chemicals. Selection and further reproduction of the higher-yielding strains can 
raise yields by 20-fold or more. Another technique that can be used to increase yields is gene 
amplification, where copies of genes can be inserted back into a cell, by vectors such as plasmids. 
However, this process must be closely linked with the retesting of antibiotic production and 
effectiveness. Moreover, it is very important to control the level of impurities that can be produced 
by bacteria. That is why the purpose is to obtain the strain that at the same time does not produce 
a higher amount of unwanted compounds. The desired production amount of the product is highly 
depended on the bacterial strain. In some cases strains producing 2-3 g/L of the product is 
enough, however, for other strains 25 g/L will require further work to increase the production 
[80, 81]. 
 
Daptomycin production is done during a fermentation process. Oxygen concentration, 
temperature, pH, and nutrients must be in the optimal proportions. Moreover, bacteria need to be 
stimulated to produce antibiotics. For this reason a stress agent for example acid needs to be 
added into the fermenter. Another challenge for the research team is to develop optimal 
==PAGE:44==
CONFIDENTIAL 
41 
 
conditions for stimulating bacteria to produce large-scale antibiotics, but to avoid toxicity, as this 
can kill the bacteria [81]. 
 
3.4.1.3 Purification development and optimization 
Once the fermentation process is completed, the antibiotic must be extracted and purified. 
However, as presented in Fig. 3.2 the steps of purification development and fermentation 
development are on-going in parallel since this is essential for the fast development of a new API. 
Usually, during fermentation a lot of related substances are produced, that need to be removed or 
reduced. Moreover, different raw materials added during the fermentation have to be extracted 
from the final API. If there is a monograph available scientists know what aimed quality is and 
need to fulfil the given requirements. If a monograph is not available it is needed to define quality 
goals, which are normally based upon available guidelines from the FDA [79]. 
 
A typical purification process is divided into primary purification, secondary purification and final 
handling (Fig. 3.4). . The graphic was prepared based on data collected during the interview with 
Frode Flem, the Project Manager in Xellia Pharmaceuticals. During primary purification scientists 
usually remove residues from fermentation and other components that need to be removed and 
concentrate the solution before further purification. In the primary purification, the purity of the 
antibiotic is normally unchanged. While in the secondary purification, the purity is increased to 
obtain wanted end purity. In final handling, isolation of the product is performed. Based on the 
quality criteria, the R&D team has to develop the purification process to meet these requirements 
[79, 81].  
 
 
Fig. 3.4: Purification steps: primary, secondary and final handing [80].  
 
3.4.1.4 Synthesis development and optimization  
During a purification process, changes in the molecule can be done. However, this process, can 
possibly insert other impurities. That is why another purification is needed. Synthesis step seldom 
has 100% conversion towards the wanted end molecule and often a mix is produced. Then 
additional purification is needed in order to remove the unwanted components.

Synthesis development is an additional step and only some antibiotics requires it. For an example 
daptomycin is not a semi-synthetic antibiotic, as the fermentation process produces the wanted 
molecule [80]. 
3.4.1.5 Analytical development 
The analytical development process consists of:  
 
 
Isolation of fermentation impurities; 
 
Characterization of reference listed drug (RLD) and API; 
 
Development of analytical methods.  
 
During fermentation many kinds of secondary metabolites are produced, which can be very 
similar to the molecule of interest. Identification of impurities and their isolation is important to 
control fermentation and purification process, however, it is one of the most challenging part of 
API development. That is why analytical methods, such as UPLC to determine the purity, GC to 
investigate the solvent content and NMR to characterize the structure , have to be applied in the 
early phase of both fermentation and purification [78].  
 
For daptomycin, approximately 20 impurities have been isolated and characterized. Some of them 
were very difficult to remove during purification. Therefore, isolation of impurities is time 
demanding [79].  
 
Moreover, knowledge about the purity profile of RLD is essential during the development process 
as it is required to have a similar purity profile of the molecule in comparison to the RLD. Some of 
the methods the API team has to develop by themselves, but often monographs are available.  
A monograph is published by different authorities in order to have a common set of requirements 
and analytical methods to use for the analysis. However, for the same drug product, the criteria in 
a monograph might differ between the different authorities. Then different customers also might 
have their own criteria that an API supplier need to fulfil. That brings additional complexity to the 
development process [79, 80].  
 
3.4.1.6 Production Scale-up  
The methods used during the laboratory research must be applicable to the large scale 
manufacturing. Going from laboratory scale to pilot scale and finally, full production scale Xellia 
Pharmaceuticals do the fermentation development in 2-30L scale before scaling up to 200-1000L 
pilot scale. Typical production scale is 20-40000L.  
 
As the company does not have a dedicated pilot plant for purification so called ‘big lab scale’ is 
used. For example one fermentation batch of 40000L could be split into several smaller 
purification batches.

CONFIDENTIAL 
43 
 
Dependent on the purification process itself and type of unit operations needed for purification it 
can be conducted as follow [79, 80]:  
 
 
Chromatography: laboratory development 1-5cm column diameter, scale-up 10-30cm 
column diameter, production scale 20-100cm column diameter. 
 
Extraction: laboratory development 0,1-5L, scale-up 10-100L, production scale 1000-
20000L. 
 
Precipitation/crystallization: laboratory development 0,1-2L, scale-up 5-50L, production

3.4.1.7 Method transfer and Technology transfer to production site  
Analytical method transfer is a transfer of the analytical methods from R&D to the production site. 
Such a transfer have its own requirements in comparison to the technology transfer of  
a production process (Fig. 3.2). All analytical methods need to be validated for their intended use, 
typically it is done by R&D, and then all methods are required to be verified at the production site 
and controlled to obtain similar results [78, 79]. 
 
Technology transfer of a production process such as fermentation, purification, and synthesis  
(if the process includes this step) is usually done both by reporting of the process in tech transfer 
reports and face-to-face discussions. These reports and documentation are further used while 
filing DMF or CTD [79].  
 
The challenge behind this step is that the production site is often not known during the 
development, or such knowledge comes late in the development work [80].

During the literature review different ways to classify and divide the Finished Dosage Form 
development process were found. However, this thesis is based on the internal company 
document ‘FDF guideline Xellia’. According to this guideline the process can be divided into  
5 phases (Fig. 3.6). 
 
 
Fig. 3.6: Finished dosage form development process [77].

Each of these phases plays a critical role in the development of a drug product, and information 
learned in one phase drives the development of the next phase. The individual phases will be 
presented in the following subchapters. 
 
3.4.2.1 Pre-formulation development 
The aim of the pre-formulation development is to generate information about characteristics of 
reference listed drug (RLD) and to gain knowledge on physicochemical and biological properties 
of drug substance (API). For internally developed API, substantial information is already available 
from the API development process [77, 84]. 
 
The activities during this phase include literature search and characterization of drug substance 
and reference drug product. During literature search goal is to identify the most relevant available 
literature data including scientific publication related to a drug substance and its characteristics, 
and information in relation to reference listed drug by review of regulatory information as well 
as a collection of all relevant patent publications. 
 
Moreover, various screening studies are conducted, such as solubility and degradation studies 
(when temperature, pH, light stability, and the potential of the drug to oxidize are checked) in 
order to determine the conditions in which the API is the most stable as well as to evaluate and 
understand possible degradation pathways [77].  
 
All pre-formulation studies serve as a basis for the selection of suitable formulation, to predict the 
behavior of the drug substance in final dosage form and to facilitate the planning of the 
development process by identification of critical parameters at this stage [84]. 
3.4.2.2 Formulation development  
Once the pre-formulation development is done, the formulation development phase is conducted.  
 
The goal of the formulation development phase is to define the optimal qualitative and 
quantitative composition of the final dosage form, which during production would yield a finished 
product of high-quality attributes [84, 85]: 
 
 
Accurate drug content and identity; 
 
Sufficient stability of a drug substance during storage and administration; 
 
Usage of appropriate containers that ensure the maintenance of sterility, adequate dosing 
and administration; 
 
Adequate safety and tolerability, including the absence of microorganisms, physiological 
tolerance of the formulation, adequate tonicity, solution pH and free of particles. 
 
Active pharmaceutical substances are rarely administered alone. In most cases, the active 
pharmaceutical substance will have to be transformed into a medication using one or more inert 
components, known as excipients.

Moreover, selection of suitable container closure system which ensures maintenance of sterility 
and integrity of the product from the moment it is manufactured till it is delivered to the patient, 
throughout the entire shelf life is a very important part in the development of FDF [87].  
 
Both aspects: excipients and container closure systems are further described below. 
3.4.2.2.1 Excipients 
According to United States Pharmacopeia (USP) an excipient is any component, other than active 
substance which is present in a medical product or used during manufacturing [82]. Along with 
the main functionality to transform API into an appropriate final dosage form that can be utilized 
safely, excipients may facilitate administration and modulate the release of the active component. 
In addition, they can stabilize API against degradation from the environment and impart useful 
properties to the formulation. Excipients have an impact on patient comfort as they are able to 
lower tissue irritation and pain by making the solution more isotonic or near physiological pH [77, 
84]. 
 
There are multiple groups of excipients which could serve different functions within parenteral 
dosage forms, including diluents, tonicity and pH adjusting agents, solubility enhancing agents, 
stabilizers, antioxidants and preservatives. Selection of suitable excipients is based on type of 
dosage form and its characteristics as well as characteristics of drug substance, and it is evaluated 
during pre-formulation development phase [77, 87]. 
 
Further requirements that must be applied to excipients concern microbiological contamination 
compatibility and stability. Many components used as excipients could be contaminated with 
microorganisms. Pharmacopeias, therefore have set a limits to the content of bacteria and fungi, 
limits strict especially for substances used in parenteral formulations. For parenteral formulation 
special type of excipients, “parenteral grade” are provided by material suppliers and selected by 
final dosage forms manufacturers [87]. 
 
In parenteral final dosage forms which are delivered in primary packaging intended for multiple 
use, excipients for protection against growth of microorganisms must be added. Based on the USP 
antimicrobial agents (preservatives) must be added in their bacteriostatic or fungistatic 
concentrations and must be present, at any given time during product shelf life.  
 
Moreover, interactions between drugs and excipients are also possible and may affect the 
therapeutic effect and safety. Therefore, the compatibility of the drug substance with excipients 
should always be evaluated. Furthermore, special attention needs to be paid to the impurity 
profile of the excipient as presence of impurities can have an impact on the quality of the finished 
product and may for instance lead to degradation of the API [77].   
 
For selection of excipients important information is whether excipients are already approved for 
parenteral use can be checked in several ways. Approved excipients are listed on the FDA website 
[77, 83].     
3.4.2.2.2 Container closure system 
Drugs which are administrated in the injectable form are packed into containers made of glass or 
plastic. Containers in this case are understood as ampoules, bottles, vials, syringes, cartridges and 
==PAGE:49==
CONFIDENTIAL 
46 
 
IV bags. Of the above, only the ampoules are always glass and the IV bags are always plastic, other 
containers can be composed of either glass or plastic and include rubber materials, such as rubber 
stoppers for vials and bottles and rubber plungers and rubber seals for syringes and cartridges 
[77].  
 
Most of small volume injectables (with volume up to 100ml) are packed into glass containers. 
Sterile products, such as ophthalmic solutions, large and small volume parenterals are usually 
packed into thermoplastic polymers. According to Michael J. Akers, the author of the book ‘’Sterile 
Drug Products: Formulation, Packaging, Manufacturing and Quality’’ there are three major 
difficulties associated with thermoplastic polymers:  
 
1. Penetration of vapors and other particles in both directions through the wall of the plastic 
container; 
2. Leaching plastic components into the product; 
3. Absorption and/or adsorption of drug molecules or ions on the plastic material. 
 
On the other hand, the advantages of using plastic packaging materials are that they do not break 
like glass, and also significantly reduce weight [77, 110]. 
 
The most common container closure system for parenteral products is nowadays the glass vial, 
closed with a rubber stopper and an aluminium crimp cap. The glass vial can have different 
dimensions (2-100 ml). While choosing the appropriate rubber it is important to avoid rubber 
stoppers made of natural rubber as it brings a risk of the presence of latex that is a well-known 
allergen. Rubber stopper has defined dimensions and elasticity which allow the introduction of  
a needle to the vials and resealing as soon as the needle is withdrawn. The stopper and vial is 
closed using an aluminium crimp cap that also serves as a closure [85, 86, 88].

3.4.2.3 Process development 
The manufacturing process development is conducted in parallel with formulation development, 
and is based on API characteristics and type of dosage form. The goal of this phase is to define  
a process in the laboratory scale, which reflects the production scale possibilities and assures 
manufacture of the product of predefined quality on a large scale [86]. 
==PAGE:50==
CONFIDENTIAL 
47 
 
 
Many of anti-infectives including daptomycin and voriconazole are made by the lyophilization 
process. Lyophilization (freeze-drying) is the most common manufacturing process for unstable 
products and has taken a great prominence in the parenteral drug industry. It is mainly used for 
products which cannot be marketed as liquid dosage forms or cannot be manufactured as a dry 
powder [77, 86].  
 
The principle of the freeze-drying technique is based on the observation that frozen water has  
a significant vapor pressure and can be removed by sublimation. Freeze-drying systems consists 
of a temperature-controlled drying chamber with adjustable trays, where liquid product filled into 
suitable vials is placed as well as condensation chamber and a vacuum pump (Fig. 3.7) [109]. 
During laboratory process development, parameters for freeze-drying cycle are defined [84]. 
 
Fig. 3.7: Freeze-drier [109]. 
 
Freeze-drying consists of three stages: 
 
1. Freezing: the freezing conditions are selected to achieve complete solidification of the 
formulation solution (normal pressure, temperature up to -50°C based on formulation 
composition thermal properties). 
2. Primary drying: the frozen water is removed by sublimation under reduced pressure, and 
as a result pores are formed in the product at the place of the disappearing ice crystals, 
leading to a fine, highly porous structure. 
3. Secondary drying: serves to remove traces of remaining moisture (under vacuum, at the 
temperature up to 40°C). 
 
In addition, appropriate sterilization method has to be selected. This is done based on product 
characteristics and could be terminal sterilization or sterile filtration. For the lyophilized products 
mostly sterile filtration of the intermediate product is used, not terminal sterilization, due to their 
temperature sensitivity [87,109]. 
 
==PAGE:51==
CONFIDENTIAL 
48 
 
During filtration, the separation of the microorganisms is accomplished by a mechanical sieve 
effect or by adsorption using membrane filters with a defined pore structure. Membrane filters to 
produce bacteria-free solutions should have a pore diameter of maximum 0.20-0.22 µm. Selection 
of the most suitable filter membrane material is based on characteristics of formulation solution. 
 
The manufacturing process based on freeze-drying technology and filtration sterilization method 
usually consists of the following steps: 
 
1. Dispensing and compounding of the bulk solution; 
2. Filtration; 
3. Filling into pre-sterilized vials and partial vial stoppering; 
4. Freeze-drying and full vial stoppering; 
5. Vial sealing and optical inspection ; 
6. Labelling and packaging.  
 
Moreover, during this stage critical steps and parameters of the manufacturing process are 
evaluated and defined. It includes the order of material added during compounding, the 
concentration of the bulk solution, filter compatibility study, mixing and temperature parameters 
in compounding, and freeze-drying cycle parameters. Furthermore, to ensure the quality of drug 
products, hold time studies for each manufacturing phase as well as for overall manufacturing 
process have to be evaluated under defined environmental conditions during production (such 
as: temperature, humidity, oxygen, light) [85, 87]. 
 
 
3.4.2.4 Analytical methods development 
In parallel with the formulation and process development activities, appropriate analytical tools 
and methods are developed to evaluate the API and finished drug product characteristics.  
 
Some of the commonly used analytical methods during FDF development are Ultra-performance 
liquid chromatography (UPLC), High-performance liquid chromatography (HPLC), Liquid 
chromatography-tandem mass spectrometry (LC-MS), Karl Fischer titration, microscope, particle 
analyzer as well as microbiological methods: bioburden, sterility, bacterial endotoxins, microbial 
assays, antimicrobial effectiveness and susceptibility testing [84]. Developed accurate and 
reliable analytical methods are used to constantly monitor the quality characteristics of all 
prepared formulation during development. Special attention is taken to monitor the level of 
impurities and impurity profiles of different formulations manufactured under different 
conditions to determine the potential influence of added excipients and manufacturing process 
parameters on stability of drug substance [85].   
 
In addition, fast-screen stability methods are established under accelerated conditions (higher 
temperature and humidity) to evaluate the stability of the formulation in long term condition and 
facilitate selection of the most suitable formulation and container closure system [87].

3.4.2.5 Process compatibility and scale-up  
After laboratory phase is completed, the manufacturing process is further optimized in bigger 
scale throughout the manufacture of the scale-up batch, in size 10-100 times bigger than 
laboratory scale experiment, in production facility intended for commercial production [87]. 
 
When the final formulation and manufacturing process are defined in laboratory-scale it is very 
important to ensure that production in a manufacturing facility will be performed in the same way 
and the drug will be in contact with the same materials as during the development phase. 
Therefore, a scale-up batch is produced to ensure that the product made in production have the 
same quality specifications in comparison to laboratory scale. 
 
During the development process, many scientific reports are written covering all the development 
steps. These reports are then summarized in regulatory documentation (Module 3 of CTD) needed 
for submission. Knowledge reflecting the product and process design at the laboratory scale is 
shared with a production site, with all details, risk assessment and documentation [77, 85]. 
 
3.4.3 Challenges behind R&D  
Each of the R&D phases (API and FDF) consists of many stages, which occur in parallel and are 
complex. Each stage is carried out by trained experts who are specialized in a specific field.  
 
Based on discussions with two experts Knut Danielsen, Senior Director of API R&D Department 
and Frode Flem, Project Manager on the daptomycin API development project, identification and 
characterization of impurities have been identified as key challenges during API development 
phase [78, 80].  
 
Through FDF development stage lyophilization in general has been identified as the most 
challenging process, however it strongly depends on a project scope and characteristic of the 
product [87].  
 
The complexity behind selecting ANDA or 505(b)(2) pathway for the R&D department can only 
be analyzed on the stage of FDF as the API for voriconazole is supplied by an external company to 
Xellia. Development of the daptomycin FDF was more straightforward process while comparing 
to voriconazole FDF. While developing ANDA the scientist team exactly know what is the goal and 
have to find available methods and techniques to develop a product, however, during working on 
505(b)(2) very often there is a need to change an excipient or formulation and the FDF team has 
to do various tests to first of all find an optimal product and later to confirm its effectiveness and 
safety. That shows that the scope of the project is different for ANDA and 505(b)(2).  
 
However, there were identified difficulties while working on daptomycin FDF but they are related 
to the physicochemical properties of the antibiotic. The challenge was to establish optimal pH 
range and to prevent foaming during production. It was solved by slowing down the addition of 
API. As the daptomycin was filed to the FDA as ANDA it was not allowed to change or add any 
extra excipients to suppress foaming [85].

from perspectives of selected departments. The specificity of work in these departments has been

4.1 Main findings 
Below, the four main findings are listed which are further discussed in the subsequent paragraphs.  
 
1. Commercial potential together with IP landscape are the main decisive fields during 
generic drug development process in generic pharmaceuticals companies. 
2. The choice between ANDA and 505(b)(2) registration pathway does not unequivocally 
affect the complexity of antibiotic drug development process. 
3. In addition to drug product development strategies that define a regulatory pathway, 
there are also strategies for deciding what type of Paragraph should be chosen to certify. 
4. Close collaboration with proper communication flow between departments is needed to 
succeed with a generic drug product.  
 
 
4.1.1 Commercial potential together with IP landscape are the main 
decisive fields during generic drug development process in 
pharmaceuticals companies 
When the project was initiated the assumption was that R&D development phase was the most 
challenging throughout the whole process. However, after initial research it was realized that the 
key challenges in generic drug development are related to the IP landscape and the commercial 
potential including the market competition. The Legal department and the commercial teams 
mainly determine the strategy of the project and its commencement. It can be concluded that IP 
landscape and commercial potential specify what can be done regarding a project and 
departments such as Regulatory Affairs and R&D indicate how it can be achieved.

4.1.2 The choice between ANDA and 505(b)(2) registration pathway 
does not unequivocally affect the complexity of antibiotic drug 
development process 
One of the focus areas of the project was to identify the differences between the two regulatory 
pathways ANDA and 505(b)(2). Each of the development stages were analyzed to understand how 
these two different pathways affect the complexity of antibiotic drug development process. The 
research findings show that while investigating the patent landscape and commercial potential 
there are no clear correlation between chosen pathway and the complexity in the development of 
generic drugs. However, for the FDF department, as well as a Regulatory Affairs, the chosen 
pathway has an impact on the workload.  
 
When investigating IP landscape each project should be analyzed individually no matter which 
regulatory pathway is chosen. That is why the decision about choosing ANDA or 505(b)(2) 
pathway does not have an impact on project complexity or workload when analyzing from the 
Legal Department point of view. In the examples used in the research, the daptomycin project filed 
via the ANDA pathway was more demanding for the Legal Department and more time consuming 
than voriconazole, which was filed through the 505(b)(2) pathway. However, this cannot be 
considered as a general rule as it results from the complicated and very variable legal situation 
that pertain branded version of daptomycin (Cubicin®). Hospira, another generic pharmaceutical 
company was able to invalid Cubist’s patents that protected Cubicin® which significantly impacted 
application process for generic daptomycin.  
 
The complexity behind selecting ANDA or 505(b)(2) pathway for the R&D department can only 
be analyzed on the stage of FDF as the API for voriconazole is supplied by an external company to 
Xellia. In the analyzed cases of daptomycin and voriconazole it can be concluded that the choice 
of 505(b)(2) pathway for voriconazole contributed to increase complexity during development 
phase. The main challenge was finding a substitute for the sulfobutyl ether beta-cyclodextrin 
sodium. If the company decided to develop voriconazole on the ANDA pathway, it would use the 
same cyclodextrin derivative that was used in the original Vfend® formulation and thus save time 
which was spent on finding the substitute. During daptomycin development (ANDA pathway) the 
main difficulty was related to the physicochemical properties of the drug: finding the right pH 
range and avoiding foaming during production. The challenge was not related to the chosen 
regulatory pathway.  
 
From the regulatory point of view the only difference that has been identified was observed in the 
comprehensiveness of the dossier for 505(b)(2). While filing 505(b)(2) for parenteral drugs all 
five modules of CTD have to be provided while for ANDA only first three modules are required.  
 
Global Portfolio and Sales & Marketing departments have comparable work load for both ANDA 
and 505(b)(2) projects.

4.1.3 In addition to drug product development strategies that define a 
regulatory pathway, there are also strategies for deciding what type of 
Paragraph should be chosen to certify 
This finding is discussed in the following subsections that covers: company’s manufacturing 
capabilities impact on a decision about drug development strategy, impact on the stated 
certification, and examples of strategies that were implemented by other companies while 
deciding about regulatory pathway.  
 
4.1.3.1 Company’s manufacturing capabilities and available resources as deciding 
factors while choosing a strategy for drug development 
As it was described in Chapter 3.3 Xellia generally aim to register drugs under ANDA section due 
to lower costs and shorter time needed for development and approval. Using Xellia as an example 
it can be noticed that the decision about a drug product development strategy, which further 
defines a regulatory pathway, is connected with the company’s manufacturing capabilities and 
available resources. For instance, approximately 15 years ago when Xellia started the daptomycin 
project the company was mainly producing API. That is why it was planned to develop the product 
in collaboration with another pharmaceutical company, TEVA, who was supposed to manufacture 
daptomycin in its finished form using Xellia's API. However, over time Xellia was growing and 
gradually changing its strategy from being an API manufacturer to become an integrated FDF 
manufacturer. That fact had an impact on the daptomycin project, resulting in the decision that 
Xellia developed the antibiotic in its finished form. Looking into the future it seems that Xellia will 
be more focused on developing innovative 505(b)(2) drugs and preferentially introduce these on 
the market under its own brand. However, this step is time demanding to implement as it requires 
additional resources.    
 
On the other hand, on the market, there are generic pharmaceutical companies which have other 
strategies than Xellia. For example, Nevakar or Latitude are specialized in developing ‘innovative 
generics’ and their primary strategy is to file an application under 505(b)(2) section [90, 91]. 
Therefore they avoid litigation processes due to patent infringement. 
 
4.1.3.2 Impact of the stated certification (Paragraph I, II, III or IV) 
In addition, another significant finding of this thesis is the importance of using different 
paragraphs in the generic drug application. Based on the examples investigated in the project, the 
impact of the stated certification (Paragraph I, II, III or IV) is evident. The chosen paragraph may 
determine the success of the project. The decision to initially file ANDA with Paragraph III 
certification for daptomycin was taken to avoid sharing information with the public that Xellia 
was working on a generic version of this drug. It allowed monitoring the litigation situation and 
take action and change certification to Paragraph IV at the most appropriate time. This 
successfully resulted in Xellia being first to market with a generic version of daptomycin and 
thereby achieved a competitive advantage.  
 
In the case of voriconazole, the company used the 505(b)(2) pathway with Paragraph IV, which 
states that the listed patent is invalid or will not be infringed. Choosing this pathway allowed to 
==PAGE:57==
CONFIDENTIAL 
54 
 
modify excipients, therefore, Xellia could support the application with the statement that they did 
not infringe any patent listed in the Orange Book for Vfend® as they changed the excipient which 
was protected by the patent. On the other hand, if Xellia decided to continue their primary 
intention to develop voriconazole as ANDA, then the non-infringing aspect would probably not be 
possible to fulfil. In that case, the company would have to prove that the patent is invalid. In 
general, proving that the patent is invalid is more challenging to demonstrate than proving that 
the patent is non-infringed [61]. 
 
4.1.3.3 Strategies implemented by other pharmaceutical companies    
Looking from broader perspective there can be identified other strategies used by pharmaceutical 
companies while deciding about regulatory pathway. The reason behind choosing 505(b)(2) can 
be to circumvent the first ANDA filer’s 180-days exclusivity. As an example, this strategy was 
applied by generic company, Dr. Reddy’s Labs. They developed a generic version of the 
cardiovascular drug Norvasc® developed by Pfizer and approved in 1992. However, the company 
Torpharma was the first to file ANDA and got 180-day exclusivity, thereby blocking Dr. Reddy’s 
Labs from launching their product. That is why Dr. Reddy’s Labs decided to change the regulatory 
pathway to 505(b)(2) and modify the drug composition in the comparison to the original product. 
It allowed to avoid being blocked for 180 days and gain potential market advantage over 
competitors [49]. 
 
Another example why generic manufacturers may decide to file an application under 505(b)(2) is 
the possibility to gain exclusivity that is primarily designed for new drugs filed under 505(b)(1). 
This strategy can be noticed in Austedo®, the medicine that is used to decrease involuntary 
movements in Huntington's disease. Austedo® is a modified version of Xenazine® which was 
approved in 2008. The implemented change was replacing one or more hydrogen atoms by 
deuterium. Due to the structural difference between Austedo® and Xenazine®, the drug was able 
to get 5-year exclusivity for New Chemical Entity. When comparing it with exclusivity which is 
applicable for ANDA (180 days), the possibility to gain few years exclusivity filing 505(b)(2) 
application looks very favorable for pharmaceutical companies. However, in this situation 
application filer should expect that it may be required to provide clinical studies which will 
significantly enlarge costs and extend development time [49].  
 
Moreover, each project must be analyzed individually. There are no specific paths to follow when 
defining a strategy of particular case. The examples listed above can be used as inspiration for 
subsequent projects. The key when choosing a strategy is individual analysis of the patent 
landscape and market situation.

various departments is needed to succeed with a generic drug product 
The thesis project has shown that the generic antibiotic development is a complex process where 
various departments such as Legal, Regulatory Affairs, Global Portfolio with Sales & Marketing, 
and R&D department, which includes both API and FDF need to be actively involved. Looking into 
this process from a broad perspective there are many activities that take place in parallel. These 
activities were described in Chapter 3.

In addition, before and during API and FDF development legal department has to monitor and

Chapter 5 – Conclusion 
_______________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________  
Before starting the thesis project, two months were spent on researching and understanding 
generic antibiotics development and functioning of this industry. Having that knowledge five 
months ago the hypothesis was stated that ‘the complexity of generic antibiotics development is 
driven by challenging R&D work (API and FDF), the given IP landscape, market conditions and the 
regulatory strategy chosen for market entry’. It was investigated by analyzing the work of 
different Xellia Pharmaceuticals departments that are involved in generic drug development 
process. Moreover, two different regulatory pathways (ANDA and 505(b)(2)) were described and 
their impact on development process was analyzed.  
 
After conducting the research the hypothesis was confirmed, however, gained knowledge allows 
to extend the statement. First of all, there were identified different roles which the departments 
have and their impact on generic drug process. As a result, a commercial potential together with 
IP landscape have been identified as the main decisive fields in generic pharmaceutical 
companies. Secondly, the outcome of the research clearly showed that close collaboration with 
proper communication flow between departments is needed to succeed with generic drug 
product. Looking into two regulatory pathways (ANDA and 505(b)(2)) it was noticed that they do 
not always affect complexity and workload. For instance, the choice of regulatory pathway does 
not impact the work intensity under patent landscape and commercial investigations. However, 
choosing 505(b)(2) pathway fields such as R&D and Regulatory Affairs showed higher workload. 
Finally, it was observed that in addition to a drug product development strategy that further 
define regulatory strategy, there are also strategies for deciding what type of Paragraph should 
be chosen to certify. That is why the decision about a patent statement has to be carefully 
considered by pharmaceutical companies. 
 
Summing up, pharmaceutical industry is a very complex and dynamic field. Generic 
pharmaceutical companies have to face many difficulties and challenges, and in parallel, experts 
from various specializations need to be involved and collaborate while developing generic 
antibiotics.

Chapter 6 – Perspectives 
_______________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________________ 
The purpose of this chapter is to look into the antibiotics topic from a broader perspective that 
was not covered in the research. Currently antibiotics are widely discussed regarding the issue of 
antimicrobial resistance (AMR) crisis that causes a serious threat to global public health.  
 
AMR is when microorganisms (bacteria, fungi, viruses and parasites) gain resistance to 
antimicrobial drugs such as antibiotics, antifungals, antivirals, antimalarials and anthelmintics 
[93]. Therefore, these drugs become ineffective and infection can spread on a large scale. In 2004, 
over 70% of pathogenic bacteria were resistant to at least one of the currently available antibiotics 
[92, 93]. Currently, at least 700,000 people die each year due to drug-resistant diseases. AMR is 
occurring naturally over time, however extensive overuse of antimicrobials in humans and 
animals is speeding up the process. World Health Organization (WHO) released a report in 2019 
calling for urgent reaction to prevent the emerging AMR crisis. According to the WHO report, if no 
action is taken, drug-resistant diseases could cause more than 10 million deaths every year by 
2050. These numbers can be seen on Figure 6.1. The AMR crisis will affect all regions however, 
Africa and Asia are predicted to be harmed the most [94].     
 
 
Fig. 6.1: Number of deaths worldwide due to AMR every year by 2050 [95]. 
 
Antimicrobial resistance is a broad and complex topic that affects all of society. That is why there 
is a need for coordinated actions through all countries to minimize spreading of AMR. To achieve 
it a collaboration between various sectors such as human health, animal health and agriculture 
with a support from political side is needed.  
 
According to WHO guidelines there is a need to strengthen the knowledge and awareness of 
antimicrobial resistance to promote infection prevention by regularly washing hands, avoiding 
==PAGE:62==
CONFIDENTIAL 
59 
 
close contact with sick people, preparing food hygienically, and respecting the vaccination 
schedule. Moreover, antibiotics should be used only when prescribed by physician and follow 
prescribed recommendations to limit spreading of AMR [96].  
 
The action is also needed from animal health and agriculture sectors. Antibiotics should be only 
provided to animals under veterinary supervision. Antibiotics should not be used as prevention 
in healthy animals or to promote their growth. Moreover, animals that are carrying resistant 
bacterial are potential risk for farmers and others who have contact with them. In addition, 
exposure to crops that were treated by anti-microbial substances also brings a risk with resistant 
bacteria infection, as food is one of the possible vehicles to transmit resistant bacteria from 
animals to human beings. Moreover, consumption of food carrying antibiotic-resistant bacteria 
can lead to the acquisition of antibiotic-resistant infections [92].  
 
Another problem that should be highlighted while analyzing AMR situation is a declining number 
of pharmaceutical companies that conduct research in order to develop new antibiotics. 
According to the WHO report over the past three decades, the number of large pharmaceutical 
companies that fund and maintain studies under R&D of antibacterial therapies has declined 
dramatically [92, 96]. Pharmaceutical companies often switch from anti-infectives to more 
profitable therapeutic areas, for example, musculoskeletal and CNS. That is why no major new 
class of antibiotics has been discovered since 1987 and only a few antibacterial agents are in 
development to meet the challenge of multidrug resistance what increasing the AMR crisis and 
lack of effective treatment [92, 94, 96]. 
 
Therefore, there is a need for political actions to support the healthcare industry to invest in R&D 
of new antibiotics, vaccines and diagnostics to prevent and control the spreading of AMR. An 
example is QIDP designation which aim is to encourage companies to develop medicines for 
pathogens that pose a serious threat to public health. Moreover, in the U.S. it is possible to obtain 
GAIN exclusivity which provides 5 years exclusivity on the market for drugs approved with QIDP 
designation and a priority review [97]. This designation and GAIN exclusivity are described in 
chapter 3.3.  
 
According to Francesca Prestinaci the author of the article ‘Antimicrobial resistance: a global 
multifaceted phenomenon’ the antimicrobial resistance is especially urgent regarding antibiotic 
resistance [98]. That is way, In October 2019, WHO implemented a new project ‘AWaRe 
Classification Database’ that is a part of Essential Medicines List where antibiotics are grouped 
into three categories: ‘Access, ‘Watch’ and ‘Reserve’. The ‘Access’ group contains antibiotic that 
have activity against a wide range of pathogens, which show lower resistance potential. According 
to the WHO these antibiotics should be easily available, affordable and quality-assured. The 
second group ‘Watch’ antibiotics is indicated for higher resistance potential pathogens and the 
“highest-priority critically important antimicrobials”. That is why these antibiotics are indicated 
only for specific and limited use. The last, ‘Reserve’ group include only so called ‘last-resort’ 
antibiotics that can be used only in the most critical circumstances when antibiotics failed. 
‘AWaRe’ was implemented to improve usage of antibiotics and ensure appropriate treatment, 
therefore, reducing antimicrobial resistance [99, 100, 101].  
 
Xellia Pharmaceuticals in its portfolio has 4 drugs that are on the Essential Medicines List and  
3 antibiotics that belong to the Reserve group [102]. One of them is daptomycin, the antibiotic 
==PAGE:63==
CONFIDENTIAL 
60 
 
which was used as an example in the report, the second is Polymyxin B and the third is Colistine 
[103]. Xellia as a leading global manufacturer of anti-infectives, that provide a meaningful 
treatment for serious infections, is actively involved in actions to prevent AMR. First of all, the 
company conduct responsible manufacturing. It means that Xellia practices several techniques to 
ensure that all waste obtained during production are discharged under pre-defined acceptable 
standards. It allows to reduce impact on environment and lower AMR development [104]. The 
waste discharge problem was especially noted in China and India where regulations regarding 
discharge of untreated water waste did not control it properly. As a result, high number of 
pharmaceutical manufacturers were discharging waste generated during production of 
antibiotics into rivers and the surrounding environment. However, hopefully this problem will be 
solved as Indian government has already established limits on the amount of antibiotic residue 
permitted in wastewater released by pharmaceuticals manufactures on the 23rd of January 2020. 
In addition, pharmaceutical companies should not only implement discharge limits for antibiotic 
waste in their site but also require it from their suppliers, to lower the risk of spreading antibiotic 
resistance [105, 106].  
 
The next action to prevent AMR is to provide a consistent supply of quality established anti-
infective for responsible, human medical use only. Most of Xellia’s products are intendent for use 
only by qualified healthcare providers. Moreover, the anti-infectives offered by Xellia are 
affordable and responsibly priced as part of the commitment to a reliable supply [104]. 
 
In addition, the company invests in R&D to provide new innovative treatments based on 
established antibiotics. Xellia’s aim is to produce safer, easier to use and more efficient products 
to preserving the safety and long-term effectiveness of existing anti-infectives [104].  
 
The example of Xellia shows that generic pharmaceutical companies can be actively involved in 
counteracting the global AMR crisis. In addition, Xellia as a long-term manufacturer of anti-
infectives with extensive know-how would be an ideal candidate to extend its R&D activities to 
also include development of new and innovative antibiotics.
