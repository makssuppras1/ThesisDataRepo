Abstract
The current tendency towards the use of renewable biomass feedstocks instead of fossil fuels,
has created the need for development of new, innovative production processes within the chemical
and biochemical industries. The replacement of oil with biomass as the primary material for fuel
and chemical production is basically the idea behind the bioreﬁnery concept.
Glycerol is selected as the raw material to be converted to value-added chemicals due to the fact
that is -currently- produced in big amounts in bioreﬁneries as a by product from diesel production.
Its eﬀective utilization has consequently been the focus of many bioprocesses.
The case of the simultaneous production of two platform chemicals using glycerol as a starting
material is investigated, namely of 3-hydroxypropionic acid (3HP) and 1,3-propanediol (1,3PDO).
Glycerol is subjected to biotransformation where recombinant resting cells of Lactobacillus reuteri
are used in an aqueous solution.
The resting cells initially grow in a fermenter at 37oC and
then the biotransformation of glycerol is performed in a stirred reactor, under mild conditions.
This biotransformation has 3 main products, 3-hydroxypropionic acid (3HP) and 1,3-propanediol
(1,3PDO), that are of interest as well as signiﬁcant amounts of 3-hydroxypropionaldehyde (3HPA)
as by product.
The goal of the project is to develop a methodology with the objective of a co-production in
equimolar amounts. The methodology tackles problems such as reaction analysis, reactor design,
determination of the separation units that achieve the product puriﬁcation etc.
and is based
on model-based tools, such as databases (CAPEC database), property prediction tools (ICAS-
ProPred) and commercial simulators (PRO/II). Thus, the optimal production process towards 3HP
and 1,3PDO simultaneously and compare it to the existing methods that produce one product,
either 3HP or 1,3PDO. After the methodology is applied to all three cases, the best is determined
in terms of productivity, product yield, sustainability and economics. Two sub-cases are considered
for the co-production case, one isolating and the other recycling part of the intermediate product,
to evaluate the eﬀect the recycling has on the product yields.
The co-production (recycling case) was determined to be a very promising alternative, because
it indeed shifts the equilibrium of the biotransformation and achieves good product titers and
product yields and very high productivity rated, while being sustainable, proﬁtable and simple.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Up until mid 19th century, fossil resources such as coal, mineral oil and gas were the main sources
for production of energy, chemicals and materials and that derived as a consequence of the rapid
growth of petrochemical reﬁneries (Dishisha, 2013). These reﬁneries consume much energy and
cause heavy pollution, so with their progress, big amounts of greenhouse gases (GHG) that derive
basically from pyrolysis and gasiﬁcation processes are emitted leading to heavy environmental
deterioration (Chen et al., 2017).
Thus, that triggers the need, to shift to greener approaches to meet the increasing demands of
the growing population in addition to fossil resources being limited and environmentally unfriendly.
Their negative environmental impact such as global warming, the greenhouse eﬀect, acid rain etc.,
made this need even more substantial for today’s society (Dishisha, 2013). Eco-friendly, green,
bio-based and renewable raw materials that are both cheap and available were put into focus as
alternative sources for renewable energy.
Hence, great interest is shown nowadays in the shift from fossil fuels to more renewable feed-
stocks that are based on biomass (Dishisha et al., 2014). Industries are constantly making research
on clean energy and on producing chemicals that have a lower carbon footprint and can, however,
be considered eﬃcient alternatives to the petroleum-based products (Pachapur et al., 2016).
1.1
Bioprocesses
1.1.1
An overview
Bioprocesses are biological processes aiming at the production of industrial chemicals and com-
pounds (Sindhu et al., 2017). A bioprocess is deﬁned as any kind of process that utilizes living
2
==PAGE:17==
1.1. BIOPROCESSES
cells or their components to eﬀect a desired physical or chemical change and involves an upstream
and a downstream part. Their special characteristic diﬀerentiating them from chemical processes
is that they can be easily inﬂuenced by various process parameters.
They deal with the use of natural and recombinant microorganisms (such as yeast, fungi or
bacteria), enzymes as catalysts in organic synthesis (Keshavarz, 2005). They play a great role in
the areas of food, pharmaceuticals, vitamins, specialty chemicals etc.
Figure 1.1: The role and applications of biotransformations as of Keshavarz (2005).
Bioprocesses usually consume less energy and use inexpensive raw material as feed (Sindhu
et al., 2017).
They produce a wide range of products such as enzymes, antibiotics, proteins
and biopolymers. They have wide applications in the food, chemical as well as pharmaceutical
industries as ﬁgure 1.1 is illustrating.
As Keshavarz (2005) suggests, bioprocesses generally consist of ﬁve sections that need to be
investigated. These are: (i) catalyst and preparation of the raw material, (ii) reactions, (iii) biocell
recovery for reuse or destruction, (iv) product recovery and puriﬁcation and (v) waste disposal.
1.1.2
Enzymatic bioprocesses
By deﬁnition, a catalyst is a substance that aﬀects a chemical reaction, altering its rate and speed,
without undergoing any change. Enzymes are natural catalysts that are used to increase the rate
of the chemical reactions that take place within living cells, without them being chemically aﬀected
(Keshavarz, 2005). This is performed by providing an alternative reaction pathway; they break
and remake bonds. The activation energy for this new pathway is often less than the activation
3
==PAGE:18==
1.1. BIOPROCESSES
energy of the normal pathway (Bennett et al., 2005). Most enzymes used in biological processes
are proteins.
The reactants of a reaction catalyzed by enzymes are called substrates (Keshavarz, 2005). The
catalysis takes place only in a speciﬁc place of the enzyme which is called active center. A catalyst
enhances the reaction rate, whereas an inhibitor decreases it.
This is the reason why a great
number of enzymes use a non-protein component to increase or reactivate their catalytic activity,
which is referred to as cofactor (Keshavarz, 2005).
The enzymes requiring a cofactor are inactive in the absence of one (apoenzymes). The apoen-
zymes together with their cofactors are termed as holoenzymes.
There are generally two types of cofactors and these are:
• small organic molecules that bound to the enzyme, called coenzyme and
• metal ions and organic molecules that loosely bound to the enzyme and are called prosthetic
group.
Enzymes are remarkable catalysts because they are capable of accepting a wide range of com-
plex molecules as substrates and have a unique selectivity (Schmid et al., 2001).
Their high
selectivity together with their operation under mild conditions (Woodley, 2008) deﬁnes them as a
very good option for process synthesis. The process steps are importantly reduced and that leads
to reduced E-factor (mass ratio of waste to product). Thus, enzymes are considered a very good
and environmentally friendly alternative to the conventional chemical catalysts.
Regarding the enzymatic inhibition, two kinds of inhibition are observed in bioreactions; the
reversible and irreversible ones. In the reversible inhibitions, it is possible for its action to be
canceled if the concentration is decreased, whereas in the irreversible ones there is no way of
canceling its action, due to the formation of covalent bonds.
Two kinds of enzymatic bioprocesses are going to be investigated in this report, namely fer-
mentation and biocatalysis. Their characteristics are brieﬂy described in the following sections.
Fermentation
Fermentation is a bioprocess that can produce diﬀerent kinds of commercial products Keshavarz
(2005), with the help of microorganisms. Biomass, yeasts, metabolic products, antibiotics, amino
acids, vitamins, microbial enzymes are only a few of these products.
These microorganisms require water, a carbon source (such as glucose, starches, sucrose, lactose
etc.), energy, a nitrogen source (such as ammonia, ammonium salts etc.), minerals, vitamins and
4
==PAGE:19==
1.1. BIOPROCESSES
growth factors.
The reactions are performed in special reactors called fermenters and the process is operated
either in batch, fed-batch or in continuous mode.
When operating continuously, the feed and
nutrients need to be constantly sterilized and it is very crucial that no contaminants enter the
fermenter, because that might lead to a change in the strain’s characteristics.
Fermentation can be an aerobic or an anaerobic process. The cell growth starts as soon as a
quantity of living bio cells is added to the fermenter, in a solution containing the required nutrients
at the correct operating conditions (Keshavarz, 2005). It is a very sensitive bioprocess, meaning
that any small change on the fermentation environment can cause a change in the process itself.
When cells are growing, they undergo through 4 diﬀerent phases. The ﬁrst one is called lag
phase, when there is no special change in their number. The duration of lag phase is determined
by the characteristics of the bacterial species as well as the media, each time. The next phase is
referred as exponential growth phase or logarithmic phase, when an increase in the cell number is
going on, that is almost exponential with time. The third fermentation phase is called stationary
phase, in which due to the depletion of nutrient or to accumulation of undesired toxin, there is
a pause in the cell growth. It usually is the time when the nutrients get fully exhausted. If the
stationary phase is caused by the exhaustion of the nutrient, then a dilution with an inert solvent
can assist, without aﬀecting the cell number, whereas if it is caused by the accumulation of toxin,
a dilution can increase the number of cells.

Finally, the fourth fermentation stage is the death phase, during which the number of pro-
duced cells is declining in an exponential relation. Figure 1.2 can give a complete overview of the
fermentation phases. Figure 1.2 gives a schematic overview of the four phases.

1.2. BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION
Biocatalysis
Biocatalysis is a process that besieges biological systems to catalyze the conversion of one com-
pound to another (Ghisalba et al., 2010). The catalyst can consist of whole cells, cellular extracts
or even isolated enzymes. Its basic characteristic that diﬀerentiates biocatalysis as a process from
fermentation is that the biotransformation is independent of cell growth, thus no biomass is formed.
It is an industrial process used a lot nowadays that has a big potential to create cleaner as well
as more eﬃcient bioprocesses and even replace existing chemical processes (Liese et al., 2016). It
enables very high selectivities to be achieved therefore, it plays an important role for the production
of chemicals. More and more biocatalysts are being discovered every day either as wild-typed or
as engineered enzymes.
Biocatalysis in some cases allows reactions that are not easily conducted by organic chemistry
by replacing a few chemical steps (Petersen and Kiener, 1999). It involves highly chemo-, regio-
and stereoselective bioconversions to occur, thus simplifying manufacturing processes. For those
biocatalytic conversions that do not require any regeneration of coenzyems, both enzymes and
whole cells can be used (Schmid et al., 2001). When cofactors are required, though, whole cells
are preferred since they enable the cofactor regeneration.
In addition, despite the fact that enzymes present relatively low yields when they are put in
non-aqueous solutions compared to the aqueous ones, there are many developments targeted in
that direction so as to enable the enzymes to properly function in organic solution as well, having
high activities and consistent selectivities.
Regarding the economic feasibility of biocatalytic processes as Schmid et al. (2001) suggests,
it depends on several factors that regard mainly the type of biocatalyst to be used.
1.2
Background and motivation
It is expected that the implementation of bioprocesses can contribute to a greener and more
sustainable environment (Heinzle et al., 2007). They are economically competitive processes in a
growing number of industries. In order for them to be the optimal candidate for process synthesis,
however, a series of challenges have to be confronted, a few of which are presented hereby.
1.2.1
Bioprocesses’ limitations and challenges
Bioprocesses also present a few limitations, which need to be treated if good product yields are
to be obtained. One of bioprocesses’ biggest limitation is the sensitivity of the enzymes in many
6
==PAGE:21==
1.2. BACKGROUND AND MOTIVATION
process parameters, such as temperature, pH (Cen and Tsao, 1993). Therefore, their constant
control is of vital importance so as to maintain the activity of the enzymes.
Another big challenge is creation of big amounts of waste.
The waste leaves the process
boundaries as solid, liquid, and gaseous streams (Heinzle et al., 2007). It has to be isolated from
the product of interest and treated accordingly. It is very positive, though, that the by products
and other wastes that are generated have a low pollution potential, since bioprocesses use water
as the main solvent and the catalysts used are not toxic.
The produced waste in combination to the low product titer that is typically obtained in
bioprocesses (usually does not exceed 15 g/L) drives the need for bioprocesses to recycle the
biomaterials that are used or produced (such as enzymes or products). Thus the waste is reduced
and the reaction conditions can be potentially inﬂuenced. That can also lead, apart from increased
product titer, to higher product yields and productivities.
Another limitation of bioprocesses is that their development can last several years and require
many development steps. The cost of the development is depending on the product, the complexity
of the process, and the demand of the application. According to Heinzle et al. (2007), a typical
development of a bioprocess can last around 10-15 years in order to be fully implemented. Plus,
the research is involving simple or more complicated metabolic engineering.
1.2.2
Motivation and objective of the thesis
According to the aforementioned limitations of bioprocesses, derives the motivation to research
and design a process that uses renewable feestock, produces small amounts of waste, operates
under mild conditions, is performed in relatively little reaction time and applies simple metabolic
engineering.
The challenge this process sets is to produce two products at the same time in
equimolar quantities so that the feedstock is exploited to the maximum, an the bioprocess operates
once towards double production.
A methodology is developed and applied aiming to assist in the complete understanding of
the metabolic engineering of the reaction and the parameters that can inﬂuence and increase the
production. thus achieving high product titer, product yield and productivity. The process that
is set into focus is the utilization of glycerol for the production of 1,3-propanediol (1,3PDO) and
3-hydroxypropionic acid (3HP) using metabolic engineering and the enzyme Lactobacillus reuteri.
Currently there are processes developed towards the production of either 1,3PDO (both in mini
and industrial scale) and 3HP (in mini scale) separately and one that is targeted in the equimolar
co production (in a lab scale) (Dishisha et al., 2014). Thus, the challenge of the process that is

Chapter 2
Theoretical background
2.1
The desired products and their importance
2.1.1
3-Hydroxypropionic acid
3-Hydroxypropionic acid is a β-hydroxycarboxylic acid, a structural isomer of lactic acid and a
bio-based chemical of increasing importance (Dishisha, 2013). It has a wide range of industrial
applications but its main importance is that it is a precursor of acrylic acid, which is a raw
material of great demand, used in paints and detergents (Honjo et al., 2015). In addition, 3HP can
be chemically converted into acrylic esters and it enables the sustainable production of polymers,
plastic, paints, etc. In the past, it was produced biologically from glycerol by Klebsiella pneumoniae
and Escherichia coli strains from glucose at neutral pH by Escherichia coli and lately, at even
lower pH by the yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae. It is preferable to run and operate the process
at lower pH in order to reduce the downstream cost as well as the produced waste. Moreover, the
neutralization or the salt conversion to free acids will deﬁnitely add an extra cost to the process
(Werpy et al., 2004).
It is important to point out that neither chemical nor biological industrial processes have been
established yet, for the production of 3HP, mainly because of the lack of economic resources as
well as of the environmental compatibility for the majority of the suggested production routes
(Dishisha, 2013). In addition, its industrial application still requires further research because of
the low product concentration and reaction productivity.
Much attention is drawn to the biotransformation of 3HP from sugars and especially glycerol,
so that the global market demand is met; reaching 3.63 million tons per year in 2008 (Werpy et al.,
2004; Yu et al., 2016). The current fermentation needs involve the improvement of the microbes

of the reactions.
Despite the lack of concrete information about the volumetric demand of 3HP in the following

with a great potential of growth, for the aforementioned reasons (Dishisha et al., 2015).
2.1.2
1,3-Propanediol
1,3-Propanediol is a bulk chemical of increasing importance used in the chemical industry nowa-
days. It is used in the manufacture of polymers, cosmetics, medicines, lubricants as well as as
an intermediate in the heterocyclic compounds synthesis (Drozdzynska et al., 2011). Due to its
adaptable properties and its use as a monomer in the synthesis of various polymers, it is consid-
ered to be one of the most important compounds produced by glycerol. Its major application ﬁeld
is the textile industry where its reductive nature makes it a suitable monomer for polymer and
copolymer synthesis via reaction with dicarboxylic acids (such as succinic acid and lactic acid).
It can also be used as a chemical additive in food, pharmaceuticals, personal care, fragrances,
detergents, cosmetics, insect repellents as well as in various types of medicine such as vitamin H
and immunosuppressive drugs.
Its high reactive nature makes it a good candidate for many polymerization and condensation
reactions to produce eﬃcient and economical biodegradable polymers to replace the current syn-
thetic pollution-causing plastics and thus, prevent the ecological imbalances that can be caused by
toxic pollutants polluting the environment.

industry.

Moreover, the costs of production were high.

A big advantage of this method is that the byproducts involved in the process are non toxic.
There are some limitations present in this type of production, such as the relatively high cost of
the substrates (Drozdzynska et al., 2011).
2.1.3
Production Pathways
Great attention is being drawn today in bio-based processes especially in comparison to chemi-
cal processes, due to the fact that there are no high temperatures or pressures involved and the
generation of high levels of contaminants can be avoided (Ganesh et al., 2012). Until now, almost
all studies have focused on the 3HP production using genetically engineered microorganisms. A
signiﬁcant number of companies and research groups have tried to develop recombinant microor-
ganisms to produce higher levels of 3HP from bio based glucose and glycerol . There are signiﬁcant
advantages when comparing the biological synthesis routes of the previously mentioned chemicals
to the chemical routes (Jiang et al., 2009). The most signiﬁcant ones are:
• The gradual shift from the expensive petroleum dependence to the use of renewable resources
which will happen with the improvement and optimization of biological processes
• Even though fermentation as a process produces quite big amounts of carbon dioxide, they
are signiﬁcantly smaller than the ones produced by conventional chemical processes.

an alternative feedstock for glucose, if its higher availability, high degree of reduction and lower
price are taken into account. Glycerol is obtained as a by-product during the transesteriﬁcation
of vegetable oils and animal fats. Until now, it had a low price on the market as the outcome of
biodiesel industries which have resulted in producing huge amounts of crude glycerol (Jiang et al.,
2009).
Glycerol can be converted to 1,3PDO by various enzymes such as Citrobacter, Klebsiella, Clo-
stiridium, etc. It is quite an old method, dating back to 1881, when it was ﬁrst reported. However,
focus is put upon the organisms taking part in this process since they are proven to be pathogenic
(Drozdzynska et al., 2011).

2.2.2
3-Hydroxypropionic acid production process
Many attempts have been made in order to determine the optimal conditions for the biological

used to produce 3HP such as Escherichia coli, Chloroﬂexus aurantiacus, Klebsiella pneumoniae,

Description of the pathway
The aim of the study in Sankaranarayanan et al. (2014) was to determine which kind of 9 diﬀerent
E.coli

process is fed-batch, operating under mild conditions. The yield and productivity obtained at this

performed in a reactor with a working volume of 50 mL, at 37°C, at 650 rpm with a pH of 6.5 -

What is important to note for this process presented by Sankaranarayanan et al. (2014) is
that there are a few signiﬁcant issues occurring that are aﬀecting it with the use of glycerol as
a substrate.
These are the accumulation of the toxic intermediate 3HPA which results in the
deactivation of the catalytic activity of DhaB. So, there is a need for the expensive coenzyme B12
that is able to reactivate DhaB. 3HPA is toxic to the cell growth as well as the 3HP production
even though it is its immediate precursor. One more limitation is the toxicity of the product 3HP
itself on the cell growth and on its production, due to the fact of its high toxic eﬀects even before
its concentration reaches the commercial demand. More speciﬁcally, the inhibitory limit for 3HP
to the bacteria is determined to be at 18.06 g/L.
As a result, the E.coli
strain W was determined to be the most toxicity tolerant one, giving

2.2.3
1,3-Propanediol production process
Like the 3HP case, the case of 1,3PDO has also drawn big interest regarding its production since
its importance as a platform chemical is constantly increasing over the past few decades. However,
there have not been many processes so far, that are able to produce 1,3PDO in high yields and
titers, so research has been focused on ways that it could be produced eﬀectively.
The biggest driving force for the production of 1,3PDO is the belief that improved chemistries
(both petrochemical as well as biological routes) can enable and further develop its production
(Nakamura and Whited, 2003). The biological routes involve the optimization of fermentation
processes with glycerol as a substrate and further research on metabolic engineering pathways.
Diﬀerent bacteria are utilized as part of the metabolic engineering research towards 1,3PDO such
as Citrobacter, Clostridium enterobacter, Klebsiella and Lactobacillus species.
Description of the pathway
The aim of the study in Wilkens et al. (2012) was to optimize a biotechnological process to produce
1,3PDO that would result in high product titer. The process developed presented one of the best
results that are published so far for natural producers and occurred as a need to produce 1,3PDO
by applying microbial conversion of renewables, rather than by producing it chemically from fossil
resources.
Crude glycerol directly gained from a biodiesel plant was used as feedstock and Clostridium
butyricum was used as the biotransformation bacteria. The process is a fed-batch fermentation of
1 L working volume, operating for 32 h under anaerobic conditions at 32°C, at 400 rpm with a pH

of 7. In addition, preliminary experiments had shown that a basic pretreatment of the feedstock
led to a performance increase.
What is noteworthy about this process is the comparison between the crude glycerol used as a
feedstock and pure glycerol. The diﬀerence was mainly noted in the byproducts that were formed;
with crude glycerol’s acid formation shifted more towards lactic acid during the biotransformation,
rather than to butyric acid. The reason for this could probably be the accumulation of impurities
caused by the initial dilution of the feedstock due to its high water content that derives from the
low glycerol concentration of the crude glycerol.
The outcome of this process was the fact that the biotransformation of glycerol to 1,3PDO
by Clostridium butyricum has noted one of the highest productivities for natural producers. The
purity of glycerol can also have a small eﬀect, mainly due to the existence of fatty acids, salts or
heavy metal ions in the crude glycerol. This is the reason why the removal of the ﬂoating layer as
well as a certain dilution are highly recommended for the crude glycerol.
Moreover, the titer of 1,3PDO can vary according to the working volume of the process, when
mini and big scales are compared (with mini scale yields being slightly higher). Wilkens et al.
(2012) propose a few process optimization techniques, such as automatic feed implementation, in
order to increase the ﬁnal titer and process productivity in a larger scale.
2.2.4
Simultaneous production of 3-hydroxypropionic acid and 1,3-propanediol
This co-production of the two chemicals has been extensivly researched using mostly strains of
Klebsiella pneumoniae or Escherichia coli. It was noted, however, that these two bacteria, when
used, had a signiﬁcant amount of by products such as lactic acid, ethanol, butanol, succinic acid,
acetic acid etc. that are greatly lowering the product yield and require complicated downstream
processes, thus increasing the operational cost.
As a result, there was a need to ﬁnd a microorganism that would be able to form the two
products, pure with as less byproducts as possible. So, Dishisha et al. (2014) used the wild-type
strain Lactobacillus reuteri and operating in a mini scale, ensured a byproducts- and antibiotics-
free process that would thus have simple downstream.
Description of the pathway
The aim of this study was to gain a good understanding of the utilization of glycerol, to determine
the metabolic bottlenecks and with the aid of a proper metabolic strategy gain a better overview
of the 3HPA distribution into reductive and oxidative pathways with L. reuteri. Main goal of the

Step 2.1: Reaction pathway determination
Crude glycerol, derived from biodiesel production is used as a substrate since it is cheap, it has

Clostridium butyricum is used for the production of 1,3PDO because it is a strain that was char-
acterized by rapid growth, high tolerance to the product as well as by the ability to use crude
glycerol at the lowest purity (namely the one coming out of the biodiesel plant stream), according
to (Wilkens et al., 2012). In addition, using crude glycerol it is presenting among the best results
published so far, for natural producers having a very simple process.
Pretreatment of the bacteria to be used
The bacterial strains of this study have been collected from soil samples. They were stored in 50%
pure glycerol at -80oC in order to be able to be used experimentally. Moreover, prior to the start
of the biotransformation, three generations of precultures were grown at 32oC under anaerobic
conditions with an addition of mineral salt medium.

Case study: dual product production
In this chapter, the developed methodology presented in chapter 3 is applied to an approach

5.1
Case study 3: simultaneous co-production of
3-hydroxypropionic acid and 1,3-propanediol from glycerol
The co-production of 3HP and 1,3PDO is the ﬁnal case that will be investigated step by step
according to chapter 3. It is also among the objectives of this report, to describe and analyze the
feasibility of such production process towards the two products.
Case study 3 is divided to two sub-cases:
• sub-case 3a: one of a straightforward production of the two products and
• sub-case 3b: one in which the produced intermediate 3HPA together with water will be
recycled back to the reactor.
This diﬀerentiation is based on Le Chateliers principle for chemical equilibriums.
A + B ⇌C + D
(5.1)
According to the principle, when an equilibrium is established between four chemical com-
pounds A,B,C and D and a change in the conditions occurs (such as a concentration increase of
one of these compounds), then the position of the equilibrium will move to counteract this change
(Dmitriev, 2007).
Thus, it is expected that if 3HPA (that is A or B) is recycled back to the reactor, then the
desired products 3HP and 1,3PDO (that are C and D) will be produced in bigger concentrations.

5.1.1
Step 1: Problem formulation
The equimolar production of 3HP and 1,3PDO. Glycerol deriving from biodiesel production is

The objective is the development of a process able to exploit the dual metabolic pathway and pro-

the process are determined, a simulation of the process is performed and evaluation of the process
with respect to the economics and the environmental impact.
5.1.2
Step 2: Reaction Pathway and Analysis
Step 2.1: Reaction pathway determination
The metabolic pathway glycerol follows to its biotransformation consists of three reaction steps,
all of them occurring simultaneously.
Selection of the bacteria
Resting L.reuteri cells were used, a bacteria that does not use the glycerol feed as a carbon source,
thus ensures there is no kind of bacterial growth within the reactor during the operation. It only
uses glycerol as an electron acceptor. It synthesizes adenosylcobalamin (vitamin B12) which is a
very important co-factor for the enzyme glycerol dehydratase. So, the resulting product of the
process is noted by Dishisha et al. (2014) to have such a high purity, that the downstream process
is expected to be much simpliﬁed, thus simple end economic. Last but not least, L.reuteri is a
bacterial strain that is open to metabolic engineering studies and can be easily genetically modiﬁed,
so that the utilization of glycerol is even more eﬀective.
Pretreatment of the bacteria to be used
As it can be observed in the ﬁgure, L.reuteri is initially fed into the fermenter to grow, with
40 g/L of glucose as a carbon source, 5 g/L 1,2-propanediol and fresh water. Nitrogen is fed to
the system to maintain the conditions anaerobic. The cells grow inside the fermenter at 37°C
for approximately 11 h. Then, they are ready to be fed to the biocatalysis reactor where the
biotransformation occurs. Glycerol is fed in this reactor and the biotransformation starts.

However, a very important remark is the inhibitory mechanisms to which the bioconversion
is subjected to, that need to be overcome if high yields and productivities are the aim.
The
intermediate aldehyde that is produced (3HPA) is toxic. Apart from it being toxic to the bacterial
cells, it can also be toxic to one speciﬁc enzyme, responsible for the biotransformation to 1,3PDO,
namely of propionaldehyde dehydrogenase (PduP), at concentrations that exceed that of 0.6 g/L.
In addition, it is stated that high concentrations of both glycerol and 1,3PDO can also inhibit
the action of glycerol dehydratase. This is the reason that the process was designed with glycerol
as the limiting feed, with its concentration is regulated at 50 g/L. Regulating glycerol at this con-

Step 2.3: Determination of the reaction conditions
The conditions under which the process is performed are in complete accordance to the desired
result; the simultaneous co-production, whilst achieving the highest yields and productivity possi-
ble as well as overcoming any potential inhibitory eﬀect. Both the cell growth and the biocatalysis
are performed under mild conditions, more speciﬁcally 37oC temperature, 1 bar pressure and 7 pH.
Step 2.4: Decision on the type of operation
The operation mode of the process is very important for the ﬁnal products formed. The biocatalysis
process as presented by Dishisha et al. (2014) is a batch process, due to the fact that otherwise
if operated continuously, there are big chances of genetic instability of the enzymes. So, the case
is investigated here, the product synthesis process operates for a total of 17 h (11 h for the cell
cultivation plus 6 h more for the biotransformation process).
However, for both sub-cases that 3a and 3b, (the isolation and storage of the intermediate
3HPA and the recycling of 3HPA together with water back to the reactor respectively), 4 batch

One fermenter and one biocatalysis reactor are required. In the ﬁrst one, the cultivation of the

used for the compounds separation and downstream processing.

In the case of fermenter for the cell cultivation, a reactor able to grow 35 kgCDW per biotrans-

6.2
Conclusions
In this Master’s thesis a conceptual methodology for the setup of a dual product process towards the
production of 3HP and 1,3PDO in equimolar quantities has been developed. Process engineering
methods and tools have been applied to the methodology giving a good understanding of each part
of the metabolic pathway and process synthesis of the production process.
Main achievements:
• The development of a dual production bioprocess targeted to the equimolar production of
3HP and 1,3PDO of high purity and with high productivity in an industrial scale.
• A conceptual methodology enhancing the understanding of the metabolic pathway starting
from the system of reactions, to the ﬁnal puriﬁed products, that is applied to three diﬀerent
cases.
• An evaluation and comparison of the three production processes that utilize the same feed-
stock in terms of yield, productivity, product capacity, economics and sustainability.
So, between the two cases regarding the 3HP production, case studies 1 and 3, they both have
very similar results in terms of capacity, product yield and utility costs. What diﬀerentiates them
is the productivities each one achieves and the raw material cost. Regarding the production of
1,3PDO, the co-production process is favorable in terms of productivity and utility costs. Apart
from that, case 2 gives better product yield, productivity and ﬁnal capacity. It is an eﬀective
alternative to utilize glycerol in a sustainable way and create value out of it.
Taking everything into account, the co-production process that has been developed in this
project is a good option to be considered for the production of two increasingly important chemical

6.3
Future perspectives
The eﬀorts to improve bioprocesses have been increasing over the last decades. This project has
investigated the setup of a novel bioprocess that produces two equimolar products simultaneously.
There were challenges linked to this investigation however, that generate the need for further,
future work.
6.3.1
Limitations and challenges
A series of challenges have been faced in this project, basically on the application of the conceptual
methodology regarding the development of the dual production process. Initially, the knowledge
of the two products and especially 3HP is still limited. Especially for 3HP, there are no concrete
properties (ﬁxed and temperature dependent) available in the literature nor much experimental
analysis done. The same applies to 3HPA. The knowledge of 1,3PDO is much broader and research
is conducted already regarding its production in an industrial scale.
This lack of knowledge caused a few limitations. The ﬁrst one regards the reaction kinetics
part, where the is no complete kinetic model available to analyze the reaction performance, obtain
a total understanding of it and its behavior and potentially optimize it.
In addition, another
limitation occurred during the process simulation. Due to the fact that the compounds involved
in the process still require a lot of research, there were no compound libraries available in any
simulation tool. Thus, these libraries had to be made from scratch, using predictive tools (such as
93
==PAGE:108==
6.3. FUTURE PERSPECTIVES
ProPred), that give a ﬁrst overview of their behaviour. Only after the generation of the libraries
was the simulation of the two processes (involving these compounds) possible. Last but not least,
the lack of properties did not make the determination of the exact environmental impact of the
process possible, so as to complete the sustainability analysis.
6.3.2
Future work
There are a series of challenges still remaining that in case they are faced, can improve the inves-
tigated co-production process and potentially bioprocesses in general.
To begin with, a complete research and development of the properties of 3HP and 3HPA should
be done This is a crucial step, not only deriving from the currently increasing importance of 3HP
but also from the need to get a full understanding of the compound, its synthesis routes and its
behaviour in reaction systems. A database should be made that includes all the properties, not in
a predictive, but in a measured and experimented way.
The knowledge of the compounds will lead to a complete knowledge of the metabolic pathway,
fully understand every enzymatic transformation. Thus, it could be a good possibility to interfere
in the genetic engineering of the reactions and perform genetic alterations that together with the
adaptation of the reaction variables can lead to an increased reaction performances.
Another important suggestion is to perform a life cycle assessment analysis (LCA) to evaluate
the environmental impact of the process. In this project, due to the lack of compound information,
only an approximation could be given about the environmental impact. It would be recommended,
however, that it is veriﬁed by measured, experimental properties of the compounds involved.
All in all, it is advisable to perform a larger scale analysis of the co-production process. In this
project, it achieved a capacity of 0.3 kt/yr, so the economic evaluation is performed on a smaller
scale than the actual current production (around 120 kt/yr for 1,3PDO, whereas 3HP is still not
produced in a larger scale). In case the process is designed to achieve this capacity, then an overall
evaluation can be performed, including capital and operating costs and a cumulative cash ﬂow in
a certain time frame can be obtain, so that the proﬁt of the production can be determined long
term.
