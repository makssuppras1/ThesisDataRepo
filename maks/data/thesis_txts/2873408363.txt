Abstract
As startup ecosystems grow increasingly global, national policy design plays a critical
role in shaping where and how entrepreneurial activity takes place. In Scandinavia, Nor-
way and Denmark offer two contrasting cases. While Denmark has recently eased sev-
eral founder-facing tax policies, Norway has moved in the opposite direction, tightening
taxation on wealth, unrealised gains, and cross-border exits. These shifts raise impor-
tant questions about how taxation influences founder and investor decisions and, more
broadly, about how policy frameworks can better support high-growth entrepreneurship
in small open economies.
This thesis examines how founders and investors in both countries interpret and respond
to startup-related policies, how these policies influence decisions to incorporate, scale,
or relocate, and how they shape the ambition, ownership, and structure of new ven-
tures. The research adopts a qualitative and exploratory design, grounded in 21 semi-
structured interviews with founders, investors, and policymakers across both countries.
Using grounded theory, the study identifies recurring behavioural patterns and policy feed-
back loops based on participants’ first-hand experiences.
The findings show that in Norway, tightened tax policies have contributed to defensive
growth strategies, founder relocations, and reduced capital reinvestment. In Denmark, re-
cent reforms, especially around stock options, have improved founder confidence, though
challenges remain in retaining talent and coordinating public funding. Across both coun-
tries, there is a shared frustration that policy frameworks fail to differentiate entrepreneurial
equity from traditional wealth, placing early-stage ventures at a structural disadvantage.
For policymakers, the findings implicate the need to rethink how taxation interacts with liq-
uidity constraints, long-term risk-taking, and founder mobility. For founders and investors,
the results highlight the importance of early legal and financial planning, as well as the
need for more direct engagement in policy dialogues. Ultimately, the thesis calls for a
more adaptive and nuanced approach to startup policy, one that treats founders not as
tax subjects, but as key drivers of national innovation capacity.

1
Introduction
Entrepreneurship is widely recognised as a key driver of economic growth. In many coun-
tries, fostering entrepreneurship has become a national priority due to the significant value
it brings to the economy. Governments play an active role in this process by design-
ing and implementing policies aimed at strengthening their entrepreneurial ecosystems.
These policies directly influence the nature and level of entrepreneurial activity within a
country (Minniti, 2008). It is therefore in a country’s best interest to design effective poli-
cies that foster entrepreneurship. However, determining which policies are beneficial and
which may be detrimental to founders and startups is a complex task. Governments often
look to other, comparable countries for inspiration, yet there is no universal guide to fol-
low. As Minniti (2008) argues, entrepreneurship policies are not one-size-fits-all and must
be tailored to each country’s specific context and institutional environment. A policy that
proves effective in one country may yield entirely different outcomes in another, due to
differences in economic structures and institutional contexts (Simón-Moya et al., 2014).
1.1
Problem Background
Scandinavian countries share many foundational values, yet take distinct policy approaches
to entrepreneurship. This makes the region well-suited for comparative analysis of how
different policy choices affect startups. Among these countries, Denmark and Norway
stand out as particularly relevant cases. Despite their similar economic structures and cul-
tural foundations, they have adopted entrepreneurship policies with notable differences
in design and execution. These contrasts offer a valuable opportunity to explore how
national policy variations shape entrepreneurial outcomes.
Denmark recently abolished the so-called “tax from hell,” a controversial tax on unrealised
gains. This policy had sparked widespread concern among entrepreneurs, as it required
startup founders to pay taxes on the estimated value of their shares, even if they had not
yet realised any actual income or liquidity from them. The tax was seen as especially
damaging to early-stage founders, who often hold large equity stakes on paper but lack
the cash flow to meet such obligations. Its removal was celebrated as a significant vic-
tory for the Danish startup community and demonstrated that policy can be adapted in
response to the real-world needs of entrepreneurs. Beyond this reform, Denmark has
implemented a broader set of policies that influence the entrepreneurial ecosystem, each
of which warrants critical evaluation. Eventhough they have done a great job, issues still
persist according to founders operating in Denmark.
Norway, in contrast, has moved in the opposite direction by introducing a similar tax on
unrealised gains, the Wealth tax, effectively tightening its fiscal stance while other coun-
tries are easing theirs. Entrepreneurs and investors interviewed in this study expressed
concern that such policies undermine long-term commitment, encourage relocation, and
discourage entrepreneurial ambition. Although national support instruments such as In-
novation Norway and SkatteFUNN remain active, the shifting tax structure is increasingly
seen as incompatible with the risk and liquidity profiles typical of startups.
1.2
Problem statement & research questions
As previously discussed, policies play a critical role in shaping entrepreneurial ecosystems
and influencing startup activity. While considerable research has examined specific policy
domains, such as taxation, employment regulation, or access to finance, there is a notable

gap in the literature when it comes to understanding how these policies are experienced
by founders themselves. Founders are directly affected by the policy environment, yet
their perspectives remain unexplored. In the context of Denmark and Norway, there is
a lack of comparative research that investigates how founders in each country perceive
and respond to national policy frameworks.
This thesis therefore examines how founders and investors in both countries interpret and
respond to startup-related policies, how these policies influence decisions to incorporate,
scale, or relocate, and how they shape the ambition, ownership, and structure of new

Research Question 1:
In what ways do national policies on wealth and capital taxation shape founders and in-
vestors’ willingness to remain, scale, or relocate within the startup ecosystems in Denmark
and Norway?
Research Question 2:
What policy improvements could help create a more coherent and founder-responsive
startup environment in Denmark and Norway?
Drawing on qualitative interviews with founders, investors, and policymakers, this study
explores the lived experiences that exist behind formal policy frameworks. It aims to
uncover not only what rules are in place, but how they are understood, navigated, and
occasionally challenged by those within the startup landscape. In doing so, the research
contributes to a more nuanced understanding of the interaction between policy design
and entrepreneurial behaviour, offering insights for policymakers seeking to foster more
competitive and founder-oriented environments.
1.3
Thesis Structure
This thesis is structured into five main chapters, in addition to the abstract and introduction.
Chapter 2 presents a review of existing literature on the relationship between public policy
and entrepreneurship in a global context. It begins by examining how government policy
shapes entrepreneurial ecosystems and then explores specific policy domains, including
taxation, employment regulations, access to finance, and exit conditions. The chapter
concludes with an overview of the policy and entrepreneurial landscapes in Denmark and
Norway, the two focus countries of this study.
Chapter 3 outlines the research methodology, including the overall research design, data
collection procedures, data analysis techniques, as well as a discussion of the study’s
limitations and ethical considerations.
Chapter 4 presents the empirical findings derived from qualitative interviews. The struc-
ture of this chapter mirrors the thematic framework used in the literature review to ensure
consistency.
Chapter 5 offers a discussion of the findings in relation to the research questions. It reflects
on the motivations behind the study, highlights its contributions to existing literature, and
outlines its limitations. In addition, it considers the implications for various stakeholders
and provides recommendations for future research.
Finally, Chapter 6 concludes the thesis by summarising the key insights, linking them back
to the research questions, and offering reflections on potential directions for future policy
development.

2
Literature review
2.1
Introduction
Entrepreneurship plays a vital role in driving innovation, economic growth, and job creation
within a country. However, the sustainability and success of startups depend not only on
the ambition and abilities of individual founders but also on the entrepreneurial ecosystem
in which they operate. This literature review examines how various governmental policies
shape these ecosystems and influence founder decision-making. It draws on theoretical
frameworks and empirical studies to explore how policy instruments can either foster or
hinder startup formation, growth, and innovation. Although many studies emphasise the
importance of tailored, country-specific policy design, there remains limited understanding
of how combinations of policies interact with national ecosystems, particularly in smaller
countries such as Denmark and Norway.
To achieve this, the literature review is organised into three main sections. The first sec-
tion explores the concept of the entrepreneurial ecosystem and the role of policy within
it. The aim is to provide a high-level understanding of how countries can design poli-
cies and build ecosystems that promote entrepreneurship, growth, and innovation. The
second section examines specific policy areas, including taxation, access to capital, hir-
ing, subsidies, and other relevant themes. Given the limited research on these topics
in Denmark and Norway, this section first considers evidence from a global perspective,
drawing on examples from countries with thriving entrepreneurial ecosystems, such as
Israel and the United States and others. Understanding which policies have proven ef-
fective, and which have not, provides valuable insights that can inform policy development
in Denmark and Norway. The final section focuses specifically on Denmark and Norway,
offering an overview of their entrepreneurial ecosystems and assessing the strengths and
weaknesses of each. This provides a foundation for understanding the realities of oper-
ating a startup and the entrepreneurial culture in these countries.
2.2
Entrepreneurial ecosystems and the role of policies
Spilling describes ”an entrepreneurial ecosystem as a representation of a diverse set of in-
terdependent actors within a geographic region that influence the formation, and eventual
trajectory of the entire group of actors and potentially the economy as a whole” (Spilling,
1996, as cited in Cohen, 2006).
Entrepreneurial ecosystems play a critical role in driving innovation, job creation and en-
abling regional economic development. Cohen (2006) provides a useful framework for
mapping entrepreneurial ecosystem components, as shown in the table below. Through
interaction they help facilitate the creation and scaling of new ventures. In some cases
these ecosystems not only support individual startups but can also create spillover effects,
helping other actors within the ecosystem in the form of funding experience, research, tal-
ent allocation and knowledge transfer.

While the fundamental components of entrepreneurial ecosystems are widely recognised,
their configuration and impact often vary significantly across different geographical con-
texts. The shape an ecosystem takes is determined not only by the actors involved but
also by the broader institutional and cultural environment in which it develops. As Au-
tio & Fu (2014) observe, entrepreneurship is deeply embedded within national systems,
and policies that prove effective in one country may not easily be transferred to another.
Understanding these national particularities is essential, especially when evaluating the
effectiveness of policy interventions designed to promote entrepreneurship.
Lundström and Stevenson (2001) define entrepreneurship policy as measures aimed at
motivating, enabling, and skilling individuals to start businesses and foster entrepreneur-
ship. This broad understanding highlights the multifaceted role of policy across the en-
trepreneurial journey. Seemingly minor regulatory decisions can, over time, influence the
composition and confidence of entire startup ecosystems. Given that the entrepreneurial
landscape differs across countries, governments tend to implement targeted policies to
strengthen specific areas of entrepreneurship. For instance, some aim to foster a more in-
novative entrepreneurial environment, while others focus on encouraging growth-oriented
ventures (Lundström and Stevenson, 2001).
Patanakul and Pinto (2014) argue that policy should extend beyond financial support and
also promote essential conditions for startups, such as adequate infrastructure, invest-
ment in research and development, workforce development, and other critical factors.
They emphasise that governments should move away from isolated policy measures and
instead develop a comprehensive policy framework that can be strategically implemented
to support specific areas of entrepreneurship.
Ahmad & Hoffmann (2008) argues that the success of a policy often depends not only
on what is implemented, but also on how it interacts with other policies or governmental
frameworks, such as the legal system, the labour market, and cultural norms. Further-
more, policy design should be adapted to local contexts. According to Simón-Moya et al.

Studying these examples provides valuable insights into how certain policy measures can
influence the development and performance of startups.
2.3
Effect of Tax Policies on startups
Tax policies can have a significant effect on entrepreneurship in a country and can there-
fore influence all stages of the entrepreneurial process. They can shape how many star-
tups are formed and also affect the sustainability of those startups to a certain extent
(Fazio et al., 2020). Tax policies and regulations help define the business environment
and can encourage startups to grow within a country or drive them to leave. It is con-
sidered one of the most important factors when it comes to doing business in a specific
country (Baliamoune-Lutz & Garello, 2014). Governments create tax policies not only
to increase revenue but also to guide entrepreneurship in specific directions. However,
tax policy on its own cannot determine the success of a startup, as many other factors
also play a role. Understanding how tax systems affect startup behaviour is essential for
creating policies that support a startups and their ability to scale.
Many research papers suggest that governments should be careful in implementing tax
policies. Governments should create specific policies tailored to various industries, matu-
rity of startup or other factors in order to make sure that other startups do not suffer from
certain policies designed to promote a particular area of entrepreneurship. (Sun et al.,
2025)
While tax policy is a tool to influence the entrepreneurial landscape to some extent, gov-
ernments can’t look at tax policies as a stand alone tool. They have to consider other
policies as taxes alone are not an effective tool to significantly boost entrepreneurial ac-
tivity (Liu, 2014)
Gordon & Sarada (2018) argues that broad-based tax policies are often inefficient. These
types of policies may unintentionally support businesses that do not contribute to innova-
tion or long-term economic growth. Instead, Gordon & Sarada suggests that governments
should focus their tax incentives on businesses that demonstrate characteristics likely to

2.3.1
Effects of different taxes on startups
Most of the studies conducted on the effect of taxes on entrepreneurial formation, found
that progressive marginal tax rates reduce the likelihood that someone will become an
entrepreneur (Gentry & Hubbard, 2000). This is because one might reason that for the
same amount of effort, the reward is higher when taxes are lower, which might encour-
age them to start their own venture. In a study by Wall & Georgellis (2006), similar results
were found, but they also observed a U-shaped relationship between marginal tax rates
and entrepreneurship. This means that in some cases, higher taxes can also promote en-
trepreneurship. For government policies, it’s not only about the amount of entrepreneurs
but also about the quality of entrepreneurship and to create an entrepreneurial landscape
with high growth that can provide jobs and other benefits to a country (Asoni & Sanandaji,
2014).
According to Asoni & Sanandaji (2014), countries with higher tax rates often create envi-
ronments in which founders opt for stability and comfort, rather than pursuing competition,
risk, and aggressive expansion. In a study on Indian small business owners by Singh and
Patel, it has been shown that higher tax rates discourage reinvestment and lead to slower
innovation (Singh and Patel, 2022, as cited in Celestin et al., 2025).
To properly study the relationship between taxation and entrepreneurship, it is essential
to examine both the various types of taxes and the different stages in the entrepreneurial
journey. Some tax policies may influence the likelihood or conditions under which startups
exit the market, while others may play a more direct role in shaping how businesses grow
and scale.
2.3.2
Income Tax
Income tax is a tax imposed on the total revenue generated by you or your business (Ce-
lestin et al., 2025). Lower income tax rates can improve the financial liquidity of a business
or individual. This allows them to invest more in growth initiatives. In contrast, higher in-
come taxes may have the opposite effect by limiting disposable income and reducing
available investment capital (Celestin et al., 2025).
Further supporting this point, research conducted by Harju & Kosonen (2013) found that
lower income tax rates can motivate entrepreneurs to work harder and generate more
revenue. In one notable case, a Finnish tax reform that only applied to small business
owners resulted in a 5% increase in turnover. This suggests that even relatively small
changes in tax policy can have a measurable effect on entrepreneurial performance.
2.3.3
Corporate Tax
Corporate tax is based on the net income of a company minus expenses, which deter-
mines its financial contribution to the government (Celestin et al., 2025). A high corporate
tax rate on business profits can discourage equity financing and instead incentivise the
use of debt-based financing (Henrekson et al, 2010). Higher tax rates also reduce the
amount of retained earnings available to the firm funds that could otherwise be reinvested
to support growth and scaling. This constraint on internal financing may ultimately lead
to lower growth rates for startups (Henrekson et al., 2010, as cited in Michaelas et al.,
1999).
2.3.4
Capital Tax
Capital tax is a tax imposed on the profit derived from the sale of capital assets, calcu-
lated as the difference between the asset’s acquisition cost and its selling price (Hourani

& Perret, 2025). High capital taxation can reduce investment incentives for startups. To
attract greater levels of capital, governments may need to lower these taxes (Ahmad &
Hoffmann, 2008). Research has shown that taxing capital gains at high rates can dis-

(Meade, 1990, as cited Henrekson et al., 2010). High capital income taxes may also lead
entrepreneurs to retain profits within their companies in order to avoid heavy taxation.
While this behaviour can help reinvest resources in the business, over time it may trap
capital in older firms rather than reallocating it to newer and potentially more innovative
ventures.
Early-stage startups, however, typically lack retained earnings and are more dependent
on external investment. In such cases, high capital taxation can act as a significant bar-
rier to securing the funding necessary for growth. Moreover, high capital taxes may dis-
courage founders from pursuing innovation and expansion if the financial rewards are
substantially diminished (Henrekson et al., 2010).
2.3.5
Tax Incentives
Tax incentives are a common policy used by governments to help amongst others, star-
tups. They can help reduce financial pressure in the early stages, allowing them to focus
more on research & development and growth to become a sustainable business (Ahsan
et al., 2021). These incentives can play a vital role in attracting investment from private
and financial institutions. By making startups more attractive, these tax incentives could
promote access to funding and reduce the cost of capital for a startup (Xiong et al., 2024).
Tax incentives, when used to promote research and development (R&D), are commonly
referred to as R&D tax credits. These incentives have become a central component of the
policy framework in many European countries (Mitchell et al., 2020). Mitchell finds that
R&D tax credits have a significant positive effect on low-growth, young firms, while the
impact on high-growth firms is less pronounced. As such, these policies should be more
specifically targeted towards slower-growth startups. In general, R&D-related policies are
considered attractive and valuable tools for supporting startups. The OECD (2024) also
confirms that these incentives have the greatest effect on smaller firms, which typically
engage in lower levels of R&D activity compared to more mature companies.
2.3.6
Wealth Tax
Several countries have equity/wealth, whereas Norway and Denmark would be deemed
most relevant for this thesis. In Norway, the wealth tax targets the total net worth of
individuals on an annual basis and includes property, bank deposits, and shares valued
at 1 760 001,- NOK and over. Every year on December 31st the wealth tax is calculated,
regardless of increases or decreases in asset value throughout the year. A government
report from 2020 concluded that the tax can create liquidity problems as it applies to
founder ownership stakes in startups, even when such shares are illiquid and generate
no cash flow, causing issues for founders struggling to make profits within their businesses
(Røed et al., 2020). This can have significant effects in the early stages of a startup when
valuations may be high on paper but liquidity is low.
Up until the end of 2024, Denmark imposed what was commonly referred to as the ”En-
trepreneur tax” or Iværksætterskat, which was part of the broader mark-to-market taxa-
tion scheme (lagerbeskatning) targeting unlisted shares. Under this scheme, corporate
shareholders with less than 10% ownership in unlisted companies were taxed annually

2.3.7
Exit Policies
Exit policies are policies designed for governments to secure their share of assets which
were created within their economy, for individuals who give up tax residency. These poli-
cies typically apply to unrealised capital gains and are designed to protect the domestic
tax base.
In Norway the exit policy or exit tax in the common tongue, comes into play if one has more
than 500 000,- NOK in unrealised gains on shares or securities at the time of departure.
The exit tax can be deferred or postponed for up to 12 years if one chooses to move to
another EU/EEA country (Skatteetaten, n.d.), and if the individual chooses to move back
within the 12 years the tax might be relinquished, given the move wasn’t “tax-motivated”
which is subject to consideration for the tax administration (Skatteetaten). Until 2024, Nor-
way’s exit tax was only triggered when shares were sold, meaning it was rarely collected
in practice. However, the current government reformed the policy to include taxation on
unrealised gains. In addition, 70% of all dividends must now be used to pay down the exit
tax, which must be settled within a 12-year period (Finansavisen, 2024).
The Norwegian Exit Tax has received criticism by SME owners, and startup founders alike
for “killing companies and growth” by forcing businesses to sell off assets in order to pay
for taxes which is determined based on market value, and not dividends/earnings. Fasting
(2024) argues that the exit tax appears to be an attempt to counteract the harmful effects
of relocation by creating obstacles for people who want to move, but it doesn’t address
the reasons for the recent increase in relocation for Norwegian businesses: the high level
of wealth taxation for Norwegian business owners.
Similarly, Denmark applies an exit tax when individuals give up Danish tax residency while
owning shares, whether in private or publicly listed companies. Under Danish tax law,
such shares are treated as if they had been sold at market value on the date of departure.
As a result, individuals become liable for tax on any unrealised capital gains, even though
no actual sale has occurred. The taxable gain is calculated as the difference between the
acquisition cost and the market value at the time of exit (Skatteforvaltningen, n.d.). For
unlisted shares, there is a DKK 100,000 exemption per company. If the value of shares
in a given company is below this threshold, exit tax does not apply to those holdings. No
similar exemption applies to listed shares, which are fully subject to the rules.
When relocating to a country within the EU or EEA, individuals may apply to defer payment
of the exit tax under specific conditions. Typically, the deferral lasts until the shares are
sold, disposed of, or the individual breaches the reporting obligations. While there is no
strict legal maximum period, in practice, deferral is monitored, and obligations continue
until a taxable event occurs (Skatteforvaltningen, n.d.). Deferral requires thorough and

2.4
Access to Finance / Financial Market Regulations
Entrepreneurial financing plays a critical role in supporting startups, particularly in the early
stage of venture development. Scholars argue that policy design should aim to foster a
holistic entrepreneurial ecosystem, rather than relying on isolated measures to generate
impact. Building such a system is a complex task, with access to finance playing a central
role.
2.4.1
Subsidies
Subsidies are non-repayable financial contributions or support mechanisms provided by
the government or public institutions with the aim of reducing early-stage friction and pro-
moting innovation, employment, or economic development. These funds are typically
granted to new ventures to offset startup costs, fund R&D, and help with commercialisa-
tion. Startups and businesses in the early stages of development often suffer from the
liability of newness, and they simply don’t have the credibility which in many cases is
needed in order to raise funding due to lack of results to show for. And as Kleer (2010)
argues, the receipt of such a subsidy can be considered a signal of quality of a new ven-
ture (Kleer, 2010 as cited in Söderblom et al., 2015), which means the subsidies can help
new ventures overcome initial hurdles and be more attractive to external investors. 
Subsidies come in many shapes and forms, with grants being the most common, offering
non-repayable funding for specific purposes or sectors. Some are also provided as R&D
tax credits, which allow startups to reduce their tax burden or get money back for spending
on innovation projects. Others appear as wage subsidies, helping cover the cost of hiring
talent, reducing the financial burden of job creation. There’s also soft loans, loans offered
with below-market interest rates, extended repayment terms, or other favourable terms.
As well as, loan guarantees which involve a public institution promising to cover part of a
loan’s value if the startup defaults, thereby reducing the lender’s risk and making banks
more willing to offer credit to firms that lack sufficient collateral or financial history.
Kösters (2010) argues that subsidies are often justified by the presence of positive ex-
ternalities associated with entrepreneurship, such as knowledge spillovers, increased
competition, and diversity among firms (Audretsch & Keilbach, 2004, as cited in Kösters,
2010). These effects are often underappreciated by private investors, as they focus solely
on firm-level returns (Audretsch et al., 2007, as cited in Kösters, 2010), but policymakers
have to design interventions that aim to support the economy by helping startups grow in
situations where the market doesn’t work well on its own. This often means stepping in
Comparing the Impact of Government Policies on Startups in Denmark and Norway
9
==PAGE:16==
when promising businesses can’t get the funding they need. However, Isenberg (2010)
says: ”it’s a mistake to flood even high-potential entrepreneurs with easy money: More is
not necessarily merrier. New ventures must be exposed early to the rigors of the market.
Governments should stress test new ventures by meting out money carefully, to ensure
that entrepreneurs develop toughness and resourcefulness”.
However, just knowing that market problems exist isn’t enough to justify giving out sub-
sidies. The main challenge is that governments don’t always have enough information
to identify the startups that really need help, those that wouldn’t happen without support,
but would bring real value to society if they did. Without efficient policy targeting, subsi-
dies may distort the market by giving an advantage to supported firms, where subsidised
ventures displace more efficient, non-subsidised firms, thus reducing overall economic
efficiency (Santarelli & Vivarelli, 2007, as cited in Kösters, 2010). 
Kösters analysis suggests that, for non-R&D startups, subsidies often show no significant
impact on either firm survival or employment growth, which questions their effectiveness.
This might correlate with the fact that when subsidies are allocated to the most promising
startups, those with high potential private and social returns, they are less likely to disrupt
market selection and more likely to yield desired economic effects (Stam et al., 2009, as
cited in Kösters, 2010). This “picking winners” strategy means that the government tries to
carefully choose the most promising startups to support, those with the highest potential
for success and impact. The goal is to avoid wasting money by giving subsidies to every
applicant, including those that are unlikely to grow or benefit society. In other words, by
being selective, policymakers try to make sure public funds go to startups that are most
likely to succeed and create real value, yet, paradoxically, the startups that appear most
promising are often those least in need of public support. This complicates the challenge
of allocation subsidies and raises doubts about the government’s ability to outperform
private capital in identifying high-potential ventures (Parker & van Praag, 2006; Stiglitz &
Wallsten, 2000, as cited in Kösters, 2010).
2.4.2
SME lending regulations
While venture capital is arguably the most prominent source of funding for startups, as
discussed in a later chapter, traditional bank lending remains a viable and accessible fi-
nancing option for many SMEs and early-stage startups. Typically, these businesses are
in the initial phases of development and have limited financial histories, which increases
the perceived risk for banks and other lenders. Unlike venture capitalists, who seek equity
stakes and long-term returns, banks expect regular interest payments and full repayment
of the loan principal. To address these challenges and improve credit availability, many
countries have introduced targeted SME lending regulations aimed at fostering a more
enabling financial environment. A common regulatory approach involves prudential mea-
sures that offer preferential treatment for SME lending. Following the 2007–2008 global
financial crisis, the Basel III framework was introduced to strengthen regulation, super-
vision, and risk management in the global banking sector (Basel Committee on Banking
Supervision, 2017). Under this framework, certain jurisdictions apply lower risk weights to
SME exposures, thereby incentivising banks to lend by reducing the capital required for
such loans (Cusmano, 2018). Additionally, some regulators allow more flexible liquidity
coverage ratios or simplified documentation processes for SME loans.
2.4.3
Government-backed loans
Government-backed loans can be seen as a key instrument used by public authorities
to improve startups’ access to capital, in early or high-risk phases where private credit
markets may fall short. These loans are typically offered at subsidised interest rates, with

2.4.4
Venture Capital Regulation
Startups oftentimes explore a wide range of fundings sources, with Venture Capital being
one of the more common. Da Rin & Hellmann (2020) define venture capital as: ”Venture
capital firms (referred to as VC), also known as General Partners, are financial interme-

growth potential. With preferred equity and strong control rights, VCs actively engage in
governance and decision-making within their portfolio companies, aiming to drive growth
and profitability (Jeon, 2024).

Externalities/Open innovation
Governments can capture and internalise a greater share of returns in the form of ex-
ternalities which benefit the economy as a whole (Avidor, 2011). Even though not part
of Avidor’s key drivers, externalities were crucial to Israel’s innovation success. Israel,
through the creation of the innovation cluster Silicon Wadi (Silicon Valley in Hebrew) laid
out the groundwork for open innovation, knowledge creation and transfer. Such clusters
are widely recognised for their ability to generate economic spillovers that are distributed
across the wider ecosystem and are not exclusively captured by the firm that originated
the innovation (Audretsch & Feldman, 1996).
Roper et al. (2013) suggest that openness can produce positive externalities by facili-
tating more effective knowledge diffusion, and while Alfred Marshall coined the term, his
apprentice Arthur Pigou defines externalities as effects of the market economy that are
not captured in price systems (Pigou, 1920). They are called externalities because the
effects affect others which the creator of the effects hadn’t considered or set out to af-
fect. We have both positive and negative externalities. A positive externality may be that
a company conducts research into new ways of producing, which another company can
benefit from. In this case, the company carrying out the research does not necessarily
think that their work will have a positive social impact, but the research is something that
benefits others in the same industry. Negative externalities deal with the opposite, and
are effects that have a negative impact socially (affecting the community).
The development of Silicon Wadi was not accidental. It was enabled by deliberate govern-
ment efforts to co-locate R&D institutions, defence contractors, startups, universities, and
international investors. This closeness facilitated informal interaction, knowledge sharing,
labour mobility, and collaboration between firms, all characteristics of what Chesbrough
(2003) later conceptualised as open innovation. Within the open innovation model, ideas
and technologies flowed across organisations, supported by joint ventures, public-private
partnerships, and university spin-offs.
These dynamics created a great environment for innovation. The presence of skilled tal-
ent, specialised suppliers, venture capital, and supportive infrastructure lowered transac-
tion costs and accelerated technological development. Also, the entrepreneurial ecosys-
tem benefitted from a “recycling” mechanism: successful founders often reinvested cap-
ital, experience, and networks into new ventures, further intensifying the externalities
across the ecosystem (Senor & Singer, 2009). In this sense, externalities were not merely
incidental outcomes; they were intentionally developed through ecosystem design and
alignment of institutions. By doing so, Israel demonstrated how targeted policy and clus-
ter development can transform localised innovation into widespread national competitive-
ness.

2.5
Hiring Policies
Hiring plays a vital role in helping startups grow, innovate, and succeed over time. That’s
why it’s important to look at the policies that help, or get in the way of, attracting and
keeping great talent. This section explores how various labour-related policies such as
labour market regulations, payroll taxes, access to international talent, and equity-based
compensation affect startups’ ability to attract, retain, and incentivise employees.
2.5.1
Labour Regulation and Employee Protections
Labour market regulation, in particular, can have a considerable impact on startup growth.
These regulations include provisions related to hiring and firing practices, minimum wage
requirements, and other employment conditions. Research suggests that higher levels
of labour market regulation are associated with lower productivity (Bassanini & Venn,
2007). Supporting this view, Bailey & Thomas (2015) find that a 10 percent increase
Comparing the Impact of Government Policies on Startups in Denmark and Norway
15
==PAGE:22==
in the level of regulation leads to a 0.63 percent decrease in hiring. They further argue
that regulatory burdens negatively affect both the formation of startups and the expansion
of their workforces. Conversely, more flexible labour regulations have been shown to
enhance the adaptability of high-risk startups, thereby increasing their potential for long-
term growth.
There are, however, mixed findings regarding the effects of labour protections. These
protections include mechanisms such as unemployment insurance and sick leave, which
are designed to reduce employee risk. Henrekson et al. (2010) argues that strong labour
protections may discourage entrepreneurial activity by decreasing employees’ willingness
to leave secure positions. When the benefits associated with employment are high and
safeguarded by regulation, individuals may be less inclined to assume the risks associated
with launching a new business, thereby limiting new business formation. At the same time,
certain aspects of labour-related policy can encourage entrepreneurship. A generous
social insurance system, for instance, can lower the personal financial risks of starting a
business and thereby increase the likelihood that individuals will pursue entrepreneurial
ventures.
2.5.2
Talent Acquisition and the role of stock options
Access to international talent also plays a vital role in the development and competitive-
ness of startups. According to Palagashvili (2021), approximately one-third of startups
are heavily reliant on highly skilled foreign employees or contractors. Many startup lead-
ers emphasise that greater access to the global labour market is essential to support their
growth trajectories and international scalability.
One common strategy for attracting top talent in startups is the use of employee stock
options. These options grant employees the right to purchase a portion of the company’s
shares, effectively giving them a stake in the business. The value of these shares may in-
crease depending on the startup’s performance or the achievement of specific milestones.
Startups frequently use stock options as an alternative to offering high salaries, allowing
them to manage limited cash flow while still remaining competitive in the labour market
(Gompers & Lerner, 2001, as cited in Holmer, 2024).
Furthermore, evidence suggests that the use of stock options can enhance employment
stability by reducing the incidence of lay-offs and creating a more resilient workforce
(Kruse, 1993, as cited in Holmer, 2024). Holmer (2024) also notes that employee stock
ownership is associated with productivity gains of between 3.5 and 5 percent, with the
effect being especially pronounced in smaller firms. 
Holmer (2024) finds that stock options are a key driver of success in venture capital–
backed startups. As such, he argues that if the European Union wishes to enhance its
global competitiveness and become a stronger player in the innovation economy, reform-
ing the taxation of stock options should be a priority (Henrekson & Sanandaji, 2018).
Several policy instruments affect how stock options are treated. In many countries, stock
option gains are subject to taxation, but the rate and classification vary. Gains may be
taxed as personal income, capital gains, or under alternative schemes, each with different
financial implications for employees (Holmer, 2024). Countries that offer more favourable
tax treatment of stock options, particularly those taxing gains at capital gains rates, tend
to experience higher levels of venture capital investment and high-growth startup activity
(Henrekson & Sanandaji, 2018).
Henrekson & Sanandaji argue that relaxing these rules could be a targeted and effective
way to support entrepreneurship without requiring broad-based tax cuts. Tailoring tax in-

2.5.3
Payroll and income taxation
Hiring decisions are also influenced by payroll taxes. When payroll taxes are high, star-
tups may be discouraged from expanding their teams, particularly when it comes to hiring
highly skilled workers. Depending on whether the tax burden falls on the employer, the
employee, or both, the result can be either increased wage costs or reduced net wages.
In both cases, this can hinder growth, especially for labour-intensive startups (Henrekson
et al., 2010).
The taxation of earned income is similarly important, as it affects employee behaviour
and can ultimately impact productivity. Progressive labour taxation directly influences
decisions in the labour market by reducing the after-tax return on work. This reduction may
lead to a decline in both the quantity of labour supplied and the effort exerted. Moreover,
progressive tax structures may discourage employees from acquiring new skills, as the
financial returns on such investments are reduced (Henrekson et al., 2010).  
2.6
National Case Study: Denmark and Norway
This thesis focuses on two countries: Denmark and Norway. While the previous sections
of the literature review introduced various entrepreneurship-related policies from around
the world, this section narrows the focus to the national level. It provides an overview
of the entrepreneurial landscape in each country, highlighting key policies, challenges,
and structural characteristics. The aim is to establish a contextual understanding of both
ecosystems, offering background insight into the specific environments in which startups
operate.
2.6.1
Norway
Norway’s economy has traditionally benefited from a strong international competitive ad-
vantage in sectors such as oil, gas, fisheries, and metals, as well as their sovereign wealth
fund (Oljefondet/The Oilfund) which is the largest in the world of its kind. However, there
is growing consensus that the country’s future economic development will depend on its
ability to build a more diversified economy (Nordbakken, 2024). Therefore, innovation and
entrepreneurship are becoming important key drivers of long-term growth, and as Nord-
bakken (2024) argues, reversing the stagnation in innovative entrepreneurship observed
over the past decades is essential if Norway is to succeed in building a more dynamic and
future-oriented economy.
Norway ranks high in global rankings related to quality of life, digital infrastructure, and
institutional trust, and has put in significant effort in building an ecosystem that supports
startup activity (WHR, 2025), but the number of startups that successfully scale into in-
ternational growth companies remains limited. As of 2023, Norway only had 7 unicorns,
which refers to a privately held startup company valued at over one billion US dollars,
whereas the first company to qualify for unicorn status - Autostore didn’t get there until
2019. The Norwegian government has, to combat this, gradually shifted its focus from
general business support to more targeted programs aimed at high-growth, and export-
oriented startups.
Starting a venture in Norway comes with its challenges. One of them being that it’s a
relatively small and undiversified domestic market, which often requires startups to inter-
nationalise early, and in some cases before they are fully mature (Nordbakken, 2024).
Especially for growth-oriented ventures that rely on access to capital and strategic part-
ners beyond national borders. Another is related to tax policy and ownership structures.

Recent debates around the newly implemented “exit tax”  have sparked concern among
founders and investors, who warn that it may discourage entrepreneurship or lead to cap-

Cultural perceptions of entrepreneurship in Norway have also evolved slowly.
Tradi-

and university graduates. Higher education institutions have also increasingly integrated

Governmental funding and support initiatives towards startups
Norway offers a wide array of funding mechanisms for startups, ranging from government-
backed loans and innovation grants (StartupLink, 2023) to venture capital, private equity,
and tax incentives. However, Norway still lags behind leading startup nations like Estonia
(Invest in Estonia, 2023), or even its neighbour Sweden (OECD, 2023), when it comes to
the overall accessibility and efficiency of capital allocation. Much of this is due to struc-
tural challenges in the Norwegian funding landscape, such as limited early-stage venture
capital, risk-averse investment culture, and a relatively small pool of competent capital
that actively contributes beyond funding (Oslo Business Region, 2024).
Like the Yozma model, where the state acts as a fund-of-funds, Norway has Argentum,
which primarily backs private equity funds; Nysnø, which focuses on climate-related in-
vestments and funds; and Investinor, which operates more broadly with a sector-agnostic
mandate, investing both directly in startups and indirectly via funds. However, compared
to Yozma, these institutions tend to operate with less dynamism and competition, and of-
ten face criticism for their limited impact on building a self-sustaining VC ecosystem, and
many founders and experts argue that these entities remain too passive and bureaucratic
to close Norway’s early-stage funding gap ( Bjerknes, 2024).
Grants and funding options
The Norwegian government’s entrepreneurship and inno-
vation arm; Innovation Norway (IN) helps stimulate innovation in the Norwegian ecosys-
tem by offering subsidies in the form of grants, loans, and matching arrangements (Inno-
vasjon Norge, 2025). IN can be seen as one of the most active support mechanisms for
Norwegian businesses and they allocate around 870 million Euro’s as grants, loans and
guarantees for innovation projects (typically scale ups), and 130 million Euro’s for founders
and entrepreneurs. The funds from IN are largely used to reduce risk for projects that ben-
efit society in the form of value creation, reductions in emission and/or better services or
products (Haugli, 2023).
IN has recently gained criticism for their bureaucratic approach to venture investing, es-
pecially when compared to VC’s and that they’ve supported “too many weak projects” in
the past as a consequence of supporting small businesses in rural areas to stimulate re-
gional growth. To combat this, Furseth (2025) calls for a revision of IN as a whole, where
he argues for splitting the organisation in two; a regional section and a growth division
for startups. The employees at IN also inhibit little, to no entrepreneurial experience, and
Furseth (2025) suggests that IN should map out all employees and their competencies
and consider setting requirements in hiring related to relevant startup or investor experi-
ence.

Venture Capital
Norway’s venture capital investment in 2024 amounted to just 1 billion USD, ranking it 23rd
behind the United States, which topped the list with 190.8 billion USD (Statista, 2024). If
one looks at VC investments as a share of GDP, the U.S invested just shy of 1 percent of
their GDP compared to Norway with 0,038% in 2024 (InvestEurope, 2024). 
While there is no exact record, various compiled sources indicate that Norway has at least
50 active venture capital firms, and regardless of numbers, many Norwegian VC firms
remain small in size, conservative in risk appetite, and limited in sector specialisation.
If one compares to countries like Sweden, where VC firms often take on a more active,
hands-on role in scaling startups, Norway’s VC environment is often criticised for being
passive, with fewer firms offering the strategic guidance and international networks that
founders need to grow globally.
Also, the absence of strong domestic lead investors in early-stage rounds force many
Norwegian startups to seek funding abroad, usually in Sweden, Germany, or the U.S.,
often relocating operations or incorporating elsewhere in the process. This weakens the
long-term potential for value creation in Norway and experts argue for policy changes that
stimulate domestic VC formation, improve regulatory conditions for investment (i.e. stock
options and capital gains), and facilitate cross-border capital mobility.

2.7
Research Gap
The literature review has provided an overview of key policy areas and their effects on
founders and entrepreneurial activity. It began with a broad examination of policy instru-
ments and their general implications for startups, before narrowing its focus to the specific
contexts of Denmark and Norway. While existing research has shed light on how govern-
ment policies influence entrepreneurship, particularly in areas such as taxation, funding,
and regulation, notable gaps remain. In particular, few studies offer comparative insights
into how national frameworks shape founder decision-making across nations. Moreover,
there is limited understanding of how policies interact in practice, whether they reinforce,
contradict, or neutralise each other, and how this dynamic affects startups.
2.7.1
What Has Been Well Studied
Much of the reviewed research focuses on startup formation and entrepreneurship rates.
In other words, many studies have investigated how specific policies influence the cre-
ation of new startups. While this work is valuable, it offers only a partial view, as it often
examines individual policy areas, such as taxation, in isolation rather than providing a
comprehensive understanding of how various policies interact to affect entrepreneurship.
Certain policy themes have also received more attention than others in the existing liter-
ature. For example, there is extensive research on the effects of taxation on startups.
In addition, much of the research has concentrated on more established entrepreneurial
hubs, such as the United States. These studies predominantly adopt quantitative ap-
proaches and provide limited insight into the lived experiences of founders, which are
essential for understanding how startups actually navigate policy environments.
2.7.2
Gaps in Existing Research
As noted above, there is a lack of studies examining the effects of policy on startups
that are already operating and in the growth phase. While startup formation is impor-
tant, the survival and scaling of startups are crucial for building a dynamic entrepreneurial
ecosystem, as highlighted in the literature review. Most existing studies focus on indi-
vidual policies and fail to offer a more holistic view of the range of policies that influence
founders and entrepreneurs. Furthermore, there is a clear lack of founder-level qualitative
research. As a result, little is known about how entrepreneurs perceive and interact with
policies in their daily work, and how these policies influence their decision-making. 
Although various studies have examined policies at the national level, there is no com-
parative research that focuses on Denmark and Norway from the perspective of founders
rather than from aggregated statistics on the entrepreneurial landscape. In addition, re-

cent research on policy design in either Denmark or Norway is scarce, with most studies
relying on quantitative methods to assess the state of entrepreneurship.
2.7.3
Why This Gap Matters
Addressing this gap is important for creating a stronger entrepreneurial ecosystem in
both Denmark and Norway. Without research that incorporates founder-level perspec-
tives, policymakers may lack the necessary insights to improve the startup environment.
Policies are rarely evaluated based on founders’ experiences, yet these individuals are
directly affected by such measures. Understanding how founders experience and per-
ceive policies is vital for assessing whether these policies are truly effective. Policies that
appear sound on paper may succeed or fail in practice in ways that only founders can
reveal. There are also broader policy areas that are not specifically designed to target
startups but that can nonetheless influence founders and employees working within star-
tups. Such policies may indirectly affect the development and performance of startups.
Examining these measures is important for understanding the underlying rationale and
cultural attitudes in a country toward entrepreneurship and the creation of new ventures.
A comparative analysis can provide valuable insights into how each country’s policies
function in practice, offering lessons that could help create a stronger and more supportive
landscape for founders seeking to scale high-growth startups.

Methodology
3.1
Research Approach and Design
This thesis explores how founders and investors in both countries perceive and react to
startup-related policies, how these policies impact decisions around incorporation, scal-
ing, or relocation, and how they affect the ambition, ownership, and overall structure of
new ventures.
To capture the complexity of these experiences, the research adopts a qualitative and
exploratory approach, guided by grounded theory methodology. Grounded theory is a
qualitative research methodology developed by Glaser & Strauss in 1967, designed to
generate new theories directly from systematically collected and analysed data, rather
than testing pre-existing hypotheses or relying heavily on prior literature (Glaser & Strauss,
1967). Consistent with the exploratory nature of grounded theory, the study was initially
designed with a broad focus, which was progressively refined as key themes and patterns
emerged through data collection and analysis. The study moves abductively between
empirical data and theoretical frameworks, aiming to bridge the gap between policy design
and founder experience.
3.2
Data Collection
Data for this research was collected to address the problem statement. A structured set
of tools and methods was employed to ensure that data was gathered in a systematic and
coherent manner. The following section outlines the methods used in detail.
3.2.1
Semi-Structured Interviews
Semi-structured interviews are used as the primary data collection method, and while
questions were standardised across participants, the researchers dove deeper into emerg-
ing themes or interviewee specific experiences where relevant, allowing for free-flowing
conversations. The interview guide was structured around five core policy domains de-
rived from the literature: taxation, access to finance, labour policy, administrative burden,
and equity incentives. To ensure relevance, three guides were developed to better fit dif-
ferent interviewee profiles; one for investors, policy makers and founders. Participants
were selected through purposive sampling to ensure relevance to the study’s objectives,
and were recruited via LinkedIn, accelerator programs, university networks, and snow-
ball sampling. Interviews were conducted online or in person, lasting 30–60 minutes,
and recorded with participant consent, as well as supplemental emails and short phone
calls when necessary. The final sample includes 21 key market players, spanning startup
founders, policymakers, politicians, startup lawyers and both angel and VC investors in
Denmark and Norway.
3.2.2
Sampling Strategy
Interviewee candidates for this study were selected based on their background and expe-
rience in relation to startup activity. A strategy was first developed to effectively identify
and contact relevant stakeholders. To avoid a narrow focus on a single group, such as
only serial founders, multiple stakeholder categories were defined:
Founder: An individual who has established and is currently operating, or has previ-
ously exited, a startup venture. May include first-time founders or those with a single
entrepreneurial experience.
24
Comparing the Impact of Government Policies on Startups in Denmark and Norway
==PAGE:31==
Serial Founder: An entrepreneur who has founded and exited multiple startups, typically
with a track record of repeated entrepreneurial activity across different ventures.
Investor: An individual who allocates capital to startups, either directly (as an angel in-
vestor) or indirectly through structured vehicles such as venture capital firms, family of-
fices, or corporate funds.
Policymaker: An individual actively involved in shaping or influencing public policy, in-
cluding elected officials (e.g., members of parliament), political advisors, or representa-
tives of government-affiliated policy institutions.
Incorporating a mix of these profiles provided a broader and more nuanced understand-
ing of the research topic. First-time founders and serial founders were the primary focus
of the study, as they are most directly impacted by startup-related policies. Investors
were initially included to support and contextualize the founder perspectives, given their
important role in startup success. However, during the interviews, it became evident
that investors offered significant insights into the policy environment, and they were sub-
sequently treated as key stakeholders. Policy makers were included to provide a gov-
ernmental perspective on entrepreneurship policy, though they proved more difficult to
access.
Stakeholders were primarily contacted via LinkedIn and email. An initial outreach batch
was sent to approximately 35 relevant individuals in each country. Following the first
few interviews, a snowball sampling effect occurred, leading to additional highly relevant
interviewees within the scope of the study.

3.3
Data Management
Most interviews were recorded and transcribed using Microsoft Teams. To enhance data
quality, the researchers additionally used OpenAI’s Whisper transcription model on the
video recordings to generate alternative transcripts. These were then cross-checked with
the original Microsoft Teams transcripts (when available), manual notes, and audio record-
ings to ensure completeness and accuracy. In cases where organisational privacy regu-
lations restricted recording or transcription, one researcher conducted the interview while
the other simultaneously took detailed notes.
To ensure that important details were not lost, all interviews were processed on the same
day they were conducted. This allowed the researchers to supplement the transcripts with
relevant context and clarifications while the conversations were still fresh in memory. To
maintain participant confidentiality, all identifying information was anonymised. Only the
final notes and transcripts were stored in a secure folder, and all original audio and video
recordings were permanently deleted.

Validity, Reliability, and Limitations
Several measures were taken to ensure the trustworthiness of the study. The sample
included founders from a variety of sectors and company stages to capture a broad range
of perspectives. Verbatim quotes were used throughout the findings to anchor interpre-
tations in the data and provide transparency. Regular peer debriefing among the re-
searchers helped minimise individual bias and support consistent interpretation. In ad-
dition, coding decisions and the development of themes were documented in detail to
create an audit trail and reinforce analytical reliability.
The study is subject to some limitations. As with most qualitative research, the findings are
Comparing the Impact of Government Policies on Startups in Denmark and Norway

not intended to be statistically generalisable. There is also a risk of bias in how intervie-
wees reflect on past events or portray their experiences, especially in relation to govern-
ment policy. Furthermore, interviewees with more negative experiences may be under-
represented, either due to reluctance to participate or recruitment limitations. These risks
were addressed by aiming for thematic saturation and being transparent about the ana-

3.6
Ethical Considerations
All participants were informed in advance about the purpose of the research, the vol-
untary nature of their participation, and how their data would be managed, stored, and
used. Informed consent was obtained verbally before each interview. Anonymity and
confidentiality were guaranteed to all interviewees. Personal identifiers were removed
during transcription, and quotes were anonymised using ID codes. Several participants
explicitly stated that full anonymity was a precondition for their participation, and this was
strictly respected throughout.
Data was stored securely in accordance with DTU’s data protection standards and the
General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR). Files were encrypted and access was limited
to the research team. Once the relevant insights had been extracted, the raw recordings
and identifiable data were permanently deleted to minimise data retention risks.
The researchers conducted this study independently, with no prior affiliations to any of the
participating startups, investors, or policy organisations. While this detachment helped
maintain analytical neutrality, reflexivity was maintained throughout the research process
to minimise bias. Analytical decisions, including coding and theme development, were
grounded in participant narratives and reviewed jointly by both authors to ensure consis-
tency and transparency.

4
Analysis & Findings
This section presents the empirical findings derived from semi-structured interviews with
startup founders, policymakers, and relevant experts in Denmark and Norway. The pur-
pose is to explore how government policies shape the development of startups. While
some policies have a direct impact on startups or their founders, others exert indirect
effects that nonetheless influence the startup ecosystem. Although each founder’s ex-
perience is distinct, several common themes emerged throughout the interviews. Only
findings that are directly relevant to the research questions are included. The analysis is
structured thematically, and within each theme, comparisons between Denmark and Nor-
way are presented to provide a clear and coherent overview of cross-country differences.
The interview code names, as outlined in the methodology section, are used consistently
throughout the findings to attribute specific quotes or statements to the respective inter-
viewees. This approach ensures clarity and traceability while maintaining the anonymity
of participants.
4.1
Why Founders Choose Norway or Denmark
This section aims to provide a deeper understanding of how the interviewees began their
entrepreneurial journeys, with particular attention to whether external factors such as gov-
ernment policies played a role in influencing their decision to start a company. In addition,
the interviewees shared their perspectives on the motivations of other entrepreneurs in
their network, offering broader insights into the factors that drive individuals to pursue
entrepreneurship.
Most of the founders said that they had a certain drive which is fuelling them to go on
this risky entrepreneurial journey. As DK-F3 mentioned, “Building a startup is to be on
a mission, or make a difference in the world. And that is often, I think, a stronger force
than all the policies or legislation they can throw after us, entrepreneurs.” It is impor-
tant to recognise that each founder comes from a different background. Some may grow
up in environments with strong support systems, while others may not. As a result, the
motivation to pursue entrepreneurship can stem from various sources, though it is often
driven by a desire to create change. Interviewee DK-F3 noted that Denmark is generally
a very safe country and provides a societal safety net. According to DK-F3, this security
enables individuals to take entrepreneurial risks, as they know they will have the oppor-
tunity to recover if their ventures fail. While this can be viewed as a positive feature of
the Danish system, DK-F4 offered a contrasting perspective. DK4 argued that hunger
and ambition are essential for entrepreneurial success, observing that founders in the
United States often appear more driven than those in Denmark. In DK-F4’s view, building
high-growth startups requires individuals who are willing to take significant risks and are
strongly motivated to build and scale their ventures.
Several founders noted that this safety and stability may help explain why many Danish
startups remain small and sustainable rather than transitioning into high-growth compa-
nies. Founders who aim to build large-scale ventures are not primarily motivated by policy
incentives or financial gain, but by a deep, intrinsic desire to create and build something
meaningful (DK-F2, DK-F3, DK-F5, DK-F8).
Ultimately, when asked why they chose to build their companies in Denmark, all inter-
viewed founders mentioned that they enjoy living in the country. They described Denmark

as a great place to live and raise a family. Although the entrepreneurial ecosystem may
differ from that of other countries, most founders expressed satisfaction with the system
and believed that the policies shaping this lifestyle align with the kind of life they aspire to

Similar to the danes, the Norwegian interviewees mentioned their motivation to enter the

they been accepted into YCombinator they would’ve moved their business abroad. While
the interviewees feel a strong connection to Norway, they also recognise the limitations
of its ecosystem and that chance be it, they might’ve gone elsewhere. 
Several interviewees reflected on what they saw as a broader cultural attitude towards
entrepreneurship, particularly in Norway. While experimentation and tinkering are often
encouraged, success, especially financial success, can provoke scepticism or resent-

may face cultural, as well as financial, barriers to scaling their companies domestically

4.2
Access to funding
Access to funding came up often and became a dominant theme across all interview
groups. Norwegian founders and investors (NO-I1, NO-I2, NO-PM2, NO-F5) highlighted
a lack of sufficient early stage funding, and while public funding and support were seen
as central and important, they were often-times depicted as helpful but not sufficient on
their own. In Denmark, most founders noted that while there is extensive support avail-
able during the early stages of a startup, accessing funding becomes significantly more
challenging in the later growth phases.
4.2.1
Soft-Funding & Grants
All interviewed founders acknowledged that the availability of soft funding and grants in
Denmark is a significant advantage. They noted that such funding plays a crucial role
in helping startups get off the ground. Several successful startups, which have since
grown and created employment opportunities, began with early-stage grants such as the
Microlegat from the Danish Foundation for Entrepreneurship. This demonstrates that soft
funding can be an effective tool in supporting the initial phases of startup development.
These findings are consistent with the views of the Norwegian founders, who also consider
the soft funding landscape in Norway to be valuable, though they expressed concerns
about the abundance such support schemes. In particular, Founder NO-F5 and NO-F6
noted that without access to such funding, their ventures would not have survived. Their
experiences highlight the critical role that early-stage support can play in ensuring the
survival and growth of startups.

Ease of getting funding

”I believe that Danish startups sometimes receive too much financial support and assis-

Tax Policy
When talking to founders about tax policies, several issues were raised. In this section,
findings related to tax policies are discussed. There are many different policies in place,
but only the ones that the interviewees specifically mentioned as either positive or negative

4.3.1
General Attitudes Toward Taxation
It appears that all the interviewees were generally accepting of Denmark’s high tax rates
because they believe in the system and benefit from it themselves. However, most of the
founders mentioned that they had considered relocating and explored options for reducing
their tax burden. Founder DK-F4 and NO-I1 highlighted that almost all founders evaluate
these possibilities when their startup reaches a certain size. NO-I2 mentioned this never
affected their decisions on their operations or choice of location, but that it adds a layer
of negativity, making the task of building companies even harder. Some choose to stay,
while others eventually leave the country to avoid paying high taxes. Founder DK-F5
agreed and added that many founders choose to relocate when they begin scaling their
startups and approach a potential exit.
Some founders and policy makers (DK-F4, DK-F5, DK-F8, DK-PM1) expressed that even
though they accept the high tax rate, they find it extremely difficult to navigate the tax
landscape. Founder DK-F5 mentioned that while everyone in Denmark pays a substantial
amount of tax, 95 percent of people do not understand what they are paying for or know
the specific tax rules, which is concerning. Founder DK-F4 related this to entrepreneurs,
as startup founders also need to navigate this space. They are willing to pay taxes, but
they do not want to face unexpected surprises. These founders already experience a lot
of uncertainty within their startups, so adding more uncertainty only worsens the situation.
Founder DK-F4 shared that each time they engage with a startup, whether through angel
investment, board participation, or advisory work, they spend additional money just to

figure out the tax implications. Doing this repeatedly slows down the process, and startups
often cannot afford those delays. The process should be clearer and faster.
4.3.2
Wealth & Exit taxation
Founders in both Denmark and Norway consistently raised concerns about the taxation of
unrealised gains on shares, whether through wealth tax, mark-to-market taxation, or exit
tax. In both countries, these policies were described as distorting incentives for ownership,
recruitment, and long-term value creation.
The taxation of unrealised gains was particularly relevant in the context of stock options.
All Danish founders criticised Danish rules that historically taxed employees on the un-
realised value of shares and warrants, even when those shares weren’t realised.Though
reforms have softened these rules in recent years, several founders (DK-F3, DK-F4, DK-
F5, DK-I1, DK-F6, DK-PM1, DK-F7) still described these mechanisms as harmful to star-
tups’ ability to incentivise employees through ownership schemes. They highlighted how
this exposure to taxation on unrealised illiquid shares forced employees to either sell pre-
maturely or fund tax liabilities personally. As DK-F3 noted, this risk becomes especially
problematic when valuations fluctuate, employees face taxation on inflated values, even
if those values subsequently collapse.
DK-F5 mentioned:
“We are not in a good position here in Denmark to build a tech startup with an incentivized
program based on warrants and options, at least not with the current system where the
tax from hell applies.”
DK-F3 explained their personal experience with the mark-to-market taxation (lagerbeskat-
ning) of minority holdings (<10%) in unlisted companies. Under these rules, corporate
shareholders could face annual taxation on unrealised gains. While this was intended for
institutional investors, it also affected founders holding minority stakes after raising capital.
The problem was widely recognised as causing liquidity issues, forcing premature sales.
DK-F5 added that this primarily hit employees or founders post-dilution below 10% own-
ership, making Denmark less attractive for startups relying on early-stage equity-based
compensation or angel investors.
As of January 2025, this particular mark-to-market rule for dividends on unlisted shares
has been abolished through the Iværksætterpakken 2024, addressing some of these con-
cerns. There still remains a great deal of uncertainty regarding what part of the policy is
still in place.
The system for both exit and wealth tax is similar in Norway,  and most view the fact that
these taxes are calculated on unrealised gains as the biggest hurdle, prompting founders
to be taxed on bloated valuations stemming from investment rounds. NO-PM2 mentioned:
”Wealth tax and exit tax in combination, is the ultimate punishment for being successful.”
Several interviewees explained how these policies force founders to make strategic de-
cisions that prioritise tax minimisation over growth. NO-F2 pointed out that companies
actively aim to keep valuations low to reduce exposure to wealth tax:
“In Norway, you should keep valuation as low as possible because wealth tax will knock
you down.”
NO-I2 views money raised in funding rounds as a planned loss, so taxing this capital, or
“runway” as they put it, makes no sense. They add that taxing wealth is fine, but capital
raised for a startup isn’t wealth. This reflects a broader critique within the Norwegian
startup ecosystem: taxation frameworks fail to distinguish between operational capital

and personal wealth, which can create distortions in how companies plan for growth and
investment.
Exit Tax
Exit tax was identified as a barrier when hiring international talent, as it creates uncertainty
for potential employees considering relocating to Norway. One founder (NO-F4) explained
that the exit tax stopped them from even considering moving or hiring international people
with options, and this was echoed by other founders mentioning that the exit tax made it
hard to incentivise key hires from abroad, identifying it as a huge blocker (NO-F5). NO-I1
echoed this, adding that when companies they’ve invested in move abroad, they have
less opportunities to share value, which in turn makes it less attractive for them to make
investments in Norway. 
Beyond recruitment, founders described how these taxes shape long-term ownership de-
cisions. NO-F2 noted that when valuations increase and taxation on paper wealth be-
comes unsustainable, the instinct is to leave Norway to avoid future tax liabilities. NO-F3
agrees, and points out that the already frailing ecosystem doesn’t benefit from having
all the successful founders pack up their things and leave. NO-I1 added that modern
tech founders can go anywhere, and when it’s not facilitated for them in Norway, they
leave, and the new way of building successful companies, current politicians haven’t un-
derstood. 
NO-PM2 linked the tax regime to broader ecosystem-level consequences. As a com-
parative example, they highlighted how Sweden abolished their wealth tax in 2006–2007
and has since surpassed Norway in entrepreneurial performance. They also argued that
these policies discourage the accumulation of private capital necessary for reinvestment
into new ventures: “In the case of an exit you will be realising capital, which then again
puts you in the wealth tax situation, which is actually a kind of reduction of an effective

Discussion
This thesis examines how founders and investors in both countries interpret and respond
to startup-related policies, how these policies influence decisions to incorporate, scale,
or relocate, and how they shape the ambition, ownership, and structure of new ventures.
Through a qualitative, exploratory research using grounded theory and semi-structured in-
terviews, the preceding Findings chapter uncovered how policies interacted with founders,
investors and policy makers behaviour, highlighting commonalities and key differences
between the two countries.
The findings revealed that Norwegian policies, particularly those related to wealth and
exit taxes, exert a strong influence on founder behaviour, often driving decisions about
valuation strategies, ownership structures, and even relocation. In Denmark, while stock
option taxation emerged as the primary concern, its impact is felt most acutely in relation
to talent attraction and retention, rather than as a direct driver of relocation. Across both
ecosystems, the data point to taxation frameworks as contributing to defensive growth
strategies and a broader hesitation around long-term domestic scaling.
This chapter now transitions to a deeper analysis and interpretation of these findings. It
will connect the emergent themes back to the original research questions and the exist-
ing literature on entrepreneurial ecosystems, taxation, and policy design. In doing so, it
aims to clarify the theoretical and practical implications for both founders and policymak-
ers, particularly in light of comparative examples such as the Yozma program in Israel.
Finally, this chapter will reflect on the study’s limitations and suggest avenues for future
research that can contribute to refining policy approaches for supporting entrepreneurship
in Denmark and Norway.
5.1
Motivation
The motivation for this research stemmed from recent changes in taxation policies in both
Denmark and Norway. The former eased up on their taxation policies, allowing for in-
creased flexibility for founders, investors and advancements in the startup ecosystem,
whereas the latter has tightened theirs, making it more difficult for entrepreneurs to suc-
ceed. This divergence spurred what could be potential learnings from both entities, trying
to depict which strategy would pan out to be “most effective” and to envision the long-term
consequences of these actions.
Until the 2024–2025 reform, Denmark’s limiting tax policies were widely criticised by its
startup community for imposing financial burdens on entrepreneurs, particularly through
the taxation of unrealised gains on shares. When these policies were finally relaxed, it
was hailed as a major victory across the ecosystem. This raises a critical question: why
would Norway now move in the opposite direction, seemingly replicating a system that
Denmark has just abandoned?
The researchers were driven by a desire to better understand these contrasting policy
paths and contribute to the ongoing dialogue about how public policy can more effec-
tively support entrepreneurial activity. By focusing on first-hand accounts from founders,
investors, and policymakers, this research seeks to shine a light on the lived realities
behind formal policy frameworks and add practical insights to the academic debate on
startup ecosystems.

5.2
Methods & Findings
This study adopts a qualitative and exploratory research design, using semi-structured
interviews as the primary data collection method to explore how founders, investors, and
policymakers in Denmark and Norway experience and interpret government policies af-
fecting startups. A total of 21 participants were selected through purposive and snowball
sampling to ensure relevance and diversity across key stakeholder groups, and inter-
views were conducted throughout April, May and June 2025. Data collection focused
on five core policy domains and was analysed through a grounded theory approach to
identify recurring themes and patterns.
Research Question 1:
In what ways do national policies on wealth and capital taxation shape founders and in-
vestors’ willingness to remain, scale, or relocate within the startup ecosystems in Denmark
and Norway?
5.2.1
The Influence of Wealth and Capital Taxation on Founder and
Investor Behaviour
National tax regimes play a decisive role in shaping where and how entrepreneurial activity
unfolds. For founders and investors in Norway and Denmark, policies related to wealth
and capital taxation have emerged as key factors influencing decisions to remain, scale,
or relocate. This section draws on empirical findings and relevant literature to further
examine how such tax regimes affect behaviour and long-term strategic planning in the
startup ecosystem.
Defensive Behaviour and Taxation of Unrealised Gains
The wealth taxation illustrates a clear case of negative externalities, which Pigou defined
as unintended effects imposed on others by actions not meant to affect them (Pigou,
1920). A policy originally designed to promote job creation and generate tax revenue
has, according to one investor, cast a shadow over the entire Norwegian startup ecosys-
tem, inadvertently discouraging founders and investors. As Pigou’s definition suggests,
such policies may generate broader adverse effects beyond their intended targets. For
instance, by taxing founder-held shares as part of personal wealth, despite these often
being tied up as operational capital, tax authorities unintentionally constrain reinvestment,
hiring, and risk-taking, thereby distorting incentives across the startup landscape.
The findings indicate a strong sentiment among founders in both countries that current tax
policies penalise illiquidity and entrepreneurial risk-taking. In Norway, the annual wealth
tax, based on the market value of shares regardless of cash flow, was frequently cited as a
structural barrier. Similarly, Denmark’s former mark-to-market regime (lagerbeskatning),
which taxed unrealised gains on unlisted shares, and noted that even with the progress
made with the launch of the Entrepreneurship Package (Iværksætterpakken), issues still
persist, especially in regards to taxation of stock options. In both countries, interviewees
described scenarios where they were “taxed on value that didn’t exist in their bank ac-
counts,” especially when high paper valuations were not yet monetised or liquid.

Exit Taxation and Mobility Constraints
As NO-I1 notes, when conditions in Norway are not favourable to entrepreneurship, founders
increasingly choose to relocate. This reflects a broader shift in how successful companies
are built today, less tied to geography and more responsive to global opportunities and
policy environments. Exit taxes, which are designed to secure the government’s share
of value created within their economy, directly inhibits mobility between markets and was
noted as interviewees as a way of locking in those who, greatly affected by the wealth tax,
wanted to relocate.
The findings indicate that exit tax policies create both practical and symbolic barriers to
founder and employee mobility. Practically, the obligation to pay tax on unrealised gains
at the time of exit forces founders to treat illiquid, paper based valuations as if they were
liquid assets. This can place pressure on cash flow and forces strategic decisions that
prioritise tax mitigation over long-term planning. Several founders described the policy
as a sign that the domestic system does not support entrepreneurial success, especially
when success is defined by growth, internationalisation, and eventual exit.
This is echoed in broader literature. Tax policy is widely recognised as one of the most im-
portant structural factors affecting business formation, retention, and expansion (Baliamoune-
Lutz, 2014). The ability to relocate freely is a critical component of a globally competitive
startup environment. When tax regimes penalise this mobility, they risk not only losing
entrepreneurial talent, but also undermining broader ecosystem growth.
Notice of the planned exit tax forced many founders to leave the country quickly, some-
thing NO-I1 views as a lost chance of sharing value with teams they’ve invested in, dis-
couraging future investments in Norway. This resonates with Gordon & Sarada’s (2018)
argument that broad based tax policies can be inefficient, especially when they fail to dif-
ferentiate between types of capital and entrepreneurial intent. Rather than protecting the
national tax base, such policies may accelerate capital flight and reduce the long-term
reinvestment of gains into new ventures.
The behavioural effects are not limited to founders alone. Interviewees also emphasised
how these constraints extend to hiring and retention. When international employees are
brought into the Norwegian tax system, they too may become liable for exit taxation, par-
ticularly if they are compensated in equity. This creates additional legal and financial com-
plexity and has led some founders to avoid hiring international talent altogether. These
combined effects reflect a policy environment that discourages global ambition, even as

Talent, Ownership, and Stock Option Taxation
The taxation of employee stock options emerged as the most heavily criticised policy
among Danish founders interviewed in this study. Although Denmark has largely elimi-
nated what is often referred to as the “tax from hell,” the findings indicate that elements
of this tax remain active in practice. Under the current rules, employees who hold stock
options may be taxed on the unrealised value of their options, which undermines the at-
tractiveness of equity-based compensation.
Founders consistently emphasised that more favourable stock option terms are neces-
sary, as the existing framework hinders their ability to hire top talent, affects the quality of
work, and places pressure on already limited cash flow. These concerns are supported by
prior research. Studies have shown that stock options allow startups to remain compet-
itive in the hiring market while preserving financial resources (Gompers & Lerner, 2001,
as cited in Holmer, 2024). This is particularly important in Denmark, where wages are
reported to be very high according to the founders. As a result, finding alternative ways
to compensate employees becomes crucial for startups operating with limited financial
resources. In addition, stock options help align employee interests with those of the com-
pany, fostering a sense of shared ownership and long-term commitment (Henrekson &
Sanandaji, 2018).
One founder noted that employee motivation and performance were negatively affected
by the inability to offer compelling equity packages. This aligns with Holmer’s (2024)
findings, which associate employee stock ownership with productivity gains of between
3.5 and 5 percent. As both the findings and the literature review suggest, well-designed
stock option regulations are critical for the success of startups. Denmark must take this
into account, as the current policy landscape places founders at a disadvantage. More-
over, existing research points to a broader challenge at the European level, where stock
option policies lag behind those in the United States. Holmer (2024) argues that if Europe
aspires to become globally competitive in entrepreneurship, reforming stock option policy
is essential.
Research Question 2:
What policy improvements could help create a more coherent and founder-responsive

5.2.2
Towards a More Coherent and Founder-Responsive Startup
Environment
The findings presented in Section 4 highlight how taxation and regulatory environments
in Norway and Denmark shape founder behaviour, risk-taking, and mobility. Building on
these insights, this section explores how policy frameworks might be reformed to foster a
more coherent and founder-responsive startup environment. By drawing on comparative
examples, stakeholder input, and academic literature, the following subsections outline
opportunities for governments to better align taxation, funding, and venture support poli-
cies with the realities of modern entrepreneurship.
Rethinking Taxation Policies
Taxation policy plays a key role in shaping the entrepreneurial environment, not only by
influencing individual founder decisions but also by depicting a country’s broader atti-
tude towards innovation and value creation. The findings indicate that founders and in-
vestors in both Norway and Denmark, feel that their current tax regimes fail to support
entrepreneurial growth, especially in terms of early stage liquidity and mobility across
borders. One of the issues that were highlighted the most was the taxation of unrealised

gains which was described in section 4.3.2. As Gordon & Sarada (2018) argue, broad-
based tax policies can have the unintended consequence of suppressing innovative en-
trepreneurship, particularly when they fail to differentiate between types of capital or levels
of liquidity. This was echoed by several of the Norwegian interviewees, who described
how taxing shares in unlisted startups as if they were readily tradeable assets fails to take
into account the realities of early stage growth. As NO-I2 put it, treating runway capital
as wealth sends the wrong signal and distorts decisions about hiring, reinvestment, and
even incorporation.
The recent reforms in Denmark were consistently referenced by Danish interviewees as
a positive shift in support and political direction. Although issues still remain, the reform
illustrates the value of proper dialogue between policymakers and the startup ecosystem,
and how targeted changes can create meaningful progress in founder confidence and mo-
tivation. Norway, by contrast, has steered towards a tightened tax regime at a time when
global competition for talent and capital is increasing. Several founders (NO-F2, NO-F3,
NO-F7) described how the current system promotes short termism, encourages defensive
structuring, and undermines Norway’s ability to retain high performing entrepreneurial tal-
ent. This aligns with the concerns raised by Asoni & Sanandaji (2014), who stress that
the goal of entrepreneurship policy should not merely be to increase the number of star-
tups, but to enable the creation of high-quality, high-growth firms with long-term economic
impact.
A more coherent and holistic approach to taxation would better align with the risk and liq-
uidity ratios of entrepreneurial assets. While Norwegian interviewees are generally nega-
tive towards the new tax policies, they argue for adjustments: higher deduction thresholds,
clearer carve-outs for operational capital, and the ability to nullify exit tax if ventures fail
(NO-PM2, NO-I2), rather than abolishing taxes altogether. This reflects a desire not for in-
dividual treatment, but for recognition of the structural differences between entrepreneurial
equity and traditional forms of wealth. The findings highlight the need to rethink how tax
policies interact with startups, not only to improve fairness or reduce administrative bur-
dens, but to build an environment that encourages long-term innovation. The interviewees
also highlighted a lack of clarity around taxes as a major issue. Many founders find it ex-
tremely difficult to understand and navigate the tax landscape, adding an unnecessary
layer of uncertainty. According to DK-F4, this is not something founders should have to
worry about. So creating a well calibrated tax system can be a powerful tool for encour-
aging entrepreneurial ambition and reinvestment. Poorly targeted policies however, risk
undermining the very growth they aim to tax.
Lessons from Israel’s Yozma Programme
When questioned on how to build a more coherent startup ecosystem, the investors men-
tioned successful models in other countries. The Israeli Yozma model, as covered in the
literature review, was launched in 1993 and was highlighted as a benchmark for public
and private collaboration in venture funding. Its aim was to catalyse early stage invest-
ment in Israel by creating government-backed venture capital funds that required private
co-investment. The programme has been credited in boosting Israel’s entrepreneurial ac-
tivity and making it a global tech hub, something NO-PM2 mentioned would’ve been hard
to predict at the time.

help combat funding gaps, and they argued strongly for its focus on the state acting not
only as a financier but also a structuring agent (NO-PM2, NO-I1). They believe that Nor-
way lacks a similarly strategic mechanism to crowd in private capital and develop high
quality, domestic venture funds. This concern is echoed in the literature, where Sanan-

The Yozma programme provides a great example of how targeted, well aligned public
support can unlock untapped potential in the startup ecosystem, without crowding out
private actors. Interviewees noted that current efforts in Norway and Denmark are often

but rather inspiration. The success of Yozma rested on understanding the local market’s
specific barriers to capital formation, and designing a tailored, time limited intervention
that built long-term institutional capacity. Both Norway and Denmark may benefit from
considering a more structured and catalytic role for the state in the venture funding land-
scape, not as a dominant investor, but as an enabler of professionalised private activity
(NO-PM2, NO-I1).
Addressing the early-stage funding gap is central to strengthening the Nordic startup
ecosystem, and Yozma provides a clear example of how this can be achieved while foster-
ing long-term venture capital development. As some interviewees observed, particularly
those with international exposure (NO-F3, NO-I2, DK-F3), the absence of credible local
follow-on funding remains a major bottleneck for Nordic startups, pushing many to incor-
porate or scale abroad. This aligns with Sanandaji & Henrekson’s (2011) findings that a
lack of capital infrastructure often limits high-growth entrepreneurship. A Yozma inspired
initiative could help retain startups by building local growth-stage capital infrastructure that
supports founder-driven scale.
Improving Public Funding Coordination
The findings from the interviews and the literature reviewed in this study confirm that sub-
sidies, such as soft funding grants, play a vital role in supporting early-stage startups as
they navigate initial challenges. As discussed in the literature review, subsidies are de-
signed to reduce early friction, fund R&D, and support commercialisation during the most
vulnerable phases of a startup’s life cycle. According to Söderblom et al. (2015), grants
can also act as a quality signal to investors, increasing startup credibility and attractive-
ness. This was echoed by several founders in this study, such as NO-F5 and NO-F6,
who stated that their companies would likely not exist today without early-stage grants.
These examples illustrate how well-designed subsidies can create a strong foundation for
growth, especially in the early phases. However, the findings also highlighted that when
subsidies are too accessible or poorly targeted, they may undermine the overall quality
of entrepreneurship. Founder DK-F1 noted that overly generous support can encour-
age the development of immature or under-validated concepts, turning the early startup
phase into what DK-F1 called a form of gambling. A lot of startups are therefore built
on immature ideas and are not on a path of long-term growth. DK-F6 further critiqued
the system, referring to ”Grantrepreneurs”, individuals who optimise for grants rather than
product-market fit. These critiques were presented in both Denmark and Norway. Multi-
ple Norwegian Interviewees (NO-F1, NO-F2, NO-PM2) felt the same and believe startups
should face hardships as a startup is not meant to be easy. This will eventually create
a healthy entrepreneurial environment. These concerns reflect warnings in the literature
from Isenberg (2010), who argues that governments should be cautious not to over-fund
entrepreneurs, as early exposure to hardship is critical for developing resilience and disci-
pline. Similarly, a study highlighted that inefficiently targeted subsidies can distort market
selection and reduce long-term economic efficiency (Santarelli & Vivarelli, 2007, as cited

to the relative scarcity of high-growth startups. This perceived lack of pressure may also

those in need of support to remain viable, while overlooking ventures at both ends of

support should be directed toward high-potential startups, even if they appear less depen-
dent on public funding at the outset. However, as Kösters (2010) points out, this strategy
is inherently paradoxical: the startups most likely to succeed are often the least reliant
on government assistance, raising concerns about whether public actors can effectively
outperform private capital in identifying and supporting such ventures (Parker and van

for startups with exceptional growth potential or to allocate resources toward sustaining
the broader base of ventures in need of immediate assistance, a strategy some research
suggests may yield more equitable or stable outcomes. Taken together, the findings and
literature suggest that funding-related policy design faces a difficult balancing act. On one
hand, accessible support is vital for enabling early-stage experimentation and de-risking
innovation. On the other, excessive or untargeted support may reduce founder selectiv-
ity, inflate poor-quality ventures, and dull the competitive edge needed to scale. Future
policy efforts should therefore aim to preserve the positive externalities of public fund-
ing while incorporating mechanisms that ensure selectivity, alignment with private market
incentives, and encouragement of founder resilience.
Building Stronger Dialogue Between Startups and Policymakers
A recurring theme across both the Norwegian and Danish interviews was the perceived
disconnect between those designing policies and those within startup ecosystems. Sev-
eral of the founders and investors expressed frustration that key regulatory decisions are
made without input from entrepreneurs or others with understanding of startups. This was
evident in both countries, and interviewees described a lack of structured mechanisms for
sustained dialogue, which in turn contributes to policies that appear misaligned with the
operational realities of early stage companies. In Denmark, DK-F4 noted that while polit-
ical attention towards entrepreneurship has increased in recent years, engagement with
the startup community remains largely symbolic, and often limited to press friendly visits
or surface level consultations. NO-F3 echoed this and mentioned that policy frameworks
are often designed around larger, established firms, with little regard for the capital con-
straints, and risk profiles that define startup’s. NO-I2 observed that founders are often
forced to “react” to policy, rather than being given a role in shaping it. In addition, many
founders in both countries noted that policymakers are often driven by personal interests
related to re-election. This creates a focus on short-term policy decisions, rather than the
long-term strategies required to build a sustainable entrepreneurial ecosystem, which,
according to DK-F6, can take 15 to 20 years to develop.

When startups are not adequately considered in legislation, unintended consequences
are more likely to occur, including burdensome tax obligations or ineffective grant struc-
tures. As Henrekson & Sanandaji (2011) argue, entrepreneurial ecosystems function best
when governments act not as top-down planners, but as responsive partners, embedding
entrepreneurial logic into policymaking processes. One concrete example raised by NO-

that the process leading up to this policy change lacked dialogue with affected stakehold-

wave of relocations that may have undermined the policy’s fiscal goals. Similarly, sev-
eral interviewees in both countries described how grant application criteria or stock option
frameworks reflected bureaucratic or legacy logics, rather than startup dynamics.
To address this, participants called for more institutionalised channels of communication.
Suggestions included creating founder advisory boards within relevant ministries, inte-
grating startup representatives into regulatory working groups, and expanding the role
of entrepreneurship focused intermediaries (e.g. incubators, accelerators, and science
parks) as feedback channels. NO-F7 proposed that policies undergo “ecosystem test-
ing,” whereby reforms are piloted or reviewed with input from startup stakeholders before
full implementation.
This aligns with recent policy literature that emphasises co-creation and iterative policy-
making as key to building responsive entrepreneurial ecosystems (Isenberg, 2010). When
entrepreneurs are treated as policy partners, not just recipients, the design of tax, funding,
and regulatory frameworks can become more adaptive, founder friendly, and resilient. In
both Norway and Denmark, building stronger feedback loops between startups and the
state may be a critical step in closing the gap between ambitious political rhetoric and the
day-to-day challenges of entrepreneurship.
5.3
Managerial And Policy Implications
The findings and analysis point to a set of practical implications for those navigating the
Danish and Norwegian startup landscapes. For founders and investors, much of this
relates to how policy shapes everyday decisions around ownership, hiring and long-term
planning.
5.3.1
Implications for Founders and Investors
A clear takeaway is the importance of addressing tax and legal structures early. Several
interviewees noted how decisions around company valuation, employee compensation
and international expansion had to be carefully calibrated to avoid unintended tax conse-
quences. In this landscape, founders must treat legal and financial structuring as a core
part of building the company, not an afterthought.
In Norway, the wealth tax and exit tax were mentioned repeatedly as key reasons for
adopting defensive strategies. These included limiting valuations, splitting ownership or
moving operations abroad. In Denmark, founders were generally more optimistic, but
continued to struggle with the complexity of equity compensation and stock options. While
recent reforms have helped, issues remain.
Some founders suggested alternative approaches, like combining smaller equity stakes
with consultancy contracts, offering flexible work terms or creating stronger non-monetary
incentives tied to purpose and career development. These solutions are not ideal, but re-
flect a growing need to adapt when traditional compensation structures do not function as
intended. Another point that came through clearly was the need for founders to be proac-
tive in engaging with the policy landscape. Several mentioned the importance of joining
52

==PAGE:59==
relevant forums, building relationships with intermediaries and staying ahead of legislative
changes. Reacting too late can lead to missed opportunities, or worse, structural choices
that are difficult to unwind.
For investors, understanding local regulatory context is critical. Several mentioned that
taxes and grant rules shape how founders make decisions, especially when it comes to
scaling or exiting. Investors entering these ecosystems may need to rethink timelines and
exit expectations. Returns might still be there, but the pathway to achieving them often
looks very different than in more founder-friendly jurisdictions.
5.3.2
Implications for Policymakers
From a policy standpoint, the findings point to five clear areas where adjustments could
make a meaningful difference.
Reassess taxation of unrealised gains: Current frameworks treat illiquid startup equity
as if it were realisable capital. This leads to risk-averse behaviour, distorted valuations and
premature relocations. Reforming this would remove a key friction point for early-stage
growth.
Fix the stock option structure: Stock options remain one of the most founder-friendly
ways to attract talent. Both countries could benefit from simplification and alignment with
international best practices. Equity incentives must work in practice, not just on paper.
Clarify and coordinate public funding: Grants are valuable but often feel fragmented.
Founders want more transparency, clearer selection logic and better alignment with pri-
vate capital. Public support should complement, not compete with, other forms of financ-
ing. Early-stage funding should not be too easily accessible; there needs to be a balance
to ensure that startups still encounter a degree of challenge early on in their journey. Fac-
ing some initial struggle is often considered important for building resilience and long-term
success.
Create stronger founder–government dialogue: Founders repeatedly described feel-
ing shut out of the policymaking process. Suggestions included advisory boards, pilot
reviews and deeper collaboration with intermediaries. Policy must be informed by those
who live with its consequences.
Reduce administrative barriers to scaling: Starting up is relatively straightforward.
Scaling is not. Regulatory hurdles around fund formation, international hiring or expan-
sion remain too high. Cutting this red tape would enable more local growth and long-term
reinvestment.
What founders are asking for is not special treatment but structural alignment. The tax
system should recognise the difference between operational capital and personal wealth.
Grant systems should be predictable and built around growth, not maintenance. Dialogue
should be continuous, not reactive. If these conditions are met, both ecosystems stand
to benefit from more ambitious companies, more attractive investment opportunities and
more long-term value creation.

This thesis contributes to the literature on entrepreneurship and public policy in three main
ways. First, it offers a comparative lens on how wealth and capital taxation influence
founder and investor behaviour in small open economies, complementing prior studies
that often focus on larger or more homogenous ecosystems. The study adds empirical
depth to existing critiques of broad-based taxation, such as those raised by Gordon &

5.5
Limitation of study
Apart from the valuable insights generated through this study, it is important to acknowl-
edge several limitations. Firstly, the data was collected through semi-structured inter-
views with 21 founders, investors, and policymakers from Denmark and Norway. Due to
the small sample size, the results are not statistically generalisable to all startups in either
country. Additionally, interviewees were recruited via LinkedIn, email and snowball sam-
pling. All interviewees resided in their country of origin, either Denmark or Norway. As
a result, the study does not include perspectives from founders who may have relocated
due to policy-related challenges. These recruitment methods and the self-selection of
interviewees may introduce selection bias and limit the representativeness of the sample.
During the initial outreach phase, scheduling interviews proved more difficult than antic-
ipated. Startup founders, in particular, often face time constraints due to the dynamic
nature of their work, making participation in the study more challenging. Although par-
ticipants represented a range of sectors, not all industries were covered. This may limit
the scope of the findings, as policy experiences can vary significantly across sectors.
Some startups may benefit considerably from certain policies, while others may find them
irrelevant. This variation was evident in the interviews, where some founders showed
extensive engagement with the policy landscape, whereas others had limited awareness
or involvement. As a result, certain sectors and perspectives may be under-represented.
Furthermore, the study’s findings reflect a specific point in time. Policy outcomes may take
years to materialise, and policy environments are subject to frequent changes. Conse-
quently, the insights presented here may not fully capture the long-term effects or evolving
nature of entrepreneurship policies. Ongoing monitoring and revision of such findings are
therefore necessary to maintain their relevance and accuracy.
The thesis focuses on Norway and Denmark, which occasionally made data collection
challenging due to language barriers. While one of the researchers is Belgian, the other
is Norwegian, which made it easy to collect Norwegian sources. However, collecting and
interpreting Danish material proved more difficult, particularly when key reports, articles,
or websites were only available in Danish. This sometimes limited the researchers ability
to fully assess certain policy documents or public discussions in Denmark without external
assistance.

Firstly, to gain a more comprehensive understanding of the motivations behind startup
founders relocating to other countries, future studies could include participants who have
moved abroad due to policy-related reasons. Their perspectives would help shed light on

Secondly, future research could expand the dataset through the inclusion of large-scale

by capturing responses from a more diverse range of sectors, industries, and startup
stages.
Thirdly, many interviewees in this study referenced Sweden as a country with a strong

could examine the impact of EU-level regulation on national startup policy in countries
such as Denmark and Norway. Furthermore, it would be valuable to explore how more
harmonized European policies could facilitate cross-border growth and improve scalability

Conclusion
This study set out to understand how taxation and policies shape founder and investor
behaviour in Norway and Denmark. What the findings show is that these mechanisms do
much more than influence the margins of entrepreneurship, it actively shapes and struc-
tures it. Decisions about whether to build long-term, scale locally, hire internationally, or
relocate altogether are often shaped by policy choices that, to many lawmakers, appear
unimportant. For those operating within the ecosystem, however, these policies carry
actual and immediate consequences. Across both countries, there is a growing tension
between the political rhetoric and practical reality. Policymakers speak of fostering inno-
vation, making their nations “the best places in the world to start a business”, whereas the
systems they design fail to acknowledge how startups actually function. The taxation of
illiquid assets, the limitations of their stock option schemes, and the absence of targeted
follow-on funding mechanisms are not just design flaws. They shape real decisions, of-
ten prematurely, and steer motivation for entrepreneurship away from what could’ve been
high impact and domestic ventures.
It’s in Norway where this is most evident. While the aim of tax redistribution may be
broader welfare, applying the same framework to early stage entrepreneurial capital leads
to distortions. Founders are being taxed on value that doesn’t exist in their bank accounts,
and investors face exit conditions that contradict the risk-reward logic of venture building.
The result? Defensive structuring, diluted ownership and a growing wave of relocation,
not necessarily because founders want to leave, but they recognize that staying long term
comes at too high of a cost. Denmark in contrast, shows that reforms are possible and
while still imperfect, the 2024-2025 changes helped restore some degree of optimism in
their startup community. They show that policymakers are willing to listen and adjust, and
that it can build trust and increase founder ambition. However, the issues around stock
option taxation and the need for more coherent funding strategies remain both unresolved
and they risk capping their entrepreneurial potential just as the momentum is starting to
pick up.
A recurring theme across the interviews is not that founders are asking for special treat-
ment. What they’re seeking is recognition, that startup equity isn’t like traditional wealth,
that early-stage liquidity is limited, and that international mobility is increasingly the norm
for ambitious founders. When governments fail to take these realities into account, the
unintended consequences are critical. Misaligned policy doesn’t just reduce competitive-
ness, it signals a lack of seriousness about building high-growth companies at home. The
lesson from this research is that ecosystems are built or broken not only by vision, but by
structure. Founders respond to incentives. So do investors. If those incentives tell them
to limit growth, postpone hiring, or incorporate elsewhere, they often will, even if they don’t
want to. And in countries like Denmark and Norway, where competition is harsh, these
structural signals tend to have big impact.
What’s needed isn’t blind deregulation or direct copy of other nation’s successful poli-
cies. It’s a shift in mindset, seeing entrepreneurship as a unique engine of value creation.
That means stepping away from one-size-fits-all frameworks, embedding entrepreneurial
logic into how policy is made, and building lasting channels for real dialogue between
founders and the state. Without that, Norway and Denmark risks continuing to produce
promising startups, only to see them scale, hire, and exit elsewhere. With it, there’s a

real opportunity to design startup environments that are not just resilient, but genuinely
founder-responsive. That is the kind of ambition this research argues for.

Founder Interview Guide
I. Opening & Background
• Can you briefly tell us about your company — what you do, when you started, and
where you’re currently based?
• What motivated you to start your business in [Norway/Denmark]?
• Have you ever considered incorporating your company in another country? If so,
which one(s), and why?
• Have you ever considered relocating operations abroad — either partially or en-
tirely? What would drive such a decision?
• In hindsight, would you choose the same country for incorporation again? Why or
why not?
II. Government Grants and Subsidies
• Have you applied for any public grants, innovation subsidies, or soft funding? What
was that experience like?
• Did the availability (or lack) of grants influence where you chose to incorporate or
operate?
• Have you ever felt that you needed to adjust your business model to fit funding
criteria?
• Do you think these funding schemes would still support you if you moved abroad or
scaled internationally?
III. Taxation
• How would you describe the impact of tax policies (e.g., corporate tax, income tax,
VAT, capital gains) on your decisions as a founder?
• How would you describe the overall tax environment for startups in Denmark/Nor-
way?
• Has the tax structure ever made you consider moving the company or registering
elsewhere?
• Are you aware of Norway’s/Denmark’s exit tax? If so, has it affected your personal
plans or company exit strategy?
• Do tax incentives (e.g., R&D credits, investor tax schemes) influence your decision
to remain in your current country?
• Are there specific tax policies that incentivize or discourage founders from scaling
their startups locally rather than moving abroad?
• How do taxes on unrealized capital gains (e.g., lagerbeskatning in Denmark, wealth/exit
tax in Norway) affect startup founders and employees?
