Abstract
This report is going to investigate the security in satellite communication, with the spe­
cific focus of Geostationary orbit satellites. It will look at the theoretical aspect of satellite
architecture and investigate where the security breaches might be. From that standpoint
the report will do a experimental experience to observe what can be achieved with the
baseline of no to very little experience in satellite communication. Based on this expe­
rience an initial RISK assessment will be conducted, where each finding will be scored
based on the likelihood and the impact. With the RISK assessment, possible solution will
be listed and discussed, if they might be able to close possible vulnerabilities and how
they may be the new way of using satellite communication.

Introduction
Satellites have been around for decades and is more important to communication than
some might know, because it is this piece of hardware that are placed so far out in space,
that it might be forgotten how crucial it is for today’s communication. If someone is lucky,
they might actually see the cleanse of a satellite moving, even though it looks like a
star[2]. Without the possibility to communicate, especially in rural areas, business like
ships, drilling platforms, IoT devices and more would face a hard time, which is why satel­
lites have been a growing part of today’s network architecture, to support and provide
better coverage. With the increase of traffic through satellites, it have been a natural
step to also make internet traffic a part of satellite communication and over the years an
increased demand have raised for connecting a huge amount of IoT devices[3]. By satel­
lites being a part of network architecture, the amount of data coming from satellites, have
increased and with the amount of traffic running through satellites, data might be more
exposed than some might think. Satellites are these complex piece of hardware, that are
flying up in space for many years. This means, that the technology and the functionality, is
of old date. So what does that mean for everyone who are using satellites for communica­
tion. Is the information secured against others who tries to get a hold of these information
travelling through satellites and is it even possible to capture these data. Doesn’t it re­
quire advanced equipment or access to these communications of some short. No matter
what, satellites are up there in space and they are meant to stay there forever, either as
a functional part or as garbage flying around. The bigger question is, what can they tell
us of interesting stuff no one should know about.
The focus of this project is to investigate the Digital Video Broadcast for Satellites with
the baseline of little to no knowledge of how satellite communication operates. What is
the possibility to get information that are classified as sensitive or to use captured informa­
tion for further exploitation by only using simple equipment, that everyone can research
the internet to find. To only use public available software that everyone can download and
use. How tricky is it in real life to listen to satellites and what are they actually revealing
of those who uses it. In this project a experimental tryout is trying to answer some of
these questions, followed by an initial risk assessment. Based on this risk assessment,
research will show if it is possible to close some of these potential threats, that satellite
communication might have.

1.3
VSAT network architecture
VSAT is the short­term for ”Very Small Aperture Terminal” and is used for many different
purposes, such as internet access, video broadcasting, telephony and more[5]. It refer­
rers to as a small­sized earth station which is used for transmitting or receiving data in
satellite communication networks. It consists of two main parts, a transceiver that has to
be placed outside in clear sight towards the satellite and a device that are able to process
the data transmitted from the transceiver to the device[5][6].
These VSAT are able to communicate with satellites that are flying around in space and
can be located at different distances and positions from earth. Depending on the equip­
ment used for connecting to the satellites, satellites are place in different positions[7]:
• Geostationary Orbit (GEO)
• Low Earth Orbit (LEO)
• Medium Earth Orbit (MEO)
• Polar orbit and Sun­synchronous orbit (SSO)
• Transfer orbits and geostationary transfer orbit (GTO)
• Lagrange points (L­points)
In this project the focus is on the GEO satellites, which are circling around earth above
the equator, as shown in figure 1.2.
Figure 1.2: Geostationary Orbit (GEO)[7]

The thing about these satellites is that they are placed where they appears to be station­
ary. however, this is not the case, instead they are travelling around the earth with almost
the exact same rotational speed as the earth. According to The European Space Agency,
GEO satellites should move around earth with 3 km pr second in order to match the speed
of the earth, with an altitude of 35.786 km[7].
As this might indicate, these satellites are placed in GEO because of the need to have
satellites placed in a ”fixed” location so that e.g. telecommunication, maritime communi­
cation and more can use these satellites for communication. This way they can always
have a satellite dish or antenna pointing directly at the ”fixed” location to retrieve or send
data. GEO satellites also have a really large coverage of earth, illustrated in figure 1.3.
Figure 1.3: Footprint from GEO[8]
In fact, three satellites placed in GEO can almost provide coverage to the entire world[7].
Now, this is because of the distance that these satellites are placed. The higher altitude a
satellite have, the larger the area is for the satellite coverage. Theoretical is can never be
more than half of the globe, whereas the LEO can not cover as large an area, because
they are placed closer to earth. Satellites at GEO are also being used to relay information
from and to satellites that are not placed in GEO or stations that are not able to transmit
or receive data. This means that the world will always be connected, because the GEO
satellites can send the information towards the other satellites that needs the information
by beaming the signal to them. These satellites are referred to as Tracking and Data Re­
lay Satellites (TDRS)[9].
The spectrum that the satellite operates in is 1­40 GHz, where it is divided into sub­bands.

called the Ku­band, K­band and the Ka­band[10]. The Ka­band is often pronounced ”K
above band” because it is in the frequency of 26­40 Ghz and it operates above the NATO

1.4
Digital Video Broadcasting for Satellite
Because of the increase of Internet Protocol (IP) traffic and to get better coverage, satel­
lites was included in IP networks to support already existing terrestrial communication net­
works. This really provides some benefits such as broadcast capability, geographic cov­
erage, rapid deployment and support for mobile stations. Because standards for IP trans­
portation over satellites was exclusive to only some providers, the equipment tended to
get expensive. For that reason, European Telecommunication Standards Institute (ETSI)
defined the open standard Digital Video Broadcast (DVB)[15].
DVB enabled the possibility to transmit World Wide Web (WWW) using DVB satellite hub
stations for transmitting data to small satellite dish which is also used for TV distribution.
The standards for DVB was publish through ETSI, where some of the DVB standards are
listed below[15]:
• Digital Video Broadcasting for Satellite (DVB­S).
• Digital Video Broadcasting for Cable (DVB­C).
• Digital Video Broadcasting for Terristrial (DVB­T).
DVB­S was introduced in 1995 and was the standard for satellite television. The devel­
opment of the standard lasted from 1993 to 1997 and in 2005 the second addition, called
Digital Video Broadcasting for Satellites 2 (DVB­S2) was ready to be launch from it’s suc­
cessor of the first generation for broadcasting and DVB Digital Satellite News Gathering
(DSNG) and contribution services[16][17].
DVB­S2 have more options when it comes to encapsulation the format, as there are sev­
eral options for input streams. The most important one to mention is the Generic Streams,
as this come as an standard in DVB­S2. The generic stream format is an addition to the
well known Moving Picture Experts Group (MPEG) Transport Stream (TS) used in DVB­S
and can be used for providing digital TV but also introduces the opportunity to build IP
networks or dedicated data streaming[16][17][18].
The MPEG­TS is used in most digital broadcasting systems and the standards of Ad­
vanced Television Systems Committee (ATSC) family on the baseband data where there
are allocated 188 byte fixed sized TS stream, where the first 4 bytes are intended for the
header, as shown in figure 1.5.
These 188 TS packets was initial intended to bring audio and video streams, but was
eventually used for transporting IP traffic. The header in figure 1.5 have the following
fields[19]:
• The first field in the header is the synchronisation bit that have the pattern 0x47.
• Next is three fields transport_error_indicator, payload_unit_start_indicator, trans­
port_priority that states how the payload should be processed. transport error in­
dicates if there have occurred an error, payload unit start indicates the start of the
payload and the transport priority indicates that this has priority.
• PID identifies the stream that a given packet belongs.
• Transport scrambling control is used for accessing procedures on the encryption of
payload for some TS packets.
• Adaption field control have four states:

Because of the need for supporting data services, DVB suggested the Multi Protocol En­

television (DTV) platforms, it was granting IP­based broadband data and multimedia ser­

also include Unicast and Multicast IP traffic together with audio visual streams. With the
new DVB­S2, this is more likely to exclusively be carrying IP traffic instead of content from
MPEG[16][17][18].
Because of the need for better coverage, all over the world, a research group was merged
to create an improvement of the MPE called the Unidirectional Lightweight Encapsulation
(ULE). ULE should be more native to IPv6/Multi Protocol Label Switching support, offer
better simplicity, be more efficient and better flexibility by optional extension headers[17].

1.4.1
Multiprotocol Encapsulation
The MPE is widely used in IP and MPEG­2 gateways, as well as decapsulators/receivers,
because MPE have been the only option for a long time for IP­to­MPEG­2 encapsulation.
When using MPE each IP packet has an MPE header when arriving to an MPEG encapsu­
lation Gateway, in order to form a network layer packet, which is called the Protocol Data
Unit (PDU). The PDU will afterwards be fragmented into a series of MPEG­2 TS packets,
that often exist of more than one TS packets, because the PDU can vary in size. The TS
header have the one­bit Payload Unit Start Indicator (PUSI) together with a one­byte PTR
for indication the start of a specific TS series packet payload[17].
For the MPE header format, it carries a MAC destination address, but it does not have any
payload type field, which might indicate that it is IPv4 payload, for most software receivers.
In the case it is an IPv6 packet or other similiar packets, a type­field is required in order
to de­multiplex the received packet. In the case of MPE, this would require an inclusion
of the 4­bytes Logical Link Control/Sub­Network Access Point (LLC/SNAP) header.
IP packets are not always aligned to fit into the fixed space of TS packets, which means
that either padding or packing is necessary, if one or more bytes are not used. Both option
are fine to use, but it is important that the encapsulators and the receivers are aligned on
the method to use, where padding is often used as this is the default.
Figure 1.7 shows the basic structure of an MPE Subnetwork Data Unit (SNDU). Look­
ing at the structure it has a multiple fields that also are specific to MPEG, which is not
necessary to include[17] and some headers in the MPE section is not necessary to carry
IP packets and deliver it to the destination[15].
Figure 1.7: MPE structure[17]
The TS is a subnetwork layer and have already divide the traffic into logical channels, but
this is mandatory in MPE to declare the recievers MAC address and thereby this gives an
overhead of 6 bytes. MPE do also carry pure IP payload, without discriminating betweem
IPv4 or IPv6, or carry the data with the LLC/SNAP header, even though there is no uniform
representation of the type of data encapsulated.

1.4.2
Unidirectional Lightweight Encapsulation
As an alternative to MPE, ULE was develop to be much more simple and lightweight for
encapsulation, without the loss of flexibility. The approach of ULE is to do the so called
”data piping”, where it is directly mapping the PDU into the TS payload and only adds a
small header.
Looking at figure 1.8 it can be observed that there two fields in the header, the Length and
the Type field. The Length field is to specify the length of the SNDU and the Type field is
to specify which type of payload it encapsulates. The Type field will specify whether it is
IPv4 datagrams or IPv6 datagrams and so on.
Figure 1.8: ULE structure[17]
Further the ULE also have the opportunity to add a 6­byte destionation address, that is
corresponding to the receivers Network Point of Attachment (NPA). The NPA address field
can sometimes be corresponding to the receivers MAC address and is used to identify the
receiver. This will be mandatory if the receiver is a router, that would have to forward it to
it’s final destination, otherwise this field can be left out, because the IP layer will be enough
to do the filtering. If the case is, that there are additional SNDU­level signaling, that can’t
be carried in the existing headers, there is the option to add an extension header before
the PDU that are able to carrying the extra data. At the end of the structure, a CRC­32
is added to make sure there is proper reception and synchronization. The CRC­32 is an
error­detecting function to validate if there are changes between the source of the sender
and the receiver.
The ULE is constructed with the purpose of being more simple compared to MPE, as ULE
is only using the most sufficient/necessary headers for de­encapsulate/encapsulate and
forward IP datagrams. Because of lightweight headers, the ULE saves both bandwidth
and processing time for de­encapsulate/encapsulation[17].

1.4.3
Generic Stream Encapsulation
Besides the ULE another alternative is the Generic Stream Encapsulation (GSE)[25]. Fig­
ure 1.9 illustrates the encapsulation operation where the PDU, that are ready to be trans­
mitted, are encapsulated in GSE packets, either in one or multiple packets[26][16].
Figure 1.9: GSE encapsulation[26]
Each PDU are encapsulated into a single GSE packet or fragmented and encapsulated
into multiple GSE packets. GSE packets are designed to have a general variable length.
This is due to match the input of IP traffic with minimum overhead[26][16].
These GSE packets will then be packed into fields of BB Frames, where they can also be
sent into different BB Frames. However, it differs from the previous step, as they may not
be sent into consecutive or transmissions with the same parameters, such as modulation
format, coding rate and more. Further, there are no assumed constraints on the position
of the GSE packets in the frames, but the GSE packet can not be re­ordered once they
are between the step of encapsulation and de­encapsulation. The Base Band frames can
have fixed or variable length.
CRC­32 is only added to the last PDU fragmented packet for verification of the reassem­
bly operation and do thereby not include integrity check for each GSE packets. Instead
the GSE relies on the physical layer to do the necessary error detection and correction
probability[26].
If a system is using ACM, the parameters for transmission are typically changed dynami­
cally on frame­by­frame basic. The receiver will only decode the ones where the param­
eters are compatible with the receivers current channel conditions, which means that the
receiver usually only will decode a subset of the transmission. Unlike other encapsulation
method, the GSE do not require the payload_unit_start_indicator to indicator the position­
ing of the GSE packets in the frame, but requires that the base band frame always start
with the header of the first GSE packet[26].

encapsulated into TS cell/cells. If the section layer packet has to be split into multiple
TS cells, it might not fill the entire TS cell and therefore would be leftover space. When
using padding, it will padd the left over space with padding bytes and be considered as
overhead. In figure 1.10 this is shown for MPE, where the only difference between ULE
and MPE, is the overhead number.

the next section layer packet. However, the leftover space should be more than 2 bytes,

DVB­S Satellite Broadband Communication
The topology of satellite networks have different architecture solutions depending on the
need and the possibility that the individual have. However, in every DVB­S architecture
some basic part are present, those are listed below:
1. VSAT satellite dish at the end­user.
2. GEO satellites.
3. Ground station (GS) that receives the request from the satellite and sents the re­
sponse back to the satellite.
4. Terrestrial network topology.
In every scenario, the GEO satellite beams the signal back to earth leaving a large foot­
print. This gives the opportunity for a potential listener to also receive the signal and to
capture the data. Outsider who might listen to such a connection could be various kinds
of organizations or individuals. For this explanation they will be referred to as listener­
s/hackers/attackers and will be marked with red in every scenario presented.
One of the most common topology is when a user do not have the options to use ter­
restrial network, because of rural areas and have to use direct satellite communication,
like when the end­user wants to access the internet or watch TV. The user will use satellite
communication for the request and for the response, illustrated in figure 1.12.

However, with this approach it will enable an outsider of the communication to be able to
listen/receive the transmission, because of the down­link for the GEO satellite to the GS
and from the GEO satellite to the end­user[27][15].
If an end­user wants to create a request for a web page, the user will send a request
towards the satellite. The satellite will then relay the information it receives into a beam

Maritime VSAT network architecture
For maritime VSAT network the end­point user also have a VSAT installed, which is used
to any communication with the Internet Service Provider (ISP) through satellite, as shown
in figure 1.15.
Figure 1.15: Maritime satellite communication[8][28]
Once the end user want to sent any request to the ISP, their VSAT transmit a request to
the satellite and forwards it to a GS that is located on a known place on the ground. The
GS will take the request and create the IP format and transmit it terrestrial to the specific
server. Once the server have the data ready for the request, it will send the response
back through terrestrial to the GS. When the GS get the data it will forward the data back
to the satellite in GEO[28].
The procedure to this point is very similar to the previous mention scenarios and once
the satellite needs to transmit the data back down to earth, it leaves a large footprint after
the beam. This is in order to be more efficient and ensure that, e.g. a vessel will still
receive the response. However, this also enable other end­users to listening to that com­
munication, which is not intended.
In this situation it is not possible to really create a fully eavesdrop attack, because the
signal is much more focused towards the GS. If one would try to complete a fully eaves­
drop attack against this sequence, their hardware setup would need to be very close to
the GS, in order to listen to the incoming beams, unlike the previous explained scenarios
where the attacker could be in another country. However, the footprint beamed back to
earth from the satellite to the end­user is, as told before, large enough for an attack to be
far from the receiver and still be able to listen on the response[28].

1.7
Modulation
For every communication the end­points needs to agree on the modulation that is used
for the signal. This is important for the end­user to read the received signal properly and
because of the distance, that these signals have to travel. Modulation is used when either
some digital or some analog information have to be send wireless and the information sig­
nal, the signal that carries the data, is not able to travel far or quick enough to the receiver.
Therefore a modulation can be used and is when the information signal is added to the
high frequency carrier signal, giving the result of an modulated signal. This signal will
travel from the sender to the receiver, and if the receiver is informed about what kind of
modulation it is supposed to receive, it can turn it back into the original information. When
scanning for different frequencies for communication on the satellites, another aspect is
the different kinds of modulations that are used. There are 4 different modulations that
are frequently used for satellite communication, those are QPSK (Quadrature Phase Shift
Keying), 8PSK (8 Phase Shift Keying), 16APSK (16 Amplitude and phase­shift keying)
and 32APSK.[29]
Figure 1.16: Modulations [29]
When using the QPSK each symbol represents two binary digits, this is symbolised in the
figure 1.16 (a). The Q in QPSK stands for quadrature, which means it is a four phase
shifting and there is four different phases that can be send in this modulation. Figure 1.17
illustrates a simulation of this, where each of the phases is a symbol being send, which
represent 2 bits (00, 01, 10 or 11).[30]
8PSK is like the QPSK, but instead of having 4 phases it have 8 phases. This means that
each symbol being transmitted will cover three bits instead of two bits that are used in
Security in satellite communication

Figure 1.17: Phases [30]
QPSK. Therefore, with every symbol send, there is 50% more data. This can either do so
the data transfer can be faster with the same bandwidth or the bandwidth can be lowered
without losing the transfer speed.[31]
APSK come with 16 and 32 phases, where the big difference from the PSK to APSK,
is that there is both a phase and an amplitude modulation, where there was only phase
modulation in PSK. When amplitude modulation is now a possibility, there is 16 or 32
possible different signals to send, which means that there can be four or five bits pr symbol.
This will again optimize the bandwidth so the data transfer can be even faster or even more
people can make use the limited data transfer over satellites. As seen in figure 1.16 (c
and d) when APSK is used there is phase shifting as in PSK, but now it can have different
amplitudes as well. The distance between these circles is defined from the sender and

to determinate how big a part of the signal is actual data and how big a part is used for

2.5
Signal Strength
When the information is send from the satellite down to the receiver it is important that the
line is clear in sight from GEO satellite to the dish, as a lot of things can disturb the signal.
When it comes to penetrate buildings, trees, water etc, a lower frequency band comes in
handy, as waves are better at passing objects at a lower frequency. Unfortunately it does
not have the ability to transfer data as fast as a higher frequency and therefore it is not
suitable for data transfer in satellite communication, where the bandwidth is imitated. If it
is shared by a lot of people it is preferred to optimize it as much as possible. The fact that
satellite communication is needed to be at a higher frequency is a part of the reason why
it is so important how the satellite dish is configured. It is important to have a clear line in
sight to the satellite, as all obstacles in between can be a reason for not getting a signal
or getting a bad signal with poor quality.[42]
Objects between the GEO satellite and the satellite dish has an impact on the signal
strength and quality as well. With the compass, a satellites location was roughly located
and the elevation level was set to match the specification for the given satellite. The sat­
finder was then used to fine­tune the satellite dish into the exact position, both horizontal
and vertical. The sat­finder would give indications on how great the signal is, from a given
satellite. A test on how objects destroys the signal was done to understand why some of
the satellites was either low in signal or unreachable. The result is that, in some of the
cases, that it was necessary to move the satellite dish as a tree, a bush or a building was
disturbing the signal. It was therefore necessary to move the satellite to avoid trees and
bushes by either lift a bit up, or move it a bit to the side. Due to the location where the test
was performed (Copenhagen), a lot of buildings was in the way of some of the satellites.
Another thing was, that some satellites was so far away, that the elevation was too low to
be possible. It would simple had to point through the earth. Therefore the dish was moved
to another location in the less populated area to get data from some satellites.[43][44]

Weather conditions
Another impact on the signal strength and quality was the weather. Theoretically this
should not have a great impact and should only be from things like heavy rain, strong
wind, space weather etc, but the experience of this was different. One of the reasons for
this can be found in the satellite dish, as the dish size is at the minimum of what is recom­
mended for the size for obtaining data at some of those satellites chosen in this project.
The signal, was on a sunny day a bit weak in strength and quality, but when trying record­
ing on windy days or rainy days, the signal strength and quality dropped even more and
sometimes too much to be able to use this signal. This resulting in not being able to get a
lock on the satellite. Heavy rain and stormy weather was encountered during the project
on the 22nd of September[45] and the 10th of October[46]. The day before both of this
coincidences, the satellite dish was point towards a given satellite that it was capturing
data from. However, when the stormy weather was starting the next day, it was close to
impossible to find the lock again. Even trying to get locks on new possible satellites was
also impossible. For that reason, it was concluded that the weather had something to say.
The weather was also having more influence on the communication with the satellites
far away, then the once directly south. The reason for the distance having an impact is,
that the signal was already a bit poor, so when it got a bit worse, a lock was not possi­
ble. Closer satellites was sometimes so good that it could handle a bit of noise from the
weather.

3.1
Preparation for the assessment
The first step of the risk assessment is to define the purpose, scope, assumptions and
constrains, information to be used and the process for identifying vulnerabilities. This

3.1.1
Purpose
The purpose of this risk assessment is to give information on potential threats and risks
on the three presented VSAT architectures in section 1.3. The risk assessment is an
initial assessment intended to analyse the full VSAT architecture and the protocols used
for communication, where the purpose is to answer the listed questions:
1. How is the use of VSAT communication affected to malicious activity, like hackers,
scientist, script kiddies, etc. trying to get sensitive data from the communication.
2. How is the use of VSAT communication changing the risk for individuals day to day
communication or companies business strategy if this is employed as the standard
architecture for communication ?
3. How can the initial risk assessment be used to create a baseline for future VSAT
architecture designs and how to guide/inform of existing security problems.
4. How can potential threats against VSAT communication be minimized based on

5.1
Low Earth Orbit
LEO satellites are located between 160 to 2,000 kilometers above the earth and make
LEO satellites the shortest distance from earth compared to any other satellites in orbit.
The LEO satellite is not at the same place above earth but is traveling around the earth
and is not required to travel along the equator like the GEO satellites, but can travel at any
direction around the globe. For this reason, these satellites are really well for integrated
satellite­terrestrial communication networking. LEO satellites move with the speed of 7.8
km pr. second, which will take them 90 minutes to circle around the Earth. With this speed
they travel around the earth about 16 times a day[7].
Figure 5.1: Satellites in LEO[7]
This has some significant pros and cons for the communication as the time it takes for
the signal to reach the satellite and getting back to earth, is a lot less than GEO satellites.
However, the GS do also have a lot harder to track these satellites in LEO, due to the
speed, as they requires much more effort. They are often used for larger constellation
with multiple satellites, in order to support, e.g. telecommunication for the aim of giving

The constellation of LEO satellites differs in two ways, single layer and multiple layer net­

5.2.1
Encryption
Encryption of data is very important part of keeping the data protected against attacks
like eavesdropping. A lot of the information captured in the experiment and in similar
projects, got information from communications where their were no encryption applied or
at least very weak encoding. A huge part of communication today is to ensure that no
one else is listening or modifying on the messages being sent, for securing the internet
traffic from outsiders. These are the two key elements in the CIA triad, integration and
confidentiality. There are multiple algorithms used for encryption, some are more secure
than other, but they can be divided into two groups, synchronous and asynchronous en­
cryption. Synchronous encryption covers the algorithms where the same secret key is
used for decryption/encryption and asynchronous encryption have a private/public key
pair, where the private key is what is used to de­crypt the messages sent to the receiver,
and the public key is what the sender use to encrypt the message.[160]
In online communication there is always third parties that are going to be a part of the
communication, like the internet service provider (ISP). With the use of end­to­end en­
cryption, it will store the encryption keys at the end point and encrypt every messages
before it leaves the end point. This will ensure that any intermediaries cannot read the
messages. They might be able to eavesdrop the data that are being transferred, but if
modern encryption algorithms are applied, it will take too long to de­crypt the data for it
to be useful[161][162][163]. This is what Starlink is claiming to apply, though the lack of
public available information.
5.2.1.1
Security by Obscurity
This is a interesting point of view, that the information about the encryption algorithm is
very poor. It do even apply a little bit of security by obscurity (STO) which have proven not
to be a good way of applying security. STO is this controversial principle in security that
is based on the premise that secrecy of some function in the system can be applied as
security[164][165]. Examples of this concept could be by hiding the key to the front door
under a rock or the door mat[165]. Another example could be by hiding classic known
ports to services by using different ports, without additional security to it.
In encryption it is commonly known, that algorithm are public to everyone who might have
an interest in testing. Companies often employ rewards if someone is able to find security
flaws in their algorithms. This is a way to improve the algorithm and fix unknown flaws/vul­
nerabilities that the developers did not think of. This is because you should never rely on
the secrecy of the algorithm but rather on the strength of the keys. However, STO can be
good in combination with strong security, but should never rely only on STO[164][165].

the satellite and back down to earth. However, this might not be the real problem.
During this project a lot of internet traffic was encounter where a huge amount of the traf­
fic was using protocols that do not use encryption. This is of course a great indications,
that all the communication through satellite are not updated and that, the communication
through satellites are still used quite a lot. This might lead to the question if companies or
humans are aware of the amount of internet traffic that exist over satellite communication.
Sometimes it can be the lack of awareness that are the real problem and that it needs to
be put under the light. This is something that have proven to be effective in other scenar­
ios, not particularly to satellite communication, but in general. If something is brought to
the light, the awareness of the problem will increase.
This specific problem is facing an increase awareness as companies are getting more
aware of the problem. The increased awareness, can help people to understand that
there is a problem and to look for new solutions to their problems. It might also be, that
many companies are aware that a lot of their communication do not use encryption and
have the opinion that it will be to expensive to add or the fact, that they do not transmit
anything that needs encryption.
Another aspect to the subject is, that it might not be straight forward to perform this kind of
attack. Because the end­user are so far from each other, an attacker would need to have
a strategy setup, if someone wants to perform a man­in­the­middle attack successful. An
attacker might need two satellite dishes to perform the attack, one that would be close to
a GS and another that would listen to the beam attended to the end­user. Even though
this setup might make the attacker able to perform a given attack, it is also important to
know, that everything that is communicating with GEO satellites will take some time to
travel. So if an attack would perform an attack that needs multiple steps to be successful,
this can indeed be tricky. A lot of things can happen during the request or waiting for the
response. The signal could get lost or the information that the attacker is using to perform
the attack might get invalid during the attack.
The most difficult part isn’t to do the setup and start listening to the satellites. This is
quite straight forward, but of course sometimes it can be tricky. The thing that might be
real hard, is to find the information about the satellites. Information that could lead to un­
derstand which satellites that are interesting to listen to. This isn’t really public information
or at least, this isn’t something that someone can just find by a simple google search. It is
quite simple to find the frequencies for satellites that are used to broadcast satellite tele­

However, it seems not to be public which satellites are distributing internet communica­
tion and used by companies for internet connection. Therefor, a deep search might not be
enough to find the target satellite that an attacker would wanted to get information from.

Conclusion
The experiment showed how straight forward it is to capture some kind of data from satel­
lites in GEO. It also showed how, that to find the interesting satellites with internet and the
frequency to get a lock, is a bit more challenging. These information was not easy to find
as it did take time researching the internet for information. However, after enumerating
the needed information and running multiple tests, it showed that with a minimal cost of
equipment, it was possible to capture data transmitted by satellites. Within the captured
data, sensitive information was discovered and information that should not be intended
for others to read. Information that could also be used for crafting other attacks against
specific targets.
This shows that there are flaws in the principle of satellite communication and that there
was unencrypted data transmitted which could be obtained by hardware accessible for
everyone. One of the reasons for this problem, was located at the functionality at GEO
satellites. Whenever these satellites was sending the received signal back to earth, they
created a large footprint after the beam. Because of this beam, it was possible for some­
one in another country, to also being able to receive the signal. If the communication was
using insecure protocols or carrying information that was not protected, this would give an
possible attacker the opportunity to steal the information. Information that could be used
for attacks in the future or already sensitive data, that should not be public for anyone
else, than the intended receiver and sender.
A possible solution could be to move satellite communication from the GEO satellites to
the LEO satellites. The LEO satellites has, due to being much closer to earth, way lower
latency and some of companies are already putting a lot of effort in applying LEO satellites
to space, which will eventually give more bandwidth. LEO satellites is more suitable for
implementing encryption and some providers even has it as standard for the use of their
communication. With latency as low as promised it will even give the opportunity to add
end­to­end encryption like a VPN. With this in mind, LEO satellites will cover the problem
where encryption could be missing duo several aspect, as latency and bandwidth.This
could improve the security and would remove a lot of the threats associated with sniffing.
At the same time it would provide a faster connection to the world, which is beneficial.
