Abstract
Multi-material laser powder bed fusion enable functionally graded parts with unique appli-
cations in demanding use cases such as the aerospace- or medical industry, or nuclear fusion
reactors. The commercial AM-industry is mainly focused on single-material capabilities. In-
suﬃcient work is done to open up the technology, enabling researchers to study the process.
This project has undertaken designing, testing and implementing a powder handling system
capable of dispensing and mixing multiple materials for use in an open-source laser-based
powder bed fusion platform. The powder handling system include an integrated hopper,
powder dispensing and a mixing-subsystem. Preliminary work is done to transfer the gained
knowledge of the dispenser to a non-contact recoater for spreading the powder across the
powder bed. The vibratory powder dispenser system has three main control parameters:
Dosing time, dosing angle and vibration frequency. The system was characterised by weigh-
ing the dispensed powder and by measuring frequency response with an accelerometer. A
powder-speciﬁc calibration methodology was proposed, in which a factorial experimental de-
sign produce a multi-parametric regression model. The model allowed control of the dosed
powder through dosing time and vibration tray angle. The dispenser system was shown to
be capable of doses between 0.1g to 2.5g with relative standard deviations between 1.76-
12.14%. The precision was tested at a dose of ¯m = 0.902 ± 0.0088g (α = 5%, n=54). The
parametric model was evaluated in a hypothetical multi-material scenario and resulted in a
mean error relative to the target of ¯e = −0.0022g. A characterisation method was designed
for the mixer sub-system but remains inconclusive. The non-contact recoater concept needs
for a deeper understanding - and modelling - of the powder ﬂow. The dispenser system is a
ﬂexible solution capable of handling small amounts of feedstock in a multi-material context.

1
Introduction
The purpose of this project is to develop, manufacture, implement, test and characterise a
powder handling system capable of multi-material printing, used in a laser-based powder bed
fusion 3d-printer. The laser powder bed fusion (L-PBF) machine is an open-source printer.
The focus is on achieving a functioning powder handling system within the time frame of the
project. The goal is to get the in-progress L-PBF printer running, thus building knowledge
and treading the ﬁrst steps towards an industry-ready open-software, open-hardware L-PBF
system. Computational methods of the physical system or the feedstock particle-system are
done in this project, although it could provide crucial information towards optimisation of
the systems.
Figure 1: Aspects of a powder handling system for L-PBF which is dealt with in
this project
Powder mixing and multi-material powder dispensing is the main focus of this work, with
the dispensing as the ﬁrst priority. The powder recoater was dealt with preliminarily and
was derived from working with the dispenser system.
Multi-material capabilities could open up to a whole new category of additive manu-
factured components, while control over the hardware may enable mixing unconventional
powders, e.g. constructing alloys in-situ from their constituents.
1.1
Additive manufacturing
Additive manufacturing (AM) or 3D-printing - used interchangeably - covers an ever growing
range of processes where a digital model are materialised into a 3-dimensional physical object
Page 1
==PAGE:21==
1 Introduction
in an additive manner, in contrast to the traditional subtractive processes. The possible
materials include; polymers (thermoplastics and thermosets), ceramics, composite materials
(including concrete) and metals. The technology dates back to the early 1980’s where Hideo
Kodama made a proof of concept printer using photopolymers and Charles Hull invented
stereolithography (SLA). AM began to see actual use as a prototyping tool in the late 1980’s
[1].
Metal AM is a more recent addition to the AM-palette, but have been maturing in
the past few decades, and are now seeing actual industrial use. Currently, the metal AM
segment is focusing on developing practical end-use applications. Typical examples of metal
AM applications include tooling for injection moulding with advanced conformal cooling,
personalised medical and dental implants, and aerospace components [2]. The ability to
produce complex parts with design ﬂexibility, tailoring material properties and potential
multi-material capabilities make metal AM an interesting area from the point of view of
many ﬁelds.
The feedstock within metal AM is either powder, wire or sheet stock. The feedstock is
a common way of dividing the various processes, shown in Figure 2. In the wire processes,
the wire is fed not unlike a MIG/MAG welder and melted using various heat sources (laser,
electron beam or electrical arc). The most common metal AM methods are the powder
methods, where the granulate material is fused directly in powder bed fusion (PBF) and
directed energy deposition (DED) creating (near) fully dense parts using high power energy
sources. The heat source is often a laser in an inert atmosphere, or sometimes an electron
beam, the latter requiring a vacuum. Alternatively, binder methods suspend the powders in
an adhesive material which require post sintering processes to achieve fully dense parts. The
binder jetting and powder bed fusion processes spread the powder onto a build platform,
while DED spray the powder onto the substrate [3, 4].
AM are still fast-moving and little are commonplace and well deﬁned due to the youth
of many of the practises. In an eﬀort to halt the growing number of acronyms and avoid
confusion, the ISO/ASTM 52900:2015(E)I standard aim to deﬁne terminology which the
following text will adhere to as much as possible.
The following work will concern itself with the powder bed technology where the powder
is fused using a laser, hence; laser powder bed fusion also called selective laser melting (SLM)
as seen in Figure 2.
State of the industry
Common for industrial 3D-printers are a limited set of adjustable
print parameters and proprietary hardware which inhibits replacements of even simple parts
or to perform simple adjustments. The ’closed material business model’ where the user are
required to use proprietary materials are mainly seen within the polymer 3d-printers con-
trolled e.g. through chips in the canisters. The metal powder AM segment typically allow the
user to source their own feedstock [2]. However, the original equipment manufacturer (OEM)
often sell and supply powders which are tuned to their machine. The powder characteris-

1.2
The laser powder bed fusion process
The L-PBF process is a multi-physics process with complex interactions across several length-
scales.
A common machine design can be seen in Figure 3.
A piston move the powder
reservoir a deﬁned distance upward and the build platform move a relative distance down. A
powder spreader - usually referred to as a recoater - transport and spread the powder from
the reservoir to, and across the build platform. The recoater can take the form of a roller,
seen below, or a blade - either rigid or ﬂexible. The recoater deﬁnes the layer height and
move excess powder to an overﬂow bin.
Figure 3: The L-PBF process visualised at the macro-, meso- and micro-scale. All
three scales have a signiﬁcant impact on the resulting part. [5]
The heat source with power P are controlled using mirrors and lenses and scan the powder
bed selectively to fuse the particles with a scanning speed v. These process parameters along
with the hatch spacing h (distance between melt tracks) and layer thickness t make up the
energy density Eρ, expressed in Equation (1) [6]. The energy density is used to compare the
main process parameters above to the ﬁnal part densiﬁcation, porosities, surface roughness
etc.
Eρ = P
vht
(1)
The incident laser beam is commonly assumed to have a Gaussian spacial energy distri-
bution with 2σ taken as the beam diameter. According to [5] the laser power operating space
is typically P ≈50 −1000W with a beam velocity of V ≈0.1 −3m/s and a wavelength of
w ≈1µm. The beam can penetrate up to the layer thickness - typically 20−100µm through
the open-pore powder bed which make the absorptivity higher than normal ﬂat surfaces.
Which follows, that the laser heat source must be regarded as volumetric, with the energy
input distributed within the powder layer [5].
The layer thickness t is the relevant process parameter in the case of the following work,
Page 4
==PAGE:24==

as it interacts with the powder granulometry or powder size distribution (PSD), which will
be unpacked shortly in Section 2. The layer thickness is also what ultimately deﬁnes what
amount of powder must be dispensed.
The fused part have a complex thermal history due to the rapid heat cycling induced with
each new layer, with cooling rates up to 106K/s. The presolidiﬁed layer is heated rapidly
as the fresh layer is melted, and wishes to expand. The cooler and more rigid parts of the
part constricts this expansion and this temperature gradient mechanism (TGM) may build
up compressive stresses within the part. The melted layer cools and solidiﬁes and in doing
so contracts - again counteracted by the already solid layer underneath resulting in tensile
residual stresses at or near the surface [3].
The travelling melt pool experience Marangoni convection where the hotter molten ma-
terial in the centre is driven outward towards the cooler regions driven by surface tension and
capillary forces. The liquid then gather suﬃcient surface energy to ﬂow back and complete
the convective loop. This action helps to consolidate the presolidiﬁed and the new layer [5].
Tuning the process parameters and using appropriate feedstock and atmosphere are crucial
to forming continuous melt tracks minimising eﬀects like balling, gas bubbles, vaporisation of
the metal itself and oxide layer formation - either due to an active atmosphere or inclusions
from within the feedstock itself [3].
The microstructure can be to controlled through the process parameters.
Although
small grains are common for L-PBF (compared to e.g. casting) resulting in higher material
strength in the immediate vicinity of the laser beam (the heat aﬀected zone (HAZ)) due to
the extreme cooling rates. The grain size are manipulated through the energy density Eρ
where faster scan speeds are shown to produce smaller grains. Meta-stable phases are often
formed in L-PBF meaning post-processing is typically required to bring back ductility and
fracture toughness [3]. Areas deeper within the part experience a repeated heat-cycling of
decreasing amplitude as the distance to the incident laser beam increase [5]. This eﬀect have
been shown to - under the right circumstances - be able to signiﬁcantly decompose the α′
martensite in Ti-6Al-4V to a lamellar (α + β) structure with tuneable length scales [7].

2.1
Powder for laser powder bed fusion
The basis of these three functions of a powder handling system is: The powder feedstock. An
understanding of the powder and its key characteristics are crucial to understanding how the
powder handling system ﬁt into the process chain of the part. The powder handling system
must function with powders that are optimised with regards to the ﬁnal part properties and
not necessarily dispensing etc. This aspect of the process is presented ﬁrst in the literature
review. How the powder is characterised is introduced, and what qualities aﬀect the powder
handling and the ﬁnished part. Properties like the particle shape, particle size distribution
(PSD) and particle surface along with the packing ability of the powder is of particular
relevance because of their eﬀect on the powder rheology.
2.1.1
Particle shape
The particle shape or morphology aﬀect the apparent density of the powder along with the
ﬂowability, where smooth and spherical particles are preferred due too the increasing packing
ability [3]. Figure 6 illustrate this trend. Powders for metal AM are typically manufactured
using gas atomising (GA) or water atomising (WA) because of the ability to produce small
and round particles. GA generally produce more spherical particles due to slower quenching
rates and lower heat capacity. Reactive materials such as titanium-alloys are often made
with various plasma processes, e.g. plasma atomisation (PA) [3]. Using smooth and round
particles helps with increasing the density of the powder layer by avoiding clumping of the
powder and helping the powder layer mechanism by ﬂowing better [8].
Figure 6: How powder morphology aﬀects apparent density. [9]

2.1.2
Powder granulometry
The particle sizes of the powder is often described through the PSD. The range of sizes can
be mono-modal, bi-modal or sometime tri-model, i.e. a distribution with one or more peaks
as seen in Figure 7(a). The powder stock is often described by three particle diameters: D10,
D50 and D90. D10 indicates 10% of the powder smaller than the stated value, D50 is the
median size i.e. 50% of the stock is smaller and D90 indicate 90% of the stock is smaller
than the given diameter.
The multi-modal distributions are reported to increase packing of the powder bed by
ﬁlling voids [8] as seen in Figure 7(b). A similar eﬀect can be achieved by combining coarse
and ﬁne particles with narrow PSD’s. Besides forming a dense powder bed, which in itself
is beneﬁcial, the increased density have been reported to increase the laser absorption by
having more surface area presentable to the incident beam - especially so for smaller particle
sizes. The higher density also increase the thermal conductivity of the powder bed leading
to a wider melt-pool, thus increasing the melt-path overlap [3].
(a)
Figure 7: (a), various sketched PSD’s seen in metal AM. Bottom, packing arrangement of
diﬀerent modes of PSD. [3]
Page 9

Small particle sizes are shown to impact the surface quality, layer quality, part density
and mechanical properties in a positive way, however, decreasing the size has a detrimental
eﬀect on the rheology as the Van der Waals forces now dominate as much as 80-90% instead
of the otherwise dominant gravitational force at larger length scales [8]. The smaller par-
ticles tend to agglomerate - sometimes decreasing the packing factor and powder handling
capabilities, in turn leading to poor part density [8].
A mean particle size smaller than
30µm tend to agglomerate easily while a narrow PSD have better rheological properties. It
is generally believed that a wide PSD with adequately many small particles will lead to a
high density. But rather disconcertingly, it has been shown that a narrow PSD resulted in
better mechanical properties [3]. The PSD of a powder is not a static property and change
throughout the various process stages and a diﬀerence is seen between virgin and recycled
powders with more powder agglomeration as the powder gets used. Particle size segregation
can also be observed throughout powder handling in general [10]. Mixing fresh and used
powders can negate some of these feedstock ﬂuctuations. While not a focus of this project,
evaluation the PSD of the dispensed powder is of great interest due to the aforementioned
importance of a proper PSD.
2.1.3
Powder rheology
The powder for L-PBF must be well-ﬂowing as the powder should form a dense and homo-
geneous layer prior to solidiﬁcation. The rheology is of course of particular relevance to a
powder handling system. While it may be a product of properties selected to result in a
satisfactory part, it is these ﬂow properties the system has to work from. Powder suitable
for AM is generally considered very free-ﬂowing.
The ﬂow through a funnel or oriﬁce is typically taken has an expression of the ﬂuidity
of powder [11], such as described in ISO 4490-2018II which deﬁnes a 60 degree funnel from

The huge surface area derived from the µm particle sizes - result in the electrostatic

inﬂuence the state of the powder greatly [10]. All three aﬀect the feedstock in terms of
how well the particles ﬂow, to which degree they conglomerate etc., and they are properties
which can be diﬃcult to control. This has raised criticisms of how reproducible and truly
descriptive the Hall ﬂowmeter device is, nevertheless it is still in much use [11].

Another old yet simple way of characterising the ﬂow is ﬁnding the Hausner ratio:

Screw feeder
A screw feeder is somewhat related to the volumetric dispenser introduced above, however
the dosing is not dependant upon a predeﬁned volume as the dispensed amount is a function
of screw rotations which can be controlled ﬁnely [23]. However the accuracy tend to decrease

Vibrations are known to aﬀect powders in various ways - segregation are possible just as
the possibility of increased cohesion of small particles have been reported although not
consistently so [12]. In relation to vibratory dosing the ability to manipulate the frictional
forces by introducing vibrations are widely known. When vibrating in the plane of the sliding
direction, the friction force will have an accelerating eﬀect for a short duration, thus lowering
the time-averaged friction force, ¯µ. This is sometimes illustrated with the inclined plane -
Figure 17. A similar eﬀect is seen when vibration perpendicular to the sliding direction, even
when the particle remains in contact with the surface. The oscillating frictional force cause
the particles to slide at a lower angle than otherwise [37].

2.6
A word of (metal powder) safety
Keeping the metal powder protected from the environment like oxygen or sparks in particular
if the powder is reactive like aluminium, titanium and magnesium [50]. One of the prime
tasks of the metal container or hopper is to disrupt the so-called ﬁre triangle and explosion
pentagon in Appendix A2.2 - minimising/eliminating static build-up through grounding, dust
cloud formation and oxygen supply are prime examples. The ignition energy gets smaller
with decreasing particle size [51], very reactive metals like magnesium burn in pure N2 and
CO2, and aluminium and magnesium powders are water reactive; producing steam and H2
[50]. Reactive metals like the aforementioned are not considered directly when designing the
system, although the safety considerations are of pressing importance, nevertheless.
Shielding humans from any metal powder - even the non-reactive - is important due to
short term and long term eﬀects, outlined in [51]:
• ”Skin contact, which may lead to some form of irritation or allergic reaction (e.g.
dermatitis). In the case of nano-powders, the particles can penetrate the skin and
become absorbed into cells in various parts of the body, including the brain.”
• ”Eye contact, resulting in a mechanical irritation and cause damage.”
• ”Inhalation and either ingestion by swallowing or entering the lungs, resulting in pos-
sible breathing problems.”
Luckily the particles are not nano-scale, however powder build-up in the lungs are in
general not recommended.
All experiments are carried out in fume hoods and handling
outside the fume hoods are done wearing appropriate personal protective equipment like
respiratory equipment with properly rated ﬁlter.
Frequent cleaning of free powder is recommended to avoid unwanted combustion with a
conductive scoop and natural ﬁbre broom in the absence of a appropriate vacuum system.
”Cleaning” equipment with compressed air is unwise due to the concerns stated above [50].
2.7
In conclusion
Regarding powder dispensing in a PBF-context, it seems volumetric designs with a few niche
examples of screw and pneumatic options are used. Regarding 3D/freeform multi-material
PBF, the ultrasonic vibratory capillary designs are more prevalent. In-situ mixing is not
documented and the recoater mechanism seem to be either over-looked or behind patent-
cladded doors.

Another actuator were tried; a DC vibration motor used in e.g. smartphones. The frequency

An oﬀ-centre mass is spun and thus vibrates the motor and deliver a sinusoidal force to
the system. The forces is a result of the unbalanced mass mu, its distance to the centre of
rotation e and its angular velocity ω. The equation of motion of the system can be written
as [53]:
m¨x + c ˙x + kx = muω2e sin ωt
(9)
where m is the mass of the system, x is the displacement, c is the dampening coeﬃcient,
k is the stiﬀness assuming the vibrating system can be modelled as suspended from a spring
and viscous dampener. ¨x and ˙x are the acceleration and velocity, respectively.
Neither the rotational speed nor the eccentric mass was measured due to the heuristic
approach of the design process. The motor was rated to 11.000RPM at 5V, and used as a
black box with the motor voltage as input and vibrational energy as output. The voltage
was set via a serial connection to the Arduino. The voltage was controlled by pulse width
modulation (PWM) using a transistor. The Arduino PWM works on a scale of 0 to 255,
where 255 represent a 100% duty cycle i.e. constant 5V in this case. The motor is rated at
2-5V, where a PWM value of 30 - equating to 11% duty cycle - was required for the motor
to spin. 102 equates to 2V and it was observed to be the setting at which the motor had
energy of any signiﬁcance. Schematic of setup can be found in the Appendix, Figure A.8.

• Simple design from which to learn more about the powder state throughout the process.

After receiving the manufactured components, the systems were tested. The systems were

A series of basic characterisational experiments were undertaken. For the vibratory dis-

7.1.4
Fast fourier transform
The acceleration response was analysed in Matlab, using the built-in fast fourier transform
(FFT) algorithm. The discrete Fourier transform was computed, thus converting the time
domain of the acceleration response to the frequency domain. The peaks of the frequency
spectrum were identiﬁed manually.
The accelerometer setup used were only able to measure up to an acceleration of 5G
(49m/s2). Therefore, the results of motor voltage of 4V and above were saturated/overloaded,
rendering the actual acceleration response meaningless - i.e. the amplitude of acceleration.
However, the FFT results does carry meaning. While the peaks and valleys of the waves are
’clipped’, the wave period remain the same. The resulting frequency spectrum is therefore
true for the most part. The numerical value of the magnitude of the peaks become unreliable,
but they were on par with the lower voltages and not of particular relevance. Multiples of
the fundamental frequencies can appear due to the square nature of the ’clipped’ wave, but
this was not observed.
The sampling rate were 1kHz, which deﬁned the possible frequency spectrum. Frequen-
cies over 500Hz were not possible to detect, hence the Nyquist rate, where to measure a
given frequency requires at least double the sampling rate.
7.1.5
The factorial terminology
In the factorial experimental designs, two or more factors are studied - i.e. angle and dosing
time etc. The levels of the factors are changed simultaneously in an ordered manner such
as each experimental sequence (replication) are comprised of all the possible combinations.
The eﬀect of a factor (dosing time) is deﬁned to be the change in response (dosed powder)
produced by a change in the level (length of dosing time) of the factor (dosing time). This
is called the main eﬀect. The response in this case is the amount of dispensed powder in
weight. In a two-factor design (say angle and dosing time) with both factors having two
levels (3 degrees and 5 degrees, and 2s and 20s, respectively), the main eﬀect A (e.g. angle)
can be thought of as the diﬀerence between the average response at the high and low level
[57]. Using ﬁctitious dosing values:

7.3.2
Energy-dispersive x-ray spectroscopy
A new approach was needed. Many SEM’s are capable of EDS where the primary electron
beam excite emission of characteristic x-rays, and each element present can be detected
(elements heavier than Na, Z=11). Because the electron beam can be focused and moved,
an elemental analysis can be made in microscopic areas or scanned across a surface, thus
creating an elemental map [58].
Quantitative analysis is not advised when analysing powder because of the more complex
electron scattering, but the qualitative results should be reliable [58]. Choosing two alloys
with suﬃciently diﬀering chemical compositions should allow the EDS to diﬀerentiate the
two powders. The elemental map would be the key through which the correct alloy could be
assigned to each particles and make a more rigid analysis of the mixing state backed by a
chemical analysis. Each particle would be isolated using image segmentation (e.g. k-means)
and the statistical mixing analysis used previously could characterise the state of mixture.

Results and discussion
In the eﬀort to develop a powder handling system for multi-material dosing and a mixer to
combine these powders, the systems have so far been designed and the characteristics of the
dispenser has been explored. The stable process parameters have been established in the
previous section regarding the preliminary dispenser experiments, and now the accuracy and
predictability of the system can be investigated. It was found:
• 3V are the most stable motor voltage setting - used as a proxy for the frequency. The
main frequency response was measured to be 270Hz.
• The dosed amount should be independent of the amount of powder in the canister, as
long as there is a signiﬁcant amount of powder in the canister. The dosed amount is
expected to increase with decreasing amount of powder in the canister. The diﬀerence
between 679g and 1380g was found to be on average 0.0079g.
• The ﬂow is most stable when the powder is constrained in width but mostly height.
The powder is dosed through an oriﬁce 1.5mm high and 20mm wide. The constraint
goes all the way back to the funnel - 40mm deep.
• The RSD is expected to be within 1-6%.
The following results and discussion section will explore the stability of the dosing of
a single amount and how it relates to supplying a suﬃcient amount of powder for a given
layer. Next, a method is developed to calibrate the system to a speciﬁc powder - and speciﬁc
condition of the system. From the previous data is it expected that a calibration is needed
whenever major changes are made to the system, e.g. installed in the L-PBF machine. The
shown method is a heuristic approach - a black box method - where the purpose is not to
ﬁnd the optimal solution, but a solution that is precise enough while simultaneously being
time eﬃcient. This will enable experimentation with several powder types. Integrating a
capacative scale will speed up this process even further.
The analysed data also suggested certain settings could decrease the diﬀerences in dosing
behaviour between powders, perhaps negating a major calibration step.
After which the mixer is explored and a method for analysing the mixing state is designed
and investigated.

Discussion
Not looking at aggregate values but single doses in relation to a set program
showed promise of the system and the wanted ﬂexibility of a continuous vibratory system
compared to e.g. a ﬁxed-volume volumetric solution. Knowing how the powder density,
powder rheology etc inter-plays with the dispenser, allows setting the system properly for a
given amount, and controlling the dosed powder in a continuous manner through the applied
vibrational energy - controlled trough the voltage.
To push the obtained model, a composition proﬁle was initially made to see if the pre-
diction model would work outside the factor levels - i.e. model parameters. The angle was
set to 3 degrees, which meant the dose time rose to over 20s at the end of the ’print’. While
the error was the same under 20s, the model overdosed immensely when going above 20s.
See Appendix A6.5 for results.
Perhaps the longer dose times introduce non-linearity in the form of an increase in inertia
of the powder. A similar behaviour was seen in literature, where the dose rate was monitored
through a real-time capacative sensor, used for PID-control. They found systematic over-
dosing when dispensing small amount because the stopping-phase of the powder constituted
most of the dosing operation [25]. Although, the model looks to behave as expected up until
the steady increase in dose time halt and ﬂattens out to a constant dose time. The problem
could lie in the powder reservoir in the dosing tray, where a new powder state has to be
formed. Something the model did not contain.
In conclusion
The prediction model obtained through the factorial design were able to
dose according to a set proﬁle, taking dose time and angle into account allowing these two

to be adjusted at will. This conﬁrms the method of calibration as a solution to obtaining
the relationship between control variables and dispensed powder.
However, seemingly only inside the tested parameters, highlighting the factor levels
should be expanded to longer dose times. The dosing tray may need to constrain the powder
ﬂow to a higher degree - or in another manner - than with the used ﬂow restriction inserts.
Simply making the powder travel path shorter by making the tray smaller in length may

A proposed workﬂow for calibrating a completely new powder, with relatively unknown
ﬂow characteristics; is to do a simple 22 factorial design to ﬁnd the parameter space with few
replication. As there is no evidence of curvature within the model, the intermediate levels
should be superﬂuous. Various oriﬁces could be included as categorical factors. Then run
the actual dosing calibration using a one-variable linear model with an angle that accomplish
the target dose - supplemented by additional experiments to enhance the model. I.e. setting
dosing time to 2s and 20s, and angle to 3 and 6 degrees at 2 or 3 replications. Choose the
angle that accomplish the target mass and dose at various dosing time in between to build a
well-deﬁned linear model. This results in an initial 8-12 runs and subsequent runs as needed
at the wanted angle. If a large range of doses is expected, building the full factorial regression
model may make sense, as done above.

10 Conclusion
10
Conclusion
In summary, the purpose of the project was to design, manufacture, test and implement a
powder dispenser and powder mixer for use in the L-PBF experimental platform in devel-
opment at the AM-group at DTU. The lessons learned during the process along with the
capabilities of the powder handling system is presented.
10.1
The powder dispenser
Design
A vibrational powder dispenser system was designed, and the desired functionality
was achieved. The vibration tray was chosen to be simple in its design to allow for other vi-
brational actuators and enable experimentation with the outlet geometry. An actuator which
worked satisfactory was found, and suggestions were made to a new vibration tray design;
The tray should constraint the powder from all sides, and the area of ﬂow should be variable.
The intended modularity was also achieved to a certain extend. The mounting plate
mean the dispensers themselves can be switched around and removed without tools. The
canister was designed in such a way to expand or decrease the canister volume through
changing the threaded polycarbonate tube. The screws secured in the vibration dampeners
however had to be secured with thread glue meaning the tray are themselves glued in place
to each canister sub-system. The canister itself however unscrews and can be taken apart for
cleaning and access to the vibration tray for e.g. cleaning. The ability to adjust the angle
of the tray meant a further possibility of adjusting according to the powder used.
The side-by-side approach of the dispenser systems allows dosing of up to three materi-
als during a print - separately or simultaneously. The round design choice is not the most
space-eﬃcient but easy to clean and threaded assemblies are easy to disassemble.
It was found that a multi-material metal powder dispenser requires:
• A safe and easily cleanable container.
• A stable and precise actuator.
• A geometry which constrains the well-ﬂowing powder feedstock, as it otherwise intro-
duces unwanted variation in the ﬂow.
The vibratory approach was found to be a good solution to the task at hand, fulﬁlling
the need of a wide dosing span at suﬃcient precision and accuracy. Speciﬁcally regarding
the vibratory dispenser:
• It is important to decouple the dynamic vibratory dispensing unit parts of the sys-
tem that may change mass or stiﬀness throughout use. These changes may introduce
unwanted and/or uncontrollable dosing variations through variations in the vibration
response.
Page 99
==PAGE:119==
10 Conclusion
• Powder-speciﬁc calibration is required as the rheology can diﬀer signiﬁcantly between
powder types.
• Using a multi-parametric control approach can expand the possible dosing space, but
introduce the need for an eﬃcient calibration methodology.
Had the vibration motor been more stable and the powder amount in the canister had
a smaller eﬀect on the dynamic response of the powder, the motor frequency - i.e. motor
voltage - had been a third and useful control parameter to include in the model providing a
signiﬁcantly larger dosing range.
Capabilities of the vibratory dispenser system
A system with the desired precision
of around 5% RSD or lower was achieved. By altering the design and further investigation
suggest even smaller doses may be achieved.
The dispenser system was shown to be capable of doses between 0.1g to 2.5g with relative
standard deviations (RSD) between 1.76-12.14% depending on the given control parameters.
54 consecutive doses with an average of 0.902 ± 0.0089g (α = 5%), a RSD of 3.71% was
demonstrated. This expected precision of the system with a 3σ shift of the dosing target is
expected to overdose an average of 2g per printed mm.
Using the linear regression model developed through the factorial experimental design,
a dosing time sequence was made, corresponding to a material transition (from A to B).
The dosing time sequence resulted in a mean error relative to the target of ¯e = −0.0022g.
The prediction model systematically under-dosed by 400ms but a more simple single-variable
experimental approach may provide more precise model.
A calibration methodology for introducing a completely new powder was also proposed;
doing a 22 factorial experimental design with the angle and dosing time as factors - with
just a few repetitions - to map the possible dosing space, perhaps with some centre points
to check for curvature of the model - although the tested region appeared to be very linear.
Afterwards, a ﬁxed angle experimental plan to map the dosing characteristics of the new
powder with higher accuracy and to save time.
The dispenser within the tested parameter settings were not shown to handle doses be-
low 0.1g. It is postulated, that controlling the ﬂow oriﬁce, tray angle and dosing time can
result in lower doses than this. The shown low doses amounts to ≈5wt% of a 2g steel layer.
Using a capillary dosing design, described in Section 2.3.3, could provide fractions as low as
0.05wt% with doses down to 1mg. Pulsing the motor at 800ms or under was found not to
be a good strategy. 2s were the lowest tested stable dosing time.
Non-contact recoater
While the concept of spreading the powder by controlling the ﬂow
from a tray through vibrations seem like a interesting starting point, the need for rigorous

Implementation
True implementation of the dispenser system was not possible due to
other work being done with the L-PBF machine. The dispensing system is likely ready for
implementation and true testing.
10.2
The mixer
A simple mixer was designed and manufactured. It’s main process parameters was controlled
via an Arduino. While several solutions were presented, the tumbler was chosen. The tum-
bler showed promise in its ability to prepare the layer for recoating. The mixer’s ability to
mix - or rather how well - could not be concluded. The samples was gathered according to a
factorial experimental design but the method of analysis was not the optimal, especially not
within the project’s time frame. The device was however able to spread the powders from a
single pile throughout the length and drop the powder in a line ready for deposition.
The powder mixing characterisation methodology was not tested to completion, however
the steps to achieving the goal seem within reach and are deemed possible:
• Collect powder sample on conductive substrate.
• Mount and prepare using metallographic methods.
• Obtain an elemental map using e.g. EDS of the particle cross-sections.
• Using image analysis the particles are segmented and assigned to a powder type.
• Perform statistical analysis of the segmented and indexed image to attain degree of
mixedness.

11
Recommendations for
further work
The recommended direction of the further work with the developed powder handling system
is lastly presented.
11.1
Further work regarding dispensing system
Although the overall goal regarding precision and control, the system’s (potential) ability to
dose outside the tested regions are yet to be demonstrated. In particular, the lower bound is
of interest as this would enable adding smaller amounts of (alloying) elements which. This
would mean another tray design where the powder has a shorter travelling distance and is
more constrained - or perhaps even better: A capillary module capable of dosing in the range
of mg more reliably.
The ability to function with less free-ﬂowing powders like carbon is also yet to demon-
strated. Besides controlling the fractions of various alloys within each layer, by constructing
the alloys themselves from their constituents in each layer, perhaps a new kind of function-
ally graded material is possible, e.g. changing the carbon content within the part negating
the common diﬀusion-based carburizing post-process. I.e. constructing alloys in-situ.
Changing the actuator is also recommended. The motor clearly worked, was easy to
control and very inexpensive. Although, it was seemingly unstable at higher voltages (4-5V)
which are bound to cause loss of precision. The higher frequency of a piezo-transducer is
supposed to work better with small particles [37] and could move away from the natural
frequencies of the system with the biggest amplitudes seen in the lower order natural fre-
quencies. A piezo-actuator is furthermore able to be controlled more precisely in terms of
modulating the frequency as done in [17], where the frequency proﬁle is tailored to a spe-
ciﬁc powder trough dynamic powder ﬂow characterisation using a rotary powder analyser -
improving upon the stability. A piezo element may also be able to dose smaller amounts by
being able to decrease the energy input, where as the DC-motor has a minimum starting
voltage. While the driving and control of a piezo-element can be more involved.
A more robust tool-less mechanism for removing the dispenser system is also recom-
mended but is not crucial. A more stiﬀhinge for the vibration tray is also recommended.
The main recommendation however, is to introduce a weighing device into the process
chain, preferably a capacitive scale capable of real-time measurement of the ﬂow through
the sensor. The capacitive scale can be placed around the powder ﬂow which mean a small
physical footprint and relatively simple mechanical integration. This could form the basis
of a closed loop feedback system in turn make the dispensing system less susceptible to
Page 102
==PAGE:122==
11 Recommendations for
further work
instabilities in e.g. the actuator or powder irregularities. It would also provide a data stream
from each individual layer, and the calibration process could be automated or perhaps an
automatic part of every print job. This could be an important part of lowering the barrier of
entry to do multi-material prints and the time-expenditure required for experimenting with
powder types.
11.2
Further work regarding non-contact recoater
Modelling the powder ﬂow across the non-contact recoater, e.g. using the DEM described
in 2.5 in combination with the vibrations could provide insight as to how the ﬂow can be
controlled. Geometry to be placed in the ﬂow could also be explored. By having a non-
contact recoater, the printed components are subjected to signiﬁcantly less forces - or no
forces at all, potentially allowing for less support structures and no catastrophic collisions.
Integrating a visual system that can detect abnormal deformation when printing several
components at a time, could stop the laser scanning of the bad part and continue the other,
thus saving precious time and powder. This is however speculative and remains to be tested
and properly documented by the company Velo3D that claim some of these capabilities
already.
11.3
Further work regarding mixing analysis
Getting access to an EDS-device with a larger area would surely increase the signiﬁcance of
the results, although simply scanning more areas might achieve the same - and of as high
resolution as possible. The most pressing problem is the obstruction of the x-ray signals due
to the large height diﬀerences of the relatively large particles. Pressing the sample holder with
less force into the powder appear to increase the distance between the particles. The larger
distance between the particles may help, but ultimately the samples should be prepared in
the classic metallographic manner; encasing in resin followed by grinding and polishing of
the surface. The particle cross-sections should allow for a complete mapping of the surface
making the method usable. The brightness contrast created by the height diﬀerences could
also be negated, making it easier for the image segmentation to diﬀerentiate segment the
image into individual particles and remove the background.
Characterising the powder mixture is surely useful for understanding the L-PBF process
and entire process chain, it is however not a substitute for a similar analysis of the mi-
crostructure post-melting, as the chaotic nature of the process may induce extra mixing or
segregation to the sample - and the resulting microstructure is the most (or only) important
aspect at the end of the day. However, a connection between the powder state at the various
process steps, is interesting if nothing else.
