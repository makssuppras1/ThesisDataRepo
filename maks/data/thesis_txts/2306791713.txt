The thesis deals with urban drainage in the context of the challenges the cities of Denmark will

implementation of alternative surface structures to retain water from extreme rain events.

ABSTRACT 
With predictions of increasing frequency and intensity of rain events in the future, new 
approaches to mitigate flooding are required. An alternative method of mitigating flooding is to 
direct runoff from extreme rain events to flood designated public infrastructure that will suffer 
less cost from flooding. Structures that deal with extreme rain events can also include design 
aspects that handle everyday rain and delay of stormwater for compliance with service levels of 
drainage systems. The study worked with a case study concerning a flood problem in Gladsaxe 
commune in Greater Copenhagen, which would be simulated using a MIKE FLOOD model. 
Parts of this study was made in collaboration with an architectural engineering student thesis 
project.  
The case study model layout was modified to include runoff from green areas, which resulted in 
a significant increase of expected flooding from flood simulations.  Two modelling approaches 
were considered for implementing surface structures into the model. Modelling in 2D proved to 
be difficult and have low precision. The study chose to model solutions exclusively with 1D 
tool, proving to be capable of modelling sufficiently realistic and leading to a model 
implementation method where solutions could be included in network simulations testing 
service levels.  
The study would have to develop its own methods to support a process of finding potential spots 
to retain flood water, and subsequently design the solutions and related collection systems, 
including associated model implementation methods. The design concepts would include 
functions making the implemented solutions provide benefits to everyday rain and service level 
capacities.  
Maps of several different kinds were produced showing important relevant information needed 
for planning and to support collaboration. In collaboration with the architectural engineering 
student thesis, twenty-two floodable spots in the case study area were pointed out together with 
suggestions of primary function, such as playground or recreational use. Several general solution 
concepts relevant for model implementation were made and proved to be sufficient for suggested 
surface structures and associated collection system to be implemented into the model. 
Implemented structures included parking lots, a stormwater road, a playground, cascades and 
other general structures. The model implementation methods managed to include benefits for 
everyday rain and service level capacities by including storage for infiltration and delaying rain 
water when included in design and another concept was also introduced to allow for planned 
flooding of structures when pipe capacities at designated points are exceeded.

Introduction

1 INTRODUCTION 
The International Panel of Climate Changes (IPCC) confirms that anthropogenic greenhouse 
gas emissions are causing the average temperature of the earth to increase. IPCC have also 
concluded that global warming will affect precipitation patterns, likely leading to more 
frequent and intensive rain in northern Europe (IPCC, 2014). In Denmark, an update of the 
database of rain estimations indicated that rain intensities for durations between 10 minutes 
and 3 hours had increased in the order of 10% compared to earlier estimations (Madsen, 
Arnbjerg-Nielsen, & Mikkelsen, 2009). 
In Denmark 2012, an agreement was made between the government and the municipalities 
that all municipalities were obliged to make Climate Adaptation Plans (CAP), defining the 
path to adapting the municipality in regards to increased precipitation in the future. The action 
plan also comes with an extra focus of growth potential by developing new solutions 
contributing to a green adaptation (Klimatilpasning.dk, 2015). The city of Copenhagen has 
with an economical evaluation in their climate adaptation plan concluded that implementing 
SuDS will have the largest net saving of money, as compared to other scenarios only using 
conventional methods to mitigate flooding (City of Copenhagen, 2011). 
Another reason for increasing risks of pluvial flooding in urban areas are due to increasing 
worldwide urbanization. As impervious surfaces increase in urban areas, so does the amount 
and velocity of runoff (Skougaard Kaspersen, Høegh Ravn, Arnbjerg-Nielsen, Madsen, & 
Drews, 2015). As existing urban drainage system have been dimensioned and constructed 
under circumstances assuming contemporary precipitation patterns and less impervious areas 
in the city it is clear that many urban areas will in the future increasingly experience more 
frequent and intense flooding.  
Hydraulic drainage systems are extensive and complex making them difficult to understand. 
Recently hydraulic modelling programs have been made to simulate these networks. Drainage 
network and flood simulations are increasingly becoming more common in planning practices 
as a tool for planning and analysing weak points in urban drainage systems and flood risks. 
(Rubinato, Shucksmith, Saul, & Shepherd, 2013). Modern Danish legislation requires 
municipalities to get maps of flood risks within the municipalities using flood simulations 
(Klimatilpasning.dk, 2015). 
There has since the 70s and even more since the 90s been an increasing focus on sustainable 
planning practices and technologies of handling stormwater and also everyday rain. Many 
terms have emerged in different locations in the world, making communication sometimes 
more difficult in this area, as the terms usually also have slight differences in their inherent 
meanings (Fletcher et al., 2014). The terms Low Impact Development (LID), Water Sensitive 
Urban Design (WSUD) and Sustainable Drainage Systems (SuDS) are three common terms 
that relate to the purpose of implementing alternatives to regular drainage pipe systems. This 
thesis will use the term SuDS, as it originates from Europe, has a wide definition and is one 
of the more commonly used (Fletcher et al., 2014).

2 AIMS AND OBJECTIVES 
2.1 Aims 
The thesis aims to develop a process of planning and designing solutions to store flood water 
on the surface at events of extreme rainfall. The thesis methodology will be mainly divided 
into three parts; screening, planning and finally solution design and model implementation. 
The methodology and concepts developed by this thesis will be applied to solve a flood 
problem within a case study scenario, the flooding of Buddinge station in Gladsaxe commune.  
The two main aims of the thesis: 
1. Developing a method and producing necessary data in order to identify spots where 
large amounts of water can be retained on the surface during extreme rain events. 
2. Developing SuDS concepts and associated model implementation methods useable 
for retaining water on the surface, while also providing benefits to everyday rain and 
service level capacities. 
A secondary aim of the thesis is the collaboration with an architect, in order to develop early 
stage cross-disciplinary collaboration in planning using flood simulation tools and also 
creating general SuDS-concepts applicable within and outside of this study.  
2.2 Objectives 
Screening and planning 
The process includes analysing the problem, understanding the area in connection with the 
drainage system and developing solutions based on relevant data and maps.  Since the case 
study area is initially entirely unknown, a way of doing the problem analysis is to represent 
data visually on maps. This makes it more accessible and understandable also for people with 
little prior knowledge. 
The first aim of the thesis will be fulfilled by carrying out these objectives: 
 
Setup of a network-flood model relevant to the case study 
 
Problem and system analysis 
 
Solution spot suggestions made in collaboration with architect 
Solution design and model implementation 
The second part is developing SuDS concepts useable for designing solutions with the needed 
functions to mitigate flooding and subsequently implementing solution designs into model to 
evaluate behaviour and efficiency of solutions.  
The second aim of the thesis will be fulfilled by carrying out these objective: 
 
Proposals of general SuDS concept relating to storage and collection system 
 
Design and implementation of solutions into model, using solution spot suggestions 
and SuDS concepts made by study 
 
Implementation of several solutions combined together in one scenario to solve 
flooding at Buddinge station

3 THEORY 
3.1 Modelling floods in MIKE 
The setup of a model requires three parts, a hydrological (precipitation-runoff) model, a 
hydrodynamic network model and a 2-dimensional surface (2D overland) (DHI, 2015). 
Precipitation is used to test the system.  
 
Figure 1. Main components and links in flood model (DHI, 2015) 
3.1.1 Network model 
The layout of the hydraulic drainage network system can be setup in MIKE URBAN, usually 
with simplifications with smaller pipes and small drainage grates are not included. MIKE 
URBAN uses the MOUSE engine to calculate the flow of water in pipes, by numerical 
calculations in basic 1D solving Saint Venant equations (DHI, 2014a) 
The setup and definition of catchments are also included in the network model as it is 
necessary for the model to know which manholes different catchments contribute to.  
3.1.2 2D overland 
To simulate water flowing on the surface, a 2D terrain model is required, which is based on 
topography maps or also called DEM. Topography maps of high resolution are usually easily 
available on the internet, e.g. at websites maintained by governmental institutions.  
The topography works as a 2D overland model on which the movement of water can be 
simulated. MIKE FLOOD allows the network model of MIKE URBAN to be connected to 
the 2D overland, by coupling the manholes of the network model with 2D cells on the 2D 
overland, where water will go when the manholes surcharges in the network model. The 
resolution of the 2D overland is variable, with higher resolutions being more desirable, but

SCALGO 
The Scalable Algorithmics (SCALGO) platform allows for flood related analyses of 
topographic maps (DEMs are available within the platform), estimating flash flood spots, 
flow paths and watersheds. The platform also allows for terrain modifications using simple 
tools within the platform, making it possible to create ditches, obstacles or flat depressions.   
3.1.3 Precipitation 
Real world rain events are different and result in varying runoff volumes and different runoff 
dynamics. It is very important to understand differences of rain series as the system is tested 
in various ways depending on the characteristics of the used design storm. A popular synthetic 
design storm to use in modelling is the Chicago Design Storm, as it can easily be varied to 
different durations and return periods and also contains rainfalls of shorter duration within the 
same return period. CDS rainfall profiles for different areas in Denmark are available, by use 
of Intensity-Duration-Frequency (IDF) rainfall data of local areas (DHI, 2015). Important to 
note is the accumulated depth of a CDS storm increases with the duration, see Figure 2.  
 
Figure 2. Accumulated depth of 10-year-event CDS profiles with varying length (DHI, 2015). 
3.1.4 Hydrological model 
To calculate runoff quantities from catchments, different hydrological models can be used. 
The runoff models calculate runoff based on the rain intensities and several assumptions or 
simplifications, creating an estimated hydrograph for every catchment in the model. 
Runoff Models (DHI, 2014b) 
Time-Area (Model A) – Runoff is based on initial loss, size of catchment and a continuous 
hydrological loss. The runoff hydrograph is determined by the concentration time and shape 
of catchment. Infiltration aspect is not included, as all green areas are disregarded.  
==PAGE:15==

Kinematic wave (Model B) – Runoff is calculated as an open channel flow, taking into 
account gravitational and frictional forces. Several hydrological losses and size of catchment 
determines runoff amount. Different hydrological parameters can be applied for different 
surface types. Uses Horton’s equation to determine infiltration. Also includes surface storage 
of water filling depressions (small amounts).  
Linear Reservoir (Model C) – Runoff calculations are based on the current calculated water 
depth in the catchment, meaning that more accumulated precipitation depth will lead to more 
runoff. Runoff amount is controlled by initial losses, size of catchment and infiltration losses. 
Also includes Horton’s equation.  
Including infiltration 
Different hydrological models make different assumptions or simplification of the amount of 
water lost to wetting, surface storage and more importantly infiltration. The runoff in the 
model are estimated by first calculating the hydrological losses and subtracting it from the 
precipitation, thus leaving something usually called “effective precipitation” (DHI, 2015).  
There are various ways of calculating infiltration, all being simple, empricial and theoretical 
equations. The two common methods applied in MIKE are either by calculating infiltration 
as a combination of initial loss, constant loss and a proportional loss, or calculating by using 
Horton’s equation (DHI, 2015).

Where:

3.2 Choice of solution modelling approach 
3.2.1 General considerations 
The study had two significantly different options of modelling the solutions, either by 
modelling in 2D by modification of DEM using tools SCALGO or by using MIKE 1D tools. 
Both approaches have different benefits and drawbacks. As it was not immediately clear 
which was more suitable, both were tested by modelling one of the solutions suggested in the 
thesis (see section 6.3 )  twice using each approach as to compare results and method. The 
solution modelled was the playground (see section 6.3  for implementation using 1D tools, 
and see Appendix A for implementation using modification of DEM).  
3.2.2 Implementing solutions 
Modelling in 2D 
Modelling the solutions in 2D was considered for this study by modifying the DEM using 
tools available in SCALGO. The terrain modification tools are however not very precise, as 
they are limited to flattening areas, and creating paths with a specified increase/decrease of 
elevation and of a specified width.  
The implementation also loses further precision depending on the chosen 2D overland 
simulation resolution, which is commonly quite low (this study chose 2.8m x 2.8m, see below 
in section 4.2.3). This does not have an important effect when modelling one compartment 
storage solutions, but every other aspect solution designs and surface collection systems. 
Ditches would have to be modelled with twice the width of the 2D overland resolution, as 
parts of the diagonal flow paths otherwise would have cells disappear when aggregated to 
2.8m. Ditches would also have to be very modelled much deeper than realistically needed in 
steep terrain, as the aggregation can lead to the ditch and surrounding area in some spots be 
of the same elevation.  
Modelling in 1D 
The 1D tools available in MIKE URBAN have been developed over long time, and have many 
useful functions. The precise shape of nodes and links are more easy to model, as there are 
features such as basin geometry, CRS and natural channels. The many functions and precision 
makes the solution behaviour in the model more predictable.  
Modelling the water movement in 1D means that water is contained and “locked” within the 
nodes and links existing in the model, as links will not overflow to the sides or a basin won’t 
overflow until the specified geometry is exceeded. The implementation into the model 
includes a rough assumption that modelled solutions, ditches and roads can be shaped to 
contain the water also in real life. 
3.2.3 Analysing solution behaviour 
Modelling in 2D 
Using zonal statistics will include all water on the surface in the region, summing water in 
solutions and collection system with unwanted flooding. This requires specifically created 
polygons to calculate water in solution and collection system. There is also no suitable method 
of estimating out how much water passes a point of interest. Hydrographs have a great worth, 
as they are very useful to understand when and how fast a solution fills up or empties. This 
kind of results are however unrealistic to produce when modelling in 2D.   
==PAGE:19==
Theory 
 
9 
 
Modelling in 1D 
There are many options to analyse parameters and different aspects of the system using 
functions in MIKE URBAN or MIKE View, making it easy to get detailed information on 
solution behaviour. Hydrographs can be shown in every point of the system.  
3.2.4 Simulation times and workflow 
Modelling in 2D 
Modelling in 2D requires running a full flood simulation to get a first result, and furthermore 
the big need for further work to analyse the results. Higher resolution in flood simulations, 
which would make implementation more reliable and precise, comes at the cost of simulation 
times. It was estimated that a simulation using a 2D overland resolution of 0.4m x 0.4m in 
this study would take around 50 hours to run, which is a problem for the workflow.   
Modelling in 1D 
Initial implementation of solutions into the model can be tested with network simulations, in 
order to check if the implemented solution behaves as expected or to discover flaws in the 
design. The short simulation times when using network simulations greatly improves the 
workflow of implementation and makes it easy to quickly detect mistakes and improve design. 
By being able to test the whole system using only the network simulations, very long 
simulations can be run to test historical rain. Running LTS is a powerful tool to evaluate the 
efficiency of solutions and to test the system design with different historical rain series, as to 
detect important problems with solution designs and improve them.  
3.2.5 Conclusion on why to use 1D to model solutions 
It is possible to model in 2D, but it comes with the burden of being unprecise, long simulation 
times difficult to fit with a good workflow and difficulties to get feedback on the behaviour 
of the implemented solutions. Modelling in 1D on the other hand, seemed flexible enough to 
also model water on the surface and with greater precision. It was ultimately decided to use 
exclusively 1D tools to implement solutions into the model, as this modelling approach was 
deemed flexible enough to develop model implementation methodologies and was easier to 
work with.  
Developing a methodology of including all solutions in 1D will additionally allow to run 
longer simulations making it possible to test the system using historical rain series or LTS 
(however not used in this study).

4 CASE STUDY 
4.1 Case study Background 
Gladsaxe commune 
The municipality of Gladsaxe is part of the capital region and located just on the border north 
of Copenhagen municipality on Zealand. As of 1. January, the population amounted to about 
68.000 people and covers an area of 2.500 hectare. The focus of the case study, Buddinge 
station, is located close to the centre of the municipality. Gladsaxe commune is divided into 
seven drainage systems, where Buddinge station is located within Utterslev Mose catchment 
(Gladsaxe Municipality, 2015), see Figure 5. 
Buddinge Station 
Buddinge station is a railway station with an important traffic way passing underneath. It has 
become an area with a high frequency of flooding, creating recurring problems in traffic 
(Gladsaxe Kommune, 2014). A light rail is planned to be constructed passing below Buddinge 
station. The project is scheduled to be finished around years 2020/21, which has led to a 
political agreement of solving the problem of flooding at Buddinge station within the period 
of 2014-2021 and in combination with the construction of the light rail. The project has been 
funded with 90 million DKK from 2915-2018 and additional 40 million DKK from 2019-
2022 (Gladsaxe Kommune, 2015). 
Pipe system and basin 
The drainage system of Gladsaxe municipality was established in the period between 1900 
and the end of the 1970-ies. The last 25 years there have also been renovations of the pipe 
system, primarily using a technique where a plastic cover is inserted into the pipe, without the 
need to dig up the ground (Gladsaxe Municipality, 2015). 
The drainage of Utterslev Mose is entirely handled by a combined sewer system, meaning 
that both wastewater and rainwater is collected and transported in the same pipes. The system 
is drained by a main pipe, referred to as Vangederenden, placed on the border to Gentofte 
municipality and shared with said municipality (Gladsaxe Municipality, 2015). 
There is a basin located at Vadgårdsvej, very close to Buddinge station. It was constructed in 
1932 and has since been enlarged several times, latest in 1975. The current capacity is 
specified as 8.100 m3, before it starts to overflow. The basin is drained by Vadrenden and 
Vangederenden (Gladsaxe Municipality, 2015).  .  
The utility company Nordvand in charge of constructing and maintaining the drainage system 
in the municipality uses two different dimensioning criteria when dimensioning pipe sizes, 
see Table 1. Nordvand uses factors accounting for expected increasing precipitation due to 
climate changes (Gladsaxe Kommune, 2015).

4.2.1 Reference rain scenario and simulation lengths 
For both problem analysis and planning, the study used a single rain event as a reference to 
test all proposed solutions with. The study chose a reference rain scenario of CDS rain of 2 
hours’ duration and 50 year return period with Intensity-Duration-Frequency data for 
Gladsaxe municipality.  
A rain event of an intensity corresponding to a 50-year return period was chosen because EU-
directives mandates that transport hubs of major importance may only be disrupted by 
flooding every 50 years (Technical Committee CEN/TC 165, 2008) and Buddinge station has 
been clearly defined by the municipality as a transport hub of major importance (Gladsaxe 
Kommune, 2015). A climate factor of only 1.0 was used, meaning that climate changes are 
not accounted for, as the case study aim is to solve an existing problem.  
Two durations were used for simulations (both with 2 hours of rain followed by dry weather), 
3 hours to estimate severity of flooding and 4 hours to estimate drainage network performance 
for problem analysis. The first duration was determined by examining entire time series of 
flood results and finding that most severe flooding at Buddinge station always happened in 
the period between 2 and 3 hours into simulations. The 4-hour duration for problem analysis 
was found looking at results from the weir method (see section 5.2.4 below). Results shown 
in Figure 6, indicated that the pipes at Buddinge station would be at stress for up to 3 hours. 
An extra hour was used as a safety factor to make sure to include more time for the system to 
empty.

4.3 Changing hydrological model 
Some assumptions were made in order to make the change the hydrological model from model 
A to model B: 
 
All buildings are steep impervious - all other impervious areas are flat. 
 
Model A data of imperviousness is correct.  
 
The soil type of a catchment was always 100% of one kind, either clay or sand.  
 
Model A is correct for smaller return periods.  
Five parameters had to be calculated for Model B. These calculations were either based on 
the topographic map, data in Model A, or the soil type map of Denmark available from GEUS 
(GEUS, 2011). All data was imported to ArcGIS and calculated there.  
 
Slope of catchment – Topographic terrain map (with obstacles removed) would be 
aggregated to 10m x 10m, so that unevenness of terrain was removed. The mean slope 
of every catchment would be calculated.  
 
Length of catchment – Created smallest possible circles around catchments and 
calculated diameter, which should be equal to the longest distance of the catchment.  
 
Imperviousness steep – Used building polygons (provided by Kortforsyningen) to 
estimate ratio of buildings in each catchment.  
 
Imperviousness flat – Used equation: “Model A imperviousness % - Imperviousness 
steep = Imperviousness flat”. 
 
Perviousness – Majority of Gladsaxe is clay, with the area north of Buddinge station 
and commercial area being sand (GEUS, 2011). Clay was deemed as low pervious 
soil type and sand as medium, with high pervious soil type not included in model.  
It was noted that the Model A supplied with the network model used a reduction factor of 0,8 
for every catchment, meaning that rain intensity for runoff calculation was reduced by 20 %. 
In theory, green areas should produce very little or nothing at such a low intensity event, and 
thus make runoff results from Model B comparable with Model A during rain events of low 
return periods. Comparisons of hydrographs from a 1-year rain event were made to calibrate 
the model B, see Appendix E. Model B with a reduction factor of 0,8 was closest to the 
original model at 1-year-rain events. Based on the results showed in Appendix E, the study 
chose to reduce rain intensity by 20% to mimic the reduction factor used in Model A.

5 METHODOLOGY  
5.1 Introduction to methodology 
This part of the paper will describe the methodology used in order to reach the aims and 
objectives of this thesis. The case study problem of Buddinge station flooding would serve as 
a possibility to test thesis solution designs and model implementation, and at the same time 
show the use of them by solving a real world problem and putting them in a realistic context.  
5.1.1 Overall process – From understanding the problem to solution 
Solving the case study problem would also require understanding of the underlying reasons. 
The area and drainage system was at the beginning entirely unknown and would require a 
screening, as to understand what contributed to the flooding of Buddinge station and also how 
the drainage system was built in general.  
A planning step was needed to in order to examine the entire case study area and to finding 
spots where solutions could be implemented. Finding solutions spots would also mean 
identifying and understanding the function and context of each spot, having a significant 
importance for the corresponding solution suggested for it. A vital part of assuring the 
viability of a solution spot would also be to analyse the extent and shape of the collection 
system that could be constructed around a solution spot.   
Getting knowledge about the reason of flooding and the available solution spots would make 
it clearer what kind of solutions that would be needed. Different concepts used for solution 
designs would have to be made together with an associated model implementation method. 
The case study would be used to test solution designs and model implementation to evaluate 
performance and behaviour. With several validated solution design, a final scenario would be 
made based on a good knowledge of the reason of flooding and using a combination of 
solutions to finally stop the flood problem. The flood problem would be considered as solved, 
when no flooding occurs during simulations using the reference rain scenario (a CDS rain 
event of 50 years return period, based on the criteria of no flooding EU directive of no major 
transport hubs flooding more often than every 50 years, see section 4.2.1).  
The process from problem analysis to stopping flooding at Buddinge station goes as follows: 
1. Screening 
a. Problem and system analysis 
b. Estimating amount of water to retain upstream to mitigate flooding at 
Buddinge station 
2. Planning 
a. Suggesting solutions in different locations where water can be retained 
b. Estimating extent of solution spot collection system 
3. Solution design and model implementation 
a. Developing solution designs and model implementation methods 
b. Implementing solutions into model for testing 
c. Implementing several solutions in one scenario to prevent flooding at 
Buddinge station and other important local flooding 
The methods and tools used to accomplish these steps and reach set goals are sorted in the 
same three main categories as shown in the process described above.

5.3.2 Estimating extent of collection system 
To understand which area a solution spot can be expected to collect from, an estimation of a 
catchment area has to be made. SCALGOs function of easily estimating flow paths and 
watersheds, makes it a good tool for estimating the maximum extent collection system, as 
terrain changes (removing obstacles or simulating ditches) can easily be made and included 
in the calculation of flow paths and watersheds. 
SCALGO calculates watersheds at different rain depths, meaning that small depressions will 
have individual watersheds at small rain depths, but will overflow at more intensive rain and 
join with bigger depressions forming increasingly bigger watersheds. By implementing a 
solution into the SCALGO model as an unreasonable big depression and calculating for a 
==PAGE:31==
Methodology 
 
21 
 
very large rain depth, the largest possible watershed will always form around the solution to 
indicate which area water can be collected from.  
It is important to realise that the difference of a flow path direction can be dependent on a 
very small elevation difference. For example, regular roads can be divided between two 
different watersheds as they are usually shaped to have a little peak in the middle, separating 
flow paths. Using SCALGO terrain editing tools to create lowered interpolated paths through 
terrain leading to the solution are preferably used, where there is reason to implement ditches. 
This study chose to disregard the option of using pumps, as it is more unreliable than a 
collection system using gravity. 
5.4 Solution design and model implementation 
5.4.1 Designing solutions with benefits for every kind of rain 
The focus of the study is to make use of urban landscapes and infrastructure to retain water 
during extreme rain events to reduce downstream or local flooding. Other viable concepts that 
does not relate to surface structures will be disregarded, such as underground solutions (e.g. 
underground basins or soakaways).  
To keep with the modern approach to urban drainage and the development of more sustainable 
structures, concepts have been thought of and deemed suitable when adding additional 
benefits, aside from just flood mitigation. SuDS can have several functions and benefits, e.g. 
substituting drinking water, reducing load on WWTPs, infiltrating to ground water and more. 
SuDS functionalities can largely be divided into three categories, everyday rain, design 
(relating to the service level of drainage systems) and extreme events (flooding).  
There are many positive aspects to taking care of everyday rain using more sustainable 
structures, such as rain gardens where water can infiltrate into ground water or delaying 
stormwater to reduce overflows. Generally, solutions become costlier in the amount of water 
they are designed to handle and there are also limitations to how much can be expected to 
infiltrate. Many SuDS designs are designed to handle problems within their own domain, be 
it everyday rain, service level or extreme events, without adding additional benefit to other 
domains. 
This study has focused on designing solutions for extreme events. There are however good 
possibilities when designing solutions to deal with extreme events, to also implement a design 
that allows for benefits for everyday rain and retention of stormwater in order to comply with 
service level criteria.  
5.4.2 SuDS concept designs for storage 
The actual design of a solution storage can vary a lot. The difference between a parking lot 
and a playground is considerable. There is however not an actual need for the details when 
making general SuDS storage concepts. The concepts presented below are suitable for model 
implementation, while being general enough to include most solution storage designs. 
Storage designs concepts 
The challenge consists of introducing infiltration, retention of stormwater, and making sure 
to save space for extreme events, all in the same solution. The storage space delaying 
stormwater can be used both for extreme rain events and to comply with service level criteria 
by delaying water. This can be made possible by smart placement of drainage pipes in varying 
sizes and at different elevations in the solution, creating three storage spaces for different 
functions. Two general concepts can be seen in Figure 10 and Figure 11, where both figures

Figure 10 illustrates an easy concept, where everyday rain fills a storage space that is 
permeable and allows water to infiltrate, instead of going to the WWTP. When the solution 
is filled with more water than is feasible to infiltrate, a small drainage pipe will drain the water 
over time so that the solution will empty over time. By making the drainage pipe very small, 
the basin can also delay water to the drainage system. A second drainage pipe placed at a 
higher elevation will make sure that the basin always save storage for extreme events. Using 
two pipes also makes it possible to make the draining of the basin more dynamic, so that the 
storage solution has a very low initial drainage capacity, but is still able to discharge more if 
stored water is built up during a very long medium intensive event.  
Figure 11 shows a somewhat different concept of a solution where there is no infiltration and 
a weir is implemented to control the direction and target of overflow when maximum capacity 
of the storage is exceed. Some locations in the case study area was pointed out to have 
contaminated soil, and was thus not suitable for infiltrating water. Those solutions simply 
disregarded the everyday rain aspect of the concept, thus the drainage pipe is located at the 
very bottom. The weir is added to the design, as there can in some cases be very sensitive 
structures close to the solution. The solution can be designed to overflow to other solutions, 
which are intended as a last resort storage (e.g. stormwater roads). Another option is 
overflowing to the sewer systems, as perhaps future improvements of the drainage system 
will free up space and allow for this.

Modelling SuDS storage concepts 
Modelling the actual storage is made with a basin node and a basin geometry corresponding 
to planned solution shape. Storage can also in special instances be made with manholes or 
links, if the manholes and links are modelled in way, so that water can’t flow out and instead 
will accumulate. A drainage pipe should always be implemented to make the storage solution 
able to slowly discharge to the drainage system, and not have stored water build up over time.   
Infiltration storage is implemented, by connecting the drainage pipe a bit above the bottom so 
that the storage has to fill the infiltration space before it starts to discharge through the 
drainage pipe. Important to watch out for is that MIKE URBAN automatically generates water 
in the bottom of basins or manholes up to the lowest placed link, which means that if 
infiltration space is to be modelled, then the connecting link should be connected to the bottom 
of the basin. 
To model overflow from the storage, a weir should be connected just a few centimetres below 
the storage maximum elevation. The weir can either be connected to a close manhole leading 
the water back into the sewer, or connected to a secondary solution. 
5.4.3 SuDS concept designs for collection system 
Gathering runoff to solutions can generally be done by a separate rain collection system or by 
keeping it on the surface flowing on either roads or in ditches. The study chose to not utilise 
the existing pipe system, due to it being a combined sewer system and the surface structures 
to be flooded was exclusively public spaces.  
Modelling ditches 
The study focused on using the ditches as collection system, as some notable benefits would 
be lower costs, delaying water and easier conceptualisation of collection system. Ditches work 
better with flooding surface structures, as they are also on the surface and easily flow into the 
solution storages.  
==PAGE:34==
Methodology

Modelling a ditch is simple. This study chose to model ditches as links using CRS with 
varying cross section areas and shape depending on the context. Manning numbers can be set 
by creating a new material alternative with data corresponding to green areas, making the 
water flow slower as expected from water moving on green surfaces.  
Cascades 
Green ditches have the benefits of delaying water due to a higher roughness of the grass, and 
to also infiltrate water over time. These benefits can be improved by implementing a cascade 
design, where the ditch level is flattened in segments and weirs are placed with intervals 
downstream, forcing runoff to gather in some amount before it can flow over each weir, see 
Figure 12. This delays and enhance infiltration as the ditches can also perform as small rain 
gardens where water can infiltrate over longer time. This concept can also be increased in size 
to be implemented as larger solutions in steep terrain.  
 
Figure 12. Concept sketch #3 of ditches with cascade design.

Three different options of modelling cascades were tested in this study, with an additional 
fourth discussed.  
1. Expected storage in cascade included in basin geometry 
2. Modelling the cascade using natural channel in MIKE 
3. Expected storage of cascade moved to upstream manhole

might be located far from the actual storage in the cascade. It’s also not realistic when ditches 
start to flood or overflow to other parts of the system, as the water would be located at the 
incorrect location.  
Modelling cascades using natural channel is probably the most realistic approach, however 
more difficult and more unstable in the model, requiring smaller time steps during simulation.

Modelling throttle pipe-overflow point 
Implement a collection system (or use an existing one if possible) leading to the overflow

be connected to the manhole acting as an overflow point, as it is preferred to measure all the 
contributing runoff from connected upstream link.

The concept ditch-throttle pipe can also be combined with stormwater roads, as can be seen

is available when the ditch is overflowing, allowing for a large storage amount. Additionally, 
the road can either be shaped to act as a transport link or to just act as storage for extreme 
events and then flow back to the ditch when pipe capacity frees up.

An important consideration to take into account, is the overlapping of a flooded stormwater 
road in the network system, and water in the 2D overland moving onto the street. Since only 
manholes are coupled, flood water located on top of links in the 2D overland, will not move 
into the stormwater link unless touching the 2D coupling cells of a manhole. This is a critical 
problem when other manholes and links on the same street surcharges water to 2D overland.  
This overlapping can be seen as both bad or good. It’s bad as the water on the street should 
be included in the link to easier understand total water depth and to later be drained into the 
ditch and away from the area. Not accounting for this might lead to double counting storage 
capacities. It’s good as flood water on the street indicates that the drainage system somewhere 
is not functioning properly and is discharging.  
If the flooding should be included in the link, then it can easily be solved by connecting 
manholes on the street with the stormwater roads using weirs, that are placed at the top of the 
manholes, thus discharging into the stormwater road link instead of onto the surface model.   
Modelling in 2D 
This concept could possibly be improved by combining the modelling approach with 
modelling in 2D, to avoid double counting. The road could be elevated in the DEM preventing 
water in the surface model to gather and thus avoiding double counting capacity when the 1D 
stormwater road link is full. This approach would however also stop water from surrounding 
areas to gather on the road also when the link is not full, meaning it’s not a fix all approach. 
Modelling the entire stormwater road in 2D was still not seen as a good option, due to the lack 
of precision discussed in section 3.2.

6.2.2 System analysis 
Analysing the pipe system shown by relative sizes (see section 5.2.1), it becomes evident the 
local drainage system is built with the purpose of the basin collecting most of the runoff, as 
the maps show all areas connecting to some main pipes of considerable size leading to the 
basin, while Vangederenden which drains the area is quite small in comparison to the overall 
system, see Figure 23.  
 
Figure 23. Aerial view overlaid with pipes in relative sizes. 
The pipe system of the area can roughly be divided into three arms (north arm, west arm and 
south arm) based on the three largest pipes leading to the basin. The west arm drains 
immediately into the basin, whereas the north and south arm directs the water to a junction 
where the water can either go to Vangederenden or the basin.  
The west area (FID 7, 8, 9, 10, 11, 12 and 13) is closely connected to the basin, with only one 
very small pipe connecting it to the problem area. This should mean that rain water from this 
area primarily discharges into the basin, and has no direct influence on the problem at 
Buddinge station.  
The south part of the case study area (FID 2, 4, 5 and 6) is connected to two parallel pipes, 
one with considerably larger diameter, located under Buddinge station leading on to 
Vangederenden and the basin. This should mean that all the water discharging due to 
overloaded pipes come from said zones. As the two pipes merges at Vangederenden, it can 
be assumed that water removed from one will free up the same amount of water for the other.  
The north area (FID 14, 15, 16 and 17) has a large drainage capacity to the basin and is also 
most closely connected to Vangederenden. The area is in comparison to the west and south 
area quite small, meaning it has relatively low importance.  
It can be seen in Figure 24, that the basin and South arm shares the flow capacity in 
Vangederenden. Analysing the pipes of Vangederenden using MIKE View to view the 
discharge hydrographs, it can be seen that it has very limited capacity (between 0,06 m3/s and 
0,57 m3/s during reference scenario, see Figure 24).

The basin has a very large maximum capacity (simulations show 20849 m3, without

to discharge water out of the system explains the increasing flooding at Buddinge station over 
time, also after the rain event has finished.

6.2.4 Conclusion of problem 
The general cause of flooding in the case study area is simply due to an overloaded pipe 
system, as the drainage network is not designed to handle rain events of this intensity. 
Documents from Gladsaxe commune clearly states that service level design is flooding every 
10 years, as the system is entirely a combined sewer system.  
It was concluded that basin exceeding design capacity is the main cause of flooding at 
Buddinge station, as this results in the south arm being unable to discharge water gathering in 
pipes, and instead surcharges to cause an increasing flooding at Buddinge station. As the basin 
reaches 20.800 m3 in the simulations, far more than the overflow point (8.100 m3), it can only 
be concluded to retain the difference (12.700 m3) upstream as solution is not very feasible.  
The analysis of the pipes system and basin showed that removing water from the south area 
should have largest positive impact on the problem. The solution is to locally implement 
solutions upstream in the south arm to reduce the specific water contributing to the flooding 
at Buddinge station. 
Using the weir method, it could be appreciated that at least 3387 m3 of water should be 
removed from the pipe system to stop most of the flooding at Buddinge station.  It was also 
found that the total amount of water in the pipes discharging to the area was 8947 m3 during 
the overflowing weir scenario. This means that at least 1/3 of the water produced as runoff 
upstream has to be stored somewhere else.

7 DISCUSSION 
7.1 Theory and model layout 
Modelling approach 
The study initially tried to model solutions in 2D, which turned out to be a poor choice. 
Modelling in 1D greatly improved workflow. Network simulation times allowed to almost 
instantaneously get proper feedback on solution behaviour or mistakes. Using hydrographs to 
understand how flow rates at different time steps and the accumulated discharge were 
necessary to improve on design and to present results in an understandable way.  
Tools for modelling in 2D proved to be much more difficult to use, had long simulation times 
which made a good workflow difficult, and very few tools were available for analysing the 
solution behaviour. The main reason of the problems with modelling in 2D was the low 
modelling resolution used in simulations and terrain modification tools lacked functionality 
to modify DEM with precision. This does not mean that modelling in 2D is impossible, as 
improvements can probably be made making it a viable option in the future. Modelling in 2D 
was not suitable when developing and optimising solutions, but could however possibly be 
more suitable when having a final “not-to-be-changed” design to be implemented.  
Model runoff calculations and impact on flooding 
The biggest uncertainty of the model is the inclusion of green areas and infiltration capacities. 
Changing hydrological model from model A to model B, resulted in greatly increased runoff 
and leading to increased quantities of flood water, and subsequently increasing the need for 
more solutions and retention storage in the case study area. It is however difficult to say with 
certainty that the modelled runoff was correct, as the runoff calculations were made using 
many assumptions. No sensitivity analysis was made, but it was generally considered that 
assumptions of infiltration and Manning numbers had the greatest influence on the runoff 
calculations. 
Good data in infiltration capacities are difficult to get as it can vary from location to location. 
In this study, soil type maps were used to roughly appreciate the soil types in the area and 
assigning those areas a generic infiltration rate capability found in literature. There is a high 
uncertainty of the actual infiltration capabilities of the soil in the area. The model setup also 
assumed that the soil was completely dry at the beginning of the rain event, but it would also 
be equally correct if assumed that soil would be very wet at the beginning of the scenario, 
something that would increase the runoff.  
The assumption of different Manning numbers had a great impact on the rate of runoff and 
how much time the drainage system has to react and lead water away. This study used 
Manning numbers found in literature, which were smaller than default values in MIKE 
URBAN for both impervious and pervious area. This assumption meant more time for the 
drainage system to handle flooding. Using default Manning numbers would probably have 
increased flooding in the case study model.  
Network and flood simulation uncertainties  
The network model given to the project was simplified, which is common for models of this 
scale. It was also known that several SuDS was implemented in the area, for example 
soakaways in the housing areas to the west. This was not implemented into the model, and 
would obviously make the calculated runoff differentiate from reality.  
The choice of including green areas in the runoff calculations also introduces an uncertainty 
into the network model. The sub-catchments of the MIKE model are always connected to a 
==PAGE:70==
Discussion 
 
60 
 
specific manhole, probably based on the general drainage system of the area. Green areas are 
however usually not connected to drainage systems, and there is therefore an uncertainty to 
where runoff from green areas actually go. Flooding in the MIKE model appear where there 
are manholes, but might in reality start to appear other places when green areas are not 
connected.  
7.2 Screening 
The behaviour of the basin turned out to be an unexpected challenge in solving the flood 
problem at Buddinge station. It was initially expected that reducing the peak of runoff going 
to Buddinge station would help solve the problem. The problem however turned out to be 
more complicated as the flooding at Buddinge station would build up over time and manholes 
at Buddinge station would keep surcharging also after the rain had stopped. The reason being 
the south pipe arm located under Buddinge station being unable to discharge water and 
therefore would surcharge at the lowest point, here Buddinge station. To solve the problem, 
it would be necessary to design a system that would continually collect water, even when 
water quantities in the pipe were not overloading the system.  
The weir method showed that at least 3387 m3 of water should be removed from the south 
arm in order to prevent flooding. This was compared with the total amount of water going to 
the manholes with weirs attached, 8979 m3. This indicated that large parts of the upstream 
area would have to be connected to the planned solutions.  
7.3 Planning 
Solutions spots and categories 
Gladsaxe is an urban area where all of the surface already is occupied and designated for 
different functions. The overall problem with flooding designated areas in the city, is to find 
areas that are robust enough to withstand these events without taking damage and have enough 
size to store large volumes of flood water. The process in finding solution spots proved to be 
one of the trickier and time consuming parts of this study. Generally, it was more difficult to 
find available space, than it was to find a good solution spot. Almost all of Gladsaxe commune 
is already claimed for different functions.  
The studies in collaboration found several solution spots and these were categorised into six 
different categories, where the sixth one would be referred to as combination, indicating a 
larger type with several ideas and functions implemented into one larger solution design. 
Three of the categories, activity zone, recreational zone and climate garden were areas which 
could be claimed and used for public/community means and also redesigned to handle water 
to different extents. The two other categories, climate parking and stormwater roads were 
infrastructure types that would continue to work as normal, but would be flooded when 
needed. Half of the twenty-two suggested solutions spots would turn out to be existing 
infrastructure, with eight are parking lots and three roads. 
The category of climate garden, was in the end disregarded in this study due to some important 
difficulties implementing this category. The architect engineering student highlighted the 
difficulty of implementing solutions over an area with several private stakeholders, making 
this category less feasible to implement in real life. The category was still included in the 
other study as to show the concept, as it not impossible either. The concept was however not 
developed and this study could not solve some important concerns, mainly due to the 
depressions pointed out would not be able to drain back to the sewer system. This thesis put 
some time into it, but did not manage to produce any concept and subsequently neither a

model implementation that made sense. This category was deemed to not have enough 
potential to develop fully.  
Collection system 
The study chose to fully focus on using ditches as they were simpler to implement and focus 
could instead be moved to the SuDS concepts and solutions. The method using SCALGO 
proved to be simple and efficient in estimating the extent and design of a solutions collection 
system. The extent of the collection system was important for the solutions to work, as a 
solutions storage capability is only as good as the capability of the collection system to collect 
that amount of water.   
Using a separate rain water collection system could possibly present new opportunities and 
other chances to control water to flood designated spots when needed to. The most obvious 
use would be the possibility of intercepting existing pipe systems and direct surcharge to 
floodable surface structures. The study tested this quickly in the final scenario with the south 
parking lot, with positive results concerning the behaviour of the solution, see section 6.5.2, 
however with the negative effect of less flooding removed from the collected catchments. The 
inability to reduce local flooding was probably because of pipe dimensions being based on 
service level design. 
7.4 Solution design and model implementation 
7.4.1 SuDS concepts 
This study produced two general storage solution design concepts which were not supposed 
to represent a final design, but to demonstrate the different parts in a context to how storage 
solutions could look and function concerning everyday rain, service level and extreme events. 
This approach proved to be useful in developing a modelling implementation. The study 
didn’t have a need of a more detailed storage concepts.  
The study produced three concepts designs related to the collection system. Cascades and 
stormwater roads (with ditches on the side) meant utilising the collection system as storage, 
and only one, the throttle pipe-overflow point, related to creating a dynamic control of water. 
Storage design 
Modelling storage proved to be the easiest part of modelling both conceptually and in 
behaviour, and also succeeded in including benefits for everyday and service level. Specifying 
the available storage capacity for different depths and attaching drainage pipes at correct 
elevations is simple and very precise and would be applicable for many kinds of structures, 
for example parking lots and the playground. Monitoring the amount of volume in the storage 
node over time is also simple.  
Cascade 
Ditches in collection systems have great potential to in themselves store everyday rain and 
infiltrate. The concept is simple, but have great potential, as a long stretch can probably retain 
water of such quantities that it affects the performance of the drainage network when testing 
for service level return periods. Cascades can possibly be designed of such sizes that they 
significantly affect the system during an extreme rain event.  
The study developed two different model implementation methods for cascades, with one 
being more realistic and difficult, and other less realistic and simple to implement. Both 
methods stored intended quantities. This study would suggest to use the simple one, using 
==PAGE:72==
Discussion 
 
62 
 
several manholes in a cascade, as the loss in realism is not significant and it still stores 
intended quantities.  
Throttle pipe-overflow point implementation 
This concept can probably be used for every suggested solution, and in combination with both 
a separate rain water collection system and ditches. It was very useful when implementing 
parking lots, as it will only overflow at greater runoff peaks (decided by dimensioning). The 
throttle pipe-overflow point model implementation proved to work as intended both times 
when used in this study. The results showed that the implemented model layout efficiently 
cut of the peak of the runoff and moves it to the parking lot.  
The behaviour is very related to the shape of the rain event, which in this study created a very 
clear concentrated peak runoff, where also a 2-year rain event would cause the structure to 
overflow to some extent as the peak is intense and sharp. The concept will probably behave 
differently when tested with a very long low/medium-intensity rain, as this would allow for 
larger quantities of water to discharge into the throttle pipe. This stresses the importance of 
testing this design with historical rain series. 
Stormwater road-ditch 
The stormwater road-ditch was a very simple concept to implement, as all that is needed is a 
CRS of the road shape. The used method gives a good control, as it in one graph will 
automatically calculate the cross section area of both the ditch and of the stormwater road, 
giving clear results of the storage capacity in both.  
A good benefit was the storage space of the stormwater road being included together with the 
collection system. This meant that the ditch modelled in the road could act as a transport link 
during smaller rain events, but would start to store water when the collection system would 
be overloaded.  
A weakness is flood water moving on the surface model will accumulate on the road in the 
2D surface model, and not properly interact with the stormwater road link. Different 
modelling alternatives, including combining the model implementation with modelling in 2D 
was explained in section 5.4.4. Different methods would come with both benefits and 
drawbacks. This study did not manage to develop a final fix all model implementation method 
to avoid all associated problems.  
7.4.2 Modelled solutions 
The problem and system analysis lead to the conclusion that to stop flooding at Buddinge 
station, it would not be enough to reduce peak runoff, but to also retain large amounts of 
runoff going into the system also after peak rain intensity. This had a large influence on the 
choice on the design of the implemented solutions, where many had to be designed to 
continually collect water also when the drainage system was not under stress.  
Dimensioning 
The study ran short of time when it reached the solution implementation stage, meaning that 
less care was made to the details in dimensioning storage and collection systems, and more 
weight to prove that suggested SuDS concepts could be implemented into the model and 
would work as intended. Storage capacities were made by rough estimations using available 
area and making a guess on maximum water depth possible in a solution, which proved to be 
enough, as water quantities collected in the scenarios in many cases proved to be less.

Discussion

Playground 
The playground turned out to be a success, and an easily implemented solution. The area did 
not experience any flooding itself and did not contribute to the problem with extreme amounts 
either. Flood results after implementing it did still show that it had a very positive effect. This 
is probably mostly due to design choice of collecting all water from the entire area, and in a 
way severely delaying the runoff going to Buddinge station. Using the cascade was suitable 
with the playground, as this part of the collection system would barely contribute with water 
during smaller rain events, and thus make the playground less likely to fill its hypothetical 
stormwater storage space, which should mean less wearing down of the structure.   
Parking lot next to town hall 
The throttle pipe proved to be quite small, as the structure would overflow to the parking lot 
also at a 2-year rain event, however of only 63 m3. The parking lot designed by the 
architectural engineering student did include some green storage spaces, where this probably 
could be stored. This does prove that the throttle pipe-overflow point is not suitable if 
absolutely no water is allowed except at a crisis. However, the parking lot provided from 
collaboration thesis, did show that maybe some storage to smaller amounts of water could be 
included in a parking lot design. This would make the smaller flood volumes of 2-year rain 
events to less of a problem, as the parking lot would contain some green storage spaces, which 
could act as a buffer.   
Commercial district and nearby stormwater road 
The overflow function implemented in this scenario proved to work well, as all the runoff 
water that could not be contained by the stormwater retention solution in the commercial 
district would go to the stormwater road.  Simulation results from a 2-year rain event showed 
that only the stormwater retention space of the commercial district was used, with the 
stormwater road having no water on the road.  
Parking lot close to bus station 
The impact of implementing the small retention capacity on the parking lot had an impact of 
unexpected magnitude. This study has no sure explanation of this, other than that the pipe 
stretch in the reference scenario was very prone to surcharge. Removing water can have 
changed the hydraulics in a dramatic way. The possibility of a fault in the model or program 
should however not be excluded.  
If results are true, then it indicates that even small solutions like this, can be worth 
implementing when placed at the right location. This strategic placement was achieved by 
using the map of overflowing manholes.  
Final scenario 
The flooding at Buddinge station was stopped by implementing the already implemented and 
tested solutions, plus two additional ones. The estimated water to be collected/storage 
capacity of the solutions was estimated at around 4190 m3, as compared to the estimation of 
removing 3387 m3 from the system to stop flooding at Buddinge station. The implemented 
solutions would almost exclusively lead to decrease in flooding in their immediate area, where 
areas without implemented solutions would have the same flood quantities as in reference 
scenario.  
The success of the final scenario serves to validate a successful screening, planning and model 
implementation process developed and used in this study.

7.4.3 Combining several SuDS concepts 
The designs of the solutions implemented in this study showed in different ways, that 
combining different SuDS concepts would make the system more robust. An implemented 
example is the stormwater road combined with the ditch-throttle pipe concept, and also to 
only use the stormwater road when the storage capacity of the commercial district is exceeded. 
The benefits for all kind of rains, lies in successful combinations of ideas which does not need 
to be similar, but a smart use of available space. There are for example no connection between 
the intended use of a cascade and throttle pipe-overflow point, but there is also no limitation 
to implementing them in sequence, which would give benefit for both everyday rain and 
control of runoff peaks.   
7.5 Collaboration and process direction to reach set goals 
The collaboration aspect of the two studies was initiated as an interesting opportunity to test 
what two students of different disciplines could produce when using each other’s knowledge 
and capabilities. The collaboration together with the architectural engineering student 
unfortunately turned out to be of not much importance to this study, although much time 
invested in it. This does not necessarily mean that the collaboration is a failure, as the work 
made by this study were in several ways necessary for the other study. Most importantly, the 
solution spot planning step was also necessary for the other study to succeed, which would 
have been impossible without the maps and problem analysis made by this study.  
The main reason of this study having no notable results from the collaboration is the lack of 
direction and experience in the beginning, as the process direction started from a blank slate. 
Both this study and the other had to start with gathering knowledge about SuDS and to get 
inspiration from existing plans, for example the Copenhagen climate adaptation plan. This 
study did only at a late stage understand the importance and challenge of designing a smart 
collection system, and failed to communicate and stress the importance of including the 
collection system in the design collaboration.  
In the end, the collaboration proved to not be vital for this study to reach aims and goals set 
This study managed to create its own SuDS concepts applicable for model implementation of 
solutions. Detailed design of the solutions was not necessary; as only general estimations was 
enough for model implementation. 
7.6 Further suggestions to improve on study 
Using soakaway nodes in MIKE 
The study had knowledge of, but not access to the tools of the new MIKE 2016. The soakaway 
tool available in the new release could have had a significant importance for the model 
implementation of the concepts, as infiltration was implemented when feasible. This thesis (if 
it had had access to MIKE 2016) would have implemented soakaways to account for storage 
in cascades, and would also have moved the infiltration compartment of solution storages out 
of basin nodes to soakaways. This would have made it possible to run historical rain series 
simulations, where infiltration capacities of implemented solutions could be tested.  
Further investigation of collection system design for surface structures 
This study found that a surface structure’s ability to store water is no better than the capability 
of a collection system’s ability to gather the water to fill it. More investigation could probably 
be made to design a more clever. collection system and also to put more research into what 
kind of surfaces and areas that are feasible to collect water from, as there is also the issues of 
pollution.

8 CONCLUSION 
The idea to stop flooding at Buddinge station by retaining water on surface structures 
upstream was proved possible in the flood model of the case study area. The different 
structures to retain runoff was suggested in collaboration with an architectural engineering 
student thesis. Smart design of the structures used would also give other benefits to society 
and rain water handling.  
The laid out process and methodology developed by this study proved successful in 
understanding the reason of flooding, identifying several spots in the case study area and 
implementing relevant solution design into the model. The final scenario where the flood 
problem was solved, used a combination of several solutions, some retaining only peak runoff 
and other collecting stormwater and delaying discharge.  
The choice of including runoff from green areas using model B in MIKE proved to greatly 
increase estimated flooding in the scenarios, and was deemed more realistic than the (in 
Denmark) more commonly used model A. Uncertainties of great importance in the model was 
assumptions of infiltration rates and Manning numbers for the different land types.  
Two modelling approaches structures, either modelling in 2D by modifications of DEM or by 
using MIKE 1D tools, were considered for the model implementation of surface. Modelling 
in 2D proved to be very difficult, having poor workflow, low precision and few options to 
visualise results. The study chose to model exclusively in 1D as related tools enabled a good 
workflow, high precision and the possibility include surface structures also in network 
simulations allowing for testing using long historical rain series.  
The methodology and maps produced for finding solution spots allowed for the architectural 
engineering student to be deeply involved in this part of planning. The collaboration aspect 
in this step lead to an early categorisation and suggestions of primary functions of suggested 
solution spots (e.g. playground or recreational themes).  Existing infrastructures were also 
suggested to be floodable structures, consisting of parking lots, roads and football fields. All 
of the suggested infrastructure would require reshaping to work as retention for flood water. 
The study could not find other floodable types of infrastructure, indicating a restriction of 
what the types of infrastructure that can be used for this purpose.  
The proposed SuDS concepts for storage were primarily designed to handle runoff from 
extreme rain events, but to also include functions relating to everyday rain and service level 
of drainage system. This was primarily achieved by smart placement of drainage pipes, 
creating space for infiltration and delaying storm water. Model implementation methods 
would also include infiltration space and stormwater delay, making the surface structures 
relevant also when testing the drainage system for service levels. The model implementation 
method of storage differed in no significant way between implementing a playground and a 
parking lot. 
Three other SuDS concepts relating to the collection system was also suggested, with 
associated model implementation methods. A cascade concept, relevant to utilise storage 
capacities of collection system and increasing infiltration capabilities of the system. A throttle 
pipe-overflow point concept to control both maximum runoff discharge to sewer system and 
point of overflow, allowing for controlled flooding of chosen structures. A stormwater road-
ditch concept, where the limited discharge from a ditch on the side of the road allows for the 
road to retain water when runoff amounts exceed. Soakaway nodes (only available in MIKE 
2016) should be added into the model implementation methods to include actual infiltration 
of the solutions into the model. 
==PAGE:76==
Conclusion 
 
66 
 
Design of several solutions implemented into model used combinations of proposed SuDS 
concepts. Results showed that robustness and efficient use of solutions improved with smart 
combinations of several concepts or when placing solutions in sequence. Implemented 
solution designs provided benefits to everyday rain and service level design by delay of 
stormwater, without any obvious sacrifices to capability to retain runoff from extreme rain 
events. Results confirmed that a surface structure storage capability is only as good as the 
associated collection system capability to collect water for it.

B.3 
Bluespot 
 
Figure 57. Zoom-in map on roundabout and town hall, overlaid with Bluespot. 
A map of the natural depressions in the topography can be made. These kind of maps are 
generally referred to as “Bluespot maps” in Denmark, indicating spots where water will gather 
in extreme rain events. It does however not take into account the drainage system, meaning 
that most depressions will not fill if the drainage system works. It is a relevant map to 
understand which buildings are in danger when flooding start occurring. 
The method includes every natural depression, without filtering sizes and shallow depths. The 
study chose to filter away depressions smaller than 50 m2 and from those depressions, to filter 
away pixels of less depth than 0.1 m.  
B.4 
Flow paths 
 
Figure 58. Zoom-in of flow path map, here with small flows in light blue to bigger in dark blue. 
The flow path map indicates the natural flow paths of water on the surface, and is also capable 
to calculate the size of the contributing area to each cell. It is very useful for understanding 
where water will go once flooding starts to occur.  
When making the map, all holes in the topographic maps have been filled up, meaning that the 
method also shows where the water goes after a depression have been filled up and overflows. 
The study used a topographic map with obstacles such as trees removed, but with buildings 
added.  
The method of producing the map is however not without flaws. In this study, a very large area 
south of the railway is considered a depression by the tools, and subsequently filled. The flow 
path tool then considers the area entirely flat and depicts the flow paths as sharp lines passing 
over the area until exiting in the east. This means there is no understanding of the natural flow 
paths in this area. An alternative fill was performed, where only depressions with less than one 
meter from their bottom to pour point were filled, resulting in many bigger depressions left 
unfilled, acting as endpoint for many flow paths. This is closer to reality, as many of the

Figure 59. Zoom-in of watersheds based on flow paths. Red stars show watershed pour points.

Topographic map – Available at Kortforsyningen in resolutions of 5m*5m and 0.4m*0.4m 
and in two versions, either “photorealistic” with buildings and trees, or a touched up version 
with buildings and trees removed. When using a topographic map for flood simulations, it is 
recommended to use the version with obstacles removed and to manually add buildings.  
When aggregating topographic maps (using aggregate tool), it is suggested to use median of 
aggregated cells so that the edges of buildings still are sharp. Using median produces an 
aggregated map most similar to base map, as it keeps drastic elevation differences important 
to outline buildings.

Exporting data from MIKE view 
1. Get Excel-plugin to modify dbf-files 
2. Create dbf-file in ArcCatalog, create relevant fields.  
3. In MIKE View: Click Plot 
a. TS in nodes – select data type of interest 
i. List – click select all and draw graph 
b. Right click graph and select accumulate values 
i. Click calculate and copy nodes and column of interest 
c. Copy into dbf-file and save. 
4. Drag dbf-file into ArcMap and join with manholes shapefile with identical nodes. 
5. Optional: Use field calculator to sum runoff and discharge to get an estimation of total 
water contributed from specific nodes.  
Result is in total m3. Suggested Symbology: Exclude 0-5, proportional symbols with smallest 
size bigger than 1. Natural breaks.  
Extra: Making selections in MIKE View – create selection on MIKE URBAN, save and change 
file end to .nse. It is now possible to use it in MIKE View when choosing nodes or links from 
list.

Figure 66. Yellow lines indicate delineation of model DEM. Light blue indicates small flow paths, with 
increasingly darker blue colours indicating flow paths of increasing size. 
As can be concluded from Figure 66, there are no flow paths starting outside of the model DEM 
and going inside. This means that even if there is flooding occurring outside of chosen area, it 
will not affect the area of interest.
