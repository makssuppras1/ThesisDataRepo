Abstract
Supporting systems (utilities, waste handling) in chemical and biochemical production
plants can limit the production capacity in case of failure, inappropriate design or ineffec-
tive operation. At the Chr. Hansen site in Avedøre, the production capacity is limited by
the current functioning of their neutralization system. The neutralization system ensures
that the wastewater resulting from cleaning of equipment fulfills the pH requirements for
discharging the wastewater to the municipal wastewater treatment plant. This thesis has
analyzed how the current operation of the neutralization system limits the production
capacity and possible solutions to optimize the neutralization system have been identified.
First, information about the facility and the neutralization system was collected by studying
internal documents, talking to employees and looking at data from the automation sys-
tem. Next, bottlenecks of the neutralization system were identified by analyzing relevant
process data. This analysis showed that the cleaning of one specific unit (the UHT2) puts
a particular load on the neutralization system. The current capacity of the neutralization
system results in a limited flexibility of starting cleaning programs (CIPs) for the various
units and it was found that 3.3% of the time, new CIPs could not be started in the fermenta-
tion department. The longest CIP delays were mainly caused by long neutralization times.
However, the data analysis showed that most often the bottleneck for the neutralization
process is the discharging of the neutralization tanks and not the neutralization time.
Based on the data analysis, a list of optimization options was made. A qualitative evalu-
ation was performed, which resulted in a shortlist of two solutions; a solution regarding
the implementation of pumps to decrease the discharge time of the neutralization tanks
and a solution involving the reuse of parts of the water used in the cleaning of the UHT2
to reduce the load to the neutralization system. The two solutions were evaluated based
on expected cost and their impact on increasing the production capacity. It was concluded,
that both solutions is recommended to be implemented with the first priority being in-
stalling pumps to decrease the time for draining the neutralization tanks.

Resumé
Ineffektive utility og affaldshåndteringssystemer kan begrænse produktionskapaciteten i
kemisk og biokemisk industri. Dette er tilfældet på Chr. Hansens fabrik i Avedøre hvor pro-
duktionskapaciteten er begrænset af deres neutraliseringsanlæg. Spildevand fra rengøring
af udstyr på fabrikken sendes til dette anlæg for at blive pH justeret inden det kan sendes til
det kommunale spildevandsanlæg. Denne afhandling analyserer hvordan den nuværende
drift af neutraliseringsanlægget begrænser produktionskapaciteten og præsenterer løs-
ningsforslag for hvordan neutraliseringssystemet kan optimeres.
Til at starte med blev der indsamlet informationer om fabrikkens afdelinger og neutralis-
eringsanlægget fra medarbejdere, interne dokumenter samt deres kontrolsystem. Data fra
kontrolsystemet blev anvendt til at identificere flaskehalse for neutraliseringsprocessen.
Dataanalysen viste at neutraliseringsanlægget bliver særligt belastet når et bestemt stykke
udstyr (UHT2) rengøres. Når neutraliseringsanlægget er hårdt belastet er det ikke muligt
at starte nye rengøringsprogrammer for udstyret i de forskellige afdelinger og det blev
bestemt at dette er tilfældet 3.3% af tiden i fermenteringsafdelingen. De længste perioder
hvor nye rengøringsprogrammer ikke kunne startes var hovedsageligt forårsaget af lange
neutraliseringstider. Dataanalysen viste dog at flaskehalsen for neutraliseringsprocessen
ofte er dræningstiden og ikke neutraliseringstiden.
På baggrund af dataanalysen blev der lavet en liste af optimeringsforslag. To forslag blev
udvalgt baseret på en kvalitativ analyse. Det ene løsningsforslag omfatter en installering
af pumper for at mindske dræningstiden mens det andet løsningsforslag handler om at
mindske belastningen på neutraliseringsanlægget ved at genanvende dele af det vand
som bruges til at rengøre UHT2. Disse to løsninger blev evalueret baseret på forventet
pris og deres indflydelse på at kunne øge produktionskapaciteten. Det kunne konkluderes
at begge løsningsmuligheder er værd at implementere hvor første prioritet bør være at
installere pumper for at mindske dræningstiden for neutraliseringsprocessen.

Introduction
This chapter will introduce general problem areas and topics in the chemical industry
which this thesis is related to. The objective of the thesis will be presented along with a
description of the management of the project.
1.1 The importance of supporting systems in chemical
production
Chemical and biochemical plants produce products that are desired by mankind such
as food, medicine, fuel etc. In the research of methods for producing a certain product,
the focus is on the raw materials and the pathway to transform them into the desired
product. However, when the product must be manufactured, supporting systems such as
utilities and disposal of waste become crucial. These systems are not directly involved in
the chemical composition of the product. However, the utilities are important for estab-
lishing the adequate conditions for the conversion of raw materials to the desired product.
Furthermore, chemical and biochemical processes produce not only the desired product
but also by-products and waste streams which must be disposed in accordance to legal
requirements. Ineffectiveness or failure of supporting systems can lead to down-time in
production.
The Chr. Hansen site in Avedøre is an example of a facility for which the production ca-
pacity has been restricted by an ineffective supporting system. Chr. Hansen is a bioscience
company which produces bacteria cultures. These cultures are used for food production
(fresh dairy, cheese, meat), plant protection as well as to improve human and animal
health. The production site in Avedøre constitutes two production lines which produces
numerous distinct types of bacteria cultures. Therefore, the production pattern is irregular
and variable over time. This challenges the supporting systems at the site such as the neu-
tralization system because the load on these systems varies. The neutralization unit is an
important supporting system that ensures that wastewater streams are neutralized which
is a requirement for it to be pumped to the municipal wastewater plant. The wastewater at
the site arises from the cleaning of rooms and equipment. Currently, cleaning programs are
delayed or interrupted occasionally due to limited capacity of the neutralization system.
Consequently, this can cause delays in the production.

1.2 Problem definition
The objective of this thesis is to answer the following question:
How does the current neutralization system at the Chr. Hansen site in Avedøre limit the
production capacity and can the production capacity be increased?
To answer the question above a number of activities must be accomplished. First, the
Avedøre facility and its processes must be describe in order to understand how the neutral-
ization system is associated with the production capacity. In addition, a detailed description
of the current neutralization system is necessary. The information about the facility and
the neutralization system is not readily available and therefore, the understanding of these
aspects will be gained from the combination of studying internal documents, talking to
the employees and looking at data from the automation system used at the site. As the
next step, bottlenecks for the neutralization process must be identified. This analysis will
be based on historical process data retrieved from the automation system. Based on the
knowledge about the functioning of the current neutralization system and its bottlenecks,
solutions for increasing the production capacity will be identified. The possible solutions
will be assessed to select one or more candidate for further analysis. In the end, the possible
solutions will be evaluated.
1.3 Project plan
The problem definition and the description of the various activities in this project was
described in the previous section. A plan must be made for the execution of the different
activities in order to ensure the project is finalized within the given time frame. Therefore,
this section will describe the project management of the thesis.
The activities mentioned in the previous sections are grouped in a set of work packages.
The sequence and the estimated duration of the work packages constitute the schedule of
the project which is shown in Figure 1.1.
The work will mainly be performed at the Chr. Hansen site in Avedøre. This ensures proper
contact to the employees which is an important source of information about the facility
and its processes. Once a week, the work will be carried out at the office of Harper & Vedel,
a consultant company with broad experience in chemical production technologies. Harper
& Vedel is competent in assisting the idea generation phase of the project as well as the
analysis and evaluation of the identified options. On a two-weekly basis, status meetings
will be held with the supervisor from DTU regarding the progress of the project. The
supervisor from DTU will mainly be used to get feedback on the report writing, assisting
on technological questions, and to ensure the report reaches the adequate academic level
of a Master’s thesis.

Description of the facility
This chapter presents a description of the Chr. Hansen site in Avedøre. The different
departments and their relations will be presented. The departments have very different
purposes, but they are among other things connected in the sense that they use the same
supporting systems. This entails that the load on the supporting systems must be studied
for the entire facility. Besides the overall facility description, a more detailed process
description of the individual departments will also be provided.
2.1 Facility description
2.1.1
Overview of the facility
At the production site in Avedøre, Chr. Hansen produces bacteria cultures to be used in
the food industry. Furthermore, human health products are transported from the site in
Roskilde to the Avedøre site to be freeze-dried and packed.
An overview of the process at the Chr. Hansen site in Avedøre is illustrated in Figure 2.1.
As the first overall step in the facility, the bacteria cultures are grown in a fermentation
process in the FDA department. After the fermentation process has completed, the bacteria
are separated from the fermentation broth before they are cooled to form small pellets.
The pellets are packed in bags and the bags are then transferred to a storage room with a
temperature of -55◦C. Potentially, the product is ready to be sold and transported to the
customers at this point. However, it can be necessary to make the product more resistant
towards higher temperatures because it can be challenging to ensure the low temperature
of -55◦C during transport. This is achieved by lowering the moisture content of the product.
Furthermore, the customers usually need a mix of different bacteria cultures. Thus, the
facility is equipped with two different routes for meeting these demands as illustrated in
Figure 2.1. In the G-pack department different types of bacteria cultures can be mixed and
the customized product is packed and stored at -55◦C.

The different departments have not been build at the same time. The first departments

2.1.2
Supporting systems at the facility
Supporting systems involves utilities and the handling of waste. An overview of the utili-
ties and their application on the Avedøre site is presented in Figure 2.3. Figure 2.2 gives
a simplified overview of the materials entering and leaving the Avedøre site. The fermen-
tation process requires substrates, additives and a bacteria starter culture. As process gas,
air or nitrogen is utilized.
Nitrogen is also used for cooling the bacteria cultures in the last unit operation in the
fermentation department. For less extreme cooling operations, water is used. Additionally,
water is required as solvent, for heating, cleaning and sterilization. Additional chemicals
used for cleaning are acidic and alkaline solutions. These chemicals are also used for pH
adjustments during the fermentation process as well as to neutralize the wastewater that
arise from the cleaning of the equipment.
The neutralization system for handling the wastewater is described in detail in Chapter 3.
After being neutralized, the wastewater is send to the municipal wastewater treatment
plant called Renseanlæg Avedøre.
Besides from the wastewater, the process produces the desired product (bacteria cultures)
as well as an eluate in the separation step in the fermentation department which is a
useful product for Solrød Biogas that uses it to produce biogas and fertilizer.

2.2 Process description
This section presents a detailed process description of the various departments at the
Chr. Hansen site in Avedøre. A special focus will be on the fermentation department
because it is a big contributor to the load on the neutralization system.
2.2.1
Fermentation
In the fermentation department (FDA) distinct types of bacteria cultures through fermen-
tation. Two production lines (L1 and L2) are available for the fermentation process. Each
production line contains two fermenters that share equipment for up-stream and down-
stream unit operations. The raw materials and conditions (fermentation time, temperature,
yield etc.) depend on the specific type of bacteria. The process flow diagrams for the pro-
duction of bacteria cultures on the two production lines are illustrated in Figure 2.4 and
Figure 2.5.
The two production lines are very similar but differs in capacity where the capacity of L2 is
twice the capacity of L1. Another significant difference between the two production lines
is that it is only possible to perform an aerobic fermentation on L1.
The main equipment in the fermentation production lines are the fermentation tanks
where the bacteria cultures are grown from a starter culture that is added to a mixture
of substrates. The substrates are often solid powder materials which must be dissolved
in water before it is added to the fermenter. Two mixing tanks are available for the
preparation of the substrates. These two tanks share a stirring tank and therefore only
one tank is used at a time to dissolve the substrates. The mixing tank is filled with water
which is directed to the top of the stirring tank to which the substrates are added. From
the bottom of the stirring tank the solution is directed back to the mixing tank. Liquid
solutions of alcalase and polysorbate can be added to the mixing tank. Polysorbate is a fat
source which contribute to the cell membrane when the bacteria cells split into two and
alcalase is an enzyme to break down the protein substrates.
When the mixing of substrate and additives is complete, the mixture is sterilized by an
ultra-high temperature (UHT) processing. In this process, the mixture is directed through
heat exchangers in which the mixture is heated with a countercurrent flow of hot water.
In L1 plate heat exchangers are used while shell-and-tube heat exchangers are installed in
L2.

produced at the Chr. Hansen site in Avedøre are anerobic which means that the fermen-
tation process requires the absence of oxygen. In this case, the fermentation tanks are
supplied by nitrogen gas. In case of aerobic fermentation processes which require the
presence of oxygen the fermentation tanks are supplied by air. To enhance the dissolve-

Fermentation is an exothermic process and therefore cooling is necessary. The fermenters
are cooled by ice water in a cooling jacket surrounding the tanks. The cooling jacket
is sufficient to cool the product during fermentation. However, when the fermentation
process must be terminated, the product must be cooled rapidly which is difficult using
only the cooling jacket because of the limited cooling of the center of the tank. Therefore,
the product is recirculated from the bottom of the tank to the top passing through a heat
exchanger.
After the fermentation has been terminated by cooling, some types of bacteria cultures
tend to aggregate. To break down the aggregates, the product mixture is directed through
a homogenizer. In the homogenizer the bacteria in the aggregates are separated by using
pistons to force the mixture through a small gap. The movement of the pistons creates
fluctuating flows. Therefore, the mixture is directed to a buffer tank which makes it possible
to ensure a steady flow to the next process step which is the separation of the bacteria
from the fermentation broth. The separation occurs in two disc stack centrifuges where the
product of interest leaves the centrifuges in the bottom while a large amount of the water
and remaining nutrients/additives (the eluate) gets separated out in the top. The eluate
is the waste-product which is transferred to a large eluate tank where it is stored before
it is picked up by Solrød Biogas. The product of interest is directed to two concentrate
tanks. On the way to these tanks it is possible to introduce additives (a cryoprotective and
ascorbate), which are mixed with the product using static mixers. The cryoprotective coats
the bacteria to make them robust for freeze-drying while ascorbate is added to increase
the shelf-life of the bacteria cultures.
After the potential addition of additives, the product is cooled by liquid nitrogen in a pel-
letizer to form small solid pellets. The two production lines use the same sized pelletizers
and therefore, L1 has two pelletizers at disposal while L2 has four. The pellets are packed
in bags and moved to a storage room.

Other departments

Neutralization system
This chapter describes the neutralization system for processing wastewater at the Avedøre
site. First, the requirements for the wastewater is presented. Next, the current operation
of the neutralization system is introduced. The contributions of wastewater to the neutral-
ization system are outlined. Lastly, the consequence of having a limited capacity of the
neutralization system is described.
3.1 Requirements for the wastewater
The equipment and rooms in the facility must be cleaned regularly to avoid contamination
of the product. As a result, large amounts of water as well as acidic and alkaline solutions
are required. In order to dispose these aqueous solution to the municipal wastewater
treatment plant the following requirement must be fulfilled:
The pH of the wastewater must be in the range of pH 6.8 – 9.2.1
Additional requirements are limits for the temperature of the wastewater and limits for
the content of specific compounds. These aspects are not considered in this thesis.
3.2 The neutralization process
3.2.1
Phases of the neutralization process
The neutralization system consists of three tanks in which the pH of the incoming wastewa-
ter is adjusted to meet the requirements established by the municipal wastewater treatment
plant. Each tank operates in phases as illustrated in Figure 3.1. One neutralization cycle
consist of the following six phases:
• Standby: A tank is said to be standby when it is available for being filled. The content
in a tank that is standby is usually between 0 - 1500 kg.
• Filling: Waste water enters the tank. Only one tank is being filled at a time.

• Filling and pre-neutralization: The filling of the tank continues while a pre-neutralization
occurs. The set point for the pre-neutralization is presented in Table 3.2. This phase
starts when the 60% of the capacity of the tank is reached corresponding to a con-

3.2.2
The neutralization cycle time
A parameter that can describe the capacity of the neutralization system is the time of one
neutralization cycle. If the neutralization cycle time can be lowered, more waste water
can be processed and the production capacity can be increased. The neutralization cycle
time is the sum of the duration of each of the various phases:
tC = tF + tN + tW + tD + tS
(3.1)
where
tC: Duration of one neutralization cycle.
tF: Duration of the filling of the tank (including the filling and pre-neutralization phase).
tN: Duration of the neutralization phase.
tW: Duration of the potential waiting time before the tank can be emptied.
tD: Duration of the discharging of the tank.
tS: Duration of the standby phase.

3.2.3
The neutralization phase
In the neutralization phase, an acid-base titration is performed to make the final pH
adjustments of the wastewater. Acid-base reactions are very rapid and they are therefore
diffusion-controlled [3]. Thus, the neutralization tanks are equipped with agitators to
ensure a high reaction rate.
As outlined in Table 2.3, the cleaning agents used at the site are sodium hydroxide (NaOH),
P3-mip (alkaline cleaning agent mainly consisting of NaOH), nitric acid (HNO3) and oxo-
nia (acidic disinfectant containing hydrogen peroxide and peracetic acid). The chemicals
used for the pH adjustment are primarily NaOH and HCl but in some cases HNO3 is used
instead of HCl. NaOH is a strong base while HNO3 and HCl are strong acids. It is assumed
that in an aqueous solution, the acids are completely dissociated to form hydronium ions
and their conjugate base. Based on these assumptions, theoretical titration curves for the
neutralization process have been drawn in Figure 3.3. In this figure the permitted pH
range of the wastewater is marked. The figures illustrate that the volume range for the
addition of acid or base to meet the pH requirement is very narrow which complicates the
neutralization process.

4.3.2
Wastewater contributions from the cleaning of specific units
Three types of data are used to estimate the amount of wastewater that arise from the CIP
of the individual units. These are CIP plots, valve position data, and flow rate data.
The method for calculating the amount of CIP fluid that is directed to the neutralization
for a particular unit:
1. The CIP line used to clean the unit is identified (see Figure 1 and 2).
2. In the control system, trend plots is opened for the particular CIP line to show the
CIP plots for all the equipment that uses that CIP line. Furthermore, trend plots for
the valve and the flow rate at the CIP return are added.
3. The CIP plot for the particular unit is used to find a time period where the unit is
cleaned. The plot is used to identify if the CIP is long or short. While also having the
==PAGE:41==
Chapter 4. Methodology
41
CIP plots for the other equipment visible, the zooming function at the plot is used to
zoom in on the CIP plot of interest until the other CIP plots is not shown.
4. Alternately, the data for the valve position and the flow rate are extracted and in-
serted in Excel. Step 3 is important to ensure that the valve position and the flow
rate that is extracted is only related to CIP of the unit of interest. This prevents
overestimating the wastewater contribution.
5. The data is converted to a csv-file and processed in Python. The Python script extracts
the flow rates only in the time period where the valve position data is 1 meaning
that the CIP fluid is directed to the neutralization. The amount of CIP fluid [kg] that
is directed to the neutralization is calculated from the flow rates as a function of
time using the trapezoid rule, a method for numerical integration [1].
The procedure describe above is used for all equipment. However, the procedure is slightly
different for the UHTs which each have their own CIP line. The UHTs do not have CIP

were only used for CIP. It is also used to store and send water through the UHT after the
substrate have been send through the UHT. Thus, to find the valve position data and the
flow rate data only related to the CIP of the UHTs, more information is required. For the
UHT2, a plot of the various CIP steps are available which makes it easy to detect when
CIP of the UHT2 begins and ends. Such data are not available for the UHT1.
As described in Table 3.6 in section 3.3.1, the CIP procedures for UHT1 involves the
opening of the valve to the neutralization five times where the second and the fourth valve
position opening occur when the CIP tank is emptied. Thus, to identify a period where
the CIP of the UHT1 was run, a period with five valve openings was identified and data
for the level in the CIP tank for the UHT1 was used to confirm that the tank was emptied
during the second and fourth valve opening.

The filling phase
The duration of fillings has been determined from the filling data and tank level data.

are not always filled to the exact same level. Therefore, tank level data is utilized to give
comparable filling times by applying the following procedure for each of the three tanks:
1. The filling data is used to determine the start and end times of all complete fillings
during the considered week of operation of the tank. In this way the duration of the
fillings is determined.
2. The tank level at the start and end times are extracted from the tank level data. This
enables the calculation of the total mass of wastewater that has entered the tank
during the individual fillings.
3. An average filling rate is calculated as the total mass entering the tank during the
filling divided by the duration of the filling.
4. The average filling rate is used to calculate a characteristic filling time for the case
where the tank is filled from 0 kg to 17,000 kg.

The duration of the neutralization phase has been determined from the tank phase data
and the pH data.

The duration of the neutralization phase can be determined by only using the tank phase
data. However, the neutralization time is expected to depend on the composition of the
wastewater. Therefore, it is of interest to find the duration of the neutralization phase
depending on the pH when this phase starts. To carry out this analysis the pH data are
used to determine the pH of the solution in the neutralization tank when the neutralization
phase of that particular tank starts. The pH of the solution is calculated as an average of
the pH in the bottom and in the top of the tank.
The discharging phase
The duration of the discharging has been determined from the discharging data and tank
level data.
As a starting point, only the discharging data is used to determine the average discharge
time for each of the three tanks. In this way it is investigated if the discharge time is similar
for the three tanks.
As described in section 3.2, the discharging of a tank will stop when the content reaches
1500 kg if another tank is full. Therefore, there are two relevant times for discharging; the
time for the complete emptying of the tank and the time for emptying the tank until 1,500
kg is left. The tank level data has been used to study two cases; the duration of a fully
discharge and an incomplete discharge, defined as
• Fully discharge: The level in the tank when the discharge starts is greater than
16,500 kg and the level when the discharge ends is below 50 kg.
• Incomplete discharge: The level in the tank when the discharge starts is greater
than 16,500 kg and the level when the discharge ends is between 1,000 and 1,500
kg.
4.3.4
Frequency of CIP start delays and CIP interruptions
As described in section 3.4, CIP can be delayed in two ways. Either the CIP is not allowed
to start or the CIP is interrupted during the CIP. For a month of operation (September
2023), a data analysis have been performed based on the actual capacity data to determine
the number of occurrences of CIP start delays and CIP interruptions. The procedure for
this analysis is:
• CIP start delays: To determine the number and the duration of CIP start delays
during the month, the periods where the actual capacity is equal to or below the
’ready’ requirement are extracted.
• CIP interruptions: To determine the number and the duration of CIP interruptions
during the month, the data are analyzed by considering one data point at a time.
Starting with the first data point it is determined if a CIP can be started by comparing
the actual capacity to the ’ready’ requirement. If the actual capacity is greater than
the ’ready’ requirement, the data point is assigned a ’ready’ signal. Otherwise, it is
==PAGE:44==
44
Chapter 4. Methodology
assigned ’not ready’. For the subsequent data points, their assigned signal depends on
the signal for the previous data point. If the previous signal is ’ready’, the subsequent
data points is assigned ’ready’ until the actual capacity becomes equal to or below
the ’not ready’ requirement. If the previous signal is ’not ready’ the subsequent data
points is assigned ’not ready’ until the actual capacity becomes greater than the
’ready’ requirement.
It is important to note, that the analysis described above is only based on the data for the
actual capacity and therefore, the number of CIP start delays and CIP interruptions do
not express the real number of occurrences of these events but rather express the number
of CIP start delays if a CIP is constantly requested and the number of CIP interruptions
if a particular CIP is constantly running. In this way, the number of CIP start delays and
CIP interruptions determined by this method express the maximum number of delays and
interruptions that may have occurred for a particular department/unit. If the real numbers
should be determined for the CIP start delays it requires information about when CIP is
requested which is not readily available, especially not for periods that is more than two
weeks old. If the real numbers should be determined for the CIP interruptions it would
require information about the time periods for when the CIP is running and the CIP time
for an uninterrupted CIP.
In principle it would be possible to determine the actual number of interruptions in FDA
because of the availability of the CIP plots for all equipment in FDA which shows when
the different types of flushes are in progress. However, performing such an analysis is
considered too time consuming whether it is performed manually or by making a Python-
script that incorporates the CIP plots.

4.5.1
Performance
The performance in this project is the ability of a solution to increase the production
capacity. Thus, it is of interest to quantify the increase in the number of extra batches that
can be produced when implementing the solution. However, this requires a full model of
the facility which is not available. Therefore, a simpler and indirect quantification of the
increase in the production capacity has been made focusing on the reduction in the load
on the neutralization system.
If the load can be reduced, more wastewater can be processed which means that the
production capacity can be increased because it involves the necessity for more CIPs.
However, reducing the load with a certain percentage does not correspond to the same
percentage with which the production capacity can be increased. Despite of this, the
quantification of the reduction in the load makes it possible to screen and compare possible
solutions because a high reduction in the load enhances the probability of achieving a
significant increase in the production capacity.

Thus, the only information needed to calculate the cost is the number and price of the
major equipment items. However, for conceptual designs, the actual quantities of the
individual equipment items may be unknown (for example the amount of piping required)
and limited specifications of the equipment (for example tank size, pump type etc.) can
make it difficult to find an exact price for the items. Therefore, a range for the quantities
and the prices can be made from which a minimum and maximum possible cost can be
calculated.
It is of interest to determine the probability for obtaining a specific cost within this range.
This can be done by applying the Monte Carlo method. In general the Monte Carlo method
is used to estimate the probability of the possible outcomes of a problem with uncertainties.
The specific procedure for the Monte Carlo analysis performed for estimating the cost of
selected optimizations of the neutralization system is:
1. The major equipment items that appears in the conceptual design of a possible
optimization of the neutralization system are listed.
2. For each of the equipment items, three estimates are made for the cost and the
required quantity; a best-case estimate, a most likely estimate and a worst-case es-
timate. These estimates are made in collaboration with Harper & Vedel. For the
required quantity of each type of equipment, the three estimates will be made in-
dependently. For the cost of each type of equipment, the best-case estimate and the
worst-case estimate will be calculated from a most likely estimate:
xbest = 0.7 · xlikely
(4.3)
xworst = 2 · xlikely
(4.4)
The procedure above follows the practice of cost estimation established by AACE
international (the Association for the Advancement of Cost Engineering) [6]. Accord-
ing to their recommended practice, the expected accuracy range for a cost estimate
for a concept screening with a limited project definition is -20% to -50% and +30%
to +100%. As can be seen from Eq. 4.3 and 4.4, an accuracy range of -30% and
+100% is used in this thesis.
3. Three estimates for the Lang factor is made. The minimum value is chosen to be 2.5,
the most likely value is expected to be 3 and the maximum value is set to 4.
4. A base estimate is calculated for the total cost using Eq. 4.2 by using the most likely
values for all parameters.
5. A probability distribution is specified for the inputs. It is assumed that the Lang factor
and the quantities follows a PERT distribution while the cost is assumed to have an
Erlang distribution. The PERT distribution and the Erlang distribution are based on
three and two parameters, respectively. The parameters are determined from the
estimates established in Step 2 and 3 according to the practice used in Harper &
Vedel [7].

This section presents the results for the frequencies of CIP start delays and CIP interrup-

The results from the analysis of possible CIP start delays and CIP interruptions are pre-

The maximum number of interruptions of CIP in FDA and Gpack are identical because they
have the same ’not ready’ requirement. The maximum number of interruptions for the
two departments is 8 with an average duration of approximately 8 min. This means that
during the considered month of operation, the Gpack had to restart a CIP up to 8 times.
Since the FDA and Gpack have different ’ready’ requirements, the maximum number of
CIP start delays differs between the two departments.
For the FDA the maximum number of CIP delays is 200 with an average duration of 7.1
min while it is only 119 for GPack with an average duration of 6.5 min. This means that
for 3.3% of the time during the considered month of operation, it has not been possible
to start a new CIP in FDA and for 1.8% of the time it has not been possible to start a new
CIP in Gpack.
For the UHT2, the maximum number of CIP start delays is 468 with an average duration
of 9.1 min meaning that as a maximum 9.9% of the time a new CIP cannot be started for
the UHT2. In reality this number is most likely much lower because it expresses periods
where the actual neutralization capacity have been lower than 35% which in many cases
is expected to be caused by the CIP of the UHT2 itself.
Table 5.6 shows a high value for the maximum duration of the CIP interruptions for the
UHT2. As discussed in section 5.1, significant amounts of wastewater are disposed to the
neutralization when the UHT2 is cleaned. Starting the CIP of the UHT2 at a time where
the actual capacity is above but close to 35% will probably result in an interruption of
the CIP of the UHT2 itself because the actual capacity quickly decreases to 25% (the ’not
==PAGE:61==

61
ready’ requirement for the UHT2). If a high number of CIPs are running in FDA and Gpack
at that time, the actual capacity may reach 0% which cause CIP interruptions in these two
departments. In that situation, all three neutralization tanks are full. When the first tank
has finished discharging, the actual capacity will immediately become 33.33%. This value
is greater than the ’ready’ requirement for FDA and Gpack and therefore the CIPs in these
departments will continue and restart, respectively. The actual capacity will decrease but
only until the next tank has emptied where the actual capacity increases with 33.33% and
most likely will become above 35%. Then, the CIP of the UHT2 will continue and proceed
as long as the actual capacity is greater than 25%. This thought example illustrates how
the CIP of the UHT2 can be interrupted multiple times, especially when it is started at a
time where a large number of other CIPs are running. In addition, the start of a CIP of
the UHT2 at an inconvenient time may cause interruptions and delays of CIPs in FDA and
Gpack.

ruptions for all departments/units. The minimum observed duration is around 12 s while
the maximum delay is more than an hour. A small duration is probably occurring when
the actual capacity becomes below one of the requirements shortly before a tank is done
discharging and the actual capacity immediately increases by 33.33%. The minor CIP
delays are out of importance opposite the long CIP delays which may cause delays in the
production.
A case study has been made for the three longest CIP start delays observed in FDA during
September 2023. The detailed study is shown in Appendix C. This study shows that long
delays are mainly caused by long neutralization times. The long neutralization times were
often caused by failures of adding HCl. In another case, the reason for the long duration
of the neutralization process was due to an repeatedly overshooting of the amount of
chemicals used. In one case the long neutralization time could not be explained because
the addition of chemicals was not visible in the automation system.

5.4 Summary of the results
In this section, the results will be summarized and discussed to identify the main issues
and bottlenecks of the neutralization system. This will form the basis for the generation
of possible solutions for optimizing the neutralization system which is the topic of the
subsequent chapter.
The average neutralization time is 8.6 min and it has been determined that 70% of the
neutralization times were below this value during the considered week of operation. The
average discharge time is 17.9 min. It is found that for 95% of the neutralization cycles
occurring during a week, the average discharge time is longer than the neutralization
time.
As discussed in section 3.2 and illustrated in Figure 3.2, the neutralization cycle time
will be unaffected by the neutralization time when it is lower than the discharge time.
Therefore, the data analysis shows that the bottleneck for the neutralization process is
most often the discharging.
Based on the average discharge time and the average neutralization time a theoretical
minimum filling time can be determined based on the expressions presented in Table 3.3
in section 3.2. This table shows that the minimum filling time is equal to the discharge
time when the discharging takes longer time than the neutralization. Currently, 16% of
the filling times during the considered week of operation are lower than the theoretical
minimum filling time. This may indicate a large number of CIP interruptions but in the
real system, a low filling time does not necessarily result in CIP delays if the subsequent
fillings are slow.
The number of occurrences of CIP start delays and CIP interruptions were determined
when considering a month of operation. It was found that 3.3% of the time, new CIPs
could not be started in FDA and for 1.8% of the time, new CIPs could not be started in
G-pack. The long CIP delays has the greatest chance of causing delays in the production
and it was observed that the long CIP delays were mainly caused by long neutralization
times.
The analysis of CIP delays showed how the flexibility of running a CIP is limited by the
available capacity of the neutralization. It is of interest to investigate if the CIP delays and
CIP interruptions has caused delays in the production. This would require information
about which of the units that have had their CIP delayed/interrupted as well as an insight
into the production plan and the actual production execution during the considered month
of operation. Such a detailed study has been considered too time consuming.
==PAGE:63==
Chapter 5. Data analysis
63
Another approach could have been to set up a simulation of the plant to predict the
production capacity with and without delays on the different types of CIPs. However, the
set up of that simulation is complicated by the fact, that the production plant consists of
two independent lines that produces various different products which requires different
conditions. Therefore, it has not been possible to link the operation of the neutralization
system to a specific value for the production capacity. However, it has been shown that new
CIPs cannot be started at all times which may limit the production capacity. As mentioned,
new CIPs could not be started for 3.3% of the time in FDA. This number will increase when
increasing the production because an increase in the production entails a larger amount
of wastewater.
A correlation between an increase in production capacity and the percentage of time
where CIPs are delayed is difficult to make because a distribution over time for the extra
amount of wastewater is required. The distribution is necessary to determine the progress
of the actual capacity of the neutralization from which the periods with CIP delays can be
identified. Even though the implication of an increase in production capacity has not been
predicted, it can be concluded that delays in CIPs are already occurring with the current
production capacity. The production capacity can only be increased as long as the extra
amount of wastewater will not result in additional delays in CIPs that will cause delays in
the planned production.

6.4 Automation updates
6.4.1
Change set points for the pre-neutralization
Motivation and inspiration
The current set point for the pre-neutralization defines the acceptable range for the pH to
be between 8.5 and 11.5. This means that if the incoming wastewater cause the content
in the neutralization tank to have a pH greater than 11.5, the pre-neutralization will stop
when it is reduced to pH 11.5. Therefore, the current operation of the pre-neutralization
cause the start pH at the final neutralization to be above pH 11 for approximately 50% of
the neutralizations as discussed in section 5.2.2. Since the duration of the neutralization
increases when the start pH is further away from the set point of the neutralization, it is
beneficial to ensure a start pH close to the acceptable range for the neutralization.
Description
It is possible to obtain a start pH that is closer to the acceptable range for the neutralization
if the set point for the pre-neutralization is lowered. Implementing this solution only
involves the change of one or two parameters in the automation system. These parameters
are the set point of the pre-neutralization (currently pH 10) and the acceptable deviation
from the set point (currently ± 1.5).
Risks and benefits
Lowering the set point for the pre-neutralization is expected to reduce the duration of the
neutralization and thereby increase the capacity of the neutralization system. However,
changing the set point for the pre-neutralization to reduce the pH leads to an increased
use of acid which may not be necessary in cases where a caustic flush is followed by an
acidic flush. Furthermore, the extra use of chemicals during the pre-neutralization will
take up some of the capacity intended for the wastewater.
6.4.2
Increase the limit for the filling of the neutralization tanks
Motivation and inspiration
The limit for the filling of each neutralization tank is 17,000 kg which leaves room for the
addition of up to 3,000 kg of chemicals before reaching the maximum allowed content
of 20,000 kg. It was shown in section 5.2.2, that most of the time less than 1,000 kg
of chemicals are added to perform the final neutralization. This means an unexploited
capacity of 2,000 kg in many cases. Thus, it seems possible to increase the limit for the
filling of the tanks.
==PAGE:71==
Chapter 6. Idea generation
71
Description
Increasing the limit for the filling of the tank only requires changing the set point for the
content in a tank at which the filling must end. It seems that the limit of the filling can be
raised to 18,000 kg which will result in almost 6% extra capacity.
Another possibility is to define the limit for the filling of the neutralization tanks depending
on certain scenarios. However, this solution is more complex because it will require alter-
ations in the programming of the neutralization system rather than simply changing a set
point. On the other hand, this has already been partially implemented because the filling
will stop already at 16,000 kg if the pH is below 2.5 or above 11.5. It may be possible to in-
crease this limit as it seems that the neutralization requires less than approximately 1,000
kg even when the pH is very low or high. An additional scenario that can be implemented
is to continue the filling after 17,000 kg if the pH is within the acceptable range. A more
thorough analysis should be made to define the optimal maximum filling limit in this case.
Risks and benefits
Increasing the limit for the filling leads to an increase in the capacity. However, there is
a risk of overfilling the tanks meaning that the content in the tank will reach the maxi-
mum of 20,000 kg without being completely neutralized. As a consequence, some of the
wastewater must be discharged to the sump to leave room for the addition of chemicals to
finalize the neutralization. Subsequently, the wastewater in the sump must be pumped to
one of the tanks to be neutralized. This process is time consuming and not ideal because
some of the wastewater is discharged to the sump which is open to the atmosphere and
not intended for regular use.
The risk of overfilling can be mitigated with the scenario based definition of the filling
limit or by making it possible to transfer wastewater between the neutralization tanks
which currently is not possible.
6.4.3
Include feed-forward control based on wastewater composition
Motivation and inspiration
For the current operation of the pre-neutralization, the analysis presented in section 5.2.2
showed that in many cases the pH is above pH 11.5 which is the upper limit for the set
point of the pre-neutralization. One reason for this behavior may be that there is not time
for neutralizing the last flush to the tank before the filling ends and the final neutralization
starts. This may be solved by including feed-forward control to the regulation of the pH
during pre-neutralization.
==PAGE:72==
72
Chapter 6. Idea generation
Description
The conductivity is already measured at the various CIP returns which may be used in
a feed-forward control of the pre-neutralization process. The conductivity of a flush is
related to the concentration of ions and therefore does not distinguish between acid or
base. Therefore, using the existing conductivity measurements must be accompanied by
information about the type of flush. This information is available. Otherwise, pH sensors
must be installed at the CIP returns.
A more complex solution involves the incorporation of predictions of the wastewater
composition in the regulation of the pre-neutralization process. It is near impossible to
predict when specific CIPs are run because there is not a fixed pattern for when the various
units are used due to the production of different products. However, when a particular CIP
is requested it is possible to estimate when acid or base will enter the neutralization tanks
as well as the concentrations. In this way, the chemicals used during pre-neutralization
may be reduced because the knowledge about the composition of the wastewater gives
the opportunity to wait with the addition of chemicals in cases where both a caustic and
an acidic flush enters the tank.
Risks and benefits
Implementing this solution is expected to ensure a pH in the beginning of the neutraliza-
tion that is within the set point range for the pre-neutralization. In this way the duration
of the final neutralization process can be decreased. Furthermore, if a predictive control
of the pre-neutralization can be implemented, a reduction in the use of chemicals may
be achieved. Using the conductivity sensors and information about the type of flush is a
simpler solution. However, one concern is that the time it takes the wastewater to travel
from the conductivity sensor to the neutralization tanks is to small.
6.4.4
Avoid unnecessary incomplete discharges
Motivation and inspiration
A case study presented in section 5.2.3 showed that it is not always necessary to interrupt
the discharging process when the content reaches 1,500 kg and another tank is full. The
application of this rule only considers two of the tanks. It is expected that unnecessary
incomplete discharges can be avoided by also considering the third tank which is filling.
==PAGE:73==
Chapter 6. Idea generation
73
Description
A simple way to avoid unnecessary interruptions of the discharge is to add a constraint
to the rule expressing a limit for the level in the tank that is filling. Only if the content in
the filling tank is above the limit, mconstraint filling, when the content of the discharging tank
reaches 1,500 kg, the discharge will stop. In this way the discharge phase will operate
according to the following rule:
When a tank is discharging while another neutralization tank is full, the discharge
will stop when the content reaches 1,500 kg if the content in the tank that is filling
is above mconstraint filling.
The limit for the filling tank can be defined to be
mconstraint filling = mfull −rmax filling · (tfull discharge −tincomplete discharge)
(6.3)
where
mconstraint filling: The content limit of the tank that is filling at which the discharging of
another tank is interrupted if the third tank is full.
mfull: The content at which the filling phase of a tank stops.
rmax filling: The expected maximum filling rate.
tfull discharge: Duration of a full discharge.
tincomplete discharge: Duration of an incomplete discharge.
Another option is to introduce the possibility of interruption the discharge phase at any
content in the discharge tank (not only at 1,500 kg) depending on a defined constraint for
the content in the filling tank, mconstraint filling. In this case the discharge phase will operate
according to the following rule:
When a tank is discharging while another neutralization tank is full, the discharge
will stop when the content in the tank that is filling is above mconstraint filling.
The limit for the content in the filling tank can be defined as
mconstraint filling = mfull −rmax filling · tswitch
(6.4)
where
tswitch: A buffer time to account for fluctuations and to allow the system some time
to switch between the phases for the neutralization tanks.
Risks and benefits
Avoiding unnecessary incomplete discharges results in an increased capacity as a content
of 1,500 kg constitutes 8.8% of the current capacity of 17,000 kg. However, the benefit of
implementing this solution is only gained in limited cases.

6.4.5
Include automatically delays of non-critical CIPs
Motivation and inspiration
The request and start of CIPs occur automatically when a piece of equipment has been used.
This means that many units upstream are cleaned simultaneously because they are done
being used approximately at the same time. The same applies for the units downstream.
Thus, the load to the system can be very high in some periods and very low in other periods
which was shown by the broad distribution of filling times in section 5.2.1.
In periods where the load to the neutralization is high, there is a risk of requested CIPs
being delayed or ongoing CIPs being interrupted. As a result the production may become
delayed. The delay in the production will only occur if the delayed/interrupted CIPs are
bottlenecks for the next production step and the duration of the delay/interruption is
significant. Therefore, it could be beneficial to prioritize specific CIPs and deliberately
postponing non-critical CIPs in situations where the available capacity of neutralization
system is usually limited.
Description
The implementation of solutions where CIPs are prioritized requires an assessment of the
importance of the CIPs of the various units. To maximize the production, the fermenters
must be cleaned immediately after discharging the product. In this way the fermenters
will be ready for a new batch as quickly as possible. Therefore, the CIP of the fermenters
is critical.
In cases where the the yield from the fermentation is high, a large amount of product must
be processed in the pelletizers which is time consuming. In such cases, the pelletizers may
become the bottleneck for the production and should therefore be cleaned immediately.
A simple possibility of prioritizing CIPs in FDA is to define requirements for starting/continuing
CIP depending on the actual capacity of the neutralization system for each unit instead of
the entire FDA. This has already been done for the UHT2. In this way the ’ready’ require-
ment could be decreased for fermenters and pelletizers while it can increased for other
units. Furthermore, the ’not ready’ requirement can be increased for non-critical units. The
challenge with the implementation of this solution is that the choice of requirements is
based on trial-and-error.
A more complex solution could involve prioritizing and delaying CIPs based on predictions
of the load to the neutralization system. When a new CIP is requested it is possible to
estimate if starting the CIP will result in a actual capacity of 0% before the new CIP is
done. The estimation can be made from the current actual capacity, the current phases of
the tanks and the wastewater contribution as a function of time for the new CIP as well as
for the CIPs that is already progressing.

6.5.2
Avoid directing water flushes from the UHTs to the neutraliza-
tion
Motivation and inspiration
The calculations of the wastewater contributions from the cleaning of the individual units
in FDA in section 5.1 showed that the UHTs (especially UHT2) put an extra load on the
neutralization system compared to the other units. One reason for this result is that all the
water flushes of the UHTs are directed to the neutralization. In most cases this is not nec-
essary because the water only contains large amounts of caustic and acid residues in the
initial part of the water flush. The last part of the water flushes does not require neutral-
ization and it will therefore be beneficial if these flushes are not send to the neutralization
tanks.
Description
All units have water flushes as a part of their CIP programs. However, one reasons that
the UHTs put an extra load to the neutralization system is that all the water flushes of
the UHTs are directed to the neutralization whereas the last water flushes of other units
are collected and used in the first water flushes of the units. Thus, a possible optimization
==PAGE:77==
Chapter 6. Idea generation

of the CIP of the UHTs is to similarly reuse the last water flush. In this context it can be
considered if the UHTs should have their own tanks for the recycled water or if the water
should be directed to the existing recirculated water tanks. Furthermore, the conditions
for when the water can be reused must be established. It may be possible to use the same
requirements as defined for the other units which are based on conductivity as discussed
in section 3.3.1.
Another possibility for limiting the amount of wastewater to the neutralization when
cleaning the UHTs is to send parts of the water flushes directly to the sewage system.
Even though the existing conductivity sensor on the CIP return for the UHTs can be used
as a basis for where the water should be directed, it may be necessary to implement a
pH sensor. If the pH of the water flushes is continuously measured and it is within the
acceptable range, it is not necessary to process the water in the neutralization tanks.
Both solutions to avoid directing water flushes from the cleaning of the UHTs to the
neutralization requires new pipe lines, new valves and adjustments in the automation
system. Reusing the water may require a new vessel while sending the water directly to
the sewage system may require a new pH sensor.
Risks and benefits
Both of the suggested solutions will result in a reduced load to the neutralization system
when the UHTs are cleaned. Furthermore, the water consumption is reduced if some of
the water can be reused.
The possibility of reusing the water must be analyzed in more details. It is uncertain if
there is room for an additional vessel or if the capacity of the existing recycled water
tanks used by the other units is large enough to receive water from the UHTs as well. In
addition, the UHTs are considered particularly contaminated with product residues after
use because the high temperatures in these units can cause the organic materials to burn
on to the surface of the walls. Thus, there may be a risk that the wastewater from the
cleaning of the UHTs contains a higher amount of compounds that when being reused can
deposit on valves etc.
If it is not possible to reuse the water, a concern for the implementation of the other
possible solution is that it is unknown if the sewage system can handle receiving the water.

6.6.1
Reuse caustic flush of UHTs
Motivation and inspiration
The entire amount of caustic used for the cleaning of the UHTs is directed to the neutral-
ization whereas parts of the caustic flush of the other units are reused. Thus, a possible
improvement is to also reuse the caustic flush of the UHTs.
Description
All units uses a NaOH solution (∼1%) as the caustic flush except for the UHT1 which uses
a P3-mip solution. The following suggestions will only apply to UHT2.
The caustic flush of the UHT2 may be reused in a similar way as for the other units. In
this case the caustic flush is either directed to the neutralization or a NaOH tank based on
conductivity. Currently, the UHT2 has one CIP tank in which all CIP fluids are prepared.
Therefore, implementing this solution requires a separate CIP tank to collect the NaOH
solution. Otherwise, the caustic flush can be directed to the NaOH tank used by the
remaining equipment on L2.
In the current CIP procedure for the UHT2, approximately 2,000 L of NaOH solution
is prepared and recirculated during the caustic flush. If the NaOH solution is directed
to either the neutralization or a NaOH tank for reuse instead of being recirculated it is
uncertain if 2,000 L is enough.
Another possibility is to introduce a new unit operation for the recovery of the caustic
solution. In this way the current set up with preparing 2,000 L NaOH solution and recircu-
lating it can be maintained. At the end of the cleaning step, the 2,000 L of NaOH solution
can be directed to a caustic recovery unit.
The caustic recovery unit can also be used by the remaining units which direct parts of
the caustic flush to the neutralization before the set points for temperature, flow rate and
conductivity are reached. A commercialized caustic recovery process involves membrane
technologies [4].
Risks and benefits
Reusing the caustic flush of the UHT2 will reduce the load to the neutralization system
and the consumption of NaOH and water.

6.6.2
Introduce an extra neutralization tank
Motivation and inspiration
The data analysis in section 5.3 showed how the current capacity of the neutralization sys-
tem consisting of three tanks limits the flexibility of running CIPs in different departments
at the site. The load to the neutralization system is expected to increase in the future due
to the launch of the NFDP department and an interest in increasing the production on L2.
In order to make the current and future executions of CIPs independent of the neutraliza-

Description
It is suggested to install an extra neutralization tank with the same functionalities as the
existing tanks. The installation of an extra tank first involves finding a physical location
for the tank which must be placed in a sump. Secondly, various pipe connections must
be made to connect the new tank to the pipes with the incoming wastewater as well as
connecting it to the existing chemical tanks and the sewage system. Furthermore, electrical
installation of new valves and dosing pumps for the addition of chemicals are required.
Finally, changes must be implemented in the automation system to be able to control the
operation of the new tank.
Risks and benefits
Introducing an extra neutralization tank will increase the physical capacity of the neu-
tralization system. Furthermore, the addition of an extra tank establishes a more robust
neutralization system in case of unexpected failures as well as planned maintenance of the
tanks. However, the benefit of installing an extra tank will only be gained in cases where
the neutralization time exceeds the discharge time. This is illustrated in Figure 6.3 which

running CIPs with the current load to the system which is expected to increase substantially
in the future due to the opening of the NFDP department and the aim of increasing the
production capacity.

neutralization capacity is options involving minor or major process modifications. These

8. Avoid directing water flushes from the UHTs to the neutralization.

11. Introduce an extra neutralization tank.
No. 8 and 10 are suggestions that both decrease the load to the neutralization system
whereas No. 9 and 11 are modifications on the operation of the neutralization system. Both
of these aspects are worth analyzing. However, solution No. 10 is expected only to decrease
the load to the neutralization to a minor degree and therefore, it is of more interest to
examine solution No. 8. Furthermore, the data analysis showed that the bottleneck for the
neutralization process is most often the discharging. Therefore, it is prioritized to analyze
solution No. 9 which involves the installation of a pump to decrease the discharging time
rather than considering No. 11 regarding an extra tank which is only beneficial when the
neutralization time exceeds the discharge time.
To summarize, the solutions that will be further analyzed are:
8. Avoid directing water flushes from the UHTs to the neutralization.
9. Establish a constant discharging rate.

7.2 Reusing water flush of the UHT2
7.2.1
Conceptual design
This section will present the conceptual design for reusing parts of the last water flush in
the cleaning of the UHT2. In this design, the following functionalities must be included:
• Requirements must be established for when the water in the last flush of the UHT2
can be reused.
• The water that can be reused must be stored until it can be used as a part of the first
water flush in another CIP.
• The temperature of the water must be adjusted when it is reused in another CIP
according to the requirements described in the CIP procedures.
Either the UHT2 can have its own recirculated water tank or the water can be directed to
the existing recirculated water tanks used by the other units on L2. One concern about
using the existing tanks is the capacity. Therefore, it is decided to make a design where
the UHT2 gets its own recirculated water tank. In this way, the water of the last cleaning
step is collected and reused in the first water flush of the UHT2. If the water had been
directed to the existing recirculated water tanks, it could not have been used in the first
water flush of the UHT2 because the UHT2 requires soft water (IECW) to avoid deposits
on the walls.
The conceptual design of introducing a recirculated water tank for the UHT2 is visualized
in Figure 7.5 which incorporates the following equipment:
• Tanks: One tank to collect the recirculated water.
• Heat exchangers: A heat exchanger to establish the required temperature of the
water flush. In principle the UHT2 itself can adjust the temperature of the water
flush.
• Pumps: One pump to direct the water from the recirculated water tank to the CIP
tank. Depending on the location of the recirculated water tank, an extra pump may
be necessary for pumping the water to the recirculated water tank.
• Valves: 10-30 valves (only the 5 most essential valves are shown in the conceptual
design in Figure 7.5).
• Pipes: 20-100 m depending on the physical location of the recirculated water tank.
It seems unlikely that there is room for the tank in the FDA. It is unknown if the tank
can be positioned elsewhere inside the facility. Otherwise, it must be placed outside.

reusing the water of the last cleaning step of the UHT2. It has been estimated that the last

mS/cm. The procedure for making this estimate is described in Appendix E.

to ensure that the CIP delays were not caused by the neutralization.
It was observed that when the cleaning of the UHT2 caused CIP start delays, it was most
often during the first flush or during both flushes of the UHT2. Situations where it is
only the last flush that cause CIP delays are limited. Figure 7.7 shows a classical example
with reasonable neutralization times where the first flush causes short CIP delays in both
FDA and G-pack but where the actual capacity is relatively high during the last flush. The
reason for this behavior is as described in section 5.3. If the UHT2 is cleaned at the same
time as a number of other units, the actual capacity will quickly decrease and when it
becomes below 25% the CIP of the UHT2 will be put on hold. Meanwhile the other CIPs
will continue but as tanks are emptied the actual capacity increases and when it gets
above 35%, the CIP of the UHT2 will proceed. However, it will be put on hold if the actual
capacity again gets below 25%. In this way, the CIP of other units are prioritized which
means that often the last flush of the UHT2 occurs when most of the other units will have
completed their CIPs. This is illustrated in Figure 7.7, where it can be seen that the first
flush of the UHT2 is interrupted three times but where the last flush is uninterrupted and
does not cause the actual capacity to get low enough to cause CIP delays.

7.3 Recommendation
This section will present a comparison and discussion of the two selected solutions. In the
end, a recommendation will be made for Chr. Hansen about the strategy for enhancing
the neutralization system and increasing the production capacity.
The two selected solutions have been evaluated based on the expected impact on increas-
ing the production capacity and the estimated cost of implementing the solution.
The solution with the most significant impact on increasing the production capacity is the
option involving pumps for draining the neutralization tanks. The benefit of decreasing
the discharge time will be gained at all times in varying degrees depending on the neu-
tralization time. It was found that decreasing the discharge time will entail that up to an
additional 45% amount of wastewater can be processed. The most significant impact of
introducing pumps is gained when the neutralization time is below the new discharge
time which will be the case for at least 70% of the neutralization cycles (see section 5.4).
The solution involving process modifications of the CIP system of the UHT2 will only have
an impact on the load to the system when the UHT2 is cleaned. The main issue of the
neutralization system arise when the available capacity becomes low enough to delay or
interrupt CIPs. It was observed that it was only in limited cases that the last water flush
of the UHT2 caused CIP delays. However, increasing the production capacity will result in
more wastewater and if the last flush of the UHT2 is not reused the number of CIP delays
will increase.
The suggested budget for implementing pumps for draining the neutralization tanks is 4.1
mio. DKK while it is 8.3 mio. DKK for the solution of reusing the last water flush in the CIP
of the UHT2. Thus, the implementation of pumps results in the least expensive investment.
However, the payback period should also be taken into consideration. The payback period
is the time it takes to recover the cost of implementing the solutions. The payback period
can be determined based on the additional profit resulting from the increased production
capacity when implementing the solution as well as the expenses for the daily operation
(human resources used for maintenance, materials, electricity etc.). Both solutions have
extra expenses with respect to maintenance and electricity but the solution of reusing
water when cleaning the UHT2 will result in less expenses for water. The expenses for
water includes purchasing tap water, converting it to soft water and discharging it to the
municipal water treatment plant.
==PAGE:95==

As described in the analysis for both solutions (section 7.1.3 and 7.2.3), it has not been
possible to quantify how much the process modifications will increase the production
capacity. Therefore, it is not possible to determine the payback period in this thesis.
No major drawbacks have been identified for the two selected solutions and therefore it
is recommended to implement both suggestions if possible. In this case, the next step for
Chr. Hansen is to refine the designs of the solutions and to make a quantification of how
much the production capacity can be increased to calculate the expected payback period.
If both solutions are implemented, a reduced load to the neutralization system as well as
an optimized operation of the system will be achieved. However, if one of the two projects
must be prioritized, it should be the implementation of pumps for optimizing the draining
of the neutralization tanks. This option has the lowest cost and makes possible to process
more wastewater at all times.
In the current situation, the implementation of pumps will probably decrease the number
and duration of CIP delays. However, it is not possible to eliminate the occurrences of CIP
delays completely because some CIP delays are caused by extremely long neutralization
times. Since the neutralization times are generally low, the long neutralization times seem
to be caused by failures somewhere in the system. Thus, it is suggested to investigate how
sporadic long neutralization times can be eliminated.

Conclusion
The objective of this thesis was to identify how the neutralization system at the Chr. Hansen
site in Avedøre limits the production capacity and to find possible options for optimizing
the neutralization system such that the production capacity can be increased.
The operation and bottlenecks of the current neutralization system was examined based
on relevant process data. The load to the system was investigated by considering the
wastewater contributions from the cleaning of each unit in the fermentation department as
well as the distribution of filling times. It was found that the absolute largest contribution
of wastewater occurs when the UHT2 is cleaned. During a CIP of the UHT2 around 51,000
kg wastewater is send to the neutralization which is more than the double amount of the
second largest contributor.
The analysis of filling times showed that the load to the neutralization system is not evenly
distributed and the neutralization system does not limit the production capacity at all
times
An analysis of the frequency of CIP delays caused by the available capacity of the neu-
tralization system during a month of operation showed that 3.3% of the time new CIPs
could not be started in the fermentation department. This quantified and confirmed the
general knowledge from Chr. Hansen that the neutralization system causes problems in
the production.
A case study showed that the longest CIP delays in the fermentation department was
caused by long neutralization times. However, an analysis and comparison of discharge
and neutralization times revealed that most often the bottleneck for the neutralization
process is the discharge time.
Based on the data analysis a list of possible solution were identified to optimize the
neutralization system. A qualitative evaluation was performed, which resulted in a shortlist
of two solutions which were further analyzed. One solution suggested the implementation
of pumps to decrease the discharge time of the neutralization tanks. The other solution
proposed reducing the load to the neutralization system by reusing a large amount of the
last water flush of the UHT2.
==PAGE:98==
98
Chapter 8. Conclusion
Conceptual design were presented for both solutions to identify the major equipment items
and their cost in order to suggest a budget for implementing the solutions. The suggested
budget for introducing pumps is 4.1 mio. DKK while it is 8.3 mio. DKK for introducing
a tank for collecting the last water flush of the UHT2. Besides having the lowest cost,
the introduction of pumps also makes it possible to process more wastewater at all times
whereas reusing the last water flush of the UHT2 only makes it possible to reduce the load
to the system when the CIP of the UHT2 occurs.
It was shown that with the current production capacity the solution regarding the UHT2
only have minor impact. However, it is expected to have a more significant impact if the
production capacity is increased and more wastewater should be processed.
To be able to increase the production capacity at the Chr. Hansen site in Avedøre, it is
recommended to use pumps for draining the neutralization tanks as well as to reduce
the load to the neutralization system by reusing the last flush of the UHT2. Thus, the
next step for Chr. Hansen is to refine the designs of the solutions and to use a full model
of the facility to quantify how much the production capacity can be increased when also
considering other bottlenecks at the facility. Even if both solutions are introduced, CIPs may
still be delayed in cases with long neutralization times. Therefore, it is suggested to further
investigate how long neutralization times can be eliminated. However, the first priority
should be to implement the pumps for decrease the time for draining the neutralization
tanks.

E Estimate of the reduced water consumption for the CIP

In this appendix, the procedure for estimating how much of the last water flush that can
be reused when cleaning the UHT2 is described.
For the remaining units in FDA, the last water flush is directed to a recirculated water tank
when the conductivity becomes below 5 mS/cm. It is considered to establish the same
requirement for reusing the water of the last cleaning step of the UHT2.
The amount of wastewater in the last water flush that can be reused has been determined
from the following sets of data:
• Conductivity data: For points in time, the data express the conductivity [mS/cm] in
the CIP return against time.
• Valve position data: Described in Table 4.1.
• Flow rate data: Described in Table 4.1.
• CIP step plot: A plot of the CIP steps in the cleaning procedure of the UHT2.
For three randomly chosen CIPs of the UHT2, the following method was applied to calcu-
late the amount of wastewater that can be reused:
• In the automation system, the four data sets were plotted in the same window.
• The CIP step plot was used to find a time period where the UHT2 is cleaned.
• The conductivity data was used to zoom in on the last water flush when the conduc-
tivity was below 5 mS/cm.
• Alternately, the data for the valve position and the flow rate were extracted and
inserted in Excel.
• The amount of wastewater was determined in the same way as described in step 5
in the procedure for calculating the wastewater contributions from the cleaning of
specific units (see section 4.3.2).
Table 8 presents the calculated amount of the last water flush of the UHT2 that can be
reused when having considered three cases. The table shows that the average amount is
around 15,400 kg.
