Abstract
This thesis encompasses the design of a mechanical transmission system design for an innovative ma-
rine propulsion concept called the stadium propeller, designed specifically for DTU’s participation in
the Monaco Energy Boat Challenge. The stadium propeller consists of vertically oriented foils traveling
horizontally in a stadium shaped path. The theory proposes an energy-efficient alternative to traditional
screw propellers, improving the hydrodynamic efficiency significantly.
The project thoroughly investigates and evaluates various mechanical solutions to control the foil motion
and angle of attack. In conclusion a fully constrained double guide rod mechanism was determined as the
optimal solution to ensure operational reliability and high efficiency at design speed. An analytical and
numerical solver in MATLAB was developed to simulate the hydrodynamical forces and mechanical re-
sponses under varying operational conditions such as cruising, max speed, startup, reversing and braking.
Comprehensive studies, including hydrodynamical modeling using XFOIL and the extrapolation method
Viterna-Corrigan for post-stall conditions, were used to analyze the concept’s efficiency, predicting a
hydrodynamical efficiency of 85% and a mechanical efficiency of 91.5%. The mechanical system was
thoroughly evaluated resulting in a design proposal, including detailed analysis of the critical compo-
nents such as the cart, track and foil structure.
This thesis offers an extensive and detailed foundation for further experimental research and highlights
key mechanical and hydrodynamic aspects necessary for advancing the stadium propeller from theory
to a working prototype. The results confirm that it should be theoretically possible to reach an overall
efficiency that would be competitive compared to traditional screw propellers.

1
Introduction
The aim of this project is to contribute to the improvement in energy efficiency of marine propulsion
through development of the innovative stadium propeller concept. The propeller described in this project
is developed specifically for DTU’s entry into the Monaco Energy Boat Challenge. The design space is
large, and the concept includes a lot of uncertainty, which is explored through numerous design iterations
and detailed mechanical analysis.
This project provides insight into the stadium propeller concept by applying it directly to a real world
application. Through a rigorous development process, the concept is continuously refined with the goal
of transitioning the propeller from predominately a theoretical concept to a practical and mechanically
feasible propulsion unit.
The hydrodynamics of the stadium propeller is modeled based on existing models and fundamental
foil theory. The project also includes a purpose built analytical numerical solver that is used to model
and simulate the hydrodynamics and mechanics of the propeller depending on operating conditions, e.g.
speed of the vessel and propeller.
Based on the results of the predictive model and development of the concept, a design proposal is
carried out, detailing how the mechanical system is envisioned to be built. This includes design and
construction of the critical parts of the design as well as general considerations for component selection,
manufacturing and assembly.
The initial scope of the project included a working prototype intended for initial testing and valida-
tion, but was limited to a design proposal, due to the unforeseen complexities of the propeller concept
unraveled during the course of the development process.
One of the major contributions made by this project is the development of a mechanically controlled sta-
dium propeller movement retaining the core principles and functionality of the concept while facilitating
mechanical power transmission between the foils and motor unit.

Project background
While maritime transportation has proven to be one of the most energy-efficient modes of transport,
in 2018 it was still responsible for 2.9% of the global CO2 emissions[1]. In an effort to combat climate
change, an EU initiative mandates that vessels have to achieve net-zero emissions by 2050[2].
As a
consequence of this move to sustainability, more expensive and greener fuel alternatives are becoming
increasingly common leading to significant interest in improving vessels’ energy efficiency.
The Monaco Energy Boat Challenge (MEBC) started in 2013, and is an event where universities from all
over the world compete in multiple disciplines and are challenged to design, build, and race an energy-
efficient boat powered by alternative energy sources such as solar energy, hydrogen, and electric power.
The MEBC directly addresses key challenges facing the industry in the ongoing transition. In line with
the university’s focus on sustainability and research in novel technology, DTU will participate in the
challenge from 2025 onward. Among other new developments, the stadium propeller concept stands out
due to its novelty and potential benefits.
In an inspiring paper, Sanchez-Caja et al. [2022] proposed the innovative ship propeller concept, consist-
ing of multiple straight, vertical foils traveling horizontally around a circuit resembling a sports stadium.
This idea proposes a potential increase in open-water efficiency of 13% compared to a traditional pro-
peller.
Arguably, this concept, when compared to a traditional screw propeller, can be considered
revolutionary, provided it can be sufficiently implemented in practice.
2.1
Traditional propeller
In order to fully understand the concept proposed by Sanchez-Caja, it is helpful to consider some back-
ground information on the traditional screw propellers that are currently implemented on most marine
vessels. This design has been used as the main propulsion method for motorized marine vessels for over
a century due to its simplicity and high mechanical efficiency.
2
==PAGE:9==
Figure 1: Traditional screw propeller[3]
A typical screw propeller works by converting rotational energy into thrust based on Newton’s third
law. As the propeller rotates, it pushes water towards the stern and in response the vessel is propelled
forwards, leaving a wake behind it.
Looking closely at the screw propeller, it is made up of blades that resemble wings with a very no-
ticeable twist along the span. The blades create axial thrust at the cost of a driving torque, the ratio
between which correlates strongly to the efficiency. The forward speed of the vessel and the rotational
speed of the propeller shaft also impact the efficiency.
A possible area for improving the screw propeller is its hydrodynamic efficiency, which is normally in the
range of 60-80% [4], depending on the specific design. The shortfall in efficiency is mainly attributable
to vortex losses, viscous friction acting on the surface of the propeller blades, and residual tangential
flow downstream of the propeller. Cavitation can generate further losses.
After many years of industry optimization, the traditional screw propeller design has reached a stage
of maturity where further improvements yield diminishing returns compared to the significant advance-
ments achieved in the past. [4] There are some factors that impact the screw propeller’s efficiency that
3
==PAGE:10==
is hard to remedy, such as tip vortex formation and cavitation due to pressure differences or drag from
the blade itself.
2.2
Fixed wing aircraft
Much of aviation is based around the same core principle as the screw propeller. when an aircraft is
moving fast enough the wings will generate an upwards lift force large enough to carry the aircraft.
During take off the wings will have an increased angle of attack to generate more lift but at the cost of
less efficiency.
Figure 2: Cross-section of a typical plane wing under various conditions [5]
The typical aircraft wing profiles typically resemble the NACA2412 or NACA4412 or similar airfoils that
have good lift to drag ratios. Airfoils are normally characterized based on camber, thickness to chord
ratio as well as leading and trailing edge shape.
2.3
Voith-Schneider propeller
The Voith-Schneider Propeller (VSP) is a propulsion system which draws heavily on a lot of the theory
developed for aircraft. Unlike traditional screw propellers, the VSP consists of a circular arrangement of
vertically mounted adjustable blades that rotate around a central hub. This system works particularly
well for vessels that require exceptional maneuverability and stationary thrust capability such as tug-
boats.
Each blade in the VSP acts like a wing, generating lift as it moves through the water.
The blade
pitch is continuously adjusted during rotation, allowing the system to control the direction and magni-
tude of thrust dynamically. This makes it possible to achieve highly responsive thrust in any direction
4
==PAGE:11==
without requiring the vessel to change its orientation.
Figure 3: Voith Schneider Propeller [6]
The VSP has a high hydrodynamic efficiency compared to standard screw propellers when used in its
typical low-speed application where precise thrust control is needed[7], however, at higher vessel speeds
the advantage is diminished since the incoming flow from the vessel advance speed will have a significant
drag effect on some of the foils. The inefficiency stems from the vertical foils of the VSP not having full
angular freedom to be at the most optimal angle at all times around the circular pattern.
The stadium propeller can be considered a derivative of the VSP, and shares some important similarities
as well as significant differences.

3
Main theory of the stadium propeller
In this section the main hydrodynamic theory and working principle of the stadium propeller is covered.
Due to foil theory being an essential aspect of the analysis and further development of the propeller
concept, this section also provides an overview of the fundamental principles and terminology.
3.1
Stadium propeller
The stadium propeller concept works by considering a number of foils moving along a stadium shaped
circuit. The main advantages of such a system compared to a screw propeller includes:
A) Better hydrodynamic efficiency, B) less cavitation, C) Better access to propeller blades, D) possible
removal of the rudder and E) good directional thrust control.
Disadvantages include: A) Vertical axis drive, B) Complexity of pitch control, C) generally using more
moving parts and D) Expected lower mechanical efficiency.
Figure 4: Motion of the foils in the stadium-shaped path
Figure 4 illustrates the motion of the foils of the propeller and serves as an overview of the concept.
The foils move identically to one another, and are equally spaced around the track. The foils enter four
characteristic sections; two straight sections and two curved sections. When moving in a straight section,
the foil is actively generating thrust, and when moving in a curved section, the foil is in an inactive tran-
6
==PAGE:13==
sition state. When one foil leaves a curved section, another foil simultaneously enters a curved section,
ensuring consistent thrust delivery.
The stadium propeller concept requires two counter-rotating propulsion units to ensure that the re-
sulting propulsive force is forwards along the longitudinal axis. The foil movement in the two curved
sections is different, one moving with the leading edge upstream and one with the trailing edge down-
stream. Consequently, having only one stadium propeller would result in adding a yaw moment to the
vessel.
This is more efficient than other concepts like the Voith-Schneider propeller due to its long straight
sections, here the foils can generate efficient thrust by having the optimal angle of attack (AoA) relative
to the inflow velocity that the foil sees. The shape generates some difficulties when it comes to the
mechanical drive system, since the angle of the foil should ideally vary with the vessel speed, but still
has to move in the desired shape.
The Voith-Schneider propeller moves in a circular motion, something that can be achieved with a rela-
tively simple linkage system that alters all the foil angles at once. Usually it is not a challenge to move
something in a straight or circular path, the complexity arises when these paths are combined.

3.2
Fundamental foil theory
Figure 5: Diagram of a symmetrical NACA foil profile
At its core, the stadium propeller uses foils to generate lift, and by extension, thrust.
This project
considers exclusively NACA foils, which are a standardized set of foil profiles commonly used in practical
applications. For NACA foils, the geometry of the profile is defined by 4 characteristic digits. The first
two digits describe camber, which affects the symmetry of the profile, and the last two digits describe
the thickness of the foil as a percentage of the chord c, itself defined as the distance from leading to
trailing edge. Provided the foils are rigid, the profile is required to be symmetrical, i.e. without camber.
This is due to the foils alternating their directions on the two straight sections, which requires symmetry.
Consequently, only foils of the type NACA00XX are considered.

3.3
Efficiency
A high hydrodynamic efficiency is the largest potential benefit of using this propulsion system. The
efficiency is defined as the ratio of thrust power to drive power. Essentially, how much power is needed
to drive the propeller relative to how much power is associated with the forward motion of the ship.

delivered by the powertrain.

Mechanical efficiency can be calculated from the power delivered to the propulsion system compared to

One of the main challenges for this project is to design a transmission that can maintain the high

Figure 8: Guide rod concept
Figure 8 shows the guide rod concept for controlling the foil angle. A rod offset from the foil’s axis

Figure 9 shows the cam follower concept for controlling the foil angle. A follower rigidly connected to

the trailing edge will point into the local water stream, resulting in extremely high angles of attack in

cruising speeds presents a major disadvantage of the concept, with additional concerns for the complexity
of having multiple stages/gears for active or manual control of the foils.

5.2
Spring- and cam system
It is imperative that the spring system match the moment curve seen in figure 19 as closely as possible
so that the AoA will generate the most efficient thrust at all times. Since the moment curve appears
to be non-linear the spring system should aim to behave somewhat similarly. Non-linear springs such
as Belleville springs combined with a cam-arm are analyzed to determine how precise the curve can be
matched.
Figure 20: Belleville springs
Belleville springs, also known as disk springs, are made from circular disks that have been formed into
a conical shape. These springs have the advantage that the force-displacement curve can be designed to
achieve non-linear behavior. The springs can be stacked in either parallel, series or combined configu-
rations to achieve the desired stiffness and travel. The thickness to height ratio of the disks affect the
nonlinearity, with high ratios having more linear spring rates and lower ratios being more non-linear.
The force-deflection relationship can be determined from eq. (2.72) in "Machine Elements Analysis and
Design, second edition [11]. The force-deflection equation is highly non-linear and therefore has use cases
for specialized applications. The characteristic curves can be seen in Appendix A.
By also utilizing a preload on the spring stack, it is possible to get a large range of different spring
behavior. Since it is not the foil itself that is in contact with the spring system but a cam-arm, the arm
can be designed to assist in achieving the needed moment. By utilizing different shapes for the cam arm,
such as ellipse, rhombus, semicircle or even an arbitrary geometry, the design possibilities increase.
5.3
AoA stability
In a spring system like this, stability is a key factor to consider in relation to the design robustness. An
analysis is carried out to determine what happens if the AoA varies from the set angle. Ideally, the foil
will converge back to the desired foil angle, however, if it diverges from the desired foil angle the solution
would be unstable and not work. This stability is strongly dependent on the location of the pivot point.
If placed around the center of pressure, it could be considered marginally stable, realistically, it should
25
==PAGE:32==
be placed a fair distance toward the leading edge to provide enough stiffness for the stability. If placed
further toward the trailing edge than the center of pressure, the system becomes unstable, as the foil
attempts to invert itself.
5.4
Conclusion
From the hydrodynamic moment curves in figure 19, it is clear that it is very difficult, even using both a
non-linear spring and cam system, to closely match the required moment. Furthermore, assuming such a
system could be achieved, the vessel would not be able to accelerate, since the system is only designed for
cruising, which is a required feature of a propeller. Due to these critical factors, the passively controlled
spring- and cam system has not been developed further in this project.

Concept evaluation: Mechanically adjusted angle
Since the spring- and cam system was determined to be unfeasible, the guide rod concept is selected for
further analysis and evaluation as a potential solution. Here, the foil angle is controlled via a predefined
guideway connected to an offset guide rod. The foil angle on the straight sections can be altered by
displacing the guideway, which does not affect the curved sections since they are free to align with the
flow direction, thus ensuring they are not actively generating thrust. Since the foils are given rotational
freedom in the curved sections, it is important to consider their motion. The two curved sections are
distinctly different, due to the local flow conditions. Recall the section numbering from figure 4. The
local flow is determined from the drive and advance speed, at section 4. This is fairly straightforward
since the foil will always be pointing the leading edge forward. However, at section 2 a problematic
phenomenon occurs at advance ratios around 1, i.e. drive speed and advance speed being close to equal,
as the local water flow approaches zero.
Figure 21: Foil rotation directions
Figure 21 shows the motion of the foil in section 2 at two operational conditions around advance ratio

marks the path of the foil’s pivot point as it travels along the stadium track, and the green line marks

The analysis shows that the foil will rotate either clockwise or counterclockwise in this section, depend-
ing on the flow of the water. At advance ratios below 1, e.g. during startup, the foil rotates clockwise
similarly to the Voith-Schneider propeller, while at advance ratios above 1, e.g. during cruising, the foil
retains its orientation by rotating counterclockwise, as was originally intended in the stadium propeller
concept.
Operating the propeller at or around an advance ratio of 1 is problematic, as the local magnitude
of flow approaches standstill and the foil orientation is instantaneously indeterminate. The foil also has
inertia which is unaccounted for in this analysis, resulting in a very difficult to predict orientation of the
foil under these conditions. One solution could be to avoid operating the foil at advance ratio J = 1,
however this is very hard to achieve as the conditions will always cross this point momentarily when
going from startup to cruise conditions.
This behavior raises significant concerns with allowing the foils rotational freedom in the curved sec-
tions, as the foil could potentially be inverted upon entering the guideway in the following straight
section, which would be entirely unintended and likely lead to catastrophic failure of the propeller. One
of the most important aspects of propulsion units is their operational reliability, and with this concept
there is an unacceptable amount of uncertainty associated with the rotational freedom in the curved
sections.
6.1
Analysis and design of a fully constrained foil path
A potential solution to increase the predictability and reliability of the foil motion involves extending
the narrow section of the guideway to the curved sections, thereby constraining the angle at every sec-
tion. Constraining the foils along the entire path does present some new challenges. Previously the foil
angle at the straight sections could be altered by displacing the guideways in a straightforward manner,
however, now that the foils are constrained along the entire path, moving the guideway will also impact
the curved segments. Different speed settings are analyzed to study how the optimal guideway changes
in shape, considering the guideway should ensure that the AoA in the curved sections is zero.
The optimal AoA and corresponding foil angle along the straight sections for each advance speed is
known from the previously referenced data contained in appendix B. This data is used as input to
determine the guideway path for the guide rod.

Conceptual prototype
In order to confirm that the guideway constrains the foil motion as intended, a simple prototype is built

Figure 23 shows the 3D printed prototype, focusing on the curved section 2. Experimenting with the

and only when, any change in the foil angle dθ would result in a reaction force between the guideway

Figure 24: Singularity point

removed: Uncertainty and unreliability. A singularity point allows the foil to invert itself and point the
trailing edge forward, achieving the opposite of the desired outcome and likely catastrophic failure of the
propeller.
Upon closer analysis, only one of the two singularity points is problematic, due to the hydrodynamic
moment working to right the foil at one point and inverting it at the other point.
One attempt at
combating the issue involves moving the pivot point behind the center of pressure, however this would
only switch the behavior of the two singularity points, and therefore not solve the issue.
Another attempt at preventing the undesired wrong rotation involves adding another mechanism to
the foil, like a ratchet mechanism that only allows rotation in one direction. One large flaw with the
ratchet mechanism however, is that it makes reversing the propeller very difficult as the foil is not allowed
to rotate the other way around. Adding a torsion spring that would allow some rotation opposite to the
ratchet locking mechanism is unfeasible, as the foil needs to fully rotate around the cart continuously,
winding or unwinding the spring.
6.3
double guide rod
Since the singularity point arises when the guideway angle is perpendicular to the foil chord line, a solution
is to add another guideway, so there would be two guide rods connected to two separate guideways. In this
way, the foil is perpendicular to only one of the guide tracks but never both, preventing any unintended
motion. This solution does come at the compromise of eliminating the option of controlling the foil angle

angle of the local flow - through the drive speed.

7.1
Transition between sections
When the foils move between sections, they alternate between active and inactive states by changing the
AoA. This change cannot happen instantaneously and thus requires a small transition distance. In order
to keep the thrust as constant as possible, i.e. retain the quality of one foil entering an active section
when another foil leaves an active section, the length of the straight sections must satisfy the following
equation:
L = 2C + 3ts
(7.1)
Where L is the length of the straight section, C is the length of the curved section and ts is the length of
the transition span. The transition in the angle of attack is of a linear nature. The length of the curved
section is determined from the radius given in the design specifications.

Figure 40 shows the code architecture of the numerical model. The model is built like a numerical solver,
with dedicated sections for preprocessing, solving and post-processing. Based on initial input parameters,
the program simulates the foil mechanics of the propeller at different locations of the stadium path and
utilizes custom functions to isolate specialized areas of the processing.
8.1
Path geometry
The path geometry function is used to determine the foil path and creates points along the path that is
used for determining the position of the foil throughout the program. Instead of prescribing the entire
foil path using simple geometric shapes, the geometry of the cart and track is used to determine the foil
location at each point numerically.
8.2
Foil data - XFOIL
In order to determine the hydrodynamic coefficients on the foil in the pre-stall domain, a dedicated
XFOIL function is used. Instead of compiling predetermined XFOIL data into a large dataset, which
from experience can cause problems with convergence and other curious inconsistencies, the instanta-
neous AoA and Reynolds number is used as input by calling the XFOIL function directly using command
prompt at each step automatically.
If XFOIL does not converge with a certain input this is not necessarily because of stalling, it can also
be due to XFOIL sensitivity, therefore a coefficient check has been created in the code to control that
XFOIL is converging.
If XFOIl is not converging then the main code will run XFOIL again but with a minimal change to the
AoA(+0.01◦) and the Reynolds number 1.05Re, this usually makes XFOIL converge correctly with a
minimal change in the output.
This method ensures the exact data is used, instead of e.g an interpolated dataset. The coefficients
are returned to the main code where they are adjusted for finite span effects and afterwards used to
determine the hydrodynamic forces.
8.3
Viterna-Corrigan approximation
Since XFOIL itself is not suitable for large AOA, the Viterna-Corrigan method is used to approximate
coefficients for AOA higher than α = 15◦. The function uses XFOIL to determine the stalling angle, lift
and drag coefficients at stall and lower AoA to extrapolate the data for the actual input AoA. The lift
coefficient is capped at 1.5 for a more realistic extrapolation, which is standard practice in industry.

Wheel reaction forces
The Wheel reaction forces function uses the lift force, drag force the angle of the cart and cart wheel
contact point vectors to calculate the reaction forces necessary to keep the cart in force equilibrium. This
function is useful for determining and iterating on the choice of cart wheels. These wheel forces are also
used for calculating the mechanical efficiency of the propeller.

9
Design evaluation: Cart and track design options
The highest scoring concept in section 15 is the chain and cart concept. In this section, an analysis
is carried out to determine geometry of the cart and track as well as placement and amount of cart
wheels. It is essential for the design of the cart, that it is able to support the forces and moments at all
times, particularly in the worst case scenario. The loads considered include the lift and drag forces, and
consequent bending moment on the foil, weight of the cart and centrifugal effects in the curved sections.
A requirement for the design of the cart is being well connected to the track both on the straight and
the curved sections.
Another important aspect for the cart is to ensure as low mechanical energy-loss as possible.
Fric-
tional losses are expected at the contact points between wheels and track as well as the wheel bearings
themselves. The magnitude of the wheel reaction forces are an important driver of friction.
9.1
Cart design variants
In order to conceive the optimal cart design, four different overall options are considered based on their
performance.
Figure 41: Cart design variations
47
==PAGE:54==
Option A has an upper part and a lower part that can rotate independently about the foil axis. Each
half has 2 wheels that are pressed towards an inner and outer track using a spring. This ensures rolling
contact at all times and allows for a semicircular inner and outer track on the curved sections.
Option B also divides the cart into two parts that rotate about the foil axis. This concept employs
eight angled wheels, which assists in maximizing the moment arm from the wheel forces, enabling a more
compact solution. Although the wheel setup is good for supporting the required forces, ensuring the two
carts remain perpendicular to the track is difficult, resulting in non-constrained motion along the path.
Option C is a one plane cart thought to work by having the track inside the cart. With high preci-
sion tolerances the 6 wheels would always have rolling contact to the surface. This cart can support
moments well normal to the plane, but for in plane moments, even with tight tolerances or pretension,
the stiffness of the reaction moment is essentially zero.
Option D is a based on an equilateral triangle that ensures wheel contact at all points provided the
track geometry is shaped to match it. The design ensures a fully constrained path of the cart on the
track and provides good moment support by having a single rigid body.
All the design options can be modified to work running inside the track or outside the track.
De-
signing the cart to be moving on the inside of the track, the cart can be more compact and have a lower
weight, which is an advantage when it comes to reducing the centrifugal force, rotational inertia and
vibrations. Disadvantages include a more complex track geometry and limits imposed on the maximum
wheel size.
Based on the four design options, and focusing on the option that is most reliable and efficient, option
D running inside the track is considered the best solution to develop further and will in the following
sections be constructed and optimized.
9.2
Triangular cart track path
The triangular setup is advantageous due to supporting fully constrained motion of the cart, both in
straight and curved sections. The concept works by defining the path of the centerline of three wheels,
two inner and one outer. The path of the two inner wheels is defined as a straightforward stadium
geometry - a straight section followed by a semicircular section. As the two inner wheels are constrained
to this line at all times, they are moved around the path, "tracing" the path of the centerline of the third
outer wheel. This strategy ensures that for any possible location of the track, the cart only has one valid
48
==PAGE:55==
solution to its orientation, thus making the movement well defined.
Figure 42: Geometry of the stadium track for the triangular cart
Figure 42 shows paths for the centerline of the inner and outer wheels, marked in blue lines, as well as
the foil axis, marked in a dashed black line. The Red triangle is used for tracing these paths and shows
the location of each wheel centerline and foil axis on the cart. It is important to note that although the
inner track is defined by straight and circular geometry, both the outer track and foil centerline move
along special curves slightly different from exact straight and curved sections, which is necessary in order
to fully constrain the cart.
9.3
Rapid prototype concept
A good way of validating early design concepts is to do some rapid prototyping, which is a useful tool for
identifying design flaws or weaknesses and to test out design hypotheses. In this case the main objective
is to test if the cart would have rolling contact throughout the path as intended by the triangular setup.

==PAGE:56==
Figure 43: Triangular cart prototype
Figure 43 shows the 3D-printed prototype used for validating the triangular cart concept. Studying the
prototype, it is clear that the triangular wheel setup works as intended by constraining the cart at every
point on the track as well as providing solid support for forces and moments in every direction.
One design flaw the prototype exposes is an issue with the slip angle of the top and bottom wheels.
Due to the wheels being offset from the angle of rotation, the rolling path and wheel axis don’t perfectly

still exhibits overall good performance in the most important aspects.

13.1
Design
One aspect to account for in the design of the foil is the plan form. In order to avoid diverging from the
optimal foil arrangement determined by B, the plan form is kept rectangular, however, in an effort to
reduce tip vortices, a small rounded tip is added to the foils.
Another aspect to consider, is the mounting of the foil to the foil shaft and by extension the cart
assembly. Here, a transition is needed between the hydrodynamic and structural part of the foil.

For this design aspect, inspiration from a real world application is used in the form of a kayak paddle.
Figure 55 illustrates how a typical kayak paddle is transitioned from a flat surface to a shaft. Although

thrust, it is useful as inspiration for designing a transition between the hydrodynamic and structural
parts of the foil.

section minimizes the drag and cavitation effects close to the water surface.

14.1
Electrical motor
The propulsion system is powered by an electrical motor on each stadium propeller unit, the selection of
which is based on a set of essential requirements.
Additionally, the motor needs preferably to be within a certain marine class, such as IP67, that allows
for the motor to be submerged in water up to 30 minutes without taking damage, otherwise the motor
needs to be protected from the environment in another way.
The motor is mounted vertically, which means there needs to be a flange on the shaft side, allowing
the motor to be mounted to the structure of the propeller. The motor is selected based on the propeller’s
maximum continuous drive speed while the braking scenario is considered a torque spike which is only a
temporary load on the motor.
The power output of the motor is determined based on the following equation:
P = ω · T
(14.1)
Where P is the power output in W, ω is the rotational speed in rad/s and T is the torque in Nm.
The rotational speed is determined from the propeller drive speed and radius of curvature of the foil
motion.
ω = vd/R = 125rad/s
(14.2)
The torque is determined from the maximum drive force, this happens during the reverse and starting
conditions of the vessel F ≈600N and the radius of curvature of the foil motion R = 60mm.
T = F · R = 36Nm
(14.3)
This yields the required power output of the motor
P = 125rad/s · 36Nm = 4500W
(14.4)
When selecting a motor from a supplier, the rotational speed is normally specified in revolutions per
minute (RPM):
n = ω · 30
π ≈1200RPM
(14.5)

One important aspect to account for when choosing a viable motor for the propulsion system is the
weight of the motor due to the weight limit of the entire catamaran in the MEBC.
Two motor examples has been found as suitable motors for the system, the first one is LEM170-127
which is an 8 pole axial gap DC brushed motor, it is a 5kW motor that can handle roughly 16Nm at
3250RPM, so by introducing a 1:3 gearing the motor would be adequate for this system, the downside
is that it weighs roughly 8kg. [15]
The other suitable motor is the HPDM-30 from H3X it has a continuous torque of 15.8Nm and has

is still patent pending and the company is still considered a startup, therefore it would require further

This motor would also require a gearing to achieve the necessary torque.
The first suitable motor from Lynch is selected as the recommended motor, however, it might possi-
ble to find a motor that also would be suitable but at lower weight for future weight improvement.
14.2
Bearings
The stadium propeller is heavily dependent on bearings to operate efficiently. This includes bearings to
support the cart wheels, foil shaft and sprocket/drive shafts.
The wheel bearings are particularly important to optimize in terms of both size and load bearing capac-
ity, due to the spacial limitations of the cart assembly. Track roller bearings provide a compact solution
to this problem, as they are designed to be used as a combined bearing and wheel.
The track roller bearings are selected based on two main criteria; outer diameter and static load ca-
pability. The load requirements are based on the wheel forces function output and the outer diameter
is based on available space in the propeller assembly. To simplify the design of the cart, identical track
roller bearings are used for all wheels, dimensioned based on the highest required wheel force.
Based on the results in figure 48, the largest possible wheel reaction force is 2160N. Applying a safety
factor of 25%, the required load carrying capacity of the bearing is 2700N. Due to the proximity to salt
water, sealing and protection against corrosion is also an important requirement.

16
Discussion and future work
In this section the results and findings of the project are discussed and future work is proposed for further
research.
This project touches upon multiple areas within the field of mechanical engineering such as hydrody-
namical analysis, selection of machine elements, structural analysis and more. Due to diversity in focus
areas, it has sometimes been necessary to simplify certain aspects of the analysis and design, using as-
sumptions and idealized models to obtain meaningful results in the time available. Possible future work
could include expanding the analysis to model the propeller more accurately and possibly optimize the
design further.
16.1
Induced lift
In chapter 3, the induced lift was determined based on the equation from Molland [10], however, this
estimation is based on empirical data for large ship rudders. These foils typically have a maximum aspect
ratio of 3 and are designed with a tapered plan form.
An alternative approach to the finite span problem would involve using lifting line theory to better
estimate the three-dimensional lift coefficient. This theory is already widely used within aircraft and
wind turbine blade analysis. Implementing the lifting line theory would most likely lead to a lower loss
in lift coefficient due to the finite span effects. This would result in the lift coefficient being larger than
estimated in this project and consequent higher lift and drag forces. This is expected to impact the
optimal cruising conditions and characteristic advance ratio.
Another important aspect to take into account is the assumption made in regards to the effective aspect
ratio, here it was assumed that due to no cross flow the aspect ratio would be equal to two times the
geometric aspect ratio. This is normally assumed for ship rudders that are connected to the hull, for
this case the foils are not attached to a flat surface nor symmetrical like a full wing, so this assumption
should be revisited for more accurate results.
16.2
Viterna-Corrigan
For this application, where we have chosen to fix the pitch angle of the foils, there are multiple opera-
tional scenarios where the AoA will exceed the stall angle. Here, the Viterna-Corrigan method is used
to approximate the lift and drag coefficients. This method can be used up to 180◦but since it is an
extrapolation method it also comes with a degree of inherent uncertainty.
82
==PAGE:89==
Due to this uncertainty of the results using the method, the coefficients should not be seen as exact
data points, but more like a range of values. This means that if the method predicts a lifting coefficient
of 1.0, it should be interpreted in the range of 1.0±0.1, as normally a discrepancy of 10% is expected[26].
This means that to improve the credibility of the numerical solver when using Viterna-Corrigan, it
should account for this uncertainty in the extrapolated data. This does slightly complicate the model,
as estimating e.g. a higher lift coefficient is simultaneously conservative in relation to the structural
analysis and optimistic in relation to the thrust output of the foils.
16.3
CFD Model and experimental testing
Based on the notes on induced lift and Viterna-Corrigan, next steps of the hydrodynamic analysis would
likely involve a CFD model of the foils, as this would decrease the uncertainty of the results and better
simulate the flow. A CFD model would also present an opportunity for revising the plan form, e.g.
elliptical or tapered foil shape, to minimize the effect tip vortices. An initial CFD model could involve a
detailed analysis of a single foil in a fairly isolated sense, whereas a more advanced model could account
for all the foils and potentially both units’ effect on each other.
Ideally, a CFD model would decrease the uncertainty in regards to the lift and drag characteristics,
particularly in the post-stall domain. It would be interesting to compare results of a CFD analysis to
the results in this project, considering they are heavily reliant on assumptions and conventional foil theory.
In order to validate the theoretical results of the hydrodynamic analysis, an appropriate next step would
be to conduct some experimental tests. Detailed testing of a single foil in isolation could be useful for
validating the CFD model and foil design, the general performance of the propulsion unit as a whole
would require a larger setup. These latter experiment could be carried out in a long water tank where
force sensors could be mounted to the propeller. With this setup, it could be possible to measure the
combined thrust of the propeller system and corresponding power requirement during different speed
settings.
16.4
Acceleration
Although the model can predict thrust at any combination of drive speed and advance speed, it does
not account for dynamic changes over time such as when the vessel is either increasing or decreasing in
speed. Consequently, calculating the braking distance, acceleration and general motion of the vessel over
time would require accounting for the change in thrust, particularly dependent on the advance speed.
Future work could include a model linking more aspects of the general vessel dynamics and maneuvering
83
==PAGE:90==
to the propeller performance.
16.5
Drive speed control
The proposed strategy for controlling the AoA is to regulate the drive speed of the unit and thereby use
the drive speed to directly impact the angle of the local inflow. There is still some potential work to be
done when it comes to how the drive speed should be regulated based on the current advance speed and
what the target advance speed is set to be.
The current setup would involve the operator of the vessel directly controlling the drive speed, how-
ever a more advanced strategy could involve a control system to ensure the desired thrust. This would
involve collecting live data from the advance speed of the vessel to automatically determine the drive
speed based on the predicted thrust of the mathematical model. This could aid in operating the vessel
more predictably, reducing the human element of the control.
16.6
Flow irregularities
The propulsion unit has been designed and analyzed based on a forward motion alone, meaning that
the advance speed flow has been assumed to come head on. In reality the flow will be more complex,
especially during maneuvering.
Another important scenario that will change local flow situation, is during maneuvering of the ves-
sel. During a turn to port or starboard, the propulsion units should change drive speed accordingly.
This will have some impact on the flow near the foils that is currently not accounted for in the model.
Traditional screw propellers are designed to also account for the vessels wake field, the wake field occurs
due to hull friction and it will result in the propeller having to operate in non-uniform flow. It is hard
to predict how much non-uniform flow will impact the stadium propeller concept without having highly
detailed CFD models and real life experiments, this project has not included these flow inconsistencies
so this would have to be investigated further.
16.7
Corrosion protection
The intended application for the design proposal is to be used as the propulsion unit for competing in
the MEBC, where the competition’s 3 disciplines will last a combined time of roughly 4 hours. This
means that for the competition, corrosion is not of upmost importance, but if the stadium propeller
should be used for long term testing it is going to be very important to make design changes that will
84
==PAGE:91==
ensure protection against the corrosive seawater.
For instance one could probably exchange the chain with a belt made of some sort of polymer as this
would eliminate the corrosion risk in that part but it would most likely have a negative effect on the
mechanical efficiency as belts can slip and have added friction losses.
For the outer part of the propulsion units the painting has to be approved for maritime usage and
depending on the placement of the propeller system it could need a safety rating sufficient for submerged
parts. Assuming the foils are manufactured from carbon fiber, this will not be the main issue, but if
corrosion starts setting into the cart and track it could quickly impact the mechanical efficiency and have
consequences for the structural integrity. Future work in this area could include investigating strategies
for either sealing the transmission from seawater, or potentially designing the entire unit to be immersed.
16.8
Industry application and manufacturing
This project proposes a design concept based on the stadium propeller that theoretically has a high
hydrodynamic efficiency and high mechanical efficiency, sufficient to compete with traditional screw pro-
pellers. However, considering a scaled up industry application for e.g. merchant vessels, the design
would likely require a major rework to account for the difference in scale and manufacturing methods.
The cart and chain setup in particular presents a major challenge when scaled up, due to the large forces.
An industry implementation would most likely require a diesel electric motor to power the propulsion
system, which typically has a lower efficiency than a standard diesel two stroke engine.
Integrating the propulsion unit will create an entire new set of engineering problems to solve, rais-
ing concerns regarding sealing of the carts’ movement. This would like require a complex sealing system
that allows the foil to move in the stadium path but keep the water out of the internal mechanics. Such
a sealing system could potentially reduce the mechanical efficiency considerably due to additional friction.
The design of the stadium propeller as outlined in this project places strong requirements on the toler-
ance and fit of the mechanical components, particularly in regards to the cart-track interface. Although
aspects of the manufacturing and assembly of design proposal have been considered in this project, due
to time constraints this has not been a major focus and the design will likely need additional attention
and possibly revisions to the construction to account for manufacturing methods and costs.
Before a prototype of the propulsion unit can be manufactured, an essential step is to produce as-
sembly and machine drawings, including accurate GD&T to ensure the components fit well and function
85
==PAGE:92==
as intended.
16.9
FEA credibility
ANSYS has been used for two FEAs in this project, for the cart and for the foil. In general, simulation
tools are a great way to analyze complex geometries and detailed loading scenarios it is important to
remember that depending on how well the simulation is set up the results can vary accordingly.
In the FEA for the cart the load case was assumed to be a sufficient approach to determine the carts
structural integrity and deformation, for future work it could be good to create another simulation with
a remote force representing the lift force and then constraining the wheel contact points.
In the carbon fiber simulation the layup and material was accounted for but for the complex geom-
etry of the foil there is simulation improvements that can be done to get a more realistic result such as
accounting for the laminate thinning towards the trailing edge as the wing geometry requires a sharp
tail.
The profile had to be changed to a thicker version and this means that there will be change in hydrody-
namical performance but from experience it will not be a critical change as the thickness only increases
slightly.
A small increase in thickness is very advantageous for the carbon fiber usage as it makes it possible to
create thicker laminates that then makes the foil stiffer.
For future work the main code is set up in way where the foil is an input, so all that is needed to acquire
new data and plots would be access to new optimal data (θlock,vd,va) and so on.
16.10
Weight and mechanical efficiency optimization
A large focus of this project has been to design the stadium propeller system with a good overall ef-
ficiency. The high hydrodynamic efficiency lends itself primarily to the concept at its core, while the
mechanical efficiency is largely a result of the mechanical setup of the design proposal. The mechanical
efficiency can potentially be improved by reducing the radial forces on the cart wheels as this is directly
linked to frictional losses. This can for instance be achieved by reducing the total weight of the cart,
thereby reducing the centrifugal force in the curved sections, or possibly by optimizing the cart wheel
arrangement. Considering that the cart FEA resulted in a somewhat high safety factor at the highest
wheel loading case, the cart construction has some room for weight improvement.
Another potential area of improvement is the total weight of the propulsion system, By lowering the
total weight of the unit, and consequently the vessel itself, it will require less thrust to accelerate. It is
86
==PAGE:93==
also worth considering that there is a maximum weight requirement for the MEBC that has to be upheld.
Future work would include optimizing the weight of the stationary components, i.e. track, guideways
and connecting structure along with selecting a lighter weight electrical motor.

Conclusion
Over the course of this project, the stadium propeller has evolved and undergone a series of changes
leading up to a final design proposal. Substantial effort has gone into researching the principles of the
stadium propeller concept and potential solutions to the mechanical design challenges, reducing uncer-
tainty and clarifying the design space.
Multiple methods of controlling the foil angle have been considered including passive and adjustable
control systems, with a fully constrained path emerging as the mechanically superior option, at some
cost to the efficiency at lower vessel speeds. A detailed mathematical model of the propeller has been
developed, utilizing solid and fluid mechanics theory. This model is useful as a basis for calculating
essential geometry and forces of the propeller.
The proposed design offers a way of achieving a high hydrodynamic efficiency at the design speed with
acceptable mechanical efficiency. At cruising speed, the propeller exhibits a total combined efficiency
of 78%, which is an improvement over a conventional screw propeller setup, typically ranging between
65-72% efficiency[4]. This resulting shows promise for the stadium propeller, considering the conservative
approach and potential improvements to the design..
Despite these compelling results, several challenges were highlighted, particularly regarding mechanical
complexity, precise manufacturing tolerances, and the need for detailed CFD-simulations and experi-
mental validation. Future work should focus on physical prototyping and testing to verify the simulation
results as well as refining the mechanical system for improved robustness.
Since propeller efficiency will become increasingly more valuable for companies aiming to reach CO2
neutrality before 2050, the stadium propeller has potential for future research and investment. The out-
put of the project can be used as basis for future improvements to the design and continued research of
the stadium propeller concept, as it provides valuable knowledge in the mechanical transmission design
considerations and its potential real world implementation.
