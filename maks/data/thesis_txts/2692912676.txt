Abstract
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is the main cause of dementia and is a one of the greatest medicinal
challenges of our century. Despite enormous research eﬀorts, current treatment is only
symptomatic and does not delay or prevent the progression of the disease. There is estimated
a total of 40 million peoples, who suﬀer from AD and the number is thought to reach
approximately 100 million in 2050. AD is a progressive and irreversible neurodegenerative
disease, which lacks a well-known cause or pathogenesis. More than 200 research projects,
with focus on AD, have failed or been abandoned, emphasizing that there is a high demand
for a new innovative treatment of AD.
Glucagon-like peptide 1 receptor agonist (GLP-1RA) has been proposed as a treatment for
AD, with one proposed small-molecule GLP-1RA being Quercetin. Quercetin is a naturally
occurring ﬂavonol and preliminary results have shown promising results in treatment of
neurodegenerative diseases. However, this is hampered by low penetration of the blood-brain
barriers to Quercetin.
In general, brain diseases are some of the most diﬃcult to treat, since the brain is ef-
fectively protected from foreign substances. Recent studies in the Qvortrup group and group
of collaborator M. Rosenkilde have shown a great potential for a shuttle peptide that allow
selective crossing of the BCSFB, which is the pathway we wish to build on in the current
project. Targeting the BCSFB as a route for drug delivery to the brain is a novel approach
that until now has not received much attention. Nevertheless, it is striking to notice that
most neurotropic virus get access to the brain through the BCSFB. Based on the results from
Qvortrup group and group of collaborator M. Rosenkilde we will develop a protein toxin
technique allowing crossing of the BCSFB and selectively deliver a drug in the cerebrospinal
ﬂuid (CSF). The aim of this project is to construct a brain-speciﬁc AChE-cleavable linker,
targeting an enzyme that is highly expressed in the brain and over-expressed in AD patients.
Following hydrolysis of acetyl the GLP-1RA is released upon spontaneous intramolecular
cyclization by formation of a six-membered lactone ring. Another important feature of the
AChE-cleavable linker is the azide handle. This is used for bioconjugation to the shuttle
peptide enabling selective delivery of drugs in the brain through crossing of the BCSFB.

Resumé
Alzheimers (AD) er hovedårsagen til demens og er en af de største medicinske udfordringer i
dette århundrede. På trods af enorme forskningsindsatser er den nuværende behandling kun
symptomatisk og forsinker eller forhindrer ikke sygdommens progression. Det anslås, at i alt
40 millioner mennesker lider af AD - og det forventes at antallet vil nå cirka 100 millioner i
2050. AD er en progressiv og irreversibel neurodegenerativ sygdom, hvortil der endnu ikke
er fundet hverken årsag eller patogenese. Mere end 200 forskningsprojekter med fokus på
AD er enten mislykkedes eller blevet opgivet, og derfor er der stor efterspørgsel på en ny
innovativ behandling af AD.
Glukagon-lignende peptid 1-receptoragonist (GLP-1RA) er blevet foreslået som en mulig
behandling for AD. I dette projekt er Quercetin foreslået som en mulig GLP-1RA. Quercetin
er en naturligt forekommende ﬂavonol og har vist lovende resultater i behandling af neurode-
generative sygdomme. Dog har Quercetin lav penetration af blod-hjerne-barrierer, hvilket
vanskeliggør levering til hjernen.
Hjernesygdomme er nogle af de sværeste sygdomme at behandle, da hjernen er eﬀektivt
beskyttet mod fremmede stoﬀer, såsom lægemidler. Nylige undersøgelser i Qvortrup-gruppen
og gruppen af samarbejdspartnere, M. Rosenkilde, har vist et stort potentiale for et shuttle-
peptid, der muliggør selektiv passage af blod-cerebrospinalvæske-barrieren (BCSFB). Det
er denne tilgang, vi ønsker at benytte i dette projekt. At benytte BCSFB som en rute for
målrettet levering af lægemidler til hjernen er en ny tilgang, der indtil nu ikke har fået
meget opmærksomhed. Ikke desto mindre er det påfaldende, at de ﬂeste neurotrope virus får
adgang til hjernen gennem BCSFB.
I dette projekt er målet, at udvikle en proteintoksin-teknik, der muliggør krydsning af BCSFB
til selektiv levering af lægemidler til hjernen. Dette projekt har fokus på at konstruere en
hjernespeciﬁk AChE-kløvbar linker, der er målrettet mod et enzym, som er udtrykt i hjernen
og overudtrykt hos AD patienter. Efter hydrolyse af acetyl frigøres GLP-1RA ved spontan
intramolekylær cyklisering ved dannelse af en seksleddet lactonring. Et andet vigtigt træk
ved den AchE-kløvbare linker er azid-håndtaget. Dette benyttes til biokonjugering mellem
et shuttle-peptid og den AchE-kløvbare linker til selektiv levering af lægemidler til hjernen
gennem krydsning af BCSFB.

Alzheimer’s Disease
Alzheimer’s disease (AD) is the most widespread form of dementia and is seen as one of the
greatest medicinal challenges of our century. In total, 40 million people are estimated to
suﬀer from dementia worldwide, and this number is estimated to reach approximately 100
millions in 2050 [1]. AD is a progressive and irreversible neurodegenerative disease that lacks
a well-known cause or pathogenesis. There is several symptoms and signs of AD, which is
described as cognitive insuﬃciencies such as memory loss, language diﬃculties and executive
dysfunction as well as non-cognitive symptoms which include psychiatric and behaviour
changes (depression, delusion or agitation). Another common symptom is diﬃculties with
performing daily activities. It is estimated that approximately 6-8 % of individuals over
65 years of age have AD, and the risk of AD increases with age. The growing expansion
of lifespan, leads to a rapidly increasing number of patients with AD. This has led to an
increasing interest and growth of research in dementia. Despite all research eﬀorts, there is
still no eﬀective treatment options for the disease [2].
There are two main pathological hallmarks of AD, amyloid plaques and neuroﬁbrillary
tangles. The amyloid cascade hypothesis suggests that accumulation of amyloid β (Aβ)
triggers neuronal dysfunction and cell death in the brain [3]. Accumulation of Aβ-protein is
the most investigated and well understood hallmark of AD; therefore this is the Hallmark
described in this thesis.
1.1
Accumulation of Amyloid-β-protein
The amyloid cascade hypothesis suggests that β-amyloid1−42 (Aβ1−42) depositions in the
brain is the central cause of Alzheimer’s disease and this accumulation is the factor initiating
other pathophysiology factors of AD [4]. The accumulation of Aβ-peptides is a result of
misregulated cleavage of amyloid precursor protein (APP) [1]. APP is a type 1 membrane
protein and is expressed in most tissues but is found in highest concentration in the brain,
more speciﬁc in the synapse of neurons [5]. The human APP gene is located on the long arm
of chromosome 21, the gene has a span of approximately 240 kb and at least 18 exons. Thus,
splicing of the gene generates APP mRNAs encoding several isoforms of APP in the range
from 365 to 770 amino acid residues. APP is assumed to modulate cell growth, motility,
neurite outgrowth and cell survival, but the precise physiological function of APP is not
known [6].
Two major pathways for post-translational modiﬁcation of APP has been determined (ﬁgure
1). The ﬁrst pathway is the non-amyloidogenic pathway, where APP is ﬁrst cleaved by
α-secretase, releasing a large extracellular domain (sAAP-α) and a membrane-associated

C-terminal fragment consisting of 83 amino acid residues (C83). APP C83 is further cleaved
by γ-secretases to release two smaller peptides: P3 peptide and APP intracellular domain
(AICD). These two small peptides are rapidly degraded, with P3 being secreted to the
extracellular environment. These two small peptides are not considered involved in the
formation of neuroﬁbrillary tangles and loss of synaptic function. AICD is not as well known
as P3 but is not believed to play a role in neuroﬁbrillary tangles or loss of synaptic function
[4]. The second pathway is the amyloidogenic pathway, where APP is primarily processed
by β-secretase releasing the extracellular domain, sAPP-β, which is similar to sAAP-α but
6-16 amino acid residues shorter. This cleavage does also generate a membrane associated
C-terminal fragment consisting of 99 amino acids (C99). The next step in the amyloidogenic
pathway is the cleavage of C99 by γ-secretases, releasing AICD and the amyloidogenic Aβ
peptides which aggregates and ﬁbrillates to form amyloid plaques in the brain leading to
loss of synaptic function [7]. The released Aβ peptides varies in length as β-secretase is not
as selective as α-secretases. Aβ peptides may then by aggregation form ﬁrst oligomers and
later plaques.

Current and future strategies for treatment of
Alzheimer’s disease
AD is the main cause of dementia and is currently one of the main medicinal challenges.
Dementia occurs mainly in elderly people, older than 60 years. Growing lifespan due to
better medicinal care, leads to an increasing in patients with dementia [8]. However, despite
all the hard research eﬀorts, at the moment, there are no eﬀective treatment for the disease
[1, 9, 10].
2.1
Treating Alzheimer Disease with Acetylcholinesterase Inhibitors
All current treatments for AD and dementia try to counterbalance the neuro-transmitter
imbalance that occur in the disease. One current way of treating AD is by acetylcholinesterase
inhibitors (AChEIs), such as Donepezil, Galantamine and Rivastigmine [1, 9]. The develop-
ment of AChEIs is based on the cholinergic hypothesis, suggesting that the progressive loss
of limbic and neocotical cholinergic innervation in AD is critically important for higher brain
functions such as memory, learning and attention. The primary cause for dysfunction and
death of cholinergic neurons in the basal forebrain is thought to be neuroﬁbrillary degenera-
tion which gives rise to a widespread presynaptic cholinergic denervation. The AChEIs main
function is to increase the availability of acetylcholine at synapses, by reducing the breakdown
of acetylcholine levels in the brain of patients with AD through inhibition of the enzyme
acetylcholinesterase in the synaptic cleft. This method has been clinically proven useful in
delaying cognitive decline in AD. AChEIs enhances the central cholinergic neurotransmission
and tends to mitigate decline in cognition, at least the ﬁrst year of treatment. Further decline
can happen, and even temporary discontinuation of these drugs can result in rapid decline
and is also associated with greater risk of nursing home placement [11].
The most common side-eﬀects from current AChEIs (Donepezil, Galantamine and Rivastig-
mine: see Fig 2) are diarrhea, nausea and vomiting, triggered by the inhibition of the
cholinomimetic action in the gastrointestinal tract [12, 13]. More severe side-eﬀects are
cardiac arrhythmias, especially bradycardia or syncope, active peptic ulcer or gastrointestinal
bleeding history and uncontrolled seizures [1, 12, 13].

2.2
Treating Alzheimer’s Disease with Non-competitive N -methyl-
d-aspartate receptor
Moderate to severe AD can be treated with the non-competitive N -methyl-d-aspartate
(NMDA) receptor antagonist; memantine, which has low-to-moderate aﬃnity [1, 9]. Meman-
tine binds to open NMDA receptor-operated calcium channels, which blocks NMDA-mediated
ion ﬂux and thereby lowers the dangerous eﬀects of pathologically elevated glutamate levels
that leads to neuronal dysfunction [14]. NMDA has been approved by Food and Drug
Administration (FDA) for moderate to severe AD either as a monotherapy or in combination
with AChEIs, as they have complementary mechanisms of action. NMDA monotherapy has
demonstrated both short- and long-term beneﬁts for patients with moderate or severe AD,
assessed by evaluating activities of daily living, cognition, and behavioural and psychological
symptoms of dementia [15].
Since 2003 there has been no new drugs approved by FDA for AD and no approved disease-
modifying treatments (DMTs) for AD, despite many long and expensive eﬀorts [16, 17]. More
than 200 research projects in the last decade have failed or been abandoned [1]. Therefore,
there is a high need for new and innovative treatments of AD.

Is Alzheimer’s Disease the new Metabolic Disease?
Type II diabetes (T2D) is a known risk factor for development of AD, since the two diseases
has pathologies in common. AD involves dysfunctional insulin signalling in the brain, and
patients with T2D have a two-fold risk for developing AD [18]. Recently researchers have
proposed the term ´Type-3-diabetes´ (T3D) for Alzheimer’s disease due to the shared
molecular and cellular features among Type I diabetes (T1D), T2D and AD. There has been
identiﬁed two main key points to explain this association: insulin resistance and inﬂammatory
signalling pathways [19, 20]. Insulin is produced by pancreatic β-cells and its main function
is to regulate the blood-glycose level in the systemic circulation. This is done by promoting
glycose uptake by our cells and by inhibiting both glucose production and release in the
liver. The release of insulin is promoted by high plasma-glucose concentration, acetylcholine,
cholecystokinin, amino acids and incretin hormones such as Glucagon-like peptide 1 (GLP-1)
[21]. Beside controlling blood-glucose levels and metabolism, insulin plays a crucial role as
a growth factor. Insulin-like growth factor-1 receptor (IGF-1R) and insulin receptor (InR)
signalling controls cell growth, survival and metabolic functions in the brain [22].
Insulin signaling in the central nervous system (CNS) is thought to regulate metabolic
pathways in peripheral tissues, such as the liver and adipose tissue, these eﬀects are mediated
by the actions of insulin in the hypothalamus. Dysfunction of the hypothalamus is believed
to be correlated with T2D, impairment in the hypothalamus insulin signaling can therefore
be resulting in disruption of metabolic pathways due to lack of control [3, 21].
Insulin plays many roles in neurons, which are mediated by signaling through two major
eﬀector pathways: the insulin-IRS-AKT and MAPK pathways. Insulin receptors are highly
enriched in synapses, both in the presynaptic axon terminal and in the postsynaptic density
compartment. Here insulin receptors have a high impact on neurosynaptic functioning.
Insulin enhances neurite outgrowth, modulates catecholamine release and uptake, while also
regulating traﬃcking of ligand-gated ion channels [23, 24]. Insulin’s role in the brain is
to maintain the cognitive functions, synapse formation, neuronal survival, neuroprotective
processes and synaptic plasticity [23].
3.1
Is GLP-1 the new Treatment for AD?
Besides treatments with insulin itself, insulin-sensitizing medicines commonly used to treat
T2D have attracted interest as a potential therapy for AD, with main focus on glucagon-like
peptide 1 receptor agonists (GLP-1RAs). The natural GLP-1RA is the incretin hormone
GLP-1 produced in pancreatic L-cells, which increases the insulin release when activating
GLP-1R on pancreatic β-cells [21, 25].

3.1.1
GLP-1 and Central Nervous System
GLP-1 is a 30 amino acid long endogenous gut peptide hormone which is a derivative from
the tissue-speciﬁc posttranslational processing by prohormone convertase 1/3 (PC1/3) of the
proglucagon gene (ﬁgure 3). GLP-1 binds to GLP-1R coupled to the cyclic AMP pathway.
GLP-1R stimulation enhances pancreatic islet β-cell proliferation, glucose-dependent insulin
secretion and lowers the levels of glucose in the blood [18]. In skeletal muscle and adipose
tissue, insulin promotes membrane traﬃcking of the glucose transporter GLUT4 from GLUT4
storage vesicles to the plasma membrane, thereby facilitating the uptake of glucose from the
circulation [24].
GLP-1 is mainly produced in enteroendocrine L cells of the gut and is secreted into the blood
stream when food enters the duodenum [26, 27, 28]. GLP-1 release is independent of the
content of the food being consumed. GLP-1 is also produced in the CNS, predominantly in the
lower brainstem and is transported to regions in the CNS and is believed to stimulate insulin
pathways in the same way as systemic circulating insulin [29]. In the brain preproglucagon is

4
Drug Delivery to the Brain
Some of the most diﬃcult diseases to treat is brain diseases because they are very complex
and not yet fully understood. In addition, the brain is eﬀectively protected from foreign
substances by numerous barriers, including the blood-brain-barrier (BBB) and the blood-
cerebrospinal ﬂuid-barrier (BCSFB) [35, 36]. These barriers allow substances necessary for
the brain to pass through the blood circulation, while they hinder foreign substances to cross,
including therapeutic molecules. The ability for molecules to cross the barriers depends on
its physiochemical properties and the drugs need to be small (MW < 400 Da), have less
than 8 hydrogen bonds and be lipid soluble [36]. High doses of drugs in the blood circulation
is often needed to achieve a therapeutic eﬀect in the brain, rendering it hard to reach the
therapeutic window. This lowers the chances of bringing drug for CNS to the market [36].
Multiply strategies have been developed to enhance the uptake of of drugs in the brain. Two
of the major strategies is the usage of active transport and disruption of the BBB chemically
by ultrasound [35], but although being researched for a decade, a success story is still lacking.
Therefore, there is a need for new and innovative treatment strategies.
4.1
The Blood-Brain Barrier
The BBB prevents the brain uptake of most foreign substances, such as most pharmaceuticals.
This property of BBB arises from the epithelial-like tight junctions within the brain capillary
endothelium [36]. The BBB is a semi-permeable membranous barrier, localized at the
interface between the blood and the cerebral tissue. It composes of a complex system of
endothelial cells, astroglia, pericytes and perivascular mast cells. The BBB control the
exchange between the two compartments, allowing only certain molecules or ions to pass
through by diﬀusion or occasionally by facilitated diﬀusion, passive transport or active
transport. Therefore the BBB is responsible for maintaining and creating homeostasis for
neural functions, protecting the system from toxic insults, regulating the communication
between the periphery and the CNS and providing the brain with nutrients [37]. Another
factor excluding molecules from passing the BBB is the present of various extracellular
and intracellular enzymes, such as cytochrome P450 enzymes, which protects the CNS by
degradation of molecules [38]. The protecting of the system is achieved through 1) prevention
of the paracellular diﬀusion of hydrophilic compounds; 2) activation of eﬄux transport of
hydrophobic molecules and drugs from the brain to the blood. [36].

4.1.1
Crossing the Blood-Brain Barrier
In general, only lipid solible (lipophilic) molecules can cross the BBB. Other molecules require
certain cell endogenous transport systems, such as carrier-mediated transport, receptor-
mediated transport, or absorptive-mediated transport [36, 38]. There is ﬁve basic mechanism
by which solute molecules can cross membranes:
Firstly, is the simple passive diﬀusion, which is a spontaneous process, dependent on the
magnitude of the concentration gradient. This is the route lipid-soluble molecules, such as
anesthetics and heroin, enters the brain [36].
Secondly, are the solute carriers (SCL), a family of membrane transport proteins, which
facilitates bi-directional movement of solutes across the cell membrane. SLC transport does
not require adenosine triphosphate (ATP) since the movement across the cell membrane is
driven either by electrochemical gradient (Na+ or H+ gradient) or by concentration gradient
established by the solutes that are being transported. SLC is classiﬁed as either facilitated
transporters or secondary active transporters [36, 38].
Thirdly, is the carrier-mediated eﬄux, which uses ATP hydrolysis to pump molecules across
the membrane and then force the eﬄux of solutes against a concentration gradient.
Fourthly, is the receptor-mediated transcytosis (RMT). This transport system utilizes the
vesicular transport system of the endothelial cells. RMT is commonly induced by binding of
large molecules (peptides and proteins) to receptors (insulin receptor, transferrin receptor
etc.) which are express on the endothelial cell membrane [36, 38].

Another strategy may be to target the BCSFB instead of BBB, as this would allow drugs to

4.2
The Blood-CSF Barrier
The BCSFB is localized in the choroid plexus (CP) of the brain ventricles. The CP is an
epithelio-endothelial convolute comprising a highly vascularized stroma with fenestrated
capillaries and a lining of epithelial cells joined by tight junctions (TJs), which are crucial in
forming the BCSFB. CP is critical for maintaining brain homeostasis and BCSFB permeability
[39]. The BCSFB is the interface between blood and CSF, which is a ﬂuid in the brain and
in the nervous system that ﬁlls the ventricles (consisting of the four large cavities inside the
brain) [21]. The CSF exchange nutrients freely with interstitial ﬂuid (ISF), which surround
the neurons, the only interface between these two ﬂuids is the single layer of feneatrated
endothelium. This is what makes CSF an excellent target for drug delivery to the brain,
because the drugs can enter the brain through the CSF-ISF exchange [21, 39, 40].
Targeting BCSFB as the route for drug delivery to the brain is a novel approach and has
not yet been applied, but is the approach taken in this project. Recent studies by Qvortrup
group and collaborater M. Rosenkilde has shown a great potential in crossing the BCSFB
[41].
4.2.1
Crossing the Blood-CSF Barrier
The transport mechanisms across BCSFB involves a variety of transmembrane proteins
that enable selective passage of molecules over the BCSFB. The crossing of the BCSFB is
important for the exchange of metabolites and xenobiotics between blood and CSF. The
transporters mainly belong to the ATP-binding cassette (ABC) transporter and SLCs. Two
main types of ABC transporter are especially expressed in the CP; the multidrug resistance-
related protein (MRPs) and multidrug transporters P-glycoprotein (PgP) [39]. Recent studies
by M. Rosenkilde have identiﬁed GPR125 as a potential receptor for trancytosis exclusively
across BCSFB [41]. It is this pathway we wish to target in this project.

4.3
SH-peptide as Carrier to Cross BCSFB
Mumps-infection is an acute infection characterized by swelling of the parotid gland (Parotitis)
[40]. When Mumps virus (MuV) enters the bloodstream, it enters the CNS, where it causes
aseptic meningitis, viral encephalitis and can lead to long-term complications related to
brain infections [40]. Treatments of MuV have become so good, that it is only in rare cases,
that the infection of MuV leads to long-term complications or death [42, 40]. How MuV
enters the brain through CP into CSF is not yet well understood, but a study of rodents
suggests that it enters CSF through CP or via infected immune cells crossing the BCSFB [43].
MuV is a member of the Rubulavirus genus family and has a single-stranded, negative-sense,
nonsegmented RNA genome. The genome of MuV encodes nine proteins, one of these is the
small hydrophobic SH-protein (the MuV-pSH) [42]. MuV-pSH consist of 57-amino-acids and
is a type I integral membrane protein with the N-terminus facing towards the extracellular
surface of the infected cell [42, 43].
In recent studies by M. Rosenkilde, a nine N-terminal amino acids of MuV-pSH was found
to be a ligand for GPR125 [41]. GPR125 belongs to an adhesion G protein-coupled receptor
family and are shown to be speciﬁcally expressed in the CP [48]. Other studies have shown

Acetylcholinesterase
Acetylcholineesterase (AChE) is a cholinergic enzyme primarily found at postsynaptic neuro-
muscular junctions, especially in muscles and nerves. AChE is known to be distributed in
nervous tissue such as the brainstem, cerebellum, peripheral and autonomic nervous systems
[44]. The most important feature of AChE is its key role in cholinergic neurotransmission.
AChE terminates eﬀectively the transmitter acetylcholine (ACh).
Figure 8: The AChE active site is buried deep within the enzyme and contains three amino
acid residues crucial for the catalytic activity: serine 200, histidine 440 and glutamate 327.
A graphic illustration of a simpliﬁed overview of the breakdown of ACh by AChE.
When a neural signal propagates and excites or activates a cellular membrane the neuro-
transmitter ACh is released. ACh receptors are activated by undergoing a conformational
change and by the membrane releasing calcium ions. The downstream eﬀect of a signal
initiated with ACh essentially results in ampliﬁcation and propagation of the cellular signal
[44]. AChE is a serine hydrolase and the trimethylammonium head group binds to the
‘anionic’ sub-site of the enzyme’s active site and the ester-bond region binds to the ‘esteratic’
sub-site. When AChE interacts with the ACh substrate, it results in the hydrolysis of ACh,
hereby controlling the amount of ACh in the synapse, shown in ﬁgure 8. AChE is a serine
hydrolase and creates a tetrahedral intermediate through acid-base reactions with a catalytic
triad (serine, histidine, acid residue). The histidine triad in AChE allows transference of a
proton between the oxygen moiety in serine and ACh, thus removing choline to form a new
acetylated serine. When the acetylated serine is deacetylated, the regeneration of free AChE
begins. Initially, aspartate stabilizes the protonated histidine, which releases acetic acid
and a new free enzyme (Figure 9). The conformational binding of ACh to the active site is
inﬂuenced by interactions between amino acid residues (tyrosine, phenylalanine, tryptophan)
which makes up a peripheral anionic site (Figure 8).

to avoid exposure to light, since light would result in the formation of I2. The conditions for
the reaction are shown in scheme 15.
The equilibrium exchange of a halogen atom in alkyl halides for another halogen atom is
known as the Finkelstein reaction. The ﬁrst halogen-exchange reaction was reported in the
mid 1800s by W.H. Perkin [50], but was later studied more intensely by H. Finkelstein in 1910
[51]. When alkyl chlorides and bromides (1◦, 2◦, 3◦, benzylic, etc.) were boiled in acetone in
the presence of NaI, the corresponding alkyl iodides were formed. The Finkelstein reaction
capitalizes on the substantial solubility diﬀerence between sodium-halides in organic solvent.
For example, NaI dissolves rapidly in acetone, whereas the solubility of NaBr and NaCl is
low in organic solvents, thus leading to precipitation. This is utilized to shift the equilibrium
towards halogen-exchange, accordingly to the Le Chatelier principle. The most often used
Finkelstein reaction is the conversion of alkyl chlorides and bromides to the corresponding
alkyl iodides, which would otherwise be diﬃcult to prepare [52].
Scheme 16: The proposed Mechanism for halide-exchange in the Finkelstein reaction [52, 53].
The mechanism of the Finkelstein reaction is described as a typical SN2 reaction, the ﬁlled
orbital of the nucleophile (halide ion, I in NaI) interacts with the σ∗orbital of the carbon-
halogen bond, resulting in an overall inversion of conﬁguration [52]. The proposed reaction
mechanism is shown in scheme 16 [52, 53].

Conclusion
The aim of this project was to design an Acetylcholinesterase-cleavable linker, for fusion toxin
proteins in improved delivery of drugs to the brain, for the treatment of AD. The design
of the AChE-cleavable linker comprised an advanced linker technology, that should enable
direct targeting of AD by crossing the BCSFB. Delivery of drugs through crossing of BCSFC
and selectively release in CSF is a novel strategy and has not yet been proposed. Therefore,
this strategy could be a revolution not only for treatment of AD, but more generally for
treatment of neurological diseases. Upon enzyme-mediated cleavage by AChE, the designed
enzyme-cleavable linker undergo a spontaneous intramolecular cyclization, forming a six-
membered cyclic lactone with simultaneously release of the small-molecule drug. Hence the
acetyl-moiety of the designed linker is important for brain-speciﬁc drug release. Two possible
spacers (S1 and S2) for the AChE-cleavable linker were investigated. The spacer contained
an azide-functionality, allowing later attachment of the shuttle peptide through CuAAC.
The proposed synthetic strategy with spacer S2 required the preparation of compound
14, which was never successfully obtained. One possible explanation for the diﬃculties
in preparing compound 14 is suggested to be an intramolecular ring-closing reaction to
form a structure called a crown-ether. The order of the synthetic steps should therefore
be reconsidered. For a future synthetic pathway of spacer S2, it is suggested to start the
synthesis from a chloridated glycol-chain.
The proposed synthetic strategy for the linker containing spacer S1 requires several synthetic
steps. Due to time limitations in the project, not all steps were investigated in the laboratory.
Diﬀerent approaches towards target molecule 1 was tried, to identify the most optimal
strategy. This included the preparation of an amide and the sequence of nosylation and
alkylation. It was determined to use reagent 17a for the synthesis of the amide (compound
18). Whereas it was chosen to nosylate the backbone amine before alkylation. Even though
the laboratory work showed to point the project on the path of ﬁnding the optimal strategy
for the target molecule 1, there is still a lot of work to be done and more reactions need to
be optimized.
When an optimal strategy for the TM 1 has been identiﬁed, the next step is to facilitate an
assay for proof of concept of enzyme-mediated hydrolysis, resulting in small-molecule drug
release. The ﬁnal step is to conjugate the linker to a FP with an SH-peptide by CuAAC and
submitting the conjugate to testing in the group of collaborator M. Rosenkilde for study of
targeted delivery of GLP-1 agonists to the brain by crossing BCSFB as a potential treatment
of AD.

Mechanism of Allyl Protection
Scheme 25: Mechanism for Alloc protection of a carboxylic acid.
The allyl protecting group is often used for protection of alcohols, due to its relative stability
towards both acidic and basic condition. The allyl group permits orhogonal protection
strategies, which is highly needed for this project. Isomerization to the more labile enol
ether employing KOtBu, with subsequent mild acidic hydrolysis, is one deprotection strategy,
however, this approach is only useful if the substrate is not base sensitive. Deprotection can
also happen under mild conditions, by employing the reagent combination of polymethylhy-
drosiloxane (PMHS), ZnCl2 and Pd(PPh3)4.

Mechanism of Boc Deprotection
Scheme 27: Mechanism of Boc deprotection of abitrary primary or secondary amine. Drawn
with inspiration from [70].
The Boc protecting group is a carbamate and can be removed simply with dilute aqueous
acid [70]. Acid will hydrolyse Boc upon protonation of tert-butylcarbamate which promotes
release of the tert-butyl cation. This results in the formation of a carbamaic acid which can
spontaneously decarboxylate to from the free amine. The tert-butyl cation can deprotonate
and form isobutylene gas. Boc i stable towards base, since the carbonyl group is to hindered
to be attacked by OH−and Boc is strongly resistant to basic hydrolysis [70].

Mechanism of Fmoc Deprotection
Scheme 29: Mechanism of Fmoc deprotection of abitrary primary or secondary amine. Drawn
with inspiration from [70].
Fmoc contains an acidic proton, with pKa around 25 [70], as its conjugated base is an aromatic
cyclopentadienyl anion. The anionic aromatic product undergoes immediately elimination,
upon deprotonation, leading to the formation of carbamaic acid. Fmoc deprotection is a
E1cB (E1 conjugate base) elimination and takes place with a secondary or tertiary amine as
the base (DEA). The carbamaic acid decarboxylate spontaneously resulting in the loss of
CO2 from the eliminated product, liberating the free amine [70].

CuAAC reaction
Scheme 33: CuAAC reaction for the conjugation of spacer and SH peptide.
Cu(I)-catalyzed azide-alkyne cycloaddition (CuAAC) of terminal alkynes, uses Cu(I) to
make the reaction faster and gives the 1,4-substituted triazole completely selective [70].
CuAAC was published by Meldal and Fokin–Sharpless in 2002 [71]. CuAAC is unaﬀected
by a variety of functional groups and can be achieved with many sources of Cu(I) catalysts
and solvents, such as aqueous solvents [71]. This is what makes CuAAC such a successful
and applicable reaction. With Cu(I) as a catalyst the activation barrier is easily overcome
[71]. The pre-catalyst for the reaction is often a Cu(II) salt (such as CuSO4) together with a
reducing agent (such as sodium ascorbate) to form Cu(I). Another approach is to use a Cu(I)
compound, such as CuBr or CuOAc together with a base or a amine ligand and a reducing
agent, to avoid aerobic oxidation to Cu(II).
