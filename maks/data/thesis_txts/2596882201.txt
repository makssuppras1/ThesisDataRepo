Abstract 
Livestock is the greatest source of methane (CH4) emissions in Denmark, constituting 78 % of anthropogenic CH4 
emissions.Denmark’slivestockemissionsarecalculatedandreportedaspartoftheannualgreenhousegas
inventory report published by Danish Centre for Environment and Energy, following the guidelines provided by 
a comprehensive IPCC methodology report published in 2006. In North America however, studies have shown 
that using IPCC-based reporting schemes to calculate national livestock emissions, in some cases, led to 
significant underestimations of measured emissions. European and Danish research on this matter are largely 
absent. In 2019, a refinement of the 2006 report was published, which incorporated scientific advances from 
2006 to 2019. The objective of this study was to find out how CH4 emissions from livestock, calculated using the 
national methodology (DK) and the IPCC methodologies from 2006 (B06) and 2019 (R19), compare with 
measured CH4 emissions using a tracer gas dispersion method. For this purpose, six case farms in Denmark were 
selected: three cattle farms (C1, C2, C3), and three pig farms (P1, P2, P3). Theoretical CH4 emissions were on 
average estimated to 16.9-18.5 CH4/hour (C1), 5.1-5.9 CH4/hour (C2), 0.8-1.5 CH4/hour (C3), 2.2-4.8 CH4/hour 
(P1), 3.5-6.6 CH4/hour (P2) and 1.1-2.1 CH4/hour (P3). In general, cattle farm CH4 emissions were dominated by 
enteric fermentation emission, constituting 79 % of the total emission at cattle farms, due to large feed intakes 
by ruminants. Pig farm CH4 emissions were dominated by manure management emission, constituting 73 % of 
the total emission at pig farms, due to large farms with several thousands of animals, whose manure was handled 
in slurry based systems, which allow significant CH4 production. CH4 emissions were quantified using ground-
based remote sensing of CH4 emission at farm-scale, using controlled release of tracer gas. Measurement 
campaigns were performed at each farm with a frequency of two months, for periods of 5-16 months. The 
measured emissions were 20.8 ± 0.9 kg CH4/hour (C1), 7.9 ± 0.3 kg CH4/hour (C2), 1.3 ± 0.1 kg CH4/hour (C3), 5.8 
± 0.2 kg CH4/hour (P1), 11.9 ± 0.5 kg CH4/hour (P2) and 3.8 ± 0.2 kg CH4/hour (P3). A full year of measurement 
was only performed at C1 and P1, why it was recommended to complete full year of measurements at the four 
remaining farms (C2, C3, P2, P3). The theoretical and measured emissions were compared, and an error percent 
(%) of theoretical emissions calculated. Measured livestock emission was underestimated by -27 %, -30 % and -
45 % on average using R19, B06 and DK, respectively. The findings would increase the total annual anthropogenic 
CH4 emissions in Denmark by +35 %. Cattle farm emissions were relatively well estimated when using the models, 
which led to underestimations of the measured emissions by -10 % (R19 and B06), and -25 % (DK). For pig farms, 
using the models led to, on average, great underestimations; by -44 % (R19), -51 % (B06) and -64 % (DK). Overall 
for cattle and pig farms, using DK led to the greatest underestimation of emissions. This study indicated the main 
reasons: 1) beef cattle were not represented in the normative standards leading to underestimated feed intakes, 
2) housing based manure production possibly led to an underestimation of manure load at the pig farms and 3) 
country-specific CH4 conversion factors (MCF) for pig manure did not reflect the conditions at the pig farms. 
Overall for the six farms, R19 compared best with measurements, primarily due to an implementation of a 
temperature/retention model for a liquid/slurry MCF, why this study reinforced the replacement of B06 with 
R19. Moreover, the study implied the need for a comprehensive verification of slurry manure emissions at 
manure tank level, at minimum a monthly discretization, in order to tune the MCF models (DK and R19).

Chapter 1. Introduction 
1.1. Background and project definition 
Climate change is altering global physical, biological and managed systems (Field et al., 2014). The alterations 
pose risks for human and natural systems, thereby challenging sustainable development. That climate change is 
driven by human emission of greenhouse gases (GHG) has long been expressed by the scientific community. 
According to the Intergovernmental Panel on Climate Change (IPCC) it is extremely likely (95-100 % probability) 
thathumaninﬂuencehasbeenthedominantcauseoftheobservedwarmingoftheplanetsincethemid-20th 
century (Myhre et al., 2013).  
Atmospheric methane (CH4) is the second largest contributor to climate change after carbon dioxide (CO2). When 
looking at the globe as a whole, the net energy balance of the Earth has increased with 2.29 watts per square 
meter (W/m2) for 2011 relative to 1750 (Myhre et al., 2013). Emissions of anthropogenic CH4 alone have caused 
a radiative forcing1 of 0.97 W/m2, only exceeded by radiative forcing from CO2 (Myhre et al., 2013). While the 
mechanism affecting the overall energy budget of Earth is the same for all GHGs, the magnitude of their impact 
differs, described by their global warming potential2 (GWP). The GWP is an important factor, when comparing 
the contribution of different GHGs to global warming. The emission of each GHG is converted to CO2-equivalents 
(eq) by multiplying the emitted mass by its respective GWP. CH4 is a potent GHG with a GWP over a 100-year 
time horizon of 21-28, when not considering climate change feedbacks (Table 1.1). Considering climate change 
feedbacks, the GWP of CH4 has been assessed to 34 (Myhre et al., 2013). The Danish inventory utilizes the Fourth 
Assessment Report3 as reference for the GWP i.e. 25 (Nielsen et al., 2019).

Denmark’s
livestock emissions are reported as part of the national GHG inventory report published by Danish 
Centre for Environment and Energy (DCE) at Aarhus University, Denmark. Like other countries committed to the 
United Nations Framework Convention on Climate Change (UNFCCC), and later on the Paris Agreement, Denmark 
is obliged to annually calculate and report GHG emissions. For this purpose, the IPCC periodically issues 
comprehensive methodology reports, which provide reporting guidelines for countries to calculate their national 
GHG emission. The IPCC reporting guidelines are based on calculations utilizing statistical activity data and source 
specific emission factors, which in turn are estimated according to livestock productivity, diet quality and manure 
management circumstances (Dong et al., 2006; Gavriolva et al., 2019). The uncertainty of emission factors should 
be calculated and reported together with the emissions according to the IPCC guidelines (Dong et al., 2006; 
Gavriolva et al., 2019). The uncertainty of emission factors for livestock in Denmark is set to ± 20 % (Nielsen et 
al., 2019). CH4 monitoring station observations in Europe combined with inverse modelling showed that 
uncertainties for CH4 emissions might be underestimated for some CH4 sources, but not for livestock emissions, 
which generally showed good consistency with the uncertainty range of the UNFCCC (Bergamaschi et al., 2015). 
In Canada and the United States (US) however, studies have indicated that using IPCC-based methodologies to 
calculate livestock CH4 emission, in some cases, led to significant underestimations of measured emissions (Baldé 
et al., 2016; Miller et al., 2013; Turner et al., 2015; Wecht et al., 2014). Based on satellite observations of 
atmospheric CH4 in North America, Miller et al. (2013) pointed to an underestimation by -200 % of livestock CH4 
emission in the US Environmental Protection Agency (US EPA) methodology. Baldé et al. (2016) used a 
micrometeorological technique to measure CH4 emissions from liquid dairy manure storage in a cold climate and 
found annual underestimations by up to -60 % (Baldé et al., 2016). Similarly, a global study, revising emission 
factors based on a review of field studies, suggested that current GHG emission factors for dairy manure 
generally are underestimated in the US and Europe (Owen and Silver, 2015). In fact, revised emission factors 
nearly doubled liquid manure CH4 emissions for Europe. Similarly, VanderZaag (2018) documented, through 
calculations, a systematic underestimation of emissions from liquid manure management, when using the IPCC 
methodology (VanderZaag, 2018). Contrary, other studies found no significant differences between IPCC-based 
methodology and other CH4 emission assessments. For example, Desjardins et al. (2018) found that aircraft-
based CH4 flux measurements agreed well with IPCC-based estimates for agricultural land with less than 10 % 
wetland area (Desjardins et al., 2018). Similarly, Arndt et al. (2018) indicated that US EPA methodology, which is 
based on IPCC reporting guidelines, can be used to estimate CH4 emissions from animal housing if feed intake is 
available (Arndt et al., 2018). To

Project objective 
The objective of this study was to compare theoretical IPCC-based estimates of CH4 emissions with CH4 emissions 
measured at farm-scale. In total, three IPCC-based inventory methodologies were evaluated due to their 
relevance for Denmark. The national Danish calculation method is based on the, at the time, newest IPCC 
methodology report from 2006. Since then, a refinement of the IPCC report from 2006 has been published, which 
ought to constitute the basis of future methodologies. Thus, three distinctive models for are relevant when 
investigating emission from Danish farms are: 

The R19 model; 2019 IPCC guidelines refinement, which uses relevant scientific knowledge advances 
from 2006 to 2018 to improve the methodology 

The B06 model; 2006 IPCC guidelines (the basis model for R19 and DK) 

The DK model; National methodology, which is based on B06 with country-specific adaptations  
The study was based on six commercial cattle and pig farms in Denmark. CH4 emission measurements can be 
used to evaluate reporting methodologies and bring more information to improve inventories and access 
seasonal fluctuations. Emissions should ideally be measured at a farm-scale in order to capture the reality of a 
farm as an integrated, multi-component emission source, and duplicated in different regions to understand the 
effect of climate and regional management on emissions (VanderZaag et al., 2014). In this project, CH4 
measurements were performed using a tracer gas dispersion methodology combined with vehicle borne remote 
sensing to obtain emission rates. This measurement technique is preferred over other techniques, as it provides 
the integrated CH4 emission at farm-scale and has been shown to provide reliable emission data with a relative 
low uncertainty. The thesis goal was synthesized into one main research question and four sub-questions. The 
overall question to be answered from this project was: 
How do theoretical CH4 emissions calculated using national reporting schemes compare with CH4 emission rates 
measured using a tracer gas dispersion method at six farms in Denmark? 
The sub-questions were: 
I.
What are the theoretical CH4 emissions from six case farms located in Denmark based on 
national and international IPCC-based inventory models? 
II.
What are the measured CH4 emissions from six case farms utilizing a tracer gas dispersion 
method? 
III.
How do the theoretical emissions and measured emissions compare? 
IV.
How do the findings impact the national GHG inventory?

Chapter 2. Theory 
2.1. CH4 emission from livestock production in Denmark 
According to the Danish national GHG inventory report, CH4 emission from livestock production in Denmark is 
222 kt CH4, corresponding to 5543 kt CO2-eq, constituting 78 % of total anthropogenic CH4 emissions in Denmark 
(7110 kt. CO2-eq) (Nielsen et al., 2019). CH4 emission from livestock comes from two sources in livestock 
production; enteric fermentation and manure management. Whereas enteric fermentation refers to the 
anaerobic breakdown of feed by microorganisms in the digestive system of animals, manure management refers 
to handling, storage and treatment of livestock manure i.e. urinary and fecal excretes. CH4 is formed from manure 
due to a presence of enteric microorganisms excreted into the manure by the livestock, a high availability of 
substrates4 and anaerobic conditions, which tend to prevail (Reay et al., 2010). Enteric fermentation is a 
fundamental digestion process occurring in many animals, and is particularly enhanced in ruminants such as 
cattle, which utilize the process to digest feed with a high fiber5 content (Millen et al., 2016). Whereas animals 
feed generally consists of a combination of sugars, starch, raw protein, raw fat, acids, ash and fibers, the natural 
ruminant diet contains significant amounts of the fibers pectin, hemicellulose, cellulose and lignin, which are the 
major components of cell-wall structure in grass (Millen et al., 2016). These structures are digested in ruminants 
by means of rumination, which is a cycle of feeding, regurgitating and re-chewing. In this process, CH4 escapes 
from the animal through breaths and burps (Gavriolva et al., 2019).  
Enteric fermentation is the largest source of livestock CH4 emission, contributing 67 % of total livestock CH4 
emission in Denmark. Manure management emission constitutes the remaining emission; 33 %. Cattle and pigs 
are by far the largest sources of CH4 emissions compared to other livestock in Denmark i.e. sheep, poultry, goats, 
horses, fur farming, deer, ostriches and pheasants (Figure 2.1). The main CH4 sources for all livestock categories 
in Denmark are enteric fermentation in dairy cows (91 kt CH4), management of pig manure (43 kt CH4), enteric 
fermentation in non-dairy cows (40 kt CH4), and management of cattle manure (26 kt CH4) (Figure 2.1).

2.2. Background on livestock production in Denmark 
Cattle and pig population 
In Denmark, there are about 1.5 million cattle of which the largest group is dairy cows with a population of 
570,000 animals (Figure 2.2). Cows are defined as female cattle that have given birth to a calf (calved) at least 
once. Female calves are primarily raised to become a dairy cow and male calves are typically sold to farmers 
specialized in meat production i.e. beef cattle farmers. Theterminology‘non-dairycattle’includeallcattlethat
are not dairy cows. Heifers, i.e. female cattle that have not calved yet, is the most abundant non-dairy cattle 
subgroup with a population of 471,000 animals over the age of six months. In national published sources, non-
dairy cattle in Denmark are grouped according to sex and age as follows; heifers above six months of age, heifers 
under six months of age i.e. heifer calves, bulls over six months of age, bulls under six months of age i.e. bull 
calves, and suckling cattle (Nielsen et al., 2016). Suckling cattle are cows, often a dairy breed, that are kept in the 
beef herd with the purpose of feeding the young ones with milk. Suckling cattle and dairy cows are typically 
slaughtered for meat production after ended use.   
Due to the magnitude of Danish dairy production, the most common cattle race in Denmark is the dairy breed 
Sortbroget Dansk Malkerace (SDM); a dairy breed with a population of 850,000 animals in Denmark of dairy and 
non-dairy cattle. SDM constitutes around 70 % of all dairy cattle in Denmark and 55 % of all cattle in Denmark 
(SEGES, 2020). The second-most abundant dairy cattle race is Jersey, which constitutes 12 % of the dairy cattle 
population in Denmark (SEGES, 2020). In Denmark, the a relatively large fraction of the dairy production is 
organic. Currently, 12 % of the Danish dairy production is organic. Furthermore, the portion of organic production 
in Denmark is expected to increase in the future (Danish Agriculture and Food Council, 2020). When looking at 
the beef cattle population in Denmark, the breed diversity is larger than in the dairy industry. Hybrids of different

Production of pigs constitutes a significant part of Danish livestock production with a population of over 12 
million pigs (Figure 2.2). In 2018, Denmark ranked fifth in the production of pig meat in Europe, only surpassed

Feed 
Pigs in Denmark are primarily fed barley and wheat. In addition, soy and rape can be added for extra proteins 
and vitamins and minerals added for extra nutrients (Danish Agriculture and Food Council, 2019b). The natural 
feed of cattle is grass (also called pasture). In Denmark, only cattle that is produced organically must have access 
to outdoor grazing areas (Økologisk Landsforening, 2015). Organic cattle in Denmark must have access to 
outdoor grazing areas in the period April 15th to November 1st, when weather conditions and animal 
considerations are taken into account (SEGES, 2019). Cattle at conventional farms (the majority of farms in 
Denmark) are typically inside the housing throughout the year. These cattle are typically fed silage, which is a 
chopped, fermented feed source, primarily from annual crops like grass and corn (Andersen, 2015). A decreasing 
proportion of cattle are also fed hay, which is dried grass. In this report (based on the IPCC wording), pasture, 
silage andhayarereferredtoasthefeedtype‘forage’.Cattleat Danish farms (conventional as well as organic 
farms) receive feed supplements from concentrate feeds; typically soybeans, grains or peas that are processed 
and pressed into pellets or powder, which are easily digestible feeds with a high energy content (Andersen, 
2015). The diet of organic cattle must consist of a minimum 60 % forage on a daily basis, whereas there is no 
regulation on conventional cattle with regard hereto. However, to maintain healthy digestion, conventional 
cattle receive a large part of their feed intake from forage (Andersen, 2015).

Housing systems and manure types 
Most of cattle and pig manure in Denmark is handled in slurry-based production systems, where the manure 
(liquids and solids) are handled together as slurry manure (Lund et al., 2019). Slurry-based systems often involve 
a slurry pit below parts of the housing, which collects manure that seeps between spaces (slats) in the floor. 
Housings with slatted floor must have a solid part of the floor for the animals to rest (Danish Agriculture and 
Food Council, 2019b). 
Danish cattle housing consists of a variety of systems (Table 2.1). All cattle except for calves (i.e. cattle under the 
age of six months) are mainly housed by loose holding, which means that cattle can walk around freely. The 
walking area floor can be solid or slatted. Manure from solid floors is removed via flushing with water or manual 
scraping. Dairy cows and heifers often have individual beds filled with sand as their resting area (Figure 2.4). 
Suckling cattle, calves and to some extent dairy cows, heifers and bulls are kept in deep bedding i.e. a thick layer 
of straw (or other organic materials such as sawdust, wood chips or heather) on solid floor (Figure 2.4). New 
bedding is continuously sprinkled on top of the existing bedding in order to keep the animals dry. In deep bedding 
housing, areas for resting and activity are not necessarily separated. The manure mixes with the bedding, which

Ventilation in housing 
Livestock buildings use two types of ventilation, gravitational (natural) and forced (mechanical), to remove 
moisture and heat and maintain air quality (Rzeźnik	and	Mielcarek,	2016). Danish cattle farms are typically 
naturally ventilated in open houses (Nielsen et al., 2019). For pig farms, mechanical ventilation is often utilized 
due to the large heat production from the pigs, which creates a high air exchange inside the housing. Mechanical 
ventilation is utilized to prevent draft by distribution the air evenly (Miljøstyrelsen, 2019).

As an alternative to storing the raw slurry on the farm until utilization as fertilization, some farmers send manure 
to biogas production facilities, where controlled anaerobic degradation of the manure takes place while 
capturing the produced biogas; a mixture of CH4, CO2, hydrogen sulphide (H2S) and hydrogen (H2), which can be 
used for electricity or heat production (Reay et al., 2010). This process is also referred to as biogas treatment. 
Currently, 17 % of the total amount of livestock slurry in Denmark is delivered for biogas treatment i.e. 22 % of 
the total amount of cattle slurry and 12 % of the pig slurry (Nielsen et al., 2019). The amount of livestock slurry 
being used for biogas production is increasing (Nielsen et al., 2019).  
The biogas production may take place in a local digester placed at the farm, which typically receives manure from 
one or a few farms. More frequently, the biogas production occurs at larger centralized biogas facilities that 
receive manure from several farms and other biodegradable products such as crops and slaughterhouse waste. 
After biogas treatment, the digestate (i.e. the digested organic material) is sent back to the farm where it is 
stored in manure tanks and later on applied to agricultural fields, similarly to how raw manure is typically 
handled. Producers who send manure to central digesters receive a mixed digestate from a combination of co-
digested manure and other waste types. In 2017, the total energy production based on biogas was estimated to 
11,158 PJ, and out of this, the manure based biogas plants account for approximately 87 % produced at 33 large-
scale plants and 59 farm-level plants (Nielsen et al., 2019).

2.3. IPCC basis model from 2006 
Through the IPCC, thousands of experts from around the world synthesize the most recent developments in 
climate science, adaptation, vulnerability, and mitigation every five to seven years. Among these, vital guidelines 
reports are synthesized with the purpose of aiding countries in calculating their GHG contribution. The 
methodology utilizes activity data and source specific emission factors (EF). For livestock emissions, an EF (kg 
CH4/head/year) is a factor connected to a livestock category and states the CH4 emission per head for a livestock 
category. The EF should be multiplied with the number of the livestock category in order to obtain the total 
emission (Dong et al., 2006). The IPCC methodology offers three Tiers for EF calculation. Tier 1 and Tier 2 
represent the least demanding calculations, and are therefore most likely to be utilized by the countries around 
the world (Kariyapperuma et al., 2018). Tier 1 uses livestock population data and default IPCC EFs to estimate 
emission. Tier 2 is more detailed and advised when emissions from a specific livestock category make up a 
significant	 share	of	 a	 nation’s	 GHG	emissions.	 Tier 3 involves sophisticated, peer-reviewed, country-specific 
models (Dong et al., 2006; Gavriolva et al., 2019) and is advised only when such high quality and specific data is 
available at a national level. The IPCC suggests Tier 2 for calculation of the enteric fermentation EF for cattle and 
the manure management EF for cattle and pigs. Tier 1 is suggested for calculating the EF for enteric fermentation 
in pigs (Table 2.3).

EFenteric increases with feed intake i.e. a high feed intake leads to high EFenteric. In Tier 2, countries are encouraged 
to use country-specific data when determining feed intake e.g. from reported feeding plans. As an alternative, 
the IPCC provides a calculation method (Equations 2.2-2.9). Feed intake is principally calculated as the sum of 
net energy requirements for important body functions; maintenance (NEM), activity (NEA), lactation (NEL), 
pregnancy (NEP) and growth (NEG), taking into account the DE, which reflects the portion of feed intake that is 
digested i.e. not lost as excretes, urine and fermentation gas. Because of significant variation, DE values should 
be obtained from local scientific data wherever possible. Due to the general inaccessibility of these data, the 
IPCC provides a default range for the DE according to different feed types for cattle (Table 2.5).  
Small sized feed particles are easier to digest, because microbial digestion of feed by enteric fermentation is 
most effective when feed particles are small; 1-5 millimeter (mm) in size (Nørgaard, 2004). The breakdown of 
feed particles in the rumen, especially fibers, requires a large surface area of the particles and the relative particle 
surface area is inversely proportional to the particle length. The physical break-down of particles happens during 
eating and re-chewing. Cutting the feed before serving it to the animals, or replacing forage with concentrates, 
therefore reduces the eating and rumination time (Nørgaard, 2004). Concentrate-based diets are associated with 
high DE, because concentrates have a particle size of less than 6 millimeter (Nordic Feed Evaluation System, 
2006) and are high in nutrients. Pasture diets with concentrate supplements are associated with medium DE. 
Low quality forage, such as crop by-products, are associated with low DE.

Evidently, CH4 production from enteric fermentation is proportional to the feed intake and Ym (cf. Equation 2.1). 
A high CH4 production comes from a high feed intake and from a large fraction of the energy in the feed being 
converted to CH4 emission. DE, feed intake and growth (or milk production) are co-dependent phenomena. The 
feed intake is largely affected by the DE. A low DE will typically lead to lower feed intake, because less time during 
the day is available for eating, re-chewing and ruminating. Thus, a low DE of feed will lead to lower feed intake 
and consequently reduced growth and CH4 emission, but at the same time a larger associated CH4 production 
per unit of growth or production. Conversely, high DE will often result in higher feed intake and increased growth 
but at the same time a smaller amount of feed required per unit of growth and consequently lower associated 
CH4 production per unit growth or production. A 10 % error in estimating DE will be magnified to 12-20 % when 
estimating EFenteric (Dong et al., 2006; Gavriolva et al., 2019). 
Manure management emission factor 
The manure management emission factor (EFmanure, kg CH4/head/year) represents the amount of CH4 generated 
from handling manure produced by one animal in a year in a specific manure management system. The EFmanure 
is calculated from a potential maximum CH4 production, determined by the amount of volatile solids excretion 
rate (VS) in the livestock manure, the density of CH4 and the maximum CH4 producing capacity for manure 
produced by the livestock category (Dong et al., 2006). The potential maximum CH4 production (VS'⋅ 0.67'⋅'B0) 
describes the CH4 production under perfect conditions, thus rarely achieved in practice. The EFmanure is therefore 
obtained by multiplying with a CH4 conversion factor (MCF, %), which reflects the portion of the potential 
maximum CH4 that is achieved (Equation 2.10). In the case of more than one manure management systems 
utilized for an animal category, the fraction of manure managed in each manure management system (MSS, 
dimensionless) must be collected.

fractions. The potential for CH4 production increases with VS excretion; a high VS excretion gives a high potential

The most important refinements were several retention times, manure temperature lagging behind the air 
temperature instead of equal to, and assuming that 5 % remains after manure removal from storage (proposed), 
instead of complete emptying. The average percent of manure removed from the manure tank is 95 % as default, 
but this value can also be replaced by an input of the user. Besides the mandatory and optional user inputs, the 
model consists of fixed inputs and calculations. The model works by setting the manure temperature to the 
previous month’s ambient temperature. E.g. the ambient temperature in January 2019 in Denmark was 1.9 C, 
hence the manure temperature of February 2019 is set to 1.9 C and so on. If the ambient temperature is lower 
than 1 C, the manure temperature is kept at 1 C (minimum T2). If manure is only removed once per year (count 
of yes = 1), the manure temperature for each month is calculated by subtracting 3 degrees C (the damping 
temperature) from the monthly mean ambient temperature. The minimum T2 of 1 C is still used as lower bound 
in this case. The minimum T2 and the damping T2 are optional user inputs i.e. IPCC default values as a starting 
point but can be replaced if necessary.  
The model runs with a monthly discretization for three years to ensure VS available has stabilized on an annual 
basis. The results from the third year (stabilized conditions) are used (VanderZaag, 2018). The first two years act 
as “build up” of organic load in the manure tank based on manure load and emptying conditions. Thus, using the 
model assumes that the management (load and emptying routines) and ambient temperatures are not varying 
inter-annually. From the calculated manure temperature (T2) for each calendar month, an f factor, which 
characterizes the temperature response of CH4 producing microorganisms, is calculated for each month utilizing 
the Vvan’t Hoff-Arrhenius equation (Equation 2.12). The f parameter factor is used to calculate a conceptual 
entity of VS consumed, which equals the VS available in storage multiplied by the van’tHoff-Arrhenius factor, 
which is temperature dependent. The term VS consumed does not represent the real VS degraded but instead a 
conceptual quantity of VS removed from the liquid/slurry storage against the total CH4 potential at 35 °C i.e. the 
conceptual proportion of the B0 consumed at that point (Gavriolva e al., 2019). The VS available in the storage is 
calculated by a mass balance from the VS loaded to storage this month added to the VS available in the previous 
month and subtracted by the VS consumed for CH4 production previous month. Finally, the annual MCF is 
calculated from the ratio of predicted VS consumed over one year to the total VS that entered the storage over 
one year.

The normative standards system calculates standards for relevant livestock categories i.e. cattle, pigs, poultry, 
furred animals, horses, sheep and goats. Each category is subdivided in weight classes, age classes etc. relevant 
for the livestock production in Denmark. The calculations are step-wise mass balances calculations with the goal 
to determine the composition of fertilizer (Figure 2.8). For manure production used in the calculation of the 
EFmanure, the results of step two is used. Step one calculates the excretion by the livestock based on recordings 
and calculations of feed intake combined with statistics on nutrient contentsindiets.Nutrients’retention(what

coefficients of different nutrients. Step two calculates the manure leaving the housing, for storage or to be used 
directly as fertilizer, in relevant housing systems in livestock production in Denmark. Default nitrogen loss from 
the housing system are subtracted. Contribution of nutrients from bedding materials and the soaking of urine 
into the bedding material and faeces is calculated in order to establish values for slurry (faeces and urine 
together) and separately for faeces (manure or deep bedding) and urine (liquid manure). For each housing 
system the manure type (slurry, deep bedding and liquid) is defined and standards are calculated. Step three 
(the final step) subtracts an expected loss of nitrogen and dry matter during storage from the manure leaving 
the house, giving the fertilizer composition (Poulsen et al., 2006).

conditions (Sommer et al., 2004). During national studies conducted to cover e.g. emission from anaerobically 
digested material (Petersen et al., 2016), it became evident that the currently used MCFs for cattle and pig slurry 
(the default values from the 2006 IPCC Guidelines) were not properly reflecting the Danish conditions. Especially 
the pig slurry MCF was underestimated in the IPCC guidelines.  
 
The MCF model used an Arrhenius function with parameters based on Petersen et al. (2016) combined with data 
from Elsgaard et al. (2016). A degradation function took into account the loss of VS during storage. The average 
retention time of manure in Danish livestock housing was included (17.25 days and 18.49 days for pig and cattle, 
respectively). The model used annual monthly mean temperatures from Danish weather statistics for a set of 
relevant years. The temperature in pig housing was assumed to be constant at 18.7 °C. Cattle housing 
temperature was assumed to equal the air temperature plus 5 °C.  The outdoor storage temperature of manure 
was expected to follow the outdoor temperature to a great extent. The outdoor storage temperature of manure 
was estimated with a temperature function, which takes into account the loading with slurry, the actual amount 
stored and the solar radiation. A general storage profile for manure tanks was developed together with SEGES13 
(cf. Figure 2.7). The MCF for undigested slurry was estimated based on the emission from housing and outdoor 
storage based on average conditions in Denmark. The MCF for digested slurry was calculated based on the 
emission from housing and outdoor storage of digested manure. Thus, the digested manure MCF includes the 
manure emissions from the raw manure stored in the barn.

2.6. Summary of model approaches 
This section provides a brief summary of model approaches to livestock CH4 emission factor calculation. The 
three models are the newest IPCC model (R19), the 2006 IPCC model (B06) and the Danish country-specific model 
(DK). For all models, the first step is to define livestock categories and gather representative information for each 
category. Independent of model chosen, the annual average population of each livestock category is required. 
Two emission factors (EF, kg CH4/head/hour) are estimated for each livestock category; for enteric fermentation 
and manure management, respectively. The enteric fermentation EF of the livestock category is multiplied with 
the annual population to obtain an emission contribution from enteric fermentation. Parallel, the manure 
management EF is multiplied with the annual population to obtain an emission contribution from manure 
management.    
For enteric fermentation, the IPCC models encourage Tier 2 estimation of cattle emission and Tier 1 estimation 
of pig emission (Figure 2.10). For Tier 2 estimation of cattle emission, average daily feed intake and CH4 yields 
(Ym) should be gathered from country-specific research or other public sources. If not available, B06 provides a 
simple calculation method to estimate the feed intake from assumptions about weight, growth, activity, milk 
production, pregnancy and feed digestibility (DE). Default Ym values are also provided, which should be selected 
based on the livestock category and feed quality. The advancement in Ym from B06 to R19 is a slightly broader 
range of Ym values based on a more detailed selection scheme (Table 2.16). Opposite to what the IPCC advises, 
the DK model uses Tier 2 (not Tier 1) to estimate emission for pigs. As encouraged by the IPCC, the DK model 
utilizes country-specific feed intakes based on normative standards and country-specific Ym values for grazing 
and non-grazing dairy cattle and for pigs. For non-dairy cattle, Ym values from B06 are used (Table 2.16). 
For manure management, the IPCC models encourage Tier 2 estimation of cattle and pig emission (Figure 2.11). 
For Tier 2 estimation of emission, average daily manure production and CH4 conversion factors (MCF) should be 
gathered from country-specific research or other public sources. If not available, the IPCC provides a simple 
calculation method to estimate the VS from the estimated feed intake (from enteric fermentation) and DE. 
Default MCF values are also provided, which should be selected based on climatic conditions and the manure 
management system utilized for the livestock category. The advancement from B06 to R19 is a more detailed set 
of climatic conditions and a temperature/retention model for liquid/slurry storage (Table 2.17). As advised by 
the IPCC, DK utilizes Tier 2 to estimate manure emission for cattle and pigs. DK utilizes normative standards for 
manure production together with country-specific MCFs for raw cattle slurry, raw pig slurry, digested pig slurry 
and digested cattle slurry derived for Danish conditions (Table 2.17). For other manure management systems, 
MCF values from B06 are used.

2.7. Literature evaluating IPCC-based livestock emission estimates   
In general, only few studies have been performed, which compare measured CH4 emission at commercial cattle 
and pig farms with estimates from IPCC-based models (Table 2.18). Some studies were performed on manure 
tanks only (Baldé et al., 2016; Flesch et al., 2013; Kariyapperuma et al., 2018; Park et al., 2006) whereas one 
study was performed on the whole facility (Arndt et al., 2018). Due to the lack of farm-scale studies, some 
regional and national level studies have been included. Regional and national studies generally show that 
livestock CH4 emissions are likely underestimated using the US EPA methodology, but due to the coarse 
geographical resolution, the reasons are not identified (Miller et al., 2013; Turner et al., 2015; Wecht et al., 2014). 
Studies at smaller scales, often performed on manure tanks over time periods longer than a year, give ambiguous 
indications of the accuracy of the IPCC models. In some cases, the IPCC MCF for liquid manure storage was too 
high compared to the measurements, e.g. Kariyapperuma et al. (2018), and in other cases, the IPCC MCF for 
liquid manure storage was too low compared to the measurements (e.g. (Baldé et al., 2016; Flesch et al., 2013; 
Kariyapperuma et al., 2018). A general absence of European studies is seen, since all of the identified studies 
were performed in North America (the US and Canada).

3.2. Estimation of theoretical CH4 emissions applying IPCC-based modelling  
Development of an Excel spreadsheet model 
Farm-specific, theoretical emission rates were estimated using the three models; R19, B06 and DK as described 
in Section 2.3, 2.4 and 2.5, respectively. Adapting the models for farm-scale use required minor adaptations, 
which are presented here. The CH4 emission from each livestock type at the farm (i.e. dairy cows, bulls above six 
months of age, heifers above six months of age, heifer calves, bull calves, sows, fattening pigs or weaners) was 
calculated by multiplying the estimated annual EF for the livestock category with the activity data (the number 
of livestock) and thereafter converting to emission per hour (Equation 3.1). This was performed separately for 
enteric fermentation and manure management. CH4 emission from one farm was then estimated by calculating 
the sum of CH4 contributions from enteric fermentation and manure management from each livestock category. 
Theoretical emission calculations were performed by setting up a spreadsheet model in Excel 2016 utilizing the 
methodology described previously (Equations 2.1-2.18 and Equation 3.18). The spreadsheet was structured with 
one worksheet per farm. Each farm worksheet constitutes a brief farm summary, input data, methodology and 
results (Figure 3.13). The methodology constitutes input data, equations, Ym, MCF), other parameters and 
calculations (Figure 3.14).

Distribution of manure handled in more than one management system 
All three models require that the portion of the manure managed in a given manure management system (MS) 
is estimated in percentages. The MS was used together with the MCF for a given manure management system 
to calculate the EFmanure (cf. Equation 2.1). Due to the structure of the DK model, the MSs should only be 
calculated for manure excreted in the housing part of the year i.e. not for manure excreted in the grazing period 
of the year.

The estimation of MS was required for livestock categories, for which the manure was management in more than 
one manure management system. If the livestock manure was only managed in one system (e.g. as slurry), the 
MSslurry was 100 %. For livestock manure that was handled in more than one handling system, the MSs were 
calculated from the number of grazing days in a year (if grazing was utilized at the farm) and assuming that 
livestock partly housed in deep bedding would excrete 60 % of manure in the bedding and 40 % in other areas 
e.g. on the slatted floor. An example is heifers above six months at C1, where a portion was excreted on the field 
during grazing (MSpasture), a portion was managed as deep bedding (MSdeep bedding) and a portion was managed as 
slurry (MSslurry) (Calculation example 1). The sum of the MSs (MSpasture+ MSdeep bedding + MSslurry) should be 100 %. 
In this case (and for all grazing livestock i.e. relevant for C1 and C3), the MSpasture was calculated as a first step. 
For grazing livestock, the MSpasture was equal to the ratio of total number of grazing days in a year to the number 
of days in a year. The remaining portion of manure excreted in a year was split between deep bedding and slurry 
according to an assumption for cattle in deep bedding with a large eating space, for which 60 % of the manure is 
expected to be excreted in the deep bedding and 40 % is excreted in the eating space (Danish Energy Agency, 
2015).  
 
CH4 conversion factors for biogas treatment 
In order to estimate emissions from farms where biogas treatment of manure was utilized, a selection of MCF 
for biogas treatment had to be made. For B06, the country-specific MCFs for biogas treatment provided in the 
DK model (2.61 % for digested cattle manure and 10.45 % for digested pig manure) were used due to the absence 
of an IPCC default value and the encouragement to utilize a country-specific value. For R19, a MCF value of 3.55 
% was selected for biogas treatment, which corresponds to a high quality digester followed by open storage of 
the digestate in a cold climate (cf. Table 2.10). The choice is justified by:  

the open storage of digestate in all farms storing digestate (C2 and P3) 

an average 2.4 % loss of CH4 (m3/m3) from agricultural biogas production plants in Denmark (Scheutz and 
Fredenslund, 2019).  
The MCF of 3.55 % assumes an average loss of CH4 from the digester at 1 %. Thus, the actual loss of CH4 at Danish 
plants might be slightly higher. However, the loss is not expected to be high enough to justify selecting a value 
for low quality digesters i.e. loss of 10 % of the specific volume of CH4 produced.

Volatile solids excretion rate for pigs in the Danish model 
The emission from manure is determined from the annual production of manure, which in the DK model is 
provided by normative standards from Lund et al. (2019), which gives annual normative standards for manure 
excretion based on animal category and housing system (cf. Table 2.12). However, for weaners and fattening 
pigs, the excretion is provided for ‘one produced	weaner’	and	‘one produced	fattening	pig’	due	to the short 
lifespan of these animals in livestock production i.e. under a year. A produced weaner requires a weight increase 
of about 25 kg (from 6.6 kg to 31 kg) and a produced fattening pig requires a weight increase of about 82 kg 
(from 31 kg to 113 kg). An annual manure production was however required for model purposes. The annual 
manure production was estimated by multiplying the normative standards for manure production by the number 
of production rounds in a year. 4 production rounds per year were assumed for fattening pigs at P1 and P2 and 
3.3 rounds were assumed for fattening pigs at Pig farm 3 (P3) (See farm descriptions). For weaners, eight 
production rounds per year were assumed at P1 and P3. P2 did not have any weaners. 
Feed digestibility 
Feed digestibility (DE, %) is required in several parts of R19 and B06 models. Although the use of country-specific 
DE were encouraged, these could not be provided by the farmers. Therefore, from the default DE ranges

Modification of the temperature/retention model for monthly use 
The temperature/retention model for calculation of the liquid/slurry MCF in R19 described in Section 2.4 was 
modifiedtouseonamonthlybasis.ThemotivationforthiswasthepossibilitytoevaluateR19’scapabilityof
tracking any intra-annual variations in the measured emission rates. A snapshot of the original model and the 
modified version can be seen in the Appendices (Figure A1 and Figure A2, respectively). The justifications for the 
model modifications made in this project were: 

The incentive for developing the temperature/retention model in the first place was that to use monthly 
discretized temperature as input (as opposed to the annual average temperature) (Gavriolva et al., 
2019). 

The temperature/retention model was already calculating CH4 production at a monthly discretization 
(VanderZaag, 2018). 

The monthly MCF was calculating utilizing the same procedure as calculating the annual MCF i.e. the 
MCF is equal to ratio of predicted VS consumed (kg) to the VS that entered the storage (kg) (cf. Section 
2.4). 

Averaging the monthly MCFs for all 12 calendar months gave an annual MCF identical to the annual MCF 
calculatedas‘original’outputofthemodel(cf.Section2.4). 
Consequently, using the monthly liquid/slurry MCF should be managed cleverly in the discussion and evaluation 
of the models. The capability of R19 to predict intra-annual variation should be evaluated in light of that this was 
not the original purpose of the model. The original purpose of the model was to calculate an annual MCF which 
incorporated the intra-annual variation in CH4 emission as a result of intra-annual variation of air temperature 
and actual retention times of the manure. Therefore, errors made by the monthly MCF are expected and should 
be handled with care. Further, the lack of the intra-annual variation in B06 and DK MCFs should not be seen as 
shortcomings of those models, since MCFs are developed to be used on annual basis. 
3.3. Quantification of CH4 emissions using the tracer gas dispersion method 
CH4 emissions were quantified using the tracer gas dispersion method (TDM). The TDM is a ground-based, 
remote sensing technique targeting whole CH4 emission at farm-scale. The method applies a controlled release 
of tracer gas at the farm with downwind concentration measurements of CH4 and tracer gas (Figure 3.15). This 
set-up has been used for various heterogeneous CH4 sources, for example landfills (Mønster et al., 2014), waste 
water treatment plants (Delre et al., 2018; Samuelsson et al., 2018), biogas plants (Fredenslund et al., 2018) and 
dairy farms (Arndt et al., 2018). For total quantification of CH4 emission at facility, TDM is preferable to on-site 
point measurements because it is less sensitive to short term variations (Fredenslund et al., 2018). On-site 
measurements is preferable if the purpose is to uncover leakages and perform mitigation strategies (Fredenslund 
et al., 2018).  
==PAGE:70==
3.3. Quantification of CH4 emissions using the tracer gas dispersion method 
 
70 
 
The principle of TDM is that the emission of CH4 is simulated using an inert tracer gas, in this case acetylene 
(C2H2) which has similar atmospheric behaviour as the target gas, CH4. Thus, the two gases are mixed and 
dispersed by the wind in a similar way. A controlled release of tracer gas at the location of the CH4 source(s) 
combined with simultaneous, downwind CH4 and tracer gas concentrations detections can therefore provide 
quantitative information about the emission ratio.  
 
Figure 3.15. Schematic drawing of the dynamic plume tracer dispersion method (left) The example is for a landfill but the principle is the 
same (Mønster et al., 2014). Example of Google Earth file showing the plumes of CH4 (red) and C2H2 (orange) (right). The example is from 
Cattle farm 2 from August 2019 (own illustration). 
A study evaluating the sensitivity of TDM, showed that uncertainties can be minimized by placing the tracer gas 
close to the source, and that driving downwind the CH4 source (1.2-3 km) reduces errors related to tracer 
placement (Mønster et al., 2014). However, driving too far downwind a small CH4 source results in a lower plume 
signal. The importance of a high plume signal within a plume traverse has been shown, as it provides more 
reliable (waste water treatment) plant-integrated emission quantifications (Delre, 2018). The CH4 emission rate 
can be obtained with several data treatment methods i.a. the peak height ratio, scatter plot, Gaussian plume and 
with plume integration (Mønster et al., 2014). The advantage of the plume integration over the other methods 
is that it is less sensitive to whether the CH4 and tracer plumes are fully mixed (Mønster et al., 2014). The method 
has been validated in a number of field tests. Wherefrom the method uncertainty, including quantification 
variability, has been accessed to be less than 20 %, when using best practise (Mønster et al., 2014; Fredenslund 
et al., 2019). 
Analytical platform 
The CH4 and C2H2 (trace gas) concentrations were obtained with a cavity ring down spectroscopy (CRDS) analyzer. 
CRDS uses an optical technology in which the mole-fraction of the gas is obtained by measuring the decay of 
infrared laser light in a sample cell (Samuelsson et al., 2018). The small flow cell (less than 10 cm3) and a long 
optical path length (15-20 km) allows for high precision i.e. low uncertainty in the ppb- and ppt-level. A diagram 
of the basic components, which comprise the CRDS analyzer and a detailed description can be seen in Crosson 
==PAGE:71==

(2008). A description of the spectrometry performance can be seen in (Mønster et al., 2014). Before October 
2019, one instrument equipped with lasers detecting CH4 and C2H2 (G2203, Picarro, Inc., Santa Clara, CA) was 
used. The CH4/C2H4 analyzer recorded every 0.5 second with a precision of 0.77 ppb and 0.06 ppb for CH4 and 
C2H2, respectively. After October 2019 two instruments were used, one equipped with a laser detecting C2H2 
(JADS2001, Picarro, Inc., Santa Clara, California), recording every 3 seconds with a precision of 0.6 ppb, and one 
instrument with a laser detecting CH4 (G1301, Picarro, Inc., Santa Clara, California) recording every 3 seconds 
with a precision of 0.1 ppb (Wastine et al., 2008). The change in equipment was due to equipment failure. 
Measuring procedure and overview of campaigns 
Measurement campaigns were performed about every second month for one year in order to ensure that 
measurements were representative of annual inventories, according to best practice (Arndt et al., 2018; 
Desjardins et al., 2018). The duration of each measurement campaign was typically two hours. For each farm the 
specific days to perform measurement campaigns were selected to ensure certain weather conditions i.e. 
overcast and desired wind direction as well as stable atmospheric conditions during measurements. The weather 
conditions i.e. wind direction, air temperature, air pressure and wind velocity were recorded at the farm during 
the measurement campaign (Table 3.21).  
At initial visits to each farm, screening of CH4 sources was performed by driving around the farm in a vehicle 
carrying the instruments (Figure 3.16). The photo shows the placement of a Global Positioning System (GPS), 
which was used to record the position during measurements campaigns. Main CH4 sources were typically 
housings at cattle farms, and manure tanks at pig farms. The C2H2 bottle(s) were placed as close to the sources 
as possible (Figure 3.16). An initial screening was performed at the beginning of each measurement. Background 
concentrations of CH4 and C2H2 were obtained upwind the farm, prior to collecting plume measurements. Table 
3.21 shows the date, time, weather conditions, driving distance to the farm, number of traverses made, number 
of tracer gas release points and release rate for each measurement campaign. 
During each measurement campaign, cross-plume CH4 and C2H2 concentrations were collected repeatedly, 
typically through about 10-30 transects. Tracer gas was released at a controlled flow. The uncertainty of gas 
release is a combination of gas purity and flow meter uncertainty (Mønster et al., 2014). Therefore, the 
flowmeter was adjusted once during the campaign to ensure a stable flow. Furthermore, the C2H2 bottle(s) were 
weighed before and after to cross-check the tracer release. The selection of roads for traverses were chosen 
based on available roads at the down-wind distance, the optimal distance to source, and by considering other 
CH4 emitters nearby. The driving distances were selected to minimize the error related to the tracer placement 
while maintaining a sufficient plume signal.

The 700-790 dairy cows caused 81-85 % of the emission from enteric fermentation, while only representing 35-
65 % of the total number of animals in the farm. Of the different cattle types on the farm, dairy cows had the

general, the enteric EF is determined from the feed intake and the Ym (cf. Equation 2.1). For the dairy cows at C1, 
the feed intake was high in order to support an energy demand for lactation. Hence, the dairy cow’s milk

body function for dairy cows, the quantity is likely very important for estimating feed intake with R19 and B06. 
The manure management system at the farm was mainly slurry-based, consisting a collection of slurry manure

53 % assumed to be excreted on the fields, while the animals were grazing (cf. Table 3.4).

modelling. An organic in Denmark farm likely has a reduced DE compared to conventional farms in Denmark, 
because of the minimum forage requirement of 60 % of the energy in the diet. Forage-based diets might 
challenge the uptake of feed,becauseDEislowerformore‘rough’dietssuchasforage(incontrast,concentrate-

other things being equal, because a larger part of the feed is lost as fermentation gases i.e. lost as CH4 emission. 
Therefore, if the feed at the farm in generally had a lower DE than conventional farms, which are more common, 
the models could have caused an underestimation of the CH4 emission. This could explain that the dairy cows at 
the farm consume more feed than the average Jersey dairy cow in Denmark according to the normative 
standards; 7165 kg dry matter/head/year over the normative standards for feed intake of 6645 kg dry 
matter/head/year for dairy cows of small breeds (cf. Table 2.13), because more feed is required to compensate 
for the greater energy loss via CH4 emission. On the other hand, a reduced DE requires a prolonged rumination 
time, which is known to reduce the feed intake, because less time during the day is vacant for eating, why the 
farm (if the DE was low) could in fact have a lower feed intake than the average Jersey dairy cow in Denmark.  
Nonetheless, the underestimations by -11 % to -19 % of the measured emission rate were within the expected 
maximum ±20 % uncertainty of the measurement method known from controlled release tests (Mønster et al., 
2014; Fredenslund et al., 2019). This could indicate that Jersey dairy farms with liquid/slurry management are 
well understood by the models in terms of predicting the CH4 emission rates. The best prediction was made using 
R19. This was apparently due to the refined MCF in R19 for liquid manure storage, which was calculated from 
the temperature/retention-model specifically for the farm. 
The measured CH4 rates showed relatively large intra-annual variation. Lowest emissions were seen in June 2019 
(13.0 ± 1.6 kg CH4/hour) and highest were seen in November 2019 (29.8 ± 4.0 kg CH4/hour) but a clear seasonal 
pattern in the measured emission rate was not observed. The low measured emission rate in June 2019 and 2020 
could be due to the low number of dairy cows on the farm and the absence of the heifers, which were grazing in 
fields outside the farm (S. Madsen, personal communication). The measured emission rate in increased from 
winter/spring 2019 to fall 2019, but the rate did not decrease correspondingly the following winter and spring in 
2020. This could indicate that 2020 was associated with higher CH4 emission rates than 2019, since January, 
March and June 2020 were well above February, April and June 2019. However, it is suspected here that the 
higher emission rates in spring 2020, compared to spring 2019, are expressions the expected and inevitable inter-
annual variation in CH4 emission rates at a farm. 
Using R19 gave a higher contribution from manure management to the overall theoretical emission rate, 
compared to when using B06 and DK. In particular, September and November had very high manure 
management emission rates, constituting significant portions of the overall theoretical emission rates. This was

As seen from Figure 4.1, there was only a slight intra-annual variation in enteric fermentation emission rate 
according to the models. The small variation was caused by intra-annual variations in activity data i.e. the number 
of animals on the farm. The feed intake did not vary, as it was modelled as an annual average, as suggested in 
R19 and B06, which calculate one enteric EF for the year. DK however calculates a separate enteric EF for the 
winter period (EFhousing) and summer period (EFgrazing) (Equation 2.13 and Equation 2.14, respectively). The two 
EFs were then summed to get an annual EF. Generally, this is not a problem, as theoretical emissions usually are 
meant to be express the annual emissions to report in annual GHG inventories. However, it might be misleading 
in this project, because the hourly emission rate was converted from the summed annual EF per year, why 
possible seasonal dynamics did not show in the plot (Figure 4.1). Thus, it might appear that the enteric 
fermentation emission rate was constant, while it could in fact be changing over the year when using DK. 
For dairy cows, it can easily be justified to use the annual average enteric EF to calculate the hour emission rates 
for the respective months, because the enteric EF for dairy cows was not expected to vary intra-annually. Firstly, 
because of the very small difference between winter and summer feed for the dairy cows at the farm. The 
normative standards suggest the same energy content in the winter and summer feed i.e. 18.9 MJ/kg dry matter 
(cf. Table 2.14). Secondly, Ym for grazing and non-grazing dairy cows using DK is identical; 6.0 % (cf. Table 2.11). 
These two assumptions corresponded well with the assumption for the farm, since the dairy cows were fed 
forage both in winter and summer i.e. ensilaged pasture during winter and fresh pasture during summer with 
concentrate supplements all year. For heifers however, the normative standards suggest a larger difference 
between the energy in winter feed (the housing period, 25.8 MJ/feed unit) and the summer period (the grazing 
period 18.8 MJ/feed unit) (cf. Table 2.14). Thus, with an average feed intake of 1571 feed units/year, the heifer 
enteric EF theoretically differs by 27 % from winter to summer i.e. from 34 kg CH4/head/year in the grazing period 
to 47 kg CH4/head/year in the housing period. However, heifers caused on average 13 % of the enteric

the deep bedding and 9-10 % from the slurry system. 
The manure management at the farm consisted of slurry management and the use of deep bedding. The raw 
slurry manure collected through the slatted floor was stored at the farm for two-three days, after which it was 
collected by truck and sent to Thorsø Biogas plant. The deep bedding was retained in the house for up to two 
months, followed by digestion at the biogas plant. Because Thorsø biogas plant is a centralized digester, digestate 
received at the farm, and stored in the two tanks, was a mixed material. For the deep bedding, biogas treatment 
was not applied in the models. This was because the bedding is retained in the house for two months and then 
collected by truck and transported to the biogas plant, where it is used in biogas production. However, due to 
the expected high emission from deep bedding from the housing, the subsequent biogas treatment of the 
bedding was expected to impact the total emissions to a very small extent and were therefore not included in 
the modelling.

a low number of dairy cows coincided with the lowest number of heifers. Thus, the emission rate variation was 
caused by changes in activity data, which influenced the emission rate although to a small extent. This was in 
contrast to the previous farm, C1, for which the R19 MCF for liquid/slurry storage caused large intra-annual 
variations in the total emission rates e.g. a high emission rate in September 2019 (cf. Figure 4.1). For C2, the total 
theoretical emission rate did not vary as much, because raw manure was not stored at the farm but sent for 
biogas treatment. Hence, the refined MCF for liquid/slurry storage was not applicable, since the spreadsheet 
model only applied to storage of raw manure i.e. not digested manure. It is speculated here that this was likely

Cattle farm 2: Comparison with measurements 
The averaged measured emission rate was 7.9 ± 0.3 kg CH4/hour during the campaign from March 2019 to May 
2020 (cf. Figure 4.3). Using the three models underestimated the measured emission rates. The best estimation 
was made using DK, causing an underestimation by -25 %, possibly because DK included the bedding material in 
the calculation of the VS production. R19 and B06 did not include the bedding material in the calculation. Likely 
hereof, the underestimations using R19 and B06 were numerically larger; -34 % and -35 %, respectively. Overall, 
the measured emission rates were not predicted well by the models, which, even when taking a potential 
maximum uncertainty of ±20 % on the measuring method into consideration, did not encompass the averaged 
measured emission. Besides the uncertainty of the method, the model also involved uncertainty. The uncertainty 
of livestock EFs in DK is reported to be ± 20 % (Nielsen et al., 2019). When taking the uncertainties of the 
measurement method and the model into consideration, using DK lead to an acceptable theoretical emission 
estimation, determined by the uncertainty of ± 20 % for the model and ± 20 % for the measurements1. Using R19 
and B06 still led to an underestimation of measurements. 
A possible reason, for the general underestimation of emission rates using the models, is a potential 
underestimation of the feed intake i.e. the cattle in reality consumed more energy than was estimated using the 
models. This would lead to an underestimation of the enteric EF, followed by an underestimation of the enteric 
emission rate and the overall emission rate. The exact feed intake for the dairy cows at the farm could not be 
provided by the farmer. Instead, normative standards were used (which covers feeding plans of only 15-18 % of 
the dairy cattle population in Denmark). Although SDM, which was the cattle type at the farm, represent the 
most abundant cattle species in Denmark (i.e. 70 % of all dairy cattle in Denmark and 55 % of all dairy and beef 
cattle in Denmark), the feed intake at the farm might be larger than the average feed intake proposed by the 
normative standards. Using R19 and B06 gave an 8 % lower dairy cow feed intake using for DK. The dairy cow 
feed intake estimated by R19 and B06 was based the milk production reported by the farmer. However, the feed 
intake was likely underestimated using these models too.  
In addition, some of the underestimation might be caused by an underestimation of the MCFs; either the deep 
bedding MCF or the biogas treatment MCF, thereby underestimating the manure EF and finally the manure 
emission rate and overall emission rate. The retention time of bedding in the housing at the farm was 
approximately two months. However, only two options for deep bedding MCFs were provided in R19 and B06; 
below or above one month. Hence, a retention time longer than one month was used for the farm. A retention 
time well above one month (e.g. two months) might be associated with higher emissions than expected.

July vs. September, respectively. The longer retention time of the manure in the tanks at P1 allowed for high 
emissions in August. In general, a very high MCF reflects a combination of a high manure load in the manure 
tanks (VS available) and a high manure temperature (Figure 4.6). One of the two is insufficient to produce a high 
MCF. Besides the timing of the removal of manure from manure tanks, the average emptying efficiency also plays 
an important role. The lower the emptying efficiency, the larger becomes the available manure load left in the 
tank available for CH4 emission. The average emptying efficiency was 65 % at C1 and 55 % at P1. The lower 
removal efficiency and the longer retention time of the manure in late summer caused the larger average MCF 
at P1 (MCF of 30 % vs. MCF of 25 % at C1) as well as larger intra-annual variation in the MCF. In addition, the 
intra-annual variation in the MCF was reflected better in the overall CH4 emission rate at P1, because of the large 
contribution of manure management emission rate on the total emission rate (71-83 % of the total emissions at 
P1 % vs. only 10-31 % at C2).

encouraged). The R19 MCF for biogas treatment of 3.55 % was valid for biogas treatment of unspecified manure

followed by open storage of the digestate (no gas collection) in cold climates (cf. Table 2.10). The country-specific

specifically calculated for pig slurry, which has a higher degradable VS content than cattle manure, and thus a 
higher potential for CH4 production (Nielsen et al., 2019). The country-specific MCF calculation includes CH4 
emission from raw pig manure in the housing and pre-tank, based on the average retention time of manure in 
pig housing (17.25 days) (cf. Section 2.5). Further, DK takes into account that digestion often occurs as centralized 
biogas plants, why digestate often is a mixture of e.g. cattle manure, pig manure and crop residues. The digestate 
characteristics (dry matter and volatile solids content) were provided by digestate samples from eight biogas 
plants in Denmark (Nielsen et al., 2019). However, where the country-specific MCF is based on an expected 
average retention time of pig slurry in Danish pig houses (17.25 days), the retention time of the manure at the 
farm was significantly higher (37-55 days). Because long retention times are associated with higher CH4 emission, 
the country-specific MCF for biogas treatment might be too low for the farm. Parallel, the R19 MCF does not take 
into account the emission during the retention of raw manure in the housing prior to biogas treatment, which 
can be significant.  
Only very small inter-monthly variations in theoretical emission rates were seen. These variations were caused 
solely by changes in activity data with increasing CH4 emission rate for increasing number of livestock on the 
farm. The farm housed 2400 fattening pigs in November and January 2019, 2800 fattening pigs in March 2020 
and 3400 fattening pigs in June 2020 (cf. Table 3.18). Thus, the theoretical total emission rate in June marginally 
surpassed the emission rate of March 2020, which surpassed the emission rate in November and January for all 
three models (cf. Figure 4.9). However, the variation was small compared to variation caused by the R19 monthly 
MCF for liquid manure storage (derived in the temperature/retention-model) employed at C1, P1 and P2.

The measured campaign average CH4 emission rate was 3.6 ± 0.2 kg CH4/hour (cf. Figure 4.9). It was evident that 
the measured CH4 surpassed the theoretical CH4 emission rates. R19 underestimated the measurements the 
most with an average underestimation by -71 %, because of the low MCF for biogas treatment (3.55 %), which

DK. The very high emission rate measured in November might not be fully representative of the general emission 
from the farm, because the raw manure was being mixed at the approximate time of the measurements, which 
might have enhanced emissions for some time.

4.2. Discussion 
On average for all farms, the theoretical emission rates underestimated the averaged measured emission rates 
by -27 %, -30 % and -45 % when using R19, B06 and DK, respectively (Figure 4.10). Thus overall, using R19 gave 
the best estimation of measured emissions, why this study reinforced the replacement of B06 with R19. Using 
DK gave the poorest estimations of the measured emission rates. Assuming that the underestimation of -45 % 
when using DK, seen for the six farms, is representative for livestock production in Denmark, the CH4 emission 
budget in Denmark should be adjusted. Due to the large impact of livestock on the total anthropogenic CH4 
budget, increasing livestock emission by +45 % would result in a +35 % increase in the total anthropogenic CH4 
emissions from 7110 kt CO2-eqv to 9605 kt CO2-eqv and a +5 % increase in the total GHG emissions from all 
sectors in Denmark. Thereby, livestock would be the cause of 84 % of the total anthropogenic livestock emission 
(as opposed to 78 %). However, CH4 emissions from more farms need to be measured in order to determine the 
general emission for Denmark’s
livestock
production.  
A comparison with studies in North America (due to a lack of European studies, cf. Table 2.18) gives an ambiguous 
result. Regional and national studies indicated that US EPA CH4 emission estimate, which are based on the IPCC-
approach, were underestimating measured emission rates (cf. Table 2.18). Comparing CH4 emissions measured 
using satellite observations of atmospheric CH4 and a chemical transport model with theoretical emissions Wecht 
et al. (2014) found a -40 % underestimation of total livestock emission in the US EPA methodology, which is 
similar to the performance by DK. A factor two (corresponding to -200 %) underestimation of livestock CH4 
emission in the US found from comprehensive atmospheric CH4 data and inverse modelling by Miller et al. (2013) 
was much higher than observed in the current study. Using atmospheric covariance dynamics and an aircraft-
based measurement platform, Desjardins et al. (2018) measured CH4 emissions from an agricultural region 
(covering 700+ farms) in Canada and found a good agreement between measured and IPCC-based theoretical 
emissions. However, a comparison of these studies with Danish conditions can be troublesome due to 
differences in US EPA and Danish methodology, climatic differences and farm managerial differences such as 
livestock type, livestock breed, typical feed, farm size and manure management.

A general good agreement between measurements and theoretical emissions was seen for the cattle farms in 
this project, for which, on average, underestimations of -10 % using R19 and B06 and -25 % using DK were seen 
(cf. Figure 4.10). Modelled emission rates at cattle farms were dominated by enteric fermentation emission, 
which for dairy farms (C1 and C2) was caused mainly by milk production. Milk production required high feed 
intakes in order to support the high energy requirement for lactation. The importance of providing farm-specific 
feed intake for dairy cows as input to the model for correct emission estimation had been indicated by previous 
studies e.g. Arndt et al. (2018), who investigated CH4 emission from two Jersey dairy farms in California using 
three independent measurement techniques incl. vehicle measurements with a tracer flux ratio method. Arndt 
et al. (2018) found that enteric fermentation from Jersey dairy cows in California correlated well with monthly 
US EPA based estimates, predicted from the farm specific feed intake (Arndt et al., 2018). One the one hand, this 
was also seen for C1, where farm specific feed intake was used as input in DK, resulting in a good agreement 
between DK and the measured emissions. The measured emission at C1 was also well tracked by R19 and B06, 
solely by providing the models with farm specific milk production and average dairy cow weights (i.e. without 
the use of farm specific feed intake). On the other hand, it was not possible, when using R19 and B06, to track 
emission at C2 well based on farm specific milk production and dairy cow weights (both models underestimated

the measured emission). Using DK, which was provided with the normative standards for feed intake for large 
dairy breeds in Denmark, resulted in an acceptable agreement between the model and measurement, when 
taking into account model and measurement uncertainties. In the evaluation of theoretical emission estimates 
at C2, it was seen that the extensive use of deep bedding at the farm, and the inclusion of the bedding material 
as part of the manure production in DK, led to a better tracking of measured emissions than was seen for R19 
and B06, as it increased the manure emission significantly compared to R19 and B06. This might act as a 
compensation for the slight underestimation of the enteric fermentation emission in DK – a compensation that 
was lacking in R19 and B06. Generally, the reasons for underestimation of C2 and a good tracking of C1 by the 
models were (although speculated) not properly identified. It is hereof speculated that Jersey dairy cattle have 
been a more frequent study object in literature (which form the basis of models) or that C2 was an outlier farm 
with particularly high feed intake. 
For Limousin beef cattle at C3, large feed intakes were required to support primarily maintenance of mature 
animals and fast growth of growing cattle thus not for milk production. R19 and B06 tracked well the emission 
based on farm specific livestock weight, growth and literature milk production (cf. Figure 4.10). As the only farm 
(in all six case farms), an overestimation of measured emissions was found, although the overestimation was still 
within the potential maximum uncertainty of the measurements. DK underestimated the emission from C3, likely 
due to an underestimation of enteric fermentation emission, caused by a very low feed intake in DK, provided 
by the normative standards, which are solely based on dairy cattle data (Nielsen et al., 2019). A lack of 
representation of beef cattle in the normative standards causes not only a problem when estimating the enteric 
fermentation emission, but also for manure emission, because the normative standards are also the base of the 
estimation of the housing-based manure excretion in DK. This study indicates a necessity of providing farm-
specific feed intake in DK, if beef cattle emission ought to be tracked, unless the normative standards are 
expanded to include beef cattle. 
On average, pig farm emissions were less well tracked than cattle farms using the models, and generally highly 
underestimated (cf. Figure 4.10). Pig farms were underestimated by -44 %, -51 % and -64 % when using R19, B06 
and DK, respectively. Theoretical emissions from the pig farms were significant due to large farms (several 
thousands of animals) and because the manure was exclusively handled in slurry-based systems, where 
anaerobic conditions, which are required for CH4 production, are known to prevail. The only exception was P3 
for which biogas treatment was utilized i.e. the system was still slurry-based but only digestate was stored in the 
manure tanks. Further, raw slurry was still present in the housing and pre-tank. Because most of the emission at 
pig farms came from manure management as suggested by the models, and because the manure was only 
handled in slurry-based systems, this study speculates that that underestimations of slurry/liquid manure 
emission were the primary cause of the overall underestimation. 
CH4 emission from manure is essentially described by 0.67 · B0 · VS · MCF, which is the CH4 density, the maximum 
CH4 producing capacity, the manure production and the fraction of the maximum CH4 producing capacity utilized 
(cf. Equation 2.10). From these parameters, mostly the MCF has been pointed out in literature as a source of

underestimation, especially the MCF of liquid manure storage (Baldé et al. 2016; Flesch et al., 2013; Park et al., 
2006). This culminated in 2018, where VanderZaag (2018) documented the systematic underestimation of the 
MCF of liquid manure management in the IPCC methodology and proposed the temperature/retention model 
based on Mangino et al. (2001), which was implemented in R19 (Gavriolva et al., 2019; Mangino et al., 2001; 
VanderZaag, 2018). At P1 and P2, where farm specific R19 liquid/slurry MCFs were employed, R19 tracked the 
total emission far better than B06 and DK (cf. Figure 4.10). However, the limited emission measurements at P2 
(having only measurements from November 2019 to May 2020) limits the possibility to make a full evaluation, 
because of an expected large intra-annual variability of manure emission.  
The country-specific MCF for especially raw pig manure was seemingly not reflecting well at the two pig farms 
(P1 and P2), which utilized management and storage of raw manure, perhaps because the MCF was calculated 
for utilization on the Danish livestock population (i.e. not on specific farms). Instead, the on average about three 
times higher R19 MCF for liquid/slurry storage was a better match for the two pig farms, although in the case of 
P2 still too low, likely because of an incomplete measurement dataset. This confirms the justification for 
development of the refined calculation model in R19 for MCF of liquid/slurry storage. Furthermore, it is here 
suspected that the R19 MCF for liquid/slurry storage might be a better alternative than the country-specific pig 
slurry MCF for the management of raw pig slurry in Denmark. This is suspected firstly because of the significantly 
better match by R19 for P1 and P2, and secondly because the R19 liquid/slurry MCF is developed based on 
advancements in the global research field from 2006 to 2019, creating a particularly strong foundation for the 
R19 MCF temperature/retention model. However, a specific error source in the Danish country-specific MCF 
calculation cannot be identified here, since the calculations were performed utilizing a country-specific MCF 
model, which essentially estimates the CH4 emission from manure temperature, the temperature response of 
microbial activity described by the Arrhenius equation and manure retention i.e. overall similarly to R19. The 
farm conditions and the assumptions behind the model, which were for the average conditions in Denmark, were 
somewhat different for the manure retention time. Where P1 and P2 had an average manure retention time of 
30 days in the housing, the retention of manure in pig housing was assumed to be on average 17.25 days in the 
country-specific MCF model. This could explain why the country-specific MCF for raw pig slurry were too low for 
P1 and P2. The same was valid for P3, where the retention time of raw slurry in the housing was 37-55 days i.e. 
twice the retention time assumed for the country-specific MCF model. At P3, where biogas digestion of slurry 
was utilized, the MCFs of all the models were likely too low, causing emission underestimations of -71 %, -45 % 
and -59 % for R19, B06 and DK, respectively. Here, DK underestimated the least due to the inclusion of emission 
of raw slurry from housing. Similar to P2, more measurements are needed to deduct a full evaluation. Generally, 
measurements from pig farms with shorter retention times and from manure storage tanks could be carried out 
to understand better the emission from liquid/slurry manure.  
In addition to the lower country-specific liquid/slurry MCF, the manure production was significantly lower for 
pigs in DK than in R19 and B06, because normative standards for VS according to housing was used rather than 
calculated from the feed intake, as R19 and B06 suggest. Evidently, lower housing-based VS led to the lower 
theoretical CH4 emission rates, which poorly reflected the measured CH4 emission rates.

This project has shown a potentially comprehensive problem with tracking CH4 emission from pig farms in the 
Danish inventory, which could be a severe threat to climate change mitigation. Further, difficulties with tracking 
enteric fermentation emission from beef cattle farms were indicated. As a solution to the problem, this study 
suggests important future work. In continuation of the work performed in this study, more measurements should 
be conducted to improve the data set at C2, C3, P2 and P3 to cover full years monitoring of the farms. As general 
recommendations to the inventory compilers, it is recommended to include beef cattle in the normative 
standards and to calculate livestock manure production based on normative standards for feed intake i.e. not 
based on housing. This is also recommended for cattle, because the inclusion of straw in the manure production 
seemed to impact the total emission only to a small extent (other factors such as feed intake were more 
important), while decreasing the transparency of the national model. A more important recommendation is a 
comprehensive verification of slurry manure emissions at tank level, which should be performed in order to tune 
the MCF models (DK and R19) based on specific input data e.g. manure temperature, surface area, estimation of 
dry matter and volatile solids content from manure samples. As suggested by Arndt et al. (2018), CH4 emissions 
from liquid manure should be measured on a monthly basis or more frequently throughout the year to validate 
the IPCC-basedinventories’prediction for CH4 emission from liquid manure management. The verification should 
also include the storage of digested slurry, due to the increasing popularity of biogas digestion in Denmark and 
because biogas treatment potentially leads to a 90 % reduction of CH4 emission from outside storage of digested 
slurry as opposed to untreated slurry (Sommer et al., 2004).

Chapter 5. Conclusion 
Theoretical methane (CH4) emission rates were estimated for six livestock farms in Denmark and compared to 
measured emission rates. The case farms consisted of three cattle farms; Jersey dairy (C1), Sortbroget Dansk 
Malkerace dairy (C2) and Limousine beef cattle (C3), and three pig farms; one farm with sows and weaners (P1) 
and two farms with fattening pigs (P2 and P3). Livestock manure was primarily managed in slurry-based systems 
for all farms, except at C3, which did not have manure tanks, while biogas treatment was utilized at C2 and P3. 
The theoretical CH4 emission rates were calculated utilizing national and international inventory models based 
on guidelines from the IPCC, using statistical activity data and source specific emission factors. These were in 
turn estimated according to animal productivity, diet quality and manure management circumstances. The 
methodologies constituted: 

The R19 model; 2019 IPCC guidelines refinement, which uses relevant scientific knowledge advances 
from 2006 to 2018 to improve the methodology  

The B06 model; 2006 IPCC guidelines 

The DK model; National methodology, which is based on B06 with country-specific adaptations  
Theoretical CH4 emissions were on average, using R19, B06 and DK, estimated to 16.9-18.5 CH4/hour (C1), 5.1-
5.9 CH4/hour (C2), 0.8-1.5 CH4/hour (C3), 2.2-4.8 CH4/hour (P1), 3.5-6.6 CH4/hour (P2) and 1.1-2.1 CH4/hour (P3). 
In general, cattle farm CH4 emissions were dominated by enteric fermentation emission (constituting 79 % of the 
total emission at cattle farms) due to large feed intakes by ruminants, especially by dairy cows to support milk 
production. Pig farm CH4 emissions were dominated by manure management emission (constituting 73 % of the 
total emission at pig farms) due to large farms with several thousands of animals, whose manure was handled in 
slurry based systems, where anaerobic conditions are known to prevail.  
CH4 emissions were quantified utilizing a tracer gas dispersion measuring method. Measurement campaigns 
were performed at each farm with a frequency of two months, for a period of 5-16 months. The measured 
emissions were 20.8 ± 0.9 kg CH4/hour (C1), 7.9 ± 0.3 kg CH4/hour (C2), 1.3 ± 0.1 kg CH4/hour (C3), 5.8 ± 0.2 kg 
CH4/hour (P1), 11.9 ± 0.5 kg CH4/hour (P2) and 3.8 ± 0.2 kg CH4/hour (P3). A full year of emission monitoring was 
only performed for C1 and P1, why it was recommended to complete full year emission monitoring at the four 
remaining farms (C2, C3, P2, P3).  
The theoretical and measured emissions were compared, and an error percent (%) of theoretical emissions 
calculated. Measured livestock emission was underestimated by -27 %, -30 % and -45 % on average using R19, 
B06 and DK, respectively. The findings would increase the total annual anthropogenic CH4 emissions by +35 % in 
Denmark and the total GHG emissions from all sectors in Denmark by +5 %.  Cattle farm emissions were relatively 
well estimated by the models, which underestimated the measured emissions by -10 % (R19 and B06), and -25 
% (DK). For pig farms the models, on average, underestimated emissions greatly; by -44 % (R19), -51 % (B06) and 
==PAGE:101==

-64 % (DK). Overall for cattle and pig farms, DK had the greatest underestimation of emissions. This study pointed 
to the following main reasons:  
1)
beef cattle were not represented in the normative standards leading to underestimated feed intakes 
2)
housing based manure production possibly led to an underestimation of manure load at the pig farms  
3)
country-specific CH4 conversion factors (MCF) for raw and digested pig manure did not reflect the 
conditions at the pig farms 
Overall for the six farms, using R19 compared best with measurements, primarily due to an implementation of a 
temperature/retention model for liquid/slurry manure MCF, why this study reinforced the replacement of B06 
with R19. Moreover, the study implied the need for a comprehensive verification of slurry manure emissions at 
manure tank level, at minimum a monthly discretization, in order to tune the MCF models (DK and R19).
