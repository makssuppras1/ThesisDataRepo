Abstract
The ﬁeld of urban water management is expanding hence new professions such as ecologists, land-
scape architects etc. enters the planning phase of drainage systems. Detention ponds are often con-
sidered to be aesthetic stormwater control measures. Aside from being aesthetic, detention ponds
are also known to improve the quality of storm water which makes them a popular measure in urban
stormwater management.
The aim of this study is to investigate how the performance of detention ponds can be assessed in an
early planning phase. The ﬁndings are intended to ease the communication and collaboration between
the different professions involved in urban water management.
To do so, a detention pond model is constructed using U.S. EPAs SWMM and its python interface
PySWMM. A realistic range of parameters, which are found to be most inﬂuential, are deﬁned. This
includes 52 storage volumes, 17 discharge ﬂows and 2 shape properties. The model parameters are
updated one-by-one in an automated simulation process until all possible combinations are tested
(total of 1768 simulations). Based on the results it is concluded that the shape (i.e. evaporation) has
negligible impacts on the overﬂow frequency whereas the storage volume and the discharge ﬂow are
both crucial. Moreover, the simulation results are stored in an SQLite database. The database can
be used to assess the expected performance of a detention pond by looking-up the simulation results
from its volume and discharge ﬂow.
As an alternative to the long term simulations it is found that it is possible to obtain an expected return
period of a detention pond from intensity duration curves. This method is validated by comparing
the expected return period of the ones simulated. This approach is recommended as it requires less
simulation time. Also, the simple method will ensure a better compliance to how detention ponds are
currently designed in a screening phase in Denmark.
Furthermore, it is investigated how detention ponds can be put in series without increasing the over-
ﬂow frequency compared to one pond only. This is investigated by setting up various coupling sce-
narios where a ﬁxed volume is distributed into four ponds. The volume distribution and how the
detention ponds are combined differs between the scenarios. Based on this a simple set of rules is
made on how a storage volume can be distributed into several detention ponds without resulting in
more overﬂows.

1
Introduction
As a consequence of climate change it is expected that precipitation will become more extreme and that
the annual mean precipitation will increase in the high latitudes (northern regions), thus enhancing the
risk of ﬂooding (Dankers and Hiederer, 2016; Christensen and Christensen, 2007; Drews et al., 2011;
IPCC, 2015; Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2006). In urban areas this risk is further exacerbated as there is a con-
tinuous urbanisation trend, where land use change also generates more runoff due to new impervious
areas (Jensen et al., 2020). Hence, cities must adapt to the future conditions (Mrowiec, 2016; Jensen
et al., 2020). This is also the case for the cities of Denmark (Drews et al., 2011; Arnbjerg-Nielsen, 2006),
which is a country situated in Northern Europe .
Historically, many older cities in Denmark (and Europe in general), have handled the stormwater in
combined sewer systems which leads both wastewater and stormwater to the local wastewater treatment
plant (WWTP) (Fletcher et al., 2015; Lerer et al., 2015). These facilities are expensive to expand upon.
Therefore, separate sewer systems are constructed to handle the future stormwater quantity apart from
the wastewater (Barbosa et al., 2012; Jensen et al., 2020; Mrowiec, 2016; Fletcher et al., 2015).
In the recent decades the focus has been shifting away from managing the storm water quantity only.
Nowadays there is a trend of considering urban drainage system in a holistic context; new parameters
such as recreational value, ecosystem health and water quality, aesthetic value, etc. are now considered
in urban stormwater management (Fletcher et al., 2015; Roy et al., 2008; Jensen et al., 2020). Regarding
water quality, it is common knowledge that even when handling precipitation apart from wastewater it
may still harm the recipients. Runoff may not contain much nutrient but it does contain other contami-
nants e.g. heavy metals, suspended solids and polycyclic aromatic hydrocarbons (PAH’s) (Jensen et al.,
2020; Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Behera and Teegavarapu, 2014; Roy et al., 2008; Wium-Andersen et al.,
2011a,b).
Stormwater control measures (SCMs), low impact development, sponge cities, nature based solutions
(NBS), etc. (Fletcher et al., 2015), are solutions implemented in an attempt to reduce the peak ﬂow
to the recipient and / or improve the stormwater quality. These objectives can be reached through de-
tention (allowing particles to settle, and controlling peak ﬂow), retention (e.g. allowing for inﬁltration
and evaporation) or treatment (with e.g. ﬁlters) (Jensen et al., 2020). SCMs varies in scale and loca-
tion. Some solutions do not occupy much space and can be located almost anywhere (raingardens, green
roofs, swales...) other solutions have a greater area and are often located ”end-of-pipe” such as stormwa-
ter basins or the more aesthetic detention ponds (Jensen et al., 2020; Roy et al., 2008; Walsh et al., 2005).
Moreover, SCMs can be combined to enhance the treatment or reduction of peak ﬂow (Fletcher et al.,
2015; Bastien et al., 2010; Haaning Nielsen and Vollertsen, 2017; DANVA, 2018; Woods-Ballard, 2007).
Regardless of the SCM the overall trend is that SCMs are gaining more social awareness and that the aim
of the urban stormwater management discipline is expanding. As a result storm water management is no
longer just for engineers; professions such as ecologists, planners, (landscape) architects are also getting
more involved (Fletcher et al., 2015). Therefore communication and collaboration between these ﬁelds
becomes essential when planning SCMs. In Jensen et al. (2018) it is suggested that this communication
should be supported by new design- and planning tools which must be intuitive; easy understandable;
1
==PAGE:11==
and quick to use for screening purposes.
One such tool, SCALGO + NBS, is under development in a collaboration between the Technical Univer-
sity of Denmark (DTU) and the company Scalgo ApS. SCALGO + NBS is an expansion of the software
programme SCALGO Live, which uses topography data to e.g. assess where a study area is likely to
ﬂood. The goal of SCALGO + NBS is to allow the user to locate, combine and manipulate SCMs to
explore how this impacts the risk of ﬂooding.
A commonly used SCM in Denmark is detention ponds (Jensen et al., 2020). Detention ponds are often
considered to be aesthetic as it resembles lakes and ponds (DANVA, 2018; Woods-Ballard, 2007). Apart
from being an aesthetic feature detention ponds also improve the water quality as particles are allowed to
settle (DANVA, 2018; Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Behera and Teegavarapu, 2014; Walsh et al., 2005; Roy
et al., 2008). Moreover, further quality improvements can by reached by sectioning a detention pond or
coupling several smaller detention ponds (Spildevandskomiteen, 2006a; DANVA, 2018; Woods-Ballard,
2007).
1.1
Problem statement
The objective of this thesis is to model the hydraulic function of detention ponds under different situa-
tions, designs and combinations. Based on this, suggestions must be made on how to assess the perfor-
mance of detention ponds on a ﬂood screening level which can be used in the further development of
SCALGO + NBS.
To come to such recommendations the work of this study is divided into three sub-studies each exploring
different angles of detention ponds. Subsequent to these investigations a ﬁnal set of recommendations
can be made.
1. The ﬁrst objective is to setup a reliable detention pond model for long-term simulations. To do so
the following work must be conducted:
(a) Before setting up a model it is necessary to obtain knowledge on how detention ponds func-
tion, how they are designed, and which factors that impacts their water balance.
(b) A software must be selected for the modelling. For this project it is decided to use U.S. EPAs
SWMM combined with its Python interface, PySWMM.
(c) The model is constructed and validated.
2. The next objective is to create a look-up table which can be used to assess the function of a ran-
domly drawn detention pond in SCALGO Live + NBS. This is done by:
(a) Determining parameters important to the performance of a detention pond.
(b) Selecting a realistic range of the found parameters. Such a range can either be determined
through literature or preliminary tests.
(c) Performing long-term simulations of all possible parameter combinations. The simulation
results are stored into a SQLite database.
2
==PAGE:12==
3. Once the performance of one detention pond is assessed it is time to investigate how detention
ponds behave when being combined. This is investigated by:
(a) Constructing a model where more detention ponds are situated in one catchment.
(b) Deﬁning different coupling scenarios. The scenarios are used to explore how a ﬁxed volume
can be distributed among the detention ponds. Moreover, the scenarios also explore the effect
of different outlet ﬂows.
4. Based on the above ﬁndings a ﬁnal set of recommendations are made on how SCALGO + NBS can
support a user in a screening phase.

2
Theory
As the name indicates, a detention pond is a SCM which is used to detain water. Detention ponds
originate from the detention basins which were originally build to make it possible to use smaller pipes
in sewer systems (Rendsvig and Larsen, 1950). Moreover, detention volumes were already recognised as
a measure which could be used to protect recipients from both combined and separate sewer overﬂows in
the 1950’s(Rendsvig and Larsen, 1950). In the 1970’s the protection of recipients became of high priority
whereof detention ponds became a widely used measure in urban water management (Milovanovi´c et al.,
2020).
Detention ponds consist of a permanent pool (hence its aesthetic reputation) and a storage volume. In
addition to being an aesthetic feature it is the permanent pool which accounts for the treatment processes
in a detention pond (DANVA, 2018; Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Behera and Teegavarapu, 2014; Walsh et al.,
2005; Roy et al., 2008; Mrowiec, 2016). The purpose of the storage volume is to reduce the peak ﬂow
to the recipient by detaining runoff and restricting the discharge to the recipient. On top of the storage
volume there is often an emergency spillway / overﬂow structure. Detention ponds are typically located
end-of-pipe, however it is also possible to distribute the volume into several smaller detention ponds
(Ravazzani et al., 2014; Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Del Giudice et al., 2014).
2.1
Detention ponds in SCALGO + NBS
SCALGO Live is, as stated, a tool which can be used to map the risk of ﬂooding using topography data.
So-far the programme is a timeless glass-plate model i.e. a user inserts a rain depth and then SCALGO
Live predicts where runoff is routed based on the contour of the landscape. Moreover, the user can make
landscape changes and explore how this impacts ﬂooding. SCALGO Live has already received a lot
of attention from the target segment. The next step is therefore to improve the tool by implementing a
SCMs interface which allows the user to explore how ﬂooding can be impacted from the implementation
of SCMs, thereof the SCALGO + NBS project.

Detention ponds in practice
The question on how detention ponds are designed is investigated by looking into the danish recommen-
dations. Additional literature and guidelines from other countries are also presented to support the danish
practice.

to that it is often recommended to implement upstream pretreatment or a sedimentation forebay which
catches the coarser particles. Figure 2 shows a conceptual sketch of a typical detention pond seen from

2.2.1
The permanent pool
It is mainly the permanent pool which contributes to the cleaning effect of detention ponds. The most
inﬂuential process is the sedimentation of total suspended solids (TSS) (Behera and Teegavarapu, 2014;
Vollertsen et al., 2012a,b) which consequently removes problematic substances such as particulate phos-
phor, PAHs and heavy metals (e.g. Zn, Pb and Cu) (Vollertsen et al., 2012a; Pettersson, 1999; Vollertsen
et al., 2012b, 2009; Wium-Andersen et al., 2011b,a). Other processes are e.g. adsorbtion of ﬁne particles
to surfaces and plant uptake of soluble pollutants whereof pollutants are accumulated to the sediment
once plants decay (Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 2010).
The sedimentation takes time and is most efﬁcient when the pond is disturbed as little as possible. Thus,
treatment is mainly occurring during dry periods (Woods-Ballard, 2007; Zahari et al., 2020). Further-
more, the treatment efﬁciency is found to be correlated to the inlet concentration Vollertsen et al. (2012b).
Hence, treatment is best for smaller and frequent rain events due to the high concentration of TSS in the
ﬁrst period after the rain event, also known as the ﬁrst ﬂush (Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 2010; DANVA,
2018; Barbosa et al., 2012). During greater events the TSS concentration will decrease due to dilution
which then reduces the cleansing effect (Vollertsen et al., 2012b).
It has empirically been determined that the volume of the permanent pool should be ≈250m3
ha reduced.
Using greater volumes only have minor impacts on the efﬁciencies (Hvitved-Jacobsen et al., 2010;
Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Pettersson, 1999).
2.2.2
The forebay
As it is expensive to clean the permanent pool it is often recommended to implement a forebay to the
system. The forebay is intended to catch the coarser particles such as sand, which thereby can extend
the time needed in between maintenance of the permanent pool (Woods-Ballard, 2007; DANVA, 2018;
Vermont Agency of Natural Resources, 2017). The forebay can be constructed as its own section lo-
cated at the inlet side of the detention pond, separated to the permanent volume by a dam, berm, weir
or something similar (Vermont Agency of Natural Resources, 2017; DANVA, 2018; Vollertsen et al.,
2012b). Another advantage is that the forebay reduces the ﬂow to the permanent pool which then makes
the permanent pool less disturbed (Vollertsen et al., 2012b).
The most common recommendation regarding the volume of the forebay is that it should be ≈10%
of the permanent pool (Woods-Ballard, 2007; Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Vermont Agency of Natural Re-
sources, 2017; DANVA, 2018). One outlayer is the recommendation from the danish road directorate
who suggests a forebay size corresponding to 20-30% of the total pond area (Jensen, U. M., 2013).
2.2.3
The storage volume
The purpose of the storage volume is as previously stated to detain water and control the outﬂow to the
recipient e.g. through throttle pipes i.e. reducing the reducing the peak ﬂow. The storage volume does not
contribute much to the treatment of the inlet water (Vollertsen et al., 2012b) as treatment mainly occurs
during small events and dry periods in the permanent pool.
The needed volume for storage can be determined in many ways. Some simply uses empirical rules-
6

of-thumb (e.g. ﬁxed volume per area), other uses rainfall-runoff relations, empirical/statistical design
storms, long term simulations; or a mix of any of the above (Vermont Agency of Natural Resources,
2017; Spildevandskomiteen, 2005; Debo and Reese, 2002; AKAN, 1989).
The standard method in Denmark for determining the size of the storage volume is based on the practices
described by the Water Pollution Committee of The Society of Danish Engineers (in danish Spildevand-
skomiteen, abbreviated to DWPC). The method of sizing detention volumes (traditionally as basins) was
already described in the sixth document of the committee published in Spildevandskomiteen (1953),
which refers to the methods presented three years earlier in an article from the danish paper Ingeniøren
(Rendsvig and Larsen, 1950). Only minor changes have later been conducted to this method.
The overall idea of the method used in Spildevandskomiteen (1953) is to design a storage volume only
based on the speciﬁc discharge from the storage unit (outﬂow per area reduced) and parameters related to
an intensety-duratuion-frequency curve (IDF curve). The methodology is presented below. In addition,
appendix A contains a more thorough go-through of the deduction of the most relevant equations from
Rendsvig and Larsen (1950).

2.2.4
Shape
It is now known how the volumes in the system are determined, but this volume must also be shaped
correctly to enhance the performance and safety of the detention pond.
To avoid hydraulic short-circuting and hydraulic dead zones it is often recommended to maximise the ﬂow
path in the pond (Woods-Ballard, 2007; Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Vermont Agency of Natural Resources,
2017; DANVA, 2018; Jensen, U. M., 2013; Schmidt et al., 2009). If these are not avoided the inlet water
will not fully mix with the remaining water in the pond. Hence, the actual retention time is reduced
by which the treatment is also reduced (Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Woods-Ballard, 2007; Wium-Andersen
et al., 2011b). Therefore, it is often recommended to have a width-to-length ratio of at least 1:3 (Vollertsen
et al., 2012b; Winter et al., 2011; Woods-Ballard, 2007; DANVA, 2018; Wium-Andersen et al., 2011b;
Debo and Reese, 2002). In addition, hydraulic short-circuiting is also avoided when implementing a
forebay, or alternatively sectioning the pond (Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Woods-Ballard, 2007; Vermont
Agency of Natural Resources, 2017; DANVA, 2018).
In Denmark, The depth of the permanent pool and the forebay is often recommended to be in the range
1-1.5 meters (Vollertsen et al., 2012b; DANVA, 2018; Vollertsen et al., 2012a; Madsen et al., 2007).
However this recommendation varies between different countries and regions. Vollertsen et al. (2012b)
have conducted a small literature review on this topic, showing the smallest recommended depth is 0.6 m
and the greatest is 3 meters. Most recommendations are in the same order of magnitude as in Denmark,
though. Shallow ponds are more vulnerable towards re-suspension caused by wind. In deep ponds oxygen
depletion can occur at the bottom during summer if the depth is too big (Woods-Ballard, 2007; Madsen
et al., 2007; Bentzen, 2008). Due to sedimentation the depth of the permanent pool will decrease over
time and it is recommended to clean the bottom when the depth is ≈0.8-0.6 meters (Vollertsen et al.,
2012b).
The slope of the pond has no crucial effect on the water quality, however, it is important to ensure that
a proper slope is chosen due to safety reasons and maintenance accessibility (Vollertsen et al., 2012b;
Woods-Ballard, 2007; Debo and Reese, 2002). For this reason it not recommended to have a slope any
steeper than 1:3. Such a steep slope can also cause problems regarding the accessibility, therefore it is
often suggested to rather use a slope of 1:5 (Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Woods-Ballard, 2007; DANVA,
2018; Debo and Reese, 2002)

2.3
Storm Water Mangement Model
The very ﬁrst version of the U.S. Environmental Protection Agencys Storm Water Management Model
(SWMM) originated already in 1971 (Rossman and Huber, 2016). This model has undergone several
reﬁnements since then, and today SWMM is a widely used tool for dynamic modelling of rainfall-runoff
and sewer systems.
One crucial input to SWMM is the precipitation data containing one or more events, which is assigned to

3
Methods
In the theory it is presented how detention ponds are designed to cope with a speciﬁc volume. However,
inside SCALGO + NBS the user should be able to freely draw in SCMs and landscape changes to explore
how stormwater can be managed in an acceptable way. Therefore, the methodology is designed to answer
the questions: 1) how will a randomly drawn detention pond perform and 2) how does series coupling
impact this behaviour. To get around these objectives the methodology is divided into the three parts as
introduced in the problem statement:
3.1 The ﬁrst part covers how a detention pond can be modelled through SWMM (v. 5.1.015) and how
this can be manipulated using PySWMM.
3.2 The next goal is to automate different manipulations of the created PySWMM model from 3.1.
This is done to answer how detention ponds function under various designs and conﬁgurations.
3.3 The last part of the methodology covers how detention ponds are expected to perform when putting
them in series.
3.1
Constructing a model for one detention pond using SWMM and PySWMM
The ﬁrst draft of the detention pond model is created inside SWMM. The system is assumed to consist
of a catchment which directs its runoff to a detention pond from which water in the storage volume can
either leave through an outlet or through an overﬂow weir (as illustrated in ﬁgure 2).
The system is kept as simple and generic as possible; for instance, there are no pipes. Pipes would require
assumptions on e.g. pipe diameter, length and roughness. Consequently, energy losses and ﬂooding could
also occur from pipes and nodes, which is outside the scope of this project.

3.1.1
Rain series
The rain series which will be used for this project is obtained from a DWPC measurement station. The
measurement station is located in Copenhagen (speciﬁcally ’Kløvermarken’) where the annual precipita-
tion is 636 mm (according to the DWPC spreadsheet). The precipitation data is measured by a so-called
RIMCO tipping bucket rain gauge with a resolution of 0.2 mm (Jørgensen et al., 1998; Overgaard et al.,
1998). After each minute the number of tips are translated into an intensity [mm/min].
The rain series begins in January 1979 and the last data point is collected in December 2019 (almost 41
years of data). Due to the length of the precipitation series it is decided to aggregate the data into 10
minutes resolution. Moreover, to make the rain series compatible with SWMM it is necessary to convert
the rain series from mm/10 minutes to mm/h. This is simply done by multiplying the rain series by 6.
The rain series is plotted over time in ﬁgure 7. Here it can be seen how the precipitation varies between
the years and how some intensities occur more frequently than others. In addition it must be mentioned
that data is missing for a period of four month (Jan-Apr) in 1983 which is also implied from the plot. The
annual precipitation obtained from the rain series is 600 mm/yr (sum of rain divided by 41 years).

3.1.2
Catchment
A catchment of 1 hectare is created in SWMM. The area is assumed to be 100% impervious; In other
words, the area of the catchment is 1 reduced hectare. This is chosen to simplify the calculations later on
as several parameters are normalised to one reduced hectare.
As the entire area is impervious and there is no inﬁltration from the catchment (f=0 in equation 7) the
only hydrological process which is considered in the catchment is evaporation. Evaporation is assumed
only to occur during dry periods and as long as there is water stored on the catchment (δd
δt > 0 from eq.
7).
The evaporation is dependent on the season; Therefore, it is decided to use monthly averages in SWMM
[mm/day] (deﬁned in the climatology tab). The potential evaporation is obtained from the The Danish
Metrological Institute, who calculates this based on the Makkink equation (Scharling, 2001; Plauborg

3.1.3
Detention pond
In SWMM, the detention pond is implemented as a ’storage unit’. The incoming runoff can either leave
the pond as discharge through the outlet, overﬂow or evaporation. Based on the above it is possible to
construct a mass balance of the detention pond:
δS
δt = Q−ep −a−w
(9)
Equation 9 presents how the storage changes over time with respect to the incoming ﬂow (Q, the product
of runoff rate q and the catchment area) the pond surface evaporation (ep), the discharge ﬂow (here the
speciﬁc discharge, a, only occurring when the water level exceeds the permanent pool) and the weir ﬂow
(w). All units in equation 9 are in m3/s.
In SWMM the detention pond is constructed by using the following settings: The top of the pond, in-
cluding weir height, is located 20 MSL. The invert level of the pond is located in (20−dPP −dSV −dw)
MSL. The outlet is located on top of the permanent pool (located dPP meters above the invert level), and
the weir is located on top of the storage volume (dPP + dSV meters above the invert level). This is also
illustrated in ﬁgure 8.

The volume of the pond is interpreted from a depth-area curve. SWMM interpolates the area and volume
in-between the user deﬁned data points. It is assumed that the slope of the pond is constant for within the
permanent pool and storage volume. It is therefore sufﬁcient to deﬁne the area at the bottom, at the top
of the permanent pool, at the bottom of the storage volume and at the top of the storage volume (ﬁgure
8). From here it is seen that the top of the permanent pool and the bottom of the storage volume is in the
same height which is not a valid input in SWMM. The bottom of the storage volume is therefore located

3.1.5
Manipulating the model using PySWMM
Every time a design parameter (e.g. depth, volume etc.) is updated, then several other model settings
should be updated in SWMM. This is both very time consuming and errors can occur when manually
having to change all these parameters after each model modiﬁcation. The advantage of manipulating the
17
==PAGE:27==
SWMM model through PySWMM is, that it becomes far easier to update model parameters. Therefore,
the model is manipulated through PySWMM.
The very ﬁrst step is to install the PySWMM package. This is done in by opening the python terminal (in
this case ’Anaconda Prompt’) in which the code: pip install PySWMM is entered.
Afterwards the generated input ﬁle (.inp ﬁle) can be loaded into PySWMM and subsequently system
compartments can be edited and simulations can be initiated (McDonnell1 et al., 2020).
3.1.6
Updating the system when design parameters are changed
The wanted design of the pond (i.e. volume, width:length ratio, slope, depth) is deﬁned in the beginning
of a simulation. Any impacted parameters must then be updated. Through PySWMMs predeﬁned handles
it is possible to edit the inlet offsets for both the outlet link and the weir. It is also possible to edit the
invert elevation, max depth and initial depth of the detention pond in accordance to the setup from table
1.
However, it was quickly discovered that storage units can only be manipulated using node handles. It is
only possible to update parameters such as depth and invert elevation. In other words, it is not possible to
update the storage curve (shape and volume) in PySWMM. Another important handle which is missing
is the handle which can edit the ﬂow through the outlet link (the speciﬁc discharge ﬂow). These issues
are solved by creating a programme which can modify these parts of the .inp ﬁle (manipulated as a .txt
ﬁle). This is done before the .inp ﬁle is loaded into PySWMM.
To ensure that the self-made handles function it is decided to run different pond conﬁgurations using both
SWMM and PySWMM. If the models give identical results then the PySWMM model can be trusted.
The model will be tested using eight different detention pond designs. These will be further presented in
section 3.2.2.
3.2
Automating simulations of one detention pond in PySWMM
The idea behind automating the long term simulations is to obtain representative results of how different
designs of detention ponds function. Then, if a planner draws in a random detention pond in a catchment
it is possible to look-up the results from the simulated design which is closest to the one drawn which
ultimately improves the basis for decision-making.
The next step is to assess which parameters matter to the number of overﬂows from the detention pond,
and how this can be impacted from the detention pond design. Looking back at equation 9 it can be
deduced that either the volume, the discharge ﬂow or evaporation must increase if overﬂows are to be
prevented. These are parameters which a planner can inﬂuence while sketching the detention pond.
Moreover, the planner is likely to know the discharge permission of the area as it is determined from
regulation.
Hence, the goal is now to determine a realistic range of different discharge ﬂows, volumes, and shape
properties which are used in real life situations. Using Python and the PySWMM interface it is possible
to run a simulation for each possible combination of the deﬁned volumes, discharge ﬂows and shapes.

Regarding the discharge ﬂow it is as mentioned often determined from regulation. Literature studies
conducted by respectively Christensen et al. (2018) and Jensen et al. (2020) shows that the most common
discharge permission is around 1, 2 or 10 L/s/red ha in Denmark, but it can also be smaller, greater or
unlimited. Based on this it is decided to construct a list of speciﬁc discharge ﬂows which have ﬁner
steps when a < 2 and then the step gradually gets coarser. The speciﬁc discharge ﬂow list looks as the
following:
a = [0.5,0.75,1,1.25,1.5,1.75,2,3,4,5,10,20,30,40,50,75,100]

to assess a realistic range of volumes. The overall recommendations regarding the shape are also clear
(and summarised in table 1) but to reduce the number of simulations it is decided to run a preliminary
test on the shape to assess how ﬁnely this should be varied. The method of these two preliminary results
are presented in section 3.2.1 and 3.2.2.
Once these ranges are deﬁned the automated simulations are initiated. The relevant results from each
simulation are stored into an SQLite database. This is the ﬁnal table which can be used to look-up how

3.2.3
Performance parameters stored to SQLite
The relevant performance parameters are stored for each unique combination of shape, storage volume
and speciﬁc discharge in a SQLite database. Such parameters covers the number of overﬂows, the over-
ﬂowing amount, the total pond discharge through the outlet, the amount evaporated, the average fraction
available of the storage volume, the number of overﬂows, the simulated return period and ﬁnally the
expected return period based on IDF curves (i.e. the DWPC spreadsheet).
Regarding the number of overﬂows it is decided to use a separation period of 24 hours to count them as
separate events. The return period is then calculated by dividing the duration of the precipitation series
with the number of events (T = years of data / number of events = 40.96 yr / number of events). A script
from Guidje (2020) is borrowed for this calculation.
The expected return period is not possible to calculate as it is only indirectly a part of the equations used
for the storage volume (return period determines the value of c and α in equation 6). It is therefore
decided to make a work around on this issue.
This is done by utilising the same script used for screening a realistic range of Vrk. I.e. Vrk is calculated
through the DWPC spreadsheet for various combinations of speciﬁc discharge (the a list from section
3.2) and return periods (this time ranging from 0 to 200 years using a step of 0.5 yr). It is also determined
to vary the safety factor (SF = 1, 1.2, 1.3, 1.4, 1.45, 1.7 and 2.0 which are values suggested in Spilde-
vandskomiteen (2014)). This gives a 3-dimensional table with 7 layers, 400 rows and 17 columns which
can be used as a look-up table (ﬁgure 10). It thereby becomes possible to to look up the return period
by ﬁnding the location of the volume which is closest to the one of interest in the column containing the
corresponding discharge ﬂow. The belonging return period can then be found from the row number. This
is done for all safety factors and thereby seven return periods are determined for each simulation. This
methodology is moreover tested using both the most recent version, v4.1, of the DWPC spreadsheet from
Spildevandskomiteen (2014) and the older version v4.0 from Spildevandskomiteen (2006b) to test how
these differs.

Vollertsen et al. (2012b) suggest that treatment can be enhanced by putting detention ponds in series,

been explored before (Del Giudice et al., 2014; Ravazzani et al., 2014; Haaning Nielsen and Vollertsen,

discussed what is likely to happen and later explore what actually happens. The scenarios are designed
by imagining how a planner would try to distribute the volume and discharge ﬂows in a real life situation.

more than one of the upstream ponds.
This conﬁguration will increase the pressure on the downstream detention pond. This is not necessarily
a problem under small events if there is plenty of room available down streams. During a little greater
events the risk of overﬂow in the downstream detention pond is exacerbated, as the downstream detention
pond is a ”bottle neck”.
Explanatory example It is assumed that the catchment, and therefore volume, is divided as
the following between the detention ponds:
• The ﬁrst pond receives water from 12% of the catchment, i.e. Vol1 = 0.12·Vol0
• The second pond receives water from 13% of the catchment i.e. Vol2 = 0.13·Vol0
• The third pond receives water from 15% of the catchment, Vol3 = 0.15·Vol0
• The downstream pond receives water from 60% of the catchment, Vol4 = 0.6·Vol0
If a rain event occurs and ﬁlls up 80% of all detention ponds then detention pond 1 will be the
ﬁrst one to empty. By the time pond 1 is empty, the downstream pond will effectively have
received 2 · a0 during that time period. I.e. the received volume in detention pond 4 is at this
moment:
Qup,1 = 0.8·0.6 Vol0 +2·0.8·0.12 Vol0 = 0.672 Vol0
Which is greater than the volume available in the downstream pond (0.672 > 0.6). Unless the
evaporation can even out this difference it is expected that the downstream pond will overﬂow
even though the system is only ﬁlled with 80% of the total volume.

4.1.2
Discussion
It is conﬁrmed that PySWMM and SWMM gives identical results and that the system parameters are
updated properly. It must also be conﬁrmed that simulation results makes sense. This is assessed by
investigating the water balances.
Looking into the model behaviour it is shown on ﬁgure 17 how storage and overﬂow are the two most
inﬂuential processes under great precipitation events. This is deduced as inflow ≈storage until the pond
overﬂows after which inflow ≈over flow. The impacts from evaporation and discharge ﬂow therefore
seems negligible during great storms. When the rain stops around 19.30 (7.30 pm) it visually looks like
the ∆S is zero but this is caused by the scale.
Looking at the smaller event in ﬁgure 19 instead it is obvious that the discharge ﬂow is present and that
it does have an impact; though its importance is greater for smaller events. Here ∆S gets below zero
when there is no inﬂow (i.e. the detention pond empties). From ﬁgure 19 it can also be noticed that
the discharge ﬂow stops at 6 pm (18:00) on the 15th, which occurs when the water level in the pond is
≈1m = dPP. This conﬁrms that the outlet link functions as intended as it should stop discharging when
the water level is 1 cm above the permanent pool.
Regarding the evaporation it is seen that the evaporation from the catchment does not start until the end
of a rain event which conﬁrms that the evaporation only occurs during dry periods as selected in SWMM.
In addition it can be noticed that it is hard to see an effect of the pond surface evaporation in either
of the ﬁgures (ﬁgure 16 17 and 19). However, from table 9 it can be concluded that some evaporation
must occur, as the volume evaporated is greater than 0. This suggests that the evaporation is close to
insigniﬁcant - even in July which has the highest potential evaporation (table 4). This will be discussed
in greater detail when exploring the importance of the shape of the detention pond. The most important
38
==PAGE:48==
conclusion to draw for now is that the PySWMM model is considered trustworthy. Hence, it is now
possible to start the work regarding the automated simulations.

4.2.8
Discussion on automated simulations
Based on the long term simulations it can be concluded that the shape of the detention pond potentially
can reduce the number of overﬂows and the overﬂowing amount. However, the impact is not signiﬁcant.
Especially when considering that the evaporation can be increased with more than 50% when using larger
surface areas, there is still no signiﬁcant effect on the overﬂowing amount as these more or less still follow
the 1:1 line in ﬁgure 32. What is more interesting is that the discharged amount can be reduced with more
than 10% due to evaporation, which thereby reduces the stress on a receiving water body.
The greatest discharge reductions occur when the volumes are maximised and the discharge ﬂows are
small (e.g. 0.5-1L/s/red ha), as this combination increases the time where water is stored in the storage
volume with great areas. The smaller values of speciﬁc discharge are known to be quite common (Jensen
et al., 2020; Christensen et al., 2018), and depending on the safety factor used when designing a detention
pond it is also possible that someone would aim for the greater storage volumes. However, it can be
discussed if this speciﬁc combination is frequently used in real life, though. Especially when looking at
the CDF curve in ﬁgure 24 there are only few volumes greater than 70-80 mm/red ha for safety factors
1-1.7 and when SF = 2 there are only few values above 100 mm/red ha.
In appendix E there is a ﬁgure similar to ﬁgure 32 where only the simulations using Vrk < 70 mm/red
ha are presented. This shows that the discharged amount is then only reduced with 10% for very few
simulations and it is far more common that it is reduced with approximately 5%.
48
==PAGE:58==
Based on all this it is recommended to neglect the shape of a detention pond when assessing its perfor-
mance in a screening situation. The performance can thereby be estimated only from the volume and the
discharge ﬂow. It is therefore recommended only to use one shape setting when performing the automated
long-term simulations. This design should either be designed to give the ”worst case” results (i.e. small
areas) or the average results (i.e. choosing values in the middle of the in the suggested ranges from table
1). It is not suggested to use the designs resulting in greatest areas as this can potentially overestimate
the performance of detention pond slightly.
Alternatively, the performance of an arbitrary detention pond can be assessed by simply using the table
generated to ﬁnd the expected return period. If so, it is found to be important to use the most recent version
(4.1) of the DWPC spreadsheet when creating this table, as the earlier version (4.0) results in undersized
detention ponds when using a safety factor of 1. The DWPC spreadsheet often underestimates the return
period from a detention pond when comparing it to the simulated return period (ﬁgure 26). However,
this is considered positive in a screening situation since it is easier to remove excess volume later in the
planning process than having to ﬁnd space for more volume.
If this approach is chosen the storage volume should potentially be corrected for small discharge ﬂows.
This is suggested as detention ponds with small discharge ﬂows are not expected to be fully available. It
is therefore suggested to subtract 2% from the volume, if the discharge ﬂow is between 1-1.5 L/s/red ha,
and 5% if the discharge ﬂow is less than 1 L/s/red ha. Though, this correction is not considered to be very
crucial as the DWPC spreadsheet already tends to oversize detention ponds. Though, it would be most
correct to reduce the volume a little.
To validate that the DWPC spreadsheet can be used for more locations in Denmark, the long-term simu-
lations are also conducted using another rain series and then it is compared to the expected return period.
The SWMM /PySWMM model is tested using a historical rain series from the city of Kolding which
covers the same time period as the Kløvermarken series (≈41years). For this simulation, only one shape
is tested (design A). The expected return period is once again plotted against the simulated return period
(using both version 4.0 and 4.1 of the DWPC spreadsheet for Kolding) resulting in ﬁgure 33. Results
for all 7 safety factors are presented in appendix E. From here it can be concluded that in Kolding the
expected return periods (v4.1) are smaller than the one simulated. Hence, also in Kolding volumes deter-
mined from the DWPC spreadsheet are oversized.
Furthermore, the expected return periods for both locations are plotted against each other in ﬁgure 34.
This shows that detention ponds with the same volume and discharge ﬂow are often expected to overﬂow
less frequently in Kolding than in Copenhagen (TKolding > TKolding).
Based on this it concluded that the DWPC spreadsheet can also be used for for screening purposes in
Kolding. Additionally, it is also advised to estimate the expected return period from a DWPC station

Discussion on coupling scenarios
In all scenarios it was expected that the evaporation would increase, which turns out to be correct. The
additional evaporation is caused from the greater surface areas obtained when distributing the volume

into several ponds. It is worth noting, that the evaporation has increased more in scenario 2, than in the
other scenarios. This is because the storage volume is on average partly ﬁlled, which thereby increases
the area from which pond evaporation can occur (see appendix F for raw data on the coupling scenarios).
Regarding how ﬂows should be distributed it is now evident that a downstream detention pond struggles if
it is not allowed to drain continuously, which is seen in scenario 1 and 2 where the downstream detention
pond receives as much, or more, water than is discharged downstream (i.e. when ai,4 ≤Qup). This is
not a surprise for scenario 1, which was expected to perform worse. Scenario 2 did on the other hand
get the beneﬁt of the doubt, as the downstream detention pond ’functions as a pipe’, as long as there is
water upstream (Qup,2 = a2,4 when ∑3
J=1 w2,j = 0). Though, as stated in the methods chapter it was also
assumed that this scaling might cause problems, as this also means that the downstream detention pond
does not effectively empty while being ﬁlled with runoff from its own catchment.
Hence, it is not recommended to allow each upstream detention ponds to discharge with the full dis-
charge permission, or to aim for a ﬂow distribution where the sum of the upstream discharges equals the
downstream discharge ﬂow.
In scenario 3 all detention ponds and their ﬂows are scaled to the fraction of the total area from which
they receives water. This makes each pond behave like the basic scenario. In scenario 3 the upstream
discharges are directed out of the system using a total ﬂow of 40% of the discharge permission, whereas
the downstream discharge ﬂow accounts for 60% of the discharge permission. If the upstream discharge
ﬂows are routed to the downstream detention pond instead, and the upstream input is added to the down-
stream discharge (Qup = 0.4a0 whereof the downstream ﬂow is corrected to a4 = 0.6a0 + 0.4a0 = a0)
then it is expected that the system will still perform as the basic scenario. This is tested in appendix F
and the statement is found to be true as there is still a total of 5 downstream overﬂow events.
This calls for two conclusions: ﬁrst, if all detention ponds discharge to the recipient it found to be feasible
to scale the volume and discharge ﬂow to the area from which the ponds receive water. Secondly, if an
upstream detention pond discharges into a downstream pond, then the downstream pond should discharge
with the upstream discharge ﬂow plus its own area-scaled fraction of the discharge permission.
From scenario 4b it is not surprisingly found that the system performs better if more water is allowed
to leave the system which simply means that the detention ponds should handle less water. In scenario
4a more water is still allowed to leave the system (total discharge ﬂow is greater than the discharge
permission). However, the upstream detention ponds are expected to overﬂow more frequently as they
discharge with a ﬂow smaller than the area-scaled discharge ﬂow. The simulation results of scenario 4a
indicate that the system will still perform better, as long as more water leaves the system.
To conﬁrm this trend the results of scenario 4a are investigated further. In scenario 4a each upstream
detention pond overﬂows 5 times but the downstream detention pond only overﬂow 4 times (-1 overﬂow
compared to the basic scenario). It is decided to test the robustness of this trend by testing how the system
behaves if the area-scaled discharge ﬂows are scaled with 0.5 or 0.2 (instead of 0.8), which would call for
even more upstream overﬂows. The simulation results are presented in appendix F. With the new scaling
of the upstream discharge ﬂows the downstream pond now overﬂows 5 and 7 times, respectively. Whereas
each of the upstream ponds overﬂow 9 and 31 times. The stated hypothesis in the methodology saying
53
==PAGE:63==
that the upstream detention ponds are expected to overﬂow more frequently is thereby true. Though, it
is possible that the downstream pond can handle the extra upstream overﬂows. However, there is also a
limit to this behaviour, since more upstream overﬂows can also cause the downstream pond to overﬂow
more frequently.
Scenario 5 and 6 are two designs which are created to assess if scenario 4a can be improved by allocating
the storage volume differently. When allocating some of the storage volume from the downstream pond
into the upstream detention ponds (scenario 5) it is found that the system has more overﬂows downstream,
even though the number of upstream overﬂows are reduced. The additional overﬂows are a result of the
fact that the downstream detention pond receives the same amount of water from its own catchment but
its storage volume is now decreased. The downstream part of the system is therefore simulated as if it
was one detention pond only (i.e. an area of 0.6 ha and with a storage volume is 40% of Vol0) (appendix
F). When ignoring the upstream detention ponds the downstream detention pond still experiences 11
overﬂows. Hence, the missing storage volume downstream increases the number of overﬂows even when
the total discharge ﬂow is in fact increased. Therefore it is recommended that one must be careful when
allocating volume upstream.
On the other hand, when more volume is located downstream (scenario 6) then the system performs as
good as in the basic scenario (5 overﬂows). This suggests that it is better to allocate volume downstream
than upstream.
This is investigated further by making a new simulation based on the area and volume distribution from
scenario 6. In the sixth scenario the upstream detention ponds receive water from 40% of the total area
but the upstream storage volumes only account for 20% of Vol0. Moreover, in the sixth scenario the
total discharge ﬂow is allowed to exceed the discharge permission. In the modiﬁed scenario 6 the storage
volume and area distribution are unchanged but it is decided that the total discharge ﬂow should equal
the discharge permission (a0) instead. In scenario 3, which has shown to function quite well, both the
discharge ﬂows and storage volumes were determined based on the fraction of the area from which they
receive water. In modiﬁed scenario 6 it is decided that the discharge ﬂow is scaled to the volume fraction
from which a pond stores water instead. Hence, the sum of the upstream discharge ﬂows is 0.2a0 and the
downstream detention pond discharges with 0.8a0. The modiﬁed scenario 6 still experiences 5 overﬂows
downstream. The simulation results of the modiﬁed scenario 6 are presented in appendix F.
This calls for a modiﬁcation from the previous conclusion stating that it is feasible to scale volumes and
discharge ﬂows to the area from which a detention pond receive water. It is now recommended that an
upstream detention pond should maximally be provided with the product of its area fraction and the total
storage volume (e.g. Volup,1 ≤Vol0 Aup,1/Atot). On the other hand, an upstream detention pond can
be smaller. If so, the reduced upstream storage volume should be added downstream. Moreover, the
upstream discharge ﬂow should be scaled to the volume fraction (e.g, qa,up1 = a0Volup,1/Voltot).

5
Discussion
It has now been conﬁrmed that it is possible to construct a simple model, which can be used to obtain
relevant information on how various detention pond designs are expected to function. Furthermore, it
has also been assessed how detention ponds can be put in series without causing more overﬂows to the
recipient. The results presented so far are dependent on the assumptions made when constructing the
model.
5.1
Model uncertainties and suggested improvements
Overall the SWMM model is kept as simple and generic as possible. One of the assumptions is that there
are no pipes in the system; instead runoff is directed straight to the detention pond(s). The detention pond
then discharges through the dimensionless ’outlet links’. The outlet link represents a pipe, though, but
all assumptions regarding the diameter, length and roughness of the pipe are avoided when using outlet
links. In a real life situation it is of course important to assess whether pipes etc. cause problems in
the system. However, this is out of the scope of this project, and pipes are therefore removed to avoid
problems arising from these. Other simpliﬁcations and assumptions are discussed below:
5.1.1
Rain series
The main input to the model is the historical rain series from ’Kløvermarken’ (Copenhagen) obtained
from a tipping bucket. However, one might remember that the annual precipitation on the location of
Kløvermarken is 636 mm/yr in accordance to the DWPC spreadsheet. Though, the annual precipitation
in the historical rain series is closer to 600 mm/yr. As stated before, the rain series misses data for four
months in 1983 if this period is removed from the series the annual precipitation is approximately 605
mm/yr. Moreover, according to the danish meteorological institute the annual precipitation for the mu-
nicipality of Copenhagen is 692 mm/yr (DMI, 2020b). This suggests that the used rain series tends to
underestimate the precipitation. This underestimation may be caused by a number of reasons. Jørgensen
et al. (1998) suggests that the RIMCO tipping bucket underestimates low-intensity events and (Vejen,
2017) says that it also tend to underestimate great intensities (+ 60 mm/h). Furthermore, it is gener-
ally accepted that wind can cause rain gauges to under-catch precipitation (DMI, 2020a; Vejen, 2017;
Hodgkinson et al., 2004). This is also considered the case for RIMCO tipping bucket Vejen (2017).
Hence, the rain series should potentially be corrected for a future study. However, for this study it was
decided to use the original series. This was chosen as it makes it possible to compare the simulated return
period obtained from DWPC data with the expected return period from DWPC spreadsheet. This has
shown to be quite interesting for Kløvermarken when using the 4.0 version of the spreadsheet where it
was found that detention ponds designed with the 4.0 version rarely complies with the expected design -
even when using the DWPC data.
If the rain series is corrected to account for wind etc. it is to be expected that more overﬂows would occur
from the detention pond. However, as the DWPC designs so-far tends to be quite oversized (when using
v. 4.1 of the DWPC spreadsheet) it is considered likely that the detention ponds would still comply to the
expected design. However, this can not be validated for now.

==PAGE:65==
5.1.2
Constant discharge ﬂow
For this project it has been assumed that the discharge ﬂow through the outlet is constant as soon as the
water table exceeds the permanent pool by 1 cm. In real life it could be expected that the ﬂow is dependent
on the water level in the storage volume. However, it is also assumed that in a real-life situation an outlet
is likely to be designed to reach the discharge permission as quickly as possible. If it is known for a
speciﬁc case that the pond will not reach the allowed discharge capacity unless the pond is close to full,
then this must be accounted for already in the design phase by choosing a smaller and representative
value of the discharge ﬂow.
5.1.3
Area of the pond
One minor simpliﬁcation of this system is that the catchment area is kept constant i.e. the detention pond
area is not subtracted from the catchment. This was done as it is not possible to connect a rain gauge to
a storage unit (the detention pond) in SWMM, i.e. increasing the pond area would result in decreased
amount of runoff if the detention pond area is subtracted. However, as evaporation both occurs on the
catchment and from the detention pond, the evaporation from the catchment will be slightly overesti-
mated as the area of the detention pond in theory should be neglected. Though, it is considered that the
precipitation is of greater importance than the evaporation.
5.1.4
Evaporation
The results of this project suggests that the shape (i.e. evaporation) is of little importance when it comes
to the number of overﬂows from a detention pond, especially if the discharge ﬂow is greater than 1 L/s/red
ha. However, Hettiarachchi et al. (2019) concludes that evaporation can actually cause a pond to overﬂow
less frequently (study area: Minnesota). The evaporation is therefore assessed one last time. Various
shapes have already been tested to explore how wide and narrow designs differ. Therefore, if the shape
should become of greater importance, then it is because the evaporation data has been underestimated.
In the methods chapter it is stated that the pond evaporation is scaled with 1.2 based on the suggestions
from Plauborg et al. (2002) and Refsgaard et al. (2003) for freshwater and wetlands. However, this
suggestion is primarily based on studies regarding wetlands which are vegetated. Looking into broader
literature it is found that several studies suggests that the actual evaporation from ponds, lakes and pans
does indeed exceed the potential Makkink evaporation, but in what degree vary among locations and it has
not been possible to ﬁnd a simple scaling factor (Xu and Singh, 2000; Delclaux et al., 2007; Aldomany
et al., 2017; WINTER et al., 1995; Yao, 2009; Majidi et al., 2015). Instead, several studies conducts an
optimisation of two empirical parameters in the original Makkink equation Makkink (1957) to obtain a
better ﬁt to the potential evaporation (e.g. ßM0 and ßM1 from Scharling (2001)). Such an optimisation has
actually already been conducted to the Makkink equation used by the Danish Metrological institute but
only to ensure a better ﬁt for potential evaporation of ’well-watered grass’ (Scharling, 2001; Mikkelsen
and Olesen, 1991) and not for open water.
One study from England, Finch (2003), ﬁnds that the potential evaporation determined from the Penman-
Monteith equation must be scaled with factors of 1.02-1.37 during summer; 1.47-2.29 during autumn,
1.14-1.95 during winter, and 0.91-0.95 during spring to obtain a better ﬁt with the actual evaporation
56
==PAGE:66==
from open water. This is interesting as Scharling (2001) shows that the Makkink method overall results
in greater values of potential evaporation compared to Penman-Monteith (10% increase of the yearly
evaporation compared to Penman-Monteith). This suggests that the Makkink method should also be up-
scaled during summer, autumn and winter but probably with a little smaller scaling factors than in Finch
(2003). Moreover, during spring the Makkink evaporation should maybe even be down scaled.
Overall it is therefore considered to be fair to use a scaling factor greater than 1, at least during summer,
autumn and winter as the evaporation most likely is greater for open water. However, it cannot be quanti-
ﬁed whether the scaling factor of 1.2 should have been greater or smaller. Though, as mentioned earlier a
small test has also been conducted using the greatest and smallest monthly evaporation rates (in appendix
E) for two volumes and two ponds designs. Even when using the historically greatest monthly potential
evaporation rates together with the pond surface evaporation factor of 1.2 it has only been possible to
obtain minor differences in the number of overﬂows between a narrow and a wide design.
Lastly, the Danish meteorological institute has recently (December 2020) published a ”climate atlas”
containing data on the danish climate and predictions on how this will change under two RCP scenarios
(RCP 4.5 and RCP 8.5) (Thejll et al., 2020). This suggests that the potential (Makkink) evaporation will
increase with 1-5% in Copenhagen in the ﬁrst part of the century (2011-2040) (?). Looking into the data
it is found that the before mentioned constructed ”maximum” year on seasonal basis results in greater
potential evaporation during winter, spring and summer than the forecasted highest values from the two
RCP scenarios (the 0.9 fractiles, see table 16). During autumn the RCP scenarios have almost double the
evaporation rates than the maximum year. Though, on yearly basis it is the maximum year which has the
greatest evaporation rates. This indicates that climate change will not cause the shape to become of great
importance either as only small changes were seen in the maximum year.

5.1.5
Final remarks on the model setup
Overall it is recognised that the model is constructed with various assumptions and simpliﬁcations. How-
ever, this is the nature of models. As no simpliﬁcations are found to be alarming it is therefore generally
accepted that the constructed model and its results can be used to answer the questions stated in the
introduction.

Though, if a similar study is to be made in the future it is highly recommended to test the model thor-
oughly both through SWMM and PySWMM. First of all SWMM uses U.S. Units by default such as
square foot per second. It is possible to use SI-units (Select: ﬂow units →CMS) but affected values are
not updated automatically. Moreover, in an early version of the the model SWMM and PySWMM did
not give identical results. These differences came from problems with e.g. storage in pipes and the invert
elevation of the outlet links. Hence, table 8 containing eight tests simulated in both SWMM and PySWM
can not be used to generally accept that PySWMM will always give identical results to SWMM.
5.2
Treatment efﬁciency
So far only little has been mentioned on the treatment efﬁciency throughout this report. As stated before
sedimentation is the main treatment process and is mainly happening in the permanent pool when it is
not disturbed (during dry periods or small events) (Behera and Teegavarapu, 2014; Woods-Ballard, 2007;
Zahari et al., 2020; Vollertsen et al., 2009; Wium-Andersen et al., 2011a; Pettersson, 1999; Wang et al.,
2004). Previous studies have shown that the treatment efﬁciency of detention ponds can be estimated
well with 1st order kinetics (Wang et al., 2004; Vollertsen et al., 2012b; Vollertsen, 2017). Moreover, the
treatment efﬁciency is highly dependent on the residence time in the detention ponds (Wang et al., 2004;
Woods-Ballard, 2007; Vollertsen et al., 2012b). Many of the shape recommendations are therefore made
to ensure that settling takes place and to avoid resuspension from the bottom. Hence, even though the
shape is of little importance when it comes to the number of overﬂows the shape must not be neglected
when designing the physical shape of a detention pond.
Tsavdaris et al. (2015) has investigated the ﬂow regimes in a detention pond through computational ﬂuid
dynamics. This study suggests that oval, elliptical and triangular ponds can experience chaotic ﬂows
which reduces the capability for settling. However, these ﬂows can be calmed from subsurface berms
or through an emergent central island. This correlates well with the suggestions from (Vollertsen et al.,
2012b) who states that treatment can be enhanced by introducing measures which breaks the ﬂow (berms,
sectioning a pond or introducing a forebay). It is not clearly stated why detention ponds in series improves
the water quality (Vollertsen et al., 2012b). However, it is presumed that when distributing the volume
into several ponds the ﬂow is further restricted, which thereby also reduces the disturbance in the ponds.

6
Recommendations
6.1
Recommendations on how to assess the performance of one detention pond
When assessing the expected performance of a detention pond in a screening phase, it is recommended
to neglect the effect of evaporation and thereby only look at the volume and discharge ﬂow.
6.1.1
Long term simulations to look up performance
It is conﬁrmed that the constructed SWMM/PySWMM model can be used to generate a table containing
long-term simulation results of various volumes and discharge ﬂow combinations. When creating such
a table in the future it is not recommended to vary the shape because the evaporation is found to be
negligible. To reduce simulation time it is further recommended not to simulate the greatest discharge
ﬂows (40 L/s/red ha +) as simulation results become quite similar no matter the volume. Moreover, the
rain series is a crucial input to the detention pond model. It is therefore recommended to update the rain
series to one representative for the area of interest.
Once such a table is generated it can be used to look-up the expected performance of a randomly drawn
detention pond. To do so, the drawn volume must be converted to mm/red ha, i.e. Vrk ( Vol[m3] =
1.2·10Vrk [mm/red ha]·A [red ha]). Moreover, the discharge ﬂow assigned to the drawn detention pond
should also be converted to [L/s/red ha].
Speciﬁcally for SCALGO + NBS it is suggested to create such look-up tables for different parts of the
country. This is recommended as a SCALGO + NBS user should be able to draw in a detention pond
anywhere in Denmark. The performance of the drawn detention pond is then looked up in the table which
is made closest to the one drawn. To reduce the simulation time it is recommended to pick out e.g. 3-
10 locations in Denmark but if time is not an issue then simulations could also be conducted for all 83
DWPC rain gauges.
6.1.2
Determine return period from IDF curves (simpler approach)
A less time consuming method is simply to use the expected return period from the look-up table gener-
ated from the DWPC spreadsheet alone. This approach has shown to understate the return period, though.
In other words, less overﬂows are simulated when using historical data compared to what is expected.
However, the excess volume can easily be removed in a later planning stage, whereas it can be problem-
atic to ﬁnd space for more volume. Using the DWPC spreadsheet is indeed far simpler, but it will give
a useful answer to the probably most crucial question in a ﬂood screening situation: How often will the
detention pond overﬂow?
For this project the DWPC look-up table consisted of 400 return periods (0.5-200 yr), 17 discharge ﬂows
and 7 safety factors (as illustrated in ﬁgure 10). On a regular laptop it took only 15 minutes to generate
such a look-up table for one location. I.e. if this process is automated for all DWPC rain gauges it
would probably take less than a day to generate all 83 look-up tables. Beside from being faster, another
advantage is that there will be compliance between how storage volumes are currently estimated in an
early planning phase (the DWPC spreadsheet, Spildevandskomiteen (2005)) and how SCALGO + NBS
59
==PAGE:69==
interprets a storage volume in a screening phase.
6.1.3
How to assess the performance outside Denmark
As SCALGO + NBS should not only operate in Denmark a solution could be to combine the two ap-
proaches. Hence, the simple approach is recommended and validated in Denmark whereas long-term
simulations can be used outside Denmark.
Alternatively, if precipitation data is available for the country / area of interest then IDF curves for various
return periods can be made. Based on the IDF curves c and α can be determined (from equation 1) which
can be used to manually calculate the needed storage volumes (equation 6). It thereby becomes possible
to generate the look-up table used to assess the expected return period (as ﬁgure 10). Though, using
equation 6 to calculate the storage volume has shown to give greater volumes than the DWPC spreadsheet
(ﬁgure 23), which thereby can result in even more oversized volumes.
Finally, for other locations than Denmark it is recommended to use the approach (long term simulations
vs construction of IDF curves) which is least time consuming.
6.2
Recommendations on how to support a user while drawing a detention pond
6.2.1
Suggesting a needed volume
First of all, one could imagine that a user would beneﬁt from a simple tool inside SCALGO + NBS
which suggests a volume to aim for. I.e. the user gets a volume by typing the wanted return period, the
discharge ﬂow (discharge permission), a safety factor and the reduced area (which can be found using
SCALGO Live). This could either be done by creating a direct link into the DWPC spreadsheet, or by
using the DWPC look-up table(s) (ﬁgure 10). Alternatively, the look-up table generated from long-term
simulations could also be used for this assessment. If the long-term simulation approach is chosen it is
possible to vary the safety factor by running the automated simulations with a rain series scaled to the
safety factor (multiplying the rain series with SF).
6.2.2
Support the user while shaping the detention pond
Moreover, if the user is allowed to select the wanted width:length ratios, slopes and depths, then it be-
comes possible to generate a suggested geometry of the detention pond. This tool is already created for
this project as it has been used to generate the storage curves for the PySWMM model using a rectangular
detention pond (appendix B). Furthermore, in an early stage of the project the storage curve could also
take the shape as a truncated elliptic cone. However the volume was slightly miscalculated when insert-
ing the elliptic areas inside SWMM whereof only the rectangular shape has been used in the modelling.
Though, the deduction of how elliptic areas can be determined based on volume, slope, width:length
and depth is presented in appendix B. Hence, it is already possible to suggest pond dimensions for a
rectangular and elliptic detention ponds.
Finally, once the user starts drawing the volume it is suggested to raise warnings if the shape recommen-
dations from table 1 are not followed due to safety and quality reasons.

6.3
Recommendations when putting detention ponds in series
The possibilities are endless on how detention ponds can be combined. The effect of putting detention
ponds in series was therefore assessed by testing different combination options when distributing a ﬁxed
volume into four detention ponds. Based on the conducted simulations it is not possible to make a full
assessment on how all possible combination of detention ponds are expected to behave. However, the
results can be used to set up some general rules on how a ﬁxed volume can be distributed into several
volumes without worsening the performance compared to a scenario using one pond only.
6.3.1
How to distribute a volume into several ponds
One of the main lessons is that one should be careful not to allocate too much volume upstream. Therefore
it is recommended not to allocate more volume to an upstream location than product of the area fraction
from which the upstream detention pond receives water and the total volume (Vup ≤(Aup/Atot)Vtot).
Another conclusion is that it is important to distribute the discharge ﬂow properly (often equal the dis-
charge permission). It is therefore recommended to scale the discharge ﬂow to the volume fraction of the
upstream detention pond (aup = (Vup/Vtot)atot). The downstream storage volume and discharge ﬂow is
determined based upon the upstream volumes, discharge ﬂows and how the ponds are connected.
This is included in ﬁgure 35 which contains a step-by-step explanation on how a ﬁxed total volume
(across all ponds in the system) can be distributed with respect to a speciﬁed discharge permission without
resulting in more downstream overﬂows. It is generally assumed that the weir overﬂow is always routed
downstream in the system whereas the regular discharge ﬂow can also be directed out of the system
along the way (into e.g. the recipient or other SCMs). It must be noticed that the volumes in the ﬁgure
are only concerning the storage volume. The volume of the permanent pool should therefore also be
added to the volumes (250 m3/red ha). Moreover, ﬁgure 36 presents the same methodology but from a
users perspective.

6.3.2
Assessing freely drawn volumes
The suggested methodology in ﬁgure 35 should as stated ensure that overﬂows should not happen more
frequently than if the entire storage volume is located end-of-pipe in one detention pond. However, this
does not allow the user to have completely free reins. System improvements / worsenings can for instance
be made by changing the volumes and discharge ﬂows more freely. However it is for now not possible to
fully quantify the impacts when putting freely drawn detention ponds in series on a screening level.
Though, when thinking logically about it, it is possible to assess the topic to some extend. The rationale is
that if an upstream detention pond has a greater return period than a downstream detention pond, then it is

the downstream detention pond which determines how frequently overﬂows occur. This was for instance
seen in coupling scenario 5 where a greater part of the total volume was allocated into the upstream
ponds making them overﬂow less frequently. However, this also made the downstream detention pond
more vulnerable towards the loading from its own catchment whereof the missing volume downstream
became the controlling cause for the number of overﬂows.
Hence, as long as the expected upstream return period is greater than, or equal to the expected return
period downstream, then it is the downstream return period which is representative for the entire system.

6.3.3
Final remark on coupling detention ponds
The downstream impacts from more upstream overﬂows could be an interesting topic for a future study.
The matter can of course be answered by more analytical approaches such as the long-term simulations
conducted in this study, or by the work from Vollertsen (2017) and Del Giudice et al. (2014). However,
these tools also requires analysis with historical data or site speciﬁc assumptions. The tools are all indeed
very helpful - but maybe not in a screening situation where the results should be given immediately.
Nonetheless, it has for now been possible to setup some rules on how a ﬁxed volume can be distributed
into several detention ponds without resulting in more overﬂows. Alternatively the performance can also
be assessed by simply ensuring that the furthest downstream detention ponds has the lowest return period,
as this will determine the number of overﬂows. Though, the simpler approach can result in a greater total
volume, which will give greater construction costs. The ﬁrst approach is therefore recommended, as this
is more cost-efﬁcient. However, if the user wants to draw in volumes more freely the simpler approach
can also be used.

Conclusions
Throughout this report it has been investigated how the performance of detention ponds can be assessed
in a screening situation. To do so a detention pond model has been constructed using U.S. EPAs SWMM
and its python interface PySWMM. PySWMM lacks several manipulation options for detention ponds.
Self-made handles were therefore made as a workaround to this problem. Once the SWMM-PySWMM
model was validated it became possible to automatically update selected model parameters one-by-one
and run the simulation using historical precipitation data. The model parameters which were tested
was the storage volume (n=52), the discharge ﬂow (n=17) and two shapes. This resulted in a total of
1768 simulations exploring the performance of detention ponds. The simulation results were saved to a
SQLite table which can be used as a look-up table for assessing the performance of a detention pond. The
simulation results suggest that the shape is of little importance when it comes to the overﬂow frequency
whereas the storage volume and the discharge ﬂow are essential parameters.
Moreover, another look-up table was created containing the expected return period obtained from the
DWPC spreadsheet, which is a common tool for determening storage volumes in Denmark. The spread-
sheet determines the needed storage volumes based on IDF curves. It was found that the simulated return
periods are in compliance with the expected return periods from the DWPC spreadsheet. A simpler
approach is therefore to look-up the expected return period of any storage volume and discharge ﬂow
combination. This approach requires less simulation time and will also ensure a compliance to the cur-
rent procedure in Denmark when assessing detention pond volumes on a screening level. Though, the
DWPC spreadsheet can not be used outside Denmark. Though, if IDF curves are available for other loca-
tions it is still possible to create a simple table containing expected return periods. Otherwise long-term
simulations can be made with the SWMM/PySWMM model using data for the area of interest.
The last objective was to explore the effect of coupling detention ponds. Various coupling scenarios have
been tested and compared to a basic scenario consisting of one pond at the furthest downstream location.
The total volume across all ponds in the system is kept ﬁxed between the scenarios and the discharge
ﬂows are determined from the discharge permission. Based on this it has been possible to deﬁne a set of
rules which, if followed, ensures that the performance is not worsened when allocating the volume from
one detention pond into several smaller detention ponds. It is thereby recommended that when allocating
a volume into several upstream detention ponds, then an upstream volume (Vup) should never be greater
than the area fraction from which it receives water scaled to the total volume (Vup ≤Aup
Atot ·Vtot). The
upstream discharge ﬂow (aup) is recommended to be determined from the discharge permission and its
volume fraction (aup = Vup
Vtot · atot). If the upstream discharge ﬂow is directed to a downstream detention
pond, then aup must be added to the downstream discharge ﬂow. Alternatively, a quicker approach is
simply ensure that a downstream detention pond has an expected return period which is equal, or greater,
than the upstream return periods. However, this approach can result in a greater total volume.

G
Scripts and .inp ﬁles
G.1
PondSize.py
Script containing functions relevant to the shape of the detention pond - including the ’self made handles’
which is used to manipulate the storage curve.
Function: DimPondRect
Determines the lengths and areas of a rectangular detention pond based on the wanted volume, depth,
width:length ratio and slope.
Function: DimPondEllip
Determines the lengths and areas of an elliptic truncated detention pond based on the wanted volume,
depth, width:length ratio and slope.
Function: ManipulateInpStorageCurve
Updates the storage curve by manipulating the .inp (”ﬁlename.inp”) ﬁle as a .txt ﬁle. The function ﬁve set
depth-area combinations. Moreover, the ﬁle must know the index (row number) of where the storage unit
is ﬁrst deﬁned in the .inp ﬁle (SU line), and also where the storage curve should be inserted (SC line).
Use following code to search for this index: Explore=open(”ﬁlestring”).readlines()
Figure G.1: Finding the relevant indexes
xxvii
==PAGE:108==
ManipulateDrainage
Updates the ﬂow through the outlet link. Must know the name of the .inp ﬁle, the ﬂow in [m3/s], and the
name of the ﬂow curve.
Figure G.2: Finding the drain index
Function: datStorageCurve and datStorageCurve2
Creates .dat ﬁles which can be loaded in SWMM to update the storage curves. Difference between
the functions is that ”datStorageCurve” creates a .dat ﬁle for both an elliptic and a rectangular shape.
”datStorageCurve2” creates one only .dat ﬁle, and the shape is depended on the input to it.
G.2
SVK Script.py
Function: ManipulateSVK
Manipulates the DWPC spreadsheet. Needs the name of the script, the coordinates of the wanted DWPC
station, a wanted safety factor, the return period and the discharge ﬂow. Function is for instance used in
a loop to create a look-up table with expected return period.
G.3
SQLite script.py
Creates SQLite table which will be used to store data from automated simulations.
G.4
GetReturnPeriod.py
Function: get return period
Determines the return period between overﬂows. Borrowed from:
Guidje, A. H. (2020), Automated long-term simulations in swmm for calculating performance indicators-
for nature based solutions, Master thesis, Department of Environmental Engineering, TechnicalUniversity
of Denmark, Kongens Lyngby
xxviii
==PAGE:109==
G.5
Main PySWMM.py
Main script for automated long-term simulations. The wanted discharge ﬂows, shapes and volumes are
deﬁned as lists. The .inp ﬁle belonging to the system of interest is manipulated by some of the above
functions. Then the .inp ﬁle is loaded by PySWMM and remaining parameters are updated. The model
then loops over these parameters until all possible combinations are simulated. Relevant simulation
results are stored to SQLite.
G.6
Main Before loop.py
Same as the above, but there is no loop around the model. This script can therefore be used to explore
one setup at the time.
G.7
Main Coupling.py
Script used to manipulate the system with more detention ponds. The different coupling scenarios are
deﬁned inside the script (+ some additional tests). Also contains a lot of code for plots etc.
