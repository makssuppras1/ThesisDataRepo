Abstract 
Asparagopsis is a genus of red macroalgae known for its methane-reducing properties and commercial 
value. Incorporating it into ruminant livestock diets has been shown to reduce enteric methane emissions 
by as much as 98% without negatively affecting animal health, making it a target for GHG emission 
reduction strategies. Large-scale cultivation of this algae requires efficient, non-invasive methods to 
monitor biomass and physiological status for both process control and the optimization of cultivation 
conditions. Traditional methods of measuring biomass and physiological status via pigment content and 
photosynthetic rate are often time-consuming, destructive, and impractical for real-time, large-scale 
monitoring. This study evaluated the application of hyperspectral imaging for developing a biomass 
estimation model and detecting physiological responses to stress in Asparagopsis armata. Algae were 
cultivated under four treatment conditions differing in light and nutrient availability. Spectral data was 
collected using a hyperspectral camera and analyzed using PLS regression and reflectance indices. The PLS 
model estimated biomass with high accuracy (R2 = 0.91, RMSE = 0.23 g FW/L). Pigment-related 
reflectance indices responded sensitively to stress, whereas reflectance in the near-infrared region remained 
stable across treatments. Green algae contamination introduced spectral interference at high levels with 
distinct spectral characteristics, allowing for the development of indices to quantify contamination. Overall, 
hyperspectral imaging proved to be a promising tool for non-invasive biomass estimation and physiological 
health monitoring in commercial Asparagopsis cultivation, with strong potential to support real-time 
process control and optimize production efficiency.

Part I: Literature Review 
 
Application of Spectroscopy Techniques in Asparagopsis spp. 
Cultivation: A Review 
Author: Sarah Julie-Christoffersen Bartels (s233215@dtu.dk) 
1. Introduction 
Asparagopsis spp. is a genus of marine red macroalgae with significant applications in biotechnology and 
climate change mitigation, largely due to their biologically active secondary metabolites properties. These 
include antimicrobial, anticancer, anti-inflammatory, and antioxidant effects, in addition to their ability to 
reduce methane emissions in ruminant livestock (Ponte et al., 2022). Recent research has increasingly 
focused on optimizing large-scale land-based cultivation of the tetrasporophyte stage of Asparagopsis spp., 
which is rich in these bioactive compounds (Figueroa et al., 2006; Mata et al., 2006). Among the most 
valuable compounds produced by Asparagopsis spp. are phycobiliproteins including phycoerythrin and 
phycocyanin, as well as halogenated secondary metabolites like bromoform which play a key role in its 
methane-reducing properties (Zanolla et al., 2022).  
Of its many promising applications, Asparagopsis spp. has gained attention particularly for its role in 
methane reduction in livestock agriculture. Methane is a potent greenhouse gas with a global warming 
potential 80 times greater than CO2 over 20 years (IPCC, 2013).  Ruminant animals like cows and sheep 
are responsible for a major share of methane released by the livestock sector, which accounts for 
approximately 35% of all anthropogenic methane emissions worldwide (Eisen & Brown, 2022). 
Eliminating these emissions has the potential to lower radiative forcing by around 0.22 W/m2, which 
corresponds to approximately 4.4% of the radiative forcing projected by the end of the century (Eisen & 
Brown, 2022). Thus making it a key target for emission reduction strategies. It was found that bromoform 
inhibits methanogenesis in the rumen, and further studies showed that supplementing cattle feed with 
Asparagopsis spp. can reduce methane production by up to 98% in vitro, without negatively impacting 
animal health or product quality (Kinley et al., 2016; Roque et al., 2021). Both Asparagopsis taxiformis and 
Asparagopsis armata accumulate significantly higher levels of bromoform than other algae, with its in-
tissue bromoform content being between 0.1-5% of dry weight, influenced by factors such as genetic 
lineage and environmental conditions (Mata et al., 2012; Paul et al., 2006). These groundbreaking findings 
==PAGE:14==
11 
 
have been supported by several other studies, reinforcing the idea that the inclusion of Asparagopsis spp. 
in livestock diets can significantly reduce enteric methane emissions, offering a sustainable solution to 
mitigate one of the key contributors to greenhouse gas emissions in agriculture (Kinley et al., 2020; Roque 
et al., 2019, 2021). 
To unlock the full potential of Asparagopsis spp. in methane mitigation and other applications, establishing 
a large-scale, specialized seaweed farming industry is necessary. Consistent and reliable production in 
photobioreactors (PBRs) is key for industrial long-term cultivation, ensuring both stable biomass yield and 
bromoform content. (Zanolla et al., 2022). However, maintaining optimal growth conditions is challenging, 
as Asparagopsis spp. are sensitive to abiotic factors such as light, temperature, pH, salinity, and nutrient 
availability (Dishon et al., 2023). This makes it essential to develop reliable methods for monitoring growth 
and physiological status in real time, allowing for adjustments to be made as needed to maintain optimal 
conditions for both growth and compound accumulation. Spectroscopy techniques have emerged as a 
promising tool for this purpose, offering a non-invasive and efficient method to assess the physiological 
responses and bioactive compound production of Asparagopsis spp. in near real time. 
This literature review examines the current state of research using spectroscopy techniques in algae studies 
and their relevance to Asparagopsis spp. cultivation. While spectroscopy has shown promise as a tool for 
real time monitoring, its full potential is still underexplored. Challenges remain in adapting these techniques 
for use in algae cultivation, particularly in standardizing measurements and correlating spectral data with 
biomass growth and compounds of interest. By summarizing published findings and identifying key 
knowledge gaps, this review aims to inform future research. 
2. Environmental Factors and Monitoring Challenges 
The cultivation of Asparagopsis spp. is influenced by several environmental factors including light 
conditions and nutrient availability. In order to understand the effects of these commonly studied factors 
for improved production, it is important to be able to efficiently and reliably monitor the biological 
parameters they influence such as growth rate, photosynthetic efficiency, pigment content, and bioactive 
compound composition. 
2.1 Key Environmental Drivers of Growth and Compound Production 
Light intensity and quality (wavelength composition) significantly impact the photosynthetic performance, 
growth, metabolism, and biochemical composition of Asparagopsis spp. Excessive levels of 
photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) can exceed the algae's photosynthetic capacity, potentially 
leading to photoinhibition and physiological stress (Nath et al., 2013). It is therefore essential to understand 
==PAGE:15==
12 
 
how Asparagopsis spp. respond to different light intensities and spectral compositions in order to optimize 
growth and bioactive compound production. Light conditions also affect the competitive dynamics within 
cultivation systems, as certain spectra or intensities may favor the proliferation of green algae contamination 
(biofouling). Contamination is a significant challenge in algae cultivation, as it nearly impossible to fully 
prevent or eliminate it from a system. Once present, it can reduce product yield and quality by outcompeting 
the cultivated algae for light, nutrients, and other resources. To achieve an efficient system in terms of 
energy use and product yield, light conditions then need to be carefully balanced to support biomass growth, 
secondary metabolite synthesis, and minimize green algae contamination.  
The availability of nitrogen plays an important role in the growth, secondary metabolite production, and 
pigment content in Asparagopsis spp. tissues. When algae acquire resources in excess of their growth 
demands, these surplus resources are put towards secondary metabolite production (Mata et al., 2012). For 
example, an increase in nitrogen availability leads to a decrease in the tissue C:N ratio, as the production 
of nitrogen-based compounds increases (Mata et al., 2012). In contrast, nitrogen limitation in red 
macroalgae leads to degradation of photosynthetic pigments as they are utilized as a secondary nitrogen 
source, as well as reduced photosynthetic efficiency and increased vulnerability to photoinhibition  (Barufi 
et al., 2012). 
2.2 Limitations of Current Sampling Methods 
Monitoring the effects of environmental factors on Asparagopsis spp. growth and metabolism usually 
requires samples to be collected from the cultivation system for analysis, which can be time-consuming and 
labor-intensive. The time required for these analysis varies depending on the methodologies used, ranging 
from minutes to hours or even several days. For example, measuring fresh weight biomass is relatively 
quick and straightforward, whereas metabolite analysis takes longer due to the need for extraction steps and 
chromatographic methods (Sá et al., 2022).  
The most commonly used methods for assessing Asparagopsis spp. biomass include fresh weight and dry 
weight measurements, both of which provide accurate estimations when consistent, but require significant 
time and manual effort (Dishon et al., 2023; Hargrave et al., 2024; Mata et al., 2006). Furthermore, 
measuring dry weight requires the destruction of the biomass through drying, which results in the loss of 
viable product and makes it inapplicable for continuous monitoring applications. Measuring fresh weight 
does not destroy the biomass, but it is invasive and can cause stress to the algae. Photosynthetic rate is 
typically assessed using photosynthesis-irradiance (P-E) curves based on the rate of oxygen production 
(Dishon et al., 2023; Resetarits et al., 2024; Zanolla et al., 2022). Generating P-E curves requires multiple 
measurements at different light intensities which can be time consuming, and the accuracy can be affected 
==PAGE:16==
13 
 
by fluctuations in environmental factors like temperature and CO2 concentration. Photosynthetic pigment 
concentrations, including chlorophyll, carotenoids, and phycobilins, are typically determined through 
chemical extraction followed by spectral absorption analysis using a UV-Vis spectrophotometer, a method 
that is both time-consuming and destructive to the sample (Dishon et al., 2023; Zanolla et al., 2022). 
Bromoform content is analyzed by taking tissue samples and conducting a gas chromatography-mass 
spectrometry (GC-MS) analysis (Dishon et al., 2023; Hargrave et al., 2024; Zanolla et al., 2022). This is a 
highly accurate but expensive and time-consuming process, that requires complex instrumentation and 
specialized technical expertise.  
Given the drawbacks of these methods, the industry requires a more efficient method to continuously 
monitor Asparagopsis spp. in large-scale production. 
3. Spectroscopy Techniques 
Spectroscopy-based techniques offer a promising alternative to conventional sampling and wet-lab analysis 
methods. With a range of optical technologies that measure the interaction between light and matter, they 
allow for a non-invasive and non-destructive analysis of biological samples for faster and automated 
decision making. These methods are particularly well-suited for monitoring algae, as they have already 
been proven to be able to assess key biological parameters such as pigments, biochemical compounds, and 
photosynthetic efficiency in real-time. However, while these initial findings are encouraging, studies 
applying spectroscopy to algal research are still relatively limited. Further research is needed to fully 
explore their potential, refine methodologies, and collect sufficient data to produce more reliable and 
conclusive results.  
3.1 2D Fluorometry 
2D process fluorometers, such as the BioView system (DELTA Light and Optics, Hørsholm, Denmark), 
enable the qualitative and quantitative detection of biogenic fluorophores, including proteins, 
photosynthetic pigments, coenzymes, and vitamins, both inside and outside cells (Glindkamp et al., 2009; 
Sá et al., 2022). This fluorescence technique can capture spectra within the 280 - 700 nm range, scanning 
both excitation and emission wavelengths (Glindkamp et al., 2009) (Fig. 1).  
==PAGE:17==
14 
 
 
Fig. 1. Generalized schematic of a BioView 2D fluorometer setup (DELTA Light and Optics, Hørsholm, Denmark) 
showing the use of excitation and emission filter wheels and a fiber-optic light guide attached to a bioreactor. 
(Glindkamp et al., 2009) 
A study by Sá et al (2022) using 2D fluorometry to assess the cell wall integrity of Dunaliella salina (green 
microalgae) demonstrated the ability to identify amino acids and pigments within the fluorescence spectra, 
both intra- and extracellular. This was achieved using the non-destructive method of immersing an optical 
fiber probe in the sample (Sá et al., 2022). These findings suggest that 2D fluorometry can be a valuable 
tool for getting detailed biochemical information in place of traditional chemical analyses. However, its 
most appropriate application is likely within laboratory settings when working to optimize production, 
rather than in system monitoring of Asparagopsis spp. Although this optical technique is useful for in-line 
measurements since it can be directly attached to PBRs via a fiber-optic guide (Glindkamp et al., 2009; 
Marose et al., 1998; Sá et al., 2022), its application is best suited to microalgae cultures or other 
homogenous samples. When applied to macroalgae, the optical fiber only captures data from the material 
directly in front of it, which can vary from one sampling moment to the next. Nonetheless, 2D fluorometry 
holds significant promise as a laboratory-based tool for advancing our understanding of algae physiology 
and optimizing cultivation strategies, especially when used in controlled experimental settings.  
 3.2 Infrared Spectroscopy 
Infrared (IR) spectroscopy gives valuable insights into molecular structures by detecting biological bonds 
such as C-H, N-H, O-H, and S-H within a wavelength range of typically 780 – 2500 nm (Sá et al., 2022). 
This technique has been used in bioprocess monitoring to measure metabolites like ethanol, glucose, and 
fructose (Glindkamp et al., 2009). The development of Fourier Transform Infrared (FTIR) spectroscopy has 
expanded IR applications to more dilute biological samples, including those in the food industry and cellular 
cultivations such as microalgae and cyanobacteria (Sá et al., 2022). In macroalgae research, FTIR has been 
used as a non-destructive method for analyzing valuable polysaccharides, such as carrageenan, fucoidan, 
==PAGE:18==
15 
 
and alginate (Vandanjon et al., 2023). By measuring C-Br bonds, IR spectroscopy is also a viable method 
for measuring brominated carbons like bromoform, although there are no published studies confirming its 
effectiveness and there is no fully developed and validated methodology. 
However, as with 2D fluorometry, FTIR presents challenges for application to in-line monitoring of 
macroalgae. Traditional FTIR spectroscopy typically requires specific sample preparation, such as drying 
or pressing the samples into thin films or pellets (Mettler-Toledo, 2023). While Attenuated Total 
Reflectance-FTIR (ATR-FTIR) is able to take real-time measurements of solids and liquids with no 
sampling preparation (Mettler-Toledo, 2023), integrating it into photobioreactor systems introduces the 
problems of biofouling in the flow cells and the non-uniform distribution of suspended macroalgae in water 
when doing single point sampling. So, while infrared spectroscopy and its related techniques offer a non-
destructive, chemical-free method of detecting key macromolecules and valuable compounds, its practical 
application for in-line monitoring of macroalgal cultivation systems is limited. Its greatest utility is in 
laboratory-based analysis to support optimization and potentially measure bromoform, rather than real-time 
system monitoring.   
 3.3 Chlorophyll Fluorescence Spectroscopy 
Chlorophyll spectroscopy is an effective method for evaluating photosynthetic efficiency, as it gives fast 
and non-invasive information about the fate of absorbed light energy. Pulse Amplitude Modulated (PAM) 
fluorometry is a widely used technique for measuring chlorophyll fluorescence, particularly in higher 
plants, as it can determine key photosynthetic parameters that can be used to evaluate the functional state 
of photosystems I and II (PSI & PSII) (Sá et al., 2022).  
The most commonly used parameters for looking at photosynthetic performance include the maximum 
quantum yield (Fv/Fm), which reflects the full potential efficiency of PSII when it is relaxed in a dark-
adapted state, and effective quantum yield (ΦPSII), which indicates the actual efficiency of PSII under light 
conditions (Maxwell & Johnson, 2000; Murchie & Lawson, 2013). Both provide different insights into the 
efficiency of PSII. By analyzing changes in these parameters, PAM fluorometry gives real-time information 
about the performance of photosynthetic organisms. The electron transport rate (ETR) in PSII is also a 
useful indicator of photosynthetic performance and a sensitive stress response metric, with declines in ETR 
under environmental conditions such as high light, nutrient limitation, or temperature fluctuations 
indicating impaired electron transport (Figueroa et al., 2006). Since Fv/FM requires a dark adaptation period 
(10-30 min) to ensure that all PSII reaction centers are fully relaxed (Maxwell & Johnson, 2000; Murchie 
& Lawson, 2013), it is unsuitable for in-line monitoring in PBRs where production would be interrupted. 
==PAGE:19==
16 
 
However, ΦPSII and ETR can both be measured without dark adaptation, making them better suited for 
monitoring.  
While chlorophyll spectroscopy techniques have seen growing application in microalgae and macroalgae 
research, their use in Asparagopsis spp. studies is still limited. Nonetheless, the well-established principles 
of chlorophyll spectroscopy provide a strong foundation for advancing research on this genus. In particular, 
parameters such as ΦPSII and ETR have the potential to be used for in-line monitoring of photosynthetic 
performance and stress responses of Asparagopsis spp. under fluctuating environmental.  
 3.4 Hyperspectral Cameras 
Hyperspectral cameras can capture significantly more detailed information than spectrophotometers, as 
they provide both spectral and spatial information from a single sample. By combining imaging and 
spectroscopy, hyperspectral imaging (HSI) produces a three-dimensional data cube (x, y, λ) that contains 
detailed spectral data (reflectance) for each pixel in an image (Bhargava et al., 2024). Shown in Fig. 2 is 
the process of capturing a hyperspectral image and the resulting hyperspectral data cube, which can be 
described as a stack of 2D images for each wavelength band filtered by the camera.  
 
Fig. 2 Computed Tomographic Imaging Spectrometer (CTIS) system and the hyperspectral data cube reconstruction 
process. Light passes through a series of lenses and a diffractive optical element (DOE), and produces multiple spectral 
projections on the image sensor. These projections form the CTIS image, which consists of a zero-order (central image) 
and multiple first-order diffractions. The projections are then computationally reconstructed into a data cube using 
Expectation-Maximization (EM) or a convolutional neural-network (CNN), such as the U-Net model. (Qtec, n.d.). 
This technique provides a comprehensive view of the sample across a broad range of wavelengths, made 
possible by covering a continuous portion of the light spectrum with a high spectral resolution. HSI usually 
captures near-infrared, visible, and short-wavelength infrared spectra within the range of 400 - 2500 nm 
depending on the camera (Bhargava et al., 2024). Camera types are classified by the method of data 
acquisition (Fig. 3), each offering different trade-offs in terms of spatial resolution, speed, and suitability 
for dynamic or static samples (Qtec, n.d.)

17 
 
 
Fig. 3 The following figures demonstrate the different methods of HSI acquisition. From left to right: point scanning 
(whisk broom), line scanning (push broom), spectral scanning, and snapshot (Qtec, n.d.) 
HSI has recently gained traction in agricultural research for estimating chlorophyll content, biomass, and 
detecting early signs of nutrient stress and plant disease (Ruszczak et al., 2022). In algal research, it is 
increasingly used for remote sensing in natural environments, such as oceans and lakes, as well as in 
laboratory cultivation systems. Studies on various micro- and macroalgae species have shown that the bio-
optical properties of algae can be detected by this technique, enabling the development of spectral indices 
and models to analyze photosynthetic pigments and estimate biomass, with potential for many more 
applications to be explored (Salmi et al., 2021).  
Among the available spectroscopy techniques, hyperspectral imaging is especially well-suited for in-line 
monitoring in macroalgae cultivation systems. Unlike single-point sensors (e.g., fluorometers or FTIR), 
HSI captures spatial data over a broader sampling area that allows for a more accurate measurement of non-
homogenous materials like suspended macroalgae. Additionally, it checks all the boxes of both being able 
to estimate biomass and detect a wide variety of compounds and molecules. Given its ability to capture 
high resolution spectral and spatial data in real time, HSI is one of the most promising technologies for real-
time, non-invasive monitoring of Asparagopsis spp. cultivation.

4. Spectroscopy in Asparagopsis spp. Research 
 4.1 Estimating Biomass 
For both laboratory and commercial cultivation of algae, biomass should not only be reliable and easily 
measurable, but also standardized to ensure consistent accuracy across different conditions and trials. Rapid 
data availability, ideally in real-time, is crucial in order to support timely decision-making, such as choosing 
the optimal time for harvest or adjusting culture conditions. 
Spectroscopy-based biomass estimation techniques rely on how algae interacts with light, influencing 
reflectance in specific spectral bands as metabolically active compounds and algae biomass absorb and 
reflect light at different wavelengths (Morgado et al., 2024). Reflectance in the visible and near-infrared 
(NIR) regions has been shown to be particularly important for finding these correlations (Morgado et al., 
2024). As a result, spectroscopy techniques covering these spectral ranges are especially applicable. 
In a recent study, Morgado et al. (2024) measured reflectance from microalgae biofilms grown under 
different light and nutrient regimes, and identified spectral bands and reflectance indices most strongly 
associated with biomass and chlorophyll content. Reflectance indices are mathematical combinations of 
reflectance values at two or more wavelengths, used to highlight specific characteristics of the target sample 
(Morgado et al., 2024). The study found that reflectance in the red-edge (700 - 800 nm) and NIR regions 
(700 - 2500 nm) remained relatively stable across conditions, while the blue, green, and red visible light 
regions (400 - 700 nm) varied significantly in response to environmental conditions (Morgado et al., 2024). 
This suggests that while pigment-related regions reflect dynamic physiological responses, the NIR region 
may be a more stable indicator of biomass and possibly structural features of algal cell walls. 
There are several challenges that must be addressed when applying spectroscopy-based biomass estimation. 
First, optical sensors must be calibrated for specific sampling locations to account for various sources of 
interference (Rousso et al., 2021). Additionally, compound concentrations in cells can fluctuate depending 
on environmental factors such as light regimes, physiological state, and nutrient availability, thereby 
influencing the reflectance spectra (Bertone et al., 2018; Morgado et al., 2024). A robust monitoring method 
much be able to produce accurate measurements under a variety of conditions. Despite these challenges, 
spectroscopy-based monitoring presents a promising avenue for real-time, non-invasive biomass estimation 
in Asparagopsis spp., provided that physiological variability is properly accounted for and sensor systems 
are appropriately calibrated.

 4.2 Monitoring Physiological Condition 
To efficiently evaluate the physiological state of cultured algae, it is important to find processes that have a 
quick and sensitive response to stressors. In Asparagopsis spp., changes in photosynthetic pigment 
concentrations and photosynthetic efficiency are among the earliest and most sensitive indicators of 
physiological stress. These changes can be detected using spectroscopic techniques. 
In addition to having chlorophyll-a, Asparagopsis spp. is characterized by a high abundance of the 
phycobilin pigments phycoerythrin and phycocyanin (Dishon et al., 2023). These red pigments absorb light 
in the blue-green visible light range, and give the algae its red coloration. The abundance and composition 
of pigments in algae are known to change in response to environmental factors such as light quality and 
intensity, as well as nutrient limitation (Barufi et al., 2012; Dishon et al., 2023; Figueroa et al., 2006). These 
changes in pigment composition reflect the algae’s acclimation responses to environmental factors and can 
serve as early indicators of physiological stress. In extreme cases, complete pigment depletion (bleaching) 
indicates cell death and a complete loss of photosynthetic capacity.  
Unfortunately, the common issue of green algae contamination can confound the interpretation of spectral 
data, potentially masking physiological stress responses in Asparagopsis spp. The unique characteristic of 
having red phycobilin pigments that are not present in green algae and absorb light in a different spectral 
range than chlorophyll, differentiates them from green algae. This difference can be leveraged to detect 
contamination. While studies specifically using spectroscopy to distinguish and quantify green algae 
contamination in macroalgae cultivation are currently lacking, research on multispecies differentiation of 
microalgae has demonstrated that spectral differences from unique pigment compositions can be used to 
distinguish between taxa(Wei et al., 2017). Applying similar methods could potentially be applied to 
distinguish green algae contamination from Asparagopsis spp. and improve accuracy of health monitoring. 
In addition to looking at pigments, photosynthetic performance via chlorophyll fluorescence is also a good 
way to detect stress in Asparagopsis spp. In a study by Figueora et al (2006), PAM fluorometry was used 
to evaluate the physiological condition of A. armata. Key parameters such as Fv/FM, ΦPSII, and ETR were 
measured along with chlorophyll-a, phycocyanin, and phycoerythrin. The study found that physiological 
degradation of algae was associated with a decrease in photosynthetic pigment levels and a decline in 
photosynthetic performance of chlorophyll. However, after three days of recovery under optimal conditions, 
PAM-fluorometry parameters returned to pre-stress levels. This shows that finding a critical threshold of 
FV/FM or other stress-responsive metrics could serve as an early warning system to prevent complete culture 
failure. (Figueroa et al., 2006)  
==PAGE:23==
20 
 
In conclusion, spectroscopy stands out as a highly effective tool for identifying stress conditions in algae 
cultures and quantifying their response to environmental stressors. By utilizing parameters such as pigment 
abundance and photosynthetic efficiency, these techniques are invaluable for monitoring algae health and 
ultimately optimizing growth conditions. The technologies that would be suitable for monitoring these 
parameters include PAM fluorometers for photosynthetic efficiency, as well as hyperspectral cameras 
capable of detecting changes in the spectral bands associated with pigments. 
5. Application 
Spectroscopy has significant potential for real-time monitoring and process control applications in 
Asparagopsis spp. cultivation. Literature suggests that this approach can be highly effective in tracking 
algae health and ensuring stable production by providing crucial insights into multiple physiological 
parameters. Establishing stress metrics or parameters and conducting further research on the limits of 
Asparagopsis spp. stress tolerance will be essential in identifying the point beyond which recovery is no 
longer possible, allowing for timely intervention. Additionally, continuous monitoring of algae health can 
help maintain growth conditions that optimize both biomass growth and bioactive compound production 
while signaling potential stressors that could impact bromoform content. Being able to then track biomass 
growth via spectroscopy biomass estimation methods has also been shown to be feasible according to 
literature.  
Applying spectroscopy technologies in Asparagopsis cultivation presents several technical challenges that 
must be addressed in order to ensure their effectiveness in large-scale production. One challenge is 
identifying and removing background noise and components that can obscure spectral signals, such as light 
scattering from tank walls or variable lighting conditions, which lead to inaccurate readings. Without 
precise data processing techniques and procedures, variability in measurements could reduce the reliability 
of spectroscopy for monitoring algal health. Another challenge is the lack of a standardized spectroscopy 
protocol for macroalgae cultivation. Developing a universally applicable method that is simple, consistent, 
adaptable, and reproducible across different production sites is essential for widespread adoption. 
Variability in cultivation conditions, such as differences in biomass density, water quality, and lighting, can 
impact spectral data measurements, making standardization crucial for getting comparable results across 
facilities.  
Implementing process controls monitoring at an industrial scale requires advanced software capable of 
processing and interpreting complex spectral data in real time. This software must provide clear, actionable 
insights for technicians, allowing them to make informed decisions about optimizing growth conditions, 
preventing stress-induced culture failures, and responding swiftly to system malfunctions. Without an 
==PAGE:24==
21 
 
intuitive and user-friendly interface, the potential benefits of using spectroscopy could be underutilized and 
its effectiveness in large-scale operations limited.  
6. Conclusion 
This review has outlined the importance of real-time, non-destructive monitoring tools in optimizing large-
scale Asparagopsis spp. cultivation. Traditional monitoring methods, while accurate, are time-consuming, 
labor-intensive, and often destructive, making them unsuitable for continuous process control in 
commercial systems. Spectroscopy techniques, particularly hyperspectral imaging, 2D fluorometry, FTIR, 
and PAM fluorometry, have shown significant promise in measuring biomass and algae health parameters 
of interest. Each technique offers unique insights with their own benefits and limitations. 2D fluorometry 
can detect pigments and metabolites, FTIR can identify macromolecules, PAM fluorometry measures 
photosynthetic performance of chlorophyll, and hyperspectral imaging captures spectral-spatial data that 
allows for pigment detection, biomass estimation, and stress detection. Among these, hyperspectral imaging 
stands out as the most practical and applicable option for in-line monitoring of suspended macroalgae. 
Unlike the single-point measurements taken by the other methods, it captures the heterogeneity of 
suspended macroalgae across a larger area, making it particularly well-suited for cultivation systems where 
algae distribution is uneven. It also has the benefit of being multifunctional, reducing the need for multiple 
specialized instruments. 
These findings directly inform the objectives of this thesis by justifying the exploration of hyperspectral 
cameras as a tool to support automated monitoring and process control in Asparagopsis spp. cultivation. 
Further research is needed to validate biomass estimation models, identify stress-related spectral regions, 
and establish spectral data acquisition and processing protocols that produce accurate, reproducible, and 
comparable data.

Utilizing Hyperspectral Imaging to Monitor and Optimize the 
Growth of Asparagopsis spp.

1. Introduction 
Asparagopsis is a genus of marine red macroalgae that has attracted growing commercial and scientific 
interest due to its methane-reducing properties and high-value bioactive compounds (Ponte et al., 2022). Its 
inclusion in the diets of ruminant livestock has been shown to reduce enteric methane emissions by up to 
98% without harming animal health, making it a target for GHG emission reduction strategies (Kinley et 
al., 2016; Roque et al., 2019). This property is primarily due to its high concentrations of bromoform, a 
bioactive compound found in significantly higher concentrations in Asparagopsis spp. than in other algae 
species (McConnell & Fenical, 1977). To realize its full potential as a methane mitigation strategy on a 
global scale, large-scale cultivation systems must be developed and optimized for the Asparagopsis genus. 
In commercial algae production, monitoring biomass growth and biomass health status is critical for 
ensuring good product quantity and quality. Reported growth rates for Asparagopsis taxiformis typically 
range from 6 - 13% fresh weight (FW) per day at 90 µmol photons m⁻² s⁻¹ (PAR) (Torres et al., 2023), 
with substantial variation depending on biomass density and cultivation conditions. Consistently tracking 
growth rates is essential for optimizing harvest timing, estimating productivity, and detecting stagnation 
or decline before losses occur. Monitoring the physiological status of the algae, including stress and 
overall health, is crucial for maximizing growth rates and manipulating culture conditions to increase 
accumulation of bioactive compounds. Early identification of physiological stress can serve as a warning 
signal, allowing corrective actions to be taken before cultures deteriorate beyond recovery. 
Traditional methods for monitoring algae parameters such as weighing dried biomass, chemically 
determining pigment content, and measuring photosynthetic rate via oxygen production, while accurate, 
are often time-consuming, labor-intensive, and destroy the sample. Many of these techniques require 
removing samples from the system for off-site analysis, delaying feedback and disrupting the culture. These 
limitations make traditional methods impractical for routine or continuous monitoring, especially in 
commercial-scale systems where timely decision making is essential. As a result, there is an urgent need 
==PAGE:30==
27 
 
for in-situ, non-destructive alternatives that can provide real-time insights. Hyperspectral imaging (HSI) 
has emerged as a particularly promising tool in this regard. In contrast to spectroscopy technologies such 
as fluorometers or infra-red spectroscopy with single-point sensors, HSI captures both spatial and spectral 
data across a larger sampling area, making it well-suited for non-homogenous suspended macroalgae. While 
line scan hyperspectral cameras are often used in controlled or industrial settings due to their high spectral 
and spatial resolution, they are not ideal for imaging moving samples. Snapshot hyperspectral cameras are 
better suited for imaging the suspended macroalgae, as they capture all spatial and spectral data in a single 
frame. With its ability to deliver high-resolution real-time data without disturbing the culture, HSI offers a 
powerful, non-invasive approach for monitoring Asparagopsis spp. in cultivation settings.  
For real-time biomass estimation to be effective, it must rely on spectral features that remain stable under 
physiological stress. Since metabolically active compounds are highly responsive to environmental 
conditions, regions of the spectrum less affected by physiological change, such as the near infrared (NIR) 
which interacts with tissues and cellular structures (Hu, 2022), offer a more reliable basis for quantifying 
biomass. The physiological health of Asparagopsis spp. is strongly influenced by environmental factors, 
particularly light conditions and nutrient availability, both of which directly affect photosynthetic pigment 
content. Light intensity and spectral quality (wavelength composition) determine photosynthetic 
performance, metabolic activity, and biochemical composition, and excessive exposure to 
photosynthetically active radiation (PAR) can lead to photoinhibition and physiological stress (Nath et al., 
2013). Similarly, nutrient availability plays a critical role in algal physiology, influencing pigment 
biosynthesis, growth rate, and secondary metabolite accumulation. Under nitrogen-limited conditions, red 
macroalgae degrade their photosynthetic pigments to use them as secondary nitrogen sources, which 
results in decreased pigment content, lower photosynthetic efficiency, and heightened susceptibility to 
photoinhibition (Barufi et al., 2012). With photosynthetic pigments being so closely tied to physiological 
function, changes in their concentration and associated spectral signatures offer a sensitive and early 
indicator of stress. Monitoring pigment content and its spectral expression is therefore a promising 
approach for evaluating the health status of Asparagopsis spp. in cultivation systems. 
This study investigates whether a snapshot hyperspectral camera can be used to both reliably estimate 
biomass and detect physiological responses in Asparagopsis spp. to stress-inducing environmental 
conditions. Since Asparagopsis taxiformis and Asparagopsis armata are morphologically very similar and 
can only be reliably differentiated genetically, this study assumes that findings for one species are generally 
applicable to the other, given their comparable physiological traits. It is hypothesized that spectral 
characteristics not primarily influenced by photosynthetic pigments can be used to accurately estimate 
biomass, independently of physiological condition. It is also hypothesized that the spectral signatures of 
==PAGE:31==
28 
 
pigments will be influenced by physiological responses to environmental stressors such as light conditions  
and nutrient limitation, and can be used to quantify algal health and stress. The experiment objectives are 
to: 
1. Develop a robust predictive model for estimating Asparagopsis spp. biomass density using 
hyperspectral data. 
 
2. Assess changes in spectral reflectance over time in response to varying light intensity  and 
nutrient availability, and evaluate the potential of using it to monitor physiological status and 
inform process control.

2.2.1 Biomass Cultivation 
For the nutrient conditions, the nutrient-depleted treatment was prepared using filtered seawater supplied 
by Nordsøen Forskerpark A/S (Hirtshals, Denmark), which is known to have very low nutrient 
concentrations (~0.2 mg/L NO3-N, PO4-P under detection limit of 0.1 mg/L). All other treatments received 
nutrient-replete water sourced from the filtered effluent of a marine land-based recirculating aquaculture 
system (RAS) farm producing Atlantic Salmon (Salmo salar), which is known to contain sufficient nutrient 
concentrations to support macroalgal growth due to the high feed input and nutrient-rich waste generated 
by the fish production system. This is the same RAS water used by Maripure ApS (Hirtshals, Denmark) in 
their production system, where it is passed through a filtration system (drum filter, protein skimmer, UV, 
filters of 50 and 10 µm). Temperature (18 °C), bottom aeration ensuring continuous suspension of the algae 
in the water column (2 L/min), and photoperiod (16:8 light-dark photoperiod) were maintained as controlled 
variables to ensure consistent environmental conditions across treatments. Temperature was chosen based 
on the reported optimal growth range for A. armata (15 - 24 °C) and its upper tolerance threshold of 29 °C 
(Mata et al., 2006). The photoperiod was selected according to what has previously been shown to support 
optimal growth (Dishon et al., 2023). Light intensity was measured with an Apogee Mode SQ-520 PAR 
sensor with immersion correction settings and then adjusted to achieve PAR levels as close to the target 
treatment values as possible.

2.2.2 Hyperspectral Imaging Setup 
The camera used in this study, provided by NewTec A/S (Odense, Denmark), was a computed tomography 
imaging spectrometer (CTIS) camera, also known as a snapshot hyperspectral camera. It measures spectra 
in a wavelength range of 600 - 850 nm with 45 spectral channels at a spectral resolution of 1.72 nm to 
produce a three-dimensional (2D + λ) hyperspectral data cube of an object. (Qtec, n.d.)  
When capturing CTIS images of the macroalgae it is important to maintain a consistent set up to minimize 
the influence of external variables. With this in mind, the placement and angles of the camera, lighting, and 
sample were measured and marked to keep uniformity across all samples taken throughout the experiment.

In hyperspectral imaging, image quality is very dependent on the environmental lighting and the lighting 
system, which must provide continuous, broadband illumination across the spectral range of interest. Unlike 
LEDs, which emit light in narrow, discrete spectral bands, halogen lamps emit a continuous spectrum 
spanning the visible to near-infrared (NIR) regions (Specim, n.d.). Typically, in imaging setups the light 
source would be placed next to the camera, directed towards the subject. In this experiment’s setup, the 
halogen lamp (Soleo E27 40 W, 230 V, 2700 K) was mounted through a cutout in the top panel of the 
photobooth, 15 cm above the water level in the beaker. This top-down illumination of the sample reduces 
glare and direct reflections from the curved glass surface. However, it should be noted that this positioning 
may increase the likelihood of light scattering into the camera lens, potentially influencing reflectance 
measurements. With this in mind, the chosen lighting configuration and use of a glass beaker provided the 
best balance between minimizing reflection artifacts and having realistic and reproducible imaging 
conditions for samples in water.

2.6 Data Analysis 
2.6.1 Hyperspectral Data Cube Reconstruction  
The CTIS hyperspectral camera (NewTec, Denmark) used in this study captures a 2D projection composed 
of multiple diffraction orders, which must be computationally reconstructed into a 3D hyperspectral data 
cube to retrieve its full spatial-spectral information. This reconstruction process is important because each 
diffraction order represents a unique angular view or projection of the CTIS image, encoding different 
combinations of spatial and spectral data. 
The central (zero-order) projection keeps the full spatial resolution but lacks spectral separation. The 
surrounding first-order diffractions introduce spectral dispersion, but distort the spatial features. Fig. 9 
below illustrates how spectral data is distributed spatially across projections in a CTIS image.  
 
Fig. 9 Example of a CTIS image of a pattern with line targets. This demonstrates how spatial and spectral information 
are distributed across projections, with 1st order projections that display spectral dispersion in different directions. 
==PAGE:39==
36 
 
In each first-order diffraction, the line pattern that appears in focus indicates the spatial direction that the 
spectral data is being dispersed in. The blurred lines represent spatial features that are spread across 
wavelengths and lose clarity. These directional differences are key to the reconstruction process, as they 
allow the overlapping spatial and spectral signals to be disentangled and recombined into a coherent 
hyperspectral data cube. The horizontal and vertical first-order diffractions give the most critical 
information, while corner diffractions give additional data that contributes to an even more accurate 
reconstruction. However, the information in the corner diffractions is usually limited, and increasing the 
exposure to capture more spectral information quickly overexposes the zero order image, which should be 
avoided.   
Reconstructing the hyperspectral data cube from these projections is computationally complex and has been 
extensively studied. Two reconstruction approaches were available in the camera’s built in processing 
system: Expectation-Maximization (EM) and a convolutional neural-network (CNN) using a U-Net model. 
EM is a traditional “manual” method that estimates the most likely hyperspectral cube by maximizing the 
probability of observing the given projections, but it requires a lot of computational power and is often 
slower. The U-Net model on the other hand, offers a faster and more accurate reconstruction, making it the 
preferred option for this study. (Peters, 2024) 
2.6.2 Reflectance Correction  
To ensure accurate spectral analysis, all hyperspectral data cubes were corrected using a reflectance model 
based on black and white references. This correction is considered a crucial pre-processing step in 
hyperspectral imaging, as it accounts for pixel intensity variations caused by both external and internal 
factors. External factors include uneven illumination across the FOV or changes in lighting conditions 
during sampling. Internal factors may come from the sensor, such as variations in pixel response or the 
presence of defective pixels. (Qtec, n.d.)  
Reflectance was calculated using equation 2 (Qtec, n.d.): 
Reflectance = 
input - black reference
white reference - black reference    (eq. 2) 
        
The black reference is first subtracted from both the input (sample) and white reference (all in .ppm file 
format), which were then reconstructed into hyperspectral data cubes. Finally, the reflectance was obtained 
by dividing the corrected input cube by the corrected white reference cube.  
The black reference is used to account for sensor noise and identify “hot pixels”, a type of defective pixel 
that appears abnormally bright in dark images. A hot pixel can be seen on the left side of the CTIS image 
==PAGE:40==
37 
 
in Fig. 9. Black references only need to be acquired when there are changes in exposure time, image size 
(cropping), or ambient temperature. The white reference is used to characterize the illumination profile and 
the sensor’s spectral response to it. It corrects for spatial and spectral non-uniformities introduced by the 
light source and compensates for inconsistencies in pixel sensitivity across the sensor. It also helps identify 
“dead pixels”, which appear as abnormally dark pixels in a bright image. White references are usually 
acquired whenever changes are made to the exposure time or lighting conditions of the imaging setup. By 
applying these corrections, the resulting data is presented as absolute reflectance rather than relative 
intensity, allowing for more reliable comparisons across samples and time points. (Qtec, n.d.) 
2.6.3 Extraction of Mean Algae Reflectance Spectra 
To isolate the spectral data of the A. armata, i.e., differentiate algae pixels from the background of the 
hyperspectral data cube, a segmentation mask and K-means clustering were tested. Segmentation was 
performed using Otsu’s thresholding method applied to the mean intensity of the first 15 spectral channels 
(Python 3.12.7). This approach gives a robust binary mask that separates the algal biomass from the higher-
intensity background. K-means clustering was tested as a method to further refine the identification of algae 
pixels (Python 3.12.7). The clustering was applied to algae pixels identified by the segmentation mask, 
using their reflectance spectra as input. Three to five clusters were compared to determine which number 
could effectively isolate algal structures from blurry and ambiguous edge pixels. The scripts were provided 
by NewTec (Odense, Denmark). The mean algae reflectance spectra for each hyperspectral data cube was 
then calculated by averaging the reflectance values of all segmented algae pixels across the 45 spectral 
channels. 
Otsu’s method was done with the threshold_otsu function in the scikit-image package (van der 
Walt et al, 2014). K-means clustering was done with the Kmeans function from the scikit-learn 
package (Pedregosa et al, 2011). 
2.6.4 Partial Least Squares (PLS) Regression Model  
A partial least squares (PLS) regression model was used for developing a biomass estimation model as it is 
well-suited for modeling relationships where there are many correlated explanatory variables. In the case 
of spectral data, the explanatory variables are the reflectance values from the 45 spectral channels, while 
the response variable is biomass density. A script for training a PLS regression model for biomass estimation 
using the segmented mean algae reflectance was provided by NewTec (Odense, Denmark).  
Outliers were first removed using the Mahalanobis distance technique, and samples above the 95th 
percentile were excluded. The remaining data was then split into 80% for training and 20% for testing. 
Different numbers of PLS latent components were tested by calculating the cross-validated root mean 
==PAGE:41==
38 
 
squared error (RMSE) (prediction error) to identify the optimal number of components that balance model 
performance without overfitting. The number of components with the lowest RMSE value was selected, 
and the model was fitted accordingly. The model’s performance was then evaluated using R², which 
indicates how well the model explains variance in the data (with 1 representing a perfect fit), and RMSE. 
Coefficients of the selected model were then examined to identify which wavelengths had a positive or 
negative influence on biomass prediction.  
From the scikit-learn package (Pedregosa et al, 2011), PLS regression was done using the 
PLSRegression function and the dataset was split using the train_test_split function. Prior to 
model fitting, outliers were removed using mahalanobis from SciPy (Virtanen et al, 2020). 
2.6.6 Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM) 
In this study, a GLMM was applied to statistically evaluate how explanatory variables (stress treatment, 
time, and spectral channel) influenced changes in the reflectance spectra of A. armata. Generalized linear 
mixed models (GLMM) extend linear models by incorporating both fixed and random effects, allowing for 
the analysis of complex, hierarchical data structures. Data exploration was done following the protocol 
described by (Zuur et al., 2010).  
GLMM was specifically chosen to account for known sources of non-independence in the experimental 
design. The experiment was conducted in an industry setup, where multiple reflectance HSI measurements 
were taken over time from the same replicate tanks. These repeated measurements from the same replicates 
violate the assumption of independence, and the values are likely to be correlated. GLMM is known to be 
a robust solution to dealing with this pseudoreplication (Bolker et al., 2009; Zuur et al., 2010). Additionally, 
the experimental system included two shelves with separate temperature regulation systems, introducing 
the potential for shelf-specific random variation. Therefore, random effects for replicates and shelves were 
considered during model selection. GLMM was also appropriate due to its capacity to handle various 
distributions for the response variable. Reflectance is continuous data usually bound between 0 and 1 and 
potentially skewed. In this case, models were fitted using a Gaussian distribution with an identity link 
function, which was deemed appropriate for the measured reflectance values ranging between 0.4 and 1.03 
and approximately symmetric, but the GLMM framework would also allow for alternative distributions like 
Beta (strictly bounded between 0 - 1) or Gamma (positive continuous with values >1) if needed.   
To reduce the influence of the temporal autocorrelation and improve the independence of observations, only 
measurements from Day 3 and Day 7 were included in the model. This also helps minimize potential 
confounding effects from initial acclimation responses to the new experimental environment. In addition, 
hyperspectral data inherently exhibits spectral autocorrelation, as reflectance values at adjacent 
==PAGE:42==
39 
 
wavelengths are highly correlated. To avoid violating the assumption of independent predictors, only select 
spectral channels were used as explanatory variables. Selection of these channels was done by calculating 
the average reflectance and standard deviation for each spectral channel using only the hyperspectral data 
cubes at the highest biomass density (3.0 g FW/L). This biomass density was chosen for two key reasons. 
Most importantly, because the purpose of this analysis was to look at spectral changes caused by algae 
stress, variability caused by different biomass densities would have been irrelevant. Additionally, the 
highest biomass density provided the most reliable data, as lower biomass densities had a higher risk of 
being an outlier due to the limited number of algae clumps potentially being outside the camera’s FOV 
during image capture. For consistency across the model, only data from this highest biomass density (3.0 g 
FW/L) was included in the final dataset used for analysis. Spectral channels were then ranked by their 
standard deviation values to identify the most stable and most variable wavelengths. This dimensionality 
reduction preserved meaningful spectral information, while addressing the non-independence of 
wavelengths. The fixed covariates are therefore Treatment (categorical with four levels), Day (categorical 
with 2 levels), and Channel (categorical with three levels), with all possible interaction terms (i.e., two-way 
and three-way interactions) included in the full model. To incorporate the dependency among nested 
observations, Replicate and Shelf were tested as random group intercepts and Shelf/Replicate as a nested 
random effects, which are assumed to be normally distributed with mean 0 and variance σ2. 
Parameter estimation in mixed models is usually done using Maximum Likelihood (ML), which estimates 
both fixed and random effects by maximizing the full likelihood of the data. This process involves 
integrating likelihoods over all possible values of the random effects, which can be challenging for GLMM. 
A limitation of ML is that it treats fixed effects as if they are known with certainty when estimating variance 
components (i.e., the variability due to random effects), potentially leading to underestimated random effect 
variability and overconfidence in fixed effects estimates. An alternative approach is Restricted Maximum 
Likelihood (REML), which maximizes the likelihood of a transformed set of residuals that are independent 
of the fixed effects, and can therefore account for uncertainty in fixed effects and provides more accurate 
variance estimates (Bolker et al., 2009). Model selection was carried out in two stages. First, different 
random effects structures were compared using the full fixed effects model, which included all three-way 
interactions between Treatment, Day, and Channel. This step was performed using REML. Once the optimal 
random effects structure was selected, model selection for the fixed effects was carried out using ML. 
REML was then used to estimate the variance components of the final selected model.  
Model comparisons were based on Akaike Information Criterion corrected for smaller sample sizes (AICc) 
(Akaike, 1998). AIC is generally preferred over p-values in GLMM because it evaluates the model as a 
whole without relying on uncertain degrees of freedom for individual parameters, making it more robust in 
==PAGE:43==
40 
 
complex hierarchical settings (Bolker et al., 2009). Competing models were evaluated using the difference 
in AICc (∆AICc) relative to the best-fitting model. Models with ∆AICc ≤ 2 were considered equally 
plausible, and the simpler model was preferred in accordance with the principle of parsimony.  
For validation of the final selected model, model residuals were visually inspected using Q-Q plots and 
residuals versus fitted values plots to assess normality and homoscedasticity.  
GLMMs were fitted using the glmmTMB (Brooks et al., 2017) package in R v4.4.3. Model selection was 
done using the AIC function from base R (R Core Team, 2025) for random effects, and the dredge 
function from MuMIn (Barton, 2025) for fixed effects. Residual diagnostics were conducted using DHARMa 
(Hartig, 2022). Interaction effects were visualized using effects (Fox & Weisberg, 2019). 
2.6.5 Principal Component Analysis (PCA) 
Principal Component Analysis (PCA) was also performed to explore patterns in spectral variation across 
treatments and sampling days, and to compare which wavelengths contributed most to the spectral variation 
compared to those identified as influential in the PLS regression. Specifically, PCA was used to reduce the 
dimensionality of the dataset while preserving as much of the variation as possible. This allowed for the 
clustering or separation of treatments and the identification of the two principal components most 
responsible for the variability in the data. The spectral matrix (reflectance and wavelength) was 
standardized using z-score normalization, which centers each variable and scales it to unit variance. This 
step is essential in PCA when variables (in this case reflectance at different wavelengths) are on the same 
scale but may vary in magnitude, ensuring that all wavelengths contribute equally to the principal 
components. The number of principal components used was determined based on the total variance 
explained, with a threshold of 80%. The five spectral channels with the highest absolute loadings for each 
principal component were extracted to identify the wavelengths contributing most strongly to the variance. 
PCA assumes linear relationships between variables, and that principle components capture meaningful 
structure in the data. It does not account for class labels like treatment and biomass density, making it an 
unsupervised method that is primarily exploratory.  
Z-score normalization was done using the base R scale function, and PCA was performed using the base 
R prcomp function with centering and scaling enabled. (R Core Team, 2025) The package factoextra 
(Kassambara & Mundt, 2020) was used to make a scree plot.

3.1.1 Visual Observations 
Visual observations of Asparagopsis spp. will always be a valuable tool in generally assessing the state of 
algae health, especially for those experienced in the industry. On Day 3 of the experiment, the A. armata in 
the control treatment appeared to be declining in health compared to the start of the trial. It was believed 
that this was due to the stress of being centrifuged for three days in a row. As a result, imaging was put on 
hold to minimize further stress. On Day 7 all treatments were sampled again, and the experiment was

had occurred off the coast of Hirtshals, Denmark at the time of the experiment setup (March 31st, 2025) and

Based on visual observation the control and low light treatments maintained their red pigmentation 
throughout the experiment, while the high light and nutrient depleted treatments experienced pigment

Green algae contamination in Asparagopsis spp. cultures is a major concern, as they are quickly able to 
outcompete the red algae under certain conditions. Their early detection is vital to preventing losses in 
target species biomass and minimizing time-consuming cleanup. While contamination may be visually 
detectable in transparent photobioreactors (PBRs), this becomes more challenging if opaque cost-effective 
materials are used. A quantifiable method for detecting contamination is therefore preferable to relying 
solely on visual inspection.

3.2 Spectral Data Extraction 
The results of testing a segmentation mask and K-means clustering on the hyperspectral data cubes to isolate 
algae spectral pixels from the background, found that using four clusters successfully isolated distinct algal 
structures and ambiguous pixels into separate clusters. However, since clustering was done independently 
on each hyperspectral cube, the resulting cluster labels were not consistent. It was deemed impractical to 
exclude specific clusters without looking at each data cube individually. Fig. 14 below shows a visualization 
of what the segmentation mask and clustering look like when applied to a hyperspectral data cube, with 
more examples available in Appendix D: Data Analysis. 
 
Fig. 14 From left to right: mean image of the cube, the resulting black and white image from an Otsu’s method 
segmentation mask, and the K-means clustering map with algae pixels from the segmentation mask divided into 4

While clustering maps may be useful for more accurately extracting algae pixels when just looking at a few 
samples, this method was not used for the final extraction of mean reflectance intensity for this study. 
Instead, the binary segmentation mask was chosen to be the most accurate for this purpose.  
Reflectance data from the segmented algae pixels can be plotted to examine key features, including shifts 
in intensity and spectral shape across the visible (600 - 700 nm) and near-infrared (700 - 850 nm) regions 
captured by the hyperspectral camera (NewTec, Denmark). Lower reflectance typically suggests greater 
light absorption, which is often associated with higher pigment concentrations, greater biological activity, 
or denser biomass (Gitelson et al., 1999). Therefore, higher reflectance may indicate pigment degradation, 
reduced physiological activity, or cellular breakdown, resulting in less light being absorbed. The visible 
light region (400 - 700 nm) generally shows lower reflectance due to strong absorption by photosynthetic 
pigments (Gitelson et al., 1999; Hu, 2022). The NIR region (700 - 2500 nm) usually exhibits higher 
reflectance, as light in this range is weakly absorbed by pigments and instead scattered by the structural 
==PAGE:53==
50 
 
complexity of algal tissue (Hu, 2022). Some variability in the spectral data is expected due to a limited 
number of measurements, which was a constraint of the experimental timeframe and should be considered 
when interpreting the results.

biomass density. Here the variation in spectra due to biomass density is clear.

in the visible light range. Overall, being able to see varying spectral characteristics across biomass densities

The PLS regression model estimates biomass based on the spectral characteristics of the segmented algae 
pixels, rather than relying on 2D area coverage in the image. The segmented mean algae reflectance spectra 
derived from each hyperspectral data cube captures differences in how the light interacts with the algae, 
whether it is dense or sparse. Denser or more layered algae absorbs and reflects light differently than sparse 
algae, leading to changes in the spectral signature. For example, dense algae can cause stronger back 
scattering, more self-shading, and greater light attenuation, which results in lower overall reflectance. In 
contrast, sparse algae produces brighter spectral signatures without the layers of biomass present to absorb 
the light. These optical differences can be detected in the spectral data, allowing the PLS model to associate 
specific reflectance patterns with varying biomass densities, even if the 2D area coverage of algae were to 
remain similar.

3.3.2 Principal Component Analysis (PCA) 
The PCA performed on spectral data across all biomass densities revealed components comparable with 
the key wavelengths identified by the PLS regression model. It was found that PC1 and PC2 explain 63.8% 
and 30.7% of the total variance, respectively, accounting for 94.5% of the variability in the spectral data. 
The loadings showed that PC1 was mainly influenced by reflectance in the red-edge region with the top 5 
channels being 702 - 730 nm, while PC2 was explained by variation in the NIR region with the top 5 
channels being 815 - 844 nm (Fig. 21). These findings corroborate the PLS regression results and further 
support the conclusion that wavelengths in the red-edge and NIR region are among the most influential for 
biomass estimation in Asparagopsis spp. See PCA score plots in Appendix D: Data Analysis.

3.4 Algal Health and Contamination Monitoring 
In order to investigate physiological stress related to pigment content, as well as the presence of 
contamination, specific channels were selected for analysis based on their sensitivity to changes in algal 
condition.  
For the selection of spectral channels, as described in 2.6.6 Generalized Linear Mixed Model (GLMM), the 
high light treatment data on Day 7 was first excluded due to the significant contamination, as the focus was 
to characterize the spectral features of A. armata specifically. The results of ranking spectral channels across 
all replicates by their reflectance standard deviation, found that channels in the NIR range were the most 
stable, while the greatest variability was observed in the visible light range between 600 - 675 nm. The most 
stable was channel 44 (844 - 850 nm), which is typically insensitive to changes in physiological stress or 
pigment concentration since it is primarily influenced by structural components of biological tissue and 
scattering (Hu, 2022). The most variable was channel 4 (606 - 611 nm), which falls within the expected 
absorption range for phycocyanin in red algae (610 - 660 nm), consistent with literature (Nobel, 2020). This 
supports the hypothesis that there are photosynthetic pigments in A. armata that are particularly sensitive 
to stress and exhibit noticeable spectral shifts in response. The next most stable and variable channels were 
all located immediately adjacent to these key wavelengths, differing only slightly in standard deviation.  
To explore how contamination could be detected spectrally, the previously excluded Day 7 high light 
treatment was analyzed independently to find channels that set it apart from the non-contaminated spectra. 
It was found that the channel with the lowest reflectance (i.e. highest absorbance) was channel 11                
(657 - 663 nm), which corresponds with what is typically associated with chlorophyll-a absorption (Hu, 
2022; Morgado et al., 2024). This increase in chlorophyll-associated absorption is to be expected with the 
presence of contaminating green algae, distinguishing its spectral signature from that of A. armata.

Nesting Replicate within Shelf resulted in a slightly higher AICc (∆AICc = 2), making it plausible but adds 
complexity without a clear benefit. The model including only Shelf as a random effect failed to converge, 
indicating that this grouping structure may not capture meaningful variation in the data. This, along with 
the minimal improvement in fit from including Shelf, suggests the difference in temperature between 
shelves contributed little to the overall variability in reflectance.  
Among the models with ∆AICc ≤ 2, the version including Replicate alone was selected for its simplicity 
(parsimony). Including Replicate as a random effect is also important because it best reflects the structure 
of the experimental design. Repeated measurements were taken from individual replicate tanks, each of 
which may exhibit unique random sources of variation such as differences in handling, microenvironmental 
conditions, or biological variability.

On Day 3, all stress treatments significantly affected reflectance compared to the control. Specifically, the 
nutrient depleted treatment resulted in a higher reflectance, while both the low light and high light 
treatments showed significantly lower reflectance. These results suggest that the low and high light 
treatments initially (Days 1-3) increased their pigment concentrations in response to the altered irradiance, 
while pigment bleaching under nutrient depletion contributed to elevated reflectance. In the high light 
treatment, the sudden exposure to high irradiance may have triggered an upregulation of pigment synthesis 
to increase photosynthetic capacity or dissipate excess energy to prevent photooxidative damage (Murchie 
& Lawson, 2013; Vaquero et al., 2014). However, this response cannot be sustained over time, as prolonged 
high light exposure eventually leads to photoinhibition and degradation of photosynthetic pigments and 
photosystems. Under low light conditions, algae often responds by increasing pigment concentration to 
improve their light-harvesting efficiency (Vaquero et al., 2014). This adaptive response is possible in the 
short term using internal nutrient reserves. However, without enough energy for active nutrient uptake, 
pigment synthesis becomes constrained, limiting the algae to maintaining rather than further increasing 
pigment levels. This limitation likely explains the relatively stable pigment channel reflectance.  
The interaction between Treatment and Day revealed that pigment reflectance in the nutrient depleted 
treatment increased over time (pigment bleaching), while the high light treatment decreased over time 
(contamination). The control also decreased over time from a combination of improved physiological 
condition and contamination. In contrast, the effect of the low light treatment remained relatively stable 
over time, with overlapping 95% confidence intervals in all spectral channels suggesting no significant 
time-related changes. 
Treatment effects on RRed (chlorophyll-a) did not differ significantly from those on R606 (phycocyanin), 
reflecting their similar stress responses that is characteristic of pigment-related spectral channels. The RNIR 
channel was significantly different with a higher reflectance, which can be attributed to increased light 
scattering in the NIR range compared to the visible spectrum. While the interaction between treatment and 
time (days) clearly affected the pigment-sensitive channels (R606 and RRed), the NIR response stayed stable 
across treatments with the exception of the increase in reflectance for the high light treatment at Day 7, 
caused by increased scattering of the high cell density of microalgae.

The NDPI and NDVI indices showed similar trends across treatments. In the nutrient depleted treatment, 
both indices decreased over time, which is consistent with visually observed of pigment bleaching likely 
caused by nitrogen limitation. The low light treatment stayed relatively stable, suggesting the algae 
maintained a healthy level of pigment content despite having no biomass growth and nutrient uptake. Since 
the low light treatment both maintained its pigments and remained free of visual signs of contamination, its 
index values likely represent the closest approximation of A. armata in its unstressed state in this study. The 
high light treatment remained stable and then exhibited a notable increase in both indices between Days 3 
and 7. While this trend in the NDVI is explained by the contamination, the increase in NDPI is unexpected. 
A. armata appeared visibly bleached by this point, and green algae does not contain phycocyanin. This 
seemingly elevated absorbance in the phycocyanin-associated channel (R606) is likely caused by the strong 
absorbance of chlorophyll-a “overpowering” and masking the phycocyanin signal. Like the high light 
treatment, the control also showed increasing NDPI and NDVI from Days 3-7, likely due to the partial 
contamination. Without contamination, the control would have likely followed the same stable trend as the 
low light treatment.

Given the confounding influence of contamination on NDPI and NDVI, NDPRI becomes a very useful tool, 
as it can capture the relative relationship between phycocyanin and chlorophyll-a reflectance. When 
compared with reflectance data, NDPRI may help indicate green algae contamination (ratio approaching 0, 
with low reflectance in the 600 - 700 nm range) and pigment bleaching (ratio approaching 0, but with high 
reflectance in the same range). Both the high light treatment and nutrient depleted treatments showed a 
decline in NDPRI after Day 3, though the magnitude was more pronounced in the high light treatment. The 
decrease in NDPRI for the nutrient depleted treatment was consistent with the spectral “flattening” observed 
at Day 7 (Fig. 16).  
The analysis of NDIs across treatments provided a basis for estimating optimal index values and thresholds 
indicative of algal stress or contamination. What might be considered “healthy” or optimal values for NDPI 
and NDVI were estimated as the mean of the control and low light treatment values on Days 1-3, before 
the onset of significant green algae contamination. This approach represents a midpoint between the mildly 
stressed control and the low light treatment that remained stable but had elevated pigment content as part 
of its acclimation response. NDPRI was the most stable in the low light treatment, making its mean for the 
duration of the experiment the most suitable reference for an uncontaminated pigment ratio. Based on this, 
the optimal index values are: NDPI at 0.14 ± 0.03 and NDVI at 0.23 ± 0.03, both representing healthy, 
uncontaminated pigment levels where deviations may indicate pigment bleaching or contamination. The 
NDPRI was estimated to be optimal at 0.10 ± 0.02, with values below this threshold potentially indicating 
contamination or pigment bleaching, depending on whether reflectance in the R606 and RRed channels is low 
(~ 0.4 - 0.5) or high (~ 0.8 - 0.9). 
Since the stability of RNIR must be confirmed before it can reliably be used as a reference channel, the 
recommended use of these indices is that NDPRI be assessed first to identify potential contamination, as 
this is the main factor that has been shown to cause a significant increase in NIR. Further confirmation can 
be obtained by looking at the RNIR reflectance directly, with values exceeding 0.85 indicating high levels of 
contamination. An example of which can be seen in Fig. 22 at the high light treatment on Day 7. If there 
are no signs of high contamination, NDPI and NDVI can then be used to assess pigment content and infer 
the physiological condition of A. armata. Among the pigments, the phycocyanin content was the most 
species-relevant indicator of health, although chlorophyll-a content remains a valuable source of supporting 
information. Overall, each of these indices give important insight into algae health and green algae 
contamination levels, and together have strong potential for real-time monitoring and process control.

3.5 Key Findings 
A PLS regression model was able to accurately estimate biomass density (R2 = 0.906, RMSE = 0.234 g 
FW/L) across various physiological states of algae that were confirmed to have affected the spectra that the 
model is based on. Accuracy is lost with high levels of contamination (NDVI > 0.23 and NDPRI < 0.1), but 
this should not be a common occurrence in production. Spectral regions with the most influence on the 
model, based on regression coefficients and on the loadings of PCA principal components (PC1 and PC2), 
were in the red-edge and NIR regions (700 - 850 nm). These findings support the hypothesis that 
hyperspectral imaging can be used to develop accurate and robust biomass prediction models for 
Asparagopsis spp., independent of variations in photosynthetic pigment reflectance caused by algal health. 
The responses of A. armata to the stress treatments were clearly reflected in the temporal trends of the 
reflectance indices, the GLMM results, and in the NDIs. The GLMM applied to the segmented mean algae 
reflectance data confirmed that reflectance was significantly influenced by the interaction of treatment, 
time, and spectral channel. The GLMM results and the trends of the NDPI and NDVI indices confirmed 
that reflectance in the R606 (phycocyanin) and RRed (chlorophyll-a) channels respond similarly and 
sensitively to environmental stressors. The NDPRI builds on this relationship, highlighting differences 
between the two pigments to distinguish between healthy pigment ratios and those indicative of bleaching 
or contamination. The high light treatment showed a significant decrease in reflectance due to green algae 
contamination, which also elevated NDPI and NDVI values (0.38 and 0.43 respectively). The green algae 
contamination had confounded the spectral data, as the strong chlorophyll-a absorbance of green algae 
disproportionately affected the visible and red-edge light regions, as represented by the low NDPRI value 
of 0.055. In the control treatment there were initial signs of stress due to a combination of centrifugation 
and handling stress, as well as an initial acclimation response. By Day 7 there was visually observed 
contamination that likely influenced the spectral signatures of the control. The low light treatment did not 
show significant changes in reflectance or pigment content over time, suggesting that although growth and 
nutrient uptake were minimal, the algae was not stressed. As such, the low light treatment is the most 
reliable reference for healthy spectral and pigment values in this study. The nutrient depleted treatment 
resulted in a gradual increase in reflectance in the pigment-sensitive channels R606 (phycocyanin) and RRed 
(chlorophyll-a) over time, indicating pigment bleaching. This was reflected in the low values of the NDPI 
(0.03 ± 0.03), NDVI (0.11 ± 0.03), and NDPRI (0.08 ± 0.02). Collectively, these findings support the 
hypothesis that photosynthetic pigment content in Asparagopsis spp. are sensitive to physiological stress 
and show detectable spectral shifts, and can be used to detect and monitor physiological responses to stress-
inducing environmental conditions.

4. Conclusion 
This study set out to evaluate the use of hyperspectral imaging (HSI) for monitoring Asparagopsis spp. in 
controlled cultivation environments. The findings support both hypotheses, demonstrating that HSI can be 
used to (1) develop a robust and accurate model for estimating biomass density independent of 
physiological condition, and (2) detect physiological responses to stress-inducing environmental conditions 
through changes in pigment-related spectral reflectance. The ability to estimate biomass using non-invasive 
hyperspectral data is a significant step forward for monitoring biomass growth throughout production, 
rather than relying solely on initial and final measurements. Additionally, the sensitivity of pigment-related 
spectral features to nutrient depletion and light stress confirms HSI’s value in tracking algal health in real 
time. Together, these applications make HSI a powerful tool for process control in Asparagopsis cultivation. 
By allowing corrective actions to be taken before a batch is compromised, this monitoring of several 
parameters can improve yield consistency, reduce biomass losses, and increase operational efficiency in 
large-scale production systems. 
 
5. Future Research 
One of the primary challenges in applying hyperspectral imaging (HSI) at scale is its high cost, largely due 
to the number of spectral filters required. A more cost-effective alternative is a multispectral camera, which 
uses fewer spectral channels and therefore requires fewer filters. For exploratory studies like this one, the 
full continuous range of the hyperspectral camera has proved essential for accurately identifying which 
spectral bands are most strongly correlated with various parameters. These findings can then inform future 
in-system trials, to assess whether a limited set of spectral channels is sufficient for routine monitoring in 
production settings. 
Regarding biomass estimation, the predictive model developed in this study should be strengthened by 
training it on a larger and more diverse dataset, including samples collected directly from operational 
production systems. This would ensure the model is exposed to the full range of environmental variability 
and cultivation conditions encountered in real-world settings. Importantly, hyperspectral biomass 
estimation offers a non-destructive alternative to traditional centrifugation and drying methods, which this 
study has shown to cause physiological stress. In future experiments focused on optimizing cultivation 
conditions, this biomass estimation method could streamline data collection and reduce handling stress on 
the algae, a potential confounding variable in physiological response measurements. Additionally, 
==PAGE:70==
67 
 
reflectance indices have the potential to be used to calculate the ratio of Asparagopsis spp. to contamination 
in a sample, which can then be applied to the biomass estimation model in order to improve its accuracy. 
For algal health monitoring, future work should focus on correlating reflectance indices (ie. NDPI and 
NDVI) with chemically quantified pigment concentrations. This would enable the development of more 
precise thresholds for physiological stress, including the point at which algal health has deteriorated beyond 
recovery, and would allow for direct quantification of pigment content from spectral data alone. Given that 
phycocyanin and chlorophyll-a are pigments with potential commercial value, expanding the monitoring 
system to quantify them directly could be an additional use for the hyperspectral camera. To support this, 
future hyperspectral systems should consider including the absorption range for phycoerythrin                 
(~540 - 570 nm), the most abundant red pigment in Asparagopsis spp.  
Due to the confounding effects of green algae contamination on the reflectance spectra of Asparagopsis 
spp., minimizing contamination in the system as much as possible is essential to achieve precise and 
sensitive monitoring of algae health and biomass. In order to do this, spectral analysis is needed to find the 
threshold at which contamination starts to significantly distort spectral signals. It will also be necessary to 
generate a calibration curve linking NDVI, NDPI, and NDPRI to known contamination levels, and to 
establish thresholds for when contamination requires immediate action. Quantifying a specific 
contamination level at which inflow should be shut off or the Asparagopsis spp. harvested before product 
quality worsens would be beneficial for process control.  
This study showed that even at low light conditions (16 PAR), A. armata was able to maintain a stable 
concentration of pigments with minimal green algae contamination. Although the light intensity needs to 
be increased to reach the biomass growth necessary for production, it is at the very least useful to be aware 
that the A. armata isn’t stressed at low light levels and can survive with limited light and nutrients. This 
could be especially advantageous during periods of system downtime or when energy efficiency is a 
priority. Using both the biomass estimation model and contamination level characterization approaches 
from this study would help to find a balance in optimizing biomass growth, contamination, and energy 
consumption. Furthermore, the observed trend of increased pigment concentration under low light suggests 
a possible strategy of briefly applying low light conditions before harvest to boost pigment yields. 
Hyperspectral monitoring would then be able to track pigment content to verify this effect and determine 
optimal timing.  
In summary, the findings from this study open several promising avenues for applied research and 
technological development. With further refinement, hyperspectral imaging could become an essential 
component of real-time monitoring and process control systems in Asparagopsis cultivation.
