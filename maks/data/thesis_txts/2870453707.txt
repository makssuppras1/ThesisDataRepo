ABSTRACT
The launch of JWST marks a turning point in the exploration of habitable worlds beyond
our Solar System.
With its powerful instruments, humanity now has the opportunity to
reveal the conditions on some of the billions of Earth-sized planets in our galaxy. This
thesis aims to characterize the atmospheric and surface properties of TRAPPIST-1 b by
measuring its dayside thermal emission using secondary eclipse photometric observations
from JWST/MIRI. The dataset includes five secondary eclipses at 15 µm and four at 12.8 µm,
yielding eclipse depths of 709 ± 59 ppm and 475 ± 57 ppm, respectively. These values
are in 1 𝜎agreement with previously published results by Ducrot et al. (2024) and Greene
et al. (2023). The inferred brightness temperatures are inconsistent with a bare-rock model
assuming zero albedo and no heat redistribution. However, they align well with the best-fit
scenarios proposed by Ducrot et al., suggesting that TRAPPIST-1 b may either be a bare-rock
planet with an ultramafic surface or host a CO2-rich atmosphere with photochemical hazes
that lead to atmospheric thermal inversion. Overall, this work highlights TRAPPIST-1 b
as a key target in the broader effort to understand the diversity and potential habitability of
terrestrial exoplanets.

NOMENCLATURE
Exoplanet. A planet outside the Solar System.
Habitable Zone (HZ). The orbital region around a star where conditions may allow liquid
water to exist on a planet’s surface.
Lightcurve. A graph representing the variation of brightness of an object over time.
M dwarf. The smallest and coolest main-sequence stars (spectral type M), characterized by
low luminosity, long lifetimes, and frequent stellar activity.
Mid-Infrared. The region of the electromagnetic spectrum with wavelengths with a range
of approximately 5- 25 5 µm.
Near-Infrared. The region of the electromagnetic spectrum with wavelengths with a range
of approximately 0.7- 5 5 µm.
Photometry. A technique that measures the intensity of light from celestial objects over time
across a specific wavelength band.
Pre-main Sequence Phase. An unstable period before a star enters its main sequence that
is characterized by intense radiation.
Secondary Eclipse. The event in a transiting exoplanet system when the planet passes behind
its host star, obscuring the light contribution of the planet.
Semimajor Axis. The longest radius of an elliptical orbit, representing the average distance
between a celestial body and the object it orbits.
Spectroscopy. A technique that analyzes the dispersion of light from celestial objects into its
component wavelengths, revealing information about their composition, temperature,
motion, and atmospheric properties.
Stellar Facula. A bright, magnetically active region on the surface of a star that emits more
radiation than the surrounding photosphere.
Stellar Flare. A sudden burst of energy from a star’s surface caused by magnetic reconnec-
tion, emitting radiation across the electromagnetic spectrum.
Stellar Spot. A darker, cooler region on the surface of a star caused by strong magnetic
activity.
Terrestrial Planet. A planet composed primarily of silicate, rocks or metals.
Transit. The passage of a celestial body in front of its host star as seen from an observer’s
perspective, causing a temporary dip in the star’s brightness.
Ultra-cool Dwarf (UCD). An M-dwarf with an effective temperature of less than ∼3000 K.

INTRODUCTION
1.1
Exoplanet Discovery and Demographics
Since the discovery of the first exoplanet, 51 Peg b, almost three decades ago (Mayor
et al., 1995), the study of extrasolar worlds has become a central focus of astronomical
research and has transformed humanity’s understanding of the universe and our place within
it. According to the Exoplanet Archive of NASA, at the time of writing, more than 5800
planets have been discovered, exhibiting a huge diversity in size and characteristics (https:
//exoplanetarchive.ipac.caltech.edu/). These discoveries include gas giants larger
than Jupiter, terrestrial planets smaller than Earth, and planets covering the size range in
between, most often without an equivalent in our Solar System. The extreme diversity of
exoplanet types is usually described in the literature based on size (e.g., Earth-sized, Jupiter-
sized), mass (e.g., Super-Earth) or composition and stellar flux (e.g., hot Jupiter, hot rocky
planet).
Multiple observational techniques are used for the discovery of exoplanets (Wei, 2018), with
the vast majority (Kaushik et al., 2024) of currently known planets having been detected via
the transit and radial velocity (RV) methods. The transit method identifies exoplanets by
measuring the temporary dimming of a star’s light as a planet passes in front of it, provided
the orbit is aligned with the line of sight of the telescope. In contrast, the RV method detects
exoplanets by observing the Doppler shift in the stellar light, caused by the gravitational
influence of an orbiting planet. Transits determine the approximate radius of a planet, while
RV measurements constrain its mass within a range. Combining these methods allows for the
inference of planetary densities and, consequently, their internal bulk compositions (Eisner
et al., 2021).
Both techniques are biased toward the detection of giant planets, as their larger masses and
radii result in a higher signal-to-noise ratio (SNR). In the case of transits, larger planets
obscure a greater portion of the stellar disk, while for RV measurements, they induce a
stronger gravitational effect on their host star. Notable space telescopes that have collected
extensive time-series data for transit detection include Kepler and TESS, while high-precision
spectrographs such as HARPS and ESPRESSO have been instrumental in RV studies. In
addition to transits and radial velocity, other exoplanet detection methods include direct
imaging, gravitational microlensing, astrometry, and more.
==PAGE:10==
2
Observational studies have demonstrated that exoplanets are ubiquitous (Borucki, 2016;
Ricker et al., 2015), with the majority of stars expected to host one or more planets (Cassan
et al., 2012). Due to the inherent detection bias favoring giant planets, the earliest discovered
exoplanets predominantly had masses exceeding that of Saturn and semimajor axes of less
than 0.5 AU (Morton et al., 2014). However, these initial detections were not representative
of the broader exoplanet population. Subsequent surveys, particularly those conducted by
the Kepler Mission, have revealed that close-in giant planets are relatively rare compared to
a diverse range of exoplanetary populations (Wright et al., 2012). These studies suggest that
the majority of exoplanets fall within a size range between Earth and Neptune (Batalha et al.,
2013; Silburt et al., 2015). This population (with radii of 1 𝑅⊕< 𝑅< 4 𝑅⊕) includes both
terrestrial planets and volatile-rich, lower-density sub-Neptunes, with multiple demographic
studies aiming to characterize their distribution and investigate the physical and chemical
conditions that govern their formation and evolution (Fulton et al., 2017; Parc et al., 2024).
Figure 1.1 illustrates the masses of discovered exoplanets (as of April 2024) plotted against
their orbital periods, alongside the planets of our Solar System for comparison. Additional
information on the graph includes the discovery method (indicated by the color of the markers)
and the multiplicity of planetary systems (represented by the marker size).

along with the Solar System planets. A larger marker size represents higher planet multiplicity

As expected, the transit method is most effective for detecting planets with shorter orbital
periods, which correspond to smaller semi-major axes. Stacking multiple transits increases

the SNR, making the transit dip (which is typically minuscule compared to the total stellar
luminosity) more pronounced and easier to detect. Since planets with shorter orbital periods
complete more rotations within an observation window, their transits can be observed and
stacked more frequently, further enhancing their detectability. Additionally, planets that orbit
closer to their host stars have a higher probability of transiting from the perspective of the

and small planets (e.g., Mercury) appear isolated on the graph. In reality, the distribution
of exoplanet sizes and orbits is likely much broader than current detections suggest. These
limitations emphasize the need for advancements in observational technology and more
refined analysis techniques to improve planetary detection capabilities.

1.2
Motivation for Characterizing Terrestrial Exoplanets
The question of whether life exists elsewhere in the universe or is unique to Earth has
fascinated humanity for millennia. Liquid water is a fundamental requirement for all known
forms of life. To maintain it on a planet’s surface, appropriate temperature and atmospheric
pressure are necessary—conditions most commonly found on terrestrial planets (Kaltenegger,
2017). Consequently, the discovery of rocky worlds naturally invites comparisons with Earth
and draws significant interest from both the public and the astronomical community.
The presence of an atmosphere on a planet not only facilitates the existence of liquid water
on its surface but also plays a crucial role in supporting the elemental cycles that regulate life
(Lammer et al., 2009). As a result, atmospheric detection becomes a key starting point in the
search for habitable worlds. Furthermore, characterizing the atmospheres of terrestrial planets
is essential for understanding planetary formation and evolution (Selsis, 2004)—helping to
place Earth within the broader context of planetary diversity.
The launch of the James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) in December 2021 marked a turning
point in exoplanet research. Among its four primary scientific objectives is the study of
exoplanetary atmospheres, with a particular focus on terrestrial planets. Its unprecedented
sensitivity enables high-SNR observations, while its broad wavelength coverage allows for the
detection of key atmospheric species that absorb in the infrared. The advanced capabilities of
JWST enable the detection of faint atmospheric signals from Earth-sized planets, overcoming
the low-SNR limitations of earlier telescopes.
The significance of this research extends beyond academic interest. Understanding terrestrial
exoplanets and their atmospheres has profound implications for humanity’s place in the
universe. At the time of writing, no conclusive evidence of an atmosphere has been found
around a rocky planet beyond our Solar System.
However, as observational technology
advances, analytical techniques improve, and new discoveries are made, the study of terrestrial
exoplanets will remain central to answering one of humanity’s most profound questions: Are
we alone in the universe?

1.3
Objectives of this Thesis Project
The goal of the thesis project is the atmospheric characterization of the exoplanet TRAPPIST-
1 b through photometric secondary eclipse observations in two different wavelength bands.
The necessary technical and astrophysical background, as well as the description of the data
analysis process, are provided in Chapters 2 and 3, respectively.
The specific objectives of this thesis can be described as follows:
• Acquisition and reduction of data corresponding to nine secondary eclipse photometric
observations of TRAPPIST-1 b, conducted by the MIRI instrument onboard JWST.
These observations were performed using filters spanning two different wavelength
bands, with five observations centered on 15 µm and four centered on 12.8 µm.
• Fitting the reduced data with a model to remove observational systematics and a model
to detect the planet-to-star flux ratio (eclipse depth).
• Calculation of the planet’s brightness temperature based on the eclipse depth and the
calibrated stellar flux.
• Comparison of the results with different atmospheric and surface models to infer
conclusions about the conditions on the planet.
TRAPPIST-1 b is currently one of the most studied terrestrial exoplanets for atmospheric
characterization using the secondary eclipse method with JWST. This is largely due to its
close proximity to an ultra-cool star, which has gained significant attention for hosting seven
terrestrial exoplanets. At the time of writing, similar studies have been published for only
the 15 µm wavelength (Greene et al., 2023) and across both wavelength bands (Ducrot et al.,
2024).
This report presents an analysis starting from the uncalibrated telescope data while employing
alternative pipelines compared to previous studies.
Given the high cost of JWST data
acquisition, multiple independent analyses play a crucial role. Agreement between different
studies would enhance the validity of results, while discrepancies could reveal potential
weaknesses or systematic errors in data reduction pipelines and methodologies.

BACKGROUND AND THEORETICAL FRAMEWORK
This chapter provides the theoretical background necessary to support an understanding of this
thesis project. It covers both the astrophysical and technical aspects required to comprehend
the data analysis methods and the discussion of results presented in later chapters. Emphasis
will be given on terrestrial planets orbiting M-dwarf stars, as well as the specific instruments

2.1
Terrestrial Exoplanets: Key Atmospheric Concepts
Terrestrial exoplanets are primarily composed of silicates, rock, and metals, typically having
masses less than 2𝑀⊕(Henry et al., 2024). Their masses and radii can vary significantly
depending on composition, as illustrated in Figure 2.1. Unlike Neptunian planets and gas
giants, terrestrial planets have atmospheric envelopes that constitute only a minute fraction of
their total mass, if present at all. Nevertheless, studying these thin atmospheres is crucial, as
they offer valuable insights into a planet’s formation history, geological activity, and potential
habitability. All life forms on Earth depend, directly or indirectly, on the presence of an
atmosphere (Borduas et al., 2018). This highlights the importance placed by the exoplanet
research community on detecting and characterizing atmospheres around terrestrial planets.
Accordingly, it is important to briefly outline the mechanisms through which terrestrial
planets gain and lose their atmospheres.
Atmospheric Acquisition and Loss
The atmosphere of a terrestrial exoplanet can originate from different sources. The availability
of volatiles is primarily determined by the composition of the protoplanetary disk during
planet formation or by the accretion of volatile-rich planetesimals shortly thereafter (Forget
et al., 2014). Additionally, potential magma oceans often cool relatively quickly from the
bottom up due to pressure differences. This process can lead to the accretion of volatiles
such as H2O, CO2, CH4 and N2, which cannot be stored in significant amounts in solid form
(Elkins-Tanton, 2008). Finally, even volatiles trapped within the mantle can be released over
geological timescales through volcanic outgassing (Lammer, 2012).
The density and composition of an exoplanetary atmosphere are constantly subject to abrupt
or gradual alterations caused by atmospheric sink mechanisms. The most significant of these
are atmospheric escape and interactions with the planet’s surface.
==PAGE:15==
7
Figure 2.1: Mass-Radius diagram of terrestrial exoplanets. The figure displays data points for
known rocky planets, including all the TRAPPIST-1 system planets, along with theoretical
composition curves indicating different internal structures. The blue and brown density limits
represent pure water and pure iron compositions, respectively, while the intermediate curves
correspond to various mixtures of mantle and core materials of the Earth (Santerne et al.,
2018).
Several processes can lead to the loss of atmospheric species to space, including hydrody-
namic expansion and blow-off, Jeans escape, ion pickup, photochemical escape, and atmo-
spheric sputtering (Lammer, 2012). All these mechanisms involve atmospheric molecules
acquiring sufficient kinetic energy to overcome the gravitational influence of the planet. At
lower altitudes, this gravitational influence is stronger, and atmospheric density is higher. As
a result, increased collisions between molecules shorten the mean free path of atmospheric
particles, making escape less efficient. Furthermore, high-energy photons from stellar ra-
diation, particularly extreme-ultraviolet (XUV) and X-rays, are absorbed primarily in the
upper atmospheric layers, preventing significant penetration into lower layers. Consequently,
atmospheric escape is most efficient in the upper atmosphere, where the combination of lower
density, weaker gravitational confinement, and greater energy absorption facilitates the loss
of atmospheric particles (Owen, 2019).
Hydrodynamic escape induced by water photolysis in the upper atmosphere due to extreme-
ultraviolet (XUV) radiation is considered one of the most significant water escape mechanisms
from the atmospheres of terrestrial exoplanets (Wordsworth et al., 2022). In this process, water
vapor in the upper atmosphere is photolyzed by the most energetic radiation from the host
star, leading to the dissociation of H2O into hydrogen and oxygen. The resulting hydrogen,
being the lightest element, readily escapes through hydrodynamic escape, a mechanism where
==PAGE:16==
8
atmospheric expansion and blow-off occur due to intense radiative heating (Guo, 2019).
Hydrodynamic escape occurs when radiative heating in the upper atmosphere exceeds cooling
mechanisms, causing the exosphere to expand adiabatically and enabling atmospheric loss.
This process is most efficient when the thermal energy of atmospheric gases surpasses the
planet’s gravitational binding energy, particularly for lighter elements like hydrogen and
helium. It is especially significant on planets with weaker gravity and high stellar irradiation
(Lammer, 2012).
In addition to hydrogen loss, oxygen produced from water photolysis may also escape if
the hydrogen outflow reaches a critical threshold. If hydrogen escape rates are lower, the
remaining oxygen can accumulate in the atmosphere, forming an abiotic oxygen reservoir,
or be absorbed by the planetary surface (Hunten et al., 1987). Over time, this process can
theoretically remove multiple Earth-oceans worth of water, particularly for terrestrial planets
orbiting M-dwarfs during their highly active pre-main-sequence phase (Fromont et al., 2024).
Volatile species can also be removed from the atmosphere through interactions with the
planetary surface. Wordsworth et al. (2022) describe how magma oceans, water bodies, or
solid surfaces can act as atmospheric sinks for different species under varying thermochemical
conditions. Examples include the sequestration of atmospheric H2O into the crust or mantle
through interactions with igneous rocks, as well as the depletion of SO2 and H2SO4 in
ocean-bearing planets, due to the high solubility of oxidized S species in water.
The Habitable Zone and Instabilities
The habitable zone (HZ) is a key concept for assessing a terrestrial exoplanet’s potential
habitability. It is classically defined as the orbital region around a star where the incoming
stellar flux allows for the presence of liquid water on the planet’s surface (Kasting et al.,
1993).
This definition is based on the extreme dependence of known life on water and
primarily considers CO2 and H2O as the dominant greenhouse gases sustaining planetary
habitability. The underlying mechanism is the long-term carbonate–silicate cycle, which
regulates carbon exchange between the atmosphere, surface, and interior, maintaining stable
conditions over geological timescales (Watson et al., 1981). Recent studies, such as the one
from Ramirez (2018), try to factor in additional considerations to refine the HZ concept,
making it a more effective framework for characterizing exoplanet habitability. As shown
in Figure 2.2, the HZ shifts inward for cooler stars and extends outward for hotter stars, as
higher stellar temperatures allow similar insolation levels at greater orbital distances.
The runaway greenhouse (RG) effect is commonly used to define the inner edge of the HZ,
while the outer edge is often determined by the point where CO2 freeze-out occurs (Moore et
==PAGE:17==
9
al., 2023). An increase in received stellar radiation can trigger the evaporation of large bodies
of liquid water, enriching the atmosphere with water vapor—a potent greenhouse gas. This
process accelerates the greenhouse effect, leading to a significantly warmer climate, which,
in turn, further increases ocean evaporation. As water vapor concentration saturates in the
upper atmosphere, H2O molecules become susceptible to escape via XUV-driven photolysis,
as previously described. Once a critical threshold of stellar irradiation is surpassed, a RG
effect occurs, potentially depleting a planet’s water reservoirs on short timescales (Goldblatt
et al., 2013).
Figure 2.2: Schematic representation of the habitable zone (green) across different planetary
systems. Regions considered too hot for life are shown in red, while those too cold are
depicted in blue. Image Credit: NASA Kepler Mission
Conversely, a decrease in stellar irradiation can lead to the condensation and freeze-out of
atmospheric CO2 and, forming surface ice.
This process further accelerates cooling by
exponentially reducing greenhouse gas concentrations in the atmosphere. As the planet loses
its ability to retain heat, global temperatures drop, eventually causing surface water to freeze
permanently (Mischna et al., 2000; Kasting, 1991).
Other positive feedback mechanisms can also render a planet uninhabitable or at least unfa-
vorable for life. For instance, planets with liquid water surfaces are susceptible to runaway
glaciation, as water’s radiative properties vary significantly with temperature due to phase
changes. Lower temperatures resulting from reduced stellar flux can cause liquid water to
freeze into ice and snow, increasing the planet’s albedo. This enhanced reflectivity leads to
greater stellar radiation being reflected back into space, further cooling the planetary surface
and reinforcing the glaciation process (Kasting et al., 1993). Over time, this feedback loop can
==PAGE:18==
10
push the planet into a global snowball state, where surface water remains frozen indefinitely
(Gérard et al., 1992).
The climate of a terrestrial exoplanet is strongly influenced by its rotation, obliquity, and
spin rate, which determine the latitudinal distribution of insolation and shape both seasonal
and diurnal cycles (Forget et al., 2014). Planets with small semi-major axes often exhibit
low obliquities and tidally resonant orbits, with the most extreme case being tidal locking,
where the planet remains in a 1:1 spin-orbit resonance. This is particularly common around
M-dwarf stars and applies to TRAPPIST-1 b, the planet studied in this thesis project (Agol
et al., 2021).
In a tidally locked state, the planet has a permanent dayside and nightside, potentially creating
strong day-night temperature gradients. The nightside temperature is largely determined by
the efficiency of heat redistribution between hemispheres (Auclair-Desrotour et al., 2020).
If heat transport is inefficient, the nightside temperature can drop below the condensation
threshold of greenhouse gases, causing them to freeze out and condense onto the surface.
This can initiate atmospheric collapse, which often turns into a runaway process as the
greenhouse effect diminishes, further accelerating cooling and condensation in the cold trap
region (Wordsworth et al., 2022).
Overall, the atmospheric conditions of a terrestrial planet are shaped by a combination of
astrophysical, geological, and chemical factors, making it impossible to cover all aspects in
this introductory section. It is important to emphasize that the information presented here
is based on theoretical studies and models. The first direct observation of an atmosphere
around a terrestrial exoplanet would be a major breakthrough, helping to refine and expand
our theoretical understanding.

2.2
Atmospheric Characterization of Exoplanets
The atmospheric characterization of exoplanets involves observations aiming to retrieve the
properties of their atmospheres such as the temperature and pressure profiles, chemical com-
position, presence of clouds and hazes, as well as the circulation of the received stellar energy.
Constraining those properties can yield important conclusions about the planet’s formation
history, climate conditions, and habitability. Due to the extreme distance of other stellar
systems from the Sun, achieving the desired SNR for the atmospheric characterization of
exoplanets requires both state-of-the-art photometric/spectroscopic equipment and advanced
atmospheric modeling and statistical inference methods. Even for the closest systems, the
signal is faint enough for the atmospheric characterization to constitute a challenge. As a
general rule, the larger the planet’s atmospheric annulus is, the easier its observation be-
comes. Consequently, atmospheric retrieval has been performed for a significant number of
gas giant exoplanets while their terrestrial counterparts have shown no conclusive evidence
of atmospheric presence at the time of writing.
Observation Techniques
Planetary atmospheres are intrinsically three-dimensional objects, with both small and large-
scale variations of temperature, chemical composition, and cloud coverage. Spectroscopic
and photometric observations of exoplanets provide the means for their atmospheric char-
acterization. Some of the most frequently applied observation techniques implemented to
collect the necessary data are:
• Transmission spectroscopy during the planet’s transit. During a transit, starlight passes
through the planet’s atmosphere, revealing its composition via absorption features. In
wavelengths where the atmosphere is transparent, the stellar light is dimmed only by
the terrestrial disk of the planet, while in wavelengths where the atmosphere is opaque,
the atmospheric annulus also contributes to the dimming, which increases the transit
depth.
• Observation of the planet’s secondary eclipse. This technique involves photometric or
spectroscopic studies of the planet as it is occulted by its host star. Eclipses can provide
valuable information about the atmospheric temperature and chemical composition of
the planet. Given its significance to this study, a more detailed description of this
technique will be provided in Section 2.3.
• Phase curves, which correspond to time series observation of a whole orbit of the
planet around its host star. Monitoring the light variations of a planet over its orbit can
==PAGE:20==
12
yield insights into atmospheric dynamics and heat distribution. Phase curves demand
extensive observational time, which can be a significant disadvantage for high-demand
telescopes like JWST.
• Emission spectroscopy of directly imaged planets. While the three aforementioned
techniques receive the combined star-planet light as input, direct imaging manages to
isolate the faint planetary signal by suppressing the starlight with a coronagraph. These
observations are of high contrast and high angular resolution and can be effective for
large planets, adequately separated from their host star. However, direct imaging of
Earth-sized planets exceeds the capabilities of current instruments and remains a key
objective for future, more sophisticated telescopes.

2.3
Secondary Eclipse Observations
The moment when a planet disappears behind its host star is known as the secondary eclipse
(or occultation). During this event, the received light originates solely from the star. By
observing the system just before, during, and after the eclipse, it is possible to determine the
relative contribution of light from the exoplanet. This light can be either reflected radiation
from the host star or thermal emission from the planet. While both components can, in theory,
be found across the entire spectrum, reflected light dominates at shorter wavelengths (visible
and near-infrared), whereas thermal emission from the planet is the primary contributor at
longer wavelengths (mid-infrared).
Planets with small semi-major axes are particularly favorable for secondary eclipse observa-
tions. Firstly, shorter orbital periods reduce the required telescope time, making observations
more efficient. In addition, a smaller semi-major axis results in a higher incident stellar flux,
leading to a stronger detectable signal from the exoplanet. Finally, a close-in orbit increases
the likelihood of tidal locking, ensuring that the same side of the planet is observed during
each eclipse. In an atmosphere at equilibrium, this consistency allows repeated observations
across multiple orbits, enhancing the significance of any detectable signal (Alonso, 2018).

the brightness temperature of the planet with high confidence. In addition, if the photometric
broadband filters operate within a range that overlaps with key atmospheric absorption bands
(e.g., 15 µm for CO2), indications on the atmospheric composition of the planet can be pro-
vided. Even for a lack of atmosphere on a planet, eclipse emission photometry can constrain
surface properties, such as albedo and mineralogy (First et al., 2024), unlike transmission
spectroscopy.

M Dwarfs as Host Stars
Although M-dwarf stars (or red dwarfs) are not visible to the naked eye, they are the most
common stellar type in our galaxy, comprising the majority of its total mass. They are the
smallest, coolest, and least luminous stars, placing them in the bottom right corner of the
standard Hertzsprung-Russell (HR) diagram, as shown in Figure 2.5a. Their masses range
from 0.075 M⊙(the stellar mass limit) to 0.6 M⊙(Henry et al., 2024). Red dwarfs are
ubiquitous, making up approximately three out of four stars in the sky. Figure 2.5b illustrates
their prevalence by depicting the stellar population distribution in our solar neighborhood,
assuming a total of 100 stars.
Due to their low individual masses, M dwarfs have exceptionally long main-sequence life-
times, lasting trillions of years, which is several times longer than the lifespan of solar-type
stars. Consequently, no red dwarf has significantly evolved since the beginning of the uni-
verse, nor has any reached the final stages of its stellar evolution. In fact, a red dwarf with
just 10% of the Sun’s mass is predicted to have a lifespan of approximately 12 trillion years
(Laughlin et al., 1997).

Red dwarfs are very suitable targets for atmospheric characterization studies. Most of them
are expected to host planets in their systems, while at least 40% could be orbited by Earth-
sized planets in their habitable zone (HZ) (Kopparapu, 2013). In addition, the small orbital
periods of the planets around such stars not only increase the probability of transit but
also decrease the required observational time, which is usually technically and financially
demanding. Smaller stellar luminosities also lead to a larger planetary signal, which means a
larger transit depth for the case of transits and a higher planet-to-star flux ratio for the case of
secondary eclipses. However, despite those advantages, M dwarfs are completely different
hosts than Solar-like stars and possess characteristics that can prove disadvantageous to the
existence of an atmosphere around a terrestrial planet.

M dwarfs are highly active stars that emit a significant portion of their radiation in the XUV
and X-ray spectral regimes (Scalo et al., 2007). This energetic output is particularly intense
during their pre-main sequence phase—a prolonged period of instability before the star settles
onto the main sequence. For M dwarfs, this phase lasts significantly longer than for more
massive stars, extending up to 1 Gyr and producing energy outputs that are one or even two
orders of magnitude higher than those of the main sequence phase (Reid et al., 2006).
This extended pre-main sequence phase can lead to the depletion of early planetary atmo-
spheres throughvariousmechanisms, includingXUV-drivenhydrodynamicescape(Wordsworth
et al., 2014), as described in Section 2.1. Additionally, the prolonged exposure to extreme
stellar irradiation means that once the star transitions to the main sequence, the boundaries
of its habitable zone (HZ) may shift inward due to the reduced energy output. As a result, a
planet that currently lies outside the HZ may have been inside the HZ during its early history,
and vice versa (Luger et al., 2015).
However, even during the main sequence phase, red dwarfs do not exhibit a stable luminosity
that varies only due to planetary influences. Instead, their high stellar activity introduces
additional variability that can interfere with observations. This includes spectral distortions
caused by unocculted star spots and temporal disruptions from stellar flares, both of which
can complicate the detection and characterization of exoplanets.
Flares are caused by magnetic reconnection events in the stellar corona. These events release
stored magnetic energy, accelerating charged electrons along magnetic field lines toward
the lower atmospheric layers, such as the chromosphere and photosphere.
When these
high-energy particles interact with the plasma in these regions, they heat the plasma and
generate intense bursts of electromagnetic radiation. While the produced emission peaks
in the ultraviolet, the effect of flares spans a much broader wavelength range, including the
infrared (MacGregor et al., 2021). Flares are more frequent on red dwarfs compared to
larger stars. For reference, small flares with energies of approximately 1030 erg occur on the
Sun on a monthly basis, while on M dwarfs they constitute a daily phenomenon (Howard
et al., 2023). With the extreme precision required for the atmospheric characterization of
exoplanets through transit spectroscopy and secondary eclipse photometry, flares have the
potential to significantly alter the planetary signal.
Faculae and spots represent areas on the stellar disk with higher or lower local temperatures.
The surface temperatures of M dwarfs, especially within spots, can be low enough for
molecular species to be present and produce spectroscopic signals (Lim et al., 2023). In the
case of transit observations, these inconsistencies across the stellar disk can pose observational
challenges due to a phenomenon known as the Transit Light Source Effect (TLS). The out-
==PAGE:27==

of-transit, disk-averaged stellar light serves as the reference for the incident light in the
observation, which is then compared to the stellar light received during the planet’s transit.
However, if the planet crosses a region of the star that is either cooler or hotter than the
average, the transit-chord-averaged emission will differ from the hemisphere-averaged stellar
emission. The TLS effect and its potential complications are schematically illustrated in

A significant advantage of secondary eclipse observations is their immunity to the TLS
effect, as the stellar disk remains unobscured and unchanged before and during the eclipse.
However, eclipse studies are feasible only for planets orbiting ultra-cool dwarfs (with effective
temperatures below 3000 K) and a small semi-major axis, even with the most advanced
observational equipment (see Section 2.3). As a result, the planets that are suitable for such
studies are also the ones most exposed to the intense irradiation of their stellar hosts, which
can strip away their atmospheres, leaving them as barren, airless "dead" rocks. Additionally,
due to their proximity to low-mass stars, planets around M dwarfs are more likely to be
tidally locked. This means the same hemisphere of the planet consistently faces the telescope
at a given orbital phase, simplifying data interpretation by eliminating the need to account
for longitudinal variations across different observations. Finally, even for rocky planets that
manage to retain a gas envelope, tidal locking can lead to atmospheric phenomena such as
atmospheric collapse, as discussed in Section 2.1.

James Webb Space Telescope: Capabilities and Relevance
Overview
The James Webb Space Telescope (JWST) is currently the most advanced instrument available
for the high-quality observations required for the atmospheric characterization of terrestrial
exoplanets. Launched on December 25, 2021, JWST is stationed at the second Lagrange
point (L2) and has already completed multiple observational cycles, collecting valuable
astronomical data.
The telescope features a 6.5-meter primary mirror and is equipped
with four instruments that provide extensive infrared coverage with unprecedented precision.
To maintain these instruments at the extremely low temperatures necessary for infrared
observations, JWST employs both active and passive cooling techniques. The mid-infrared
instrument (MIRI) is cooled using a cryogenic system, while multiple layers of heat shields
passively dissipate thermal energy, ensuring optimal performance (Banks et al., 2008). Figure
2.8 illustrates the telescope’s design, highlighting its modular mirror and multi-layered heat
shield.

JWST has significantly advanced the atmospheric characterization of exoplanets, surpassing
its predecessors, such as the Hubble and Spitzer Space Telescopes, in precision. Its large

the first time, a telescope is capable of conducting photometric and spectroscopic observations
of terrestrial exoplanets with a reasonable likelihood of success.

The TRAPPIST-1 System
The TRAPPIST-1 system is a planetary system of seven known terrestrial exoplanets orbiting
a cool red dwarf, about 40.66 light years away from Earth (Brown et al., 2021). This 7.6 Gyr
old system (Burgasser et al., 2017) has attracted the attention of the astronomical community
due to its multiplicity of small, transiting planets and the low luminosity output of its star,
which makes it an ideal target for atmospheric characterization studies.
The TRAPPIST-1 Star
TRAPPIST-1 is an M8V (Costa et al., 2006) type ultra-cool red dwarf with a mass of
∼0.09 M⊙, a radius of ∼0.12 R⊙and an effective temperature of 2566 K. It is orbited
by seven rocky planets with masses of 0.3 −1.4 M⊕and radii of 0.75 −1.1 R⊕(Agol et al.,
2021). As a stellar host, TRAPPIST-1 is particularly active, still emitting a significant portion
of its radiation in the XUV regime. The planets of the system receive an amount of XUV
radiation that ranges from 10 to 1000 times the one received by Earth from the Sun (Birky
et al., 2021). This amount is, of course, expected to have been larger during the pre-main
sequence period of the M dwarf.

Two to four of the TRAPPIST-1 planets are believed to reside within the system’s habitable

solely based on their current irradiation levels. The ability of a planet to sustain a stable
atmosphere or retain significant water reserves—both crucial for life as we know it—depends
on a complex interplay of multiple factors, as discussed in Section 2.1. While model-based
studies of potential atmospheric compositions for TRAPPIST-1 planets exist (Turbet et al.,
2020; Krissansen-Totton et al., 2022), these must be complemented by direct observational
data to provide a more definitive understanding.
During the first three observation cycles of JWST, all seven TRAPPIST-1 planets have been
observed in transit, while the two innermost planets (TRAPPIST-1 b and TRAPPIST-1 c)
have also been analyzed through secondary eclipse observations. Their proximity to the star

results in a significant planet-to-star flux ratio, making them the most favorable targets for
such studies. However, at the time of writing, no results have been published regarding the
outer planets.
Findings from transit transmission spectroscopy (Lim et al., 2023) and secondary eclipse
photometry (Ducrot et al., 2024; Greene et al., 2023; Zieba et al., 2023) for the two innermost

3.3
Data Processing
Stage 1
For the first stage of Frida, the *uncal.fits files obtained from the MAST archive are used
as input. This stage performs the ramp-to-slope fitting, a process in which the ramp behavior
of the groups within each integration for every pixel is fitted into a slope. The fitted slope
represents the total flux received by the pixel during each integration (where higher slopes
indicate greater flux) while the slope variance provides an estimate of the measurement’s
uncertainty.
==PAGE:40==
32
This fitting procedure reduces multiple data points into a single count-rate value, effectively
compressing the data without significant loss of information. Moreover, it enhances the
signal-to-noise ratio (S/N) of the observations by averaging the multiple readings of each
group over time. This averaging process mitigates the effects of detector readout noise and
other potential random noise sources (Casertano, 2022).
For the ramp-to-slope fitting, Frida utilizes the official JWST pipeline, jwst. The output
from this first stage of data processing is saved in *rateints.fits files, which serve as the
input for the second stage.
Stage 2
During the second stage of Frida, cosmic rays are detected and removed using time-series
analysis, spotting 5 𝜎outliers in the light curves of each detector pixel. They are then
substituted with values smoothed using a Gaussian filter applied over 10 integrations. Figure
3.2 illustrates an example of this process.
Figure 3.2: A cosmic ray is detected, removed, and replaced over 10 integrations with values
smoothed using a Gaussian filter for a single pixel during the GTO 1177 observation on
03/12/2022.
During Stage 2 of Frida, the jwst pipeline is utilized once more to perform flat-fielding,
the calibration method in astronomical imaging that compensates for variations in pixel
sensitivity and illumination across the detector.
Frida offers options for both aperture photometry (using circular or hexagonal apertures) and
optimal extraction. For this project, the latter is used. Optimal extraction assigns nonuniform
weights to individual pixels, minimizing statistical noise while preserving photometric accu-
racy. This method uses a normalized, smoothed, median-weighted spatial profile to define
==PAGE:41==
33
the pixel weights, effectively representing the point spread function (PSF). It downweights
noisy pixels located far from the PSF core, where the signal contribution from the target is
minimal. The optimal extraction process is thoroughly described by Horne (1986).
Optimal extraction is applied within a square region centered on the target star. To further
exclude pixels that contribute only noise, a flux threshold—referred to as the "z-cut"—is
applied. This threshold is defined as the fraction of the maximum flux value above which
the pixels are included in the analysis. Both the box size and z-cut value are adjustable
to ensure key PSF features (such as the core and outer rings) are captured while excluding
background noise. In this analysis, a 20 × 20 pixel box and a z-cut value of 0.02 are used for
all observations.
Although JWST maintains highly stable pointing, a small oscillatory drift is observed along
both columns and rows, with amplitudes of a few thousandths of a pixel. To correct for this
drift, the normalized, smoothed PSF is adjusted at each integration. The products of Stage 2
of Frida are saved in *calints.fits files, which are used as inputs for the next steps of
lightcurve preparation and fitting.
The *calints.fits files also serve as input for calculating the calibrated stellar flux 𝑓∗
using aperture photometry. The calibrated stellar flux represents the total incident flux used
in the calculations. By multiplying this value by the planet’s eclipse depth, the flux received
by the planet is calculated. More details on this calculation are provided in Section 4.3.
3.4
Lightcurve Fitting
Through the *calints.fits files obtained during Stage 2, Frida performs the preparation
and fitting of the lightcurve. For this analysis, both individual and joint fits are conducted.
In the individual fit, the light curves are fitted separately for each independent observation,
and the weighted average of the resulting eclipse depths of each wavelength is used. The
weighted average is obtained through inverse variance weighting (see Appendix B). In the
joint fit, all observations at the same wavelength are fitted simultaneously, resulting in two
fits: one for five observations at 15 µm and another for four observations at 12.8 µm.
Since the MIRI detector can retain thermal excitation from previous observations, several
minutes were removed from the beginning of each new observation to account for the nec-
essary settling time. Additionally, to mitigate other noise sources, it was often necessary to
discard minutes from the end of certain time series. The number of minutes removed from
each time series is shown in Table 3.3. It differs among lightcurves since the ramp behavior
caused by detector settling was different in each observation. The processed and trimmed

Choosing an appropriate systematics model is crucial for obtaining an accurate eclipse light
curve. Over-fitting can lead to the inclusion of exoplanet signals or photon noise in the
systematics model, distorting the final result. Under-fitting, on the other hand, may fail to
correct instrumental systematics, leaving residual trends in the final eclipse model.
Photon noise occurs because photons arrive at the detector randomly and independently,
following Poisson statistics. While a Poisson distribution is inherently asymmetric, the Cen-
tral Limit Theorem ensures that for large photon counts, it closely approximates a Gaussian
distribution.
Since photon noise represents the fundamental astrophysical noise limit, a
well-fitted light curve should have normally distributed residuals centered around zero. The
presence of non-Gaussian residuals could indicate remaining systematics, suggesting that
further improvements in the model may be necessary.
The goodness-of-fit in each observation is assessed to ensure that the data are neither overfitted
nor underfitted. First, the light curve is visually inspected to check for signs of overfitting, such
as artificial "twirls" or clusters of closely spaced points deviating from expected trends. Then,
Allan deviation plots generated by Frida are examined to evaluate the noise characteristics
of the residuals. An Allan plot measures how the root-mean-square (RMS) noise behaves as
a function of bin size (in time or integrations), helping to distinguish between photon noise-
dominated cases and correlated noise due to systematics. If the noise follows pure white noise
behavior, the Allan deviation should decrease proportionally to 1/√𝑡, where 𝑡is the binning
time. Consistently higher values from this trend suggest the presence of correlated noise,
while consistently lower values may indicate an underestimation of the error bars, making
them smaller than those expected from pure photon noise. The Allan deviation plots of every
observation are included in Appendix A.1.

4.3
Calibrated Stellar Flux
The calibrated stellar flux, 𝑓∗, is obtained through aperture photometry. An aperture radius of
5 pixels is used for the 12.8 µm observations, and 8 pixels for the 15 µm observations. These
radii are chosen to enclose the core of the point spread function (PSF), where the majority
of the stellar flux is concentrated. For background subtraction, an annulus with an inner
radius of 10 pixels and an outer radius of 20 pixels is applied at both wavelengths. Several
alternative background annuli were tested, all yielding consistent results, indicating that the
chosen configuration effectively captures the background without including stellar flux.
From the .fits file header, Frida retrieves the flux calibration information: the flux density
(in MJy/sr) corresponding to 1 count per second (cps), and the solid angle (in steradians)
subtended by each pixel. The measured flux is then converted to units of Jansky (Jy).
The resulting stellar fluxes are 2.477±0.097 mJy at 15 µm and 3.397±0.111 mJy at 12.8 µm.
These uncertainties include a 3% systematic error in the stellar flux, reflecting the estimated
absolute photometric precision of MIRI, as described in the STScI report on the temporal
behavior of the MIRI Reduced Count Rate.
The values of this analysis are in agreement with the calculations of Ducrot et al., who
reported 2.528 ± 0.097 mJy for 15 µm and 3.42 ± 0.12 mJy for 12.8 µm, as well as with
==PAGE:52==
44
Greene et al., who determined a flux of 2.589 ± 0.078 mJy for 15 µm. The 3% systematic
was also used in these studies after personal communication with the STScI.

DISCUSSION
The calculated secondary eclipse depth and brightness temperature results can be compared
to different surface and atmospheric models to draw conclusions about the conditions of
TRAPPIST-1 b.
The dayside brightness temperature for a tidally locked planet can be
determined by considering the planet’s dayside energy budget and is given by (Burrows,
2014):

Here, 𝑇𝑑represents the dayside brightness temperature of the planet, 𝑇∗is the stellar tempera-
ture, 𝐴is the planet’s Bond albedo, and 𝑎is the star–planet separation. The parameter 𝑓is the
heat redistribution factor, which approaches 2/3 in the case of no heat redistribution, and 1/4
for complete redistribution (Koll, 2022). The latter ( 𝑓= 1/4) corresponds to the scenario in
which the planet absorbs all incident stellar radiation and re-radiates it isotropically over 4𝜋
steradians. The corresponding temperature in this case is the equilibrium temperature, 𝑇𝑒𝑞,
which represents the minimum possible measurable brightness temperature of the planet.
Conversely, applying 𝑓= 2/3 and 𝐴= 0 to Equation 5 describes a scenario in which all
incident radiation is absorbed with no heat redistribution, leading to a maximum temperature.
It is important to note that these temperature estimates are valid only in the absence of internal
planetary heating or other factors that could complicate the simplified energy budget described
by Equation 5.
Based on this, Greene et al. calculated an equilibrium temperature of 𝑇𝑒𝑞= 400 K and a
maximum temperature of 𝑇𝑚𝑎𝑥= 508 K for TRAPPIST-1 b, while their observed value was
centered at 503 K. The proximity of their observed value to 𝑇𝑚𝑎𝑥led them to conclude that
the planet likely lacks a significant atmosphere and instead is a planet with zero albedo and
no heat redistribution.
The temperature values derived in this study (shown in Table 4.3) differ from both 𝑇max and
𝑇eq by more than 2 𝜎. This result rules out the scenario of total heat redistribution and also
==PAGE:55==
47
disagrees with the conclusion of Greene et al., as described above. A possible explanation is
that the planet is a bare rock with a non-zero albedo, reflecting part of the stellar radiation
and leading to a cooler surface temperature. Alternatively, the presence of an atmosphere
could redistribute heat from the dayside to the nightside and potentially contain atmospheric
constituents with absorption features within the spectral width of the filters.
The scenario of a bare, atmosphere-less rocky planet is currently the leading interpretation for
TRAPPIST-1 b within the exoplanet research community. Models indicate that the present-
day stellar activity (and especially the heightened activity during the pre-main-sequence
phase) would likely prevent the retention of any substantial atmosphere on the TRAPPIST-1
planets (Van Looveren et al., 2024; Gialluca et al., 2024).
A planet’s surface albedo is influenced by multiple factors. Space weathering, driven by
micrometeoroid impacts and prolonged stellar irradiation over billions of years, is expected
to significantly reduce the albedo of a terrestrial planet lacking an atmosphere (Lyu et
al., 2024). However, according to Mansfield et al. (2019), even a very thin residual at-
mosphere—potentially too tenuous to be detected—could be sufficient to prevent space
weathering. Furthermore, if a rocky exoplanet exhibits plate tectonics, continuous volcanic
resurfacing could counteract the darkening effects of space weathering, periodically resetting
the surface reflectivity.
Four models with different surface compositions are plotted across a portion of the mid-
infrared spectrum in Figure 5.1. The blue line represents the blackbody emission at 𝑇𝑚𝑎𝑥,
while the blackbody emission corresponding to maximum heat redistribution at 𝑇𝑒𝑞is also

while the vertical uncertainties represent the 1 𝜎errors of the measurements. Data points
corresponding to Ducrot et al. and Green et al. results are also shown for comparison.
The results for 12.8 µm appear to align well with a feldspathic surface, in contrast to the
case of 15 µm.
However, while this surface composition is plausible for small celestial
bodies comparable in size to the Moon, it is considered unlikely to form on larger, Earth-
sized planets (Elkins-Tanton, 2012). Basalt, a low-albedo rock commonly found in the Solar
System (Taylor et al., 2009), is also excluded as a primary surface material from the 1 𝜎range
of the findings. The higher albedo of an ultramafic surface shows very good agreement with
the 15 µm eclipse depth values and is within approximately 1 𝜎agreement for the 12.8 µm
values. Notably, it falls entirely within the 1 𝜎range for the 12.8 µm values of the joint fit,
which is not shown in the graph.

Conditions change in the presence of photochemical hazes in the atmosphere. Haze particles
are strong absorbers in the optical range but absorb much less efficiently in the infrared. As a
result, they induce an inverse greenhouse effect, cooling the lower atmosphere while trapping
==PAGE:58==
50
stellar radiation higher up (McKay et al., 1991). This thermal inversion leads to CO2 features
appearing in emission around 15 µm, enhancing the flux at these wavelengths compared to
other spectral bands.
Ducrot et al. suggest that the strong stellar activity of TRAPPIST-1, combined with the
close proximity of TRAPPIST-1 b to its host star, could promote haze formation in the
planet’s atmosphere. The orange line in Figure 5.2 represents the best-fit model from their
study, which describes a CO2-rich atmosphere with hazes. This model, inspired by the hazy
atmospheric conditions of Titan, appears to be in 1 𝜎agreement with the results of this
analysis.
However, Ducrot et al. also identify a key limitation of this scenario: on Titan, hydrocarbon
haze formation depends critically on the photodissociation of CH4. From a chemical and
thermodynamic standpoint, the coexistence of CO2 and CH4 in a hot, CO2-rich atmosphere
like that of TRAPPIST-1 b is considered unlikely. Nevertheless, experimental results suggest
that hydrocarbon haze formation may still occur in such an environment if H2S is present
(He et al., 2020), even in the absence of CH4. Overall, their study concludes that although
a hazy, CO2-rich atmosphere is less likely than a bare-rock scenario, it remains a viable
possibility—one that warrants further exploration through both experimental and theoretical
approaches.
The differences in calculated planetary temperatures across studies (as shown in Figure
4.4) can be partly attributed to variations in data reduction and fitting procedures resulting
from the use of different pipelines. However, a significant portion of the discrepancy arises
from variations in the calculated stellar flux.
Currently, no spectroscopic observations
of TRAPPIST-1 have been conducted in the mid-infrared, which would provide a direct
and accurate measurement of the stellar flux in this regime. Instead, the flux is typically
extrapolated using models such as SPHINX (Iyer et al., 2023) or measured with broadband
photometry.
Any deviation between the true stellar emission and the assumed model flux (whether due
to inherent model limitations or unaccounted-for photospheric heterogeneity) propagates as
systematic errors in the inferred planetary emission values. The models tend to overestimate
flux values when compared to broadband photometric observations (Greene et al., 2023;
Zieba et al., 2023), likely due to the departure of the star’s emission from ideal blackbody
behavior in the mid-infrared.
For small stars, such a departure can be due to molecular absorption features and star
spots (Miles et al., 2023). According to Fauchez et al. (2025), measuring the star with the
==PAGE:59==
51
MIRI MRS mode would provide significantly more detailed information than broadband
photometry when interpreting exoplanetary secondary eclipse data. Photometry offers only
integrated flux measurements over broad wavelength ranges, which can obscure spectral
features and introduce uncertainties.
In contrast, the high-resolution spectral data from
MRS would enable a more precise assessment of calibration issues and potential stellar
mid-infrared variability. This improved accuracy would enhance the calibration of eclipse
depth measurements, reducing uncertainties in planetary emission spectra and ensuring that
most observed variations originate from the exoplanet rather than from inconsistencies in the
stellar flux.

C h a p t e r 6
FUTURE PERSPECTIVES
The four photometric JWST MIRI observations of TRAPPIST-1 b at 12.8 µm and the five at
15 µm analyzed in this study confirm that stacking multiple similar observations significantly
improves constraints on the planet-to-star signal in secondary eclipse measurements. Each
individual observation had a 1 𝜎uncertainty of approximately 100–200 ppm, but combining
them reduced this to 50–60 ppm. While this represents a significant improvement, even tighter
constraints are required to more accurately characterize the planet’s surface and atmospheric
properties. For example, Ducrot et al. (2024) estimated that a precision of 29 ppm in the
15 µm band—roughly half of the current uncertainty—would be necessary to distinguish the
surface albedo with 0.1 precision at 3 𝜎confidence.
Thus, a possible next step would be to conduct additional similar observations and combine
them with the existing dataset. However, there is a possibility that secondary eclipse obser-
vations alone may not suffice to differentiate between the two leading atmospheric scenarios
suggested in this study. This raises the potential need for complementary techniques such
as infrared phase curve measurements and medium-resolution spectroscopic stellar obser-
vations. The secondary eclipse results presented here are consistent with both a bare-rock
scenario featuring an ultramafic surface and a CO2-rich atmosphere containing photochem-
ical hazes. A JWST phase curve observation of TRAPPIST-1 b would capture the emission
of the planet over most of its orbital phase, providing latitudinal emission information that
could be compared with the surface and atmospheric interpretations discussed in this work.
Ducrot et al. suggest that such a phase curve could constrain the planet’s heat redistribu-
tion efficiency—and consequently its surface pressure—which would help identify the most
probable atmospheric model.
As mentioned in Chapter 5, another type of complementary observation could involve acquir-
ing MIRI MRS stellar spectra near the time of eclipse, reducing dependence on potentially
biased stellar models or broadband photometric stellar characterizations.
Fauchez et al.
(2025) estimate that a spectrum with resolution 𝑅= 5800, covering wavelengths from 5 µm
to 20 µm, could be obtained for TRAPPIST-1 b in just 2.6 hours. This would help mitigate
the impact of the strongly non-blackbody spectral behavior of the star in the mid-infrared.
Although detecting atmospheric signals on a terrestrial planet remains the top priority in
exoplanet research, studying planets with a confirmed atmospheric absence can also be
==PAGE:61==
53
scientifically valuable. By tightly constraining the surface composition and morphology of
an airless planet, researchers can gain insights into its geological conditions and potential
tectonic activity, its formation history, its gravitational interactions with other planets and the
host star, as well as the effects of extreme stellar irradiation on a terrestrial planet’s surface.
Paragas et al. (2025) recently published a spectral library for bare-rock surfaces on terrestrial
planets, expanding upon commonly used models such as those by Hu et al. (2012), which
are based on room-temperature reflectance measurements of potential exoplanet surface ma-
terials. The new library incorporates a broader range of compositions, higher temperatures
(500–800 K), and varied surface textures, including fine powder, slabs, and coarsely crushed
materials. This expanded dataset could enable a more comprehensive surface characteriza-
tion of TRAPPIST-1 b by providing a greater diversity of potential surface conditions for
comparison.
Finally, a planet with no atmosphere can contribute to the atmospheric characterization of
another planet within the same system. Variations in the transit spectra of a bare rock should be
attributed solely to stellar activity. As a result, these variations can be used to mitigate stellar
contamination from the transit spectra of another planet in the system, isolating its potential
atmospheric signal (provided that the two transits occur close in time). Rathcke et al. (2025)
demonstrate this technique using back-to-back transits of TRAPPIST-1 b and TRAPPIST-1
c, finding consistency in the stellar contamination signals between the two planets and using
the innermost planet’s transit spectrum as a reference for the stellar contamination. This
approach significantly increased the SNR in the <2 µm and, potentially, the 2-5 µm range for
the TRAPPIST-1 c transit. Overall, confirming TRAPPIST-1 b as a barren rock could enable
high-SNR transit observations of the TRAPPIST-1 planets in the habitable zone.

CONCLUSIONS
This study presents a comprehensive analysis of secondary eclipse photometric data for
TRAPPIST-1 b, acquired with the MIRI imager on JWST using the F1500W and F1280W
filters. Using the uncalibrated telescope data as input, a complete data reduction and fitting
process was performed, leading to the following key findings and conclusions:
• The results are in full agreement with the characterization findings of Ducrot et al.
(2024), supporting the scenario of a planet with either no atmosphere and an ultramafic
surface or a CO2-rich atmosphere with photochemical hazes in the upper layers which
lead to thermal inversion, causing CO2 features to appear in emission. The bare rock
scenario is more favored by models and theoretical studies of terrestrial exoplanets that
orbit M dwarfs.
• The validation of the Frida pipeline as a reliable tool for analyzing JWST uncalibrated
data, performing all necessary reduction stages and light curve fitting. The extracted
planet-to-star flux ratio values from the eclipse observations are in full agreement with
previously published results.
• The calculated planetary brightness temperatures are lower at 15 µm and higher at
12.8 µm compared to the findings of Ducrot et al. (2024), but remain well within the
1 𝜎range. This difference may stem from variations in data reduction pipelines or
differences in the calculated stellar flux values. Compared to Greene et al. (2023), the
temperature at 15 µm is significantly lower, ruling out the scenario of a planet with
zero albedo and no heat redistribution.
• Additional similar observations would help further constrain the eclipse depth and
improve measurement precision.
Complementary techniques, such as MIRI MRS
spectroscopy of TRAPPIST-1, could aid in planetary characterization by accounting
for the star’s non-blackbody behavior in the mid-infrared.
Since the two leading
models imply vastly different surface pressures—zero for the bare rock and significant
for the CO2-rich atmosphere—a phase curve observation, which can constrain surface
pressure, may be key to identifying the most likely scenario.
==PAGE:63==
55
Overall, the search for atmospheres around Earth-sized planets is pushing the limits of even
the most sophisticated equipment currently available to researchers. TRAPPIST-1 b, a planet
that orbits closely around a cool star—ideal for secondary eclipse observations—is also one
of the most well-studied exoplanets, with its physical characteristics and ephemerides tightly
constrained. Yet, even under such favorable conditions, the calculated eclipse depth still
carries significant uncertainty, which is propagated to the planet characterization process.
However, with ongoing efforts to address systematic issues of JWST and to better understand
the stellar phenomena that negatively impact observations, it remains possible to obtain clear
evidence of atmospheres around terrestrial planets, a great first step in searching for habitable
worlds.

BAYESIAN INFERENCE
The process of retrieving exoplanetary atmospheric properties has advanced significantly over
time, evolving from basic grid-based sampling techniques to more sophisticated Bayesian
inference methods.
These approaches conduct a thorough examination of the posterior
distribution of model parameters, given a dataset and any prior knowledge available. By
effectively exploring the model space, Bayesian inference ensures a more in-depth assessment
of possible solutions. At the core of Bayesian inference lies Bayes’ theorem, which, in the
context of atmospheric retrieval, can be expressed as:

Various Bayesian inference techniques exist, each employing different strategies to efficiently
sample the model parameter space and estimate posterior distributions. In this study, the
Markov Chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) method is used, which enables a thorough and robust
exploration of parameter space by generating a sequence of samples that follow the posterior
probability distribution. In MCMC, the exploration of the parameter space begins with an
initial guess and progresses through a random walk, where each step in the "chain" depends
only on the previous step. Over time, the full sequence of steps (referred to as the Markov
chain) effectively maps out the joint posterior probability distribution of all model parameters.
This approach ensures that the posterior distribution is sampled efficiently and extensively,
even in high-dimensional spaces. Additionally, MCMC allows for the incorporation of prior
distributions for the parameters, making it a flexible and powerful method for Bayesian
inference.
This chapter is based on the concepts and methods discussed in Madhusudhan, N. (2018).
"Atmospheric Retrieval of Exoplanets." Annual Review of Astronomy and Astrophysics, 56,
391-442.

The analysis of the secondary eclipse photometric observations on TRAPPIST-1 b was
performed using Frida, an end-to-end JWST pipeline developed for exoplanet transmission
and emission spectroscopy and photometry by the Exoplanet Group of the Space Department
in Denmark’s Technical University. Frida is written in Python and utilizes the following
packages:
• NumPy – for numerical computations (Harris et al., 2020).
• SciPy – for scientific computing and optimization (Virtanen et al., 2020).
• h5py – for handling HDF5 files (Collette, 2013).
• pandas – for data manipulation and analysis (McKinney et al., 2011).
• Numba – for Just-In-Time (JIT) compilation to speed up computations (Lam et al.,
2015).
• Hampel – for outlier detection using the Hampel filter (Mtrofficus, n.d.).
• batman – for modeling transit and eclipse light curves (Kreidberg, 2015).
• UltraNest – for nested sampling in Bayesian inference (Buchner, 2021).
• lmfit – for nonlinear least-squares minimization and model fitting (Newville et al.,
2016).
• plotly – for interactive data visualization (Kovapohja, 2022).
• PyPDF2 – for interactive data visualization (Fenniak et al., 2022).
• ray – for distributed computing and parallelism (Moritz et al., 2018).
• kaleido – for static image export of Plotly figures (Mease, 2021).
• george – for Gaussian Process (GP) regression (Ambikasaran et al., 2015).
• tqdm – for progress bars and iterative task tracking (Costa-Luis, 2019).
