Preface
The size of this Master Thesis corresponds to 32.5 ECTS points for each student and were conducted
in the period from 27nd of January 2020 to the 3rd of July 2020, by Simone Bruhn and Emilie Brisson
Jørgensen at the Technical University of Denmark. The thesis were conducted to fulﬁl the require-
ments for acquiring a Master of Science degree in Architectural Engineering in collaboration with
the department of Management Engineering under the division of Quantitative Sustainability
Assessment and the department of Civil Engineering.
The subject of the thesis highly originates from the authors common interest in the sustainability
paradigm, and how it have become such a complex word creating great confusion among and
within industries.
The motivation of this thesis was to reach different stakeholders in the industry. The LCA chapter
reﬂects an understanding of what such an assessment requires and how assumptions are made;
however, the results targets stakeholders in need of baseline values for comparison. Even when
the MCDA reﬂects mathematical knowledge, the results are for everyone in need of a transparent
methodology. The purpose of reaching stakeholders with different positions in the industry as well
as interest is the holistic approach of creating transparent supporting tools for decision-making in
multidimensional projects, e.g. the implementation of sustainability.

Abstract
The sustainable development agenda is one of the biggest challenges the world is facing. The
transport sector is responsible for 20% of the global greenhouse gas emissions. Thus sustainable
initiatives in the construction of roads can have a signiﬁcant impact on global emissions. However,
the actual decision and implementation process of national road networks is signiﬁcant and highly
complex.
Prior research has provided valuable insights into the transformative power of sustainability goals
and Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis.
However, previous research neglect to include the challenges of stakeholder perspectives and
measurable KPI’s into the equation.
To address this gap, this thesis uses well-recognised MCDA and sustainability theories to develop a
holistic methodology called an Integrated Design Process for Multidimensional Decisions (IDP-MDD),
which can be used in a broad sense. The data are collected through qualitative and quantitative
interviews with municipalities, contractors and the Danish Road Directorate.
The study addresses many literature subjects from decision analysis to road construction theory
and KPI’s. Among other ﬁndings, the study highlights the beneﬁt of increasing knowledge sharing
in integrated design processes.
The study’s limitations are evaluated and included through the construction of the LCA and LCC,
where the lack of inadequate data is one of the primary sources of errors. Further, one limitation is
the time framework of the thesis, as stakeholder involvement typically implies workshops in design-
ing KPIs, thus this phase was done by the authors.
The ﬁndings implicate that integrated design processes are an essential key to implementing
sustainable initiatives and knowledge sharing in the early design phase. The study further presents
the importance of future research and practical implementations in the industry.
At last, it should be noted that more research needs to be conducted to support these ﬁndings.

2. Trends of Globalisation
"Globalisation has become the buzzword of
the last two decades. The sudden increase in
the exchange of knowledge, trade and
capital around the world, driven by
technological innovation, from the internet to
shipping containers, thrust the term into the
limelight."
- The Economist (C.R, 2013)
With a world in progressive growth, the rev-
olution as well is in rapid development, and
even though the notion of globalisation is to
ﬁnd everywhere in today’s world, however,
the outcome remains unknown (Stiglitz, 2007).
Due to the rising awareness as well as the
consequences of global warming, people
are now facing the impacts of a changing
climate. Droughts, ﬂoods, and sea level rise
are just some of many extreme consequences
which increasingly inﬂuences the daily life of
people around the world (Brown et al., 1987).
The arising global complications and press-
ing development needs, forms today’s mod-
ern description of sustainable development
through the 17 Sustainable Development
Goals, also referred to as the SDGs. The goals
were presented in 2015, by the United Nations
General Assembly and has 169 subsidiary tar-
gets. The SDGs present a vital statement of
"leaving no behind" and are a part of the 2030
Agenda (UN, 2019).
Recent publications imply that any successful
path to achieving the 2030 Agenda is through
sustainable cities. Today, about 55% of the
world’s population lives in the city, and with
current rates of growth that proportion will be
nearly 70% by 2050. With growing cities comes
growing impacts, and in 2019 80% of the total
global GDP (Gross domestic product), output
came from urban areas. In the same year,
cities were responsible for around 75% of the
total global carbon emissions and 66% of the
total global energy use, elevating the tem-
peratures known as the phenomenon "urban
heat island" (Independent Group of Scientists
appointed by the Secretary-General, 2019).
2.1
A multifaceted system
The challenges are vast and multifaceted how-
ever cities are also hubs of creativity and in-
novation, with the concentration of research
institutions, universities, large commercial cen-
tres, infrastructure systems, as well as a multi
outlet for social and cultural exchange (In-
dependent Group of Scientists appointed by
the Secretary-General, 2019). Further, science
estimates that more than 60% of the urban
footprint expected to be built by 2050, is yet
to build, making cities hubs for a sustainable
blueprint development with a global perspec-
tive (Wahba, 2019). Cities are thereby becom-
ing the metabolism of the world.

2.2
Infrastructure levels
The infrastructure is one of the most compre-
hensive parts of a city since it refers to all the
basic services and systems an organisation or
a country necessitates to function properly.
Infrastructure is a term used to describe all
the essential facilities, organisation structures,
and services for cities at local, regional, na-
tional, and global level.
The term thereby
covers overall physical systems such as water,
heating, waste management, roads, public
transportation, communication, as well as
services like education, healthcare, law en-
forcement etc. (Liyin Shen et al., 2011).
Infrastructure is usually designed to support a
ﬁxed maximum use by a ﬁxed maximum pop-
ulation within a ﬁnite timeline.
This creates
immense challenges since the preparations
should be able to predict the density of the
future population and the use, to ensure that
the systems are designed and built to support
people many years ahead (Boyd, 2020).
2.2.1
A common denominator
Today the building- and construction sec-
tor1 are responsible for 30% of the total CO2
emissions and 35% of all waste in Denmark.
10% of the total CO2 emissions are exclusively
from the production of building materials, and
building- and construction processes.
The primarily CO2 emission from the construc-
tion sector can exclusively be found in two
areas (Regeringens Klimapartnerskaber, 2019):
• Activities on the construction site with
equipment and machinery.
• The use of two dominating materials; as-
phalt and concrete.
While the cities infrastructure are already un-
der tremendous pressure, future urbanisation
trends and the fact that rural areas are be-
coming a part of the city, further implications
and emissions will appear along with a need
for massive investments. The decisions made
today will thereby shape the resilience of the
urban landscape for decades to come (Inde-
pendent Group of Scientists appointed by the
Secretary-General, 2019).
Considering that the consumption of natural
resources and released emissions are more
signiﬁcant from large public infrastructure ac-
tivities than from other industries (Liyin Shen et
al., 2011), major investments in efﬁcient, mod-
ern, and clean infrastructure is highly essential
in attaining not only the sustainable develop-
ment objectives but also the urban growth.
Infrastructure thereby offers an illustrative case
to spur the transformative drive of sustainable
development covering a common denomi-
nator to the success of both the agenda of
climate change and tackling poverty (Bhat-
tacharya et al., 2015).
This makes future infrastructure projects one
of the strongest inﬂuencer’s in the efforts of
changing the consequences on the environ-
ment (Liyin Shen et al., 2011).

3. Sustainable Development and Awareness
When looking into the underlying issues of
the globalised world and how every system
evolves with immense speed, it is highly im-
perative to connect it to the state of the
environment based on the concept of sustain-
able development.
The modern thinking of sustainability and sus-
tainable development is based on the rapidly
growing awareness the terms have gained
over the past years, making them widely
adopted as buzzwords among numerous dis-
ciplines from local to global levels, as well
as a highly integrated part in governmental
and non-governmental strategies (Purvis et al.,
2019).
Figure 3.1: The tree pillars of sustainability
Evolution shows that even if sustainability has
played an increasingly important role in mul-
tiple industries over the years and is a highly
discussed matter, there persist a variety of
interpretations and implementations methods.
The meaning of the concept is thus strongly re-
lated to the context in which it is applied, and
the supporting massages towards environmen-
tal and social well-being thereby suggest, that
it has gradually become mainstream rather
than a fringe idea (Brown et al., 1987; Creech,
2012; Purvis et al., 2019).
Sustainability describes an ambitious long-
term holistic development concept, embrac-
ing a total life cycle perspective encompass-
ing social responsibility, environmental protec-
tion, and economic practice, illustrated in Fig-
ure 3.1 (Purvis et al., 2019).
3.1
Networks of Knowledge
An important key to action is to recognise
that the implementation and achievement
of sustainable development are by balanc-
ing the utilisation of stakeholder involvement,
innovation, science and technology, as well
as political strategies (Independent Group of
Scientists appointed by the Secretary-General,
2019).
Designers, politicians, different engineering
professions, and other stakeholders are faced
with the unavoidable responsibility of under-
standing and translating strategic sustainabil-
ity objectives into speciﬁc and tangible ac-
tions at design and project-speciﬁc levels. This
has proven to be an extremely challenging
task since the complexity has intensiﬁed the
multidimensional perspectives of sustainability,

3.2 Transformative Power
19
combined with the lack of structured method-
ologies and knowledge at various hierarchical
levels (Ugwu & Haupt, 2007).
As the sustainable development concept has
gained increasing popularity across various
sectors, national governments have set up
different programs and declaration to meet
the objectives outlined in high-proﬁle inter-
national conferences. However, the lack of
inadequate understanding and translating
of the cumulative impacts and interactions
of the many sub-level sustainability indicators
further compound the degree of difﬁculty in
the appraisal of sustainable designing.
The increasing level of international focus,
spurs the urgent need for translating these
national strategic sustainability objectives into
techniques and methods facilitating decision-
making at multiple interfaces of project levels,
spanning from conceptualisation to design,
construction, operation and decommissioning
(Ugwu & Haupt, 2007).
Current sustainability initiatives,
processes,
frameworks, and strategies are highly focused
on national and global strategic objectives
and aspirations, i.e., macro-level perspective.
However, it is paradoxically at the micro-levels,
that it has to be translated into tangible, prac-
tical actions, which requires a holistic ap-
proach through active stakeholder participa-
tion (Ugwu & Haupt, 2007).
3.2
Transformative Power
The transformative power of sustainability lies
within the key to acknowledge that, while the
present state of imbalance across the three
dimensions arises from not having fully realised
the comprehensiveness of the interlinkages
and the excessive prioritising of short-term
thinking, it is these same interlinkages that can
lead to the desired transformative change
(Independent Group of Scientists appointed
by the Secretary-General, 2019).
decision-making in multidimensional projects
is often complex and seemingly intangible.
The sustainability principle involves such holis-
tic considerations since it consists of inherent
trade-offs between environmental and eco-
nomic factors, as well as the sociopolitical
level (Kiker et al., 2005).
Multidimensional projects with an environmen-
tal framework involve numerous abatement
strategies and additional indicators such as
safety, cost and beneﬁt, ecological risk, envi-
ronmental impacts and human values. Not all
of these indicators are easily condensed into
monetary values and thereby comparable,
which is partly because of ethical and moral
principles associating with environmental con-
cerns (Kiker et al., 2005).
Environmental decisions are a coupling of
multidimensional networks, drawing on mul-
tidisciplinary knowledge bases. It involves a
manifold of stakeholders with different objec-
tives and priorities, presenting accurately the
type of challenge or problem that behavioural
decision research shows humans are inade-
quately equipped to solve unaided (Kiker et
al., 2005).
3.3
The Strategic Level
Sustainability seeks to create long-term value
by embracing all opportunities as well as man-
aging the associated risks (Galbreath, 2009).
While short-term efforts such as CO2 reductions
is quite implemented, many organisations are
still unclear as to what their long-term strategy
should be.
The deﬁnition implies that if organisations are
going to create long-term value through the
approach of sustainability, it is about under-
standing the opportunities and risks associ-
ated with the development. It is perhaps best
framed and comprehended through the per-
spective of the social issues (Galbreath, 2009).
Through the social dimension, the term value
creation appears, which is as well an impor-
tant objective for the understanding and trans-
lation of macro-level policies. By accounting
the industry context into the strategy, the im-
portance of the different perspectives from
the intensity of competitive sectors, capital
requirements, and access to distribution chan-
nels are evaluated.
There is a wide variety in conceptual frame-
works and tools describing the areas of strat-
egy development which should be regarded
as mutually supportive. However, it is still ob-
served that those taking a holistic approach
to the strategy, captures a more beneﬁcial
meaning than those with an isolated view
(Feurer & Chaharbaghi, 1995).

4. Road Networks
Several studies have already analysed dif-
ferent perspectives on sustainable infrastruc-
ture project appraisals.
The development
of measures are usually created by non-
governmental, governmental, and occasion-
ally in collaboration with academia institutions
(Amiril et al., 2014).
The previous perspective have for example re-
ferring to several classiﬁcations of infrastructure
as policy formulations, project planning, imple-
mentation, and operating strategies through
cost-beneﬁt analysis (Liyin Shen et al., 2011).
Although several attempts at approaching
the sustainability assessment of infrastruc-
ture projects, no standardised or commonly
agreed methodology is currently available.
Further, as seen in other industries, the envi-
ronmental and social effects are not appropri-
ately assessed in the feasibility of the project
study but are often identiﬁed during or after
the implementation phase (Liyin Shen et al.,
2011).
4.1
A Strong Inﬂuencer
With the ongoing urbanisation, the afﬂuence
of a city highly beneﬁts from the establish-
ment of available, affordable, efﬁcient, con-
venient, and sustainable transportation net-
works (Knupfer et al., 2018). However, with the
unreasonable expansion of infrastructure and
road networks which are the primary method
of transport inland, the rapid increase in ur-
banisation, i.e. the infrastructure may pose an
obstacle towards the development of sustain-
able cities.
Road networks and their interconnectedness
is a fundamental part of a society’s technical
infrastructure. Since it determines the level
of sustainability within a city, a sustainable
society simply cannot be maintained or built if
that part is left out of the equation (Rao et al.,
2018).
It thereby becomes clear that the establish-
ment of effective and available road networks
are of a signiﬁcant necessity to spur the sus-
tainability of the society, the economy and
the environment. However, with the expansion
of road networks over the next years, several
environmental obstacles will appear (Rao et
al., 2018).
4.1.1
The Value of Investment
The industry of road networks is not only play-
ing a strategic role in the economic develop-
ment but is as well often the centre of scientiﬁc
and political debates, due to the immense
movements of goods and emissions (Henke
et al., 2020).
Road networks are related to substantial en-
vironmental impacts and cost burdens al-
located between the construction, mainte-
nance, and use (Batouli et al., 2017). Many
sustainable factors linked to the construction
of road networks, see Figure 4.2, are often in-
terconnected, thus a more holistic approach
is necessary.
Road networks thereby require well planned
investments through the whole life cycle, as
it is often reported that ineffective, inefﬁcient,
and even wasted investments occur. It ap-
pears that governments and municipalities

An Integrated Design Process (IDP) is a key to
achieve a more sustainable sector, and by
adopting it in the early design phase, a higher
capacity for inﬂuencing and adding value
can be achieved (Díaz & Antön, 2014), see

While there is no universal or straightforward
framework describing how to integrate sustain-
ability into the creation of strategies, there
are several key elements required for effec-
tive implementation.
In general, large de-

by implementing IDP, which designate the in-
clusion of all stakeholders. IDP is perceived
as a non-linear process, utilising an iterative
process approach.
It is not a preordained
sequence of events, but emerging features
of ongoing learning which varies between
projects (Perkins & Will, 2007).
On the contrary, traditional design processes
are often comprehended as linear, where
sequential work routines are usually not sup-
ported by appropriate design optimisation ef-
forts, which naturally leads to higher costs (Löh-
nert & Sutter, 2003).

6.2
The Road Build-up
When construction a road it can be divided
into horizontally and vertically build-ups. Ver-
tically, the road structure differs considerably,
as it consist of various material layers deter-
mined by the horizontal loads from the means
of trafﬁc. Horizontally, the road is separated by
elements that serve the trafﬁc load from vehi-
cles, cycles and pedestrians (Birgisdóttir, 2008).
Figure 6.2: Urban road proﬁle, Stigsborg.
The urban road in Stigsborg is categorised as
a type 6 Danish Urban Road, consisting of two
lanes with a total lane width of 7m, a parking
area of 2 x 2m, a cycle lane of 2 x 2.25m, and
a lane for pedestrians of 2 x 2m, which gives a
total width of 19.5m, as illustrated in Figure 6.2.
6.2.1
Vertical layout
The vertical layers are designed based upon
the type of project, by which the different
layers can vary.
During the 2. World War multiple concrete
roads were constructed however, due to poor
technology, the method was phased out. To-
day, asphalt is thereby the dominating mate-
rial used in Denmark, still following the principle
of the 1960s.
Thus there is no alternative to asphalt the ac-
tual selection is based on the different types
and properties. Compared to the construc-
tion costs, this likewise forms the basis for the
selection of the pavement (Puckman, 1987).
In recent years, there has been a signiﬁcant
focus on noise reducing pavements. However,
today the focus has changed to include the
aspect of climate changes, by reducing the
emissions from the construction process, the
use phase and by adjusting the resilience of
the material.
The road construction consists of several mate-
rial layers, both unbound granular layers and
bound layers that are either bound by bitu-
men or cement. As illustrated in Figure 6.3 a
traditional vertical structure is assembled by a

Recycling potentials
When pavements reach EoL they are often
recycled and then reused as construction
materials, however sometimes they remain
in place and are then reused as part of the
supporting structure for new pavements, or it
is removed, and landﬁlled (Babashamsi et al.,
2016; Birgisdóttir, 2008).
Pavements are produced with high quality
aggregates, and as the sources of these ag-
gregates are depleted, growing importance
given to incorporating recycled co-products
and waste materials are therefore essential in
the paradigm of circular economy and global
warming, as well as the preservation of high
quality aggregates (Babashamsi et al., 2016).
Reuse of pavements as construction materials
in new pavements have a vast potential in
the global warming debate and depleted
raw materials, and new research also shows
that about 30% of the wearing course can be
replaced by the same type of reused wearing
course, without compromising quality and
safety. Additionally, it has only been possible
to reuse up to 10%, based on European prod-
uct standards.
Regarding the base layer, there are no restric-
tions on the allowed quantity of reused mate-
rials, as long as it can be documented that
the product complies with the same restric-
tions as virgin produced asphalt. However, a
stable reuse level in Denmark is about 30%
(Miljøstyrelsen, 2018).
Recycled asphalt is stored at the asphalt
plants, and for many years recycled asphalt
has been manged by mixing all the differ-
ent types in one big pile.
This means that
the ﬁrst years, reused asphalt was a mix of
wearing courses and base layers making it
perfect in the production of new base layers,
but not in the production of wearing courses,
since wearing courses are a mix of aggre-
gates with higher quality than the base layers
(Babashamsi et al., 2016).

7. Problem statement
Sustainable decisions are a coupling of multi-
dimensional interconnected networks, draw-
ing on multidisciplinary knowledge bases. It
involves a manifold of stakeholders with dif-
ferent objectives and priorities, presenting
accurately the type of challenge or problem
that behavioural decision research shows, hu-
mans are inadequately equipped to solve
unaided (Kiker et al., 2005).
Thus the sustainable agenda has gained in-
creasing importance; there is no universal or
straightforward framework describing how to
integrate the multidimensional perspectives.
In general, large design projects presuppose
a holistic method by implementing Integrated
Design Processes (IDP), which designate the
inclusion of all stakeholders. IDP thus creates a
synergy of skills and competencies, involving
simulation tools and stakeholder involvements,
leading to a high level of system integration
and transparency (Löhnert & Sutter, 2003).
The aim of this Master Thesis, thus investigates
the multidimensional challenges arising when
looking into the underpinning basics of the
sustainability concept through stakeholder
involvement.
The thesis intents to design a methodology,
framing an Integrated Design Process for Multi-
dimensional Decisions (IDP-MDD), to formulate
a transparent decision-making process.
To demonstrate the IDP-MDDs feasibility, the
method has been realised through a real life
case study related to the industry of road net-
works, thus creating a proof of concept.
The research questions are then delineated as
followed:
Main question
How to frame a sustainable integrated design method, to support
multidimensional decision-making through stakeholder involvement?
Sub-questions
• Which conclusions can be drawn from the importance of Key Performance Indicators?
• What is the value of an Integrated Design Process?
• How can sustainable tools support the multidimensional decision-making?
• How to create transparency in complex decision-making processes?

8. The Method
Project decisions can be deﬁned as multiple
interconnected networks, which are highly
combined and multifaceted, only magniﬁed
with the sustainability concept, adding even
further complexity to the topic at hand.
The complexity of projects, i.e., designing en-
closes in the unknown and is highly related
to the many aspects and conditions not only
stated by multiple decision makers (DM) with
different priorities and objectives but as well
linked to the given project, the environment,
the circumstance and the location. Even if
related projects have some overall common
essential reference points, no project is the
same.
This makes it even more comprehensive and
abstract to create baselines and evolve new
strategic decision support tools.
The understanding and translating of the var-
ious sustainable objectives presented in dif-
ferent declarations are now up to the indus-
tries to evolve.
Infrastructure projects differ
from, e.g. the building industry for which there
are multiple existing tools developed for as-
sessing sustainability. The difference mainly in-
cludes the diversity in the nature of the project,
construction practice and operational require-
ments, variety in design standards, a signiﬁ-
cant impact on urban development, and the
overall project management. Nevertheless,
infrastructure projects still have a considerable
impact on the sustainable construction envi-
ronment (Ugwu & Haupt, 2007).

Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis (MCDA) is a
general term for systematic approaches de-
signed to support the analysis of multiple alter-
natives in complex problems involving multiple
criteria.
MCDA is a tool, to guide decision makers with
several priorities and thereby make a decision
support system (DSS) more transparent when
dealing with complex issues (Barfod & Leleur,
2014).
The complexity of multidimensional decisions
in association with the concept of sustainabil-
ity often involves various criteria of distinctive
characteristics, which requires a holistic and
subjective approach to support the decision.
Multi-Criteria Decisions Analysis (MCDA) is a
mathematical decision tool capable of cop-
ing with such complex and multidimensional
heterogeneous considerations, characterised
as a choice among alternatives (Cegan et al.,
2017).
MCDA is thereby a method making it possi-
ble to advance future sustainable delineations
based on a structured, transparent, and reli-
able decision.
The purpose of a MCDA is to compare and
rank different option of alternatives and to
evaluate their "sustainable" consequences
according to the criteria established.
One of the greatest strengths of MCDA is the
possibility to apply the multiple criteria with
their own dimensions. On the other hand, one
of the greatest weaknesses of MCDA, is fairly
discussed in the literature, as the subjectivity
of the weighting step (Hermann et al., 2007).
However, by implementing all the multiple
stakeholder perspectives and their objective
weighting of the importance of the criteria,
the MCDA becomes more reliable (Kiker et al.,
2005). This process is deﬁned as The Multi-Actor
Multi-Criteria Analysis (MAMCA) (MAMCA,
2020).

9. Key Performance Indicators
One of the most signiﬁcant challenges in the
appraisal of sustainable design takes root in
the inadequate understanding of the interac-
tions and increasing impacts of the various lev-
els of sustainability. As the realisation to design
sustainable increases, so thus the challenges
of achieving such objectives at micro-level.
The ﬁrst essential prerequisite at addressing
the development of sustainability indicators is
through active stakeholder participation and
decision-making (Ugwu & Haupt, 2007).
Sustainability indicators, with the overall deﬁ-
nition Key Performance Indicators, KPIs deter-
mines the elements of a project and express
when they should be achievable. They are
quantiﬁable outcome-based statements used
to measure if a project is on track to meet the
objectives or goals (Enochson, 2019). Con-
cerning signiﬁcant projects like road networks,
KPIs are also used to ascertain a common
understanding of the project’s baselines.
The anatomy related to the utilisation of KPIs is
comprised of four steps. Every KPI must have
A Weighting/Measure, A Target that matches
the measure and time period, A Data Source
deﬁning how each KPI is being measured and
tracked and thereby avoiding grey areas, and
A Reporting Frequency since the KPIs might
have different reporting needs (Enochson,
2019).
This section thus covers the fuzziness of peo-
ple and the implementation of a stakeholder-
perspective-driven process through qualita-
tive and quantitative interviews with core de-
cision makers from the industry.
9.1
Method
The selection of KPIs in projects with a mani-
fold of decision makers can be a complicated
affair; however, a necessary process to avoid
bias’ since people have different perceptions
of sustainability and its context of importance.
Since there are no limits to how many KPIs
a project can cover, the indicators can be
divided into different levels with Level 1 as
the focus area and the goal. The higher the
level index, the more speciﬁc an indicator is
deﬁned.
In this study, 24 Key Performance Indicators
were selected by the authors, though based
on previous studies by industries, academia
papers, industry learnings such as certiﬁcation

10. Life Cycle Assessment
Attempts at standardising the Life Cycle As-
sessment (LCA) procedure for road construc-
tions have been made, but no government-
issued guidelines are currently available (Har-
vey et al., 2011).
The LCA conducted in
this study therefor follows the commonly ac-
cepted structure of ISO 14040:2008 and ISO
14044:2008 (DS/EN ISO 14040:2008, 2006; DS/EN
ISO 14044:2008, 2008) covering all four phases.
Since the application of LCA to pavements is
still in its early phase at this point, there are not
many study’s conducted to use as point of ref-
erence. Further, the existing LCAs comes with
a number of uncertainties in the addressed
data and details of the approach, as they
only evaluates some of the life cycle phases
(Schlegel et al., 2016). Thus the LCA performed
in this study intend to assess the scenarios en-
vironmental performance over a long-term
calculation period, as well as implementing
all relevant life cycle phases.
The LCA will not be the only factor deter-
mine if a certain pavement design can be
considered sustainable, but it will be equally
weighted in a holistic and multidimensional
assessment of sustainability through the per-
spective of the MCDA.
10.1
Scope deﬁnition
The LCAs conducted in this study are pre-
senting a comparative overview of the en-
vironmental imprint, which is a part of the
multidimensional perspective of choosing the
best pavement scenarios for the same road
type with equal functional unit. The execution
of the LCAs target experts in the ﬁeld, however
the results are used as a reference point to
illustrate the importance of incorporating all
perspectives of sustainability, as well as looking
into more than just global warming potentials
(CO2 emissions).
The modelled life cycle phases are based on
the methodological guide presented in DS/EN
15978:2011 developed to quantify the envi-
ronmental impacts of buildings (DS/EN ISO
15978:2011, 2012), however not all phases are
a match to modelling an LCA on road con-
structions.
The system boundary are illustrated in Figure
8.6, and the LCAs are carried out with a cradle-
to-grave scope.
10.1.1 Reference ﬂow
The functional unit (FU) represents a reference
unit to the compared systems as well as the
function of the analysed system.
With the
same FU across studies, it is possible to com-
pare LCAs.
The FU for pavements is often
deﬁned by their geometry, levels of trafﬁc
supported, and service life. Thus, to be able
to compare the pavements, the width and
length of the road should be the same for all
scenarios.
In this study the road type is categorised as a
type 6 Danish Urban Road, which is illustrated
in Figure 6.2, and consist of two lanes, with
a parking area, a cycle lane, and a lane for
pedestrians on each side, with a total length
of 1 km and total width of 19.5 m.
==PAGE:69==

Further, the pavement thickness is determined
based on the level of trafﬁc and can therefore
vary depending on the materials (Araújo et
al., 2014; Vidal et al., 2013). The scenarios are
illustrated in Figure 6.4.
The functional unit (FU)
In this study, FU is deﬁned as the Use of
1 km type 6 Danish Urban Road (19,5
meters width), with a trafﬁc intensity of
class T5 and speed limit 40 km/t, oper-
ated and maintained for 100 years.
With an analysis time-horizon of 100 years
the pavement is replaces a couples of times,
however it is important to know about the
difference between the technical service life
and the functional service life.
Every time a car/truck etc. runs over the road,
it causes a structural attrition and depending
on the layers thickness, technically the road
should collapse after a period of time.
Based on the handbook (Baltzer et al., 2017)
the technical service life of the road is 20 years,
meaning that technically all layers should be
replaced after that period, however since it
is extremely rare these days that the lowest
layers (Granular sub-base, Granular layer and
Base layer) are replaced, they are dimen-
sioned with extra centimetres in order to make
it comply with future trafﬁc intensity situations
[VD].
This means, that it is the two top layers (Binder
layer and Wearing course) that are replaced
and maintained over the 100 years service life.

10.1.2 LCI modelling
There are several identical processes which
could have been left out, however to give a
full picture of the imprint, as many processes
as possible involved in the road construction
have been included in the analysis.
For all cases of recycling the processes related
to preparing a product for the next phase of
life, were included in the system analysis, e.g.
remixing of asphalt, and remelting of iron.
All recycling processes has been modelled as
cut-off, meaning that no crediting from these
process are included, and all multi-functional
processes have been solved by system expan-
sion.
In the production of GAB as base layer, 30%
of the materials have been assumed to be
reused materials, this is exclusively accounted
for in S0-S3 since S4 BL is of a different construc-
tion material (PermaGAB). In the production
of the wearing courses a 10% share of reused
material have been assumed for all scenarios,
however this have only been accounted for in
the use phase when the wearing courses are
replaced (Miljøstyrelsen, 2018).

phases were neglect, due to inconsistency
when performing an LCA on road construc-

Operational water use (B7) are not compliant
with modelling of road constructions. Opera-
tional energy (B6) could have been modelled
as energy use related to city lightning at each
side of the road, however the input needed
to model lightning in the production phase
requires a comprehensive data research and
the necessity to make a single LCA.
The inventory data needed to perform this
LCA study can be divided into two categories:
primary- and secondary data, also referred to
as foreground- and background system (San-

10.2.2 Construction phase
In the construction phase of road construc-
tions, no waste were assumed. There might be
a risk in making this consumption, however no
data were found to base the assumptions on.
Additionally, it were assumed that the waste
allocated to the transportation of aggregates
are residues from the crushing process, and
this have already been accounted for in the
production phase (EPDs and ecoinvent pro-
cesses), as well as the fact that the machine
used to transport asphalt, drives directly to the
site from the production plant. The possibility
for waste in the truck are of cause there, how-
ever assumed to have no impact.
Furthermore, assumptions related to the en-
ergy use from heavy vehicles used in the
construction phase were included. These as-
sumptions were collected in section 10.2.6.
In some of the construction process, the as-
phalt might need to be applied by hand or
smaller machines, however this were not ac-
counted for.
At the end of the construction phase road
marking and drainage grids were added. The
road marking is assumed to be done by ma-
chine as described in section 10.2.6.
Furthermore, it were assumed that there were
placed approximately 80 pieces of drainage
grids per FU. The grids were added in S0-S3
and were assumed to be 42cm x 35cm x 6cm
with an estimated share of iron of 75%. Since
S4 already collects the water in the construc-
tion the drainage grids are not added in this
scenario.
In the all scenarios, it should be noticed that
it were assumed to excluded the drainage
systems and water collection, as this was pre-
sumed to be added as a part of residential
areas. Further, road equipment, lightning, and
green areas were left out of the model.
10.2.3 Use phase
In the use phase of road constructions B1-B4
were included.
B1 were deﬁned as the use of the road over
its 100 years service life (cars, trucks, busses,
cyclists, pedestrians etc.).
The emissions release from trucks and cars
were based on the amount of diesel and
petrol, where a standard fuel consumption of
0.286 kg/km diesel for heavy goods vehicles
and 0.0125 kg/km diesel and 0.0536 kg/km
petrol for cars, were assumed based on Eu-
ropean Average (Milachowski et al., 2005).
Further, the trafﬁc intensity were estimated to
be 6033 pc. light passenger vehicles per 24
hours (68.81% running on petrol and 31.19%
running on diesel) and 880 pc. heavy good
vehicles running on diesel (Baltzer et al., 2017).
B2 were deﬁned as the machines used to
sweep the road once a year for S0-S3 and two
times a year for S4, as well as snow removal
and de-icing. The machines used for snow
removal are described in section 10.2.6.
It were assumed that the use of de-icing cor-
responds to 0.9 kg per m2 municipality road
(Birch et al., 2013).
B3 were deﬁned as the repair of pavement
over its technical service life. It were assumed
that 10% of the total roadway area is repaired
during the different pavement types service
life, see Table 10.1. This also includes the cycle
lane were 10% is likewise repaired over its ser-
vice life.

10.5
Discussion
In this study the service life of 100 years was
chosen based on previous papers and rec-
ommendations (Birgisdóttir, 2008; Harvey et
al., 2016). However, this topic is among other
things particularly discussed in many industries,
in terms of what is the most accurate to give
the best recommendations and in terms of
making the analysis comparative.
The literature review shows that existing arti-
cles have different FU covering 20, 45, and
100 years and TechBrief (Harvey et al., 2011)
describes that the preferences of the anal-
ysis period change depending on project
type. They recommend a 50 to 60 years anal-
ysis period as appropriate for the evaluation
of constructing new pavement alternatives,
whereas 20 years might be acceptable if the
evaluation is about rehabilitation strategies.
The ﬁnal selection of the analysis period has
signiﬁcant impacts on the ﬁnal environmen-
tal imprints, due to x times of changes in the
pavement design, trafﬁc volume and composi-
tion over time, energy sources, and changing
technologies. It is possible to make predictions
for the future, however most LCA studies as-
sume that current technologies and practices
are modelled as future scenarios as well, and
thereby it becomes somewhat constant over
time (Harvey et al., 2011).
Furthermore, it is notable that this study does
not account for all life cycle phases and
thereby not all inputs, which of cause inﬂu-
ence the ﬁnal results, thus resulting in lower
environmental impact. However, as this was
neglected in all scenarios, the comparison of
the environmental imprints are not affected,
and thereby this omission is found to be insignif-
icant.
10.5.1 Concluding Remarks
When solely looking at the results of the LCA
the performance of S0 and S4 are consider-
ably close, but in general the results works
in favour for S4, where 7 out of the consid-
ered 9 impact categories have the best per-
formance (the smallest impact).
When considering the 7 impact categories fur-
ther, it is found that S4 only performs between
0.19% and 14.36% better than S0, with only
0.7% better performance in the impact cate-
gory global warming.

11. Life Cycle Cost
Like the Life Cycle Assessment (LCA), there is
no governmental guidelines or procedure for
conducting Life Cycle Costing (LCC) for pave-
ments at this moment (Harvey et al., 2011).
Further, cost calculations highly depend on
the society and the involvement of supply and
demand, making it as complex to estimate.
Since the application of LCC to pavements is
still in its early stage at this point, there is a lack
of academia conducted to use as a point of
reference. Further, the existing LCCs comes
with several uncertainties in the addressed
data and details of the approach, as they
only evaluate some of the life cycle phases
(Schlegel et al., 2016).
The LCC performed in this study intend to as-
sess the scenarios’ economic performance
over a long-term calculation period.
Like the LCA, the LCC will not be the only fac-
tor determining if a certain pavement design
can be considered sustainable. Still, it will be
equally weighted in a holistic and multidimen-
sional assessment of sustainability through the
perspective of the MCDA.
11.1
Scope deﬁnition
The LCCs, like the LCAs, conducted in this
study is presenting a comparative overview
of different scenarios just from an economic
point-of-view.
The LCC is, as the LCA, a part of the multidi-
mensional perspective of choosing the best
pavement scenarios for the same road type
with an equal functional unit.
The modelled life cycle phases, is like in the
LCA, based on the methodological guide
presented in DS/EN 16627:2015 (DS/EN ISO
16627:2015, 2020) developed to quantify the
economic impacts of buildings (DS/EN ISO
15978:2011, 2012), however not all phases are
a match when performing the LCC of road
constructions.

12. TOPSIS
The Technique for Order Preference by Sim-
ilarity to Ideal Solution (TOPSIS) is an MCDA
method that allows to rank alternatives of
multiple objective decision-making problems.
The method makes it possible to evaluate and
rank a set of alternatives in the descending
order according to their relative closeness to
the positive ideal solution and the greater the
distance to the non-ideal solution (Dammak
et al., 2015). The ideal- and non-ideal solu-
tion are determined based on the attribute
type deﬁned as a beneﬁcial attribute or a
cost (negative) attribute, and thus capable of
handling multidimensional problems (Kalbar
et al., 2017).
TOPSIS is a mathematical method using linear
algebra that builds upon the theory of having
m alternatives that are to be ranked, which in
this case are based on the different road con-
struction scenarios evaluated by n number of
attributing criteria which are referring back
to the Key Performance Indicators from the
stakeholder involvement found in section 9.4,
the LCA and the LCC (Dammak et al., 2015;
Kalbar et al., 2017). Further, each indicator is
assigned a weighting according to their impor-
tance. Especially this step is fairly discussed in
the literature as the weakness of MCDA since
it depends on either subjective or objective
methods (Kiker et al., 2005).
TOPSIS is an algorithm based on multiple steps
described in the following sections (Dammak
et al., 2015; Kalbar et al., 2017).

12.3
The weighting
The weighting is probably the most important
step in the MCDA and has a signiﬁcant inﬂu-
ence on the overall results. It is also in this step
where the MCDA has some classical barriers
since the weighting of the criteria are mea-
sured in crisp numbers.
The weighting of the criteria are in classical
MCDA methods obtained through decision
makers judgements and preferences (Wang
& Lee, 2009), however, this comes with un-
certainties arising from information that is not
quantiﬁable, incomplete (Frini, 2017) and as
well highly personal and society based (Sohn
et al., 2020). It does not mean that this weight-
ing method is wrong it, however, means that
when looking at the results, it is of utmost im-
portance that the fuzziness of people is taken
into account. They may have answered one
thing now but will have another meaning to-
morrow.
Another frequently used weighting method is
the centroid weighting system, also known as
ROC Rank Order Centroid. This type of ranking
converts all weightings from all involved deci-
sion makers into an average weighting, giving
all involved parties an equal inﬂuence on the
ﬁnal results.
Two ROC weightings was conducted to com-
pare the individual decision makers weightings
with an average weighting of all respondents.
However, the main part of this study aims to dif-
ferentiate the involved DMs, to show how vital
the collaboration is in the early design phases,
as well as to ensure transparency, which is why
the ROC weightings are not that important
though interesting.
In this study, the weighting was obtained in the
KPI chapter (chapter 9), where each involved
decision maker was asked to differentiate the
importance based on an overall overview
between the indicators and speciﬁc between
road constructions in respectively Urban roads,
Motorways, and Country roads.
The decision makers were divided into two cat-
egories of comparison; Experts represented
by Arkil, Per Aarsleff, and the Danish Road
Directorate, and Municipalities represented
by Roskilde, Odense, Aarhus, and Aalborg.
In addition to the weighting provided by the
eight decision makers, two test weightings
were conducted to test the sensitivity of TOP-
SIS.
W01: Same weighting of all indicators
W02: Weighting based on the difference
between max and min value for each indica-
tor and based on all decisions makers.

13.2
Discussion
MCDAs weakness is the weighting procedure,
as it related to people and their perspective.
Often, using MCDA, the weighting is done by
the authors; however, in this study, the weight-
ing is done by the eight decision makers.
Even when this study uses multiple stakehold-
ers perceptive to evaluate on the ideal so-
lution, people are en general inadequately
equipped to make such weightings unaided,
as their aspects are always clouded by pro-
fession, position in the company, an previous
knowledge as well subjective opinions (Kiker
et al., 2005).
The two decision makers, from the Danish
Road Directorate, showcase this part. Even
if they are from the same company, they are
weighing the importance of the indicators
quite differently, resulting in different scenar-
ios being closest to the ideal solutions. This is
highly a result of their position in the company
and what they see as important.
Further, it should be noticed that even when
the results of TOPSIS looks like this today, it
might look totally different if we ask the same
questions about weightings tomorrow, as the
weighting is profoundly related to society as
well.
Although TOPSIS presents the weakness of
weighting, the method still represents an abil-
ity to capture these uncertainties inherent in
human reasoning, and by that showing the
importance of reaching an agreement at the
early phases of a project. It further, exempliﬁes
a method capable of analysing inputs with
different units, and in general, selecting the
best scenario based on a more transparent
viewpoint.
13.2.1 Concluding Remarks
Even if the weighting is related to uncertain-
ties, it was concluded that they do not make
the results useless, as they just showcase the
importance of all decision makers being a
part of the project from the start, as well as
reaching an agreement.
Even if the decision makers do not have a
100% agreement on the best scenario, a ma-
jority of the decision makers weights S2 as the
closest scenario to the ideal solution, along
with the sensitivity parameters.
Further analysis would be preferable to ﬁnd
common ground, as the results of TOPSIS addi-
tionally shows how diverse the decision makers
interpret the dimensions of sustainability.

Integrated design process (IDP) is somewhat
complicated and unclear, and no speciﬁc

the method in practice (Bygherreforeningen,

When it comes to the perception of sustain-
ability, a vast of deﬁnitions surfaces among
industries, making it even more complicated
and comprehensive to understand and im-
plement. Previous when framing sustainability,
most decision makers prioritised the economic
level as covering for all three dimensions. To-
day, the environmental dimension is consid-
ered necessary as well, though governmental
climate actions, however, the comprehen-
sion of this level is still complex and mostly
related to reducing CO2 emissions. The third
dimension of sustainability, the social level, is
however yet considered to be a complex and
unmanageable parameter, which is often
challenging to quantify, and therefore often
omitted in the process.
Since there often lies more complex interpre-
tation in the social dimension, thus making
it difﬁcult to measure, the approach is a bit
different from the others. In, e.g. the building
industry, the deﬁnition is more explicit in the
associated with indoor environmental factors
such as indoor climate, whereas the dimen-
sion is even vaguer in the road construction
industry.
In this study, the social level is thereby incor-
porated from the perspective of stakeholder
involvement, as this is a critical factor in the
implementation of sustainability into project
designing and changing business-as-usual.
The stakeholders perspective is related to
the KPI selection and the establishment of
the indicators and their weighting. Typically
the establishment of KPIs is created together
with the stakeholders, through a workshop,
nevertheless, as this study is time-limited and

14.1
MCDA
Multi-Criteria Decision Analysis is an opera-
tional method; however, time-consuming, as
it requires a certain amount of input data as
well as necessary mathematical skills; thus,
challenging to adapt in the industry. Never-
theless, the method presents various beneﬁts,
as it makes multidimensional decision-making
more transparent.
There are multiple different theories related
to the MCDA methodology, yet the required
amount of input data is different, and they are
as well following different steps.
However, even though there are different
steps, MCDA can handle multiple parameters
with different units, thus capable of assessing
multidimensional indicators, e.g. comparing
LCAs and LCCs, which are two indicators
showcasing the difﬁculties of the sustainable
development agenda.
Since MCDA is profoundly affected by the
outcome of the LCA and LCC, implementing
the method in the early design phase create
sources of errors, i.e. the method is effected
by those errors occurring in the assessment
of the other methods. As known, when per-
forming an LCA or LCC in the early design
phase, the stakeholders possess minimal input
knowledge as quantities, material types, etc.,
making sources of errors unavoidable.
When dealing with these multidimensional
challenges, an MCDA can assess decision-
making by the use of qualitative and quan-
titative inputs, which is a fundamental need
of data typology. However, there are authors
questioning the explicit incorporation of quali-
tative and mixed data, as the utility of outrank-
ing based methods, is based on the manip-
ulation of the information in the input stage
(Cinelli et al., 2014).
However, newer studies additionally argu-
ments, that by including both the qualitatively
and quantitative assessment of input data, a
stronger foundation for the decision process is
constructed. This argument relates to the inter-
connectedness of approaching sustainability
and the obstacles occurring when asked to
put different key performance indicators into
boxes. By implementing the qualitative assess-
ment as well, the results are based on more
reliable evidence and easier interpret (Sohn
et al., 2020).
14.2
Beneﬁts of IDP-MDD
One signiﬁcant beneﬁt of the integrated de-
sign process is the ability to increase knowl-
edge sharing, thus strengthening decision-
making through the entire project. By incor-
porating a single step, the methodology ad-
vances a stakeholder-consensus-based tactic,
translating environmental assessments, includ-
ing social and economic considerations.
The methodology of the Integrated Design
Process for Multidimensional Decisions (IDP-
MDD), thereby increases the transparency of
decisions, by a single string of numbers the
impact prioritisation for the sustainability con-
cept becomes clear. IDP-MDD is thereby a
step towards demystifying the necessitate for
a holistic approach in the ﬁeld of environmen-
tal assessment.
This paves the way towards Life Cycle Assess-
ment (LCA) capable of embodying the multi-
ple dimensions of sustainability, thus creating
opportunities for an effectively and quanti-
tatively incorporation of these issues in envi-
ronmental governance in public and private
sectors decision-making processes (Sohn et
al., 2020). It thus provides a straight forward
method designating the source of weighting
and the substantial necessity for an individual
as well as a collaborative analysis.
The method thereby offers an ability for an
easy embodiment of strategic planning into
the assessment of various alternatives with
an integrated environmental angel of the
investment.
Such advantages could be a
great source of decision justiﬁcation and in
the use of reporting to stakeholders when
transparency is the driving factor and a desire
for enlightened shareholder primacy.
In any applicable situation, the method pro-
motes the transparency of decision support,
which is a fundamental requirement in the ap-
plication of well-planned multidimensional per-
spectives in the context of decision-making.

Chapter 14. Discussion
Where some stakeholders are happy that an
everyday basis framework for sustainability is
established, others see new issues arising.
Even though governmental interference is
highly discussed as both positive and nega-
tive, the different decision makers within the
industries also hold an enormous responsi-
bility in creating a more sustainable sector.
The stakeholders must change their perspec-
tive and "old habits" in the formulation of a
more transparent, integrated and sustainable
project in the future. Further, obstacles arise
when discussing the prospects of governmen-
tal interference. As the method may be useful
in spurring the sustainable transition, it is not
driven by the desire of the stakeholders, which
can result in an industry that only meets the
minimum requirements to comply with the
legislation. The innovative solutions developed
and driven by the stakeholder thereby dies in
the process.
It is unclear what will happen in the future,
but many factors indicate that the industry
will move towards more sustainable develop-
ment. The question is just how will it happen
and who will be the front runners in the de-
velopment. Will new actors set the agenda
and pressure or inspire other stakeholders to
join the sustainable revolution, or maybe the
industry will restructuring itself. However, the
speed of such a conversion is considered low,
since the transition naturally will have a lower
cadence.
14.4
Applicability
and
Recom-
mendations
The globally focus on sustainable develop-
ment agenda has intensiﬁed the pressure on
stakeholders to create project and strategies,
targeting the environmental impact, without
signiﬁcantly increasing the costs.
With the increasing demands, the indication is
clear; the stakeholder involvement must ﬁnd
new and more economically approaches in
the creation of a sustainable industry.
As stated earlier in section 4.2, IDP is a method
designated to include all stakeholders, creat-
ing a high level of system integration.
Since there is no clear nor unequivocal way
to practice IDP trough a project, IDP can both
proceed successfully or unsuccessfully in a
project.
Although there are different perspectives of
IDP and how to practice it, the following fac-
tors are always more or less standard for the
IDP deﬁnition:
• Creating sub-goals, goals and objectives
• Facilitated by a person who is not directly
involved in the project itself, but who is
responsible for the process.
• Iterative process that opens up new
knowledge and technology
• All stakeholder involved participates on
an equal term and is a part of the
decision-making heard
• Clair stakeholder roles
• Having a holistic understanding and
mindset trough out the process
• Focusing on the total life cost
• Use of expert
• Use and integration of intelligent tools
(BIM)
As there is no stated framework for the integra-
tion of ID, there is a need for a more "go-to"
method when it comes to sustainable devel-
opment in the industry.
The industry is far from practising and estab-
lishing the integrated, holistic method (like
MCDA), and far too often, the industry ex-
clusively embrace terms like CO2, Global
Warming, construction costs etc.
Spurring
the need for a more integrated and holistic
collaboration, where more of the sustainable
dimensions are taken into consideration and
prioritised in the process.
Based on the above information, a guide
has been developed to accommodate the
outlined needs. The guide takes a basis in
the above factors, including the outcome of
the conducted interviews. The guide thereby
embraces the idea of creating a more inte-
grated design process, where all stakeholders
are included in the early design phase, by
having a clear role throughout the process. In
the guide, clear objectives have been set up
to create a clear overall scope.
The guide is an attempt to establish a more
holistic, sustainable approach and to show-
case how IDP can be practised in projects.
Of course, as all projects are different, phases
and objectives can change; thus this guide is
an attempt to formulate clear guidelines for a
more sustainable approach in future projects
and collaborations between the various stake-
holders. The Guide can be found in Support-
ing Information VII.

15. Conclusion
Through the study, the importance of an inte-
grated design process and its applicability to
support decisions were investigated. The IDP-
MDD methodology were showcased through
a case study related to road networks and
different scenarios, as a proof of concept, as
well as to show the signiﬁcance values of such
method.
Based on the previous analysis chapters a
short conclusion on the applicability and the
conﬁdence of the IDP-MDD methodology are
presented.
• The perception of sustainability is highly
related to the knowledge and industry,
as well as subjectivity. By addressing and
establishing Key Performance Indicators
through stakeholder involvement, the
complexity and different perspectives
are managed. Thus, translating the multi-
dimensional aspects of sustainability and
the three dimensions interconnected-
ness through a transparent process.
• When addressing the multidimensional
concept of sustainability in design pro-
cesses, it is unavoidable to discover how
many different ways it is interpreted and
executed. When including the concept
of sustainable development in the frame-
work of the project, it is thereby of utmost
importance to ﬁnd common ground be-
tween decisionmakers, by settling on the
sustainable indicators and their impor-
tance for a given project. On a much
higher degree, all decision makers must
be a part of the early design phases,
to share knowledge, perspectives, and
experience. Even if this were done for
some projects, no project is the same,
and these perspectives will vary.
• Based on the difﬁculties in translating
the multidimensional dimensions of sus-
tainability, most sustainable tool, such
as Life Cycle Assessment and Life Cycle
Costing showcase hard numbers. These
quantities can assist decision-making by
establishing a supporting background
and comparison between different alter-
natives. Even-though these tools bring
qualiﬁcations to the table they only assist
one dimension at a time, i.e. the environ-
mental and the economic. Further, the
different impact categories related to
LCA can be hard to single out, resulting
in what is known, i.e. global warming
and CO2 emissions, even if all categories
are of importance.
• To make the quantities from the sustain-
able tools more transparent, i.e. base
the ﬁnal solutions on more than one in-
dicator, e.g. the economic perspective,
they can be combined with the Multi-
Criteria Decision Analysis method. MCDA
creates such transparency in complex
multidimensional decision-making pro-
cesses, by implementing numerous indi-
cators and weighting formats, through
qualitative and quantitative interpreta-
tion, creating a strong foundation for the
decision process.
It can thereby be concluded that stakeholder
involvement is necessary to support multidi-
mensional decision-making, through the prac-
tice of the IDP-MDD methodology.

B. Appendix - KPI
Table B.1: The KPIs selected for this study.
X0
Life Cycle Assessment (LCA)
The purpose of this criterion is to reduce emissions in the local and global en-
vironment, and to reduce resource consumption through a road constructions
lifetime and disposal
X1
Global warming
The purpose of this criterion is to reduce global warming by reducing the emis-
sions of global and local greenhouse gases associated with road projects
X2
CO2
The purpose of this criterion is to reduce CO2 emissions associated with road
projects
X3
Material consumption
The purpose of this criterion is to reduce the material consumption for the con-
struction of road projects
X4
Biodiversity
This criterion focuses on the biotopes of urban and rural areas for wild ﬂora and
fauna sufﬁcient to ensure genetic exchange between populations
X5
Resilience
This criterion is about the practice of designing systems to endure social, physical,
and economic stocks and stress
X6
Air Quality/Air Pollution
The purpose of this criterion is to reduce the pollution of air
X7
Water and soil protection (pollution)
The purpose of this criterion is to protect and improve water and soil resources in
connection with urban development and road projects.
X8
Energy consumption
The purpose of this criterion is to reduce energy consumption (primary energy
demand) and emissions to air, water and land, and to increase the share of
renewable energy.
X9
Waste management
The purpose of this criterion is to make the best use of waste resources and to
reduce the waste of resources.
X10
Life Cycle Cost (LCC)
The purpose of this criterion is to reduce the total life cycle cost and to achieve
a balance between construction and operating costs.
X11
Financial risk
The purpose of this criterion is to reduce the ﬁnancial risk that may be associated
with various projects.
X12
Financial effects in the municipality
The purpose of this criterion is to analyse the ﬁnancial effects in the municipality
and to promote a balance between the municipality’s costs and revenues.
X13
Local economic stability
The purpose of this criterion is to promote urban development in areas of high
local economic stability, which can increase the likelihood that the urban area is
fully realised and that it can be continuously adapted to market developments.
X14
Operation & Maintenance
The purpose of this criterion is that long-term maintenance of the road’s qualities
is achieved through cost-effective operation and maintenance. Operation and
maintenance have a major impact on both costs and environmental impact
in the long term. Often excess the operating and maintenance costs far the
construction costs.
X15
Construction site & process
The purpose of this criterion is to promote as efﬁcient and resource-economic
construction phase with as few impact as possible on the road project.
X16
Impact from construction process
The purpose of this criterion is to promote as efﬁcient a construction phase as pos-
sible with the fewest possible nuisance of the road project. This can be anything
from noise nuisance to degradation of air quality and irritation on bypass.
X17
Noise Reduction
The purpose of this criterion is to reduce the noise level.
This can be
done, for example, by choosing the right paving according to the type of
area/environment/surroundings.
X18
Public safety
The purpose of this criterion is to promote public safety by designing and plan-
ning roads to reduce accidents and inconveniences.
X19
The effect of the development of the local
community
The purpose of this criterion is to promote the connects of different areas, as well
as detours to reduce noise and pollution.
X20
Public health (impact)
The purpose of this criterion is the emission of substances that affect both the
surrounding community and the workers are reduced.
X21
Integrated planning
The purpose of this criterion is to increase the potential for value creation through
the integration of interdisciplinary competencies in the planning process.
X22
Information and Communication
The purpose of this criterion is to involve stakeholders and future citizens and users,
as well as political, organisational and ﬁnancial involvement, and to increase
integration with relevant municipal projects and plans.
X23
Mounting and retaining sustainable mea-
sures
The purpose of this criterion is to monitor and maintain the planned sustainable
measures throughout the process.
