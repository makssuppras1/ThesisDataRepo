Abstract
This thesis studies acoustophoretic particle motion in acoustofluidic devices. The acoustic
second order fields responsible for the acoustophoretic particle motion are the acoustic
radiation force and the streaming field which induces a stokes drag force on suspended
particles. The particle size determines which of the two is the dominating force. In typical
devices the stokes drag is the dominating force for sub-micron particles which hence follow
the streaming field, while larger particles are dominated by the acoustic radiation force.
Handling particles in a label-free and gentle manner has therefore mainly been possible
for micrometer-sized particles.
Recent experiments of acoustofluidic packed-bed devices have shown that they are
capable of trapping sub-micron-sized particles. These devices might then serve as a method
of high-throughput enrichment of nanometer-sized particles, such as extracellular vesicle
cells. This thesis contains numerical simulations of an acoustofluidic packed-bed device
resembling a two-dimensional version of the device used in the experiment. Through these
simulations a potential trapping mechanism for the nanoparticles was identified.
The
particles were confined in parts of fluid channel where the streaming field appeared like a
rotational field and caused a swirling-like motion that could confine the particles.
Furthermore two modes of operating the acoustofluidic device were investigated: oper-
ating at a constant voltage drop across the transducer electrodes (CV) or operating with
constant averaged electrical dissipated power (CP). Comparing the two modes of opera-
tion it was found that the constant power setup was most advantageous choice in typical
acoustofluidic devices, a conclusion that agrees with literature.

1.1
Acoustofluidics
Applying ultrasound in the low MHz range in microfluidic devices work particularly well
because the wavelengths may fit into the sub-millimetre sized channels and cavities [1].
Standing pressure waves may then form in the sub-milimeter sized fluid channels known as
resonance modes [1]. Fluidics combined with MHz acoustics is termed acoustofluidics [2].
A basic acoustofluidic device consists of an elastic solid вД¶sl containing a micrometre-sized
channel filled with a liquid вД¶fl which is glued using an adhesion layer вД¶ad onto a transducer
вД¶tr. A sketch of a typical acoustofluidic device displaying a fluid resonance can be seen in
Fig. 1.1. Operating acoustofluidic devices at resonance modes is advantageous since they
are stable, reproducible, a maximum of acoustic power is delivered from the transducer to
the fluid, and the spatial patterns of the resonance are controlled by the geometry of the
microfluidic channel [1].
Inside the fluid channel the acoustics give rise to two important second order acoustic
fields that cause acoustophoretic particle motion: The acoustic radiation force due to
scattering of acoustic waves and drag forces from the induced acoustic streaming [1, 3, 4].
Fig. 1.1a shows a particular motivating acoustophoretic motion where the particles
have gathered into the center of the channel due to the acoustic radiation force. The
acoustic streaming for such a resonance is shown in Fig. 1.1b, and the drag force arising
from the streaming field along with the radiation force acting on a particle are shown in
Fig. 1.1c. Sub-micron sized particles are typically dominated by the drag force and hence
follow the streaming field, while particles larger than about 1 ¬µm are dominated by the
acoustic radiation force [5].
Acoustofluidics hence allows manipulation of particle motion for micrometre sized-
particles in a gentle and label-free manner [6] and allows separation based on size, density
and compressibility of particles [7]. Acoustofluidic devices have been applied increasingly
in biology, environmental and forensic sciences, as well as clinical diagnostics [8], e.g.,
in cell synchronization [9], separation of bacteria and blood cells [10, 11], and particle
enrichment [12].

Acoustophoresis has largely been limited to manipulation of micrometre-sized parti-
cles [13] where the acoustic radiation force dominates. There is however great motivation
in acoustophoresis of sub-micrometre-sized particles since extracellular visicles (EVs) are
small-sized particles in the range of [30, 1000] nm [14вАУ16]. These particles are promising
candidates for biomarker discovery, offering potential as early disease detection, but iso-
lating them is a challenge due to the presence of abundant proteins and lipoproteins in
blood plasma [14, 17]. The current golden standard within EV isolation is ultracentrifu-
gation, but it requires long processing time, has large sample volume requirements, and
inconsistent EV purity and recovery [14, 16, 18].
Recently the Acoustofluidics Group at the Department of Biological Engineering, Lund
University, Sweden demonstrated that applying ultrasound to a packed-bed device causes
nanometer-sized particles to be trapped [14]. When the ultrasound was turned off the
trapped particles were released, so the device can serve as a way of high-throughput
isolation and enrichment of nanometer-sized particles [14].
Currently, acoustofluidic devices are mainly based on acoustically hard silicon or glass

1.2
Outline of this thesis
In this thesis many numerical simulations for acoustofluidic devices were conducted, with
the end goal of simulating a packed-bed acoustofluidic device. Only acoustofluidic devices
where the solid channel consists of the conventional high acoustic contrast materials such
as Pyrex and borosilicate glass were investigated. There reason is twofold. First of all the
resonance modes are simpler and hence served as a better starting point in the project
than polymers. Secondly, because experimental data was received for a packed-bed device
with a borosilicate glass channel, and a comparison between simulation and experiment
was needed.
The thesis is structured as follows: First the theory behind acoustofluidics is uncovered,
then the numerical simulation approach is described, followed by numerical validation.
Then two modes of operating acoustofluidic devices are discussed: operating with
a constant voltage drop or operating with constant electrically dissipated power. This
discussion is supplemented by comparing results from a simulation with literature.
Lastly the results of simulations for a packed-bed device operated in constant power
mode are presented and discussed. The results are compared with the experiment con-
ducted by the Acoustofluidic Group at Lund University, and a potential nano-particle
trapping mechanism is discussed.

Chapter 2
Theory
In this chapter the theory needed to describe acoustofluidic devices is introduced in which
classical continuum theory is used. The mathematical treatment of this theory is com-
plicated by containing nonlinear partial differential equations with tensors of up to rank
4. We therefore start out by defining the mathematical notation. Perturbation theory is
used to simplify the governing equations, and then constitutive equations are presented
which allow us to solve the governing equations.
A schematic over the typical devices and hence physical systems considered in this
thesis is shown in Fig. 1.1. The figure illustrates an object consisting of 4 different subdo-
mains: a fluid domain вД¶fl, two solid domains without electromechanical coupling вД¶sl, вД¶ad,
and a solid domain with electromechanical coupling, i.e., a transducer вД¶tr. The theory is
the same for the two solid domains without electromechanical coupling вД¶sl, вД¶ad so we will
only consider the three unique types of domains вД¶tr, вД¶sl, вД¶fl in this chapter.
2.1
Continuum fields
We will throughout this entire thesis make use of classical continuum field theory. This
means that we disregard the atomistic nature of matter, and instead consider matter as
continuous, as well as introduce matter particles. These matter particles should have a
probe volume вИЖвД¶in the mesoscopic regime in order for the theory to be valid.
The position of the matter particles is defined as their mass averaged or centre-of-
mass position (CoM), which will be denoted r(t). One can either use a Lagrangian or
Eulerian description, i.e., whether to let the considered probe volume вИЖвД¶follow the mass
particles in time, or to keep the volume fixed in time. In the Lagrangian description the
probe volume follows the mass particle and hence, in general, the CoM position has a time
dependence r(t), so then any Lagrangian field g will have the spatiotemporal argument
g(r(t), t).
As for the Eulerian description one keeps the probe volume fixed in time so вИВtr = 0,
but then the amount of molecules in that volume will, in general, be time dependent due
to the advection of molecules, and any field g defined in this volume will also be time
dependent g(r, t). The Lagrangian description is often used for solids, while the Eulerian

2.2
Mathematical notation
The mathematical notation will be like that of H. Bruus [21], so tensor fields that have
a rank higher than 0 will be written in bold font, g(r, t). Throughout this thesis g is
reserved to denote an arbitrary tensor field of arbitrary rank N, while A is reserved to
denote an arbitrary tensor of rank 1, i.e., a vector. The only tensor fields that will not be
written in bold font are tensor fields of rank 0, i.e., scalar fields. It should furthermore be
noted that often the spatiotemporal argument (r, t) is not written explicitly in order to
abbreviate the mathematical notation and ease readability.
This thesis considers many tensor fields of different ranks and a tensor fields of rank N
will have N associated directions and hence contain 3N components. The tensor fields of
rank 0, i.e., the scalar fields considered in this thesis are the mass density ѕБ, the pressure

Acoustic actuation strategies and
numerical validation
In acoustofluidics a standing pressure wave with a perfect nodal line in the ey-direction
in the fluid is an ideal configuration for acoustophoretic separation [1].
Two different
strategies of obtaining standing pressure waves are shown in Fig. 4.1, where the central
difference lies in the transducer.
In Fig. 4.1a the acoustics are actuated by a harmonically varying potential applied to
the top electrode and a grounded electrode in the bottom. PZT (PZ26) with a height of
1 mm has an in particular interesting resonance at about 2 MHz, where the transducerвАЩs
top and bottom surfaces are expanding and contracting along the ez-direction. Since the
transverse and longitudinal motion are coupled the y component of the displacement field
uy also has a variation along the top surface. The oscillating transducer surface can then
be used to actuate acoustic standing waves in a fluid by gluing a solid containing a fluid
to the top transducer electrode surface.
An alternative way of actuating the acoustics is shown in Fig. 4.1b, where a groove
has been extruded in the top electrode splitting it up in two separate electrodes that are
biased with a positive and negative electric potential. Intuitively this causes one of the
electrodes to contract while the other expands which actuates a motion that can be used
to create standing pressure waves in the ey-direction.
This thesis will mainly concentrate on the transducer type in Fig. 4.1a, because exper-
imental data was received for this transducer type, and it will be denoted the standard
transducer. The other transducer type Fig. 4.1b will be denoted as the split transducer.
The split transducer has been included in this thesis because it is effective in causing a
motion almost purely in the ey-direction, which was harder to achieve with the standard
transducer setup. The split transducer is mainly used as an important validation example
to validate that the governing equations and boundary conditions have been implemented
correctly in the numerical software COMSOL, in particular for the streaming field v2. The
only difference between the transducer setups lie in the implemented boundary conditions,

Chapter 5
Electrical impedance of
acoustofluidic devices
Acoustofluidic experiments are typically conducted by fixing the electrical dissipated
power, e.g. because the biological samples require the dissipated power to be bellow a
certain level. On the other hand, the numerical computations presented so far employ a
constant voltage on the electrodes as a Dirichlet boundary condition. We want to bridge
this gap by being able to conduct numerical computations that resemble the constant
dissipated electrical power setup. To do so we will introduce the electrical impedance Z
and the corresponding electrical admittance Y , since they relate the dissipated electrical
power with the applied potential over the device electrodes. Since the electric fields are
coupled with the mechanical fields in the transducer which are themselves coupled to the
adhesion, solid and fluid domains any mechanical or electrical resonance will affect the
impedance and hence the dissipated power. The admittance is frequency dependent, and
the potential difference over the electrodes must also be frequency dependent in order to
keep the electrical power constant. In this chapter we will show a derivation for the scaling
factor, that is to be implemented in the numerical computation, such that it resembles a
constant power setup.

The packed-bed device
As we have already seen in Section 4.3.1 F rad becomes incapable of focusing nano-particles
(ap вЙ≤1¬µm), since the stokes drag force caused by flowing along the streaming field begins
to dominate. There is however still a lot of motivation in attempting to control nano-
particles, as mentioned in the introduction Section 1.1, since, e.g., extracellular vesicles
such as exosomes are nanoparticles [14].
M. S. Gerlt and T. Laurell from an experimental group at the Acoustofluidics Group
at the Department of Biomedical Engineering, Lund University, Sweden, have recently
demonstrated a way to trap nano-particles and release them in a controlled manner. Let-
ting a fluid with a low concentration of nano-particles flow through such a trapping device
can then be a mechanism to up-concentrate the nano-particle content. The group at Lund
University found that filling up the fluid channel with large micrometer-sized seed parti-
cles (ap вЙИ50 ¬µm), caused the much smaller nanometre-sized particles to be trapped when
exciting the system acoustically.
Adapted figures from the paper by M. S. Gerlt and T. Laurell [14] are shown with
permission in Fig. 6.1. The device, which is called a packed-bed, is shown in the Fig. 6.1a
with a side and top cross sectional view and a photograph. In Fig. 6.1b the experimental
setup is shown, syringe pumps are connected to the packed-bed by a valve, so that either
the buffer liquid phosphate-buffered saline (PBS) or the sample containing the nano-
particles (PBS with nano-particles) could be inserted into the packed-bed device.
In
Fig. 6.1c the fluorescnece intensity can be seen for different stages of the experiment:
phase (I), phase (II), and phase(III). Before phase (I) (blue curve) the fluorscence signal
was recorded for 30s to establish a baseline. In phase (I) the nano-particles are inserted, in
phase (II) insertion of nano-particle stops and the buffer liquid is inserted instead, in phase
(III) the acoustics is turned off, releasing the nano-particles. The trapping efficiency is
determined by dividing the amount of released particles (green area) by the total amount
of particles (red+green area).

Chapter 7
Conclusion and outlook
In this thesis numerical studies of acoustofluidic systems has been conducted, involving
a piezoelectric transducer вД¶tr, an elastic solid channel вД¶sl containing a fluid вД¶fl which
is adhered to the transducer by an adhesion (glue) layer вД¶ad.
First two strategies of
actuating acoustic pressure waves inside the fluid were investigated, and the numerical
implementation was validated.
Then a comparison of operating the acoustofluidic device with constant dissipated
electrical power (CP) or constant voltage drop over the electrodes (CV) was conducted.
Since the governing equations for all first order fields are linear scaling the solutions with
K is allowed. In a numerical simulation the constant voltage fields are determined and
the scaling factor K give the constant power fields gCP = KgCV for all first order fields
and gCP = K2gCV for all second order fields. Hence, the mode of operation becomes
particularly important for the acoustic second order fields: the acoustic radiation force
F rad,CP responsible for the acoustophoretic motion and the streaming field vCP
2
which
the particles drag along.
The specific value of K can be found through the electrical
admittance between two of the electrodes, and will vary throughout a frequency sweep.
In typical acoustofluidic experiments there is a limit to the allowed average electrical dis-
sipated power [31], which causes K вЙ•1 when conducting frequency sweeps. It is hence
always advantageous to use the constant power setup. A conclusion which confirmed the
findings of F. Lickert, H. Bruus and M. Rossi. A specifically motivating example advocat-
ing for the use of the constant power setup was shown in a particle tracing simulation for
a resonance with K2 = 10.07 that verified the focusing time scaled as ѕД foc,CP = KвИТ2ѕД foc,CV.
A packed-bed device was then simulated, where the fluid channel вД¶fl was filled with 160
spherical seed particles in the (y, z) cross section, with a radius of ap = 50 ¬µm. Recently,
such a device was found capable of trapping nanoparticles by the Acoustofluidics Group at
the department of biomedical engineering, Lund University, but the cause is yet unknown.
In a simplified effective fluid model of the packed-bed device where the only man-
ifestation of the seed particles were an alteration of the fluid material parameters, the
magnitude of the admittance |Y | was compared with experimental data. Generally there
was a good agreement with experimental data, especially when the effective fluid damping

is a very significant fraction of the fluid domain in a packed-bed. Hopefully this will also
allow a better convergence on v2. Finally if the trapping mechanism was confirmed in
3D it would be interesting to simulate a polymer-based solid channel. In such a case the
acoustic mode is not as well confined in the fluid, which complicates the resulting acoustic
fields even further. However, the advantages of doing so is to enable inexpensive and easily
manufactured acoustofluidic devices allowing for one-time use cases.
