Abstract
This study investigates the potential for utilising distributed fiber optic strain sensing
technology to develop a novel plate used to determine soil deformation and strength char-
acteristics. This plate can be used to directly measuring the horizontal strains under
loading, which from the proposed method can be used to obtain the Young’s modulus of
the soil foundation. The strains are measured through Luna Optical Backscatter Reflec-
tometer 4600 due to its high spatial resolution and range. A numerical model is created
in ABAQUS in order to obtain formulations to infer the Young’s modulus based on the
strains measured, as well as executing a sensitivity analysis for investigating the effect
the of plate parameters.
The approach of the proposed method involves tracking the location of distinct points
of zero and maximum strain along the plate and relating their shift in location to the
changes in the elastic moduli of the supporting medium. The approach is direct, load-
independent, and allows for determination of the elastic moduli, assuming only that
the plate characteristics are known. In this thesis, the development of fundamental ex-
pressions that relate between the location of distinct strain points and the variation of
elastic moduli is first presented. Then, based on modelled results, the design process
of a prototype flexible strain sensing plate is described. Finally, as an initial validation
step the proposed method was demonstrated on a simple mechanical setup.
Two flexible plates of different sizes have been instrumented with distributed optical
fibers, in order to verify the prototype for two different plate sizes. These two plates are
loaded with different load radii, as well as through several loading steps. As foundation
support for these tests, four different expanded polystyrene plates have been used, with
different elastic modulus provided by the manufacturer. From evaluating the horizontal
stresses, the sensitivity of the measurements is evident. It is observed that for increasing
load radius, the inferred elastic modulus becomes closer to the elastic modulus provided.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Accurate prediction of soil deformation and strength characteristics are important to
ensure appropriate design of road pavements, mat- and raft foundations. The standard
approach for determination these characteristics are based on large-scale in-situ exper-
iments, i.e., the static Plate Load Test (PLT) [1, 2]. These tests involve application of
a load having known dimensions and intensity, and measurement of the resulting me-
chanical responses. Such procedure is slow and cumbersome; it is essentially relevant
for sparse time intervals. Portable lightweight devices may also be used, i.e., the Light
Weight Deflectometer (LWD) [3, 4]. Although, these methods are faster, their interpre-
tation often involve crude assumptions.
In order to overcome some of the aforementioned limitations and assumptions, this thesis
investigates the potential for utilising distributed fiber optic strain sensing technology
to develop a novel plate load test that are: (i) practical (i.e., light-weight and fast), (ii)
direct (i.e., no post-processing or advanced analysis involved) and (iii) accurate (i.e.,
minimizing model assumptions).
The thesis commences with a literature review presenting the current industry standard
methods for determination of soil deformation and strength characteristics, as well as an
overview of the leading strain sensing technologies trending in geotechnical engineering.
From the literature review the thesis objectives are defined, followed by methodology
utilised and development of interpretation method for inference of soil elastic moduli.
Then, the development of the flexible strain plate and experimental design of a small-
scale mechanical setup is discussed. Finally, a sensitivity analysis and interpretation
of experimental results are discussed; followed by a summary of the main findings and
recommendations for future work.

2.1
Standard Methods for in-situ characterization of
soils
This section presents the standard methods for performing in-situ characterization of
soils. Determining soil characteristics is vital in order to safely execute infrastructure
projects. If the soil is overestimated, the costs of the projects will likely be higher than
necessary, while the safety will be compromised if the soil is underestimated. In addition
to this, testing of soil may be time consuming and difficult to interpret in a short window
of time.
When determining the characteristics of the soil, two methods have been commonly used
in the industry; the static Plate Load Test (PLT) and the Light Weight Deflectometer
(LWD), respectively. These two tests are executed in-situ. Here, the elastic modulus
can be found for each test, by the help of assumptions related to the behaviour of the soil.
Both of these methods can be used to increase the understanding of the behaviour of
the desired ground under known loading conditions. From this, the capacity of the soil
can be further investigated, such as settlement predictions.
2.1.1
Plate Load Test
The Plate Load Test (PLT) is a test used for investigating the capacity of the ground,
as well as settlements for the past decades. Further, the test can be used for evaluation
and design of pavement structures [5]. The shear strength of the soils, as well as the
modulus of the subgrade reaction can be determined through this method. This method
is also commonly used for compression of materials with grain size diameter less than
150 mm [6]. The PLT is popular when designing for shallow foundations.
In order to execute the PLT, certain requirements to the apparatus need to be met. A
loading device with sufficient weight is required in order to influence the surface that
is under investigation. Further, a hydraulic jack with sufficient capacity is required in
order to apply the load on the surface. In addition to this, a set of bearing plates are
required to ensure rigidity. The diameter of these bearing plates needs to vary for rigid-
ity to be achieved. As the load is placed on this surface, it is desirable to measure the
deflection of the surface. For this to be achieved, dial gauges are used to measure the
==PAGE:18==
4

deflection. These dial gauges will be mounted on a deflection beam. A plate load test
can be executed as long as the above mentioned requirements are met. A plate load test
is illustrated in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: Illustration of a Plate Load Test [7].
This method has been used in the industry, where it is common that a truck or trailer
is used as the loading device. Both of the wheels will then be used as supporting points,
where the wheels should be at a considerable distance from the circumference of the
applied bearing plates. The loading of the soil is done through several loading and un-
loading steps. This is done at a given rate with uniform increments. Inhomogeneities in
the soil may occur. This needs to be considered when executing this method and can
be solved by adding materials such as fine sand in order to obtain a full contact.
Results are obtained for each load increment, where both the normal stress and the
settlement at the middle of the plate is measured. This data is further used to calculate
the strain modulus which is dependent on both the radius of the loading plate, as well
as the maximum average normal stress [8]. As well as the strain modulus, the subgrade
reaction can be calculated from the average normal stress.
However, the PLT allows for a range of stresses, and measurements of the deflection can
be done for both the loading phase, as well as the unloading. This opens for a more
accurate estimating of deflections of recoverable soils [3].
It is evident that in order to execute the PLT, large and heavy equipment is necessary.
Due to this, the influenced zone will be larger than the investigated depth of the ground.
The contact pressure between the loaded plate and the soil is not completely known, as
full contact cannot be verified. Due to the short duration of the loading, the settlements
measured are not the ultimate settlements.

2.1.2
Light Weight Deflectometer
Due to the slow and cumbersome nature of the PLT device, the Light Weight Deflectome-
ter (LWD) have increased in popularity over the past decades. The LWD is a portable
device used to rapidly investigate the properties of surface soils [9]. In addition to this,
the use of LWD is common in relation to construction quality control [3]. The device is
approximately 20 kg, and a test can be executed in two minutes at the test location.
For the LWD device, the plate style and plate diameter may vary. The essential compo-
nents of the LWD are defined as a loading plate, a geophone as a response sensor, load
housing, a mounting stud, a force transducer, a pre-load nut, an urethane load damper,
drop mass, and a guide rod. These components are illustrated in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Illustration of a Light Weight Deflectometer [9].
The LWD test is simple, as an impulse load is applied onto the loading plate and further
into the soil as a drop mass. The impulse time varies between 15 and 30 ms and the
magnitude of the max force is varying between 7 kN and 20 kN [9]. A geophone is mea-
suring the response of the surface, and the soil modulus ELWD derived from the input
and response of the surface can be derived.
The deflection of the plate is determined through the geophone sensor used for the LWD
test. Similar to the PLT, the LWD calculates the elastic modulus through the applied
stress, deflection and radius of the plate, in addition to a shape factor depending on
==PAGE:20==
6
2 Literature Review
an assumption of the stress distribution. Further, all LWD devices calculate the elastic
modulus by the use of elastic half-space theory [4].
The device calculates the peak deflection by integrating the signal from the geophone.
A consequence of this is that the maximum deflection usually does not occur at the
same instant as the peak load. Another consequence is that in addition to recoverable
deflection, the maximum deflection may also include some permanent-plastic deflection
[3].
As the device is portable and can be operated by one man, the execution of the LWD
test is not considered time consuming. However, the correct interpretation of the data
is essential, for example when assuming the stress distribution. This understanding is
important in regards to the influence depth, as well as relationship between stress and
strain for both the lab and the field. As the load is dynamic the loading rate could be
a limitation. Further, the stiffness of the buffer will also affect the measurements made
with the LWD device [4].
2.2
Distributed Fiber-optic Strain Sensing
In recent years, new sensing technologies have been developed for structural health
monitoring of traditional civil engineering structures. One leading technology in this
connection is distributed fiber-optic strain sensing. This technology allows for continu-
ous measurements of strain along a fiber-optic cable. Different to traditionally sensing
technologies, the distributed fiber-optic is not depending on any specific points along
the measured path. The amount of accurate, spatially resolved data obtained from the
distributed fiber-optic strain sensors is unprecedented [10].
Light is transmitted into the fiber, where the light pulses interacts with the medium
particles and acoustic waves [10]. These interactions are called scattering, and occurs
due to imperfections in the material structure within the medium. An important effect
in fiber-optic sensing is backscatter, which is when the scattered light is transmitted in
the opposite direction to the propagation direction [10]. The more imperfections and
impurities, the more lateral scattering will occur, in addition to the scattering in the
forward direction as for a homogeneous material. There are three scattering effects that
are considered to be the main effects of light scattering; Raman, Rayleigh and Brillouin
scattering, respectively.
What sets the optical fibers apart from for example metallic wires is that that the fibers
guides light signals representing information. This makes it possible to transport more
information faster and over long distances. Depending on the characteristics of light
transport, the fibers are differentiated between single and multi mode. While the single
mode fiber has a small core, the multi mode fiber has a larger core. Due to the larger
core of the multi mode fiber, the fiber is considerably larger compared to the single mode

fiber, as well as it typically is operating on a lower wavelength.
2.2.1
Geotechnical applications
Although the high resolution of the distributed fiber-optics makes way for accurate mon-
itoring of small scale projects, large structures can also benefit from the use of the fiber-
optics. Strain sensing is not yet common for geotechnical applications. However, the
utilization of strain sensing within the geotechnical topic has been investigated through
several papers where different cases are discussed.
2.2.1.1
Piles
The use of strain sensing has been investigated for a concrete bored pile, where the tem-
perature has been monitored during curing of the pile [11]. To measure the temperature,
fiber-optic sensing cables are embedded in the pile while casting the concrete, where the
aim was to conclude on the thickness of the concrete cover with respect to the heat trans-
fer process in relation to the adjacent ground. For the investigation conducted by Rui et
al. (2017), the distributed fiber-optic sensing cables were attached to the reinforcement
cage of the pile at four different axial directions, which provides result presenting the
change in temperature distributed along the axial direction of the whole pile.
Gao et al. (2019) also investigated the use of distributed fiber-optic sensing cables for
a bored concrete pile. Here, the deformation of the pile was investigated, which can be
done by monitoring the strains along the axial direction of the pile. Similar to Rui et al.
(2017), the Brillouin optical time domain reflectometer technology is used for monitoring.
The results from this study provides additional data for the deformation characteristics
of this type of pile, that can be used as support. Figure 2.3 illustrates how the optical
fiber is fixed to the reinforcement in a bored concrete pile.

2.2.1.2
Monitoring of landslides and structures
Landslide is a geomorphic concept where the mass in a slope changes state from stable
to unstable, and starts moving. Such mass can be soil, sand or rocks, and this movement
is largely caused by the poor stability of the slope, as well as the gravity force.
Distributed fiber-optics can be used to identify the evolution of landslides, by creating
a large scale physical model of the respective slope according to Schenato et al. (2017).
From this experiment, it was evident that the use of distributed fiber-optics can detect
precursory signs of failure, which then makes room for a further improvement of the
warning systems related to landslides [13]. However, placing the fiber-optical cable in
an extreme environment like this is difficult due to the sensitivity of the fiber-optics.
Similar to monitoring landslides, there is a large potential in monitoring structures by
the use of fiber-optics. By placing distributed fiber-optic sensors in beams, columns and
other structural components of the building, the building can be accurately monitored
during the whole of its lifetime. Further, it is possible to create a digital model of the
building, where every change in strain will be illustrated virtually in that model. With
this in place, it will be easier to schedule maintenance of the building.
2.2.1.3
Sprayed Concrete Lining of Tunnels
Distributed fiber-optics can be embedded in sprayed concrete lining in tunnels, in order
to monitor the changes in strains. An accurate continuous monitoring of strains in the
tunnel lining will make easier to localize areas in the sprayed concrete lining where prob-

lems will occur, as the non-uniformly soil pressure is difficult to monitor by single point

Embedding distributed fiber-optics in sprayed concrete lining has been done in the Cross-

2.2.1.4
Pavements
Chapeleau et al. (2017) investigated detection of cracking in pavements, which is an
increasing problem due to increased traffic. This study was an attempt to replace the
visual inspection with sensors placed in the pavements. By placing sensors in the pave-
ments, the change in strains could be monitored in a non-destructive way. Through this
article, an increase in strains was monitored before any visible damage. Later, cracks
occurred in the places where the increase in strains were observed, thus indicating a
relationship between areas with increased strains and cracks [15].
Levenberg et al. (2020) proposed a novel method to characterize the support of soil
in slab-on-grade constructions, such as rigid pavements and raft foundations [16]. By
instrumenting fiber-optic cables on these constructions, the spatial profiles can be ana-
lyzed by continuously measuring the strains generated by applied loads. In this article,
the concept was developed on a Pasternak support model, where the parameters related
to such a model can be characterized by analyzing the strain profiles, rather than the
magnitudes of the strains. In this study, the location of zero-crossing points, as well
as the negative peak strain are considered points of interest (POI). Skar (2019) demon-
strated the proposed method on a small-scale mechanical setup.
By having the unitless Pasternak parameter (b) as the abscissa, the location of the
POI is normalized. From the results, it is seen that whether the subgrade should be
represented by a Winkler support model or a Pasternak support model can be seen during
the evaluation of the soil. As the identification of the POI is non-destructive, this can be
done repeatedly under service conditions. This type of non-destructive characterization
of the soil support is essential for facility owners, in order to evaluate and monitor the
structure in a reliable manner. This method needs further development in terms of the
capability of the distributed strain-sensing when identifying the POIs [16].

2.3
Summary
From the litereture review, it is evident that the two most common test used nowadays,
the PLT and the LWD test, have limitations in terms of accurately estimating the foun-
dation support while also being time-efficient.
When executing the PLT, large and heavy equipment is necessary and will affect the
ground severely. Also due to the large equipment, the test is not considered particularly
time efficient. Although it is measuring the settlements of the ground, these settlements
are not the ultimate settlements, due to the short loading. When calculating the pa-
rameters through the data received from this test, it is based on the assumption of the
contact pressure, as this is not known.
Similar to the PLT, the contact pressure used for calculations is also assumed for the
LWD test. However, for the LWD the contact pressure is assumed based on different
soil types and whether the plate is considered flexible or rigid. This also puts a lot of
trust in the person interpreting the data obtained. Further, the maximum deflection
usually does not occur at the same instant as the peak load.
The use of distributed fiber-optic strain sensing is currently increasing, as it is being
used in attempts to make traditional civil engineering structures smarter by continu-
ously measuring strains and temperature changes. Skar et al. (2019) and Levenberg
et al. (2020) used the distributed fiber-optic sensing technology to investigate the loca-
tion of the zero-crossing points of the strain measurements rather than the strain itself.
This approach appears promising and can be directly transferred to the PLT experiment.
This idea would be applicable to different foundation support types, which opens for
further investigations. By attempting to develop a method based on these principles,
assumptions in regards to contact pressure as seen for the PLT and LWD could be limited.
This method would then be a more direct method, load-independent method, which
would, if successful, provide a reliable alternative to the two most common methods
used in the industry.

Objective
The purpose of this project is to contribute to the development of more accurate in-

3.2.1
Luna Optical Backscatter Reflectometer 4600
For the present study fiber-optic technology and the Luna Optical Backscatter Reflec-
tometer 4600 (OBR) apparatus will be utilized. The Luna Optical Backscatter Reflec-
tometer 4600 measures reflections in an optical system by the use of swept-wavelength
coherent interferometry [17]. In other words, this technology measures the full scalar
response of the testing system, including information about both amplitude and phase.
Thus, this apparatus can be used to locate places in the fiber with crimps or bad splices
or track polarization. By implementing the Distributed Sensing option in the OBR, it
is possible to measure both temperature changes and strains in the optical fiber. Due
to the high resolution and sensitivity that the OBR offers, it is suitable to low scale
experiments where the fiber span is short. The OBR device is presented in Figure 3.1.
==PAGE:26==

Figure 3.1: Luna Optical Backscatter Reflectometer 4600 [18].
By launching a probe signal into the fiber, the return loss can be measured through the
reflectometry method. The return loss is caused mainly by either Rayleigh backscatter
and Fresnel back reflection. While Fresnel reflection is caused my air gaps in the optical
fiber, the Rayleigh backscatter is caused by the impurities in the fiber core. The OBR,
compared to the other reflectometers, does not have the problem with large dead zones
in the fibre, due to its high spatial resolution. Due to the ability to measure spectral
shift in the Rayleigh backscatter with a high frequency, the OBR apparatus can be used
to measure both strain and temperature changes in the core of the fiber [17]. The OBR
can be used to characterize short fiber networks in labs, due to its combination of high
spatial resolution and range.

4.1
Practical considerations
When designing the experiment in the laboratory, several practical considerations needs
to be taken into account. By considering these practical issues on beforehand, it may
be easier to discover potential limitations and inaccuracies in the experiment.
As the strains in this plate will be measured by distributed fiber-optic sensing, it is
important to consider the placement of the fiber, the type of fiber, and also how the
fiber will be attached to the plate. Some initial tests will be done on test beams, in
order to investigate the effect of the different fibers and gluing, as well as determining
the stiffness of the plate.
The amount of weight used as load, and the placement of it also need to be considered.
As the laboratory only have cylindrical weights of either 0.5 kg and 1.0 kg, certain limi-
tations in regards of the height of the applied load exists. Further, as the weights will
be placed on the top of the beam, a component that allows for the fiber to be placed
between the flexible plate and the applied load needs to be developed.
For this project, the flexible plates will be of the material plexiglas, with the parameters
mentioned in Chapter 3. In addition to weight used to load the plexiglas plates, weights
have also been added to the plate in strategical places in an attempt to ensure full con-
tact between the plexiglas plate and the EPS plate. Through the preliminary studies
it was discovered that full contact between the plexiglas plate and the EPS plate was
not achieved without added weights. By adding 18 kg to P250, and 24 kg to P500, full
contact may be assumed.

An understanding of the sensitivity to a parameter change is obtained by investigating

8.2
Discussion
As the inferred Young’s moduli for both a = 12.5 mm and a = 25 mm provide values
much higher than the Young’s moduli provided by the manufacturer, it questionable
whether the experiment is valid for load radii lower than a = 35 mm. Although these
radii are following equations obtained from the numerical model specifically for these
radii, it is seen that the Young’s moduli increases significantly for a decrease in load ra-
dius. As seen previously, the results appear to deviate from a log-linear trend for smaller
load radii, which makes it questionable whether results from these radii are worth con-
sidering.
By comparing the inferred Young’s moduli for P250 and P500, it is evident that the
difference between the results for the two plates is not significant. For P250, both of the
measurements made on EPS150 provides Young’s moduli that deviates a lot from the
==PAGE:63==
8.2 Discussion
49
provided Young’s moduli. When considering that the values for the other EPS plates
are relatively close to the provided values, it may be assumed that the difference lays in
the execution of the tests in the laboratory.
As this is an experiment done in a laboratory, there are a lot of different factors that may
cause inaccuracies in the data obtained from the plate strain. Although it is assumed
that the added weights creates full contact between the EPS-plate and the plexiglas plate,
it is not unlikely that the treatment of the plexiglas plate in the workshop may have
deformed the plate. This might affect the results to a high degree, as the fiber-optics are
very sensitive. The same is the case for the gluing of the fiber, as a minor difference in
tensioning of the fiber when gluing may cause a larger difference when measuring strains.
Further, it is also assumed that the plate is fully circular, which also is unlikely consider-
ing that it is cut manually in the workshop. However, this is not assumed to affect the
data obtained much, as it is the strains close to the middle of the plate that the ratios
are based on. It is also assumed that the load placed on the plate is equally distributed.
Any deviations from this placement will affect the ratios greatly, which might have been
the case for the measurements done for P250 on EPS150.

CHAPTER 9
Conclusion
9.1
Summary
In this project, the development of a prototype flexible strain sensing plate to determine
soil characteristics of a foundation was presented. This interpretation method aimed to
be direct, non-destructive and load-independent. At first, a method for interpreting the
soil response was developed based on plate theory. Secondly, a small scale plate experi-
ment was designed in order to investigate the plate properties. After that, a sensitivity
analysis was executed to investigate relevant sources of error related to the plate strain.
Finally, a test of the proposed method in a small-scale demonstration was executed.
This prototype offers a substitute to the standard methods, as they are considered ex-
pensive and time consuming, in addition to being less accurate as they are relying on
several assumptions. The proposed method aims to be practical, direct and accurate, by
being light, no advanced analysis involved, and also minimizing the assumptions. Fur-
ther, the proposed method exploits the potential offered by the distributed fiber optic
sensing, that is high resolution and the ability to perform continuous measurements.
Difficulties with this prototype have been to perform similar tests with changing condi-
tions such as two plexiglas plates, four different stiffnesses of the EPS plates and the
added weights. Although precautions have been done in order to make the tests as iden-
tical as possible, it is likely that the asymmetry and deviations in strain measurements
are a result of some of the changing conditions.
9.2
Findings
From this study, findings in regards to developing this prototype was made. Firstly,
the sensitivity of the fiber optics makes it demanding to create the ”perfect experiment”.
The slightest change in experiment setup results in different measurements, which makes
interpretation difficult. This will especially be a challenge when applying this to the in-
dustry, due to the more harsh environment. However, the change in experiment setup
can be solved by reducing the variables. For example, not changing plexiglas plate each
time will be an improvement, as well as reducing the added weights if possible. It is also
possible to increase the amount of tests executed. In this way, statistics can easier be
==PAGE:66==
52
9 Conclusion
used to neglect bad measurements.
Secondly, by increasing the load radius of the applied load, a more expected value of the
inferred Young’s modulus is obtained. From observations, a smaller load radius provides
values for the Young’s modulus that is not realistic.
Thirdly, the proposed method of inferring Young’s modulus for different foundation
supports appears promising for a load radius a = 35 mm.
Although the deviations
between the provided Young’s moduli and the inferred are not negligible, the inferred
Young’s moduli remains reasonably close to the provided. This is positive, as this project
then can be used for further studies.
9.3
Future work
This thesis provides a development of a prototype flexible strain sensing plate. Although
the results are uplifting, this is still a prototype where many improvements can be made.
The loading arrangement of this prototype is simple, as the load are placed manually
on the plate. Rubber is glued under one of the weights to allow for the fiber to cross.
This could be modified further, as this affects the load radius and the stress distribution.
As one of the goals for this prototype is to make it simple, the weights added in order
to assume that full contact is achieved is contradicting this goal. However, it should
be relatively simple to create a new arrangement to achieve full contact between the
two plates. Due to the treatment of these plexiglas plates in the workshop, it is worth
considering that the properties changes when cutting it manually. A solution to this
could be to order the plates with the preferred dimensions.
A simpler project could also be achieved by using strain gauges or a more practical tech-
nology to measure the strains, as the fiber optic may be too sensitive for this prototype
considering all of the variables related to conducting the experiment.
It is seen that the a more expected Young’s modulus is inferred by applying a larger
load radius. Therefore it would be interesting to do further studies with a larger load
radii. The same goes for the plate sizes, as more asymmetry occurred for P250 than for
P500, as well as the fact that P500 provided more suitable results for comparison with
the Young’s moduli provided by the manufacturer. It would also be interesting to inves-
tigate the same theories for the case of a flexible beam, instead of a flexible circular plate.
Although the goal for this prototype is be simple, the fiber optic could be instrumented
more thoroughly on the flexible plate. By instrumenting the plate more thoroughly,
effects such as lack of contact may be easier to observe and locate, which would be
beneficial when interpreting the obtained results from each executed test.
