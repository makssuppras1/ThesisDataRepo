Abstract
A main challenge in deploying renewable energy sources is uncertainty in power
generation. Fluctuations in wind speed or solar irradiation affect investment deci¬≠
sions on wind and solar projects. Hybrid power plants (HPPs) counteract power
fluctuations by combining different generation technologies within a shared elec¬≠
trical infrastructure. Since the electrical system is a main cost driver, this thesis
aims to minimize collection system cost for a wind solar HPP under consideration
of system reliability. A sequential optimization framework, consisting of two steps,
is used to design the radial cable layout of the HPP. Both steps apply a two¬≠stage
stochastic optimization to incorporate variability in power generation and cable
failure. A scenario tree of generation and failure scenarios account for stochastic¬≠
ity within the mixed integer linear program. In the first step the model determines
cable layout investment decisions. Therein the objective function optimizes initial
investment through adjusting radial cable topology and cable sizing, as well as
expected reliability cost based on cable failures that cause energy curtailment.
This is based on a reduced number of generation scenarios due to time series
clustering. In a second step the collection system cost is calculated based on a
generation time series with hourly resolution and the obtained cable layout, i.e.,
the recourse problem is solved. Different HPP layouts are investigated, while vary¬≠
ing the failure parameters and comparing the results with a deterministic model
that assumes no failures of any component.

1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Renewable energy sources (RES) like wind or solar energy are an option to de¬≠
liver clean energy and counteract rising CO2¬≠emissions. Even though both are
available on a global scale, their variability poses major challenges, technically
and from an economic perspective. Hybrid power plants (HPPs) combine these
technologies to improve the system regarding system stability, power quality and
potentially levelized cost of energy (LCOE). [1]. The connection of different gen¬≠
eration technologies to the same shared electrical collection system enables re¬≠
duction in power fluctuation. HPPs can utilize similar AC MV collection systems
than wind farms. While collection system cost amount for 18% of relative cost of
offshore wind farms, it resembles 8% of relative cost of onshore wind farms [2].
Therein, cable cost depends on accessibility, required cable length and capac¬≠
ity [3]. A straightforward approach to reduce investment cost is decreasing the
required cable length. However, wind farm and HPP planning is a complex pro¬≠
cess, which includes wind and solar resource assessment, layout definition that
accounts for aerodynamic and thermal effects and maintenance, as well as deter¬≠
mination of cable layout among others. Thus, optimizing an HPP over its whole
life cycle poses a multidisciplinary optimization problem with several competing
trade¬≠offs to consider [4].
Due to the synergies between wind, solar and battery storage, small¬≠scale and util¬≠
ity sized HPPs are intensively researched [5]. Since the deployment of Kennedy
Energy Park, the world‚Äôs first utility¬≠scale hybrid wind, solar and storage project
in 2018, many HPP projects have been installed and proposed [6]. Alone in the
United States 226 hybrid projects have been installed at the end of 2020 with
a generating capacity of 29.4GW and 0.8GW storage capacity with additional
projects in the pipeline [7]. Additional large HPP projects are Kavithal HPP (78.8
MW) in India or Haringvliet HPP (64 MW) in the Netherlands, showing that HPPs
are a global technology [8].
However, optimizing an HPP project includes consideration of investment cost,
as well as operation cost. In order to account for the reliability of HPP collection
systems, cable failure and resulting cost of lost energy production have to be in¬≠
cluded. Current studies on offshore and onshore wind farm collection systems
address reliability within their optimization approach [9], while this study focuses
on applying a related approach to a wind solar HPP.

Problem definition and assumptions
As stated in section 1.1, HPP projects are multi¬≠disciplinary, while this report fo¬≠
cuses on a single trade¬≠off within the optimization. Offshore wind projects include
a sequence of optimization problems to obtain a competitive design [10]. A similar
approach can be applied to HPP projects, as seen in Figure 1.1.

This project will focus on cable layout optimization of a wind solar HPP, while
taking cable failures into account. Figure 1.2 illustrates how the design inputs and
assumptions interact when determining an optimal design.
Figure 1.2: Flowchart for HPP collection system design based on [11].
Based on sizing decisions the number of WTs and PV panels serves as input for
the design. Spacing restrictions for the WTs take wake effects into account, while
a minimum distance between WTs and PV panels is deployed to avoid shadow¬≠
ing of PV panels. In addition, the dimensions and distances of PV modules define
the overall PV array location from which the PV inverter locations are determined.

The WT and PV array positions are summarized into the overall HPP layout, which
is assumed fixed during the collection system optimization. Additional inputs like
cable cost, electricity cost, reliability parameters as well as solar and wind power
time series influence the optimization, thus the design. The resulting design con¬≠
tains the cable layout connecting all WTs and PV inverters and the power flow
through each cable at each generation event. Moreover, it includes the amount of
energy curtailment caused by energy production above cable capacity and cable
failure. The optimized cable layout results in an optimal solution value as sum of
cable investment cost and expected cost of curtailed energy. In order to limit the
scope of the model, several assumptions are made:
‚ÄĘ There is no spacing constraint for the HPP. The needed area for a specific
layout does not affect investment cost.
‚ÄĘ WT and PV inverter locations are assumed to be on a 2D plane. Uneven
terrain, which would increase distances between e.g. WTs are ignored.
‚ÄĘ There are no blocked areas within the HPP due to e.g. inaccessible soil
conditions.
‚ÄĘ HPP sizing and generator layout optimization are outside the scope of this
work. WT and PV array positions are chosen based on spacing constraints.
‚ÄĘ Wake effects and shadowing of PV panels are ignored, due to priorly men¬≠
tioned spacing constraints.
‚ÄĘ Constant discount rates and electricity prices are assumed throughout the
whole project lifetime.
‚ÄĘ Constant reliability parameters are used. Fault and repair rates do not change
over time.
‚ÄĘ To reduce the problem size only failures in cables are considered. The N¬≠1
criterion is assumed. Only failure scenarios with a single cable failure are
considered. Two or more cables cannot fail at the same time.

2
State of the art
2.1
Hybrid power plants
2.1.1
Definition of HPP
HPPs combine different generation technologies within one plant and a shared
connection to the grid. They can be defined as power systems that consist of a
renewable and conventional energy source or several RES that may include a
conventional energy source. They can operate in grid¬≠connected or stand¬≠alone
mode [12]. In the context of climate change, renewable HPPs gain in relevance.
Assuming wind as primary RES, renewable HPPs can combine wind and solar PV,
wind and battery storage, or wind, solar PV and battery storage. The stochastic¬≠
ity of wind and solar time series has complementary nature and enables higher
overall energy output and capacity factor, especially in regions with higher diurnal
or seasonal complementarity [1]. Adding battery energy storage systems (BESS)
enables storage of excess energy, which would be curtailed otherwise. This can
increase AEP and capacity from the view of the point of common coupling (PCC)
[13]. The electrical infrastructure of an HPP is designed to connect all generation
technologies to a PCC. Thus, all generated power is transmitted through the PCC
cancelling out power fluctuations partially. Overplanting is applied to utility¬≠scale
HPPs, where the installed nominal power exceeds the connection capacity at the
PCC. This results in higher capacity factors and better utilization of the electrical
infrastructure [14].
2.1.2
HPP configurations
There are three main configurations of HPPs, which define the electrical coupling
of the present RES. Co¬≠located HPP solutions, WTG¬≠coupled solutions and DC¬≠
coupled HPP solutions [1]. An overview of these configurations for the wind and
solar PV case is shown in Figure 2.1.
BESS are excluded, since this report focuses on a wind solar PV HPP. Utility¬≠scale
HPPs, which are currently in operation, tend to have a co¬≠located configuration.
The WTs and solar arrays within co¬≠located HPPs have their own points of connec¬≠
tion (POC) and are connected to a shared substation. This substation connects
the HPP, as a single power system, to the grid. As shown in Figure 1.1, the control
system is also included in HPP design. Global plant controller require advanced
functionalities to aggregate WT and solar PV assets into a single system.

onshore wind farms is the doubly¬≠fed induction generator (DFIG). This technol¬≠
ogy uses back to back converters enabling functionalities like fault¬≠ride through
or reactive power control for the WT. Even though DC collection systems for wind
farms are researched [16, 17], the predominant collection systems are AC based.
As a consequence, DC collection systems have currently not been deployed for
utility¬≠scale wind integrated HPPs.
2.1.3
Market opportunities and advantages
HPPs enable access to new revenue streams on the day¬≠ahead and balancing
market. Reduced energy curtailment allows system operators to offer more en¬≠
ergy on the market. The reduction in power fluctuations enables to deliver en¬≠
hanced ancillary services. When including BESS, HPPs allow a certain degree
of energy arbitrage. Energy can be stored during high production low demand

‚ÄĘ License to operate and enter: HPP operators can use synergies between

with existing grid codes.
‚ÄĘ Power loss reduction: Active power loss can be reduced by improving reac¬≠

through symbiotic control of PV and wind power plants [20].

Where F(t) is the cumulative distribution function of the system, and f(t) its prob¬≠

Over time different interpretations of failure behaviour in technical components

2.3
Cable layout optimization
Since HPPs combine WTs and PV arrays, design approaches from wind farms
or PV systems are adapted for HPPs. Examples for application areas of opti¬≠
mization methods are the cable layout, WT layout or access roads to WTs of wind
farms. However, due to their discontinuous nature, gradient¬≠based optimization
methods tend to be less viable for those problems [31]. A reason for that is the
unavailability or unreliability of the derivative of objective function and constraints
[32]. In these cases a gradient¬≠based method cannot use the derivative informa¬≠
tion effectively to determine the global optimum. Compared to this, gradient¬≠free
discrete methods like heuristics, metaheuristics and mixed integer linear program¬≠
ming (MILP) frameworks have proven suitable for e.g. cable layout optimization.
Heuristics are fast, but do not guarantee feasible solutions. Metaheuristics tend
to provide feasible solutions, while the solution quality cannot be determined with
certainty. In contrast, the MILP framework provides certainty of solution quality
when applying state¬≠of¬≠the¬≠art algorithms as the branch¬≠and¬≠cut, while requiring
more computational time and external solvers [31]. Within this report a MILP is
applied, using the commercial IBM ILOG CPLEX solver [33]. The HPP cable lay¬≠
out optimization is formulated as MILP problem as seen in chapter 4. Further,
the applied CPLEX solver uses enhanced versions of the classic branch¬≠and¬≠cut
approach, being currently the only way to exactly address this class of problems.

4.1
Optimization framework
Between 2000 and 2015 wind power plant optimization has evolved and manual
design approaches were replaced by optimization that maximizes AEP. Prioriza¬≠
tion of AEP led to less favorable layouts and infrastructure models when consid¬≠
ering other aspects in the system like e.g. collection system design. From 2015
onwards the trend goes towards more advanced LCOE optimization to account
for various sub¬≠systems within wind energy systems [52]. Transferring these ap¬≠
proaches onto HPPs requires new design considerations. Thus, optimization ob¬≠
jectives should also address overall plant profitability. A possible way to do this
is using NPV within the objective function, as described in subsection 3.5.3 and
subsection 4.3.1. An outline for the optimization approach is given in Figure 4.1. A
sequential optimization framework is used, which consists of two steps. In the first
step the clustered power time series defined in section 3.4 and remaining inputs
from chapter 3 are combined. These inputs are used to calculate the optimum
cable layout and cable investment cost by solving a MILP through CPLEX.
Figure 4.1: Flowchart for collection system optimization.
The optimum cable layout solution corresponds to a set of binary variables. This
fixed set is given into the second step of the optimization reducing the MILP into
a linear problem. As a result the second step can be computed comparably fast.
Further, it allows to use the full time series of 8760 generation scenarios to ob¬≠
tain the exact optimal solution, flow variables and curtailment variables. Without

to objective function, design variables, constraints and applied models. There
are several ways to classify types of optimization in the literature. However, the
present case is classified according to the approach found in [54]. Since the
design variables are binary as well as continuous, neither solely continuous nor
solely discrete variables are present. The result is a mixed variable problem. In
regard to the objective function, a single objective is minimized, classifying the
problem as single¬≠objective. As seen from subsection 4.3.3, various constraints
are applied to the problem, leading to a constrained optimization. From the binary
variables and partially discrete constraints it is concluded, that in terms of smooth¬≠
ness the problem is discontinuous. The discrete nature of the problem implies a
non¬≠convex classification. Even though the model incorporates stochastic param¬≠
eters like failure rates, it is classified as deterministic. The stochastic inputs are
defined before the optimization, and the same input, e.g. same failure rates, will
lead to the same output. Repeated evaluation of the problem with the same input
will not change it. A summary of the problem classification is given in Table 4.3.

6
Conclusion
The applied method solves the HPP collection system, including simultaneous
minimization of investment and electric power cost, using a global optimization
model. Cable failures are included through failure scenarios and resulting con¬≠
tingencies are accounted for in the cost calculation. The optimization framework
compares deterministic radial layouts with stochastic radial layouts economically.
The methodology is applied to five different case studies, using two cable sets for
each respective case.
All case studies represent smaller sized HPPs and the three smallest layouts sug¬≠
gest stochastic layouts for different MTBF values. When moving towards the two
larger layouts, computation limits are met and no stochastic layouts are obtained.
Either more computational resources are necessary to study large real¬≠world prob¬≠
lems, or a more efficient algorithm like the progressive contingency incorporation
approach needs to be implemented [56].
However, most of the stochastic layouts show a slight layout change resulting in
low changes of investment cost, with low curtailment cost savings. In two cases
stochastic layouts lead to significant total cost savings of 2.24 % (Figure 5.5a)
and 9.42 % (Figure 5.14c). In both cases the stochastic layouts proves to be
more profitable for all applied MTBF values. Whether this is a result of failure
scenario incorporation or clustering inaccuracies, requires further investigation.
In addition, the reliability parameters MTTR and MTBF have a significant impact
on profitability of a layout design. The presented study cases assume the same
MTTR value, while MTBF is varied. Varying failure rates leads to new suggestions
for an optimal collection system. In most cases the specific MTBF value decides,
whether a stochastic design is competitive to the deterministic solution and break¬≠
even points lie towards rather small MTBF values. Due to the scarcity of publicly
available data regarding cable failure and repair rates, the used MTTR and MTBF
values are assumptions. It will be necessary to validate the results with more
precise data from real¬≠world HPPs or onshore wind farms.
