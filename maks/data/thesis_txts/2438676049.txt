Abstract
Natural products from secondary metabolite biosynthesis are of huge interest due to their diverse bioac-
tivities.
Especially, products of non-ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPS) and polyketide synthases
(PKS) which show antibacterial, antifungal and immusuppressant activities, etc.1 Discovering new novel
secondary metabolites through in vitro methods alone is very resource demanding, not to mention time-
consuming. Therefore, researchers often take an in silico approach to identifying gene clusters indicated
to produce novel secondary metabolites and thereby save a lot of time. The identiﬁed gene clusters can
be introduced to a suitable culturable organism for further experimental analysis.
The antibiotics and secondary metabolite analysis shell (antiSMASH) is one of the leading secondary
metabolite gene cluster detection tools. It is able to detect and analyze NRPS and PKS gene clusters but
lacks analysis for the interesting type II PKS gene clusters and their aromatic polyketide products. In
this project an antiSMASH module has been developed for this purpose. The module is able to identify
genes/proteins relevant for polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKS gene clusters. It detects the types
of the proteins, and for a few speciﬁc protein types the protein function is identiﬁed. Furthermore, it
makes predictions about the aromatic polyketide product based on these ﬁndings. The starter unit, the
number of malonyl elongations during polyketide biosynthesis, the class and a rough molecular weight of
the product are predicted.
A leave-one-out cross validation was performed in order to evaluate the general performance of the devel-
oped antiSMASH module. It showed that the module predicted the starter unit of the aromatic polyketide
product correctly 65% of the time, the number of malonyl elongations during polyketide biosynthesis cor-
rectly 83% of the time and the class of the aromatic polyketide product correctly 69% of the time.
However, these percentages might be a bit pessimistic as clusters where this information is unknown
have been included. All predictions for these clusters were deemed incorrect since one can not tell if the
predictions are correct with the information missing.
The predictions regarding the molecular weight of the product showed to have a tendency to be overes-
timated but did coincide with the true molecular weight of the product.
Overall, the antiSMASH module performs reasonably and it is deﬁnitely an improvement to the anti-
SMASH software. The addition of type II PKS gene cluster analysis to antiSMASH is a great aid to
researchers looking for novel aromatic polyketides and will hopefully lead to new discoveries.

This Master Thesis project amounts to 32.5 ECTS points and was conducted during the spring of 2018
in the New Bioactive Compounds group at the Novo Nordisk Foundation Center for Biosustainability

Chapter 1
Introduction
Natural products from secondary metabolite biosynthesis show an enormous diversity giving rise to a
lot of diﬀerent bioactivities. Therefore, many secondary metabolites are harnessed as medical drugs,
and a lot of focus and resources are put towards secondary metabolite research and discovery.
New
secondary metabolites are made accessible every day by the discovery of new bacteria, fungi and plant
species. Investigating every single new species in vitro for novel secondary metabolites would be very
resource demanding, not to mention time consuming.
Therefore, researchers often take an in silico
approach for selecting promising candidates indicated to produce novel secondary metabolites for further
analysis. However, the majority of bacterial species are unculturable making in vitro analysis impossible.2
As a solution in silico analysis of genomes from unculturable species can be performed to discover gene
clusters producing novel secondary metabolites. The identiﬁed gene clusters can be introduced to suitable
culturable organism for further experimental analysis.
Many tools have been developed capable of identifying secondary metabolite gene clusters in genomes.
Antibiotics and secondary metabolite analysis shell (antiSMASH) is one of the leading secondary metabo-
lite gene cluster detection tools which can identify terpene, thiopeptide, lanthipeptide, β-lactam, lasso
peptide, non-ribosomal peptide synthetase (NRPS), polyketide synthase (PKS) and many more gene
clusters.3 NRPS and PKS gene clusters are the most well-studied due to their products’ applications as
antibiotic, antifungal, anticancer and immunosuppressant drugs, etc.1
AntiSMASH is able to detect and analyze NRPS and PKS gene clusters but is lacking analysis of the
interesting type II PKS gene clusters and their aromatic polyketide products. This project sets out to
develop a module for antiSMASH which will be able to elaborate more on type II PKS gene clusters.
The aim is for the module to be able to identify the genes/proteins necessary for the biosynthesis of the
aromatic polyketide and based on these ﬁndings give predictions regarding the product. Some tools with
such capabilities already exist e.g. prediction informatics for secondary metabolomes (PRISM) which has
been an inspiration to the project.4
To understand the procedures and development of the antiSMASH module background knowledge re-
garding type II PKSs and several bioinformatic tools such as multiple sequence alignments, phylogenetic
trees, proﬁle hidden markov models, the basic local alignment search tool (BLAST) and cross validation
is necessary.

Chapter 1. Introduction
1.1. Type II Polyketide Synthases
1.1 | Type II Polyketide Synthases
Type II PKSs stand out from their counterparts, type I and III PKSs, by being non-modular iterative
PKSs that utilize an acyl carrier protein (ACP) during biosynthesis of a poly-β-ketone. The poly-β-
ketone undergoes cyclization and tailoring to form the ﬁnal aromatic polyketide. Type II PKSs have so
far only been identiﬁed in bacteria. However, iterative type I PKSs typically found in fungi and plants
are also able to synthesize aromatic polyketides.5
The minimal type II PKS gene cluster consists of three genes encoding: a β-ketoacyl synthase α-subunit, a
β-ketoacyl synthase β-subunit and an ACP. A high sequence homology and a conserved gene organization
makes type II PKS gene cluster detection a fairly simple task. The α-subunit is often only referred to as
the ketosynthase (KS) and it catalyzes the condensation between the growing poly-β-ketone chain and
the malonyl building blocks.5 The β-subunit is typically known as the chain length factor (CLF) due
to the discovery that the chain length of the ﬁnal poly-β-ketone primarily is determined by this speciﬁc
subunit.6,7 The terminology of KS and CLF will be used from this point on in the report. Together, the
three proteins are responsible for the poly-β-ketone biosynthesis. However, other proteins are typically
needed for cyclization and tailoring of the poly-β-ketone during its transformation to the ﬁnal aromatic
polyketide.5
Several genes encoding auxiliary and tailoring proteins are found ﬂanking the minimal PKS gene cluster.
Auxiliary proteins such as ketoreductases (KR), cyclases (CYC) and aromatases (ARO) are responsible
for the cyclization of the poly-β-ketone into the core aromatic structure. Tailoring proteins modify the
aromatic intermediate e.g. by alkylation, oxygenation, and glycosylation, maturing the product to form
the ﬁnal aromatic polyketide. Therefore, tailoring proteins include oxygenases (OXY), methyltransferases
(MET), glycosyltransferases (GT), and a few other proteins.8 Some aromatic polyketides may share the
same initial poly-β-ketone but have distinct auxiliary and/or tailoring proteins giving rise to diﬀerent
aromatic structures and tailoring modiﬁcations, and thus diﬀerent bioactivities. The many combinations
of auxiliary and tailoring proteins contribute to the vast amount of diﬀerent bioactive aromatic polyketides
derived from type II PKSs.5 Overall the biosynthesis of aromatic polyketides can be split into three
processes as depicted on Figure 1.1.
It is not a distinct split i.e. a process is not isolated and only
initialized when the prior process has come to an end. They may overlap, e.g. some tailoring modiﬁcations
do take place during cyclization etc.

1.1. Type II Polyketide Synthases
1.1.1 | Poly-β-ketone biosynthesis
The type II PKS biosynthesis of the poly-β-ketone includes several steps. First, the starter unit must
be loaded onto the KS subunit to initiate the poly-β-ketone biosynthesis, often referred to as priming
the PKS. The most common starter unit is acetate but other alternative starter units may be utilized.
The predominant theory on acetate priming involves decarboxylation of the complex malonyl-Coenzyme
A (CoA) to acetyl-CoA. The acetyl then gets transferred to the KS (Figure 1.2a). Alternative starter
unit priming requires additional proteins and is believed to take place through two diﬀerent mechanisms.
The ﬁrst mechanism involves an β-ketoacyl synthase III (KSIII), an ACP and an acyltranferase (AT).
KSIII facilitates the ﬁrst condensation between the alternative starter unit and the ﬁrst malonyl linked
to the ACP. The AT transfers the newly formed intermediate to the PKS, concluding the priming of
the PKS (Figure 1.2b).
The second mechanism involves an acyl-CoA ligase (LIG) and an AT. The
alternative starter unit is tethered to a CoA by the LIG. The AT transfers it to the PKS from the
acyl-CoA intermediate (Figure 1.2c).9

After priming the PKS, a number of malonyl elongation steps take place. First ACP receives the extender
unit malonyl from malonyl-CoA. The elongation of the starter unit or poly-β-ketone intermediate bound
to the KS onto the extender unit bound to the ACP follows via a decarboxylative condensation catalyzed
by the KS. If the ﬁnal poly-β-ketone chain length has been reached, the product is released from the
ACP. Otherwise, the newly formed intermediate is transferred back to the KS subunit by the KS/CLF
complex, ending the elongation step.5
ACP is a necessity for type II PKS biosynthesis, as it tethers starter units, extender units, poly-β-ketone
intermediates as well as the ﬁnal poly-β-ketone product during biosynthesis. This is made possible by a
phophopantetheine prostetic group which is transferred to apo-ACP from CoA via a phosphopantetheinyl
tranferase, known as holo-ACP synthase (ACPS), forming holo-ACP. Starter units, extender units etc.,
are bound to holo-ACP by a covalent thioester linkage to the phophopantetheine prostetic group. The
transfer of malonyl from CoA to ACP is not catalyzed by CoA and ACP themselves but a malonyl-
CoA:ACP transferase (MAT). Nonetheless, ACPS and MAT are typically not found in a type II PKS
gene cluster which suggests that they may be recruited from the related fatty acid biosynthesis.5 An
illustration of the overall biosynthesis of the poly-β-ketone by a type II PKS can be seen in Figure 1.3.

1.1. Type II Polyketide Synthases
1.1.2 | Cyclization of the poly-β-ketone
The poly-β-ketone synthesized by the PKS is extremely reactive and prone to spontaneous cyclization.
Therefore, CYCs and AROs, often used interchangeably, along with KRs are essential to ensure that
the newly synthesized poly-β-ketone does not form unwanted ring structures. Ketoreduction is often
applied to prevent unwanted cyclizations and thereby guide the poly-β-ketone in the right direction.
The ﬁrst-ring cyclization is crucial as it has a great inﬂuence on the ﬁnal structure of the compound.
Thus, CYCs responsible for the ﬁrst-ring cyclization have been studied intensively. The most common
ﬁrst-ring cyclizations are between carbon 7 and 12 (C7-C12), and carbon 9 and 14 (C9-C14), where the
polyketide numbering system has been applied i.e. the thioester α-carbon is termed C1 and the following
carbons have been numbered consecutively. Putative CYCs directing the C7-C12 ﬁrst-ring cyclization
are found in many type II PKS clusters but research also suggests that some KS-CLF complexes facilitate
the C7-C12 ﬁrst-ring cyclization instead of a CYC. Ketoreduction of C9 has exclusively been linked to
C7-C12 cyclization, and so it may also promote C7-C12 cyclization.11,12 C9-C14 ﬁrst-ring cyclization is
common for unreduced decaketides or larger ketides and is facilitated only by CYCs such as TcmN and

Each pattern has been reviewed in detail by Zhou, Li, and Tang (2010)12 and will be introduced brieﬂy

Tetracyclines and aureolic acids often follow a C7-C12, C5-C14, C3-C16, and C1-C18 cyclization
pattern. First-ring and second-ring cyclizations are typically facilitated by CYCs, whereas third-ring
cyclizations are thought to be spontaneous.
The mechanism behind fourth-ring cyclizations has not
yet been identiﬁed. Aureolic acids diﬀerentiate from the tetracycline pathway by having an oxygenase
responsible for the oxidative cleavage of the fourth-ring to form the acid moiety.
Anthracyclines follow a C7-C12, C5-C14, C3-C16, C2-C19 cyclization pattern. Much like in the tetra-
cycline and aureolic acid pathways, ﬁrst and second-ring cyclizations are formed by CYCs and third-ring
cyclizations are believed to be spontaneous. However, fourth-ring cyclizations are known to be formed by
a CYC such as DauD, SnoaL and AknH from the daunomycin, nogalamycin and aclacinomycin clusters,
respectively.
Tetracenomycins have their own unique C9-C14, C7-C16, C5-C18, C2-C19 cyclization pattern. Tetra-
cenomycin is the only class of linearly cyclized polyketides which have a C9-C14 ﬁrst-ring cyclization.
For the known tetracenomycin cluster CYC TcmN is responsible for ﬁrst and second-ring cyclization.
Whether the formation of the third-ring is spontaneous or enzymatic is unknown for the tetracenomycin
cluster but the fourth-ring cyclization is known to be facilitated by the CYC TcmI. Tetracenomycins’ and
anthracyclines’ fourth-ring CYCs catalyze the same C2-C19 cyclization but they have diﬀerent three-
dimensional structures and catalytic mechanisms which may indicate independent evolution.
Benzoisochromanequinones are typically formed from C9 reduced octaketides or nonaketides and have
a cyclization pattern of C7-C12, C5-C14, and O3-C15 or O15-C3. The ﬁrst and second-ring cyclizations
are cyclized by two diﬀerent CYCs e.g. the ActVIII and ActIV from the actinorhodin cluster, respectively.
To suppress spontaneous cyclization of a C2-C15 third-ring, which will happen if no downstream enzymes
are present, a ketoreduction of either C3 or C15 takes place. Thus, the ketoreduction is critical to guide
the cyclization process in the right direction. No CYC has been identiﬁed to catalyze the O3-C5 or
C3-O15 third-ring cyclization.
Angucylines have an angularly cyclized third-ring resulting in a bend fourth-ring formation.
They
are commonly formed from a C9-reduced decaketides and they have a C7-C14, C5-C14, C4-C17, and
C2-C19 cyclization pattern. The ﬁrst and the second-ring are formed by CYCs in a similar fashion to
anthracyclines, tetracyclines and aureolic acids. The unique C4-C17 third-ring and C2-C19 fourth-ring
cyclizations have been identiﬁed to be catalyzed by a CYC, e.g. JadI in the well studied jadomycin cluster.
Zhou, Li, and Tang (2010) describe another cyclization pattern for a class they refer to as angular cyclized
dodecaketides or larger, which will be referred to as pentangular polyphenols in this study. Pentangular
polyphenols such as pradamycin, benastatin, griseorhodin and fredericamycin also have a rather similar
cyclization pattern. A C9-C14, C7-C16, and C5-C18 cyclization lead to a linear tricyclic precursor and
a subsequent C4-C21 cyclization leads to a bend structure followed by a ﬁnal C2-C23 cyclization.

1.1.3 | Tailoring of the aromatic intermediate
As previously mentioned, several proteins alter the aromatic intermediate to form the ﬁnal aromatic
polyketide which is known as the tailoring process.
Methyltransferases (MET), oxygenases (OXY),
and glycosyltransferases (GT) are the most dominant and common enzymes found in type II PKS
clusters which play a role in the process.9 Other enzymes such as aminotransferases14,10 (AMIN),
halogeneases15,16,17 (HAL), and dimerases18,19,20 (DIMER) have also been found to participate in the
tailoring process but in comparison to METs, OXYs, and GTs they are rare in type II PKS clusters.
METs are enzymes which transfer a methyl group to nitrogen, carbon, or oxygen. Several MET encoding
genes are typically found in type II PKS gene clusters. The majority of METs in type II PKS biosynthesis
have an S-adenosyl-L-methionine (SAM) binding site but the sequence conservation among these enzymes
is low.9 Surprisingly, research shows that METs from type II PKS clusters tend to cluster according to
their regioselectivity on the polyketide carbon chain in a phylogenetic tree.21
OXYs incorporate oxygen into a substrate.
Cytocrome-dependent P-450 monooxygenases, ﬂavin-
dependent mono- and dioxygenases, and anthrone OXYs are known to be involved in type II PKS
biosynthesis.9 The simple anthrone OXYs perform a oxidation of naphthacenone- and anthrone-type pre-
cursors to corresponding quinone derivatives. They are very common in the tailoring process and require
no cofactors or prosthetic groups. TcmH, ActVA-orf6, and AknX from the tetracenomycin, actinorhodin,
and aklavinone clusters, respectively, are representatives of this group. Identifying anthrone OXYs in
type II PKS clusters is possible as they share a high sequence homology, furthermore, they show high
sequence to regioselectivity correlation.4
Flavin-dependent OXYs are capable of a variety of modiﬁcations including hydroxylations, epoxida-
tions, Baeyer-Villiger, and Favorskii rearrangements, where Baeyer-Villiger rearrangements are the most
widespread.9 A phylogenetic study on ﬂavin-dependent OXYs involved with Baeyer-Villiger rearrange-
ments and their regioselectivity on the polyketide carbon chain showed poor correlation.17 Furthermore,
the resulting Baeyer-Villiger rearrangement is almost impossible to predict based on the protein sequence
of associated enzymes.4 In comparison to Baeyer-Viliger, Favorskiiase rearrangements are rare. The only
known Favorskiiase rearrangemenet in type II PKS biosynthesis is catalyzed by OXY EncM in the special
enterocin cluster.9 Cytocrome-dependent P450 monooxygenases are not well-studied in the context of
type II PKSs but include OXYs EncR and DoxA of the enterocin and daunorubicin/doxorubicin clusters,
respectively.
In both cases the monooxygenase catalyze a hydroxylation of a distinct carbon atom.9
Overall, predicting the regioselectivity and the function of a OXY from its protein sequence remains a
major challenge.4

Many aromatic polyketides from type II PKSs contain sugar moieties which get attached by GTs. There-

of aromatic polyketides is of great interest for engineering novel bioactive compounds. GTs from the
urdamycin and landomycin clusters alone have successfully been used to generate more than 40 novel
compounds using combinatorial biosynthesis.9
GTs involved with type II PKS biosynthesis most likely show a so called GT-B fold.
The GT-B is
characterized by two similar Rossmann-like folds in each terminal end of the protein. The N-terminal
domain provides the acceptor-binding site and the C-terminal provide the donor-binding site which are
connected by a linker region. The active site is located in between the two domains. It has been proposed
that the donor sugar binds the C-terminal domain and the N-terminal interact with the acceptor-site of
the aromatic polyketide. Furthermore, the N-terminal is also thought to be involved with the selective
binding of the speciﬁc sugar to the C-terminal.9
Aromatic polyketides from the chelocardin and oxytetracycline clusters have been found to get an amino
group attached to them during their biosynthesis.14,10 AMINs are responsible for the attachment by
catalyzing the transamination of the α-amino group from a donor amino acid to a ketone on the aromatic
polyketide.10
HALs incorporate a halogen (ﬂuorine, chlorine, bromine, iodine, and astatine) to a substrate. HALs
have been found in type II PKS clusters such as the AZ154, chlortetracycline and dactylocycline clusters.
Common to all known HALs found in type II PKS clusters is that they incorporate chlorine in the
polyketide.16,15,22
Actinorhodin is a heterodimer of two benzoisochromanequinones. The dimerization is catalyzed by a
DIMER found in the actinorhodin cluster.18 DIMERs are rare in type II PKS gene clusters but are found
in the ﬂuostatin and lomaiviticin clusters as well.19,20
While further tailoring enzymes have been described for type II PKSs, they are too rare to be considered
for a computational analysis and have not been included in this study.

1.2.1 | Multiple Sequence Alignments
A multiple sequence alignment (MSA) is the basis on which a phylogenetic tree or a proﬁle hidden markov
model is built, thus, it is an essential tool in the ﬁeld of bioinformatics. Several diﬀerent algorithms
have been developed for this purpose.
MUSCLE (multiple sequence comparison by log-expectation)
developed by Edgar (2004)23 is one of such algorithms and it has been used in this study. In general the
computational problem of constructing an MSA is to deﬁne a model for sequence evolution which assigns
probabilities to individual sequence mutations and to identify the most probable directed graph where
mutations are edges and terminal nodes are sequences.23 Since no elegant method has been developed to
construct such a graph, an alternative heuristic approach is to optimize the sum of pairwise alignment
scores. It can be achieved by dynamic programming but the amount of computations necessary increases
drastically as the length and number of sequences increase.23 Therefore, progressive methods for which
this eﬀect is less dramatic have become popular in MSA algorithms. MUSCLE utilizes a progressive
method consisting of 3 stages.
Stage 1 drafts an alignment from a guide tree based on a kmer distance matrix.

1.2.2 | Phylogenetic trees
Phylogenetic trees are utilized to ﬁnd evolutionary relationships between species based on an MSA. The
closer two species are in a phylogenetic tree, the closer they are related and so similar sequences cluster
together in clades of the tree. Several diﬀerent approaches can be taken to construct such a tree where
the maximum-likelihood approach is one of the most accurate.
However, it comes at a cost since it
requires a lot of computational power compared to alternative methods. FastTree 2.0 is the tool used
for phylogenetic tree construction in this study, which partially solves this issue by using a approximate
maximum-likelihood method.24
The FastTree 2.0 algorithm is initialized by constructing a phylogenetic tree using neighbour joining,
a simple procedure which consists of joining the two nearest nodes according to a distance matrix. A
distance matrix is computed based on the parsed MSA and updated as nodes are joined. Neighbour
joining continues until all nodes are connected. The neighbour joined tree is improved by the minimum-
evolution (ME) method utilizing nearest neighbour interchange (NNI) for tree rearrangements. The best
tree according to the ME criterion is the one with the lowest sum of branch lengths. Diﬀerent trees are
tested using NNI where neighbours of every internal branch of the tree are interchanged leading to two
new trees per internal branch, see Figure 1.5.

reattaches it to another site in the tree, see Figure 1.6. Typically, attachment sites are tested exhaustively,
however, only moves of length two are considered for each internal node and the best choice is further

1.2.3 | Proﬁle Hidden Markov Models
A proﬁle Hidden Markov Model (pHMM) is a speciﬁc architecture of a hidden markov model explicitly
developed for depicting nucleotide and protein sequences. In general a pHMM is a representation of the
proﬁle of an MSA, and thus they are particularly useful for ﬁnding sequences similar to the depicted
MSA. In theory a pHMM is able to calculate the probability that a given sequence derives from the
proﬁle of the MSA, a high probability indicate a high degree of similarity between query sequence and
MSA. Real life applications include distant homologous protein detection, protein family identiﬁcation
and identiﬁcation of structural domains in a given protein. Proﬁle HMMs are used for remote protein
homology detection due to their high degree of sensitivity.25,26
The building blocks of a hidden markov model architecture includes states, transition probabilities and
emission probabilities. A state always has certain transition probabilities associated with it and it may
also have emission probabilities, if not the state is a so called non-emitter state. The pHMM tool package
HMMER 3.12b has been used in this study which utilizes the plan 7 pHMM architecture.

A pHMM is able to ﬁnd homologous proteins by scoring a given query sequence, the score indicates how

pHMM. Furthermore, the sensitivity of a pHMM can be tuned by deﬁning a bitscore cut-oﬀvalue and
hits below will be discarded.

1.2.5 | Cross validation
A cross validation (CV) can be used to evaluate the generalized performance of a model. In simple terms
the procedure of a cross validation is to split your data set into n subsets, also called folds. n −1 of the
subsets will be used to train a model, and the model will then be be used to make predictions about the
left out subset. The procedure is repeated for all subsets and you end up having predictions for your
whole data set. The predictions can be used to estimate the generalized performance of the model since
the prediction for each entry in the data set has been determined by a model developed independently
from the entry. Typically, it is referred to as an n-fold CV where the most extreme case of a n-fold CV is
leave-one-out CV, where n is the size of the data set. Leave-one-out CV is not common since it requires
many time-consuming model trainings and only leads to a little increase in the precision of the estimated
generalized performance of your model. However, leave-one-out CV is very suitable for this study since
the utilized data set is rather small.

Before introducing the methods used in the development of the antiSMASH module, the aim of the project
and key background knowledge will be summarized. It serves to give the reader a clearer understanding
of why the diﬀerent steps were taken in the development process.
The aim of the project is to develop an antiSMASH module able to analyze type II PKS gene clusters.
The module itself should be able to identify diﬀerent types of proteins involved in the biosynthesis of the
polyketide from a given type II PKS cluster. Furthermore, it should be able to elaborate on the speciﬁc
function of the identiﬁed proteins e.g. what cyclization a CYC is responsible for or a KR’s regioselectivity
on the polyketide carbon chain. Based on these ﬁndings predictions should be made about the aromatic
polyketide product i.e. what starter unit is employed, how many malonyl elongations takes places during
poly-β-ketone biosynthesis, what is the the class and the molecular weight of the aromatic polyketide
product.
In order to be able to develop such a module, a type II PKS gene cluster data set is necessary to serve
as the foundation on which the module can be built. Thus, a type II PKS cluster data set has been
assembled.
A method able to detect proteins involved with polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKS clusters, as well
as, a method able to identify the speciﬁc function of proteins involved with polyketide biosynthesis of
type II PKS clusters need to be developed.
As previously mentioned, pHMMs are very suitable for
picking up distant homologous proteins. Thus, they were chosen as the method for detecting proteins
relevant for polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKS clusters. At ﬁrst identifying the speciﬁc function of the
proteins involved with polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKSs seemed like a complicated task. However,
as described in section 1.1 prior studies have shown that some protein types, involved with polyketide
biosynthesis in type II PKSs, cluster in a phylogenetic tree according to a speciﬁc characteristic of their
function. For example CYCs have been found to cluster according to the cyclization they catalyze,13
and KRs and METs have been found to cluster according to their regioselectivity on the polyketide
carbon chain.11,21 Therefore, it was hypothesized that the speciﬁc function of these protein types could
be predicted by building pHMMs representing a clade or group of clades in a phylogenetic tree known
to be involved with a speciﬁc protein function of that protein type. It showed to be a valid method,
and thus it was used as the method for identifying the speciﬁc protein function of proteins involved with
polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKSs.

2.1. Collecting type II PKS gene clusters
2.1 | Collecting type II PKS gene clusters
A lot of work has been put into manually collecting and annotating type II PKS clusters in order to
create a suitable data set for the development of the antiSMASH module. Type II PKS gene clusters
were collected from several sources such as secondary metabolite databases (MiBIG,31 DoBiscuit32 and
IMG/ABC33), previous related literature4 and recent litterature concerning newly identiﬁed clusters.34,35
Cross-referencing all collected clusters against each other down to protein sequences led to the identiﬁca-
tion of some redundant clusters which were excluded leaving 89 clusters in total. Appendix B.1 contains
a table of general information about each cluster and Appendix B.2 a table of additional information
about the product(s) of each cluster.
Proteins relevant for polyketide biosynthesis in all type II PKS clusters had their protein sequences
extracted. All relevant proteins have already been described in section 1.1 but to refresh, they include
the minimal PKS proteins KSs, CLFs, and ACPs. Proteins involved in PKS priming are KSIIIs, ATs,
AMIDs, LIGs. Auxilatory proteins are CYCs and KRs. Tailoring proteins are METs, OXYs, GTs, AMINs,
HALs and DIMERs. A fasta ﬁle was created for each protein type containing all protein sequences of
that speciﬁc protein type. In total 890 protein sequences were extracted for 89 KSs, 89 CLFs, 86 ACPs,
15 KSIIIs, 23 ATs, 4 AMIDs, 8 LIGs, 205 CYCs, 93 KRs, 67 METs, 119 OXYs, 78 GTs, 4 AMINs, 5
HALs, and 5 DIMERs.
Annotations for every individual protein were added to the header of its fasta entry. The fasta header
contains the NCBI accession number of the protein, the assigned cluster ID of the cluster to which the
protein belongs, the primary cluster product, the corresponding gene name for the protein and ﬁnally
details about the speciﬁc function of the protein. The details included about speciﬁc protein function
depend on protein type. For CLFs the details were the starter unit(s) of the cluster product(s) and the
number of malonyl elongations during poly-β-ketone biosynthesis of the cluster product(s). For KSIIIs,
ATs, AMIDs, and LIGs only the starter unit(s) of the cluster product(s) were included.
For CYCs
the cyclization(s) they catalyze were included. For KRs, METs, OXYs, AMINs, GTs, and HALs their
regioselectivity on the polyketide carbon chain were included. Furthermore, for GTs the sugar they attach
and for HALs the halogen they attach were also included.
Proteins TcmN, GrhT, Aur1I, PgaM, Pd2OR, and UrdM showed to be combinations of diﬀerent protein
types, and thus they had diﬀerent protein functions for their N-terminal and C-terminal. Initially the
whole protein sequence was added to both protein type fasta ﬁles with separate speciﬁc protein function
details. However, it showed to give some complications in the later module development process, and so
they were split into N-terminal and C-terminal sequences. However, proteins with diﬀerent N-terminal
and C-terminal functions might still be present in the data set.
In the end a solid data set consisting of information about each cluster and its product(s) along with
fasta ﬁles containing sequences of all proteins relevant for polyketide biosynthesis in the collected type II
PKS clusters was constructed. The type II PKS gene cluster data set has served as the foundation on

2.2 | Development of antiSMASH module
With the foundation in place, tool development was initiated. Several steps have been taken towards the
creation of the module and they will be reviewed in this section. All described methods were combined
and implemented in an antiSMASH module which source code can be found in the supplemental zip-ﬁle,
see Appendix C.1 for a overview of the contents of the zip-ﬁle and Appendix C.2 for a short description
of each ﬁle contained in the zip-ﬁle.
2.2.1 | Detection of proteins involved with polyketide biosynthesis in type II
PKS clusters
Detecting proteins involved with polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKS clusters was a straightforward
task. For every type of protein known to be involved with polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKS clusters a
pHMM was build based on the collection of protein sequences found in the corresponding fasta ﬁle of that
protein type. An MSA was created for every type of protein using MUSCLE 3.8.31. HMMER 3.12b was
used to build pHMMs based on the resulting MSAs for each type of protein using the hmmbuild command.
The resulting pHMMs are listed in Table 3.1 and will henceforth be referred to as the generic pHMMs.
The generic pHMMs could be used to detect homolog proteins involved with polyketide biosynthesis in
type II PKS clusters and distinguish between protein types.
2.2.2 | Identiﬁcation of speciﬁc function of type II PKS proteins
Two methods have been applied to identify the speciﬁc function of proteins involved with polyketide
biosynthesis in type II PKS clusters. For CLFs, CYCs, KRs, and METs pHMMs have been developed
for each speciﬁc protein function. The same method was tried for OXYs and GTs but unfortunately it
was not applicable, thus, speciﬁc protein function identiﬁcation for GTs and OXYs were abandoned. For
proteins related to starter unit priming KSIIIs, ATs, AMIDs, and LIGs too few protein sequences were
available for constructing speciﬁc protein function pHMMs and instead BLAST databases were created.
Protein BLAST databases could simply be created from the KSIII, AT, AMID, and LIG fasta ﬁles using
BLAST+ 2.7.1. Protein homology could be inferred based on these databases, and thus the speciﬁc
function of proteins involved with starter unit priming could be identiﬁed.
Building the speciﬁc protein function pHMMs was a bit more complicated. First MSAs of the fasta ﬁles
containing CLF, CYC, KR, and MET protein sequences were made using MUSCLE 3.8.31. Afterwards
phylogenetic trees were constructed based on each MSA using FastTree 2.1.10. The resulting phylogenetic
trees were visualized and inspected with the ETE toolkit, a python framework to work with trees.36 Clades

2.2.3 | Testing validity of using pHMMs for protein detection and protein
function identiﬁcation
The pHMMs for detecting proteins and identifying speciﬁc protein function described above were tested
in order to verify they were a valid approach. First, all generic pHMMs for detecting proteins involved
with polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKS clusters were run on a combined fasta ﬁle with all protein
sequences using the hmmscan function of HMMER 3.1b2. The predicted protein type of a protein sequence
was assessed as the protein type pHMM hit with the highest bitscore. A confusion matrix was created
of the predicted and the actual protein type to evaluate the results, see Figure 3.5.
The speciﬁc pHMMs were evaluated in a similar manner. All generic and speciﬁcity pHMMs were run
on the combined fasta ﬁle with all protein sequences using ’hmmscan’ of HMMER 3.1b2. Generic and
speciﬁc pHMMs were run simultaneously to determine if the speciﬁc pHMMs were able to overrule the
generic pHMMs i.e. if they would get a higher bitscore. Again, the predicted speciﬁc protein function
was assessed as the speciﬁc protein function pHMM hit with the highest bitscore. A large confusion
matrix was constructed on the resulting predicted and actual protein type/speciﬁc protein function, and
four ﬁgures displaying subspaces of the confusion matrix were created focusing on CLF, CYC, KR, and
MET predictions. The subspaces were selected for each type of protein based on the hits of their speciﬁc
protein function pHMMs. Every row and column containing a speciﬁc protein function pHMM hit was
included in the subspace. The four resulting subspaces of the confusion matrix can be seen in Figure 3.6.
Simultaneously, as validity was tested, the sensitivity of each pHMM was tuned by deﬁning trusted cut-
oﬀs in the form of bitscore values.
It was done in order to prevent false positive hits.

2.2.4 | Polyketide product predictions
Polyketide product predictions are based on protein detection and speciﬁc protein function results which
speciﬁcally is a collection of pHMM and BLAST hits. The number of malonyl elongations is predicted
based on the CLF(s) found in the cluster. If multiple are found they are listed according to their bitscore
and E-value. Starter unit prediction is done in a similar way. To predict the starter unit(s) priming
the PKS all BLAST hits are taken into account. BLAST hits which support the same starter unit are
combined by adding their bitscores and keeping the lowest E-value. The result is list of possibly multiple
starter units with their accumulated bitscore and lowest E-value. If no blast hits are found, the starter
unit is assumed to be acetyl with a bitscore and E-value of zero.

2.3. Evaluation of antiSMASH module performance
2.3 | Evaluation of antiSMASH module performance
The performance of the developed antiSMASH module was evaluated by a leave-one-out cross validation
with the intention of getting a generalized measure of its performance. A training set was created by
leaving out a single cluster from the cluster data set. Proﬁle HMMs and blast databases were build based
on the training set and used as the basis for which the antiSMASH module made its prediction. Thereafter,
a prediction was made on the left out cluster. This procedure was repeated for every single cluster in the
cluster data set. In the end a prediction was available for every cluster which has been determined by a
prediction model developed independently from the cluster. Unfortunately, the phylogenetic tree clade
grouping could not be automated. The tool ClusterPicker 1.2.337 was tested but was not found suitable.
This meant that the prediction model was not developed fully independent from the left out cluster since
the clade groups were based on the phylogenetic trees of the proteins sequences from all clusters.
The collection of cluster predictions were compared to the actual cluster information. Tables comparing
the predictions of starter unit, the number of malonyl elongations and the product class to the cluster
information can be seen in Table 3.3, Table 3.4 and Table 3.5, respectively.
The molecular weight
predictions were also compared to the cluster information and a plot depicting the predicted product
molecular weight against the true product molecular weight was created and can be seen on Figure 3.7.

3.1. Phylogenetic trees
3.1 | Phylogenetic trees
Several phylogenetic trees have been constructed in order to deﬁne clades and clade groups representing
tentative speciﬁc protein function for which pHMMs can be build. The phylogenetic trees displayed in this
section does not reﬂect branch lengths. All branch lengths have been set to be equal in order to simplify
the visual representation of the tree and to make the branching pattern more easily distinguishable.
However, branch lengths have been taken into account when deﬁning and grouping clades for each tree.
A clade group in this context is a collection of clades and a clade is a collection of terminal nodes or a
single terminal node.
3.1.1 | Chain length factors
Figure 3.1 shows a phylogenetic tree of all CLF protein sequences of the type II PKS gene cluster data set.
Each CLF has been labelled with the class, the starter unit(s) and the number of malonyl elongations of
the product belonging to the cluster from which the CLF originates. It can be seen that CLFs indeed do
tend to cluster according the polyketide chain lengths of the cluster product. In this case chain length is
regarded the same as the number of malonyl elongations which is not entirely true but they do correlate
suﬃciently. Furthermore, sub-clustering relative to to the class and starter unit(s) of the cluster product
is also observed. Clades of the phylogenetic tree have been grouped into clade groups according to the
number of malonyl elongations for the purpose of building pHMMs.
Starting with the 7 malonyl elongations clade group it is clear that it consists of only clade 7. This clade
primarily consists of CLFs involved with benzoisochromanequinone biosynthesis. Only three CLFs which
cluster product class is unique or unknown are present. Most CLFs’ cluster product starter unit is acetyl
but CLFs with alternative cluster product starter units such as propionyl, butyryl and benzoyl are also
present. However, CLFs with alternative cluster product starter units branch oﬀearly on from the CLFs
with acetyl as cluster product starter unit.
The clade group of 11|12 malonyl elongations also consists of a single clade, clade 5. Clade 5 is very
clean in regards to the CLFs’ cluster product class as it almost exclusively is pentangular polyphenol,
only a few CLFs with a unique cluster product class are present. Furthermore, the clade can be split
into two sub-clades depending on starter unit. An upper sub-clade with the alternative starter units 2-
methylbutyryl, butyryl, hexaonoyl and hexadinyl, and a lower sub-clade with the common acetyl starter
unit. The number of malonyl elongations does not seem to inﬂuence the sub-clustering within the clade.
This is not surprising as it is a relative small change in chain length when considering the chain length
as a whole. The number of malonyl elongations would have a higher inﬂuence on shorter products as
a malonyl extension relative to the whole chain length is considerable larger for shorter products. An
example of this can be seen in clade 1 and clade 2 which will be elucidated later.

3.1. Phylogenetic trees
3.1.2 | Cyclases
A phylogenetic tree of all 205 CYCs can be seen in Figure 3.2. Each CYC has been labelled with the
class of the cluster product and the cyclization it catalyze. If the CYC is known to catalyze multiple
cyclizations they have been separated by a slash. A question mark after the cyclization indicates that
there is uncertainty about whether or not the CYC catalyze the labelled cyclization. Multiple uncertain
cyclizations can be labelled and are separated by a white space. For some CYCs no information regarding
cyclization was available. Nonetheless, all cyclizations which potentially could be catalyzed by the CYC
based on the ﬁnal aromatic structure of the cluster product have been labelled using the uncertainty
labelling method described. The clustering of CYCs in the phylogenetic tree seems, as expected, to be
according to the cyclization the CYCs catalyze.
Nine clade groups have been created from the CYC phylogenetic tree. Not surprisingly the largest clade
group is C7-C12, the most common ﬁrst-ring cyclization. The clade group include clades 1-7 and clades
24-27.
All CYCs in the clade group are involved with C7-C12 ﬁrst-ring cyclization of tetracyclines,
aureolic acids, anthracyclines, angucyclines or benzoisochromanequinones. The clades of the clade group
might seem to be spread out randomly across the tree, which is partially true. However, what is not
depicted are their close proximity to the root of the tree i.e. all branch lengths are relatively short.
On the topic of ﬁrst-ring CYCs, clade group C9-C14 consists of CYCs involved primarily with C9-C14
ﬁrst-ring cyclizations for tetracenomycins and pentangular polyphenols. A few CYCs from clusters with
unknown products are also present, which are found throughout every clade group consisting of CYCs
involved with pentangular polyphenols and tetracenomycins. This might indicate that the products of
these clusters potentially are pentangular polyphenols and tetracenomycins.
Clade group C5-C14, a common second-ring cyclization, consists of only a single clade, clade 9. Clade 8
right next to clade 9 is not included since its branch length is very long, thus clade 8 is the only clade
not in a clade group. The primary cluster product class of the CYCs in clade group 9 is benzoisochro-
manequinone but a lot of CYCs with a unique cluster product and a single CYC with tetracycline as the
class of its cluster product are also present.

two clade groups facilitate the same second-ring cyclization but CYCs found in the C5-C14/C3-16 clade
group in addition facilitate C3-16 third-ring cyclization. It is also depicted in the clade groups themselves
as a few CYCs facilitating only the C5-C14 cyclization are found in the C5-C14/C3-C16 clade group and
vice versa. The C5-C14/C3-C16 clade group only consists of clade 20. The cluster product classes of the

3.1.3 | Ketoreductases
Figure 3.3 displays the phylogenetic tree constructed from every KR protein sequence in the type II PKS
gene cluster data set. The regioselectivity on the polyketide carbon chain of each KR is labelled along
with the cluster product class. The same uncertainty labelling method as for CYCs has been applied
here.
The KRs in the phylogenetic tree do cluster according to regioselectivity as expected. However, relatively
many KRs are not in any of the deﬁned clade groups due to the clustering being disorganized in parts of
the tree. To be speciﬁc clades 3, 4, 9, 10, 11, 13, and 14 are not in any clade group. A clearer picture
of the clustering of KRs in the phylogenetic tree could be achieved by including more KRs. This would
hopefully lead to more KRs found in the disorganized regions potentially causing a clustering pattern to
become apparent.
The number of clade groups is considerable lower than for CYCs, only ﬁve clade groups with a tentative
speciﬁc protein function have been deﬁned. The largest of them all is clade group C9 containing clades
1, 2, 15, and 16. The clade group takes up half of the phylogenetic tree conﬁrming how common C9
ketoreduction is in the type II PKS polyketide biosynthesis. Not surprisingly the product classes are
tetracycline, anthracycline, angucycline and benzoisochromanequinone. All classes which do not have
a C9-C14 ﬁrst-ring cyclization. This might implicate that C9 ketoreduction is important for C7-C12
ﬁrst-ring cyclization or at least for guiding the poly-β-ketone in the right direction towards a correct
ﬁrst-ring cyclization.
Clade group C19 contain only clade 5. It consists of KRs facilitating a C19 ketoreduction in the polyketide
biosynthesis of pentangular polyphenols. Clade group C11 is right next to it which contain clades 6 and
7. The KRs in this clade group are also involved in the biosynthesis of pentangular polyphenols but they
facilitate a C11 ketoreduction.
Clade group C15, on the other hand, has KRs which are found in the biosynthesis of angucyclines and
promote a C15 ketoreduction. Clade 8 is the only clade in the clade group.
The last clade group is C17 containing only clade 12.
The KRs found in this group facilitate C17
ketoreduction in anthracycline biosynthesis.
Overall the clade groups are rather small except clade group C9. Clade group C11 only has 4 KRs which
when considered for building a pHMM is almost too few. As previously mentioned, it would help a lot
to include more KRs in the construction of the phylogenetic tree as more KRs would be found in the
existing clade groups but also new clade groups might emerge. Nonetheless, the number of KRs is limited
by the amount of type II PKS gene clusters available.

3.1.4 | Methyltransferases
The phylogenetic tree of METs can be seen in Figure 3.4. As with the phylogenetic tree of the KRs,
the regioselectivity on the polyketide carbon chain of the MET and the class of the cluster product are

Clustering according to regioselectivity on the polyketide carbon chain is observed but the clustering
does seem disorganized in some parts of the tree as with the phylogenetic tree of KRs. This is the reason
why clades 12, 17, 18, 20, 21, and 23 are not in a clade group. The phylogenetic tree could beneﬁt from
including additional METs for construction which hopefully would help clustering patterns emerge in
these disorganized regions.
Eight clade groups have been deﬁned for the tree. Clade group C13O|C17O contains clades 1-4 which
primarily consist of METs facilitating C13 or C17 oxygen methylation in biosynthesis of tetracenomycins
and pentangular polyphenols. A high degree of uncertainty is present in the group but common for all
KRs is C13O and/or C17O methylation.
Three small clade groups are present C10 , C9O and C18O containing clade 6, 8 and 9, respectively.
Each clade group consists only of three METs which signiﬁcantly reduces the ability of the pHMM
based on these clade groups to identify distant protein homologs. Nonetheless, they have been build and
implemented in the module for speciﬁc protein function identiﬁcation. The METs of clade group C10
are involved with C10 carbon methylation of tetracyclines and aureolic acids, the METs of clade group
C9O are involved with C9 oxygen methylation of tetracyclines and anthracyclines and the METs of clade
group C180 are involved with C18 oxygen methylation of anthracyclines.
Clade group Carboxy O is one of the larger groups which contains clade 10 and 11. As the name implies
the METs found in this clade group are thought to facilitate the methylation of a oxygen in the carboxy
moiety of the poly-β-ketone in the early biosynthesis.
Based on the clade group this methylation is
common in anthracycline biosynthesis but also tetracenomycin and pentangular polyphenol biosynthesis.
The clade group of C11O is also a large group and contains clades 13-16. METs thought to be involved
with C11 oxygen methylation of tetracenomycin and pentangular polyphenols are found in this clade
group. Many of the METs are known to facilitate this C11 oxygen methylation but a few of are thought
to facilitate C13 or C17 oxygen methylation.
Clade group C6|C8 contains clade 19.
The METs of the group facilitate C6 carbon methylation of
tetracenomycins and pentangular polyphenols, or C8 carbon methylation of tetracyclines. Comparing
the structure of tetracenomycin, pentangular polyphenols and tetracycline the methylation takes place
at the same relative position.
The last clade group C2O|C2N contain only clade 22.
METs involved with C2 oxygen or nitrogen
methylation of aureolic acids or tetracyclines, respectively, are located in the clade group.

3.1.5 | Oxygenases and glycosyltransferases
It is known that predicting a OXY’s regioselectivity and speciﬁc function from its protein sequence is
a major challenge, hence, a phylogenetic study will probably not reveal anything signiﬁcant on this
matter. Nonetheless, a phylogenetic tree was created for all OXY protein sequences and can be seen
in Appendix D.1. The phylogenetic study was done in an eﬀort to utilize the same method for speciﬁc
protein function identiﬁcation of OXYs. As anticipated the phylogenetic tree did not reveal anything
signiﬁcant. The clustering seem to be according to cluster product class in some parts of the tree and
completely random at others. Several diﬀerent subtypes of OXYs participate in the polyketide tailoring
process, cytocrome-dependent P-450 monooxygenases, ﬂavin-dependent mono- and dioxygenases, and
anthrone oxygenases. Creating a fasta ﬁle containing protein sequences for each individual subtype of
OXYs and performing a phylogenetic study on these might show a diﬀerent picture. It is worth testing
in eﬀorts to improve the OXY predictions in a future version of the module, especially since the OXY
generic pHMM performs very poor compared to the other generic pHMMs.
A phylogenetic tree of all GT protein sequences can be seen in Appendix D.2 and was constructed to see
if clustering occurred according to regioselectivity on the polyketide carbon chain of the GT or maybe
the sugar the GT attaches. If a clustering pattern was observed speciﬁc predictions could be made using
pHMMs with the other proteins. Unfortunately, no clear clustering pattern could be identiﬁed on the
basis of these protein details.

Chapter 4
Conclusion
An antiSMASH module for analyzing type II PKS gene clusters was successfully developed. It is able to
detect genes/proteins involved with the biosynthesis of the aromatic polyketide product of a type II PKS
gene cluster. Furthermore, the module makes predictions about the cluster product. The starter unit of
the aromatic polyketide, the number of malonyl elongations during its biosynthesis as well as the class
and the molecular weight of the aromatic polyketide are predicted.
For the development of the module a type II PKS cluster data set consisting of information about 89
clusters was assembled.
It functioned as the foundation of the module development process.
Many
pHMMs were build for protein detection and protein function identiﬁcation along with a few protein
BLAST databases used for protein function identiﬁcation focusing on starter unit priming. It was all
combined in the antiSMASH module which made product predictions from the results of utilizing the
pHMMs and BLAST databases on a type II PKS gene cluster.
The performance of the antiSMASH module was evaluated by performing a leave-one-out cross validation.
For each cluster a set of pHMMs and BLAST databases were constructed independent from the cluster
i.e. the cluster was taken out from the type II PKS gene cluster data set and pHMMs and protein BLAST
databases were constructed based on the remaining clusters. The newly constructed pHMMs and BLAST
databases were used by the antiSMASH module to make predictions about the left out cluster. In the end
a cluster prediction made by a prediction model developed independently from the cluster was obtained
for every cluster. Comparing the predictions to the actual cluster information would give an estimate of
the general performance of the antiSMASH module.
Overall, the module performed well in the cross validation. It was able to predict the correct starter
unit for 65% of all starter units found to prime the PKS of the 89 clusters. Disregarding clusters with
unknown starter units increased the prediction success to 70%. Currently, the module is very sensitive
when predicting alternative starter units and is prone to picking up proteins which are not connected
to starter unit priming. Thus, it is likely to predict an alternative starter unit instead of the common
acetyl starter unit. Tuning the sensitivity of the protein BLAST databases could improve the starter unit
prediction performance in a future version of the module.
The predictions of the number of malonyl elongations during biosynthesis were successful for 83% of the
clusters and disregarding clusters where this information was unknown increased the success to 89%.
Product class predictions were also very satisfying. The class of the cluster product was predicted correctly
69% of the time. Again, disregarding the clusters where this information is unknown or the product class

is unique lead to an increase of the performance. This time to a staggering 95%. Nonetheless, it must be
considered that a product class prediction can consist of multiple product classes making the predictions

Predictions of the molecular weight of the cluster product were in general overestimated but they did
tend to coincide with the true molecular weight of the product.
In general, all predictions would beneﬁt form a larger data set. A larger data set could be produced by

the speciﬁc function of each protein involved with polyktetide biosynthesis in the cluster.
With that said, the developed antiSMASH is absolutely capable of analyzing type II PKS gene clusters
and is an extremely useful tool for discovering new novel aromatic polyketides.

An antiSMASH module for type II polyketide synthase gene 
cluster analysis 
Rasmus Villebro1, Kai Blin1 and Tilmann Weber1 
Abstract 
The software antiSMASH can examine genomes to identify and analyze biosynthetic gene clusters for a wide range 
of natural products. Type II polyketide synthase (PKS) gene clusters have been limited to only been identified. So far, 
no detailed predictions for type II PKS gene clusters have been available. In this study, a module for antiSMASH for 
the analysis of type II PKS gene clusters has been developed. The module detects genes/proteins in the type II PKS 
gene cluster involved with polyketide biosynthesis and is able to make predictions about the aromatic polyketide 
product. Predictions include the starter unit, the number of malonyl elongations during polyketide biosynthesis, the 
class and the molecular weight of the product. The performance of the developed antiSMASH module was evaluated 
using a leave-one-out cross validation and showed reasonably results. 
Keywords: type II polyketide synthases - aromatic polyketides - secondary metabolite discovery - antiSMASH - gene 
clusters - bioinformatics
Introduction 
Natural 
products 
from 
secondary 
metabolite 
production are of huge interest due to their diverse 
bioactivities, and many are harnessed at medical 
drugs. Thus, a lot of focus is on secondary metabolite 
research and discovery. New secondary metabolites 
are made accessible every day by the discovery of 
new bacteria, fungi and plants. An in vitro approach 
to analyzing every new species for novel secondary 
metabolites would be very resource demanding, not 
to mention time consuming. Furthermore, the 
majority of all bacterial species are unculturable, and 
thereby unfit for in vitro analysis [1]. Therefore, 
researchers often take an in silico approach to 
identify gene clusters in genomes indicated to 
produce novel secondary metabolites. The identified 
gene clusters can be extracted and introduced to a 
suitable culturable organism for further experimental 
analysis. It saves a lot of resources and time, and 
overcomes the issue of unculturable species. 
Antibiotics and secondary metabolite analysis shell 
(antiSMASH) is one of the leading secondary 
metabolite gene cluster detection tools. It can 
identify gene clusters responsible for the biosynthesis 
of a wide range of secondary metabolites such as 
thiopeptide, lanthipeptide, lasso-peptides, non-
ribosomal peptide synthetases (NRPS), polyketide 
synthases (PKS) and many more [2]. NRPS and PKS 
gene clusters are the most well studied due to their 
products’ interesting applications as antibiotics, 
                                                                
1 New Bioactive Compounds, The Novo Nordisk Foundation Center for Biosustainability, Technical University of 
Denmark. 
immunosuppressants and anticancer drugs, etc. [3] 
AntiSMASH detects and analyzes NRPS and PKS gene 
clusters and their products. However, type II PKS gene 
clusters are detected but no detailed predictions are 
made. This study sets out to develop a module, to be 
included in the complete rework of antiSMASH, for 
analyzing type II PKS gene clusters and their 
interesting aromatic polyketide products. The module 
should 
identify 
genes/proteins 
involved 
with 
polyketide biosynthesis in a type II PKS gene clusters. 
The organization of type II PKS gene clusters and their 
polyketide biosynthesis will not be elucidated here 
but we will refer to two excellent reviews by B. Shen 
(2000) [4], and C. Hertweck et al. (2007) [5] for more 
information. In this study, proteins considered 
involved with polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKS 
clusters have been based on these two reviews. To 
summarize they include the minimal PKS proteins: 
ketosynthase (KS), chain length factor (CLF) and acyl 
carrier protein (ACP). Proteins involved with starter 
unit priming: ketosynthase III (KSIII), acyltransferase 
(AT) and acyl-Coenzyme A ligase (LIG). Auxiliary and 
tailoring 
proteins: 
ketoreductase 
(KR), 
cyclase/aromatase (CYC), methyltransferase (MET) 
and glycosyltransferase (GT). Furthermore, a few 
other proteins have been considered since they have 
been found to be involved with polyketide 
biosynthesis in some type II PKS gene clusters. An 
amidotranferase (AMID) is responsible for the 
alternative starter unit priming of malonamyl in the 
oxytetracycline cluster [6]. Aminotransferases (AMIN)

2 
 
found in the chelocardin and oxytetracycline clusters, 
attach an amino group to the polyketide [6, 7]. 
Halogeneases (HAL) attaching chlorine to the 
polyketide are found in the AZ154, chlortetracycline 
and dactylocycline clusters [8–10]. Finally, dimerases 
(DIMER) have been found in the actinorhodin, 
fluostatin and lomaiviticin clusters which catalyze a 
dimerization between two polyketide products 
forming a heterodimer. All these protein types are 
identified and used by the module to make 
predictions about the aromatic polyketide product. 
The module makes predictions about the number of 
malonyl elongations during polyketide biosynthesis, 
the starter unit utilized when priming the PKS, the 
product class as one of the classes described by Zhou 
et al. (2010) [11] and finally the molecular weight of 
the product. To assess the general prediction 
performance of the module a leave-one-out cross 
validation has been performed. 
Methods 
Type II PKS gene cluster data set construction 
A data set of 89 type II PKS gene clusters has been 
constructed in this study. It served as the foundation 
on which the module was developed. The clusters 
were collected from several sources such as 
secondary 
metabolite 
databases 
(MiBIG 
[12], 
DoBiscuit [13], IMG/ABC [14]), previous related 
literature [15] and recent literature concerning newly 
identified clusters [16, 17]. Sequences of proteins 
involved with polyketide biosynthesis were extracted 
from the clusters and assembled in fasta-files, one for 
each type of protein. Annotations were added to each 
protein individually regarding its cluster of origin, 
product, gene name and function. Information about 
each cluster and its products can be seen in the 
supplementary material, table S1 and S2. 
Detection 
of 
proteins 
involved 
with 
polyketide biosynthesis 
A profile hidden Markov model (pHMM) was built for 
each protein type known to be involved with 
polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKS clusters. Protein 
sequences for each protein type were aligned using 
MUSCLE 3.8.31 [18], and a pHMM was built based on 
the alignment utilizing HMMER 3.12b [19]. To reduce 
false positive hits of the pHMMs, two trusted cut-off 
bitscores were computed for each pHMM, one for 
hits and one for domains, using the formula:

Where bitscores is a list of resulting bitscores of hits 
or domains from the given pHMM trusted cut-offs 
wish to be computed, run on all protein sequences on 
which it is based. For pHMMs based on more than five 
protein sequences c was set to 0.2, otherwise 0.5. The 
increased c value for pHMMs based on five or less 
protein sequences was set in order to counteract 
overfitting of the pHMM to the few protein 
sequences. All pHMMs and their trusted cut-off 
bitscores can be seen in the supplementary material, 
table S3. A validation of the pHMMs for protein 
detection showed almost perfect results, see figure 
S1 of the supplementary material. 
Identification of specific protein function 
Profile HMMs were also built for identifying the 
specific function of CLFs, CYCs, KRs and METs. A 
phylogenetic tree was constructed based on the 
multiple sequence alignment of each protein type 
using FastTree 2.1.10 [20]. Each tree was visualized, 
and clades were defined and assigned to a group with 
a tentative protein function. The phylogenetic trees 
with defined clades and clade groups can be seen in 
the supplementary material, figures S2-7. The protein 
sequences of each clade group were extracted and 
aligned using MUSCLE 3.8.31. A pHMM was built for 
each clade group based on the alignment with 
HMMER 3.12b. Two trusted cut-off bitscores were 
also computed for each pHMM to reduce false 
positive hits. Information about the pHMMs can be 
seen in the supplementary material, table S4. The 
protein function pHMMs were validated in a similar 
manner as the protein detection pHMMs and showed 
nearly perfect results, see figure S8 of the 
supplemental material. 
Protein BLAST databases were created for proteins 
involved with starter unit priming since too few 
protein sequences were available for pHMMs to be 
applicable. A protein BLAST database was created for 
KSIIIs, ATs, AMIDs and LIGs utilizing BLAST+ 2.7.1 [21]. 
Validation of the protein BLAST databases were not 
performed here but was indirectly assessed in the 
leave-out-out cross validation. 
Aromatic polyketide product predictions 
The antiSMASH module utilizes the pHMMs and 
protein BLAST databases to make predictions about 
the product of a type II PKS gene cluster. The number 
of malonyl elongations during polyketide biosynthesis 
is predicted based on the CLF(s) found in the cluster. 
Starter unit(s) are predicted based on protein BLAST 
hits. Protein BLAST hits of proteins associated with

assumed to be acetyl with a bitscore and E-value of 
zero. The resulting prediction is a descending list 
according to bitscore of starter unit(s) and their 
associated accumulated bitscore and lowest E-value.

CYC(s) pHMM hits. Each specific pHMM hit is

potential product classes is generated from the 
pHMM hits and the intersection of the sets is the 
predicted product class(es).

Results and discussion 
Starter unit predictions 
Table 1 show starter unit predictions from the leave-
one-out cross validation. A total of 93 starter units are 
utilized by 89 clusters due to some clusters utilizing 
multiple starter units, the frenolicin cluster (acetyl 
and butyryl) and the R118 cluster (acetyl, propionyl, 
isobutyryl and butyryl). A total of 60 starter units are 
predicted correctly of the 93 starter units utilized, 
corresponding to 65%. Disregarding clusters where 
the starter unit is unknown, increases the prediction 
accuracy to 70% (60/86). Considering that 4 of the 12 
starter units are only utilized in one cluster each, and 
thus impossible to predict correctly in the performed 
leave-one-out cross validation, the estimate of the 
prediction accuracy might still be a bit pessimistic. It 
is also noteworthy that the predictions for starter 
units utilized in two clusters each are poor. Only one 
of these predictions is correct for the starter unit 2-
methylbutyryl. 
An 
explanation 
for 
the 
poor 
performance could be different starter unit priming 
mechanisms in each cluster. Meaning all protein 
types associated with priming the PKS for the starter 
units of these clusters would not be represented in 
the protein BLAST databases. An obvious solution to 
the issue would be to include more proteins 
associated with priming of these starter units, i.e. add 
more type II PKS gene clusters to the data set utilizing 
these starter units. However, we are limited by the 
amount of properly annotated type II PKS gene 
clusters available. As soon as a starter unit is utilized 
by more than 3 clusters, the amount of correct 
predictions increases noticeably. The prediction 
accuracy for this group of starter units alone is an 
astonishing 79%. Interestingly, the acetyl predictions 
are particular poor in this group, probably due to the 
prediction method. Acetyl is predicted as starter unit 
for a cluster only if no proteins associated with 
alternative starter unit priming are found in the 
cluster. As it can be seen from the data, malonamyl is 
often predicted as starter unit for a cluster in reality 
utilizing acetyl, confirming this notion. Tuning the 
sensitivity of the protein BLAST databases by defining 
bitscore cut-off values for each protein sequence 
might hinder false positive hits and improve 
performance.

elongations during biosynthesis

during biosynthesis is limited to predict 7, 8 or 9 and 
11 or 12 elongations due to the clustering observed in 
the phylogenetic tree. The predictions from the leave-
one-out cross validations can be seen in Table 2. In 
total, predictions for 74 of the 89 clusters are correct 
corresponding to 83%. Again, disregarding clusters 
for which this information is unknown increases the 
performance accuracy, this time to 89% (74/83). Not 
surprisingly, the unique aurichin cluster where only 2 
malonyl 
elongations 
are 
performed 
during 
biosynthesis is predicted incorrectly. What is 
surprising is the 2 incorrect predictions of clusters 
performing a common 11 malonyl elongations during 
biosynthesis. The explanation for this lies within the 
clustering of the phylogenetic tree where the two 
CLFs from these clusters are not located in the clade 
group associated with 11 or 12 malonyl elongations. 
The same goes for the 5 incorrect predictions for 
clusters where 9 malonyl elongations are performed 
during biosynthesis. Nonetheless, the predictions are 
satisfying overall.

Aromatic polyketide molecular weight 
predictions 
Every predicted molecular weight of the product for a 
cluster was compared to every molecular weight of its 
known products, and only the closest pair of 
molecular weights were considered in the evaluation. 
The pairs showed a clear linear correlation with a 
pearson correlation coefficient of 0.755. However, a 
clear overestimation of the molecular weight was 
observed, see figure S9 in the supplemental material. 
It could be explained by the fact that some genes in 
type II PKS gene clusters are silent, thus not expressed 
but they will be picked up by the module, potentially 
causing an overestimation of the molecular weight of 
the product. On the other hand, some proteins are 
known to catalyze two reactions on the polyketide 
with the same protein domain/active site which is not 
picked up by the module, potentially causing an 
underestimation of the molecular weight of the 
product. In the end the molecular weight predictions 
of the type II PKS products must be considered as 
rough estimates. 
Conclusion 
The type II PKS gene cluster analysis antiSMASH 
module is able to identify genes/proteins involved 
with polyketide biosynthesis in type II PKS gene 
clusters. Furthermore, it makes predictions about the 
starter unit priming the PKS, the number of malonyl 
elongations during polyketide biosynthesis, the class 
and the molecular weight of the aromatic polyketide 
product with reasonably results. The module is 
definitely an improvement to the already excellent 
antiSMASH software. It will aid researchers to identify 
new type II PKS gene clusters, and hopefully lead to 
the discovery of novel aromatic polyketides with 
beneficial bioactivities.
