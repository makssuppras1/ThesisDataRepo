Abstract 
Metal oxides were synthesized as oxygen evolution reaction catalysts for alkaline water electrolysis. 
Co3O4 along with NixCo3-xO4 and CuxCo3-xO4, was manufactured using synthesis route involving 
the Adam’s fusion method. XRD verified the formation of spinel phase. SEM showed a small 
particle size. The spinels were then used to manufacture Ni electrodes for use in 3 electrode and 
alkaline water electrolysis cells. 
Electrochemical characterization was performed with two different setups using polarization 
techniques to determine the oxygen evolution reaction overpotentials of the electrodes. The first 
setup was performed with a 1x1 cm2 Ni mesh as electrode substrate in an 3-electrode cell with a 
reversible hydrogen electrode as reference. The second setup used a circular piece of Ni foam in a 
complete alkaline water electrolysis cell. The results gathered from these experiments provided an 
overview of the respective overpotentials of the eletrodes, which showed that the Cu based 
electrode possessed the highest overpotential while the Ni based and the pure Co3O4 had the lowest 
overpotential. The electrodes were not stable under the conditions of 1 M KOH and 30 ˚C ± 0.1 ˚C. 
The electrodes were further tested in a complete alkaline water electrolysis setup with the 
conditions 6 M KOH and 80 ˚C. Neither type of the electrodes proved to be stable as the observed 
results were similar to that of a pure Ni electrode after a few minutes of operation. As such, the 
binder used, Aquivion, was deemed to have been in too low amounts or completely incompatible, 
which led to the suggestion of using Teflon for future tests.

1. Introduction 
Hydrogen is a gaseous specie, which has many applications, such as for use in the production of 
fertilizers or manufacturing of ammonia. When used as a fuel in combustion reactions, the only 
product is water, which is why it is considered one of the cleanest fuels to exist. Figure 1 provides 
an overview of the supply and demand of hydrogen. 
 
Figure 1: An overview of the practical applications of H2 [1]. 
Hydrogen is very abundant in nature were it is bound to other elements in its monoatomic form. 
However, pure hydrogen is less abundant and thus has to be produced through different pathways.  
Hydrogen can for example be produced from the splitting of water in a water electrolysis cell or by 
steam reforming using hydrocarbons, see Reaction (1) and (2), which shows the process involving 
methane.

These reactions are highly endothermic; however, this can be solved by fuelling the reaction with 
combustion of more fuel and thereby making the process self-sustaining in terms of energy [2]. 
Although this process can be rather competitive concerning its self-sustaining capabilities, it 
generates large amounts carbon oxides and cannot be considered an environmentally clean process 
due to the emissions of greenhouse gases.

Compared to steam reforming, it is not self-sustaining and requires an input of energy from an 
external energy source. Water electrolysis is only cost-efficient on a small scale, but it is far more 
sustainable, since water is readily available in large amounts. 
Thus, effort has been put into designing and optimizing this process.  If using energy from green 
energy sources the production can be considered carbon neutral. This would especially be relevant 
for areas of the world, where there is an excess production of electricity through i.e. wind or water 
turbines, for example in Denmark where the annual production of electricity in 2014 from wind 
mills corresponded to a 39 % of the annual electricity consumption [3]. According to Mogens B. 
Mogensen, research scientist at the Department for Energy Conversion and Storage, DTU, the best 
way to utilize excess energy from wind mills and other sustainable sources is by diverting it to 
electrolysis processes using CO and H2O, thereby enabling the production of CO2-neutral synthetic 
fuels by electrolysis of water and carbon dioxide [4].  
Figure 2 shows an ideal energy system for the production of hydrogen using water electrolysis.

To produce hydrogen, the electrolysis cell needs to be supplied with excess energy. This will allow 
decomposition of water into its components and allowing the evolution of hydrogen and oxygen to 
==PAGE:9==
 
 
 
9 
 
take place [5]. If the process is to be energetically favorable, the overpotentials, which is the excess 
energy required, for the cathode and anode needs to be reduced by introducing cheaper and more 
efficient materials. Since the OER and hydrogen evolution reaction take place at the surface of the 
electrodes, the nature and state of surface materials is quite relevant to the electrolysis cell 
performance.  
Different techniques of water electrolysis are used for production of hydrogen: Polymer electrolyte 
membrane electrolyzers (PEM), alkaline water electrolysis (AWE), and solid oxide (SO). The PEM 
technology dates back to the 1950’s when the United States was involved in the aerospace race. In 
1966, General Electric developed the worlds first electrolyzer using the concept of this technology. 
The PEM technology utilizes a polymeric electrolyte which is only 0.2 mm thick, which means a 
lower ohmic loss [6]. This particular membrane has a strong acid nature, which is caused by the 
presence of sulfonic acid groups (-SO3H) [7]. These groups provides the vector for proton (H+) 
conductivity by using a mechanism called ion exchange [8]. The most commonly used membrane 
for this procedure is Nafion, which is a polymer with long sidechains mimicking the properties of 
Teflon.  
When comparing the PEM technique with AWE, a higher current density can be achieved, up to 1.6 
A/cm2, which was documented in [9]. Efficiency wise,  the PEM electrolysers are located in the 
range of 48% to 65% [7]. Since the PEM cells are utilizing a polymeric proton exchange membrane, 
the operation temperature of such cells are located below 80 °C. On the other hand, it was 
documented that the PEM cells can operate at high pressures, up to 85 bar [10]. The reason behind 
this high operating pressure is the rather compact nature of the electrolysis module, as well as the 
membrane electrode assembly (MEA), which consists of anode, cathode and polymeric membrane. 
Resistance to high pressures enables the system to operate at differential pressure, meaning that 
hydrogen can be produced at a pressure of 35 bar, while oxygen evolution is kept at 1 bar 
(atmospheric pressure) [9] [11].This is quite advantageous since oxygen at high pressure is quite 
hazardous due to it being the oxidizing agent combustion reactions. The materials most commonly 
used for the electrodes are platinum and iridium oxide [12], which are rare-earth materials and as 
such are not the most abundant, which consequently means a high production cost the electrodes. In 
Figure 3, an illustration of the PEM electrolysis cell is shown. Water is split at the anode were 
monoatomic oxygen is oxidized to O2 while the protons are transported through the solid polymer 
electrolyte to the cathode where it is reduced to hydrogen gas, H2.  
==PAGE:10==
 
 
 
10 
 
 
Figure 3: Illustrative depiction of the PEM electrolysis cell [7]. 
The advantages of the PEM electrolysis procedure includes, besides the very thin membrane, a 
smaller mass-volume characteristics and power costs [13], the reversibility [14] [15] and the ability 
to operate at a wide range of differential pressures [16] [17] [18]. The disadvantages of the PEM 
technique is the cost of the electrolyte which is high [19] [20], the corrosive nature of the electrolyte 
[21] [22] [23] and the amount of compatible catalysts is limited to noble metals.  
When it comes the purity of the hydrogen produced by this method, purities above 99.99 vol.% is 
possible [24]. Moreover, the PEM cells can operate at variable power levels. The proton transport 
across the membrane responds quickly to fluctuations in power, while in AWE, the ionic transport 
is more inert in the liquid electrolyte [9]. 
As mentioned previously, one of the disadvantages of PEM is the usage of noble-based electrodes, 
which is one of the greatest shortcomings of this technique. Furthermore, the lifespan compared to 
AWE is shorter [7]. Despite these key disadvantages, this technology has developed a lot since the 
1966’s and is today commercially available. One of the major projects involving the PEM 
technology is called Wind2H2, which is a project in development by U.S. National Renewable 
Energy Laboratory and Xcel Energy. The aim of the project is to analyse the possible configurations 
of for grid integration of hydrogen, photovoltaic and wind systems [25]. Connected to a 10 kW 
photovoltaic solar array and two wind turbines of 10 and 100 kW, are two PEM electrolysers with a

evolution technologies under different power feeding levels. Figure 4 illustrates the principle of the

The AWE technology is more ancient compared to the PEM. Already in 1902, 400+ devices were 
in operation [27]. As with the PEM, this technology is commercially available and when compared 
to PEM, it has a much longer lifetime, up to 15 years of operation [28] [29]. Furthermore, the AWE 
cells possess a significant operation efficiency within the range of 47% - 82% [7]. The operation 
principle of a typical AWE cell is shown in Figure 5. Since 1902, the alkaline water electrolysis cell 
has been improved within the following areas [7]: 
 The space between the electrodes has been reduced so ohmic losses were reduced and thus 
consequently opening up for operation at high current densities. The typical distance is 
below 1 mm, which is called a zero-gap configuration. 
 New materials as diaphragm to replace the predecessor asbestos. The diaphragms used today 
are for example an antimony polyacid impregnated with polymers [30], a composite 
==PAGE:12==

material consisting of a polysulfone matrix and ZrO2 (Zirfon) and a polyphenyl sulphide 
based separator. 
 High temperature alkaline water electrolyzers, which can operate at temperatures up to 150 
°C. The high temperature promotes the kinetics of the reactions at the electode surfaces. 
 The usage of electrocatalytic materials to reduce the overpotentials of the electrodes. This is 
especially a challenge at the anode because the oxidation of hydroxide to oxygen is 
energetically demanding. This is the reason why colbalt oxides have been considered as 
electrocatalyst at the anode. 
 
Figure 5: Illustration of the AWE in operation [7]. 
The AWE cell setup consists of a diaphragm, which separates the two half-cell reactions and limits 
the risk of mixture of the evolving gases while allowing hydroxide ions, OH-, to travel from the 
cathode to the anode. The electrolyte used is a highly concentrated solution of KOH, usually in 
concentrations up to 6 M, hence the name alkaline water electrolysis. Alternatives to KOH are 
solutions of NaOH and NaCl, but they are not used as frequently compared to KOH. Operating 
temperature ranges typically in the region of 65 °C to 100°C [31]. Since the liquid electrolyte is 
concentrated KOH, the major disadvantage of this technique is the harsh corrosive environment at 
which the system operates.  
At the cathode, hydrogen gas is produced while the generated hydroxide ions are transported 
through the membrane within the electric field. Once the hydroxide ions reach the anode, they 
recombine to form oxygen while at the same time releasing electrons. Hydrogen and oxygen gases 
developed at the cathode and anode respectively can cause efficiency issues with respect to the

the alkaline electrolyte is established. This is done to homogenize the concentrations within the cell, 
while also facilitating the evolution of gases and cooling the system [7].

2. Theory 
2.1 Water electrolysis cell 
The basic setup of an electrolysis cell consists of a cathode, anode, electrolyte to conduct the flow 
of ions and a source of power, usually a DC source since this ensures a flow of electrons from the 
anode to the cathode, see Figure 3. 
 
Figure 9: Illustration a basic water electrolysis cell [5]. 
The electrons will flow from the anode to the cathode where they will be consumed by hydrogen 
ions to initiate the evolution hydrogen gas. The negatively charged hydroxide ions travel through 
the electrolyte to the anode where they will be oxidized to oxygen. Electrons from this process will 
subsequently move to the cathode side of the cell [5]. The half cell reactions of the cathode and

In alkaline electrolysis, the electrolyte used is commonly KOH, which enhances the conductivity of 
the solution through the potassium and hydroxide ions. In proton exchange membranes, platinum is 
used as cathode material and iridium oxide as anode material. This method is more efficient than 
alkaline water electrolysis; however, as mentioned, the main drawback of the PEM cells is the 
reliance of the rare-earth metals. 
Instead of using expensive rare earth material based electrodes in the AWE, nickel is used because 
it is more abundant, cheaper and has a comparatively high activity [5]. In order to increase its 
effectiveness, the Ni electrode is coated with electrocatalytic material, which will enhance the 
overall performance of the electrodes by offering a larger surface area and increased electrical 
conductivity.

2.3 Electrocatalyst 
It is highly important to select the proper non-precious metal electrocatalyst, especially for the OER 
at the anode. This is why this master thesis and the works of others [40] [41], have been focused on 
this part of the electrolysis cell system. S. Trasatti [42] stated in an article the requisites that are 
required of the electrode material: 
1. High surface area 
2. High electrical conductivity 
3. Good electrocatalytic properties 
4. Long-term mechanical and chemical stability at the support/active layer and at the active 
layer/solution interface 
5. Minimized gas bubble issues 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
 
20 
 
6. Enhanced selectivity 
7. Availability and costs 
8. Health safety 
Basically, if an electrocatalyst is to be considered better than another, the overpotential has to be 
lower [42]. Achieving all of the requirements listed above is not possible in practice. Therefore, it is 
necessary to make a compromise when desigining electrocatalysts. 
2.4 Spinel 
The catalytic active material used to coat the nickel electrodes in this master thesis belongs to 
material group called spinels. Spinels are recognized by the general formula AB2O4, where A and B 
denotes divalent and trivalent metal ions, respectively. An example of a typical spinel crystal 
structure can be seen in Figure 11. It is shown that the divalent cations occupy the tetrahedral sites 
and trivalent the octahedral sites in the cubic unit cell, while the oxygen anions form a cubic close-
packed structure. A and B does not necessarily have to be the same metal, as with Co3O4, as long as 
the metal ions have different valencies. Spinels can be categorized into various groups such as 
aluminium, iron and chromium, were B is Al, Fe and Cr respectively. 
 
Figure 11: The spinel crystal structure [43]. 
In general, spinels are considered to be poorer conductive materials compared to metals. However, 
researchers have tested various types of spinels in alkaline water electrolysis cells, including 
cobaltite spinels due to their inexpensiveness, stability, outstanding electrochemical activity and 
high electric conductivity. In an article [44] it was proven that the spinel type Co3O4 and La-doped 
Co3O4 coating a substrate composed of Ni, exhibited very low overpotentials of the OER (235 and 
224 mV, respectively) at a current of 100 mA/cm2. This was done in a test setup with 1 M KOH 
operating at room temperature and with the respective oxide loadings 3.4 mg/cm2 and 2.8 mg/cm. 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
 
21 
 
Furthermore, the authors also proved that the BET surface area had been increased, which is a 
relevant factor for the electrocatalytic activity. 
R. N. Singh et al. synthesized spinel-type ternary ferrites with the composition NiFe2-xCrxO4 (0 < x 
< 1) by a hydroxide precipitation method [40]. The procedure was performed by following a 
hydroxide precipitation method at 70 °C at a pH of 11 and O2 bubbling. This was followed up 
drying the now SO4
2—free precipitate for 24 hours at 100 °C and sintering the sample at 400 °C for 
an additional 24 hours. Cyclic voltammetry measurements at a scan rate of 20 mV/s revealed that 
by varying the value of X in the formula from 0.2 to 1 mol in the spinel matrix increased the 
electrocatalytic activity of the electrocatalyst with respect to the OER. The catalyst material was 
applied to a Ni support in 1 M KOH at 25 °C. Values of X = 0.8 and 1.0 produced overpotentials of 
280 mV at a current density of 100 mA/cm2. 
2.5 Synthesis 
The method employed in this master thesis involves the work done by Xu Wu and Keith Scott [41]. 
In their work, they managed to manufacture CuxCo3-xO4 nanoparticles for oxygen evolution in 
alkaline media, utilizing the Adam fusion technique also mentioned in a Ph.D. thesis written by

the CuxCo3-xO4 nanoparticles by a using the starting compound CoCl2 ∙ 6H2O, which is dissolved in 
isopropanol and mixed with stoichiometrically abundant NaNO3, which is the source of substitution 
for the chloride ligands in the Co complex [41]:

will be adapted to the production of Co3O4 and NixCo3-xO4 nanoparticles.

4. Characterization techniques 
4.1 XRD 
X-ray diffraction is a technology, which is used to determine the nature of crystals. An X-ray is 
directed at a solid, crystalline material and depending on the arrangement of the atoms, the X-rays 
will be diffracted in different directions. A detector picks up the diffracted beams and the intensities 
are recorded. The measured intensities are used to generate diffraction patterns that can be used to 
estimate the crystal structure of the compound. This technique is based on the theory proposed by 
W. L. Bragg, which states that when the scattered beams undergo constructive interference the 
phase shift is 2π. This particular condition is described by Bragg’s law, see Equation (14).

n is any positive integer determined by the given order and λ is the wavelength of the beam. As 
mentioned, the diffraction pattern is acquired by measuring the intensities of the scattered beams. 
The intensities are then subsequently plotted as a function of the scattering angle. The principle of 
the Bragg scattering phenomena is shown in Figure 14.

3.2 PSD 
Particle size distribution is relevant to investigate since the size of the particles is a factor relevant 
to the active surface area of the electrocatalyst. The PSD is measured by suspending the sample in a 
solvent (water, ethanol etc.) in a cavity and then exposing it to a monochromatic laser. The principle 
is that the beams from the light source is scattered by the particles in the solution, hence the name 
static light scattering. Figure 15 illustrates the principle of technique. 
 
Figure 15: Principle of static light scattering used to obtain PSD [47]. θ denotes the scattering angle. 
The amount of scattering is largely dependent on the size of the particles and their geometry. By 
measuring the intensity of the light gathered at the detector at several different scattering angles 
relative to the incident beam, it is possible to determine the PSD of the particles. This 
implementation of static light scattering is known as multi-angle light scattering. The results 
gathered are usually plotted as Gaussian distribution.  
3.3 SEM 
Scanning electron microscopy is commonly used for topographic investigation of a solid sample. 
An illustration of a typical SEM microscope can be seen in Figure 16. 
==PAGE:28==
 
 
 
28 
 
 
Figure 16: Schematic representation of a SEM [48]. 
The principle of this technique is that an electron beam is focused onto the surface of a sample 
through multiple lenses and magnets. It is important for this technique that the sample is capable of 
conducting electrons, otherwise an image won’t be obtainable. If such a sample is used, then it is 
necessary to coat it with conductive material, such as gold nanoparticles, prior to investigation. The 
sample is mounted onto the sample holder using a carbon tape.  
The backscattered and secondary electrons from the sample are picked up by detectors, which sends 
the signals to a TV scanner for imaging of the sample. Furthermore, a utility of the SEM called 
EDX can be used for localized elemental analysis, which is somewhat analogous to XRD. 
The backscattered electrons are basically electrons reflected from the sample. Images gathered 
using backscattered electrons provides a strong contrast between elemental compositions, which is 
caused by the high energy elastic reflections of the heavier elements. This can be seen as dark areas 
on the TV scanner, whereas the lighter elements, which reflections are lower in energy, are much 
lighter in appearance. 
Secondary electrons are, contrary to the backscattered electrons, originated from inelastic 
reflections. These reflections, when shown via the TV scanner, shows a more topographic “map” of 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
 
29 
 
the surface of the sample. The secondary electron are commonly used to generate high resolution 
images of samples, down to details of only 1 nm in size. 
Another function of the SEM is the EDX. Due to the unique atomic structures of the elements, the 
X-ray emission spectrum will have identifiable peaks, which is caused by the displacement of 
electrons with the electron beam. The displaced electrons has a specific energy potential and the X-
ray emitted from the displacement is used to identify the elements found in the sample. This 
technique is useful for identifying local variations in the morphology of a sample. However, EDX is 
focused on small area of the sample, so it does not necessarily give the general composition of the 
specimen. Furthermore, elements with similar atomic structures will have peaks, which overlap. 
Nevertheless, since more electrons can be displaced from the same element, this technique is still 
useful for identification of local points of interest of a sample.

the synthesis in this project was mostly calcined at 400 °C, based on [41], using a method based on 
Adam’s fusion reactions. When comparing the experimental XRD patterns of the work presented in

for all the spinels.

The images of CuCo2O4[350] revealed that the degree of agglomeration was significantly lower, 
albeit with the same amount of erosion. Whether this was caused by the introduction of Cu to the 
spinel structure, the lower calcination temperature or a combination of both is hard to say. Testing a 
sample of CuCo2O4[400] under the same conditions in the AWE setup would be able to pinpoint the 
cause of this difference in behaviour by having the temperature as the only variable. The 
backscattered electron imagery provided showed little to no difference between the Ni support and 
the catalyst material, most likely because Ni, Cu and Co are located next to each other in the same 
period in the periodic table. Based on the measurements, it is hard to say if the loading had a 
positive or negative effect since the catalyst had poor adhesion to the substrate. 
Additional investigation of how different synthesis routes affects the efficiency of the catalyst could 
have been investigated. The overpotential of a metal doped cobaltite is documented by the research 
work performed by  R. N. Singh et al. [52], who investigated sol-gel derived spinels MXCo3-xO4 (M 
= Ni, Cu; x <= 1) used for OER. From electrochemical analysis performed with cyclic voltammetry 
at 70 °C in 30 wt% KOH, it was determined that intermediate compositions of the spinels had 
higher activity caused by the high roughness and thus higher surface area. The achieved 
overpotentials of the spinels prepared by this sol-gel procedure was 0.30 – 0.35 V at an apparent 
current density of 1 A/cm2. 
This also brings up another aspect, which could have been investigated: According to the work 
described by R. N. Singh et al. [52] intermediate values of X proved to have to high activity. Thus, 
it could be relevant to investigate different compositions.  
In paper written by Xu Wu and Keith Scott [53], samples of CuxMn0.9-x Co2.1O4 (x = 0, 0.3, 0.6, 0.9) 
was prepared and tested with XRD, SEM, TEM and cyclic voltammetry. The results of the tests 
concluded that the samples of CuxMn0.9-xCo2.1O4 had promising activities in terms of ORR and 
OER using a 1 M KOH as electrolyte, and thus could be used as a non-precious bi-functional 
oxygen electrode. Furthermore, the kinetic parameters were investigated using RDE at rotation 
speeds 100 – 3600 rpm, which could have been investigated in this project if more time had been 
available. Thus, doping with different elements and their effectiveness could be investigated. 
Another method to consider is thermal decomposition. This technique is also used to form oxide 
powders and can be used to apply thin oxide films on conductive support materials [54]. Porous 
NiCo2O4 was achieved by thermal decomposition at 400 °C, which proved to have a high active 
surface area compared to NiCo2O4 produced by cathodic electrodeposition, up to a factor 2. 
==PAGE:50==
 
 
 
50 
 
Additionally, BET measurements would have provided an insight in the available surface area. 
Hsing-Chi et al. has showed that the use of nickel cobaltite aerogels can provide ultralow 
overpotentials in the region of 0.184 V at 100 mA/cm2 [55]. Aerogels are synthetic ultralight 
materials, where instead of having a liquid component compared to normal gels, they have a gas. 
They are mesoporous and possess a very high specific surface area, high porosities and well-
connected three-dimensional through-pore structure. It is these properties which provide the 
researchers with such low overpotentials toward the OER. 
The nickel cobaltite aerogels were prepared using an epoxide addition process, where firstly 
CoCl2∙6H2O and NiCl2∙6H2O were mixed in a molar ratio of 2:1 and dissolved in ethanol. 
Propylene oxide was added in ratio of 11:1 propylene oxide/metal ion ratio. After stirring and 
leaving the solution undisturbed, the gel was obtained after 1 hour of drying at room temperature. 
Subsequent washing with ethanol was done to remove impurities, followed by a calcination at 200 – 
300 °C for 5 hours.  
The article also described an alternative method of producing nickel cobaltite nanoparticles. Instead 
of using Cl based metal precursors, Co(NO3)2∙6H2O and Ni(NO3)2∙6H2O were used. The procedure 
involved mixing Co(NO3)2∙6H2O, Ni(NO3)2∙6H2O and citric acid at a molar ratio of 2:1:3. The 
solution was heated firstly to 30 °C for 30 min under stirring, then the temperature was increased to 
100 °C. The resulting dark red viscous fluid was then dried at 120 °C for 12 hours. A fluffy solid 
product was achieved, which was ground to a powder and calcined at 600 °C for 3 hours.  
XRD analysis revealed that aerogels produced at 300 °C possessed an inverse spinel structure, and 
the NiCo2O4 nanoparticles a major inverse spinel phase and a minor phase of NiO due to 
decomposition. The aerogel sample at 200 °C had retained a normal spinel crystal structure.  
The samples were investigated in three-electrode setup in 1 M KOH at 25 °C with a Pt coil serving 
as counter electrode and a Hg/HgO as reference electrode. Each sample was drop-cast onto a 1x1 
cm2 graphite electrode. In this setup the electrochemical characterization revealed the ultra-low 
overpotential of the aerogel manufactured at 200 °C of 0.184 V at 100 mA/cm2. 
B. Marsan et al. investigated the copper cobaltite as anode material for OER through a thermal 
decomposition preparation on titanium support and nickel supports between 250 and 400 °C [56]. 
The CuCo2O4 was prepared by mixing Co(NO3)2∙6H2O and Cu(NO3)2∙3H2O with isopropanol. The

resulting solution was then applied directly to a 1 cm2 Ni substrate that beforehand had been 
degreased and etched. The isopropanol was subsequently evaporated at 60 °C. The dry nitrate 
mixture was heated in a furnace at the designated temperatures. This process was repeated several 
times in order to achieve full coverage of the Ni substrate and also to get a desirable loading in the 
region of 1.3 to 4.8 mg/cm2. When a desired loading had been achieved, the electrode was annealed 
1 – 5 hours at the previously used heating temperature of 250 – 400 °C. In Table 9, the reported 
influence of the concentration of the mixed nitrates on the number of coatings, morphology and 
mechanical stability are shown.

From Table 9, it was concluded that the optimal conditions for this procedure was using a mixing 
ratio of 0.3 M: 0.6 M. Furthermore, the influence of the decomposition temperature, the temperature 
at which the samples were heated in the furnace, was determined by performing electrochemical 
measurements for samples obtained at different temperatures. The conditions of the measurements 
were 1 M KOH at 25 °C, while using a constant potential of 700 mV vs a saturated calomel 
electrode (SCE).  The optimal mixing ratio was used on a Ti substrate with a loading of 3 mg/cm3. 
The results indicated that the optimal annealing temperature was 275 °C for 1 hour with a 
corresponding current density of 400 mA/cm2. Further analysis of the influence of the loading

showed that the optimal loading was 3.3 mg/cm2. The nature of the substrate was also found to have 
an effect and in relation to its pretreatment. SEM observations made of metal/Cu0.9Co2.1O4 
electrodes indicated the surface morphology was affected by the nature of the metal substrate used. 
Performance of the electrode was found to be related to its roughness factor, according to 
electrochemical measurements [56]. The porosity of the Ni metal appeared to be influencing the 
adhesion of the catalyst and the surface area, so a higher porosity yielded a better adhesion and a 
higher surface area. Steady-state polarization curves of a Ni/ Cu0.9Co2.1O4 electrode was performed 
in 1 M KOH at 25 °C in order to determine the anodic overpotential of the electrode. Low 
overpotentials was identified at current densities of 10 mA/cm2 and 100 mA/cm2 with 
corresponding overpotentials of 280 mV and 320 mV, respectively [56]. 
During the electrochemical tests, it was observed that several of the electrodes suffered from 
erosion perhaps due to the Aquivion binder used under the laboratory conditions. As such, it would 
be relevant to test other binders. PTFE is commonly used as a coating on kitchenware to prevent 
adhesion of unwanted substances. Thus, using PTFE as a binder may increase the durability and 
stability of the electrodes during active operation. However, PTFE is highly hydrophobic, which 
can have a negative influence on the performance of the electrode. In the work performed by Bo 
Chi et al. [51], the electrodes were prepared by dispersing the spinel powders in acetic acid and 
directly coat them on a Ni surface, which had been degreased in aquaregia and etched with HNO3. 
The solvent was evaporated from the electrode by heating it to 100 ˚C for 10 min. This was 
followed up by heating it to 200 ˚C and 400 ˚C for 2 hours. Anodic polarization curves obtained for 
the NiCo2O4/Ni electrodes in 1 M KOH at 25 ˚C at a scan rate of 0.1 mV/s revealed that the 
apparent current density at 700 mV vs. SCE was 201.2 mA/cm2. The electrodes prepared using this 
method proved stable in alkaline water electrolysis up to 8 days. Additional results gathered from

6. Conclusion 
Spinels of the type Co3O4, CuCo2O4 and NiCo2O4 were successfully synthesized using the 
principles of the Adam’s fusion reaction. Samples investigated in XRD revealed that a spinel crystal 
structure had been achieved while SEM images showed spinel particles in the range of 1 µm to 2 
µm. However, when analyzed with regards to the PSD, the results proved to be doubtful since they 
did not agree fully with the observations made from the SEM images. 
Conductivity measurements revealed the Ni and Cu doped samples showed good conductivity 
(>100 S/cm) while pure cobaltite had a poor conductivity (<10-2 S/cm). 
Electrochemical characterization showed that coatings applied to the Ni meshes, utilizing Aquivion 
as binder were not stable. Polarization measurements showed that NiCo2O4 had the lowest 
overpotential followed by Co3O4 > CuCo2O4 ≈ LSC > pure Ni. NiCo2O4 is not noticeably better, 
perhaps more like NiCo2O4 ≈ Co3O4 > CuCo2O4 ≈ LSC. This is hard tell with any amount of 
certainty, since the adhesion of the binder was poor and the loading was varying between the 
electrodes. This can be concluded as a case of an increase in performance over the pure Ni 
substrate. 
In a full cell measurements setup, the same instability tendency was observed. It was concluded that 
the cause of the instability was erosion caused by bubble formation and to the flow of the 
electrolyte. SEM analysis revealed that the Co3O4 sample had not only suffered from erosion, but 
also from severe agglomeration. However, CuCo2O4 calcined at 350 °C did not agglomerate so 
heavily and was rather primarily affected by erosion. Therefore, increasing the binder content or 
changing binder to for example PTFE, might improve the stability of the catalyst coating of the 
anode so it can support the OER more efficiently.
