Abstract 
In this paper, influence of numerous non-straight blade shapes on aerodynamic performance 
of a wind turbine is examined. The main purpose of this thesis is to provide a comparison of 
aerodynamic quantities between the Blade Element Momentum (BEM) model and a vortex 
model with respect to the blade’s deformation magnitude and direction. The research focuses 
on blades with sweep and out-of-plane deflected blades with pre-bend and cone angle 
respectively. Additionally, convergence study of the vortex model is performed for a straight 
blade in order to determine a proper simulation setup which enables user to obtain precise and 
reliable results at possibly low computational time cost. 
It appears that for the investigated vortex model one is suggested to use minimum 20 sections 
along the blade, wake length equivalent to 3 rotor diameters and time increment 
corresponding to preferably no more than 20° rotation to ensure high convergence. It is 
observed further within the thesis that vortex models are more computationally heavy than 
BEM but also more sensitive with regards to aerodynamic evaluation of the non-straight 
blades. It is estimated that the rate at which differences between BEM and vortex model 
become locally non-negligible i.e. 5-10% occur for sweep and pre-bend magnitude 
corresponding to 10-15% of blade’s radius. Cone angle associated with noticeable local 
variations in aerodynamic quantities between the two models is considered to be between 6°- 
8°. Next, it was observed that non-straight blades enable to decrease loads acting on a wind 
turbine which creates potential of using rotors with bigger swept area.

1. Introduction 
The major purpose of this thesis is to compare the Blade Element Momentum Theory (BEM) 
based calculations and vortex models that are implemented in the HAWC2 aeroelastic 
simulation code. The next main goal is to study the differences between results obtained from 
the mentioned models for non-straight blades.  
Firstly, this research is focused on convergence study of vortex models, in order to determine 
how the following parameters affect accuracy and speed of simulation: time increment, 
number of aerodynamic sections along the blade and wake age corresponding to the size of 
calculation domain. Equivalent results from CFD were used as a reference. Most vortex 
models in this work used vortex filaments, while some studies focused on vortex particles. 
This work also investigated strong, soft and loose coupling between the vortex aerodynamic 
and structural models. Furthermore this thesis looks at the computational scaling of MPI 
parallelism. 
The second point of this paper is a study of aerodynamic performance of non-straight blades: 
both forward and backward sweep configurations, pre-bend and coning.  This is done in order 
to notice certain trends and preferably to find a magnitude of deformation where the standard 
BEM solution is less accurate in estimating aerodynamic forces than vortex models. In these 
situations vortex methods have an advantage in engineering applications for these blade 
shapes. This can be attractive for blade design due to the following reasons. Usage of swept 
blades can reduce loads acting on a blade which is associated with improvement of structural 
performance - this allows to use rotors with increased swept area. On the other hand, 
application of out-of-plane deformed blades additionally helps to avoid the tower clearance 
problem. 
2. Theory description 
2.1 BEM theory 
In order to better understand the concepts described further in this paper the general BEM 
theory overview and it’s assumptions are presented within this section.  
The Blade Element Momentum theory is based on Froude’s Momentum Theory for an ideal 
actuator disk and the Blade Elements Theory originally designed by Froude, Taylor and 
Drzewiecki according to [1]. Glauert’s combination of abovementioned theories considers the 
control body as a stream tube discretized into annular elements, which are assumed to be 
aerodynamically independent [2]. Furthermore, the stream tube is considered to be generated 
by a rotor with infinite number of blades. In order to correct these assumptions, the Prandtl’s 
correction concerning tip loss factor (for finite number of blades) as well as Glauert’s 
correction for high values of thrust are introduced.

presented in Figure 2.1 which shows 1-D flow past the rotor. It can be observed that the wind 
has lower velocity U1 at the moment of passing  the rotor plane than the freestream inflow 
velocity U∞. This can be explained by the resistance caused by the pressure increase at the 
pressure side of the rotor as air approaches the rotor plane. Next it can be seen that the air

2.2 Vortex method assumptions 
In order to model the physics of the flow past the wind turbine more accurately it is crucial to 
account for several phenomena that are only dealt with by using corrections in case of BEM 
method. First phenomenon that should be highlighted is that for wind turbine blades just like 
in case of airplane wings, air will try to travel from high pressure area to lower pressure 
region and the easiest way to do that will occur where the chord length becomes shorter and 
where air particles can travel freely between the two sides of the blade, hence especially very 
close to the tip. In this manner it is explainable why the tip vortices are created. Example of 
vortex system behind a wind turbine is presented in Figure 2.6. Moreover, now annular 
elements influence each other because of vortex elements of certain non-zero strength 
(circulation) which induce velocity on all the other elements within computational domain. 
Therefore, the next phenomenon is the fact that now radial velocity component can be 
different than zero. 
 
Figure 2.6. Vortex system for flow past wind turbine [2]. Bound vortices are seen on the blades 
while tip vortices are shed in the wake.   
However after the above short introduction, for better understanding of vortex method, it is 
necessary to describe main assumptions of the lifting line theory. One can imagine creation of 
tip vortices in a different way by using term known as circulation (visualized in Figure 2.7. 
and defined in Eq. (2.11)). It is possible to consider a straight blade with constant chord 
distribution and no rotational speed moving in fluid. Assuming that bound circulation along 
the blade is constant it is clear that at the tip of the blade this constant circulation immediately 
drops to zero. Interestingly this drop in circulation can be represented by trailing vortex of 
strength equal to this drop. This trailing vortex is equivalent to tip vortex in this simple 
==PAGE:15==
7 
 
example. In reality circulation varies along the blade and in order to account for these 
differences the blade is discretized into sections with variable strength. Therefore, trailing 
vortices (of strength equal to the mentioned variations in circulation) are assumed to be shed 
in the wake from each blade’s section edge. These vortices induce velocities on all the 
computational points within the domain. The assumption of generation of trailing vortices is 
valid since vortices according to Helmholtz’s second theorem cannot end in the fluid. Once 
circulation varies along the blade and there would exist only one trailing vortex at the tip, it 
would mean that there would be bound vortices ending in fluid. If there are more than one 
trailing vortex, bound vortices can create closed loops with corresponding trailing vortices 
which is allowed.

3.2 Influence of wake age on convergence – vortex filaments 
Total wake length is one of the parameters which have an impact on vortex model outcome. 
In order to ensure a high convergence to the CFD results it is expected that wake has to be 
long enough such that induced velocities seen at the blades do not change significantly with 
increasing length. In this subsection the wake length needed for obtaining satisfactory wake 
development is investigated. The rest of the parameters remain constant, hence time 
increment corresponds to 10 degree rotation of the blade, while number of aerodynamic 
sections along the blade is chosen to be 21 – this is the original number of sections given 
within CFD simulation input files. 
==PAGE:24==
16 
 
In Figures 3.7 and 3.8 all the aerodynamic results for BEM, vortex method and CFD are 
presented. Wake ages shown vary from 0.1s to 3.0s which reveals certain trends. It is clear 
that for shorter wakes both tangential and normal forces per unit length are overestimated 
which can be explained by looking at normal velocity component (normal velocity is in 
direction of wind speed). It can be observed that normal induced velocity increases with 
increasing wake length since the total normal velocity decreases because of a better 
development of induction field. Underprediction of induction means higher values of angle of 
attack (considering constant rotational speed) which in turn enlarges forces. This can happen 
for example due to the increase in lift coefficient (until it reaches stall region) and due to the 
projection of forces to rotor plane as seen in Figure 2.5 and Eq. (2.10).  
It is also seen that normal velocity in case of CFD calculations is higher close to the tip than 
for BEM and vortex method. This is related to an alternative type of tip loss factor that is used 
in CFD simulation. These differences in normal velocity also affect force estimations near to 
the blade’s tip. Especially when looking at the normal forces one would initially expect 
increase in those forces due to the increasing angle of attack. However, when AoA (angle of 
attack) at a given radial location approaches the angle which gives maximum 𝐶𝐿 (as seen in 
Figure 3.3) there is no significant increase in lift force itself. On the other hand for higher 
AoA projection to the rotor plane supports tangential forces more and decreases normal forces 
(in comparison what one would expect for slightly lower AoA) as seen in Figure 3.7 close to 
the tip. 
Next conclusion is that convergence is visible – there are huge differences between forces 
calculated for 0.1s and 0.3s wake age, while the jump is much less noticeable when the wake 
age varies from 1.0s to 3.0s. Finally it is seen that vortex code results approach the BEM 
outcome, and they are similar to the CFD output. Interestingly, radial velocity is non-zero in 
vortex simulation and resembles CFD results precisely (Figure 3.8), whereas BEM assumes 
no radial velocity. Normally it should be exactly zero for BEM since conservation of radial 
momentum is neglected. However, the local radial direction might be pointed from hub centre 
to ¾ chord points at the blade sections instead of being directed to the points defined by 
blade’s centreline as ½ chord points.

with the time sampling and structure evolving in time. Inner loop for each of those time

moving the filament system with local freestream velocity. Inner iteration on the other hand is 
calculating strength of vortex elements at the lifting line and for the newly created elements in 
the wake.  
For the strong coupling outer aerodynamic update occurs with the same time increment as for 
outer structural update. Inner iterations of both blocks are also synchronized for strong 
coupling.

3.6 Influence of wake age on convergence – vortex particles 
Within this section vorticity is implemented as particles. Single or multiple disconnected 
particles could represent one filament. The potential advantage of using particles over 
filaments is the fact that vortex filaments can start to tangle in complicated flows and this can 
be numerically troublesome. Thus particles avoid the connectivity problem typical in 
filaments methods. 
In order to maintain continuous vorticity field particles represent a finite volume (they have 
specified core radius) and should overlap each other [8]. However, in case of particles with 
finite volume the approximation of circulation update for each iteration is difficult to perform 
in a way which ensures possibly low divergence of velocity field. This is due to the fact that 
vortex particles are subjected to stretching and direction change. This may lead to numerical 
errors especially for bigger computational domain since the flow becomes more divergent due 
to accumulation of error. Particle representation can introduce other numerical problems due 
to Lagrangian grid distortion and excessive stretching. For more details with regards to 
vorticity stretching and velocity divergence see Appendix E. Procedures which can help to 
decrease the numerical error are e.g. adjusting smoothing parameters, grid re-meshing, 
splitting particles which are subjected to extreme stretching [8], experimenting with different 
aerodynamic section distribution (instead of cosine distribution). Finally, results shown within 
this Section are obtained using vortex particles which volume is very close to zero. 
Wake age impact on convergence for particle configuration is studied analogically to Section 
3.2. However, as stated in the above introduction, simulations can become numerically 
unstable for high values of wake age, thus it was worth investigating behaviour of this 
parameter separately for vortex particles. The results can be observed in Figures 3.22 and 3.23 
where reader can see normal and tangential forces as well as induced velocities projected to 
the rotor plane and interpolated to radial locations of the CFD output. Each filament is now 
represented by 3 vortex particles, while filaments are converted into particles after time 
corresponding to 0.5 rotation of the blade (thus vortex filaments exist only in close 
neighbourhood of the rotor). The chosen time increment corresponds to 15 degree rotation 
while number of sections is the same as in original aerodynamic dataset (i.e.  21). It can be 
concluded that for shorter wake ages convergence is similar to pure filament simulation, 
where increasing wake age leads to better development of induction field and better 
==PAGE:36==
28 
 
agreement. However, it can be observed in mentioned plots that if one would like to increase 
the wake age from 0.5s to 1.0s all the results diverge from the CFD and BEM. Increasing 
wake length further causes severe numerical errors, due to the numerical problems discussed 
above. Thus, more work is needed to find a more suitable setup for longer wake 
configurations which have to be studied in the future work. It appears that the maximum wake 
length that can be achieved with particles, is not sufficient to obtain satisfactory agreement of 
the aerodynamic performance.

3.11 Remark on parallel computing MPI (Message Passing 
Interface) 
A computer cluster was used to perform all the simulations. This cluster had 20 separate CPU 
cores per node. This gives the possibility of improving the computational speed using 
parallelization. To obtain all the output within this thesis, two methods were applied: OpenMP 
and MPI. 
OpenMP utilizes multithreaded  execution with shared memory on a single node. The 
advantage of this approach is that threads communicate directly in memory. MPI on the other 
hand creates multiple single threaded processes, and uses message passing to exchange 
information between the processes. This approach has a huge advantage because it can be 
scaled up to execute across multiple nodes. 
With MPI parallelism information exchange between processes has a computational cost that 
can limit the scalability. When the parallel portion of the simulation is heavy for the number 
of CPU’s, then the overhead and serial execution takes a relatively small part of the whole 
CPU time and the simulation is scalable. On the other hand, for smaller problems the CPU 
time could be dominated by overhead and serial execution, in this case the simulation is not 
scalable. Mentioned issue is related to computational efficiency, which can be defined as 
strong and soft efficiency. Strong efficiency determines how fast it is possible to run a 
particular simulation while soft scaling helps to determine the size of the problem. Definition 
of strong efficiency is stated below:

5. Conclusions 
First, conclusions related to the convergence study of vortex models presented in Chapter 3 
will be discussed. It was observed that the investigated vortex models can successfully predict 
quantities like induced velocities and aerodynamic forces generated at the blade. While the 
vortex particles configurations for the used vortex model need to be adjusted in the future 
work, the vortex filaments can be applied for a vast span of parameters e.g. wake length. It 
appears that the vortex model describes the flow past the wind turbine more accurately than 
the BEM model, since it accounts for mutual dependence between annular elements. This 
condition was validated for the straight bladed MEXICO rotor and it is noticeable that for the 
vortex model radial induced velocity (originally equal to 0 for BEM) approaches CFD results 
with increasing wake length and number of sections along the blade. It was discovered, that in 
order to obtain a satisfactory convergence of 98-99% for the vortex model the wake length 
corresponding to at least 2.5-3 rotor diameters should be used. Moreover, blades should be 
discretized into minimum 20 sections, while the time increment should be equivalent to less 
than 20° rotation. Next, after investigation of loose coupling between aerodynamic and 
structural blocks in HAWC2/OmniVor it is possible to state that it enables the user to 
decrease the total simulation time without noticeable variations in results. The 
abovementioned was validated for the straight blade. Hence, one can attempt to apply loose 
coupling to the further study of flexible non-straight blades performance and turbulent inflow. 
==PAGE:77==
69 
 
Ultimately, MPI parallelism performance was examined in terms of computational efficiency. 
It is observed that for the size of problems which ensure high convergence, the drop in the 
strong efficiency is not drastic. Therefore one can conduct similar research with high 
utilization level of available computational resources using MPI. 
Regarding the second part of this study presented in Chapter 4 several points should be 
mentioned. It is worth to emphasize that the results obtained in Chapter 4 with vortex model 
are not equal to BEM results for the straight blade due to the high computational time which 
is required to further decrease the discretization error of the vortex model. Moreover both 
models use different assumptions and there is an inherent error in them as described in 
Chapter 3. Therefore, the relative differences in the aerodynamic quantities presented by the 
models as a function of increasing blade’s deformation magnitude were considered reliable 
for comparison purpose. First, it was shown that the vortex model is more sensitive to the 
introduced blade’s deformation than the BEM model. Starting with swept blades it is clear, 
that the relative differences in prediction of local power coefficient 𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 between straight 
and swept blades are bigger in case of the vortex model.  
For backward sweep it was observed that for the highest studied sweep magnitude (M=0.7 as 
seen in Figure 4.1) before the starting point of sweep the 𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 variations can locally reach 
20% of original 𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 value for the straight blade. This was estimated excluding the strong 
peaks in aerodynamic forces created for vortex model very close to the immediate curvature 
change (sweep starting point). On the other hand, for BEM model there are no differences 
between straight and swept blades regarding 𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 until the sweep starts, due to the lack of 
mutual influence of annular elements. Moreover, it was noticed that BEM tends to 
underestimate induced velocities when used for swept blades. This is concluded because 
𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 increases right after the starting point of sweep before it begins to decrease closer to the 
tip. This increase makes 𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 approach or even exceed the Betz’s limit which shows 
inaccuracy or inconsistency in induction calculation performed by BEM model in HAWC2. 
This effect was opposite in case of the vortex model for the part of the blade right after the 
starting point of sweep. This qualitative difference in trends led to the following observation. 
Taking for instance sweep starting at 0.4R and M=0.7 (Figure 4.9) the 𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 increased by 
10% for BEM in the middle of the blade comparing with the straight blade. On the other hand, 
𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 decreased for vortex model by 20% at the same position at the blade which indicates a 
big deviation between models. Next, it is worth to emphasize that relevant variations in 𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 
predicted by vortex model are seen even for less extreme blade shapes. Backward sweep 
magnitude of M=0.2 can already yield local differences between models of more than 10% in 
𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 before the sweep starting point. For the part of the blade close to the tip BEM and 
vortex model tend to agree better in general. Furthermore, looking at values of total 𝐶𝑃 and 𝐶𝑇 
based on vortex model results one can conclude that sweep has a potential in decreasing thrust 
forces, but it occurs at a cost of lower power extraction in comparison with the straight blade. 
What was observed is that for the used blade geometry the rate at which the total 𝐶𝑇 decreases 
with increasing deformation M is for many blade shapes higher than the rate at which 𝐶𝑃 
drops. Interestingly, sweep of very high magnitude M=0.5 or M=0.7 does not drastically 
diminish the overall aerodynamic performance since the 𝐶𝑃 and 𝐶𝑇 follow similar exchange 
rates as for smaller sweeps. It is however significant to mention that with increased magnitude 
of sweep also the blade becomes longer which makes it more expensive to manufacture. The 
same can be stated with regards to the starting point of sweep – the closer to the blade’s root it 
starts the more costly it gets. Therefore, especially the blades with sweep starting at 0.6R and 
0.8R with magnitudes between 0.0<M<0.2 are worth investigating further. To obtain more 
detailed view on the swept blade’s performance the next step would be to conduct a twist 
==PAGE:78==
70 
 
optimization study for the blade shapes of interest. Finally, it was observed that BEM is less 
sensitive in predicting total 𝐶𝑃 and 𝐶𝑇 with respect to the sweep magnitude. For instance in 
Figure 4.13 when M is varying from 0.0 to 0.2 for sweep starting at 0.8R it is seen that 𝐶𝑃 for 
vortex model diminishes from 0.47 to 0.435 while it increases from 0.44 to 0.45 for BEM. 
Thus, for this blade shape ∆𝐶𝑃,𝑣𝑜𝑟𝑡𝑒𝑥= −0.035, whereas ∆𝐶𝑃,𝐵𝐸𝑀= 0.01 which shows non-
negligible difference. Ultimately, it can be stated that applying backward sweep enables to 
decrease the loads acting on turbine’s blades which can allow usage of rotors with bigger 
swept area. 
Similar conclusions to the above can be drawn with regards to the forward sweep. However, 
while BEM model is not sensitive to the direction of sweep, the vortex model reveals some 
differences. For forward sweep the bound vortices are supporting normal induced velocities in 
the opposite direction to the backward sweep bound vortices. It appears that for strongly 
curved blades this forward sweep bound vorticity overcomes influence of the trailing vortices 
on induced velocities. This leads to increased local 𝐶𝑃 for the inner part of the blade for 
forward sweep and this in turn affects also the total 𝐶𝑃 which is in general higher than for 
backward sweep. However, improvement of the total 𝐶𝑇 is found lower. Interestingly, for 
some of blade shapes with forward sweep both 𝐶𝑃 and 𝐶𝑇 are considered higher than for the 
straight blade as seen in Figures 4.25 and 4.26 (for vortex model). 
When it comes to the out-of-plane deflection, upwind pre-bent and coned blades were 
investigated. Focusing on pre-bend, the BEM model did not capture any differences from the 
straight blade results before the starting point of pre-bend at the blade. Vortex model was 
again more sensitive to the deformation magnitude M. It was shown in Appendix B that for 
early start of pre-bend with very high M the results vary greatly between the two models. 
Difference in 𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 between the straight blade and the result for pre-bend starting at 0.2R with 
M=0.7 is at it’s local maximum equal to ∆𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐= 0.25 for vortex calculations. For BEM the 
same maximum ∆𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 is around 0.13, hence much lower. Even considering later start of pre-
bend at the blade and lower M=0.2 it is common that this ∆𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 difference between the 
models reaches more than 10% of original  𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 values for straight blade. Moreover, it was 
observed that  𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 for pre-bent blades diminishes more with increasing M than in case of 
swept blades. The same conclusion is related to the total 𝐶𝑃. On the other hand, main trends 
show that also 𝐶𝑇,𝑙𝑜𝑐 decreases more for pre-bend than for sweep configuration. However, the 
slopes of total 𝐶𝑃 and 𝐶𝑇 which are observed for pre-bend indicate that 𝐶𝑃 drops relatively 
faster than 𝐶𝑇. This is the opposite result to the sweep configuration. On the contrary, with 
higher decrease in 𝐶𝑇 and in turn in loading, there is an opportunity to use rotors of bigger 
size, while simultaneously avoiding the tower clearance issue due to the out-of-plane 
deformation. 
Equivalent conclusions can be made when considering coned blades. Out-of-plane curvature 
is constant for them (since they are straight blades oriented out of the rotor plane) and the 
differences between the BEM and vortex model results for various cone angles can be seen 
along the whole blade. Highest variations occurred for the inner part of the blade, thus closer 
to the blade’s root which is for upwind coning deeper in the wake. With increasing cone angle 
the results of vortex model simulations show higher induced velocities than for BEM (blade is 
deeper in the wake). This affects aerodynamic forces at the blade more for the vortex 
approach than for BEM. Therefore also non-dimensional 𝐶𝑃,𝑙𝑜𝑐 and 𝐶𝑇,𝑙𝑜𝑐 get lowered more 
for vortex model. This indicates that vortex approach accounts more accurately for a 
particular section’s location in the wake. Hence, the bigger the cone angle is chosen the higher 
differences between vortex and BEM models are. Total values of 𝐶𝑃 and 𝐶𝑇 show that 
==PAGE:79==
71 
 
similarly to the pre-bent blades the relative drop of power is faster than the drop of thrust with 
increasing cone angle. Moreover, it is observed that BEM model in general overestimates 
these total quantities by underestimating the induced velocities with increasing cone angle. 
For instance one can see in Figure 4.40 that within the cone angle span defined from 0° to 15° 
the 𝐶𝑃 decreases by 0.055 for vortex model and by 0.035 for BEM. This leads to a difference 
of 0.02 between the two models within this span which corresponds to roughly 5% of straight 
blade 𝐶𝑃 prediction. 
Finally, with regards to the structural performance, it was observed that for the swept blades 
the flapwise bending moment decreases with increasing sweep magnitude. However, this drop 
becomes more visible for sweep starting early on the blade and only for high magnitudes of 
sweep M=0.2-0.7. On the other hand, in case of the out-of-plane deformed blades the flapwise 
bending moment at the root remains practically unchanged but for high pre-bend magnitude 
or cone angle it increases noticeably according to the HAWC2 output. Moreover, it is worth 
to mention that for the swept blades non-negligible values of the pitching moment at the root 
were observed and they were bigger for configurations with sweep starting early at the blade. 
However, the pitching moment is not increasing drastically and it is still possible to diminish 
it by mounting the blade on the hub with an appropriate angle. This angle should be chosen 
such that pitching moment contribution from the inner and the outer part of the blade mostly 
cancel each other.

7.6 Appendix F: Curve length calculation 
After discretizing the blade into a huge number of sections it is possible to assume that these 
sections are represented by straight lines. Next, calculating their coordinates according to Eq. 
(4.1-4.2) enables obtaining length of all the sections and curve length: 
for i = 1 : length(number_sections)-1    
length_section(i) = ((zCoord(i+1)-zCoord(i))^2+(xCoord(i+1)-xCoord(i))^2)^0.5;      
curve_length(i+1)  = length_section(i)+curve_length(i); 
end 
This calculation enables interpolation of curve length for any chosen radial position along the 
blade.
