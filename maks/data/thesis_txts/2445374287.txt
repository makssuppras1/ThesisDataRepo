Abstract 
The increasing energy demand has challenged the oil companies to extend the exploration and production 
window to higher depths. However, this shift outside the “usual boundaries” comes accompanied by the 
technical difficulties that higher temperatures, reservoir depletion and thermal stress impose on the 
working conditions. The new operation circumstances need to be addressed first through research at core 
scale. 
Thus, the present study focuses on factors that may contribute to well instability such as temperature, 
reservoir depletion and thermal stress. The purposes of this study are to highlight the sensitivity of 
sandstone rock strength to high temperature and to investigate the influence of reservoir depletion and 
thermal stress on stress distribution near the wellbore and on the shear failure pressure (SFP) that is 
required to prevent borehole failure.  
 
In order to investigate the effect of high temperature on mechanical properties e.g. rock strength, and 
Poisson’s ratio, results from triaxial loading tests that were conducted on sandstones specimens at 
ambient 25℃ and reservoir temperature 165℃ and varying confinement stress are used (Orlander et al. 
2017). In this study, data from two different wellbores from deep water oil and gas field in North Sea, are 
presented in order to highlight the challenges and complexities in investigating the effect of high 
temperature and depletion on stress distributions near the borehole and the shear failure pressure (SFP) 
during production within the reservoirs. 
 
Results showed that with increasing the temperature from ambient temperature 25℃ to reservoir 
temperature 165℃, the sandstone strength showed an increasing trend from 58 to 62.63 MPa. 
Dynamically determined mechanical properties of sandstone rock from sonic logs under the reservoir 
condition, have demonstrated slightly low values from statically determined ones.  
Findings showed that due to the temperature differences between the wellbore and the formation (𝑇𝑤<
𝑇𝑜), the hoop (𝜎𝜃) and axial (𝜎𝑧) stresses reduced by 0.79 MPa for a decrease in temperature difference 
of 1℃.  
 
Results from the sensitivity of the shear failure pressure (SFP) to changes in the temperature of sandstone 
showed that raising the temperature from ambient to reservoir, reduces the shear failure pressure (SFP) 
value by 4-5% at pre-production stage in a vertical and horizontal borehole which enhances the borehole 
stability. Estimations for SFP due to thermal stress showed a decrease in the measured SFP value by 5.5 
% at pre-production stage and with a temperature difference of ∆𝑇= −10℃ in a vertical wellbore. On 
the other hand, depletion of reservoir exhibits an increase in SFP value of 7 % when depleting the reservoir 
from 0% to 90% for both the vertical and horizontal well, which makes the borehole less stable and narrow 
the mud window.  
The results inferred from this study will hopefully contribute to a better understanding of the effect of the 
high temperature, reservoir depletion and thermal stress on shear failure pressure (SFP) in order to avoid 
borehole instability issues and ensure a safe and cost-efficient operation.

1. Introduction 
The world’s increasing need for energy has contributed to the rise in the demand for oil and gas resources, 
and revealed a need for further exploration of non-conventional reservoirs. The development of non-
conventional reservoirs usually implies dealing with higher temperatures and pressures, all of these 
resulting in greater exploration and production difficulties.  
 
It is becoming common to drill high pressure and high temperature wells (HPHT). A number of reservoirs 
with depths over 5000m (Zhao et al. 2012) and temperatures over 150 ⁰C have been drilled in many 
regions across the world such as the Gulf of Mexico, South China Sea, North Sea and Sichuan Basin (Yan 
et al. 2014).  
 
As the depth of the wellbore increases, the formation in-situ stresses and high temperature encountered 
at such depths increase as well. This causes a high stress concentration around the borehole that may 
result in unexpected or unknown behavior of rock which often leads to borehole instability problems. It 
may particularly affect the stability of boreholes in vertical, horizontal and multilateral well. When these 
borehole instability problems happen during production, they cause a big loss of financial resources and 
time as they interrupt or stop the production process. 
 
High temperature is one of the factors that has a significant influence on the mechanical parameters of 
the rock. It causes thermal expansion and probably chemical changes of the mineral composing the rock. 
This may in turn lead to changes in the mineralogical and physical properties within the rock bodies, and 
thus result in changes in the micro-cracks and micro structure development and expansion or shrinking. 
All of these changes affect the mechanical properties and behavior of the rock such as rock strength 
compared to those at normal temperature (Tian et al. 2015).  
 
Thermal stresses due to temperature differences between the borehole wall and the reservoir formations 
is of great concern too. This temperature variation between the drilling fluid and the formation, may lead 
to variations in rock volumes due to the heat expansion and shrinking mechanisms of the rock material. 
The heat Expansion and shrinking of the rock formations due to the temperature differences in the 
borehole wall and formations may result in a thermal stress on the surrounding rock, which in turn 
changes the total stress distribution around the borehole. Experimental work demonstrated that when 
the temperature rises by 1℃, stress of 0.4 MPa will be generated for medium-hard rock and 1.0 MPa for 
hard rocks (Yan et al. 2014).Therefore, thermal stresses should not be ignored as the combined effect of 
such a thermal stress and the original stress at the wellbore may cause instability problems if it exceeds 
the rock strength.    
 
During the development of oil and gas reservoir, the pore pressure (formation pressure) will gradually 
experience a reduction unless pressure support is provided from a gas cap or an aquifer. This reduction 
may lead to a change in the in-situ effective stress distribution within the reservoir formations and the 
effective in-situ stress at the borehole. The magnitude of this reduction depends on the production rate 
and extent of pressure support from an active aquifer, an expanding gas cap and from the rock 
compressibility (Addis et al. 1997). As formation fluids are depleted, the drained formations start 
deforming which may induce solids production, compaction, surface subsidence, causing damage and 
borehole failure (Zoback M.D. 2007). Therefore, knowledge of changes of the in-situ effective stress with 
the pore pressure reduction is important, as in-situ effective stress affects all physical properties of rock

geophysical data, (III) rock strength, (IV) mechanism of rock deformation and failure and (V) Wellbore

It is obvious that wellbore stability problems are not only a result of changing and challenging stress

In this project, data from experimental work (Orlander et al. 2017) and petrophysical logs from 2 wells in 
North Sea are used. The influence of high temperature on rock strength and Poisson’s ratio of sandstone 
material are investigated by using data generated from mechanical triaxial compressional test at varying 
temperature (ambient and reservoir). This project further develops a numerical estimation to model the 
stresses distribution around the borehole as a function of temperature and reservoir depletion both for a 
vertical and horizontal borehole. In addition, this study accurately establishes the effect of thermal stress 
at the borehole wall as a result of temperature differences between the borehole (drilling mud) and the 
rock formation. Besides, this work investigates the impact of high temperature, reservoir depletion and 
thermal stresses on the minimum well pressure (SFP) that is needed to prevent the collapse of the 
borehole.

2. Literature Review  
Different researches and experimental studies are presented in the literature related to the influence of 
high temperature on rock mechanical properties e.g. rock strength & Poisson’s ratio. Some of those 
publications are:  Sygala et al. (2013), Tian et al. (2014), Lintao et al. (2017), Yan et al. (2014) and Zhang et 
al. (2009). Other scientific publications such as Addis et al. (1997), Mortazavi et al. (2018), Teufel et al. 
(1991), and Aadnoy et al. (1991) investigated the impact of pore pressure reduction on stress 
redistribution within the reservoir and around the borehole.  
2.1. Influences of High Temperature on Mechanical Properties of Rocks  
2.1.1. High temperature and strength of Rock  
This part presents results from studies and experimental work focused on the effects of high temperatures 
on mechanical properties of rocks in general, and on the uniaxial compressive strength (UCS) and 
Poisson’s ratio of sandstone material in specific. 
 
Some researchers show that different rocks change differently by heating them at various temperatures. 
This shows clearly that the rock strength changes depending on many factors such as, the mineral 
composition, structural and textural characteristics, strength of given mineral constituents, grain size, 
porosity, and nature of bonding between them (Sygala et al. 2013). An additional important factor, which 
affects the rock strength in high temperature, is the thermal expansion and shrinking of the constituent’s 
minerals that determine the level of contact surface between particles.  
 
The minerals composing the rock undergo chemical changes at high temperature, as different chemical 
reactions take place within the rock such as loss of crystal bound water, thermal expansion and micro 
cracking of mineral grains, re-crystallization as well as the development of voids. All these micro changes 
will lead to a macro-scale variations in the behavior of the strength of the rock (Saiang 2012).

Lintao et al. (2017) conducted experimental work examining the effect of high temperatures on 
sandstones rock. Sandstones samples were taken from an overburden rock of a coal seam in Poland at a 
depth of 450 m. The sandstones were heated to a range of temperature between 20 and 1000℃, then 
the samples were triaxially tested. In order to highlight microstructural changes, micro-computer 
tomography analyses were conducted on selected samples. The results from the experiment verified that 
high temperature has an important effect on the mechanical properties of sandstones. These authors 
emphasized that for temperature up to 400℃, reduction of moisture content has been noticed due to the 
evaporation of physically adsorbed water and gases within the rock pores. As a result, a positive impact 
of rock strength is observed due to the availability of the additional void space to allow compaction. 
Therefore, an increase in the density of the rock and its potential to resist deformation occurs due to the 
combined action of heating and axial compression. Lintao et al. (2017) concluded that alteration in the 
rock strength is directly associated to variations in microstructure of the rock. 
Yan et al. (2014) established experimentally the correlation between high temperature and rock strength 
for four different rock types e.g. granite, shale, limestone and sandstone. Uniaxial compressive strengths 
were measured at room temperature 𝑈𝐶𝑆𝑐0 and varying temperatures 𝑈𝐶𝑆𝑐. Findings from this study 
show that UCS of granite, shale, and limestone decrease with increasing temperature between 0 and 
200℃, while the UCS for sandstone increased slightly with elevating temperatures. Yan concluded that 
the motion of crystal grains increase in crystalline rocks (granite and Limestone) with increasing the 
temperature, which in turn reduces both the bonding force between the grains and the rock strength. On 
the other hand, micro-cracks in sedimentary rocks (Sandstone) closes as the temperature increases, which 
will result in an increase in the rock strength.   
Zhang et al. (2009) performed experimental study on the mechanical properties of different rocks at 
varying temperature. The specimens used in this work, were taken from a mine in Xuzhou area in china. 
At first the rock specimens were heated to the assigned temperature value. Then the specimens were 
loaded by an electro-hydraulic loading servo. Zhang stressed that sandstone rock exhibited variations in 
peak strength with increasing the temperature. A decrease of 60 MPa was observed when temperature 
of sandstone rock increased from ambient to 200℃. According to the author, the variation in the peak 
strength is due to the deformation and self-regulation of mineral particles that lead to a gradual closure 
of the micro-cracks. The same reason might result in an enlargement of the micro-cracks, leading to a 
decrease in the peak strength of sandstone.

elastic properties of reservoir rocks such Poisson’s ratio. The investigation was based on a triaxial test on 
sandstone samples from a reservoir in Brazil. The samples were tested at different temperature between 
ambient and 160℃ and at varying confining pressure from 2.5 MPa to 20 MPa in order to simulate the

2.1.3. Thermal stress due to temperature differences  
 
Yan et al. (2014) developed a calculation model based on thermo-electricity theory to establish the 
influence of temperature difference between wellbore and reservoir formation. The authors stressed that 
the stress state around the wellbore is subjected to an additional thermal stress as a result of these 
temperature differences. As a result of the additional stress, it was observed that the fracture pressure 
and collapse pressure will be both influenced and subjected to an increase if the temperature in the 
wellbore is higher than the one in the formations. Therefore, Yan et al. (2014) underlined the importance 
of considering also the thermal stress in borehole stability analysis.   
 
2.2. Effect of Reservoir depletion on in-situ stress and borehole stability 
This section gives a brief overview of previous studies and research that have been developed and 
conducted to investigate the effect of reservoir depletion on stress distribution within the reservoir and 
near the borehole and how it may affect the borehole stability. 
Addis et al. (1997) performed a study on reservoir depletion and its effects on wellbore stability 
evaluation. According to this study, producing hydrocarbons from reservoirs not only decrease the pore

3. Background Theory  
3.1. Fundamentals 
Stress is the force that is exerted on a material cross section area. When a force is applied on given 
materials, it may cause the materials to change their shape or volume. Underground formations are 
always in a stressed state due to the weight of the overlying formations, tectonic stresses and continuous 
geological changes. The underground stress state consists of three principal stresses, the vertical stress 𝜎𝑣, 
and the major and minor horizontal stress 𝜎𝐻 and 𝜎ℎ plus the pore pressure 𝑃𝑓. The vertical stress 
(pressure) on the underlying rock and fluid formations is caused by the overburden-the weight of the 
overlying formation.  
Earth’s crust consists of a number of extensive moving discrete tectonic plates that exert forces on each 
other when they collide. A sedimentary basin may be exposed to tectonic forces that create repeated 
cycles of elevation and depression, sea level changes, and erosion. These geological events will affect the 
sedimentary basin and may alter not only the rock mechanical properties but also the current boundary 
conditions of in-situ stresses and pore pressure within the formations (Fjaer et al. 2008). 
3.2. Stresses distributions around boreholes 
When a well is drilled into the formation, solid material is removed from the formation. As a result, there 
will be stress redistribution around the borehole wall. Sometimes, the redistributed stress components 
near the wellbore are greater than what the formation can support, which may result in borehole failure. 
Therefore, knowledge of the stresses distributions around the wellbore and the rock mechanical 
properties are essential for analyzing the wellbore instability problems.  
The in-situ stresses in a borehole are presented in terms of cylinderical coordinates relative to r, 𝜃, and 𝑧 
, where 𝑟 stands for the distance from the borehole axis, 𝜃 is the azimuth angle relative x-axis (𝜎𝐻), and 𝑧 
is the position along the well axis (Fjaer et al. 2008).

3.3. Rock Failure Criteria  
In order to study the in-situ stress of the rock and present the stress distribution around borehole, an 
appropriate failure criterion is required. Therefore, selection of rock failure criterion has a great 
importance during borehole stability analyses.  
 
There are several rock failure criteria available in the literature, some that consider only the minimum 𝜎3 
and maximum 𝜎1 stress such as Mohr-Coulomb rock failure criterion and Tresca criterion, while others

Criteria will be used in this study as it is most widely used method. There are three different rock failure 
types: shear failure, tensile failure and compaction failure.

5. Discussion 
Scientific literatures shows that rock strength depends on various factors such as mineral composition, 
textural, porosity, water content, density, grain size, grains shapes, fracture mechanism and micro-crack 
distribution etc. In a similar manner, some research studies describe the factors that determines the 
elastic response of the rock under high temperature, such as thermal evaporation, constituent minerals, 
thermal expansion and the degree of cementation (Sygala et al, 2013; Lintao et al 2017). 
The findings established statically from the experimental work (orlander et al. 2017), imply that high 
temperature have a significant influence on the strength of sandstones. Increasing the temperature from 
25 to 165 ℃ in the experiments (Orlander et al. 2017), raised the rock strength from 58.38 to 62.65 MPa. 
The result obtained is in a good agreement with the findings found in the literature (Sygala et al, 2013; 
Lintao et al 2017; Tian et al. 2014; Yan et al. 2013), even though the specimens used in some of those 
studies, which were taken from an overburden above coal seam mine, are not similar to the ones used by 
Orlander et al. (2017). For temperatures below 400℃, changes in the sandstone rock strength are mainly 
due to the evaporation of water & moistures and the emission of gases contained in the rock voids. This 
results in an additional free void space to allow compaction. Therefore, under axial compression, those 
free voids tend to compact and close. Thus, the rock density increases which will enhance the rock ability 
to resist deformations (Yan et al. 2013). The discussion mentioned above is valid only for the laboratory 
conditions, as in the reservoir water can’t evaporate but remain trapped in the rock. In the reservoir, due 
to the heat expansion mechanism of the mineral particles composing the rock under high temperature, 
the rock might be exposed to deformation and self-regulation. This, in turn will lead to a gradual closure 
of the micro-cracks within the rock and an increase the rock strength.       
Zhang (2009) has shown that the strength of sandstones decrease from 170 to 110 MPa with an increase 
in temperature from ambient to 200℃. In the opinion of the author, this reduction in the peak strength 
is mainly due to deformation and self-regulation of the internal mineral particles of the sandstone after 
being heated, which increases the number of micro-cracks openings, and in turn increases the porosity 
and reduces the ability of the rock mass to resists deformations. The differences between zhang (2009) 
and Orlander (2017) results, can be due to the fact that the specimens used in zhang (2009) experiment 
were taken from a mine with a shallow depth. Unlike, specimens used in Orlander (20017) experiment 
were taken from a higher depth, has different mineral composition, underwent different burial conditions, 
and were exposed to a higher temperatures and pressures.  
Sandstone mechanical properties that were determined dynamically from sonic logs under the reservoir 
conditions, were slightly lower than the static mechanical properties of the same sandstone (Table 13). 
Those differences are compatible with the results from previous studies (Fei W.et al. 2016). The static 
cohesion (𝑆0) and angle of internal friction (𝜑), are found to be greater than the dynamic ones. The static 
measurements didn’t account for the presence and effect of the fluids in the rock as the experiment was 
conducted on dry specimens. On the other hand, dynamically determined mechanical characteristics by 
using Lal’s empirical correlation, ignored the impact of increasing temperature. In the same way, empirical 
correlations are usually derived from data from shallow depth, but won’t necessarily fit deep formations. 
One of the mentioned reasons, might be behind the variation between dynamic and static mechanical 
properties.   
==PAGE:54==
 
 
44 
 
There were only a few publications reporting the impact of high temperature on the Poisson’s ratio of a 
sandstone. The behavior of the estimated Poisson’s ratio for sandstone both at ambient and reservoir 
temperature showed a general decreasing trend at low confinement stress 0.75, 3 and 10 MPa. The 
heated specimens recorded always a slightly larger values than the unheated specimens. Poisson’s ratio 
of heated specimens continued dropping when increasing confinement stress from 10 to 20 MPa, while 
an increasing trend was recorded in the unheated specimen. These data disagree with the findings of 
Araujo et al. (1997), who reported a reduction of 7.2% in Poisson’s ratio as a result of elevating the 
temperature and confinement pressure. On the other hand, findings from another study on granite rock, 
schist and diabase rock established no changes in Poisson’s ratio at temperature ranges 0-400 ℃ (Saiang 
2012). The results obtained on sandstone cannot be compared with those for granite, schist and diabase, 
as the mineral constituents are not similar. In fact, there is no explanation and indication to the reason 
for this variation and deviation in the achieved result of measuring Poisson’s ratio for the sandstone rock 
at varying temperature and stress confinement rate. Apparently the behavior of the estimated Poisson’s 
ratio might be due to an error that occurred during the experimental work as no physical quantity can be 
measured with high accuracy. Another reason might be due to in-homogeneity of the rock specimen used 
during the experiment.  
It was found that when temperature differences between the wellbore and the formation drops by 1℃, 
there is a resulting decrease in thermal stress of 0.79 MPa. The resulting thermal stresses is in a good 
agreement with the findings available in the literature (Yan et al. 2013). The resulting thermal stress at 
the borehole is due to the shrinking mechanism of the minerals composing the sandstone being cooled 
by the circulating mud. As a result of the decrease in the temperature, a tensile stress is applied on the 
borehole wall which decrease the hoop and radial stresses around the borehole. It is obvious that thermal 
stress depends not only on the temperature variation but also on the Young’s modulus and Poisson’s ratio. 
Hard brittle formation with high Young’s modulus and low Poisson’s ratio exhibits a higher thermal stress 
than that of in soft formation with low Young’s modulus and low Poisson’s ratio (Yan et al. 2013).     
The numerical model for stress distribution around the vertical and horizontal borehole showed a high 
stress concentration (hoop stresses) around the borehole as a result of the reduction in formation 
pressure (depletion). In general this results showed a good correlation with theories and with previous 
studies (Addis et al. 1997; Mortazavi et al. 2018; Aadnoy et al. 1991). In fact, the magnitude of the 
reservoir depletion depends on the production rate and extend of pressure support from an active 
aquifer, an expanding gas cap and from the rock compressibility (Addis et al. 1997). For simplicity and due 
the lack of field associated data, production rate and extend of pressure support were ignored in this 
study.   
Results from Mohr diagrams for the vertical well, show that continuous reduction of reservoir pressure 
(above 50 % reduction in pore pressure), will increase the effective hoop (𝜎𝜃,𝑒𝑓𝑓) and effective radial 
(𝜎𝑟,𝑒𝑓𝑓) stress around the borehole. This increase will lead to a larger effective differential stress that 
causes a larger Mohr circle approaching the failure line. This in turn will result in unstable borehole and 
which agrees with the theory and findings presented in some literature (Mortazavi et al. 2018). In addition, 
it was established that the effective stress increases linearly with a decrease in the pore pressure, which 
is aligned with earlier publications (Teufel et al. 1991).  
==PAGE:55==
 
 
45 
 
The overall scope of the study was to investigate the sensitivity of the shear failure pressure (SFP), which 
is closely related to wellbore stability, to changes in the formation temperature, to reservoir depletion 
during production and thermal stress. Increasing the temperature of sandstone from ambient to reservoir 
in the experiment, has increased the rock strength (Orlander et al. 2017). The changes in the sandstone 
strength, reduced the shear failure pressure (SFP) by 4% at pre-production stage (0% depletion). As a 
result, the borehole tend to be more stable. Depleting the reservoir from 100.3 to 10.03 MPa, increased 
the shear failure pressure (SFP) by 7 % in a vertical well. This increase in SFP, will lead to a less stable 
borehole. More or less, same observations were obtained when analyzing the sensitivity of SFP for 
horizontal borehole with varying azimuth angle measured relative to major horizontal stress. 
Measurements for SFP due to the resulting thermal stress, showed a decrease in the measured SFP value 
by 5.5 % at pre-production stage and with a temperature difference of ∆𝑇= −10℃. This decrease in SFP 
is due to the cooling effect of the drilling mud on the borehole wall. Thus, a lower wellbore pressure is 
required to prevent a shear failure collapse.

6. Conclusion 
The analysis of high temperature effects on the mechanical properties has been performed on 
experimentally obtained data from triaxial tests on sandstones rock from an oil and gas reservoir in North 
Sea. Experimental results e.g. uniaxial compressive strength (𝐶0), cohesion (𝑆0) and angle of internal 
friction (𝜑) were compared with values determined from sonic logs using Lal’s empirical correlation.   
Experimentally determined uniaxial compressive strength, together with reservoir depletion and thermal 
stress, were all used in a numerical analysis of wellbore stability to evaluate the sensitivity of the shear 
failure pressure (SFP) for a vertical and horizontal borehole respectively.  
Results from the sensitivity of the shear failure pressure (SFP) to changes in the temperature of sandstone, 
showed that increasing the temperature from ambient (25℃) to reservoir (165℃), increases the rock 
strength (UCS) from 58 to 62.63 MPa and therefore reduced the SFP value by 4% at pre-production stage 
in a vertical which in turn enhances the borehole stability. More or less, same results were obtained when 
analyzing the sensitivity of SFP for horizontal borehole. 
Findings from the experiments on reservoir sandstone with varying confinement stress and temperature, 
showed that Poisson’s ratio of the heated samples is generally larger than the value at ambient 
temperature for confinement stress of 0.75, 3 and 10 MPa. At a confinement stress of 20 MPa, the 
Poisson’s ratio at ambient temperature is larger than the heated value.  It should be noted that an error 
during the experiment or inhomogeneity of the samples could be behind this results. 
The contribution of the resulting thermal stress to the measurements for SFP was significant and it showed 
a decrease of 5.5 % with a temperature difference of ∆𝑇= 10 ℃ and it reduced the hoop stress by 0.79 
MPa with temperature difference of 1℃, which in turn tends to enlarge the mud window and therefore 
enhance the stability of the borehole. 
The reduction in the pore pressure have significantly increased the SFP. As a result, the mud weight 
window for both vertical and horizontal well narrows with decreasing pore pressure and the wellbore 
stability reduced.    
However, the availability and the quality of the relevant logs and well data did put some limitations on 
this work. Besides, the number of samples tested in the laboratory were relatively small to draw a solid 
conclusion on the final results.  
Specifically, when it comes to analyzing well borehole stability in HPHT, laboratory data, which are 
expensive, time consuming, and difficult to obtain, together with good quality well logs, are 
complementary tools that can help in reducing the uncertainties in the analysis of wellbore stability.
