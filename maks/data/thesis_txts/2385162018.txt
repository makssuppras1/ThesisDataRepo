Abstract
The goal of this Thesis is to study the reconstruction of room responses within a large
volume by means of a limited number of measurements. In order to do that, the sound
ﬁeld inside the room is decomposed in a number of plane waves, and their amplitudes
estimated using two diﬀerent solutions, Least Squares and Compressive Sensing.
The results of numerical simulations and measurements in two rooms show that a good
reconstruction is possible up to 300-400 Hz within a volume of at least 1.4 m3 by mea-
suring the response in 30 points. A random distribution of the measured points leads to
better numerical results, and the two solutions for the wave amplitudes estimation seem
to perform similarly.
The biggest challenge for the response reconstruction are the sudden changes of pressure
level over small areas that are characteristic of high frequencies. Therefore, the reconstruc-
tion performs better at low frequencies and in damped room, were no strong modes are
present.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The room impulse response (IR), and its frequency equivalent (FR), are said to contain
all the acoustic information of a space; and mainly depend on two aspects: source and
receiver positions, and room characteristics (geometry, acoustic properties of surfaces and
air absorption). The former involves that the response is a function of space, i.e., for any
combination of source-receiver positions, the room response will evolve diﬀerently.
Ajdler in [1] introduced the plenacoustic function (PAF), a function that gathers the IR
at any point inside a room. The interest on the PAF lies on its various applications.
For example, knowledge on the room response is fundamental for room correction appli-
cations, in which the goal is to remove or equalize to certain extent the inﬂuence that the
room has on the listening experience. However, since the IR changes with the receiver’s
location, these applications are sensitive to spatial missalignment (in [2] it is showed that
moving the listening position by 10 cm can result into changes up to 20 dB). Sampling
the PAF could be used to design a spatially robust room correction that optimizes the
reproduced sound ﬁeld over a larger space.
In addition, many multi-speaker methods for reproduction of spatial audio, such as am-
bisonics or wave ﬁeld synthesis, rely on free ﬁeld assumptions, and therefore, its perfor-
mance is degraded in presence of reverberation [3]. Other applications involve 3D sound
reproduction in virtual reality, since the immersive experience can be improved if the sound
is reproduced correctly while the user is moving through the virtual space. Estimates of
the room response are also used in telecommunication solutions for speech dereverberation,
but its performance is aﬀected if the talker or listener is moving within the room, like in
hands-free devices [4], [5]. The knowledge of all IR of the room could be used to improve
the performance of all these applications.
The acquisition of the PAF, however, entails some challenges, mostly related to the number
==PAGE:9==
2
of measurements and size of the array required. We can conceive the PAF acquisition as
a standard sampling problem performed, not only in time, but also over the space. The
Shannon-Nyquist theorem will then provide the spatial sampling rate (or microphone den-
sity) necessary to reconstruct the sound ﬁeld up to certain frequency, avoiding aliasing in
the reconstruction. In [1] Ajdler presented a theorem for uniformly sampling the PAF in 1,
2 and 3 dimensions. However, this leads into an extremely large and unpractical number
of measurements. More recently, Mignot et al. [6] attempted to reduce the number of
measurement needed by exploiting some knowledge on the propagation of sound waves,
showing that the spectrum of the PAF is sparse.
The sparsity of the PAF spectrum implies two aspects that are central for this thesis.
Firstly, it makes possible the PAF sampling with a few non-uniform spatial measurements,
way below the Nyquist frequency. Secondly, it endorses the use of the compressive sensing
(CS) framework, which comprises a series of techniques for under-sampling super-resolution
if some assumptions are fulﬁlled [7].
A rather complete study on the 3D PAF sampling (with focus on CS), was presented by
Mignot et al. in [6]. The experimental part of their study required the construction of a
2x2x2 meters and 120 microphones array, that they used to interpolate the IR inside its
volume up to 300 Hz. Compared to the uniform sampling, the number of microphones
was greatly reduced, but this large, complex array is still unpractical and far from any
viable consumer product. The scope of this Thesis is the study of possible solutions to
this problem, trying to answer questions such as: what is the minimum number of mea-
surements needed to reconstruct the sound ﬁeld in a conﬁned region?, what error can we
expect in the reconstruction?, can we, not only interpolate, but also extrapolate the sound
ﬁeld outside the array? or, in which frequency range the reconstruction is valid?
The Thesis will be structured as follows: ﬁrst, the underlying theory of sound ﬁeld recon-
struction is presented. We will see how, under some assumptions, a sound ﬁeld can be
expressed by a sum of plane waves, which makes it possible to formulate its sampling as
a set of equations and unknowns. The resulting optimization problem is then presented,
along with two ways of solving it; the "traditional" least squares and the "newer" com-
pressive sensing, are discussed. The Theory chapter ends with a brief description of how
to model the sound ﬁeld in an enclosure by superposition of modes.
The next chapter presents the experiments carried out and their results. The ﬁrst section
deals with numerical simulations, that study the performance of the reconstruction for dif-
ferent number of measurements, their spatial distribution, distance from the array centre,
frequencies and SNRs. The second section presents the results of the experiments done in
two real life scenarios: one lightly damped room and one standardized listening room.
The third and last chapter describes the conclusions that can be withdraw from this study,
along with suggestions on future work that seems interesting to follow.

Chapter 2
Theory
This ﬁrst chapter describes the theoretical background necessary to reconstruct a sound
ﬁeld based on a limited set of pressure measurements. This is not the only way of recon-
structing a sound ﬁeld, and [1] is referred for a full discussion on uniform sampling.
Firstly, the basics of linear acoustics are introduced, starting with the wave equation and
the plane wave approximation. We will then see how the pressure at one point of a sound
ﬁeld can be determined by the sum of plane waves, and how this can be formulated with
a set of equations and unknowns. The next section deals with the optimization problem
resulting from this formulation, and describes two diﬀerent methods to solve it. Finally,
the method used to simulate the sound ﬁeld (and study the reconstruction) is presented.

2.2
Optimization problem
In the previous section, a method to characterize a sound ﬁeld from a set of observations was
presented, yet some considerations have arisen; namely, the ill-posed problem associated
with the reconstruction.
The background theory needed to solve this problem will be
discussed in the following section.
The so-called inverse problems are a class of problems that aim to reconstruct a phe-
nomenon from a limited set of outcome observations. It is common to ﬁnd inverse problems
that are underdetermined, i.e. they involve a system that has more degrees of freedom
than constraints; thus, their solutions are not unique.
The process of ﬁnding the best set from the many solutions that satisfy the constrains is
usually called optimization. In the previous section, equation 2.11 presented the problem

Compressive sensing
Compressive sensing (CS) is a group of rather new techniques that aim to improve the
resolution of a signal reconstruction retrieved from very few observations. CS relies on two
main assumptions:
1. Sparsity of the signal x, which translates into a few non-zero solutions for the L steer-
ing directions. A room acoustics model based on superposition of standing modes
will be described in section 2.3, but for now we can assume that the sound ﬁeld in
a room at low frequencies will be mostly composed by the superposition of a few
(sparse) waves, being then suitable for CS.
2. Incoherence of the H columns. The mutual coherence of the columns of H is related
to the geometry of the array used to sample the sound ﬁeld, the discretization of
the space made by the steering directions and the frequency. A complete discussion
==PAGE:16==
2.3 Modelling the sound pressure in an enclosure
9
on measures of the sensing matrix incoherence can be found in [15], concluding that
random distribution of the sensors composing the array is preferred since it helps to
reduce the coherence of the H columns.
The underlying reason for these requirements is the approximation that CS makes to solve
the optimization problem. Ideally, one would like to ﬁnd the sparse non-zero elements
of vector x, that is, solutions for the l0-norm; however, this is a complex, non-convex
problem. The innovation of CS lies on recognizing that, if the process that samples the
signal (represented by H) is incoherent enough, the l0-norm problem can be relaxed and
approximated by the l1-norm problem [16], which is convex, and one can ﬁnd a numerical
solution relatively fast. The resulting optimization problem can then be written as follows
min
x∈CL ∥x∥1 subject to ∥Hx-p∥2 ≤ϵ
(2.17)
Where ϵ is an estimation of the upper bound for noise present in the sensing process.
A numerical solution for this well deﬁned problem can be found eﬃciently by using the
Matlab toolbox [17].
Both [7] and [15] have shown that CS outperforms "traditional" solutions under some con-
ditions. As we have seen, the signal to reconstruct has to be sparse and the process that
samples it has to be incoherent. In addition, a good estimation of the noise level is also
crucial for the CS reconstruction, as it sets a constrain in the optimization problem. A too
high ϵ estimation will make the signal look less sparse than it actually is; while, if a too
low ϵ is estimated, some of the sources with less strength might be ignored in the solution.
Some research papers [15] propose the re-weighting of the l1-norm problem in case that
a direct measurement of the noise cannot be obtained; in our experimental case, careful
measurements of the background noise will be taken (see Section 4).
Section 2.1.3 has already anticipated an issue regarding the angular discretization of the
space made by the steering directions. Since CS seeks super-resolution, it becomes apparent
that this method is especially sensitive to basis mismatch; i.e., if the actual DOA does
not correspond with any of the steering directions, that speciﬁc wave will not show in
the solution. Therefore, it is important to ensure a densely enough discretization of the
steering angles. In this study the number of steering directions L will be 256.

Modelling the sound pressure in an enclosure
In order to study the performance of diﬀerent measurement distributions, the sound ﬁeld
inside an enclosure is simulated by modal superposition. This is not the only method used
in room acoustic simulations, but it provides fast and accurate enough solutions for the
low frequency response inside rectangular shaped rooms. The following section will give a
brief explanation of the theoretical background for this simulation method.

Numerical results
In order to investigate the performance of the reconstruction under diﬀerent conditions, a
series of simulations have been carried out. These simulations make it possible to study
the inﬂuence that diﬀerent parameters have on the reconstruction; namely, number and
distribution of the observations, distance of the reconstruction from the observations, fre-
quency and signal to noise ratio (SNR).
Since a microphone array is usually conceived as a physical structure that sustains a regular
grid of microphones, the expressions observation distribution or measurement distribution
will be preferred in this Thesis, although all these terms will be used instinctively.
3.1
Simulations
The sound ﬁeld inside an enclosure was simulated by superposition of modes as Section
2.3 described. The simulated room is the Flanking Transmission room located in building
355 at DTU, since its behaviour is already well-known from previous research. In addition,
this room is one of the two that were measured and are studied in Section 4.
The dimensions are 3.29x2.97x4.38 meters, with a reverberation time (T60) of 2.5 seconds
approximately.
This room is lightly damped with its walls, ceiling and ﬂoor made out of concrete. The
same time constant was assumed for each mode, being T60/13.8 (experience from previous
research has shown that this is a fair assumption for this room). A total number of 1000
modes were added in the simulation, making sure that all the modes contributing to the
sound pressure in the frequency range of interest are included in the summation.

one, thus resulting in a poor reconstruction. This example illustrates the reconstruction
for one point, but in order to study the general performance we need to deﬁne more rep-

3.1.1
Distribution of observations
Since the aim is the reconstruction of the three dimensional ﬁeld, the observations have to
be distributed within a volume. During this study we will focus on two diﬀerent distribu-
tions, which are shown in ﬁgure 3.4:
• SPH: distribution on the surface of a sphere quasi-uniformly sampled. This choice
is motivated by the large amount literature available on spherical microphone arrays.
A complete discussion on the design of microphone arrays for sound ﬁeld interpola-
tion can be found in [19]. This study tries to overcome some known drawbacks of
open spherical arrays which make them unstable at certain frequencies. One way
of solving this problem is by making the sphere rigid and account for the scattering
introduced by it [10]. Other solutions lie on distributing extra microphones inside
the open sphere, but it requires more microphones, the choice of their position is
not trivial, and this will not ensure that the extrapolation outside the volume is
improved. For simplicity of the forthcoming measurements, the simulated array is
just a uniform distribution over the surface of a sphere, and therefore, instabilities
at certain frequencies might occur.
• RND: random distribution inside the volume of a sphere. The second distribution
has been chosen based on the recommendations proposed by diﬀerent studies on CS
[6], [15]. In section 2.2 the issue with coherent sensing matrices has been discussed,
and one way to reduce it is by distributing the observations randomly over the space.
It is then expected that CS improves its performance with such distribution.
Unless stated otherwise, both, SPH and RND, are simulated comprising S = 30 observa-
tions and with a radius of 0.7 meters (and volume of 1.43 m3), in such a manner that the
reconstruction over a rather large volume with a relatively small number of observations
is tested.

Figure 3.5 compares MAPE for the LS and the CS solutions when using the SPH and RND
distributions. The results show large variations of the error across frequencies, which illus-
trates its dependence on the mode shape, and ultimately on the room and source-receiver
positions. In any case, by taking the mean error at several positions, the general trend can
be distinguished and analyzed.
The points of evaluation have been divided into two groups: interpolated (inside the vol-
ume that contains the observation points, i.e. the sphere of radius 0.7m) and extrapolated
(outside of this volume).
For the interpolated points (top subﬁgures of 3.5), a general trend of worse reconstruc-
tions towards higher frequencies can be observed, except at very low frequencies, probably
caused by the drop in the frequency response at approx. 40 Hz (see ﬁgure 3.3).
The SPH array presents large errors around 243 Hz, for both LS and CS solutions. At this
frequency the wavelength λ is approximately equal to the diameter of the array (1.4 m),
thus, the large errors are more likely caused by the aforementioned instabilities and spatial
aliasing related to uniform spherical arrays. These errors do not occur in the reconstruc-
tion of the RND array, although rather high errors appear at other frequencies for the LS
solution.
In addition, the SPH array fails at frequencies around 350 and 450 Hz. Apart from the
mentioned instabilities, section 2.2 anticipated that uniform sampling schemes violate one
of the assumptions made by the CS framework, i.e. the incoherence of the sensing matrix
columns. The RND distribution seems to solve this problem to a certain extent. The best
combination seems to be the CS solution applied to the RND array.

The extrapolation, on the contrary, results in large errors of at least 100% for both arrays
at frequencies above 200 Hz. The extrapolation performed by the LS method fails com-
pletely for 100 Hz, but it seems more stable that the CS for higher frequencies.
The accuracy of the modal shape reconstruction can be observed in ﬁgure 3.6. The prob-
lematic frequencies of the SPH distribution are again reﬂected as low MAC numbers.
Assuming that good reconstructions provide MAC values between 1 and 0.9, we can ex-
pect a good interpolation up to 350 Hz. Regarding to the extrapolation, the CS solution
oﬀers higher MAC values, and even a good reconstruction up to 200 Hz when using the
SPH array.
These results might point in the direction stated in [20]: a few measurements carefully
selected inside the volume of a spherical array can be used to avoid instabilities at prob-
lematic frequencies and improve the interpolation. At the same time, by placing most of
the microphones on the surface of the sphere, the extrapolation far from its centre can be
improved.

3.1.2
Number of observations
The previous analysis of the MAPE and MAC has shown that certain frequencies cannot
be resolved by the spherical array with radius 0.7 m, and that this problem can be solved
by distributing the measured points randomly.
These results are obtained when both
arrays comprise 30 measurements, however, it is interesting to study the inﬂuence that the
number of measurements has on the reconstruction.
Figure 3.7 shows the MAPE only for interpolated points when the reconstruction is done
with growing number of observations. For the SPH array (top sub-ﬁgures) it is possible
to observe the three problematic frequencies already mentioned (white stripes at 240, 350
and 450 Hz). Increasing the number of observations does not solve this problem, since the
size of the sphere is kept constant.
The RND array (bottom sub-ﬁgures) is more stable across frequencies, and the ﬁgure
suggests that around 60 measurements would be needed within the volume of interest
(around 1.4 m3) in order to obtain a good interpolation up to 500 Hz.

3.1.3
Distance
Another interesting aspect is the performance of the reconstruction, not only in terms of
interpolation/extrapolation, but as a function of the distance from the array centre. Figure
3.8 shows the MAC number at diﬀerent distances from the array centre across frequencies.
As expected, reconstructing high frequencies and far from the array result in larger errors.
While the RND ﬁgure is more uniform, we can see what ﬁgure 3.5 already anticipated, the
SPH array is not able to resolve certain frequencies inside the volume (e.g. white stripes
at 250 and 350Hz). It is interesting to observe that in the surface of the SPH array (0.7
meters) the reconstruction error is small even for higher frequencies; and that the distance
in which the the response is correctly extrapolated is extended with the SPH array com-
pared to the RND one.

Chapter 4
Measurement results
The previous section has shown that the Theory presented in chapter 2 can be applied to
reconstruct simulated sound ﬁelds. This ideal sound ﬁelds make it possible to study the
inﬂuence of diﬀerent parameters on the reconstruction. The following section present the
results for two real life cases, in which the response has been measured in two physical
rooms.
4.1
Procedure
One of biggest practical issues faced when measuring the spatial response of a room is the
positioning of the microphones. The simultaneous measurement at many points requires
large arrays that hold a large number of microphones (such as the array used in [6]).
Since, in principle, the response of the room does not change over time, one could use
a single microphone to measure the response, move it to the next position, and repeat
the process with as many positions as required. However, this process becomes very time
consuming when the number of measurements increases, since each new point requires the
exact positioning of the microphone. In addition, this can lead to small errors on the
positioning, introducing noise in the reconstruction.
In order to overcome those problems, a UR5 robotic arm was used to measure the spatial
room response. The UR5 has the ability to reach almost any point within a sphere of
radius 0.8 m from its base centre with an accuracy of 0.1 mm. A picture of the UR5
showing its parts can be seen in ﬁgure 4.1, and a technical speciﬁcations for the equipment
can be found in the Appendix.
A 478 mm long, thin steel bar was attached to the head of the robot, which allows the
robot to reach points further away from the robot base and help to reduce the inﬂuence of
the scattering introduced by the main body of the arm. Unavoidably, some scattering will
be introduced by the measurement system, especially the rather bulky and hard base of

by this grid is 3430 Hz, provided that the measurements were noiseless.

Coherence of the sensing matrix
Due to the diﬃculty of deﬁning a clear path for the robot to move over random positions,
only the spherical array was used on the real life measurements. Chapter 2 has introduced
an issue regarding uniform arrays: in order to be able to relax the l0 problem to the l1 one
(and make use of CS), the columns of the sensing matrix H must be in-coherent. It can
be shown that uniform sampling schemes (such as our spherical array) do not fulﬁll this
assumption [15], which might cause large reconstruction errors at speciﬁc frequencies when
most of the 97 measurements on the sphere’s surface are used. A study of the coherence
of the sensing matrix columns of one speciﬁc array is not trivial, and although extremely
interesting, it is left as a future research topic.
One way of trying to solve this problem is by performing the reconstruction with just a
few of the measurements randomly taken from the total 97. Figure 4.5 shows the MAPE
across frequencies for reconstructions performed with diﬀerent number of measurements.
The left side corresponds to the LS solution, and as expected, the more microphones, the
lower the error. However, when studying the CS solution, it is surprising to notice that a
high number of measurements used results in (much) higher errors at frequencies between
300 and 400.
A, perhaps, more clear way of studying this issue can be seen in ﬁgure 4.6, which shows the
MAC for three frequencies on the problematic range (250, 350 and 450 Hz) when changing

Chapter 5
Conclusions
Throughout this Thesis we have introduced a method to sample the spatial response of
rooms with a few number of measurements, and tested it by means of simulations as well as
real life measurements. This last section deals with the conclusions that can be withdrawn
from this study and some suggestions for future work.
The study has shown that the performance of the reconstruction is greatly aﬀected by
the response of the room itself. Strong modes, causing maximum and minimum levels of
pressure, are more diﬃcult to estimate correctly, and we have seen cases in which a larger
number of measurements does not improve the reconstruction. Therefore, rooms with a
more even response will oﬀer better results than lightly damped rooms with large level
diﬀerences on the spatial frequency response.
In regards to the frequency range in which the reconstruction is accurate, low frequencies
present better results, while there is a frequency limit above which the method starts to
fail. This limit, of course, depends on the number of measurements and the size of the
array used. For the studied array (i.e., 30 measurements within a sphere of radius 0.5 m
approx.) the upper frequency limit seems to be around 300 Hz. For the FT room the
Schroeder frequency is about 500 Hz, which means that, above that frequency, the sound
ﬁeld can start to be considered as diﬀuse, and a statistical approach might be more accu-
rate. This seems to be endorsed by the results of this study, since the reconstruction at
500 Hz oﬀers poor reconstructions.
Not only the number of measurements, but especially the size of the array plays an im-
portant role. The simulations have shown that the task of interpolation is more successful
than the extrapolation one. Thus, the correct reconstruction of the sound ﬁeld within a
larger volume (or even in the entire room) would require a more spaced array that com-
prises a larger volume. An interesting topic of future research might be the study of the
==PAGE:44==
37
optimal ratio between measurements and size of the array.
Two diﬀerent distributions of the measurement points within the array volume have been
studied by means of simulations; however, only one of them (the uniform sampled sphere)
was used for the real life measurements. The measurement results have shown the ex-
treme importance of being aware of the limitations imposed by the distribution. Regular
distributions on open spheres might become unstable at speciﬁc frequencies, and maybe
even more important, they violate one of the assumptions on which the CS framework is
based. We have seen that, when applied to a uniform distribution of measurements, the
CS solution can result in totally wrong reconstructions.
This problem has been resolved by randomly selecting a few of the measurements available,
which might helped to reduce the coherence of the sensing matrix. However, it would be
interesting to measure with a totally random distribution and comparing those results and
the one presented in the Thesis. The main reason why the random distribution was not
used for the measurements is the complexity of positioning the robotic arm at points that
are randomly taken. As mentioned in section 4, the UR5 needs a clearly deﬁned path be-
tween positions that avoids restricted areas, such as the one immediately above the robot
base. Thus, although the robotic arm is a extremely useful and versatile tool that makes
possible very precise measurements in a very limited time span, more experience in robotic
control is needed to ’construct’ arbitrary arrays. The control of the robot as well as the
measurements with random distributions are left as interesting topics for future work.
This Thesis has compared two solutions of the optimization problem presented in section
2.2: the traditional Least Squares and the newer Compressive Sensing. Although some
studies have shown the super resolution oﬀered by the CS framework in under-sampling
problems, during the Thesis we have seen that it is not clear that one the solutions per-
forms better than the other. The results were similar for both, especially for the IEC
measurements, and it is not possible to conclude that one of the solutions is preferred.
Other directions for future work arise if one thinks about the possible drawbacks that the
reconstruction method can encounter. Firstly, since being in possession of a robotic arm is
not very common, a study on the inﬂuence of position miss-alignment of the measurements,
along with new, simple ways of positioning a microphone would be interesting.
Secondly, the scope of the Thesis was not the design of a particular microphone array
for sound ﬁeld interpolation, but to test the concept of reconstruction with small number
of microphones, thus, making it practical for real life applications. However, a deeper
understating of optimal measurement distributions is needed.
Thirdly, the focus of this Thesis has been the correct estimation of the frequency response
levels, disregarding the phase information. It would be interesting to perform a analogue
analysis of the error when estimating phase, and the inﬂuence that they may have on ﬁnal
applications such as room correction.
==PAGE:45==
38
From the results of this study we can conclude that it is indeed possible to interpolate the
spatial response of rooms within a rather large space by means of a manageable number of
measurements, at least in the low side of the spectrum. Once we have acquired this infor-
mation, the next logical step is the study of techniques for the correction of such responses,
which one can think will be diﬀerent according to the application and the desired eﬀect.
The identiﬁcation of strong modes and their equalization in multiple points by means of
digital ﬁltering the signals reproduced by a multi-speaker sound system is a hot topic in
acoustics nowadays, and it would be really interesting to see how the knowledge of the
spatial response can be applied for this purposes.
