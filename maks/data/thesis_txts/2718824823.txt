Abstract
When children start primary education, one of the first goals is to be taught literacy, as it enables
us to understand and carry out more advanced tasks. These skills are not straightforward to teach
others, and teachers must follow their students’ literacy development to be able to plan out teaching
activities curated toward improving these skills. However, there is a lack of detailed knowledge about
the central characteristics that contribute to the development of children’s writing skills, which is why
the Automated Tracking of Early stage Literacy skills (ATEL) project has created a framework for
following the students’ development within writing in a detailed manner.
The ATEL project has provided manually labeled children’s texts, where the labeling is performed
across features that assess children’s writing across various characteristics. The focus of this project
is to develop a data-driven machine learning approach that can extract characteristics from children’s
writing. As the number of features is extensive, a subset of features is selected for further study. These
features are Genre, Student narrator (implicit/explicit), Reference, Text organization, Semantic universe,
Holistic evaluation, and Dialogue/voices.
To characterize these features, we give a comprehensive review of several machine learning methods
grouped into (1) TF-IDF-based baseline models, (2) an LSTM model, and (3) models based on the state-
of-the-art BERT architecture. These models are implemented in Python and trained using the dataset
from ATEL. We evaluated the model performance, where we found that the models based on the BERT
architecture performed relatively well and obtained a high accuracy. We also define a smaller version
of BERT, namely BørneBÆRTa, which is trained on less data and has fewer parameters. The model
performance was lower than BERT but higher than the baseline and LSTM models. When needing to
minimize the time for training and performing inference, BørneBÆRTa can thus be used instead of the
full BERT, but with the cost of performance.
As it is expensive in terms of time and resources to manually label children’s texts, we explore
methods such as active learning for acquiring new data points in an alternative manner.
We used
the maximum entropy acquisition function. Compared to randomly sampling new data points, the
maximum entropy acquisition function improved the accuracy and F1-score. However, we could not
conclude that the entropy acquisition function was significantly better.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
In the educational system, one of the primary goals for children when they start school is to be taught
literacy, and for a good reason as it plays an integral part throughout our life. It enables us to understand
and carry out more advanced tasks later in our educational system, engages us in activities in our spare
time, and eventually helps us embark on the job market. However, literacy skills are not straightforward
to teach, as language is complex with sentence structures, grammar, special cases, and more. Teachers
need to be able to follow their students’ literacy development, to be able to plan out activities curated
towards improving students’ literacy skills, and also to catch early signs of, for example, dyslexia, where
a student might require support.
However, there is not much literature concerning the central characteristics that contribute to the
development of children’s writing skills recorded over consecutive years. This might be due to how
diﬀicult it is to track students across school years and classrooms, as suggested by [Baz+17].
The
lack of detailed knowledge can affect teachers’ ability to plan teaching activities and influences how
children learn to write.
Following the development of children is thus an important aspect.
The
Automated Tracking of Early Stage Literary Skills (ATEL) project is an initiative to create a framework
for following children’s development within writing in a detailed manner. The project is a collaboration
between DPU (Danish School of Education at Aarhus University), the National Center of Reading,
DTU, and WriteReader. Together, they examined the writing skills of Year 0 to Year 2 students from
13 primary schools in Denmark.
The writing is carried out using the WriteReader app on iPads. Many children are already familiar
with electronic devices at a young age, but they are mostly used as sources of entertainment.
In
Denmark, many primary schools have employed an initiative to provide iPads to students as early as
first grade as an integrated part of teaching. By using the WriteReader app, children can write texts
in the form of books with pages where pictures can be attached. A teacher then reviews these books
to give personal feedback or corrections for misspellings, grammar, etc. The child then receives this
feedback with the corrected text for comparison, which they can use to learn from their mistakes.
The objective of the ATEL project is to expand the WriteReader procedure by automating the
assessments of books, which requires examining the characteristics of the texts.
As of now, DPU
has manually labeled a sample of the books from WriteReader according to several features, which
we distinguish between text-level and sentence-level features. The text-level features provide general
descriptions of a book, e.g., which genre the book is written in. Meanwhile, the sentence-level features
describe the individual sentences within a book, e.g., by labeling according to the specific tense of
the sentence’s verbal phrase. The labeling is performed across a total of 11 text-level features and 7
sentence-level supercategories, where each supercategory consists of several features. It is possible to
extract a subset of these features by using already known methods within the field of Natural Language
Processing (NLP), however, some of the features are too intricate for these methods and therefore
require manual labeling. Due to the number of features, the labeling process is extensive, requiring
many hours of manual work. Therefore, there is a need to develop an automated framework that can
minimize the time spent on manual labeling while also providing aid for the teacher. It would allow
teachers to give feedback more eﬀiciently and plan teaching activities that would improve the task of
teaching literacy skills to children.

1.1
Our focus
In this project, we are provided with a dataset from ATEL containing the books written by children
using the WriteReader app, along with the annotations from DPU. We will thus use this dataset to
select some of the text-level features for further study, where the end goal is to develop a data-driven
machine learning approach that can extract these features from children’s books. Thus, we aim to
answer the following research questions:
• Which text-level features can be identified automatically using machine learning and NLP meth-
ods?
• How accurately do these classification methods perform for each category, and is there a trade-off
on performance metrics such as precision and recall?
For the ATEL project, acquiring more data requires a lot of resources, as they are manually labeled.
Therefore, it is of interest to explore methods to find unlabeled data points that provide the most
information to reduce the number of training samples needed and thereby reduce the number of data
points to be manually labeled. It is possible to do this by exploring methods such as active learning
for investigating the effect of acquiring new data in an alternative manner, summarized in the research
question:
• Which improvements do methods such as Active Learning provide for state-of-the-art models in
terms of accuracy?

1.3
Literature study
The project fits within the topic of Natural Language Processing (NLP), which is a field in Artificial
Intelligence (AI) that has had many advances through recent years. As technology has developed to
become more eﬀicient and robust, it has allowed for more complex AI systems to be created, to solve
problems within various areas. The goal of NLP is to make computers understand natural language
in the same way as humans. However, this is a diﬀicult task due to the complexity of languages, and
processing a language comes with problems that are dependent on the specific language(s) of interest.
The decision of whether the goal is accomplished is typically determined by whether the computer
program can solve a specific problem. For instance, whether it can translate a piece of text accurately,
or give a fitting answer to a question.
==PAGE:9==
1.3 Literature study
3
The NLP field has its roots in the 1950s, starting with Alan Turing and his proposed Turing
Test [Tur50], which is a way to decide whether a computer has reached a level of intelligence that
is satisfactory. It is a simple setup where a human interrogator engages in a written conversation with
a computer program. The test is passed if the human interrogator can not distinguish whether the
written responses come from a person or are generated by a program. Thus, the program is given input
that it processes to extract the necessary information to generate a reply that answers a question or
continues the conversation in a naturally convincing fashion. The Turing Test was the start of a surge
in NLP. The first methods were symbolic and rule-based, which consisted of a set of instructions defined
by a designer. However, for some tasks, one would end up with many rules to capture the complexity
of language. Therefore, data-driven and statistical methods were explored, with the usage of machine
learning and neural networks.
It is important to note that computers are not able to understand an input of raw, written language.
Different challenges occur depending on the language that one is working with in terms of grammar,
sentence structure, and more. Therefore, one of the tasks is also to find a solution for representing
words and entire texts as numbers. Examples of rather simple representations (word embeddings) are
Bag-of-Words, and TF-IDF [JM21]. Here, entire pieces of text are represented as vectors based on
the occurrence of each word in the text. These have been widely used with many machine learning
models to perform e.g. the task of text classification. However, these representations do not consider
the specific ordering of words. If we consider the two sentences
The cat chased the mouse under the couch.
The mouse chased the cat under the couch.
The Bag-of-Words and TF-IDF methods would lead to the same embedding for both sentences, although
they have different meanings. As a result, the machine learning model would not be able to distinguish
the difference between the sentences. Another problem with these types of embedding methods is that
there is a lack of synonymy between words in the created embeddings [JM21, Ch. 6]. Words with
synonymous meanings are considered as completely different words in a text.
Thus, when dealing with text data, the order of words is important for the meaning and context of
the text. Text data is sequential data, which is commonly dealt with using Recurrent Neural Networks
(RNNs). These are built upon the classical feed-forward neural network but process texts one word at a
time and stores the information using the concept of memory. Memory makes RNNs specifically relevant
for text as interpreting the meaning of a word in a sentence depends on the previous words. Using this,
RNNs learn patterns across a text sequence and thereby the context of a word in the sequence [JM21,
Ch. 9]. When processing texts using RNNs, one can take advantage of different word embeddings than
Bag-of-Words and TF-IDF. As the model processes words sequentially, one could use an embedding
vector for each word. This has been explored by using methods such as Word2Vec [Mik+13a], GloVe
[PSM14], and fastText [Boj+16]. These methods also handle the problem of synonymy as it allows for
words with similar meanings to be mapped closely together in the embedding space, thus capturing
parts of the meanings of the word.
However, it is known that RNNs suffer from the vanishing gradient problem [Hoc+01], which happens
during model training. As a remedy, a type of RNN, namely the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM)
[HS97] was created. The LSTM is widely used but is very slow to train due to how such a network can
not be parallelized. As mentioned earlier, the data is processed one word at a time, i.e., sequentially,
and the current input to the model depends on having processed the previous. This slows down the
training phase, especially with problems where one has to process large datasets.
1.3.1
State of the Art
The current State-of-the-Art (SOTA) models are based on the Transformer architecture that was in-
troduced in 2017 by a team at Google [Vas+17]. The Transformer architecture made parallelization
possible by using the attention mechanism, allowing the tokens in a sequence of text to be processed all
at once instead of sequentially, resulting in a model with a faster training time. The Transformer is an
==PAGE:10==
1.3 Literature study
4
encoder-decoder model where the encoder part of the model is used to build one of the most well-known
SOTA models, BERT [Dev+18], which was created by Google in 2018, a year after they introduced the
Transformer architecture.
The BERT model exploits an essential part of the Transformer architecture. With parallelization
being possible, it is easier to train a model with large amounts of data. The framework surrounding the
model consists of a pre-training step, where the model learns the rules and grammar of the language.
Then, using the now pre-trained model, transfer learning is applied to fine-tune the model to perform
a down-stream task such as text classification. The model has shown impressive results for various
downstream tasks. Google Search announced in October 2019 that they had using BERT models for
search queries in English [Nay19], and just two months later, it was reported that Google Search had
adopted BERT for more than 70 languages [Mon19].
Additional improvements and alterations have been made to the BERT model, such as RoBERTa
[Liu+19], which was developed to ensure more robust optimization during the pre-training step. Another
model is DistilBERT [San+19], which is a smaller, faster, and cheaper version of the BERT model.
DistilBERT learns an approximation of the full BERT model with 40% fewer parameters and which
runs 60% faster. Training a BERT model requires large amounts of data, but literature has shown that
the model can be applied to child-directed speech with a smaller model trained on less data [Hue+21].
The model also accounts for a smaller vocabulary that a child might have, compared to adults. When
testing it on a test suite developed by the authors, the smaller model has led to results comparable to
the full BERT model while being quick to pre-train.
While BERT uses the encoder part, the decoder part of the Transformer architecture is used by an-
other widely known architecture - the GPT (Generally Pre-Trained Transformer) by OpenAI [Rad+18].
This architecture was then further developed into GPT-2 and GPT-3, and in the fall of 2022, OpenAI
released ChatGPT, a chatbot based on GPT-3.5.
The chatbot has been shown to hold impressive
conversations and an immense amount of knowledge. ChatGPT can even write a piece of code in one’s
favorite programming language. Below is an example of the impressive results that can be obtained
from the chatbot:
Input query:
Write an introduction to our master’s thesis, which is about NLP for children’s text, and
where we use the BERT model.
ChatGPT output:
Introduction:
Natural language processing (NLP) is a subfield of computer science and linguistics that
focuses on the interaction between computers and human (natural) languages. It involves
developing algorithms and models that can analyze, generate, and understand human lan-
guage, allowing computers to perform tasks such as translation, summarization, and text
classification. In recent years, NLP has seen significant progress thanks to the advancement
of deep learning techniques and the availability of large annotated datasets.
One important application of NLP is in the field of education, where it can be used to
analyze and understand children’s texts. Children’s texts, however, pose a unique set of
challenges due to their simplicity, informality, and the use of non-standard vocabulary and
grammar. In this master’s thesis, we explore the use of the BERT model, a state-of-the-art
deep learning model for NLP, for the task of analyzing and understanding children’s texts.
The BERT model, developed by Google in 2018, has achieved strong results on a wide range
of NLP tasks and has become a popular choice for researchers and practitioners in the field.
It is based on the Transformer architecture, which allows it to effectively process long-range
dependencies and handle large amounts of data in an eﬀicient manner. In this work, we will
use the BERT model to perform various NLP tasks on a dataset of children’s texts, and
evaluate its performance on these tasks.

1.3.2
Resources in Danish
As Danish is a language used by few people worldwide, we must also consider NLP resources for
processing the Danish language. DaCy [EHN21] is a Danish NLP preprocessing framework made with
SpaCy [Hon+20]. It contains tools for tasks including part-of-speech (POS) tagging and named entity
recognition (NER) for the Danish language. Furthermore, DaNLP [Bro+21] is a repository collecting
datasets and models for different NLP tasks by the Alexandra institute. For Transformer-based models,
one of the most well-known sources is the Transformers library by Huggingface [Wol+20]. It contains
tools to build, train and deploy machine learning models [Wol+20]. They also have a library of datasets
from various languages, including Danish.
BERT has also been trained on the Danish language by Certainly (formerly known as BotXO)
[Cer20]. The model is trained on text data from Common Crawl, Wikipedia, debate forums (dindebat.dk
and hestenettet.dk), and OpenSubtitles. Finding data for pre-training a SOTA model such as BERT
is diﬀicult for less spoken languages such as Danish. The Danish Gigaword Corpus [Str+21] is a corpus
for Danish with over 1 billion words to create a dataset that is representative, accessible, and suitable
for Danish NLP models. The corpus has been used for training the Danish language model, Ælæctra
[Høj21].

CHAPTER 2
ATEL dataset
The Automatic Tracking of Early stage Literary skills (ATEL) project’s goal is to develop an automated
framework for following the development of children’s writing. The project has followed primary stu-
dents from 13 schools in Denmark on their writing. The students are in the first three years of primary
education and are around 6 to 8 years old. The writing is conducted using iPads that are handed out
to students, where they use the WriteReader app. Using the app, the students write texts in the form
of books that contain pages with the possibility of attaching a picture to the page. The setup on the
iPad is shown in Figure 2.1.
Figure 2.1: A screenshot of the setup for the WriteReader app, where the student can attach a picture
and write their own piece of text, while the teacher can add their feedback in another text field. Figure
is taken from https://www.writereader.com/blog/new-version-of-writereader/
After having written the book, the teacher follows up by correcting the book and giving feedback
to the student. Our primary dataset thus consists of 803 child-written books, each containing a set of
metadata consisting of a book ID, student ID, name of the school, grade, and number of pages. Each
page in the book contains the raw text written by the child, together with the same text corrected by
the teacher. We illustrate the structure with an example supplemented with the English translation:
Page 1: En gang bliv en snemand lavet i juleland og han kom til liv
Page 1: Engang blev en snemand lavet i juleland, og han kom til live.
English: Once a snowman was made in Christmas land, and he came to life.

As the students also have a choice of attaching an image to their pages, some books will include references
to the respective image. In our study, we only consider the text after being corrected by the adult. To
look further into the specifics of each book, we inspect the word count of the books. As illustrated in
Figure 2.2, the books are of varying lengths. 95% of all books contain less than 171 words, falling on
the shorter side. It is also seen that very few books consist of over 400 words, with the longest book
being an outlier by having 1515 words.
Figure 2.2: Distribution of the word count within the books in the ATEL dataset. The 95% percentile
of the word count is 171 words. The maximum number of words in a text is 1515 words.
Each book has been processed by the ATEL project, where experts from DPU (Danish School of
Education at Aarhus University) have manually annotated the books to infer categorical features from
the texts. They distinguish between the categorical features on two levels - text-level and sentence-
level. The text-level features are concerned with the characteristics of the entire text. These features
include its specific genre, narrator type, or an assessment of the text quality on a scale from 0 to 5.
The sentence-level features are concerned with the characteristics of the individual sentences in the
book. Some features revolve around, e.g., the sentence’s verbs, roles, and predicates. In total, there
are 11 text-level features and 7 main sentence-level supercategories, where each supercategory contains
several features. The high number of features makes the annotation task both long and costly. This
project is concerned with the text-level features, for which a subset is chosen. These are Genre, Student
narrator (implicit/explicit), Reference, Text organization, Semantic universe, Holistic evaluation, and
Dialogue/voices.

CHAPTER 3
Theory
In this chapter, we present the underlying theory of the methods that we apply for our project. The
project is concerned with Natural Language Processing (NLP), and therefore as a starter, the NLP
terminology will be introduced. As previously stated, the objective is to explore and apply machine
learning techniques for classifying texts written by children. We thus explain the task of text classifi-
cation, some simple methods of representing text as vectors, and simple models that utilize this kind
of text representation. We move further with more advanced methods, both for representing words
but also for models based on deep neural networks such as the LSTM and the SOTA models based on
the Transformer architecture. Furthermore, we explain the methods of active learning and combining
models using ensembles.
3.1
Natural Language Processing
NLP is a field of computer science, AI, and computational linguistics concerned with the interactions
between computers and the natural language of humans. The goal is to process natural language for
computers to gain an understanding of language that a human would have. However, natural language
is a complicated matter, as one has to consider special cases and aspects such as grammar, irony,
and ambiguity [Khu+17], which can appear within the language that is processed. While a piece of
code in your favorite programming language gives a clear-cut set of instructions, which a computer
can understand, a single natural language sentence can sometimes be interpreted in several ways, for
example,
He goes to the bank.
Here, ”the bank” can refer to a financial establishment or the land alongside a river. Thus, understanding
language highly depends on the context too.
When processing natural language, we refer to the body of the text as a document and a collection
of documents as a corpus. The entire set of items used in the document is referred to as a vocabulary,
where the individual items are referred to as be tokens. Tokens are created by breaking a large chunk
of text into smaller pieces (tokenization).
Subsequently, each token can be mapped to a unit of
information. For instance, a document can be broken at word-level, making each word a token. At last,
models not only process single items, but also a collection of items in the form of a sentence or multiple
sentences. These are referred to as sequences.
3.2
Text Classification
In our project, the objective is to be able to define models that can classify the children’s texts from
the ATEL dataset, i.e., the task of text classification, which is a technique within the field of machine
learning. Machine learning comprises a family of methods widely used today for solving various problems
in many industries and sectors. The problems mainly fit into one of two categories, namely supervised
or unsupervised learning. In supervised learning, the objective is to predict a target y from a set of
observed features X. During training, the model learns a mapping from X to y, and for supervised
learning, y is available in the dataset. On the contrary, in unsupervised learning, the target y is not
==PAGE:21==
3.2 Text Classification
15
available in the training dataset, and the goal can be to discover groups within the data or to determine
a distribution for the input space [HSM20].
In the aspect of the ATEL dataset presented in chapter 2, y comprises the feature categories assigned
to the children’s texts. As these are available in the dataset, they will be used for training the models,
which means that our task is a supervised learning problem. The target y can be either a discrete
or continuous variable. If it is discrete, the problem belongs to the category of classification, while if
continuous the problem is categorized as regression. chapter 2 showed that all labels and classes in
the features are discrete, and therefore, the problem falls within the classification category. As we are
working with 7 categorical features, shown in Table 2.1, we consider each variable as a single target y,
i.e., modeled as 7 problems {X, yGenre}, {X, yStudent narrator}, etc.
In the subsequent sections, we explain some simple machine learning methods for text classification.
However, machine learning models can not take raw text as input. Therefore, methods for finding
numerical representations of tokens are presented beforehand. These are in the form of real-valued
vector representations that encode the meaning of the word/token. Such a vector representation is
called an embedding [JM21, Ch. 6], where we distinguish between dense and sparse embeddings. To
start, the sparse embeddings are presented.
3.2.1
Bag-of-Words
One of the most simple cases of representing text is the Bag-of-Words (BoW) model. A BoW word
representation is constructed by counting the number of times individual words occur in a sequence
[JM21, Ch. 4]. This yields a representation, where a sequence is represented as a vector of word counts,
and each position in the vector represents a unique word. Figure 3.1 illustrates the concept of BoW,
where a piece of text is transformed into a vector of word counts as shown on the right. Here, It is seen
that the specific ordering of words within the sequence is not taken into account.
Figure 3.1: Example of the Bag-of-Words representation from [JM21, Ch. 4], where the idea is to
disregard the order of words in the text, and count the number of times each word appears.
The
representation of the text is thus a vector of word counts.
3.2.2
TF-IDF
Another form of word embedding is the Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) model.
TF-IDF is described as the product of two terms. First, term frequency can be defined in several ways,

appear less frequently. These terms might encapsulate more information. Thus we introduce the second
term, namely the inverse document frequency, which is a measure that describes how important each

The TF-IDF is a vector representation that can be used for text classification. It extends the BoW
representation by introducing the IDF term, which considers words that appear less often in the text
and hence, could be more important. Similarly to BoW, a drawbacks of the TF-IDF representation are
that the order of words in a sequence is not taken into account. Furthermore, the synonymy between
words is also disregarded, meaning that words with the same meaning are treated as different words
(e.g., car and automobile). A related problem with using TF-IDF on raw text is when handling different
word forms. The words ”plays”, ”playing” and ”played” are different word forms of ”play”. However,
TF-IDF would handle them as three individual words. As a remedy, one can use lemmatization or
stemming [JM21, Ch. 2] before applying the TF-IDF mechanism. These methods reduce the words to
their root, i.e., ”play” in the previous example. Furthermore, the representations created using a large
corpus can have terms that rarely appear in the documents. This results in sparse vector representations
when using the BoW and TF-IDF techniques.
We proceed by considering some of the simple machine learning classification models that can take
the BoW or TF-IDF representations as input.

3.3
Deep Neural Networks
We have now tackled some of the more simple machine learning methods for solving the problem of
text classification and move on to the topic of deep learning, which is a non-linear method that utilizes
deep neural networks. The simplest type of neural network is the feedforward neural network (FNN),
which is illustrated in Figure 3.3. It consists of layers with neurons connected to other layers of neurons
through the edges in the network. In a FNN, each neuron in layer l is connected to all neurons in layer
l + 1. The edges are assigned weights that are adjustable and influence the network’s predictions.
Figure 3.3: An illustration of a simple Feedforward neural network consisting of 4 layers. The first
layer is the input layer consisting of 4 neurons. The second layer is a hidden layer consisting of 6
neurons. The third layer is another hidden layer consisting of 3 neurons. The last layer is the output
layer, which consists of 2 neurons.
The first layer is an input layer, where the neurons represent each input feature. The neurons of
the first layer are passed to the first of (often) multiple hidden layers that apply non-linear activations
to the input. These are passed into the output layer, which produces the outputs. Besides the FNN
architecture, neural networks can take the form of other architectures, such as a convolution neural
network (CNN) or a recurrent neural network (RNN), which can capture different properties within the
input data.

3.4 Word Embeddings

3.4
Word Embeddings
Before describing LSTMs, we explain the input given to this model. As discussed in subsection 3.2.2,
BoW and TF-IDF represent an entire text as one vector and do not take the sequential ordering of words
into account. These techniques can also lead to sparse vector representations for texts where certain
words in the vocabulary do not appear. Therefore, we present methods to represent words, instead
of entire pieces of text, as dense vectors using neural network techniques like Word2Vec or fastText.
These methods are static embeddings, which usually consist of a large vocabulary. Here each word in
the vocabulary is represented by a distinct vector. The vector representation for a specific word is then
a simple look-up in the vocabulary [JM21, Ch. 6]. Dynamic (or contextual) embeddings differ from
static embeddings by having vector representations that depend on where in the sentence the words
appear. Such embeddings allow homonyms (identically spelled words with different meanings) to have
seperate vector representations [JM21]. The dynamic embeddings will be revisited later for the BERT
model.
3.4.1
Word2Vec
One of the limitations of the sparse word vectorization methods introduced so far is the lack of synonymy
between words in the created vectors [JM21]. This issue was addressed by dense embedding methods,
such as Word2vec [Mik+13a]. Word2Vec is a neural network-based embedding where the main idea is
that words with similar meanings appear in similar contexts. For example, words such as ”boat”, ”ship”,
and ”ferry” could be used in similar contexts in sentences about sailing. The model is trained using a
large corpus to predict a target word based on its surrounding context words, allowing the model to
learn the relationship between words and their meanings. There are two architectures for Word2Vec, the
Continuous Bag-of-Words (CBoW) model and the Continuous Skip-Gram model [Mik+13a]. The Skip-
Gram works well with smaller datasets and is better at representing less frequent words, while CBoW
better represents more frequent words and trains faster. We focus on the Skip-Gram architecture as it
is the foundation for other static embedding methods.
3.4.1.1

n before and after a central word, w(t), in the same sentence. Figure 3.4 illustrates the architecture.

projection matrix. The model output is a probability vector, where the indices in the vector match the
indices in the projection layer. The target for this problem is a one-hot-encoded vector for a context
word the model needs to predict.

3.4.2
fastText
Compared to previous sparse vectorization methods, Word2Vec’s dense embeddings provide improve-
ments in every NLP task. These improvements may stem from the smaller parameter space the em-
beddings provide and the synonymy they capture [JM21].
However, Word2Vec still presents some
challenges, as the method can not handle out-of-vocabulary words and ignores the internal structure of
words, which is a limitation for morphologically rich languages such as Turkish and Finnish [Boj+16].
To handle these challenges, fastText [Boj+16] was introduced and is an extension of the SGNS model
of Word2Vec. fastText handles these issues using a subword model, such that the vocabulary consists
of the words in the corpora and a series of character n-grams of the words. A character n-gram is a
sequence of subwords of length n. So if n = 3, then the word hello would be represented by itself and
the n-grams
<he, hel, ell, llo, lo>
where < and > is special boundary symbols. Words can then be constructed as the sum of the embeddings
of its constituent n-grams, whether the word is in the training corpus or an out-of-vocabulary word.

architecture. Afterwards, we introduce the Long Short-Term Memory (LSTM), which is an extension

3.5.1
Recurrent Neural Networks
A RNN [RM87] is a network architecture widely used for sequential data, and thereby text data, as it
considers the positional order of the input data, which FNNs ignore. This way, it gains an understanding
of the context of words, specifically through the concept of memory. In the classical FNN, inputs are
passed through layers that each apply an activation function to the input. In RNNs, the concept of
memory is modeled by loops in the network that retains information. Figure 3.5 illustrates such a loop,
where it is seen that the input xt is passed through a cell A and returns the hidden state ht at timestep
t. By unrolling the loop, as seen on the right, the information at timestep t pass as input to timestep
t + 1.

have values close to those at initialization.

3.6.1
Transformer
The Transformer architecture (Figure 3.11) was introduced in 2017 by Google [Vas+17] and is the basis
for many large language models, such as the BERT model. The model is a sequence-to-sequence model
initially used for machine translation. Sequence-to-sequence models take a sequence as input and output
another sequence [Neu17], where input and output sequences are typically from two different domains,
e.g., to two individual languages in the case of machine translation. What differentiates the Transformer
architecture from RNNs, is the usage of an attention mechanism. The attention mechanism enables
parallelization, which makes model training more computationally eﬀicient.

3.6.1.1
Self-Attention
Both the encoder and decoder blocks of the Transformer architecture include attention layers. The at-
tention mechanism is one of the integral parts of the Transformer architecture and has allowed for model
architectures to rely on this mechanism instead of, e.g., convolutions and recurrences. Understanding
language widely depends on context, and to this end, it is necessary to establish how the words in a
sequence relate to each other. The idea behind the mechanism is that when processing each word in
a sequence, the word is compared to a collection of other words in order, and subsequently, find those
other words that are important for our current word [JM21]. In this way, we can establish relations
between words.
Specifically, the Transformer architecture relies on the concept of self-attention, which establishes
relations between words within the same sequences. To illustrate, we consider the sentence
The cat drank the milk, because it was hungry.

relating to all other tokens within the same sequence. As seen on Figure 3.12, the values of the vector
refer to how much ”it” should attend to the other tokens. Here, a high value is obtained when relating
”it” to ”cat”.

3.6.1.4
Input and Output Embeddings
As described earlier, language models can not process raw text data, and thus the input to the model
must be a vector representation. For the Transformer, input and output embeddings are fed into the
encoder and decoder blocks, respectively. These are learned embeddings that convert input tokens and
output tokens to vectors with dimension de, i.e., the embedding size.
3.6.1.5
Positional Encoding
Subsequently, a positional encoding is added to map the inputs of both the encoder and decoder blocks
to an embedding space. The positional encoding gives the model a sense of the order of tokens in a
sequence, since the model is based on the attention mechanism and thus does not have a sequential
structure as RNNs do. The idea behind positional encodings is to encode the position of each token
as a vector of dimension de, which is added element-wise to the input and output embeddings. Thus,
the model does not learn the positional information but is provided as part of the input. The model
uses the positional information for the self-attention mechanism, and thereby the order of tokens is
considered when processing a sequence.
In [Vas+17], the authors use the sine and cosine functions of different frequencies for positional
encodings:

3.6.1.6
Linear and Softmax Layers
The output of the decoder block is fed into two main layers: the Linear layer and the Softmax layer.
The Linear layer is a fully connected layer that takes the output of the decoder and projects it into a
space with the same dimensionality as the target sequence. The Softmax layer takes the output of the
linear layer and produces a probability distribution over possible output tokens. Hence, it is used to
convert the output of the linear layer to a format used to make predictions.
3.6.2
Model Architecture
The model architecture of BERT was introduced in 2018 by Google [Dev+18] and, as previously stated,
is based on the Transformer architecture. As seen in Figure 3.11, it is possible to stack the encoder
and decoder blocks N times, which is the feature used for the BERT model. The BERT architecture is
constructed by stacking encoder block, with the authors in [Dev+18] proposing BERTBASE consisting
of 12 layers of encoders, a hidden size of 768, 12 attention heads and 110 million total parameters. They
also propose BERTLARGE with 24 layers of encoders, a hidden size of 1024, 16 attention heads, and a
total of 340 million parameters.
3.6.3
WordPiece
As inputs to the model, BERT uses WordPiece embedding [Wu+16] with a vocabulary size of 30K tokens.
The WordPiece algorithm was created for Korean and Japanese for the Google speech recognition system
[SN12], and it proposes a data-driven tokenization method. The algorithm combines the flexibility of
a character tokenizer and the eﬀiciency of a word tokenizer. The algorithm also provides a way of
naturally handling rare words.
First, the WordPiece model is trained to find the tokens that should appear in the vocabulary. In
the case of BERT, 5 special tokens are added to the vocabulary initially:
• [CLS], which is the classification token.
• [SEP], which is the separator token for distinguishing between different sentences in the input.
• [PAD] is used for padding in the sequence.
• [UNK] represents tokens not appearing in the vocabulary.
• [MASK] is used to mask words.
When training a WordPiece tokenizer its vocabulary is initialized by adding the first character and
subsequent characters from all words in a corpus. For instance, if the corpus consists of a single word
bert, then the initial vocabulary would consist of the tokens b, ##e, ##r and ##t. These tokens only
appear once in the vocabulary, and the prefix ## is added to characters inside a word. Subsequently,
the WordPiece model merges pairs of tokens in the vocabulary by assigning a score to each token pair:
score =
freq. of pair
freq. of first element × freq. of second element =
p(t1, t2)
p(t1) · p(t2).
(3.56)
Thus, the model prioritizes the pairs of tokens with high frequency, but where the individual tokens
appear less often separately in the corpus.
It iteratively adds more tokens to the vocabulary until
reaching the manually chosen maximum vocabulary size and stores the final vocabulary.
When applying the model to a document (during tokenization), the algorithm takes a word and
finds the longest subword (starting from the first letter in the word) that is a token in the vocabulary.
Then, the word is split into the longest subword. For instance, for the word birds, the model finds the
longest subword from the beginning of the word, e.g., bird, and we would obtain the split bird and
##s, assuming that both tokens are in the vocabulary.

3.6.4
Pre-training
The pre-training step in the BERT framework consists of two unsupervised tasks shown to the left of
Figure 3.10, namely Next Sentence Prediction (NSP) and Masked Language Modeling (MLM). In short,
NSP is the task where given two sentences, the model must determine whether the second sentence
follows the first. MLM is the task of masking words in a sequence and filling in the blanks. These
two tasks are trained simultaneously, and the input representation must consider both objectives. The
input to the pre-training step is two masked sentences concatenated in a single sequence. The sequence
is tokenized using WordPiece, and the [SEP] token separates the two sentences. Additionally, the first
token in the sequence is always the special classification token [CLS].
As seen on Figure 3.10, the input embeddings are denoted as E, the final hidden vector of the [CLS]
token as C, and the final hidden vector for input token i as Ti. As seen on Figure 3.14, the input
embeddings for BERT are constructed by taking the sum of token embeddings, segment embeddings,
and position embeddings, which all are constructed from the tokens. Specifically, token embeddings
identify the token being processed, segment embeddings contain information about which sentence each
token belongs to when the input is a pair of sentences, and positional embeddings encapsulate the
specific position of each token.
Figure 3.14: Input embeddings for BERT. The input to BERT is a pair of sentences, for which the

3.6.4.1
Masked Language Modeling
The MLM task is also referred to as the Cloze task in literature and is carried out by masking a percent-
age of the input tokens at random with the objective of predicting those masked tokens. Specifically, the
masking is performed by randomly selecting 15% of all the tokens in each sequence. Of these tokens,
80% are masked by replacing the token with the [MASK] token, 10% with a random token, and the
remaining 10% is unchanged. The two ladder options are for increasing the value of learning context
as the models should be able to find the right word using the context rather than by looking for the
[MASK] token. In the BERT architecture, the final hidden vectors corresponding to the masked tokens
are fed into a linear layer with the same number of neurons as the vocabulary size. Subsequently, a
Softmax activation is applied to obtain a probability distribution over the vocabulary size, which is
compared to the actual word using the Cross-Entropy Loss.
3.6.4.2
Next Sentence Prediction
While the MLM task is used to learn the context within a sentence, the goal of the NSP task is to make
the model understand the relationships between two sentences. The NSP task is a binary task carried
out by choosing sentences A and B for each pre-training example. For 50% of the sentence-pairs of the
input, B is the actual next sentence that follows A (labeled as IsNext), where for the remaining 50%, B
is a random sentence from the corpus (labeled as NotNext). The output C from Figure 3.10 is used for
NSP and determines whether B follows sentence A

3.6.5
Fine-tuning
After pre-training, it is possible to apply the model to various downstream tasks using the parameters
from the pre-trained model, i.e., transfer learning. Downstream tasks include sequence classification
or question-answering, which is the specific task illustrated to the right on Figure 3.10. Through fine-
tuning, the model learns to adapt a pre-trained model to a specified task, while still taking its starting
point in a model that has already learned language and context.
The procedure is carried out by
providing the task-specific input and adding a task-specific output layer to the model. For this project,
the input is the texts written by children, and the model is trained for the output to be the target
class/label for each feature. The fine-tuning phase has proven relatively inexpensive compared to pre-
training, as the model has already learned context and language using the MLM and NSP objectives in
the pre-training step. Thus, the BERT framework is an enabler for increasing eﬀiciency, as it takes its
starting point in the same pre-trained model and runs fine-tuning for individual tasks.
3.6.6
Robustly optimized BERT approach
The success of Transformer-based models and BERT has started a wave of applying the model for
different use cases, but also expanding the concept of the model in terms of training, architecture,
etc. A variant of BERT is RoBERTa (Robustly optimized BERT approach) [Liu+19]. RoBERTa was
proposed by Facebook AI in 2019 - just a year after the release of the BERT model and was designed to
improve the performance of BERT, as the authors found that BERT was significantly under-trained. To
summarize, they present 4 modifications to the pre-training approach and train on a large new dataset
as they discovered that more data for pre-training improves performance on downstream tasks.
In [Liu+19], the authors keep the model architecture fixed but note it as an important area of
future work.
They conduct experiments, where they take their starting point in the configuration
from BERTBASE, i.e., with 12 layers, a hidden dimension of 768, and 12 attention heads as presented
in subsection 3.6.4. In subsequent sections, we explain the primary modifications that constitute the
RoBERTa architecture.
3.6.6.1
Static vs. Dynamic Masking
The masking for the original BERT model is performed statically, i.e., sequences are masked during
data pre-processing. The training data is duplicated 10 times, masking each sequence in 10 different
ways, and given as input to the model every epoch, which means that after 40 epochs, the same mask
is seen 4 times during training. Instead, RoBERTa uses dynamic masking, where the masking pattern
is generated every time a sequence is given as input into the model, which is more space-eﬀicient when
increasing the number of steps or training with larger datasets.
3.6.6.2
Model Input Format and Next Sentence Prediction
In BERT, the authors hypothesize that the NSP task was an important factor in training the model, and
they show that removing the task leads to a decrease in performance for downstream tasks. However,
the authors in [Liu+19] question the necessity of the NSP task and conduct a comparison of alternative
training formats. They propose omitting the NSP objective, as one of their key findings is that modi-
fying the input format leads to a performance that matches or slightly improves the downstream task
performance from BERT.
Instead of passing a pair of segments, the authors propose employing the FULL-SENTENCES
format and solely training the MLM task. With this format, each input consists of whole sentences
from one or more documents, giving it a token length of a maximum of 512 tokens. A separator token
is added between documents if the input sequence is sampled from more than one document. They
also consider a format, where the input may not cross document boundaries. This input format yields
higher performance than the FULL-SENTENCES format, but results in varying batch sizes as some
==PAGE:43==

inputs are shorter than 512 tokens. Thus, FULL-SENTENCES was used for the remainder of their
experiments.
3.6.6.3
Increasing batch size
In [Dev+18], BERTBASE is trained for 1M steps with a batch size of 256 sequences, which is equivalent
in computational cost to training 125K steps with a batch size of 2K sequences, or 31K steps with a
batch size of 8K. As previous work in Neural Machine Translation has suggested using a larger batch size
during training and BERT is amenable to large batch training, the authors experiment with increasing
the batch size to 2K and 8K. They find that increasing the batch size improves both the perplexity for
the MLM objective and the accuracy for the end-task and thus proceed with training using a batch size
of 8K.
3.6.6.4
Byte-Pair Encoding
Instead of WordPiece used by BERT and explained in subsection 3.6.3, RoBERTa uses Byte-Pair
Encoding (BPE) [SHB16] for tokenization.
Using BPE in its original implementation can lead to
many Unicode characters with increasing corpora sizes. Therefore, RoBERTa uses the byte-level BPE
[Rad+19] and considers words as written by bytes instead of Unicode characters. The authors find
that BPE leads to slightly worse performance than WordPiece, but proceed with using BPE as it is a
universal encoding scheme.
The training algorithm is very similar to WordPiece and starts with a base vocabulary of all char-
acters in the corpus. However, instead of the merge rule from (3.56), the BPE algorithm iteratively
searches for the most frequent pair of tokens. These are merged and added to the vocabulary until
reaching the manually chosen maximum vocabulary size. Tokenization of a document proceeds differ-
ently than for WordPiece. It splits each word into its individual characters and applies the learned
merge rules in order instead of searching for the longest subword.
3.7
Active Learning
A big obstacle in machine learning problems is the lack of labeled data, which is also a factor for the
ATEL dataset. As seen in chapter 2, it consists of only 803 samples, and acquiring more is a resourceful
task. It is easy to get an enormous amount of unlabeled data by scraping the internet, e.g., for images or
text. But labeling data is an expensive and diﬀicult task, which makes the acquisition of a good training
set diﬀicult. Sometimes self-supervised learning is utilized on the unlabeled data to pre-train models
and avoid the issues of missing labels. But for tasks where labeled data is required (e.g., when fine-
tuning a pre-trained model), active learning can be used. Active learning is a framework where a model
learns from a small amount of data and then chooses which data points it would like to have labeled.
The active learning loop can be separated into four different steps and is illustrated in Figure 3.15:
1. The model is first trained on an initial training dataset.
2. Then, using an acquisition function, the model selects some data points from an unlabeled pool
of data and tasks an oracle to label them.
3. The oracle then annotates the data. The oracle is an external annotator that is often a human
expert.
4. The newly labeled data points are added to the training set, and a new model is trained on this
updated dataset.
This loop is repeated with the training set increasing in size and can be stopped when a desirable
model performance is achieved. The advantage of active learning is that it reduces the amount of data
that needs to be labeled [GIG17].

4.1.2
The Danish Gigaword Corpus
The Danish language is spoken by few people worldwide, which is reflected in the amount of natural
language data available for use and affects the development of modern-day machine learning models
targeted towards Danish. For training the SOTA models, a large amount of data is necessary, e.g., 160
GB data was used for pre-training RoBERTa, [Liu+19]. Thus, it is crucial to collect enough data, which
also is representative of the specific language or task to be covered. As the pre-training data from the
Gyldendal dataset is not nearly as large as the dataset used to pre-train RoBERTa, we add the Danish
Gigaword dataset to cover aspects of language not covered by the Gyldendal dataset.
The Danish Gigaword Corpus (Dagw) is a billion-word corpus created to accommodate the lack of
data for NLP in Danish [Str+21]. The goal is to create a corpus that is (1) representative, (2) accessible,
and (3) a general-purpose corpus for Danish. The data is collected from different sources and represents
various domains in both written and spoken form, where the data in spoken form has been transcribed
into text. The domain containing most words is Legal, followed by Social Media, Conversation (spoken
language, herein Danish parliament and subtitles), Web (Common Crawl), Wiki & Books, News, and a
small portion of other data taking up only 0.12% of the dataset.
4.2
Data pre-processing
In this section, we present the data pre-processing of the ATEL dataset. The dataset is processed
differently to accommodate the varied inputs for the different models since the baseline models use the
TF-IDF word embedding, the LSTM model uses the fastText embedding, etc. We also explain how the
labels are processed and represented as we work with multi-label and multi-class text classification.
4.2.1
Input
As mentioned in chapter 2, the dataset consists of 803 texts. However, 25 texts are empty or consist
only of white spaces. These texts are removed from the dataset, which leaves a total of 778 texts to
work with.
The data pre-processing for the input texts are split into two steps: A general pre-processing step
used for all models and a pre-processing step specifically applied for each model. The steps for the
general pre-processing are:
• Replacing all newline and tab delimiters as a space character.
• Removing all characters unavailable on a standard Danish keyboard interface. These characters
might be emojis that were decoded into an unintelligible format.
• Removing extra spaces between words.
• Stripping the texts from starting and trailing whitespaces.
The input text is now in a format where a more model-specific pre-processing step can be performed,
which results from the models using different embeddings.
4.2.1.1
Baseline models
The embedding method used for the baseline models is the TF-IDF embedding, but before creating
the embeddings, the texts are normalized.
First, the texts are made all lowercase, so there is no
difference between if words, e.g., are after the punctuation in the middle of a sentence. Then all forms
of punctuation are removed from the text using a regular expression. The texts are then lemmatized.
However, lemmatization requires Part-of-Speech (POS) tagging to provide the right lemma for the word.
==PAGE:49==
4.2 Data pre-processing
43
The POS tagging is done using the spaCy2 library, and the lemmatization is done using the Lemmy3
package.
Then, to get the TF-IDF embedding, the TfidfVectorizer class from the scikit-learn library is
used. The embeddings are created with the following variables:
• max_features = 1500
• min_df = 3
• max_df = 0.75
This results in a vocabulary of max 1500 words, ordered by their term frequency across the text
corpus. The words in the vocabulary need to be present in at least 3 documents and have a document
frequency of less than 75%. Furthermore, the class also removes a list of Danish stopwords. The Danish
stopwords are downloaded from the Natural Language Toolkit (NLTK) library4.
4.2.1.2
LSTM model
The LSTM model uses the fastText embedding (explained in 3.4.2). Here, the text is split between each
space, giving us tokens of words. The tokens are then converted into their vector representations using
a Danish fastText model, which is published by Meta and trained on Common Crawl and Wikipedia5.
The embedding size of the model is reduced from the default value of 300 to 100, and the max sequence
length is set to 171, which is the 95% percentile of the texts’ lengths (as shown in chapter 2), to limit
the input size for the LSTM model. Finally, we pad the input vectors, in case the sequence length is
smaller than the chosen max sequence length.
4.2.1.3
Fine-tuning Transformer-based models
When fine-tuning a Transformer-based model, the embeddings are created using a tokenizer to get the
dynamic embeddings of a text. The tokenizer is loaded using the Hugging Face Transformers library
[Wol+20] and applied to the texts.
The tokenizer automatically pads or cuts the text to the max
input length of the model. The tokenizers used are either from Certainly (formerly known as BotXO)
danish-bert-botxo6 or trained from scratch when we pre-train our model (introduced in section 4.6).
4.2.1.4
Pre-training Transformer-based models
For pre-training the Transformer-based models, we use the Gyldendal children’s books or Danish Giga-
word data. These datasets are not subject to the same general data pre-processing (described above) as
the ATEL data. We create a dataset for the model using the LineByLineTextDataset function from
the Hugging Face dataset library. The function converts each text line in the file into a row in the
dataset. Afterward, we apply a tokenizer that is loaded using the Transformers library and use a data
collator for MLM with a masking percentage of 15%.
4.2.2
Labels
We generated the labels from the text-level information available to each text. This information is
accessible as a Python dictionary, where each feature (e.g., Genre or Perspektiv) either had a single
value or a list of values belonging to them.

4.3
Baseline models
We run two types of baseline models: The first type is Largest Class, which for the multi-class problems
is executed by assigning the observation to belong to the largest class, i.e., the class with the most
observations. For multi-label problems, each label represents a binary problem. Thus, each label is
handled separately, where all observations are assigned to the class (0 or 1) with the most samples.
For the second type of baseline, we used the three models explained in subsection 3.2.3-3.2.5. For
the Naive Bayes model, we assume the likelihood to follow a Gaussian distribution and the prior to be
uniform. The Ridge Classifier is applied using a regularization strength λ = 1.0. The Random Forest
Classifier is applied with 1000 trees and using the Gini impurity function from (3.20). There is no
maximum depth of the tree, thus it terminates when all partitions are pure.
We implemented the models using the sklearn library [Ped+11], and multi-class and multi-label
features are handled differently. For the multi-label features, the MultiOutputClassifier() method
from sklearn is applied, which treats each label as a target. Thus, the strategy is to fit one classifier
per label.

4.5
Danish BERT
For the Transformer-based models, we use a pre-trained model for Danish, namely the Danish BERT
developed by Certainly [Cer20].
The model is already pre-trained on 9.5 GB text from multiple
sources, including the Danish Common Crawl, Danish Wikipedia, debate forums (dindebat.dk and
hestenettet.dk) and Danish OpenSubtitles. Hence, it is exposed to both written and spoken lan-
guage, which is relevant for this problem, as text written by children is likely to draw elements from
spoken language, mixed with, e.g., abbreviations or slang, which could be found in debate forums.
The pre-training was conducted by Certainly using a custom BPE vocabulary of 32K tokens. They
use a batch size of 1280, max sentence length of 256, probability of masking a word in the sentence of
15%, a learning rate of 2 · 10−5, and train for 1 million steps. The model is freely available on Hugging
Face for subsequent fine-tuning7.
4.5.1
Fine-tuning: Classification
The pre-trained Danish BERT is directly used for fine-tuning our classification problem using the
Transformers library from Hugging Face [Wol+20], specifically the AutoModelForSequenceClassification()
method. As each feature is treated as a separate classification problem, 7 fine-tuning tasks are run. The
model is fine-tuned on the dataset by ATEL for 100 epochs using a batch size of 16, 4 gradient accu-
mulation steps, learning rate of 2 · 10−5 with a linear learning rate scheduler, and weight decay of
1 · 10−2.
4.5.2
Fine-tuning: MLM on Gyldendal books
The Danish BERT has been trained to learn the language and context of the Danish language in general.
As it has mainly trained on data from Common Crawl, which consists of data from the web, we assume
that most data is directed toward adults. Generally, children are used to simple sentence structures that
differ immensely from some of the language used on the internet. Therefore, we propose fine-tuning
using the MLM objective on the children’s books from Gyldendal, which are written using child-directed
language.
The Danish BERT is trained for the MLM task using the children’s books from Gyldendal for 10
epochs using a batch size of 64, a learning rate of 5 · 10−5 with a linear learning rate scheduler, and
weight decay of 0. Subsequently, the model is fine-tuned for the classification of the books written by
children from the ATEL dataset using the same procedure as described in subsection 4.5.1.

4.9
Ensemble
In this study, we use the outputs of three models to create an ensemble. This is done by taking the
predictions of the Random Forest Classifier, the Danish BERT fine-tuned with Gyldendal, and the
BørneBÆRTa model without warm-up steps and fine-tuned on the GigaWord dataset. As we already
have the results from the classifiers from the previous experiments, they can be reused for the ensemble.
For each fold, we take the predictions of the three classifiers and use majority voting to obtain the
predictions of the ensemble model.
As the multi-label problems are equivalent to fitting a binary
classifier for each label, the ensemble, in this case, is performed label-wise.

with class imbalance.

model across most metrics is Danish BERT, where there is an increase in subset accuracy, F1 score,
precision, and recall when using the Danish BERT model trained with Gyldendal children’s book data.

CHAPTER 6
Discussion
In this chapter, we discuss the results from the previous chapter. We present a comprehensive exam-
ination of the performances of the different models and propose explanations of the results. We both
examine the models’ general performance and the performance of the individual features and discuss
what could have been done differently. Starting with the best-performing model, we look at the model
predictions for both when the model has provided correct and wrong predictions.
We also discuss
active learning results and propose suggestions for the comparable performance between the random
and max entropy acquisition functions. The chapter also includes a section with suggestions for how
the project can be expanded in the future, presenting new types of experiments to be conducted, other
visualizations to be made, and alternative ways of modeling the problem.
6.1
Evaluation of models
In Table 5.1, the different models are compared based on 7 main metrics. Here, we saw that on average,
the performance of the TF-IDF-based baseline models is generally low compared to the Transformer
based models. This could be due to how a large part of the features in this study requires knowledge
of context, e.g., for Semantic Universe, it would be an advantage to have contextual knowledge of the
described situation. Is the child writing about a day in the zoo with their family or describing safari
animals? These two scenarios correspond to two different labels for the Semantic Universe feature. The
TF-IDF-based baseline models would have diﬀiculty handling these samples, as the text is treated as a
bag of words.
On average, the LSTM seemed to under-perform compared to the Transformer based models. The
word embedding for the LSTM model is created using fastText and therefore has some knowledge of
word meanings. However, the model training of the LSTM in itself is solely based on the ATEL dataset,
while the Transformer-based models include a pre-training step in the framework. Through the pre-
training step, the model is exposed to more data, allowing a deeper understanding of knowledge and
context, which has proven to be an advantage over the LSTM.
From the results of the Danish BERT, we noticed that a model trained on a large dataset leads to high
performance. The model performance improves with further pre-training the model using the dataset
with the Gyldendal children’s books containing child-directed data. Although there is no statistical
difference in subset accuracy, we view the improvement in terms of the F1-score as an argument for
advancement, as the higher F1-score (and precision and recall) means fewer false negatives and false
positives. It also indicates that children’s text inherits many elements from children’s books. Therefore,
one can conduct experiments where the model is trained on the Gyldendal dataset for more epochs or
expose the model to more child-directed data. As this type of data is unlabeled, acquiring data is less
intensive than for texts written by children.
The BørneBÆRTa models generally gained similar performances that were not much worse than
the Danish BERT. The model is smaller and trained on less data for fewer epochs, so this behavior
is expected.
But on the other hand, the smaller model is quicker during training and performing
inference. Therefore, there is a trade-off between the time for training and inference at the cost of
model performance. The effect of using warm-up steps when feeding in Gyldendal data was non-existent.
However, we selected a warm-up ratio of 20%, which is much larger than presented in the literature (the
warm-up ratio for RoBERTa is 6%). To examine the effect of warm-up and other hyper-parameters,
==PAGE:70==

one could instead conduct experiments of testing a range of values such as {0.5%, 1%, 2%, 5%, 10%} for
the warm-up ratio.
The ensemble of Random Forest, Danish BERT + Gyldendal, and BørneBÆRTa (GW) does not
provide an overall improvement compared to the Danish BERT + Gyldendal alone. Since the ensemble
is created using majority voting, the performance indicates that some aspects of language are captured
by the Danish BERT model, which neither Random Forest nor BørneBÆRTa (GW) captures. However,
the model performance of the ensemble is better than taking the average model performance over the
three models.
We draw the above conclusions by choosing a single model for all 7 features. But as the problems
vary in diﬀiculty depending on the feature, we examine each feature individually. It does not seem
that any of the features are impossible to predict, as throughout the features, most models can perform
better than assigning to the largest class. Most models perform well for Genre, Reference, and Semantic
Universe, where there are improvements compared to the Largest Class model.
We also inspect whether some models yield better model performances for some features. Here, the
models based on the Danish BERT achieved a high model performance throughout all individual features.
Further pre-training the model on the MLM task on Gyldendal data led to further improvements in
all cases except for Dialogue/voices. For this feature, the Random Forest classifier leads to the highest
subset accuracy, and generally, all models performed well. However, we must consider how this feature is
highly imbalanced with most samples having only the One voice label, and assigning to the largest class
already gives a subset accuracy of 73.8%. Therefore, it is also relevant to consider F1-score, precision,
recall, and FPR. For these metrics, BERT performs better than Random Forest.
There are also cases where BørneBÆRTa does not fall far behind the Danish BERT in terms of
performance. This case is apparent for the Reference, Student Narrator, and Holistic evaluation. For
these features, the gap from BørneBÆRTa to the Danish BERT is small, and for speeding up the time
for performing inference, one could use BørneBÆRTa instead of BERT, but with the cost of model
performance. Additionally, the Transformer-based models perform well for the Reference feature in
Table 5.3. It indicates that these models are especially good at the co-reference resolution task compared
to the baseline models and LSTM. Furthermore, we treated the Holistic evaluation feature as a nominal
feature, i.e., disregarding the order of the classes. Thus, predicting a ”2” when the target is ”1” is as
wrong as if the model predicted a ”5”. Instead, we should have considered how it is an ordinal feature
and evaluated it differently.
For BørneBÆRTa, it is diﬀicult to conclude whether using the GigaWord dataset affects the classes.
To this end, one can conduct different experiments where the model is trained using the GigaWord
dataset for a selected number of epochs. After each epoch, the model is fine-tuned using the ATEL
dataset to assess the model performance. Training for 5 epochs is generally not much. However, the
dataset is large, so setting up the experiment with more epochs could allow for gaining a clearer picture
of the effect that the GigaWord dataset would have.

6.3
Active learning
In section 5.3, we show the active learning results for the Danish BERT + Gyldendal model fine-tuned
to classify the Semantic universe feature. We found no significant difference between using the random
or max entropy acquisition function to build a training set. These conclusions are drawn for the subset
accuracy and F1-score metrics for the two functions, and similar conclusions are drawn for the remaining
metrics in section A.3. We expected that a dataset composed using the max entropy acquisition function
would achieve a significantly better model performance after a few acquisition steps when compared to a
randomly built dataset of similar size. Then, when the number of labeled training samples approached
the full-sized training set, the model performance would even out between the two datasets, with a
minor performance difference expected, as the two datasets would be shuffled differently.
==PAGE:72==
6.4 Future work
66
As expected, we observe that the mean value for all metrics for a dataset built with the max
entropy acquisition function, in general, is slightly better for acquisition updates 3 to 8. However, this
performance is not significantly better, due to the large standard error of the mean.
But there might be an explanation for the comparable performance of the two acquisition functions.
In an ideal active learning setting, the acquisition size at each acquisition step would be a single data
point. But in practical applications, a batch of data points is acquired to reduce the number of times
a model needs to be retrained and, in a real-life scenario, expert time requested [KAG19]. In our case,
the acquisition function chose the top k = 32 data points, as model retraining became a computational
bottleneck. As mentioned in section 4.10, GPU batch jobs have a wall-time of 24 hours on the HPC,
so the acquisition size was selected such that a whole active learning simulation could run for a single
cross-validation fold in less time than the constraint. The large acquisition size may have resulted in
too many data points being acquired at each acquisition update to create a high-quality dataset or
acquire samples that the model was not very uncertain of. If more time and computational resources
were available, it would have been of interest to experiment with a smaller acquisition size, to see if a
dataset built using the max entropy acquisition function could achieve a significantly better performance
compared to using the random acquisition function. Furthermore, the max entropy function is not a
batch-aware acquisition function. This means that the function may choose individual data points that
are informative to the model but not necessarily so jointly [KAG19], which could reduce the quality of
some of the obtained samples.
Another observation is that the gap between the two mean metric values does not close for the F1-
score (Figure 5.9) and the macro precision score (Figure A.61) when the labeled dataset size approaches
the full-sized training set. As stated in section 4.8, we ensured full reproducibility for the active learning
experiments.
We initialized all weights in the new layers identically, and we made sure all cross-
validation folds were identical across all models One factor that has an effect is the before-mentioned
shuffle between the two datasets. The first 350 data points are similar, but the following data points
are in a different order due to being acquired by two distinct acquisition functions. The difference in
performance is a result of the models taking two different steps in the loss landscape, but considering
the small size of the dataset and that mini-batches are utilized, it seems odd that the gap is this wide.
6.4
Future work
One of the central results of experimenting with different models is that feeding the model with the
Gyldendal children’s book data improved the model performance. Therefore, training the model on
more child-directed data might improve the model’s performance further.
The acquisition of more
child-directed data will also be less intensive than acquiring data for the ATEL dataset, as it is not
required to label the data. It would only be necessary to remove titles, authors, etc. However, we must
consider that it can be expensive to acquire the rights to more books.
For the Ensemble model, we do not observe an improvement from the Danish BERT + Gyldendal
model. One can create a different model ensemble using a pre-trained model and sub-sampling, e.g., 15
datasets from the ATEL dataset. Subsequently, 15 distinct models are fine-tuned using the 15 datasets,
and a majority voting is used for 1, 3, ..., 15 models. To this end, one can inspect the development in
model performance when increasing the number of models used in the ensemble. For this solution, we
must keep in mind that training 15 models for 7 features using 10-fold cross-validation requires many
resources in terms of time and availability of GPUs.
The dataset also has a lot of class imbalance, which was apparent for most features. Therefore,
future work includes weighting the individual labels according to the size during modeling or using an
up-sampling method.
To inspect the embeddings, it is also interesting to create PCA or t-SNE scatter plots from the word
embeddings of, e.g., the Danish BERT + Gyldendal model. For each feature, we can use the plots to
inspect whether any clusters correspond to the labels in the dataset. We could also take examples from
the ATEL dataset and visualize the self-attention heads similarly to Figure 3.12. Using this visualization
allows for gaining a deeper understanding of the model predictions.
==PAGE:73==
6.4 Future work
67
Currently, we have created an individual model for each feature. Instead, it is possible to define a
single model to give predictions for all features at once, i.e., a multi-output classifier. This method can
be easier from the perspective of ATEL and the WriteReader app so that only one model is run when
assessing children’s texts instead of 7 separate ones. As a starting point, one can concatenate all labels
to create one combined multi-label classification problem. One can also use multi-task learning in the
Transformer-based models, for example, by adding more prediction heads to the BERT model.
We can expand our current framework to consider all text-level features and even sentence-level
categories. One obstacle that was present during the project was the shortage of data and that some
labels had very few assigned samples. One of the only resolutions we saw was acquiring more data. But
instead of an intensive process that requires experts to label more data manually, we can expand the
current framework to cover sentence-level categories, where annotated data is already available.
The future investigation for active learning would include all target features to see if the max entropy
function performs at the same level or better than the random acquisition function. Further additions
would also include exploring a smaller acquisition size (e.g., 4, 8, or 16) at each acquisition update to see
if a higher quality dataset would be built and a better performance achieved compared to a randomly
assembled dataset. At last, another question of interest is the use of other acquisition functions such
as max variation, BALD, or the batch aware version BatchBALD, to see if any of these acquisition
functions could improve performance.
Furthermore, as the study centers around children’s literacy, we can apply the current framework
directly to texts written by children. This would be ideal, as otherwise, the texts would be corrected for
grammatical errors, typos, etc., before being passed into the model. The correction process can either
be completed by a teacher or another machine learning model.

CHAPTER 7
Conclusion
This project addresses the problem of automating the assessment of children’s literacy, as the process is
costly in terms of time and resources. We use labeled text data from children’s writing provided by the
Automatic Tracking of Early state Literacy skills (ATEL) project, where the children are in Year 0 to
Year 2 of primary education, and the data is collected from 13 schools in Denmark. The labeled data
consists of categorical features describing the children’s texts. The purpose of the project is to explore
how a subset of 7 categorical features (Genre, Student narrator (implicit/explicit), Reference, Text orga-
nization, Semantic universe, Holistic evaluation, and Dialogue/voices) can be extracted automatically
using tools within machine learning and NLP.
To solve this problem, we utilize machine learning models, but these models can not process raw text
as input, we explore the concept of word embeddings to represent text data as vector representations.
One of the simplest methods is TF-IDF, which we use for the baseline machine learning models. However,
this method does not consider synonymy and the positional ordering of words in a sentence. Therefore,
we define an LSTM model that uses fastText embedding as input. We also explore the state-of-the-art
machine learning model, BERT, based on the Transformer architecture. The BERT framework uses
transfer learning consisting of a pre-training part followed by a fine-tuning part. BERT is a large model
in terms of model architecture, and training BERT requires large amounts of data. Hence, we also
define a smaller version of BERT, namely BørneBÆRTa.
These models are trained on the children’s dataset provided by ATEL. In general, the Danish BERT
model performed well and gave a high performance in terms of precision and recall for all features.
A performance improvement is obtained by pre-training the model on child-directed data, i.e., books
written by adults for children. We propose that this is due to how children possibly draw many elements
from the literature they read at an early age, which often is (or is similar to) Gyldendal’s children’s
books. Therefore, we suggest further improving the model by training on more child-directed data,
which does not require the intensive process of manual labeling.
The other models vary in terms of performance, and generally, most text-level features can be
identified using Transformer-based models with relatively high accuracy, precision, and recall. Some
features, e.g., Genre, Reference, and Semantic Universe are more easily predicted by most models. For
these features, the performance is an improvement from assigning all samples to belong to the largest
class. In general, the TF-IDF-based models-based methods perform worse than the Transformer-based
methods. We suggest that this is due to the disadvantages of the TF-IDF embedding method. Many
features require contextual knowledge, which is impossible to acquire through the TF-IDF representa-
tion. However, for some features such as Dialogue/voices, the TF-IDF-based Random Forest classifier
leads to the overall highest subset accuracy, but with the trade-off of precision and recall.
The LSTM under-performs compared to the Transformer-based models, although the embedding is
based on fastText and thereby has knowledge of the meaning of words. We view this as being due to
transfer learning, where the Transformer-based models have already learned about language and context
during pre-training. The LSTM is instead solely trained on the ATEL dataset. For the BørneBÆRTa
model, the model is pre-trained from scratch, where we saw that the model led to a slightly lower
performance than the full Danish BERT for some features. This model is both quicker to train and to
perform inference. Therefore, one can discuss whether the smaller model should be used instead of the
full BERT but with the cost of model accuracy.
We also define an ensemble model as a combination of three models; a TF-IDF-based model, the
best-performing Danish BERT + Gyldendal model, and a model based on BørneBÆRTa. The ensemble

model did not lead to a better performance than the Danish BERT + Gyldendal model, but the
performance is better than the average performance of the three models. One could explore this further
by using a different combination of models, e.g., using multiple fine-tuned Danish BERT + Gyldendal
models.
For active learning, the max entropy acquisition function led to improvements compared to random
sampling in terms of accuracy and F1-score. However, we can not conclude that the improvement is
significant due to overlapping uncertainties. We suggest that this is caused by using a large acquisition
size and that the max entropy function chooses individual data points that are informative to the model
but not necessarily so jointly. We propose experimenting with smaller acquisition sizes and additional
acquisition functions to see if a performance gain is possible.
