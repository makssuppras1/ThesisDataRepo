Abstract 
This study examines the relationship between the Indonesian Energy Stock Index 
(IDXEnergy) and various global energy, financial, and carbon markets. In the middle of 
Indonesia’s energy coal domination and ongoing transition efforts, IDXEnergy presents 
an intriguing case of market interdependence and forecasting potential. The first part of 
the study applies the Toda-Yamamoto Granger Causality (TYGC) method to examine 
return spillovers among selected markets, including coal and oil prices, global equity 
indices, currency exchange rates, interest rates, and the European Union Emission 
Trading System (EU ETS). The findings highlight significant bi- and unidirectional 
causalities, with coal, the S&P 500, oil, and the EU ETS as key drivers of IDXEnergy 
returns. 
Following the results, the second part of the study develops predictive models for 
IDXEnergy prices using machine learning algorithms, i.e., linear regression, support 
vector regression, multi-layer perceptron regression, recurrent neural networks (GRU 
and LSTM), and Ensemble models that combine several base models. The optimization 
of all models is performed using Tree-structured Parzen Estimator (TPE) with expanding-
window cross-validation. In addition to the standard accuracy of Mean Absolute 
Percentage Error (MAPE), the model evaluation also includes short-term trading 
simulations to gauge their profitability and risk performance. Results indicate that some 
models yield lower prediction errors and outperform traditional benchmarks, such as buy-
and-hold and technical indicators, with higher simulated profits. 
This combined approach adds to the empirical knowledge of IDXEnergy’s external 
relations while demonstrating the applicability of machine learning in making predictions 
in emerging markets. The findings could be helpful for policymakers to anticipate external 
market shocks, for investors to maximize their portfolio profits, and for researchers to 
expand the predictive modeling constructs in the emerging market contexts.

1 Introduction 
In the middle of the global effort towards renewable energy transition, coal still dominates 
Indonesia. In 2023 alone, Indonesia produced 775 megatons of coal, making it one of 
the top three producers in the world, along with China and India [1]. With 53% of the total 
production being exported to other countries, Indonesia stands as the top coal exporter 
and becomes a crucial player in the global energy stage [2], [3]. This coal domination is 
also represented in Indonesia’s energy stock index, IDXEnergy, where the top 9 listed 
companies are coal mining companies with a total market capitalization of 1,389 trillion 
rupiahs or around 84 billion US dollars [4]. 
When it comes to market performance, as of 30 April 2025, IDXEnergy yielded an 
attractive return of more than 228% in the past 4 years, leaving S&P500 (48%) and 
Indonesian stock composite index, IHSG (11%), behind [5], [6], [7]. Though the return 
was highly affected by the coal price surge in 2022 due to the Russia-Ukraine conflict, 
the investor’s optimism remains, as shown by the recent price uptrend and the price-to-
earnings (P/E) ratio of 15.1x, which is higher than the 3-year average of 9.0x [8], [9]. This 
contradicts the coal price drops and the sunset-considered coal industries. 
Despite its growing significance, literature focusing on Indonesia’s energy equities is 
limited. It is in contrast with the widely studied energy equity markets in developed 
economies, such as the US, China, and Europe, which are often related to energy 
commodities, and a few to carbon markets [10], [11], [12], [13]. Most Indonesian equity 
market studies, in the meantime, center around the composite index (IHSG) [14], [15], 
[16]. Even when IDXEnergy is considered, existing studies tend to overlook critical 
market linkages, such as clean energy, carbon markets, or global financial factors, that 
are becoming more significant in an integrated and decarbonization investment 
landscape [17], [18], [19], [20], [21]. In addition, the discussion based on spillover insights 
has minimal inclusion of empirical economic activities and policy shifts, thereby 
restricting the explanatory power of the statistical results. 
Aside from this limitation, analytical approaches to IDXEnergy have remained largely 
backward-looking and descriptive, leaving a noteworthy gap in predictive modelling. 
Moreover, most energy stock forecasting in developed economies focuses primarily on 
historical prices and technical indicators. Few studies slightly extended the model to 
incorporate macroeconomic indicators and equity markets, such as interest rates, 
inflation, and ESG stock indexes [22]. Even so, the model evaluation tends to rely solely 
on error-based metrics, without assessing practical application through investment 
performance [23], [24].  
Thus, this thesis pursues two primary objectives: 
1. Examine return spillovers between IDXEnergy and major global energy, financial, 
and carbon markets while situating the findings within Indonesia’s broader 
economic and policy context. 
2. Develop and evaluate predictive models for IDXEnergy using integrated market 
features, with performance assessed through both error-based metrics and short-
term trading simulations.

2 Literature Review 
This chapter synthesizes the theoretical frameworks and empirical evidence underlying 
the dynamic interactions between IDXEnergy and global energy, financial, and carbon 
markets. The first section starts the review by addressing the market interaction itself 
and the approaches to modelling such interaction. The second section explores market 
prediction, including theoretical lenses, the application in energy equities, and the 
modeling approaches. The third section highlights the gaps identified during the literature 
review that this study aims to address. 
2.1 Market Interaction 
Market integration theory emphasizes the increasing convergence of international equity 
markets due to liberalization, securitization, and the internationalization of financial 
systems [25]. In the context of Indonesian energy equities, the influences are related to 
the trade of energy commodities, investment flows in the global financial market, and 
awareness of the ESG concept through carbon market development. Each interaction is 
discussed separately as follows. 
2.1.1 Energy Markets 
The energy market is one of the main driving forces behind IDXEnergy dynamics, 
particularly through coal pricing. This section examines the impacts of both brown and 
green energies, drawing on recent studies and empirical evidence, including Indonesia’s 
energy transition policy context. 
Brown Energy 
Goh et al. (2025) in their paper present an econometric analysis of dynamic relationships 
between coal and crude oil prices and IDXEnergy from February 2021 to November 2024 
[17]. Using Augmented Dickey-Fuller (ADF) tests, Johansen cointegration, vector error 
correction model (VECM), and Granger causality models, the study concludes that the 
coal price has a highly significant and consistent influence on IDXEnergy prices. 
Meanwhile, crude oil has a lower effect in the short term. The study reveals one-
directional causality from both commodities to IDXEnergy, with coal being the major 
contributor, as Indonesia is a leading coal exporter. Confirming the results, Setyowaty 
and Novyarni (2025) demonstrate that price shocks in coal significantly affect and explain 
the variance in the prices of listed coal companies [18]. Their regression analysis of the 
prices during 2019-2020 confirms coal’s fundamental role in driving market valuation in 
Indonesia’s energy sector. 
The energy equities in Indonesia are closely tied to real economy trends, particularly 
through coal production and exports. As one of the world’s biggest coal exporters, 
IDXEnergy’s price is highly influenced by international coal demand and price. Stronger 
demand phases, such as the energy crisis during COVID-19 recovery and the Russia-
Ukraine conflict, boost the coal prices and revenues of Indonesian coal companies, 
triggering positive movement in their stock prices. The Newcastle coal price increase 
from $223/ton in November 2021 to $427/ton in May 2022 led to PT Bayan Resources 
Tbk’s (BYAN) – the most significant company market capitalization in IDXEnergy – 
==PAGE:17==
4 
 
revenue in quarter 4 2021 increased more than 300% year-on-year [26], [27]. This 
pumped BYAN price up by 5 times and IDXEnergy overall by 2.3 times [5]. 
In contrast to the prominent relationship with coal, IDXEnergy has a weaker and more 
complex relationship with crude oil. Part of the reason for this weakly positive crude oil 
correlation lies in the indirect effect transmission via coal. According to Zamani (2016), 
coal and oil are to a certain degree substitutable [28]. This substitution relationship is 
particularly evident in industrial applications and power generation. Therefore, they 
shared similar demand drivers, such as during the economic rebound after the COVID-
19 pandemic [29]. Thus, the indirect pathway where oil price increases, coal price 
increases, and then IDXEnergy increases, suggests that the coal market largely 
moderates crude oil’s influence. 
Green Energy 
The structural shift of Indonesia toward low-carbon energy is quite complex and not 
straightforward. While the government committed in 2021 to increasing the national 
renewable energy mix to 23%, they could only achieve 14.68% in 2024 and postponed 
the target to 2030 [30], [31], [32]. Moreover, Indonesia faced significant challenges with 
the implementation of the Just Energy Transition Partnership (JETP)—a multilateral 
commitment to mobilize $20 billion to deploy renewable energy, where they have yet to 
receive the funds [33]. This makes the government hesitate to continue the Paris 
Agreement and retire coal power plants early [33]. 
Despite the aforementioned challenges, several listed coal companies have pursued 
green diversification as a hedge against transition risk. For example, PT Alamtri 
Resource Indonesia Tbk (ADRO), formerly PT Adaro Energy Indonesia Tbk, spun off its 
$2.62-billion coal company, which later became PT Adaro Andalan Indonesia Tbk (AADI), 
to focus on renewable energy development [34], [35]. Another listed major company, PT 
Indika Energy Tbk (INDY), has also embarked on diversification by developing electric-
motorized bikes and solar power through its subsidiaries [36]. These shifts, while still low 
in terms of revenues, capture a slow but tangible transition among fossil-heavy 
IDXEnergy firms. 
The expansion of coal companies into renewables, particularly the ADRO-AADI 
corporate action, marks a capital flow response to climate policies that favor ESG and 
avoid carbon-intensive assets. This adds a green layer of complexity to IDXEnergy’s 
future valuation, particularly on how the markets begin to price transition risk and clean 
growth opportunity. It also introduces a dual-exposure dynamic within IDXEnergy: high 
profits from the coal commodity in the short term, but resilience through clean energy 
investment in the long term. 
That said, one limitation of this study is the absence of an index that can statistically 
represent Indonesia’s renewable energy sector. Due to this constraint, this study 
employs the Solactive China Clean Energy Index as a proxy for regional green energy 
development and the corresponding supply chain, which will be discussed in Subchapter 
2.1.2. While this does not necessarily reflect Indonesia’s internal renewable dynamics, it 
represents a suitable external benchmark for understanding green spillovers into 
IDXEnergy.

2.1.2 Financial Markets 
In addition to energy commodities, financial markets also significantly shape energy 
equity returns. This section explores the role of currency exchange rates, interest rates, 
and global equity trends in explaining IDXEnergy dynamics.  
Currency Exchange Rates 
Major currency exchange rates, specifically USD/IDR, CNY/IDR, and EUR/IDR, are 
crucial in determining the performance of Indonesia’s energy stock market because of 
its export-oriented economy. The USD/IDR rate is the most prominent since most of 
Indonesia’s coal exports are in US dollars. A weaker rupiah raises coal companies’ 
revenues in local currency terms, boosting profit margins and their stock prices. 
Setyowati and Novyarni (2025) affirm that this dynamic has a significant impact on stock 
valuations in the coal industry [18]. Interestingly, an empirical finding by Novianto and 
Paramita (2023) refutes the strong USD/IDR relationship with IDXEnergy [20]. They 
found that during 2020-2022, the effect of weakening rupiah against USD due to the 
Russia-Ukraine conflict, which led to capital outflow from emerging equity markets like 
Indonesia, is covered by the coal price surge [20]. 
The CNY/IDR exchange rate could be a crucial signaling variable for Indonesian energy 
equities valuation, especially since China is the second top coal importer from Indonesia 
[37]. Widajatun and Ichsani (2019) note a positive effect of the CNY/IDR exchange rate 
on IHSG [38]. The rationale behind this relationship is likely due to the fact that a weaker 
Rupiah makes Indonesian coal more competitive in Yuan terms, thereby enhancing 
export levels and improving earnings expectations. 
The EUR/IDR exchange rate, while less direct than USD or CNY influences, is found to 
be influential on Indonesian equities, especially IDX30, which is the 30 selected 
companies with high market capitalization, liquidity, and financial performance [39], [40]. 
Ardiansyah (2015) explained that there is a significant positive effect of EUR/IDR on 
IDX30, suggesting that a decline in Rupiah against the Euro corresponds with the rising 
listed stock prices [40]. This relationship can be attributed to the competitive Indonesian 
exports to Europe, which accounted for a €7 billion net positive balance for Indonesia, or 
tightening monetary policy in Europe, leading to greater investment inflows to emerging 
markets like Indonesia [41]. 
Interest Rates 
The impact of Indonesia’s interest rate on IDXEnergy has produced mixed empirical 
outcomes. Novianto and Paramita (2023) reported a negative relationship, whereby an 
increase in BI 7-Day Reverse Repo Rate (BI7DRR) decreased IDXEnergy levels due to 
the shift of investors’ appetite from riskier energy stocks to safer interest-bearing 
instruments [20]. In contrast, Sia et al. (2024) found that the IHSG is affected both in the 
short and long term by interest rates, due to its correlation with economic growth, 
macroeconomic stability, and investor confidence [42]. At the same time, Suci (2024) 
indicated that direct interest rate effects on IDXEnergy were statistically 
indistinguishable, suggesting that sectoral sensitivities or intermediary variables could 
buffer or mask monetary transmission effects [21]. Such conflicting findings imply that 
the interest rate relationships with Indonesian energy equities are subject to broader 
economic signals and index composition.

Global Equities 
The influence of the world’s premier stock market index, S&P 500, on IDXEnergy is 
primarily through the risk management and capital mobility channel. When a bullish 
market occurs in the developed market, foreign investors are willing to bear risks for 
higher yields by reallocating capital to emerging markets. Such capital inflows boost 
equities’ valuations in Indonesia, including those in the energy sector. Some studies 
found that the Federal Reserve’s quantitative easing (QE) led to substantial capital 
inflows to emerging markets, reducing bond yields and driving equity prices up [43], [44]. 
Besides, Fadli (2025) recently highlighted the price surge of PT Pertamina Geothermal 
Energy Tbk (PGEO) and PT Barito Renewables Energy Tbk (BREN), two energy firms 
listed in IDXEnergy, by 17.5% and 7.66%, respectively, due to foreign capital inflows [45]. 
These findings validate that the influence of the S&P 500 on IDXEnergy is more about 
portfolio allocation rather than direct economic effects. Hence, the dynamics of 
IDXEnergy cannot be fully understood without integrating global equity and monetary 
policy contexts. 
In contrast to the S&P 500, the Solactive China Clean Energy Index PR offers a different 
perspective by tracking major companies involved in China’s clean energy industry and 
its related supply chain, such as wind energy equipment manufacturers, as well as 
photovoltaic, solar cells, and energy systems providers [46]. China takes the lead in the 
global renewable energy supply chain, including its dominance of over 80% of the solar 
panel market share worldwide [47]. This leadership is not only a demonstration of China’s 
manufacturing capability but also positions the country as a focal player in the global 
energy transition. 
Indonesia’s renewable energy sector has significantly benefited from this massive 
production, shown by the utilization of advanced clean energy components supplied by 
some leading Chinese companies listed on this index. For instance, Longi Green Energy 
Technol-A, the third biggest company by weight in the index, has secured the deal to 
supply 1-GW solar panels to Indonesia’s flagship solar power plant for electricity export 
to Singapore [48], [49]. This agreement complements Trina Solar Co Ltd-A, another listed 
company, which has committed to supplying 1.2-GW solar panels to the same project 
[50], [51]. Besides, Jinko Solar Co Ltd, one of the top ten companies in the list, has 
contributed to Indonesia’s solar photovoltaic development way before them by deploying 
solar panels to a 100-MW project in Purwakarta, the largest operating ground-mounted 
solar PV in Indonesia, and Cirata 192 MW floating solar PV, the Southeast Asia’s biggest 
floating solar photovoltaics [48], [52], [53]. 
2.1.3 Carbon Markets  
The carbon market refers broadly to systems in which carbon emissions are assigned a 
monetary value to incentivize reductions in emissions. There are two different carbon 
market types: one is the cap-and-trade scheme, or emission trading systems (ETS), and 
the other is a baseline-and-credit mechanism, or offsetting scheme [54]. While 
companies/countries trade offsets, which represent the certified reduced emissions in 
the offsetting scheme, ETS allows companies/countries to trade pollution permits rather 
than the reduced emissions themselves [54].  
==PAGE:20==
7 
 
The European Union (EU) ETS stands as the world’s first carbon market and one of the 
largest [55]. Launched in 2005, the EU ETS has covered 40% of total emissions in the 
EU, which mainly come from the electricity and heat generation, industrial manufacturing, 
and aviation sectors [55]. The EU ETS has been used as a benchmark for carbon 
markets in the literature to analyze market interactions. 
Although no literature has specifically discussed the EU ETS in relation to Indonesia’s 
equities, some studies have examined the interaction between carbon markets and 
global energy and financial markets. Zhao et al. (2023) argued that as financialization of 
ETS becomes deeper, firms in energy-intensive industries respond more sensitively to 
carbon price fluctuations due to the higher pressure for cost reduction and ESG 
(Environmental, Social, and Governance) policies [56]. In contrast to the mature ETS 
systems, Nie, Li, and Li (2021) found that the emerging China’s ETS is a recipient of 
information from the new energy stock market, indicating a one-way spillover from 
equities to carbon [57].  
Zhang and Sun (2016) highlighted the long-run cointegration between the EU ETS and 
fossil fuel prices, especially coal and natural gas, even though with various directionality 
and intensity across the EU ETS phases [58]. They emphasized that the energy 
substitution channels, where the rising carbon prices could shift demand from coal to gas 
[58]. This demand shift may alter both energy pricing structures and the related equity 
valuations. 
Collectively, those studies capture how carbon markets, particularly the EU ETS, are 
becoming more integrated with global energy and financial markets. They transmit policy 
and market signals across asset classes and jurisdictions. These dynamics are 
particularly relevant to Indonesia, where its carbon-intensive commodity trades are more 
exposed to fluctuations in the global carbon market price. For instance, the increase in 
EU ETS price can influence global coal demand, which will affect the revenues and 
valuation of energy firms listed on IDXEnergy. 
Indonesia’s domestic carbon market development adds a new layer of interaction. This 
new carbon market was launched in 2023, covering 99 coal-fired power plants (67.6% 
of national coal-fired capacity) [59]. This market was then formalized into IDXCarbon, 
with 690,675 tCO2e carbon units already traded in the first quarter of 2025 [60]. Once 
this market matures, connectivity with commodity and financial markets in Indonesia will 
intensify. Firms with greater exposure to carbon pricing, such as coal companies, will 
face increased operating costs, which will impact their profitability. This, in the long term, 
will establish an increasingly direct relationship between IDXEnergy and carbon markets, 
paving the way for carbon-asset pricing research in Indonesia similar to that in the EU 
and China cases.

Vector Autoregression (VAR) 
VAR models are widely used to represent the dynamic linkages among multiple time 
series and have become the statistical foundation for many advanced econometric 
models. Yu and Liao (2017) employed a VAR model to examine the interconnection 
between China’s stock, money, and foreign exchange markets. Their results indicated 
that one market had influenced other markets with various lag period effects, ranging 
from one to seven days [61]. This captures VAR’s capability to distinguish between the 
timing and direction of influences in a multivariate system, providing insights into how 
different markets respond to each other over a short horizon. Nevertheless, although 
VAR determines lagged interdependencies, it does not confirm causality per se, limiting 
its application when clear directional inference is required. 
Quantile VAR (QVAR) 
The quantile VAR extends the traditional VAR framework to model return spillovers 
across different parts of the return distribution. This is particularly useful for identifying 
asymmetric and extreme-event dynamics, such as financial crises or commodity shocks. 
Abakah et al. (2023) employed this method to investigate the return connectedness 
between green bonds, carbon markets, and financial indices. They found that the 
spillover intensity was stronger at the top and bottom quantiles, especially during the 
turbulent period [62]. Nevertheless, this approach requires a large sample size or a long 
period, as demonstrated by Abakah et al., who employed a 2005 observation spanning 
seven years. 
Time-Varying Parameter VAR (TVP-VAR) 
To measure the dynamics of spillover behavior over time, researchers have employed 
TVP-VAR models. Tao, Wen, and Sheng (2024) used this model to examine New 
==PAGE:22==
9 
 
Zealand’s carbon, energy, and stock markets. They identified that both return and 
volatility spillovers increase dramatically during policy change and crisis periods [63]. 
Despite this model’s strong analytical power, it requires long and high-frequency data. 
Tao, Wen, and Sheng (2024), for example, used 3384 daily observations derived from 
113,218 hourly samples [63].  
Vector Error Correction Model (VECM) 
VECM is applied when a long-run equilibrium relationship (cointegration) exists among 
non-stationary variables. Goh et al. (2025) used this model to demonstrate the long-term 
influences of oil and coal prices on IDXEnergy [17]. However, even though they 
confirmed the existence of cointegration, it might not be robust due to the small sample 
size of IDXEnergy. Moreover, IDXEnergy is prone to structural breaks due to major 
events, such as the COVID-19 recovery, the Russia-Ukraine war, the development of the 
Indonesian carbon market, and energy companies’ diversification into renewables. 
Granger Causality (GC) 
The classical Granger causality test examines whether lagged forms of a variable 
contribute to the forecast of another. Goh et al. (2025) employed this method to show 
the influence of oil and coal on IDXEnergy [17]. However, GC assumes stationarity and 
equal integration orders, limiting its use in more general or mixed-data settings. This 
prerequisite was confirmed to be true for all variables in Goh et al.’s research [17]. 
Toda-Yamamoto (TYGC) 
TYGC addresses the limitations of traditional GC by allowing for testing causality in VAR 
systems, irrespective of the integration level difference or cointegration status. Atanta, 
Syahnur, and Dawood (2023) utilized this method to uncover the two-way causal 
relationship between exchange rates and oil prices, and the one-way relationship 
between oil prices-exchange rates and IHSG-oil price [64]. While TYGC retains the 
simplicity of the VAR framework, it assumes a constant relationship over time and does 
not capture asymmetric or economically cycle-dependent spillovers. Furthermore, TYGC 
only detects the influence of variables without measuring their magnitude. 
Diebold-Yilmaz (DY) Spillover Index 
The DY spillover index is among the most prevalent measures of the magnitude and 
direction of interdependence among financial variables, which were designed for 
analyzing both return and volatility spillovers. In their foundational work, Diebold and 
Yilmaz (2008) demonstrated this methodology using 19 global equity markets from 1992 
to 2007. They showed that return spillovers increased gradually over time in line with 
international financial integration [65]. This framework provides a system-wide analysis 
by decomposing forecast error variances into directional components, thereby estimating 
the proportion of a variable’s forecast error attributed to shocks from other variables. 
However, unlike causality-based models like TYGC, DY estimates spillover strength 
rather than conducting a predictive causation test, thereby capturing market 
connectedness more effectively. This measurement comes with a caveat of long and 
stable time series to provide reliable variance decomposition, limiting its application for 
new indices or small sample sizes.

2.2 Market Prediction 
After addressing return spillover mechanisms, this section shifts the discussion to the 
predictive dimension of market analysis. The review is initiated with a well-known efficient 
market theory that argues for the market’s predictability, followed by an application of 
energy stock prediction. Similar to the previous section, the final part of this section 
covers machine learning modeling methodologies. 
2.2.1 Efficient Market Theory 
Efficient market theory posits that the market price accurately reflects all publicly 
available information relevant to the valuation of securities. In other words, once the 
information arises, it is rapidly and accurately incorporated into asset prices, making 
technical analysis of past prices and fundamental analysis of the underlying assets 
ineffective in detecting abnormal returns [66]. Fama formalized this theory into three 
categories, i.e., weak, semi-strong, and strong form [67]. 
Under the strong-form efficiency, nothing can predict the security price because all kinds 
of information, including non-public and insider information, have been fully reflected. 
Under the semi-strong form, only public information is fully reflected, meaning only 
insider information offers predictive value. The weak form, in contrast, assumes that the 
security price has only incorporated its historical market data, such as past prices and 
volumes. Hence, technical analysis will not be effective in a weak-form efficient market; 
however, predicting using other market prices, public news, or macroeconomic indicators 
may still be feasible. 
However, the applicability of efficient market theory in emerging markets is often 
challenged due to structural limitations, such as limited financial intermediaries, poor 
corporate governance, and unreliable information [68]. Some studies have applied this 
theoretical lens to Indonesia’s stock equities, yielding diverse results. For instance, 
Budiarso (2022) rejected the null hypothesis of weak market efficiency on 755 listed firms 
from January to June 2022 [69]. On the contrary, Siahaan (2023) found that the 
Indonesian stock exchange is the most efficient stock market in ASEAN during the 2018-
2022 period [70]. In between, El-Diftar (2024) concluded that Indonesia is moving from 
inefficient to weakly efficient [68]. This discrepancy may be attributed to differences in 
time periods, market conditions, and empirical methods, underscoring the evolving 
nature of market efficiency and predictive potential in emerging economies.  
2.2.2 Energy Stock Prediction 
Predicting energy stock prices is a crucial topic due to the sector’s vulnerability to the 
dynamics of commodity markets, macroeconomic factors, and environmental policy. 
Besides statistical models, machine learning is increasingly applied to deal with the 
complexity and non-linearity of financial time series within this domain. 
Santos et al. (2022) used machine learning algorithms to predict the trend direction of 
the Brazilian energy stock market [71]. The approach utilized historical stock prices and 
technical indicators to classify trends as either increasing or decreasing. Despite the 
potential for predictive power, the study limited its feature space to endogenous market 
factors, excluding external factors that can influence stock performance. 
==PAGE:24==
11 
 
Benali and Karima (2023) compared several supervised learning models, including linear 
regression, ridge regression, and support vector regression, for one-day-ahead stock 
prices of some Moroccan energy firms [72]. Their comparison showed that linear and 
ridge regression outperformed more complex models. However, like Santos et al., their 
analysis only considered stock-specific historical features, not broader macroeconomic 
or inter-market variables. 
A more comprehensive approach was adopted by Ghallabi et al. (2024), who applied 
ensemble learning methods and a range of macroeconomic variables and exogenous 
markets, such as interest rates, inflation, and ESG stock prices of ten countries [22]. 
Their findings emphasized the value of including these variables, which increased model 
robustness. Nevertheless, they did not incorporate other relevant markets, e.g., global 
energy commodities or carbon markets.

Linear and Regularized Regression 
Linear regression models provide a foundational approach for forecasting energy stock 
prices. Ordinary Least Squares (OLS) regression is straightforward and interpretable, 
but it is susceptible to multicollinearity, which can lead to unstable coefficients and 
reduced predictive performance. 
To mitigate that limitation, regularization techniques have been widely applied. Ridge 
Regression (L2) penalty shrinks coefficients towards zero without eliminating them, 
which reduces the coefficient variance when multicollinearity exists. Lasso Regression 
(L1) shrinks coefficients and eliminates less informative predictors by setting their 
coefficients to zero. ElasticNet combines both penalties to stabilize coefficients and 
select variables, particularly effective when predictors are highly correlated. 
Benali and Karima (2023) demonstrated that these simple models could outperform more 
advanced models in predicting Moroccan energy firm stock prices. They highlighted the 
superiority of OLS and Ridge, which can reach up to R2 0.85 in some cases [72].  
Support Vector Machines (SVMs) 
SVMs are versatile and powerful models for both classification and regression tasks. 
They can handle both linear and nonlinear patterns effectively through various kernel 
selection and margin maximization techniques. Moreover, they are also not sensitive to 
outliers, unlike classic regression [73]. However, the training computation and time 
expand rapidly with large datasets, making it less practical for many observations [73]. 
Xia, Liu, and Chen (2013) performed Support Vector Regression on the next day opening 
price prediction of S&P 500 and demonstrated its good performance with an R2 of 0.9994 
[74].  
Decision-Tree-Based Models 
Decision-tree-based models such as Random Forest (RF) and Extreme Gradient 
Boosting (XGBoost) are widely used for financial prediction due to their ability to capture 
nonlinear relationships and versatility for both classification and regression tasks. RF 
follows an intuitive model construction due to its simple logic behind independent 
decision trees and averaging/voting methods [75]. XGBoost is computationally efficient 
and well-scalable for large datasets [76]. Nevertheless, neither model can extrapolate 
beyond the seen training data because their prediction is based on averages or splits 
within the range of the training input [77], [78]. XGBoost is also highly hyperparameter 
sensitive and prone to overfitting small or noisy datasets [76]. Wu (2022) confirmed this 
finding by applying basic decision trees, Random Forest (RF), and XGBoost to small 
datasets of daily stock prices over 18 months. Though all models reached beyond 90% 
accuracy, XGBoost performed the worst, with a notable difference from simple decision 
trees [79].  
Neural Network 
A Neural Network (NN) is a broad class of machine learning models inspired by the 
human brain structure. NN tries to mimic the capability of learning complex and nonlinear 
patterns within data by defining layers of interconnected nodes (neurons), where each 
node is connected by a weight optimized during training to minimize the prediction error. 
==PAGE:26==
13 
 
The adaptability of neural architecture has led to a wide range of model variants tailored 
for specific data types and prediction objectives. 
The most basic type of neural network (NN) is a feedforward neural network (FNN), 
which processes data in one pass. One of the most prevalent models under this type is 
Multi-Layer Perceptron (MLP), which employs fully connected layers to map nonlinear 
transformations from inputs to outputs. Naeini, Taremian, and Hashemi (2010) 
demonstrated that this model yielded the least error when predicting the next-day price 
of the Tehran Stock Market Index [80]. However, FNN, like MLP, does not have any 
inherent mechanisms to model sequential dependencies, motivating the development of 
Recurrent NN (RNN) that incorporates the output from previous steps into the input for 
the current steps [81]. 
RNN itself has been developed into many variants, with Gated Recurrent Units (GRU) 
and Long Short-Term Memory Networks (LSTM) being among the most popular ones for 
stock prediction. Their popularity comes from their memory gate architecture that not 
only eliminates the vanishing/exploding gradient problem commonly found in RNN but 
also captures the ability to capture long-term sequential dependencies [24], [81]. Sethia 
and Raut (2018) implemented GRU and LSTM to predict S&P 500 prices, showing that 
those models outperformed the others, including SVM, with an R2 up to 0.9486 and a 
return of 400% greater than the hold and wait strategy [82]. 
While NN models have demonstrated outstanding performance in various prediction 
tasks, their flexibility and large number of parameters come with a high risk of overfitting, 
especially when the dataset is small or the model is not well-tuned [83]. The inherent 
complexity of the models also leads to high computational demand during the training 
[84]. 
Ensemble Models 
An ensemble model combines multiple predictive models to enhance forecasting 
accuracy and robustness, particularly in highly volatile and nonlinear stock market 
predictions. The ensemble model can recognize the data patterns from the base models 
and reduce their biases. Several studies have implemented ensemble models and 
demonstrated their excellent performance. 
Li and Pan (2021) created a blending ensemble deep learning model that integrates GRU 
and LSTM. The prediction results of those base models were fed to a three-layer fully 
connected neural network to extract both short- and long-term dependencies. They 
tested the model on the S&P 500 index, and the results indicated notable improvements 
over the base models, including a 57.55% reduction in mean-squared error [23]. 
Sonkavde et al. (2023) reviewed various machine learning models and developed an 
ensemble model combining RF, XGBoost, and LSTM to forecast two Indian companies. 
The base model selection is driven by the simplicity of RF to handle high-dimensional 
and nonlinear datasets, the excellent but slow performance of XGBoost, and the LSTM 
capability of storing past information [24]. They demonstrated that their ensemble model 
outperformed the base and other individual models, with an R2 reaching 0.9921 [24]. 
Despite the improved advantages, ensemble models require higher computational 
resources due to the complexity of integrating multiple models, in addition to the 
==PAGE:27==
14 
 
individual model training itself. Moreover, the training and tuning of the ensemble models 
increase the risk of overfitting, especially when there is not enough data. Additionally, the 
ensemble model's extra layer makes it more challenging to understand the contribution 
of each model or feature to the final prediction.  
2.3 Literature Gap 
Despite the increasing number of studies on the interconnectedness of energy, financial, 
and carbon markets, a significant gap remains, particularly in relation to Indonesia’s 
energy markets. Firstly, there is an apparent lack of comprehensive studies 
concentrating on IDXEnergy. Existing analysis frequently excludes significant market 
influences, such as green energy and carbon markets, which have become increasingly 
relevant amid global decarbonization initiatives. Additionally, energy equities discussions 
are predominantly statistical, focusing on returns or price movements without adequately 
embedding these findings within macroeconomic and industry contexts. This restrictive 
framework limits the interpretative power of the conclusions, particularly in understanding 
how actual industrial activities, policy shifts, or strategic business changes impact 
IDXEnergy. 
Secondly, no forward-looking analytical model specifically attempts to predict IDXEnergy 
movements leveraging advanced approaches such as machine learning. In the 
meantime, most stock market forecasting models overlook sectoral characteristics or 
exclude crucial market variables as explanatory factors. This exclusion is especially 
suboptimal given the complex nature of energy equities. Furthermore, predictive models 
are often evaluated solely on statistical performance metrics, such as accuracy or error 
rates, without considering financial implications, like trading viability. 
To address these gaps, this thesis aims to contribute to the literature by (i) conducting a 
return spillover analysis centered on IDXEnergy within the broader economic and policy 
environment and (ii) developing predictive models that incorporate multidimensional 
market interactions and extend the evaluation to short-term trading simulations.

This chapter outlines the methodological framework employed in this study to analyze 
the spillover relationship and build predictive modelling of IDXEnergy, as illustrated in the 
flowchart in Figure 3.1. The overall process is divided into two main parts: spillover 
analysis and prediction & trading.

The initial step of this study is market selection, where the markets closely related to 
IDXEnergy are identified. The historical prices of those markets are collected and further 
preprocessed to match the required format by the spillover and prediction models. A 
basic statistical test is conducted on the preprocessed data to understand the properties 
for later model configuration. Then, the first core analysis is undertaken, i.e., market 
spillover, where the results are summarized and fed into the subsequent modelling step. 
The second part, prediction and trading, employs significantly influential markets as

initial model setup, the model hyperparameter is optimized, along with the

3.1.2 Data Preprocessing 
Data preprocessing aims to feed clean and compatible-format data to the model. The 
first preprocessing step is to remove N/A values, which are mostly associated with public 
holidays. This is particularly challenging in cross-border market analysis, where each 
country has its own public holidays, significantly reducing the available data. For 
example, Indonesia had a long Eid al-Fitr holiday from 28 March to 7 April 2025, spanning 
more than a week, while the EU had an Easter break from 18 to 21 April 2025. The 
following preprocessing step is differencing the data up to the second level to check the 
stationarity and conduct return spillover analysis. The “return” in this study refers to log 
rather than simple return, which captures the continuously compounded return [86]. 
Specific to price prediction, the data is split into six blocks under two groups. While the 
last block under the “OutCV” group is exclusively dedicated to final testing, the others 
under the “InCV” group are intended to improve the models through training and 
validation using an expanding-window cross-validation approach. This cross-validation 
method is chosen to increase the robustness of the model while still maximizing the 
number of samples utilized for the training [87]. That said, each block contains 
approximately 160 samples, which are slightly varied due to differences in data 
reformatting across various models. In selecting the best model from the InCV, the 
average metrics across all validation datasets are used. The best model is then 
evaluated on the test dataset to assess its final performance. This final test is crucial for

3.3 Prediction Modelling 
Once the spillover analysis is completed, this study utilizes the significant markets and 
their lag structures as inputs or features in the machine learning models for IDX Energy 
price forecasts. To ensure alignment with the nature of price prediction, the return lag 
structure is augmented by one additional lag. For instance, if the VAR identifies an 
optimal return lag of one, the associated price lags involve lag one and two, because 
lag-1 return is the difference between prices at these two points. 
Furthermore, when the optimal lags across markets differ, GRU and LSTM employed in 
this study adopt the maximum lag across all markets to maintain a consistent 
dimensionality of the inputs. This slight adjustment is necessary because these models 
require uniform feature dimensions. 
Apart from influencing market adjustments, the target variables – IDX Energy prices – 
are extended up to five trading days. This extension enhances the practical utility of the 
forecasts for trading applications, as market participants often consider a longer horizon 
when making trading decisions. 
The following sections provide further explanation of the model setup and optimization, 
as well as the trading simulation setup for evaluating the practical performance of 
forecasting. 
3.3.1 Model Built-Up 
This study employs diverse machine learning models stated in Table 2.2, starting from 
simple models such as linear regression, to more advanced models like LSTM. This 
broad selection is intended to strike a balance between capturing complex relationships 
that may exist between the markets and not overcomplicating the models. As an 
exception, decision-tree-based models are not selected due to their limitations in 
capturing price trends, as observed in IDXEnergy. The models employed in this study 
can be categorized into three groups: non-memory models, memory models, and 
ensemble models. To build these models, this study used Scikit-learn and Keras Python 
libraries. Each model category is briefly discussed in the following sections. 
Non-Memory Models 
The non-memory models in this study include linear and regularized regression (Ordinary 
Least Squares, Lasso, Ridge, & ElasticNet), Support Vector Regression (SVR), and 
Multi-Layer Perceptron Regression (MLPR). As these models do not incorporate 
sequential memory, the lagged prices of the influencing markets must be explicitly 
specified as independent features. Moreover, a sequential multi-step forecasting 
structure is adopted, in which the price prediction for day t+h incorporates both the 
lagged market data and previous model outputs for days t through t+h-1. This sequential 
setup allows the models to account for market temporal dependencies indirectly. 
The first model group, comprising linear and regularized regression models, assumes a 
direct relationship between the input features and the target outputs. Ordinary Least 
Squares (OLS) aims to minimize the sum of squared residuals, while Ridge regression 
enforces an L2 penalty to reduce overfitting by shrinking coefficients. Lasso introduces 
an L1 penalty that encourages sparsity, and ElasticNet combines both regularization 
==PAGE:33==
20 
 
types to handle multicollinearity among predictors. Hastie, Tibshirani, and Friedman 
(2017) discuss these regularization mechanisms and the underlying formulas 
extensively, while the ElasticNet formulation is presented explicitly in Zou and Hastie 
(2005)  [102], [103]. 
SVR, on the other hand, constructs a regression function that fits within a specified error 
margin 𝜖𝜖 and only penalizes the larger deviations. It uses support vectors and kernel 
functions, such as linear, polynomial, sigmoid, or radial basis function, to capture the 
variable relationships. The mechanism and optimization of SVR is thoroughly described 
by Smola and Schölkopf (2004) [104] 
Finally, MLPR is a feedforward neural network consisting of fully connected input, hidden, 
and output layers. Using a particular activation function, such as linear, logistic, tanh, or 
ReLU, and weight tuning via backpropagation to capture complex relationships between 
the input and target variables. Further architectural details are available in Chan Et al. 
(2023) [105]. 
Memory Models 
The memory models, i.e., Gated Recurrent Unit (GRU) and Long Short-Term Memory 
(LSTM), operate differently from conventional models, as they are inherently designed 
to handle sequential data by preserving and updating internal states over time. Rather 
than requiring manual addition of lagged features, the models are trained on continuous 
sequences of previous values to learn dynamic dependencies using their internal 
memory. Unlike the non-memory models that forecast one step at a time, GRU and LSTM 
are also trained to produce multi-step predictions simultaneously. 
GRU is a type of recurrent neural network (RNN) that has two gates: an update gate and 
a reset gate. The update gate controls how much of the previous hidden state is carried 
forward. The reset gate controls the extent to which prior information is forgotten before 
computing the candidate state. Ulah et al. (2023) describe GRU’s architecture and its 
learning process further [106]. 
LSTM enhances the memory capability by employing three gates: input, forget, and 
output. The forget gate removes irrelevant information from the previous memory, the 
input gate inserts new candidate information into the memory, and the output gate 
manages the memory for the next layer. The explanation of LSTM architecture and its 
temporal learning properties is available in Toma et al. (2019) [107]. 
Ensemble Models 
The Ensemble model would only be used when a model that excels at error metrics 
differs from one that is superior at the trading profit metrics. A stacking ensemble 
approach is used, as illustrated in Figure 3.3, where the prediction outputs from the 
lowest-error (LE) model and the highest-profit (HP) model are combined into a new 
dataset. This new dataset is used as features for the ensemble model. The ensemble 
model is based on a feedforward neural network, where the related parameters, such as 
the number of layers and connection weights, are optimized.

3.3.2 Hyperparameter Optimization 
After the model construction, the hyperparameter is tuned to improve predictive and 
trading performance. The optimization setup and metrics details are explained below. 
Optimization Model 
As this study employs complex machine learning models, a robust strategy for tuning the 
model hyperparameters is crucial to avoid unnecessary heavy computation. Therefore, 
a Bayesian optimization model is selected due to its superiority over classic models, such 
as grid search and random search. This advantage stems from its nature, which 
leverages the distribution of past observations to estimate the objective function using a 
surrogate model and selects the subsequent sample through an acquisition function. 
Although there are several ways to build the function, this study employs the Tree-
structured Parzen Estimator (TPE), which can handle non-continuous variables and 
offers a better trade-off between runtime and performance score [108], [109]. The Optuna 
library is used to implement this hyperparameter tuning in Python [110]. 
Unlike other models, TPE reformulates the optimization problem by modeling the 
conditional probability 𝑃𝑃(𝜃𝜃|ℒ) instead of 𝑃𝑃(ℒ|𝜃𝜃), where 𝜃𝜃 represents a candidate 
hyperparameter configuration, and ℒ is the corresponding objective function, such as 
validation loss. Observed trials are separated into two groups using a quantile threshold 
ℒ∗, where the good set in the minimizing objective contains trials with ℒ< ℒ∗, while the 
bad set contains the rest. The model approximates two probability densities as in Eq. 
(3.4) and (3.5) using Kernel Density Estimation (KDE) [111], [112]. The subsequent 
hyperparameter 𝜃𝜃∗ is chosen by maximizing the acquisition function in Eq. (3.6), which 
is the ratio of the two probability densities [112]. This selection criterion directs the search 
towards regions of the hyperparameter space that are more prevalent in good-performing 
groups and less common in poor-performing groups. Once a new result is obtained, the 
KDE for the two probability densities is updated, and TPE continues refining its region 
estimate, repeating the process until a stopping criterion is met.

3.3.3 Trading Simulation 
This section outlines the mechanisms followed to simulate trading based on the price-
forecasting models and selected benchmark strategies. The primary output of these 
mechanisms is trading action, which involves buying, selling, or holding the equity. 
Comparing the performance of price prediction approaches with conventional strategies 
enables a robust evaluation of the value added by predictive models. 
Predictive Model Trading 
The predicted prices of each model are used as the basis for the trading algorithm. When 
the trading horizon is beyond 1 day, i.e., period t > 0, the trading signal is triggered only 
if today’s predicted price from model m, denoted PPm,0, is the minimum/maximum within 
its forecast window. In the meantime, period 0 bypasses this condition because it can 
only predict one price, i.e., today’s price, at a time. If this requirement is satisfied, PPm,0  
will be evaluated whether it is below/above the previous day’s actual price (AP-1) with a 
model- and horizon-specific gap threshold (Thm,h). This trading signal is formally 
formulated in Eq. (3.10). The threshold is optimized via TPE to maximize the model’s 
profitability in the validation dataset. Once both conditions are satisfied and a positive 
rupiah/stock balance is available, the corresponding action is executed at the 
IDXEnergy’s closing price on the corresponding day. The balance is then adjusted 
accordingly, and the process repeats throughout the dataset.

Trading Benchmark Models 
A set of well-established trading algorithms is employed for comparison to verify the 
robustness of the predictive models. These include the buy-and-hold (BH) strategy, as 
well as several price-based indicators: exponential moving average (EMA), relative 
strength index (RSI), and Bollinger bands (BB). The following provides a brief description 
of each strategy, while more detailed explanations can be found in the literature written 
by Beers (2020), Praekhaow (2010), and Murphy (1999) [113], [114], [115]. 
BH is a passive investment strategy where the investor buys the asset at the beginning 
of the period and holds it without further trading until the end. This BH strategy is fully 
exposed to market dynamics and, therefore, reflects the asset's performance. 
Meanwhile, EMA is a trend-following indicator that smooths previous prices to indicate 
the current direction of the market. A buy signal is triggered when the asset’s price 
crosses above the EMA, while a sell signal occurs when it crosses below. In this study, 
EMA is employed using rolling window lengths of 5, 9, 12, and 21 days. 
RSI is a momentum oscillator that measures the speed and magnitude of recent price 
changes to identify potential reversal levels. It has a value range of 0 to 100, with specific 
thresholds indicating oversold and overbought signals. The standard 30 and 70 
thresholds are adopted in this study: a buy signal occurs when the RSI falls below 30, 
and a sell signal occurs when it rises above 70. Four different RSI window sizes are 
applied in this study: 5, 7, 9, and 14 days. 
BB is a strategy relying on three components: an upper band, a lower band, and a moving 
average. The upper/lower bands are obtained by adding/subtracting a specific multiple 
of the price’s standard deviation from the moving average. A buy signal is generated 
when the price reaches or drops below the lower band, while a sell signal occurs when 
the price rises to or goes beyond the upper band. In this study, BB is applied with rolling 
windows of 10, 20, and 50 days, along with standard deviation multipliers of 1.5, 2, and 
2, respectively. 
Unlike the predictive models, which forecast the future closing prices, these benchmark 
models are inherently backward-looking. Consequently, any trade signal (buy or sell) is 
executed using the opening price of the following day, introducing a slight delay relative 
to the signal’s generation.

4.1.3 Stationarity 
Stationary tests are crucial to the validity of traditional Granger causality and the setup 
of TYGC. ADF and PP tests were applied to the return levels and their first differences. 
ADF and PP evaluate whether a series contains a unit root, which indicates non-
stationarity. The series is considered to be stationary when the null hypothesis of a unit 
root is rejected. The test p-value results are displayed in Table 4.2. 
ADF and PP show a consistent result throughout the test, where the majority of variables 
exhibit stationary returns with p-values essentially at zero. The only exception is the BI 
Rate, marked in bold, which cannot reject the null hypothesis in both the ADF and PP 
tests (p-values of 0.57 and 0.62, respectively), indicating that its return is not stationary. 
However, all series, including BI Rate, are found to be stationary after first differencing. 
The ADF and PP p-values on BI Rate return change are 0.01 and 0, respectively, 
surpassing the critical 5% value threshold. 
These results have particular relevance to the appropriate setup of TYGC. The maximum 
order of integration (𝑑𝑑𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚) between variables determines how many additional lags to 
include in the VAR model before proceeding with the Wald tests under the Granger 
causality framework. In this context, given that only BI Rate is I(1), all 𝑑𝑑𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚 is set to zero 
when not assessing BI Rate (𝑑𝑑𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚𝑚= 1). That said, the TYGC tests on other than the BI 
Rate are essentially the same as the traditional GC test.

stock index. These findings will be discussed in depth as follows. 
4.2.1 VAR Lag Structure 
The VAR under TYGC tests show that all the significant influences between the markets 
occurred in a relatively short period. It suggests that changes in one particular market, 
made one to two days earlier, have a forecasting effect on IDXEnergy in the current 
period. The short lag pattern implies quick information transmission without a prolonged 
effect, as IDXEnergy responds promptly to movements in the global markets. This also 
highlights a degree of market efficiency, as publicly released data, such as oil price 
movements, is quickly reflected in IDXEnergy. 
An example of such dynamics was demonstrated on 1 August 2022, when Brent oil 
prices dropped by 9% to $100.03 following the release of weaker-than-anticipated 
manufacturing data from major economies, signaling a slowdown in global demand [116]. 
The sell-off in energy commodities was quickly reflected in the IDXEnergy return on the 
following day (-2.74%), but it diminished within two days (-0.16% on 3 August 2022). This 
phenomenon is evidence of the short-lag causality observed in the empirical model,

influence Indonesia’s energy equity valuations.

4.2.2 Significant Bidirectional Spillover 
This section presents the two-way spillovers, in which IDXEnergy both influences and is 
influenced by another market, indicating dynamic feedback between them. 
S&P 500 (Lag = 2, p <= 5%) 
This study finds, with high confidence (p-value = 0), that the S&P 500 Granger-causes 
IDXEnergy, which aligns with both market structure and investor behavior. S&P 500 
functions as a global barometer for macroeconomic expectations and risk appetite. A 
clear example occurred on 7 March 2022 when the S&P 500 dropped by 2.95% due to 
the escalation of the Russia-Ukraine war and governments’ consideration for ever-
stricter economic penalties on Russia, including cutting off imports of Russian oil [117]. 
One day later, the IDXEnergy price plunged by 3.41% despite the previous rise in oil 
prices. Such events illustrate how financial contagion spreads from developed markets, 
such as the US, to emerging markets, like Indonesia. 
Surprisingly, a causal relationship from the S&P 500 to IDXEnergy is found to be 
significant with a two-day lag and the p-value slightly below 5%. This implies that past 
movements of energy equities in Indonesia might contain information about subsequent 
changes in the US equity market. This result is counterintuitive, given that the S&P 500 
is a globally diversified index with a market capitalization of around US$30 trillion in 2021, 
while IDXEnergy is a coal-heavy index with a market capitalization of less than 10% of 
the S&P 500 [118]. Some subsequent movements were indeed observed between those 
two, such as significant increases in the IDXEnergy price on 22 July 2021, 9 August 
==PAGE:41==
28 
 
2022, and 20 October 2022, which were followed by substantial increases in the S&P 
500 on the subsequent days. However, most of those increases were driven by global 
factors, such as the oil price surge the day before and a large foreign capital inflow on 
the same day to IDXEnergy, indicating a shared global driver that might have occurred 
instead of the inherent causality from IDXEnergy [119], [120]. IDXEnergy causality 
inference could come from the energy equity’s quicker response to this kind of energy-
related signals than diversified-sector equities like the S&P 500. 
Coal (Lag = 2, p <= 0.5%) 
The TYGC test reveals a significant bidirectional causality between IDXEnergy and coal 
returns, suggesting that these equity and commodity markets have a tight relationship. 
The causality from coal to IDXEnergy is expected, due to the domination of coal mining 
companies. Increases in global coal prices typically signal higher revenue prospects and 
profitability for these firms, which are then quickly reflected in their stock prices. For 
example, the coal price surge of 40.58% to a new high record ($440/ton) on 2 March 
2022 led to a 6.88% increase in the IDXEnergy price on 4 March 2022. This extremely 
high coal price is caused by the prolonged Russia-Ukraine war, specifically the UN 
General Assembly's demand, backed by 141 countries, for Russia to withdraw all military 
forces from Ukraine [121], [122], [123]. This finding also aligns with Goh et al. (2025) and 
Setyowati and Novyarni (2025), stating the influence of coal on IDXEnergy [17], [18]. 
The causality from IDXEnergy to coal prices suggests a reverse mechanism where 
changes in Indonesian energy stocks can signal information to the global coal market. 
This might occur due to Indonesia’s position as the largest coal exporter, and changes 
in domestic policy or firms’ coal production, reflected in IDXEnergy prices, affect the coal 
price. However, this finding should be taken with caution because this study cannot 
provide evidence of real economic activity that explains the subsequent movement of 
the coal price after IDXEnergy. For instance, on 7 February 2025, IDXEnergy plummeted 
by -6.28%, and the coal price dropped by -1.49% on the following day. However, it was 
driven by different factors, with capital outflow from IDXEnergy due to President Trump’s 
trade tariffs and a decline in demand for coal [124], [125], [126], [127]. Moreover, this 
finding contradicts the results of the Granger-causality test by Goh et al. (2025), which 
highlight one-way causality from coal to IDXEnergy and a p-value of 0.08 for the other 
direction [17]. 
The findings on IDXEnergy’s bidirectional relationship with coal, as well as S&P 500, 
demonstrate the power and the limitations of statistical causality tests like TYGC. While 
the model presents two-way causality, the economic interpretation might not be obvious 
or even divergent in direction. Therefore, contextualizing the Granger causality results is 
just as important as interpreting the statistical inference. Furthermore, a deeper 
investigation using other methodologies would also be beneficial in providing a clearer 
picture of the market relationships. 
4.2.3 Significant Unidirectional Spillovers to IDXEnergy 
In contrast to mutual interactions, this section addresses unidirectional spillovers where 
a market influences IDXEnergy without feedback in the other direction. This section 
highlights external dependence, where global market signals shape domestic equity 
valuations. 
==PAGE:42==
29 
 
Oil (Lag = 1, p = 0%) 
The TYGC results confirm a unidirectional spillover from global oil returns to IDXEnergy, 
with a lag of one, indicating a rapid but non-persisting effect on IDXEnergy. This reflects 
the fast transmission of global energy demand supply signals to Indonesian energy 
equity valuations, especially in the context of oil as an international barometer of 
industrial activity. This result aligns with the finding of unidirectional effect from oil to 
IDXEnergy by Goh et al. (2025) [17]. Another example of this phenomenon than 
mentioned in Section 4.2.1 was an oil price surge (8.79%) on 17 March 2022, followed 
by a price increase in IDXEnergy the day after (1.25%). 
EU ETS (Lag = 2, p = 0.7%) 
In contrast, the EU ETS shows a one-way effect on IDXEnergy with a VAR lag of two, 
indicating that the carbon price influence spans across two consecutive days. A case in 
point occurred at the end of January 2024, especially on Thursday, 25 January 2024, 
when the EU ETS price dropped by 3.63%. IDXEnergy followed on Friday, 26 January, 
and Monday, 29 January, with drops of 0.97% and 0.51%, respectively, despite the 
stagnation of coal, oil, and the S&P 500. The downtrend of the EU ETS is attributed to a 
carbon allowance oversupply following the European Commission’s release of additional 
carbon permits [128]. This highlights the complexity of the global energy market, where 
IDXEnergy’s reaction to the converging direction is evident in both ESG-related markets 
and carbon-intensive commodities. This raises further interest in studies to quantify the 
strength of these conflicting market influences. 
4.2.4 Insignificant Spillover Relationship 
While several global variables exhibit statistically significant spillovers to IDXEnergy, 
TYGC also identifies other variables that have no substantial influence, even though they 
may appear considerable based on the literature review. These variables are currency 
exchange rates (USD/IDR, CNY/IDR, and EUR/IDR), the Solactive China Clean Energy 
Index (CCE), and the BI 7-Day Revers Repo Rate (BI Rate). Their statistical 
insignificance in the short-run Granger causality reflects the offsetting effects of the 
commodity. Moreover, this absence of causal relationships also implies that IDXEnergy 
has a degree of independence from regional risks. Its low sensitivity to domestic interest 
rate movements, regional clean energy equity index, and exchange rate dynamics offers 
a potential hedge for investors willing to mitigate exposure to such regional 
macroeconomic risks. The following discussion provides a more detailed examination of 
these findings. 
CCE (Lag = 1, p >= 15%) 
Despite the increasing interconnectivity between Indonesia’s energy companies and 
Chinese clean energy firms, the CCE index fails to reveal a significant short-term 
spillover to IDXEnergy. Therefore, the evidence of recent agreements between CCE-
listed companies and Indonesia’s flagship renewables project is insufficient to establish 
causality between these studied markets. As analyzed in the literature review, this may 
be attributed to the dominance of coal companies, where energy commodities drive 
short-term returns. Moreover, despite Indonesia’s flagship renewable projects being 
supplied by Chinese companies, they did not account for a significant proportion of the 
total component production in China. For example, in 2024, the total China solar module 
==PAGE:43==
30 
 
exports to Asia-Pacific countries alone reached 68.11 GW, while Indonesia’s flagship 
project sizes were only about 1 GW. This gap in scale underscored the minuscule size 
of renewable projects in Indonesia [129]. 
CNY/IDR (Lag = 2, p >= 5%) 
The CNY/IDR exchange rate shows a nearly significant influence on IDXEnergy with a 
two-day lag and a p-value of 5.7%. Meanwhile, Widajatun and Ichsani (2019) observed 
a positive impact from CNY/IDR on Indonesia’s composite index (IHSG) [38]. This 
suggests that energy sector equities are insulated from that impact. The lack of 
significance here may reflect the absorption of effects due to contracts typically 
denominated in USD or the overshadowing influence of commodity price movements. 
USD/IDR (Lag = 1, p >= 69%) 
The lack of significant causality from USD/IDR sheds light on the conflicting findings of 
Setyowaty and Novyarni (2025) and Novianto and Paramita (2023). While Setyowati and 
Novyarni (2025) did not explain the rationale beyond statistically significant influence, 
Novianto and Paramita (2023) pointed out that the insignificance could be observed on 
IDXEnergy in 2020-2022, which tended to follow the high return of coal energy rather 
than the decline of IDR values [20]. The dampened effect from USD/IDR could also be 
seen from the contrast difference between the return distribution of that currency rate 
and coal. As discussed in 4.1.2, coal exhibits extreme return behavior compared to the 
relatively stable USD-IDR exchange rate. Hence, it is not a surprise that the movement 
of coal prices dominates and overshadows the USD/IDR rate. 
EUR/IDR (Lag = 1, p >= 27%) 
Similar to the other currency rates, EUR/IDR has a minor effect on IDXEnergy, despite 
its significant impact on broader indices like IDX30 [40]. In addition to the return 
distribution disparity between currency rates and energy commodities explained above, 
it can be attributed to the relatively low energy trade exposure between Indonesia and 
the EU compared to other Asian countries. According to the European Commission 
report, more than 80% of bilateral trade goods between Indonesia and the EU are 
manufactured and agricultural products [130]. When it comes to coal, only Spain is 
among the top 10 largest Indonesian coal importers, with an average of 80.6 tons per 
year since 2020 [37]. This deeper look at the trading relationship explained the exclusion 
of energy equities from the EUR/IDR influences. 
BI Rate (Lag = 21+1, p >= 50%) 
BI 7-Day Reverse Repo Rate has no statistically significant influence on IDXEnergy, 
even with a longer lag structure. This finding aligns with Suci (2024), which found no 
significant effect of BI Rate on IDXEnergy in the short term. Nonetheless, the long lag 
structure comes from the monthly rate update of the BI rate, unlike the real-time 
movement of other variables, making the daily lag effect indistinguishable. The VAR 
model, which aims to capture a longer-term impact, may suggest a significant long-term 
influence of the BI Rate on Indonesian equities, as observed by Sia et al. (2024).

4.3 Price Prediction  
This section focuses on predictive models built upon the significant market results under 
return spillover analysis. This serves to demonstrate the feasibility of the IDXEnergy price 
forecasting in addressing the second main objective. The discussion begins with the 
hyperparameter optimization processes, followed by optimal model parameters and an 
evaluation of their performance across various settings. 
4.3.1 Hyperparameter Optimization  
This study utilizes the High-Performance Computing (HPC) facility provided by the 
Technical University of Denmark (DTU), which features a heterogeneous cluster 
architecture designed for CPU- and GPU-intensive calculations. It has more than 2,000 
CPU nodes, supported by large memory capacities (minimum 128 GB per node) [131]. 
For the GPU workloads, the system comprises 38 nodes with high-performance NVIDIA 
accelerators, including Tesla V100 and Tesla A100 [132], [133]. The jobs are managed 
through automatic queues depending on the hardware configurations.  
Under the TPE optimization framework, each machine learning model is set to attempt 
300 trials per horizon, resulting in a total of 1,500 trials across the five different horizons. 
An exception is made for memory models, namely GRU and LSTM, which predict prices 
across multiple horizons simultaneously, thereby limiting the number of trials to 300. For 
computational practicality, the optimization time is capped at 4.56 hours per horizon. Due 
to a long queue for GPU usage in the cluster, the GPU is only used for base models with 
neural network architectures: MLPR, GRU, and LSTM. Specifically, MLPR was executed 
on GPUV100 nodes, while GRU and LSTM utilized the more advanced GPUA100 nodes 
to support their complex memory gates.

Trading 
Under this trading framework, transaction thresholds are introduced and optimized to 
filter out price signal noise and adjust the models’ sensitivity to price changes. Lower 
thresholds indicate a sensitive trading strategy that highly responds to both observed 
and predicted price dynamics, resulting in more frequent trading. In contrast, higher 
thresholds indicate a more conservative approach, where transactions are made only 
based on strong price signals. This helps avoid reactions to minor fluctuations and 
minimizes overtrading.

thresholds at shorter-term horizons, particularly at days 0 and 1. This tendency reflects

understanding of each model’s strengths and weaknesses under different market 
conditions and predictive horizons.

5 Conclusion 
This study analyzes the relationship between Indonesia’s energy stock index 
(IDXEnergy) and major global energy, financial, and carbon markets. This study then 
extends the findings by developing predictive models to forecast IDXEnergy prices using 
machine learning. Addressing a gap in the literature that often overlooks comprehensive 
market integration and a practical predictive model evaluation in the Indonesian energy 
equity context, this study pursues two main objectives: 1) to explore return spillover 
across markets with contextual interpretation, and 2) to build forecasting models using 
the significant market features, evaluated on both predictive accuracy and practical 
trading performance. 
According to the return spillover analysis under the TYGC framework, both bidirectional 
and unidirectional impacts on IDXEnergy are found. Coal, the S&P 500, oil, and the EU 
carbon market significantly influenced IDXEnergy, with the first two also receiving 
feedback from IDXEnergy. Conversely, Chinese energy equities, exchange rates, and 
domestic interest rates do not exhibit a statistically significant causal relationship, 
indicating that IDXEnergy is somewhat isolated from the regional markets. 
In the prediction modelling, linear models consistently yield low prediction errors. 
Nevertheless, Ridge demonstrates a distinguished profit performance by surpassing all 
benchmark models, followed by other linear regression and LSTM models that 
outperform the market in both the validation and test datasets. The predictive period, 
spanning a 3-day horizon (period 2), is generally optimal for maximizing trading profits. 
These results remain consistent despite major global shocks, such as the Russia-
Ukraine war, the 2022 surge in coal prices, and US-China trade tensions, suggesting 
some resilience to market uncertainty. Nevertheless, no machine learning model in this 
study can consistently reduce risks while maintaining a competitive profit. 
This study contributes to the literature on emerging market energy equities, particularly 
by considering broader market influences, such as carbon markets and global financial 
markets. The findings provide policymakers with empirical insights, showing that, beyond 
the well-known energy commodity influences, global financial and carbon pricing 
dynamics also impact Indonesia’s energy equities. For investors, the study presents 
forecasting models that may lead to improved returns and empirical results indicating 
that simple linear regression remains highly effective. Moreover, investors can benefit 
from a hedging mechanism against regional risks through IDXEnergy, as it has an 
insignificant influence from Chinese energy equities, major exchange rates, and 
domestic interest rates. For researchers, this study demonstrates a combined framework 
of spillover analysis and predictive modelling applicable to emerging markets with limited 
data histories. 
This study comes with some limitations. Firstly, IDXEnergy’s new establishment (since 
2021) limits data observation, especially when applied to deep neural networks. 
Secondly, the representation of renewables and the carbon market remains limited due 
to the absence or immaturity of corresponding market data. Thirdly, while this study 
incorporates various external markets, it does not fully capture investment sentiment, 
corporate performance & actions, or regulatory shifts that may influence stock dynamics. 
==PAGE:60==
47 
 
Fourthly, the trading optimization framework is formulated to maximize return without 
explicitly imposing risk as a secondary objective or a constraint, which limits the models 
from delivering risk-adjusted performance. Lastly, this study does not consider trading 
transaction costs, which may affect profitability in practice. 
Future studies can extend this research framework in certain aspects. Firstly, the 
influencing variables can be expanded to include news and social media sentiments, 
price- and volume-based technical indicators, other renewable energy assets, or 
IDXCarbon (once mature), to capture a more holistic relationship and enhance predictive 
power. When risk-adjusted profit performance is of high interest, the trading optimization 
framework can be expanded to a dual-objective setup, which explicitly balances profit 
maximization with risk minimization. Incorporating trading transaction costs and slippage 
into the simulation will also be important to reflect real-world trading performance. The 
spillover analysis can also be extended to quantify the influence strength once more data 
is available, by using the DY spillover index, for example. Once longer time series 
become available, deep learning models could be revisited for more stable training 
conditions. Lastly, with more data in the future, dynamic modeling, such as time-varying 
VAR or time-varying parameters for predictive models, would also be helpful in revealing 
the market dynamic characteristics during major events, including the 2022 energy price 
surge, the Russia-Ukraine war, or the US-China trade tensions. These models would 
identify structural breaks and evolving spillover patterns, which static models may miss.
