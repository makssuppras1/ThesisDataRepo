Abstract
This thesis describes a method of designing near-optimal plate structures by projection of a
coarse ﬁnite element mesh to a considerably ﬁner mesh. The coarse scale solution is based on
homogenization based topology optimization and with an interpretation of the rank-2 micro-
structure as a periodic geometry. The results of the projections show clear similarities between
coarse and ﬁne mesh and these results can be obtained at a much lower computational cost
than with traditional topology optimization.
I
==PAGE:4==
Abstrakt
Denne afhandling beskriver en metode til at designe nær-optimale pladestrukturer, ved hjælp af
projicering fra et groft net til et meget ﬁnere net. Løsningen på det grove net er på baggrund af
homogeniseringsbaseret topologioptimering og bliver overført til det ﬁnere net ved en fortolkning
af en rang-2 mikrostruktur som en periodisk geometri. Resultaterne af denne projicering viser
klare ligheder mellem det grove og ﬁne net og det er muligt at opnå disse resultater med meget
færre udregningsmæssige omkostninger end ved de konventionelle topologioptimeringsmetoder.

Introduction
In nature, evolution naturally seeks to improve any organism relative to their environment. This
process often leads to either less energy expended for the same tasks or improved performance
in doing so. One way evolution is optimizing organisms is by using the available resources more
eﬃciently for example in birds who need to be light enough to ﬂy, yet still strong enough to
do so. Such a process can also be applied with great beneﬁts within structural engineering,
where the eﬃcient use of materials can have signiﬁcant eﬀect. Especially within the aerospace
and automotive industry is it important to have a high performance with relatively low weight.
This optimization has many applications including acoustic designs, thermal conduction and
even electromagnetic devices.
Shape and topology optimization are not new concepts. For millennia, many architects and
engineers have been trying to increase performance by changing the geometry. Examples are
the ancient Romans, who used dome structures to make up for the lack of tension strength in
the concrete buildings, and even wheels from the bronze age have shown signs of optimization
by the use of spokes instead of solid centre material, thus reducing the weight [1, 2].
During the last few decades, new tools for optimization have become available with the
increase of computational resources, which has further matured the subject as a design method,
with many industrial applications.
The application of topology optimization on plate structures is sometimes limited due to the
nature of their functionality. Often a plate must be designed to withstand surface loads, such
as snow loads, where creating through-the-thickness holes may deteriorate the other properties
[3, 4]. Many practical applications of plates have instead been constructed as laminate struc-
tures, with a thick but weak central core, surrounded by stiﬀer lamina for additional bending
strength. Individual layers like those can with beneﬁt be improved through topology opti-
mization. Another applied method of optimization on such plate structures are by varying
the thickness in diﬀerent parts of the structure [5]. This method can also work very well for
laminated structures, where the thickness easily can be modiﬁed by addition or removal of
individual plies [6].
Since the methods of topology optimization was introduced by Bendsøe and Kikuchi in 1988
[7], a variety of diﬀerent methods have been developed [8]. Common for many of the opti-
mization methods is their reliance on ﬁnite element analyses which, in the case of large scale
optimization cases, will be computationally expensive [9]. Another challenge for many methods
is that the solution should have binary properties with areas of either void or solid material [10].
One of the most popular methods of such topology optimization is the solid isotropic material
with penalization (SIMP) model, which describes the material properties as an interpolation
between solid and void. These properties are then scaled with a design parameter, but penal-
ized at intermediate values to promote a binary structure. However, SIMP is limited by the
amounts of elements thus requiring a relative ﬁne mesh, further increasing the calculation time.
Additional iterations and computation time are required to further ensure that the solution is
near-optimal.
The original work by Bendsøe and Kikuchi instead uses a homogenization based method,
optimizing an inﬁnitesimal micro-structure in each element.
This can be interpreted as a
periodic structure which can be projected from a coarse mesh to a ﬁner one, that allows ﬁner
structural features [11].
Pantz and Trabelsi have since developed a projection method that produces distinctive areas
1
==PAGE:12==
of material or void in the design domain which also ensures the continuity between the elements
with a relaxation of the design space [12]. This method has been simpliﬁed and improved
in a recent paper by Groen, which suggested methods of ensuring manufacturability by also
controlling feature sizes in the near-optimal high resolution 2-dimensional solution [11].
The projection method can also be applied to plate structures, either through a perforated
structure, a reinforcing rib structure or as an optimal core topology in the desired thin-plate
structure.
The general objective of this thesis is to expand the work of Groen to develop methods for
creating near-optimal, manufacturable plate structures. This expansion will use the homog-
enization based topology optimization on ranked materials for plates and introduce a ﬁnite
element solution for the mapping of material rotations. The mapping will also include options
for a higher order interpolation through the elements for improved accuracy. Finally, the coarse
scale homogenized solution will be projected onto a much ﬁner structure with controllable fea-
ture sizes, and the solution compared to the existing SIMP-method.
This thesis is organized as follows:
Section 2: The deﬁnition and application of the used C0-Mindlin plate element.
Section 3: Application of homogenization for deﬁning material properties in a periodic
micro-structure.
Section 4: A description of compliance based topology optimization and its application
with homogenized materials.
Section 5: How to translate the coarse ﬁnite element mesh into a much ﬁner mesh, using
the periodic properties of the ranked material.
Section 6: Some numerical examples of this method.
Section 7: Perspective for future work on the subject.
Section 8: A conclusion of the completed work and the thesis.

2
Mindlin plate element
Most topology optimization processes are based on the ﬁnite element method (FEM). This
method is designed for numerical solutions of ﬁeld problems, for example ﬁnding a displacement
ﬁeld in a structure under a certain load. In the ﬁnite element analysis (FEA), the structure is
divided into smaller parts, the so called elements, each with a set of properties. The optimization
processes can beneﬁt greatly from the FEM, where each iteration changes the properties of each
individual element.
For structural mechanics, the parameters adjusted in the optimization
process is typically a variable that indicated how much material is placed at the given location.
This is further described in section 3 and 4.
In the optimization, the design domain is ﬁrst created. This domain deﬁnes every available
element, thus also the spatial limitation of the structure. Using more elements increases both
the resolution of the ﬁnal structure and the reliability of the results and are therefore desirable.
However, using more elements also comes with a larger computational cost, so choosing the
right type of element is crucial. For relatively thin structures, a plate or shell element is often
the best choice.
2.1
Description
A plate is deﬁned as a thin, ﬂat geometry. Modelling such a structure is very inconvenient
using 3D-elements, as these will tend to lock if not many of them are used, thus having too
many degrees of freedom (DOFs) [13]. For this purpose, a plate element is used. Plate elements
diﬀer from 3D-elements in that they are planar but with an assigned thickness and that they
can bend out of plane with less risk of shear locking.
The two major plate theories used are Kirchhoﬀ’s thin plate theory and the Mindlin-Reissner
theory. The theory used in this work is the Mindlin theory, as this works with isoparametric
elements as well as takes the transverse shear into account. This allows for a more correct
modelling of thicker plate structures as well.

2.3
Mindlin test cases
The Mindlin elements are integrated using Gauss-Legendre quadrature. The amount of Gauss
points needed depends on the amount of nodes in the element, with 2 points per direction in a
bilinear element and 3 in a serendipity or Lagrange element for a full integration. This amount
of Gauss points can be reduced by 1 by reduced integration. Using reduced integration can speed
up the integration process signiﬁcantly and possibly prevent some instances of shear locking.
Another type of integration used is the selective integration, with a full integration of the
bending stiﬀness and a reduced integration of the shear stiﬀness. This integration method will,
in the bilinear element, derive the exact results from bending stiﬀness and get the correct value
of the shear stiﬀness in the middle of the element [13]. The downside of reduced and selective
integration is that they might introduce more spurious modes, for example hourglassing, where
some non-physical energyless deformations occur [14, 15].
Testing the elements with diﬀerent boundary conditions and load cases show how well the
elements work in diﬀerent cases and, thus, which element is preferable to use. One way the
Mindlin element can fail is if the element is too thin, where it starts locking. Therefore, this
test is conducted both with a clamped square plate with a distributed load and with a simply
supported square plate under a central point load using diﬀerent thickness.
A unidirectional distributed load, such as a representation of mass, on the plate element can
be applied as lumped loads. Lumped loading, contrary to consistent loading, omits the moment
terms of the distributed load. Common to both methods are that they converge towards the
correct result with mesh reﬁnement [13]. Here, the lumped loads are deﬁned,

While the reduced and selective bilinear and Lagrange elements show promising results, they

All the serendipity elements fail as they become increasingly unreliable with the decreasing
plate thickness. The bilinear elements are either completely unreliable, with the full integration,

9-node Lagrange element, even though it has large errors and requires additional elements to
get a good solution. The Lagrange element is therefore a very expensive element to use, but
the only one of these that works reliably.
3
Ranked materials
It is necessary in topology optimization to be able to change material parameters in each
element. There are various ways to do this. The SIMP-method scales the stiﬀness with the
density of the material. As areas with intermediate densities have little physical meaning, a
penalty is added to the stiﬀness for elements with intermediate densities to encourage areas of
either complete solid or complete void. The homogenization based method removes this penalty
by interpreting the material as a micro-structure with inﬁnitesimal periodic properties. While
such a micro-structure is unrealistic, it can be realised as a projection of the periodic properties
in a larger scale, which is covered in section 5. A coarse ﬁnite element mesh with these properties
can be projected onto a much ﬁner mesh, thus speeding up the process signiﬁcantly [11].
Periodic micro-structures, as described in [16], consist of alternating sections with two ma-
terials of diﬀerent stiﬀness and layering thickness. This material can be built in several ranks,
where rank-1 simply alternates sections of the stiﬀest material, known as the stiﬀer, with a more
compliant material. Since the latter will be considered as no material, it is here referred to as
void. These layers are stacked in a direction nR1
α , with a density µR1 describing the fraction
of stiﬀer in the micro structure. A density of µR1 = 1 therefore means solid stiﬀer whereas
µR1 = 0 is entirely void.
A rank-2 material is also deﬁned with layers of stiﬀer and a more compliant material, but the
compliant material is here the rank-1 material. The layers of the rank-2 material are stacked in
a diﬀerent direction, nR2
α , thus creating an anisotropic material with customizable properties.
Equally, a rank-3 micro-structure stacks alternating layers of stiﬀer and rank-2 material in a
direction nR3
α
and so on. See ﬁgure 3.1.
For a single load case in a planar disk, the rank-2 micro structure has been proven to be
optimal and will therefore be used to describe the material properties in the ﬁnite element
analyses [17].

4
Topology Optimization
Optimization is the process with which to minimize a given function, the objective, within a
predeﬁned set of constraints. The topology part of this refers to allocating material in the
design domain, thus ﬁnding the optimal geometry of a structure to minimize the objective
function. Diﬀerent kinds of objective functions can be used depending on the problem, but
for structural mechanics it is usually the compliance, as this method is relatively simple. The
optimization here will therefore be based on the compliance method.
Most topology optimization works with the application of linear ﬁnite element analyses,
where each iteration distributes material diﬀerently. As described in section 3, the material
is distributed in an orthotropic micro structure, which is used for material properties in each
element. The densities can then vary in the interval of [0, 1], where 1 means that the element
is completely solid and 0 means void.

describing the density of one rank, the total density must be deﬁned as well,

Some material rotations may still be at a 180◦rotation compared to the neighbouring ele-
ments. To properly adjust these elements, Groen suggest using connected component labelling
(CCL) which is a common method used in image processing and graph theory [11, 27] .
A simple implementation of the CCL has been used, which starts in a corner element and
labels it 1. Going through the rows of elements, the CCL-algorithm labels the elements with
the same label as a connected component, if the angular values are within ±90◦of each other.
If there’s a larger diﬀerence, a new label is issued to the element. If an element’s rotational
design variable is within ±90◦of two diﬀerent labels, the smallest label is assigned and the
higher label is relabelled as the smaller of the two.
When every element is labelled, all elements with label 1 is rotated 180◦. This process is
continued until every element has the same label. While generally eﬀective, this process may
cause some inconsistencies, see ﬁgure 5.2, but a perfect rotation ﬁeld is not always possible.
No severe problems are likely to be created as long as the inconsistencies are limited to small
areas, where the penalty of ignoring it is smaller than when attempting to ﬁt to the error. The
α2-value signiﬁcantly improves the solution at the inconsistent locations with 180◦diﬀerence,
as the unit vectors are forced to be normal to each other.

A better result of the CCL has been found if a density threshold is set. The orientations
of low-density elements are not important, as they are close to isotropic and can be ignored
through the α-values in equation (5.14). The density of any element beneath the threshold is
therefore also ignored as if they were completely void. Such an operation will only have minor
eﬀects on the ﬁnal volume fraction due to the Heaviside function. The appropriate density
threshold depends on the individual cases, but the ideal limit separates areas where one of the
principal stresses reaches 0, such that the CCL labels each area individually. More elements
will reduce the necessary threshold to produce these distinct areas. The density limit is even
further reduced with an increased core thickness. For a 50 x 50 element plate with a core
thickness tc = 0.2t, an appropriate limit has been found to be mlim = 0.01. Since these areas
are to be ignored, the material here is better spent elsewhere, and the density threshold should

be reﬂected in the Heaviside limit in equation (4.22).
Any element with a density smaller than the threshold has the material pointed in the same
direction as the surrounding element with the highest density. Without this step, the lower
density elements can cause ﬂuctuations or burrs during the mapping and projection. The ﬂuc-
tuations often results in wavy structures along the edges of the low density elements whereas
the burrs aﬀects the structure perpendicular to the same edges, bending the tips of the rein-
forcing structure in odd angles. Some of the ﬂaws may persist if there is only one low-density
element separating higher density areas.

5.4
Smoothing micro-structure
One problem with the solution shown in ﬁgure 5.3, is that it still is somewhat element based,
where the element borders can be seen clearly as the angles and densities change. It is possible
to smooth the angles further by adding more nodes or elements in the mesh before the mapping,
so the angles can get a more gradual change. This results in an intermediate mesh that can
be used as a stepping stone to an even ﬁner end projection. Additional elements added to
the intermediate mesh have density and rotation values evaluated linearly between the existing
elements. Alternatively, additional nodes can be created by using a higher order shape function
and more integration points. The amount of Gauss points are found to be NG = p + 1, for an
element order of p, which is slightly at odds with the theoretical NG = p/2 + 1 that is expected
from Gaussian quadrature [28].

5.7
Interpolation and reﬁnement of mapping function
As mentioned in section 5.4, diﬀerent interpolation functions and additional elements can be
used when deﬁning the map.
The eﬀect from the diﬀerent interpolation schemes of the mapping are summarized in table
5.4 and 5.5, with the compliance results, density and calculation time of each. These results
have been set at a scale of ˜ϵ = 25hf and with the minimum feature size adjusted to ﬁt the
volume fraction.
From the table 5.4 and 5.5, it is obvious that increasing the interpolation order or the amount
of elements in the mapping, also increases the calculation time drastically. Generally, increasing
the order by 1 roughly doubles the calculation time, whereas increasing the amount of total
elements in the mapping scales roughly linearly with calculation time. Increasing the interpola-
tion order only improves the results and volume fraction limit by a fraction. The results don’t
improve much beyond 1 interpolated element in the intermediate mesh, except for the volume
fractions. Since the latter isn’t always possible to ﬁx through a change of scaling, this may not
be the best of interpolations. It can therefore be argued that the best mapping is through a
2nd-order intermediate mesh, with two intermediate elements between every existing element
from the optimized coarse scale mesh.

time as 5-10 iterations with SIMP at the same resolution. Even with the coarse scale mesh, the
SIMP method requires at least 100 iterations, and a larger mesh would need even more. This
means that the projection method is more than 10 times faster.
Some additional examples with pressure loads include diﬀerent sets of boundary conditions
or dimensions. Some of these models, shown in ﬁgure 6.2, are composed from quarter plates
with symmetry conditions in order to save calculation time. Other examples, as shown in the
appendix C as ﬁgure C.1, are calculated as the full ﬁgure. The results and conﬁgurations from
these examples are summarized in table 6.3.
The general trend for the distributed loads is a deviation from the coarse scale mesh of about
10 %. However, one cases is diﬀerent. The square plate with simple supports in the corners have
a deviation of about 15%. The reason for this is that the ribs not all connect to the boundary
condition in the ﬁne mesh. The mapping function attempts to keep a spacing between the
ribs, thus deﬂecting them away from the boundary condition. The main idea of the structure
is otherwise clearly conveyed, with a singular point in every corner, where the reinforcing ribs
all connect.
Despite the few ﬂaws that persist in the structures, the results are generally close and show
the validity of the method. As slightly larger volume fractions can make a clear diﬀerence in
compliance, ﬁxing these ﬂaws will improve the results even further while creating better looking
designs. All of which can be obtained faster than with the existing methods.

7.2
Post processing
A few steps need to be included in the post processing. First and foremost, excess material,
with no strain energy, needs to be removed. Sometimes small structural parts, that aren’t
connected to either loads or boundary conditions, are created in the projection. These parts,
while not detrimental, are unnecessary and the material might even be redistributed elsewhere
to further improve the design.
7.3
Material modes
Two material modes have been proposed in this work. The plates can be deﬁned either as a
perforated structure or as a solid plate with reinforcing structure on both sides. These modes
are not always viable. Often it will make more sense to only reinforce one side or perhaps even
the middle between two solid plates. The latter can easily be implemented, whereas the former
requires a new plate element formulation to account for non-symmetrical plate layouts.
7.4
Element improvement
The elements used in this thesis are not quite optimal. While the plate element works well for
most cases, it has the unfortunate instability when a point support is applied. While it has been
improved with the 9-node Lagrange element, the solution is still suboptimal. A better solution
might be reached through a stabilization matrix, which can also enable reduced integration
in the bilinear element. Switching to a bilinear element also reduces the computational cost
of solving the system by a large margin.
Another improvement to the plate element is to
use a diﬀerent formulation. A formulation that enables mid-plane stresses for non-symmetric
composite plates could allow a greater variety of structures. One such example is a plate with
ribbed reinforcement on only one side.

7.5
Shell optimization
Eventually, this project was meant to be expanded to shell elements.
While there wasn’t
enough time to include it here, it is still an option worth exploring. The ground work for the
shell element has already been made. The homogenization based topology optimization is in
place and a shell element has already been implemented, but it becomes unstable during the
optimization process and has therefore been excluded. Stabilizing the current shell element is
an option, but as with the plate element, an improved element might be advantageous. Shell
elements can also be improved using stabilization matrices but whether this will also stabilize
the optimization process remains unknown.
7.6
Code optimization
Topology optimization is often limited by the vast calculation time that is needed for large scale
structures. Matlab specializes in vectors, so wherever possible, the loops should be converted
into a vectorization, which should improve the speed drastically. While some attempts has
been made to improve the calculation speed, there are still a few ways to properly vectorize
the deﬁnition of the global stiﬀness matrix, stresses and objectives as it is the same operations
for diﬀerent elements.
Unfortunately, some of the optimization has also come at a prize. To quickly deﬁne the global
stiﬀness matrix, a set of vectors with the coordinates and values of every entry in K must also
be deﬁned. These vectors each take up as much memory as the element stiﬀness matrices and
have to be deﬁned with double precision to work in Matlab. The problem with the memory
usage, is that it quickly can become rather overwhelming, especially when solving the ﬁne scale
mesh. One way to reduce it could be to deﬁne the indices for the sparse matrix as unsigned
integers and converting them to doubles when creating the K-matrix. Another method could be
to only deﬁne half the global stiﬀness matrix with these vectors, and exploiting the symmetry
conditions.
Using shell elements can be even worse, since shell elements aren’t necessarily similar, due to
rotations, and the same element stiﬀness matrix can’t be applied for all of them. The above
proposed vectorization might improve the calculation speed even more drastically here, but will
take some additional steps to work.
4-node elements will also be very advantageous in every aspect of optimizing the running
speed.

8
Conclusion
The homogenization based topology optimization has successfully been applied to a plate struc-
ture to develop near-optimal structures. This method succeeds in producing binary regions of
either solid or void while maintaining the near-optimality of the homogenized solution. The
proposed results are visually close to what can be expected from the SIMP-method, but require
a much lower computational time, considering the mesh size. The end compliance results for
both methods are also very similar for point loads, but the homogenization based method shows
signiﬁcantly better results with a distributed load.
Some post processing have been implemented to ensure feature size control and manufac-
turability and two diﬀerent optimization schemes have been proposed to control the volume
fraction. Additional post processing of the structure is still needed to improve the solution and
better ensure manufacturability but the results are even so generally close to the coarse scale
solution.
