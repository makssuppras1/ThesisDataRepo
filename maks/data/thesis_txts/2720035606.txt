1 – Abstract  
In industrial biotechnology the use of non-conventional yeasts (NCYs) as an alternative to the com-
mon host Saccharomyces cerevisiae has gained increasing attention due to their favorable phenotypic 
traits and potential advantages in fermentation processes. In order to efficiently engineer these 
NCYs genetic engineering tools that allow for high-throughput and cost-effective modifications 
are in demand. In light of this, the use of CRISPR/Cas9 technology is a promising approach, but 
traditional methods of plasmid-based expression of Cas9 and guide RNA(s), which require assem-
bly of a customized guide RNA expression cassette in E. coli, can be time-consuming and costly. 
This study aimed to develop and evaluate a system for in vivo assembly of CRISPR/Cas9-based 
plasmids with single-stranded DNA oligonucleotides for genetic engineering of NCYs that by-
passes these traditional assembly methods. 
This was done by designing, inserting, and testing different red fluorescent protein (RFP) expres-
sion cassettes into the single guide RNA (sgRNA) expression cassettes of CRISPR/Cas9 based plas-
mids to quickly screen and validate subsequent correct in vivo integration of sgRNA(s). This 
method has the potential to become a state-of-the-art molecular biology technique as it potentially 
allows high-throughput genetic-engineering experiments that could accelerate the development of 
NCY cell factories. 
Results showed that it was necessary to add a dedicated promoter to achieve efficient expression 
of RFP in NCYs Komagataella phaffii and Debaryomyces hansenii. However, the choice of promoter 
was found to be important as none of the plasmids designed for D. hansenii showed efficient ex-
pression of RFP when transformed. Despite not achieving successful integration of a protospacer 
into a sgRNA expression cassette through in vivo assembly, the study provided new insights on 
the preferred repair mechanisms of double stranded breaks in the DNA of NCYs. This includes the 
potential role of alternative DNA assembly mechanisms such as microhomology-mediated end 
joining and the possibility of increasing the homologous recombination frequency by deleting 
genes, which are involved in alternative repair pathways. These findings provide a foundation for 
future research to optimize in vivo assembly of CRISPR/Cas9-based plasmids for gene engineering 
in NCYs and to fully exploit the potential of these yeasts in industrial biotechnology.

2 – Resumé  
Inden for industriel bioteknologi har brugen af ikke-konventionel gær (IKG) som et alternativ til 
den almindelige vært Saccharomyces cerevisiae fået stigende opmærksomhed på grund af deres gun-
stige fænotypiske egenskaber og potentielle fordele i fermenteringsprocesser. For effektivt at kon-
struere disse IKG'er efterspørges gensplejsningsværktøjer, der giver mulighed for high-throughput 
og omkostningseffektive modifikationer. I lyset af dette er brugen af CRISPR/Cas9 teknologi en lo-
vende tilgang, men traditionelle metoder til plasmidbaseret ekspression af Cas9 og gRNA, som 
kræver samling af en tilpasset guide RNA ekspressionskassette i E. coli, hvilket kan være tidskræ-
vende og omkostningskrævende. Denne undersøgelse havde til formål at udvikle og evaluere et 
system til in vivo samling af CRISPR/Cas9-baserede plasmider med enkeltstrengede DNA-oligo-
nukleotider til gen manipulering af IKG'er, der omgår disse traditionelle samlingsmetoder. 
Dette blev gjort ved at designe, indsætte og teste forskellige rødt fluorescerende protein (RFP) ek-
spressionskassetter i single guide RNA (sgRNA) ekspressionskassetterne i CRISPR/Cas9 baserede 
plasmider for hurtigt at screene og validere efterfølgende korrekte in vivo integration af 
sgRNA(er). Denne metode har potentialet til at blive en state-of-the-art molekylærbiologiske tek-
nik, da den tillader genteknologiske eksperimenter med høj kapacitet og accelererer udviklingen af 
IKG-cellefabrikker. 
Resultaterne viste, at det var nødvendigt at tilføje en dedikeret promotor for at opnå synlig ek-
spression af RFP i IKG’erne Komagataella phaffii og Debaryomyces hansenii. Valget af promotor viste 
sig imidlertid at være vigtigt, da ingen af plasmiderne designet til D. hansenii viste effektiv ek-
spression af RFP ved transformation. På trods af, at der ikke blev opnået vellykket integration af 
en protospacer i en sgRNA-ekspressionskassette gennem in vivo-samling, gav undersøgelserne ny 
indsigt i de foretrukne reparationsmekanismer for dobbeltstrengede brud i DNA'et af IKG'er. 
Dette inkluderer den potentielle rolle af alternative DNA-samlingsmekanismer såsom mikrohomo-
logi-medieret endesammenføjning og muligheden for at øge frekvensen af homologisk rekombina-
tion ved at slette gener involveret i alternative reparationsveje. Disse resultater danner grundlag 
for fremtidig forskning i at udvikle og optimere in vivo samling af CRISPR/Cas9-baserede plasmi-
der til genmanipulation i IKG'er og fuldt ud at udnytte potentialet af disse gærarter i industriel 
bioteknologi.

8 – Introduction  
Non-conventional yeasts (NCYs) are promising alternatives for Saccharomyces cerevisiae, a common 
host used in industrial biotechnology [1]. NCYs offer favorable phenotypic traits that are well 
suited for several different industrial bioprocesses and can provide fermentation advantages over 
S. cerevisiae.  
For example, NCY Debaryomyces hansenii can grow in media containing high concentrations of salt, 
so industrially growing this yeast does not require the use of pure water sources [1], [2]. On the 
other hand, NCY Komagataella phaffii (formerly known as Pichia pastoris) can grow using methanol 
as its sole carbon source [3], which is a promising green feedstock for producing fuels and chemi-
cals because it is inexpensive, clean, environmentally friendly, and easily prepared [4], [5]. Hence, 
both yeasts are increasingly considered and/or applied as industrial hosts in the field of so-called 
green biotechnology [2]. 
Recently, advancements of molecular tools, design and adaptation for metabolic engineering have 
been made for the NCYs K. phaffii and D. hansenii [6], [7]. For instance, efficient gene-targeting and 
gene-editing technologies have been developed based on strains that are deficient in non-homolo-
gous end-joining (NHEJ) and/or by CRISPR (Clustered Regularly Interspaced Short Palindromic 
Repeats) technologies [6], [8], [9]. However, the development of cell factories that can provide com-
mercially feasible yields requires multiple rounds of strain construction [10]. To accelerate, auto-
mate, and significantly cut the cost associated with developing NCY cell factories, efficient genetic-
engineering tools that can be used as the basis for performing high-throughput genetic-engineer-
ing experiments are in demand [11].  
As of today, most of the available genetic-engineering tools require the assembly of DNA con-
structs using either Escherichia coli based cloning strategies or in vitro assembly by fusion-PCR re-
actions [6], [12]. These DNA assembly processes often prove to be bottlenecks in strain construc-
tion when performing high-throughput experiments because of time requirements, accumulative 
costs, low efficiency of multipart assembly, and need of optimization.   
The aforementioned DNA assembly techniques can however be bypassed in the yeast Saccharomy-
ces cerevisiae. In 1987, Kunes et al. observed that a single DNA fragment could be efficiently 
==PAGE:14==

inserted into a linearized plasmid through homologous recombination (HR) in yeast [13]. In vivo 
DNA assembly can be performed in S. cerevisiae by exploiting that PCR fragments containing short 
homologous sequence overlaps, typically around 30−50 bps, can be accurately fused by HR. Later, 
it was shown that the same method could be used to combine different DNA fragments into more 
complex plasmids or to build multipart gene-targeting substrates that contained entire pathways 
[14].  
It has recently been proved that DNA fragments also can be stitched seamlessly together in vivo in 
K. phaffii and D. hansenii either via exploiting PCR fragments or short single-stranded DNA 
(ssDNA) oligonucleotides containing short homologous sequence overlaps to form desired DNA 
constructs without the need for E. coli-based cloning (Mortensen, Strucko, Christensen, un-
published). This potentially allows fast and efficient “one-pot” genetic modification(s) of NCYs. 
But to fully exploit in vivo recombination in NCY genetic-engineering, including high-throughput 
experiments, inexpensive, simple, and robust methods allowing DNA fragments to be orderly 
stitched together in NCYs are necessary. 
9 – Aim of study  
The overall aim of this project is to further develop and optimize oligonucleotide-mediated gene 
engineering methods in NCYs as a state-of-the-art molecular biology technique.  
Specifically, new tools for easy selection/screening of strains that have plausibly undergone de-
sired modifications will be designed and tested. This will be achieved by constructing and opti-
mizing CRISPR-based plasmids with a red fluorescent protein (RFP) expression cassette integrated 
to quickly screen and validate correct in vivo integration of single-guide RNA(s).  
Eventually, the newly developed methodologies will be tested for their potential use in medium- 
to high throughput scale in NCYs strain engineering experiments.  
10 – Experimental strategy and work 
The experimental strategy and work conducted during this study aimed to further develop and 
optimize oligonucleotide-mediated gene engineering methods in NCYs as a state-of-the-art molec-
ular biology technique. The experimental strategy is simplistically described below.

1) Constructing and optimizing CRISPR/Cas9-based plasmids with a red fluorescent pro-
tein (RFP) expression cassette  
PCR amplification and gel verification were used to produce multiple PCR products con-
taining an RFP expression cassette which were then fused into a CRISPR/Cas9-based plas-
mid backbone using USER cloning. Colony PCR and restriction digestion were performed 
to ensure correct insertion of the PCR fragments. Subsequently, correctly assembled plas-
mids were then sequenced to confirm that they were correctly aligned in the desired orien-
tation and to verify the sequence of the fragments. Different designs of the new CRISPR-
based plasmids were made for easy selection and screening of NCY strains that have un-
dergone desired genetic modifications.  
 
2) Test of in vivo assembly of nourseothricin N-acetyl transferase gene 
In order minimize the amount of false positive transformants obtained after in vivo assem-
bly of CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids and to construct NCY vectors that, when assembled in vivo, 
are E. coli-sequence free (ESF), different designs were made for plasmids with a disrupted 
nourseothricin (NTC) resistance marker. These plasmids were used to test if it was possible 
to correctly assemble the NTC resistance marker in a linearized CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid di-
gested with the restriction enzyme MssI (see recognition sequence in figure 42 in Appendix 
G) without a ssDNA oligo and remove the bacterial origin of replication (ori) and the gene 
encoding the ampicillin resistance protein beta-lactamase (bla). PCR amplification was 
used to create a fragment containing an MssI site, a bacterial ori, a bla-sequence, another 
MssI cutsite, and a portion of a CTG-optimized version of the gene encoding NTC re-
sistance protein, the nourseothricin N-acetyl transferase (NAT) gene. This fragment was 
inserted into the NAT gene sequence of CRISPR-based plasmids via USER cloning, thus 
disrupting the NAT gene. A schematic representation can be seen in section 12.11 - Figure 
8. The assembled plasmids were sequenced to confirm the disruption of the NAT gene and 
subsequently cut with MssI to remove the ori and bla sequences. The resulting linear plas-
mids were then purified and introduced into NHEJ-deficient K. phaffii and D. hansenii 
strains via electroporation and tested for growth on nourseothricin-supplemented media.

Growth indicates successful repair of the NAT gene through in vivo assembly using the 
NAT sequence overlap. 
 
3) Test of oligonucleotide-mediated gene engineering method 
After constructing CRISPR/Cas9-based plasmids with different RFP expression cassettes, 
the plasmids were then introduced into wildtype K. phaffii and D. hansenii strains and 
tested for growth on nourseothricin-supplemented media. The transformants were visually 
checked for red fluorescence under a UV light with a suitable light source and filter at-
tached, and the number of transformants was counted manually. Plasmids with functional 
RFP expression cassettes were then PCR amplified to yield two linear fragments of the en-
tire plasmid with sequence overlaps in the NAT gene and in the RFP expression cassette. 
The fragments were cut with MssI to remove the RFP expression cassette and gel purified 
before being electroporated into NHEJ-deficient K. phaffii strains with: a 90-base pair DNA 
oligo, containing a sequence overlap of the tRNA in the RFP cassette, a 20 nucleotide proto-
spacer sequence and a sequence overlap of the trans-activating crispr RNA (tracrRNA), and 
a repair template, where appropriate. The transformants were tested for growth on nourse-
othricin-supplemented media. Loss of red fluorescence indicates integration of the proto-

11 – Background theory  
11.1 – Cell factories  
The overuse of fossil fuels has led to the need for sustainable chemical production using renewable 
resources. One solution is using engineered microbes, known as microbial cell factories, to make 
desired compounds from renewable carbon sources. 
Cell factories have demonstrated the potential for producing diverse, important chemicals sustain-
ably and cost-effectively without depleting non-renewable resources. [15]. A subfield of biotech-
nology called industrial microbiology uses microbial sciences to produce industrial products in 
large quantities, frequently employing microbial cell factories [16]. The biotechnology industry 
started growing rapidly in the mid-1970s driven by the development of genetic engineering and 
methodologies for DNA manipulation which allowed companies to increase the production yields 
of their cell factories [16], [17]. However, many of the natural producer organisms were not suited 
for bioproduction due to slow growth rates, low yields, sensitivity to environmental changes, and 
lack of effective genetic modification tools [18]. 
To solve these problems, various synthetic pathways for natural product synthesis have been 
moved into microbial chassis cells, like the heterologous expression of human insulin in S. cere-
visiae [19], [20]. A microbial chassis serves as a platform cell for the production of various com-
pounds or enzymes, using the analogy of a car's metal frame and referring to the cell as the funda-
mental building block onto which genetic assemblies of interest are attached [21]–[23]. 
Few model species, like E. coli, Bacillus subtilis, Aspergillus oryzae and S. cerevisiae have been used 
extensively as microbial chassis [1]. This is partially due to the fact that they all, apart from E. coli, 
are generally recognized as safe (GRAS) organisms and that they grow very well in environments 
often associated with conventional biotechnological processes [19], [24], [25]. These model microor-
ganisms are all advantageous as chassis cells because they can be engineered using a range of ef-
fective synthetic biology methods, they grow relatively quick, and their genomes and metabolic 
networks have been extensively explored [15]. 
==PAGE:18==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702 
Master thesis 
Page 17 of 120 
 
However, there are still significant difficulties with the manufacturing methods, often as a result of 
host organism constraints. S. cerevisiae and E. coli are two examples of this, as they cannot handle 
certain environmental conditions. They require significant microbial engineering work to grow 
reasonably well under the demanding settings in industrial biomanufacturing (e.g., to boost their 
capacity to utilize carbon sources other than glucose or develop thermo-, pH- or osmotolerance) 
[26], [27]. Similar issues arise when Aspergillus grows filamentously in bioreactors or industrial 
tanks, which strongly impacts productivities of fungal bioprocesses, making the process more fas-
tidious and expensive [28]. Costly feedstocks, excessive energy and water consumption, productiv-
ity loss due to contamination, and expenses associated with downstream separation can all be 
added to the list of issues that need to be resolved [29]. 
Choosing the right microbial host, the requirement for robust genetic control systems, removing 
metabolic bottlenecks, and developing strategies to increase yields and productivities are some of 
the issues faced when building cell factories. Despite all of these requirements, cell factories are an-
ticipated to one day replace our current methods of chemical synthesis [30].  
11.2 – Yeasts as cell factories  
Because the ascomycetes yeast S. cerevisiae has physiological benefits like quick growth and toler-
ance to somewhat harsh culture conditions, which make fermentation processes more cost-effec-
tive, it has become one of the most widely used cell factories [31], [32]. Yeasts like S. cerevisiae can 
carry out posttranslational modifications and synthesize complex compounds, such as pharmaceu-
ticals, that cannot be produced in prokaryotic cells [33], [34]. Additionally, yeasts are not suscepti-
bility to infectious agents like bacteriophages [35]. But the most significant benefit of using this 
yeast as a chassis is the useful and advanced genetic engineering toolbox that has been developed 
for S. cerevisiae [36].  
The majority of these tools are based on shuttle vectors, which may be propagated in both yeast 
and bacteria. Examples of these are the yeast integrative plasmids,  which are intended to be line-
arized and then integrated into the genome by homologous recombination; the yeast episomal 
plasmids, which contain the yeast origin of replication sequence known as 2µm; and the yeast cen-
tromeric plasmids, which can be maintained as an additional chromosome [37]. However, it has 
==PAGE:19==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702 
Master thesis 
Page 18 of 120 
 
been noted that shuttle vectors are unstable, which means that uneven segregations may lose them 
during cell division or by, for instance, unfavorable random integrations into the genome [38], [39]. 
As a result, integrative plasmids are favored over episomal plasmids because, once linearized, they 
enable the performance of genomic integrations. Although genomic integrations are more stable 
than episomal plasmids, more than one copy of the desired gene must often be integrated to 
achieve the plasmid-based expression levels [40]. 
S. cerevisiae is a highly characterized microorganism in terms of biology, genetic engineering tech-
niques, and fermentation processes; however, because of the frequent correlation between its pro-
duction processes and low protein secretion yields, its commercial potential as a cell factory has 
been reduced. Furthermore, the use of S. cerevisiae in the production of therapeutic glycoproteins 
for human use is limited due to its glycosylation machinery producing proteins with a short half-
life and potential for causing immunogenic reactions. [41].  
 
11.2.1 – Non-conventional yeasts as cell factories  
The use of yeast cell factories is not limited to S. cerevisiae. Biotechnologists have categorized thou-
sands of yeast species as non-conventional yeasts; many of these are currently used to produce val-
uable products and have the potential to serve as excellent cell factories. In terms of physiology, 
product needs, and the preferred metabolic pathway, many NCYs can even outperform S. cere-
visiae [42], [43]. The increasing availability of high-quality NCY genome sequences [44]–[46], effec-
tive vectors and transformation methods [47], [48] and emerging synthetic tools [7], [49]–[51] is ac-
celerating the characterization of NCYs and it enables the rewiring and engineering of NCYs for 
production purposes. Two industrially relevant cell factory candidates are the NCYs Komagataella 
phaffii and Debaryomyces hansenii. The genomes of these NCYs have been sequenced and a range of 
genetic tools is currently available [6], [45], [46], [52].

11.3 - Komagataella phaffii 
11.3.1 – K. phaffii as a cell factory  
The methylotrophic yeast K. phaffii (formerly known as Pichia pastoris) is currently regarded as one 
of the most promising hosts for recombinant protein production (RPP) and metabolites as it can 
carry out eukaryotic post-translational modification including glycosylation, disulfide bonds, and 
proteolytic modifications [53], and it can effectively secrete the product in the extracellular me-
dium [53]. 
K. phaffii has attracted a lot of interest from the scientific community since it was first used as a cell 
factory in 1980. It possesses a GRAS status granted by the U.S Food and Drug Administration, and 
its genome has been completely sequenced [54], making it suitable for industrial production. In ad-
dition to its ease of cultivation and ability to grow to high cell densities on substrates like glucose, 
glycerol, and methanol [55], [56], it has a glycosylation mechanism that can be modified to create 
entirely humanized proteins, and which does not result in hyperglycosylated proteins, in contrast 
to S. cerevisiae[57]. K. phaffii also exhibits a respiratory metabolism, which is a characteristic of 
Crabtree-negative yeast. As a result, harmful fermentation byproducts like ethanol will not build 
up at high glucose concentrations. K. phaffii can achieve extremely high cell densities in compari-
son to Crabtree-positive S. cerevisiae because of this. 
The ability of K. phaffii to secrete large amounts of proteins, either intracellularly or extracellularly, 
is another crucial characteristic [58]–[61]. Furthermore, since it only secretes small amounts of its 
native proteins, its extracellularly secreted proteins are relatively pure [62], [63]. Overexpression of 
genes like Hac1p [64] and PDI [65] are genetic alterations that can improve K. phaffii's ability to se-
crete proteins. Table 1 provides a comparison of relevant properties between S. cerevisiae and K. 
phaffii. 
To sum up, K. phaffii is an excellent cell factory for recombinant production of therapeutically and 
industrially relevant proteins. However, even though multiple tools for effectively regulating re-
combinant expression have been developed, there are no efficient genetic-engineering tools availa-
ble for high-throughput experiments, like genome-wide screens or constructing a K. phaffii deletion 
collection.

11.4 – Debaryomyces hansenii 
11.4.1 – D. hansenii as a cell factory 
Xerotolerant and halophilic D. hansenii is a non-conventional hemiascomycetous yeast that can sur-
vive in environments with high osmotic pressure, high salinity, and low water activity [1]. In me-
dia containing up to 4M NaCl, D. hansenii will grow and perform well in a variety of temperatures 
(20-35°C) and pH values (3-10) [1]. Although it is a yeast that is frequently found on the surfaces of 
fermented foods such different cheeses and cured meat, it was first isolated from seawater [66], 
[67]. When exposed to high osmotic stress D. hansenii is capable of accumulating large amounts of 
intracellular glycerol, enabling it to float in the surface of an aqueous solution. D. hansenii is an ole-
aginous yeast that is being used in biotechnology to manufacture a number of products, including 
different fatty acids, xylitol, flavenoids, and different mycocins useful against Candida, in addition 
to its use in food products [49].  
Due to its extremophilic features, including its capacity to perform effectively under high salt con-
centrations and high osmotic pressure, D. hansenii has received significant recognition for its bio-
technological potential throughout the years. [49], [68]. Its extremophilic features make it possible

11.5 – Construction and genetic engineering of yeast cell factories 
The ability to manipulate DNA and introduce heterologous DNA in a host organism is essential 
for recombinant production of proteins and other metabolites. Additionally, genetic engineering is 
frequently needed to increase a cell factory's production. Advancements in genetic tools have 
greatly accelerated the development of cell factories, allowing for the genetic engineering of previ-
ously difficult-to-manipulate organisms, including NCYs.  
11.5.1 – USER cloning for fusion of multiple PCR fragments 
In molecular biology, recombinant plasmids are crucial tools and are widely employed as vectors. 
Recombinant plasmids can be created in a variety of techniques, such as Gibson assembly, Golden 
Gate assembly, uracil-specific excision reagent (USER) cloning/fusion, or by using restriction en-
zymes and ligases. USER cloning is a straightforward and effective technique for cloning one or 
more pieces of DNA into a backbone by using unique USER compatible complementary primer 
overlaps which produce scarless plasmids [83].  
An overview of USER cloning is shown in Figure 1A. The technique relies on the plasmid back-
bone having a USER cassette consisting of a restriction site flanked by two nicking sites. Direc-
tional bases are located between the restriction and nicking sites to guarantee that the fragment(s) 
are unidirectionally inserted into the backbone. The plasmid is linearized by digesting it with a re-
striction enzyme such as AsiSI or PacI and a nicking enzyme such as Nb.BsmI or Nt.BbvCI (see 
Figure 1B), which generates USER compatible 3' overhangs.  
The fragment(s) are amplified using primers with USER tails, which consist of overhangs contain-
ing a single uracil (U) instead of thymine approximately 6-15 nucleotides from the 5’ end matching 
==PAGE:24==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702 
Master thesis 
Page 23 of 120 
 
either end of the overhangs of the USER cassette in the vector. The PCR fragment(s) and the linear-
ized backbone are treated with USERTM enzyme mix (New England BioLabs). This mixture in-
cludes an uracil DNA glycosidase that catalyzes uracil excision and a DNA glycosylase-lyase endo 
VIII that breaks the phosphodiester backbone at the 3' and 5' of the sites where uracil was excised, 
resulting in the USER compatible overhangs. Likewise, by utilizing primers with complementary 
USER tails, several fragments may be fused together and cloned into a plasmid (Figure 1C). After 
the fragment(s) and/or backbone's complimentary overhangs hybridize, the product can be trans-
formed into competent E. coli cells without prior ligation [84].

11.5.2 – Molecular biology of non-conventional yeasts 
Gene expression in NCYs is possible using episomal vectors or by using methods for genome inte-
gration carried out by the host's DNA repair system [1], [86]. One of the challenges with genet-
ically engineering D. hansenii has been to introduce exogenous DNA and keep it on a vector for ex-
pression. To maintain a vector for episomal expression in non-conventional yeasts, an autono-
mously replicating sequence (ARS) must be included in the vector for the cells to recognize and 
replicate it. Typically, a given ARS only functions in a few yeast species because ARSs usually 
have different sequences to initiate function. Species-specific ARS sequences have been identified 
and used to construct episomal vectors for both K. phaffii and D. hansenii [47], [79]. Recently, how-
ever, an ARS that can function in a broad range of yeast species was identified and named panARS 
[87]. This has enabled the development of multi-species compatible stable plasmids, and advance-
ments have been made in introduction of exogenous DNA, thus breaking down some of the barri-
ers that prevented the development of molecular biological tools for genetic approaches to explore 
D. hansenii and unlock its biotechnological potential [50], [77], [78]. 
In K. phaffii and D. hansenii, it is preferable to express heterologous genes by integrating them into 
the genome rather than using plasmids, as plasmids are not stably inherited and require selective 
pressure to be maintained. One way of doing genomic integration of a gene is by gene conversion 
through homology-directed repair (HDR). In this process, it is advantageous to create a double-
stranded break (DSB) at the integration site as it significantly increases the rate of donor amplicon 
integration by HDR [88]. There are several ways to create DSBs in the genome, such as using the 
CRISPR/Cas system, zinc-finger nucleases, transcription activator nucleases, also known as 
TALENs, or meganucleases [89], [90]. 
When DSBs are created in cells, they must be repaired for the cell to survive. The way yeast cells 
repair DNA damage varies, but the two main repair pathways for DSBs are HDR and NHEJ. Many 
genetic engineering methods relies on HDR as it allows for targeted insertion of heterologous 
genes, promoters, or DNA amplicons as well as more controlled deletions and nucleotide swap 
mutations. NHEJ can also be used to knock out genes as it often leads to small insertions or dele-
tions (indels) of nucleotides, but it is usually unfavored when genetic engineering efforts are made 
as the repair pathway can lead to false positives when trying to insert heterogeneous DNA. 
==PAGE:27==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702 
Master thesis 
Page 26 of 120 
 
NHEJ is the predominant DSB repair mechanism in many NCYs, including K. phaffii and D. han-
senii [91]. As a result, it is common practice to knock out the NHEJ pathway in order to encourage 
DSB repair by HDR and thereby enable more effective genomic integrations. This is routinely done 
by knocking out the ku70 gene or the dnl4 gene [92]. The Ku70 protein participates in the 
Ku70/Ku80 complex, which recognizes and binds to the loose DNA ends after the induction of a 
DSB, early in the NHEJ process, while the DNA ligase IV (Dnl4p) participates in the final step of 
the NHEJ pathway [92], [93]. Recent reports have showed that up to 100% homologous recombina-
tion frequencies can be achieved when the ku70 gene is knocked out in D. hansenii and similar re-
sults have been achieved when knocking out either the ku70 or the dnl4 gene in K. phaffii, thus sig-
nificantly improving HR-based integrations [6], [93], [94]. 
11.5.3 – CRISPR/Cas9 - an efficient tool for targeted genetic engineering 
The recent advent of CRISPR/Cas technology has revolutionized genome engineering, with 
CRISPR-associated protein 9 (Cas9) being the most commonly used technique. Cas9 is an enzyme 
that can bind to a single guide RNA (sgRNA) and efficiently induce DSBs almost anywhere in a 
genome, allowing specific genetic changes to be made without any scars [8]. One advantage of us-
ing CRISPR/Cas9 for efficient cutting the DNA of yeasts is that it often eliminates the need to inte-
grate selection markers in NHEJ-deficient strains. This is because the induced DNA DSB can serve 
as a negative selection mechanism. The application of the CRISPR/Cas9 system is illustrated in Fig-
ure 2.

11.6 – Transformation through electroporation  
Transformation is a technique used in molecular and genetic biology that involves introducing ex-
ogenous DNA into a cell, resulting in a genetic alteration. There are several methods for transform-
ing intact cells, including various types of yeasts, and these methods have been shown to be effec-
tive [100]. One of the most effective methods for transforming NCY cells with exogenous DNA is 
transformation through electroporation [100]. In this method, the yeast cells are suspended in a so-
lution containing the DNA of interest and then placed between electrodes. A high-voltage electric 
pulse is then applied to the cells, causing temporary pores to form in the cell membranes. This al-
lows the DNA to enter the cells, where it can then be integrated into the genome or maintained on 
a plasmid. Electroporation is a fast and efficient method for transforming NCY cells, and it is com-
monly used in genetic engineering and other applications involving yeast. 
In order to successfully perform electroporation, it is important to consider the electrical voltage 
level and the timing of the pulse. The electroporation process can be stressful for cells, so finding 
the right balance of voltage and time is crucial to avoid cell death while still achieving the desired 
effect of increased cell permeability [101]. Cregg, J. M., & Russell, K. A. (1998) & Minhas et al. (2009 
& 2019) have published protocols for electrotransforming K. phaffii & D. hansenii, respectively, that 
==PAGE:31==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702 
Master thesis 
Page 30 of 120 
 
involve using a sodium phosphate buffer containing dithiothreitol (DTT) to make the cells compe-
tent, and applying an electrical field of 2 kV and 2.3 kV, respectively, while using sorbitol or su-
crose as an osmotic stabilizer [80], [102], [103]. 
Electroporation is commonly used to introduce plasmid DNA into cells, but it can also be used to 
deliver other types of molecules, such as RNA, proteins, carbohydrates, and small molecules. In 
general, the highest transformation efficiencies are achieved when using supercoiled plasmids, alt-
hough there are some exceptions. For example, Candida, Pichia, and Tetrahymena are usually trans-
formed more efficiently with linear rather than supercoiled plasmids. Additionally, plasmids that 
will integrate into the host genome are typically most efficient when delivered in their linear form 
[103]. 
11.7 – In vivo assembly of DNA 
Conventional methods for genomic alterations often involve multiple steps and can be cumber-
some and time-consuming [6], [12], [104]. These methods typically involve constructing circular 
plasmids in vitro using ligation or DNA assembly, transforming them into Escherichia coli, multi-
plying the target DNA, linearizing the circular DNA, and finally, transforming it into the organism 
where the genomic alterations are to be made (Figure 4A). This makes it time-consuming to per-
form genetic engineering in non-conventional yeasts like K. phaffii and D. hansenii.

In vivo assembly of DNA in yeast is a method of constructing and modifying DNA in living yeast 
cells. This process involves introducing individual DNA fragments into yeast cells and allowing 
them to assemble and replicate inside the cells (Figure 4B). This allows for the creation of larger 
and more complex DNA constructs than is possible with traditional methods of DNA cloning and 
construction [14], [105]. 
The process of in vivo assembly of DNA in yeast begins by ordering or amplifying the individual 
DNA fragments that will be used in the assembly. Most in vivo assembly methods really on PCR 
fragments containing short homologous sequence overlaps, typically around 30−50 bps, to accu-
rately be fused by HR [14]. These fragments are then introduced into yeast cells using a technique 
called electroporation or by chemical transformation, which creates temporary pores in the cell 
membrane to allow the DNA to enter the cell [103], [106]. Once inside the yeast cells, the DNA 
fragments are able to assemble via HDR and replicate, resulting in the creation of a new, larger 
DNA construct. 
In vivo assembly of DNA in yeast offers a number of advantages over traditional methods of DNA 
construction. Because yeast cells have a higher capacity for DNA replication and modification than 
bacterial cells, they are able to support the creation of larger and more complex DNA constructs. 
Additionally, in vivo assembly allows for the integration of multiple DNA fragments into a single 
construct, which can be difficult to achieve with other methods. This can be especially useful for 
creating large and complex gene libraries, or for combining multiple genetic modifications into a 
single organism [105]. 
In addition to these advantages, yeast-based DNA assembly is also a relatively quick and easy pro-
cess where transformants can be obtained after 4 days compared to 7 days using conventional 
cloning methods. This makes it a highly efficient method for constructing and modifying DNA. In 
vivo assembly of DNA in yeast eliminates the need for in vitro DNA assembly and cloning steps 
and does not require the use of type II restriction enzymes or DNA assembly kits. This makes it a 
more affordable option for lower-income laboratory settings or for high-throughput engineering, 
where the cost of these tools can be a significant expense.

In vivo assembly of DNA has recently been established in K. phaffii where Nishi et al. (2022) ac-
complished one-step in vivo assembly of multiple DNA fragments and genomic integration in a 
NHEJ-deficient K. phaffii strain [7]. The yeast assembly method has to my knowledge not been doc-
umented in published work in D. hansenii to date. However, a recent study by Strucko et al. 
demonstrated that 90-nt ssDNA oligonucleotides were sufficient for direct repair of DNA breaks 
induced by sgRNA-Cas9, resulting in precise point mutations and single/double gene deletions 
reaching 100% efficiencies in a NHEJ-deficient D. hansenii strain [6].  
This study aims to investigate if DNA fragments can be stitched seamlessly together in-vivo in K. 
phaffii and D. hansenii via short ssDNA oligonucleotides to form desired DNA constructs without 
the need for E. coli-based cloning. Compared to the methodology developed by Nishi et al. (2022) 
this approach would have the advantage of requiring less DNA oligonucleotides (primers) to form 
a desired DNA construct as ssDNA oligonucleotides will be used instead of PCR amplified ampli-
cons to connect DNA fragments. This potentially allows fast and efficient “one-pot” genetic modi-
fication(s) of non-conventional yeasts and make high-throughput experiments, inexpensive and 
simple. 
11.8 – High-throughput engineering 
High-throughput engineering is a powerful tool that allows scientists to rapidly analyze and ma-
nipulate large numbers of yeast cells at once. This approach has revolutionized the way that re-
searchers study yeast and has led to numerous discoveries and breakthroughs in a variety of fields 
[107]. 
In the field of biotechnology, high-throughput engineering has been used to optimize yeast strains 
to produce biofuels, pharmaceuticals, and other valuable products [26], [108]. By using automated 
techniques to screen thousands of yeast strains at once, scientists can identify the optimal condi-
tions for yeast growth and metabolism, leading to increased efficiency and productivity [109]. 
High-throughput engineering has also been applied to the study of yeast genetics and genomics. 
By analyzing the DNA of large numbers of yeast cells simultaneously, researchers can identify ge-
netic mutations and variations that may be responsible for specific traits or behaviors [110]. This 
==PAGE:34==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702

has led to a better understanding of how yeast cells function and how they can be manipulated for 
various applications. 
Recent advancements in genome editing methods such as CRISPR/Cas and high-throughput omics 
tools have made it increasingly possible to strategically design yeast cell factories for synthetic bi-
ology applications. This includes multiplex genome engineering, which is a rapidly expanding 
field. [107]. As a result of the ability to construct strategically designed yeast cell factories, it is now 
possible to simultaneously express multiple enzymes and integrate multiple loci into the genome 
of NCYs, which can greatly accelerate efforts in metabolic engineering and strain development 
[40], [99]. Examples of single and multiplex genome modifications in the yeast S. cerevisiae include 
methodologies like:  
- 
Donor DNA delivery (Figure 5A), which can either be plasmid-based or oligo-based like in 
the yeast oligo-mediated genome engineering method [111], where small homologous oli-
gonucleotides are used to hijack the native recombination system of S. cerevisiae to intro-
duce genome modifications in an iterative manner. 
- 
CRISPR/Cas9 mediated genome editing (Figure 5B), like the CrEdit methodology, where 
Multiple sgRNAs can be used to target specific regions in the yeast genome, and the 
CRISPR/Cas system can be used in combination with these sgRNAs to create DSBs which 
significantly increases the efficiency of homologous integration making it possible to edit 
and manipulate genomic DNA [112]. 
- 
Pre-placed gates methods (Figure 5C) like the gene amplification system CASCADE [40] 
which involves inserting synthetic sequences into the genome in advance that serve as en-
try points or "gates" for the integration of new genes. These gates can be used in combina-
tion with either CRISPR/Cas9 or meganucleases to create DSBs in the gates which signifi-
cantly increases integration efficiency. This also allows fine-tuning of gene expression by 
controlling the copy numbers of the genes of interest.

Multiplex genome editing in yeasts has typically been studied in the model species S. cerevisiae, 
however, these methods are increasingly being applied to NCY species as well [6], [7], [113]–[117].  
While genome-scale multiplex engineering has the potential to generate NCY strains with a wide 
range of diversity, the lack of standardized, effective automation tools for the design, creation, and 
screening of genomic libraries remains as a major bottleneck to the widespread use of this ap-
proach [118], [119]. 
To address this bottleneck in high throughput genetic engineering of NCYs, new tools for easy 
screening and selection of strains that have plausibly undergone desired CRISPR/Cas mediated ge-
netic modifications will be constructed in this project. It will be achieved by constructing and opti-
mizing CRISPR-based plasmids for genome-scale engineering of K. phaffii and D. hansenii with an 
RFP expression cassette integrated to quickly screen and validate correct in vivo integration of sin-
gle-guide RNA expression cassettes.

12.3 – Cultivation media 
After USER fusion cloning of various PCR products, the cloned E. coli were grown on selective lys-
ogeny broth (LB) plates (5 g/L Bacto Yeast extract, 10 g/L Bacto Tryptone, 10 g/L NaCl, 20 g/L Bacto 
Agar, 100 mg/L ampicillin, Milli-Q water) at 37°C over night (O/N). Afterwards cells were stored 
at 4°C. E. coli DH5α strains harboring cloned plasmids were grown in liquid LB medium supple-
mented with ampicillin (10 g/L tryptone, 5 g/L yeast extract, 10 g/L NaCl, and 120 mg/L ampicil-
lin). 
Yeast extract peptone dextrose (YPD) medium (10 g/L Bacto yeast extract, 20 g/L Bacto peptone, 
2% D-(+)-Glucose (w/v), Milli-Q water) was used for general K. phaffii and D. hansenii cultivation 
and storage. YPD agar plates (YPD medium with 20 g/L Bacto Agar and 100mg/L nourseothricin 
(NTC) added where appropriate) were used for general streaking out of strains or for selecting 
transformants. YPD_sorbitol (YPD supplemented with 182 g/L sorbitol) was used for transfor-
mation of K. phaffii and D. hansenii.  
12.4 – Genomic DNA extraction from K. phaffii & D. hansenii strains 
The NCY cells were mechanically disrupted using small glass beads and a breaking buffer in a 2 
mL tube. A 10 µL loop with K. phaffii or D. hansenii biomass was added to the tube, along with 200 
µL small glass beads, 400 µL breaking buffer, and 100 µL 1M lithium acetate (LiAc). The tube was 
then shaken at high-speed using a FP120 FastPrep machine to facilitate lysis of the cells. The lysate 
was centrifuged to pellet the cell debris and other insoluble material. 150 µL of the supernatant 
was transferred to 1.5 microcentrifuge tubes. Genomic DNA was precipitated from the superna-
tant by the addition of 15 µL 5M NaCl and 400 µL ice-cold 96% ethanol, followed by vortexing. 
The mixture was then centrifuged to pellet the genomic DNA. The pellet was resuspended in 400 
µL ice-cold ethanol and centrifuged again to remove any remaining contaminants. The genomic 
DNA was finally resuspended in 200 µL milliQ water to create a solution suitable for downstream 
applications.

12.5 – PCR 
To amplify PCR fragments, a standard polymerase chain reaction (PCR) procedure was followed. 
The fragments were then combined and inserted into plasmids or co-electroporated with oligoes 
into the non-conventional yeasts D. hansenii and K. phaffii. A list of all PCR products, along with 
the primers and templates used, can be found in Appendix C. To prepare the PCR mixture, a PCR 
tube was filled to a total volume of 50 μl with 20 μl of Milli-Q water, 25 μl of Phusion U PCR Mas-
ter Mix (ThermoFisher), 2 μl of a 10 μM forward primer, 2 μl of a 10 μM reverse primer, and 1 μl 
of DNA template. 
Before placing the PCR vial(s) in the Bio-Rad T100 Thermal Cycler, they were spun briefly. The 
samples were run through the following program, with a sample volume of 50 μl and a lid temper-
ature of 105°C: 
- 
5-minute denaturation step at 98°C,  
- 
34 cycles of  
o 30 seconds of denaturation at 98°C,  
o 30 seconds of annealing at 59°C (or depending on Tm of primers),  
o 72°C for appropriate amount of time of extension (15-30 seconds per kb).  
- 
After the 34 cycles, a final extension step was performed at 72°C for 5 minutes.  
- 
The samples were then stored at 12°C until they were collected. 
The PCR products were run on a 1% agarose gel by electrophoresis for 20 minutes at 100 V to ver-
ify band size.

for sequencing or not. The cell suspensions were then disrupted in a PCR cycler using a program

12.9 – DNA purification  
To purify the PCR products, the commercially available NucleoSpin Gel and PCR Clean-up Kit 
(MACHEREY-NAGEL) was used. This kit was used to purify DNA from both the PCR mixture 
and the gel bands. If the template used in the PCR was genomic DNA, the PCR product was puri-
fied directly from the PCR mixture. However, if the template was a plasmid, 1 µl of DpnI re-
striction enzyme (DTU Bioengineering) was added to the PCR mixture and allowed to digest for 1 
hour at 37°C before 4µl of the mixture was dyed and loaded into a gel for gel electrophoresis. If 
only a single band was observed, the PCR fragment was column purified, but if multiple bands 
were observed, the PCR fragment was gel purified. When purifying from agarose gel, the gel was 
placed on a UV Safe ImagerTM (Invitrogen) and the gel bands were manually cut and placed into 
2 ml Eppendorf tubes. The purification kit instructions were followed, with some exceptions. For 
example, when column purifying directly from the PCR mixture, the agarose gel melting step was 
not necessary. The collection tubes with washed columns ready for elution were placed for 5 
minutes with the lids open in a heatblock set to 60°C. After the addition of demineralized H2O for 
eluting the DNA, the collection tubes with columns were left for an additional 5 minutes with the 
lids closed before collection by centrifugation for 1 minute at 11.000 G.  
12.10 – Propagation of plasmids 
To propagate plasmids, 4 mL of liquid LB media supplemented with ampicillin containing an E. 
coli strain harboring a plasmid was incubated O/N at 37°C and 180 rpm. The plasmids were then 
purified from the culture using the GenElute™ Plasmid Miniprep Kit (Sigma-Aldrich) according to 
the manufacturer's protocol. At the final elution step, half the volume of demineralized H2O (50 µl) 
was added to the column and incubated for 10 minutes at 60°C. After spin-down, an additional 50 
µl of demineralized H2O was added to the column and the mixture was spun at 11,000 g for 1 mi-
nute.

12.14 – Transformation by electroporation  
Precultures of K. phaffii and D. hansenii were prepared by inoculating a colony in 12 mL YPD media 
in 50 mL culture tubes followed by incubation for approximately 12-16 hours O/N at 30°C and 200 
rpm. To create main cultures, each preculture was diluted to an initial OD600nm of 0.1 in 100 mL 
of fresh YPD media in a 500 mL shake flask. For K. phaffii main cultures baffled 500 mL shake 
flasks were used, while unbaffled 500 mL shake flasks were used for D. hansenii cultures to avoid 
foam formation. The main cultures were incubated at 30°C and 150 rpm for 4-7 hours to reach a 
final OD600nm of around 1-1.6. The main cultures were subsequently divided into two 50 mL cul-
ture tubes, which were then spun down in a centrifuge at 3,000 G for 5 minutes to pellet the cells 
and the supernatants were discarded. And additional wash step was performed, where the pellet 
was resuspended in 25 mL of sterile demineralized water before being spun down in a centrifuge 
at 3,000 G for 5 minutes to pellet the cells and the supernatants were discarded. The pellets were 
gently resuspended in 16 mL treatment buffer consisting of 100 mM LiAc, 10 mM DTT, 0.6 M sor-
bitol, and 6 mM Tris HCl pH 8.5 (Trizma® hydrochloride from Sigma Aldrich). After incubating 
the cells at room temperature for 30 minutes with agitation every 10 minutes to avoid precipita-
tion, they were pelleted a third time by centrifugation at 3,000 G for 5 minutes. The supernatant 
was then discarded, and the cells were retained.  
After the pellet was gently resuspended in 1.5 mL of ice-cold 1 M sorbitol, the cells were washed 
three times by spinning them at 11,000 G for 30 seconds and resuspending them in 1 mL of ice-cold 
1 M sorbitol each time. For the third resuspension, 3 mL of ice-cold 1 M sorbitol was used instead 
of 1 mL. The washed cells were then distributed into 1.5 mL microcentrifuge tubes, with 200 µL of 
competent cells in each tube.  
Before electroporation, 1 µg of circular plasmid or 1 pmol of PCR amplified linearized plasmid 
fragments, 1 µg of repair template and/or 10 pmol ssDNA oligo, unless otherwise specified, were 
added individually to a given cuvette and gently mixed. The cell mix was incubated on ice for at 
least 5 minutes before electroporation.  
The cell mix in each tube was transferred to a sterile 0.2 cm Gene Pulser/MicroPulser Electro-
poration Cuvette previously chilled on ice. The cuvettes were placed back on ice for 1-5 minutes 
before the cells were electroporated using the predefined Pic (Pichia pastoris) program for K. phaffii 
==PAGE:54==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702 
Master thesis 
Page 53 of 120 
 
cells or setting the Bio-Rad MicroPulser manually to 2.3 kV for D. hansenii cells. After applying one 
electroporation pulse, the cells were immediately transferred into 1 mL of ice-cold YPD_sorbitol 
and chilled on ice for at least 5 minutes. The cells were then incubated at 30°C for 3 hours without 
shaking.  
After incubation, the cells were pelleted by centrifugation at 3,000 G for 5 minutes. The superna-
tants were discarded, and the pellets were gently resuspended in 50 µL YPD_sorbitol. The trans-
formed cells were then plated on YPD supplemented with 100mg/L NTC and incubated for 3-7 
days at 30 °C.  
A cleaning procedure was implemented to wash the cuvettes after electroporation. The cuvettes 
were rinsed three times with demineralized water. A blunt needled syringe was used to apply the 
water with pressure to ensure no cell debris was left in the cuvettes before they were rinsed 3 times 
with 70% (v/v) ethanol. The cuvettes were subsequently submerged in 96% ethanol and left there 
for 20 minutes. In a sterile lab bench, the ethanol was discarded from the cuvette, and it was left to 
dry on the bench. Each cuvette was then covered and transferred to a freezer box, which was then 
stored in a -20 °C freezer.

13 – Results 
 
The aim for this thesis was to construct, optimize and evaluate CRISPR-based plasmids with an 
RFP expression cassette integrated to quickly screen and validate correct in vivo integration of 
sgRNA(s). The vectors constructed in this study were based on general Cas9 plasmids with an 
NTC resistance marker optimized for K. phaffii (pDIV151) and for D. hansenii (pDIV489). The ex-
pression of mCherry was evaluated by visually checking for red fluorescence under a UV light 
with a suitable light source and filter attached. Correct assembly of the NTC marker was checked 
by counting dead/live cells after transformation of intact and digested vectors with the disrupted 
NTC marker inserted.  
Plasmid vectors used in this study are given in Section 12.2, Table 4. HT-CRISPR-based plasmids 
constructed as part of this thesis were evaluated and introduced into the individual hosts by elec-
troporation. All the RFP expression vectors were first evaluated using sDIV084 (K. phaffii wildtype) 
and sDIV115 (D. hansenii) to test the different designs for the expression of the reporter. The plas-
mids with functional reporters were then introduced as linear fragments into the NHEJ-deficient 
strains sDIV147 (K. phaffii  u70Δ::uidA), sDIV179 (K. phaffii ku70Δ IS1::mVenus), and sDIV165 (D. 
hansenii  u70Δ::uidA) to test if it was possible to remove the reporter, restore the selection marker 
and assemble a functional CRISPR/Cas9 complex.  
13.1 – CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid construction and verification 
A plasmid library consisting of 24 different CRISPR-based vectors with an RFP expression cassette 
were designed to increase chances of achieving successful gene editing in NCYs. 12 designs were 
made specifically for K. phaffii, and 12 designs were made specifically for D. hansenii to be able to 
compare effectiveness of different constructs in the two hosts. The purpose of the designs was to 
test if:  
- 
A dedicated promoter is needed to drive mCherry expression or if the Kp_TEF2 and 
Dh_TEF2 promoters can be used to drive the transcription of the 
Aa_Gly_tRNA/Dh_Gly_tRNA and mCherry.  
==PAGE:57==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702

- 
A single MssI cut site would be enough to remove the RFP cassette via in vivo integration 
of a protospacer. Additionally, designs were created to determine whether a single cut site 
positioned upstream or downstream of the RFP cassette was sufficient or if two cut sites 
were necessary.  
- 
Any sequence overlaps in the tRNAs would result in RFP removal but no insertion of a 
protospacer in its place.  
A schematic overview of the different RFP expression cassette combinations can be seen in Figure 
6 & 7. The plasmids were all constructed in a way so that it would be possible to integrate one or 
multiple protospacers through in vivo assembly. The RFP cassettes were flanked by a tracrRNA 
back-bone and by transfer RNAs (tRNAs) or one tRNA and an HDV sequence. After digesting the 
vector with the MssI restriction enzyme, the RFP cassette would either be completely removed, or 
the vector would be opened upstream or downstream of the RFP cassette. The linearized vector 
could subsequently be assembled in vivo by transforming it into an NCY with a ssDNA oligo of 90 
nucleotides consisting of 35 nucleotides that were complementary to the end of the Aa_Gly_tRNA 
or the Dh_Gly_tRNA sequence followed by a 20-nucleotide long protospacer and the first 35 nucle-
otides in the tracrRNA backbone. The in vivo assembly mechanism for integration of a protospacer 
after MssI digestion was illustrated on Figure 12.

not changed. The arrays are made of three PCR fragments each:

14 – Discussion 
In this study, it was attempted to expand the genetic engineering toolbox of K. phaffii and D. han-
senii by developing a system for in vivo assembly of CRISPR/Cas9 based plasmids with ssDNA oli-
goes for genetic engineering of NCYs that does not rely on lengthy assembly procedures of cus-
tomized gRNAs expression cassettes in E. coli or preparing customized PCR fragments for in vivo 
assembly in NCYs.  
14.1 – Testing different designs for RFP expression in CRISPR plasmids using sDIV084 
and sDIV115 
Initially, different designs for RFP expression in CRISPR plasmids were constructed and tested us-
ing sDIV084 and sDIV115. All but one of the constructed plasmids (pDIV-U36) were maintained 
well in the transformants, and it was possible to validate 9 out of 12 plasmids to be used in K. 
phaffii and 3 out of 12 plasmids to be used in D. hansenii. For successful and easy selection and 
screening of NCY strains that have undergone desired genetic modifications using the newly de-
signed CRISPR plasmids it was crucial that the designs enabled a detectable expression of RFP.  
Four out of the nine verified plasmids for K. phaffii, namely pDIV763-764 & pDIV767-768, enabled 
detectable expression of RFP when transformed into sDIV084. All four plasmids had an Aa_TEF1 
promoter inserted after the Aa_Gly_tRNA to drive the transcription of RFP, which pDIV765-766 & 
pDIV769-770 did not have. Thus, the results indicate that it is necessary to add an additional pro-
moter after the tRNA to get RFP expression. However, the choice of promoter is not irrelevant. 
None of the plasmids designed for D. hansenii showed detectable expression of RFP when trans-
formed into sDIV115. The inefficent expression could either be due to the choice of promoter in-
serted after the Dh_Gly_tRNA or unstable plasmid copy-numbers in the transformants.  
In all designs for D. hansenii where RFP expression was observed, the Aa_TEF1 promoter was used 
to drive the transcription of a CUG-optimized mCherry CDS. In hindsight, it would have been in-
teresting to include a species-specific promoter to test if it would yield detectable RFP expression. 
Additionally, it would also elucidate if the inefficient expression of RFP was due to the choice of 
promoter in the designs or if it was caused by a problem with the plasmid copy-number.

It was decided to only use the plasmids pDIV763-764 & pDIV767-768 for the subsequent oligonu-
cleotide-mediated in vivo assembly experiments. Although the D. hansenii vectors did not satisfy 
the RFP expression requirements, it is still worth noting that many of the elements used for all the 
vector designs originate from other species than K. phaffii & D. hansenii, suggesting that they may 
be likely to function in other yeasts. 
14.2 – Oligonucleotide-mediated in vivo assembly method tested in K. phaffii  
A main focus of this study was to test if it was possible to integrate a 20-nucleotide long proto-
spacer sequence into the constructed CRISPR-plasmids via in vivo assembly. Several strategies for 
reducing false positives and improving the in vivo assembly efficiency were developed and tested.  
Firstly, efforts were made to improve the in vivo assembly efficiency, as the initial oligonucleotide-
mediated in vivo assembly experiments with PCR linearized fragments of four different K. phaffii 
vectors resulted in many transformants being formed on YPD-NTC plates that had not assembled 
the vectors correctly. The screening of transformants using fluorescence revealed that the 20-nucle-
otide protospacer was not successfully integrated into the CRISPR-plasmids through in vivo as-
sembly when a linear PCR vector fragment containing the full NAT gene and the panARS se-
quence was used. 
This emphasized the importance of separating the survival elements of NCY plasmids on different 
PCR fragments to reduce plasmid self-closure [125]. Preventing self-closure of a plasmid can be 
achieved either by disrupting and restoring the selective marker or placing one gene copy of the 
selective marker on a separate linear DNA fragment from the yeast origin of replication.  
To prevent self-closure, current in vivo assembly methods for CRISPR plasmid construction in S. 
cerevisiae separate the yeast origin of replication and selectable marker into two distinct PCR frag-
ments that can be joined via overlapping sequences of 20-50 nucleotides [105], [126]. Inspired by 
this, and in vivo assembly strategies developed for filamentous fungi [127], it was decided to dis-
rupt the NAT marker to test if it was possible to assemble a plasmid without a ssDNA oligonucleo-
tide in an attempt to reduce false positives obtained during in vivo assembly of CRISPR/Cas9 plas-
mids and create ESF NCY vectors.

This study found that in vivo assembly using a 60 bp sequence overlap was sufficient for success-
fully repairing the NAT gene after MssI digestion without the use of an ssDNA oligo. All colonies 
tested by cPCR revealed successful repair of the NAT gene. Furthermore, no growth was observed 
in the control group where NCY cells were transformed with an intact plasmid featuring a dis-
rupted NAT gene. Additionally, the bacterial ori and bla gene was successfully removed from the 
vector, showing that it is possible to use the in vivo assembly technique in NCYs to construct ESF 
vectors.  
Selecting transformants efficiently is a crucial tool for identifying colonies with correct integration 
of a protospacer. Thus, after several unsuccessful attempts to disrupt the NAT gene in pDIV763-
764 & pDIV767-768, it was ultimately decided to disrupt the gene using PCR as a method for fur-
ther experimentation in order to improve the success rate of in vivo assembly of the plasmids. By 
implementing this alteration (disrupting the NAT gene on both PCR fragments, leaving a 52 bp 
overhang), the assessment of the success of oligonucleotide-mediated plasmid assembly and sub-
sequent genetic modifications in K. phaffii became more efficient, as only colonies that correctly as-
sembled the NAT ends of the PCR fragments could survive on YPD-NTC plates. 
Secondly, to minimize false positives, such as growth on YPD-NTC plates and loss of red fluores-
cence without integration of the protospacer via in vivo assembly, various plasmid designs were 
evaluated. It was hypothesized that the use of two identical tRNA sequences could result in unin-
tended HDR as the tRNA sequences could serve as a templates for in vivo assembly of the two 
PCR fragments, thereby omitting the RFP cassette of the CRISPR-plasmids without incorporating 
the protospacer. This hypothesis was validated by sequencing the presumed correctly in vivo as-
sembled pDIV763 plasmids from sDIV147 transformants, which revealed that the protospacer 
from PR_DIV3204 had not been integrated between the two tRNAs. In its place, only a single 
tRNA was found, implying that the other tRNA had been used as a template for in vivo assembly 
of the two PCR fragments, thereby removing the RFP expression cassette from the CRISPR-plas-
mids without incorporating the protospacer.  
This discovery unfortunately limits the design options for multiplexing oligonucleotide-mediated 
in vivo assembled CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids as the tRNA-based sgRNA expression cassettes are 
highly similar in their genomic content due to the presence of two or multiple tRNA sequences.

To mitigate the tRNA loop out problem, designs with one tRNA sequence and an HDV sequence 
were used instead of two tRNAs. The results from the attempted in vivo assembly of vectors with 
these designs suggest that it may be an effective solution for minimizing false positives, as no non-
fluorescent colonies were observed when PCR linearized fragments of pDIV767 & pDIV768 were 
co-transformed into sDIV147 along with the ssDNA oligo PR_DIV3204. Whether multiple sgRNA 
expression cassettes can be inserted with this design remains unknown, but it would be worth pur-
suing in future studies.  
Despite multiple efforts to optimize the in vivo assembly of CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids in NCYs using 
ssDNA oligonucleotides, no plasmids were obtained with a correctly assembled sgRNA expres-
sion cassette. Unfortunately, the time constraints and the utilization of all the remaining linearized 
vector PCR fragments for the OCH1 knockout experiments precluded further investigation of this 
issue.  
However, it would have been interesting to investigate if the lack of transformants was due incor-
rect in vivo assembly of the CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids or if it was due to an unrepaired DSB induced 
by a correctly assembled CRISPR/Cas9 complex. An experimental setup designed to test this was 
actually included in this study, where the four different K. phaffii CRISPR-plasmids were to be as-
sembled with a protospacer that did not cut anywhere in the genome of sDIV179. Unfortunately, 
the NAT gene and the panARS sequence were both present on one of the two linearized vector 
PCR fragments, which resulted in >1000 colonies which were all fluorescing red, indicating that the 
ssDNA oligonucleotide was not used to bridge and join the ends of the two nonhomologous 
dsDNA fragments. As formerly stated, the experiments were not repeated due to time constraints 
and depleted linearized vector PCR fragments.  
Another potential strategy for testing the in vivo assembly of CRISPR/Cas9-based plasmids for 
gene engineering in NCYs could be to separate the Cas9 encoding sequence and the sgRNA ex-
pression cassette and place them on two distinct plasmids, as Jacobus et al. did in 2022 [126]. Their 
EasyGuide plasmids support in vivo assembly of multiple gRNAs for CRISPR/Cas9 applications in 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae. This approach offers a controlled way of constructing plasmids in vivo 
and verifying them. Additionally, multiple gRNA plasmids can be pre-assemble, tested and veri-
fied before a CRISPR/Cas9 encoding plasmid is added. However, their method relies on using

multiple resistance markers for multi sgRNA expression cassette assembly, which can limit its use 
in multiplexing high-throughput experiments. This is because the cost and complexity of the re-
quired media for selecting transformants increases with each additional selectable marker used. 
The question regarding the lack of transformants in some of the OCH1 knockout experiments still 
remains unresolved. It is unclear whether this was a result of incorrect in vivo assembly of the 
CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids, or if it was caused by an unrepaired DSB induced by a correctly assembled 
CRISPR/Cas9 complex using the provided ssDNA oligoes. 
A study conducted by Strucko et al. in 2022 proved that ssDNA oligonucleotides are sufficient for 
Cas9-mediated gene editing in a NHEJ deficient D. hansenii strain. In their study they demon-
strated that ssDNA 90-mers can be applied as repair templates for gene deletions and point muta-
tions. Specifically, they targeted the ADE2 gene with a ssDNA oligo composed by a fusion of 45-nt 
upstream and downstream ADE2 sequences and a ssDNA oligo dictating the introduction of a 
stop codon in the middle of this repair template. A key finding of the study was that, while high 
efficiencies were achieved for both point mutation and deletion of ADE2, the number of trans-
formants obtained in the gene-deletion experiment was significantly lower, being approximately 
34-fold lower compared to the point mutation experiment. The authors speculate that repair of the 
Cas9-induced DNA DSB in ADE2 is more difficult when the deletion oligonucleotide, rather than 
the point-mutation oligonucleotide, is used as a template for repair and that more cells die due to 
unfinished DNA repair. This might also have been the case in the OCH1 gene deletion experiment 
in this thesis. The repair oligonucleotide PR_DIV3204, which is composed by a fusion of 45-nt up-
stream and downstream of the OCH1 sequence, needs to bind to two regions that are both quite 
distant (approximately 54-1161 bps) from the Cas9-induced DNA DSB. Thus, the binding of the 
deletion-oligonucleotide might create a barrier for efficient repair in this experimental setup and 
hence also prevent transformant colony formation due to cell death, even though the CRISPR/Cas9 
plasmid was assembled correctly.

attempted to construct strains of K. phaffii and D. hansenii with a disrupted mVenus expression cas-

14.3 – Further optimizing in vivo assembly of CRISPR/Cas9-based plasmids for gene 
engineering in NCYs 
Although the results obtained in this study did not show that it was possible to integrate a proto-
spacer into a sgRNA expression cassette via in vivo assembly, a previous research project con-
ducted in the Eukaryotic Molecular Cell Biology research group showed that it is indeed possible 
to use ssDNA oligoes to bridge and join the ends of two nonhomologous dsDNA fragments in 
NCYs and change up to three base pairs in the process (Mortensen, Strucko, Christensen, 2022, un-
published). However, when trying to introduce more bases between the ends of the two nonho-
mologous dsDNA fragments, the NHEJ deficient NCYs seems to prefer bridging the gap using HR 
of DNA further upstream and downstream of the gap, as seen in the experiment where the identi-
cal tRNA sequences were used as repair template to fix the gap between the two dsDNA PCR frag-
ments rather than using the provided ssDNA oligonucleotide. Alternatively, it was also observed 
that the K. phaffii transformants had looped out both the RFP expression cassette and the sgRNA 
expression cassette elements, leaving a fragmented Kp_TEF1 promoter in their place after in vivo 
assembly of the CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid pDIV763. 
HR and classical NHEJ are the two main repair mechanisms for DSBs in NCYs, yet alternative 
mechanisms also exist such as microhomology-mediated end joining (MMEJ). This repair mecha-
nism does not require the involvement of the Ku70 protein, which is a necessary component in 
NHEJ in S. cerevisiae [128]. Currently, there are no studies on MMEJ in K. phaffii, however it is 
known that this repair mechanism only needs a very small amount of homologous nucleotides (8-
20 nucleotides) in S. cerevisiae [129].  
==PAGE:85==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702 
Master thesis 
Page 84 of 120 
 
The Kp_TEF1 promoter and Kp_AOX1 terminator used in all the K. phaffii plasmid designs have 
multiple adenine and thymine rich homologous sequences of up to 9 base pairs. These could po-
tentially be enough to facilitate HDR or an alternative repair of the gap between the two nonho-
mologous dsDNA fragments after MssI digestion of the vector PCR fragments. Therefore, it is pos-
sible that an alternative repair with a similar mechanism as MMEJ could be occurring in K. phaffii 
to bridge the gap between the two vector PCR fragments used in the experiments.  
MMEJ is thought to be partially dependent on a DNA ligase called Dnl4 in S. cerevisiae [128]. Previ-
ous studies have shown that deletion of a gene encoding a Dnl4 homolog in K. phaffii results in 
higher gene targeting efficiency for HR compared to disruption of Ku70 [7], [92]. This suggests that 
Ku70 disruption alone may not entirely prevent repair by classical NHEJ or that another Dnl4-de-
pendent DSB repair mechanism, such as MMEJ, may be occurring in K. phaffii. Therefore, deletion 
of Dnl4 could be considered as an additional strategy to improve HR frequency in K. phaffii. Addi-
tionally, one would think that a double deletion of the Dnl4 homolog and Ku70 would further im-
prove the HR frequency in K. phaffii. However, counterintuitively, when Nishi et al. (2022) deleted 
both Ku70 and the Dln4 homolog in K. phaffii it decreased the gene targeting efficiency of HR, 
while the same gene deletion combination resulted in improvement of gene targeting efficiencies 
for Ito et al. (2018).  
Cai et al. (2021) suggests that knocking out the dominant DNA repair pathways (NHEJ in K. phaffii 
and D. hansenii) without providing an efficient alternative will lead to a lower survival rate. In 
their study, they focused on enhancing the HR frequency of K. phaffii by overexpression of proteins 
like Rad52 that are known to be involved in HR from studies in S. cerevisiae [130]–[132] and delet-
ing genes like Ku70 that are known to be involved in NHEJ [133], which improved the HR effi-
ciency of K. phaffii by more than 54 times. Thus, enhancing the HR machinery in K. phaffii and other 
NCYs like D. hansenii may be a more effective strategy for optimizing the oligonucleotide-medi-
ated gene engineering methods presented in this thesis.  
Oligonucleotide-mediated DNA assembly methods have been developed for S. cerevisiae by Gibson 
back in 2009 [134], and in vivo DNA assembly techniques have also recently been developed for K. 
phaffii [7], although they are not based on using oligonucleotides. Gibson demonstrated that S. cere-
visiae can take up and assemble at up to 38 overlapping ssDNA oligonucleotides and a linear 
==PAGE:86==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702 
Master thesis 
Page 85 of 120 
 
double-stranded vector in a single transformation event. The oligonucleotides could overlap by as 
little as 20 base pairs and could be as long as 200 nucleotides. Interestingly, during the assembly of 
six 200-mer oligonucleotides, 44% of the DNA fragments were synthesized by yeast (960 out of 
2200 nucleotides were added in vivo), demonstrating that the yeast can fill out gaps of up to 160 
nucleotides using the oligonucleotides as templates. It would be interesting to further investigate, 
if NCYs could reach similar assembly efficiencies by enhancing the HR machinery and deleting 
and/or downregulating the NHEJ machinery. Alternatively, using longer ssDNA oligoes of higher 
quality, like Gibson (2009), could also be tested, to see if the ssDNA oligonucleotides are the bar-
rier preventing efficient HDR of two non-homologous dsDNA ends in NCYs K. phaffii and D. han-
senii. 
14.4 – Towards high-throughput construction of NCY cell factories 
In recent years, the development of yeast cell factories has relied increasingly on high-throughput 
genetic engineering technologies. One significant challenge in this field is the construction of 
CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids with unique gRNAs containing a 20-nucleotides protospacer tailored to 
each genomic target and the associated design and construction of GTSs, which can be labor-inten-
sive and costly. The process of creating this specific gRNA can take a significant amount of time, 
typically spanning 3-5 days. This includes the initial cloning in E. coli, purification, validation, and 
finally transforming it with the Cas9 into the yeast strain. Using oligonucleotides could potentially 
serve as effective and cost-efficient repair templates for introducing new protospacer into sgRNA 
expression cassettes on plasmids and as GTSs during homology-directed repair, making them a 
valuable tool in large-scale genetic engineering projects. Using oligonucleotides as GTSs has been 
employed successful in different Aspergillus species [135] and was also tested in D. hansenii recently 
[6]. However, challenges with low HR frequency and suspected MMEJ DSB repair mechanisms 
must be overcome before oligonucleotide-mediated gene targeting and editing can be widely 
adopted as a leading technology for high-throughput genetic engineering of NCYs.

15 – Conclusion  
The main focus of this study was to develop and evaluate various CRISPR-based plasmid designs 
for efficient screening and validation of correct in vivo assembly of functional sgRNA expression 
cassettes using single-stranded oligonucleotides. To achieve this, various approaches were em-
ployed to integrate unique 20-nucleotide protospacers into CRISPR/Cas9 vectors through in vivo 
assembly in NCYs, where an RFP expression cassette had been inserted for simple fluores-
cent/non-fluorescent screening of potential transformants. 
Evaluating the different designs demonstrated that it was necessary to add a dedicated promoter 
to get RFP expression in NCYs K. phaffii and D. hansenii. However, the choice of promoter might be 
important, as none of the plasmids designed for D. hansenii showed detectable expression of RFP 
when transformed into sDIV115 after 4 days of incubation. This inefficient expression could be due 
to the choice of promoter or a problem with the plasmid copy-number. Although none of the con-
ducted experiments resulted in a successful integration of a protospacer into a sgRNA expression 
cassette through in vivo assembly, it was observed that the NCYs preferred to bridge the gap be-
tween the two nonhomologous dsDNA fragments using HR of DNA further upstream and down-
stream of the gap. Furthermore, it was also observed that some K. phaffii transformants had looped 
out both the RFP expression cassette and the sgRNA expression cassette elements, leaving a frag-
mented Kp_TEF1 promoter in their place after in vivo assembly of the CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid 
pDIV763. After carefully examining the promoter and terminator sequences it was suggested that 
alternative DNA assembly mechanisms such as MMEJ could be a factor in the repair of DSBs in 
NCYs.  
To optimize in vivo assembly of the CRISPR/Cas9 plasmid, one potential approach would be to 
delete the Dnl4 gene and its homologs. This strategy has been shown to increase homologous re-
combination (HR) frequency in K. phaffii and could potentially also work in D. hansenii. Further-
more, if the HDR pathway is upregulated, as demonstrated by Cai et al. (2021) in K. phaffii, this 
strategy could be even more effective. However, further research is needed to fully understand the 
implications of NHEJ, HDR, and other repair mechanisms such as MMEJ in ssDNA oligonucleo-
tide mediated in vivo assembly of CRISPR/Cas9-based plasmids for gene engineering in NCYs. Al-
ternatively, using longer ssDNA oligoes of higher quality could also be tested, to see if the ssDNA 
==PAGE:88==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702 
Master thesis 
Page 87 of 120 
 
oligonucleotides are the barrier preventing efficient HDR albeit this would increase the cost associ-
ated with each high-throughput experiment.  
Overall, the study has provided new knowledge intended for expanding the genetic engineering 
toolbox of K. phaffii and D. hansenii by developing and testing a system for in vivo assembly of 
CRISPR/Cas9-based plasmids using ssDNA oligoes for genetic engineering of NCYs that does not 
rely on lengthy assembly procedures of customized sgRNA expression cassettes in E. coli or pre-
paring customized PCR fragments for in vivo assembly in NCYs. However, achieving efficient and 
robust in vivo assembly of plasmids with ssDNA oligonucleotides remains a challenge in K. phaffii 
and in D. hansenii.

16 – Future perspectives  
 
In this work, different CRISPR/Cas9-based plasmids with an RFP expression cassette were con-
structed, tested, and optimized for genetically engineering NCYs using oligonucleotide-mediated 
in vivo assembly to integrate protospacer(s) into sgRNA expression cassettes. Next, it would be 
desirable test the constructs in strains with enhanced HR frequency and/or in strains with deleted 
Dnl4 homologs. Additionally, using longer, higher quality ssDNA oligoes could help determine if 
they are the limiting factor in the system's performance. 
In the future, the use of oligonucleotide-mediated in vivo assembly of DNA has the potential to 
significantly impact the development of NCY cell factories. The ability to efficiently and cost-effec-
tively assemble DNA constructs in vivo in NCYs can greatly accelerate the process of strain con-
struction and enable high-throughput genetic-engineering experiments. This can ultimately lead to 
the development of NCY cell factories that can provide commercially feasible yields, making them 
a viable alternative to traditional Saccharomyces cerevisiae hosts in industrial biotechnology. 
One of the key advantages of using oligonucleotide-mediated in vivo assembly is the ability to by-
pass the need for E. coli-based cloning methods, which can be time-consuming, costly, and often 
prove to be bottlenecks in strain construction. In addition, in vivo assembly can also facilitate the 
seamless integration of multiple DNA fragments, such as entire metabolic pathways, into a single 
plasmid, which can be crucial for the development of advanced cell factories. 
Furthermore, the use of in vivo assembly in combination with CRISPR-based gene-editing technol-
ogies can greatly enhance the ability to perform precise genetic modifications in NCYs. This can 
allow for the development of NCY cell factories that have specific traits that are well-suited for dif-
ferent industrial bioprocesses and simplify studying phenotypical traits, like D. hansenii’s ability to 
grow in high salt concentrations or how K. phaffii use methanol as a sole carbon source. 
In addition to assembling CRISPR/Cas9 plasmids it would be beneficial to use less complex and 
less expensive GTSs directly in the organism of interest for gene targeting and gene editing. Single-
stranded oligonucleotides have been shown to function as repair templates during HDR to create 
gene deletions and point mutations. When paired with CRISPR/Cas9, the use of oligonucleotides 
can greatly reduce the cost of individual experiments in large genetic engineering efforts, making 
==PAGE:90==
Emil Ørsted Christensen – s173702 
Master thesis 
Page 89 of 120 
 
high-throughput genetic engineering more feasible. Furthermore, the entire process of genetically 
modifying NYCs could be fully automated, from ordering ssDNA oligoes for integrating a proto-
spacer and as GTS for gene knockouts and knock-ins, to handling and electroporating NCY cells 
using liquid handling robots and high-throughput electroporation systems. However, more re-
search is needed to fully understand the implications of in vivo assembly mechanisms in K. phaffii 
and D. hansenii before high-throughput construction of NCY cell factories can be accomplished.

B.1 – Primer preparation 
Primers were designed and ordered from Integrated DNA Technologies in collaboration with To-
mas Strucko (DTU Bioengineering). Once received, the primers were centrifuged in a Bio-Rad Mini 
centrifuge, mixed with sterile Milli-Q water to reach a final concentration of 100 µM, and then in-
cubated for 5 minutes at room temperature before being vortexed. A 10 µM working solution was 
then prepared in a 1.5 ml Eppendorf tube, and both the stock and working solutions were stored at 
-20 °C.
