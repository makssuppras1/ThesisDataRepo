Abstract
This project deals with modelling of bolted steel joints in 2D frames. The approach is
based on the component method as presented in Eurocode [1] but expands the method
by including directional stiﬀnesses. This is done based on an assumption that when
only considering rotational stiﬀness the method becomes inaccurate for joints with
small moments. The project initiates this by introducing basic theory regarding stiﬀ-
ness such as Hooke’s Law and combination formulas for diﬀerent spring systems. This
is followed by a description of the strength and stiﬀness properties of speciﬁc joint
components using theory presented in Eurocode and [2]. The components described
are picked based on the experimental setups which are modelled later in the project.
Following the theoretical part of the project the method of modelling the joint is as-
sessed. This is done by presenting how a joint is divided into single components which
are modelled individually in an FE model. Then a generalized method of analysing
the behaviour of the components in a joint under loading is introduced. This is done
by describing the procedure for a single and a combined multi-linear component re-
spectively. Subsequently the modelling of the previously described components is laid
out in detail for each one before setting up the model for experimental comparison.
The experimental comparison consist of comparing the model with two diﬀerent ex-
periments. The ﬁrst is a setup consisting of a beam and column connected by bolts
and a set of angle cleats described in [3]. Modelling this required a lot of assumptions
especially regarding the column since this turned out to hardly be described at all.
It was therefore chosen to model the setup using two diﬀerent columns. One was an
inﬁnitely stiﬀcolumn and the other was a column with properties which were mainly
assumed based on the little information available. The results showed that the stiﬀ-
ness of both models were much larger than what the experiment showed. This was
assessed by reducing bearing stiﬀness of the beam web. This was done since at least
one of the bolts yielded in bearing before the block tearing caused the joint to fail.
The bolt bearing therefore should be reduced since the linear stiﬀness used from the
Eurocode is initial and becomes less accurate when the component approaches yield-
ing. The applied method of reducing the bearing stiﬀness gave better ﬁtting results
for both models. The reduction was estimated to be too large and instead it was sug-
gested that more components were described by bi- or multi-linear stiﬀnesses. This
would obtain a reduced stiﬀness of the joint by reducing several component stiﬀnesses
slightly instead of reducing a single component too much.
==PAGE:7==
vi
The second experiment used for comparison was a gusset plate joint presented in [4].
This setup was better described and was modelled without signiﬁcant assumptions.
The experiment varied with 3 diﬀerent load eccentricities giving a larger basis of com-
parison. In this case the initial model results showed a good ﬁt with the experiment
stiﬀness. The model stiﬀness deviated the most for the setup with the smallest eccen-
tricity. This was found to, as for the ﬁrst experiment, be caused by the fact that the
bolt bearing approached yielding more in this case than in the other two. Again the
conclusion therefore was that a bi-linear description of the stiﬀness would improve the
model.
Finally the basis for expanding the component method was tested by modelling both
experiments only taking rotational stiﬀness into account. This showed that for the
ﬁrst experiment where the moment took up 32 % of the joint capacity the directional
stiﬀnesses had a signiﬁcant impact on the model results. In the second experiment
the moment was far more dominating and took up more than 90 % of the capacity.
This was very evident when the models with and without directional stiﬀness were
compared since there was no signiﬁcant diﬀerence. From this it was concluded that
expanding the component method is relevant since it makes it applicable to more
joints.

Resumé
Dette projekt omhandler modellering af boltede stålsamlinger i 2D rammer. Tilgan-
gen til dette er baseret på komponentmetoden, som den er beskrevet i Eurocode [1],
men udvider metoden til at inkludere retningsstivheder. Dette gøres på baggrund
af en antagelse om at ved kun at tage hensyn til rotationsstivheder, bliver metoden
unøjagtig for samlinger med små momenter.
Projektet indledes med at introduc-
ere grundlæggende teori om stivhed såsom Hooke’s Lov og kombinationsformler for
forskellige fjedersystemer. Dette er efterfulgt af en beskrivelse af styrke- og stivhed-
segenskaber for speciﬁkke samlingskomponenter med udgangsunkt i teori fra Eurocode
og [2]. De beskrevne komponenter er udvalgt på baggrund af de eksperimentelle op-
stillinger, der modelleres senere i projektet.
Efter den teoretiske del af projektet behandles måden hvorpå, samlingen modelleres.
Dette gøres ved at præsentere hvorledes samlingen er inddelt i enkelte komponen-
ter, som modelleres individuelt i en FE model. Derefter introduceres en generaliseret
metode til at analysere komponenternes opførsel under last. Dette tager udgangspunkt
i en beskrivelse af proceduren for hhv. en enkelt og en kombineret multilineær kom-
ponent. Efterfølgende beskrives modelleringen af de tidligere beskrevne komponenter
i detaljer for hver enkelt, før modellen sammenlignes med forsøg.
Den eksperimentelle sammenligning består i at sammenligne modellen med to forskel-
lige forsøg. Det første er en opsætning, som er beskrevet i [3], der består af en bjælke
og en søjle, som er samlet af bolte og et sæt vinkelproﬁler. At modellere dette forsøg
krævede en del antagelser især vedrørende søjlen, eftersom det viste sig at den dårligt
nok var beskrevet i artiklen. Det blev derfor valgt at modellere opsætningen med to
forskellige søjler. Den ene var en inﬁnitesimal stiv søjle, og den anden var en søjle med
egenskaber, der hovedsageligt var antaget på baggrund af den smule information, som
var tilgængelig. Resultaterne viste, at stivheden af begge modeller var langt højere,
end hvad forsøget viste. Dette blev behandlet ved at reducere hulrandsstivheden af
bjælkekroppen. Det blev gjort eftersom mindst én af boltene ﬂød i hulranden, før
blokudrivning forårsagede at samlingen svigtede. Hulrandsstivheden skulle derfor re-
duceres, eftersom den lineære stivhed benyttet fra Eurocode er en initialstivhed og
bliver mindre korrekt, når komponenten nærmer sig ﬂydning. Den benyttede metode
til at reducere hulrandsstivheden gav bedre resultater for begge modeller. Reduktion
blev vurderet til at være for stor og i stedet blev det foreslået, at ﬂere komponen-
ter blev beskrevet ved bi- eller multilineære stivheder. Dette ville opnå en reduceret
stivhed af samlingen ved at reducere ﬂere komponentstivheder en smule i stedet for

komponentmetode er relevant, eftersom den gør metoden brugbar for et større antal
samlinger.

1 Introduction
This project investigates modelling the stiﬀness of bolted steel joints in 2D frames.
Modelling of steel joints is a topic that is already treated in Eurocode [1] through the
component method. The method does however only consider the moment-rotation re-
lation, thus only taking rotational stiﬀness into consideration. This limitation ﬁts well
with joints where the section forces in the joint is dominated by the moment making
the normal and shear force less inﬂuential. However in joints where the moment is
small compared to these forces the rotational stiﬀness will be less inﬂuential for the
joint displacements. The method developed in this project is therefore extended to
include horizontal and vertical stiﬀnesses instead of just rotational.
Initially the project present the basic theory behind the component method and how
spring stiﬀnesses are combined. This is followed by an assessment of the basic strength
and stiﬀness properties of a chosen list of components. This is done using the infor-
mation available in Eurocode which is supplemented with block tearing theory from
[2]. Subsequently the general method of modelling the individual components in a
bolted steel joint is described. This includes a generalized description of the script
used to calculate the force-displacement curve and strength of a joint. A more detailed
description of the way in which the previously presented components are modelled in
the script is then presented. These components are chosen based on two experimental
setups which are then modelled. The ﬁrst is a joint between a coped beam and a col-
umn presented in [3]. This is modelled and the results are compared to the experiment
data. Based on this the model is evaluated and potential changes to improve it is ex-
amined. The second experiment which is modelled is a gusset plate joint described in
[4]. The results of this too is compared and discussed to evaluate the model. Finally
the diﬀerence between the expanded component model and the Eurocode version is
investigated by modelling the two experiments using both versions. This is done to
determine the eﬀect of expanding the component method and whether this is relevant
at all.

Terminology
Component (Stiﬀness component)
Individual stiﬀness contributions of parts e.g. shear, bearing or tensile stiﬀness.
Combined component element
Individual components combined to create a single component element which can re-

Connecting part
A part in the joint without a deﬁned capacity. It only contributes with a stiﬀness and

Connection
The speciﬁc manner in which two parts are joined.
Directional displacement
Displacement in a straight line, typical either horizontal or vertical.
Directional spring
A spring which resist directional displacements.
Joint
The combination of parts which connects two structural parts.
Mechanism
The physical process (movement) linked to the activation of a stiﬀness component.
Part
Units in a joint e.g. bolts, angle cleats, gusset plates etc.
Procedure
Series of calculations carried out to obtain a certain result or value.
Properties
General term describing stiﬀness, strength or similar of a part or a component.
Relative displacement
The displacement of an individual part relative to the joint.
Rotational spring
A spring which resist rotational displacements.
Sub-component
Single components in a combined component.
System
The manner of which one or more springs are combined.

2 Theory
2.1
Component Method
This project is based on the idea of considering a steel joint as a combination of
individual components when describing its physical properties. The components are
identiﬁed as parts of the joint with individual properties which can be consisting of
e.g. strength, stiﬀness or requirements for deformations and loads. When the desired
components have been identiﬁed and suﬃciently described they are combined to form
the entire joint. This methodology is already partly incorporated in Eurocode however
it does only consider the relation between angular displacements and moments. In
this project it is expanded to also include displacements in two dimensions to obtain
a more complete picture.
The component properties used in this project are mainly described using the cur-
rent Eurocodes. However some are described using additional sources both to obtain
more accurate results and to investigate deviations in the model when compared to
experimental data.
2.1.1
Hooke’s Law
One of the main properties used in the model is the component stiﬀness which in
general is considered as linear springs. The relation between deformation and force in
these springs are described using Hooke’s law. Hooke’s Law describes a linear relation
between the force applied to a spring F and the resulting deformation δ. This relation
is described by a spring constant k which is unique to each spring.
F = k δ
(2.1)
The same relation can be applied for materials using Young’s Modulus E. This de-
scribes the force-displacement relation for a material assuming this is linear elastic.
σ = E ε
(2.2)
In this project steel is considered to be linear elastic and Hooke’s Law can therefore be
applied. In addition to describing the deformations caused by normal stresses it can
also be used to calculate shear deformations. This is done using the shear modulus G
which gives the relation between the shear stress τ and the shear strain γ.
τ = G γ
(2.3)

Chapter 2. Theory
2.2
Force distribution
In order to determine the capacity of the individual joint components it is necessary
to investigate the distribution of the joint forces in the individual part. It should be
noted that the set of forces in the individual parts is not necessarily identical since it
can vary throughout the joint.
2.2.1
Bolts
This section describes the force distribution in a bolt geometry located in the plane i.e.
where the bolts are only loaded in shear. Bolt geometries perpendicular to the plane
where the bolts are subjected to tensile forces will be assessed later. When an in-plane
bolt geometry is exposed to eccentric loading the forces in the individual bolts should
be calculated to determine the strength. This can be done by locating the centre of
rotation (RC) at which no displacement occurs when the joint is subjected to a given
eccentric load. By rotating about this point the individual bolt displacements can be
found purely from the angle of rotation and the bolt forces can be directly calculated.
However to distinguish between displacements from normal and shear force as well as
moment in the joint a method of superposition is used instead. This approach takes
the moment in the elastic centre (EC) and combines this with normal and shear forces.
Displacements are then correspondingly found from each of the three loads and are
then superpositioned. The deﬁnition and calculation of EC will be treated in Section
3.2.
When describing the bolt geometry and calculating capacities the edge distances and
the distances between bolt holes are deﬁning. The naming of these used in this project
are for good measure deﬁned in Figure 2.4.
Figure 2.4: Deﬁnition of e1, e2, p1 and p2 in bolted joint
e1 is the edge distance in the force direction.
p1 is the bolt hole spacing in the force direction.
e2 is the edge distance perpendicular to the force direction.
p2 is the bolt hole spacing perpendicular to the force direction.

Bolt force moment
When a bolted joint is subjected to an eccentric load a moment will occur. In the
following the relation between the moment and the rotational displacement related
to this in the form of a rotational stiﬀness Kϕ will be derived. It is assumed that
the rotation occurs in EC where the Origin is located and that the moment solely
originates from the rotation.
In order to derive the rotational stiﬀness the displacement of each bolt caused by
the rotation is initially considered. The general geometry of the considered case is
illustrated in Figure 2.7.

bolt forces Fx,ϕ,i and Fy,ϕ,i multiplied by the corresponding distance to EC.
Mϕ,i = Fx,ϕ,i (−yi) + Fy,ϕ,i xi

Block Tearing
Beside the force distribution in the bolts the distribution of the forces in the block
tearing mechanism related to the bolt geometry should also be determined. This is
done since the force distribution along the block tearing failure path deﬁnes the ca-
pacity of it.
Block tearing describes the failure mode where a cut-out is torn out of one of the bolted
parts. The cut-out is deﬁned by the failure path which passes through the outer bolts.
The bolt holes weaken the cross section along the path causing it to fail along this.
The path varies dependent on the geometry of the joint and is found as the path of
least resistance. The geometry of the cut-out can generally be put into two categories,
C-cut-outs and L-cut-outs, which will be treated separately in this section based on [2].
When calculating the capacity of block tearing the method presented in [1] bases this
on the net length of the path i.e. subtracting the width of the holes. If instead basing
the calculations on the observations described in [2] only the normal stresses should
be calculated from the net length hn and bn where the width of the bolt holes are
subtracted. The shear stresses should instead be calculated as acting across the gross
lengths hg and bg. This is reasoned in test results showing shear failure occurring close
to the outer edge of the bolt holes. This diﬀerence can be considered either by directly
using the net and gross length in calculations. However it can in some cases be more
convenient to use an equivalent thickness instead when calculating normal stresses.
This is simply calculated by multiplying the thickness t with the ratio between the
net and gross length.

The aim of the project was to implement a steel joint in an FE model of a 2D frame.

General model structure
The initial idea of this project was to create a model where the entire joint was rep-

Subsequent to the initial component calculations a loop is initiated. For each loop
a small load increment ∆P is added and the increase in section forces from this
throughout the joint is calculated using the FE model. The load increment is deﬁned
by ∆P = βP and can be adjusted by the factor β to obtain the desired accuracy
when running the procedure.
For the multi-linear component the associated section forces are then used to deter-
mine whether the component has reached a yielding criterion fy,m_n. When such a
criterion is reached the yield data (total load, displacement, etc.) is saved and the
new component stiﬀness is calculated. The yield criteria used in this model are based
on material strength but can also be chosen as deformation criteria. If the ﬁnal yield
criterion fy,M_n is reached the stiﬀness is reduced to 0 and the loop is ended. Ending
the loop when a single component loses its stiﬀness only makes sense when the com-
ponents are connected in series. If parallel connections are used the loop should run
until all components have reached their ultimate yield criteria.
As seen from Figure 3.3 the procedure varies slightly for the combined component.
The yield criteria for the combined component apply to the sub-components, which
are denoted i, and it does not fail until all of these have failed. This is however only
the case when the sub-components are not connected in series in which case only one
would have to fail. The reason it is chosen to illustrate the procedure for this kind of
combined component, is that this project focuses on bolted steel joints, and this is the
one that applies to a group of bolts in a joint. The procedure for a speciﬁc combined
bolt component can be seen in Appendix B where a ﬂow chart for the model of one
of the experiments is drawn.

4 Experimental comparison
In order to evaluate the developed model, it is conﬁgured to match two diﬀerent ex-
perimental setups. The results from the model are then held up against the results
of these. In the following each experiment is described separately. This is followed
by a description of how each setup is modelled. This is generally done by combining
relevant stiﬀness components in the model and setting them up in a similar structure
for each case. Relevant assumptions will be made and presented when necessary. The
model and experiment results are then compared and possible alterations of the model
to obtain more accurate results are presented and discussed.
For both experimental setups the block tearing mechanism determines the strength.
However the setup is very diﬀerent for each case and will be discussed when assessing
the results. Furthermore only data from a single run of the ﬁrst experiment presented
is compared to the model while data from diﬀerent load eccentricities are compared
for the second.
4.1
Coped Beam with Angle Brackets
The ﬁrst presented experiment is a bolted joint connecting a coped beam and a col-
umn described in [3]. The article describes 17 executed tests with varying geometric
dimensions and small variations in the joint e.g. varying between tee connections and
angle cleats. One of the joints and the results from this were chosen to compare with
the model results. The speciﬁc joint was chosen for several reasons. One was due to
the failure of it being a clean whole block tear-out. Another because it is symmet-
ric and the risk of out-of-plane movements are therefore small. Finally it was partly
chosen to compare the obtained results with the ones presented by [8]. This however
turned out to be less relevant due to some extensive ﬂaws found in the project that
will therefore not be used.

Discussion of Model Results
From the presented comparisons it is concluded that some or more components in
the joint is too stiﬀ.
The sub-components in component element 1 is considered
to be relatively stiﬀdue to the large dimensions of all parts and the block tearing
in component element 3 is already described by a bi-linear spring with a reduced
stiﬀness. The sub-components assessed is therefore the ones in component element 2
that are all calculated as individual linear springs which might give inaccurate results.
Of the sub-components in this, the bearing of the angle cleats and the shear of the
bolts are not expected to contribute to large deformations. The bearing of the beam
web however is relevant to investigate further since at least one of the bolts reach
yielding in both initial models. This makes it interesting to examine the stiﬀness
since the one given in the Eurocode is an initial stiﬀness. The initial stiﬀness does
not necessarily apply when yielding is approached which Eurocode in fact addresses
in Section 6.3.1 of [1]. This however is related to rotational stiﬀness, but similar ten-
dencies are assumed to be true for directional stiﬀness as well.
The issue of ﬁnding a method of investigating the bearing stiﬀness further was dis-

5 Discussion
Based on the results from the comparison between the model and the experimen-
tal setups some aspects of the proposed method could be adjusted to improve the
model. The main part of he stiﬀness components used are linear according to the
values presented in Eurocode. These are all initial stiﬀnesses and the comparisons
with especially the coped beam experiment imply that this does not ﬁt when com-
ponent capacity is approached. A proposed method of reducing bolt bearing stiﬀness
was presented and showed improved model results. This was however estimated to be
reducing the bearing stiﬀness to much when using the bolt bearing capacity from Eu-
rocode. Instead a less reduced stiﬀness could be introduced for all components instead
of just for the bolt bearing. This might be done by replacing all linear springs with
bi-linear ones to more accurately describe the full-range stiﬀness. A proposed method
in the Eurocode of reducing the rotational stiﬀness when 2/3 of the moment capacity
is reached might be applied assuming that this can be transferred to directional stiﬀ-
nesses. Otherwise experimental work would have to be done to better determine the
stiﬀness development under loading of each component.
In addition to the initial stiﬀnesses given in Eurocode the capacities from this might
also make it hard to describe the entire force-displacement curve found in an experi-
ment. As seen in Figure 4.12 from [10] the bearing strength calculated from Eurocode
is considerably lower than the one obtained in testing. If more accurate model results
are required the strength might be increased either by determining an empirical factor
to be multiplied with the Eurocode strength or by using alternate strength calcula-
tions.
A signiﬁcant assumption regarding the width of the deforming steel in a block tearing
mechanism was made by setting dbt = 2 d0. This was roughly investigated using a
sensitivity analysis with the gusset plate model data. The results showed that the
assumption was a good ﬁt with the the test data and that decreasing or increasing
it made a worse ﬁt. The value might however be investigated further using a larger
amount of experimental data for comparison since a single set of test data are not
deemed suﬃcient to base a ﬁnal conclusion on.
As a ﬁnal point of discussion it seems relevant to look at the basis on which the project
is made. The presented method is based on an assumption that the standard compo-
nent method limited to the moment-rotation relation is insuﬃcient. Each of the two
experimental setups are therefore modelled using only the rotational stiﬀness. This

5.1. Further work
69
From the comparisons made for the two experimental setups it is seen that the Eu-
rocode approach is suﬃcient when describing joints where the capacity is mainly
determined by the moment.
If instead modelling joints where the moment is less
dominating compared to normal and shear forces the results will be less accurate.
The need of including directional stiﬀnesses thereby depend on the force distribution
in the joint but the use of the expanded stiﬀness model makes it applicable to a wider
range of joints.
5.1
Further work
The developed model could be both enhanced and improved if further work was put
into this project. Initially this would include a more detailed investigation of the stiﬀ-
ness properties of individual components. This especially concerns the variation in
stiﬀness depending on how close the component is to yielding. A better understand-
ing of this would make it possible to introduce multi-linear springs instead of the
linear ones taken from Eurocode improving the model accuracy. This could also in-
clude examination of the observed deviation between the tested component strengths
and the ones calculated using Eurocode. A more exact calculation of the component
strengths would enable the model to calculate a larger part of the force-displacement
curve obtained from experiments. Furthermore the model could be applied to a wider
range of joints by describing more stiﬀness components and how these are incorpo-
rated. Finally an expansion of the model making it applicable for 3D frames would
be interesting, but presumably also a comprehensive task.

6 Conclusion
Based on the component method presented in Eurocode this project has developed a
method of modelling bolted steel joints in 2D frames that includes directional stiﬀ-
nesses. The method incorporates the joint in a 2D FE frame model by splitting it
into individual components. These are each modelled as separate elements which are
then combined to form the entire joint. This is done to take the variation in section
forces throughout the joint into consideration. All of the components presented in this
project was modelled with linear stiﬀnesses except for the block tearing component
which was modelled as bi-linear. Setting up the model to match experimental setups
showed varying results.
The ﬁrst comparison showed that both the model stiﬀness and strength was signiﬁ-
cantly lower than the experiment. This was concluded to mainly be due to the use
of linear stiﬀnesses of the bolt bearing components that either yielded or approached
yielding, making the initial stiﬀness values from Eurocode insuﬃcient. Based on this
a reduction of the stiﬀness was estimated and the results showed a highly improved ﬁt.
Even though the reduction was found to be too large the conclusion that component
stiﬀnesses should be reduced when yielding is approached was conﬁrmed. This was
recommended to be done replacing linear springs with bi- or multi-linear ones.
Comparing the model result to the second experiment showed a better ﬁt regarding
stiﬀness. Since the only yielding component in this setup was the block tearing com-
ponent, which was already modelled with a bi-linear stiﬀness, the result was consistent
with the conclusions made from the ﬁrst comparison. The strength however was still
lower in the model. This was concluded to mainly be caused by the fact that Eurocode
strengths tend to be lower than the strength determined from experiments.
Overall it was concluded that the method used in the developed model can be applied
when modelling steel joints. The main issue with the current model was found to
be the use of linear stiﬀnesses for most components. Instead it was recommended
that bi- or multi-linear stiﬀness components were used to improve the accuracy of
the model. Finally the relevance of the extended component method was evaluated
by comparing it to models set up without directional stiﬀnesses. This showed that
in joints with relatively small moments the inclusion of directional stiﬀness has great
impact on the force-displacement curve of the joint. The extension of the component
method is therefore very useful since it expands the range of joints to which it can be
applied.
