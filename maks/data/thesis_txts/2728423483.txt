Abstract
This master thesis focuses on the study of hydrodynamic response analysis of the IEA 15MW
floating wind turbine (FOWT) platform. All the model parameters are provided by the 1st
FOWT Comparative Study project. And the whole report mainly includes two parts, one
is the hydrodynamic analysis based on potential flow by the software HydroD and Sima
within the SESAM software package. And then other part is to analyze the platform from
the aspect of computational fluid dynamics (CFD), which will be performed by Starccm+.
The numerical modeling in the SESAM is mainly performed within three software, GineE,
HydroD and Sima. The .FEM files will be applied as the input file to HydroD for solving
the first-order transfer functions and the second-order transfer functions. Then those co-
eﬀicients will be input into the Sima to perform the final simulation with all components
coupled.
The FOWT hydrodynamic analysis by CFD software is the main part of this study, be-
cause of its long calculation cycle and the need to establish a high-fidelity model. And the
platform will be modeled only half to save computational costs. There are several tests that
will be performed in Starccm+. Including static test, free decay test and empty tank test.
All the above simulations are the prerequisite for the final simulation with the focused wave
involved. The static test will make the platform find its static position in the calm water,
which makes the following tests will start from an equilibrium position. The free decay test
will solve the damping of the floater for surge, heave and pitch motion, and the first and
second order damping coeﬀicients could be extracted. The empty tank test is performed
for the purpose of verifying the incident focused wave, to make sure the Starccm+ could
generate the expected focused wave through wave linear superposition.
Both the results generated by SIMA and Starccm+ will be compared with the data record
from the experiment.
Key words: FOWT, hydrodynamics, CFD, catenary system

Introduction
1.1
Background
The energy issue, which is connected to human growth, peace, and life, has always been
one of the topics that receive the greatest attention on a global scale. And the world is
getting more and more sustainable to make the whole human beings have a green future.
To make this object achievable, wind energy is developing significantly with the demand
for a sustainable future.
The utilization of wind energy stands as a significant milestone in humanity’s pursuit of
harnessing natural resources for sustainable power generation. Evidence from historical ac-
counts in ancient Greece and Rome reveals that inhabitants of that era pioneered the use of
windmills to support fundamental agricultural and industrial endeavours, including tasks
like milling grains and facilitating water transportation.
Dutch windmills played a significant role in the growth of windmill technology during the
17th century. During this period, the Netherlands meet significant hydrological issues due
to broad low-lying terrains that need intricate drainage and flood control systems. To han-
dle this serious problem, the Dutch devised and built massive pumping windmills, often
known as ”Dutch windmills.” This innovation not only solved their water-related problems,
but it also made an indelible impression on the worldwide landscape, distributing windmill
knowledge and laying the groundwork for future generations to use wind energy.
Since about 71% of the Earth’s surface is covered by water, while land accounts for ap-
proximately 29%, using the expanse of the ocean to improve wind power generation is a
really interesting idea. Nowadays, the offshore wind industry is moving further offshore
with larger water depths, which makes it more cost-eﬀicient to use floating substations to
support the topside structures, including the tower and the turbine, etc. Another tendency,
driven also by cost reduction, is that larger turbines are being considered. Therefore, both
the industry and the academy are making effects to design floating offshore wind turbines
(FOWTs) with larger capacities for a truly sustainable future.
1.1.1
Development history of FOWT
When the time came to enter the twenty-first century, floating wind turbines ushered in
rapid development because humans realized the value of green energy and the plentiful
energy provided by ocean wind. And it could be divided into several stages to describe the
development of the FOWTs.
• Early Research and Experiments:At this stage, the preliminary research is fo-
cusing on the utilization of traditional fixed-bottom wind turbines in shallow waters.
And several experimental small floating wind turbine projects have appeared at the
same time to evaluate the performance and dependability for offshore conditions.
The capacity of wind turbines is typically between a few hundred kilowatts and low
megawatts at that time, and there is still a long way to go to satisfy the needs of
businesses.

• Technological Advancements and Early Commercialization: From the mid-
2000s to early 2010s, large offshore wind turbines began to appear, and the capacity
of wind turbines gradually increased from several megawatts to high megawatts. The
concept of floating offshore wind turbines has attracted attention and is being explored
for deployment in the deep sea.
• Technological Innovations and Accelerated Commercialization: Since the
2010s to now, the construction of large commercial offshore wind farms has led to
the further development of floating wind turbine technology. And innovative designs
of floating wind turbines are emerging, including various types of floating platforms,
mooring systems, and control technologies. Wind turbine capacity continues to in-
crease, with the introduction of larger capacity floating wind turbines in the tens
of megawatts. At present, the largest floating wind turbine is the MySE 16.0-242
16MW floating wind turbine launched by MingYang Smart Energy[1], which has been
installed and put into use in China in the first half of 2023.
1.1.2
Types of FOWT Platform
There are several types of floating platforms to satisfy the different geographical conditions
and demands[2]. The key types of FOWT platforms are shown in Fig1, including semi-
submersible, spar, barge and tension-leg platform.

Semi-submersible
Semi-submersible floating wind turbines are partially submerged underwater and are kept
in balance by buoyancy and anchoring systems. This design is more flexible to adapt to
changes in waves and wind.
Spar
This type of floating wind turbine uses a long columnar structure as a platform, and uses

Barge
A barge is a flat-bottomed boat with a large water plane area. It is characterized by a small

Tension-leg platform(TLP)
A TLP is characterized by an excess of buoyancy and is pulled down into the water by

1.1.3
Neccesarry of analysis of IEA 15MW FOWT
The International Energy Agency-15-240-RWT 15-megawatt(IEA 15MW) floating wind tur-
bine is designed by the University of Maine (UMaine). And The semisubmersible is a generic
steel version of the UMaine patented concrete floating foundation technology developed in
collaboration with the U.S. Department of Energy.
The offshore wind turbine assembly has grown substantially in recent years, with units
ranging from 8 to 12 MW becoming the standard. According to current trends, 15MW
floating wind turbines will be the main route for floating wind turbine commercialization in
the future. However, there are currently few hydrodynamic response analyses for the 15MW
floating wind turbines(FOWTs). Based on the project of the First FOWT Comparative
Study, it is particularly necessary to analyze the hydrodynamic response in the deep water
condition at the extreme environment of the FOWTs when it is coupled with the mooring
system and the focused wave will be introduced as the incident wave.

1.2
Object of the thesis
Figure 2: Floating offshore wind turbine reference coordinate system [5]
This project is totally based on the First FOWT Comparative Study from The Collaborative
Computational Project in Wave Structure Interaction(CCP-WSI) and High End Computing
Consortium in Wave Structure Interaction(HEC-WSI). Several experiment cases consider
the hydrostatic and hydrodynamic response of a 1:70 scale model of the IEA 15MW reference
wind turbine (IEA-15-240-RWT) [6]and UMaine VolturnUS-S semi-submersible platform [7]
has been applied. And all the data including the free decay time series of the body, and
focus wave components are given.
During this full study process, the high-fidelity hydrodynamic model in commercial CFD
software StarCCM+ will be developed for an IEA 15MW FOWT. The model test data will
be available from the 1st FOWT Comparative Study to this project and will be used to
validate the hydrodynamic model. The results will also be compared with a high-fidelity
model from SESAM, which is commonly applied by the industry in design and analysis.
Through this project, it not only provides a better understanding of the analysis of the
FOWT platform in the industry area through some specific software e.g. SIMA, HydroD,
GeniE but also can have a guide for the mooring system modelling and apply for the force
coupling for the first order and second order wave theory. In addition, this project serves
as an illustration of the comparison between CFD and hydrodynamics applications.

1.4
Numerical Modeling tools
For this study, the numerical modelling tools could be divided into two parts. The first
one is the SESAM software package, which contains GeniE, HydroD, Sima and other tools.
And another one is the Starccm+. The utilization of SESAM is prevalent in both static and
dynamic analyses of marine structures. The hydrodynamic analysis of this study of the IEA
15MW floating offshore wind turbines (FOWT) primarily relies on the potential flow theory,
with adjustments made to account for viscous forces in order to enhance the accuracy of
the obtained results. One notable benefit of this approach is its rapid calculation speed and
reasonably high level of accuracy. However, it is important to acknowledge that there are
also evident drawbacks associated with this method. The process of fluid separation and
the impact of turbulence on the surface of the platform has not been adequately addressed.
Contrarily, Starccm+ is a prevalent computational fluid dynamics (CFD) tool extensively
employed in the field. It possesses the capability to account for the impact of turbulence
and fluid categorization on object reaction, hence enabling precise numerical calculations.
In contrast to Sesam, this alternative exhibits a protracted calculation cycle.

connected to the rest of the Sesam system through the Sesam Interface Files. It may either
be used as a stand-alone tool or as one of several activities in a Sesam Manager workflow.

Half geometry model applied in Starccm+
To conserve computational resources and reduce computational time, a strategy involving
the utilization of a half-geometry model and the application of boundary conditions based
on symmetry planes was employed..
This approach leverages the inherent symmetry of the system by performing calculations
on only one-half of the geometry, effectively halving the computational workload. More-
over, the implementation of symmetry plane boundary conditions accurately emulates the
behaviour of the entire system(albeit at the expense of neglecting discrepancies in the
shedding of vortices from the upstream and central columns). By adopting this methodol-
ogy, a significant reduction in computational complexity and resource demands has been
achieved, all while maintaining the physical accuracy of the problem. This technique proves

3.2.1.1
Panel model
The panel model is a computational method for simulating the dynamics and behaviour
of objects immersed in a fluid environment. The proposed modelling approach consists of
dividing the surface of the platform into a set of discrete panels and then evaluating the
pressure and forces acting on each panel using principles of potential flow theory. By ex-
pressing the interconnection among panels as a set of linear equations, predictions about the
properties of objects in the fluid can be made. Nonetheless, it is important to acknowledge
that panel models rely on the assumption of potential flow and do not take into account
factors such as viscosity and turbulence methods.

3.2.1.2
Morison model
For the geometry structure that is applied in this study, the viscous force is a neglectable
part of the total loads. As explained before, the panel model is based on potential flow
theory, and will not solve the drag force problem. Hence, in the practical case, the Mori-
son model will be introduced to combine with the panel model for solving the whole problem.
Since the Froude-Krylov force has been calculated by the panel model, the inertia force part
in Morison’s Equation should be 0 to satisfy that only the drag force component can be get
from that equation.

with a minimum value of 0, but even if Cm takes the smallest value, the F-K force will still
have an effect on the final result. Therefore, the method adopted here is to convert the
Morrison model into thin beams with a positively small cross-section, so that the F-K force
calculated by the Morrison equation can be close to zero and will not affect the correct

5
Conclusion
The hydrodynamic analysis of the IEA 15MW FOWT has yielded valuable conclusions re-
garding the wave loads effect, dynamic response and the numerical modelling method. And
it also presents an example for analyzing the dynamic response of offshore structures by the
high-fidelity models in CFD software and industrial software.
First, the interaction between the floating structure and the fluid plays a crucial role in
determining its dynamic response. Hydrodynamic forces acting on the platform, such as
drag, inertia, and added mass effects, must be carefully analyzed and incorporated into the
numerical modelling process. Therefore, it is not enough to apply the potential flow theory
alone to solve the response of the floating body under the focused wave, and the Morrison
Equation should be included in the scope of use to supplement the viscous drag force on
the object. At the same time, considering the lower natural frequency of the floating body
and the mooring system itself, second-order forces such as slow drift force also need to be
included.
Secondly, by comparing the motion response of the floating body under the first-order force
and the second-order force, it can be found that the low-frequency characteristics of the
mooring and the structure itself can affect the motion of the floating body, causing the
floating body to produce slow drifting motion after experiencing waves
In addition, for high-fidelity modelling in software, various properties of the fluid should be
fully considered. In order to capture the wave motion in the starccm+ model, the grid near
the free surface needs to be refined to cope with the constant change of wave elevation, and
at the same time try to ensure that each wave height contains at least 20 grids to ensure
the accuracy of wave capture. Furthermore, the fluid separation phenomenon that occurs
near the sharp corner of the pontoon should be considered.
By comparing the data from different software, it can be found that compared to the results
from Sima which only relies on hydrodynamics, the results of starccm+ are more accurate,
because it takes into account the factors of turbulence characteristics around the floating
body, fluid separation and other characteristics. Therefore, it is feasible to use the CFD
method to simulate and verify the motion of ocean structures.
5.1
Limitations in this study
Given the time limits of the actual work and the characteristics of the software that have
been applied, there are some imperfections in this study, which are as follows:
• Firstly, during the numerical modelling process in Sima, the catenary system is se-
lected to model the mooring system. However, the dynamic characteristics of this
kind of mooring line will be ignored after it coupling with the floating body. And
makes the accuracy of results be affected. In fact, to make the dynamic response of
the mooring line be included, the case with the mooring system modelled by the slen-
der system has been applied, but because of time limitations it has not been finished,
and this part will continue to be worked in the next stage.
54
==PAGE:62==
Hydrodynamic modeling of an IEA 15MW floating offshore wind turbine August 20, 2023
• Secondly, for the mooring system established in starccm+. Due to the inability of
the ground to provide support for the contact of the mooring line, it was necessary
to address the excessive pretension of the mooring line. Consequently, adjustments
were made to the mass property of the mooring line, resulting in some effects on the
dynamics of the system in the surge direction. In certain instances, the establishment
of contact between the anchor line and the ground can be facilitated by employing
the Discrete Element Method (DEM). And this function will be learned and applied
in the next study stage. In the current study, in order to mitigate the impact of rope
mass changes on surge motion, a spring coupling approach has been utilized to replace
the mooring line solely along the y-axis for the provision of restoring force.
5.2
Future work
Theoretically speaking, it is imprecise to model the half platform through the symmetry
plane boundary condition, because the vortex shedding of the upstream column and the
central column must be asymmetric. However, considering that this study is based on the
model scale, and the fluid separation caused by the corner of the pontoons is dominant, so
the error caused by unsymmetric vortex shedding was ignored in the numerical modelling.
Therefore, in future work, the numerical modelling of complete model-scale platforms and
full-scale objects will be the main aspects of in-depth analysis.
