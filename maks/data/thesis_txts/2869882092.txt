Abstract
The transition towards a renewable-based power system increases the need for eﬀicient balancing solutions
to manage variability and uncertainty in electricity generation.
This thesis investigates the role of storage
technologies in the European balancing market, focusing on their impact on market dynamics, price formation,
and system adequacy.
The investigated storage technologies in this thesis involve batteries, pumped hydro and seasonal reservoir-
based hydro. To represent these storage technologies in the balancing market, a mathematical formulation is
developed to model their dynamics, ensuring a realistic representation of storage behavior while accounting for
the influence of day-ahead scheduling decisions. This formulation is implemented in the balancing model within
the Balancing Tool Chain (BTC). Using the BTC, simulations are performed for scenario years 2030, 2040, and
2050 under different storage deployment setups of the storage technologies.
The results show that storage technologies will play a major role in the balancing market, providing a large
share of both up- and down-regulation. Over time, batteries emerge as the dominant storage technology, a trend
strongly linked to the increasing battery capacities used as input to the model. With increased deployment,
storage helps mitigate inadequacies by reducing both the magnitude and frequency of unresolved imbalances.
This leads to more eﬀicient use of transmission capacity, improving cross-border balancing and reinforcing the
benefits of a European-wide market clearing platform, such as MARI. The CO2 impact of storage varies across
technologies, but in combination, storage reduces emissions by replacing fossil-fueled up-regulation. Addition-
ally, storage lowers system costs by reducing balancing prices and limiting extreme price fluctuations. However,
these cost benefits diminish when accounting for battery degradation.
Future work should focus on enhancing the integration of balancing market operations into long-term investment
decisions by incorporating feedback on inadequate balancing capacity. This will ensure that future storage
and generation expansion accounts for balancing flexibility needs. Additionally, this thesis does not consider
hydrogen storage or electric vehicles (EVs), both of which could play a significant role in future balancing
markets. Future work should also explore their potential contributions to system flexibility and their interactions
with existing storage technologies.

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Motivation
The European Union aims to achieve net-zero greenhouse gas emissions by 2050, leading to major changes in
the electricity sector. The share of variable renewable energy sources (VRES) is increasing, with wind and
solar expected to generate up to 70% of Europe’s electricity by 2050 [67].
As VRE penetration increases,
forecasting errors will lead to larger power imbalances that must be resolved through balancing markets. In
response, balancing markets play an increasingly critical role in maintaining grid stability, ensuring eﬀicient
resource allocation, and enabling cross-border electricity trade. To accommodate these changes, traditional
market frameworks must evolve to support faster response times, decentralized generation, and greater reliance
on short-term flexibility [65].
Storage technologies are widely recognized as a key enabler of this transition, enhancing system resilience,
supporting grid stability, and facilitating the eﬀicient integration of renewable energy in balancing markets.
However, their participation in balancing markets remains an area of ongoing development, requiring improved
methodologies to quantify their value and assess their interactions with existing market structures. By providing
fast and flexible responses to imbalances, storage technologies can complement conventional balancing reserves
and enhance system resilience. Understanding the role of storage in balancing markets is crucial for system
operators and policymakers, as it directly affects market eﬀiciency, security of supply, and the overall cost
of integrating renewable energy. While various energy system models exist, many do not fully capture the
operational constraints and market participation of storage in balancing markets [66, 55].
Accurately representing storage technologies in balancing market models is essential to assess their contribution
to flexibility services. Proper integration of storage into balancing market frameworks can provide valuable
insights into its interaction with other flexibility resources, its impact on price volatility, and its role in cross-
border electricity exchange.
1.2
Objective and Research Questions
The objective of this Master’s thesis is to assess the role of storage for flexibility services in European balancing
markets by developing and integrating a mathematical model of storage into the DTU Balancing Tool Chain.
The mathematical model will encompass multiple relevant storage technologies and will be implemented within
the balancing model in the DTU Balancing Tool Chain.
The integrated model will be used to analyze the value of storage technologies in future European electricity
balancing markets. Additionally, this thesis will investigate the consequences of boundary conditions in the Day-

Scope and Limitations
This thesis focuses on assessing the role of storage in European balancing markets by integrating a mathematical
representation of storage technologies into an existing optimization framework.
To limit the scope of the study, the following main assumptions have been made:
• The analysed storage technologies include only Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS), Pumped Storage
Hydropower (PSH), and Seasonal Storage Hydropower (SSH).
• The study does not consider sector coupling. Demand-side flexibility, electric vehicles (EVs), power-to-gas
(P2G), hydrogen storage, heat pumps, and other cross-sector interactions are not included.
• This thesis does not consider electrotechnical properties such as voltage stability, reactive power, and
system inertia.
• The state of charge (SOC) of storage technologies follows a simplified linear relationship with power
output. Effects related to temperature, degradation, self-discharge, or other nonlinear dynamics are not
included.

market modeled in Balmorel. It is assumed that investment decisions from the Capacity Development

offset by 5 years compared to the Capacity Development model.
• The balancing market model in BTC represents intra-hour balancing of mFRR.

2.1
Electricity Markets in Europe
The European electricity market operates through several distinct platforms, each designed to facilitate the trad-
ing of electricity over different time horizons and for various purposes. These markets provide mechanisms for
long-term energy planning, short-term adjustments, and real-time balancing, ensuring the reliable and eﬀicient
distribution of electricity across the region [32]. This section provides an overview of the current framework of
electricity markets, future prospects of electrcity markets and the requirements related to intergrating balancing
markets across Europe.
2.1.1
Current electricity markets in Europe
The major electricity markets in Europe include the futures, day-ahead, intraday, balancing, and ancillary
services markets. The existing framework, structure, and operation of each of these markets will briefly be
dicsussed in relation to the current market structures.
Figure 1: Timeframes of electricity markets in Europe based on [32] and [21].
Forward and Futures Market
The Forward and Futures Markets both involve the trading of electricity contracts for delivery at a future date,
anging from days to several years before delivery. It is primarily used for hedging against price volatility and
facilitating long-term planning [9].
3
==PAGE:22==

The Futures Market deals with standardized contracts traded on exchanges like the European Energy Exchange
(EEX). These contracts are primarily financially settled, meaning that most do not result in physical delivery.
The market is cleared through centralized clearinghouses, which act as intermediaries to ensure that both sides
of the contract are honored. Prices are continuously updated based on expected future supply and demand
conditions. In the Nordic region, participants engage in the futures market through platforms such as EEX to
manage long-term price risks and hedge against price fluctuations [36, 37]. The Forward Market operates through
over-the-counter (OTC) contracts, which allow for greater flexibility in terms of delivery volume, timing, and
location. These contracts are often physically settled, meaning electricity is delivered as per the contract terms.
The clearing process in forward markets is bilateral, meaning it is directly handled between the contracting
parties, without the use of a central clearinghouse [64, 7].
Day-Ahead Market
The Day-Ahead Market is a key electricity market where trades are made for delivery on the following day.
Market participants submit bids and offers for each hour of the next day, and prices are set based on the
equilibrium between supply and demand.
The Day-Ahead Market is cleared using an auction mechanism. The market opens the day before delivery
and closes around noon, at which point all bids and offers are matched to set hourly prices for the next day.
All bids and offers for each hour are submitted and aggregated, and the market clearing price is determined
by the intersection of the supply and demand curves. The Day-Ahead Market handles substantial volumes of
energy, often measured in terawatt-hours (TWh), and serves as the main platform for scheduling electricity
across Europe. The Nordic countries participate in the Day-Ahead Market through Nord Pool [49, 53, 50], .
Intraday Market
The Intraday Market provides continuous trading of electricity on the same day as delivery. It enables partici-
pants to adjust their positions after the Day-Ahead Market closes, providing flexibility to respond to real-time
changes in demand or generation [64]. Though smaller in volume compared to the Day-Ahead Market, the
Intraday Market handles significant energy transactions on a short-term basis, with trading occurring up until
an hour before the delivery period. Prices tend to be more volatile due to the immediacy of balancing real-time
supply and demand adjustments [51].
In contrast to the auction-based clearing in the Day-Ahead Market, the Intraday Market operates on financial
contracts agreed bilaterally between buyers and sellers, facilitated through a central platform. As a result, there
is no formal clearing mechanism in the Intraday Market. Instead, trades are continuously matched and cleared
as soon as the buyer and seller agree on terms. The Nordic countries actively participate in the intraday market
through Nord Pool [54, 52].
Balancing Market
The Balancing Market is managed by Transmission System Operators (TSOs) to maintain the real-time balance
between electricity supply and demand. Participants provide balancing services by adjusting their generation
or consumption to match real-time needs. Balancing services are categorized into different types of reserves
depending on the speed and nature of the response required. The main balancing services include Manual
Frequency Restoration Reserve (mFRR), Automatic Frequency Restoration Reserve (aFRR), and Frequency
Containment Reserve (FCR). The Nordic synchronous area distinguishes between FCR-N (Normal Frequency
Containment Reserve) and FCR-D (Frequency Containment Reserve for Disturbances) [25].
The Balancing Market operates in multiple stages:
4

• Reserve capacity procurement: TSOs procure balancing reserves in advance to ensure suﬀicient
flexibility for system balancing. This includes procurement of mFRR, aFRR and FCR capacity, which is
typically secured hours to days before real-time [21].
• Intra-Hour Balancing Energy Market: Providers with pre-procured mFRR capacity must submit
mFRR energy bids, while other participants may submit voluntary mFRR energy bids. TSOs select and
schedule these bids before real-time (intra-hour) based on forecasted imbalances. The activation of mFRR
balancing energy is cleared using marginal pricing, where the price is set by the last activated bid. [31].
• Real-time activation of reserves: In real-time, reserves are activated based on system needs to sta-
bilize the system and maintain frequency balance. FCR and aFRR respond automatically to frequency
deviations, providing immediate system stabilization.
2.1.2
Future Development of Balancing Markets
The European Union’s target for carbon neutrality by 2050 drives the push for higher shares of renewable energy
in the electricity grid. To accommodate this, electricity markets will need to evolve to support the large-scale
integration of variable renewable energy sources (VRES) like wind and solar while maintaining grid stability
and promoting market eﬀiciency [32].
The Electricity Balancing Guideline (EBGL) [19] is a regulatory framework established by the European Union
to harmonize and improve the operation of balancing markets across member states. It is part of the broader
effort to create a more integrated and eﬀicient internal electricity market in Europe. The EBGL was adopted
by the European Commission in 2017 and sets out rules and procedures for the operation of balancing mar-
kets. The EBGL pushed for the integration, harmonization, and optimization of balancing markets in Europe
by setting specific guidelines for how balancing energy should be exchanged across borders and establishing
common platforms for the exchange of balancing energy. This initiative seeks to improve the eﬀiciency and
coordination of real-time balancing actions by harmonizing processes and facilitating cross-border cooperation.
This initiative enables TSOs to access a wider pool of resources, ensuring a more cost-effective and reliable
response to imbalances in the grid. A major step toward this goal is the implementation of platforms like MARI
(Manually Activated Reserves Initiative).
MARI is the platform for exchanging manual Frequency Restoration Reserves (mFRR) across European TSOs.
It standardizes how mFRR is procured and activated, allowing for eﬀicient cross-border use of balancing energy.
The MARI platform is already operational in several countries, with ongoing efforts to extend participation
across all EU TSOs [20, 33]. By streamlining the procurement and activation of balancing reserves, MARI
enhances cross-border market eﬀiciency and standardization [31].
Participation in the mFRR market is standardized through the mFRR Implementation Framework (mFRRIF)
by the Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators (ACER). MARI introduces several changes compared
to the current Nordic setup, including:
• Reducing activation time from 15 to 12.5 minutes,
• Shortening gate closure time from 45 to 25 minutes before delivery,
• Lowering the minimum bid size from 5 MW to 1 MW,
• Adopting 15-minute granularity.
These updates align with European standards, harmonizing product definitions and enhancing cross-border
market integration. Further details on the MARI framework defined by ACER in the mFRRIF and mFRR
product requirements are provided in Appendix A.

2.2
Overview of Energy Storage Technologies
Energy storage technologies play a critical role in providing flexibility services and ensuring reliability in balanc-
ing markets. According to [66], the increasing penetration of intermittent renewable energies such as wind and
solar necessitates robust solutions for energy storage to manage the variability and maintain grid stability. This
section explores various storage technologies and assesses their potential roles in future European electricity
balancing markets.
Li-Ion Battery Energy Storage Systems (BESS)
Battery energy storage systems (BESS) is a type of electrochemical storage system. This section focuses on
Lithium-Ion (Li-Ion) batteries, the most widely used type of battery storage, to simplify the discussion on specific
eﬀiciency values and performance characteristics. Li-Ion batteries are known for their high energy density, very
high ramp rates, and instantaneous response times, making them essential for managing short-term energy
fluctuations. However, they come with higher costs compared to other storage technologies [60]. Additionally,
concerns exist about resource scarcity, particularly regarding materials like lithium and cobalt, and the life
cycle environmental impacts of battery production and disposal, which are important considerations for their
long-term sustainability [68, 24].
Pumped Storage Hydropower (PSH)
Pumped Storage Hydro (PSH) stores energy by pumping water from a lower reservoir to an upper reservoir
when excess power is available. During periods of high demand or low power generation, water is released back
into the lower reservoir through turbines, generating electricity. This is a well-established technology known for
its reliability, long lifecycle, and large-scale energy storage capacity. It offers considerable capacity for energy
storage with relatively high eﬀiciency and moderate costs, making it suitable for both short and medium-term
storage applications. PSH requires significant elevation differences between the upper and lower reservoirs and
is therefore only suitable in regions with appropriate topographies, such as mountainous areas. PHS is the
most widely implemented large-scale energy storage technology, representing 99% of the world’s capacity for
large-scale energy storage [44, 60].
Seasonal Storage Hydropower (SSH)
In this thesis, Seasonal Storage Hydropower (SSH) is used as a common term for reservoir-based hydro power,
including cascaded hydro plants with significant storage capacity.
Run-of-river hydro plants and cascaded
systems with minimal storage are not considered storage technologies and are excluded from this classification.
Unlike Pumped Storage Hydropower (PSH), SSH plants depend on natural inflows rather than grid electricity
to fill reservoirs. While this reduces operational costs, it makes them vulnerable to hydrological variability and
limits their ability to store surplus energy.
SSH plants store potential energy in reservoirs created by dams, using seasonal water inflows like rainfall
or snowmelt for replenishment.
Electricity is generated by releasing water through turbines, making SSH
suitable for long-term and seasonal energy storage. These plants offer high eﬀiciency (typically over 90%), long
operational lifespans, and the ability to provide flexibility services by quickly adjusting power output through
turbine flow control. However, SSH plants have high capital costs and are only viable in regions with abundant
hydropower resources [35, 43].
Hydrogen Storage
Hydrogen storage involves converting electrical energy into hydrogen through the process of electrolysis, where
water is split into hydrogen and oxygen. The hydrogen is then stored in tanks or underground caverns and
6
==PAGE:25==

used later to generate electricity via fuel cells or combustion turbines when needed. This storage method is
particularly advantageous for long-term and seasonal energy storage, as hydrogen can be stored for extended
periods without significant energy loss. However, hydrogen storage faces challenges such as high capital costs
and reduced eﬀiciency due to the multiple energy conversion steps involved [40, 44].
Flywheels
Flywheel energy storage systems store electrical energy in the form of rotational kinetic energy in a spinning
mass, typically a rotor. Energy is stored by accelerating a flywheel to very high speeds and extracted by drawing
off energy to slow it down. With extremely high ramp rates, flywheels provide frequency regulation and power
quality management, making them ideal for very short-duration services. However, they are limited by lower
energy capacity and higher costs [40].
Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES)
Compressed Air Energy Storage (CAES) systems store energy by compressing air in an underground cavern
during off-peak times. When power is needed, the compressed air is released to drive a turbine connected to
a generator. CAES provides a medium to long-term energy storage solution with moderate ramp rates. It
offers larger storage capacity at lower costs and is particularly effective in geographic regions with suitable
underground formations [44].
Thermal Storage
Thermal energy storage involves storing heat or cold to be used later for heating or cooling applications, or for
power generation. It can include a range of technologies, from simple hot water tanks to advanced materials
storing heat as latent energy. Due to the thermal inertia and the materials used, these systems typically exhibit
the slowest ramp rates among the discussed technologies. Thermal storage is usually used for long-term energy
management, being the least expensive option but offering lower eﬀiciency [13].

2.3
Energy Modelling Tools
Energy system modeling has developed significantly in response to the growing complexity of modern power
systems, particularly with the increasing penetration of VRES. Various energy modeling tools exist designed
to address different aspects of energy systems. However, relatively few models simulate multiple electricity
markets, including the day-ahead, intraday, balancing, and real-time control stages.
Fattahi et al. [38] review the modeling challenges for low-carbon energy systems, highlighting the increasing
need for flexibility, storage, and the integration of VRES. The paper highlights that not all models are equipped
to simulate multiple electricity markets, including day-ahead, intraday, and balancing markets, which is a
significant limitation as balancing markets are essential for real-time grid stability. Many energy models are
tailored to answer specific research or policy questions. For example, some models are adept at simulating day-
ahead markets but may not fully capture real-time balancing market intricacies. Tools that focus on long-term
investment decisions, such as SIFRE, tend to overlook real-time operational aspects [39]. This specialization
leaves a gap in tools that are capable of simulating all electricity market stages harmoniously. Some tools have
the capability to simulate both investment and operational decisions, but few incorporate balancing markets.
Several reviews, such as those by Ouwerkerk et al. [56] and Candas et al. [14], focus on open-source models
8

and their adaptability for renewable integration but do not investigate in depth the specific markets simulated.
Although the open-source movement has increased transparency and flexibility in energy system modeling, de-
tailed simulations of interconnected markets, such as the day-ahead, intraday, and real-time balancing markets,
are often lacking. Open-source models such as oemof offer flexibility but often require customization to simulate
detailed market operations [56].
Existing literature on energy modeling tools generally focuses on flexibility, renewable integration, or method-
ological advancements rather than on simulating multiple electricity markets simultaneously [38]. This indicates
a gap in current research: while many tools provide valuable insights for either operational or investment pur-
poses, few achieve comprehensive simulation across multiple interconnected electricity markets.
2.3.1
Energy Modelling Tools Incorporating the Balancing Market
The paper by Ringkjøb et al. [59] provides a comprehensive review of 75 modelling tools designed to simulate
and analyze energy systems with high shares of VRES. This review offers an updated overview of the available
tools, their capabilities, and their application in various contexts, providing valuable insights to researchers
and system planners seeking to model energy systems with high shares of VRES. The authors categorize the
modelling tools into various types based on their purpose, approach, and methodology. The approach and
methodology include spatiotemporal resolution and technological and economic characteristics. The purpose of
the tools range from short-term system operation and dispatch optimization to long-term investment strategies.
The paper highlights the need for tools that can handle both short-term operational challenges (e.g., real-
time balancing of supply and demand) and long-term planning (e.g., infrastructure investment under uncertain
future conditions). Some models combine operational and investment decision-making, providing an integrated
approach to both short-term and long-term challenges. Examples include Balmorel and OSeMOSYS. However,
these models do not take into account the balancing market operation, only the day-ahead operation.
Out of the many tools reviewed by Ringkjøb et al. [59], only a few have the capability to simulate multiple steps
of the power system operation including the balancing markets. These include COMPOSE, DER-CAM, ETM
(2), PLEXOS, and AURORAxmp. These tools are designed to simulate market processes separately, making it
challenging to model the full range of interactions between different electricity markets. Additionally, most of the
tools that simulates the balancing market lacks the integration of long-term investment planning. Ringkjøb et al.
point out that models often focus on one part of the energy system, leaving gaps in the harmonization of multi-
market simulations. Among the tools capable of simulating the balancing market in the paper by Ringkjøb et
al. [59], PLEXOS is by far the tool that simulated the balancing market most detailed. Furthermore, PLEXOS
is the only tool in the paper that is capable of long-term investment planning and simulating multiple steps of
the power system operation including day-ahead, balancing and real-time dynamics.
PLEXOS
PLEXOS excels in simulating real-time energy markets, including detailed models of futures, day-ahead, in-
traday, balancing, and ancillary services markets.
In terms of balancing markets, PLEXOS is capable of
representing balancing energy and reserve markets with high detail, covering aspects like market clearing (for
both balancing capacity and energy), activation of reserves, and market participant bidding strategies. Fur-
thermore, it can model frequency control reserves (FCR), automatic frequency restoration reserves (aFRR),
manual frequency restoration reserves (mFRR), and replacement reserves (RR), providing a powerful platform
for detailed short-term operational and electricity market simulations.
PLEXOS also includes detailed modeling of storage technologies, such as batteries and pumped hydro, within
the balancing market.
Batteries are represented with charge and discharge eﬀiciencies, cycling constraints,
and state-of-charge tracking, allowing them to provide reserve capacity and energy arbitrage. Pumped hydro

storage is modeled as a reservoir-based system with pumping and generation constraints, enabling flexible energy
shifting in response to market signals [29] [30].
However, the PLEXOS tool focuses on real-time operations, economic dispatch, and co-optimization of energy
markets and it may fall short in certain areas critical for long-term planning, particularly when analyzing the
future impact of high shares of vRES on the grid. PLEXOS does not inherently prioritize the system-wide, high-

HIREPS
A case study by Ortner and Totschnig [55] introduces the HiREPS (High Resolution Renewable Energy Pathways
Simulation) model, which is specifically designed to tackle the long-term impacts of renewable energy integration
on electricity systems. HiREPS excels in modeling forecast errors for wind, solar, and other vRES, generating
high-frequency demand time series that reflect the evolving generation mix.
By simulating the interaction
between supply, demand, and transmission with a strong focus on renewables, HiREPS allows for an in-depth
analysis of how balancing markets will evolve in the future.
The paper by Ortner and Totschnig [55] addresses the scarcity of models simulating the balancing market
by employing the large-scale mixed integer optimization model HiREPS to analyze the future relevance of
balancing markets.
The HiREPS model simulates day-ahead, intra-day, and balancing markets, offering a
detailed comparison of their relevance. By incorporating historical data to model forecast errors in renewable
energy generation and consumption, the study provides a comprehensive analysis of how these errors may evolve
and affect market dynamics by 2030. The results indicate that variable renewable energy sources, especially wind
power, are expected to be a major driver of demand for balancing energy. Additionally, storage technologies
are highlighted as crucial participants in the balancing market due to their inherent flexibility.
The HiREPS model include storage technologies like pumped hydro storage, compressed air energy storage
(CAES), Power-to-Gas (P2G) and hot water storage. These technologies are part of the larger optimization
framework for managing supply and demand over longer time horizons. However, for the balancing market sim-
ulations in the case study by Ortner and Totschnig [55], pumped hydro storage is the only storage technology
that is modeled and actively used. The pumped hydro storage plants are represented with natural inflows and
reservoir levels allowing HiREPS to account for the state of the reservoirs, reflecting a detailed and realistic
treatment of hydro resources. In the case study, the HiREPS model does not allow for batteries to actively par-
ticipate in the balancing market. Small-scale batteries in households and electric cars are indirectly considered
through adjustments to the hourly load curve, reflecting their influence on overall demand rather than their
direct participation in balancing markets.
This literature review did not identify other relevant papers directly addressing balancing market models. Hence,
there is a significant research gap in this area of balancing market models, underscoring the need to enhance
balancing market models, particularly in their capacity to integrate energy storage technologies, which are
crucial for future energy systems.

This chapter will describe the methodology behind the Balancing Tool Chain (BTC) and its underlying as-
sumptions. First, the entire tool chain, including all submodels, will be presented. Afterwards, the main focus
will be on the Balancing Model, which is the specific submodel that forms the core of this thesis.
3.1
Overview of the Balancing Tool Chain
Balancing power systems becomes more challenging with the increasing share of variable renewable energy
sources within these systems. The power system involves several interdependent subprocesses that interact with
various electricity markets. In order to effectively balance power systems with a high share of renewable energy, it
is valuable to have a tool that encompasses all these subprocesses. The DTU Balancing Tool Chain is designed for
this purpose. It integrates processes ranging from the day-ahead spot market and hour-ahead balancing markets
to real-time dynamic simulations. This comprehensive approach is essential, as each subprocess influences the
others, and addressing them in isolation can lead to ineﬀiciencies and inaccuracies.
An overview of the structure of the DTU Balancing Tool Chain can be seen in Figure 3.

The market processes included in the BTC are the day-ahead (DA) market, the balancing market and area
and frequency control based on the available VRE forecasts at the given point in time. Notably, the intraday
market is not modeled in the BTC framework. In the BTC, the DA market is modeled in Balmorel, which
uses DA VRE forecasts calculated from CorRES with hourly resolution to determine the available wind power.
Balmorel creates an hourly generation schedule based on these forecasts. This schedule is then used as input to
the balancing market model, which compares the DA wind dispatches with updated hour-ahead (HA) forecasts
to calculate the system’s imbalance. The optimization algorithm of the balancing model then determines the
necessary generation adjustments to minimize this imbalance. Both the day-ahead and balancing model use the
Unit Commitment (UC) methodology to ensure realistic generation behavior, considering ramping and other
UC constraints. Finally, the Area and Frequency Control model analyzes the deployment of automatic reserves,
using real-time VRE generation and generation plans from the balancing model to simulate frequency deviations
and automatic reserve deployment.
3.1.1
VRE generation
The VRE generation time series is simulated using CorRES (Correlations in Renewable Energy Sources), which
is a tool capable of simulating both wind and solar generation by using a combination of meteorological time
series and stochastic simulations to provide consistent VRE generation and forecast error time series [23]. In the
BTC, CorRES is used to generate day-ahead VRE forecasts with hourly resolution, hour-ahead forecasts with
a 5 minutes resolution, and real time VRE generation with 5 minutes resolution. The VRE forecasts simulated
by CorRES in this framework are solely based on uncertainties in wind power forecast.
3.1.2
Day-Ahead Market Model
The day-ahead market is modeled in the Balmorel energy system model, which is a partial equilibrium model of
the electricity and heating sector. Balmorel is an open-source model coded in GAMS modeling language, and
operates as a multi-period model with flexible time and area resolution [57, 58] .
In the BTC, the Capacity Development model is first run as a BB2 optimization in Balmorel.
This is an
economic dispatch and investment run, which optimizes the investment in generation, storage and transmission
capacity for a whole year using time aggregation with the objective of minimizing total costs. Then the Storage
and Maintenance model is run, which is a BB1 optimization in Balmorel that solves the economic dispatch and
operations for a whole year using time aggregation while minimizing total costs. The Storage and Maintenance
model uses the capacities from the Capacity Development model as input, and the output is planned mainte-
nance, storage contents, and fuel allocation. Subsequently, the outputs from the BB2 and BB1 runs are used
as input to the Day-Ahead Market model, which is a BB3 run in Balmorel. This model solves the economic
dispatch iteratively for each season (week) with 168 time segments per season corresponding to an hourly res-
olution. The economic dispatch and operations decisions are optimized using the known capacities from the
previous runs with the objective of minimizing total costs. In this day-ahead model, Balmorel creates an hourly
generation schedule (and updated storage contents) using unit commitment based on based on demand forecasts
and the day-ahead VRE forecasts from CorRES.
3.1.3
Balancing Market Model
The balancing model is coded in GAMS modeling language and takes as input the hour-ahead wind forecast
simulations from CorRES, with a temporal resolution of five minutes, along with outputs from Balmorel. The
output from Balmorel to the Balancing Market model includes the generation and transmission capacity as well
as the hourly generation schedule, unit status and tie line flows. The hourly day-ahead schedules are converted
to 5 minutes temporal resolution before being used as inputs to the Balancing model. The balancing model

actual market conditions. The model computes the necessary adjustments of flexible generators to address
imbalances caused by discrepancies between day-ahead wind dispatches and updated wind generation forecasts

The primary output of the Balancing Market model is the adjusted operational schedules for generators, ensuring
that generation aligns with consumption on an intra-hour basis. The outcomes from one hour directly influence
the operational constraints and capabilities in subsequent hours, creating a continuous inter-hour linkage. The
adjustment of operational schedules accounts for the flexibility of units, allowing only those with suﬀicient
responsiveness to participate in the balancing market. Units unable to adjust within the hour-ahead foresight
have their schedules fixed based on the day-ahead dispatch.
3.1.4
Area and Frequency Control Dynamic Model
The dynamic model of area and frequency control ensures real-time grid stability by managing frequency de-
viations caused by power imbalances based on discrepancies between hour-ahead VRE forecasts and real-time
VRE generation. In real time, power imbalances affect system frequency, which is critical for maintaining sta-
bility. The model uses outputs from both the VRE generation models and the Balancing Market simulations as
inputs to enact control actions that maintain frequency within safe operational limits. The Area and Frequency
Control Model is out of scope of this thesis, and the principles of the model will not be further explained.

Chapter 5
Modeling of Storage
Given the findings of the State-of-the-art chapter and the Methodology in the Balancing Tool Chain, it is
recommended to include batteries, pumped hydro storage and seasonal reservoir-based hydro storage in the
Balancing model. This chapter will describe the mathematical formulation of the modeling of these storage
technologies. Afterwards, the implementation of the storage technologies into the existing framework of the
Balancing Model will be briefly described.
5.1
Mathematical Formulation
This section will first describe the common mathematical formulation of both batteries and pumped hydro as
the formulation of electrical storage. Afterwards, the formulation of the seasonal reservoir-based hydro storage
will be presented. The definition of all sets, parameters and decision variables can be seen in the Nomenclature.
The mathematical modeling presented in this section serves as an extension to the existing optimization problem
of the balancing model, which is defined by the objective function and constraints in Equations (1.1) to (1.21)
in Section 3.3.
5.1.1
Formulation of Electrical Storage
This section outlines a shared mathematical framework for modeling both batteries and pumped hydro units as
their fundamental working principles are similar. Both technologies increase their storage level by consuming
electricity (charging) and decrease it when generating electricity (discharging). The differences between these
technologies are captured through technology-specific parameters, such as eﬀiciency, maximum energy capacity,
and charge/discharge limits. In this way, the unified approach streamlines the modeling of electrical storage
technologies, allowing the same core formulation to be applied across different types of storage systems by
adjusting only the relevant parameter. Moreover, this framework ensures that other pure storage technologies
can be integrated into the Balancing Model in future work without requiring modifications to the core equations.
The available storage capacity of the electrical storage units in MWh is here referred to as the State-of-Charge
(SOC). Although SOC typically denotes the available storage capacity as a percentage of the rated capacity
for batteries, in this context it will represent the absolute storage capacity (in MWh) for both batteries and
pumped hydro.

6.3.1
S1 - Temporal Constraints on Storage Levels
This sensitivity analysis examines how the temporal constraints applied in the model affects the system behavior.
As the electrical storage units are modeled differently from the seasonal hydro power plants, this is done
separately for electrical storage and seasonal hydro storage, respectively.
Electrical Storage Units (Batteries and Pumped Hydro)
In the current modeling framework of batteries and pumped hydro, constraint (2.4) ensures that the SOC
returns to its day-ahead SOC at the first timestep of each day. Thereby, the batteries and pumped hydro
units are allowed to deviate from the day-ahead SOC (still considering the upper and lower SOC bounds) in
the remaining hours of the day. In this sensitivity analysis, the daily SOC constraint is changed from a daily
time horizon to an hourly time horizon. When the time horizon changes to hourly, the model becomes more
conservative ensuring that the balancing market does not affect the day-ahead market at all as the day-ahead
market is in hourly resolution and the changes within the hour do not affect the hourly schedule when returning
to its day-ahead SOC at each hour.

6.3.3
S3 - Bidding Prices of Batteries and Pumped Hydro
The bidding prices of batteries and pumped hydro were modified when implementing modeling of storage to
the model. This was done in order for the bidding price to reflect the cost related to up- and down-regulation
of batteries and pumped hydro, which use electricity for charging and discharging.
However, the bidding
prices for these electrical storage technologies have until now only been based on the eﬀiciency and the day-
ahead price, without accounting for other cost factors. For batteries in particular, degradation costs should
ideally be reflected in the bidding price, as frequent up- and down-regulation reduces battery lifetime leading
to more frequent re-investments in batteries. However, realistic modeling of degradation cost in the model
is computationally heavy as degradation is a complex, nonlinear function of factors such as SOC, depth of
discharge (DoD), and cycling frequency. Accurately capturing these dynamics requires detailed cycle aging
models or mixed-integer formulations, significantly increasing computational complexity and solver time. To
reflect the impact of degradation in a very simplified manner, this sensitivity analysis increases the bidding
price of batteries uniformly to represent the additional cost associated with capacity loss and shortened lifetime
due cell degradation.

Chapter 7
Discussion
The results indicate that storage technologies play a significant role in balancing markets. In 2030, Seasonal
Hydro, Pumped Hydro, and Batteries provide almost equal volumes of balancing services. As forecasted power
imbalances increase towards 2040 and 2050 due to increased VRE penetration, the demand for balancing
reserves grows. The results suggest that batteries take on a larger share of balancing services in later years,
while the contributions from seasonal hydro and pumped hydro remain unchanged. However, this trend is
directly linked to the input assumptions regarding available capacity. The model assumes an increase in battery
capacity over the scenario years, while hydro storage capacity remains constant. In reality, the deployment
of storage technologies will depend on economic and regulatory conditions, technological advancements, and
market incentives. Sensitivity analysis reveals that a reduction in available battery capacity directly impacts
its role in the balancing market, emphasizing the importance of realistic capacity assumptions and day-ahead
scheduling when modeling future electricity markets.
Additionally, the decision-making process of battery
owners in real-world markets is more complex than assumed in this study.
For instance, it may be more
economically attractive for battery operators to participate in multiple market segments and allocate capacity
for Frequency Containment Reserves (FCR). This study does not account for batteries participating in other
markets, which could significantly influence the availability and utilization of batteries.
Furthermore, this thesis does not consider other flexibility resources that could compete with the investigated
storage technologies in future balancing markets. Future balancing markets will likely be influenced by other
technologies and sectors that provide additional flexibility. Sector coupling is expected to play a central role in
balancing markets by integrating electricity, heating, and transportation, allowing for more dynamic flexibility.
Electrified industrial processes, district heating with large-scale heat pumps, and thermal energy storage can
adjust electricity consumption in response to market signals, reducing reliance on conventional storage solu-
tions. Demand-side flexibility from these sectors enables better alignment between electricity generation and
consumption, particularly in systems with high shares of variable renewables [2]. Additionally electric vehicles
(EVs) with vehicle-to-grid (V2G) capabilities could potentially be crucial flexibility resources that should be
explicitly considered in balancing market models. EVs can dynamically adjust their charging schedules and pro-
vide bidirectional energy flows, enabling them to support system balancing and reduce peak demand. Similarly,
hydrogen electrolysis, when strategically operated, can provide flexible demand by absorbing excess renewable
generation and shifting energy use to periods of lower demand. Comparing the role of batteries to other emerg-
ing flexibility options is necessary to ensure that future market designs account for the most cost-effective and
eﬀicient solutions.
Thereby, the finding of this thesis should be interpreted with caution, as they rely on several simplifying as-
sumptions. The model provides insights into potential trends within its predefined scope and given assumptions

7.1
Future Work
Future analyses should integrate strategic battery bidding to mitigate degradation and reinvestment costs.
Incorporating degradation costs directly into optimal scheduling could improve calendar aging effects and reduce
long-term costs [8, 45]. Degradation-aware scheduling methods, such as rainflow cycle counting or equivalent
full cycle tracking, could enhance economic eﬀiciency but come with increased computational complexity.
Additionally, future studies should expand the model scope to include sector coupling, demand-side flexibility,
hydrogen storage, heat pumps, and other emerging technologies that may shape the future balancing market.
This would provide a more comprehensive understanding of the flexibility landscape and ensure that the model
reflects the full range of options available for maintaining system stability in a renewable-dominated electricity
system .
Future research could also focus on integrating wind and solar curtailment within the balancing
model. By incorporating curtailment decisions, it would be possible to distinguish between actual balancing
power inadequacies and imbalances that could be mitigated through flexible renewable generation.
A drawback of the BTC structure is that capacities throughout the entire tool chain are determined based
on investment runs in Balmorel, which do not account for the balancing market. Consequently, the capacity
investments do not consider which technologies might be beneficial at finer time resolutions, where fast ramping is
particularly important. This leads to short-term storage technologies not being included in Balmorel’s optimal
investment decisions, which in turn affects the available technology capacities in the balancing model.
A
major improvement would be to introduce feedback mechanisms between the balancing model and the Capacity
Development model.

This would allow investment decisions to incorporate information about inadequate balancing capacity, ensuring
that additional flexible capacity is installed in the system when needed. Furthermore, the Day-Ahead market
model should be adapted to leave some capacity available for balancing services rather than committing all
flexible resources in advance. For instance, if storage technologies such as batteries and pumped hydro are
deployed, their state-of-charge (SOC) should ideally remain around 50% to maximize their ability to provide
both up- and down-regulation.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The main objective of this thesis was to assess the role of storage for flexibility services in European balancing
markets by integrating a mathematical model of storage into the balancing model of the Balancing Tool Chain.
The scope was limited by only considering batteries and hydro power plants.
The first step in addressing the main objective consisted in developing a mathematical model capable of repre-
senting storage in the balancing market. This was achieved by formulating a mathematical model for electrical
storage units, consisting of batteries and pumped hydro, as well as for seasonal storage hydro power plants. This
distinction in hydro power plant modeling was primarily made to ensure consistency with the way generators
are represented in the day-ahead model, as maintaining coherence across the entire tool chain is crucial.
When representing storage technologies in the the balancing market model, accurately updating the storage level
of each unit at every timestep is essential to realistically reflect available energy content. Additionally, day-ahead
scheduling of storage units must be carefully considered to prevent storage operation in the balancing market
from interfering with its day-ahead scheduled dispatch. However, adherence to the day-ahead schedule imposes
strict boundary conditions on storage operation in the balancing market, requiring additional constraints in
the modeling of storage that significantly increase computational time due to the high temporal resolution of
the balancing market. To mitigate this, certain constraints were relaxed, allowing for minor deviations that
improved computational eﬀiciency without substantially impacting results.
With the mathematical modeling framework in place, the main objective of this thesis could be addressed
by applying the developed model to analyze the role of storage in providing flexibility in future European
balancing markets. The main findings indicate that the investigated storage technologies will play a crucial
role in providing balancing services with batteries being the dominating technology in 2050 accounting for 41%
of the activated up-regulation and 53% of the activated down-regulation. This trend is primarily a result of
the assumptions regarding available storage capacity, where battery capacity increases over time, while hydro
storage remains constant.
The results show that the integration of storage significantly enhances both power and energy adequacy, which
becomes increasingly important in 2040 and 2050, as power imbalances escalate due to higher VRE penetration.
Seasonal hydro helps mitigate upward inadequacy by providing stored energy during periods of low generation,
while batteries are particularly effective in mitigating downward inadequacy. The findings highlight that a
well-balanced mix of storage technologies is essential to maintain system stability and reliability under a high-
renewables scenario.
The results demonstrate that integrating storage technologies enables a more dynamic use of interconnections,
leading to increased transmission utilization. By facilitating more eﬀicient power redistribution, storage re-

Appendix A
MARI Framework
The MARI platform introduces key features such as a 15-minute time resolution for bid submission and activa-
tion, along with shorter gate closure times of 25 minutes before delivery. These elements support harmonization
across European balancing markets and enable closer-to-real-time market operations. The requirements for
participating in mFRR balancing markets across Europe are standardized through the mFRR Implementation
Framework (mFRRIF) by the Agency for the Cooperation of Energy Regulators (ACER). The mFRRIF outlines
how the MARI platform operates, and the framework of MARI is shown in Figure 70.
Figure 70: Framework of MARI processes based on [33, 20]
The processes of MARI consists of Balancing Service Providers (BSPs) submitting their bids to the respective
TSOs, who then forward coherent bids to the mFRR platform within the gate closure at T-25 minutes (BSP
Gate Closure). TSOs also estimate their balancing demand and Cross-Zonal Capacity (CZC) by T-10 minutes
(TSO Gate Closure Time). The Activation Objective Function (AOF) of MARI then optimizes the clearing
of bids and demand, considering CZC limits, and communicates the selected bids to TSOs by T-7.5 minutes.
TSOs send activation signals to BSPs, who ramp up or down accordingly. Cross-border schedules and remaining
CZC are sent to TSOs for execution, followed by settlement of financial flows between TSOs.

activations, and can be activated at any point in time between two points of scheduled activation.

Methodology
B.1
Assumptions and Limitations in the Balancing Model
This section highlights key assumptions specific to the Balancing Model, including a discussion of how some of
these assumptions relate to the other models within the BTC.
B.1.1
Optimization period
The balancing model optimizes over a year, but is divided into 24 sub-models, each representing approximately
half a month. Each of these 24 sub-models is solved separately and there is no feedback between each of these
periods. This means that the balancing model has full foresight for approximately 2 weeks at a time and then
no foresight.
Figure 72: Optimization period and foresight of the Balancing Model compared to Balmorel.
B.1.2
Inputs to the model
• Data from CorRES: The forecast power imbalances used as input correspond to the forecast errors
between the DA VRE-forecast and HA VRE-forecast generated by the CorRES model. As the intraday
market is not modeled in the BTC, the forecast errors between the DA VRE forecast and the HA VRE
forecast are to be resolved solely by the balancing model. Thereby, the power imbalances to be resolved
in the balancing market in the BTC structure are more significant than in real-life as forecast errors
cannot be resolved continuously in the intraday market in this framework. The VRE forecasts simulated
by CorRES in this framework are solely based on wind power and do not consider uncertainties regarding
solar power or other VRE sources.
• Data from Day-Ahead model: All time series data input to the model are based on hourly DA-results
from Balmorel converted to 5-minutes intervals. This includes the power schedule, unit status of each

generator, transmission line flows and the day-ahead price.
There are some mismatches between the
results from Balmorel and the inputs used in balancing model. For instance, the transmission line flows
in Balmorel are restricted to 95% of the transmission capacity, while the transmission line flows in the
balancing model can be 100 % of the transmission capacity.
• Data from Capacity Development model: Static data input to the balancing model such as gener-
ation technology data (e.g. power capacity of each generator) and transmission line capacities are based
on the results from the Capacity Development model in Balmorel. It is assumed that investment deci-
sions from the Capacity Development optimization model are implemented 5 years later. Hence, outputs
from the Capacity Development model in 2025 represent the installed capacity in 2030, whereby the
optimization of the Balancing Model is offset by 5 years compared to the Capacity Development model.
B.1.3
Flexible generators in the balancing market
In the Balancing Model, only flexible generators with controllable power output can provide balancing services.
The model distinguishes between 2 types of flexible generators, namely Flex and FlexFS.
• Flexible Generators: The generators categorized as Flex represent generators that can adjust power
output within the MARI time requirement only if they are already online in the DA-schedule.
• Fast-Start Flexible Generators: The FlexFS represent fast start generators that can provide balancing
services (adjust power output) within the balancing time requirement even if they are not committed in
the DA-schedule.
Seasonal hydro, pumped hydro and gas generators are categorized as FlexFS. Most conventional power plants
and combined heat and power plants (e.g. coal-fired, wood pellet and waste-to-energy plants) are categorized
as Flex. Wind, solar, batteries and hydro run-of-river are categorized as non-flexible meaning that they cannot
provide balancing regulation at all. Thereby, wind and solar curtailment is not possible in this model.
B.1.4
Balancing Power Inadequacy
In time steps where the anticipated power imbalances cannot be covered by the flexible generators, the model
applies different mechanisms to avoid infeasibility, depending on whether the power imbalance is positive or
negative. If the area requires additional generation (negative imbalance), the model can apply load curtailment
or activate backup generators, which are defined as natural gas generators with very high costs. If the area has
excess generation (positive imbalance), the model can apply negative load curtailment, which represents the
activation of virtual load. It is important to note that load curtailment and negative load curtailment do not
represent actual curtailment and are solely implemented to avoid infeasibility. They are modeled with a high
penalty cost and are only activated when there is inadequate balancing power in the system. The curtailment
does not account for actual system load, interruptible demand, or online renewable generation, and therefore
does not represent actual load curtailment or wind/solar curtailment.
The sum of load curtailment, negative load curtailment and generation from backup generators represent the
volume of imbalances that cannot be covered by the balancing market. Hence, this amount will have to be
balanced by the Area and Frequency Control Model.
B.1.5
Storage Technologies
In the current version of the balancing model, storage technologies are not modeled properly, which causes
mismatches with the day-ahead results from Balmorel. While Balmorel includes multiple storage technologies,
these are either improperly represented or excluded entirely in the Balancing Model. Batteries are categorized

==PAGE:81==

as non-flexible meaning that they cannot be activated in the Balancing Model at all, even if battery capacity
is installed. Hydro storage technologies, including both pumped storage hydro (PSH) and seasonal reservoir-
based storage hydro (SSH), can be activated in the balancing model, but lack constraints to account for reservoir
levels. This omission causes hydro storage to be treated as an unlimited resource in the Balancing Model, which
conflicts with the energy storage constraints in Balmorel.
These discrepancies result in unrealistic system behavior and can lead to situations where a hydro storage
unit is unavailable in Balmorel, causing the activation of more expensive generators. In the same timestep,
the Balancing Model assumes the hydro storage unit is available, as storage constraints are not considered.
Consequently, the same hydro storage unit can be used for up-regulation in the Balancing Model, leading to
an up-regulation price cheaper than the day-ahead price unless explicitly corrected by enforcing bidding prices
to exceed or undercut the day-ahead price. Proper modeling of storage in the Balancing Model will therefore
mitigate this mismatch between Balmorel and the Balancing Model, ensuring aligned results throughout the
BTC.
Given the findings of the literature review, it is recommended to include batteries, pumped hydro storage and
reservoir-based seasonal hydro storage in the Balancing model. These technologies are well-established, with
proven track records in providing flexibility services.
Additionally, their characteristics align well with the
requirements of balancing markets, such as fast response times and large-scale capacity.
