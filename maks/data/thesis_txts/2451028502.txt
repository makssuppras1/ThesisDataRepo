Abstract
This M.Sc. Eng. thesis describes an experimental and modeling investigation of high-
temperature wood pyrolysis with conditions relevant for suspension ﬁred boilers. The sus-
pension ﬁring of biomass produces CO2 neutral energy with lowered emission by reducing
the use of fossil fuels. Pyrolysis has the most signiﬁcant role in the overall combustion
process since it is responsible for converting of > 80 % mass fraction and determines the
properties of the formed solid residue. The experiments performed involved development
of a methodology for obtaining pyrolysis conversion data using a drop tube reactor with
the intended use for evaluation of pyrolysis models. The methodology was tested for
six diﬀerent sizes of pine wood particle between 0.6-2mm at three diﬀerent temperatures
(1000, 1200, and 1300 °C). The results showed increased degree of pyrolysis conversion
as a function of increased temperature or reduced particle size with a maximum of 61 %
conversion at 1300 °C. The results obtained showed increased amount of uncertain due to
particle fragmentation and lowered amount particle collected as either the size decrease
or temperature increased.
A 2D computational ﬂuid dynamics model of the drop tube was developed able to calcu-
late particle residence time, temperature history, and devolatilization rates for biomass
particles. A modiﬁed version of the single ﬁrst-order devolatilization kinetic model, where
the Arrhenius parameter is estimated to overcome the eﬀect of kinetics and heat transfer
limitations was implemented in CFD and Matlab. The model had increased performance
against experimental data as the temperature was increased, but did not ﬁt experiments
at all temperatures. A sensitivity analysis of the model revealed that the model had
a high sensitivity to its inputs, providing a reason for deviation the model and exper-
imental data. In the end, both the experimental methodology and modeling showed a
promising result; however, it requires further work to make conclusive remarks about the
high-temperature pyrolysis of biomass.

Aim of the study
The complete burn out of biomass in pulverized fuel power plant boilers is essential to
ensure high boiler eﬃciency and to prevent carbon in the bottom and ﬂy ash.
The
combustion conversion of biomass happens by pyrolysis and char combustion, where the
pyrolysis process converts the largest mass fraction of the biomass. Majority of the ex-
isting research on biomass pyrolysis has a focus on low-temperature conditions and long
residence time, which does not represent the environment in the industrial scaled pul-
verized boilers. High-temperature studies have been mainly performed on small (below
150 µm) or large (above 3 mm) biomass particles. However, the biomass particles used
in pulverized fuel power plant boilers often have a size between 0.05 to 2 mm.
The thesis aimed to investigate the high-temperature pyrolysis of wood using experiments
and computational ﬂuid dynamics at conditions relevant for suspension ﬁred boilers.
The experimental part covers the development of a methodology for obtaining pyrolysis
conversion data using a drop tube reactor. The methodology was developed with the
intended for evaluation of pyrolysis models. Further, the methodology was tested for
six diﬀerent sizes of pine wood particle between 0.6-2mm at three diﬀerent temperatures
(1000, 1200, and 1300 °C). A 2D computational ﬂuid dynamics model of the drop tube
reactor was developed able to calculate particle residence time, temperature history, and
devolatilization rates for biomass particles. The model employs a modiﬁed version of
the single ﬁrst-order devolatilization kinetic model where the Arrhenius parameters are
calculated to overcome heat transfer limitations. The model was implemented in the
CFD model and Matlab.

Literature review
This chapter includes a short literature review to provide the necessary background for
the high-temperature pyrolysis. Section 1.2 describe the composition of biomass together
with its chemical content. Section 1.3 provides an overview of the processes included in
the particle combustion. Pyrolysis is described in details section 1.4 together with how
the operating parameter inﬂuence the product yield (section 1.4) and the conventional
kinetics models (section 1.5) found in the literature. Section 1.7 review the frequently
applied methods in pyrolysis studies for determination of the char yield.
1.1
Introduction
As a part of the increased environmental concern producing renewable energy and reduc-
ing emissions have become an essential part of the political agenda. The fossil fuel-based
power plants, especially coal-ﬁred power plants, make a substantial contribution to the
greenhouse gas emission. Changing from burning coal to biomass oﬀers an operation,
which reduces reliance on fossil fuels and produces CO2 neutral energy while reducing
the emission of SOx and NOx [1]. The Danish power production company Ørsted have
stated, that they will be utterly independent of burning coal by 2023 [2]. The transition
from coal to biomass leads to several technical challenges at the power plants due to the
diﬀerence in chemical content and structure. The eﬃciency of the plants is challenged
by the ﬁbrous structure of biomass, making it ineﬀective to mill to a small diameter and
giving a highly nonspherical shape.
The majority of the particles, therefore, have a diameter above > 300 µm and a ﬂake
or cylinder-like shape making them diﬃcult to fully convert at the short residence time
in boilers [3]. The high salt content in biomass compared to coal leads to a lowered life-
time of the equipment due to depositions and accelerated corrosion [4]. The combustion
conversion of biomass happens by pyrolysis and char combustion, where the pyrolysis
process converts the most signiﬁcant mass fraction of the biomass. The products and
their formation rates from pyrolysis regulate ignition, ﬂame stability, emission gasses re-
lease, soot generation. Understanding pyrolysis is, therefore, essential to achieve higher
combustion eﬃciency and ﬂexibility for biomass combustion.

1.2
Biomass composition
Formally, biomass as a fuel can be deﬁned as combustible materials from the growing of
plants or processing waste from agriculture [5]. It covers a wide range of solid materials:
woods, straws, algae, seaweeds and other agricultural residues [6]. In the bio-reﬁnery in-
dustry, biomass has been an essential feedstock for more than a century for the production
of various chemicals. During the oil crisis in the 1970s, a broad interest in the production
of fuels from biomass using pyrolysis was observed. While the price for petroleum has
stabilized, resulting in reduced interest in its production, depletion of fossil fuels reserves
may change interest. In the present, biomass is widely used for generation of heat and
power in the combustion sector [7]. All biomass materials consist mainly of carbon, hy-
drogen, and oxygen; however, the chemical composition is unique for each type. The
diversity of biomass material composition makes it challenging to use and makes it de-
manding to design equipment with high utility requirement [7]. The biomass used in the
Danish pulverized ﬁred power plant facilities consists primarily of wood pellets, however
more ﬂexible operation by introducing other sources is desired.
1.2.1
Wood structure
The structure of the wood stem can be roughly be divided into the following parts: bark
(inner and outer), cambium, sapwood, and heartwood as seen in ﬁgure 1.1. Each part is
responsible for a well-deﬁned physiological function.
Figure 1.1: The structure of the wood stem showing the orientation major tissues [8]
The outer bark consists mainly of dead cells and has a higher content of extractive
compared to the rest of the tree (47-48 %). The layer provides protection for woods
inner parts from injury and desiccation. The inner bark consists of living cells having
the role of transporting sap and larger molecule around in the tree [9]. The cambium
3
==PAGE:16==
layer is responsible for wood growth, which happens by cell division, and a formation of
bark cells in the outer part. Due to the cell division, the wood cell is expanding in both
length and diameter, forming the cell wall [10]. The inner wood structure consists of two
parts sapwood and heartwood. The outer layer, sapwood contains living cells responsible
for transporting water and sap to the crown of the tree. The sapwood also functions
as a nutrition reservoir for the tree by containing diﬀerent extractives [10]. The inner
part of the wood is called the heartwood and mainly consist of dead cells which provide
mechanical support for the tree [3]. There exist two main groups of woods; softwood
(conifers, gymnosperms) and hardwood (angiosperm). Hardwoods are often referred to
porous woods and softwoods to as non-porous woods [3].
For combustion, the most
crucial diﬀerence between them is their chemical content.
1.2.2
Chemical composition
The chemical composition of wood mainly consists of biopolymers, extractives, and salts.
Biomass also has a signiﬁcant content of water commonly between 4-12 % [11] but may
be up to 50 %. The water content is capillary liquid water, water vapor in pores and
hygroscopic bound water making it diﬃcult to complete dry [12]. As shown in Figure 1.2
the biopolymers (Cellulose, Hemicellulose, and Lignin) are found as ﬁber prolonged in
the longitudinal direction, making biomass an anisotropic material [5]. The directional
dependence has shown to give diﬃculties in the mathematical description of the shape,
but also for the milling of the biomass particle. The biopolymer accounts for 95-98 % of
the total wood content [13].

in table 1.1.
The extractives in biomass consist of a broad range of mixed organic
compounds, which can be extracted using conventional solvents. The group of organic
molecules includes low-molecular weight sugars, inositols, amino acids, simple fats, and

1.3
Particle combustion
Particle combustion describes the burning of solid fuels by four steps as shown in Figure
1.4: (i) heating and drying, (ii) devolatilization, (iii) gas-phase oxidation, and (iv) char
combustion.

number for biomass particles at conditions relevant for suspension ﬁred boilers have been
shown to have a value above 0.2 even for particles with a size of 300 µm [20]. Internal
heat transfer will therefore often have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on combustion of biomass in

the heat transfer or the reaction kinetics is the controlling mechanism.
As the particle is continuously heated, devolatilization starts at approximately 300 °C
for coal and 200 °C for biomass. The process includes release of volatile matter (light
gasses and tar) and the formation of solid char (mainly carbon). The volatile matter
leaving mainly consist of CH4, C2H4, CO, CO2, H2O, H2, CH2O and, tar (heavier carbon
containing compounds) [5,21]. Details on the devolatilization are included in section 1.4.
Whether the oxidation of volatile matter occurs in the gas ﬁlm or the bulk ﬂow depends
on the oxygen concentration. The oxidization products mainly consist of H2O and CO2 if
the right mixing conditions are met together with the right amount of oxygen. Modeling
of evolving species can be tricky since it depends on the fuel composition and the pyrol-
ysis conditions. Notably, the formed tar includes a variety of heavier carbon-compounds,
which complicates the modeling of oxidation behavior in gas phase [4]. At the conditions
realistic for combustion, the tar constitutes a minority compared to the light gasses ox-
idation (including C1 to C3 species) and modeling is often limited to only include light
gases. The oxidation behavior of the light gasses has well-established reaction mechanism
and incorporated in CFD models. The gas phase chemistry is essential for the ﬂame phe-
nomena, thereby ﬂame stability and ignition/extinction [4].
The ﬁnal and time limiting step is char oxidation, which is signiﬁcantly inﬂuenced by
particle size. An important issue is to have complete char burnout before the particles
are exiting the high-temperature region of the boiler. Reducing the char oxidation time
can be achieved by milling the particle to reduce the size [4]. The char oxidation consists
of exothermic reactions between the solid carbon (char) and oxygen to form CO and
CO2 [5]. A simpliﬁed model typically applied for char oxidation is the shrinking core
model (SCM). The model is described in detail by Levenspiel [22]. The model neglects
the ash content and assumes that physical phenomena included are mass transfer (gas ﬁlm
diﬀusion or pore diﬀusion) or chemical reaction (on the core surface). The output from
the SCM estimates a time for complete char burnout based on the fuel type and particle
geometry together with size. Correct estimation of burnout time from the model requires
a heat balance of the particle to estimate particle temperature [4]. The estimated time
for complete burnout is vital for the design of correct boiler dimensions. The time for
complete char oxidation depends on the devolatilization process since it has been shown
to inﬂuence the amount of char formed and its reactivity.

1.4
Pyrolysis
Pyrolysis is deﬁned as the thermal degradation of material in an inert atmosphere. For
combustion often the more general term devolatilization is used due to in combustion
the particle environment is not necessarily is free of oxygen. The pyrolysis reaction in-
volves producing solid (char and soot), liquids (tar and water) and light non-condensable
gasses [23]. Tar is a condensable organic liquid at room temperature with a structure like
the parent fuel, but more abundant in hydrogen [24]. For biomass, the tar is a mixture of
acetaldehyde, acetic acid, acetone, hydroxyl acetone, furans, levoglucosan, benzene, and
toluene [21]. Moreover, the solid residue of the char consists mainly of aromatic carbon.
The soot is a solid product, which starts to form at high temperature from gas phase
reactions [25].
Pyrolysis is involved as a part of any heat treatment of biomass, whether it is a single
step or as a partial process like combustion. Pyrolysis can be used to generate biofuels
and bio-oil or as a gasiﬁcation process [26, 27]. The yield of the diﬀerent compounds
depends on the type of pyrolysis, which also inﬂuences the formed chars morphology
and reactivity [28]. The reactivity of the char has been found to increase if produced at
higher heating rates and higher temperature [14,29,30]. The diﬀerent types of pyrolysis
are commonly classiﬁed based on heating rate, peak temperature, and particle residence
time. Classiﬁcation commonly varies among slow, intermediate, fast, and ﬂash depending
on peak temperature and residence time as listed in table 1.3. The relative distribution
of product formed at the diﬀerent conditions decides the application where the fast and
ﬂash pyrolysis generate more liquids and gasses compared to the slow and intermediate.
Flash pyrolysis is an improved version of fast pyrolysis giving a higher liquid, but more
challenging to operate at an industrial scale [31].

1.4.1
Mechanism of pyrolysis
The mechanism of pyrolysis consist of three stages; low-temperature process, primary
pyrolysis and secondary pyrolysis as shown in ﬁgure 1.5.
Figure 1.5: Stages in pyrolysis [24]
The low-temperature process consists of removal of adsorbed water, disruption of hy-
drogen bonds and distillation of host molecule [4]. After the heating and drying steps,
biomass similarly to coal goes through rupture of organic bonds and partial melting of the
solid to form metaplast. The amount of metaplast depends on the fuels aliphatic content
and the heating rates. The primary pyrolysis consists of depolymerization by breaking of
weak bonds (primarily aliphatic bonds) forming molecule fragments (radicals) together
with the release of light gasses like hydrocarbons (up to C3), CO, CO2, H2 and H2O from
functional groups in the fuel molecule [24]. The fragments can abstract hydrogen and
can have two fates; evolve to tar if the units are small enough to leave the particle by
evaporation or make cross-linking reactions to repolymerize. The cross-linking reaction
allows metaplast solidiﬁcation to form char. The process is proceeding until donatable
hydrogen atoms are depleted [4].
Secondary pyrolysis consists of removal of more gasses: HCN, CH4, CO and H2 leaving
the char consisting of mainly aromatic carbon. The formed tar has the potential to re-
act in secondary reaction to form char and more light gasses or cracking thermally to
become soot particles in the gas phase [44]. In pulverized boilers, the pyrolysis process
is completed within 1 sec due to high heating rates, which prevents char oxidation. The
ﬂame formation and combustion of volatiles prevent oxygen from diﬀusion into the par-
ticle, and mainly N2 is present within the particle [3]. Biomass compared to coal forms
11
==PAGE:24==
a more considerable amount of oxygenated compounds, more volatile matter, and lower
char yield. The amount of produced tar, gasses, and char depends on the operational
conditions. The combustion process beneﬁted from a low char yield and increased volatile
matter.
1.5
Parameter inﬂuence on pyrolysis
The research in literature containing high-temperature pyrolysis focus on the inﬂuence
of the following parameters: particle size and shape, heating rate, peak temperature, as
well as the organic and inorganic matter. This section summarizes the ﬁndings.
1.5.1
Peak temperature and heating rate
Studies with diﬀerent peak temperature and heating rates have revealed diﬀerences in the
products formed. In general, there is an increased amount of volatile matter and reduced
char yield for temperatures above > 1000 °C.
Trubetskaya et al. [14] Studied the eﬀect of varying the peak temperature between 1000
to 1400 °C showing an increased amount of H2 and CO as the temperature was in-
creased together with a reduced char and tar yield. Other studies have shown that above
a temperature of 1200 °C mainly gasses are formed together with a minor amount of
char [36, 45]. The formed char have a higher carbon content as the peak temperature
increases leading to a more reactive char [46,47]. The increased peak temperature leads
to a more extensive formation of soot particles, reducing soots mean diameter and in-
creasing the reactivity [29]. Trubetskaya et al. [18] found that the residence time has a
decreasing eﬀect on char yield when the temperature increases.
The heating rate is closely related to the peak temperature and the eﬀects can be hard
to separate.
Trubetskaya et al. [14] compared the biomass char yield from pyrolysis
experiments in a wire mesh reactor to a drop tube reactor. Less char was formed in
the drop tube reactor at the same peak temperature due to the higher heating rate. In
general, high heating rate reduce the char yield and makes it reactive [23]. The heating
rate aﬀects the swelling and melting of biomass, therefore, having an inﬂuence on the
tar formation process through bond-breaking, cross-linking, and internal mass transport
rates [3]. Experiments with slow heating favor the tars secondary reaction to form char.
The yield has been observed to be 15-17 % char yield at low heating rates (10-20 °C/min)
compared to 1-10 % at high heating rates (104 −105 °C/s) [32]. Further, biomass is under
a greater inﬂuenced by heating rates compared to coal, which has been proposed to be
due to the high cellulose content [3].

1.5.2
Size and shape
The shape and size of biomass particles have an impact on transport phenomena (mass
and heat trasnfer), reaction kinetics and the ﬂuid-particle interaction of the solid biomass
in a ﬂuid. The shape and size is, therefore, an essential topic for combustion of biomass [9].
The size determines if there is a signiﬁcant temperature gradient within the particle, which
for larger particle temperature leads to a lowered pyrolysis conversion at the short resi-
dence time. The consequence for large particle is a reduced eﬃciency with a high carbon
content at the bottom of the boilers [3]. Models in Literature have proposed diﬀerent
reaction kinetics within larger particle as simpliﬁcation to account for the diﬀerence in
the heating rates in a particle.
For fast pyrolysis at T > 1000 °C, the char yield is lowered for small particle compared
to larger particle due to the higher heat ﬂux within the particles or the altered heating
rates [3]. Generally, the particle can be assumed to be isothermally thin for a size inter-
val, if the same char yield is obtained. Bitowft et al. [45] studied small particles in the
range of 250-630 µm at temperatures between 1000-1400 °C showing complete pyrolysis
of the particle where all particle had a similar char yield (≈17 %). Septien [27] found
the same char yield for beechwood particles of 0.35mm compared to 0.8mm from drop
tube experiments at temperatures above 1000 °C but observed a diﬀerence in the ﬂue gas
composition. Trubetskaya et al. [18] use a wire mesh reactor to show similar char yield
for straw particles in the range 0.05-0.85mm at 1250 °C and concluded that the parti-
cles could be seen as thermally thin below 0.425mm. Studies have, therefore showed for
the intermediate sized (0.5-2mm) particle often cannot not be regarded as isothermally.
However, it will still depend on temperature, heating rate, and the biomass type.
Zanzi [23] has proposed diﬀerent mechanism govern the tar formation, which is depen-
dent on the particle size. For small particles, secondary reactions happen in the gas phase
and for large particle secondary cracking happens, which hinders the primary pyrolysis
product to be released [3]. The secondary cracking reactions for the larger particles favors
char formation due to the lower heating rate. Further, metaplast is mainly formed on the
surface for larger particles [48].
Challenging in the study of particle size is the irregular shape of the particles. The ﬁbrous
structure makes it diﬃcult to grind and energy consuming, where the result is ﬂattening
rather than crushing. The shape of biomass is, therefore, highly irregular and sphericity
between 0.4-0.7 has commonly been reported depending on particle size [49]. The type of
milling has shown to only inﬂuence the size and not the shape of particles [48]. The results
are, that commonly applied geometrical shapes (spherical and cylindrical) for modeling

are challenging to apply to biomass. For modeling purposes, the particles represented
as an inﬁnite cylinder with the martin minimal diameter oﬀers an approximation of the
shape [49].
The minimal Martin diameter (xma,min) is the smallest diameter dividing
the particle area into two halves with equal size [3]. Another way to represent the odd
shape of biomass particles is as a rectangular cube with the dimensions shown in Figure
1.6. The width is described by xma,min, the thickness as 2/3 xma,min, and the length as

Organic matter
The main content of biomass is its organic compounds hemicellulose, cellulose, and lignin.
Low heating rate experiments have revealed three temperatures where one of the com-
ponents starts to decompose due diﬀerence in their reactivity, as seen in Figure 1.7.
Hemicellulose is the most reactive component starting to decompose at temperatures be-

The char formation in biomass may be due to re-polymerization of levoglucosan, which
is the main tar product at low-temperature [54]. The low temperature and low heating
rate favor the dehydration reaction to form more stable anhydrocellulose sugars.

The mechanism of hemicellulose pyrolysis has been proposed to be polymer breakdown
into water-soluble fragments and further into monomer units, where the monomers de-

to cellulose, produce more gas at elevated temperature than tar without levoglucosan
formation. The pyrolysis behavior of cellulose has been studied intensively; however,
the mechanism is not fully understood [15]. Low temperature and low heating rates of
cellulose pyrolysis mainly form levoglucosan together with furans and light oxygenate
compounds [56]. At elevated temperatures and higher heating rates, the formed levoglu-

been proposed levoglucosan cannot repolymerize easily and is therefore further reduced

Lignin pyrolysis has been shown to give a variety of products: aromatic hydrocarbons,
phenolics, hydroxyphenolics, and guaiacyl-/syringyl-type compounds [58]. The primary
lignin pyrolysis has been shown to yield CO2 from carboxyl groups, CO from oxygen
groups, H2O from hydroxyl groups, hydrocarbon and H2 from aliphatic at 900 °C [59].
Reactions at higher temperature might involve breakage and rearrangement of stronger
bonds in the aromatic ring together with additional release of H2 and CO [3].
Lignin pyrolysis, compared to hemicellulose and cellulose, gives a higher char yield, low
tar yield together with an increased amount of CH4 and H2 [60]. Lignin can react with
cellulose to increase the char yield and lowers the tar yield [61]. Lignin compounds tend to
inhibit the polymerization of levoglucosan from cellulose favoring the formation of lower
molecular weight products. On the other hand, cellulose can enhance the formation of
lignin-derived compounds: guaiacol, 4-methyl-guaiacol, and 4-vinyl-guaiacol [62]. Demir-
bas [46] found that high-temperature pyrolysis favors higher char yield for compounds
with higher lignin content by comparing olive husk (50.6 % lignin) to corncob (15.5 %
lignin). Studies of the individual components pyrolysis mechanism at temperatures above
1000 °C were not found in the literature.

For development or validation of pyrolysis models, experimental data of char yield or
volatile release as a function of particle size, temperature and residence time is required.
For pyrolysis experiments in DTR or EFR, the char yield requires quantity analysis on
either the solid residue or the gas phase composition. Variation in tar composition makes
it diﬃcult to determine the gas phase composition, and as a result, the standard has
become to analyze the solids residue [67]. Commonly, the ash tracer method is applied

1.8
Conclusion
Biomass pyrolysis has been studied intensively over the years to understand how the
product yield is a function of the operational parameters. Low-temperature studies dom-
inates the literature and the high-temperature pyrolysis knowledge relevant for combus-
tion application is limited. Nevertheless, the current state of the art was presented. The
operation parameters determines the char yield where the yield decreased with particle
size, increasing peak temperature, increasing heating rates, and reduced potassium yield.
The product formation further depends on the relative distribution of the structural com-
pounds where increasing the lignin content gave higher char yield. Higher hemicellulose
content gave a higher tar and reduced char yield. Lignin and cellulose can interact to
increase char yield. Tar yield increases until a temperature of 1000 °C where it starts to
decrease and reaches zero at temperatures above 1200 °C where non-condensable gasses
becomes the main product.
Experiments have shown for particles of intermediate size (0.5-2mm) the char yield, and
volatile composition is highly dependant on the heating rates. Increasing the tempera-
ture tends to give a similar char yield distribution across diﬀerent sizes, but the particles
can often not be regarded as been isothermally thin.
The kinetic modeling of high-
temperature pyrolysis is often limited to a single ﬁrst-order kinetic rate model as a con-
sequence of the inability to extrapolate low-temperature models and a reduced amount
of high-temperature kinetics studies. In the end, a section describing methods to esti-
mate char yield was present together uncertainties and a discussion of its validation for
high-temperature studies. The validation of the method mainly depends on the setup
and type of biomass. The overall conclusion is that there a still relevant to information
to reveal about the high-temperature pyrolysis of biomass.

Experimental procedure
This chapter describes the experimental investigation of fast pyrolysis of pine wood in the
drop tube reactor. The experimental work aimed to obtain char yield data for diﬀerent
particle size (0.5-2mm) at diﬀerent temperature (1000-1300 °C) at high heating rates.
The chapter includes a detailed description of the setup, methodology for determination
of char yield, and the test matrix carried out.
2.1
Experimental setup
The P&I of the drop tube used in present work are shown Figure 2.1 together with pic-
tures of the actual setup in Figure 2.2-2.3. Appendix A includes the full dimensions of
each of the diﬀerent parts.
The main components of the drop tube reactor are two long ceramic pipes (the inner:
1625mm long with an inner diameter 30mm and the outer 1500mm inner diameter of
42mm) enclosed by an electric oven from Scandina going from 0.14 to 1.4m on the outer
tube. The oven includes four heating elements where each has a length of 250mm and
the temperature can be controlled having a maximum of 1600 °C. The furnace and pipes
are glued together where a cooling water system in the top and the bottom keeps the
glue from been damaged. Supply of the primary gas goes through the biomass feeding
chamber in the top and secondary gas through the annulus between the tubes from the
bottom. Both inlet gas ﬂows are controlled by mass ﬂower controllers (Bronkhorst High-
Tech model F-201CV Mass ﬂow controller). Biomass particles suspended in nitrogen
(primary ﬂow) enters into the inner pipe from the top. The secondary nitrogen ﬂow is
heated by the surrounding electrical oven to reach a temperature of > 1000°C.
The hot N2 enters the inner pipe through eight holes with a diameter of 3mm located at
a position 1.29m and 1.30m from the bottom (therefore 33.5cm from the reactor inlet)
mixing with the primary ﬂow containing the particles. The N2 injection occurs in the
radial direction of the main tube and particle are coming in the axial direction, resulting
in the particle path can be inﬂuenced by the injection jet. To reduce the inﬂuence of
the gas injection on the particles the third tube in the system (called protection pipe in
ﬁgure 2.2 A) purpose is to let the particle enter the inner pipe 10mm below the injection
point in a more laminar developed zone. The protection pipe was a new addition to the

Final method
The ﬁnal method for conversion experiments includes 50 particles and an injection of ﬁve
particles at a time. Optical microscope pictures were taken of selected particles in each
size class to track the conversion of the individual particles before and after experiments.
An image of all the collected particles was used to determine the number of particles since
it can be challenging to identify the small size of the collected pieces with the naked eye.
The total mass was assessed before and after each experiment using an analytical weight
scale.
The solid yield was determined using modiﬁed the direct weight measurement
method taking the collection eﬃciency into account.

Conclusion
The chapter presented the methodology for the obtained pyrolysis conversion data ob-
tained from the drop tube experiments for the milled Chinese pinewood sample. The
development of the char yield showed that the traditional ash tracer method is unsuit-
able due to the low ash content in the wood. An adjusted version of the direct weight
measurement taking the number of particles collected was applied. The experiments in-
cluded six diﬀerent particle sizes between 0.6-2mm at three diﬀerent temperatures (1000,
1200, 1300) °C. The results showed increased volatilization as a function of increased tem-
perature or reduced particle size. The overall conversion degree in the experiment was
low with a maximum of 60 % conversion for 0.71-0.85 mm particles at 1300 °C. Smaller
particles size or higher temperature lead to uncertainties in measured yield calculation
due to a smaller amount of particles was collected, and a higher degree of fragmentation
of the particles. The fragmentation makes it diﬃcult to determine how many particles
are collected and makes the results uncertain. Further work should include repeating the
experiments to assign the related errors and potential, increasing the number of particle
in each experiment, together with an adjustment of the method to reduce the dependency
on collection eﬃciency.

CFD modeling of the drop tube
reactor
The following chapter describes the CFD modeling of the drop tube to provide insight
into the internal behavior in the reactor. The calculations include the particle parameters
(residence time, temperature, velocity, and path), which are challenging to measure ex-
perimentally together with the gas phase behavior (temperature and velocity). A simple
devolatilization model developed by Leth-Espersen et al. [72] is further implemented into
the CFD model and compared with the experimental results from chapter 2.
3.1
Introduction
Several technical diﬃculties exist for ﬁring or co-ﬁring of biomass in the industrial scaled
suspension ﬁred boilers. Growth in the available computational power has made CFD
modeling accessible as an economically eﬃcient way to research the combustion process.
The principle of CFD modeling is to generate a computational domain (the mesh) where
transport phenomena problem can be numerically solved by discretization methods of the
transport equations. The common software for CFD modeling is either Fluent or CFX,
both from Ansys INC. The combustion/devolatilization behavior of biomass is examined
by solving a Reynold averaged Navier-Stoke equation (RANS) coupled with heat transfer
modeling (including convection, conduction, and radiate mechanism) and species trans-
port to account for the ﬂuid phase behavior. The RANS model is the simplest class of
turbulence models oﬀering the most economical approach for solving complex turbulence
ﬂow and often suitable provide the level of accuracy required for engineering applica-
tion [73]. The most common models found in combustion studies of suspension ﬁred
boilers are versions of k-ϵ or k-ω [74]. CFD studies in the literature of drop tube reactors
have applied k-ω [75] or complete laminar model [39,42,43] depending on the gas veloci-
ties and dimensions of the reactor.
Reactions in the gas phase and the particle behavior are solved in submodels. Often the
particles equations are formulated as a solid object passing through the grid cells where
the ﬂuid condition in the cell are used for calculation of the particle trajectory, tempera-
ture, and the devolatilization rates [7]. CFD modeling can, therefore, provide insight on
particle and its pyrolysis behavior. Due to the nature of no universal turbulence model,
40
==PAGE:53==
the ﬂuid behavior is signiﬁcantly diﬀerent depending on the model selected. Experimental
validation of CFD modeling is, therefore, often required to ensure reasonable accuracy.
More sophisticated ﬂuid models than the RANS models can often increase the prediction
accuracy; however, they increase computational power requirement with orders of mag-
nitude. With the right model and model setup, the behavior can be modeled accurately
to use for research, process design, and process optimization.
The system modeled is the drop tube presented in the previous chapter to supply the
experiments with information about particles behavior in the reactor and evaluate a
pyrolysis model. Figure 3.1 shows a cross-sectional view of the SolidWorks model used
for the construction of the reactor. The 3mm nitrogen injection holes are located in two
planes and are almost symmetrical around the protection pipe. The symmetry could,
therefore, be exploited to reduce the problem to a 2D axis symmetric problem. A 2D
model would reduce the computational power without sacriﬁcing the main features of
the system. In the following section, the developed 2D CFD model of the drop tube is
presented in details.

3.2
2D model
The axisymmetric 2D model was drawn in Solidworks 2017, meshed in ICEM CFD 18.1
(structured mesh with only quadrilateral elements) and simulated using Fluent 18.1.
The model was drawn to match the dimensions of the real drop tube. The deviation
from the 3-dimensional geometry is the eight injection holes approximated into a 2D slit.
The slit dimensions match the surface area of holes in the inner tube, resulting in a slit
height of 0.63mm. The simulation was limited to a temperature of 1200 °C in the reactor.
Figure 3.2-3.3 shows the model geometry. Modeling was limited to the reactor pipes
and setting an inlet speed on particles accounted for the acceleration of the particle in
the feeding chamber. The model includes multiphysics: heat transfer (convection, con-
duction, and radiation), ﬂuid ﬂow, particle tracking, and devolatilization kinetics. Table
3.1-3.4 describes the model settings. The model included both solid and ﬂuid zones to
describe the heat transfer in the system. The oven heating was approximated by a bound-
ary condition where the wall called heating wall in Figure 3.2 had a temperature of 1230
°C. Table 3.2 shows the physical properties for Al2O3. The setting for the ﬂuid, nitrogen,
was the standard ﬂuent nitrogen properties. [73].
The carrier gas has a low Reynolds number (0-20) in the reactor; nonetheless, the ﬂow
ﬁeld is complex and potentially turbulent in some zones. The k-ω SST Intermittency
Transition Model in ﬂuent, a Laminar-Turbulent Transition model, accounting for the
turbulence behavior in the system was applied. The model is an extension of the k-ω
model to better model laminar and turbulence transitions [73].
The high temperature of the system makes radiation the major source of heat transfer
to the particles. The discrete ordinate (DO) model allows for particle heating calcula-
tions and is recommended for combustion studies [73] [74]. The discrete ordinate model
settings were 5 × 5 discretization, 3 × 3 pixels, and ﬁve energy iterations per radiation
iterations. For a detailed description of a speciﬁc model, the reader is referred to Fluent
theory guide [73]. The mesh used for the simulations had approximately 500000 elements
where the distribution of the element was to have most elements in zones where a high
degree of accuracy is required (near the slit). Additional information about mesh is in-
cluded in Appendix D.
For particle simulations, the ﬂow ﬁeld and temperature across the reactor was obtained us-
ing steady-simulations. The simulations were determined as converged when the residual
error was in the order of 10−6 for DO model, energy, velocities, and turbulence quantities.
An order of magnitude lower for the residual order was tested for the turbulence quanti-

A 0D model developed by Leth-Espensen et al. [72] was implemented into the CFD
model. The model assumes that the biomass particle is spherically isothermal thin par-

from pyrolysis reactions on particle temperature. The 2D model has been validated for
small particles (78.8 µm) and large particles (> 3mm), however, not for the medium sized
particles as examined in this work. The model has the following assumptions:
• Isothermal particle

• Particle is spherical
• Kinetics can be described by the SFOR mechanism.

Figure 3.4 shows a comparison between the CFD models temperature prediction and
experimental data from the reactor. The experimental data was measured by having a
thermometer lowered into the reactor 5 cm at a time. The diﬀerence between the model

the high-temperature gradients in area. Assuming the model overpredicts the inﬂuence
will be a slight overprediction of the volatile release from the particles, but since it is
below 1200 °C and for a short distance, the inﬂuence on the heating rate of the particles
will be minor. The results further show that the gas peak temperature in the reactor is
not reached before near the outlet of the protection pipe.

secondary ﬂow rate having a signiﬁcantly higher velocity than primary ﬂow rate leads
to a mixing zone of the two streams. The inﬂuence of the zone could be minimized by
increasing the primary ﬂow; however, it would require a signiﬁcant increase to match the
secondary ﬂow rate. An increase in primary ﬂowrate would increase the inlet velocity
of particles leading to lower residence time. Chapter 2 showed an already low degree
of conversion and therefore increasing the primary ﬂow is not desirable. The primary
ﬂow would also increase the chance of a positive gauge pressure in the reactor, thereby
complicates the operation of the setup. The secondary ﬂow rate could also be decreased,
but experimental measurement showed that it leads to a high percentage of oxygen in
the system. A recirculation zone is therefore diﬃcult to avoid altogether.
Figure 3.7 shows a plot of turbulence intensity and that the recirculation leads to a
turbulence zone in the system. The deﬁnition of turbulence intensity is ﬂuctuation in
velocity over the average velocity and the maximum turbulence intensity of 2 %, can
52
==PAGE:65==
be considered to be a low to medium value [73]. The purpose of including a turbulence
model is to predict the particle behavior in the reactor correctly. The low intensity means
that the particle is likely to experience a limited amount of dispersion due to turbulence.
Turbulence models are often developed and calibrated to account for turbulence behavior
at high Reynolds numbers. The low turbulence behavior in the system could, therefore,
be a shortcoming of the model from using it outside of the range, where it has been
developed to have an accurate prediction. Majority of the CFD studies of drop tube
reactors in literature have utilized a laminar ﬂuid for drop tube reactor except for at a
very high ﬂow rates (> 50 NL/min) [75]. The low predicted turbulence, therefore, agrees
with the literature that the drop tube should be mainly a laminar ﬂow. The conclusion
for the protection pipes inﬂuence is that the particles can still be inﬂuenced; however,
the velocity magnitude of nitrogen is signiﬁcantly decreased in the recirculation zone
compared to at the injection point.

Sensitivity analysis
The sensitivity analysis included changes the inputs parameter to the model with ± 10
% and see the relative change in predicted volatile released for three diﬀerent particle
size. Table 3.9 lists the outcome of the analysis, showing the dp = 0.68mm particle are
barely aﬀected, as a consequence of the model predicts these particles to be fully reacted
even when the temperature drops or residence time changes. The other two particles
sizes have a relative change in the same order, except for an increase in gas temperature,
decrease in residence time or changing the particle diameter. The 1.7mm particle shows
a signiﬁcant increase in the volatile release if the temperature is increased, starting to
overcome the slow heating of the particle. The 0.925mm particle have more consider-
able inﬂuence from the change of particle diameter compared to the 1.7mm particles.
The inputs for calculation of the apparent Arrhenius parameter (KC and AR) have a
neglectable inﬂuence of volatile release. A potential reason for the lack of inﬂuence from
KC is due to its low content in the pine wood, meaning that ten percent does not increase
the amount signiﬁcantly. Similar is the profound inﬂuence from the aspect ratio on the
apparent rate constants. Leth-Espensen et al. [72] found that the eﬀect of changes in the
aspect ratio on apparent rate constant increases as the particle size is lowered, however,
have an overall low inﬂuence.
The residence time changes show that even ±10% leads to a minimum 40 % change in
volatile fraction release due to the fast pyrolysis reaction rate. Correct estimation of the
residence time is, therefore, essential. The sensitivity for of residence times calculation in
CFD modeling was tested from a simulation with inert particles’ with varying density and
shape factor. Table 3.10-3.11 lists the outcome and shows both the density and shape
factor can have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence on the calculated residence time. The outcome
of the sensitivity analysis shows that almost all parameters have a signiﬁcant inﬂuence
on the amount of volatile fraction released. The deviation from the 0D model to the
experimental data can, therefore, potentially be explained by the high sensitivity for the
model inputs.

3.5
Conclusion
A 2D CFD model of the drop tube including ﬂuid ﬂow, heat transfer, particle, and a mod-
iﬁed version of the single ﬁrst-order kinetics, was developed to account for the particles
and ﬂuid behavior in the drop tube. The models showed that the protection pipe in setup
was able to reduce the direct inﬂuence from the secondary nitrogen injection; however, led
to the creating a recirculation zone near protection outlet. The particle modeling applied
showed an increased average residence time from 0.6 to 1.1 seconds as the particle size
reduces from 1.7mm to 0.65mm. The distribution in residence was found to increase as
the particle size decreased. The outcome from the recirculation zone was the dispersion
of the particle trajectory, which increased for decreasing the particle size; however, the
overall inﬂuence was minor. The version of the single ﬁrst-order kinetics applied in this
work predicted full pyrolysis for particles below 0.85mm at a temperature above 1200 °C.
The model’s performance increased with temperature compared to experimental data but
failed to predict experimental data over several temperatures. The potential explanation
was discussed, and through a sensitivity analysis of model inputs, both OD model and
CFD was shown to be highly sensitive to the model inputs. The sensitivity of especially
for the residence time of particles potentially explain the signiﬁcant deviation between
the model and the experimental data.

Discussion of work
4.1
Discussion of work
The outcome of the experimental work and modeling showed that high-temperature stud-
ies of biomass pyrolysis are challenging subject to research. The experimental work in-
vestigated the drop tube as a method to study biomass at temperatures above 1000 °C.
The low ash content and that not all particles are collected in experiments challenged the
determination of conversion by conventional methods.
A methodology where the missing particle is taken into account showed promising re-
sults; however, due to shortcomings in time, the reproducibility from the method was not
be tested. The number of particles collected can be challenging to determine for smaller
particles or high temperature, leading to uncertain in the method. The outcome might be
that the methodology only provided a method for pyrolysis conversion for larger particles
or low-temperature experiments. The methodology could, therefore, be optimized further
to provide a better framework for getting reliable pyrolysis data. The change will mainly
be to improve the collection eﬃciency in the experiments. Changing the method to have
a decreased dependency on collection eﬃciency requires improvement of the collection
system or as discussed in chapter 2, single particle injection and collection at a time.
Increasing the number of particles could further improve the quality of the experiments.
The purpose of the collection of experimental pyrolysis conversion data was to evaluate
a pyrolysis models.
The overall modeling outcome had a shortcoming in explaining the experimental pyrolysis
data over several temperatures. The models used in CFD modeling utilize simpliﬁcation
to avoid having a substantial computational power requirement and are coupled together,
making it challenging to explain the exact reason for the observed deviation. Notably, the
0D pyrolysis model was shown to be sensitive to the model input parameters and espe-
cially the residence time predicted by the particle modeling. Naturally, biomass varies in
its composition (water, biopolymers, and ash content) depend on the season and environ-
ment it originates from resulting a natural variation for the physical properties. Further,
the sample size of the experimental data is rather small and are associated with potential
uncertainties, which makes it diﬃcult to conclude about the precision of the model. The
0D model performance should, however, be evaluated with more experimental data and
64
==PAGE:77==
diﬀerent types of biomass before conclusively remarks on its precision can be made.
For a more general modeling validation, experimental measurement of the particle res-
idence time in the reactor would provide indispensable value. The residence time mea-
surement is diﬃcult because of the short residence time in the reactor. However, a recent
study by Rußig et al. [82] developed a method for measurements of particle residence
time in a drop tube reactor. The method used impregnated char with a small amount of
a radioactive tracer element and measure the gamma-ray signal through-out the reactor.
The signal was measured then used to calculate the residence time. If a similar method
can be developed to be suitable for the setup is not known, but could be examined.
All in all, there still is a lot to explore and experiments to make with the drop tube
reactor, which can be used for extending the understanding of the biomass pyrolysis
behavior.
4.2
Suggestion for future work
There is still information to be revealed about biomass pyrolysis, which can be studied
using the drop tube setup at the department. Further, work will mainly include obtaining
more experimental data using the drop tube to validate the model applied. The experi-
ments present in chapter two should be repeated to determine if the methodology applied
leads to reasonable or unreasonable uncertainties in data. The pyrolysis behavior of the
medium sized particles should further be investigated at a higher temperature, altered
gas velocities to change residence time, and for other types of biomass. Experiments at
a higher temperature would probably require replacing the reactor tubes with a more
heat resistant material to avoid cracking of the tube. The 0D model should be tested
with more data and having its parameter estimated from narrow ranges to see if it could
provide a better result. For obtaining better results in general with the setup, ﬁnding
out how the particles are disappearing/passing through the collection system should be
investigated. Development of an experimental method to measure the residence time
could provide a way to validate model results.

4.3
Overall conclusion
The study aimed to explore high-temperature pyrolysis of woody biomass through CFD
modeling and experiments in a laboratory-scale drop tube reactor. A methodology was
developed including 50 particles, ﬁve injected at a time, and the solid yield determined
from a modiﬁed version of the direct weight measurement method taken the number
of collected particles into account. The results showed increased degree of pyrolysis as
a function of increased temperature or reduced particle size with a maximum of 61 %
conversion at 1300 °C. The method showed an increased amount of uncertain at higher
temperature or smaller particle sizes due to a smaller amount of particles was collected,
and a higher degree of fragmentation of the particles.
A 2D CFD of the drop tube was developed to simulate the particle behavior in the
reactor using the ﬂuents discrete phase model. The outcome showed an increased average
residence time from 0.6 to 1.1 seconds as particle size decreased with an increased spread
in residence time. The thermal history of the particle was found from an isothermal heat
balance able to explain the low degree of conversion due to the low peak temperature of
particles. A modiﬁed version of the single ﬁrst-order kinetics taken non-isothermal eﬀects
into account in the apparent Arrhenius parameters was implemented in the CFD model
and also solved in Matlab. The model failed to explain the data at all temperatures but
had an increased performance at a higher temperature. The model predicted the largest
particle size (1.7mm) considered to require approximately 1.7s for full devolatilization
at 1200 °C. Through sensitivity analysis the model was shown to be high sensitivity for
the inputs parameters, especially residence time, which could explain the deviation to
experimental data. Validation of the results was found to depend on the development
of an experimental method to measure residence time and test the reproducible of the
experiments. Overall, both the experimental method and modeling showed potential but
requires further work for making conclusive remarks about high-temperature pyrolysis of
biomass.

Estimation of heating rate
To estimate the heating rate the procedure present by Leth-Espensen et al. [52] is used.
The estimation of heating rate of particles use the following assumptions.
• A single value for the heating rate
• particles are spherical
• particle size approximated by the sieving size
• No particle pores
• Particles are isothermal
• Uniform particle properties though out the particle
• No chemical reactions included in heat balance
• All gasses are inert and ideal
• The temperature around the particle are estimated as the average between the
initial and ﬁnal temperature
• Initial gas temperature is 298K
