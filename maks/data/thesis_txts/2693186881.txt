Abstract
ACS convolutions consist of a modification to convolutional layers of 2D Convolutional
Neural Networks. This modification allows 2D networks to directly process 3D inputs
through a tri-planar analysis of data with three independent sets of filters. In this thesis,
we explore the use of ACS Convolutions for semantic segmentation of medical images.
We provide a comparison of segmentation performance between a standard ACS and 3D
U-Net architecture. Our findings show that the ACS U-Net is either equivalent or superior
in relation to its 3D U-Net counterpart.
Furthermore, through the use of Group Normalization, we inspect the impact of the inde-
pendent normalization of axis-specific information that results from the analysis of data in
ACS convolutional layers. We verify that this contributes to make the value distributions
of axis-specific feature maps more distinct. We also find that this normalization strategy
enables superior segmentation performance for both datasets considered, comparatively
to Batch Normalization. Additionally, we identify a performance issue that is tied to the
use of Batch Normalization in our considered ACS U-Net. Group Normalization eliminates
this issue.
Lastly, we assess the feasibility of introducing a classification task to differentiate between
axis-specific feature maps in anACS network in order to boost segmentation performance.
We conclude this task does not bring any added benefit for segmentation performance.

Introduction
Semantic segmentation consists of partitioning an image into different entities by assign-
ing a label to each of its elements. It is an actively researched topic in Computer Vision
and an essential component in many visual systems. Its range of applications includes
autonomous vehicles [1, 2], computational photography [3], and video surveillance [4],
among many others. In the medical field, its interest rests on delineating specific anatom-
ical or fundamental structures, as shown in figure 1.1. It is used to diagnose, evaluate
and monitor conditions [5, 6], preemptively planning surgical interventions, real-time as-
sistance as they are underway [7], and quantitative analysis in clinical studies [8].

The speed at which radiology services and consequent treatments can be provided is
limited by the availability of trained professionals for the analysis of medical images. The
number of medical images produced yearly has been observed to grow [10, 11]. In some
countries, the demand for complex Computed Tomography (CT) scans and Magnetic Res-
onance Images (MRI) is growing at three times the pace of the radiologist workforce [12].
Thus, radiology departments can struggle to meet their work quotas, part of which is im-
age segmentation. The manual segmentation of medical images is a long process. It
can take a team of four full-time radiologists three months to annotate a collection of two
hundred CT scans [13]. The use of automatic systems for the segmentation of desired
target structures can help to speed-up this process by alleviating the workload of radiol-
ogists. By providing reliable segmentation results, radiologists can, for example, reduce
time spent on cases of a simpler or more repetitive nature and focus their attention on the
most complex cases.
Deep Learning methods, namely the use of Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs), are
currently at the forefront of Computer-aided Diagnostic systems which can provide this
sort of assistance. The use of medical data in a Deep Learning context introduces an
extra set of considerations when compared to regular images. Medical images such as
CT scans, MRIs and Ultrasounds are three-dimensional. At the same time, the number

of parameters in CNNs’ convolutional kernels grows exponentially as a function of their
dimensionality. Consequently, CNNs that incorporate native 3D operations have a con-
siderably greater number of parameters than their 2D counterparts. Thus, they are larger
to store, less stable, bring significant inference latency [14], and are more prone to over-
fitting, especially for the smaller datasets commonly present in the medical field. Due to
a much higher memory usage, compromises in network architecture such as depth [15],
and filter number [16] are often made. Memory consumption also limits input dimensions
and batch size, even in modern GPUs [17, 18, 19].
As a result, there is a divide in the approaches for processing medical images in Deep
Learning. Some use CNNs with native 3D operations and directly handle 3D inputs. Oth-
ers choose 2D models and incorporate volumetric information in a multitude of different
ways. Instinctively, a CNN which natively accounts for three-dimensional context sounds
like the obvious choice to model 3D inputs. However, many publications purposefully
avoid using 3D models. Model dimensionality is an ever-present variable in the medical
imaging field largely because there does not exist a linear debate around it. Performance
wise, there is no definitive empirical evidence for the superiority of a given approach
across all experimental settings [19, 20, 21]. In fact, 2D CNNs are used for processing 3D
medical data because they are capable of achieving state-of-the art results [13]. Choos-
ing a 2D model does not imply settling for inferior results when compared to an equivalent
3D architecture. Given the current uncertainty and divide in the field, it is relevant to ex-
plore and improve upon practices that contribute to the discussion on which approach to
take. Namely, 2D methods which address issues associated with 3D approaches and
that attempt to bridge the gap between 2D and 3D modelling are particularly interesting
to consider.

Medical Decathlon [22].
With this in mind, this thesis focuses on the ACS convolution operator [23]. Using these
convolutions, 2D CNNs can directly process 3D inputs by modelling spatial information
along the axial, coronal, and sagittal axes of data (see figure 1.2) with three independent
partitions of their 2D convolutional kernels. The issue of large parameter size of native 3D
models is thus tackled by adopting two-dimensional convolutional kernels. Conveniently,
one is still left with a network that can directly process 3D inputs. This is unlike other 2D
approaches which must decompose 3D data into 2D elements to function.
In this thesis, different possibilities will be explored for CNNs that use ACS convolutions.
The goal is to improve segmentation performance on medical datasets. Motivated by the
tri-planar nature of the kernels partitions, we investigate network architecture elements
and considerations that account for the way data is processed in a CNN that uses ACS
convolutions. Namely, the impact of the individual normalization of axis-specific informa-
tion for ACS CNNs is investigated. Additionally, we investigate the feasibility of leveraging
the kernel splits to add a classification task which differentiates between axis-specific
information. Resulting from our experimental setting, we also provide a comparison of

1.1
Hypotheses
This thesis sets out to investigate the following hypothesis:
1. AnACS U-Net model achieves identical segmentation results compared to an equiv-
alent 3D U-Net, in terms of Dice Coefficient and Hausdorff Distance.
2. Using group normalization with three channel groups in an ACS CNN, makes the
three value distributions of axis-specific feature maps more distinct.
3. Group Normalization with three channel groups enables higher segmentation per-
formance for ACS models when compared to Batch normalization, in terms of a
higher Dice Coefficient or lower Hausdorff distance.
4. For ACS models, adding an auxiliary classification task that differentiates between
axis-specific feature maps, contributes to better segmentation performance in the
form of a higher Dice Coefficient or a lower Hausdorff Distance.

1.2.1
Convolutional Terminology
Before proceeding, given the multiple references to elements of convolutional layers through-
out this thesis, it is important to define a fixed terminology to ensure consistency.
1. Convolutional Kernel or Convolutional Layer: The kernel or convolutional layer
consists of all convolutional parameters. Let Cin ∈Z+ and Cout ∈Z+ denote the
dimensionality of the input and output channels of a 2-dimensional convolutional
kernel. Let k1 ∈Z+ and k2 ∈Z+ denote its two spatial dimensions. The kernel thus
consists of parameters W2D ∈RCout×Cin×k1×k2. These are also commonly called
the weights of the kernel.
2. Filters: A convolution kernel is composed of several filters. These are the elements
responsible for analyzing information of the input given to the kernel. The number
of filters corresponds to the Cout dimension of the kernel. Each individual filter thus
holds a set of parameters Wfilter ∈RCin×k1×k2. Each filter analyzes information of
patches of size k1 × k2 along the spatial dimensions of the input while considering
the entire information present in the channels of each of those patches. Figure 1.3
exemplifies a filter analyzing multiple regions of the input to the convolutional layer.
The analysis of information by a single filter results in a specific output feature map.

1.2.2
Modelling 3D Context with 2D Models
The three-dimensional nature of medical imaging datasets implies a choice of model di-
mensionality. Most obviously, one can use a CNN with 3D convolutions to process 3D
inputs [17, 24, 25, 26, 27]. Nonetheless, there have been a multitude of publications
that adopted different methods of modelling 3D medical volumes with 2D models that this
section will categorize.
1.2.2.1
2.5D Approaches
These approaches consist of using 2D CNN architectures while trying to integrate volu-
metric information. Given the nature of their operations, this CNNs architectures can only
process the original 3D volumes through their decomposition in 2D slices.
1.2.2.1.1
Multi-Slice Methods
Let B ∈Z+ and C ∈Z+ denote the batch size and channel dimensions of a 2D-tensor.
Let H ∈Z+ and W ∈Z+ denote denote its two spatial dimensions. For simplicity, let us
assume B = 1. In these methods, the two-dimensional input tensor given to the network
of shape 1 × C × H × W is arrived at by concatenating C 2D tensors of shape 1 × H × W
along the channel dimension. These 2D tensors generally consist of neighbouring 2D
slices of the medical volume.
[28], [29] and [30] treat a 3D volume as a multi-channel tensor for the segmentation of
medical images. Thus, a 2.5D representation of the input is reached by stacking neigh-
bouring slices along the channel dimension to form the input tensor. Rather than neigh-
bouring slices, [31] assigns slices of different axes into different input channels and used
a single network for lymph node detection. While effective, there is no spatial alignment
between data in each channel.
In [32], a 2.5D architecture with a multi-branch decoder is proposed. Adjacent slices are
stacked along the channel dimensions and fed through the encoder. There is one decoder
branch per intended output such that for an input tensor with input channels C, there are
C −2 branches. The authors intend to reestablish the slice specific features provided by
the encoder by associating each particular decoder branch with a specific slice.
Although these methods intuitively grant the network volumetric context, there is an under-
lying peculiarity. Incorporating spatial information into the channel dimension of the input
tensor violates the principle of local spatial modelling done in CNNs. In CNNs, spatial
information is analyzed locally while information along the channels dimensions is con-
sidered in its entirety by the convolutional filter that processes it [33]. Therefore, handling
data in this manner can imply an asymmetrical analysis of it. This is due to the non-
local treatment of information the channel dimension, along which the slices are stacked.
Figure 1.4 illustrates this phenomena.
For the segmentation of transrectal ultrasound images, [34] did not observe a significant
improvement in segmentation performance with the use of neighbouring slices. [35] also
did not obtain a higher Dice Coefficient for brain-region segmentation performance in their
2.5 approach (7 neighbouring slices to predict the segmentation masks of the 3 central
ones) compared to a standard 2D baseline. Recently, [19] showed through a comprehen-
sive empirical study that multi-slice approaches do not appear to improve segmentation
performance compared to a standard 2D or 3D data representation. Also, they have
shown that increasing the number of slices fed to the network does not necessarily trans-
late into better results. This can happen for different reasons. As more slices are stacked,

1.2.2.1.2
(Tri-)Planar Methods
Most methods in this section adopt a tri-planar representation of 3D medical images
through their decomposition into 2D slices along their principal three orthogonal axes.
Separate 2D CNNs are used for processing slices of each axis. Their results are then
fused. Figure 1.5 represents a generic tri-planar architecture for the processing of volu-
metric medical data.
[36] and [37] use an ensemble of three CNNs, one for each axis, and combine them for
the task of tibial cartilage segmentation and pancreas segmentation, respectively. In a
similar fashion, [38] and [39] employ one CNN for the axial, coronal and sagittal, axes for
hepatic vessel and femur surface segmentation. For classification of lung diseases, [40]
also employs three different convolutional paths which are then fused.
Besides training three different networks and fusing their results, [13] adopts a multi-slice
representation into each CNN and achieves state-of-the-art results in pancreas segmenta-
tion through progressive refinement of the segmentation masks. Acoarse network roughly
segments the liver in an initial stage. This segmentation mask is then cropped around the
region of interest and sent to a second fine-scaled network which iteratively updates it
using the ground truth segmentation for a fixed number of steps.
While showing promising results, the choice of three data axes constitutes a ceiling in
terms of the spatial information that is considered. The use of simple modules, such as
majority voting, to fuse predictions from three independent CNNs may also not be ideal
at accounting for the information present in each component’s prediction and its relation
to others [14]. [19] has provided empirical evidence to the poor performance of standard

1.2.2.1.3
Recurrent Architectures
By looking at a volume as an ordered sequence of slices, it is possible to integrate Recur-
rent Neural Network elements to model spatial dependencies between adjacent slices.
[42] utilizes a U-Net like architecture with a one-directional Gated Recurrent Unit [43] at
the end of the encoder to process 3D volumes slice-by-slice. Thus, they model the in-
terdependency of spatial information in adjacent slices. Their empirical results showed
improvements with their recurrent modification.
In [44], the intra and inter slice contexts are handled by different neural components. The
authors handle intra-slice context with an ensemble of U-net like networks. Secondly
they make use of Convolutional LSTM modules [45]. These are responsible for taking the
intra-slice feature maps generated before and modelling inter-slice context from which the
segmentation map is generated.

1.2.2.2
Hybrid Approaches
Hybrid approaches use both 2D and 3D model elements for the analysis of 3D data. In
principle, these models can make use of the best of both the 2D and 3D world such as
using weights pre-trained on large scale natural image datasets and natively learning
spatial 3D context.
[46] proposed a hybrid convolutional network to segment livers and liver tumors. In it, 2D
convolutions are used throughout the encoder. The resulting feature maps are stacked to
form a 3D tensor and processed by 3D convolution layers. For the segmentation of brain
regions, [47] converts volumetric data to two dimensions at an initial stage by flattening the
output of a learnable 3D convolution layer and then continues processing with a standard
2D U-net like architecture to produce slice by slice segmentations. In [48], the authors
use a 2D U-Net like architecture to extract intra-slice features and then a 3D model to
model the volumetric inter-slice information.
In AH-NET [49], first a fully 2D architecture is initialized with the goal of obtaining a pre-
trained 2D encoder which will model inter-slice spatial information. Since the goal is only
to obtain the encoder, the decoder utilized in this phase is very lightweight as it is to be
discarded later on. Upon convergence, a new randomly initialized 3D decoder is trained
while the weights of the 2D learned encoder are kept locked and its convolutional kernels
modified to allow the processing of 3D inputs. Once the decoder also converges, there is
then a phase of jointly tuning both elements of the architecture.
[14] make use of a tri-planar method while fusing the results with a lightweight 3D CNN.
Their 3D network takes the original volume and the Axial, Coronal and Sagittal segmen-
tation map volumes obtained from the 2D networks concatenated as different channels
to arrive at a segmentation. [6] also use a 2D tri-planar method but this time to extract

Multi-Task learning consists of performing more than one task from a shared representa-
tion of the input. The same set of features are used for different tasks such as segmen-

to generalize across the required tasks. This can prove useful in a setting with limited
scale datasets to avoid overfitting. It can also be a way of enriching the encoder’s learned
representation with information that generalizes to several tasks.

Deep Learning Fundamentals
Deep Learning is a subsection of Machine Learning which uses a class of models entitled
Artificial Neural Networks. These have attained an immense popularity in recent times
due to delivering state-of-the-art performance in a variety of tasks and fields such as
image classification [60], image segmentation [61, 62], machine translation [63, 64] and
many more. Their success has been enabled by the evolution of modern hardware and
the computing power that it provides. This justifies their widespread adoption in the last
decade, despite their introduction and early conceptions going as far back as 1943 [65].
2.1

found in training. This can be problematic, specially for smaller batch sizes. As statis-

accurately. Consequently, several alternatives have been proposed which do not explore
the batch dimension for normalization. Alternatives such as Group Normalization [72],
Instance Normalization [73], and Layer Normalization [74], do not compute the mean and
variance along the spatial dimension of tensors. Instead, the channel dimension is used.
[75] recently provided evidence that the effectiveness of Batch Normalization is not tied to
the reduction of the internal covariate shift phenomenon as initially thought. Rather, the
authors claim its impact rests in making the optimization landscape smoother by allowing
for more predictive gradients and the employment of higher learning rates which allow
faster network convergence.
2.2
Convolutional Neural Networks
The modularity of neuron layers makes it theoretically possible to increase the modelling
capacity of a feed forward neural-network indefinitely by adding more hidden layers at the
cost of an increased number of parameters and computational complexity. This becomes
unfeasible when dealing with high dimensional data such as images given the number of
neurons required. Also, the fact that each input unit (a pixel value in the case of gray-
scale 2D images) is linked to a single neuron of the first layer, makes feed-forward neural
networks sensitive to changes in the absolute position of visual information in images. As
an example, a network trained to detect the presence of cats with images which always
Exploring ACS Convolutions
17
==PAGE:26==
have cats in their the top left corner, may not be able to identify cats present when given
new images where cats are present in other areas. Given the one-to-one mapping be-
tween each pixel pixel position and input neuron, feed-forward neural networks are not
shift invariant. They are therefore not commonly used in Computer Vision.
Convolutional Neural Networks [76] were proposed to address the issues inherent to the
use of multi-layer perceptrons for the processing of visual inputs. CNNs are neural net-
work architectures with a fixed set of parameters per layer which are shared across dif-
ferent sections of the input data. This enables the learned representations of the network
to be shit invariant. While reusing layers for different areas of the input solves the shift in-
variance problem, it introduces the issue of not allowing the network to account for global
image context properly.
In CNNs, data is passed through a pipeline consisting of learnable convolutional kernels.
The output of these convolutions are known as feature maps. These are the data elements
which circulate in the network.

2.2.2
Downsampling Feature Maps
Pooling is a major part of the architecture of CNNs. It is used to reduce the spatial dimen-
sions of feature maps. Similarly to convolution operations, a sliding window is passed
through the input. A pooling function is then applied such as max (figure 2.4) or average
pooling. Regions are thus summarized and their information retained by reducing multiple
values to a single one.
Another common way to downsample feature maps is through regular convolution oper-
ations with a higher stride. This will effectively result in smaller feature maps given the
lesser sections of the input which are convolved by the kernel. Either through pooling or
strided convolution, the successive spatial reduction of feature maps allows for increas-
ingly more abstract representations of the data.

2.2.3
Upsampling Feature Maps
While pooling allows for the downsampling of feature maps, the upsampling of feature
maps, meaning the increase in their spatial dimensions, is also used in CNN architectures.
Transposed Convolutions, also referred to as fractionally-strided convolutions or as de-
convolutions (despite not being an actual deconvolution operation in mathematical terms)
are commonly used for upsampling feature maps. In this operation, the input and output
relate to each other in a reverse manner compared to normal convolutions. It is desired
to go from a tensor that has the shape of the output of a normal convolution to a tensor
that has the shape of its input. In this line of thinking, if we see the input to a transposed
convolution as the output of a regular convolution operation, we can think of the trans-
posed convolution operation as a way to attain a feature map of the shape of the input
which was given to that convolution operation. In practice, a transposed convolution can
be substituted with a regular convolution by interleaving its input with rows of 0 values.
Figure 2.5 schematically shows the transposed convolution operation.
Transposed convolutions thus employ learnable parameters for the upsampling of feature
maps. Alternatively, and also commonly done, the upsampling of the spatial dimensions of
feature maps can be done through operations without learnable parameters. For example,
these can be linear upsampling, nearest neighbour upsampling or unmax-pooling. In the
latter, the pooling index is shared from a previous layer and used to upsample the feature
map in that section.

2.3
Deep Learning Methods for Semantic Segmentation
2.3.1
Early Approaches
In 2012, with the use of CNNs, Deep Learning in Computer Vision hit a landmark by out-
performing other approaches in the task of Image Classification [60]. Researchers were
quickly drawn to the use of these models due to their success and capacity to indepen-
dently learn relevant feature representations for the task at hand.
The first attempts at using Neural Network models for semantic segmentation consisted
mainly of reusing architectures used for image classification such as AlexNet [60] and
VGG [79] by tweaking their fully connected layers (FCLs) [80, 81]. These approaches
suffered from the computational demand inherent to the use of FCLs. Results were gen-
erally not up to standard and therefore refinement techniques such as Conditional Ran-
dom Fields and Super Pixels [82, 83] were used. However, these techniques are still used
throughout more recent works.
Despite working for image classification, the succession of down-sampling operations in
image classification networks produces low-resolution feature maps which account poorly
for spatial information given their small size relative to the input. They are also invariant to
spacial transformations due to the nature of CNNs. Thus, the use of a few fully connected
layers to project their information into an output with a resolution closer to the input image
proved to not be enough for achieving good segmentation results.
2.3.2
Fully Convolutional Networks
Fully Convolutional Networks [61] (FCNs) constitute an important landmark for the task
of semantic segmentation in Deep Learning. Despite still consisting of the adaptation
of a classification network, this work differed in major ways from previous approaches,
namely:
1. As the title indicates, no fully connected layers were used in the network architecture.
This led to a significant speed up in the inference time for a given image.
2. Their network is capable of outputting a dense segmentation map for inputs of arbi-
trary sizes. This is due the upsampling of feature maps done by Transposed Con-
volution operations and the non-use of FCLs.
3. The use of skip connections, previously described in [84]. Skip connections allow
to preserve information which would otherwise be lost by storing and forwarding
feature maps at certain depths to deeper layers thorough concatenation or addition.
Coarse feature maps are progressively turned into dense pixel predictions through learn-
able Transposed Convolution kernels with the aid of information present in previous layers.

several segmentation datasets compared to previous methods while speeding up learning

2.3.3
Encoder Decoder Models
After FCNs, new publications on semantic segmentation have since overwhelmingly made
use of the principles introduced. Their architecture was extended to use skip connections
from earlier layers [85, 86] which resulted in a more moderate upsampling process and
greater precision. This trend eventually manifested itself in Encoder-Decoder (ED) ar-
chitectures such as SegNet [87] and U-Net [88], the latter having been introduced in the
context of medical images.
In ED architectures, the downsampling and upsampling of features maps are performed in
a symmetrical, gradual and hierarchical manner. The encoder gradually reduces the spa-
tial dimension with pooling layers while deeper convolutional layers increase the number
of filters used. The encoder is thus responsible for learning an effective compact spatial
representation of its input in the form of a certain number of low-resolution feature maps
just as in a classification architecture. The decoder then gradually upsamples the feature
maps, increasing their spatial dimensions while the number of filters used in convolutional
layers decreases along the upwards path. It does so aided by the use of skip connections
that carry over information from layers of equivalent depths in the encoder. This allows
for the creation of hierarchical and depth specific features. Figure 2.7 schematically rep-
resents the U-net architecture, the most iconic ED model.

state-of-the-art performance on natural image segmentation datasets by introducing new
architectures, modules and methodologies which have the ED principles as a basis [89,

vanilla U-net baselines. There has also been work using Neural Architecture Search [95,
96] in order to find the must suitable U-net architecture for a given task [97].

2.4.2
Binary Cross Entropy Pixel Wise Loss
Cross Entropy is a measure of the difference of two probability distributions for a certain
event. The binary cross entropy for a prediction p and target value t, is defined as:
BCE(p, t) = −p · log(t) −(1 −p) · log(1 −t)
(2.6)
It is generally used as a loss function for the task of classification. One can consider
each element of a predicted segmentation mask in the output of a network as a distinct
classification attempt and consequently make use of this loss. To turn the model’s output
into a probability distribution, traditionally a softmax or sigmoid function is applied.
2.4.3
Hausdorff Distance
The Hausdorff Distance measures how far two set of points are from each other according
to a distance metric. Specifically, between two set of points A and B, the Hausdorff
distance is the maximum distance of any point of A to its closest point in B. Using the
euclidean distance, we can define it as:
HD(A, B) = maxa∈Aminb∈B||a −b||2
(2.7)
This metric is not symmetric as HD(A, B) ̸= HD(B, A). It is therefore common to com-
pute the symmetric Hausdorff Distance as:
HDsym(A, B) = max(HD(A, B), HD(B, A))
(2.8)
The Hausdorff distance is widely used for evaluating the quality of the obtained segmen-
tation from models in the medical image community given that it takes into account the
spatial position of each single point. It is however not often minimized directly as a loss
function possible due to the challenge it poses from an optimization stand point. In [101],
three Hausdorff distance inspired losses for the task of semantic segmentation in medical
images are proposed.
In its purest form as in equation 2.7, it is very sensitive to outliers given that only the maxi-
mum distance is considered. A possible workaround is to consider only certain percentiles
of the computed distances for evaluation of model performance. Figure 2.8 exemplifies

3.1
ACS Convolutions
Several approaches that use 2D convolutional kernels for processing 3D inputs were de-
scribed in section 1.2.2.3. Among these, ACS convolutions [23] do so by partitioning 2D
convolutional kernels three-ways. Spatial information from the axial, coronal, and sagittal
axes of the input data is analysed by three independent sets of filters. Thus, a tri-planar
analysis of data takes place in ACS convolutions. This is done by projecting the filters
of each convolutional kernel partition into a 3D space along different directions (see fig-
ure 3.1). Differently from the traditional tri-planar approach, in ACS convolutions, a single
network processes data. Thus, the necessity of fusing results from different CNNs is elim-
inated. Also, rather than using a single final fusion module, all convolutional layers of the
network adopt a tri-planar analysis of data. As a result, each layer acts as its own fusion
module of tri-planar information from the previous layer.

3.2
Architectural Elements and Training Considerations for
ACS CNNs
In ACS convolutions, a third of the filters in a convolutional layer model a specific axis
of the input. Given the tri-planar nature of the kernel splits, we hypothesize there may
be a benefit in accounting for how axis-specific feature maps are computed. For a given
ACS convolutional layer, the sets of axis-specific filters operate independently from each
other. Consequently, in all likelihood, each set of axis-specific filters learns to map data
differently. Also, information of different axes of medical images is not the same. If it were,
a 2D CNN that operates on slices of a single axis of medical images would yield equally
as good results as any method which integrates volumetric context. In this scenario, there
would be no necessity of integrating volumetric information into CNNs, and no efforts to
do so would be underway.
Thus, we aim to consider ways to adequately match or leverage the way data is processed
in anACS setting. To this end, this section presents two CNN architectural elements which
aim to enhance the tri-planar principles of ACS Convolutions.
3.2.1
Normalization of Axis-Specific Information
Previously described in section 2.1.1, normalization is a fundamental component in neural
network models. Its most common form is Batch Normalization (BN) [71]. However, there
are other alternatives. One of them is Group Normalization (GN) [72]. The difference
between BN and GN rests in the tensor dimension along which the mean and standard
deviation are computed. In GN, the batch dimension is not used to compute the mean
and variance which feature maps are normalized by. Rather, the channel dimension of
tensors is used.
Let us consider a 2-D tensor of size N × C × H × W where N ∈Z+ denotes the batch
size, C ∈Z+ the channel dimension, and H ∈Z+, and W ∈Z+ consist of its spatial
dimensions. GN considers the tensor’s channel dimension C as G ∈Z+ equally sized
groups. Normalization is then applied for each group individually by using its mean and
variance. Figure 3.6 illustrates the difference between BN and GN.

their individual normalization. This scenario would correspond to histogram (e) and the
use of BN in an ACS network. Using BN, each channel (or feature map) is normalized

individually according to a mean and variance computed along the batch dimension. This
operation does not consider information present in other channels, i.e., in other feature
maps from the same axis. Rather, the values of the same feature map across batch el-
ements are considered. Reversely, if same-axis feature maps are normalized by their
same-axis joint mean and variance, their characteristics can be better preserved in the
resulting normalized distribution.
By more adequately conserving the characteristics of their individual elements, we aim to
make the three sets of axis-specific feature maps more distinct between each other. This
way, information regarding the analysis of the three separate axes of the input will likely
be better separated and more easily distinguishable. Given the independent analysis of
information in an ACS convolutional layer by three sets of filters, this practice serves as a
way to further enhance the distinct nature of the resulting axis-specific information. This
scenario is likely more optimal for network learning.
Overall, we aim to account for the tri-planar nature of ACS tensors in the choice of normal-
ization layers. Thus, through the use of GN with three channel groups, we will investigate
if doing so is beneficial over using BN in an ACS CNN.

3.2.2
Distinguishing Between Axis-Specific Feature Maps
We now consider a multi-task learning scenario by incorporating an additional classifica-
tion task. Architecturally, a classification branch is added to the encoder of an ACS CNN,
as seen in figure 3.9.
Figure 3.9: Multi-Task Learning architecture. The encoder and decoder remain unchanged from
the original ACS CNN architecture. A classification branch is added to the last encoder block.
Feature maps that result from the final encoder block are fed into a classification branch.
The classification branch is tasked with learning to distinguish between feature maps that
correspond to the modelling of different axes. By introducing this auxiliary classification
task, we aim to achieve additional guidance and regularization of the encoder during train-
ing. We hope this then translates into superior segmentation performance.
In an ACS convolutional layer with Cout filters, it is possible to access ≈Cout/3 axis-
specific feature maps per batch element, after the convolutional operation. Thus, in the
final encoder block, we randomly select one axis-specific feature map from each batch
element while maintaining a balance between the number of feature maps selected from
each axis. For example, for a batch size of six, six feature maps corresponding to two
axial, two coronal and two sagittal feature maps are selected and fed into the classifi-
cation pipeline. This process is exemplified in figure 3.10. The classification branch is
then tasked with differentiating between the three separate classes (axial, coronal, and
sagittal). The feature maps are selected randomly per batch element. For example, let
us consider an ACS convolutional layer with sixty filters. Each batch element will contain
twenty axial, twenty coronal and twenty sagittal feature maps to choose from. If, for a
given batch element, we aim to choose an axial feature map to give to the classification
branch, any one of the available twenty is equally as likely to be the one that is selected.
Thus, we set out to investigate if the kernel split mechanism at the core of ACS convo-
lutions allows for the employment of an additional classification task that can boost seg-
mentation performance. Its self-supervised nature implies it can be easily implemented
across ACS CNNs.

The Medical Decathlon [22] originated from an effort to create a dataset repository which
can be used as an universal benchmark for medical segmentation task performance. It
gathers ten different datasets comprising different target structures and modalities, col-
lected from different sources. The decathlon itself consists of an ongoing competition
where users submit their segmentation results for each dataset.
Access to the ground truth segmentation masks of the test set is not granted. The only
way to obtain a performance indicator on the test set is by participating in the medical
decathlon competition itself. This imposes the limit of one submission per day and a
considerable time to obtain the results from the evaluation server. Thus, the training,
validation and testing data partitions used in this experimental setup were generated using
only the training data provided.

effects of the proposed methods. Testing them in a generic architecture maximizes their
applicability to a broader audience.

4.2.2
Rationale of U-Net Model Size
To avoid resorting to a patch-wise approach, and meet memory constrains on the hard-
ware used (NVIDIA Tesla V100 GPU with 32 or 16 GB of memory), a low number of filters
was used. This allowed to process full volumes of BRATS volumes directly. From pre-
vious experimentations with the same network using more filters (equalling 17, 266, 241
parameters), a patch-wise approach was necessary as input volumes of BRATS could
not fit into memory. It was also observed that the size of the patches in relation to the
original volumes plays a critical role for proper segmentation of target structures. An ini-
tial experimentation with patches of size 80 × 80 × 80, yielded poor results. For example,
for the BRATS challenge, [17] purposefully compromised on batch size to feed the net-
work larger sections of the input. Other publications also choose larger input sizes even
if detrimental to batch size [100]. Defining how large patches should be, from what re-
gions of the volume they should come, or if the whole volume should be decomposed into
patches are questions that arise in a patch-wise approach. Feeding entire images to the
network was a desired characteristic for simplicity. Thus, as in other publications [17, 19],
a compromise in filter size was done.
Across the publications reviewed, there does not seem to exist a concrete deterministic
rule to how large a model should be to adequately match the size or sample quantity of
a dataset. For example, a standard U-Net model with half a million trainable parameters
[19] can consistently achieve results that are not proportionally far from a more complex
U-Net based approach which uses sixty million parameters [41] on the same datasets.
Thus, the notion of more parameters equalling significant better performance is not clear
for medical applications. For example, traditional image classification architectures can
be unnecessarily deep for medical applications [113]. From our part, this allowed to com-
fortably use a small model to serve our purposes.

4.3.1
Loss Function
The loss function used to train the models for segmentation consisted of the Dice Coef-
ficient (DC) loss, previously described in section 2.4. Concretely, the soft DC loss was
used. Thus, no thresholding was done on the model output logits to produce a binary
segmentation mask. This was considered safer than arbitrarily thresholding the models
output. The DC loss was averaged over each channel of the model output. Each channels
corresponds to the predicted segmentation mask of a target class.
For the task of classification, the Cross-Entropy loss was used to distinguish between
feature maps of specific axis. However, only the segmentation loss in the form of the DC
was used to determine model selection. Classification serve as an auxiliary loss and is
thus not considered for model choice.
Let LDC be the DC loss, Lcls the classification loss, and L the final global loss. For training
the model with the classification task, L is computed as:
L = LDC + α ∗Lcls
(4.2)
Where α is the weight attributed to the classification loss.

Data Augmentation
It was decided not to use data augmentation techniques when training models. Data aug-
mentation of samples introduces variability across different runs due to a non-deterministic
nature. Given that our aim was to replicate as much as possible the experimental setting
across different experiments with the same data, data augmentation techniques were de-
cided against.
4.4
Reported Metrics
The metrics reported in this thesis are collected by performing inference one sample at a
time. Once a model is selected, metrics are collected by processing the test set samples
one at a time regardless of the batch size used during training. This also holds true for
the segmentation examples provided.
The concept of batch image processing is not always present in CNN applications for the
segmentation of medical images. In systems which use CNNs, the necessity to process
a single sample may arise. Also, multiple images may not have the same size between
each other which makes their processing in a batch manner non immediate. Even if a
collection of images is fed to the model, there is no assurance that batch size will be the
same as the one used in training. To ensure the generalization abilities of the analyzed
methods, metrics are collected for the simplest and most generic case of single image
processing. Thus, a batch size of 1 is used to collect segmentation metrics on the test
set.

5.1.2.2
Discussion
The introduction of the 3D U-Net allowed for a clearer understanding of the impact of
normalization choice. In general terms, our experimental setting favours the use of GN
over BN for the datasets considered. For BRATS, we obtain superior results in both the
ACS U-Net and the 3D U-Net models. For LIDC, this happens for the ACS U-Net.
For BRATS, this improvement is not entirely surprising. GN has been empirically shown
to be better suited for use with smaller batch sizes [72]. For example, the winning entry for
the BRATS 2018 competition [17] used GN as normalization layers. Other top performing
methods for this dataset also choose GN or other alternatives to BN [100, 111, 116, 117]
due to the use of small batch sizes. Thus, for both the ACS and 3D U-Net models, the
fact that GN outperforms BN should not come as a surprise for BRATS.
For LIDC, the 3D U-Net is the only instance thus far where there is a lack of statistical
significance for the average increase in segmentation performance provided by GN. The
fact that the batch size is not as small as in BRATS may be a reason why the gap in
performance between the use of BN and GN is not as pronounced for the 3D U-Net.
Apart from that, the most interesting takeaway from the use of a 3D model has not yet
been discussed. The major performance gap between the BN and GN variant of the ACS
model is not present in the 3D model. Consequently, we can infer this phenomenon is
related to the use of ACS convolutions in our experimental setting. For LIDC, GN seems
to be addressing an issue that is tied to the use of BN with the U-Net ACS. In the next
section we will attempt to identify the reason for the performance gap between the GN
and BN variants of the U-Net ACS.
As a final note, it is important to point out that larger batch sizes do not always directly cor-
respond to better BN performance. Several publications address a known phenomenon

[118, 119, 120]. However, these consider much larger two dimensional datasets (such

pared to a baseline of 512 in ImageNet, and [118] classifies a low batch regime to be from

experimental setting with 3D data and small scale medical segmentation datasets.

5.1.4
Normalization Impact on Distribution of Axis-Specific Feature Maps
Aside from the information provided by segmentation metrics, we aim to develop an intu-
ition of the effects of normalization strategy in a model which uses ACS convolutions. To
that end, we analyse the feature maps which result from each encoder and decoder block
after the network is trained.
We set out to investigate if normalization strategy has an impact on the distribution of
values found in axis-specific feature maps for ACS models. As initially hypothesized, if
GN contributes to a better separation between axis-specific information, this distinction
should be both identifiable and quantifiable in the value distributions of axis-specific fea-
ture maps. Intuitively, a greater difference in the distribution of values may imply a more
distinct nature, or clearer separation of the information contained in axis-specific feature
maps.
We define an axis-specific distribution of values as all values of each set of axis-specific
feature maps. For example, for a network block which outputs 5 axial, 5 coronal, and
5 sagittal feature maps, the axial distribution corresponds to all the values found in the
5 axial feature maps. The same happens for the remaining sets. The distribution is the
collection of these values found across all training samples processed by the network.
To have a statistical indicator of the difference among distributions, two statistical tests
were performed. The two-sample Kolmogorov–Smirnov (KS) test [122, 123] was used. It
tests the null hypothesis that two samples are drawn from the same distribution. Given
its two sample nature, it does not match the fact that there are three axis-specific feature
map distributions. Thus, the test is conducted for each of the three possible combinations
of two axis-specific feature map distributions. Additionally, a K-sample Anderson–Darling
(AD) test [124] is conducted. Similarly, the null hypothesis is that the k provided samples
come from the same population. This test allows for the use of all three (k = 3) axis-
specific distributions.
The previous tests indicate whether two distributions are drawn from the same underlying
distribution. To quantify their difference, we resort to the Jensen-Shannon divergence
(JSD) [125]. We report its squared root, also called the Jensen-Shannon distance (JSd).
Based on the Kullback-Leibler (KL) divergence [126], the JSD has the practical advantage
of being symmetric. For two probability distributions P and Q, the JSD is defined as:

5.1.5
Discussion and Summary Regarding Normalization Strategy Impact
We have thus reached the end of the investigation regarding the impact of normalizing
axis-specific information in ACS models through Group Normalization. In section 5.1.1 an
initial assessment of this impact was done for ACS models. In section 5.1.2 we analysed
the impact of the same normalization strategy on an equivalent 3D model. In section 5.1.3,
we identified a performance gap likely caused by batch size in our ACS BN model. Lastly,
in section 5.1.4, we quantified the impact of normalization strategy on the difference be-
tween distributions of values of axis-specific feature maps. To conclude this section, a final
discussion will be done in terms of the hypotheses (section 1.1) that were investigated up
until now.
Our first hypothesis stated that an equivalent ACS U-Net and 3D U-Net model achieve
identical segmentation results. In light of our empirical results, we reject this hypothesis.
While at first, normalization strategy does not seem directly linked to the analysis of this
hypothesis, it turned out to be determinant. There are two separate findings to report.
For the BRATS dataset, both the BN and GN variants of the ACS U-Net outperformed the
top performing 3D U-Net. While further investigation is necessary to determine exactly
why this happens, the reduced sample size of BRATS may be a factor to consider. The
scale of the dataset possibly benefits from the use of a smaller model. For LIDC, the
scenario is different. The top performing variants of the ACS U-Net and 3D U-net achieve
statistically equivalent performance. In this sense, if considering only the LIDC dataset,
the hypothesis would have been accepted. Nonetheless, for this dataset, this claim is
highly contingent on the use of GN for the ACS model. As investigated, the BN variant of
the ACS U-Net severely underperforms.
Our second hypothesis stated that the three value distributions of axis-specific feature
maps in ACS networks, are made more distinct with the use of Group Normalization. As
shown in section 5.1.4, we have found strong empirical evidence to support this claim.
For both datasets, it is evident that the JSd reported a greater, statistically significant
difference amongst the axis-specific distributions which result from most network blocks,
when compared to the use of Batch Normalization. Thus, we have no reason to reject
this hypothesis.
Our third hypothesis stated that GN enables higher segmentation performance for ACS
models when compared to BN. Given the results of section 5.1.1 we accept this hypoth-
esis. Both for the BRATS and LIDC dataset, the introduction of GN allowed for average
superior segmentation performance. Statistical significance was always found in at least
one metric. The individual analysis of the use of GN in a 3D U-net model (section 5.1.2)
would indicate that this phenomenon is not particularly tied to the use ofACS convolutions.
GN is also beneficial for BRATS when using a 3D U-Net. However, more than beneficial,
GN is determinant to avoid subpar performance for the LIDC dataset in the ACS U-Net.
This phenomenon indicates that normalization strategy may be of special importance for
ACS models. It seems likely there is an underlying issue with the use of BN for our ACS
U-Net. Although unidentified, we have shown that the use of GN addresses it, and that
the increase of batch size enhances it.

5.2.3
Discussion
We have investigated the feasibility of the leveraging the axis-specific nature of feature
maps in ACS CNNs to introduce a classification task that can boost segmentation perfor-
mance. In our experimental setting, having the model learn to distinguish between axis-
specific feature maps does not have any impact on segmentation performance. Thus, we
reject the hypothesis which stated this practice was possible.
The underlying issue seems to be with how trivial this task is for the model. Despite the
random selection of feature maps, the network is quickly able to fully learn the classifica-
tion task. The investigation conducted in section 5.1.4 can help us understand why this
happens. As was empirically shown, both for the GN and BN models, axis-specific fea-
ture maps consist of three distinct distributions. The two statistical tests conducted were
unanimous in concluding that the distributions of axis-specific feature maps are different
amongst them. Even though the computed JSDb is amongst the smallest for the final
encoder block (table 5.13a), the distribution of values are still distinct. Thus, we conclude
that the difference between axis-specific feature maps is such that anACS CNN can easily
distinguish between them.
To visually observe the distinct character of axis-specific filters, figure 5.9 shows two-
dimensional projections of information of three axis-specific filters, one filter of each axis
is present. This projections concern axis-specific filters for the last encoder block of the GN
and BN variants of the ACS U-Net. No matter the combination of filters tried, plots always
evidenced this pattern. The information of each axis-specific filter is always projected into
its own individual cluster. Transposing this information into our setting, the classification
branch is tasked with distinguishing between these three clearly separate clusters. This
task turns out to be trivial.

Conclusion
6.1
Summary and Discussion
This thesis consisted of an exploration of ACS convolutions. These enable the processing
of 3D inputs by a 2D CNN. In these convolutions, a tri-planar analysis of 3D inputs is con-
ducted with three independent sets of filters. We investigated the impact of normalization
choice. Through Group Normalization, we inspected the effect of the independent nor-
malization of axis-specific information that results from the analysis of data in ACS CNNs.
Additionally, we have assessed the feasibility of introducing a classification task to differ-
entiate between axis-specific feature maps, in order to boost segmentation performance.
Based on our experimental setting, we have verified that for a U-Net architecture, ACS
convolutions are either equivalent or superior to their 3D counterpart. In this regard, ACS
convolutions are a viable alternative to 3D convolutions, one that is still under explored.
ACS convolutions have advantages over 3D convolutions. By using 2D CNN architec-
tures a lower parameter size is present, which makes models less prone to overfitting
and lighter to store. A smaller number of FLOPS also takes place, making computations
more efficient. These factors can contribute to their preference over 3D convolutions.
Through the analysis of normalization strategy, we have assessed that Group Normal-
ization with three channel groups is superior over Batch Normalization for our ACS U-
Net model. Group Normalization provided superior segmentation performance for both
datasets used. Furthermore, Group Normalization was determinant to prevent the ACS
U-Net from severely underperforming comparatively to the 3D U-Net for the LIDC dataset.
The underlying issue was identified to be related or enhanced with an increase in batch
size. In this regard, a higher batch size does not necessarily contribute to better perfor-
mance when using Batch Normalization layers in an ACS context. The reason as to why
that happens is unclear. The fact that the two considered 3D models did not exhibit this
behaviour indicates that it is not a dataset specific phenomenon. We have also ruled out
model capacity as a reason for this occurrence.
With that said, until the issue is identified, and as a way to prevent it, Group Normaliza-
tion should likely be the default normalization choice for ACS models. It proved to be a
significantly superior choice of normalization in our experiments, one that likely carries
special significance in an ACS context. We have also showed that Group Normalization
with three channel groups contributes to making axis-specific information more distinct.
Intuitively, we believe this is a desired behavior to have in an ACS CNN.
Lastly, we have verified that an ACS U-Net can easily learn to distinguish between axis-
specific feature maps. This knowledge leads to no difference in segmentation perfor-
mance. We attribute this to the trivial nature of the task given how quickly and accurately
the network is able to learn to perform this distinction. It seems that axis-specific infor-
mation is different enough such that its differentiation does not present any relevance or
difficulty for model learning.
Outside of the defined hypotheses, something that was evident at times in this thesis was
that model size can be varied for the LIDC dataset without an impact in segmentation per-
formance taking place. Our ACS U-Net ACS with 5% of the trainable parameters of other
publications [23, 15] reaches essentially the same performance for the LIDC dataset. Also
for LIDC, upon a posterior verification, the performance of the smaller 3D U-Net was not
Exploring ACS Convolutions
65
==PAGE:74==
not statistically significant from the U-Net 3D with three times its size. This phenomenon
serves as a good indication that one should not immediately use a large scale model be-
fore truly understanding the needs of their experimental setting. If it is possible to achieve
equivalent performance with more compact models, one should aim to use the smaller
models. ACS convolutions are a good stride in this direction. The Deep Learning commu-
nity should not be wasting computational resources when there is no need to do so. The
scale of the field and its increasing environmental impact merit further attention to how
large a model needs to be.
6.2
Future Work
Despite verifying that Group Normalization makes axis-specific information more distinct,
we did not establish causality between this phenomenon and the increase in segmenta-
tion performance observed. Thus, for future work, one of the first items would be to verify
that this practice actually consists of leveraging the tri-planar nature of the kernel splits
of ACS convolutions. For example, it is pertinent to understand whether setting the num-
ber of channel groups to three is the most optimal form of Group Normalization for ACS
CNNs, or if perhaps other group numbers would yield similar performance. A compre-
hensive analysis on the relation between the separation of axis-specific information and
segmentation performance would be a welcome addition. A bigger separation between
individually normalized groups of feature maps can also also happen in a 3D CNN through
the use of Group Normalization. Thus, it is important to understand what significance it
carries for ACS models.
For future work, a detailed inspection would have to be conducted around the phenomenon
observed for the LIDC dataset. It is important to get to the core of the reason why the
Batch Normalization variant of the considered ACS U-Net underperforms. Analogously,
one must understand why exactly Group Normalization prevents this. It is paramount to
understand whether it is caused by a factor tied to our particular experimental setting, or if
it relates to a systemic problem with ACS convolutions. The first step in this regard would
be to focus more on the LIDC dataset. Given that the problem seems to be enhanced with
batch size, cross validation should be performed on an increasing number of batch sizes
across all models to identify the threshold at which performance is significantly affected.
Different model sizes and architectures should also be tried with a constant comparison
between ACS and 3D counterparts. Hopefully this practice would lead us to understand-
ing the phenomenon. It would also be relevant to experiment with a greater number of
datasets, models and model sizes to see if the issue arises anywhere else.
A deeper inspection as to why the ACS U-Net is superior to the 3D U-Net for the BRATS
dataset should also take place. As previously mentioned, one could look into model size
in relation to dataset scale. The aim would be to understand if the ACS U-Net is superior
for BRATS due to its reduced parameter size, or if this fact is actually tied to the type of
convolutions used.
In broader terms, it is relevant to establish a clearer understanding of the relationship
between ACS and 3D CNNs. More work comparing ACS and 3D convolutions would
be a welcome addition to the debate between the choice of 2D or 3D CNN approaches
for the modelling of medical images. It is relevant to understand the actual feasibility of
substituting 3D convolutions with ACS convolutions, or when to use one instead of the
other. This can only happen by applying it to a greater variety of experimental settings. In
this regard, a large-scale, systematic study covering a wide range of datasets and models
would be a welcome addition.

The expansion of ACS convolutions is also a possible scenario for future work. As shown
in [41], the analysis of a greater number of axes can be beneficial. Thus, having more
than three axes analysed by the filters of ACS convolutional layers could be a way to
improve upon ACS convolutions. Another idea rests in the way filters are split in an ACS
convolutional layer. Depending on the dataset, there may be more information along a
certain axis of the input images. This can happen in terms of quantify or relevance for
the task at hand. Thus, it is possible that an uneven assignment of filters could be a way
to more efficiently use ACS convolutions for certain scenarios rather than always splitting
them equally for the analysis of each axis.

A.2
Two-Dimensional Projection of Feature Maps
To project data onto a two-dimensional space, we use Umap [129]. Analogously to, t-SNE
[130], Umap consists of a dimensionality reduction technique. Its main advantages over
the latter consist of computational speed and a greater ability to preserve global structure
of the data. Despite the stochastic nature of both, Umap has been observed to produce
more consistent results across runs. For our purposes, the computational speed highly
favored the use of Umap.
It is important to note that plots displayed across both network variants are not directly
comparable. There is no direct correspondence between information present in a feature
map across different training runs, even for the same network architecture.
For this visualization, one axis-specific feature map from each set of axis-specific feature
maps is selected. Afterwards, its information is stored along all training samples pro-
cessed. Thus, each data point represents the learned representation of three different
axis-specific filters for each training sample. Each axis-specific filter is identified by its
own different color. No difference was observed in the Umap projections between feature
maps corresponding to information of elements from the training or test set.
