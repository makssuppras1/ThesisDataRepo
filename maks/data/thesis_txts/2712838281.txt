Abstract
Current Deep Learning models face an underlying issue: they are, for the most part,
treated as black boxes. The input is known, the output is known, the algorithm is also
known but the explanation on how to reach the output (at least in human terms) lacks de­
tail. Explanations methods do exist at the moment for these models, but their reliability is
still questioned by researchers, for example, in medical imaging. In this thesis, an analysis
will be made on how stable explanability methods can be through common data augmen­
tation techniques such as geometrical rotations or color transformations (Hue, Brightness,
and Saturation changes, among others). The initial hypothesis is that even though pre­
dictions of the images can be stable to this data augmentation techniques (usually used
to improve accuracy even), their explanations will not be as stable.
In order to prove or disprove the hypothesis, an analysis of different explanation methods,
with different Convolutional Neural Networks (CNN) will be made for classifying ImageNet
colour images. By using the package iNNvestigate, a full on comparison can be done
in this case for multiple explanability methods and multiple CNN models. By creating
this experiment a new toolbox was made and with it the opportunity to test many more
augmentation techniques, models and even different tasks.

Introduction
Computer vision has been one of the driving forces of AI research for the past two decades
and its market is continuing to grow every year. Convolutional Neural Networks (CNNs)
have found great success in these computer vision problems. Ideally, achieving a full
understanding of what is being learned by the CNN could greatly advance us knowledge­
wise, for example in medical imaging where CNNs are already being used [1]. For some
of these fields, explainable AI is still seen with skepticism [2] since explanations are not
being useful still for the researchers but only for developers and auditors. As suggested
in the article, explainable AI should advance before it can be used for this purpose.
A way of advancing forward on its understanding is to associate properties from human
explanations with AI explanations and see how well they correlate. During this thesis, the
focus will be on the equivariance and invariance properties of explanations.
When a human does see a cat for example and tries to discern whether what they are
seeing is a cat or a dog or something else they look specifically at certain features such as
the whiskers, the tail, the fur, etc. These features should remain the same, for example, if
the image of the cat is rotated or if their colors are changed (for example a scene with less
brightness such as an afternoon in comparison to a midday­taken image). This concept of
them not varying is called equivariance in the case of geometrical transformations (since
for a static image, the features would move in space) and invariance in the case of color
transformations (since for a static image, the features would remain in the same spot).
These types of transformations can be considered data augmentation.
Data augmentation can be used to improve accuracy in Machine Learning classification
tasks [3] and it is used for training in multiple classification tasks. It is known that the pre­
dictions are robust when facing data augmentations. During the thesis, different explana­
tion methods and different Neural Networks will be explored to see if their explanations
are also robust when facing them.
Ideally when building models for classification tasks, both, model predictions and explana­
tions, should be invariant to perturbations in color, contrast, and brightness. On the other
hand, when facing geometrical transformations such as translation, scale, and rotation
of the images, the explanations should be equivariant. Even though translation equivari­
ance has been partially achieved in CNNs [4], creating CNNs equivariant to these other
properties is currently being researched [5, 6].
Despite this, CNNs are still sensitive to small changes in training data of these equivariants
as seen in adversarial examples [7]. Yet the most typical [8, 9, 10] way of building robust
models is by using data augmentation techniques.
Current understanding of Data augmentation is quite limited, as seen in [11], whereby
changing the scale between training and test an important drop in accuracy can be seen
which should not be expected due to equivariance. Explainability methods have become
popular due to the need of explaining the black­box algorithms to make them fully trust­
worthy [12].
For some classification tasks, such as ImageNet, which will be the focus of the thesis, for
translation, rotation or scaling, the explanation is expected to be translated, rotated, or
scaled, and therefore the transformation would be equivariant. On the other hand, color,

brightness, and contrast changes should be fully invariant. The objective of this thesis
is to find how robust these invariant or equivariant properties are and where the limit lies
where they are no longer preserved across different explanation methods and ML models.
In this thesis, firstly, to compare the explainability results, correlation and the Structural
Similarity Index Measure (SSIM) [13] will be used to quantitatively evaluate how good the

2.1
Explainability in Machine Learning
Explainability in the context of Machine Learning (ML) can be understood as gaining
knowledge on how the input is converted (throughout the black box) to an output.
As defined in [14]:
Given an audience, an explainable Artificial Intelligence is one that pro­
duces details or reasons to make its functioning clear or easy to understand
Explainability, as previously mentioned, is one of the main barriers in AI at the moment.
This downstreams from the fact that AI accuracy improvement, comes not only as a by­
product of increased performance in software but also from the use of more complex
algorithms. These more complex models are perceived as black boxes even by develop­
ers [15]. As Figure 2.1 shows, there are multiple different purposes of explainability in ML
across diverse audience profiles.
In the context of image classification and Deep Neural Networks (DNN), which is the
interest of this dissertation, DNNs are seen as black­box algorithms. Therefore, in order
to understand the process from its input to its output a post­hoc explainability method
must be used.

Deconvolutional Networks (DeconvNets)
Deconvolution, as proposed in [24], is an explanation technique for Convolutional Net­
works by using De­convolutional Networks. These are networks that use the same build­
ing blocks as the CNNs (pooling and filtering) but aim to increase the size (going from
output to input size) instead of reducing it (input to output). Deconvnet can show the full
path going from the output back to the input. Even though they were originally proposed
for unsupervised learning, in [24] they are not used for it but for unveiling the activity of
intermediate layers and pieces of a pretrained CNN.

The explanation method starts with the layer that is wanted to be projected down and the
reconstructed signal will be initialized equal to the activations of that layer. From there
onwards, backpropagation is performed all the way. Different casuistics can be found
while doing so:
• MaxPool: When encountered with MaxPooling, firstly a list of indices where the
inputs were pooled and passed up in the forward pass is found. After that, during
the backward pass, the reconstructed signal values are passed only to these indices
equating to zero for the rest of them.
• ReLU: When encountered with a ReLU layer, the reconstructed signal should be
passed only if it is positive, if not it should be zeroed.
• Weights: When CNN layers or any weight multiplications are encountered, the
transposed of the weights have to be multiplied with the reconstructed signal and
passed down.
It is important to notice that the method does not invert the CNN exactly but only the
pixels favoring the activation of a hidden layer. Even though it is called deconvolution, an

2.2
Invariance and Equivariance
In mathematics, an invariant is a property that remains unchanged throughout a series of
transformations. In the context of functions, a function remains invariant with respect to
a transformation if and only if the action of the function after the transformation is equal
to the action of the function before the transformation. In the context of this thesis, these
transformations will be image augmentations. More formally, for the experiments, for a
feature representation f of an image i under a group transformation G, if
f(G(i)) = f(i)
(2.10)
then f is invariant under G [27].
On the other hand, equivariance is a form of symmetry that when applying a certain trans­
formation to, both a domain, and a codomain of a function (in this case, the explainability),
the function commutes with the transformation. Following the same notation as in invari­
Analysing invariance and equivariance for Data Augmentation in Computer Vision

==PAGE:20==
ance, if
f(G(i)) = G(f(i))
(2.11)
then f is equivariant under G [27].
Thus, in explainability, for ImageNet [28] classification, an equivariance is intuitively ex­
pected to happen when explaining an image and performing a geometrical transformation
(rotation). Intuition will lead us to the conclusion that a rotated image’s explanation should
be the same as the original image explanation’s rotation.

Augmentation Methods
Throughout the experiment multiple augmentation methods are going to be used; they can
be separated into two categories, the geometrical transformations, and the color transfor­

The package imgaug [29] will be used to perform this augmentation methods.
2.3.1
Geometrical Transformations
For affine transformations, imgaug provides the function Affine which applies transfor­
mations to batches of images. In particular, the option to be used for the rotations will be
rotate.
Rotate function is defined to apply an affine rotation on the y­axis to input data, where it
can be either an image or a batch of images.
By specifying either a range of angles (i.e (­45,45)) it will randomly create rotations be­
tween the two angles. In the experiment, a certain degree will be chosen by selecting a
specific number of degrees for reproducibility purposes.
It is important to note that when applying affine rotations, new pixels are often generated
(i.e when translating to the right, new pixels are generated on the left). There are multiple
modes available on how to generate them.
Methods to use when filling newly created pixels
It follows the same meaning as in other Python implementations such as skimage [30]
and numpy.pad [31, 29].
• Constant: Pads with a constant value. (This value can be chosen in the code (cval)
or left unentered for cval = 0).
• Edge: Pads with the edge values of the array (i.e: Replicates edge values through
the new pixels)
• Symmetric: Pads with the reflection of the vector along the edge of the array.
• Reflect: Pads with the reflection of the vector mirrored on the first and last values
of the vector along each axis.
• Wrap: Pads with the wrap of the vector along the axis. (i.e. the first values pad the
end and the last ones pad the beginning).
Each of these methods have their unique differences and therefore all will be compared
throughout the experiments.

3
Methodology
3.1
Introduction
The hypothesis which stands as the basis of the experiments is that explanations of geo­
metrical rotations will be equivariant and explanations of color rotations will be invariant.
In order to check these hypotheses an experiment is needed, in this case, a pretrained
CNN will be used for the classification algorithm. This CNN is already pretrained in Ima­
geNet [28] so for the sake of simplicity and speed since explanations take a significantly
longer time a subset of ImageNet called Imagenette [32] will be used.
In order to compare different explanation methods iNNvestigate [22] provides us with
a framework to create a comparison between different explanation methods such as the
ones explained in Chapter 2.
The experiments consist of creating batches of images which will then be run through the
CNN in order for them to be predicted by the CNN and then explained with the use of
iNNvestigate.
These images will be firstly predicted and explained as they are and then they will be
augmented by using either color rotations or geometrical rotations and then predicted
and explained again in order for them to be compared with the original ones.
For the color rotations, the raw explanations of the rotated images can be directly com­
pared to check for invariance.
In the case of geometrical rotations, since they are equivariant the comparison will be
between the original images’ explanations and the rotated images’ explanations rotated
back. Since these rotations are creating these new areas of pixels (that are created fol­
lowing the different modes in 2.3.1) in order to minimize their difference with the other
experiment the correlation will be calculated only within the area not affected by these
new areas of pixels.
3.2
Datasets
Throughout the experiments in order to perform the experiment on color images Imagenette
will be used.
It is a subset of 10 classes easily classifiable from the bigger dataset
Imagenet. Originally created by Jeremy Howard of FastAI, it is usually used to try new
experiments/ideas/algorithms when running them with the entirety of imagenet would be
too time­consuming.
The preprocessing to the dataset depends on the model being used but since iNNvesti­
gate uses keras.applications implementations of the networks, their preprocessing is
also imported from keras.

3.3.3
ResNet50
ResNet, short for Residual Networks is a classic neural network used as a backbone for
many computer vision tasks. This model was the winner of ImageNet challenge in 2015.
The importance of these networks comes from their success at training extremely deep
neural networks (150+ layers). Prior to ResNet, some of the deepest neural networks
were VGG19 (19 layers) or GoogleNet (22 layers) training very deep neural networks
were difficult due to the problem of vanishing gradients. Resnet achieved first place at
the ILSVRC 2015 classification competition with an error of only 3.57% [37]. Additionally,
it also came first in the ImageNet detection, ImageNet localization, COCO detection, and
COCO segmentation in the ILSVRC [38] & COCO [39] competitions of 2015.
In general, when working with Deep CNNs to solve a computer vision problem, it may be
a possibility at first to think that adding layers could improve accuracy. These additional
Analysing invariance and equivariance for Data Augmentation in Computer Vision
17
==PAGE:26==
Figure 3.4: VGG19 architecture [36], adaptation from [33]
layers can help solve complex problems more efficiently as the different layers could be
trained for varying tasks to get highly accurate results.
Even though by increasing the number of stacked layers the model gets enriched, it can
even not improve its accuracy nonetheless. As the number of layers increases, the accu­
racy may saturate or even degrade after a point resulting in a worse performance of the
model overall. This behaviour does not come from overfitting. It may come from the ini­
tialization, optimization function, or more likely, from the vanishing or exploding gradients.
Residual Networks (ResNets) come to solve this issue by using residual blocks to improve
the accuracy of the models. These residual blocks allow for connection skipping which
diminishes the issue of the vanishing gradient by setting up an alternate shortcut for the
gradient to pass through. Additionally, they enable the model to learn an identity function
ensuring that the higher layers of the model will perform better than the lower layers.
All in all, residual blocks make it considerably easier for the layers to learn identity func­
tions. As a result, ResNet improves the efficiency of deep neural networks with more
neural layers while minimizing the percentage of errors. Skip connections add the out­
puts from previous layers to the outputs of stacked layers, making it possible to train much
deeper networks than what was previously possible. This network will be used through its
implementation in keras.applications.resnet50 which comes pre­trained in ImageNet.
This pre­training as indicated in [34] is a port from the one [37] provides.

5
Conclusions
Throughout the dissertation, a theoretical framework has been presented to discuss the
achievance of invariance and equivariance with respect to a data augmentation transfor­
mation. This metric, ρ (Correlation Criterion), shows the relationship between predictions
and explanations’ correlations through different amounts of augmentations. A definition
of equivariance or invariance (depending on the type of transformation) has been set with
respect to the value of this parameter. When reaching a value > 0.8, it is defined to
achieve these mathematical properties.
Alongside this theoretical framework, an experimental framework has been set to com­
pare explanation methods, data augmentation techniques, and different CNN models.
Based on iNNvestigate [22] implementation of CNNs and explanation methods, the frame­
work allows the user to choose explanation methods, data augmentation techniques, CNN
models, and different classes from imagenette [32] to analyze.
A set of experiments across explanation methods, models and data augmentation tech­
niques has been performed. From them, through the theoretical definition of achievance,
equivariance and invariance are not always achieved for all explanation methods.
It can be seen that these properties are achieved in different capacities across models
and explanation methods. There is a similar behavior across augmentation methods re­
gardless of their type (geometrical or color­wise).
Not all explanations can be used for all CNN models as LRPZ data for ResNet50 and
Densenet201 (Figure 4.3). On the other hand, explanation methods seem to perform
similarly in equivariance and invariance achievance regardless (mostly) of model and data
augmentation.
A hierarchy can be found across data augmentations and CNN models, where Deep Tay­
lor, Deconvnet, and Guided Backpropagation perform (as a mean) better than the other
explanation methods being tested for invariance and equivariance. It is to be expected
that Gradient and Input ∗Gradients perform worse since they are heavily affected by the
Shattered gradient effect (Figure 2.3).
Although extended work has been published for ‘fair­washing’ [45, 46, 47], and they show
that explanations are easily manipulable, this work sheds new light on how robust some
of the explanations methods can be for certain data augmentation techniques.
It has been shown that for certain combinations of models, explanations, and data aug­
mentation techniques, explanations can be as robust (for small enough perturbations) as
predictions.
From [45, 46, 47] it could be expected that explanations are less robust than predictions,
but this seems to be the case when facing adversarial examples. When facing simple
data augmentation techniques, on the other hand, some explanation methods hold the
same robustness as their network predictions.
A great deal of effort has been made to produce a toolbox based on iNNvestigate [22] and
with the use of imgaug [29] that could be used to test many more augmentation techniques,
models and even different classification tasks that due to time constraints, could not be
tested.
Analysing invariance and equivariance for Data Augmentation in Computer Vision
33
==PAGE:42==
Even though experiments show invariance and equivariance are achieved for some ex­
planation methods, future work can be proceeded in trying to improve them through the
tuning of CNNs.
Further experiments are required to check whether or not, performing data augmentation
during training improves equivariance (or invariance) in the particular transformation being
used for training.
Another approach could be tuning cost functions by incorporating additional terms to take
into account explainability. This approach could lead to improvements in Correlation Cri­
terion ρ since the explanations would be improving.
Overall, explainable AI provides a lot of benefits already but in order to provide meaningful
information to scientists, researchers, doctors, and other high­level users, there is still
work to be done. AI’s explanations should keep improving to reach this level and forcing
them to have human explanation properties is a way to move forward on this path.

A
Computational Optimization
When changing from the initial local Jupyter Notebook set up, closer to iNNvestigate ex­
amples [22], performance was an issue since it would take several minutes to analyze
(explain, predict and perform transformations) around 10 images. It was also easily tak­
ing a lot more RAM space than what was needed.
To find the local bottlenecks of the algorithm when transferring the software to HPC, pro­
filing rose as a way of improving its performance and memory consumption.
As can be seen in Listing A (line_profiler of unoptimized code), line_profiler pro­
vides a line by line description of how much time is spent in each line. In this case, lines
193 and 295 as well as 339 take most of the time. These three lines contain the analysis
of both original and rotated images as well as the prediction of the original ones. From
these lines, it was possible to see also that setting up the model (line 80) and collecting
the images (line 135) were also taking part of the time.
Therefore, the code was changed to minimize the number of predictions, initializations,
and explanations by batching multiple images together, running the script for multiple
classes at the same time (in this original code it had to be initialized for each class), and
batch images together independently of their classes. These batches will be used for aug­
mentation, analyzing, both prior and post augmentation, and prediction. These improve­
ments added to using GPU (that also drastically accelerated each of these processes)
greatly improved performance.
memory_profiler was also used to check for memory leaks. As can be seen in Figure A.1,
there were major spikes related to the loading of the CNN model as well as the creation
and saving of all images being analyzed. Drops can also be seen every time that the loop
would finish and the data was liberated from the RAM. This figure lead to the conclusion
that unnecessary data was being stored and led to changing the way of storing the data
(currently through bigger batches of Pandas DataFrames) containing now data across
classes and reducing the number of model initializations to one across all explanation
methods, classes and types of transformations.
Since Listing A can be tough to read, it can be more visual to use gprof2dot to check ex­
actly which functions take up most of the time. Figure A.2 shows a zoom of the area where
most of the computational time was being consumed. It shows that , as seen in Listing A,
analyzing (formulating explanations), predicting and setting up the CNN model in use (in
this case VGG16) take most of the computational time (around 75% of all computational
time).
The need for this computational optimization comes from the size of the experiment which,
although seemingly small, by having to test all combinations shown in Section 4.1 it ends
up adding up to more than 800.000 images being analyzed. The code shown in Listing A
used to take 60s to analyze 15 images with 15 angles (around 225 explanations and 15
predictions). Therefore an improvement of around 30% which was achieved by these set
of changes as well as migrating to GPU (which adds up to 15% improvement in perfor­
mance) will be noticeable. The total run time of the experiment was of around 30 hours
in 12 parallel process running at the same time.
