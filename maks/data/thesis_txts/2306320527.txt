We have studied the harmful eﬀect of air pollution for many years.
We know how
dangerous it is, and what it does to our body. But measuring air pollution is centralised
to the government, universities or organizations. Citizens are unable to measure air
pollution, and it leaves them unaware of their own exposure. This thesis aims to develop
a system that allows the user to gain insight into his exposure. This is done by collecting
geolocated data and providing feedback with interpretations of data collected.
The
system utilizes social shared data to map air pollution and live measures to provide on-
point feedback. This enables a user to connect air quality to the real time environment.
The system should cause a behavioural change for the user, so that he will take action
to reduce or limit his exposure as much as possible. The thesis will also explore the
potential market and identify key customer segments. The goal is to have an initial
prototype and proposed app design and a business strategy for taking this idea out into
the market. Potential beneﬁcial methods of visualization will be proposed and used for
the app design.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Air pollution is harmful for our health and recognized by WHO as a global problem
(Fact sheet 1.). The pollutants are invisible, except for smoke and smog, and therefore
largely undetectable without technology.
In this project, we aim to create a service
for transforming direct measurements of CO, NO2, O3, particle matter into simple
visualizations and real time feedback. The information should cause behavioural change
in the user to reduce the user’s personal exposure. At the same time, the information will
be uploaded to an online database. Many user’s personal data can help the individual
to plan a pollution free bicycle route and can be used by city authorities for making
policies in transport and urban planning.

the world from 2005 and 2014 (Figures 1.2 and 1.3). It is revealing how air pollution is

level of air pollution in Asia is a big concern for citizens in their daily life [1]

The severity of an individual’s exposure is determined by the length of exposure and
the intake of particles. A user can therefore reduce exposure, by reducing the length of
his travel, his particle intake, changing the travel plan, or avoiding polluted areas for
a given period of the day. In this project, we propose a system that can measure the
air pollution level in real time. As a user collects the concentration of particles, he can
receive feedback to take preventive measures against exposure. For this he needs a device
that can sample and yield information to warn him of dangerous exposure. I utilize an

Arduino device [2] to capture the pollutant value, transfer the data to a smartphone
for visualization and geographic localisation. All data are automatically uploaded and
stored anonymously in a central server. I created visualization on top of Google Maps
by using Python scripts.
In this project, I have designed a User Interface (UI) design for a smartphone application.
The design contains visualization tools described in this report.
I have developed a
business plan where I researched the market that could beneﬁt from detailed air pollution
measures. I use London and Beijing as a potential market and Copenhagen to collect
data and ultilization of the device, app and scripts. City governments would be able to
create regulations to reduce air pollution, redirect traﬃc and identify pollution culprits.
The data also has inherent value for exploring health implications of pollution exposure
and will be of value for medical research. This data combined with other big data can
be analysed and identify how patient groups contracted their health condition.

2.2.1
Europe: London and Copenhagen
Europe has 8 of 10 countries with the most bikes per capita. Here we ﬁnd countries
like Netherlands, Denmark, Germany and Finland. Today, many cities like Copenhagen
support biking. Nearly half of Copenhageners bike to work. 40% of households in Den-
mark don’t own a car. This result, stems from years of policy changes and city planning
thinking bicycles ﬁrst and cars second. In 1960, most of European cities changed to more

Prototype Arduino Gas Sensor
The ﬁrst gas sensor prototype was created in KAIST, South Korea by a team from
the Mechanical Engineering Department. The team included a German and Chinese
student. Their goal was to provide AQI measurements as precise as possible with a low
cost, small formfactor device. I could not retrieve a prototype from them, as it is still
work in progress. Therefore, I created a prototype using generic sensors (Figure 5.1).
The MQ-7 and MQ-9 sensor provide a ﬁnalized air quality index, AQI, value. The team
in Korea aim to measure particle matter, humidity and even sound to provide precise
AQI values. For this project, I use the output values of the two sensors, to simulate the
presence of air pollution. The precision of sensors may vary. They require some time
to attain working temperature, and the sensors could deteriate over time and require
ongoing calibration. This type of small sensors could measure signiﬁcantly diﬀerent in
just 6 months. I therefore focus on the relative diﬀerence of values for visualization.

Discussion
The idea for this project sprung from a need in Korea for the population to be more
aware of pollution. Air quality in Daejeon had tended to become bad very sudden. The
professors at KAIST wished students to develop a service that contained the hardware
sensors and the software app system. The idea was to enable citizens to make their life
healthier by gaining insight to the pollution surrounding them.
For this project, I created a stand-in prototype using two Arduino sensors, MQ-7 and
MQ-9, to mimic the device in Korea. I found the MQ-7 sensor to measure similar AQI
values comparing two diﬀerent times of day. The MQ-9 sensor was however much better
at descriminating diﬀerences in air quality.
To explore the market, we selected three major target cities; London, Copenhagen and
Beijing. We found that pollution is a relevant topic within all cities, and a critical issue
especially for London and Beijing. Copenhagen is a good example of a city with low
pollution, while air pollution is a major issue for inhabitants and the local government in
London and Beijing. For merchandising the Clean Bike concept, the number of citizens
in the city can be a key factor for success. We found the number of daily bike commuters
to be near 5% of the population in Guangzhou, Shanzhen and London, while a similar
proportion can be expected in Beijing. This would be a potential 2.6 million daily bike
commuters in the four cities.
This revealed that these four cities are good candidates for the device, as biking is a
popular means of transportation. Here there is a potential to target bikers as the main
user group and use them for collecting air quality measures. The market is not only
limited to bikers, and could be used by pedestrians and on city transportation such as
buses. If used for walking, running or in a vehicle, we could derive a method to determine
the method of transportation.

month, from 30.000 initial users to 310.000 users over 3 years, will be enough to provide
a ROI of 10 times, if a revenue of 1.7 DKK per user per year can be achieved. This
conﬁrms there is a potential for a venture case. Another option to explore is the potential
for governmental supported sponsorships to pay for the development cost of 2 million
DKK. Something that could be attractive considering the medical implications of air
pollution.
The Arduino sensor was able to transfer air quality measurements to the application,
where data was processed and stored online in parse. The application was able to react to
the pollutant measure, and change smiley and display the current value. The application
also interacted with a Google map with an overlay of the processed pollutant data.
Using Python, I processed the data to create data visualizations that provides potential
beneﬁt for both a user and government. The geolocated plots can illustrate which geo-
graphic areas are currently polluted, by connecting real time air quality with locations.
A script for calculating the occurrences of each pollutant value gives an insight into the
severity of the route currently measured, by colouring each severity according to the 6
colours of the AQI scale. Based on the colour scale a user is able to judge if exposure
was good or bad. A government could be interested in a high level view. To visual-
ize this purpose I plotted the pollution for each rode in Copenhagen. It can provide
insight to consistently bad areas, possible caused by a non-traﬃc related reasons. The
last experiment aimed to measure a speciﬁc route at two diﬀerent times of the day. I
choose to measure after midnight and after work at roughly 16:30. The MQ-7 sensor
did not provide distinct information. As this sensor is only measuring CO, it is likely
that CO is not a signiﬁcant pollutant in Copenhagen – in accordance the extensive use
of catalytic converters in cars. The MQ-9 sensor on the other hand, yielded a signiﬁcant
diﬀerence at the day and night measurements. The geolocation data needs to be robust.
A problem was observed during the last two weeks of this project. The ﬁrst data sample
did not record geolocation changes, while the second sample stopped halfway.
While the prototype application provides a functional application to measure and store
pollution data, it is not a functional app for real users. I designed graphic user interface
for the app, with the visualizations created during this project.
I performed a user
study on the app design to evaluate the areas in need of improvement. Overall users
liked the app, but thought some of the graphs for displaying routes were in need of
better explanation.

Chapter 14
Conclusion
I managed to create a system that could measure the current level of air pollution, and
combine the pollutant data with time, longitude and latitude coordinates. The system
sensor provided uncalibrated measures, but allowed us to depict realistic visualizations of
air quality in a geographic area. This geographic visualization was used in the application
and put onto a Google Map as overlay. We also visualized the pollution level for a route
measured by the user. The visualization tells the user how good and badly polluted
the trip has been, by calculating the occourences of a given value. Clicking his route
graph, a user is able to see the travelled route, how long it took and the mean AQI.
Furthermore the uploaded user-generated data generates a conglomerate of information
available to other users as they plan their next trip and as they share information with
their health providers. Furthermore, the detailed information can be of great value for
city government planning.
To fully function the system still needs further development, but it provides an insight
in to how we can convey a large dataset of air quality measures, without overwhelming
the user. The application focuses on the users own personal measures, but also gives the
user the value on contributing to a central database, to the common good of the cities
inhabitants and the city government.

Future Work
It is vital for Clean Biking to have a low cost and small sensor. We rely on the Depart-
ment of Mechanical Engineering in KAIST, Daejeon, Korea to ﬁnalize the gas sensor for
retail.
An important aspect of Clean Biking will be to electronically calibrate sensors according
to local stationary air pollution measurements. This will ensure that all data uploaded
are homogeneous.
Market expansion is needed after the London and Chinese market. We have a large
market to explore in Europe and USA.
The application could also be improved by additional functions. A travel planner would
allow users to better plan routes. More work is also need to ﬁgure out how to convey
the severity of pollution of a route.
