Abstract
In the new international standard “ISO 22955: Acoustics – Acoustic quality of open of-
fices”, six different space types are described in terms of acoustic characteristics and
target values. Space type 6 specifies the most common open office type which include
mixed activities. The standard prescribes target values for the descriptor in situ acous-
tic attenuation of speech (DAS) between different types of activities in the same space.
A detailed strategy for evaluating acoustic quality in open office spaces that encompass
human aspects is promising to call for further investigation. Present study takes an ex-
plorative approach where the acoustics of five open office spaces are examined to verify
and substantiate proposed DAS values in table 6. In total, 16 measurement paths on
DAS for various study purposes are measured including workstation noise levels in of-
fices. Results show the reach of target on DAS in open office space is complicated, but
measurement paths to enclosed rooms are straightforward. There is some indication DAS
values in table 6 are excessive based on the occupied noise level measured. DAS target
can guide acoustic space planning to be important for proper acoustic quality in open of-
fice. It is concluded that certain practical guidelines can be helpful when evaluating open
offices through table of ISO 22955.

aspekter, vil kræve yderligere undersøgelse. Mit studie tager en eksplorativ tilgang, hvor
akustikken i fem åbne kontorlokaler undersøges for at verificere og underbygge fores-

ved evaluering af åbne kontorer gennem tabel 6 i ISO 22955.

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Our auditory system seems unchanged to maintain the same function and form today as
compared to ages ago. What has changed dramatically is the context in which we live our
daily life, i.e. the environment we operate within for the most of our awaken hours. On
an average basis, we spend most time indoors (estimated to 90 %) at home, at work, in
societies’ shared places, etc.. [1]. The working environment has evolved from cell-office
spaces to spaces that enable flexibility and openness for agile working principles[2]. To-
day, offices including mixed activities are widely applied [3]. Open office spaces are used
for a variety of reasons, not least for economic motives [2]. As a result of the rise of
open offices, an extensive amount of documentation regarding noise problems has been
published [4][5][6]. Acoustics in our indoor environments is arguably neglected and often
down prioritized, or not even thought of in many examples. This is despite the fact that
most of the time is spent indoors. Acoustic is reported to be the second most important
problem in offices which many people may recognise [7]. Spread of irrelevant speech (i.e.
lack of speech privacy) is the utmost problematic noise source in the open office space
which is associated with risks among users such as annoyance reactions, reduced cogni-
tive performance, and psycho-physiological reactions [8]. Intelligible speech sounds ac-
cess our cognitive system for negative effects on acoustic quality. In 2012, room acoustic
descriptors for open-plan offices were standardised in ISO 3382-3 which relies on STI for
assessment of speech intelligibility[9]. Parameters for evaluating the spread of irrelevant
speech were included. it is the degree of speech intelligibility from adjacent workspaces
in the open office landscape that determines noise disturbance, rather than loudness of
speech [10]. High STI of irrelevant speech cause higher noise disturbance compare to
if STI is low [6]. Therefore, STI measurements are used in 3382-3 to evaluate STI with
increasing distances from workstations. Standard 3382-3 is being used more and more in
global markets but is still not widely applied [3]. An updated version of the standard was
published in 2022 [3]. A study from Finland examined many offices showed irrelevant
speech to be a major contributor to noise distraction [11].It was shown that descriptors
found in 3382 are weakly correlated with users’ perceived acoustic satisfaction, except
for measures of ”distraction distance” that proved to better link objective and subjective
assessments. The study also showed that combining use of sound absorbers, screens
and sound masking systems supports acoustic quality in the open office landscape. It
seems that speech privacy in the open office landscape is important for users’ acoustic
satisfaction, which therefore provides good reasons to evaluate offices using speech in-
telligibility measures to ensure acoustic quality.
In paper [12] from 2019: “ISO 3382-3: Necessary, but not sufficient - A new approach
to acoustic design for activity-based offices” Harvie-Clark explores from this findings. It
is argued that measurements according to 3382-3 are measured in unoccupied offices
to not include important contributing factors for acoustic satisfaction. Office culture, oc-
cupation ratio, and user’s environmental control and other factors of non-acoustic nature
contibute to acosutic satisfaction as well as the room response to sound ([13]) ([12]) [8].
It is further argued that targets for acoustic quality should be based on the needs of the
users in the offices in question. Based on this reasoning, a new approach for acoustic
evaluation of offices that include mixed activities was presented [12]. From start eval-

1.2
Prior work
Acoustic evaluation of open office spaces which encompasses analysis of human activity
was introduced in standard NF S 31-199 ”Acoustics — Acoustic performance of open-
plan offices” (2016) [15]. It provides a framework for evaluating the spread of irrelevant
speech between four different types of workspaces, where focus is on reducing the intelli-
gibility of speech from adjacent workspace and ensuring good speech conditions at short
distances. Standard NF S 31-199 partly proposes acoustic measurements in occupied
conditions to include human aspects. In Harvie-Clark’s paper [12] discussion is given on
importance to include human aspects for proper acoustic quality of open office spaces.
Room acoustic response is only one of several factors that contribute to acoustic sat-
isfaction which called for new ways to approach largely reported dissatisfaction in open
office spaces. A theoretical background on how human aspects can be included was
is presented. Room acoustic descriptor for in suit attenuation of speech DA,S is calcu-
lated from acoustically characterise workspaces within the office on sound emission and
noise sensitivity. DA,S reflects specifically how workspaces in the open office space relate
to each other meaning how much attenuation of speech is appropriate for good acous-
tic quality. Similar workspace is not up for an equal amount of attenuation as dissimilar
workspace. DA,S calculated provides guidance various stakeholders in office design can
logically allocate and distribute acoustic resources. ISO 29955 ”Acoustic quality of open
office spaces” available May 2021 was inspired by the earlier published french standard to
focus on inclusion of human aspects into acoustic evaluation. ISO 29955 gives guidelines
and discusses the most important aspects of room acoustic design for acoustic quality.
Table 6 fully builds on the theoretical framework presented in paper [12] to suggest a ma-
trix of potential DA,S values between workspace-pairs. No practical measurement data
is found for these potential DA,S values. One recent study was conducted to investigate
the ISO 29955 relevance for different stockholders which was based on interviewers [13].
Present study practically evaluates the new approach and its acoustic parameter DAS to
contribute to the field of office acoustics.

Theory
Theory related to office and acoustic includes in this section. Functionality of human
hearing and speech is in brief described. Working environment are described and its
associated issues. Relevant room acoustic topics are described. Finally, ISO 29955
is described with detailed description of DAS. Reference value for logarithmic values
presented in dB is 20 µPa , otherwise mentioned.
2.1
Human hearing and speech
Our abilities of hearing and speaking is crucial to us and our development as a species.
The auditory system and, for us the most important acoustic signal, speech is suggested
to be well matched [16]. Human hearing and speech are in brief described in this section.
2.1.1
Auditory system
Our auditory system divides into the peripheral system and the central system. The pe-
ripheral system is furthermore divided into three sections called the outer- middle and
inner ear. From pressure variations in air, sound enter the outer ear (pinna), which in the
middle ear (eardrum) is strengthen and further processed in the inner ear (cochlea) . At
the cochlea transduction from vibrations to electrical activity take place which is further
sent to the central auditory system for interpretation [16]. Complex sounds like speech
can be interpreted from our high spectral- and temporal sensitive auditory system [16].
Human can detect auditory information in the frequency range from about 20 Hz to 20
000 Hz, but this greatly varies among individuals [16]. Our frequency sensitivity is not
equal, we better detect sound between about 1000 Hz to 5000 Hz than in the very low
or very high frequency range [17]. In terms of sound level, we can perceive sound from
extremely low levels at our hearing threshold (20 µPa) up to harmful levels at our pain
threshold (20 Pa). In the frequency range important for speech, sound is amplified by the
eardrum [18]. On time aspects, our hearing system can detect sudden level variations
and separate subsequent acoustical events up to certain limits[17] [19] [16]. Our audi-
tory system holds localization abilities possible from comparing the characteristics of the
sound arriving at our ears. In free sound fields localization of sound sources are great,
while inside rooms it is more complicated due to the many reflection that mixes with the
direct sound [17]. The auditory system makes a complex and sensitive system .
2.1.2
Speech
Human speech enables us to communicate and express thoughts and feelings to others
very explicit[16]. Speech is produced from our lungs as airflows that passes through our
vibrating vocal cords. Stream of air is repetitively chopped up to create pressure patterns
defined by the vibration frequency of our vocal chord. The fundamental frequency of
sound is set by the vibration frequency of our vocal chord [17]. Men, females, and children
have different fundamental frequency ranging from 50 Hz to 350 Hz [17]. We alter the
fundamental frequency by the muscle in the larynx to change the character of speech.
Variation as such may translate from happy to dull messages. From our vibrating vocal
cords, vowel sounds are produced characterised by various frequency spectra[20].
These frequency spectra are modified from articulation by means of our throat, mouth,
and nose to produce consonant sounds [16]. Consonants are crucial for understanding
of speech occurring mostly in the upper frequency range [17]. In comparison with vowel
sounds, consonant sounds are spoken more quietly (about 10 dB lower), which easily

Speech sounds are interpreted by our auditory system, which causes communication fail-
ures at times. Ideally, auditory information is interpreted in a meaningful way, but for
example, under poor acoustic conditions, intelligibility of speech reduces which leads to
misunderstandings. Speech spectrum produced by the speaker is not identical to the one
the listener receives [20]. In reverberant rooms or under exposure of high noise level
the communication conditions worsen. Room acoustic is one factor which influences the
talker-transmission-listener path. Speech intelligibly are also in the hands of the speaker’s

intelligibility. In noisy environments, humans show fantastic ability of pick out relevant
sounds from irrelevant sounds . However, at high noise levels masking of speech occurs
. Masking describes the situation of having the relevant sound unable to be perceived
due to another sound (masker) [22]. The speaker changes speech level depending on
the background noise level following the mantra higher speech level in high noise level
(Lombard effect) [14]. In noisy conditions of substantial masking effect, the speaker typ-
ically adjust the distant to the listener to compensate for the noise level in the room [23].
Redundancy of speech refers to the robustness of the speech content, hence in noisy
situations we still can understand the talker’s message . Although, speech intelligibility
needs to be at certain levels to understand even in noisy acoustic scenes . Human speech
and auditory system seem well matched for us to understand speech in difficult acoustic

2.2
Working environment
The office constitutes the space for companies to perform professional or administrative
tasks [ISO22955]. Since great many people spend time in the working environment, the
quality of offices is of importance on both an individual and organizational level [2] [24].
The dominant part of work in the Western society is office work [2]. For a lot of people the
working environment plays a significant role in life and in some cases makes the utmost
important environment in daily life ([2]). The quality of indoor environment hangs on sev-
eral factors as lighting, air quality, temperature, acoustics including visual aspects [24].
These aspects of the working environment vary in importance, acoustics being in many
studies highly ranked for user satisfaction ([7]). Aside from such technical demands, many
human and social factors impacts satisfaction and perception of the working environment
(kth). A holistic viewpoint serves an excellent working environment which is why many
aspects need to be included In the multi-disciplinary field of office design [13] [2].
2.2.1
Background
In a historical context, office spaces have changed over time adopting more efficient office
layouts to suit trends and developments in society [2]. Over the last decades, a shift from
manufacturing-based to a more service and knowledge-based economy has taken place
[24]. An increase in awareness of aspects of health and well-being of employees has
risen in line with this development [24]. From the era of the first World War, supervised
routine work took place in large open spaces which later adopted to new office environ-
ments for several reasons[2]. Cell-offices were gradually introduced in the 1950s . Later
in the mid-1960s, the open office landscape was re-introduced for the company Behringer
in Germany(1960) with the aim to increase communication for a more efficient organiza-
tion. [2]. Indoor climate aspects also got more attention and a more democratized model
took place in these office[2]..Later around 1970s the concept of combi-office was formed
to take the best from the open-plan office and cell-office into consideration[2]. Around this
era, the first activity-based layout office was introduced, but it took years for the concept to
emerge [25]. Today, open plan office is commonly applied [24]. Further on activity-based
layout increases in use to enhance productivity, flexibility, user satisfaction, as well as
drivers of cost motives and brand establishment [26]. From the SARS-CoV-2 pandemic,
which led to increased remote work, demands on better office solutions and new ways of
working became clear [13] [26].
Many categories of office spaces can be found based on their architectural and functional
features from cell-office to activity-based-layout, to range from closed to fully open spaces
[2] [27]. Definition of offices varies greatly in literature [27].Office spaces can roughly
categorize into as conventional offices as cell-offices with full partitions and open office
spaces including open-plan offices and activity-based layouts ([28]) [27]. These three
office types are described as follows.
2.2.2
cell-office
The cell-office consists of fully separated rooms accessible via corridors and a central
meeting place [29](Figure 2.2). Occupants perform tasks of focused and non-collaborative
character on routine [27]. Rooms can be individual or shared with few occupants. Office
equipment may be situated in the room or outside the room in common areas [27]. Cell-
office does not fall under the category of open office spaces [14].

Open-plan offices, or shared office space, are floorplate occupants share without full par-

With the increase use of open-plan offices, indoor environment problems have arisen as
noise problems and lack of speech privacy, improper lighting, reduced air quality, etc...
[24]. From the free flow of information, occupants are constantly exposed to unwanted
speech which impairs acoustic quality in these offices. [14]. From another perspective,
the picture of these offices are distorted by the stereotypical thinking comparing cell-offices
and open-plan offices. The question at the issue is more nuanced with great individual
variations in offices. Studies show examples of open-plan offices with high user satis-
faction. Media denigrate open-plan offices because of the intolerable prevailing acoustic
environments. Despite that, acoustic aspects are still overlooked for serious consideration
in the design of new offices [13] [30].

Spatial decay of sound level
Sound level from an active sound source decreases with distance. This is because same
sound energy distributes over larger surfaces as the distance from the source increases.
In free-field, direct sound propagates freely to not mix with sound reflections. In free
field conditions, sound pressure level from a point source changes by 6 dB for every
halving or doubling of distance referred to as the inverse distance law. In room acoustics,
direct sound is interfered with sound reflections to create reverberate sound fields. Hence,
sound decay processes are not as simple. Sound level close to a point source in the
room decay according to inverse distance law and further away decay rate decreases to
approach reverberant sound level in the room illustrated in figure 2.7.

2.3.5
sound absobers
Sound energy in the room can either absorb to dissipate in form of heat, reflect back into
the room, or transmit from the enclosed volume . The use of sound absorbers aims to
control of absorption and reflection of sound in the room [14], whereas sound transmis-
sion is treated by means of building acoustics. In room acoustics, there is no distinction
between sound being absorbed or transmitted since in both these cases sound energy dis-
sipates from the investigate room [17]. Various designs and functions of sound absorbers
exist where the most common are porous absorbers, membrane absorbers, resonator ab-
sorbers which have different applications in design of acoustics in the room. Absorption
mechanisms are the same for all these to change sound energy into heat [18]. The three
main motives for use of sound absorbers are to control reverberation time, noise level,
and echos in the room [17]
Absorption coefficient α ranks the absorptive effect from 0 to 1. The absorbing effect
are depends on the sound frequency and angle of incidence of the incoming sound[18].
Higher α, higher material efficiency, and vice versa. In the extreme example from room
acoustics, open windows or doors are ideal absorbers (α = 1), and on the other end, fully
reflective hard walls takes α close to zero [18]. Total sound absorption is obtained by
multiplying the surface area S and α,
A = S · α from [18]
(2.2)
where A is the total sound absorption in m2, or sabines, and S is the surface in m2. Indeed,
larger surface area and greater α increases sound absorption in the room.
Various mounting strategies and coverings influences α, mostly applied to extend the low-
frequency region where the sound absorber is effective [17]. General characteristics of
sound absorbers for porous, membrane, and resonator absorbers is shown in figure 2.11.

Sound masking system
Artificial masking sounds can cover unwanted sound by use of sound masking systems.
Sound masking systems constitute an electro-acoustic setup with an array of loudspeak-
ers emitting masking noise which adjusts noise level in the room. White or pink noise and
nature characterized sound signals employs as maskers with varied pros and cons [14]
([52]).
In the open office space, sound masking system aims to adjusts background noise level
for masking of irrelevant speech [52]. Sound masking system installed in the office can
adjust background noise level to an appropriate level to enhance acoustic quality. Nor-
mally noise reduction is wanted in many acoustical investigations but contradictory low
background noise levels can cause dissatisfaction because occasional sounds from hu-
man activity or office equipment are heard to a greater extent ([12]). In other words, low
background noise levels (below 40 dBA) can lead to poor acoustic quality (29955). In-

is found ([14]), while other sources recommend no higher levels than 46 dBA[6].
Attitude toward sound masking systems is widespread and the use of this technology
varies globally. Faulty installation not performed by relevant experts can cause negative
effects like fatigue and performance decrease among occupants . Noise from heating,
ventilation, and air conditioning is sometime thought of to provide wanted masking effect,
however, these systems are not designed with such purpose. Misuse of such systems
to compensate for acoustic objectives can lead to adverse effects in other indoor climate
aspects. Features of the office vary from case to case which is why use of sound masking
systems should be considered in context [14].

-Space type 6 combining activities within same space.

2.4.2
Theoretical background
The theoretical concept of DA,S is described in paper “ISO 3382-3: Necessary, but not
sufficient - A new approach to acoustic design for activity-based offices”. Human activity
is included in the five-step method to come up with appropriate attenuation of speech
between workspaces in terms of DA,S:
1. Acoustic characterise the source and reception workspace in term of liveliness.
2. Overview level of speech at source position.
3. Overview workstation noise level at reception position.
4. Decide on SNR between workspace pair.
5. Calculation of DA,S from source level, SNR, and workstation noise level.
Each step in the process is further described.
Liveliness rating LR (Step 1)
Firstly identification of liveliness of human activity at respective workspace takes place.
Liveliness quantifies the acoustic environment from quiet to turbulent as single numbers
from 1 to 10 [liveliness]. Liveliness rating (LR) can be obtained from A-weighted sound
level measured for 5 minutes. LR depend on sound level and fluctuation strength with
time. Human activities rich in interaction and communication likely cause livelier events
(high LR), while concentration demanding human activity are more quiet (low LR). Sec-
ond row in figure 2.13 presents LR for respective workspace.
Speech source level Ls (Step 2)
Human activity at the source workspace cause gradual degree of noise emission. Speech
source level Ls at the source workspace can be assumed. ISO 29955 applies Ls of 57
dBA, but as mentioned other levels can be estimated to better match with human activity
at the source point. Long-term frequency spectrum of human speech is found in standard
ANSI-S3.5 for vocal effort of normal, raised, loud, and shouting ([53]).
Workstation noise level LW S (Step 3)
Workstation noise level LW S at the reception workspace represent the steady-state noise
level during normal occupancy ratio in the office. Occupied measurements of LW S include
human aspects to some extent. [12]. For example, occupancy ration in the office may be
reflected in such measure. Size of the office and ongoing activity may also influence LW S.
Particularly, large open office show higher noise levels, vice versa [12]. A-weighted sound
level are measured at individual workspace in the office following informative Annex E of
ISO 29955. It can be discussed how well LW S are representative. Human activity alters
throughout the day in the workspace, and can also intensify during work periods or similar.
Measurement time also influences accuracy and reliability. Fully representative LW S of
the workspace is pointed out to be difficult to capture. Third row in table 2.13 presents
assumed LW S. Equal LW S are assumed in ISO 29955 for respective workspace.
Signal-to-noise ratio SNR (Step 4)
From evaluating the acoustic relationship between workspace in terms of noise emssion
and noise sensitivity related to human activity assumed SNR are obtained. The question
of issues are how sound emissive the source position is and how noise sensitive the recep-
tion workplace are [14]. Following equation is suggest from paper [12] for predicted SNR:

Methodology
The project consists of five offices in København, Denmark, where their individual acous-
tic quality is examined. Offices vary significantly in size, layout, acoustic treatment, and
other interior design choices. Prior to measurements, characterisation of workspaces is
done to clarify on what basis division of individual floor plates is performed. Office case
A to E is described from office design, human activity, and acoustical standpoints. Ob-
jective measurements of DA,S is conducted in all offices as well as unoccupied Lb and
occupied LWS measurements. STI is measured in office B. Subjective ratings in form of
a questionnaire is performed in office A to E.
3.1
Characterisation of workspaces
Various human activities take place in the office to very extensively from case to case.
Offices of more traditional open-plan style with fixed seating for every occupant exist as
well as offices of activity-based layout which more clearly have sections which support
occupants’ work task at hand. The process of sectioning the floorplate is ambiguous be-
cause it is not always clear what the inherent purpose of workspaces is, or that given
categorise of workspaces overlaps so the workspace in question could fall into many cat-
egories. Although, the line must be drawn somewhere to allow for a meaningful analysis
of the offices’ performance and to find acoustical needs for each workspace. This al-
lows for a human-centered and detailed evaluation of acoustic quality in the office. The
main acoustic perspectives from which workspaces are judged are sound emission and
sound sensitivity. Indeed, some areas produce more noise than others. In similar man-
ner, some workspaces intuitively keep more acceptance of noise (low noise sensitivity),
while other workspaces are very disturbed by noise from adjacent areas. To point out the
illustrative examples, an informal meeting area in the office is likely to have high sound
emission and low noise sensitivity due to the ongoing activity in such place. In opposite,
areas where occupants conduct work requiring high concentration likely produce much
less noise and are very noise sensitive to demand speech privacy and minimal distrac-
tion. It should be clear that categorisation is dependent on the office at hand and much
dependent on context. Workspaces included in this study given from ISO 29955 are: In-
formal meetings (open plan), collaborative, non-collaborative, focused individual. In this
section, these workspaces are described from the author’s perspective in visual terms as
well as acoustic terms. Sectioning of floorplates in offices A to E is conducted on the basis
of the following description.
3.1.1
informal meeting workspace
Informal meeting workspace is not thought of to be used singly to strictly work-related
tasks, but instead, a space for occupants to communicate in an informal manner. Work
related activities may occur with possibilities to communicate both with next seated co-
workers as well as with people situated at adjacent furniture arrangements. Sound emis-
sion raises from ongoing activity from many occupants talking simultaneously. Main noise
source is conversational speech but also occasional noise from use of tableware to office
items. Informal meeting workspace have very low sensitivity to noise. LWS of 48 dBA is
suggested [15] [12].

3.1.2
collaborative workspace
Collaborative workspace is relatively large to be centrally situated in the office. Irregular
arrangements of low- and standing tables, sound absorbing furniture, lounge seatings,
vegetations, individual booths, writing boards, or similar, are used. Characteristic fea-

normal work-related activities. Acoustic environment is lively compared to calm. Hence,
while human activity tasks place it is noisy. Multiple noise sources of passages of occu-
pants, phone calls, equipment sound, and work-related conversational speech contribute
to noise emission. Collaborative workspace holds some sensitivity to noise. LWS of 45
dBA is suggested , while another other source says LWS span from 45 dBA to 50 dBA [15].
3.1.3
non-collaborative workspace
In comparison to collaborative space, non-collaborative workspace is quieter to support
mainly individual work activity, even though interactions between occupants can occur.
Screens and low dividers are often employed in various ways to separate workstations.
Because of the intended use of the workspace being individual work, sound emission is
low but may rise sporadically from short-distance speech. Non-collaborative workspace
holds increased noise sensitivity because ongoing activity requires more concentration
from the occupants.LWS of 42 dBA is suggested .Standard NF S 31-199 mention LWS
from 40 dBA to 45 dBA [15].
3.1.4
focused individual workspace
Some work tasks require speech privacy which the focused individual workspace sup-
ports. This workspace holds very high noise sensitivity to support occupants to conduct
concentration-demanding tasks. Individual focused workspace is often separated from
livelier areas both visually and acoustically. Separation from adjacent noisier activities
makes utilization of various screens, or full partitions or individual booths appropriate
to achieve low noise levels and sufficient rejection of unwanted speech from adjacent
workspaces. Sound emission is small, or none existing, because of the silent character
of work tasks conducted. LWSof 40 dBA is suggested.

Conclusion
A widespread of room acoustic conditions and office layouts in five offices were explo-
ratively examined according to ISO 29955. Table 6 of ISO 29955 presents a new ap-
proach of evaluating acoustic quality in open office spaces with mixed activities where
acoustic needs are identified based on ongoing human activity at workspaces.
Very few offices fulfil the potential target on DAS in Table 6, but most offices come close to
such fulfilment. Results showed workstation noise levels are higher compared to sugges-
tions in ISO 29955 which indicate DAS values are too high. Offices cases mostly reached
estimated targets on DAS. Further studies whether the reach of the estimated target of
DAS corresponds to the users’ perceived acoustic satisfaction are called for.
Offices’ layout proved important to fulfil DAS values, especially noise-sensitive workspaces
required separation with long distances from the noise source, or users’ access to en-
closed rooms in the open office landscape to ensure speech privacy. Acoustic space
planning can be performed from guidance of DAS values to support office design for proper
acoustic quality. It is concluded certain practical guidelines should be maintained when
using ISO29955. Attention should be paid to the most critical measurement path in the
office in question.
