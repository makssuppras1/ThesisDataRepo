Abstract
This thesis explores the fabrication and characterization of 4D-printed multimaterial
microrobots using Two-Photon Polymerization (2PP) and computer vision for automated
alignment. 4D printing enables dynamic, stimulus-responsive behaviour by integrating smart
materials such as hydrogels into 3D printed structures.
In this work, microrobots were
fabricated using a rigid IP-L backbone and a phenylboronic acid (PBA)-based hydrogel that
responds to fructose concentrations by swelling or shrinking. A key challenge in multimaterial
2PP is achieving accurate alignment between printing steps. To address this, an existing
automated alignment workflow developed by POLYMIC group and based on OpenCV was
adapted and optimized for PBA-based hydrogel printing.
The alignment script detects
circular markers and compensates for positional shifts, ensuring accurate positioning of the
hydrogel on top of the pre-printed backbone. The detection algorithm, which was optimized
for the PBA-based hydrogel, was validated on two distinct microrobot designs and under
different alignment marker spacings, demonstrating its robustness across geometries and
layout variations. Post-fabrication, the actuation behaviour of the hydrogel was evaluated by
exposing PBA-based printed pillar arrays to varying fructose concentrations and measuring
their swelling response.
Area changes were quantified using both manual (ImageJ) and
automated (Hough Circles-based) methods. Statistical analysis confirmed strong agreement
between the two approaches, demonstrating the reliability of the automated method for
characterizing swelling-based actuation in 4D printed hydrogel structures.
In summary, this work addressed two key objectives. First, the existing automated align-
ment workflow, originally developed for multimaterial 4D printing, was successfully adapted
and optimized for use with PBA-based hydrogels. This demonstrated that the established
multimaterial printing process is compatible with PBA, enabling reliable integration onto
pre-fabricated IP-L microrobot backbones. This adaptation paves the way for more targeted
experiments aimed at identifying optimal printing parameters to improve structural stability
and responsiveness. In future work, microrobot designs could be fabricated using a broader
sweep of printing parameters to further optimize PBA hydrogel integration. Fine-tuning
these conditions could enhance the actuation behaviour of the hydrogel component. How-
ever, accurately characterizing swelling and shape change for non-circular features, such as
triangular hydrogel elements, remains challenging. At present, manual analysis with Im-
ageJ remains the most trusted method for such irregular shapes, as they are not yet easily
measured with automated scripts. Second, a new custom script was developed to automate
the detection and area measurement of circular hydrogel microstructures. This tool sig-
nificantly reduced manual effort and analysis time while maintaining accuracy. Although
currently tailored specifically for circular shapes, the script demonstrated consistent and
reproducible performance across multiple batches and experimental conditions. As such, it
presents a valuable tool for future studies involving large datasets of similarly shaped struc-
tures. Extending this approach to other geometries could further broaden its application
in the automated analysis of 4D-printed systems and support faster, scalable evaluation of
microrobot components and their actuation responses.

Introduction
1.1
Purpose of the Project
4D printing is an advanced version of 3D printing that enables structures to undergo
shape transformation over time in response to environmental stimuli. This functionality is
achieved by integrating smart materials, such as hydrogels, into printed structures, which
are able to respond in changes in their surroundings after the printing process. This project
focuses on the fabrication of 4D printed, multimaterial microstructures using 2PP. Two
materials were used: IP-L resin, which forms the backbone of the microrobots, and a PBA-
based hydrogel that changes size in the presence of fructose by swelling or shrinking.
The overall aim was to develop a reproducible and semi-automated pipeline for 4D
microfabrication and functional evaluation.
To support this, the project was structured
around three main objectives:
• Optimize an existing alignment script to work reliably with PBA-based hydrogel by
adjusting image processing parameters and tuning the parameters for alignment marker
detection through OpenCV.
• Evaluate the robustness of the alignment algorithm when either the design of the first
print or the marker spacing is altered, testing its adaptability to changes in geometric
layout.
• Develop an automated image analysis method to quantify hydrogel actuation under
varying fructose concentrations and compare it with manual measurements for valida-
tion.
This work contributes to refining existing tools and developing new ones for more effi-
cient analysis and fabrication of smart 4D microstructures.

Theoretical Background
2.1
From 3D to 4D printing
Additive manufacturing (AM), also known as 3D printing or rapid prototyping, is a tech-
nique that builds objects layer by layer based on digital models developed using computer-
aided design (CAD) software. According to the American Society for Testing and Materials
(ASTM), AM is classified into seven categories: material extrusion, vat polymerization, ma-
terial jetting, sheet lamination, powder fusion, direct energy deposition, and binder jetting.
Beyond these established types, newer techniques such as cold spray and additive friction
stir deposition have also been introduced [1]. Vat polymerization is a technique where light-
sensitive materials are selectively exposed to radiation or light, causing them to polymerize
layer by layer to form a 3D structure. Typically, this technique is used to process resins
and photopolymers [2]. Based on the type of curing light source, vat polymerization can be
categorized into several subtypes, including stereolithography (SLA), digital light processing
(DLP), two-photon polymerization (2PP), and volumetric 3D printing [2].
SLA is recognized as one of the earliest 3D printing technologies [3]. The origins of 3D
printing date back to 1981, when Dr. Hideo Kodama developed an early rapid prototyping
system that built objects layer by layer using UV-curable resin [4]. A few years later, in
1986, Chuck Hull filed the first patent for SLA. He is widely recognized as the “inventor of 3D
printing” for his contributions to the development and commercialization of SLA technology
and the .stl file format, which remains a standard in the field of 3D printing [4]. In 1988, he
established the company 3D Systems Corporation, which the same year launched the first
commercial SLA 3D printer, known as the SLA-1 [5]. This innovation marked a significant
milestone in the development and public awareness of 3D printing technologies [3].
Since then, AM technologies have continued to evolve. One of the most significant ad-
vancements is 4D printing, which builds upon the 3D printing by integrating smart materials
capable of dynamically altering their shape or properties post-fabrication. This additional
capability introduces the "fourth dimension", transformation over time, enabling structures
to autonomously respond and adapt to external stimuli [6]. This dynamic response is trig-
gered by external energy sources, such as changes in temperature, light exposure, pH, or
applied voltage and other environmental cues [7]. 4D printing enables the creation of dy-
namic structures whose shape, properties, or functionality can be tuned by appropriately
embedding smart materials throughout the 3D volume [8].
2.2
Two-Photon Polymerization Technology (2PP)
Two-photon polymerization (2PP) is an advanced high-resolution 3D microfabrication
technique based on direct laser writing (DLW) that enables the creation of intricate structures
with nanoscale precision.
It operates through a nonlinear optical process in which two
near-infrared (nIR) photons are absorbed simultaneously to initiate polymerization in a
photosensitive resin. This nonlinear absorption ensures that polymerization occurs only at
the focal point of the laser, allowing sub-diffraction-limit fabrication and achieving feature
sizes below 200 nm [9].

Theoretical Background
In 2PP, the laser 's focal point is crucial for initiating polymerization within a pho-
tosensitive resin. When the laser is precisely focused on the resin, polymerization occurs
only within the specific focal volume (voxel), forming a solid structure while leaving the
surrounding resin unaffected. This selective polymerization occurs because the laser inten-
sity reaches the required threshold exclusively at the exact focal point, while outside this
region, the intensity is insufficient to trigger the chemical reaction. This spatial confine-
ment allows for precise control over the dimensions of fabricated structures. Compared to
SLA, which utilizes Single-Photon Absorption (SPA), 2PP relies on Two-Photon Absorption
(TPA) 2.3, a nonlinear process where two photons are simultaneously absorbed to excite the
photoinitiator in higher energy state[10].
Since its initial introduction in the 1990s for fabricating spiral-shaped microstructures
[11], 2PP has evolved into a widely used technique in biomedical and microrobotic applica-
tions due to its superior resolution and ability to create intricate 3D structures with complex
geometries using a wide range of materials [12]. Additionally, combining 2PP with 4D print-
ing enables the creation of dynamic and responsive microstructures that can change shape
post-fabrication in response to external stimuli[13].
2.3
Physics of Two-Photon Absorption (TPA)
2PP operates under the principle of TPA, a process in which a molecule absorbs two
photons stepwise or simultaneously to reach an excited state. The most common method
of excitation is through the absorption of a single photon. However, advances in laser tech-
nology have made TPA an essential technique. In contrast to SPA, where excitation occurs
directly through the absorption of a single high-energy photon, TPA involves two lower-
energy photons that together provide the necessary excitation energy. This process requires
high-intensity femtosecond laser pulses to ensure sufficient photon absorption, effectively
bringing the material to the same excited state as in SPA. TPA can occur through two
distinct mechanisms. In stepwise TPA, a molecule absorbs one photon and temporarily en-
ters a real intermediate energy state before absorbing a second photon to reach the final
excited state. In contrast, simultaneous TPA occurs when two photons are absorbed at
the same time, utilizing a virtual intermediate state that lacks a defined energy level. The
latter mechanism is inherently non-linear, requiring a high photon density to be effective
[14]. This non-linear nature of TPA has significant implications for fabrication. Unlike SPA,
which follows a linear absorption pattern in which energy absorption is directly proportional
to light intensity (I), TPA is proportional to the square of the light intensity (I2). As a
result, in a focused laser beam with a Gaussian intensity profile, the absorption rate of TPA
decreases twice as fast compared to SPA. This effect results in enhanced spatial confinement
of energy precisely around the laser’s focal point, producing a highly localized voxel within
which polymerization exclusively occurs. This property is key to the high resolution achieved
in 2PP, allowing for the fabrication of structures with nanoscale precision [15].

2.4
Hydrogels
Hydrogels are 3D structures composed of hydrophilic polymers that form networks
through either chemical crosslinking (covalent or ionic bonds) or physical crosslinking (hy-
drogen bonding, van der Waals interactions, or physical entanglement). These materials can
absorb and retain substantial amounts of water while maintaining their shape, making them
highly attractive for various applications. Since their initial discovery by Wichterle and Lim
in the 1960s, hydrogels have gained significant interest across multiple fields [18].
Stimuli-responsive hydrogels, also known as smart hydrogels, are soft materials that
exhibit reversible changes (swelling, shrinking, bending) when exposed to external stimuli.
These stimuli can be physical ( temperature, light, electricity, magnetic fields) or chemical
(pH, humidity, salts).
Due to their high water content, flexibility, biocompatibility, and
shape memory, these hydrogels are well-suited for biomedical applications [19].
2.5
Why Microrobots?
In the field of microrobotics, microrobots are defined as sub-millimetre untethered mo-
bile objects engineered to propel themselves autonomously or be actuated by external fields
[12]. Their ability to reach small spaces and operate with cellular-scale precision has un-
locked a wide range of uses [20]. Microrobots have demonstrated broad utility across many
fields: in medicine and biotechnology, they enable targeted drug delivery, minimally inva-
sive microsurgery, in vivo cell transplantation, real-time diagnostics and tissue engineering;
in manufacturing and micromanipulation, they perform micro-assembly of complex parts,
on-chip inspection and precision handling of microscale components or biomaterials; in en-
vironmental monitoring and remediation, they carry out in situ detection and removal of
pollutants in water, soil and air; in agriculture, they facilitate precision delivery of nutrients
or pesticides, microscopic seed planting, harvesting and pest control; and in exploration,
they probe microscopic environments in deep-sea and extraterrestrial terrains [21].
2.6
4D Multimaterial Microrobots
Traditional microfabrication often involves a single photoresist and a sequential process
to produce static polymer structures. In contrast, multimaterial 2PP allows the integration
of different resins with distinct functions. In our approach, one resin forms a rigid structural
backbone, while the second is a sugar- and pH-responsive hydrogel that swells or shrinks in
response to environmental changes. While a stepwise approach, where each photoresist is
processed independently, is commonly used in multimaterial microrobot fabrication, other
strategies involving simultaneous material deposition have also been demonstrated [22].
In this project, sugar-responsive hydrogel is explored as smart material capable of re-
versible deformation. Specifically, PBA-based hydrogel is utilized due to its ability to un-
dergo volumetric changes when exposed to sugar-containing environments. When the im-
mobilized PBA groups within the hydrogel network interact with sugars such as fructose in
aqueous media, they form boronate esters. This interaction introduces negatively charged
groups into the network, resulting in electrostatic repulsion. This change reflects an increase

3.1
Nanoscribe Photonic Professional GT2
The Nanoscribe Photonic Professional GT2 is a high-precision 3D printer that utilizes
2PP to fabricate microscale structures. As mentioned before, 2PP relies on TPA, where an
infrared femtosecond laser (780 nm) is focused through a microscope objective, delivering
sufficient intensity at the focal point to trigger the simultaneous absorption of two infrared
photons. This results in an energy transition in the UV spectrum. This occurs because the
simultaneous absorption of two infrared (IR) photons provides an energy equivalent to that
of a single ultraviolet (UV) photon. Since the photoresist is designed to polymerize upon
UV exposure, this energy transition effectively initiates the photopolymerization process,
enabling localized structuring within the material. The printer constructs 3D structures
layer by layer by precisely scanning the laser focus using Galvo mirrors, which enable rapid
movement of the focal point in the x- and y-directions within a defined scanning range. The
precise depth positioning in the z-direction is achieved by moving the objective lens, allowing
accurate adjustment of the laser's focal point within the resin [24].

system, facilitating successful microfabrication of the designed structures.

3.3
Materials
3.3.1
IP-L Resin
IP-L is a negative-tone photoresist material used for high resolution microfabrication
via 2PP. In negative-tone resins like IP-L, regions exposed to the laser undergo polymeriza-
tion and harden into solid structures, while unexposed areas remain soluble and are removed
during development. IP-L exhibits a high Young’s modulus of approximately 4.73 GPa,
making it suitable for creating rigid microstructures. Additionally, IP-L is certified biocom-
patible and non-cytotoxic according to ISO 10993-5, enhancing its relevance for biomedical
applications.
3.3.2
PBA-based Hydrogel
The sugar-responsive hydrogel used in this study was based on a phenylboronic acid
(PBA)-functionalized photoresist formulation, specifically designed for 2PP. This hydrogel
system is pH- and sugar-responsive, capable of reversible swelling and actuation in aqueous
environments with different fructose concentrations [26]. Used as a second material in the
multimaterial 2PP workflow, printed onto pre-fabricated IP-L backbones. The formulation
was prepared by PhD student, Marcin Piotr Piekarczyk, for use in this project.

3.4
Printing Workflow
For readers unfamiliar with the printing process, it is important to understand a few
key concepts that define the workflow. The structure is fabricated by printing lines in the
x–y plane, which are then stacked layer by layer along the z-axis. These individual layers
are referred to as slices, and the distance between adjacent printing lines within a layer is
called the hatching distance.
Two critical parameters in this process are Laser Power and Scan Speed, which together
determine the exposure dose delivered to the resin. Laser Power defines the intensity of the
focused laser beam and is typically expressed as a percentage from 0% to 100 %, with higher
values corresponding to greater energy output. Scan Speed describes the velocity at which
the laser focus moves during printing and directly influences the exposure time per point.
The laser system is calibrated such that an output power of 50 mW is achieved when
the Power Scaling is set to 1.0 and Laser Power is at 100 %.
Power Scaling acts as a
multiplier for the overall laser power. The effective laser emission is therefore determined
by the combination of Power Scaling and Laser Power (%). For most applications involving

3.5
Automated Alignment using computer vision
In multimaterial microrobot fabrication, one of the key challenges is ensuring that the
second material is printed perfectly aligned with the previously printed backbone structures,
as even small stage drifts or angular misalignments can cause the second material to be
misplaced. To solve this, an automated alignment script is used to detect the actual position
of the previously printed structures and apply corrections before printing the second material.
This is done by identifying alignment markers, four hemispherical features printed in a square
layout during the backbone printing.

3.5.1
Pre-Alignment Workflow
Before running the automated alignment script, a set of initial calibration steps is per-
formed to provide the system with a precise reference for where to print the second material.
A test voxel is first printed to identify the print origin, the laser's default position, in the
live view image. These coordinates are input in the script. At this point, an annotation
scale bar is drawn, with its left edge overlapping the test voxel. This bar serves as a visual
reference for the following manual alignment steps. To determine the rotation angle of the
substrate, each array is printed with three arrow markers during the first printing step using
IP-L. We need to use two arrows that lie on the same horizontal line. For higher precision,
it is recommended to use the left arrow marker from the outermost left array and the right
arrow marker from the outermost right array, ensuring a wider distance between reference
points. Alternatively, it is also possible to use two arrows from the same array. These mark-
ers appear in the live view within the ZEN software. Using the joystick, we align the left
edge of the annotation bar with the centre of the left arrow marker. This position is recorded
and defined as coordinate (0, 0). Next, we align the bar with the centre of the right arrow
marker, which is defined based on the orientation of the arrows:
• If the arrows point north:
– The right marker is assigned the coordinate (–1, 0).
– The first array is the top right array.
• If the arrows point south:
– The right marker is assigned the coordinate (1, 0).
– The first array is the bottom left array.
By providing the coordinates of these arrows in the ZEN software, the rotation angle of the
substrate is automatically calculated. This angle is then input into the alignment script
for accurate positioning. This rotation angle, along with the array dimensions (rows and
columns) and marker orientation (north or south), is input into the alignment script. The
last step before running the automated alignment script is to move the stage manually to
the first array. To ensure correct alignment, the laser's default origin is aligned with the
centre of the first array in the live view image. A visual representation of this pre-alignment
procedure is provided in the Appendix D.

from the previously printed structures, which could disrupt accurate surface detection. Once

The dp controls the resolution scaling of the image that the function uses for detecting
circles. Specifically, it defines the inverse ratio of the accumulator resolution to the input
image resolution. A value of dp = 1 means the accumulator has the same resolution as the
input image, while higher values reduce the accumulator size (and thus processing time), at
the cost of precision. The minDist parameter sets how far apart detected circles need to be,
helping to avoid overlapping or duplicate detections. The param1 and param2 values adjust
how the function detects edges and how confident it must be to recognize something as a
circle. Finally, minRadius and maxRadius limit the search to circles within a specific size
range, which helps focus the detection on the expected markers and ignore other shapes or
noise in the image.

4.3
Validation with Updated Alignment Marker Distance
To evaluate the robustness of the alignment algorithm, a new experiment was conducted
in which the four alignment markers were printed further apart on the substrate. Specifically,
the centre-to-centre distance between markers was increased from 40 µm to 3 × 40 µm. This
modification aimed to test whether the existing detection parameters in the alignment script
remain effective when the alignment marker spacing is changed. In this experiment, the
square frames with holes were printed using IP-L resin. In the second printing step, PBA
hydrogel was used to fabricate pillars of three distinct heights within the holes.
A detailed layout and printing parameters are provided in Appendix B.2 and Figure 36.
For successful printing with the new alignment marker spacing, several modifications were
required in the alignment script to account for the increased distances between markers.
These changes are focused on: circle detection, circle filtering, and square detection. In the
circle detection step, the minDist parameter was updated from 220 pixels to 660 pixels to re-
flect the increased spacing between alignment markers. This change ensured that the Hough
Circle Transform would correctly distinguish between distant markers and avoid detecting
multiple circles too close to each other. In the circle filtering step, the radial_distance
parameter, which defines the expected distance of the alignment markers from the centre of
the array, was updated from 180 to 540 pixels to reflect the new marker spacing. This value
was chosen by tripling the initial value, since the markers are placed three times farther
out from the centre. The rad_max was also increased. This parameter defines how much
deviation is allowed between the detected position of a marker and its expected location,
measured in squared distance. In the script, the squared distance from each detected circle
to the centre of the array is calculated as follows:
d2 = (x −xcenter)2 + (y −ycenter)2
This is then compared to expected square distances, which is calculated from the
radial_distance parameter.
If the difference between these two values is smaller than
rad_max, the circle is accepted. Since the marker spacing was tripled, the expected square
distance increased by a factor of 9. To maintain the same tolerance, the rad_max value was
also scaled by a factor of 9, increasing from 30000 to 270000. For the square detection step,

The workflow consists of the following steps:
• The input image is first enhanced using CLAHE and smoothed using Gaussian blur to
reduce noise.
• Edges are extracted using the Canny edge detector and blended with the original image
to improve contrast.
• Circles are detected using the cv2.HoughCircles() function, and their positions and
radius are stored.
• Detected circles are filtered based on their x-y positions to focus on relevant regions of
the image.
• For each valid circle, the area is calculated using the formula A = πr2 (in pixel units),
and then converted to µm2 using a known pixel-to-micron conversion factor (0.08
µm/pixel).
The script displays the detected circles visually and prints the corresponding area and
radius measurements in both pixels and micrometers squared.
It is important to note that the output from the automated script is sorted based on the
detected area size, from largest to smallest. This order does not correspond to the physical
positions of the pillars in the image. To determine which detected circle represents the top,
middle, or bottom pillar, the y-coordinate of each center point must be examined. Since
the pillars are vertically aligned, the x-coordinate remains constant, while the pillar at the
top will have the smallest y-value, with increasing values corresponding to the middle and
bottom positions. To determine how well the automated method performs compared to the

In the manual analysis, the area of each hydrogel pillar was calculated using two or-
thogonal diameter measurements: one taken along the vertical axis and the other along the
horizontal. This method assumes an elliptical shape and uses the two diameters to estimate
the area. In contrast, the automated script detects and fits a circle to each pillar, computing
the area based only on a single radius derived from that fit. As a result, the two methods
are based on different geometric assumptions: ImageJ approximates an ellipse, while the
script assumes circular symmetry. This discrepancy in approach can lead to differences in
calculated area values, even when visual alignment appears acceptable.

6
Conclusion
The initial Hough Circle Transform parameters were insufficient for detecting the align-
ment markers due to the low contrast between the two materials solid (printed) IP-L and
liquid (uncured) PBA-based hydrogel, caused by their similar refractive indices.
A key
improvement step involved enhancing the image contrast and reducing blurriness. This pre-
processing significantly improved the visibility of the markers and facilitated the fine-tuning
of the circle detection parameters.
The fine-tuned circle detection parameters, initially optimized using one microrobot
design, were successfully validated on different designs across three independent batches,
each containing 10 arrays per design. Subsequent SEM imaging confirmed the successful
printing of the PBA-based hydrogel onto the pre-printed IP-L structures.
These results
demonstrate that the detection method is robust, specifically tailored to the shape of the
alignment markers rather than the microrobot design itself, and performs reliably across
multiple batches, as long as the marker size and layout are well-defined and consistent.
To evaluate the robustness and adaptability of the automated alignment workflow, the
spacing between the four alignment markers was increased in a new design. This modification
required corresponding updates in the alignment script to accommodate the altered geometry
while preserving the overall layout. The high success rate of printing the PBA-based hydrogel
on the updated IP-L design demonstrated that the workflow is both scalable and robust,
capable of adapting to variations in marker layout without compromising alignment accuracy.
PBA-based hydrogel pillars, each with the same diameter (10µm) but different heights
5 µm (Short), 10µm (Medium), and 15µm (Tall) were printed to evaluate area measurement
accuracy using two methods under four different conditions: PBS, and 5 mM, 10 mM, and 20
mM fructose concentrations. From three different batches, one array per batch was selected,
each containing nine pillars (three Medium and three Tall). ImageJ was used as the reference
(baseline) method, while a custom script based on the Hough Circle Transform was developed
for automated area measurement. The script performed reliably for Medium and Tall pillars
but failed to produce consistent results for Short pillars, which were therefore excluded from
the statistical analysis. A comparison of the percentage Mean Absolute Error (MAE %)
between the two methods was conducted using ANOVA. The results showed that fructose
concentration had a significant effect on the difference in measurement accuracy between
the two methods. A follow-up Tukey HSD test revealed that the difference in accuracy was
statistically significant between the 10 mM and 20 mM conditions, indicating that higher
fructose concentrations may impact the script performance relative to the ImageJ method.
This is possibly due to increased hydrogel swelling at higher fructose concentrations, caused
by greater water uptake. This leads to a refractive index closer to that of the surrounding
medium, which negatively affects the accuracy of automated circle detection by reducing
image contrast and blurring feature edges.
For easier visualization, a qualitative classification system was introduced based on how
far the difference in measurements between the two methods deviated from the mean and one
standard deviation. This approach facilitated the interpretation of the results, revealing that
for both pillar sizes, the detection using the script was stable and reliable, as the majority of
the data points fell within the "Good" classification. Interestingly, most of the "Excellent"

Conclusion
classifications were observed under the 10 mM fructose condition for both Tall and Medium
pillars, potentially indicating a favorable measurement environment. However, due to the
limited number of samples, further experiments are needed to confirm this trend.
By selecting the best-performing array from the previous qualitative classification, Im-
ageJ versus script-based area measurements were plotted separately for Tall and Medium
pillars across all conditions. This visual comparison revealed that the strongest agreement
between the two methods occurred for Medium pillars under 5 mM and 10 mM fructose
concentration, and for Tall pillars under 10 mM and 20 mM. These results suggest that
both pillar geometry and environmental conditions (fructose concentration) influence the
consistency between manual and automated measurements. Notably, the 10 mM condition
appears to offer optimal contrast or feature clarity for both pillar sizes, supporting its po-
tential as a favorable condition for automated detection. However, further validation with
larger datasets is necessary to generalize these findings.
The consistency of area detection across different batches and pillar heights confirmed
the robustness of the method with respect to varying experimental factors. The results re-
vealed high consistency in detection performance, indicating that there was no batch-specific
bias in the automated process. The script consistently produced reliable measurements across
both pillar height and batch, thereby validating its reliability and enabling the final—and
most critical—assessment in this objective. A paired t-test confirmed the statistical agree-
ment between the two methods, overall validating that the automated script is a reliable and
accurate alternative to manual measurements, while also offering improved efficiency.
As a final step to ensure the completeness of the study, swelling behaviour was evaluated
for both pillar heights using one batch under all fructose concentrations. This assessment
was performed separately using ImageJ and the automated script. The results confirmed
that the hydrogel swelled with increasing fructose concentration, with Tall pillars exhibiting
greater swelling compared to Medium pillars. This may be attributed to their increased
separation from the substrate, which allows for more uniform exposure to the surrounding
solution. A non-linear swelling trend was observed in the script-based measurements, which
was also visually evident from the processed images. This highlights the need for caution:
deviations from expected trends might reflect actual physical changes, but also could arise
from limitations in the detection method. Therefore, it is important to visually inspect the
results alongside automated output.
These findings align with the main objectives of the project, which were to demonstrate
reliable integration of PBA-based hydrogels in microrobots and to implement an efficient,
automated method for measuring the area of hydrogel microstructures.

Verification of Swelling Trends in Batch 2 – Array 1
To verify that Batch 2 – Array 1 followed expected swelling behavior under increasing
fructose concentrations, bar plots were created based on area measurements from both man-
ual (ImageJ) and automated (Hough Circles) methods. These visualizations demonstrate
consistent swelling trends and support the selection of this batch for method comparison.
Figure 67 compares the mean area of medium and tall pillars under different conditions
(PBS, 5 mM, 10 mM, and 20 mM), as measured by ImageJ and an automated Hough
Circles script (Figure 67a and Figure 67b respectively).
Across both methods, the Tall
pillars consistently exhibit larger mean areas than the Medium pillars at all concentrations.
This can be attributed to their greater height, which places them higher in the solution and
allows more uniform swelling in all directions due to increased exposure to the surrounding
medium.

(b) Measurements via Hough Circles script.

under four conditions (PBS, 5 mM, 10 mM, 20 mM) for Batch 2, Array 2.

H
Assumption Testing
Before performing ANOVA, it is essential to verify that the fundamental statistical
assumptions are met from our dataset. The R code used to perform these assumption checks
is provided in Appendix I.1.
H.1
Experimental Setup Summary
The experimental design consists of three batches (B1, B2, B3), each containing Array 1.
Within each array, six pillars are measured — three of medium height (blue) and three of tall
height (red). Each pillar’s area was quantified using two different image analysis methods,
and the percentage absolute error (relative difference between methods) was computed.
This value serves as the continuous dependent variable for statistical evaluation.
H.2
Statistical Assumption Testing Requirements
• The dependent variable is continuous (Absolute Error %).
• The independent variables are categorical (e.g., Height, Condition, Batch).
• Residuals are normally distributed within each group (Normality).
• Variances are homogeneous across groups (Homoscedasticity).
To evaluate whether the assumptions for ANOVA were satisfied, the following diagnostic
tests were conducted:
• Normality:
– Q–Q plots (visual inspection)
– Shapiro–Wilk test (formal statistical test)
• Homogeneity of variances:
– Residuals vs. Fitted plots (visual inspection)
– Breusch–Pagan test (formal test)
H.2.1
Assumption Testing on Full Dataset of Individual Measurements
The first set of diagnostic tests was performed on the dataset containing all individual
pillar measurements (see Tables 16, 17, and 18).
In the assumption tests, the Absolute Error % was used as the continuous dependent
variable. The categorical independent variables were Height, Condition, and Batch.

H.3
Assumption Checks Prior to Paired t-Test - Averaged Dataset
Before conducting the paired t-test to compare Script-based measurements with ImageJ-
based measurements, it was important to verify that the test’s statistical assumptions were
satisfied:
• The dependent variable must be continuous: In this analysis, the dependent variable
was the difference between Script and ImageJ measurements, measured in µm2. Since
these are numeric values that can take on any value within a range, they meet the
requirement of being continuous.
• Observations must be independent: Each Script and ImageJ value comes from the
same sample, so they are naturally paired. But each sample is only measured once,
and each pair of values is independent from the others. This satisfies the assumption
of independence for the paired t-test.
• The differences should be approximately normally distributed: To check this, normality
tests were performed using Q-Q plots and the Shapiro-Wilk test.
• There should be no extreme outliers: A boxplot was used to check for outliers in the
difference values.

Silanization of glass substrates
The substrates were silanized under appropriate solvents in the fumehood. This protocol
outlines the procedure for silanizing glass substrates. The silanization agent used was 3-
(Trimethoxysilyl)propyl methacrylate (TMPS).
Cleaning
In the solvents fume hood:
• Rinse substrates with acetone.
• Rinse substrates with isopropanol (IPA).
• Collect solvents used for rinsing and dispose as C-waste.
• Dry the substrates using N2/air gun.
• Place the substrates in sample holders.
Oxygen Plasma Treatment
• Place the sample holders with substrates inside the oxygen plasma chamber. Treat
with oxygen plasma for 15 minutes (Program P2).
• Ensure the oxygen line is connected.
• Run the same program for 15 minutes, without the substrates inside the chamber to
ensure that the chamber is clean.
Prepare Silanizing Solution
While plasma program is running:
• In a Teflon silanization beaker, mix:
– 100 mL ethanol
– 1 mL silanizing agent (use a 5 mL syringe and 19G needle)
• Just before adding substrates, add 0.5 mL of 30% acetic acid.
• Mix well and cover the beaker to avoid evaporation.
Note: Adjust the total volume of the silanization solution depending on the number of
sample holders used. Multiply the recipe by 4 when silanizing one batch (13 substrates in
one holder), and by 3 when silanizing two batches (26 substrates across two holders).

Substrate Preparation for Backbone Printing
1. Place Nanoscribe multi-round sample holder bottom side up.
2. Mount the substrate onto the sample holder with adhesive tape.
3. Apply a drop of immersion oil on the bottom side of the substrate, in the centre.
4. Flip the sample holder top side up.

placing it back into the printer for the second print.
6. Apply a drop of IP-L resin onto the top surface of the substrate, in the centre.
7. Wipe with a paper tissue, excessive resin from the syringe.
8. Dispose this in C waste bin.
9. Insert the sample holder in the Nanoscribe printer, bottom side up.
10. Click Live and on Set Exposure in Zen software.
11. On Nanowrite, click on Approach Sample, and click Yes.
12. In Nanowrite click Load, to load your job file and then Print.
13. When printing is done, click in Nanowrite Exchange holder, and take the sample
out for development.
Development
1. Remove with a paper tissue the immersion oil from the bottom side.
2. Detach the substrate from the holder by removing the adhesive tape.
3. Develop the printed sample by immersing it in a beaker with isopropanol (IPA) for 15
minutes.
4. Allow the sample to air dry.
5. Immerse it again in a beaker with IPA for another 10 minutes.
6. Let it dry inside the fume hood.
7. Dispose IPA in C waste container.

Hydrogel Actuation Protocol
1. Secure a square coverslip onto the substrate on the cell that is formed from the tapes.
2. Place the it onto the stage of the optical microscope.
3. Using a 10 µL pipette with a tip attached, carefully dispense 5 µL of your solution at
the edge of the cell. Capillary action will draw the liquid into the chamber and cover
the microstructures. Add more solution if needed to fully cover the structures.
4. Dispose of the used pipette tip in the designated C-waste bin.
5. Locate the microstructures using low magnification. Gradually increase magnification
and refocus to improve clarity.
6. Capture images of the microstructures, then carefully move the stage away from the
microscope lens.
7. To remove the solution, place a cleanroom tissue at the same edge of the cell where
the liquid was introduced. The tissue will absorb the solution via capillary action.
8. Discard the used cleanroom tissue in the C-waste bin.
9. To ensure no residual solution remains, dispense a 5 µL drop PBS into the cell, then
use a cleanroom tissue to absorb it.
10. (Optional: Allow the substrate to dry or inspect further as needed.)
11. If a different solution is to be tested, repeat steps 3 through 9 accordingly.
