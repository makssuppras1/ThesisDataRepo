Abstract
The manual process of synchronizing video input from multiple cameras and detecting the start and
end of rounds in professional combat sports video production is time-consuming and prone to errors.
The advancement of computer vision technology offers an opportunity to enhance the efficiency and
accuracy of this process. This thesis project proposes using the output from DeepStrike, a computer
vision model developed by Jabbr.AI, to automate these tasks and improve the quality of the final
product. By applying techniques and heuristics for frame-offset determination between video streams
and round detection on video footage, a fully automated system is developed to increase the efficiency
and precision of video production in combat sports.
The evaluation criteria for this project encompass utilization of hidden annotated samples. These
will be used to assess the system’s performance on unseen data and validate the heuristics devel-
oped on top of the model outputs. Preliminary results demonstrate the proposed system’s potential in
enhancing video production workflows and accuracy in combat sports.

Introduction
Video production in combat sports is pivotal to delivering an immersive competition experience to fans
worldwide. With the surge in the number of events and an ever-growing fan base, the demand for high-
quality video production has escalated. Nevertheless, the traditional manual process of synchronizing
raw video input from multiple cameras, identifying the commencement and conclusion of rounds, and
detecting match pauses is labor-intensive, time-consuming, and susceptible to errors.
Synchronization and detection tasks serve as fundamental pillars of video production. The synchro-
nization of frame offsets across multiple cameras ensures a coherent and consistent viewer experi-
ence. It allows for smooth transitions between different camera perspectives, capturing the intensity
and nuances of combat sports from every angle. In the absence of effective synchronization, the final
produced video may be disjointed and confusing, impairing the viewing experience.
Similarly, the accurate detection of rounds and pauses in combat sports matches is a crucial element
of the storytelling process in video production. It allows for the structuring of the video in a manner
that accurately represents the flow and narrative of the match. It also enables the insertion of replays,
highlights, and advertisements at appropriate intervals. Without reliable detection, essential moments
could be missed or misrepresented, and the pacing of the broadcast could be disrupted.
The recent years have seen remarkable advancements in computer vision technology, particularly
those utilizing deep learning approaches. These technologies have found successful applications
across various domains, opening up a unique opportunity to enhance the efficiency and accuracy of
combat sports video production. Despite the successful implementation of computer vision in other
sports, its potential in automating combat sports video production remains largely untapped.
This thesis project investigates the potential of DeepStrike, a computer vision model developed by
Jabbr.AI, in automating video synchronization and round detection tasks in combat sports. This project
addresses the following research questions:
• To what extent can the outputs of the DeepStrike model be processed to automate frame offset
synchronization and round detection in combat sports video production?
• How does the incorporation of additional training data influence the performance of the Deep-
Strike model in frame offset synchronization and round detection tasks?
• How do alternative state-of-the-art deep learning models compare to the DeepStrike model in
automating combat sports video production tasks?
The remainder of this thesis is organized as follows:
Chapter 2 offers an in-depth review of relevant literature, focusing on the applications of computer
vision and deep learning in sports video production. Chapters 3 detail the methodology employed in
this study, delving into the implementation of video production algorithms with and without the use of
DeepStrike, and providing a comparative analysis.
Chapter 4 showcases the real-world implementation of the study’s findings, illustrating the transfor-
mation of raw video footage into produced cohesive content. Chapter 5 presents the results and an
analysis of the performance of the system, considering the addition of training data. It includes a com-
parative analysis of traditional manual processes, the DeepStrike model, and alternative methods not
utilizing DeepStrike
Chapter 6 delves into potential future research avenues in this domain, elaborating on the implications
of the present findings and pinpointing prospective enhancements. The thesis is then concluded in
Chapter 7, providing an overarching synthesis of the research and its outcomes.

Background
In this section, we will provide a comprehensive overview of the relevant theories, models, and previ-
ous research in the field of using computer vision and deep learning for video production in sports.
Computer vision (CV) and deep learning have been applied to various tasks in image and video anal-
ysis, including object detection, segmentation, and classification. These techniques have been used
to automate tasks in sports video production, such as tracking players and detecting events of interest
to the viewers. Combat sports, however, present unique challenges in video production, due to the
fast-paced and dynamic nature of the competition. Synchronizing raw video input from multiple cam-
eras is a critical aspect of video production, but it requires accurate determination of the frame offset
between the video streams. Additionally, detecting the start and end of each round and pauses in the
match is a complex task that requires recognition of key events in the video.
Despite these challenges, advances in deep learning have the potential to improve the efficiency and
accuracy of video production in combat sports. This section will review the relevant literature in the
field, highlighting the strengths and limitations of existing approaches and the opportunities for future
research. By presenting a comprehensive overview of the background, this section will provide a
foundation for the proposed methods and results in the following chapters.
2.1
Literature Overview
The purpose of this literature overview is to examine the recent research in this field, including the
key challenges, solutions, and limitations of existing systems. The goal of this review is to identify
the most common approaches used to address these challenges and to compare the strengths and
weaknesses of these approaches. This section will furthermore provide a comprehensive examination
of the recent research in this area, with solutions and limitations of existing systems.
2.1.1
Object Detection and Tracking in Combat Sports
The study ”Automation of Computer Vision Applications for Real-time Combat Sports Video Analysis”
[1] focuses on the potential applications of Human Action Recognition (HAR) in combat sports. The
authors aim to develop a prototype automation client that accurately classifies human movements
in a video of a combat sports competition or training session. The client is developed using CV ar-
chitectures integrated with deep learning algorithms. The purpose of this client is to improve athlete
efficiency by categorizing specific chains of human actions, such as posture and accuracy, and to
score a competition using a specific ruleset. The study found that real-time object detection and track-
ing combined with real-time pose estimation can generate performance statistics from a combat sports
athlete’s movements in a video.
The paper ”On the Use of Deep Learning for Video Classification” [2] provides a comprehensive and
concise review of the use of deep learning models for video classification, including applications in
combat sports. The authors highlight the key findings based on existing deep learning models, such
as the type of network architecture used, the evaluation criteria used to measure success, and the
datasets used. The authors also provide insight into newly developed deep learning architectures and
traditional approaches. The critical challenges based on benchmarks are highlighted, and the authors
summarize the benchmark datasets and performance evaluation matrices for video classification. The
authors propose new research directions to solve the challenging video classification problem.
In the realm of multi-view video analysis, the paper ”DALES: A Software Tool for Automatic Multi-
View Video Annotation” [3] presents a software tool, DALES that is adept at semantic extraction from
multi-view videos, specifically tailored for environments with multiple cameras. DALES is capable of
identifying, quantifying, and segmenting objects within video streams. These identified objects are
then subjected to a labeling process, and the frames containing these objects are annotated with

2.1.2
Event Recognition in Combat Sports
In ”MMSys’22 Grand Challenge on AI-based Video Production for Soccer” [4], the authors focus on
automating the video production pipeline for soccer. The authors aim to assist the automation of the
pipeline using artificial intelligence, with a particular focus on the enhancement operations, such as
event clipping, thumbnail selection, and game summarization, that take place after an event has been
detected. The authors note that while event tagging has received the most attention, there are other
operations involved in the end-to-end video production pipeline that also need to be addressed.
The study ”A Survey on Video Action Recognition in Sports Datasets, Methods, and Applications”
[5] presents an extensive review of video action recognition for analytics in various individual and
team sports, including combat sports. The focus is on existing solutions and methodologies, including
statistical learning-based methods for traditional computer vision, deep learning-based methods with
2D and 3D neural models, and skeleton-based methods using auxiliary information. The authors also
discuss the importance of data collection and annotation in sports video analytics, emphasizing that
the quality of data directly impacts the performance of action recognition tasks. Additionally, they
note the unique challenges presented by sports datasets, such as accessibility, expertise required for
annotation, and the multi-purpose nature of the data.
The paper identifies several challenges when applying action recognition baselines on sports videos in
practical situations, including dense and fast-moving actions, camera motion, cut and occlusion, long-
tailed distribution and imbalanced data, multi-camera and multi-view action recognition, and transfer,
few-shot, and zero-shot learning.
Dense and fast-moving actions pose a challenge for traditional action recognition baselines, which
are designed to handle actions with longer durations. Fast-moving characteristics require the action
recognition algorithms to capture short-lived events from the video stream and tolerate background
changes that could confuse the judgment in such scenarios. Furthermore, sports like combat sports
involve dense action distribution compared to other sports, requiring action recognition algorithms to
be more sensitive to the boundary between two actions.
The study ”Video Event Recognition for Surveillance Applications (VERSA)” [6] describes a general-
purpose framework for defining and recognizing events in live or recorded surveillance video streams.
Although not specific to combat sports, the approach could be applied to event recognition in this
domain. The method uses a declarative logic language to define the spatial and temporal relationships
that characterize a given event or activity. The high-level architecture is designed to work in Extensible
Markup Language (XML)-based, services-oriented environments and can be thought of as subscribing
to the XML annotations streamed by a lower-level video analytics service.
By discussing various methods and challenges associated with event recognition in combat sports
and sports analytics in general, this section provides a comprehensive examination of the recent re-
search in this area. By incorporating insights from studies such as ”A Survey on Video Action Recog-
nition in Sports Datasets, Methods, and Applications” and ”Video Event Recognition for Surveillance
Applications (VERSA),” we can gain a better understanding of the current landscape and the future
directions for event recognition in combat sports. As the field continues to develop, we can expect fur-
ther advancements in both the methodologies and applications of video action recognition for sports

2.1.3
Synchronization Techniques for Multi-Camera Video Production
The paper ”Clustering and synchronizing multi-camera video via landmark cross-correlation” [7] presents
an efficient method for identifying and synchronizing distinct audio events in time. This approach im-
proves upon existing synchronization techniques by introducing a scalable and efficient solution that
minimizes false positives. The method, consisting of three main components - landmark extraction,
event clustering, and synchronization refinement - uses an innovative ”match-and-merge” algorithm to
refine synchronization. Evaluation results demonstrate near-perfect clustering and synchronization,
underscoring its significant computational efficiency. This study illuminates the critical role of audio
event synchronization in diverse applications, such as multi-camera video recordings, transcription
alignment, and large-scale audio file organization. The proposal effectively addresses the challenges
of current synchronization methods, marking an important stride in the field.
”Self-Supervised Human Pose based Multi-Camera Video Synchronization” [8] presents SeSyn-Net,
a self-supervised framework for automatic video synchronization using human pose as input. The
method addresses limitations of previous techniques and shows effectiveness, robustness, and ap-
plicability across various experimental conditions. The authors build two datasets, NTU-SYN and
CMU-SYN, for evaluating the methods.
SeSyn-Net employs a self-deviation loss (Ldevi) and an
interval-consistency loss (Lintv) for training a feature embedding network. Experimental analyses
demonstrate the method’s stability and robustness under different conditions, including initial offsets,
dropped frames, pose estimation errors, and cross-dataset training and testing.
In the paper ”Feature-Based Multi-Video Synchronization with Subframe Accuracy” [9], an algorithm
is proposed that achieves temporal synchronization across multiple videos that capture the same dy-
namic scene. Instead of relying on explicit object tracking, the authors utilize a feature-based matching
approach, making it versatile and applicable to a wide range of scenes, even those with complex mo-
tion. The algorithm works by identifying matching trajectory pairs from a large pool of potential matches
and uses a RANSAC-based optimization approach to determine the optimal synchronization param-
eters. When dealing with multi-video synchronization, the method selects a subset of video pairs that
provide the most information, preventing the RANSAC algorithm from being skewed by outliers. This
method overcomes the limitations of previous synchronization algorithms by offering a robust solution
that can handle complex motion and does not depend on object tracking. The authors validate their
method through comprehensive experiments, demonstrating its robustness and precision in synchro-
nizing videos with intricate motion patterns. This approach holds significant value in multi-camera
video production, where accurate synchronization of multiple videos is essential for coherent scene
reconstruction and analysis.
Lastly, ”Synchronization of multi-camera video recordings based on audio” [10] presents two methods
for synchronizing multiple video streams recorded during the same event based on the presence of
identical audio. The audio-classification method was only successful when the audio was noise-free,
while the audio-fingerprinting method proved to be much more robust and accurate, requiring an audio
overlap of three seconds. The audio-fingerprinting method has a synchronization precision of ± 11.6
ms and can be effectively applied to synchronize multiple camera recordings.
In this literature overview, we have examined the recent research in computer vision and deep learn-
ing for video production in sports, with a particular focus on the unique challenges faced in combat
sports. We have discussed the advancements in object detection and tracking, event recognition,
and synchronization techniques for multi-camera video production. Despite these advancements,
there are still gaps and limitations in existing research, particularly in addressing the fast-paced and
dynamic nature of combat sports. Our research will focus on addressing these challenges to improve
the efficiency and accuracy of video production in combat sports.

2.2
Similar Approaches
Computer vision has been increasingly used in sports to enhance performance analysis and support
decision-making. The application of computer vision in sports can be broadly divided into two cate-
gories: activity recognition and player tracking. Activity recognition in sports involves recognizing and
classifying actions performed by players in a video sequence, such as recognizing shots, tackles, and
dribbles in soccer or punches, kicks, and grappling in combat sports. Additionally, activity detection
in sports involves identifying the start and end times of specific actions and events in the video se-
quence. Many computer vision techniques have been used for activity recognition and detection in
sports, including traditional computer vision techniques and deep learning approaches. Traditional
computer vision techniques, such as feature extraction, motion analysis, and event detection, extract
features from video sequences, such as motion vectors, optical flow, and spatio-temporal features,
and use these features for classification. However, these techniques are limited in handling complex
and varied actions in sports videos.

WSC sports

Hawk-eye
Hawk-Eye is a computer vision system, owned by Sony, and first used in cricket in 2000. It visually
tracks the trajectory of the ball and creates a moving image of its statistically most likely path, known
as Shot Spot. The system relies on six to seven high-performance cameras, typically positioned on
the stadium roof, to track the ball from different angles. The video from these cameras is triangulated
and merged to produce a three-dimensional representation of the ball’s trajectory. While not perfect,
the system is said to be accurate to within 3.6 millimeters and is widely accepted as an unbiased
second opinion in sports.
The system operates based on the principles of triangulation, using visual images and timing data
from high-speed video cameras located around the playing area. The data store contains a predefined
model of the playing area, including game rules. Hawk-eye identifies the group of pixels corresponding
to the image of the ball in each frame and calculates its position by comparing it with at least two
physically separate cameras at the same instant. Successive frames create a record of the ball’s
path, predicting its future flight and interactions with the playing area. A graphic image of the ball path
and playing area is then generated, providing information to judges, TV viewers, or coaching staff
almost in real-time. It also features a back-end database and archiving capabilities for extracting and
analyzing trends and statistics about individual players, games, and ball-to-ball comparisons.
Hawk-Eye technology has been used in various sports, including cricket, tennis, and association foot-
ball, to adjudicate on contentious decisions, track ball trajectories, and analyze player performance.
Its accuracy and real-time capabilities have made it an indispensable tool in modern sports.

TrackMan
TrackMan is a launch monitor technology used in golf to capture and analyze various performance
metrics. It employs Doppler radar for tracking the 3D movement of the club head through the impact
zone and the full flight of the golf ball until landing. Trackman 4, the latest version of the system,
features dual-radar technology with two separate radar arrays for independent tracking of club and
ball, providing precise tracking and reducing errors in data reporting.
The system collects a wide range of data, including club head speed, face angle, ball speed, launch

and it can adjust the reported data based on factors such as the type of golf ball used, wind, tem-
perature, and altitude. Additionally, Trackman is compatible with course simulator software, allowing
users to virtually play on some of the world’s top golf courses.

2.3
Key Challenges
Automating the video production of combat sports poses distinct challenges due to the dynamic and
unpredictable nature of such events. Addressing these challenges is paramount to ensuring a seam-
less viewing experience. This section will elaborate on the primary challenges faced in this endeavor,
specifically emphasizing the importance of video synchronization and event detection.
2.3.1
Video Synchronization
Video synchronization is the process of aligning raw video input from multiple cameras to produce a
cohesive, multi-angle viewing experience. The objective is to accurately determine the frame offset
between concurrent video streams.
Deep learning models are particularly useful for these tasks, as their output contains patterns that can
be matched between video streams, allowing for precise synchronization. However, the process isn’t
devoid of challenges. Consider the following:
• Varying Camera Angles: Different camera angles can present different perspectives of the
same event, making it challenging to find common patterns for synchronization.
• Lighting Conditions: Changes in lighting, shadows, or reflections can alter the appearance of
scenes, potentially leading to synchronization errors.
• Occlusions: Objects or individuals obstructing the camera’s view can create gaps in the data,
complicating the synchronization process.
Addressing these challenges ensures that the final video output is not only synchronized but also
provides a consistent and immersive viewer experience.
2.3.2
Event Detection
Event detection, in the context of combat sports, primarily refers to the identification of significant
moments such as the start and end of rounds, or unexpected breaks. Automating this process implies
training deep learning models to recognize these key events, thus eliminating the need for manual
annotation.
However, this task is far from straightforward. Consider the following complexities:
• Dynamic Nature of Combat Sports: The fast-paced and unpredictable movements in matches
make it difficult to pinpoint specific events.
• Referee’s Gestures and Signals: Different referees may have slightly different gestures or
signals for the same event. Recognizing these nuances is essential.
• Variability in Inter-round Activities: During breaks between rounds, different competitions
might display varied content, ranging from advertisements to highlights of previous rounds or
even close-ups of fighters and their corners. This variability makes it difficult to establish a
consistent pattern for event detection.
• Differing Number of Rounds: Not all combat sports matches have a uniform number of rounds.
While some might have a fixed number, others might vary based on the competition or cham-
pionship rules. This inconsistency poses a challenge in predicting the end of the match or seg-
menting the footage appropriately.

• Filmed with the whole ring within the frame

• Professional video production filmed with multiple cameras

Cross-Correlation
Cross-correlation is a time-tested statistical tool used to gauge the correlation between two series.
In the context of video synchronization, it quantifies the similarity between two signals based on an
applied offset. This technique, inspired by synchronization methodologies applied to audio data as
demonstrated in [10] and [7], became the cornerstone of our approach.
Before delving into the process, it’s crucial to understand that the signals extracted from the videos
are essentially time series data. Each of these series represents a sequence of events captured by
the camera, and the primary challenge is to perfectly align these sequences from different camera
perspectives. This alignment ensures that a specific moment or event in the fight is viewed coherently
and consistently from all angles, facilitating accurate analysis.
To commence the synchronization process, we first load data from dictionaries into pandas dataframes
using the load_video_dataframes function. The dataframes encapsulate the signals provided by the
DeepStrike model. For instance, the ’boxing’ signal indicates ongoing boxing activity, ’paused’ denotes
inter-round pauses, and ’break’ captures unanticipated disruptions within rounds.
The mathematical representation of cross-correlation between signals s1 and s2 is:
h(s1, s2) = (s1 ∗s2[−1])
(3.1)
In this equation, h(s1, s2) denotes the cross-correlation, the asterisk represents convolution, and s2[−1]
signifies the time-reversed version of s2. Reversing one of the signals is essential in this computation
to determine how much one signal should be shifted to align optimally with the other. A resulting
cross-correlation value close to 1 indicates a strong positive relationship between the two signals at a
given offset, meaning they are closely synchronized at that point.
Utilizing the numpy.correlate function, we compute the array of cross-correlation values:
i(s) = argmax(s)
(3.2)
For effective cross-correlation, it’s imperative that the signals are of consistent lengths. Thus, we
pad the shorter signals to match the length of the longest signal. To ascertain the optimal alignment
between signals, we detect the peak of the cross-correlation function. This peak corresponds to the
frame offset essential for synchronization.

While the cross-correlation method used in this study has proven effective for video synchronization,
it is important to acknowledge its limitations and the assumptions made during its application. Firstly
we assume that the signals from the different cameras are continuous and similar in nature. If there
are significant differences in the signals due to factors such as camera angle, lighting conditions, or
occlusions, the cross-correlation may not yield accurate synchronization.
The method finds the offset where the correlation between the signals is maximum. However, this does
not necessarily guarantee perfect synchronization, especially in align where the signals have repetitive
patterns or noise. Furthermore one should not assume that there is single peak in the function which
corresponds to the correct offset. In reality, there may be multiple peaks, and the highest peak may
not correspond to the correct offset.

The Sum of Squared Differences (SSD) method, while effective in certain contexts, has its limitations
when used for video synchronization. Even minute discrepancies or noise in the signals can lead
to high SSD values. This sensitivity can obscure the true alignment point, especially when dealing
with video data where changes between frames are subtle. SSD prioritizes the absolute differences
between signals. In cases where the overall patterns or shapes of the signals are more significant
than their absolute magnitudes, the SSD method might not yield optimal results.
Directly computing the SSD for every potential offset in lengthy signals can be computationally de-
manding. This could lead to prolonged synchronization durations, particularly in the absence of opti-
mization strategies.
The SSD landscape can present multiple local minima, complicating the search for the global minimum
that truly signifies the most suitable synchronization point. The method assumes that the signals are
approximately aligned from the outset. Deviations from this assumption can affect the accuracy of the
derived frame offsets.

Trajectories, in the context of video synchronization, are sequences of feature points tracked over
time. Their construction provides a temporal context, essential for understanding the movement and
changes in the video. However, it’s crucial to recognize that not all trajectories contribute equally to
synchronization. Some might be noisy, transient, or simply irrelevant, potentially leading to inaccura-
cies. To ensure robust synchronization, it becomes imperative to filter out such trajectories.
The length of a trajectory serves as a primary indicator of its reliability. A trajectory that spans
only a few frames often arises from transient features or noise. Such short-lived trajectories can be
misleading, as they might not represent consistent or meaningful motion in the video. Therefore,
setting a minimum length threshold helps in discarding these potentially unreliable trajectories.
Another vital aspect to consider is the variance of the trajectory. This metric quantifies the extent of
movement of a feature point over time. Trajectories with minimal movement, or low variance, might
not be as informative since they don’t capture significant temporal changes. These static or near-static
trajectories are filtered out based on a predefined variance threshold.
In scenarios involving stereo vision or multi-camera setups, the alignment with epipolar lines be-
comes a significant criterion. The epipolar geometry, which describes the geometric relations between
different views, can be leveraged to assess the consistency of trajectories across views. For every
point in a trajectory, its alignment with the corresponding epipolar line in another view is evaluated

Trajectory Matching and Video Synchronization
The process of video synchronization involves comparing the trajectories of feature points across two
or more videos. A crucial metric in this comparison is the residual error between trajectories, which
quantifies how well they temporally align. Mathematically, for two trajectories T1(t) and T2(t + θ),
where t is the time and θ is the temporal offset, the residual error E can be defined as:
E(θ) =
∫
(T1(t) −T2(t + θ))2dt
(3.4)
This equation delineates the squared difference between two trajectories in relation to the temporal
offset θθ. The objective is to discern the θ that minimizes E. A minimized residual error ensures optimal
temporal alignment between videos, resulting in seamless transitions and a unified video presentation.
To determine the synchronization parameters, trajectories from one video are matched with those from
others. This is accomplished by minimizing the residual error. Given the potential presence of outliers
or erroneous trajectory matches, the synchronization process is fortified using the RANSAC (Random
Sample Consensus) algorithm.
RANSAC is an iterative estimator tailored to deduce parameters of a mathematical model from a
dataset that could harbor outliers. In the realm of video synchronization, RANSAC identifies the most
plausible synchronization parameters by repetitively selecting random data subsets, fitting them to
the model, and evaluating the fit based on the count of inliers. This methodology ensures accurate
synchronization even in the face of occasional trajectory mismatches.

Statistical change detection
The Cumulative Sum (CUSUM) algorithm is a well-established method for detecting abrupt changes
in a dataset. At its core, CUSUM operates by monitoring the ”cumulative sum” of deviations from a
reference value (usually a mean) over time. Mathematically, the CUSUM at time i can be represented
as:
Si = max(0, Si−1 + Xi −µ −drift)
(3.5)
Where:
• Si: Cumulative sum at time i
• Xi: Data point at time i
• µ: Reference value, typically the mean of the data series
• drift: A compensation value to account for expected changes in data
A change is detected when Si exceeds a predefined threshold, indicating that the observed values
have consistently deviated from the expected values beyond an acceptable limit. This makes CUSUM
sensitive to both small and large shifts in the mean level of a data series. It’s particularly beneficial
in real-world situations where the time of change is unknown and the post-change mean is uncertain.
Our specific implementation of CUSUM allows us to detect when the ’boxing’ data series (representing
the intensity of boxing activity) rises significantly and the ’break’ series (representing the rest period)
drops significantly. This marks the start of a round. Conversely, we detect the end of a round when
the ’break’ series rises significantly and the ’boxing’ series drops significantly.
The CUSUM algorithm takes as input two parameters: threshold and drift. The threshold parameter
represents the minimum change in the data that will trigger a detection, while the drift parameter
is used to compensate for expected normal changes in the data, thus preventing false detections.

During our implementation, we encountered challenges in tuning the drift and threshold parameters

considered. Moreover, if certain detected change points seem illogical within the context, increasing
the drift can help in filtering out these detections. Our experiments iterated upon these suggestions to
ensure optimal detection of rounds and breaks in our specific dataset.
To automatically determine the most effective values for threshold and drift, we employed a grid search
methodology. Here we define a grid of parameters and then evaluate the performance of the algorithm
for each combination of these parameters. This exhaustive search is computationally demanding but
ensures that we explore a wide range of possible parameter combinations. We established a range
of potential values for both the threshold and drift. The grid was then constructed by pairing each
threshold value with every drift value, resulting in thousands of combinations. For each combination,
we applied the CUSUM algorithm on our dataset, and then compared the detected changes to our
known round breaks. The goal was to find the parameter combination that minimized the error between
detected and actual breaks.
This process, however, has its limitations. The granularity of the search was defined by the number of
values in our grid, which meant that we were possibly overlooking optimal values that lay between our

given the dynamic nature of a boxing match. As a result, we had to rely on some iterative fine-tuning to

4.4
Inter Round Highlight Reel
In this section, we discuss the techniques used to extract the most significant moments from the
boxing matches and display them during inter-round breaks. The highlights serve to increase the
overall understanding and entertainment value of the footage for viewers.
The most action-packed moments in a match, or ”highlights”, are determined based on the action
values calculated for each frame using the DeepStrike model. For both red and blue fighters, peak
action sequences are extracted for each camera angle. The buffer frames parameter is adjustable,
controlling the number of frames included before and after the peak frame. The longest sequences
for red and blue fighters are then returned as the highlight segments. A slow-motion effect is applied
by repeating each frame of the highlight sequence twice, slowing the playback speed to half. Further-
more, a delay is introduced before the start of the highlight reel, allowing the viewer to get a view of
the fighters returning and settling into their respective corners. If the inter-round break is longer than
the total length of the highlight sequences, the main footage resumes.
Storing the video frames for the highlight reels can be a challenging task due to the amount of mem-
ory they require. Rather than loading all frames into memory at once, the highlight sequences are
stored on the disk as separate video files and loaded into memory only when needed for display. This
approach helps in minimizing memory consumption at the cost of disk space.

Further work
The journey through automating combat sports video production through deep learning approaches,
has unraveled several pathways that can be further pursued to advance the state of the art. The
success and challenges faced throughout this endeavor highlight the avenues for further work and
refinements that could lead to an even more robust system. Below are some of the areas we believe
hold significant potential for advancement.
6.1
Synchronization through Pose-Estimation and SeSyn
The challenges faced in video synchronization using general features have underscored the need for
a more targeted approach, especially in the unique context of combat sports. The paper by [8] offers
a promising direction in this regard by introducing pose estimation for synchronization.
Pose estimation holds potential for several reasons:
1. Resilience to Diverse Camera Angles: Combat sports videos often come from various sources
with diverse vantage points. While traditional synchronization methods may falter when cam-
era perspectives vary dramatically, pose estimation can effectively capture and compare the
positions and movements of fighters across different angles.
2. Distinct Human Actions: Combat sports have specific, recognizable postures and movements.
Pose estimation’s ability to identify and track key points on fighters’ bodies offers a precise mech-
anism to detect these unique actions, even when they overlap or occur simultaneously.
3. Real-time Tracking: Given the fast-paced nature of combat sports, rapid actions and split-
second movements are frequent. Traditional methods might miss these swift moments due to
their reliance on broader features. In contrast, pose estimation’s frame-by-frame analysis can
provide real-time synchronization based on the minutiae of each movement.
In future endeavors, utilizing the SeSyn method for pose-based synchronization could pave the way
for more accurate and real-time video production in combat sports. By focusing exclusively on the
dynamics inside the boxing ring and harnessing the distinctiveness of fighters’ actions, this approach
may offer a robust solution to the synchronization challenges encountered in the current system.
6.2
Refinement of the CNN method
Our experiments with the Resnet-18 model showcased its potential in the round detection tasks. How-
ever, the model can be further fine-tuned to better understand the nuances of combat sports. A more
curated dataset, which emphasizes raw amateur footage, can be used to refine the model.
In it’s current state, the method outputs rudimentary activity tags, when it could easily be modified to
yield timestamp of the detected events like the other methods. Due to the long time it takes to run
inference through the samples, we will need to look into accounting for the downsampling.
6.3
Overcoming Grid Search Limitations in the CUSUM method
The efficacy of this method largely depends on the hyperparameters used, which we initially optimized
using grid search. While effective to a degree, grid search exhibits some limitations. The nature of
grid search is exhaustive, checking all possible combinations of provided hyperparameter values.
This brute force method can either overlook the optimal threshold values for CUSUM, if the grid is
too coarse, or become computationally demanding if the grid is too dense. Given the variability of the
data, round transitions might not always have the same characteristics, making an optimally-tuned
CUSUM even more crucial.

• Bayesian Optimization: This method can optimize the hyperparameters for CUSUM by itera-
tively selecting the next set of hyperparameters based on prior evaluations. It builds a probabilis-

• Random Search: Random search has been shown to be more efficient than grid search in cer-

6.3.1
Dynamic Thresholding: expanding out approaches
In our research, the dynamic thresholding methods were employed for threshold setting. However,
there are other promising methods and models for future exploration:
1. Adaptive Gaussian Thresholding: Instead of using a simple global value as a threshold, adap-
tive thresholding considers small neighbors of pixels. For instance, in adaptive Gaussian thresh-
olding, the threshold value is the weighted sum of neighborhood values where weights are a
Gaussian window. This might be beneficial in scenarios where there’s a drastic change in activ-
ity within a short span, making a global threshold less effective.
2. Time-Series Anomaly Detection: Time-series anomaly detection algorithms, like Seasonal-
Trend decomposition using LOESS (STL), can be adapted to detect significant shifts in the data.
These models, by identifying patterns and seasonality, can pinpoint anomalies or significant
changes, which in the context of our research, can be the start or end of rounds.
3. One-dimensional Convolutional Neural Networks (1D CNNs): 1D CNNs, initially designed
for time-series data, can capture temporal patterns. By training a 1D CNN on labeled segments
of combat sports videos, the model can learn the subtle patterns that signify round starts and
ends, making it more adaptive than traditional thresholding methods.
By integrating machine learning models tailored for 1D data and sophisticated thresholding tech-
niques, we can pave the way for a more nuanced and precise system for event detection.
