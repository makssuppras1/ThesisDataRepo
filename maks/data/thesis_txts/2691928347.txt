Abstract
In this work, a broad view on the framework of small┬¡scale PV┬¡installations and an anal┬¡
ysed impact of mixed orientations are presented with a case study of buildings in Oslo,
Norway. An optimization method to determine the best mix of orientations for determined
scenarios of energy and cost reduction was constructed and tested on buildings of the
case study.
Increased popularity in small┬¡scale renewable generators have paved the way for a re┬¡
vision of the European legislation on the role of the producing consumer in the energy
grid. Existing framework in the Norwegian energy market is awaiting an update on the
limited participation opportunities of active customers, where adaptation of European law
will introduce new possibilities in ways of achieving economic feasibility of an installation.
By considering the established and renewed framework conditions of energy producing
consumers, the buildings of the case study were found to comply with energy coopera┬¡
tion schemes of either citizen energy communities or in aggregation. Upon which revenue
stream and possibilities of mixed orientations will depend on the Norwegian adaptation of
the rules.
The direct impact of mixed orientations was determined by discovering and analysing the
consumption and production patterns of different case locations and orientations. In doing
so, a comprehensive literature review aided in the creation of a generalized optimization
model, minimizing on behalf of the imported energy and cost of consumption. It was
found that optimized PV configurations perform better than market standard in purely self┬¡
consumption of energy, but obtained the best results when allowing hourly overproduction.
The impact of mixed orientations proved to be of less importance in a cost minimization
scenario, where solving for optimality resulted in the majority of panels optimized on total
annual production.
In addition, several areas for further improvement and research based on this project has
been identified and are presented.

1.1
Motivation and Background
The share of solar energy in the global energy mix continues to grow as the popularity,
availability, and efficiency steadily increase. [1] This is clearly seen in market trends,
where 2020 showed a 23% increase in electricity supply from photovoltaic (PV) panels
alone, followed by an expected growth of 18% this year (2021) [2]. However, PV expan┬¡
sion is often underrated in forecast models, amongst other seen with the expected decline
in installed capacity for 2020 [3], whereas the aftermath showed a great increase, even
during a world spanning pandemic. An impressive technology resilience and popularity
of PV in the modern energy market can be deduced from this development. Continuing,
combined PV and wind capacity is expected to surpass the electricity giant coal by 2024
[3], and an annual decline in electricity supply from source can also be seen for other
fossil fuels, such as gas and oil [4]. Economies of scale combined with increasing politi┬¡
cal focus on emission reduction and climate agreements will, undoubtedly, transform the
energy mix and grid structure we have today. This is a change that is recognized and
supported by the transmission grid operators of Europe (ENTSO┬¡E) [5].
European PV development is still secondary to the capacity giant China, but as instal┬¡
lation of distributed generation is moderate in the larger markets of China and the US,
Europe and the EU is rapidly moving forward. [3] The third energy┬¡ and Clean energy
for all Europeans┬¡ package of the EU is much to thank for this development [6], including
the Renewable Directive (RED) of 2018 [7] and the renewed Internal Marked for Elec┬¡
tricity directive (IEMD) of 2019 [8], which promotes increased renewable penetration and
facilitates for alternative market actors of both larger scale and decentralized/distributed
management. These changes in the European legislative framework is also highlighted
by the ENTSO┬¡E, as a driving force for the proceedings of a low carbon European energy
system. The ENTSO┬¡E consortium recommends increased focus on close┬¡to┬¡real time
electricity markets and increased coordination between the Transmission System Opera┬¡
tor (TSO) and Distributed System Operator (DSO) as the flexibility of distributed resources
are bound to increase, including PV. [5]
However, variable production and demand mismatch decreases the feasibility of dis┬¡
tributed rooftop solar installations, especially in regions with significant seasonal differ┬¡
ences. Seasonality is clear on a yearly scope, but can also be seen on a smaller scale,
where the daily generation profile might mismatch the consumption in peak magnitude
and timing. An installation may prove to exceed demand at one point, while coming short
in another. A larger capacity can minimize some problems and prove technical optimal┬¡
ity, but often comes at the expense of economic feasibility. Additionally, limited export
revenues from production contributes to diminish the profitability of larger rooftop instal┬¡
lations. Political instruments work towards increasing the opportunity of renewable instal┬¡
lations through market actors, policies, support schemes, and carbon penalties. [8, 7,
9]
The legal framework for enabling final customers to sell self┬¡produced electricity, thus
becoming active customers, is still under┬¡developed in the Norwegian framework, where

1.2
Research Question and Method
This project is a self┬¡standing work from the author based on the personal motivation of
interest in PV systems and possibilities of this technology in the coming years. Derived
from the motivation and background of the previous section, a research question at the
center of this project is defined.
Under which conditions would it be valuable to combine the production of a PV system
with different, fixed orientations?
This question can be answered in numerous ways, for instance: a numeric approach
can look at the energy focused picture of import reduction by obtaining a higher degree
of self┬¡sufficiency, the economic picture of generating energy for both revenues from grid
export and substitution of import, or the revenues of providing services from PVs. Another
approach can be discovering the potential of such an energy source and describing the
opportunities it induces, for example: how can this energy be utilized in what legal entities
or in what way is there a need for this asset.
The scale of the question is also an important decision, especially in this case, where PV
installations range from off┬¡grid single panels to large┬¡scale commercial PV power plants.
Different sized installations will bring along different opportunities. The cooperation with
the consulting company Asplan Viak AS in Oslo includes the opportunity to look at this
question with the realistic backbone of real consumption data from existing buildings in
Oslo. With this possibility, the scope of the project has been defined:
The study investigates potential values of the combined generation from PV panels of
mixed orientations, with selected public buildings in Oslo as case. Including mapping of
the current and future possibilities of installations in relation to the framework conditions
of active customers.

2.1
Values of PV generation
Regardless of location, PV installations provide an easily quantifiable value from energy
generation. This value can either be regarded in monetary terms or in terms of energy.
The latter tends to substitute an already existing energy requirement procured from an
alternative source, also culminating in monetary terms of comparison, however, this pro┬¡
cess can also be considered at a narrower stage, acknowledging the direct wattage re┬¡
placement. Different values of PV generation returns different conditions and objectives
on the usage. Three key values have been defined in this project, whose conditions are
quantified by the means of optimization models. This section covers the description and
justification of these values.
2.1.1
The Value of Self┬¡Consumption
The unfavourable conditions of PV installations in Norway, described in Section 3.3.1,
promotes the objective of obtaining a higher degree of self┬¡sufficiency from PV. This in┬¡
cludes replacing bought energy with self generated energy, thus reducing the full eco┬¡
nomic disadvantage corresponding to grid price of energy. This value is measured in
terms of self┬¡consumed electricity, and has an optimized objective as shown in Section
4.6.1. This value is also relevant for installations with an unreliable alternative source
of energy. Optimizing configurations on behalf of self┬¡consumption ensures a source of
energy matching the requirements of the location, within the limits and disadvantages of
volatility accompanying PV energy. Conditions such as these highlights the importance
on the value of self┬¡consumption, where overproduction that exceeds the demand is lost.
The model of Section 4.6.1 concerns the public buildings in Oslo, all grid┬¡connected, but
introduces an optimization tool with a potential secondary use of support in planning an
off┬¡grid solution.
Mixed orientations of PV modules are optimized on behalf of minimizing imported elec┬¡
tricity, to quantify the aggregated production profile of the installation and discovering the
value of self┬¡consumption.
2.1.2
The Value of Revenues from Overproduction
A drawback of PV generation is the reliance on an uncontrollable natural resource, the
sun. This causes a predictable production profile which may not match the profile of
demand. Mismatch in demand and supply will either cause a surplus or lack of energy.
Grid connected systems may offer this excess as an asset for producing revenues, thus
increasing the profitability of the PV system. The value of revenues from overproduction
is a monetary value concerning the potential of mixed orientations in reducing the annual
energy cost of a building. This is an advancement of the value of self┬¡consumption, not
only considering the reduction of bought energy, but also a secondary reduction through

2.1.3
The Value of Local Production
The third and final value defined and analysed in this project is the value of local produc┬¡
tion. This value considers a broader view on profitability than just for the owner of the PV
installation.
The established model of energy production is focused around centralized large scale
power plants, often located in close vicinity to hubs of high demand. In a digitalized world
with high energy demands, production from centralized plants has to be transported fur┬¡
ther away. Common daily routines and habits leads to common hours of energy demand,
which in turn increases the required quantity of transport. Another complementary fac┬¡
tor considering PV is the volatility of production. A region is likely to have similar po┬¡
tency of the solar resource throughout a day, thus resulting in uniform power profiles of
grid feed┬¡in. This combined burden on the distribution grid can cause congestion, dam┬¡
ages, and in worst case of the recipients, blackouts. [19, 20] Normally, this problem is
solved by the local DSO through grid reinforcement, giving rise to additional costs on
the consumers through tariffs. Distributed energy resources such as PV installations, of┬¡
ten residential, can contribute negatively on one side, by increasing the total amount of
transported energy, but can also contribute positively through flexibility of the consumer,
especially through aggregation. [21] Flexibility through aggregation can contribute both
through demand response and as an advancement of the established grid model through
locally produced energy. This may postpone the grid reinforcement and thus provide
value to the DSO. Although the total transported amount will increase, shorter distances
from source will use a smaller portion of the grid than a centralized power plant. Cor┬¡
rect and efficient energy management and knowledge of the grid from energy producing
access points would be of high importance in such a scenario. [22, 23]
Locally produced energy, when managed correctly, can help improve the overall welfare
of the grid. However, this value is hard to quantify, as no flexibility market is established in
the Scandinavian region [24]. An example of quantification on the value of local produc┬¡
tion is introduced in the optimization model of Section 4.6.3, promoting local grid feed┬¡in
through an incentive on hours of high regional demand. Not only is the welfare consid┬¡
erations focused around the lowering of congestion, but also through ensurance of main┬¡
taining the ÔÇØcustomer baseÔÇØ of the grid. Increased accessibility of self┬¡sufficiency through
distributed energy resources may incentivise traditional consumers to declare indepen┬¡
dence and disconnect from the distribution grid. For instance, it is expected that 10% of
Australian consumers have left the grid by 2050 [25]. This assigns the designated costs
of those consumers on the remaining in the grid through increased tariffs, thus reduc┬¡
ing the consumer surplus and economic welfare of the system. Allocating a portion of
the active customer production to the grid through an economic mechanism may help in
incentivising customers to stay connected.

2.2
Solar Potential and Installation Standards
Interest in small┬¡scale solar installations in Norway, and Oslo, is growing rapidly, despite
the high latitude and generally low solar resource for winter months. This is mostly due
to incentives through the State energy fund ENOVA, supporting installations with refunds
up to 35% on the total investment of the installation. [26] This economic support is set
to reduce on the 1st. of July 2021, as the market price and economical feasibility of PV
has improved to a point were such a strong incentive is no longer needed to continue the
growth of distributed PV installations. [27] Another reason for the growth in interest of
later years comes from the slowly retracting belief of the poor conditions of solar resource
overshadowing the economic feasibility. The realisation that the production potential is
higher than what previously expected is settling, and citizens start considering alternatives
to purely grid bought energy for coverage of demand. Additionally, the climatic aspect and
impact of the Paris agreement, aswell as heavy marketing from PV vendors and energy
suppliers is likely to have caused a role in the popularity growth.
The solar resource of Oslo can be seen in the top left of Figure 2.1, with comparison
of other regions of Southern Norway. The average totals is found at a daily value of
around 2.7 kWh/kWp. This value is calculated from an annual average, thus omitting the
significant seasonal differences. Own calculations on the production from an example
installation showed that the daily total of lowest producing winter month could be over one
magnitude lower than the highest producing summer month, depending on configuration
of the installation.
PV panels produce the highest when exposed to direct sunlight but also yields production
from scattered and reflected rays, as further elaborated in Section 3.6. The suns trajectory
across the sky and the configuration of PV panels is the first consideration in determining
the production potential of an installation. A higher tilt would receive more direct rays
of sunlight for low solar altitudes, while simultaneously reducing the received amount of
scattered radiation. Similarly, the compass orientation of the module face determines the
intraday period of production peak, as the sun traverses across the sky. This difference
of production profiles, gained from mixed orientations of panels, is the backbone of the
numeric analyses performed in this project. The consequences on production profile can
be seen in Figure 2.2, amplified by showing the curvature of hourly maximum values with
extreme tilts in the four cardinal directions, throughout a full year.
The curves of the figure hints on the possible profile manipulation that can be obtained
by mixing the orientations of an installation, where a clear difference in the intraday pro┬¡
duction peaks can be seen in the different curves. The East facing 90┬░ module shows
a sharp incline in the early hours when the sun is rising, with a more gradual decrease
as the sun passes the face of the module around noon. The opposite trend can be seen
for the vertical West facing module, which reaches its production peak in the last hours
of sunlight. The vertical North module has a generally lower production, with a morning
and evening increase originating from the longer summer days. A completely different
picture can be seen with the 10┬░ tilted E/W facing module, showing a much more evenly
distributed production profile which reaches its peak at solar noon. Aggregation of the
production from mixed orientations can be manipulated to form a different profile than a
uniform layout, optimized to suit the purpose of the installation.

2.3.2
Aggregated Entities
Aggregation in terms of energy production is the combination of several generating units
or load points. A larger quantity of either, regarded as one, is easier to handle and benefit
from. [45] The combined distributed energy resource collected in an aggregation will have
a greater market impact and can function as a production unit on a much larger scale than
what singular rooftop installations can. Considering the number of locations owned by KID
in Oslo, the collective renewable energy production will have a large quantity of energy
eligible for revenue generation. IEMD and RED ensures that all active customers, also
those participating in an energy community, can freely participate in aggregation schemes.
A common aggregation structure is the Virtual Power Plant (VPP), with existing estab┬¡
lishments all over the world. The best known in Europe is perhaps the German Next┬¡
Kraftwerke, aggregating the production, storage, and flexibility of over 10,000 units since
2009. [46] Typically, a VPP consists of generation units of various resources, storage
components, and flexible loads with trading in either the day┬¡ahead, intraday, or a bal┬¡
ancing market such as the NordPool regulation market. [45, 47, 48] A VPP solves the
intermittency of solar generation in combination with other renewable resources and stor┬¡
age, ensuring an optimized benefit from excess production and secondary energy assets.
A VPP only consisting of PV production assets would not be capable of market participa┬¡
tion in other forms than through the wholesale market of energy trading, however, as PV
is an important resource of a complete VPP, a similar structure to that of Next┬¡Kraftwerke
can be seen as relevant for this project. This structure is based on building owners renting
the production or storage assets from the company, thus letting the company use their roof
or available area for energy production. The Norwegian state owned energy production
company Statkraft is a shareholder of the first VPP in Europe, but no equal structure for
Oslo or Norway could be found in the literature review of this project. However, the current
government proposes the introduction of VPPs as one of the focus areas in increasing
efficiency of the energy grid. [49, 50]

factor of loss. Instead, the PV system components are assumed pre┬¡procured and without
impact on the results of the optimization. The exception is the rack┬¡price, which signifies

Shading
Shading of the PV modules is partly covered by the horizon profile embedded in the ra┬¡
diation resource, but also present for near objects that casts shade on a module. The
obstruction of radiation comes from any structure between the source of radiation and the
module surface, and is usually modelled with a 3D software that tracks sun position and
corresponding obstruction from adjacent buildings, trees, or other panels on the panel
array. This requires detailed information and modelling of each case study performed,
where the cast shade is depending on the final configuration of the PV array. The opti┬¡
mization model in this project calculates the optimal configuration based on the radiation
resource and a given available area, so a final detailed layout of the PV installation is
not determined. A standard value for shading can be used, but will not be representable
and accurate in a generic model as presented in this project. Instead, a more detailed
modelling with sun path and object obstruction can be performed for a given case based
on the results of the model. The production in an optimization scenario, being dependant
on layout, creates a feedback loop, which can be implemented in an optimization model
but is beyond the scope of this project.
During a year, the PV modules of an installation will cast different shadows throughout the
day depending on the position of the sun. Modules are heavily susceptible to shadows,
as shading of a single cell, known as partial shading, can decrease the output of an entire
module and panel string, thus resulting in decreased production. Most solar panels comes
with bypass diodes, which cuts of a string of cells in the case of partial shading and lower
the losses. This will still decrease the production of the module but creates a parallel circuit
that avoids thermal buildup and the formation of hot spots, which can induce permanent
damage [98]. For the sake of simplicity, the modules in this project are assumed to not
have shading from neighbouring panels. This implies a minimum area required for each
module, depending on the configuration.
The shadow cast by a module reaches minimum when the sun is at solar zenith and
the shadow is a direct projection of the module, which size is dependant on the solar
altitude and module tilt. Opposite, the maximum shadow is infinite at solar altitudes of 0┬░,
considering a beam of light without reflection from other angles. The annual worst case
scenario of shading is found at the winter solstice, while the vernal and autumnal equinox
results in similar results. The average of the winter solstice and autumnal equinox at 11
AM was chosen for calculation of the required area per tilt and orientation. The worst case

Electrical losses
Transporting generated DC electricity from PV modules to the inverter, and conversion to
AC, induces electrical losses along wires, connection points, system balancing (module
mismatch), energy availability (generic loss for shut┬¡down┬¡events), and efficiency of the
conversion. These loss factors are combined in one comprehensive factor called array
loss, including the crystalline module electrical loss of light induced degradation (LID, one┬¡
time effect occurring when exposed to sunlight). [99, 51, 106] The system boundary of
considerations in this project is defined at the conversion to AC, so losses induced from
AC wiring and potential transformers are not included. Additionally, only one year of sim┬¡
ulation is performed, so annual degradation of the PV modules is not considered. Finally,
auxiliary consumption from electrical assets are not included in the losses calculation.

Section 3.7. Calculation of PV Production
Choosing an diffuse isotropic transposition model indicates defining the diffuse radiation
as uniform in magnitude, independent of direction. This is a simplification, as the diffuse
radiation is seldom uniform. Diffuse radiation originates from direct sunlight being scat┬¡
tered in the atmosphere, dominantly by Rayleigh and Mie scattering effects. Rayleigh
scattering concerns particles in the atmosphere smaller than the wavelength of radiation,
which magnitude strongly depends on said wavelength. Mie scattering concerns larger
particles, such as aerosols and dust, which concentration depends on the location of
measurement. [56] Thus, polluted areas such as cities and industrial regions will have
more scattering. Another important factor for scattering is local differences in cloud cov┬¡
erage. Isotropic models are conservative and tends to slightly underestimate Gpoa but
are more easily implemented and understood. [55] Additionally, the final difference be┬¡
tween isotropic and anisotropic models are not too significant. The PVgis calculation of
Gpoa is performed with the use of an anisotropic model called Muneer [97]. This model
is credited with the best performance in a study by the European Solar Radiation Atlas in
2000 [97], but also shows low performance in scenarios of predominantly diffuse radiation
[112]. In comparison, the PVgis model for equal azimuth and tilt configuration as the Liu
and Jordan model of 1961, resulted in an average of 6.8% higher Gpoa when analysing
10 randomly chosen configurations. Similarly, the maximum calculated Gpoa was 4.41%
higher for Muneer.

4.1
Solving for Optimality
The data flow of the model is shown in Figure 3.1, where the optimization steps are per┬¡
formed in the Julia/JuMp language, executed by the commercial MIP solver Gurobi. Solv┬¡
ing a MIP optimization problem follows a few common optimization methods. This section
will describe some of the basic methods used in the Gurobi solver.
Prior to initiating other methods, the solver will try and simplify the MIP┬¡problem by locat┬¡
ing hard constraints that locks decision variables to a fixed value. These are identified
by combining the effect of all constraints and goes by the name problem presolve. An
example of presolving in this project would be defining all inclined sub┬¡variables with no
eligible available area as zero, thus reducing the total search range of possible solutions.
[113]
The next step is to relax the problem by imposing continuous linear domains on the non
presolved decision variables. These are then used to solve the objective function to op┬¡
timality. From the solution of this initial solve, the process called branch and bound can
begin. [113]
Based on the continuous decision variables of the relaxed solution, the solver imposes
constraints of each sub┬¡variable by defining a binary domain as the rounded up and down
integer value. This means the variable can either take the upper or lower value of the
domain, thus creating two paths. The collection of these paths are called a decision tree
with branches defining each decisive step. Each branch will create a new, increasingly
simplified, MIP┬¡problem, called a node, where the same steps can be repeated. Finally,
each branch deemed feasible will have the same number of nodes as there are sub┬¡
variables in the MIP┬¡problem. The first branch with integer values for all nodes, which
also fulfills all supplemented constraints, is the first feasible solution of the MIP┬¡problem.
[113]
Completing the first feasible branch iteration enables the solver a simplification method
to avoid complete exploration of every possible branch. If the relaxed solution of another
branch node is lower than the first feasible solution, that whole branch can be discarded
as secondary to the found solution. This is explained by the impossibility of the integral
solution being superior to the discovered relaxed solution. The current best solution is
known as the upper bound of the optimal solution, while the lower bound is the minimal of
the remaining branches left unexplored. When the difference between lower and upper
optimal bounds is zero, optimality is demonstrated. If the solver is unable to find any
branches fulfilling all requirements, the MIP┬¡problem is considered unfeasible. [113]
However, depending on the model, there are other methods of improving the search al┬¡
gorithm for optimal solutions. One example is the cutting planes technique. This method
supplements the objective with constraints found through logical combination of existing

constraints, thus making it more complex but also limits the feasible region. This will force
the optimal solution of the relaxed problem closer to the real integral solution. [113]
Another example is heuristics. One of the heuristics utilized by Gurobi is a more careful
investigation of certain branches in the decision tree, performed by identifying relaxed
variables relatively close to an integer value. This algorithm will force that integer value
upon the variable and restart the heuristics process for the consecutive node. The rea┬¡
soning behind this is that an integral solution relatively close to the relaxed solution is
likely to obtain a better optimal solution than other solutions with more ÔÇØdistanceÔÇØ from the
relaxation. This will make it easier to identify and, in turn, discard secondary branches.

A key feature of the branch and bound method is that the branches beyond the first node
are independent of eachother. Thus, they can be run simultaneously on different cpu┬¡

sion trees. All the above methods are used in solving for optimality in this project, including
additional methods not publicly described by the Gurobi documentation. [113]

Discussion
6.1
Considerations of Results and Model Limitations
Although the optimization of mixed orientations on self┬¡consumption showed promising
development in allowance of a certain degree of overproduction, the inclusion of economic
parameters in Scenario 2 showed that a great variety of configurations was less beneficial
when allowing for complete utilization of the location. Nevertheless, in comparison with
market standard configurations, a certain gain could be proved from a non┬¡uniform layout.
Scenario 1 and 2 were purely considered from an energy and economical standpoint, and
limitations in the modelling procedure introduces uncertainty in the realistic adaptation of
these results. The production performance in relation to the electrical impact of choos┬¡
ing mixed orientations was considered fixed and further analysis beyond the scope of the
project. This fixed value is unlikely to represent the full impact on the aggregated produc┬¡
tion and will have an impact on the results, not only in terms of energy, but also in terms of
economics through increased requirements for balancing of the system. This introduces
another limitation on the validity of the results, the scope boundary of economic impact
after procurement of the system elements. Increased rack price was introduced to reflect
on the additional costs of choosing mixed orientations, but inclusion of system costs would
likely show a different picture, amongst other based on the number of modules for SCP
depth and coverage of energy import expenses.
Another model limitation is the area usage. Both the inclined and flat area are completely
filled to capacity for several of the models. For the given constraints, filling the available
area to capacity is a natural move, however, this is often not the case for a realistic instal┬¡
lation. Depending on the location of the PV system, different considerations of available
area has to be made, including fire hazards, weight tolerance, and maintenance path┬¡
ways. These considerations, being location specific, was chosen to be left out because
of the general nature of the created model. The corresponding reduction in eligible avail┬¡
able area eligible for each building was not analysed, this includes the space requirement
of secondary components such as inverters.
6.2
Maximization of TIP
An attempt of obtaining a more general model for better analysis on the value of self┬¡
consumption was performed through the maximization of the TIP performance indicator.
This indicator showed a promising trend for discovering the optimal allowed overproduc┬¡
tion in Section 5.2.3. However, the resulting model, as shown in Appendix A Section A.2,
turned out to be too complex for the computer used for this simulation. The problem de┬¡
rives from the additional variables of the model, which totalled to more than 67 million
non┬¡zero values, thus reaching the memory limit of the computer. This section describes
and comments on the attempts performed in an attempt of bypassing the limitation of the
memory usage.
A known factor of high memory usage was the production dataset. To minimize the stor┬¡
age space of this set, the matrix was written to a hierarchical data format file through
the HDF5 [114] package of Julia to reduce the storage space to a minimum. This trans┬¡
formation makes the optimization model open the file and collecting the required infor┬¡
mation before closing it again, thus requiring little to none temporary memory. Although

glected to save memory space. One of these was found to be the paralellism of node

6.3
Framework Conditions for the KID Buildings
The existing legal framework for active customers in Norway is found limiting on the po┬¡
tential of utilizing the entire available production area by restricting the participation in
wholesale trading of energy and the peer to peer trading. The coming Norwegian legal
implementation of new possibilities in structure and market design for active customers
will provide additional opportunities for the utilization of roof area. The municipality owned
buildings of this project have the potential of taking an active role in the new framework
structures by utilizing the potential of undeveloped rooftop areas for PV production. A
secondary public service can be provided by participating in energy communities or ag┬¡
gregation schemes while still economically benefit in reduction of imported electricity and
revenues from produced energy. The results of the optimization model in this project
shows how mixed orientations can be accommodated to answer the needs of demand,
beyond and past the metering point.
KID fulfills the criteria of membership for both energy communities seen in Table 2.1 of
Section 2.3.1, however the way of defining local in REC might be disputed considering

participation all over Oslo. That being said, the CEC model shows promise in a future
energy structure for the facilities of KID. While the current legal framework prohibits non┬¡
centralized trading, a potentially economically beneficial scenario where one of the main
facilities adapts the coming possibilities and starts an energy cooperation in a CEC with
neighbouring residential homes or the numerous school buildings of the municipality scat┬¡
tered around Oslo. The location and size of the large buildings may utilize the rooftop area
for orientations and production quantities not available for smaller surrounding buildings,
thus facilitate a community wide reduction in energy import, which would not be available
on an individual scale. Completion of the legal framework of this is not concluded for
Norwegian adaptation, and the economic significance is hard to quantify. Yet, a scenario
can be imagined where the community shares expenses on system installation according
to consumption levels and collectively benefit from the production. The main buildings,
having relatively high consumption levels throughout the day, may benefit from the pro┬¡

7.1
Concluding Remarks
In this study, a view on the impact of mixed orientation in PV production is modelled with
public buildings in Oslo as a case study. The existing Norwegian and European framework
conditions of producing consumers is demonstrated and discussed. The development of
new energy cooperatives in the coming years has the potential to change the behaviour
of active customers and promote an increased renewable penetration in the energy mix.
The ownership structures of the analysed buildings was found to comply with additions
of market actors in the revised directives of the EU. The case study buildings can benefit
from the participation in citizen energy communities or aggregation schemes, depending
on the Norwegian adaptation of this legislation.
The modellings of the case study was compared with performance of market standard
installations, with promising results of the first scenario in increased self┬¡consumption
of mixed orientations when allowing more than 1.4% of overproduction from the annual
average consumption. This need for overproduction was found to originate from mismatch
of the production and consumption profiles, with high volatility in the daily demand of
the case location. Results implied an optimal level of allowed overproduction in relation
to self┬¡consumption that requires further investigation. The value of mixed orientations
proved to have a less significant impact in the second scenario when allowing unbounded
overproduction in the minimization of imported electricity cost. Results showed benefits of
supplementing the production profile of a flat installation with vertical eastern┬¡orientated
panels to increase production in the morning hours of high el┬¡spot price and consumption.
The additional expenses of inclined panels was included in a sensitivity analysis, showing
a critical point of reduced optimality on 4% increase in costs per tilt increment.
The third scenario was modelled on a wider perspective on the value of local production,
demonstrating how mixed orientations could benefit in total reduction of energy costs in
periods of high regional consumption with incentivised export. The results reported on
changes in the optimal configurations depending on the market conditions, but is specu┬¡
lated of demanding model additions for unlocking the full potential.
An overall consideration of the modelling results is inconclusive on the reported benefits of
mixed orientations, as limitations of the model prevents a concrete postulation. However,
these models demonstrates the groundwork for deeper analysis on the inclusive system
wide benefits and show valuable results on the potential of manipulating the production
profile, as opposed to market standard installations.

Section 7.2. Recommendation and Future Work
7.2
Recommendation and Future Work
Suggested areas for further exploration is as follows:
ÔÇó Inclusion of installation costs. Including a more realistic picture on the costs of
the whole system and prolonging the simulation period accordingly would provide
valuable insight of a more realistic impact on the value of mixed orientations. This
inclusion would likely show a different picture than the results of this project, amongst
other based on the number of modules for SCP depth, and should be considered in
a model adaptation of further work.
ÔÇó Production usage dispatch. Including a feature to the model which allows for
the choice of self┬¡consumption and export of energy could provide further insight
in the choice of the optimal configurations. A secondary objective of this model
advancement is earmarking production in aggregated scenarios, thus avoiding the
assumption of all included locations behind the same meter.
ÔÇó The global optimum of overproduction. Results of Scenario 1 implied an optimal
value of allowed overproduction in regards to the objective of minimizing energy
import. A deeper analysis on the self┬¡consumption of Scenario 1 could provide
valuable prospects for future projects of PV installations.
ÔÇó Maximization of TIP Another interesting outcome of the results of Scenario 1 was
the evolution of the TIP performance indicator across sensitivities. Further work on
the model in Appendix A.2 could provide beneficial results.
ÔÇó Accuracy of rack price. An accurate increase in expense for higher tilts was not
obtained in the process of this project due to unwillingness from the industry. The
real market value on the increase in costs for increased tilts would provide more
insight in the economic impact of mixed PV orientations. This objective of further
work should be researched in relation to the inclusion of installation costs, as the
rack price would be included in system procurement and likely be deprecated over
its lifetime.
ÔÇó Electrical impact. Inclusion of electrical parameters and corresponding costs for
different system layouts would provide a deeper understanding of the technical im┬¡
pact of mixed PV orientations.
ÔÇó Ground coverage ratio. Another inclusion which should be considered in a model
adaptation is the ground coverage ratio depending on system size. Thus avoiding
the assumption of eligible usage of the entire area.
