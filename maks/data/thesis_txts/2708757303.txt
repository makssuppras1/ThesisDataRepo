Abstract
Mitigation of CO2 emissions is a key task when considering the global warming threat.
Lately, CCUS (Carbon Capture Utilisation and Storage) sector is gaining centrality
and proves to be an interesting research topic for the future. Within this, Power to
X represent a new trend in this challenge offering possibilities to convert chemicals
using electricity input from renewable sources mitigating emissions and improving
recycling of products in the plants.
This report investigate the development of a
Power-to-Methanol plant, assessing its performances offering possible integration sce-
narios with existing facilities, specifically a Carbon Capture and HOFOR District
Heating plant installed at Amager Resource Center (ARC) WtE facility in Copen-
hagen, Denmark.
After modelling the methanol synthesis plant using Aspen Plus, four different
scenarios have been developed considering different combinations within the three
plants analysed in this work. Different strategies for waste heat recovery have been
implemented and investigated using the Energy Analyser tool inside the software,
comparing composite curves between the scenarios. Finally a sensitivity analysis on
the recycle rate value has been performed, observing the behavior of the plant when
varying the recycling of the feedstocks.
Results show that the developed plant can reach a high conversion rate that can be
further improved by increasing the recycle rate. Moreover, the implementation within
the three plants considered is proven to be feasible and useful when trying to recover
the waste heat generated during the process. Investigation on technologies shows
that lowering forward temperature in the District Heating or shifting to a LTDH or a
ULTDH will improve this performances and increase the quantity of District Heating
generated by the plant while having no need for external heating.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This work represent a study in Power-to-X sector, within CCUS (Carbon Capture,
Utilization and Storage). In this case, the aim is to develop a methanol synthesis
plant, operating via CO2 direct hydrogenation process, as an alternative to the more
developed and popular CAMERE process.
The main part of the study, more than exclusively assessing the performances of
the plant, is intended to evaluate different integration possibilities with existing or
”under-development” facilities, as HOFOR District Heating or Carbon Capture sys-
tem examined in [1], considered as a core literature for this thesis. By introducing the
integration within different energy plants, the purpose is to theorize a combination
of power plant requiring net-zero energy consumption that will help in the future to
reduce costs implied in this development.
Hypothesis
Considering the purpose of developing an alternative Methanol Synthesis plant
operating through direct CO2 hydrogenation, evaluating different waste heat recovery
and utilization strategies within different plants assessing that the plant developed
is conceptually able to be integrated usefully helping a net-zero energy consumption
environment, this overall hypothesis has been formulated:
Developing a methanol synthesis plant through CO2 direct hydrogenation will be re-
vealed to have comparable performances to the actual systems used and will be found
a valuable and adaptable plant when talking about waste heat recovery and overall
emission and power consumption reduction.
Purpose of the project is to furnish elements that can help to prove this hypothesis.

2.1
Introduction on Power to X
Energy scenario nowadays is characterized by focusing efforts on Green House Gases
abatement, that are considered one of the main responsible for global warming and
one of the major threats to climate change and most of the countries worldwide
have to cope with the big challenge of reducing emissions before 2030 to fight this
phenomenon. At the same time, as predicted by International Energy Agency in [2],
energy demand is going to increase by 50% and nowadays, energy sector is responsible
for 78% of GHG emissions [3]. So there is a urgent need to find a solution to cover
the rising demand reducing waste and emissions, trying to make production more
eﬀicient introducing the concept of recycling materials while spreading electrification
of end users.
At the same time, use of renewable source for electricity production is becoming
fundamental to replace carbon-based methods trying to offer a more sustainable so-
lution while covering the demand despite their fluctuating supply. In this scenario,
Power-to-X technologies are gaining space offering the possibility to convert electric-
ity as primary energy carrier into a secondary product like heat, fuel or chemicals
that can be utilized, stored or transported more readily.
Power input can be taken both from carbon-based processes, making integration with
existing facilities easier to happen, but also from RES processes, creating a sustain-
able environment that allows an higher share of GHG emissions reduction.
Usually PtX technologies, involves the use of chemical processes base on hydrogen, due
to its high energy content LHVH2 = 119.9 MJ
kg (more than double than methane) [4],

2.2
Introduction on Amager Bakke WtE facility and Carbon
Capture Project at ARC
Amager Bakke WtE plant in Copenhagen is composed of two boiler lines with the
same capacity of 35 tMSW /h, integrated with two District Heating networks, respec-
tively HOFOR DH and CTR DH. Figure 2.1 depicts an overview of the plant and its
connections.
Figure 2.1. Schematic of ARC WtE facility from [1]
Integration with District Heating networks in the plant is realised spilling steam
directly from turbines. Respectively HOFOR DH has a nominal production of 81.4
MJ/s, while CTR DH realises a larger value, receiving more heat. This difference is
due to the temperatures considered in the networks. Both utilise a return temperature
of 50°C, but while HOFOR DH considers a forward temperature of 90°C, CTR DH
uses a value between 105 and 107.5°C, that allows an higher heat streams collection.
However, plan for 2030 is to decrease these values to 45/85 for HOFOR DH and 45/95
for CTR DH [1].

glomerate consisting of Amager Resource Center (ARC), Technical University of Den-

ture at Amager Bakke WtE facility. This has been found responsible to emit 480000

2.3.1
Water Electrolysis
Production of hydrogen is a key process to run Power-to-X plant and to produce
chemicals. Conventional production of hydrogen is done by water electrolysis that
involves the use of water and an electricity input to split the molecule into hydro-
gen and oxygen. Main technologies used for this purpose are Alkaline Electrolysis
(AE), Proton Exchange Membrane (PEM) and Solid Oxide Electrolysis (SOE). While
the latter two still encounter development problems and economic disadvantages, in
terms of feasibility and initial investment, the alkaline electrolysis is considered as a
conventional low emitting method for short terms hydrogen production [9].
Lonis et al. [10] compared use of AE to SOE for Power-to-Methanol production.
No relevant differences has been found in eﬀiciencies, while the main gap is rep-
resented by the purchasing costs related to development of technologies, attesting
values equal to 1000-1200 €/kW for Alkaline Electrolysis and 2000 €/kW for Solid
Oxide Electrolysis. Purchasing electricity from renewable sources, will also allow AE
to realize an high effective large production [11], confirming its status as preferable
technology for hydrogen production in a methanol synthesis plant, being suitable for
a wide spectrum of installed power [5].
Considering renewable sources, Rivarolo et al. [12] compared the use of different
origins to power electrolysis. With currently methanol selling prices, only hydro power
is a feasible sources for the process, mainly due to the stochastic nature of wind and
solar power. In fact, inconstant production of electricity would generate problems in
the components, reducing lifetime of the AE (with frequent on/off), causing thermal
stress to the methanol reactor. However, in the future, with the possible increase of
demand and prices, also wind and solar power would become feasible energy sources
for the plant.

2.3.2
Methanol Synthesis
Considering methanol production from hydrogen, there are mainly two routes consid-
ered for this purpose [13]:
• Two steps conversion, where CO2 is converted into CO and then react with
Hydrogen to synthesize methanol (CAMERE process)
• One step conversion, where CO2 reacts directly with hydrogen producing methanol
and water (CO2 hydrogenation)
Marlin et al. [14] compared these two paths investigating on advantages and disad-
vantages that lead them, as this work, to evaluate usage of the direct hydrogenation
process for methanol synthesis observing the CRI plant operating in Iceland since
2012, with a capacity of 4000 tons/year. Two steps conversion is globally identified
as the conventional route for it compactness. However, this system is rich of disad-
vantages, mainly from an environmental point of view. In the conventional process,
methanol is produced from a ”syngas” (composed by a mixture of CO2, CO and
H2) derived from gasified coal or natural gas through the Reverse Water Gas Shift
This lead to formation of heavy by-products during the reaction, as Ethanol, DME,
Ketones and Acetone. To separate these from methanol in order to extract the final
product, the plant has to include several expensive components for the distillation.
However, as some of this elements has a lower boiling point than methanol products,
the separation from crude methanol is diﬀicult to be realized and usually generates
emissions leads to a loss in methanol production. Moreover, conventional path present
an high exothermic reaction occurring, so that there is a need for a coolant system
to mitigate the temperatures.
On the other hand, the direct hydrogenation of CO2 presents clear advantages when
compared to the use of syngas. Despite being less reactive and consequently using
larger reactors, this route ”simplify” the chemistry involved to only two reactions:
3H2 + CO2 −→CH3OH + 2H2O
3H2 + CO2 −→CO + H2O
This means that the spectrum of by-products is way less wider than the con-
ventional route, that makes the separation process easier and achievable utilising a
distillation column [14]. Using hydrogenation route lead to a better carbon utilisation
than conventional, because CO2 and H2 are added in separate streams, so the stoi-
chiometric ratio can be adjusted to the needs and based on the catalyst requirements
in the reactor. Furthermore, unconverted gases can be recycled into the reactor, giv-
ing a major opportunity to mitigate the emissions of the plant. Leonzio et al. [15]
investigated this possibility, comparing three different layouts: once-through reactor,
==PAGE:22==
8
Background and motivation
recycle reactor and membrane reactor with water. Results showed that the recycle
reactor has the highest eﬀiciency (69%) with an higher possible conversion of CO2.
Previous model have been developed in [13] and [16] using Aspen Plus consider-
ing direct CO2 hydrogenation and are used as inspiration for this work. Comparing
plant sizes, the findings shows that increasing the plant size results in lower opera-
tional costs mainly affected by the material purchase (86%) [16].
Common opinion is that developing direct hydrogenation process for methanol
production using Hydrogen from RES will result in using more CO2 than emitting
and will help to keep the technology competitive for it future development [16].

At the same time, in the lower part, CO2 feed (stream S7) is taken from the

3.1.1.2
Methanol reactor
Methanol reactor layout is shown in Figure 3.3 For this work, an adiabatic reactor
(REquil) operating at 210°C is used, considering a pressure drop of 2 bar during the
process. The feed is heated in an heat exchanger (component B2) before entering the
reactor. In the reactor, elements are combined together and two reactions take place.
The exothermic (∆Hr = -49.2 kJ/mol) CO2 hydrogenation and the Water Gas Shift

(WGS) reaction:
3H2 + CO2 −→CH3OH + 2H2O
3H2 + CO2 −→CO + H2O
Considering stechiometric values, the reactor is fed with a ratio 3:1 between H2
and CO2 respectively to represent the ideal reaction and maximize the conversion.
Products stream is then condensed in a two step heat exchanger (B5 and B4) and
pass trough a gas liquid separator that divide the product stream. On the bottom
part (stream S25) the crude methanol, composed by a mix of methanol and water,
continues its path to the distillation column. On the upper part (stream 11), the
un-reacted gas are collected and the recycle stream is mixed with the reactor feed
after being compressed back to 80 bars. This loop is useful to recover part the gas
that didn’t participate in the reaction, increasing the total conversion of the plant
and decreasing the waste of the products from the plant. From the recycle stream,
about 2% of the gases are purged to prevent the buildup of inert gases.

3.1.1.3
Methanol distillation
The last part of the plant is composed by the distillation process. It occurs in two
subsequent distillation columns operating at different pressure. Both are modelled in
the software using a RadFrac component.
For the first distillation column (B14), an operating pressure of 8 bars is considered.
It consists in a 10 stages distillation column that handles the liquid stream (S25)
coming from the gas-liquid separator (B11) after it is depressurized in a valve (B21)
and then heated to 60°C in an heat exchanger (B24). It separates the crude methanol
(mainly composed by methanol and water) from the other gases that are present in

The last distillation column (B15) handles the crude methanol (stream S14) and

4.2.1
Scenario 2
Scenario 2, as previously presented, explore the possibility of integrating the methanol
synthesis plant modelled in this thesis, with the Carbon Capture plant analyzed in
the reference [1] by Ebbe Hauge Jensen. This is done by channeling the excess heat
from the electrolyzer to the reboiler as shown in 3 section.
In this scenario, electrolysis is performed at higher temperature to promote the
integration with the reboiler of the CO2 capture plant. The excess heat from the
AE slightly decrease to 4601 kW. Considering that this value is referred to a plant
operating with 6.4% of the carbon dioxide effectively available from the CC plant,
this value have been scaled up to compare the two facilities. Our input is equal to
300 kmol/h of H2 and 100 kmol/h of CO2. Converting it into a mass flow, their
values are, respectively 605 kg/h and 4401 kg/h. Comparing the CO2 used in this
work with the reference data from [1], the value used is equal to 6.4% of the reference.
Considering a CO2 input of 19.12 kg/s (=1564 kmol/h), this will require an H2
feed stock of 9459,072 kg/h (=4692 kmol/h) with respect to the stoichiometric ratio
of the reactions. Calculating the new electricity input for the alkaline electrolysis
using formulas [17]:
Elin = 47.99kWh/kgH2 · 9459.072kgH2/h = 453940kW = 454MW
(4.1)
Eout = LHVH2 · ˙mH2 = 120MJ/s · 9459.072kgH2/h = 315302kW = 315MW
(4.2)
Equation 4.1 refers to the new electrical input needed by the electrolysis while
Equation 4.2 evaluate the new chemical output derived from the hydrogen produced.
Using the new results to scale up our model, the value for electrolysis utilizing the
actual CO2 input has been found equal to 73.2 MW. This value indicate that using all
the input available from the carbon capture plant, the excess heat from the Alkaline
Electrolyzer is almost able to cover the demand of the reboiler.

CHAPTER 5
Discussion
Observing the results given in Table 4.1, considering the input CO2 of 100 kmol/h,
methanol synthesis plant is able to produce 94.92 kmol/h of methanol, using the dis-
cussed method for Scenario 1. However, due to the low conversion of the CO2 direct
hydrogenation process, the un-converted gases represent a big part of the reaction
products. In a once-through process, this quantity is too high to be left unnoticed,
and in order to keep an high conversion and to avoid the waste of this valuable stream,
the recycle adopted represent an essential method. What makes this stream valuable
is its composition. As observed in the above mentioned table, the recycle flow is
composed at 94% by CO2 and H2, that represent also the input feed in the reactor.
By compressing it, restoring the pressure and mixing it again with the reactants, help
the reactor to save a big quantity of input flows.
As it may be seen in Table 4.3 and Table 4.2, major impact on the energy balance
in the plant is given by the excess heat from the electrolyzer, that makes available
an high quantity of heat at high pressure and temperature and for this reason it is
considered to be the the main and only contribution to the integration with Carbon
Capture plant theorized in Scenario 2. Other important gain in energy can be visible
in the Heat Exchanger Network, but due to the different temperatures detected, this
heat is mostly useful when integrating the plant with the District Heating. A power
input is needed to run the electrolyzer and the compressors along the plant, totally
accounted by 30 MW for the designed facility. Considering only the methanol synthe-
sis plant, the composite curves helped the preliminary analysis, giving the possibility
to assess the potentials for available heat and supporting the integration scenarios
developed.
Results for the integration scenarios suggests that the integration theorized could
be supported by the designed methanol synthesis plant. In fact, raising the AE oper-
ative temperature to facilitate the combination with the CC plant doesn’t affect the
excess heat on a relevant level with a total decrease equal to 1.6%. This helps the
union of the three plants, so that it can be seen how the methanol synthesis facility
reacts well to this kind of different possibilities. Lowering the forward temperature
has been found as a valuable method to increase the utilisation of waste heat along
the plant. Leaving S17 stream aside the analysis for the full integration scenario 4,
acting on the DH forward temperature result in an higher level of District Heating
generated. Although this result doesn’t consider the CHP contribution, that is used
==PAGE:58==

to cover the remaining heat demand of the DH, needed to increase the temperature
of the stream to 85°C. Shifting to a LTDH and ULTDH, as can be seen in results for
scenario 4b and 4c, is useful to a further raise in the District Heating generation and
no noteworthy difference was found between the two structures regarding this value.
Sensitivity analysis performed shows that an increase in the Recycle Rate con-
sidered for the plant can be considered as an improvement. As observed in Results
section, rising the RR will increase the overall conversion rate of the plant while
decreasing the percentage of CO2 in the purge gas (and consequently in the recycle
loop), leading to less emissions. At the same time, the 27% increase in the recycle
stream does not represent a problem for the plant that can handle this growth. A 5%
increase in the methanol conversion will also lead to an increase in the production op-
portunity, making the plant more attractive, also because the composite curve shows
possibilities for integration within Carbon Capture and DH facilities also using the
new Recycle Rate.
Results of this report suggests that, an integration within the three plants is
feasible under an energetic point of view.
The exothermic nature of the reaction
happening in the system, as well for the excess heat from the electrolyzer, makes room
for connections and different possibilities for heat usage. Considering the methanol
plant itself, a recycle reactor structure is preferable among the others as the un-reacted
gases still contain a huge amount of feed stocks that can be still used and recovered
for further conversion. Regarding the integration with the Carbon Capture plant,
it represent a feasible solution using the excess heat coming from the electrolyzer.
However, in this work, the operative temperature of the AE is considered as 140°C,
that, even if still in the range of possible temperatures, will lead to decreasing lifetime
of the technology and consequently to a greater expense from the economic point of
view for the components used in the system. Further integration with District Heating
reveal to be useful as it gives possibility for using a large amount of the available heat
in the system. Lowering forward temperature leads to benefits because widen the
range of heat source for the integration, increasing the quantity of DH generated.
5.1
Future work
As a newly developed technology, this study represent a theoretical overview of pos-
sible scenarios that has to be developed and studied in the next years. Considering
the integration scenarios studied, it would be interesting and valuable to evaluate
the actual gain in terms of energy that can be achieved by the plants where this
facility is installed. Evaluation could be performed on varying single components in
the plant. For example using a different reactor or considering another technology for
water electrolysis, following the status of current research. Shifting to a Low Temper-
ature District Heating has demonstrated to have beneficial effects on the plant and
represent a future improvement to the integration.

CHAPTER 6
Conclusion
This thesis work investigated performances of a methanol synthesis plant through
direct CO2 hydrogenation and its integration with a Carbon Capture and a District
Heating at ARC WtE facility in Copenhagen, Denmark. First, an assessment of the
chemical and energetic process of the single plant were investigated, giving a picture
of the overall performances. Then, starting from these results, four different scenarios
were developed, changing integration strategies within the plants. Every scenario is
then further investigated changing the temperature considered in the District Heating
system, using composite curves tool to help with the analysis.
The plant layout, also considering the exothermic nature of the reactions occur-
ring, offers integration possibilities using the large quantity of waste heat available
from the process. The Alkaline Electrolyzer implemented for hydrogen production is
revealed to give the biggest contribution in terms of heat and due to its high tem-
perature is been found suitable for integration with Carbon Capture plant. Further
District Heating implementation has revealed as a good strategy to collect and direct
every other heat contributions with acceptable performances.
Investigation in Scenarios 3 and 4, demonstrate that lowering temperature utilised
in the District Heating, keeping constant the contribution given to che carbon capture
plant, will favor the integration, being able to collect more heat from the methanol
plant reducing waste. Shifting to a different DH facility, implementing a LTDH or
a ULTDH, has been found as a further improvement in the performances of the in-
tegration, and the plant was able to generate the highest level of power useful for DH.
The project hypothesis detailed in chapter 1 is supported by the findings of this
thesis work. Methanol synthesis plant developed, considering CO2 direct hydrogena-
tion has proved to have acceptable performances in terms of conversion rate and is
suitable for integration with different facilities. Emissions in the plant are considered
to have origin only from the electricity input, if taken from a carbon-based source,
as the CO2 used as a feed stock is mostly converted in the reaction thanks to the
recycling of the un-reacted gases implemented in the project. Utilising renewable
sources to provide the necessary electricity input to run the plant will bring a further
reduction in the emission, leading to a net zero scenario.
The sensitivity analysis performed gives interesting results on the recycle rate
investigation. Pushing the recycle rate to the upper value of 99.9% is revealed to be
==PAGE:62==
48
Conclusion
feasible and still leave possibilities of integration with considered technologies, even
if with lower performances.
Based on this results it may be concluded that developing a methanol synthesis
plant based on CO2 direct hydrogenation has revealed to be a reliable technology
regarding energy and chemical point of view, proving flexibility when integrated with
other plants and for a large waste heat recovery and utilisation strategy.
