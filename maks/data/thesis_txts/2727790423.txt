Abstract
In response to the growing complexity and sophistication of cyber threats, this thesis emôÙ
barks on a detailed study of machine learning and its application in network intrusion
detection. This exploration is anchored by an exhaustive analysis of 220 scholarly artiôÙ
cles, published between 2000 and 2023. This study investigates the crucial aspects of
network security threats and the role of machine learning in combating them. We dissect
the influences of dataset choice and their temporal relevance, delve into the effectiveness
of various evaluation metrics, and draw a comparison between traditional machine learnôÙ
ing and deep learning techniques. Acknowledging the challenges posed by high falseôÙ
positive rates and intensive computational resource requirements, this work emphasizes
the constant need for innovative approaches in the dynamic landscape of cybersecurity.
Our aim is to shed light on potential areas for future research, thereby enhancing the efôÙ
ficiency and robustness of network intrusion detection systems, and setting the stage for
future advancements in this critical domain.

Introduction
In the everôÙexpanding realm of the Internet, where individuals and businesses engage in
a multitude of daily transactions, a profound transformation has taken place. The COVIDôÙ
19 pandemic has acted as a catalyst, propelling society deeper into the digital landscape.
However, alongside this digital revolution, traditional crimes have found a new breeding
ground, orchestrated by cyber criminals who meticulously exploit network vulnerabilities
in a coordinated fashion, posing an imminent threat to our interconnected world [1]. InôÙ
terestingly enough, over 2,200 cyber attacks occur every day, at a staggering pace of
roughly one attack every 39 seconds [2]. These contemporary threats have evolved into
highly sophisticated and pernicious adversaries, capable of inflicting widespread damage
on both private organizations and public institutions. With the rise of cloud services, smartôÙ
phones, and the Internet of Things, the complexity of defending against cyber attacks has
reached unprecedented levels, rendering conventional protective measures such as fireôÙ
walls, intrusion detection systems, antivirus software, and access control lists obsolete.
As a result, there is an urgent need to develop innovative strategies aimed at developing
practical solutions to combat these persistent and dynamic cyber threats, ensuring the
security and resilience of our interconnected digital landscape [3, 4, 5].
Researchers have long relied on traditional intrusion detection systems (IDSs) as a corôÙ
nerstone of cybersecurity defenses. IDSs, also referred to as Network Intrusion DetecôÙ
tion (NIDS) systems, are specialized applications that analyze network traffic or requests
to identify intrusive activities, including unauthorized access, abuse, and vulnerabilities
originating from internal and external sources. Initially introduced in 1980, IDSs have
undergone significant evolution, but they continue to face challenges. One significant
disadvantage is the persistently high rate of false alarms, which can overwhelm security
analysts with nonthreatening alerts and potentially lead to the neglect of severe attacks.
Additionally, existing IDSs frequently have limited capacity to detect unknown attacks that
emerge on a regular basis in the dynamic network landscape [6, 7, 8].
In response to the aforementioned concerns, researchers have shifted their focus to the
development of intrusion detection systems that make use of machine learning techôÙ
niques. Machine learning algorithms endow computer applications with the capacity to
acquire knowledge from historical network traffic, enabling them to detect modified or
previously unknown attack patterns within network traffic [9]. While machine learning
techniques are used to identify and categorize various network attacks, the effectiveness
of them has not yet reached the desired level. Furthermore, many existing solutions
demonstrate limited efficiency when applied to largeôÙscale datasets, posing challenges in
effectively processing comprehensive data.Furthermore, an extensive amount of research
in the field has relied significantly on outdated databases. It is worth noting that certain
frequently employed datasets were derived from network traffic as early as 1998. As a
consequence, these datasets fail to capture the dynamic nature and intricate complexities
inherent in modern cyber threats [10, 11, 12].
Notably, within the realm of machine learning, deep learning has recently emerged as a
category of immense interest within the research community. This emerging paradigm
excels at handling large datasets, allowing the identification of optimal feature sets and
improving the classification of lowôÙfrequency attacks beyond the capabilities of alternative
techniques. Nevertheless, the incorporation of deep learning methodologies in Network

Research Method
This section outlines the technique for choosing literature for this paper. An exhaustive
search for relevant studies and articles in the subject was conducted, followed by a careôÙ
ful selection of publications based on their relevance and significance to the research
questions.
The guidelines in [14, 15] were followed to achieve a thorough and complete evaluaôÙ
tion. These guidelines describe how to conduct a systematic literature review and how to
choose publications for inclusion using snowball sampling.
To identify relevant literature on the topic of detecting malicious logs, an exhaustive search
was carried out across several databases and sources such as ACM Web of Science,
IEEE Xplore, Scopus, and Google Scholar. The search phrases utilized were all pertinent
to the subject matter.
After obtaining the search results, a preôÙscreening procedure was carried out to exclude
irrelevant research and articles. The type of article and the studyãs relevance to the reôÙ
search topics were among the preôÙscreening factors.
The remaining studies and publications were then submitted to a fullôÙtext assessment,
in which the abstract, introduction, and conclusion were assessed for relevance to the
research topics.
Finally, a final selection of papers for inclusion in the literature review was determined
based on the findings of the fullôÙtext review. The articles chosen were thought to be the
most relevant and crucial to the research topics, and they were utilised to offer a thorough
review of the present state of knowledge on the issue of detecting cyber threats in Network
Traffic Logs.

ãÂ Population: The subject matter for all four research questions (RQ1, RQ2, RQ3,
RQ4, RQ5, RQ6) is centered around the expansive realm of detection of malicious
intrusion. This encapsulates a wide array of both existing and evolving strategies,
methodologies, and techniques engaged in detecting cyber threats within diverse

ãÂ Intervention: The main intervention is around the use of machine learning techôÙ

ãÂ Comparison: We embark on an inôÙdepth examination of diverse machine learning

ãÂ Outcomes: The efficacy and efficiency of various tactics and approaches utilised

Limitations of KDDCUP99 dataset
Several critiques from the academic community have highlighted notable limitations in the
dataset that could potentially affect the validity of Intrusion Detection System (IDS) assessôÙ
ments. One specific limitation is the presence of redundant records within the training set.
Duplicates introduce biases, affecting the overall performance and accuracy of intrusion
detection models. Careful analysis is necessary to account for the influence of duplicates
on outcomes. Additionally, the dataset suffers from missing records, disrupting its charôÙ
acteristics and creating information gaps. Researchers should acknowledge these gaps,
as they impact data reliability and completeness. Furthermore, a limitation involves the
discrepancy in TTL values. Absence of certain TTL values poses challenges for intrusion
detection systems, potentially affecting their effectiveness in realôÙworld scenarios [69].
Despite these limitations, it is essential to note that the KDDCup99 dataset continues to
be a valuable resource due to its extensive use in historical studies. Consequently, it
provides a useful point of reference for understanding the evolution and progress in the
field of network intrusion detection.

Conclusion: The Influence of Dataset Age and Timeliness on NIDS
Addressing the posed research question, it is unequivocal that the age and recency of
datasets significantly influence the proficiency of Network Intrusion Detection Systems
(NIDS) in combating the perpetually evolving landscape of cyber threats. The rapid deôÙ

sulating contemporary threat types for the construction of robust NIDS. Consequently,
datasets with significant age might fall short in providing pertinent insights regarding emergôÙ
ing cyber threats, thereby diminishing their effectiveness in training NIDS to detect and
mitigate such advanced threats.

like KDDCupã99 and NSLKDD from 1998, albeit widely deployed, are deficient in terms of

ENISA identifies these as common attacks, their inadequate inclusion in these datasets
could potentially impede their applicability in fabricating NIDS adept at effectively counôÙ
tering the evolving cyber threats.
However, this assertion should not undervalue the significance of the older datasets. They
continue to provide an invaluable resource for comprehending the historical progression of
cyber threats and offer benchmarking value. Numerous studies acknowledge that despite
their understanding of the limitations of KDDôÙCUP99 and NSLôÙKDD in reflecting realôÙworld
scenarios, they continue to use these due to limited research using more realistic datasets
[91].
RealôÙworld network data flows pose a notable challenge in acquisition due primarily to

the development and public availability of datasets that are upôÙtoôÙdate, representational
of modern threats, and equipped to evolve in tandem with the dynamically changing landôÙ
scape of cyber threats. This will facilitate the creation of effective machine learning techôÙ

EnsembleôÙBased studies

Exploring Auto encoders (AE) in Network Intrusion Dection Systems
Autoencoders (AEs) are increasingly applied in Network Intrusion Detection Systems

Stacked Autoencoders (SAEs)
Sparse Autoencoders (SAEs) have gained considerable attention in the field of Network
Intrusion Detection Systems (NIDS) due to their exceptional classification capabilities. A
study conducted by Javaid et al. [190] demonstrated the potential of SAEs in addressing
the limitations associated with limited data in NIDS. The results revealed that SAEs, when
combined with SelfôÙTaught Learning (STL), exhibited promising performance in learning
informative feature representations from unlabeled data. This effectiveness was observed
in both 2ôÙclass and 5ôÙclass classification scenarios, underscoring the value of SAEs in
NIDS.
Subsequent investigations have further explored the potential of Sparse Autoencoders
(SAEs) in enhancing network anomaly detection accuracy. Dinh et al. [191] demonstrated
the effectiveness of employing SAEs in a deôÙnoising approach, leading to substantial imôÙ
provements in detection performance. In a similar vein, Khan et al. [192] proposed the
TSDL method, which leverages a stacked autoencoder with a softmax classifier on unlaôÙ
beled data, resulting in high recognition rates for both traditional and emerging intrusion
attacks. Moreover, Choi et al. [193] and Yan et al. [194] highlighted the effectiveness of
SAEs in scenarios involving a mixture of normal and abnormal traffic data, thanks to their
capacity to capture intricate patterns. However, Yan et al. [194] encountered challenges
in effectively detecting lowôÙfrequency attack samples (R2L and U2R), emphasizing the
difficulties arising from imbalanced data distributions.
Furthermore other technique have also showed SAE promise in handling the challange
with challange with unlabeled data such such as SpAEs have shown promise in handling

Denoising Autoencoders (DAEs)
Denoising Autoencoders (DAEs) have proven to be invaluable tools in the context of NetôÙ
work Intrusion Detection Systems (NIDS), particularly when faced with the challenge of
limited labeled data. A notable study by [196] employed a Deep Neural Network (DNN)
architecture integrated with DAEs to perform unsupervised preôÙtraining and feature exôÙ
traction. The results surpassed those of other deep learning methods, demonstrating
superior classification metrics on the CICIDS2018 dataset. Building upon this success,

Conversely, deep learning techniques, encompassing MLP, CNN, RNN, LSTM, BiôÙLSTM,

Noteworthy trends in this area include an increasing inclination towards hybrid deep learnôÙ

This comparative analysis offers a solid foundation for comprehending the contrasting

7.5.2
Major Challenges and New Approaches in Network Intrusion
Detection Systems
In response to Research Question 5, this section discusses the significant challenges
faced by existing NIDS and proffers possible solutions. Noteworthy obstacles involve
issues of scalability, computational complexity, false alarm rates, and attackôÙtype speciôÙ
ficity. These are further complicated by the need to process highôÙdimensional data and
to adapt to dynamically shifting realôÙworld conditions.
Here, we delineate these challenges:
1. Computational resource requirements: Deep learning models such as the Big
Databased Hierarchical Deep Learning System (BDHDLS) often necessitate subôÙ
stantial computational resources to realize their optimal performance. This imposes
constraints concerning scalability and resource availability, potentially hampering
their effective implementation.

2. Lengthy training process: A comparative study of stacked RBMs and AutoenôÙ

3. Dependence on labeled data: Machine learning techniques, including deep learnôÙ
