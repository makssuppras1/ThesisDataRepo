Abstract  
Due to their fast growth microalgae appear to have great potential for producing renewable 
energy and therefore reduce world dependencies on fossil fuels and mitigate greenhouse gas 
(GHG) emission. Uneconomic production is identified as the major obstacle for 
commercialization of algal biodiesel.  
In order to improve the sustainability of microalgae production, different attempts were made 
to improve the productivity, wastewaters from the Kohtla-Järve (KJ) were assessed for their 
potential as medium for microalgae growth and the biomass generated was characterized in 
order to determine the content of high added value products and offset the production costs. 
This biorefinery approach coupled with wastewater treatment constitutes an innovative strategy 
for improving the economics of the entire concept. The overall economics of wastewater-based 
algae production will be assessed based on multiple products that can potentially be obtained 
from the biomass as well as the performance in removing nutrients in wastewater.  
Microplate screening between two freshwater algal strains, Chlorella sorokiniana and 
Scenedesmus obliquus showed more robust growth of C. sorokiniana in two cycles of 
cultivation in the KJ wastewater. An attempt to improve biomass productivity of C. sorokiniana 
was made by combining mutagenesis and adaptive evolution. Unfortunately, repeated 
cultivation in wastewater in microplate for 15 generations did not show any improvement in 
growth rate.  
Continuous cultivation of C. sorokiniana with wastewater was carried out in a plat panel 
photobioreactor with constant light supply and controlled temperature. Biomass productivity 
was examined at four dilution rates (4.32 d-1, 3.6 d-1, 1.8 d-1 and 0.72 d-1). Due to either low cell 
density or low flow rate, the extreme conditions exhibited low biomass productivities. Based 
on the fitting curve obtained from four measured points, the productivity peak was estimated 
to be 1.52 g l-1d-1 at the dilution rate of 2.41 d-1. Nutrient removals, except for COD, were found 
to be inversely proportional to dilution rates.  
The highest removal efficiencies for nitrogen (94.2%) and phosphorus (82.7%) were observed 
at the lowest dilution rate (0.72 d-1). By contrast, the consumption of COD was constant and 
independent of dilution rate. This is probably because a fraction of organics in the wastewater 
was not degradable by microalgae.  
Cellular compositions of the biomass obtained at the highest dilution rate were determined for 
lipids, protein and pigments. The average yields of FAME, protein, lutein, chlorophylls and β-
carotene were 62.4 mg, 388.2 mg, 1.03 mg, 11.82 mg and 0.44 mg, respectively. Furthermore, 
pigment yields at three dilution rates (4.32 d-1, 3.6 d-1 and 1.8 d-1) did not show significant 
change. 
The economic potential analysis revealed that more than 80% of revenue could be obtained 
from the production of pigments, i.e. chlorophyllin (53%) and lutein (10%) as well as from 
reduction in discharging cost of the treated wastewaters (23%). These resulted to be the primary 
profits to pursue. Nevertheless, due to the low yield of FAME and the low market price of 
biodiesel, this specific revenue resulted to be the least profitable (1%).  
Even when taking into account all these different revenues combined, this cultivation strategy 
==PAGE:4==
3 
 
resulted to be uneconomical. The maximum potential annual revenue was estimated to be 75.4 
million EURO, which however was much lower than the production cost (170.6 million EURO) 
that took in consideration only basic utilities. Power consumption for artificial light is 
responsible for the majority of production cost. Sensitivity analysis highlighted the necessity to 
avoid the use of artificial light, which could reduce the production cost by 94%. This was the 
only way to ensure a positive economic potential.

Chapter 1: Introduction 
1.1.Background  
At present, the global economy strongly relies on fossil fuels (i.e. petroleum, coal and natural 
gas) for chemicals, energy and materials. However, fossil fuels derived from ancient biomass 
deposit are non-renewable in short-term, whilst energy consumption is expected to grow with 
the increasing population and expanding economy (Hannon, Gimpel et al. 2010). In addition, 
climate change resulted from the increasing atmospheric concentration of greenhouse gases 
(GHG) is evidently related to the use of fossil fuels (Mann, Bradley et al. 1999). Recent 
concerns about climate change and diminishing oil reserves motivate the increasing demand 
for replacement of oil with renewable raw material for fuel and chemical production (Greenwell, 
Laurens et al. 2009). 
Renewable energy can be derived from various sources, such as biomass, direct solar, 
geothermal, hydropower, ocean as well as wind energy. As reported by IPCC (Figure 1), 12.9% 
of world primary energy supply in 2008 was renewable, while biomass (10.2%) was the largest 
contributor. However, the majority (roughly 60%) of biomass was consumed as traditional 
energy for cooking and heating in developing countries. Advanced bioenergy technologies, 
such as lignocellulosic-based transport fuels, biomass thermal conversion and algal-derived 
biodiesel are at pre-commercial stage or R&D phase (Mitigation 2011). 
 
Figure 1. Shares of energy sources in total global primary energy supply in 2008 (Mitigation 2011).

Microalgae as a promising feedstock for production of biofuel have received worldwide 
interests. Microalgae (Figure 2a-d) are unicellular photosynthetic microorganisms, including 
both prokaryotic microalgae (i.e. cyanobacteria) and eukaryotic microalgae (i.e. green algae, 
red algae and diatoms). Algae convert carbon dioxide to algal biomass, which can be used to 
produce potential biofuels, foods, feeds and high-value bioactives (Figure 2e-h). Various 
renewable energy forms including bio-oil, biodiesel, ethanol, methane and hydrogen can be 
obtained from algae by thermochemical or biochemical conversion (Demirbas 2010). 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
Figure 2. Commercially grown microalgal species: (a) Spirulina, (b) Chlorella, (c) Dunaliella salina, (d) 
Haematococcus pluvialis; Bio-products from microalgae: (e) biofuels (e.g. biodiesel), (f) health food, (g) 
animal feed, (h) bioactives (e.g. ω-3 fatty acids). 
 
Apart from renewable and carbon-neutral, the most important merit of microalgae is the fast 
growth that enables all year round production. The doubling time of microalgae in the 
exponential growth can be as short as 3.5 h. Hence, the productivity of algal biomass on unit 
area is much higher (10-100 times) than traditional biomass feedstocks (Table 1). Also, it is 
possible to produce microalgae on infertile land, thereby avoiding competition with food 
production. Because of these, microalgae shows the only realistic land requirement to achieve 
significant replacement of fossil diesel (Chisti 2007; Ziolkowska and Simon 2014).

In spite of the great potential of microalgae, commercial scale production of algae-based 
biodiesel is presently not viable. Due to the high production cost, algal biodiesel can hardly 
compete with the price of petroleum-based diesel. Current application of algal biomass is 
centered around high-value products (health, cosmetics, nutraceutical and food) (Raja, 
Hemaiswarya et al. 2008; Pienkos and Darzins 2009; Acién, Fernández et al. 2012). Therefore, 
it has been realized that biofuel must be produced simultaneously with value-added co-products 
following a biorefinery strategy (Foley, Beach et al. 2011; Olguín 2012).  
Biorefinery is analogous to petroleum refinery, but utilizes biomass as the feedstock. Algal 
biomass is similar to conventional plant biomass, primarily containing lipid, carbohydrates and 
protein. Aiming to maximizing profits and material efficiency, biorefinery attempts to exploit 
all components from biomass to produce a spectrum of products that target different markets, 
such as pigments (for high-value, low-volume markets), polymers, building block chemicals 
(for moderate-volume, moderate-value markets), animal feed, fertilizer (for high-volume, low-
value markets) (Cherubini 2010; Foley, Beach et al. 2011). Biorefining involves a wide range 
of separation and conversion technologies where principal constituents of biomass are 
==PAGE:15==
14 
 
separated and subsequently transformed into value-added products (González-Delgado and 
Kafarov 2011). 
Moreover, algae growths must be supported with nutrients, in particular nitrogen and 
phosphorus, which are abundant and need to be removed in wastewaters. Therefore, algae 
production coupled with wastewater treatment is considered a cost-effective process with dual 
advantages (Park, Craggs et al. 2011; Pittman, Dean et al. 2011). From perspective of 
wastewater treatment, algae can be employed in tertiary treatment for complete consumption 
of remaining inorganic nitrogen and phosphorus, thereby preventing eutrophication (Zhang, 
Wang et al. 2008). Meanwhile, valuable biomass is produced. With respect to biofuel 
production, production cost can be reduced by avoiding the use of artificial medium.  
 
1.2.Problem statement and objective 
Uneconomic production of algal biomass is one of the key problems for commercialization of 
third generation biofuel. Possible ways to solve this problem include reducing production cost 
and/or increasing productivity. The objective of this project is to evaluate algae production in a 
biorefinery coupled with wastewater treatment approach. Attempts for improvement in 
productivity are made by strain selection, strain breeding and optimization of cultivation 
operation. The overall economics of wastewater-based algae production will be assessed based 
on multiple products that can potentially be obtained from the biomass as well as the 
performance in removing nutrients in wastewater.

Chapter 2: Literature review  
2.1. Applications of microalgae  
Microalgal biotechnology started in the middle of the last century have developed numerous 
applications to use chemical components of microalgae (Spolaore, Joannis-Cassan et al. 2006). 
Important applications regarding production of bio-products and phycoremediation are 
reviewed in this section.  
 
2.1.1. Bio-products from microalgae 
With exception of a few species, microalgae typically consist of large fractions of lipid and 
proteins (Carioca, Hiluy Filho et al. 2009), which can be transformed into a variety of products 
with extensive industrial applications (Figure 3). 
 
Figure 3. Algal biomass fractionation and co-product generation (Foley, Beach et al. 2011). 
==PAGE:17==

- 
Lipids 
Lipid content in microalgae mainly comprises fatty acids (FA), waxes, sterols, hydrocarbons, 
ketones and pigments. Based on the polarity of the headgroup, fatty acids are generally 
classified into neutral lipids and polar lipids. Polar lipids with two subcategories, i.e. 
phospholipids and glycolipids are characterized by the presence of a hydrophilic head and a 
lipophilic tail (Siegenthaler and Murata 2006; Cuellar‐Bermudez, Aguilar‐Hernandez et al. 
2015).  
In microalgae, fatty acids are primarily synthesized in chloroplast and used as building blocks 
for the formation of various types of lipids. Depending on the growth condition, the fatty acid 
pool may be used to synthesize cellular membrane lipids or neutral storage lipids, 
predominantly triacylglycerides (TAG). TAGs paly a role in carbon and energy storage and 
their synthesis is considered as a protective mechanism in algal cells to cope with unfavorable 
environment. Generally, algae produce membrane lipid under optimal conditions of growth, 
whereas algae alter their synthetic pathway towards formation of neutral lipids under stress 
conditions. The metabolic shifts of lipid from membrane lipid synthesis to storage of neutral 
can be triggered by environmental stimuli, such as nutrient starvation, salinity, temperature, 
light intensity and pH. Statistical analysis showed the average lipid content of green algae is 
25.5% DCW, while the content can increase to 45.7% DCW under stress (Hu, Sommerfeld et 
al. 2008) 
TAGs are the desired algal component for biodiesel production that involves transesterification 
of TAG with alcohol in the presence of catalysts (Figure 4). One major reason for converting 
TAG into FAME is to reduce the high viscosity of material such as plant oil, which leads to 
operational problems in a diesel engine (Knothe 2009). Since methanol is less expensive than 
other alcohols, it is commonly used in commercial production of biodiesel. Thus, biodiesel is 
usually known as fatty acid methyl ester (FAME) when using methanol as the alcohol 
component (Moser 2011). Saturated fatty acids with a carbon number in the range of 14-20 are 
preferred for biodiesel production, whereas lipids with high degree of unsaturation and long FA 
chain (over 20 carbons) are likely to cause poor fuel properties, such as temperature properties 
and oxidative stability (Hoekman, Broch et al. 2012; Yen, Hu et al. 2013). Moreover, 
==PAGE:18==
17 
 
phospholipids is undesirable content for biodiesel production because their phosphate moiety 
complicates transesterification, reduction and combustion processes (Foley, Beach et al. 2011).  
 
 
Figure 4. Transesterification of TAG to biodiesel. 
Poly-unsaturated FAs (PUFA) present in the fraction of phospholipid are not suitable for 
biodiesel production, but PUFAs known for their nutritional value are high value compounds 
for use in the health food market. Omega-3 fatty acids, especially eicosapentaenoic acid (EPA, 
20:5, ω-3) and docosahexaenoic acid (DHA, 22:6, ω-3) are necessary in human and animal 
metabolism. Since these compounds cannot be synthesized in higher eukaryotes, they must be 
obtained from food sources (Vanthoor-Koopmans, Wijffels et al. 2013; Yen, Hu et al. 2013). 
Algal DHA-rich oil was reported to have efficacies comparable to fish oil in clinical trials 
(Doughman, Krupanidhi et al. 2007). The quantities of PUFAs in microalgae can vary 
depending the species as well as different phases of development (Spolaore, Joannis-Cassan et 
al. 2006). As reported by Tonon et al., marine microalgae, T.pseudonana and P.lutheri, 
produced high contents of EPA and DHA in exponential phase of growth and partitioned these 
to TAG during the stationary phase (Tonon, Harvey et al. 2002).  
 
- 
Pigments 
Pigments that confer colorful appearance to microalgae are grouped into chlorophylls, 
carotenoids and phycobilins. Pigments in photosynthetic organisms like microalgae play a 
crucial role in sunlight absorption and in the protection of algae from the harmful effects of 
sunrays. For example, chlorophylls (e.g. chlorophyll a/b) are greenish accessory pigments that 
are involved in the electron transport chain in photosynthesis, while carotenoids (e.g. β-
carotene, lutein, astaxanthin) prevent oxidative damage caused by excess energy captured by 
==PAGE:19==
18 
 
light-harvesting antenna (Yen, Hu et al. 2013). These pigments have wide applications in food 
products and pharmaceuticals as anti-inflammatory and wound healing additive (chlorophyll) 
or antioxidants (carotenoids). Since chlorophylls are sensitive to light and heat, they are 
commercially produced and sold in the form of water-soluble salts (chlorophyllin), usually 
sodium copper chlorophyllin. In industry, specific algal species are specialized for production 
of particular pigments. For instance, chlorophylls are mainly produced from Chlorella species, 
while commercialized carotenoids include β-carotene from D. salina and astaxanthin from 
H.pluvialis (Christaki, Florou-Paneri et al. 2011).  
 
- 
Protein 
Due to high content of protein in algal biomass, obtaining large quantity of protein can be 
expected along with the production of biodiesel or high value products. The protein fraction of 
microalgae is now commonly used for animal feed or nutrient recycling as biofertilizer (Foley, 
Beach et al. 2011). Through a number of nutritional and toxicological evaluations, algal 
biomass has been proven to be a suitable feed supplement for conventional protein sources 
(Becker 2007). Furthermore, it is believed that fertilization based on amino acid benefits 
agriculture by supplying plants with ready-made materials for development of their structures, 
which saves metabolic energy for synthesizing amino acids from nitrate (Garcia, Fernández et 
al. 2012).  
 
2.1.2. Phycoremdiation of wastewater  
In addition to production of valuable products, the application of microalgae in wastewater 
treatment has been developed since the pioneer work by Oswald and Gotaas in 1950s (Oswald 
and Gotaas 1957). Wastewater treatment process usually comprises a primary treatment and a 
secondary treatment for eliminating settled materials and for oxidizing organics in wastewater, 
respectively. Nevertheless, inorganic nitrogen and phosphorus are usually remain in the 
secondary effluent and cause eutrophication after discharge into natural water bodies (de la 
Noüe, Laliberté et al. 1992). Phycoremediation refers the use of macroalgae or microalgae to 
==PAGE:20==
19 
 
treat wastewater. Since microalgae can grow photoautotrophically without the presence of 
organic carbon sources, their application in tertiary wastewater treatment is considered useful 
to ensure safe discharge of wastewater (Hanumantha Rao, Ranjith Kumar et al. 2011).  
Microalgae can perform comparable nutrient removal as conventional chemical-based 
technologies. Additionally, algal treatment is more ecologically safe and less expensive than 
conventional tertiary treatment (Hoffmann 1998). Furthermore, it is believed that microalgae 
can enhance the aerobic degradation of various contaminants through a synergetic interaction 
with heterotrophic bacteria, where microalgae furnish O2 to aerobic bacteria to mineralize 
organic pollutants, using in turn the CO2 released from bacterial respiration (Figure 5) (Munoz 
and Guieysse 2006). According to Benemann, viable algae production for high volume low 
value products, such biofuel and commodity chemicals in any way is impossible, but co-
production of biofuel in municipal wastewater treatment appear to be the most likely practical 
application of algal biofuel (Benemann 2012). 
 
Figure 5. Principle of photosynthetic oxygenation in BOD removal processes (Munoz and Guieysse 
2006). 
Current research on the phycoremediation is notably focused on Chlorella and Scenedesmus 
genus for the removal of COD, ammonia, nitrate and total phosphorus (TP) in various 
wastewaters that are generated by municipality, agriculture or industry. These species can 
naturally inhabit in wastewater and exhibited high efficiencies with almost complete removal 
in many cases (Pittman, Dean et al. 2011). In addition, some niche applications of specific 
microalgae (e.g. Prototheca zopfii, Ankistrodesmus braunii, Chladophora fascicularis) were 
investigated for degrading pollutants such as petroleum hydrocarbons (Walker, Colwell et al. 
1975), azo dyes (Jinqi and Houtian 1992), phenol (Pinto, Pollio et al. 2002) as well as heavy 
metals (Mehta and Gaur 2005).

2.2. Algae growth  
The growth of algae depends on the combination of a number of variables, which can generally 
be classified into abiotic, biotic and operational factors (Table 2) (Larsdotter 2006). Microalgae 
are photoautotrophic organisms that convert solar energy to chemical energy through 
photosynthesis. Light and CO2 are the two essential inputs for photosynthesis reactions in 
microalgae. Additionally, sufficient supplies of macronutrients (carbon, nitrogen and 
phosphorus) and micronutrients, including silica, calcium, magnesium, potassium, iron, 
manganese, sulfur, zinc, copper and cobalt are important for maintaining cellular metabolism. 
Furthermore, environmental conditions, such as temperature, pH and salinity have effects on 
the physiological state of microalgae. 
Table 2. Factors that influence algal growth (Larsdotter 2006) 
Abiotic factors 
Light (quality, quantity), temperature, nutrient concentration, O2, 
CO2, pH, salinity, toxic chemicals 
Biotic factors 
Pathogens, predation by zooplankton, competition between 
species  
Operational factors 
Mixing, dilution rate, depth, addition of bicarbonate, harvesting 
frequency  
 
Some microalgae species can also live on heterotrophic growth using organic compounds as an 
energy source, while some mixotrophic species can simultaneously acquire both organic and 
inorganic carbon sources (Brennan and Owende 2010). Although autotrophic growth that 
consume CO2 is attractive for mitigation of GHG, heterotrophic growths supported by organics 
tend to give higher biomass productivities, translating to superior productivities (Liang, 
Sarkany et al. 2009; Chen, Yeh et al. 2011; Gim, Kim et al. 2014). A two-phase growth pattern 
of Auxenochlorella protothecoides was observed. The first stage was dominated by 
heterotrophic growth and the second stage was dominated by autotrophic growth when carbon 
source was exhausted. This suggests that heterotrophic growth may be the preferred trophic 
mode for algae (Hu, Min et al. 2012).

2.3. Biomass production  
Microalgae are interested as a renewable and productive feedstock to supplant the role of fossil 
fuels in global economy. However, current fact is that the application of microalgae is restricted 
to production of high-value products. This can be attributed by the costly production of the raw 
material. Current cultivation technologies and technical challenges will be reviewed in this 
section.   
 
2.3.1. Cultivation systems 
Current algae farming is mainly based on suspended cultures. Open pond and closed 
photobioreactors (PBR) are the two systems that are frequently investigated for algae 
cultivation (Singh, Nigam et al. 2011). Alternatively, the concept of immobilized cultures, 
including matrix-immobilized approach and biofilm has been developed to tackle the recovery 
problem involving suspended cultures (Christenson and Sims 2011).  
 
- 
Suspended cultures 
Raceway pond (Figure 6a) is a mature technology that has been applied for algae cultivation 
since 1950s. It is characterized by a raceway shape pond with a shallow depth (about 0.3 m) 
that allows sufficient sunlight to facilitate photosynthesis and a paddle wheel that ensures 
mixing of water to prevent algae settlement and to enhance gas exchange (Singh, Nigam et al. 
2011; Cai, Park et al. 2013). The edge of raceway open systems is the relatively low cost for 
establishment, maintenance and scale-up. Operation (e.g. labor, utilities and raw materials) 
accounts for the most of the production cost in raceway pond systems (Slade and Bauen 2013). 
However, since culturing conditions of open systems are less controllable, the raceway pond 
suffers from low productivity, which can be attributed to evaporation losses, temperature 
fluctuation, CO2 deficiencies, inefficient mixing and light limitation. Furthermore, low biomass 
density of the culture greatly increases the difficulties and costs for harvesting. Additionally, 
the open systems encounter a major problem of contamination by predators or fast growing 
==PAGE:23==
22 
 
competitors in the environment. Monoculture is possible by applying extreme environments 
(e.g. high salinity), which however limit the choice for the desired algae species. (Brennan and 
Owende 2010).

Immobilized cultures  
Immobilized cells refer to living cells that are prevented from moving freely in an aqueous 
phase by natural or artificial means. Applications of immobilized microalgae include metabolite 
or biomass production and nutrient removal (Moreno-Garrido 2008). One commonly used 
method is to immobilize microalgae (entrapment and encapsulation) in polymer matrices, such 
as synthetic polymers (acrylamide, photocrosslinkable resin), proteins (gelatin, collagen) or 
natural polysaccharides (agars, carrageenans or alginates), which is considered “active” 
immobilization (Figure 7). Additionally, artificial attachment by biofilms that takes advantages 
of the natural tendency of microalgae to attach to surfaces is a “passive” immobilization 
approach (Figure 7) (Moreno-Garrido 2008; de-Bashan and Bashan 2010). Immobilization of 
algae biomass during the cultivation aims to overcome the harvesting challenges associated 
with suspended algae. However, one limitation for the application of immobilization 
technology for algal biomass production is the cost of the immobilization matrix (Christenson 
and Sims 2011).

As for the effect on cell growth, immobilization generally exerts stress on the microorganism, 
as a consequence of the interactions between the polymer matrix and cell wall (de-Bashan and 
Bashan 2010; Ozkan, Kinney et al. 2012). However, for microalgae, there is no consistent 
general conclusion. Inhibition, enhancement or no growth difference between free and 
immobilized algae have been reported by different works (Mallick 2002). In comparison with 
microalgae growing in free states, the lag phase of immobilized algae was reported longer, but 
it exhibited similar growth rate to that of free cells (Chevalier and de la Noüe 1985; Lau, Tam 
et al. 1998). Bailliez et al. reported negative effect of immobilization on the growth rate of 
Botryococcus braunii (Bailliez, Largeau et al. 1985), whereas higher maximum cell density of 
immobilized Phaeodactylum tricornutum in comparison with suspended culture was 
demonstrated by Hertzberg and Jensen. Hertzberg and Jensen’s work also showed different 
microalgae behaved differently in the gel, which suggests the effect of immobilization on the 
growth of microalgae can be species-specific (Hertzberg and Jensen 1989). Moreover, 
immobilization was reported to result in increased contents of chlorophyll (Lau, Tam et al. 1998; 
Pane, Feletti et al. 1998), hydrocarbon (Bailliez, Largeau et al. 1985), glycolate (Vílchez, 
Galván et al. 1991).

2.3.2. Challenges 
Regarding cultivation, economic production of algal biomass encounters a number of 
technological challenges, such as nutrient supply, gas transfer, photosynthetically active 
radiation (PAR) delivery, culture integrity and environment control (Christenson and Sims 
2011).  
Macronutrients, micronutrients and CO2 are essential inputs for supporting the growth of algae. 
Nevertheless, the production cost can be greatly increased for the use of commercial CO2 and 
fertilizer. A life-cycle assessment that compared environmental impacts between algae 
cultivation and conventional crops (switchgrass, canola and corn) farming demonstrated higher 
energy use, GHG emissions, and water use of algal biofuel were mainly resulted from the 
demand for fertilizer and CO2 (Clarens, Resurreccion et al. 2010). Furthermore, proper gas 
exchange for CO2 delivery and O2 removal is important for algae growth. Because of the short 
residence time, absorption of CO2 by cells may not be effective enough by simple bubbling and 
thus much CO2 may be lost to the atmosphere. Meanwhile, effective removal of O2 to prevent 
photooxidative damage remains a challenge, especially for closed reactors (Carvalho, Meireles 
et al. 2006). Since PAR cannot be mixed, while the outmost layer of cells is often exposed to 
overpowered light, the deeper portions of a culture are shaded. Due to simultaneous occurrence 
of photoinhibition and photodeprivation, PAR is usually not efficiently utilized by algae. 
Moreover, monoculture of the desired algae strains is difficult to achieve. Contamination by 
less desirable strains and other microorganisms (e.g. bacteria and fungi) is almost inevitable for 
open pond system and when wastewater is used. Fast growing species of algae are likely to 
outcompete high lipid producing strains. Finally, cost-effective control of growth parameters 
such as temperature and pH can be very difficult for large-scale cultivation (Christenson and 
Sims 2011).

2.4. Attempts for improvement 
For improvement in economics of algal biomass production, works can be focused on 
photobioreactor engineering, biorefinery based production and algal biology (Chisti 2007). 
 
2.4.1. Cultivation system design  
Factors that involve in photosynthesis are important considerations for the photobioreactor 
design, such as lighting, mixing, water consumption, CO2 consumption, O2 removal, nutrient 
supply, temperature and pH (Kunjapur and Eldridge 2010).  
For photobioreactor design, light intensity, wavelength and the frequency of cellular exposure 
to light need to be optimal for algae growth. Sunlight as a free resource is the most commonly 
utilized light source for algae cultivation, but the natural light is less controllable as artificial 
lights (Kunjapur and Eldridge 2010). Current performances of outdoor cultivation are generally 
poor. One reason is that using sunlight is problematic in providing stable lighting, due to day-
night cycles and diurnal variation in light intensity and the varying length of time depending on 
the weather and season. Also, direct sunlight is often too intense for algae. In this case, 
innovative light sources, such as light-emitting diodes (LED) and optical fiber excited by 
artificial lights are proposed to provide stable and continuous lighting for algae cultivation. 
LED light source compared to conventional fluorescent lamps is advantageous in longer life-
expectancy and lower energy consumption. To solve the problem of self-shading in algae 
culture, optical fiber can be used as an internal light source, which enhances illumination 
efficiency by providing uniform light distribution (Lee and Kim 1998; Yeh and Chung 2009; 
Chen, Yeh et al. 2011).  
Mixing is critical to ensure the frequency of cell exposure to light and mass transfer between 
nutrients and cells. Well mixing enables even distribution of radiation to all cells, thereby 
improving the illumination efficiency. For the optimal mass transfer, several factors, such as 
agitation rate, type of sparger, antifoam agents and temperature are subject to the design in order 
to increase the contact area between liquid and gas (Kunjapur and Eldridge 2010). Installation 
==PAGE:29==
28 
 
of static mixers in tubular photobioreactor contributes to mixing by changing the liquid flow 
from plug flow to turbulent mixing. As reported by Ugwu et al., static mixers led to increased 
mass transfer coefficient (KLa) and gas hold up, resulting in higher productivity (Ugwu, 
Ogbonna et al. 2002).  
Aiming to improve the gas-liquid transfer, Zimmerman et al. developed a microbubble 
generation system based on fluidic oscillation. Bubbles generated by conventional aerator are 
typically more than 10-fold larger than the diameter of apertures (600μm) because of the 
parallel percolation instability (i.e. instead of growing in all the holes, bubbles are formed in 
only few of holes that require a lower pressure difference to overcome). The fluidic oscillator 
is able to limit the growth time for air supplies into the bubble-formation apertures. In this case, 
the growth of bubbles will be terminated before they reach a large size, so that the mean bubble 
size is limited to 700 ±25μm  (Zimmerman, Hewakandamby et al. 2009). Ying et al. 
demonstrated 30% -100% improvement in KLa and overall gas-liquid transfer (CO2 dissolution 
and O2 removal) by microfluidic microbubbles (Ying, Al-Mashhadani et al. 2013) 
 
2.4.2. Integrated process 
Nutrients and CO2 are necessary inputs for algae cultivation and account for a considerable 
portion of running cost. Combined process of biofuel production with environmental 
applications such as flue gas and wastewater treatment is considered beneficial for 
sustainability and economy. Integrations of flue gas and wastewater into algal biomass 
cultivation take advantage of CO2 and nutrients (e.g. N and P) in these wastes, which apparently 
will save the costs for commercial CO2 and fertilizer. Plus the dual benefits in mitigating GHG 
emission and preventing eutrophication, such integrated processes are promising for sustainable 
algae biomass production. However, one major obstacle for using wastes to feed microalgae is 
that the conditions provided by these wastes can be unfavorable for the growth of algae. For 
instance, SOx and NOx in flue gas are inhibitory to algae. Depending on the source of 
wastewater (municipality, agriculture or industry), contamination by microorganisms (e.g. 
bacteria and zooplankton) or toxins (e.g. heavy metal) are problematic when using wastewater 
==PAGE:30==
29 
 
as the cultivation medium. Consequently, the choice of algal strains that are tolerant to these 
adverse factors can be limited (Brennan and Owende 2010). 
Doucha et al. demonstrated comparable productivities of algae in flue gas containing 6-8% (v/v) 
CO2 (19.4-22.8 g m-2 d-1) and in pure CO2 (19.1-22.6 g m-2 d-1). The degree of CO2 mitigation 
was up to 50% depending on the injection rate of flue gas (Doucha, Straka et al. 2005). Further 
research compared the performance of flue gas from a municipal waste incinerator and a 
mixture of pure CO2 and air. In spite of the same average CO2 concentration (11% (v/v)) in 
these gas inputs, the culture supplied with flue gas showed higher growth rate and CO2 fixation 
rate. This was attributed to higher partial pressure of O2 in the mixture of CO2 and air that may 
negatively affect the algae (Douskova, Doucha et al. 2009). 
As for the use of wastewater in algae cultivation, municipal and agricultural wastewater 
characterized by high N and P content are considered suitable for algae cultivation, but 
industrial wastewater has less potential because of the generally low N and P concentration and 
high toxin concentrations (Pittman, Dean et al. 2011). Wang et al. examined the growth of 
Chlorella sp. in wastewaters sampled from four different points of treatment process flow of 
municipal wastewater: before primary settling, after primary settling, after activated sludge tank 
and centrate. Results showed that Chlorella sp. well adapted to all of the four wastewaters with 
no observable lag phase, while the highest growth rate (0.948 d-1) was found in the centrate. 
The significantly higher levels of nitrogen, phosphorous and COD are believed to be the reason 
that led to superior algal growth in centrate (Wang, Min et al. 2010). 
 
2.4.3. Strain selection 
Intrinsic ability of algae to produce biomass and desired products is a determinant factor for 
economics of the production. Palmer ranked algal genus as Euglena, Oscillatoria, 
Chlamydomonas, Scenedesmus, Chlorella, Nitzschia, Navicula and Stigeochlonium based on 
their growth robustness in organic polluted environment (Palmer 1969). A study by Zhou et al. 
isolated multiple species in the nature and 17 top-performing strains were screened in 
concentrated municipal wastewater. Five best lipid producers identified by DNA sequencing 
==PAGE:31==
30 
 
were Auxenochlorella protothecoides (77.7 mg l-1 d-1), Hindakia sp. (77.8 mg l-1 d-1), 
Scenedesmus sp. (74.5 mg l-1 d-1) and two Chlorella sp. (74.7 mg l-1 d-1 and 77.5 mg l-1 d-1) 
(Zhou, Li et al. 2011). Additionally, two Chlorella species, C. protothecoides and C. kessleri 
were found out-performing in biomass growth in 14 algae strains according to Li et al.’s 
screening in wastewater centrate (Li, Zhou et al. 2011). 
Furthermore, some attempts that cultivated algae consortium in wastewater also suggested 
Chlorella sp. and Scenedesmus sp. are relatively robust species that can grow in wastewater. 
Godos et al. reported growth of a consortium containing four algal species, S. obliquus, C. 
sorokiniana, S. platensis and E. viridis in piggery wastewater. It was found that S. obliquus was 
the dominant species within the three days, while C. sorokiniana took over the algal community 
after 6 days. The proliferation of C. sorokiniana in the end of cultivation was attributed to that 
C. sorokiniana is more tolerant to the increasing ammonium inhibition (de Godos, Vargas et al. 
2010). Another work selected C. vulgaris, S. quadricauda, E. gracilis, A. Convolutus and C, 
oviforme based on the tolerance to metals to form a consortium, which was inoculated in landfill 
leachate. Microscopic examination suggested C. vulgaris was the dominant species over the 
whole cultivation period with the use of different loading rates of treated leachate (Mustafa, 
Phang et al. 2012). Samorì et al. investigated algal community of a natural consortium growing 
wastewater  and demonstrated the genera, Scenedesmus and/or Desmodesmus naturally 
outcompeted the other algal strains (including Chlorophyta, Cyanobacteria, Euglenozoa and 
Ochrophyta) (Samorì, Samorì et al. 2013). 
 
2.3.4. Strain improvement 
With the attempt to reduce the overall production costs, strain improvement plays an important 
role in commercial biological process, as a means of developing desired traits of 
microorganisms such as increased productivity, ability to use cheaper raw material or tolerance 
to adverse environmental factors.  
The advent of DNA recombination technology and increasing understanding on genetics of 
microorganisms brings about more powerful approaches for strain improvement, known as 
==PAGE:32==
31 
 
rational genetic engineering (Rowlands 1984). However, due to poor understanding of 
regulatory mechanisms and lack of genetic manipulation tools, application of genetic 
engineering for improvement of algal strains is in its infancy. At present, most advances in 
genetic engineering of algae are restricted to research on Chlamydomonas reinhardtii (Amaro, 
Guedes et al. 2011). For instance, a recent attempt for increasing lipid accumulation was made 
by deregulating lipid catabolism. Antisense and interfering RNA was used to knockdown a 
multifunctional lipase/phospholipase/acyltransferase in the diatom Thalassiosira pseudonana. 
The resulted mutant strains showed 2.4 – 3.3 fold higher lipid content than wild-type during 
exponential phase without compromising growth (Trentacoste, Shrestha et al. 2013). In addition, 
photosynthetic systems are subject to genetic engineering to enhance the light capture efficiency. 
Beckmann et al. transformed a permanently active repressor, NAB1* into C. reinhardtii, which 
repressed the translation of genes for the light-harvesting antenna systems and consequently 
reduced the antenna size at PSII by 10-17%. The engineered strain exhibited ~50% increase of 
photosynthetic efficiency and higher densities in large-scale bioreactor (Beckmann, Lehr et al. 
2009). 
Although genetic engineering evidently could confer enhancement to microalgae in various 
traits, the GM microalgae also exhibited potential risk on their escape in environment to cause 
uncontrollable harmful algal blooms (Flynn, Mitra et al. 2013). Alternatively, conventional 
breeding methods involving random mutagenesis and selection are still reliable and cost-
effective (Rowlands 1984). Being processes of changing an organism’s genetic information, 
mutagenesis in contrast to genetic engineering can occur naturally or by physical, chemical or 
biological treatments. Moreover, it alters the natural genetic background of organisms other 
than introducing or integrating new genetic materials as what the DNA recombination 
technology does. Therefore, modified organisms through random mutagenesis should not invite 
the environmental concerns and is considered non-GM according to the EU regulation (Breyer, 
Herman et al.)  
Evolutionary engineering is another strain improvement approach that has been widely used to 
enhance robustness of organisms under a variety of stresses, such as heat, acid, osmotic and 
toxicity. Evolutionary engineering, also known as adaptive laboratory evolution (ALE) is a 
==PAGE:33==
32 
 
process of long term adaption of cells to selective pressure. In comparison with genetic 
engineering, evolutionary engineering does not require knowledge about the genome of the 
studied organism and therefore is advantageous for its simplicity and effectiveness. The history 
of adaptive laboratory evolution can date back to a hundred years ago. Increasing number of 
ALE studies have been conducted in the last 25 years, but Escherichia coli and Saccharomyces 
cerevisiae are the focuses for the majority of these investigations (Dragosits and Mattanovich 
2013). Few ALE experiments have been performed for improved phenotypes of 
Chlamydomonas reinhardtii (Yu, Zhao et al. 2013; Takouridis, Tribe et al. 2014), Chlorella 
vulgaris (Fu, Gudmundsson et al. 2012) and Dunaliella salina (Fu, Guðmundsson et al. 2013).

3.1. Choice of algal strain 
Chlorella and Scenedesmus are the most frequently found species in wastewater. Thus, they are 
expected to well adapt to wastewater environment, exhibit robust growth and outcompete other 
species. In this project, Chlorella sorokiniana and Scenedesmus obliquus are selected as 
reference strains for these genera. 
 
- 
Chlorella sorokiniana 
Chlorella sorokinina is a strain of unicellular green algae that was first isolated by Sorokin and 
Myers in 1953 (Sorokin and Myers 1953). Molecular data based on 18S rRNA sequences 
suggested that C. sorokiniana together with closely related taxa, C. vulgaris, C. lobophora and 
==PAGE:34==
33 
 
C. kessleri belong to Trebouxiophyceae class of chlorophytes. These species possess 
glucosamine as the exclusive component of the rigid cell wall, which distinguishes them from 
all other “Chlorella” species (e.g. C. prothecoides) that displays a considerable evolutionary 
distance (Huss, Frank et al. 1999). Chlorella propagates by autosporulation, which is asexual 
reproduction mode where cells reproduce by simple binary division (Yamamoto, Nishikawa et 
al. 2007; Noguchi, Kawano et al. 2014) 
According to a chemotaxmonomical chessification suggested by Kessler, all 12 taxa are 
morphologically different with exceptions of C. vulgaris and C. sorokiniana, which can only 
be discriminated by the hydrogenase activity and thermophilic property of C. sorokiniana 
(Kessler 1976). As described to C. sorokiniana GXNN01 that was isolated from wastewater, it 
has spherical to oval shape (Figure 8) and the cell size is 2.1-4.1μm for autotrophic cells and 
2.8-5.6μm for mixotrophic cells (Qiao, Wang et al. 2009). C. sorokiniana, known as the most 
thermotolerant Chlorella species to date can tolerate up to 42℃, while the optimal temperature 
for its growth is 37℃ (Li, Zheng et al. 2013). In addition, the optimal pH is 7.0 (Yoshida, Ikeda 
et al. 2006; Zheng, Chi et al. 2012). 
 
Figure 8. Microscopic image of Chlorella sorokiniana. 
 
Furthermore, C. sorokiniana is capable of utilizing various carbon sources and can live on 
autotrophic, heterotrophic and mixotrophic modes. Increased growth was shown as a result of 
==PAGE:35==
34 
 
the addition of carbon sources (Qiao and Wang 2009). A number of studies reported that 
mixotrophic condition significantly improved the growth of C. sorokiniana in comparison with 
phototrophic and heterotrophic conditions (Lee, Ding et al. 1996; Wan, Liu et al. 2011; Li, 
Zheng et al. 2014). Li et al. reported that mixotrophic growth rates at high light intensities were 
higher than the sum of growth rates obtained in heterotrophic and phototrophic cultures (Li, 
Zheng et al. 2014). As for nutrition requirement, glucose seems the most favorite organic carbon 
source for C. sorokiniana and greatly outperformed other carbon sources including fructose 
mannose, galactose, lactose, sucrose, glycerol and acetate (Li, Zheng et al. 2013). As for 
nitrogen source, growths of C. sorokiniana supported by potassium nitrate and urea showed 
relatively higher growth compared to peptone, yeast extract, NaNO3, NH4NO3 and NH4Cl (Li, 
Zheng et al. 2013; Ramanna, Guldhe et al. 2014). By constrast, ammonium nitrogen seems to 
be unfavored by C. sorokiniana. The biomass production in ammonium sulfate was less than 
one third of the biomass obtained in urea and sodium nitrate, but the lipid content was found to 
be three times higher than that found with the other two nitrogen sources (Wan, Wang et al. 
2012).

Scenedesmus obliquus 
Scenedesmus species are also widely distributed green algae in freshwaters throughout the 
world. Scenedesmus belongs to another class of chlorophyta phylum, named Chlorophyceae. 
Sequence analysis of 18S rRNA showed that the Scenedesmus genus could be sub-categorized 
into Desmodesmus and Scenedesmus-Acutodesmus lineage (Van Hannen, Lürling et al. 2000). 
The distinctive features of Scenedesmus are the fusiform shape of cells with or without spines 
and the formation of 4-celled colonies, called coenobia (Trainor 1996; Hegewald 1997), which 
can be easily recognized under microscope. Distinguished from Desmodesmus, Scenedesmus-
Acutodesmus species (e.g. S. obliquus) are morphologically characterized by acute poles and 
the lack of ornaments in the cell wall (Figure 9) (Kessler, Schäfer et al. 1997). Scemedesmus 
species mainly reproduce asexually by autospores, where new individuals are formed as a result 
of successive divisions within the parent cell wall and subsequently released as new coenobium 
(Trainor, Cain et al. 1976; Hegewald 1997; Koppram, Albers et al. 2012; Winkler and Kao 
==PAGE:36==
35 
 
2014). Production and germination of zygotes in Scenedemus has also been found, but only 
reported in laboratory condition. Therefore, even sexual reproductions exist in Scenedesmus, it 
is considered negligible in nature (Trainor 1996; Hegewald 1997).  
 
Figure 9. Microscopic image of Scenedesmus obliquus. 
 
The formation of coenobia is determined by environmental conditions. In general, Scenedesmus 
present as single cells in good conditions for growth (Hegewald 1997). Some researchers found 
that colony formation is likely to be a defense mechanism against natural enemies. Filtrate from 
cultures of herbivorous zooplankton (e.g. Daphnia magna) significantly enhanced colony 
formation in S. obliquus, which indicated that chemical cue from zooplankton could be the 
stimulus that triggered the morphological change from unicells to colonies (Lürling 2003; Yang 
and Li 2007). The optimal conditions for biomass yield reported by Guedes et al. were 30℃ 
and pH of 6 (Guedes, Amaro et al. 2011), whereas in nitrogen deficient environment the optimal 
temperature of 27.5℃ and optimal pH of 7 were reported (Hodaifa, Martínez et al. 2009; Breuer, 
Lamers et al. 2013). The optimal nutrient supplements (0.04, 0.03 and 1.0 g l-1 of nitrate, 
phosphate and sodium thiosulphate, respectively) were demonstrated by the prediction through 
3D response surface analysis. Moreover, the P- and N- deficiencies and increased concentration 
of sodium thiosulphate showed negative impacts on the cell growth, while exogenous glucose 
promoted the biomass production (Mandal and Mallick 2009).

3.2. Microplate screening  
In general, Chlorella and Scenedesmus are considered the most robust microalgae that can 
habitat in wastewater. However, variation in experimental conditions led to inconsistent 
conclusions on the most robust algal species growing in wastewater between different studies. 
Therefore, which strain between C. sorokiniana and S. obliquus can show a better growth in 
the specific wastewater we are going to use need to be proved by screening. Microplate will be 
used for screening, because its assay shows significant accordance with the conventional flask 
method and enables high throughput examination of multiple conditions.  
Microplate technology was developed as a high-throughput inhibition assay for the 
toxicological inhibitory effect on microbial growth. Microplate assays offer advantages over 
flask tests in reducing laboratory resources, space and time requirements (Geis, Fleming et al. 
2000). Skjelbred et al. managed to test the effect of three treatment factors (light, temperature 
and salinity) at multiple levels on algal growth using 96-well and 24-well microplates, which 
showed the potential of microplate in simultaneous test of many conditions (Skjelbred, 
Edvardsen et al. 2012). Due to the limitations of microplate in cultivation parameters such as 
lighting, mixing and gas transfer, measurements made in microplate is not valid for large-scale 
photobioreactor. Nevertheless, cell growths monitored through microplate screening was 
reported analogous to the data obtained from larger scale assays with 100 ml flasks 
(Eisentraeger, Dott et al. 2003). Since screening using microplate requires cultivation of 
microalgae in small volumes (2ml for 24-well microplate), florescence assay that is a more 
sensitive than measuring optical density must be used as the indicator to estimate biomass when 
signal is very weak (Van Wagenen, Holdt et al. 2014). In vivo fluorescence (IVF) has been 
proved correlated to cell number and thus a valid proxy of biomass (Gregor, Jančula et al. 2008; 
Podevin, De Francisci et al. 2014).

3.3. ALE & mutagenesis  
Using wastewater to cultivate microalgae is in development as an approach to reduce 
production cost for algal biomass. However, problems associated with wastewater quality, such 
as imbalanced nutrients and presence of toxic chemicals and microorganisms may cause 
unsatisfactory productivities for commercialization. For example, filtration of wastewater by a 
0.2 μm pore-size filter was reported to cause an increase in biomass productivity, suggesting 
that microorganisms, such as bacteria or protozoa may affect algal growth through competition 
or predation (Cho, Luong et al. 2011). Osundeko et al. revealed a correlation between oxidative 
stress tolerance and acclimation to the wastewater. Thus, oxidative stress tolerance may play a 
role in adaptation of algae to wastewater environment (Osundeko, Davies et al. 2013; Osundeko, 
Dean et al. 2014). 
In this case, adaptive evolution is proposed for developing improved algal strain that exhibits 
resistance to adverse influences and thus enhanced growth and productivity in wastewater. 
Mutations are the basis of genetically heterogeous population for selection of improved 
phenotypes. Low rates of spontaneous mutation and thus genetic variation are maintained in 
natural algal population, which as a defense mechanism enable these microbial communities to 
survive to catastrophic environmental changes. Fluctuation analysis by Lopez-Rodas et al. 
obtained resistant variants in several water pollutants (e.g. herbicides, antibiotics, heavy metals, 
sulphureous water) and attributed such adaptation to the selection on pre-existing genetic 
variation resulted from spontaneous mutation, other than individual responses to selective agent 
(López Rodas, Costas et al. 2006). During ALE experiment, selective pressures are applied to 
prolonged cultivation of a microorganism. Under the action of evolutionary mechanism 
involving spontaneous mutation and natural selection/ preservation, variants that possess the 
selective advantage will eventually dominate the cell population. (Hong 2012; Koppram, 
Albers et al. 2012; Winkler and Kao 2014).  
ALE studies have been conducted following similar experimental procedures for many years. 
The two basic laboratory selection methods are batch cultivation in parallel serial cultures and 
continuous (chemostat) cultures in bioreactor vessels. For batch cultivation, microorganisms 
==PAGE:39==
38 
 
are propagated in shake flasks and an aliquot of culture is transferred to a new flask with fresh 
medium at regular intervals (Figure 10). Similar process can be applied in deep well plates with 
smaller culture volumes, which allow multiple conditions to be examined in parallel. This 
method is advantageous for its cheap and easy setup, but variations between batches in 
population density, growth rate, nutrient supply and environmental conditions are the 
shortcomings. By contrast, chemostat cultivation providing tight control of nutrient supply and 
environmental conditions can avoid inconstant growth rates and population densities, but the 
operation cost is much higher than the costs of shake flask cultivation. Furthermore, a range of 
advances for accelerating the pace of evolution or for facilitating analysis of evolved mutant 
has been reviewed by Winkler and Kao. For instance, evolution process can be accelerated by 
mutagenesis prior to or during an evolution experiment, which is used to generate more genetic 
diversity in a population for selection (Dragosits and Mattanovich 2013; Winkler and Kao 
2014).

3.4. Photobioreactor cultivation  
Continuous cultivation is the preferred operating mode for biomass production because it can 
provide higher productivities. With continuous operation, medium is continuously input into 
the reactor and equal amount of culture is removed at the same rate. Therefore, if other culture 
parameters (e.g. culture volume, mixing, pH, temperature, etc.) remain constant, flow rate of 
medium that determines the rate of nutrient supply will largely define the environmental 
condition and thus control the growth rate of the microorganism. Dilution rate, defined as the 
==PAGE:41==
40 
 
flow of medium (𝑣) over the volume of culture in the bioreactor (V) is an important operating 
variable. At steady state, the specific growth rate of microorganism (μ) is maintained constant 
and theoretically equal to the dilution rate [1].

Furthermore, the biomass concentration at steady state depends on the equilibrium between 
specific growth rate and the imposed dilution rate. At high dilution rate, replacement of biomass 
by fresh medium is fast, resulting in low biomass concentration inside the bioreactor and vice 
versa (ndez Sevilla and Grima 1998). After acclimation to the environment, the growth rate of 
microorganism and the biomass concentration will be defined by a corresponding dilution rate. 
Therefore, a growth rate equal to the dilution rate as well as a stable biomass concentration over 
time can be considered indicators of a steady state.  
The key parameter of interest, volumetric biomass productivity calculated by equation [2] is 
determined by both dilution rate (D) and the biomass concentration (CX). Because dilution rate 
and biomass concentration are inversely related, the maximum biomass productivity need to be 
determined by examining biomass concentrations at a series of dilution rates. 
Through the influence on biomass concentration, dilution rate also indirectly affect the culturing 
parameter, such as lighting (i.e. photolimitation and photoinhibition), which in turn will affect 
physiological state of the cells (Fernández, Pérez et al. 2000). Thus, it is necessary to analyze

4.3. Mortality analysis and mutagenesis  
Mortality analysis was carried out to determine the optimal conditions regarding mutagen 
concentration and exposure time that correspond to 90% mortality of the C. sorokiniana 
population. Seven milliliters of C. sorokiniana culture taken from exponential phase (~20,000 
cells mL-1) were treated separately with different concentrations of EMS solution (0.5 ~ 2.5% 
w/v) and incubated in dark at room temperature (~ 20℃) for 90 and 120 min with shake. At 
each exposure time, three milliliters of sample from each treatment were taken and centrifuged 
at 3,000 rpm for 5 min. The supernatant was discarded and the cell pellet was washed with 1 
mL distilled water thrice. After the washing, cell pellet was re-suspended in 3 mL of MWC 
medium and incubated in dark at room temperature overnight. The next day, 100mL of each 
diluted sample (10-3 dilution) was spread in triplicates on plates with solid MWC medium 
(MWC medium containing 1.5% agar). The control group underwent the same treatment as the 
other groups, but without the exposure to EMS. When visible colonies appeared (within two 
weeks), the number of colonies were counted. Mortality was calculated by comparing average 
cell number of each treatment with the number of control. Mutagenesis was conducted using 
the conditions determined by mortality analysis. After the treatment as mentioned above, the 
mutated culture was maintained under laboratory conditions for two days and then inoculated 
in microplate for adaptive evolution.

4.4. Adaptive laboratory evolution  
Wildtype C. sorokiniana that had acclimitated to MWC-se medium and the “mutant” were 
repeatedly cultivated in 100% wastewater in parallel. Each strain was inoculated into triplicates 
of 2 ml wastewater. Fifteen three-day cycles of cultivation were performed in microplates, 
which lasted for 45 days. In the first five cycles, wastewater was processed by centrifugation, 
whereas in the later ten cycles wastewater was used as it is. Additionally, wastewater used for 
the first five cycles and the following tens cycles were originated from the same source but 
collected at different times. The initial auto-fluorescence of each cycle was maintained at the 
same level (1,000 ~ 1,500 RFU). The microplates were incubated at room temperature (~ 20℃) 
with a light intensity of 400±50 μmol s-1 m-2 and shake at 140 rpm. 
 
4.5. Photobioreactor cultivation  
In order to assess the performance of algae growth and wastewater treatment, a flat-panel 
photobioreactor (Algaemist reactor, Wageningen University) (Figure 14) was used to cultivate 
C. sorokiniana with the pretreated wastewater. In this reactor, mixing was accomplished 
through bubbling from the holes in a horizontal pipe near the bottom of the reactor and light 
was supplied with 6 LED with adjustable intensity up to 2,500 μmol s-1 m-2 (surface averaged). 
Temperature inside the reactor was measured with an electronic thermometer, which controlled 
heating and cooling of water in a reservoir tank. Water was circulated between the reservoir and 
water jacket of the reactor, thereby maintaining the reactor temperature to the set-point. 
Additionally, pH in the algal broth was monitored with a pH electrode that automatically 
maintained the pH by controlling the CO2 in the air-flow.  
The cultivation was initiated with a batch mode. The reactor was filled with 400 ml wastewater 
and 4 ml of wild type C. sorokiniana inoculum (OD680: 0.600, OD750: 0.470) from the second 
generation of algae screening was added. Parameter settings in this experiment is listed in Table

mode. The dilution rate was set to 0.18 h-1 (4.32 d-1), which is maximum specific growth rate

4.6.3. Protein determination 
For protein quantification, duplicates of 50 mg biomass were suspended in 6 ml of 6N HCl and 
transferred in close vessels for microwave digestion. To prevent oxidative degradation of some 
oxygen/sensitive amino acids the generation of an inert atmosphere was created by flashing 
nitrogen. Microwave (Multiwave 3000, Anton Paar) was employed to digest the protein 
contents (30 min at 150℃ and 500W). Samples were frozen at -80℃ for 5 hours and then 
freeze-dried overnight to eliminate HCl. The residue was suspended in 400 μl of milliQ and 
filtered through 0.22 μm  syringe filters before the protein quantification by  in-needle 
derivatization HPLC-FLD (Dionex UltiMate 3000, Thermo Scientific).  
To detect the amino-acids by fluorescence light detector (FLD) the primary amine of each 
amino-acid is derivatized into a highly fluorescent thio-substituated isoindole. The 
derivatization reagent o-phtaldialdehyde (OPA), in presence of 3-mercaptopropionic acid 
(MPA), reacts with the amino-acid to form the fluorescent isoindole. Detection by FLD is 
achieved by excitation at 337 nm and emission at 442 nm. Amino-acids are separated in a c18 
reversed phase column (Eclipse Plus C18, Agilent Technologies, USA) with an in-line guard 
column (EC 4/2 Universal RP, Macherey-Nagel, Germany) and mobile phases A (10mM 
Na2HPO4, 10 mM Na2B4O7) and B (methanol: acetonitrile: water, 45:45:10). The flow rate was 
0.420 mL min-1. Quantitative analyses were performed by means of calibration curves using a 
commercial amino-acid mix standard (AAS18 Fluka). 
 
4.6.4. Pigment extraction 
For pigment extraction, freeze-drying has been proved essential and sonication was commonly 
reported more efficient than simply steeping (Buffan-Dubau and Carman 2000; Wiltshire, 
Boersma et al. 2000). In addition, extraction efficiency is largely determined by the effect of 
solvents on specific algal species. A mixture of 90% acetone and 10% water is commonly used 
in many studies, but some studies also report that other combinations, such as 50:50 acetone/ 
methanol (Bowles, Paerl et al. 1985) correspond to a higher extraction yield. 
==PAGE:50==
49 
 
Extraction efficiencies of six solvents: 100% acetone, 90% acetone, 100% methanol, 90% 
methanol, 50: 50 acetone: methanol and 45:45:10 acetone: methanol: water were examined as 
following. Two milligrams of freeze-dried biomass were added to 3 ml of each solvent in 
duplicates. Well mixed samples were treated by sonication (Branson 3510MT) in ice bath for 
10 min. Residual biomass was separated from the supernatant by centrifugation for 10 min at 
13,000 rpm. Pigment in the supernatant was analyzed by HPLC with a 75 min separation time. 
Individual pigment standards (lutein, β-carotene, chlorophyll a and b) (DHI, Hørsholm) were 
used for calibration and quantification of pigment concentrations. 
For samples from each dilution rate, two milligrams of freeze-fried biomass in duplicate were 
extracted in 3 ml of 90% acetone and subject to sonication in ice batch for 10 min. Supernatants 
separated from centrifugation were analyzed as described above. 
 
4.6.5. Nutrient removal 
After harvest of biomass, supernatant obtained from cultivation at each dilution rate was 
collected for nutrient composition analysis. Contents of COD, total nitrogen (TN), total 
phosphorus (TP) and ammonium were determined for the supernatant as well as the influent 
wastewater. Samples were diluted to 2 – 4 folds so that concentrations were in the range of 
measurements. Tests for COD, TN and TP were performed following instructions of cuvette 
test kits (Hach Lange; LCK314, LCK238 and LCK348), while Spectroquant® ammonium test 
(Merck Millipore) was used for the measurement of ammonium.  
 
4.6.6. Statistics analysis  
IBM SPSS Statistics (Version 22) was used for statistical analysis. Data comparison was 
performed using one way ANOVA test and unpaired t-test with 95% confidential intervals.

5.1.2. Effect of wastewater pretreatment 
Regarding the processing for wastewater prior to cultivation, additional microplate screening 
was conducted to assess the effect of pretreatment on the growth of C. sorokiniana. Comparison 
was made among wastewaters treated by filtration, centrifugation, sedimentation and the 
original wastewater (control) without any processing. The average growth performances 
(Figure 18) are as expected in the order: filtration (2.23±0.04 d-1) > centrifugation (2.21±0.02 
d-1) > sedimentation (2.18±0.03 d-1) > control (2.02±0.12 d-1). While one-way ANOVA 
analysis suggests there is no significance among filtration, centrifugation and sedimentation (p > 
0.05), algae growth is significantly improved by these methods compared to growth in the 
original wastewater (p < 0.05). Considering the effects of the pretreatments, removal of large 
particulates from the original wastewater appears to have the most impact on algae growth. In 
contrast, the improvements by removal of small particulates (i.e. centrifugation) and bacteria 
(i.e. filtration) are not significant. Large particulates in the original wastewater were probably 
the cause of negative effects on algae growth by shading, which reduce light that can be 
received by algae. From the perspective of economics, sedimentation may be the most realistic 
pretreatment method in large scale production where enormous wastewater needs to be 
processed. Thus, sedimentation was applied to process wastewater for further study on 
photobioreactor scale (400 ml).

According to Figure 21, the first 4 cycles show clear acclimation, whereas the growth rates in 
the unprocessed wastewater do not show obvious trend and generally lower than the algal 
growth rates exhibited in the wastewater treated by centrifugation. The fluctuation in growth 
rates shown by successive cultivations should be due to the variations between batches. 
Furthermore, this result combining with the previous screening on the methods for wastewater 
pretreatment (Figure 21) indicate the large particles in the untreated wastewater may define the 
algae growth on a lower level. The reason for the decreased growth is unknown, while one 
direct effect that can be caused by suspended solids is the limited light penetration (Zhou, Li et 
al. 2011). Furthermore, two-tail t-test was performed and indicates no significant difference 
(p>0.05) between the wildtype and the “mutant” in every cycle.   
Recent ALE experiments with aims to improve particular traits of microalgae are summarized 
in (Table 7). Fu et al. evolved C.vulgaris and D.salina through ALE under light stress conditions 
in 2012 and 2013. In these studies, multiple growth cycles were conducted in semi-continuous 
culture in photobioreactor with working volumes over 300 ml (Fu, Gudmundsson et al. 2012; 
Fu, Guðmundsson et al. 2013). By contrast, repeated sub-culturing in flask with the liquid 
volume of 40-50 ml was applied by Yu et al. and Takouridis et al., who improved microalgae 
in nitrogen starvation and high salinity conditions, respectively. In the research by Fu et al. and 
Yu et al., microalgae were only repeatedly cultured under specific stress (i.e. light or nitrogen 
limitation) for 2-3 months. In this case, the improved phenotypes of microalgae largely relied 
on spontaneous mutation over generations. Furthermore, this approach could not indicate 
whether the improvements were consequences of evolutionary adaptation (i.e. mutation and 
selection) or physiological adaptation (i.e. acclimation). The novelties of Takouridis’ research 
were that random mutagenesis by UV radiation was utilized to generate genetic variation prior 
to sub-culturing. Additionally, the opportunity for recombination of advantageous mutations 
was provided by sexual reproduction between growth serials. Sex is advantageous for 
developing complex and mutagenic traits. The resultant strain from the sexual line was able to 
grow in up to 700 mM NaCl in comparison with a salinity limit of 300 mM NaCl for the asexual 
line (Takouridis, Tribe et al. 2014). 
Prolonged period and appropriate selective pressures are the two key elements for ALE 
experiment (Dragosits and Mattanovich 2013). The reasons for the failure of ALE in present 
==PAGE:64==
63 
 
work to improve algae growth can be considered from the perspectives of stress and time span 
for selection. First, selective pressures play an important role in accumulation of advantageous 
traits, which are usually associated with increased fitness reflected as the growth. Stresses that 
may challenge microorganisms in nature or in biotechnological processes can be categorized 
into nutrient availability and environmental stress (Nam, Conrad et al. 2011). Single specific 
stress (e.g. light, nutrient limitation or salinity) was employed to select algae for specific trait 
in previous studies (Table 7). Nevertheless, what is considered as the problem is that type and 
degree of stresses in the wastewater of present work are not specified. Wastewater condition 
can exhibit a variety of stresses to algae as reported in literature. Nevertheless, complex 
environments could lower the potential adaptive fitness that is achievable to microbial cells 
(Dragosits and Mattanovich 2013). 
 
Figure 22. Rates of genomic evolution and fitness improvement: fitness during adaptive evolution 
increases fast at the initial stage but slows down during prolonged period, which shows the beneficial 
effect of additional mutations diminish when fitness approaches the maximum(Dragosits and 
Mattanovich 2013). 
 
Additionally, time span is another important factor for the selection experiment. The duration 
of ALE in previous studies ranges from two to three months. As reported by Fu et al. in 2012, 
the growth rate of C. vulgaris was gradually increased by 45% until the biomass concentration 
reached the predicted biomass capacity of the medium (Fu, Gudmundsson et al. 2012). In theory, 
the fitness trajectory for a population is not as linear as the trajectory of genomic evolution 
(Figure 22). In contrast to the steady accumulation of mutations, the fitness improvement is 
==PAGE:65==
64 
 
initially fast and decelerates over time when the number of possible sites for beneficial 
mutations become finite and consequently the fitness gain by additional mutations declines 
(Barrick, Yu et al. 2009). Based on this, further increase in growth would not be expected in the 
current study if the fitness improvement did not appear in the beginning. However, according 
to Yu et al., the algal growths were unstable during the early stage of ALE, while gradual 
increase in biomass concentration was observed after 15 cycles (Yu, Zhao et al. 2013).  
 
5.4. Algae productivity   
Average biomass productivities and biomass concentration measured at steady states of four 
dilution rates are demonstrated in Figure 23. The cultivation was initiated with the dilution rate 
(0.18 h-1 or 4.32 d-1) close to the maximal specific growth rate (0.19 h-1) observed in a batch 
cultivation in the same wastewater. This dilution rate leads to the lowest biomass concentration 
(0.18±0.03 g l-1). With the decrease of dilution rates, biomass concentration rises to 1.44±0.01 
g l-1 corresponding to the lowest dilution rate (0.72 d-1). The highest biomass productivity 
(1.46±0.03 g l-1d-1) in the present study is exhibited at the steady state with a dilution rate of 
1.8 d-1. Due to either low biomass concentration or low flow rate, the extreme situations did not 
show high biomass productivities. The curve describing the correlation between dilution rate 
and biomass productivity is fitted to a binomial equation, with which the peak point is estimated 
to be 1.52 g l-1d-1 at a dilution rate of 2.41 d-1, corresponding to a cell density of 0.60 g l-1. 
In previous studies, the optimal dilution rates giving the maximal biomass productivity of C. 
sorokiniana were determined by separate chemostat cultivations (Cuaresma, Janssen et al. 2009) 
or deceleration-stat (D-stat) experiments (Van Wagenen, Pape et al. 2015). In Cuaresma et al.’s 
study, a photon flux density of 2,100 μmol m2s−1 was applied to chemostat experiments with 
a series of dilution rates from 0.06 h-1 to 0.26 h-1. It was found that the productivity was 
positively correlated with the increase of dilution rates, peaking at 0.24 h-1 or 5.76 d-1 and 
dropped with further increase of dilution rate. The biomass density corresponding to the 
maximal productivity (12.2 g l-1 d-1) was 2.2 g l-1. By contrast, Van Wagenen et al. performed 
D-stat experiment, in which the dilution rate controlled by software was daily decreased. This

Nitrogen starvation, in particular has been widely recognized as an environmental stress to 
stimulate the accumulation of lipid. Li et al. (2015) showed that the initial nitrogen 
concentration in the medium was positively correlated with the growth of C. sorokiniana, but 
reversely correlated with the lipid content. More specifically, the lowest growth rate was 
observed in the culture with the lowest N concentration (3 mM), whereas lipid content of 
biomass obtained in this condition was two times higher than that with 15 mM (Li, Zhao et al. 
2015). Lipid accumulation is believed to be a result of blockage of protein and starch 
biosynthesis usually appears in stationary phase with the opposite trend of the protein content 
that exhibited higher levels in exponential phase. Since nitrogen is a major constituent of 
proteins and nucleic acids, the stress caused by its limitation may regulate the metabolic shift 
(carbon flow) from nucleic/ protein synthesis for cell proliferation to energy storage (Deng, Fei 
et al. 2011). Results by real-time PCR showed that increase of lipid content with N limitation 
was associated with the unregulated expression of accD (encodes subunit of heteromeric acetyl-
CoA carboxylase) at the transition from logarithmic to stationary phase, which may be induced 
by the nutrient starvation stress (Li, Zhao et al. 2015).  
Moreover, Wan et al. characterized C. sorokiniana grown in photoautotrophic, heterotrophic 
(Wan, Wang et al. 2012) and mixotrophic conditions (Wan, Liu et al. 2011). The results 
indicated significant response in both lipid and protein content to glucose concentrations. 
Compared to photoautrophic culture, the mixotrophically and heterotrophically cultured C. 
sorokiniana showed improved productivities as well as higher lipid content. These results are 
supported by Li et al.’s findings, which demonstrated 9% of total fatty acid in the biomass from 
==PAGE:72==
71 
 
photoautotrophically cultivated C. sorokiniana. Depending on temperature and glucose 
concentration, fatty acid contents could reach over 30% of the biomass grown in mixotrophic 
or heterotrophic culture (Li, Zheng et al. 2014). In this study, a tendency of increased lipid 
content was found as a result of the increase of initial glucose concentrations in both 
heterotrophic or mixotrophic cultures, which suggest carbon availability may be a factor that 
influenced lipid accumulation. This is in agreement with Deng et al. who found microalgae 
accumulated more lipid after N or S deficiency in high carbon media, compared to the cells in 
low carbon medium. However, the result that external carbon source had little impact on lipid 
accumulation indicated carbon availability could promote lipid accumulation in N or S 
deficiency conditions, but it is not a stimulus for lipid accumulation (Deng, Fei et al. 2011). 
Furthermore, compositional changes as a response to culturing conditions may be another 
reason for low fatty acid yield. As demonstrated by Wan et al. neutral lipids accounted for about 
60% of total lipid in cells cultivated under heterotrophic culture. While polar lipids accounted 
for 96.2% of total lipid, only 2.9% of lipid was neutral lipid in photoautotrophically grown 
strain. This differentiation in lipid composition largely influences the availability of fatty acid 
for FAME production (Wan, Wang et al. 2012). Distribution of fatty acids for polar lipid or 
neutral lipid depends on physiological state of the algae that is determined by various 
environmental factors. For example, the shift of lipid production from polar lipid to neutral 
triglycerides was evidently shown by increased triglyceride: polar lipid ratios in Si-, N-, and P-
limited conditions (Lynn, Kilham et al. 2000). In addition, low irradiation induced the formation 
of polar lipids that are associated with photosynthetic and cellular membranes, whereas the 
formation of storage lipids, i.e. triacylglycerol was favored in high intensity condition 
(Khotimchenko and Yakovleva 2005). Furthermore, the effect of temperature on fatty acid 
composition of C. sorokiniana was studied by Patterson. Reducing temperature below 22℃ 
affected fatty acid composition in a way that would tend to increase protoplasmic viscosity, i.e. 
increases in carbon chain lengths and proportions of saturated fatty acids. This is considered as 
a mechanism to preserve protoplasmic viscosity in algal cells in colder habitats (Patterson 1970).  
In the present work, the high growth rate supported by sufficient nutrient supplement is 
considered the reason for the relatively low fatty acid yield. Samples used for fatty acid analysis

was harvested at the growth with a dilution rate of 4.32 d-1. Theoretically, growth rate of algae 
at steady state should be equal to the dilution rate which is the highest compared to those 
reported in literature. The fast growth was supported by the fast flow of medium into the PBR 
that provided sufficient nutrient supplies. This can be proved by nutrient removal analysis 
(section 5.6). Although results show that available organic carbon source was consumed, 
nitrogen and phosphorus were still abundant in the effluent of culture (Figure 26). Therefore, 
microalgae in this condition was unlikely to be stressed by nutrient limitation and thus tend to 
invest carbon and energy for cell growth. The high protein content 38.82±0.52%(w/w) in the 
algal biomass is an indicator for the active proliferation. In addition to the lack of nutrient stress, 
low light intensity may lead to biased formation of polar lipids, thereby further reducing the 
yield of FAME from neutral lipids.  
 
5.5.2. Pigment  
Extraction efficiencies were examined for acetone, methanol and mixed equal volumes of 
acetone and methanol as well as counterparts of these solvents with additional 10% water. As 
illustrated in Figure 26, the highest concentrations for all pigments are obtained in 90% acetone. 
In fact, the performances of pure acetone, pure methanol and the 50:50 acetone/methanol are 
comparable. However, the 10% water had opposite effects on extractions when added to acetone 
and methanol. More specific, the effects of the 10% water are +72%, -56% and -27% on acetone, 
methanol and acetone/methanol mixture respectively. This result suggests the additional water 
aided the extraction using acetone, whereas the combination of methanol and water impaired 
the extraction. Small amount of water content may facilitate the extraction by increasing 
polarity of the solvent (Rizvi 2010). Nevertheless, the increase of water content in acetone from 
10% to 50% led to 75% conversion of chlorophyll-a to chlorophyllide. With the same water 
content, methanol-based solvents caused additional breakdown/ transformation of chlorophyll-
a (93% conversion), as a result of transesterification by chlrophyllase or formation of allomeric 
forms of chlorophyll-a (Mantoura and Llewellyn 1983).

5.6. Nutrient removal  
After the harvest of biomass, nutrient contents in the supernatant were measured and compared 
with the composition of untreated wastewater. Nutrient removal efficiencies at the steady states 
of four dilution rates are shown in Figure 28. Overall, the highest removal efficiencies (> 90%) 
with the exception of COD are observed at the lowest dilution rate (0.72 d-1). With the decrease 
of dilution rate, the removals of total nitrogen, total phosphorus and ammonium are steadily 
increased. However, the removal of COD for all dilution rates is maintained around 50%. The 
results shown by nitrogen, phosphorus and ammonium removal indicate that lower dilution 
rates lead to higher percentage of removed nutrient by algae. This is also demonstrated by Van 
Wagenen et al. regarding the removals of ammonium and COD. One likely reason is that at low 
dilution rate, wastewater is retained in the reactor and experience treatment by algae for more 
time. In addition, the higher concentration of biomass resulted from low dilution rate should 
also contribute to the increase of nutrient uptake by algae.  
However, COD removal is unchanged for all dilution rates. This indicates that algae treatment 
removed approximately 50% of COD in the wastewater, whereas the other ~50% of COD was 
contributed by organics that was undegradable by microalgae. Since the up limit of COD 
removal is reached at the highest dilution rate, it is suggested that organics may be the first 
nutrient that was consumed by microalgae and at this stage microalgae were likely to live on 
mixotrophic growth. Additionally, after the exhaust of available organic carbon source, 
photosynthetic growth proceed for further algal growth and uptake of other nutrients.  
One result differs from the previous study is the removal of phosphorus. According to Van 
Wagenen et al., the phosphorus was nearly completely consumed in most measurements 
regardless of dilution rate, which indicates phosphorus was a limiting nutrient in IC wastewater. 
By contrast, phosphorus removal changes simultaneously with nitrogen removal and therefore 
it was not limited in the Kohtla-Järve influent. This can be proved by N/P ratios which is 17.3 
in the IC wastewater and 6.75 in the KJ influent.

5.8. Sensitivity analysis  
Economics of algal biomass production was assessed in three scenarios with assumptions 
regarding cost reduction. The result indicates the economic potential can be positive only when 
the cost for artificial light is eliminated (Figure 30). Specifically, substitution of artificial light 
with sunlight can reduce production cost by 94%, whereas the reduction resulted from using 
free CO2 is 3%. The elimination of CO2 cost has relatively little effect (+8%) on economics. By 
contrast, although 15% reduction in biomass productivity causes 12% decrease of the total 
revenue, economical potential can be increased by 160% and becomes positive as a result of 
substantial drop in cost for artificial light (Table 14).  
This analysis highlights that the avoidance of using artificial light is an imperative to enable 
sustainable production of algal biomass for any purpose. In the base case, at least 55.8% of the 
cost for artificial light needs to be reduced to ensure breakeven for the necessary utilities. In the 
case that excludes the costs for CO2 and light, biomass cost is reduced to 202 € t-1, which is too 
low in comparison with 5,960 € t-1 reported by (Norsker, Barbosa et al. 2011) and 2,340 $ t-1 
reported by (Chauton, Reitan et al. 2015). Exclusion of capital cost and operational cost such 
as labor and general plant overhead is one major reason for the underestimation. Furthermore, 
some basic assumptions for the calculation are different. For example, blower power accounted 
for the biggest fraction of cost in Norsker et al.’s calculation, which is relatively low in the 
present work.

Chapter 6: Conclusion and future work 
The present study demonstrated that the microalga C. sorokiniana can well adapt to the 
wastewater chosen for this assessment (KJ) and thus exhibit high biomass productivity. The 
composition of the resulting biomass was determined in order to assess if a biorefinery approach 
could offset the production costs. The results showed that, due to the low economic potential 
shown by biodiesel, other high value products, e.g. pigments, play a pivotal role in economics 
of algae production and should be the primary goal to pursue. Moreover, significant 
contribution to the overall concept derived from the profit generated by the removal of N and 
P from the wastewater. This demonstrated the benefit of coupling algae production with 
wastewater treatment. However, the economic assessment and sensitivity analysis proved that 
the energy consumption for artificial light is the major obstacle a sustainable cultivation of 
microalgae. 
Growth performance and cellular characterization in this study corresponded to a condition with 
constant light supply, which however was proved uneconomic. Hence, assessment of algal 
productivities defined by natural light source is necessary for the economic analysis in a 
scenario eliminating the cost for artificial light. Moreover, economic potential analysis 
represented here is a preliminary assessment to identify opportunities and challenges in a 
production process. Rough estimation was done by only considering part of operational costs 
for basic utilities, but capital investment are known to be a considerable cost for algae 
production with photobioreactor. Therefore, techno-economic analysis is required to examine 
the economic feasibility.
