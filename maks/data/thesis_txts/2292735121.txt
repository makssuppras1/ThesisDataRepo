Abstract 
Moving Bed Biofilm Rectors systems (MBBRs) emerged as a robust technology for wastewater 
treatment and have been shown a notable performance with respect to denitrification and 
the pharmaceuticals removal. In order to assess the performance of MBBR biofilm under 
different electron availability conditions (COD content), previous experimental research 
conducted in this department assessed the performance of a three-staged configuration 
consisting of three reactors in series (reactors S1, S2, S3), in comparison with a reference 
unstaged configuration (reactor U). The main objective of the current MSc thesis project was 
to model the primary and the secondary metabolism in the aforementioned MBBR system, 
using the data obtained from the targeted batch experiments. 
For this purpose, the first step was to develop a biological model. The biological model aimed 
at describing the denitrification process (primary metabolism) and the elimination of 
pharmaceuticals (secondary metabolism). More in detail, the primary metabolism was 
modelled using a multi-step denitrification model with and without electron competition 
among the four denitrification steps. These two models are the Activated Sludge Model for 
Nitrogen (ASM-N) and Activated Sludge Model with Indirect Coupling of Electrons (ASM-ICE), 
respectively. The predictive accuracy of these two models was tested against a first 
independent data set, including results from a batch experiment with biofilm from reactor S1. 
One of the two models was eventually selected for calibration of a more detailed data set, 
including batch experimental results from reactors S1, S2, S3 and U. Based on further modelling 
results obtained during the project, the importance of storage during denitrification was 
investigated and a two-step and a four step denitrification models based on the Activated 
Sludge Model No. 3 (ASM3) were examined and implemented. 
The Monte Carlo Method was chosen for calibrating the examined models to the 
experimental data and eventually for parameter estimation. Moreover, different objectives 
functions for the evaluation of the model calibration were tested leading to the conclusion 
that RSR (RMSE-observation standard deviation ratio) was able to provide more accurate 
prediction by calibrating multiple objective variables. A preliminary sensitivity analysis, 
considering different parameter combinations for each model, was performed by assessing 
the degree of linearization of the model output (R2) based on the assessed parameters using 
Monte Carlo simulations. 
Simulations results showed that the ASM-N model with the estimation of growth reduction 
factors for the first two steps of denitrification (ng1, ng2) and the hydrolysis rate constant (kh) 
describes adequately the denitrification process contingent on the results obtained from the 
independent batch experiment in reactor S1. The degree of the linearization was sufficient 
(R2>0.7) to consider this parameter combination as identifiable. In contrast, ASM-ICE model 
provided less accurate results and estimation of the heterotrophic yield (YH) was unavoidable. 
==PAGE:8==
Hence, the ASM-N model was selected for the calibration of a new data set of batch 
experiments, with estimation of ng1, ng2 and kh. The results showed that the ASM-N model 
was again in line with the experimental results derived from reactor S1, whereas the model 
could not match satisfactorily the experimental results from the reactors S2, U (partly) and S3 
(more evidently). Inconsistencies likely derived from the significantly different denitrification 
(mg NO3-N reduced per mg COD utilized) potentials registered in the different reactors. 
Estimation of the heterotrophic yield was then considered in the calibration, resulting in high 
estimated values (YH>0.6), which are not in agreement with literature evidences for anoxic 
systems. 
This led us to hypothesize that the storage of substrate may occur. Therefore, a new kinetic 
model, which includes the anoxic storage process was developed. The ASM3 model extended 
for a two-step and a four-step denitrification was calibrated by the Monte Carlo method and 
the selected modelling objective function as previously reported. The results show that 
improvement of predictions, as compared to experimental data, could be obtained with the 
four-step denitrification model. Estimation of the anoxic growth coefficients (ng1 and ng2), the 
anoxic storage coefficients (ng1_STO and ng2_STO) for the first two steps of the denitrification 
process and the hydrolysis rate constant resulted in a good match between the measured and 
the simulated results, satisfying the selected criterion of model linearization for parameter 
identifiability (R2>0.7). Thus, it is noticeable that the influence of the feast-famine conditions 
may be significant, since the new kinetic approach of storage process can describe sufficiently 
the experimental results, as regards the reactors S2, S3 and U. 
With respect to secondary metabolism, an Activated Sludge Modelling framework for 
Xenobiotics (ASM-X) was tested to predict the fate of three pharmaceuticals, 
Sulfamethoxazole (SMX), Clarithromycin (CLA) and Atenolol (ATE), in the MBBR reactors 
under batch conditions. Solid-liquid partitioning coefficients (Kd) and biotransformation rate 
coefficients for these pharmaceuticals were estimated. Retransformation to the parent 
compound (from, e.g. conjugated metabolites) was observed only for SMX. Estimated 
biotransformation rate coefficients for CLA and ATE were found to be higher than what 
reported in literature for activated sludge systems. 
We note that the study did not employ an appropriate biofilm model to account for the 
transport processes around and within the biofilm. Hence, diffusion limitation is only 
accounted for by lumping its effect through model calibration through setting the value of 
selected the half-saturation coefficients and the sorption coefficients.  
 
 
Keywords: Denitrification modelling, ASMN, ASM-ICE, ASM-X, MBBRs, Monte Carlo Method, 
Modelling objective, Uncertainty, Microbial storage

Chapter 1 Introduction 
The release of nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus) owing to human activities can cause 
eutrophication which has detrimental effects on the ecosystem. Therefore, denitrification is 
of great importance since it is used in order to ameliorate the water quality by removing the 
nitrogen pollutants (von Schulthess & Gujer, 1996), in combination with the nitrification. 
Denitrification is the biological reduction of the nitrate (NO3-) to nitrogen gas (N2) and it 
consists of four reduction steps, which generate nitrite (NO2-), nitric oxide (NO) and nitrous 
oxide (N2O) (Pan, et al., 2013a). The inhibition of denitrification process resulting in the 
accumulation of the nitrogen intermediates is considered to be a long-existing environmental 
issue. Specifically, attention has been paid on the N2O due to its damaging effect on the 
atmosphere (Ni, et al., 2011). 
In the last decade, the use of pharmaceuticals is an emerging environmental issue since it has 
raised large concerns from the perspective of the potential environmental impacts. The 
observed concentrations of pharmaceuticals or other micropollutants detected in 
wastewater lead to the conclusion that municipal wastewater is the main disposal pathway. 
Due to the fact that many of them are persistent and polar compounds, are only partially 
removed and are still present in the sewage treatment plant (STP) effluents. Therefore, 
attention must be paid on the micropollutants removal mechanisms and removal efficiency 
of wastewater treatment systems (Joss & Ternes, 2006).  
Among the various treatment systems, great deal of interest is given on the biofilm systems 
since they provide several advantages compared to suspended growth systems for the 
removal of conventional pollutants (i.e. COD, N) and of trace organic pollutants (i.e. 
pharmaceuticals). In particular, Moving Bed Biofilm Reactors (MBBRs) is an effective 
technology and a reliable option for biological nutrient removal (nitrification, denitrification). 
According to the literature, an enhancement of the biotransformation potential of 
pharmaceuticals in MBBRs, comparing with the activated sludge systems is indicated under 
aerobic conditions (Falås , et al., 2012). 
This master project constitutes the continuation of a previous master thesis work with the 
title ‘’ Staged and unstaged moving bed biofilm reactors for the removal of conventional 
pollutants and xenobiotic trace chemicals’’ (Loreggian, 2014), in terms of biofilm modelling. 
The most important details of the aforementioned master research work are that two parallel 
MBBR systems operating under denitrifying conditions, have been set up and assessed with 
respect to denitrification performance (primary metabolism) and elimination of more 
recalcitrant compounds, in that case pharmaceuticals (secondary metabolism).

An unstaged configuration used as a reference system and a staged configuration composed 
of three reactors in series are included in order to investigate the differences in the electron 
availability (i.e., COD availability for nitrate reduction), which has substantial impact on the 
reduction of the nitrate and the biological transformation (Plósz, 2007). 
In order to anticipate the influence of the nitrogen oxides in the denitrification and 
consequently to predict the nitrogen removal during wastewater treatment, it is essential to 
develop a denitrification mathematical model, including the reduction of N-oxides                   
(von Schulthess & Gujer, 1996; Pan, et al., 2013a). In this study, different multi-step 
denitrifications models were developed and implemented with a view to assess the 
denitrification performance. Concerning the fate of pharmaceuticals and illicit drugs in 
suspended biomass systems, an Activated Sludge Modelling framework for Xenobiotics (ASM-
X) was developed (Plósz, et al., 2010; Plósz, et al., 2012; Plósz, et al., 2013).  
Thesis objective 
The aim of this MSc thesis is to model and calibrate process model that occur in denitrifying 
MBBR system. For that reason, a biological process model was developed and calibrated 
describing the primary metabolism, using a multi-step denitrification sub-model and the 
secondary metabolism, using the ASM-X framework for pharmaceuticals. This biological 
model was calibrated using the Monte Carlo Method, therefore the experimental results 
derived from the batch experiments for the four reactors were used with a view to calibration 
methodology of the selected parameters. 
Thesis outline 
A more detailed description of this thesis work is presented below. 
Chapter 1 (Introduction) refers to the motivation for this study, the objective and the 
structure of the present thesis. 
Chapter 2 (Literature Review) presents the main findings in the literature which are important 
as a background for understanding the fundamental concepts. Hence, the heterotrophic 
denitrification (primary metabolism), the fate of the pharmaceuticals (secondary 
metabolism), the principles of the biofilm systems and precisely of the MBBRs systems are 
thoroughly analyzed.  
Chapter 3 (Methodology) demonstrates the existing experimental set-up and the basic 
operating conditions are given. Furthermore, the step-wise procedure, which is followed in 
this master project, is described.

Chapter 2 Literature review 
This chapter covers the needed theoretical background in order to carry out this project. In this 
section, an introduction of the primary and the secondary metabolism is presented. The 
definitions of the denitrification process and the fate of pharmaceuticals are given, as well as 
the general principles of the biofilm systems and more specifically the characteristics of the 
Moving Bed Biofilm Reactors (MBBR).  
2.1 Primary and secondary metabolism 
During biological wastewater treatment, microbial biomass utilizes a range of substrates. A 
typical distinction between primary and secondary substrates (and their associated 
metabolism) is considered, depending on the benefit provided by these to microbial cells. 
Primary substrates are essential for growth and the maintenance of the bacteria, providing 
the electron and energy flow to support biomass growth. On the other hand, secondary 
substrate does not provide the energy and the electrons to synthesize new biomass and 
maintain the existing one (Rittman, 1992). In the specific case of heterotrophic denitrification, 
primary substrates are represented by electron donor (biodegradable COD) and by the 
nitrogen oxides which in the absence of oxygen are the most common terminal electron 
acceptors (Plósz, et al., 2003), whereas secondary substrate typically include xenobiotic 
organic chemicals, present at ng/L to μg/L levels, such as pharmaceuticals. 
 2.1.1 Primary metabolism: Heterotrophic Denitrification  
Denitrification in combination with nitrification are the key processes in order to remove the 
nitrogen pollutants from wastewater (Tchobanoglous, et al., 2003). Heterotrophic 
denitrification process reduces the nitrates, which is generated from autotrophic nitrification, 
to nitrogen gas (N2). Thus, nitrogen is removed from the water phase in order to enhance the 
water quality (Lu, et al., 2014).  
Biological heterotrophic denitrification consists of four consecutive steps and the 
intermediated nitrite (NO2-), nitric oxide (NO) and nitrous oxide (N2O) are produced by the 
following reduction steps (Pan, et al., 2013a): 
 Nitrate reduction to nitrite 
 Nitrite reduction to nitric oxide 
 Nitric oxide reduction to nitrous oxide 
 Nitrous oxide reduction to nitrogen gas 
NO3-→ NO2-→ NO (g) → N2O (g) →N2 (g)

The high level of microbial diversity makes it challenging to determine which organisms are 
important for in- situ denitrification in wastewater treatment plants (Wagner & Loy, 2002). 
Although, many organisms have the ability to denitrify such as heterotrophic bacteria, 
archaea and some eukaryotes (e.g. fungi), the denitrification is primarily conducted by 
bacteria. Most denitrifying bacteria are facultative anaerobes using ionic and gaseous 
nitrogen oxides as electron acceptors in the absence of oxygen (Lu, et al., 2014). Pseudomonas 
are the most common specie of heterotrophic denitrifiers and can degrade complex organic 
substrates, such as aromatic compounds (Knowles, 1982).  
In the absence of molecular oxygen denitrifying organisms can respire nitrate or nitrite 
through a chain of enzymatic reactions coupled to the bacterial inner membrane. Synthesis 
of the enzymes, which catalyze the reduction steps, is induced under anoxic conditions 
including four denitrification reductases, namely nitrate reductase (Nar), nitrite reductase 
(Nir), NO reductase (Nor) and N2O reductase (Nos) (Zumft, 1997). The synthesis and the 
expression of the aforementioned enzymes play an important role to the process. The living 
bacteria in the reactor and the environmental conditions (i.e. DO, pH, COD/NO3-N) influence 
the synthesis of the new enzymes. For instance, the limited availability of carbon sources can 
result in accumulation of N2O during denitrification (Pan, et al., 2013). Hence, the deviation 
of these environmental conditions from the optimum leads to the inhibition of the complete 
denitrification resulting in the accumulations of the gas intermediates; NO and N2O (Chiu & 
Chung, 2003). 
Therefore, attention must be paid on the accumulation of N-oxide intermediates due to their 
adverse effects on the environment. The NO is considered a toxic denitrification intermediate 
to most organisms including denitrifying ones (Stein & Yung, 2003; Stouthamer, et al., 1997). 
N2O is deemed to be a potent greenhouse gas with a 300- times higher global warming 
potential than carbon dioxide and a primary ozone depleting substance (Schreiber, et al., 
2012). Despite its relatively low atmospheric concentration (~322 ppbv), it seems that nitrous 
oxide contributes to 6-8% of anthropogenic greenhouse gas emissions (Montzka, et al., 2011). 
Hence, Ν2Ο accumulation during denitrification should be avoided showing that just 1% of 
denitrified nitrogen is emitted as N2O lead to an increase of approximately 30% of the carbon 
footprint of a typical biological nitrogen removal wastewater treatment plant (Pan, et al., 
2013a). As it was mentioned before, the inhibition of the process can be provoked due to the 
fact the operating conditions deviate from the optimum. The incomplete denitrification 
results in the accumulation of nitric and nitrous oxide. 
The main factors that control the heterotrophic denitrification in WWTP and consequently 
the accumulation of N-oxide intermediates are the following (Thomas, et al., 1994): 
 The dissolved oxygen (DO) 
 pH 
 the COD/NO3-N ratio

The concentration of dissolved oxygen 
Denitrification is inhibited by the presence of oxygen since a competition between nitrogen 
oxides and oxygen as electron acceptor can occur for the heterotrophic species. At high 
concentrations of DO, the denitrification activity is deteriorated resulting in the accumulation 
of nitrogenous intermediates (Lu, et al., 2014). The presence of oxygen suppresses the 
expression and the activity of nearly all N reductases. The nitrite reductase appears lower 
sensitivity to O2 than nitrate reductase (Korner & Zumft, 1989). Regarding the nitric oxide 
reductase, its activity is approximately 10 times higher than the activity of the nitrite 
reductase leading to the conclusion that the accumulation of the toxic NO is ensured to be 
low (Otte, et al., 1996a). The most sensitive enzyme to oxygen is the nitrous oxide reductase 
and consequently the nitrous oxide emissions are high under aerobic conditions (Knowles, 
1982; Otte, et al., 1996a).  
pH 
Concerning the wastewater denitrification, the optimal pH is in the range of 7-9, and out of 
this range the denitrification activity is rapidly diminished (Grady, et al., 1999). Nonetheless, 
denitrification may occur up to pH 11 in wastes (Knowles, 1982). The N2O reduction was 
observed to have higher pH dependency than nitrate and nitrite reduction rates (Pan, et al., 
2012). Consequently, the deviation of the pH optimum provokes the inhibition of the 
denitrification resulting in higher nitric and nitrous oxides emissions. The pH is increased 
during the denitrification since the protons are consumed in order nitrates to be reduced to 
nitrogen. In particular, 3.57 gr of alkalinity as CaCO3 are produced per 1 gr of NO3-N 
(Tchobanoglous, et al., 2003). 
COD/NO3-N 
The ratio of readily biodegradable COD to nitrate (Ss/ NO3-N) is an most important factor for 
the design of the anoxic selectors. Theoretically, 2.86/(1-YH) mg COD are required in order to 
reduce 1 mg NO3-N completely to nitrogen gas. Hence, the availability of the electrons in 
organic carbon controls the activity of the heterotrophic denitrifiers (Knowles, 1982). For a 
high level of nitrogen removal, a ratio of 6-8 is required (Pan, et al., 2013). Finally, it has been 
observed, that the increase of COD/N ratio enhanced the denitrification capacity in an anoxic 
reactor treating landfill leachate (Sun, et al., 2012). 
During biological wastewater treatment, the electron donors for denitrification can be 
provided from two sources, i.e. the indigenous COD and/or externally dosed COD (e.g., 
methanol, ethanol, acetate). The former includes the COD present in the influent wastewater 
or generated within the biological reactor from, e.g. hydrolysis. The latter are organics added 
to the treatment system and specifically dosed into the anoxic zone(s) to enhance wastewater 
denitrification, e.g. methanol, acetate, etc. In this study, the focus is on internal COD sources 
for denitrification.

Specifically, there are three internal COD sources. The two main forms of organics in the 
wastewater are the readily biodegradable COD (SS) and the slowly biodegradable/ 
hydrolysable COD (XS). The last internal organic source is the slowly biodegradable COD which 
are generated by the biomass death and lysis (Ekama & Wentzel, 2008). Endogenous organic 
material is liberated by the biomass decay process producing inert biomass debris XP and XS. 
Taking into consideration, the different degradation mechanisms and utilization rates of SS 
and XS, the position of the anoxic zone in the biological treatment train has a considerable 
effect on the denitrification performance that can be achieved. Two common configurations 
are used, i.e. the pre-denitrification and the post-denitrification, depending on whether the 
anoxic bioreactor is located up- or downstream to the aerobic (nitrifying) bioreactor and on 
whether influent or externally dosed COD is used as electron donor.  
Although, post-denitrification can be based on decay products as electron donor source, in 
most cases an external carbon source is added in favor of increase of the denitrification rates 
and subsequently of reduction of the total nitrate (Tchobanoglous, et al., 2003). Some of the 
carbon sources that can be added are methanol, acetate, ethanol and glycerol which have 
been evaluated in terms of denitrification kinetics. In general, the use of carbon sources that 
have low cost and low biomass yield is desirable (Lu, et al., 2014).  
2.1.1.1 Storage during denitrification 
Several publications have appeared in recent years documenting the fact that storage process 
may occur during denitrification (Ni & Yu, 2008; Kaelin, et al., 2009).The storage products can 
be polymers like poly-β-hydroxybutyrate (PHB) and can be formed in the case that the 
substrate is not necessary for direct growth processes. The formation of storage polymers 
under anoxic conditions is coupled with energy generation and anoxic respiration, and their 
formation rate is found to be proportional to the substrate utilization rate and when the 
external substrate is not available, these stored products can be utilized (Ni & Yu, 2008a). 
2.1.1.2 Process Models for Denitrification  
Several mathematical models have been described with a view to nitrogen removal during 
wastewater treatment plant. One-step complete denitrification is proposed in ASMs models 
using Monod kinetics for growth and neglecting the nitrite as an intermediate product (from 
NO3- directly to nitrogen gas) (Henze, et al., 2000). Although, the one step denitrification 
model is applicable for a conventional wastewater process, in some cases the concentration 
of nitrite can be considerable due to the inhibition of the denitrifiers (Oh & Silverstein, 1999).

Large concerns have also been raised for the accumulation of other denitrification 
intermediates, NO and N2O. Overall, a one-step model is insufficient in predicting the 
accumulation of denitrification intermediates, and extension of the original concept have 
been proposed including two-step (NO3  NO2  N2; (Ni & Yu, 2008)), three-step (NO3  
NO2  N2O  N2; (von Schulthess, et al., 1994; Plósz, et al., 2003) and four-step 
denitrification (NO3  NO2  NO  N2O  N2; (Hiatt & Grady, 2008; Ni, et al., 2011). 
In the latter approach, each reduction step is described to have individual reaction specific 
kinetics, using NO3-, NO2-, NO and N2O as the terminal electron acceptors. Recent studies (Pan, 
et al., 2013a; Pan, et al., 2015) showed that the electron competition among the enzymes 
involved in the four steps of denitrification plays a crucial role in N2O accumulation, a fact that 
is not recognized in the previously listed multi-step denitrification models (Pan et al., 2013).  
In this study, the investigation was initially addressed at assessing and comparing two four-
step denitrification models, ASM-N (Activated Sludge Model for Nitrogen; (Hiatt & Grady, 
2008) and ASM-ICE (Activated Sludge Model with Indirect Coupling of Electrons; (Pan, et al., 
2013a), corresponding to a ”direct coupling approach” and ”indirect coupling approach”, 
respectively (Pan, et al., 2015).  
In the ASMs models (Henze, et al., 2000) and specifically the ASM3, which includes also the 
storage process (Gujer , et al., 1999), the nitrite concentration is not taken into consideration. 
Since it is significant the nitrite concentration as well as the concentration of the nitrous oxide 
to be modelled also in the case of ASM3, the model is extended to a two-step and a four-step 
denitrification model, respectively.

2.1.2 Secondary Metabolism: Pharmaceuticals  
Besides conventional pollutants, an emerging issue is the occurrence and the fate of 
pharmaceuticals in the environment. Pharmaceuticals and personal care products (PPCPs) 
originated from human therapy were found in the aquatic environment. (Heberer, 2002) 
Conventional Sewage Treatment Plants (STPs) are typically designed to eliminate 
conventional pollutants ,i.e. organic matter and nutrients (nitrogen, phosphorus) from the 
wastewater, since removal mechanisms under different redox conditions are efficient and 
applicable for these conventional contaminants (Suarez, et al., 2010).In contrast, xenobiotic 
micropollutants and among them pharmaceuticals are not eliminated completely and are 
discharged as contaminants into the water bodies. Therefore, understanding the behavior of 
PPCPs under different redox conditions is essential in order to predict the main removal 
mechanisms of these contaminants occurring in full-scale STPs and, further, when released in 
the environments (i.e. groundwater discharge, degradation in surface, etc.) (Suarez, et al., 
2010). 
According to the literature, it can be tentatively estimated that around 70% of the compounds 
are excreted in the urine and around 30% in the faeces (Hlavinek, et al., 2008). It is worth 
mentioning that pharmaceuticals are mainly excreted in parent form and/or as metabolites. 
Excreted metabolites include conjugates of the parent compound with hydrophilic groups 
that facilitate the excretion in urine (Henze, et al., 1996). Conjugates have been shown to 
retransform back to parent pharmaceuticals (Joss & Ternes, 2006; Plósz, et al., 2010; Plósz, et 
al., 2013), leading to the conclusion that metabolite need to be included when investigating 
the fate of pharmaceuticals.  
Little is known about the extent of environmental occurrence and ultimate fate of 
pharmaceuticals. With regard to potential pathways for PPCPs into the environment, it can 
be observed (Figure 4), that PPCPs can be disposed as solid waste or in wastewater effluent. 
More than 80 compounds, pharmaceuticals and several drugs metabolites have been found

downstream from municipal STPs (Heberer, 2002).

Biotransformation  
The removal by biological transformation is generally described with pseudo-first-order 
kinetics and assessed under controlled experimental batch conditions. The term “first order” 
refers to the transformation rate being proportional to the substance concentration S in 
aqueous form and the term ‘“pseudo” refers to the rate being proportional to the biomass 
concentration (expressed as gTSS/L or gVSS/L) assumed constant for short term batch 
observations (Joss & Ternes, 2006). Pseudo-first-order kinetics removal rates were also used 
to describe biotransformation of pharmaceuticals in batch experiments with MBBR carriers 
(Falås , et al., 2012). The extent of biotransformation for a specific pharmaceutical is typically 
expressed using the biotransformation rate coefficient kBio (L/(gTSS*d) or L/(gVSS*d)). Readily 
biodegradable substrates (SS) were shown to have a significant impact on the 
biotransformation of pharmaceuticals, either inhibiting (Plósz, et al., 2010) or enhancing 
(Plósz et al., 2012) the process. 
Sorption onto suspended/attached particles 
Sorption onto particulate matter can be an important process for the removal mechanism in 
municipal wastewater treatment and it depends on the tendency of micropollutants to 
partition onto the suspended solids. The solid- liquid partitioning characteristics of a 
compound is described by the sorption coefficient Kd (Joss & Ternes, 2006). Under anoxic 
conditions, hydrophobicity-independent mechanisms can influence the solid-liquid 
partitioning which is taken into account by using the sorption coefficient (Plósz, et al., 2010). 
As pointed out by Joss & Ternes (2006), the solid-water partitioning is a rapid process for 
many pharmaceuticals therefore it is considered that it has a small contribution to the 
observed pharmaceutical removal. However, low transport rates occurring in biofilm systems 
may lead to prolonged solid-water equilibrium and subsequently in that case the sorption 
contributes to the removal of the pharmaceuticals in biofilm reactors. Based on limited 
experimental findings (Falås , et al., 2012), for some compounds (i.e. Diclofenac, Ketoprofen, 
Ibuprofen etc.), the sorption process did not affect significantly the observed pharmaceutical 
removal in the case of biofilm, leading to the preliminary conclusion that the biological 
transformation is the main removal pathway in MBBRs

Retransformation 
Another factor that can influence the removal of pharmaceuticals is the occurrence of 
retransformation processes. Deconjugation of human metabolites can occur and certain 
metabolites can be transformed back to the parent compound by enzyme-catalyzed 
reactions, in which conjugated hydrophilic groups (e.g., acetyl) are cleaved. Moreover, it is 
possible that the concentration of human metabolites that can be retransformed to the 
parent compound may be greater than the parent compound itself. This may have a 
significant impact on the removal efficiency of pharmaceuticals in lab- and full-scale 
wastewater treatment systems, making evaluations misleading when based only on the 
parent compounds (Plósz, et al., 2010). 
2.1.2.2 The pharmaceuticals, SMX, CLA and ATE 
The biological transformation and the fate of three pharmaceuticals, namely 
sulfamethoxazole (SMX), clarithromycin (CLA) and atenolol (ATE) were examined in this study.  
SMX is one of the most widely synthesized sulfonamides and it is used in human and 
veterinary medicine (Radke, et al., 2009; Xu , et al., 2011). Specifically, it is prescribed to treat 
urinary infections and it is widely used for veterinary practice, aquaculture and livestock 
breeding for treating diseases promoting growth (Drillia , et al., 2005). The two major human 
metabolites of SMX are the N4-acetyl-SMX (Ac-SMX) and SMX-N1 glucuronide (SMX-Glu)

present as SMX-Glu, only 15–25% of the SMX as unchanged drug is excreted. SMX is highly 
soluble and can sorb to soil organic matter through cation bridging and the cation exchange 
mechanisms. As a result, the SMX can potentially migrate from livestock wastes and soil to

occurrence of SMX in effluents of WWTPs, in rivers and in groundwater, (Tamtam, et al., 
2008). 
CLA is an amino macrolide antibiotic and is used to treat many different types of bacterial 
infection, which affect the skin and respiratory system. It has very poor solubility at neutral 
intestinal pH, however it presents higher solubility under acidic conditions because of the 
amine protonation (Pereira, et al., 2013). Moreover, CLA undergoes rapid biodegradation to 
produce the microbiologically active 14-hydroxy-(R)-metabolite, which is mainly excreted in 
the urine with the parent compound (Fraschini, et al., 1993). As recently pointed out (Oguz & 
Mihciokur, 2014), antibiotics such as CLA have significant adsorption capacity than other 
medicines (i.e. high Kd values) and can potentially represent high risk to the environment, due 
to the tendency to accumulate in living bodies and. It is also important to remark that the 
biodegradability of this drug is quite low, hence it is possible to be discharged from biological 
wastewater treatment plants to the receiving water bodies without being treated.

2.2 Bioreactor staging for denitrification  
As mentioned earlier in this chapter, different types of COD (readily biodegradable, hydrolysis 
products and hydrolysed decay material) can contribute as electron donor in wastewater. 
Based on this concept, staging of biological reactors for wastewater treatment has been 
suggested with view to increase the reaction rates in denitrification zone. Denitrification 
efficiency was shown to be improved by using the reactor staging concept (Plósz, 2007). A 
shorter sludge retention time and less amount of competent biomass are required and 
subsequently the required settler area is reduced.  
Based on combined modelling and experimental findings, the optimum number of stages was 
found to be three (Scuras, et al., 2001). In the first anoxic stage denitrification is achieved 
through the utilization if non-limiting readily biodegradable COD (SS) present in the influent 
and hydrolysis products, thus occurring at maximum rates. The slowly biodegradable 
substrate, XS, becomes available for denitrification through the hydrolysis process producing 
SS. In the second stage denitrification is achieved through the uptake SS (now at limiting 
concentrations) and hydrolysis products, thus occurring at lower rates than in the first stage. 
In case of uneven organic substrate loading, the second stage can, also provide flexibility to 
the entire system. Ultimately, in the third stage, the bacterial community is specialized to the 
utilization of hydrolysis products of the slowly biodegradable COD, also resulting from 
bacterial decay (Plósz, 2007). As it can be observed by Figure 5, the initial and fast 
denitrification by the uptake SS is followed by slowly increasing reciprocal denitrification 
values by the oxidation of hydrolysis products of XS. Therefore, it can be concluded that the 
initial ratio SS/ XS plays a decisive role to the design of the size of the anoxic zone (Plósz, 2007) 
Due to the limited availability of the biodegradable carbon, it is hypothesized that recalcitrant 
substances and secondary substrate are consumed to higher extents in the latter stages. 
Therefore, bioreactor staging may enhance the degradation of more recalcitrant substances 
(Scuras, et al., 2001)

2.3 Biofilm and MBBR systems 
There has been a significant increase in the interest in biofilm reactors for municipal and 
industrial wastewater treatment since they present several advantages and are more 
desirable in many cases compared to activated sludge reactors The main difference between 
the biofilm reactors and the activated sludge systems is that concerning the former, the 
microorganisms are immobilised in a dense layer attached to a solid phase, while in the latter 
they are free to move in suspension, typically aggregated in flocs. Hence, a settler is not 
required in order to maintain the biomass active in a biofilm reactor. Moreover, the bacteria 
in suspension can be washed out with the water flow, while in biofilms they are protected 
against harmful shock loads (Morgenroth, 2008).

boundary layer, biofilm and substratum (Wanner, et al., 2004)

The biofilm structure is composed of bacteria embedded in a matrix of extracellular polymeric 
substances (EPS) containing proteins, free nucleic acid and water (Morgenroth, 2008). 
Basically, the EPS acts as glue and it holds the biofilm on the carriers. In addition, EPS can 
control the impact of changes in pH, temperature and concertation of toxic substances 
(Wilderer & McSwain, 2004). Biofilms exhibit complexity and heterogeneity both physically 
and microbiologically, with gradients in substrate concentrations and microbial guilds 
populating the biofilm. A typical structure of biofilm systems is presented in Figure 6 (Wanner, 
et al., 2004). 
Many biofilm systems are in use nowadays such as tricking filters, rotating biological 
contractors (RBC), fluidized bed reactors, granular media biofilters. These technologies 
present a number of disadvantages, e.g., large volume requirements (trickling filters), 
possibility of mechanical failures (RBCs), hydraulic instability (fluidized bed reactors) and 
discontinuous operation due to the necessity of backwashing (granular media biofilters). 
In order to amend these drawbacks of the aforementioned biofilm technologies, the moving 
bed biofilm reactor (MBBR) technology was developed, which use the biofilm carriers in order 
the active biomass surface in the reactors to be maximized (Rusten, et al., 2006). In this way, 
MBBR represents a combination of the advantages of suspended and attached growth 
systems. Besides reduced space requirement and no necessity of separation, specialization of 
biomass is promoted due to attached growth (Ødegaard, et al., 1994). In MBBR reactors, the 
whole tank volume is utilized for biomass growth since the carriers are able to move freely 
within the reactor. Furthermore, it is considered as a robust, compact and flexible technology 
(Rusten, et al., 2006). Concerning the municipal sector, the MBBR process is regarded as a 
technology for newly constructed wastewater treatment plants that is appropriate for carbon 
oxidation, nitriﬁcation, and denitriﬁcation (Boltz, et al., 2009). MBBR has been applied also 
for denitrification since high denitrification rates have been shown (Ødegaard, 2006). 
Ultimately, biofilm from MBBR systems has demonstrated a higher pharmaceutical 
biotransformation potential per unit biomass compared to activated sludge (Falås , et al., 
2012)

3.1.2 The batch experiments 
Targeted batch experiments were performed in order to assess and compare biokinetics of 
heterotrophic denitrification and pharmaceutical biotransformation for the biofilm grown in 
each reactor.  
Biofilm carriers were removed from reactors used for continuous flow operation and inserted 
in dedicated reactors used for batch experiments. The same pre-clarified wastewater medium 
was used as feed for all reactors. External NO3 was added to the wastewater medium, 
resulting in an initial concentration of approximately 100 mgNO3-N/L. The filling ratio was 
adjusted to 10% to ensure sufficiently detailed observations over the course of the 
experiment. Samples of wastewater were drawn from the bulk liquid over the duration of the 
experiment (48 h) and stored for analysis of conventional pollutants and pharmaceuticals. 
Carriers were removed from each reactor in order to operate at constant filling ratio during 
the batch experiment. Filtered samples were analysed for soluble COD, NO3 and NO2, 
whereas unfiltered samples were analysed for total COD and pharmaceuticals (the latter 
following additional of methanol for sample preservation). BOD7 analysis was performed on 
the wastewater medium used, and COD fractionation according to ASM1 principles 
(Roeleveld & van Loosdrecht , 2002) was performed. Biomass concentration in each reactor 
in gTSS/L was estimated from four carriers collected at t=0, according to the procedure 
presented by Loreggian (2014). 
Two sets of targeted batch experiments were performed at day 226 and day 471 from the 
beginning of the operation. A steady state was assumed to be reached in terms of biomass 
after a sufficiently long period of continuous operation.  
3.1.3 Sorption experiment 
To assess sorption of pharmaceuticals onto biofilm, targeted sorption experiments with re-
suspended biomass from reactor S1, S2, S3 and U were performed. Attached biomass from 20 
carriers from each MBBR reactor was re-suspended and washed overnight with tap water to 
ensure complete desorption of previously sorbed pharmaceutical amounts. Washed biomass 
was then spiked with pre-clarified, pre-filtered wastewater medium in 0.5 L reactors. Samples 
were collected for pharmaceutical analysis in the aqueous phase from the feed (t=0) and over 
4 h of the duration of the experiment. Sorbed concentrations could then be estimated from 
the drop in aqueous concentration between t=0 h and the end of the experiment.

Chapter 4 Model Development & Calibration 
In this chapter, the development of the biological model, used to describe processes of 
denitrification pharmaceutical removal in the MBBRs, is presented. Two multi-step 
denitrification models, i.e. Activated Sludge Model for Nitrogen (ASM-N) and the Activated 
Sludge Model with Indirect Coupling of Electrons (ASM-ICE), are described and compared. The 
alternative concept of storage during denitrification is also introduced, and extensions of the 
Activated Sludge No. 3 (ASM3) with multi-step denitrification are presented. Eventually, a 
description of the Activated Sludge Model for Xenobiotics (ASM-X), used to estimate kinetics 
of pharmaceutical elimination, is provided. Details on the calibration procedure are presented, 
including the selection of the objective function and a preliminary identifiability assessment of 
estimated parameters. 
4.1 Biological Model Development 
4.1.1. Activated Sludge Model for Nitrogen (ASMN)  
The ASM-N model (Hiatt & Grady, 2008) describes denitrification as a four-step process using 
the four process rate equations with nitrite, nitric oxide and nitrous oxide as intermediates. 
Specific anoxic reduction factors for growth in each step (ng1 to ng4) are taken into account 
and are associated to (i) the reduced maximum specific growth rate under anoxic conditions, 
as compared to aerobic conditions; (ii) the different fraction of the heterotrophic bacteria 
capable of catalyzing each denitrification step. Furthermore, this model considers also the 
nitric oxide inhibition of its own reduction and of nitrite and nitrous oxide reduction using 
inhibition coefficients (KNO,i) It should also be noted that the original version of this model 
employs a unique half-saturation coefficient for SS in all steps (KS=20 mg COD/L), except for 
nitrous oxide reduction (KS=40 mg COD/L) (Hiatt & Grady, 2008). 
Table 2 displays the stoichiometric matrix and process equations of ASM-N. It should be 
remarked that the nitrogen reduction and the carbon oxidation processes that were taken 
into consideration are directly coupled resulting in one single oxidation-reduction process for 
each nitrogen oxide compound [R1-R4] (Table 2). Additionally, according to the literature 
(Pan, et al., 2013a; Pan, et al., 2015), the ASM-N model has only been calibrated using 
experiments with spiking external carbon source (acetate, methane etc.) and the hydrolysis 
of particulate organic substrate Χs has not been taken into consideration. In this case study, 
measurements were conducted using only the content of COD in pre-clarified instead of 
spiking references substances and moreover, the hydrolysis and the decay of biomass are also 
included in the stoichiometric matrix.

4.1.2 Activated Sludge Model with Indirect Coupling of Electrons (ASM-ICE)  
As already mentioned, electron competition is not described in the previous model (ASM-N) 
and all the denitrification steps receive the required electrons for the reduction process. 
However, as pointed out in some studies (Pan, et al., 2015), the electron competition among 
the four denitrifications steps can be occur and carbon oxidation can become rate limiting. 
The possibility of competition for the same electron pool by enzymes catalyzing different 
reduction steps is contemplated in the second model tested in this project, ASM-ICE 
(Activated Sludge Model with Indirect Coupling of Electrons) (Pan, et al., 2013a; Pan, et al., 
2015). 
In this model, COD oxidation (R1) and the nitrogen reduction for each step (R2-R5) are 
decoupled and are modelled as separate processes (Table 4.,5).Electron transport from COD 
to nitrogen oxides is mediated by electron carriers. Two new state variables are included in 
the model, the Mred (reduced form of electron carriers) and Mox (oxidized form of electron 
carriers). A closed loop regeneration of Mred and Mox is established according to the equation:  
𝑀𝑟𝑒𝑑⇋𝑀𝑜𝑥+ 2𝑒−+ 2𝛨+ 
The overall level of Mred and Mox per amount of biomass (Ctot=SMred + SMox) is assumed to be 
constant.  
Most of the previous studies indicate that the calibration of ASM-ICE model was performed 
by experiments with additional carbon source and do not take into account the hydrolysis 
process. However, in this study, the ASM-ICE was calibrated with data sets derived from batch 
experiments without addition of SS and moreover it was extended in order to include also the 
hydrolysis of the particulate substrate. 
Table 4 presents the processes and the stoichiometric matrix for the extension of ASM-ICE 
used in this study. The extended ASM-ICE model includes seven processes. In fact, the 
electron competition is simulated by assuming different values of the affinity constants for 
the SMred in each reduction step.  
The model variables are expressed in concentration units of mmol Chemical Oxygen Demand 
(COD)/L and mmol Nitrogen (N)/L. Concentrations of nitrate, nitrite, soluble COD and total 
COD obtained from the experiments were converted from mg to mmol using the molar mass 
of elemental nitrogen and molecular oxygen equal to 14 and 32, respectively.  
The same procedure as previously reported was followed to calibrate this model. Again, an 
initial guess was necessary in order to calibrate the model and these values were found in 
literature as observed in Table 6. The calibration of the heterotrophic yield, the maximum 
carbon source oxidation rate and the maximum nitrite reduction rate could fit adequately the 
simulated results with the experimental resulting in the minimum error.

4.1.3 Activated Sludge Model No. 3 (ASM3) 
Apart from the aforementioned models, also the concept of storage was investigated second 
data set and specifically for the calibration for the reactors S2, S3 and U. Since literature 
evidences show the possibility of storage during denitrification, a new mathematical model 
was developed based on the Activated Sludge Model No. 3 in order to describe the storage 
process. In this model, hydrolysis, simultaneous anoxic storage and growth on XSTO, 
endogenous respiration of XH and XSTO are taken into account. The first thought was to extend 
the ASM3 to a two-step model since it is simpler. However, the inefficiency of this model to 
describe the experimental data led to the four-step denitrification model. 
In particular, the two-step and the four-step model involves mine and seventeen processes, 
respectively, some of these are the storage of the readily biodegradable substrate and the 
growth only on storage products for every step of the denitrification model. Hence, this model 
is based on the assumption that the SS is taken up from the XSTO, which is simultaneously is 
utilized for anoxic growth. In ASM3, the decay processes are replaced with the endogenous

the biotransformation of dissolved parent compounds, the sorption to suspended particles 
and retransformation of other dissolved fraction as well as the impact of primary growth 
substrate (SS) on the secondary/cometabolic processes.

4.2 Calibration method 
4.2.1 Model simulations 
The aforementioned multi-step denitrification models were implemented in Matlab R2015a. 
To be more precise, the first-order differential equations that derive from the stoichiometric 
matrix and the process rate equations for each model compound were solved by the solvent 
ode23s. The developed models were composed by many differential equations resulting in a 
more complex system which could simulated by the specific solvent, which is used in order to 
solve stiffer systems. The simulation ran from t=0 to t=2 days with time step 0.01. 
4.2.2 Monte Carlo Method for calibration 
Calibration of the previously presented models is here intended as a procedure of fitting 
experimental data by minimizing an objective function, with the estimation of biokinetic (and 
stoichiometric) parameters. The Monte Carlo method was selected for model calibration, 
allowing for estimation of parameter values.  
The output used for calibration of the selected models included concentrations of nitrate, 
nitrite, soluble COD, total COD, selected pharmaceuticals which were fitted to experimental 
results from the two sets of batch experiments.  
A preliminary assessment, using manual trial-and-error calibration, aimed at identifying most 
significant parameters.  
 
 
==PAGE:57==

Following the selection of parameters to be estimated with model calibration, different sets 
of parameter values were generated by sampling the defined parameter space. Lower and 
upper boundaries for each parameter were defined based on literature values and, when 
scarce or not available, on expert knowledge (Sin, et al., 2010). Sampling of parameter space 
was performed using Latin Hypercube Sampling (LHS) was used and it was performed in 
Matlab program (Helton & Davis, 2003).  
Initial conditions for each compound were set based on the experimental data, i.e. the initial 
concentration of each compound was set equal with the concentration for t=0 min. A sample 
matrix was generated, including all the parameter sets, and for each set Monte Carlo 
simulations were conducted. The number of the parameter sets generated, equivalent with 
the number of Monte Carlo simulation, was set to 100,000. At the end of the simulations, the 
estimated set of the parameter values corresponded to minimum value of the objective 
function.  
Monte Carlo-based calibration was repeated in order to check convergence to a unique set of 
estimated parameter values.

4.2.4 Preliminary identifiability analysis of estimated parameter combination  
The preliminary identifiability analysis typically used in sensitivity analysis assess whether the 
multivariate model can be linearized using the parameters selected to be estimated. In the 
case that model can be linearized, Equation 8 is satisfied, i.e. the R2 (‘coefficient of 
determination’) of Equation 8 is larger than 0.7. 
If R2<0.7, the model cannot be linearized, i.e. there are some other terms in Equation 8. This 
means that “there are parameter interactions (Sin, et al., 2010)”, i.e. the parameters are 
correlated. 
If R2>0.7, the model is linearized, hence the parameters are not correlated, which means that 
they are identifiable. 
Multivariate linear modes can replace the system of the ordinary differential equations that 
describe the developed model. So, a multivariate model which is first order rate is fitted to

Chapter 6 Discussion 
In this study, the modelling of primary and secondary metabolism was examined. As far as 
the primary metabolism is concerned, the focus of attention was on a multi-step 
denitrification model which includes also the modelling of the nitrous and nitric oxide. At the 
beginning, different dimensionless objective functions were tested since the simulated 
concentrations were in different units. The results imply that the RSR modelling objective 
function provided the best fit between the simulated and the experimental results therefore 
it was selected for further calibration of the others reactors by the developed models         
ASM-N, ASM-ICE and extended ASM3. 
6.1 Comparison between modelling approaches 
Setting side by side the models ASM-N and ASM-ICE which differ in the approach of the 
electron competition, this study proposes that the former model, which does not consider 
the competition among the denitrification steps, describes more adequately the obtained 
data from the batch experiments. However, regarding the new data set from the batch 
experiments (at 471 days) the calibration of the heterotrophic yield could not be avoided for 
the reactors S2, S3 and U, resulting in a high value. The most likely explanation of this finding 
is that the storage process may occur and it has to be included in order to overcome this 
limitation. The results from the new kinetic model ASM3, which includes also the storage 
process, suggest that a good match can be provided. By the implementation of this model, 
the calibration of the yield was avoided. It is remarkable that different anoxic coefficients for 
storage and growth were defined and were calibrated in this model.

6.5 Limitations of this study  
The main limitations of this study are listed below: 
 The default values, which were taken from the literature were used for the activated 
sludge model and not for biofilm. This can elucidate the fact that the decay rate and 
the hydrolysis rate constant were much lower in the model developed in contrast with 
the values from the literature. 
 The diffusion process and the content of the active biomass are not taken into 
account. In particular, in this model 50% of biomass is considered active for all the 
reactors.  
 
==PAGE:89==
Chapter 6-Discussion  
73 
 Moreover, it should be mentioned that a not very sufficient fit was experienced as 
regards the calibration of the reactor S3 and more particularly the concentration of 
the soluble COD. An important implication of this fact is that there is an overestimation 
of the consumption of the soluble COD elucidating that probably the soluble COD is 
not utilized for the reduction of the nitric and nitrous oxide as it is anticipated in the 
model. The measurement of nitric and nitrous oxide may solve this problem and 
provide a better prediction of the COD utilization.  
 Furthermore, due to lack of the conjugate concentrations, a ratio of the CCJ and CLI 
concentrations was assumed in order to define the initial conditions for each reactor 
and it was calibrated. Hence, the measurement of the retransformed concentration 
would provide a more accurate calibration of the anoxic biotransformation rate 
coefficients for CCJ and CCL.

Chapter 7 Conclusions 
The main conclusions that can be drawn from this study are the following 
The calibration procedure was developed using the Monte Carlo method, including a 
preliminary identifiability analysis based on model linearization. Different modelling objective 
functions were tested leading to the conclusion that the RSR provides a more accurate model 
prediction and therefore it was selected. Moreover, the linearization degree was assessed for 
the implemented models in order to examine the identifiability of the calibrated parameters 
which means that the parameters are not correlated (Objective 1). 
After the selection of the modelling objective function, the ASMN and the ASM-ICE models 
were compared in terms of RSR values and model linearization. The obtained results suggest 
that ASM-N describes more adequately the experimental data. Moreover, the ASM-N model 
satisfies the linearization degree (R2>0.7), whereas the ASM-ICE does not since the calibration 
of the yield was inevitable resulting in a very low value (Objective 2). 
After the selection of the ASM-N model, a new data set derived from the reactors S1, S2, S3 
and U was calibrated. As regards the first reactor the fit between the measured and the 
simulated results was sufficient. However, for the others reactors, the model was insufficient 
to match the simulated with the experimental data. The calibration of the heterotrophic yield 
could not be avoided. The obtained values of YH were high and were not in line with the values 
found in literature (Objective 3). 
The high values of heterotrophic yield coefficients led to the conclusion that the storage 
process may occur. Thus, the famine-feast conditions may have a significant influence on the 
reactors S2, S3 and U. At the beginning, a simpler two-step model was developed based on the 
ASM3, which was inadequate. Then, the ASM3 denitrification model was extended to a four-
step kinetic model and according to the results, it was sufficient to provide a good fit, although 
the soluble COD utilization is overestimated in the reactor S3. Moreover, a preliminary 
identifiability analysis was performed also for this model and the extent of the linearization 
was higher than 0.7, thus the calibrated parameters can be concluded as identifiable 
(Objective 4). 
Finally, an Activated Sludge Modelling framework for Xenobiotics (ASM-X) was tested in order 
to predict the fate of three pharmaceuticals, Sulfamethoxazole (SMX), Clarithromycin (CLA) 
and Atenolol (ATE), in the MBBR reactors under batch conditions. The calibrated anoxic 
biotransformation rate coefficients for the dissolved concentration of the CLA and ATE 
pharmaceuticals were found higher that the literature values while as regards the SMX is 
within the range given from previous studies (Objective 5).

7.1 Future perspectives 
In general, the models ASM-N and ASM3 developed in this study described the experimental 
results considerably with potential for improvements. Some limitations, which affected the 
developed models are the fact that the diffusion process is neglected, the precise content of 
active biomass in each reactor is unknown and finally the default parameter values set in the 
model are extracted from activated sludge systems. 
To sum up, this study despite the aforementioned limitations of the modelling and the need 
of further improvements, establishes some main conclusions in order to model in more 
sufficient way the primary and the secondary metabolism of the MBBR systems. Hence, 
further investigation is recommended.
