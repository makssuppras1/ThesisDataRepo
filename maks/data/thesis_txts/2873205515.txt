Abstract
This thesis investigates the interactions between oscillatory flows and flexible vegetation through
computational fluid dynamics (CFD) and fluid-structure interaction (FSI) modelling. The study
focuses on the dynamic behaviour of submerged vegetation stems under wave-induced forces,
employing both a linear Euler-Bernoulli beam model implemented in MATLAB and a non-
linear geometrically exact Simo-Reissner beam model developed in OpenFOAM. The models
are validated and optimized using experimental data, which includes measurements of stem
deflections and root forces for artificial vegetation mimics under varying wave conditions. Key
dimensionless parameters such as the Keulegan-Carpenter (KC) number and Cauchy number
(Ca) are analysed to understand the balance between drag and inertia forces, as well as the
influence of stem flexibility on hydrodynamic loading.
The linear model, based on spectral decomposition of free-vibration mode shapes, demonstrates
good agreement with experimental data for rigid stems (mimics 3 and 4), where deformations
remain small. However, its accuracy diminishes for highly flexible stems (mimic 2), particularly
for longer lengths and larger wave amplitudes, where non-linear effects dominate. The non-
linear OpenFOAM model, while theoretically capable of handling large deformations, exhibits
challenges in accurately replicating asymmetric stem motions and requires further refinement
for flexible cases. Both models successfully capture the drag-dominated regimes at high KC
numbers, but discrepancies arise in inertia-dominated flows, highlighting the complexity of FSI
in vegetation dynamics.
The findings underscore the importance of model selection based on stem flexibility and flow
conditions. The linear model offers computational eﬀiciency for rigid stems, while the non-linear
model, though more resource-intensive, provides insights into large deformations. This work
contributes to the broader understanding of vegetation-flow interactions, with implications for
coastal protection, sediment transport, and ecosystem resilience. Future research should focus
on improving the non-linear model’s accuracy for flexible stems and exploring two-way coupling
between fluid and vegetation dynamics.

Length of a segment in the discretization of the stem (m)
δx
Thickness of the stem cross-section, stream-wise (m)
δy
Width of the stem cross-section, cross-flow (m)
ϵ(τd), ϵ(τi), ϵ(xs) Error of the simulated shear stress or deflection, either relative to the analytical
value (steady case) or to another simulation with a finer resolution (spatial, temporal or
modal)
γ, γk, γn constant involved in the definition of the cantilever beam mode shapes (-)
µ
Mass per unit length of the stem material (kg)
µ′
Effective mass per unit length of the stem material, accounting for the inertia term in
the Morison force (kg)
ν
Poisson’s ratio of the stem material (-)

Wave angular frequency (also frequency of the free vibration of the beam) (rad.s−1)
ϕk
Base functions in the spectral decomposition
ρ
Water density (kg/m3)

OpenFOAM model shear stress at the root of the stem (N.m−2)

Introduction
1.1
Motivation
Understanding the complex interactions between aquatic vegetation (both emergent and fully
submerged) and hydrodynamic processes is essential for advancing our ability to model and
manage coastal environments using nature-based solutions. Vegetation plays a critical role in
modifying flow structures, dissipating wave energy, and influencing the transport and distribu-
tion of sediments, nutrients, and pollutants (C. Wang et al. 2014). These processes have direct
implications for coastal protection, particularly in mitigating erosion and reducing the impact
of storm surges.
This thesis contributes to a broader research initiative, the MARSH project, which aims to
improve predictive capabilities of vegetated coastal systems through advanced modelling. The
objectives of MARSH include:
1. The development of a fully-coupled numerical model that captures the dynamic interaction
between flexible vegetation (such as seagrasses and marshland plants) and wave-induced
flows
2. The assessment of vegetation vulnerability by quantifying mechanical failure modes such
as uprooting and stem breakage
3. The formulation of theoretical and numerical frameworks to describe wave-averaged in-
canopy force balances and associated flow patterns that govern sediment dynamics and
pollutant dispersion
4. The evaluation of the potential for vegetation to reduce storm surge impacts at field-
relevant scales
Through the lens of this larger research effort, this thesis focuses mainly on the dynamics of a
single stem submerged in a wave-induced oscillating current, to understand the range of appli-
cations of a simple linear Euler-Bernoulli beam model, compared to a non-linear finite volume
formulation of a geometrically exact Simo–Reissner beam model (Bali et al. 2022).
This project involves a close collaboration between DTU and DHI, and with the participation
of researchers from University College Dublin (Ireland) and Northeastern University (USA).

1.2
Literature review on flow-vegetation interactions
The interaction between flexible aquatic vegetation and fluid flow constitutes a complex fluid-
structure interaction (FSI) problem. This interaction depends on a multitude of factors, such as
the material and geometrical properties of the vegetation (Liu et al. 2021) and the magnitude
of hydrodynamic forces acting upon the vegetation (Bouma et al. 2005). Understanding these
relationships is crucial for developing accurate numerical models and predictive capabilities.
Accurate replication of the mechanical properties of aquatic vegetation, such as flexibility, geom-
etry, and length, is a significant challenge. These properties vary with species and seasonality,
and influence both the hydrodynamic response and vegetation motion (L. Wang et al. 2023; Cui
et al. 2023; Keimer et al. 2024). Vegetation rigidity and geometry affect drag and motion under
flow conditions, and thus must be considered when developing and validating numerical models
(Liu et al. 2021).
Numerical models that simulate flexible vegetation under hydrodynamic loading can either be:
• One-way coupled, where only fluid forces act on the vegetation without feedback into the
flow field (M. Luhar et al. 2016),
• Two-way coupled, where vegetation deformation alters the surrounding flow (Chen et al.
2019; Derksen 2021).
The former is easier to model and requires less model complexity, especially when the motion
is reduced to a 2D plane and when complex flow structures, such a vortices, are neglected. The
latter is usually accounted for in 3D models that aim to capture more complex dynamics, both
from the stem and the flow structure. Two-way coupled models are a recent research topic, which
usually requires non-linear exact beam/rod models, as well as solving flow equations. An in-
creasingly popular method is the immersed boundary method (IBM), which involves an Eulerian
structure solver in a Lagrangian fluid solver. This method has been successfully implemented
by Tschisgale et al. 2020; Kim et al. 2024; J. Wang et al. 2022; Hagmeyer et al. 2024.
To represent the complex fluid dynamics involved, accurate and reliable turbulence modelling
is crucial. Reynolds-averaged Navier-Stokes (RANS) models, such as the k–ϵ model, have been
adapted to account for vegetation-induced turbulence (Dijkstra et al. 2010; Maria Maza et
al. 2013; Chen et al. 2019).
For higher-fidelity simulations, Large Eddy Simulation (LES)
resolves larger turbulence scales and uses subgrid-scale models (SGS) like Smagorinsky and
WALE (Tschisgale et al. 2020; Kim et al. 2024; Prüter, Spröer, Keimer, et al. 2025). LES has
shown improved agreement with experiments, especially in steady current conditions (Prüter,
Spröer, Keimer, et al. 2025).
Another type of model shifts the focus of the study to vegetation patches at the canopy scale.
Patch-scale studies explore how vegetation influences the overall flow structure. For example,
Fan et al. 2025 performed experiments using live vegetation under both pure current and wave
conditions. They demonstrated that vegetation density plays a crucial role in reducing stream-
wise velocities and damping turbulence, particularly under pure current. Non-linear interactions
between waves, current, and vegetation were shown to smooth velocity profiles and reduce tur-
bulence intensity.
Although several experiments study the effect of vegetation patches on the flow structure (Fan
et al. 2025; M. Maza et al. 2015), experiments involving individual stems are vital to provide
single-stem numerical models with validation data (M. Luhar et al. 2016; Jacobsen et al. 2019).
Pioneering studies investigated the forces and motion of flexible stems in unidirectional and
wave-induced flows (Mitul Luhar and Nepf 2011; Tschisgale et al. 2020; Mitul Luhar, Coutu,
et al. 2010). Pictures of experimental setups are shown in Figure 1.1.

In an attempt to further provide experimental data in a wider variety of flow conditions and
stem geometries and material properties, Jacobsen et al. 2019 conducted a comprehensive ex-
perimental campaign, measuring stem root forces, stem deflections, and flow velocities under
varying wave conditions. Stems of different lengths and flexibilities were used to derive drag
and inertia coeﬀicients, forming a valuable validation dataset, which is employed in this thesis.
To provide reliable experimental data, artificial vegetation plays a major role in providing an
environment where the material properties are controlled and can be a fortiori numerically
modelled with accuracy. Knowledge of the exact physical properties of the stem is all the more
important, as rigidity and cross-section tend to vary along the length of live vegetation.
Artificial vegetation, often rigid, has been widely used to simplify model validation and control
for species-specific properties.
Studies have shown how rigid vegetation enhances drag and
wave attenuation, allowing focus on hydrodynamics without the added complexity of the plants’
reconfiguration (Zhang et al. 2023; Barman et al. 2024; Shi et al. 2023).
Early publications studied the behaviour of single stems in currents (e.g. steady flows), especially
regarding the stem “reconfiguration”, or in other words, how the stem bends in a current and
the influence of this reconfiguration on the drag force (Mitul Luhar and Nepf 2011).
However, realistic modelling requires considering flexibility and deformation under dynamic
loads. Flexible vegetation undergoes significant deformation under wave loading, influenced by
wave period and height (Mullarney et al. 2010; Rupprecht et al. 2017; Maria Maza et al. 2013;
Chen et al. 2019). These dynamics necessitate high-resolution flow fields and complex coupling
strategies in simulations.
M. Luhar et al. 2016 applied a modified Morison equation to model wave-induced forces on
vegetation. Their results, showcasing a drag coeﬀicient of Cd = 1.95, served as a benchmark for
numerous studies (Zhu et al. 2020; El Rahi et al. 2023). These studies found good agreement
between simulations and measured stem motion.
The approach using drag force and coeﬀicient via the Morison force is a favoured approach as
it relies on well-known hydrodynamic loading, which can be relatively easily computed within
numerical simulations. However, one of the inconveniences is that drag coeﬀicients are material-
dependent and are not easily generalised to different plant species, or even for the same species
at different seasons. Which is why some models study different and direct force computation,
without the Morison force.
Two recent publications demonstrated the implementation of an FSI model using a direct method
in REEF3D::CFD, which employs a direct forcing approach to the stem, eliminating the need for
a drag coeﬀicient or computation of the Morison force. However, their model requires providing
damping coeﬀicients to characterise the non-linear internal viscous damping of the stems.
The model was first validated in unidirectional current simulations (Prüter, Spröer, Keimer,
et al. 2025) and showed that it could accurately represent both stem bending and forces across
diverse stem lengths, current velocities, and material properties. In a follow-up paper (Prüter,
Spröer, Lojek, et al. 2025), the model performances were evaluated under wave-induced oscil-
latory current conditions. The results of the study demonstrate that the coupled FSI model
within REEF3D::CFD can accurately simulate the dynamic behaviour of submerged flexible
stems under wave loading. The simulated forces and stem displacement showed less than 10%
deviation from experimental measurements. The flow velocities computed near the stem closely
matched phase-averaged measurements from reference experiments. The simulations captured
various bending modes observed experimentally, illustrating the model’s ability to reproduce
the dynamic deformation patterns of flexible stems during wave interactions.
Additionally, proper selection of the damping coeﬀicients was essential to match experimental

1.3
Objectives
The following specific objectives are addressed in this work:
1. Develop a linear model based on Euler-Bernoulli beam theory, which uses the exper-
imental flow measurements to compute the Morison force on the beam and solve the
time-dependent motion of a stem with given material properties.
2. Successfully use the non-linear beam model1, coupled with a module to compute the
Morison force2,
3. Optimise both models to find the inertia and drag coeﬀicients to match the experimental
cases,
4. Compare the performances of the two models, and evaluate their ability to represent the
dynamics of stems with varying flexibility and under different wave conditions.
1.4
Framework
The framework of this project, is to compare the performances of two numerical models: the
first one is a linear model based on the Euler-Bernoulli beam equations, developed specifically
during the timespan of this thesis; the second is a geometrically exact finite difference model
which solves the stem motion by computing the Morison force generated by the flow that the
stem is submerged in. The models will both be compared to the experimental data in Jacobsen
et al. 2019.
The numerical models simulate the motion of a single stem in regular wave-induced oscillatory
flows, which are the exact flow conditions measured in Jacobsen et al. 2019. Therefore, the
models focus solely on simulating the motion of the stem, and do not solve for the flow itself.
Consequently, the models are one-way coupled, and the influence of the stem motion on the
flow is not taken into account. Part of the stem feedback on the flow may have been captured
by the EMF, but small flow structures have been filtered by the frequency and resolution of
the EMF. The vegetation stems are modelled as mimics of uniform rectangular cross-sections
along the length of the stem. The material and geometrical properties of the mimics have been
characterised in the internship report of Bakker n.d. The material properties, such as the Young
modulus and the density, are assumed to be uniform along the stem.
Lastly, the motion of the stem is kept in the plane defined by the flow velocities, and no motion
in the third dimension, such as twisting, is allowed. Therefore, the frontal area exposed to the
flow is always that of the width of the stem. The loading on the stems is computed through the
Morison force, including the drag and inertia terms. The effect of buoyancy is also considered,

1.5
Important dimensionless parameters
In the study of wave-vegetation interactions, particularly in coastal and estuarine environments,
understanding the hydrodynamic forces acting on submerged vegetation is crucial for predict-
ing ecological impacts, wave attenuation and sediment transport. One of the most widely used
frameworks to quantify these forces is the Morison equation, which decomposes the total force
into inertia and drag components. Two key non-dimensional parameters often emerge in this
context: the Keulegan-Carpenter (KC) number and the Cauchy number (Ca). These dimen-
sionless numbers provide insight into the dominant flow-structure interaction mechanisms and
are essential for developing reliable numerical models.

2.3
Deriving the inertia and drag coefficients from the
experimental data
For each experiment, the time series of the root force and the stem deflection are available.
Except for mimic 4, for which only the root force is available, as the stem deflection was too
small to be recorded. It allows us to find the Cm and Cd solely based on the experimental data,
which provides a “ground truth”, and can a fortiori be compared with the optimized, simulated
coeﬀicients for both the linear and non-linear models.
The expression of the Morison force can be fitted to find the optimal inertia Cm and drag co-
eﬀicients Cd that minimize the error between the measured and the fitted force (in the least
squares sense). However, it requires using the acceleration of the stem, which can be a source
of numerical noise if one uses a finite difference scheme. Therefore, in a similar fashion to what
is performed in Jacobsen et al. 2019, Proper Orthogonal Decomposition (POD) is applied to
the recorded deflection to obtain a mode decomposition with the corresponding time-dependent
weights. For the same reason, each weight time series is then fitted to a sum of sinusoid functions
whose frequencies fn are proportional to the frequency of the regular wave, f = 1
T , used for
the given experiment. The accelerations can then be derived by simply taking the analytical
derivatives of simple sinusoids, without the need to use a finite difference with a stencil, which
would otherwise introduce and amplify noise. The exact procedure is not described here as it is
the same one that can be found in the appendix of Jacobsen et al. 2019.
As mentioned in the paper, the drawback of this approach is that slow variations in the stem
motion are filtered out, so the POD method results in a regularization of the signal. However,
this does not have any significant consequences for the present analysis of the force coeﬀicients,
because the slow motion only affects the velocities and accelerations with small modifications.

3
A 1-D linear non-coupled Matlab model
Modelling vegetation and its behaviour can be a very complex and expensive task, especially if
a fully coupled flow-vegetation model is desired, with great temporal and spatial accuracy. For
this reason, the first model developed in this thesis is a relatively simple 1-D model. This model
is based on the Euler-Bernoulli dynamic beam equation, where the loading (e.g., the force acting
on the stem) is the Morison force, computed using the prescribed flow measured in laboratory
experiments. The idea behind this linear model is to evaluate how far it can be extended to
represent the stem motion accurately, before it becomes necessary to use a non-linear, more
complex model.
3.1
Equations of the model
In this section, the Morison equation is first introduced and defined to represent the hydrody-
namic loading, while the beam model is presented in a second part.
3.1.1

shredding.

deformations and stresses within a beam subject to small lateral bending. The linear Euler-

chanical engineering. However, due to the relative diﬀiculty to find all the information (which I
found to be scattered across various sources) needed to understand the model described in this
section, the choice has been made to include all theory and derivations needed in this section,
such that a reader unfamiliar with the underlying theory will find all the useful information to
understand the model.

3.2
Finite Difference Method
The finite difference method is a very powerful and widely used method for solving Ordinary
Differential Equations (ODEs).
The principle of the method is to approximate the spatial
derivatives with discrete, finite differences. The spatial domain is discretized in points separated
by a regular (or not) interval. Finite differences can be adapted for Partial Differential Equations
(PDE) by also discretizing the time dimension. This method allows for transforming potentially
non-linear differential equations into a set of linear equations.

This method was the first one implemented for the linear model. For some reasons that were
not fully understood, oscillations of very high frequency and amplitude were developing in the
motion of the stem.
Some of those oscillations could be suppressed by smoothing the flow
velocities and accelerations, as well as by implementing a ramp function for the start of the
simulation. However, the results were not satisfying. Both 3 and 5 points on the stencil were
implemented, without displaying a significant improvement in reducing the oscillations.
Therefore, another method was implemented in an attempt to obtain a solution without oscil-
lations. This method is the spectral method, which is described in the next section.

The core principle of the spectral method used in this thesis is to consider the solution of the
equation as a weighted sum of base functions ϕi. A similar method is employed for finite element
analysis, for example, where the solution is locally approximated as a sum of polynomials that
satisfy the boundary conditions. Regarding the equation of the problem in this chapter, the
solution can be written:

Where qk is the weight corresponding to the base function ϕk. However, in practice, the exact
solution can’t be obtained since an infinite sum is not achievable numerically. Therefore, the
approximation of the solution is a truncation of the infinite sum, where as many terms are
included as deemed necessary for the estimate to be close enough to the true solution. An-
other important aspect of this method, is that it allows to separate time and space, by using
known base functions that satisfy the boundary conditions of the problem, and transforming the
equation of the problem into another problem which aims to find the coeﬀicients of each base
function ϕk at each time step tn. The choice of base functions is rather important, and choosing
the right functions for a given solution can greatly reduce the number of terms in the sum, and
therefore the numerical complexity. Lastly, if the base functions are provided analytically, the
computations become independent of the discretization of the problem, since the problem is
solved for the weights, which are scalar values.
For the Euler-Bernoulli beam equation coupled with the Morison equation, a particularly wise
choice of base functions is the free-vibration mode shapes of the cantilever beam. The mode
shapes fit the boundary conditions, and the number of mode shapes to include is rather intu-
itive, since they correspond to specific frequencies of vibration of the beam. Another important
aspect that reduces the computations is that their analytical mathematical form is well known,
and lastly, they are orthogonal with the inner product involving the mass matrix, which will
appear to be rather convenient.
The equation of the problem must be transformed to include this prior assumption of the shape
of the solution (to be more exact, of an approximation of the solution). For a given base function
ϕm, the equation is projected (in the sense of linear algebra) on that function to obtain several
linear equations, with one equation per base function.

3.4
Short discussion on the two models
Chronologically, the finite difference model is the first one that was implemented. The key pa-
rameter for this method is the number of points in the discretization of the beam. The more
points, the more accurate the results, but at the cost of increased computation time and mem-
ory. However, very high frequency oscillations of high amplitude were present on the time series
of the deflection and the shear stress. Several method have been tried to reduce them (including
ramp functions to smoothen the start of the simulation, increasing the number of points in the
stencil for the finite difference scheme, POD, or trying various Matlab ode solvers) which only
lead to limited improvement, especially for the most stiff simulations (Mimic 4). Furthermore,
the time resolution (which is handled by the solver) was very high because the solver had to
make very small steps to converge. As an example, a two-second simulation with 80 points
spatial resolution required about 1,000,000 time steps for the solver, which is quite demanding
in computation time and memory.
For these reasons, and because the oscillations are not physical but rather instabilities arising
from the numerical method, the spectral method was implemented in an attempt to improve the
results. The advantage of the spectral method is that the results are no longer dependent on the
discretization of the beam, since the deflection of the beam is modelled as a weighted sum of the
free-vibration analytical mode shapes (whose analytical formulae are known). Another signifi-
cant advantage of the spectral method over the finite difference, is that the boundary conditions
are carried by the base functions and don’t have to be included in the numerical computations.
As a consequence, the number of equations involved is drastically reduced to one equation per
mode shape instead of one equation per discretized point. However, because the computation
of the morison force acting on the beam (updated at each time step) requires the beam velocity
and acceleration, the beam discretization is used during this specific step for faster computations
(the load could theoretically be kept entirely analytical through a griddedInterpolant object,
Computational fluid dynamics of flow-vegetation interactions
35
==PAGE:48==
but calling such a function in Matlab at each time step increases significantly the computation
time).
Therefore, with the spectral method, the key parameter is the number of mode shapes Nr al-
lowed in the sum, as well as the number of points in the discretization Nz needed to represent the
mode shapes. However, the latter is not as important as in the finite difference method, since it
will not change the size of the system of equations being solved, but it should simply be adapted
such that the highest mode shape can be accurately represented on the given discretization (see
Figure 3.11).
Lastly, another important feature of the spectral method is that the equation is solved in its weak
formulation (the equation multiplied by test functions), in the weighted integral sense. There-
fore, the equation is not theoretically exact locally, but rather globally, on the whole length
of the beam. The advantage is that integration acts as a low-pass filter, making this method
more stable than finite differences, but losing the local control. For the solution provided by the
Galerkin method to be meaningful, it is recommended that the solution (and the loading) be
relatively smooth and regular, without singularities/discontinuities. On the contrary, the finite
difference method solves the strong formulation of the equation, which is more accurate locally,
but more sensitive to noise and instabilities.

The variables of interest to assess the behaviour of the stem in a flow and validate the model
against the experimental data were introduced previously, but will be detailed further in this
section, especially the shear force and how it is numerically derived from the motion.

the simulated motion and how it responds to the loading. Another aspect is that the integral
method requires knowledge of the shear at one of the boundary conditions, which is fortunately
the case for a cantilever beam (no shear at the free end), but could eventually be more compli-
cated in other cases.
3.6
Initial conditions
For all the simulations performed with the models in this thesis, the initial position is a straight
beam along the z-axis, with zero velocities. The starting time t0 depends for each simulation
on the recordings of the corresponding experiment. As explained in Jacobsen et al. 2019, the
recordings of the stem motion (for mimic 2 and 3) start after about 40 periods, to wait for the
system to be in a dynamic equilibrium. Therefore, the time series of the recordings of the flow
velocities and the stem deflection are not the same. To allow the comparison between the model
and the experiments, the starting time of the simulation is chosen as the starting time of the
stem motion recordings (which is included in the flow velocities recordings). Regarding mimic 4,
the stem motion has not been recorded; therefore, the starting time of the simulation is chosen
as t0 = 20 s, for the flow conditions to be at equilibrium.
A fortiori, it would have made more sense to make it dependent on the wave period, such as
t0 = 10T, but this is deemed to have little influence on the results obtained later.

the flow, can lead to non-physical oscillations in the system. To avoid this issue, a ramp function

3.7.3
Optimization
In the Euler-Bernoulli beam equation of the problem, the loading term is the Morison force,
which is calculated using the smoothed experimental velocity measurements, as well as the ve-
locity of the beam. However, two coeﬀicients must be set to compute the force, namely the
drag and the inertia coeﬀicients Cd and Cm. To reduce the time taken to optimize the model on
all experiments, the optimization is performed on 10 periods, and not on the entire time series
available.
Search space and method
The optimization of the model is performed for each experiment by searching in the 2D space
of real numbers formed by the pair (Cd, Cm). The space is reduced greatly by assuming the
following range of values:
• Cd ∈[0.5, 8]
• Cm ∈[0, 5]
The optimal coeﬀicients are searched with an accuracy of 0.01. If the space [0.5, 8] × [0, 5] were
to be searched with a brute force approach at the given accuracy, finding the optimal pair of
coeﬀicients of the model fitted to a single experiment would have to be run about 375,000 times
to find the optimal values. Therefore, the first approach tested was to use the Optimization
Toolbox available in Matlab, with a constrained optimizer such as fmincon. However, it turned
out that the optimal pair of coeﬀicients found by the optimizer was very sensitive to the initial
search point. To avoid using a non-deterministic optimizer (in the sense that changing the initial
points would change the optimal coeﬀicients) and to make sure the optimization leads to finding
global (and not local) minima, another optimization method has been used.
Since the search space is constrained, one can search the best coeﬀicients at a lower resolu-
tion, say ∆Cm = ∆Cd = 0.1 and select the M pairs (Cd, Cm) with the M lowest errors.
Let’s call Cd,min and Cd,max the lowest and highest values of Cd found within the values
corresponding to the M lowest errors. A Cm,min and Cm,max are also obtained. The space
[Cd,min, Cd,max] × [Cm,min, Cm,max] is then searched with the desired resolution of 0.01. If the
2D error surface is smooth, the min and max values for both Cd and Cm should be relatively
close to one another, thus reducing the optimization time compared to a brute force approach,
while also outputting coeﬀicients close to the global optima.
In practice, the search is split into three rounds with the following accuracies: 0.5, 0.1, and 0.01.
After each round (except the last one), the 10 pairs of coeﬀicients corresponding to the minimum
errors are used to calculate the range for both Cm and Cd to search for the next round (M = 10).

4.1
Non-linear model description
The non-linear model is an implementation in the popular open source CFD toolbox Open-
FOAM of a geometrically exact Simo-Reissner beam formulation (Tukovic et al. 2019). This
formulation is the most general non-linear 3D beam theory, which allows for handling finite
displacement and rotations. Contrary to most 3D non-linear models that are based on a finite
element (FE) approach, this model uses a finite volume (FV) formulation and allows any initial
curvature configuration. This FV formulation has the advantage of facilitating a unified ap-
proach to multi-physics problems such as fluid-solid interactions (FSI). Additionally, it allows
both a strong local and global conservation of the governing equations. The model was later
expanded for shear-deformable geometrically exact beams (Bali et al. 2022). However, this ex-
pansion was suited for static or quasi-static problems. More recently, the model was further
extended by integrating it with a multiphase CFD solver and a six-degree-of-freedom motion
solver, applied to a moored floating body dynamics problem (Taran et al. 2025), allowing for
tackling fully dynamic problems. The implementation used in this thesis work is an extension
of the beam model, which can compute the loading based on the Morison Force with different
wave types (steady current, single or double harmonics waves, stream function theory) or based
on flow measurements that are interpolated (adapted by Niels Gjøl Jacobsen).

E = 2G(1 + ν)
(4.1)

direction of loading. For example, when a beam is compressed, most materials tend to expand
in the perpendicular directions. Many typical solids have Poisson’s ratio values around 0.2 - 0.3.

quite small (the same force that can lead to uprooting of the plant). Especially for artificial

improvement to the Euler scheme, which is much more stable and dissipates oscillations. Both
second-order schemes were more unstable and could not go further than Nt > 200. The other
numerical schemes were not modified and kept identical as provided by the model.

4.3.3
Optimization
The non-linear OpenFOAM model is optimized using the same coarse-to-fine, multi-resolution
strategy as the linear model, primarily because the error surface may not be well-suited for
gradient-based methods. An added advantage of this approach is that it enables parallel execu-
tion of simulations, which eﬀiciently leverages the High-Performance Computing (HPC) infras-
tructure available at DTU.
While individual simulations cannot be parallelized internally—as is common in CFD applications—
due to the strong coupling between local beam motion and its global deformation, multiple
simulations using different parameter pairs Cm and Cd can be executed concurrently.
Each
simulation’s error metric can be stored and compared once all runs have completed. This par-
allelism significantly accelerates the optimization process, allowing dozens or even hundreds of
simulations to run simultaneously on separate cores.
To balance optimization speed with practical constraints like storage, system load, and fair use
of the shared HPC environment, the number of processors allocated is capped at 50. In the non-
linear case, two additional refinement rounds are added compared to the linear model, resulting
in the following sequence of search resolutions: 0.5, 0.1, 0.05, 0.02, and 0.01.
Regarding the OpenFOAM simulations themselves: as the stem becomes more flexible or the
wave motion more intense (due to larger wave heights and longer periods), the solver requires
more Newton-Raphson iterations to accurately update the beam position. It was observed that
the computational cost of each time step increases throughout a simulation. For instance — in
a given case —, the solver may initially handle around 100 time steps per minute, but this rate
can drop to 5 time steps per minute after five wave periods.

To address this slowdown, a solution was implemented whereby previously completed time steps
are processed and deleted during runtime. This allows for significantly improved simulation
speed and makes it feasible to simulate the full 10 wave periods, even for highly flexible stems
under the most demanding wave conditions. However, this comes at the cost of a higher metadata
load, placing additional stress on the HPC system, which must be handled with great care.

6
Discussion
To understand the results obtained from both models with regard to the experimental measure-
ments, it is crucial to understand the meaning behind the non-dimensional coeﬀicients.
The KC number can be viewed as the ratio between the amplitude of motion of a water particle
in an oscillatory flow, over the characteristic distance of the solid, in this case, the beam width
δy. Therefore, the higher the KC, the larger the distance a water particle travels relative to the
width of the stem, resulting in an inertia-dominated flow.
In beam theory, the product EI is called the flexural rigidity. As the flexural rigidity increases,
the resistance of the beam to bending increases. EI has units N.m2, which corresponds to a
”moment per unit curvature” (curvature being inversely proportional to a distance). While the
numerator is a force (due to drag, although without the coeﬀicient Cd) multiplied by l2, which
can also be viewed as a bending moment by the fluid (so in N.m), scaled by the beam length
l to match the flexural rigidity. Low values of the Cauchy number (Ca << 1) correspond to
situations where the beam stiffness is high compared to the flow, and resists deformation very
well, while high values (Ca >> 1) indicate that the stem undergoes large deformations and
doesn’t resist the hydrodynamic loading so well.
The number L is a measure of the stem length, compared to the particle amplitude of motion.
If the stem is rather short but the particles travel a long distance within a period, L will be
relatively low (L << 1) and the structure sees a nearly uniform flow along the stem. While a
high value means that the beam can experience different velocities and phases of the flow, due
to the deformation and the deflection away from the neutral position.
The physical meaning behind CaL is not so intuitive, but it can be analysed as the Cauchy
number, scaled by the spatial variation of the loading. High CaL values suggest scenarios where
both structural flexibility and spatial flow variation are significant. CaL allows for merging into
one number: the effect of the beam stiffness, of its length, and the amplitude of the particle
motion. The idea is that a shorter stem (for which CaL scales as l4) is equivalent to a more
rigid, longer stem, which is then scaled by how the stem experiences the flow.
A case can be considered successfully optimized if the drag coeﬀicient, the root shear force, and
the tip deflection match the experimental data. Little attention is brought to the absolute values
of the inertia coeﬀicient, as most cases are drag dominated, thus there is higher variance in Cm.
Having all three variables match is meaningful for relatively rigid cases such as mimics 3 and
4. However, because mimic 2 shows larger deformations and shows a second mode activated,
the tip deflection is a lot more sensitive to the flow conditions and the coupling between the
solid and the fluid. Therefore, the results for mimic 2 will be analysed more critically, especially
considering that it falls outside the range of application of the linear model, and that convergence
was not achieved for 2L30 in the non-linear model.
Lastly, the optimized coeﬀicients are compared to the coeﬀicients fitted to the data using
the Morison force, as well as Proper orthogonal decomposition, which involves methodologi-
cal choices that influence the fitted values obtained. Therefore, the fitted coeﬀicients must not
be considered as an absolute truth, but rather an approximation of the inertia and drag coeﬀi-
cients based on the experimental results. Figure 5.2 shows that the fit is not perfect, and that
the forces with the fitted coeﬀicients diverge from the experimental measurements, especially
for a few cases with mimic 2.

Conclusion
7.1
Conclusions
This thesis explored the computational modelling of flow-vegetation interactions, focusing on the
dynamic response of submerged stems to wave-induced forces from experimental measurements.
Two numerical models were validated and optimised: a linear Euler-Bernoulli beam model,
specifically developed for this project, and an already developed non-linear geometrically exact
Simo-Reissner beam model. The linear model, implemented using a spectral method, proved
effective for rigid stems (mimics 3 and 4), accurately predicting root shear forces and stem
deflections within the limits of small deformations and beyond, as long as the deflection motion
is limited to one spatial mode. Its computational eﬀiciency makes it suitable for large-scale
simulations involving rigid vegetation. However, its applicability is rather limited for highly
flexible stems (mimic 2), where non-linear effects and large deformations violate the assumptions
of linear beam theory.
The non-linear OpenFOAM model, while capable of handling large deformations, faced chal-
lenges in replicating asymmetric stem motions and achieving convergence for the most flexible
cases. Furthermore, it couldn’t optimise the model such that the drag coeﬀicient matched the
experimental data for the most rigid stem (mimic 4), while being able to accurately simulate a
more flexible stem (mimic 3). Despite these limitations, it provided valuable insights into the
role of drag and inertia coeﬀicients in FSI dynamics.
The optimisation process for both models revealed that drag coeﬀicients (Cd) tend to decrease
with increasing KC numbers, consistent with experimental observations, while inertia coeﬀicients
(Cm) showed higher variability and less clear trends. Both models highlighted the importance of
dimensionless parameters like KC, Ca, and CaL in characterising vegetation-flow interactions.
The study also emphasised the trade-offs between model complexity and accuracy. The lin-
ear model’s simplicity is advantageous for rigid stems, but its inability to capture non-linear
kinematics limits its use for flexible vegetation. Conversely, the non-linear model, though more
versatile, requires significant computational resources and further refinement to address con-
vergence issues and asymmetric motion patterns. The OpenFOAM model must be reviewed in
more depth to make sure that the divergence of results in the linear cases (with rigid mimics)
arises from a more complete implementation of non-linear beam theory, and not from errors in
the case setups or solver.
The specific research objectives presented in the introduction can be answered as follows:
1. The linear model works well and can accurately represent the motion of the stems in the
linear domain and beyond to a certain extent.
2. The non-linear model gives reasonable results and converges in most cases, but it must be
adapted to be able to simulate the asymmetric motion of the flexible stems.
3. The optimisation framework with the multi-resolution search grid is effective and eﬀicient,
taking advantage of the HPC infrastructure to run simulations on separate cores.
4. The comparison of both models could be performed, but the limited performance of the
non-linear model could not allow for providing accurate results for the most flexible mimics.
A wide range of tools and analyses are available to study the dynamic motion of stems under
wave‑induced oscillatory flows. In this work, significant effort was dedicated to developing a
Computational fluid dynamics of flow-vegetation interactions
85
==PAGE:98==
robust numerical model. This involved first implementing a finite difference approach, which
did not produce satisfactory results, and subsequently adopting a spectral method that provided
improved accuracy.
Following the implementation of the linear model, significant time was
invested in gaining proficiency with OpenFOAM and the custom Solids4FOAM solver, including
understanding the source code and case configurations. As a result, the project timeline allowed
for less extensive optimisation of the simulations and in‑depth analysis of the results, including
exploring the sources of discrepancies and interpreting them in the context of the underlying
physics.
7.2
Future perspectives
In the analysis of the stem behaviour, several properties can be analysed to provide insights into
the dynamics of the stems, including:
• Frequency analysis: the frequency content of the time series can be studied and compared
to that of the flow (velocities and accelerations) and the experimental measurements,
• Phase analysis: similarly to what is done in Jacobsen et al. 2019, depending on the flow
conditions and the material properties, the different parts of the stem don’t experience
locally the same flow conditions, especially for the most flexible mimics,
• POD: The simulated stem motion can be decomposed using Proper Orthogonal Decom-
position to find the energy repartition on the spatial modes, both for the stem deflection,
but also velocities, accelerations, moment and shear force. In the linear model, the POD
can be compared to the spectral weights and modes,
• The sensitivity of the inertia and drag coeﬀicient around the optimised values can be
estimated to provide some sort of confidence interval around the optimal drag and inertia
coeﬀicient, to facilitate the comparison between experimental and numerical values.
Improvement can be made regarding the OpenFOAM model:
• Determining the reason for the discrepancy in results with the linear model in the most
rigid and linear cases,
• The model must be enhanced to better handle asymmetric stem motions and improve
convergence for highly flexible stems. This could involve refining the interpolation of flow
velocities or incorporating additional damping mechanisms.
• Two-Way Coupling: Once the model provides consistent and meaningful results, it can be
extended to include two-way coupling between fluid and vegetation dynamics, allowing for
feedback effects of stem motion on the surrounding flow. This would enable more realistic
simulations of vegetation canopies and their impact on flow structures. This would increase
the model complexity drastically, but it is a necessary step to develop a state-of-the-art
numerical model which can be later used for research and engineering applications.
Other suggested improvements regarding the model:
• Material Properties: Investigate the sensitivity of model results to key parameters such as
Young’s modulus, Poisson’s ratio, and damping coeﬀicients, particularly for flexible stems.
• Drag and Inertia Coeﬀicients: Develop empirical relationships for Cd and Cm as functions
of KC, Ca, and other dimensionless numbers to improve predictive accuracy across different
flow regimes.
In summary, this thesis developed and validated two numerical approaches for simulating wave-
vegetation interactions - a computationally eﬀicient linear spectral model for rigid stems and
a more sophisticated non-linear beam model for flexible vegetation. The work demonstrated

that while linear theory can adequately predict rigid stem behaviour, capturing the complex
dynamics of flexible vegetation requires advanced non-linear modelling. The same optimisation
and validation process was carried out with an exact non-linear beam model, adapted to handle
hydrodynamic loading from flow measurements, which resulted in a partially unsatisfying out-
come, being unable to simulate the motion asymmetry for highly flexible mimics, and showing
discrepancies for cases that lie within the linear domain. The findings provide valuable insights
into drag and inertia coeﬀicients across different flow regimes, while also highlighting key limi-
tations in current modelling approaches, particularly for highly flexible stems undergoing large
deformations. Future work should focus mainly on improving the non-linear model’s handling
of asymmetric motions, having a better understanding of its behaviour in the linear region, and
eventually, extending it into a two-way fully coupled model which resolves both the solid and
fluid dynamics simultaneously.
