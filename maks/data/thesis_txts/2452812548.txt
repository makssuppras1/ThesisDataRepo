Diabetes is one of the biggest threats to public health, leading to numerous serious health 
complications and causing millions of deaths worldwide every year. The treatment relies on daily 
insulin self-injections – an inconvenient for the patient procedure prone to side-effects. An 
alternative method of insulin administration could be an orally-administrated insulin drug. 
However, insulin, as a polypeptide hormone, cannot be absorbed by the small intestine, because 
the proteins and polypeptides, generally, do not cross the intestinal barrier. 
 
The project is a part of research conducted in the Center for Intestinal Absorption and Transport 
of Biopharmaceuticals (CitBIO). In the contrary to pharmaceutical companies practises, where 
the screening of thousands of drug components is performed to find the ones of therapeutical 
properties, this project focuses on one modified peptide synthetized and selected by the CitBIO 
chemists and biophysicists for its cell membrane-binding properties. The goal of the project was: 
(i) perform a preliminary study on the uptake and intracellular transport of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
peptide, (ii) provide feedback on the peptide characteristics and on the peptide interaction with 
the cell. 
 
The experiments relied on 4D (3D+time) fluorescence spinning-disc confocal microscopy, which 
allows for imaging of live biological samples with a single-molecule resolution. Two types of 
experiments were conducted and two corresponding algorithms for fitting statistical models to the 
obtained data were developed.  
Firstly, the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 photobleaching experiments resulted in characterization of the 
peptide oligomers found in the solution as well as determining the fluorescence intensity of a 
single molecule. The second type of experiments involved monitoring the interactions of the 
peptide with Caco-2 cells – a cell line widely used across pharmaceutical industry as a small 
intestine model. Experimental data analysis showed the uptake and intracellular transport of 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 and determined one related uptake mechanism. Finally, the cell membrane 
binding properties of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 were evaluated. No binding of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 to 
healthy Caco-2 cells was detected contradicting the results of the initial biophysical 
characterization. However, strong binding to the cell membrane of dying cells was observed, as 
well as to apoptotic bodies fragmented from the cell which was an unexpected but important 
finding.  
As the project was the first of its kind in CitBIO, it came to establish an experimental and data-
analytical protocol for future studies on peptide uptake and intracellular transport conducted in 
CitBIO.

1. Introduction 
1.1 The aim of the project 
This project is a part of the Center for Intestinal Absorption and Transport of 
Biopharmaceuticals (CitBIO). The overall objective of the research in the CitBIO 
is to increase the effectiveness of insulin delivery and other peptide 
pharmaceuticals through the intestinal barrier in a form of an orally taken drug – 
an intake method most convenient from the patients’ perspective and presumably 
the least prone to cause side-effects compared to alternative methods currently 
available. However, in order to formulate new pharmaceuticals that would be able 
to cross the intestinal barrier, one needs to understand first the mechanisms of 
how their components are processed by the cell. This proposed approach is 
different than the standards used in pharmaceutical companies. The base of 
developing new pharmaceuticals in the pharmaceutical industry is drug 
compounds screening, where the primary objective is to test the largest number 
of compounds available to have the highest change of identifying the ones of 
potential therapeutical properties. The compounds are usually poorly 
characterized and chosen based on efficacy, only then its bio-availability and 
toxicity is investigated [1]. This is where the project differs greatly from the 
pharmaceutical industry and proposes a new approach in drug development. 
 
Since the mechanisms of uptake and intracellular transport of the peptide used 
in CitBIO are not well known yet, the goal of this project was to perform a 
preliminary study of a peptide transport through the membrane and inside of the 
epithelial human cell and establishing a reliable method for testing various 
(modified) peptides. Thus, there were two target outcomes of this project. First 
was providing feedback on the performance of the modified peptide that has been 
selected for testing based on initial biophysical characterization. This means 
clarifying, e.g., whether the peptide expressed binding to cell membrane, if and 
how it was transported inside the cell as well as if it showed any specific 
interaction after dissolving in the imaging buffer. Second was to create an 
==PAGE:10==
10 
 
experimental and data-analytical protocol for testing other modified peptides for 
further in-depth studies conducted in the CitBIO. 
1.2 The structure of the project 
1.2.1 Project structure 
The project was comprised of both experimental and theoretical part. The 
experimental part had two main objectives. One was to use multi-colour 4D 
fluorescence microscopy (4D stands for 3D imaging in time) to observe and 
record the interaction of live cells with the chosen modified peptide in order to 
determine if and how the peptide can be taken up by the cell. This required: 
- 
handling the immortalized cell lines, 
- 
preparation of the microscopy samples, 
- 
optimization of the procedure of seeding cells on the coverslip, 
- 
labelling cells with appropriate staining agents.  
 
The second objective was to provide additional characterization of the selected 
peptide through microscopy time lapse images of the peptide diluted in various 
imaging buffer. In contrary to standard biophysical characterization methods such 
as DLS (dynamic light scattering) which merely provides an estimation for 
parameters like average molecule cluster size distribution for the bulk solution, 
this project provided the exact visual information of how the imaging buffer 
performs under experimental conditions and how the peptide molecule clusters 
are distributed in the buffer with the accuracy of a single molecule. The single 
molecule/particle analysis used in the project accounts for heterogeneities in the 
sample which may also vary depending on the buffer used for peptide solution. It 
was particularly important because the peptides used in CitBIO are modified with 
lipid chains in order to improve its membrane binding/penetrating properties. 
However, peptides modified with lipid chains have a tendency to create 
oligomers. The information on how many peptide molecules create an oligomer 
is not available in DLS measurements. But through photobleaching experiments 
and single particle analysis combined, it was possible to record the image of and 
==PAGE:11==
 
11 
analyse each oligomer cluster detected to determine the number of single peptide 
molecules in a cluster. 
 
The theoretical part was based on data collected through the experiments. The 
goal of the analysis was to develop tools that would not only be able to process 
big amounts of experimental data, but would also provide more information that 
could lead to more complex conclusions. The idea was to reach beyond the 
standard limits of experiments by the use of statistical analysis methods to, for 
example, overcome diffraction limit by fitting a statistical model of observed 
molecule based on the molecule known characteristics which allows for more 
precise spatial localization.

In addition to the internal feedback loop, the project also played an important role 
in a greater feedback loop connecting the work of the whole CitBIO (fig. 1.2).

the feedback on the peptide’s performance is given to the chemists, so that they

1.3 Motivation 
Diabetes is perceived as one of the main challenges when it comes to public 
health. It has been estimated that there were 424.9 million adults living with 
diabetes in 2017, whereas in 1980 it was 108 million. In adult global population, 
it gives the rise of the disease occurrence from 4.7% to as much as 8.8%. 
Diabetes of all types can lead to complications such as heart attack, stroke, 
kidney failure, leg amputation, vision loss or nerve damage. Furthermore, it 
increases the risk of fetal death in pregnancy as well as the overall risk of dying 
prematurely. In 2012, diabetes caused 1.5 million deaths, whereas higher-than-
optimal blood glucose lead to additional 2.2 million deaths. 43% of these deaths 
occurred before the age of 70 [3], [4]. 
Aside from being a serious threat to the global population health, diabetes brings 
substantial economic loss not only to patients and their families, but also to health 
systems and national economies through both direct medical costs and loss of 
work and wage [3]. According to American Diabetes Association, the total 
estimated cost of diagnosed diabetes in 2017 just in the United States was $327 
billion, of which $237 billion were spent on direct medical costs and $90 billion 
were related to reduced productivity [5]. 
Insulin is a hormone that plays a key role in the metabolism of the body by 
allowing glucose from the blood to enter the body cells [6]. Diabetes occurs either 
when the pancreas does not produce enough insulin or when the body cannot 
==PAGE:14==
14 
 
effectively use insulin it produces. Therefore, next to a healthy lifestyle and diet, 
medication for blood glucose control – oral hypo-glycaemic agents and insulin – 
is crucial in diabetes management [3]. Despite being one of the most common 
diseases [7], for the majority of the patients, the treatment still means relying on 
daily insulin self-injections. Even though the treatment is necessary, it is neither 
convenient, nor side-effects free due to the method of the drug intake [8]. 
Moreover, performing a self-injection is not an easy procedure and may be too 
discouraging for many patients. The compliance could be increased by 
introducing alternative methods of drug administration that would be more 
convenient for the patient [3]. The solution could be formulation of an orally 
administrated insulin. However, insulin is a polypeptide hormone and normally 
proteins and polypeptides cannot cross the intestinal epithelium without going 
through the process of digestion. Taken orally, they are broken into amino acids 
by the enzymes in the gastrointestinal tract and in that form they are absorbed by 
the epithelium. The digestion process destroys the physiological activity of the 
proteins – constituent amino acids do not have the properties of the original 
proteins or polypeptides. Nonetheless, it has been discovered that some few 
peptides and proteins express the ability to cross the intestinal barrier intact as 
long as they are administrated directly to intestines, avoiding the digestive acids 
in the stomach [9]. Understanding the process of how and why these specific 
peptides and proteins are being absorbed is essential for the formulation of orally 
administrated insulin drugs.  
 
1.4 Intestinal barrier 
The small intestine is an organ in the gastrointestinal tract where most of the 
absorption of nutrients and minerals from food takes place. The intestinal 
epithelium is a single-cell layer lining the small intestine, that constitutes the 
largest and most important barrier against the external environment. It acts as a 
selectively permeable barrier permitting the absorption of nutrients, electrolytes 
and water, while maintaining an effective defense against antigens, 
microorganism and toxins [10], [11]. The epithelium maintains its defensive

schematic representation of the intestinal epithelium cell barrier. The epithelium 
stem cells can differentiate into 4 cell linages: enterocytes (intestinal absorptive 
cells), enteroendocrine cells, mucus producing goblet cells and Paneth cells 
which are only found in the human small intestine [12].

The intestinal epithelium allows exchange of molecules between host and 
environment through two major routes: transepithelial/transcellular and

1.5 Caco-2 cell line 
The Caco-2 cell line was chosen for the experiments, since it is widely used 
across the pharmaceutical industry in studies on absorption of orally administered 
drugs. This cell line, originally obtained from a human colon adenocarcinoma, 
develops morphologic characteristics of normal enterocytes (epithelial cells found 
in the small intestine) when grown on dishes which makes caco-2 cell line a very 
good candidate for a model of the intestinal barrier [13], [14].  
Therefore, in this thesis, “cells” refer to caco-2 cells unless specified otherwise.

1.6 Spinning-disc confocal microscopy 
1.6.1 Optical sectioning – confocal laser scanning microscopy 
In an ideal microscope, only the light coming from the focal plane would be used 
to create the image of the sample which means that each produced image would 
represent only the plane (section) of the sample that the microscope was focused 
on. In practice, the light coming from outside the focal plane can also contribute 
to the imaging introducing noise to the output image. In order to improve the 
clarity of the image, one can cut the sample into very thin slices (sections) to 
ensure that no stray light would contribute to the image of the plane in focus. 
However, this solution is not suitable for, e.g., live cell imaging where preservation 
of the sample structural and dynamic characteristics is the main requirement. 
Therefore, an optical sectioning must be used due to its non-invasive nature. 
Optical sectioning is the process in which a microscope can image focal planes 
well within the volume of the sample. In this way, high quality images of thick 
samples can be produced [15].  
 
In a simple wide field microscope, the depth of field is the factor which determines 
the optical sectioning. The depth of field is an axial range, through which an 
objective can be focused without any appreciable change in image sharpness 
[16]. This value varies radically from low to high numerical aperture objectives, 
usually decreasing with increasing numerical aperture (NA) (fig. 1.5).  
 
 
Figure.1.5. Representation of depths of field relation to NA. Figure adapter from [17]. 
==PAGE:18==
18 
 
The numerical aperture of a microscope objective is a measure of its ability to 
gather light and resolve fine specimen detail at a fixed object distance (fig. 1.6) 
[18]. NA value is calculated based on refractive index of the medium between the 
objective and the opening angle of the objective (fig. 1.6). 
 
Figure.1.6.  Representation of the light focused by an objective of low (left: NA = 0.25) and high (right: NA 
= 0.95) NA. n – refractive index of the medium between the objective front lens and the specimen, θ – one 
half of the objective opening angle. Figure adapter from [18]. 
 
To sum up, generally, the higher NA, the better resolving power of the objective 
and the better optical sectioning due to shallower depth of field. Therefore, oil 
immersion objectives are used for CLSM – the NA of an oil immersion objective 
can be higher because the refractive index of oil is higher than refractive index of 
air or water (fig. 1.6) [17], [18]. 
 
To further improve optical sectioning, confocal microscopy technique must be 
used. Confocal laser scanning microscopy (CLSM) is a form of optical sectioning 
microscopy and is based on fluorescence microscopy. Laser light is used to 
excite endogenous or exogenous fluorescent molecules in the sample, and this 
fluorescence light is detected.  
In fluorescence, the excitation light is of a higher energy (higher frequency/shorter 
wavelength) than emitted light (lower frequency/longer wavelength). Jablonski 
diagram illustrating excitation process and fluorescence emission is presented in 
fig. 1.7. Photon emitted by an external source, such as laser, is absorbed by a 
Light cone 
Light cone 
==PAGE:19==

fluorophore molecule creating an excited electronic singlet state (S1). The excited 
state exists for a finite time (typically a 1-10 ns). The fluorophore is a subject to 
interaction with its molecular environment and during that time the energy is 
partially dissipated through non-radiative transitions. This leads the fluorophore 
to the relaxed singlet state (S1’). Unless other process is involved, eventually, the 
fluorophore undergoes fast decay to the ground state (So) followed by the 
emission of a photon. The emitted photon is of a lower energy than the excitation 
photon due to energy loss from non-radiative transitions. This means that the 
wavelength of the emitted fluorescence is longer than the excitation laser light 
wavelength. This difference in energy between excitation and emission is called 
Stoke’s shift and is essential for fluorescence techniques because it allows for 
filtering out the excitation light from the fluorescence signal, resulting in detecting 
signal against a low background [19], [20]. 
 
 
Figure. 1.7. Left) Jablonski diagram illustrating the processes involved in the creation of an excited 
electronic singlet state by optical absorption and emission of fluorescence – courtesy of Dominik Marti. 
Right) Absorption and emission spectra of the fluorescent molecule Atto488 [21]. Absorption and  
emission spectrum are separated by the Stokes’s shift which allows for isolating the fluorescent signal  
from the excitation light. 
 
The principle of CLSM is to use laser light as the excitation and detect filtered 
fluorescent signal from the probe. Desirably, the detected light would be emitted 
only from the focal plane. However, the fluorescent molecules would be excited

not only in the focal plane, but also other molecules in the cone of light can emit 
fluorescent signal (fig.1.8). Therefore, the confocal spatial filter has been 
introduced to the setup (fig. 1.9) to prevent out-of-focus light from reaching the 
detector. The confocal set up consists of spatial filter which is a pinhole placed in 
front of the detector, and the light source/objective pinhole that ensures quasi-
point source of light and tight focus on the sample. The two pinholes have the 
same focus – which means they are confocal [15], [19], [22]–[24].

In order to build an image using the confocal principle, the focused spot of light 
must be scanned across the specimen in some way. One solution would be to 
change the sample position, however, it is relatively slow and in case of biological 
samples this may cause wobbling and distortion of the image as well as it 
prevents the use of any manipulation to the sample during the measurement. 
Better solution is to scan the beam across the sample instead with the use of 
oscillating mirror and keep the specimen stable. The drawback of using the 
oscillating mirror as a scanning technique is relatively slow scanning, because 
the whole image must be scanned point by point. However, a new method of 
scanning has been introduced using a spinning disc. 
 
1.6.2 Spinning-disc confocal microscopy 
The spinning-disc confocal microscopy overcomes the insufficient speed problem 
of CLSM by using multiple laser beams instead of a single beam. 
The principle of scanning in the spinning disc microscope is the rotating disc with 
a set of pinholes and an image sensor such as CCD or CMOS camera (fig.1.11). 
The laser light is illuminated onto the section of the disc. The light passing through 
the pinholes, that serve for both  excitation and emission pinholes, creates 
multiple mini beans that are itself confocal. The pinholes are arranged in the 
==PAGE:22==
22 
 
pattern allowing covering the whole field of view when the disc rotates. The 
crosstalk between the individual mini beams corresponding to individual pinholes 
is prevented by spacing the pinholes far apart. It that case most of the light would 
be blocked and not reach the sample, however, placing a second disc of 
microlenses in front of the disc with pinholes significantly increases the amount 
of excitation light passing through. The fluorescence signal is collected and 
detected by the camera creating the final image almost instantaneously [24].

C) the constant pitch helical pinhole pattern of the Yokogawa spinning disk in the image field. During 
rotation of the disk, the pinholes evenly sweep the whole field of view. Figure adapted from [24].

The experiments in this project required setup capabilities of 4D imaging, where 
4D stands for 3D imaging in time, and single molecule imaging. The spinning-
disc configuration used in the project allows for both. 3D imaging is achieved by 
confocal imaging (XY imaging) and with the use of piezoelectric stage for moving 
the sample in Z direction. Single molecule imaging was possible thanks to 
sCMOS detection used in the setup. 
The microscope allows imaging in 5 different channels: Bright field (BF), 405 
channel, 488 channel, 561 channel and 640 channel, where the last four 
correspond to available laser extrications of 405 nm, 488 nm, 561 nm and 640 
nm respectively. For the fluorescence imaging, one excitation wavelength is 
chosen at a time and the fluorescence signal is detected. Due to the use of 1 
quad band emission filter blocking light at these 4 wavelengths, there is no way 
of determining the exact fluorescence spectrum or filtering out parts of it. 
 
 
2. Methods 
2.1 Imaging buffers 
In order to use the fluorescent microscopy for cell experiments, an imaging buffer 
must be used. First of all, the cell growth medium in which the cells are normally 
kept contains the dye phenol red, which is commonly used as a PH indicator for 
the cell cultures [25]. This disqualifies the standard cell growth medium from 
being used as an imaging buffer in fluorescent microscopy. Secondly, the cell 
growth medium contains multiple elements that interact with the cell and can 
potentially interact with the added substances such as the peptide. If the aim of 
the experiments is to observe interaction of the cell with the chosen substance, 
one needs to make sure that the cell uptake is going to be optimized for the 
substance, not nutrients from the cell growth medium that are often preferred by 
the cell. The interaction of the substance with cell growth medium would be 
undesirable as well in this case, since it may unpredictably influence the outcome. 
Lastly, the imaging buffers for fluorescence microscopy are formulated to 
==PAGE:24==
24 
 
minimize background fluorescence as well as fluorophore blinking, which is 
crucial for obtaining high quality and high resolution images. 
 
There is a wide variety of imaging buffers, however, one needs to choose the 
buffer suitable not only for their project objective but also the needs of the living 
sample.  
When it comes to this project, no buffer optimized to all of our experiment 
requirements exists. The perfect buffer would have three unique features. Firstly, 
it would create a realistic model of the environment in the intestine. Secondly, it 
would be optimized for single-molecule fluorescence microscopy to minimize 
fluorophore blinking and background fluorescence. Lastly, it would be suitable for 
long acquisition times to be able to monitor cells uptake for hours. 
Unfortunately, no buffer can fulfil all these needs and a compromise is necessary. 
Another aspect to keep in mind is how the peptide sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 reacts 
with the buffer. Features such as solubility or tendency to aggregation can differ 
depending on the buffer composition. 
 
The purpose of the project was to perform preliminary characterization of the 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488, therefore three buffers, that were already used in the 
centre, were chosen for the bleaching experiments;  
- 
A buffer optimized for single-molecule observation (SM buffer),  
- 
FluoroBrite DMEM – a commercially available cell medium optimised for 
fluorescence microscopy (FluoroBrite buffer) and  
- 
a simulated intestinal fluid (MinSIF buffer). 
 
The composition of the SM and MinSIF buffers is presented in the appendix B.

2.2 Peptide characterization 
2.2.1 Stock solution concentration measurements 
The peptide sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 was provided in the dehydrated form. The 
peptide sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 is an analogue of the salmon calcitonin sCal 
peptide with two C8 modification, labelled with fluorophore Atto488 (appendix C 
and D).  
In order to be able to use it in any type of experiment, it needed to be dissolved 
in a buffer. The buffer of choice was the MinSIF buffer. A precisely measured 
amount of the buffer was added to the dehydrated sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 and 
mixed to obtain the concentration of 50μM. Since it was observed that not all of 
the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 got dissolved, to verify that we indeed have the desired 
peptide concentration, a NanoDrop™ 2000 Spectrophotometer was used to 
measure the absorbance of one of the batches of the stock solution. The Beer-
Lambert equation is used to correlate the given absorbance with molar 
concentration [26]. Two measurements were taken and the average value of the 
calculated concentration was 23.44 ± 1.44 μM. Given the observation that not all 
of the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 got dissolved, this result was satisfactory enough to 
proceed with the planned experiments. 
 
2.2.2 Photobleaching experiments 
When it comes to the characterization of the provided peptide – sCal(C8,C8)-
Atto488, one goal of this project was to get a visual information on the  
homogeneity of the stock solution and size of the peptide molecule aggregates 
as well as to develop a method for the peptide quantification in live cell images.  
 
The peptide characterization was achieved by high-resolution imaging of the 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 deposited on the coverslip bottom. It was assumed that a 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 aggregates photobleach in steps corresponding to roughly 
integer multiples of a unitary level (ΔI) which is the intensity of a single Atto488 
molecule [27]–[29]. Therefore, the total initial intensity of the aggregate divided

fluorophores in the aggregate corresponding to the number of sCal(C8,C8)-

To sum up, recording the time lapse image of the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
photobleaching process and determining these photobleaching steps would 
determine the ΔI necessary for the peptide quantification.

2.3 Image analysis – the algorithm 
2.3.1 Analysis of the photobleaching experiment 
To analyse the photobleaching experiment, a way of determining the 
photobleaching steps was necessary. In order to do that, the first step was to 
localize all spots of interest. This was done using the Fiji built-in functions (as 
presented in the previous section 2.2) and this detected positions were the input 
for the step-finding algorithm. Based on the positions of the spots, the algorithm 
was calculating the sum intensity over the area 8x8 px covering the spot for each 
frame of the analysed time lapse. This data was used to create time traces of 
each spot separately. An exemplary time trace is presented in fig. 2.7. The 
fluorophore bleaches in steps (each step marked with a green arrow). Moreover, 
an even of fluorophore blinking can be noticed. Blinking is a fluctuation in the 
fluorophore intensity – this happens when a molecule goes into a dark state for a 
limited amount of time [33]. 
 
==PAGE:33==
 
33 
 
Figure 2.7. Exemplary time trace of one spot detected in the time lapse. The graph shows total intensity 
calculated over the area 8x8 px covering the spot against the frame number. The spot bleaches in steps

The next step was to create a method that would fit optimal steps into the 
bleaching time trace (as in fig. 2.8). A step-finding algorithm was developed under 
the assumptions that  
1) In a spot of multiple sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 peptides, molecules bleach one 
by one and each non-bleached molecule emits the light intensity of the 
same value ΔI (in the duration of the frame). This means that each spot 
bleaches in steps and the step-size is determined by ΔI (fig. 2.7) [27]–[29]; 
2) Two or more bleaching events are allowed at the same time point which 
means that the step-size may be equal to ΔI or integer multiples of ΔI; 
3) Fluorophore blinking (fig. 2.7) is not considered – the steps are counted 
from the end of the time trace only upwards; 
4) The step-size is not fixed – the algorithm is allowed to find the optimal step-
size and optimal number of steps for each time trace independently.

The step-finding algorithm developed in this project was based on the idea of 
adding one optimally fitted step at a time and evaluating the model fit afterwards. 
The assumption is that each molecule emits the light intensity of the same value 
ΔI which means that each intensity level in the time trace is determined by a value 
of the integer multiple of ΔI plus the background Io. Considering the fluctuations 
in registered intensity, each data point is determined by a gaussian distribution 
(due to the noise [34]) with the mean value ΔI and standard deviation.  
The molecules photobleach with time and ideally they bleach completely before 
the end of the measurement. Therefore, there is only a signal from the 
background detected at the end of the time trace. So first “step” evaluated for the 
model is the background level I0 assumed constant throughout the measurement. 
The mean over last few hundred frames is calculated as well as its standard 
deviation (fig. 2.9).

Free range of step-sizes 
Up to this point, the algorithm is capable of fitting the best model to the data 
optimizing the number of steps, but out of given set of step-sizes. The last task is 
to introduce the unconstrained step-size fitting.  
 
The function based on golden section search and parabolic interpolation was 
incorporated to optimize the step-size fitting. The general concept of this function 
is to start with two extreme step-size values (one extremely small and another 
one extremely large), perform all of the calculations (chi-squared test fitting and 
AIC analysis) for these two step-sizes and compare the final AIC values for both 
cases. The model/step-size with the smaller AIC is chosen for the next iteration, 
where the new step-size value is determined based on golden section search and 
the process of comparing two optimal models for two different step-sizes is 
repeated. With each iteration the difference in AIC between compared models 
gets smaller as algorithm approaches the optimal step-size. This repeats until the 
two compared models have the same AIC value – then the parabolic interpolation 
is used to determine the final, optimal value of the step-size.

3.1.3 SCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 photobleaching –  
the analysis of the steps 
To proceed with the photobleaching measurements, the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
was added to the ibidi plate chamber filled with imaging buffer. Two buffers were 
tested, the MinSIF buffer, on which there was the main focus, and SM buffer, for 
comparison. The FluoroBrite buffer was rejected from the final results analysis 
for the reasons mentioned in the previous section – the fluorescent aggregates 
coming from the buffer would interfere with the measurements and made it 
impossible to acquire quality bleaching time lapses for long acquisition times. 
 
It was crucial to get time lapse recording in which the fluorophore spots bleach 
completely in order to obtain the proper step size and number of steps. Firstly, 
the experiments were taken with 350 frames for each time lapse, which occurred 
to be insufficient to bleach many of the spots. In general, it was observed that the 
spots bleach very rapidly at first, however, the analysis showed that even 2000 
frames taken in 100% of the laser power is not enough to bleach all of the spots. 
Nonetheless, 2000 frames made an acceptable compromise between 
maximizing acquisition time and minimising the duration of the experiment as well 
as data files size.

Step-finding algorithm evaluation 
Each bright spot in the time lapse corresponds to the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
aggregate containing multiple fluorophores. The purpose of the developed step-
finding algorithm was to detect the steps in the time trace corresponding to a 
photobleaching event of a single fluorophore. Since all the fluorophore molecules 
are the same, the step size between intensity levels corresponding to 
consecutively bleached fluorophores also should be the same. Knowing the step 
size means finding the intensity of one fluorophore which can be used for later 
evaluation of the number of fluorophores and corresponding number of 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 molecules which is the main objective of this section. 
 
Figures 3.7.a – 3.7.e present the performance of the algorithm used on time 
traces obtained from time lapse of the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 bleaching 
experiment done under 80% of laser power in FluoroBrite imaging buffer. These 
experimental results were not considered in the final analysis of the step size ΔI 
due to short acquisition – only 350 frames were taken. However, the time traces 
obtained from the measurements are still valuable source of information on the 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 and algorithm performance. 
The graphs in fig. 3.7 shows experimental data – the sum intensity over the spot 
of 6x6 px against the frame number of the time lapse – in solid black line. The 
fitted steps are represented by coloured dashed line. Starting from setting the 
background level, steps were added one by one to the model as long as adding 
the extra step was optimally beneficial as discussed in the section 2.3.1.  
 
There are two events related to the fluorophore itself that could diminish the 
algorithm accuracy; fluorophore blinking and simultaneous bleaching of more 
than one fluorophore. As shown in fig. 3.7.e) and 3.7.b) respectively, neither short 
lived fluorophore blinking events, nor an event that last significantly long interfere 
with the step finding process. When it comes to fluorophores bleaching in the 
same time, since our time resolution is limited due to finite exposure time, it may 
happen that within a time frame more than one fluorophore would bleach. Then

Step sizes and population histograms – long acquisition measurements 
 
For the finial investigation of the step size, only long acquisition (2000 frames) 
measurements were used. As mentioned, the FluoroBrite buffer was rejected and 
the post-processing was done only for sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 bleaching 
measurements done in MinSIF buffer and SM buffer. 
 
To decrease the data files size, shorten the analysis time and ensure more 
uniform illumination of the sample within analysed region, only a part of the 
central selection of the original images was taken into consideration. All time 
lapse images were cropped to 600x600px (from original size of 1200x1200px).  
For each tested buffer and power settings, all detected spots were analysed for 
the step sizes by the step-finding algorithm. Since the process of detecting the 
spots in Fiji is not very selective, many of the analysed spots are not suitable for 
the analysis. The algorithm is allowed to fit steps under no major constraint which 
means that it is going to fit some model to all of the detected spots. When the 
steps are not clear in the time trace or if the time trace does not follow the step-
like trend due to for example excessive blinking, sedimentation of a new 
fluorescent particle or additional noise, the step-sizes detected by the algorithm 
would not represent the photobleaching properly. The example of such case is 
presented in fig. 3.8. 
 
The time trace presented in the top figure shows no clearly separated steps even 
though the particle bleaches. In the bottom figure the algorithm fitted the model 
consisting of 24 steps of the step-size equal to 8.9 [arb. unit]. Because there is 
no even steps in the data, the algorithm favoured many small steps to propose 
better fitting. With the proposed analysis method this seems to be the most 
common problem.

following analysis for each laser power considered – 60%, 80%, 100%. The 
average step-size values are consistent with the observations of the photo-
bleaching time-traces. The results are also repeatable – similar average step-size 
were found for different time-lapses recorded for samples that were prepared and 
tested on different days.  
When it comes to peptide characterization, not only the intensity of a single 
molecule was determined but also the distribution of the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
aggregate sizes was evaluated. The average number of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
molecules in an aggregate was 12 ± 8 for the minSiF buffer experiments and  
8 ± 3 for the SM buffer. What is though-provoking, is that the DLS measurements 
of unlabelled sCal(C8,C8) showed the average diameter of a molecule to be 
approx. 5.5 ± 0.5 nm for 100 μM concentration (the lowest concentration 
measured with DLS – data presented in appendix G). In our experiments, we 
used the sCal(C8,C8) labelled with Atto488 – sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 – dissolved 
in the buffer with assumed concentration of 50 μM (measured concentration 
23.44 ± 1.44 μM). Due to the lower concentration, one could assume that the 
solubility issues should be even less probable. However, it was observed that not 
only measured concentration was significantly lower than the calculated one, but 
also the aggregates observed in the experiment were much bigger, not a few 
nanometres as DLS measurements suggest. This may indicate that labelling 
process changed the properties of the peptide and made it more prone to 
aggregate into bigger molecules.  
 
No efficient way of filtering the well-fitted step-sizes was found, the steps fitted to 
all time traces were subjectively assessed and manually selected for further 
analysis. This was not unexpected because optimizing such algorithms 
performance is extremely difficult and a field of its own. However, due to the fact 
==PAGE:72==
72 
 
that there were as much as almost 3000 spots detected only for the MinSIF buffer 
measurements, it elongated the analysis time significantly.  
 
The control background measurements provided three important pieces of 
information. The first one referred to the imaging buffers used in the experiment. 
It occurred that even though all of the buffers should be suitable for live 
fluorescent microscopy imaging, some issues were encountered while using 
buffers under the experimental conditions. It was discovered that the FluoroBrite 
buffer creates fluorescent aggregates that are very similar to the sCal(C8,C8)-
Atto488 aggregates. The FluoroBrite DMEM without added antibiotics and 
Glutamx (appendix B), so in the form provided by the producer, was also tested 
and it showed the same behaviour. The discovery was made in the final phase of 
the project. Had it been discovered earlier, it would eliminate this buffer early in 
the project which would allow for obtaining better results and less irrelevant work 
eventually. Trusting that the commercially made buffer optimized for live cell 
fluorescent microscopy is suitable for the experiment without performing the 
control measurement first was a mistake. The recommendation would be to 
perform more tests on the imaging buffers and other used components before 
proceeding with experiments.  
Nonetheless, one could still potentially use the measurements taken with the 
FluoroBrite buffer. It was observed that the aggregates from the buffer are clearly 
visible in bright field (BF) microscopy they co-localize with the signal from the 
aggregates detected in 488 channel. At the same time, the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
aggregates seem not to be detectible in the BF channel. Therefore, one could 
probably filter out the unwanted signal coming from the buffer aggregates. 
 
The second important information was proving that the illumination in the middle 
range of the frame (area of 600x600 px out of 1200x1200 px of the full frame) is 
even, so that the detected intensity coming from a single molecule should not 
vary depending on the XY position of the spot. 
 
==PAGE:73==
 
73 
The third discovery revealed that the background is indeed bleaching and do not 
have a constant value throughout the measurement. The difference between the 
initial and final intensity of the background ΔIbg was approx. 100 [arb. unit] for 
100% laser power. This is a significant change provided that the average intensity 
of a single molecule was 25.5 ± 6.5 [arb. unit]. Considering that the average spot 
contained 12 molecules, it gives rough estimate for the initial intensity of the spot 
of approx. 300 [arb. unit] which is in the same order as the background intensity 
change ΔIbg. It shows that the background noise can significantly influence time 
traces, especially for the spots smaller (i.e. weaker) than average. Bleaching 
background has not been considered in the analysis for this project, however, it 
definitely should be in the future following studies. There are a few ways in which 
the background could be included in the analysis. One way would be evaluating 
the average intensity of the pixels surrounding the detected sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
spot in each frame, therefore the background would be evaluated in each frame 
separately could be subtracted from the spot intensity. Another way would be to 
use single particle tracking analysis (section 3.2.1). The algorithm fits a model to 
the spot for each frame separately, fitting the spot intensity with the background 
already considered.   
 
Lastly, it was observed that even though the acquisition time was increased and 
time lapses were elongated to 2000 frames (from initial 350 frames), still not all 
the spots bleached completely. In this case one could think that an obvious 
solution would be to just further increase the number of frames. However, 
increasing the number of frames would increase both, acquisition time and the 
size of the file. 2000 frames set with 500ms exposure time takes about 20 minutes 
and as it was discussed before, the buffers do not tolerate well being kept too 
long under the microscope, so performing the full experiment with many time 
lapses would not only mean prolonging the time of the experiment. It would also 
require changing the cover glass to a new one with fresh buffer and portion of the 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488, otherwise one would risk running into a problem of 
unwanted fluorescent aggregates. More frames in the time lapse would also 
increase the already significant size of the file, which has its own consequences.  
==PAGE:74==
74 
 
The time lapses needed to be cropped in XY plane not only to assure the even 
illumination of analysed area as well as limit the number of detected spots, but 
also to be able to run the file in MATLAB or Fiji. The cache memory of any 
available computer was not sufficient to process the full size time lapses, which, 
generally, was a reoccurring problem for many measurements taken in this 
project. 
 
3.1.5 Conclusions 
The main goal of the photobleaching experiments was to determine the intensity 
of a single sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 molecule in order to use that value in quantitative 
analysis of cell uptake experiments. It was observed that an isolated aggregate 
of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 molecules photobleach in steps, where each step 
corresponds to a single or double molecule photobleaching event. The 
experimental data allowed for extracting the size of the step through analytical 
model fitting. 
 
The experiment and analysis provided interesting and valuable information in 
terms of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 preliminary characterization. It was discovered that 
the peptide is not uniformly distributed in the solution. On the contrary, it creates 
aggregates of approx. 12 ± 8 molecules. Moreover, the intensity of a single 
molecule was determined, which can be used for determining the sCal(C8,C8)-
Atto488 molecules number in analysis of the cell uptake experiment (section 
3.2.5). The method and data acquired can be used for further characterization of 
the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 properties. Parameters such as exposure time required 
for photobleaching all molecules in the spot, time between consecutive bleaching 
events in the time trace, occurrence of events like simultaneous bleaching of two 
(or more) molecules or fluorophore blinking and more can be successfully 
investigated. What is crucial is that these detailed studies cannot be done using 
standard methods of peptide characterization such as for example DLS. DLS 
provide only the information on average particle size in the solution. But it does 
not give direct information whether these particles consists of one or more 
==PAGE:75==
 
75 
molecule of tested peptide. It does not provide any detailed information on the 
molecules interaction with the peptide in time either. 
 
In addition, the imaging buffer stability and performance under the experimental 
conditions of live cell imaging as well as the buffer interaction with the peptide 
can be visually tested and assessed as control measurements for the cell uptake 
experiments. 
 
However, introducing to the algorithm a method for assessing the background 
that is bleaching seems to be important in order to improve the accuracy of the 
experiment analysis. 
 
3.2 Cell sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 uptake experiments 
The main objective of this part of experiments was to observe and record the 
interaction of cells with the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 peptide. However, the 
experiments targets included also: 
- 
Assessment of cell coverslip seeding procedure  
In order to perform any imaging experiments with the cells, the cells 
needed to be seeded on the ibidi coverslip. The goal was to obtain cell 
groups forming a 2D layer on the bottom of the coverslip – the cells were  
not supposed to be stuck on top of each other. The distribution of the cells 
in the coverslip depends on the seeding procedure. Different procedure 
parameters such as e.g. the amount of cells seeded in the chamber were 
used and then the results were evaluated through direct microscopy 
observation of the cell deposition on the coverslip. This allowed for the 
optimization of the seeding procedure. The optimized procedure is 
described in the protocol in appendix A. 
 
- 
Autofluorescence measurements 
Cells contain molecules that can be excited by the laser light and express 
fluorescence. It is called autofluorescence to be distinguished from the 
==PAGE:76==
76 
 
fluorescence coming from added exogenous markers [42]. In general, the 
cell related measurements in the project rely on detecting the fluorescent 
signal from sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 added to Caco-2 cell culture. The 
autofluorescence from the cells is undesired because it may be detected 
as well and interfere with Atto488 signal. Therefore, it was important to 
investigate the possible contribution of autofluorescence to the acquired 
data. 
 
- 
Cell labelling 
The fluorescence microscopy provides greater contrast than bright field 
microscopy and allows for detection of different cell structures through 
labelling [43]. The fluorescent labelling agents can selectively target 
proteins, structures, and biological processes. Therefore, using various 
labels of distinct emission wavelengths enables separate observation of 
different cell elements in one experiment [44]. In the experiment the 
labelling was done to investigate with which cell structures the 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 interacts. 
 
- 
Investigation of the cells death under the experimental conditions 
The experimental setup was prepared to fulfil the needs of cells in terms 
of the environment – the microscopy chamber has been designed to keep 
a constant temperature of 37°C and the atmosphere was enriched with 5% 
CO2. These values correspond to the temperature and atmosphere kept 
in the cell culture incubator. However, after 3 months of experiments, it 
was observed that the cells, expressing otherwise normal growth, started 
to rapidly die after being put into the microscopy chamber. This interrupted 
the project schedule and made the planned cell uptake experiments 
impossible to conduct for approx. two months. The trouble shooting 
procedure was implemented which eventually lead to the improvement on 
the cell survival time under the experimental condition. However, cell 
death in the imaging buffer with sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 revealed some 
==PAGE:77==
 
77 
unexpected interesting interaction which is going to be presented and 
discussed later in the section 3.2.4. 
 
All of the measurements on cells were done with oil immersion objectives of 60x 
or 100x magnification with 300 ms exposure time, except for the measurements 
taken with high laser powers presented in the end of this section where the 
exposure time was set to 500 ms. The measurements were taken as 3D time 
lapse images (4D) which means that 2D images were taken (XY planes) for a 
range of depths determined by the distance between objective and the sample (Z 
direction) (fig.3.14). In that way so called Z-stack images were captured which 
gives the 3D image of the sample. The distance between the consecutive XY 
images ΔZ was set to 0.3 μm unless stated otherwise. The time lapse consists of 
Z-stacks of the same positions taken consecutively in time.

3.2.3 Cell labelling – membrane and early endosomes labelling 
In order to use fluorescence microscopy, one can either rely on autofluorescence 
of the sample or introduce exogenous staining agents (labels/markers). Labelling 
techniques allow for staining various structures selectively. A wide range of 
available fluorescent dies makes observation of multiple labelled objects possible 
[43], [44].  
In this project, two fluorescent markers for cell labelling were used. First one was 
used to label the membrane of the cell in order to be able to see the clear outline 
of individual cells and possibly co-localize the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 spots in the 
peripheral areas of the cells with the cell membrane. 
The second one was chosen to stain the early endosomes of the cell. Analysing

is a high possibility of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 being taken up through the process 
of endocytosis. Therefore, a fluorescent marker for staining early endosomes was 
chosen as the second label used in the project.

- 
the label must fit to the experimental setup 
The excitation of the label must have been suitable for the excitation lasers 
available in the setup; 
- 
the label must not be excited by 488 nm laser 
488 channel was reserved for sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488, and any cross-talk

- 
the label must be quick and easy to use. 
 
Membrane labelling with DiD 
The DiD label was chosen for the membrane labelling. The label is a lipophilic 
dye that binds to cell membrane since the membrane has the structure of 
phospholipid bilayer [46], [49]–[51]. It is also easy to use, no special preparation 
is required. The label is added to the cells seeded on the coverslip and is washed 
off after a few minutes right before the experiment. It has the excitation peak at 
==PAGE:92==

approx. 640 nm and the excitation spectrum of the DiD does not overlap with 488 
nm excitation [51]. 
 
The XY image of the cells stained with DiD is presented in fig. 3.24 and fig. 3.25.

filopodia. Filopodia are thin cell membrane protrusions that, among other 
functions, play a role in cell adhesion and migration processes, cell-to-cell 
interaction as well probing the cell environment [50], [52], [53]. These cell 
structures occurred to be important in the experimental results presented in the 
next section.

properties. For the Caco-2 cells, one hour of incubation in imaging medium was 
enough to solve the problem of non-active cells in the microscopy coverslip.

Since previous experiments lead to assumptions that sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 is 
taken up into the cell through endocytosis which is one of the ways in which 
substances can be brought into the cell. The endocytosis in principle consists of 
compartments that: 
a) internalize substances from the plasma membrane and sort them 
(early endosomes) to  
b) recycling back to the surface (recycling endosomes),  
c) or sort them to degradation (late endosomes and lysosomes). 
The first step in the endocytosis process is creation of a vesicle with the 
substance that is carried to the early endosomes [46]–[48], [57]. This process is 
mediated by GTP-Rab5, where the Rab5 protein can be targeted with the 
fluorescent label.

3.2.4 Cells dying under the experimental conditions:  
unexpected results 
At some point during the project, the cells seeded on the ibidi plate started to 
rapidly die under after placed under the microscope for no apparent reason. If the 
cells were left under the microscope and imaged just with the BF mode, they 
would die within 2 hours (fig. 3.33). If one tried fluorescence microscopy, the cells 
would start to die very fast and would die within minutes (fig. 3.35). There was no 
apparent reason for that change in cells behaviour that happened over the break 
in work related to Easter holidays. The setup did not seem to change in any way, 
==PAGE:106==
106 
 
the only thing that changed was the batch of cells. When one wants to work with 
cells, the cells need to be firstly thawed and then regularly passaged to keep them 
alive. Due to the holiday break, the first batch used in the project was discarded 
and a new batch was prepared after the break. If a mistake happens during the 
process of freezing or thawing, the cells may occur to be excessively sensitive to 
the environmental conditions. However, neither preparing new batches of 
different cell types nor using cells of a different cell line solved the problem. The 
trouble shooting process is described in appendix F.  
 
Eventually, two major changes were introduced into the experimental setup in 
order to improve the environmental conditions for the cell samples. First change 
was moving the probe measuring temperature in the microscopy chamber from 
the peripheral area to be close to the sample. This improved the temperature 
control – one made sure that the cells are in the set temperature of 37°C. The 
second change was to cover the extra holes in the sample holder to prevent the 
air enriched in CO2 from escaping and mixing with atmospheric air. The sample 
with cells is installed onto the sample holder on the piezo stage and is covered 
with a glass window from the top. The 5% CO2 air is pumped directly into the 
sample area. This was not completely closed before – the 5% CO2 air would mix 
with atmospheric air, therefore, the cells would experience CO2 levels lower than 
optimal.  
 
Even though the problem was not identified, the changes made during the trouble 
shooting process solved the problem of cell rapidly dying under the microscope. 
This problem delayed the project and forced long-term changes into the project 
plan. However, experiments performed on dying cells brought unexpected and 
very interesting results. Until this point, no binding of the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 to 
the cell membrane was observed. The desired interaction would show in the 488 
channel by marking the cell membrane uniformly with the signal from 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 (as it is presented in fig. 3.23). Instead, only the 
agglomerations of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 were observed to be taken up by the cell 
and transported by the organelles.

The results show strong binding to both the dead fragmented parts of the cell and 
“shrunk” cells detached from the bottom of the coverslip. Apparently, there is a 
strong relation between changes in the cell membrane that happen due to cell 
death and the binding properties of the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488. 
In the case of detached cells (fig. 3.39), one hypothesis is that when the cell 
changes its shape from flattened to spherical, and covers smaller area, therefore 
bigger part of the membrane, especially in Z direction, is imaged in one acquired 
frame. So, the conclusion would be that the peptide binding properties does not 
change, the signal was simply too low before the death of the cell to detect.  
However, there is one observation that contradicts this hypothesis. The peptide 
also binds strongly to apoptotic bodies that were fragmented from the cell (fig. 
3.37). However, these apoptotic bodies does not change the shape once they are 
fragmented. In contrary, the shape of these objects stays the same, whereas only 
the fluorescence signal coming from them changes in time. At first, when they are 
fragmented from the cells, the signal is coming from the DiD membrane label 
visible as red on the time lapse images. However, the colour changes gradually 
in time to green which indicates Atto488 label associated with sCal(C8,C8)-
Atto488. The apoptotic bodies get outlined with sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 signal 
which indicates biding to the membrane (fig. 3.23 for membrane binding 
reference). The fact that it happens gradually in time when the objects are 
stationary excludes the first hypothesis proposed.  
 
The conclusion is that there is a relation between sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 binding 
and cell membrane changes due to the cell death. Three hypotheses were 
created to explain the observations: 
1) The membrane of a dying/dead cell and apoptotic bodies has a different 
characteristics, such as e.g. composition or polarity, that enhances the 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 binding; 
2) In the process of cell, the cell membrane changes its geometrical profile 
to more curved. The membrane of smaller objects such as fragmented 
apoptotic bodies or endosomes is highly curved due to the smaller size. 
Higher membrane curvature was linked to higher protein binding [50]; 
==PAGE:118==
118 
 
3) The sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 binding does not change. When the membrane 
is changing its shape due to the cell death to more spherical, a bigger 
section of the membrane per pixel is imaged giving higher, detectable, 
fluorescent signal. 
 
The reason for sudden detection of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 binding to the 
membrane as a result of the cell death process may be explained by one or a 
combination of factors. The studies have given neither the confirmation nor 
disconfirmation of the hypotheses posed above. 
 
3.2.5 Qualitative evaluation of the peptide uptake measurements 
 
In order to perform qualitative evaluation of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 cell uptake 
measurements, firstly, the fluorescence intensity of a single sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
molecule must have been determined. In the photobleaching experiment, the 
single molecule intensity for 100%, 80% and 60% of laser power was determined 
(tab. 3.1.). Because it was not known whether the intensity of a single molecule 
fluorescence scales linearly with laser power, in order to be able to analyse the 
cell uptake data, one needed to perform the cell uptake experiments again with 
the same parameters as photobleaching experiments. 
New uptake experiments were performed with the same objective (100x oil 
immersion), with the same laser power and exposure time (500 ms), using minSiF 
buffer only. The measurements were taken as Z-stack with a Δz step of 0.4 µm 
to get the full profile of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 spots inside the cell. Unfortunately, 
due to the lack of time, the full analysis of the data was not performed. However, 
one Z-plane was chosen and three nicely isolated spots were targeted for very 
initial characterization (fig. 3.40 and fig. 3.41). With the use of single-molecule 
analysis algorithm, their intensity was extracted. Dividing the intensities of the 
spots by a single sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 fluorescence intensity obtained in section 
3.1, one could assess the number of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 molecules inside the 
organelle transporting the peptide.

of regular oval shape lying close to nuclei of the cell were avoided in order not to 
mistake the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 with autofluorescing organelles – the 
autofluorescence is very strong with high laser powers.

3.2.6 Conclusions 
The autofluorescence revealed that in order to minimize the channel crosstalk, 
the best excitation wavelength to use would be 640 nm. For lower laser powers 
– up to 10% – the autofluorescence is not visible. Whereas for the 488 channel 
which was used for sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 observation even at 5% of laser power 
the fluorescence was detected. The recommendation would be to label peptides 
with red fluorescent protein targeting 640 nm excitation for future studies. 
 
The uptake measurements revealed that the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 is internalized 
and transported by the cell in aggregates. The organelles transporting 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 express different movement characteristics. Three main 
types of behaviour were noticed: slow tortuous movement in a specified direction, 
fast movement along a straight line and staying still in one position for a limited 
period of time between movements of different speeds and directions. The 
different movements characteristics may relate to different transport processes.  
Labelling cell membrane and early endosome lead to observations that 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 is taken up through the process of endocytosis and 
transported inside the cell by early endosomes. It was observed that slow 
tortuous movement at the periphery of the cell is related to sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
being taken into inward budding of the plasma membrane which is the process 
of creating a vesicle, first compartment of endocytosis [46]. Merging of multiple 
early endosomes with sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 peptide attached to inside of its 
membrane has also been recorded. 
 
Salmon calcitonin native peptide was modified with lipid chains in sCal(C8,C8) in 
order to enhance the membrane binding properties of the peptide. Even though 
the biophysical characterization showed strong membrane binding properties, no 
binding has been observed during the planned experiments. Only when the 
unexpected episode of cell death during experiments happened, sCal(C8,C8)-
Atto488 binding to cell membrane was observed. However, the binding happened 
only for the cell filopodia fragmented from the cell and fragmented extracellular 
==PAGE:124==
124 
 
membrane vesicles (apoptotic bodies) as well as the membrane of the dying cells 
that detached from the bottom and shrunk to spherical shape. Among probable 
reasons for the binding enhancement occurring under these circumstances are: 
different properties of the membrane of dying cells comparing to healthy cells and 
change in the membrane curvature – in another studies it has been observed that 
peptide membrane binding may increase with the membrane curvature [50].  
 
Lastly, an initial qualitative evaluation of the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 localized inside 
the cell was performed. An single particle analysis was implemented to estimate 
the total intensity of selected spots. Then, the intensity of a single molecule found 
in photobleaching experiments (taken with the same laser power and the same 
imaging buffer) was used to determine the number of molecules in each of the 
spots. It was discovered that each spot, which probably represents one early 
endosome, can hold dozens to hundreds sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 molecules. This 
significantly more than the average size of sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 aggregate found 
in photobleaching experiments – 12 ± 8 molecules per spot. This may be related 
either to the higher sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 concentration leading to bigger 
aggregates or to the fact that sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 is transported inside the cell 
by early endosomes that may merge together, hence, higher number of 
molecules registered in one fluorescent spot. 
 
4. Conclusions 
Two algorithms have been developed for the analysis of the acquired data. Step-
finding algorithm and single particle analysis algorithm. Both occurred to be very 
useful tools in the analysis process and delivered desired results successfully. 
Step-finding algorithm was designed to create an optimal model to the spot 
photobleaching time traces by fitting multiple parameters: step-size, number of 
steps and the steps position in time. Single particle algorithm estimated the spot 
total intensity, background, position and size based on statistical modelling, 
allowing for tracking the molecule position in XY and intensity in time. Two 
algorithms combined could have delivered better results in photobleaching 
==PAGE:125==
 
125 
experiments by e.g. providing good background estimation and more precise 
intensity estimation. Due to the time constrains, this has not been implemented, 
however, this can be done for future studies analysis. 
 
The experiments results provided a lot of interesting information on sCal(C8,C8)-
Atto488 peptide. sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 has been synthetized with (C8,C8) lipid 
chains modifications that were supposed to enhance the peptide binding to the 
cell membrane properties. The biophysical characterization performed prior to 
this project showed strong binding properties in e.g. calcein release experiments. 
That is why this peptide was selected for the cell experiments planned for this 
project. However, the experiments did not show any binding to the cell membrane 
of healthy cells. Moreover, strong membrane binding was discovered 
accidentally, were the cells went through rapid death events.  
 
This outcome is not fully consistent with the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 binding 
properties determined by biophysicist and stands in contradiction to the results of 
the performed biophysical tests. It provides important feedback for both 
biophysicist and chemists panels on the influence of the modifications (addition 
of C8 lipid chains and fluorescent molecule Atto488) on salmon calcitonin 
membrane binding properties. 
 
Even though any cell membrane binding (in the case of healthy cells) was not 
observed, the experiments did show sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 being taken up and 
transported inside the cell. 4D imaging capability provided by the spinning disc 
microscope setup allowed for observation of the sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 
aggregates movement inside the cell. Various movement characteristics were 
pinpointed which lead to a hypothesis that sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 is taken up 
through the process of endocytosis. The multi-channel imaging capability 
enabled cell uptake observation with the use of selective fluorescent labels 
targeting cell membrane and early endosomes. Simultaneous 4D observation of 
early endosomes and sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 lead to the confirmation of posed 
==PAGE:126==
126 
 
hypothesis which means that one sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 uptake and transport 
mechanism has been proven and identified as a result of this project.  
 
Additionally, a method for qualitative evaluation of the peptide transported inside 
the cell was proposed and tested. The photobleaching experiments provided the 
information on one sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 molecule intensity. This information may 
be used in uptake experiments for evaluation of the number of molecules inside 
e.g. an early endosome. The estimation of total intensity of a single sCal(C8,C8)-
Atto488 can be done by single particle analysis. This value divided by a single 
molecule intensity gives the approximated number of molecules. Moreover, the 
single particle analysis allows accurate movement tracking because the algorithm 
estimates the position of the spot as well. The algorithm created in the project 
fitted only 2D model, however, it can be developed to fitting a 3D model. 
Therefore, with this method detailed studies on the peptide trafficking can be 
conducted. 
 
Another aspect of the experiments was the imaging buffer selection. The debate 
on which imaging buffer is the most suitable for the experiments is ongoing in the 
CitBIO. Various biophysical test and research has been made in order to evaluate 
the buffer. However, this project experiments showed that testing the buffer by 
imaging under the microscope can provide important feedback on buffer stability 
under experimental conditions. From the buffers tested in the project – 
FluoroBrite, minSiF and SM buffers – the minSiF buffer occurred to be the least 
prone to cause problems and it was the most convenient to use for experiments 
of long duration such as imaging of cellular uptake or photobleaching 
experiments. 
 
Lastly, the influence of autofluorescence should be highlighted. To avoid the 
channel crosstalk, labelling the peptide with fluorescent dye suitable for 640 nm 
excitation would be preferable. This excitation wavelength was the least effective 
in exciting autofluorescence. However, using a more selective filter for at least 
some of the experiments should be considered. In the setup a quad band 
==PAGE:127==
 
127 
emission filter was used for all channels; it only blocks the light corresponding to 
the four excitation lasers – 405 nm, 488 nm, 561 nm and 640nm, which means 
that wide spectrum of autofluorescence can be potentially detected. Combining 
a more selective filter blocking shorter wavelengths with peptide label excited with 
640nm would prevent most of the autofluorescence from detection (fig. 3.17). 
 
5. Summary 
The project main goals were accomplished. Not only did the photobleaching 
experiments provide additional input into peptide characterization not available 
through standard biophysical characterization methods, but also the first cellular 
uptake of the peptide modified in the CitBIO was recorded and analysed. 
Moreover, the analysis lead to identification of one sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 uptake 
and transport mechanism.  
 
The method for qualitative and detailed analysis of the pharmaceutical peptide 
trafficking in cells was proposed, tested and evaluated. 4D fluorescence 
microscopy performed with multiple colour channels allows for simultaneous 
observation of cell organelles and labelled pharmaceutical peptide in 3D, in time 
with a single molecule resolution. Combined with single particle tracking analysis, 
it constitutes for a powerful tool in studying peptide transport inside the cell and 
determining mechanisms of uptake as well as the amount of peptide molecules 
inside cell organelles. 
 
The guidelines for conducting experiments were established and gathered into a 
protocol (appendix A). Since this project was the first in CitBIO to combine live 
cell experiments with the modified peptide, the procedures such as seeding cells 
on a coverslip, labelling the cells with fluorescent markers, preparation of imaging 
buffers and stock peptide solution as well as procedures related to acquiring 4D 
images with the spinning disc microscope must have been optimized for the 
requirements of these new experiments and setup.  
 
==PAGE:128==
128 
 
To sum up, the project provided the feedback on properties of a modified peptide 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 and its binding abilities as well as mechanisms related to 
sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 cellular uptake and intracellular transport. The use of 
statistical models allowed for in-depth detailed analysis of the experimental data. 
The work resulted in developing method of data analysis as well as creating an 
experimental protocol providing guidelines for further studies on pharmaceutical 
peptides.

F. Trouble shooting – cells dying under experimental 
conditions 
This section presents what has been tried to solve the problem of cells dying 
under the microscope for unknown reason after break in work. 
The cells before handling the experiments just fine even though: 
- 
We used multiple lasers with higher powers (10%, 15%, 20% and even 
higher) and still we were able to work for hours on the same sample 
(neighbouring chambers were also completely fine), 
- 
We kept cells in the basement incubator, which is not sterile and may not 
work extremely well for up to 4 days, 
- 
We used non-sterile imaging buffer mixed from old components, 
- 
I changed the medium in the chambers at the microscopy lab (no sterile 
conditions) and kept cells for a day or two more in the un-sterile incubator 
(happened multiple times for different samples). 
However, after the Easter break it was impossible to conduct live-cell imaging. 
The cells started to die immediately after placing under the microscope without 
any extra stimulation and were dead within 1.5 hours. If any imaging with laser 
light was used, the cell were rapidly dying and within minutes they were already 
dead. 
 
 
What did we try to identify and solve the problem? 
- 
Preparing new cells batch. We asked for an advice a researcher 
experienced with cell cultures – according to her nothing else should be 
the problem in that case than the cells itself; 
- 
Different users. To ensure that this is not related to one person’s individual 
mistake, different users were asked to perform the live-cell imaging. The 
results were the same – cells were dying shortly after being placed under 
microscope; 
- 
Different media. To exclude the cell medium influence, different media 
were used on the same ibidi plate, in separate chambers. The plate was 
==PAGE:138==
138 
 
put under the microscope.  Standard cell growth medium, FluoroBrite 
imaging buffer, and minSiF buffer were tested. Also, new components for 
the FluoroBrite buffer were ordered and a freshly mixed buffer was also 
used for comparison, to exclude the possibility of the buffer being to old 
and degraded; 
- 
Different cells concentrations in one ibidi plate chamber (5k, 10k, 20k, and 
more in case of other users); 
- 
Different cell lines – Caco-2 & HEK293; 
- 
Doing nothing related to experiments to the seeded cells except for taking 
them out of the incubator and putting under the microscope. This means 
that the cell growth medium was not changed to imaging buffer, no labels 
added, no sCal(C8,C8)-Atto488 added, the lid of the ibidi plate was not 
even opened. Cells imaged only with BF (Bright field, no laser light used); 
- 
Using different incubators – the cells are normally kept in the GMO lab and 
then they were transferred to the lab with SD-confocal microscope for the 
experiments. For any extra incubation, they were kept in the incubator 
(non-sterile) in the SD-confocal microscope lab. To exclude the possibility 
that the other incubator influences the cells, we tried incubating cells in 
imaging buffer for an hour in the GMO lab (where they normally stay, in 
the sterile buffer and sterile environment) before the measurements. 
Again, the cells looked fine until put into and kept in the microscopy 
chamber. It is worth noticing that before Easter holidays: 
o we just kept them in the microscopy chamber for the 1h incubation 
in imaging buffer before measurements, not even in the incubator,  
o we kept the cells in the incubator in the SD-confocal microscopy lab 
up to 4 days – the incubator that is not very trustworthy (rare 
maintenance) and is not sterile, 
and the cells were completely fine afterwards; 
- 
As mentioned – we tried fresh, sterile buffer in sterile environment. (before 
we would just change the medium in the microscopy lab); 
- 
New cell growth medium for cell culturing (I mixed a new fresh portion); 
==PAGE:139==
 
139 
- 
Higher power of the laser seemed to be very harmful to the cells, so we 
used 5% only or no laser at all. It didn’t seem to matter anyways, because 
neighbouring chambers were affected the same way as the chamber we 
were experimenting on, even though these chambers were not illuminated; 
- 
We check the temperature in the chamber at the position of the sample 
holder with an external probe and the temperature was okay (36.4°C 
measured comparing to 37°C set); 
- 
We check if cells were fine when temperature and CO2 supply were off, 
the cells not only were not dying in the faster rate, but also it was possible 
to perform standard experiments on them; 
- 
The CO2 level seemed to be fine as well. It CO2 level was raised 
significantly, the phenol red die in cell growth medium would change the 
colour to yellow due to it’s PH indicating properties – this was not 
observed. 
 
To sum up, no apparent problem was identified. The trouble shooting process 
lasted approximately 1.5 month. After two major changes, covering extra holes in 
the sample holder to prevent 5% CO2 air from mixing with atmospheric air and 
placing the temperature probe close to the sample, the problem got fixed. 
However, there is no evidence that this was the solution to the problem, since in 
the tests both temperature and CO2 levels seemed appropriate.

1.3 Conducting bleaching experiment 
Materials: 
Imaging buffer, peptide stock solution, ibidi µ-Slide chambered coverslip (glass

1) Heat up the microscope to 37°C before measurements. It takes at least 1h30min for the

2) When the microscope is at 37°C, add 200 μl of imaging buffer/imaging buffers to all

3) Turn on the microscope and choose 100x objective. Put a drop of oil at the objective and put 
the sample under the microscope. 
4) Move the objective under the chamber you are going to look into. Observe the sample and 
slowly move the objective up until it reaches the oil. 
Turn on the preview in the software and choose the BF mode. Continue with slowly moving 
the objective up in order to focus on the surface of the chamber. The microscope will notify 
you when it finds the focus with a beep sound. Be careful not to hit the sample with the 
objective – control the height of the objective, the focus should be at around 2700 μm.

5a) 
To take any measurement, first switch to BF, swipe the image by 1 field of view in a

6) Dilute the stock solution in minSiF imaging buffer. 
o Add 2 μl of stock solution to 1 ml of the imaging buffer.

1.4 Everyday cell handling

4) Incubate the cells in 37°C for 3 – 4 min. (use the incubator)

1.6 Experiments with cells – under the microscope 
 
Before the measurements, change the medium in the coverslip to imaging buffer and incubate the 
cells for at least 1h. Put 150 μl of the buffer in each chamber. The reason is to incubate cells in FBS-
free medium before the measurements. 
 
In general, in the cells experiments, the optimal laser powers were 5%-20% with the exposure time 
300 ms. 
 
1) 1.5h before the experiment, turn the heating to 37°C of the microscope, open the valve of 
CO2 turn on 5% CO2, lastly turn the laser box on. Remember to cover the holes in the sample 
holder to prevent the CO2 enriched air from mixing with atmospheric air – you may use small 
Petri dish for that purpose. 
2) Turn on the microscope, choose the oil immersion objective (60x or 100x) and find the focus 
as described in the section Conducting bleaching experiment, steps (3-4). 
3) Keep the extra holes in the sample holder covered. Cells should easily survive 2h+ under the 
microscope.  
4) In order to add the peptide, remove 15 μl of the imaging buffer from a chamber and add 15 
μl of the peptide stock solution to the chamber. Cells are ready for imaging right away.
