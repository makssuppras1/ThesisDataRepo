Abstract 
Drug discovery is an essential part of improving modern healthcare. One of the more recently 
developed methods to discover new drugs is known as fragment-based drug discovery (FBDD). 
One of the advantages of FBDD is that the size of the molecules is often smaller than 20 non-
hydrogen atoms, which reduces the number of possible combinations of molecules that can be 
generated, and thus screened. The small molecule size will increase the chance for interaction with 
a possible target, as it will be easier for the molecule to fit into narrowed binding sites. Another 
advantage is that most of the fragment molecule will be part of the interaction with a target, which 
will make the ligand efficiency of a typical hit very high.  
The purpose of this project was to synthesise a library containing spirocyclic natural product 
derivatives for the use in FBDD. Spirocyclic compounds have a high degree of 3D structure, which 
is considered to be an advantage since a binding site on a target protein have a well-defined 3D 
structure. The spirocyclic structure is also predicted to increase compound solubility, which is 
important for FBDD as fragments are typically screened at high concentrations.  
The spirocyclic products of interest were obtained in a one-step synthesis employing a Prins 
cyclization reaction between the natural homoallylic alcohol isopulegol and a cyclic ketone. The 
reaction was optimised using cyclohexanone as the test substrate.

1. Introduction 
Drugs are very important in our modern world, and pharmaceutical companies and academic 
laboratories keep searching for new drugs. Back in 1900, the average lifespan in the United States 
was 48 years for a woman and 46 years for a man. Nowadays the average lifespan is 79 years for 
a woman and 74 years for a man 1. Even so, there will always be a need for new and better drugs 
now and in the future. Bacteria and viruses mutate and develop resistance to the drugs that we used 
to use. Additionally, changes in our lifestyle have increased the incidence of several diseases, e.g. 
developing of diabetes, due to unhealthy diet combined with too little exercise. 
The first section will describe drug discovery focused on fragment-based drug discovery and 
natural products. The first section also consists of an introduction to the three phases of drug action, 
as they are important when developing new drugs. This is followed by an introduction to the Prins 
cyclization reaction, which I have used to develop natural product-derived fragments, followed by 
project outline, results, and discussion. 
 
2. Drug discovery  
Discovering new drugs is a long and expensive process, and the timeline will typically be 10 to 20 
years 2. One of the very first steps is to test the binding of several compounds to one or more 
biological targets. The specific target is not always known, so a library of compounds can also be 
tested on a cellular system or whole animal, to see if they have any biological activity. This process 
is known as phenotypic drug discovery. In the cases where a promising target has been identified, 
the screening assay will be based on the isolated target, e.g. receptor or enzyme, if it is possible to 
isolate. Compounds that show binding are called hits and can be progressed on to further screening 
assays and optimisation of the structure. It is important to be sceptical and validate the results from 
the primary assays to avoid spending time and money on false-positive results 3,4.    
Once a hit compound is optimized it becomes a lead with optimized binding ability and activity. 
The optimization of a hit to a lead is done by studying the structure activity relationship (SAR), to 
determine which parts of the molecule are important for binding. The physicochemical properties 
are optimized by considering the rule of five (see section 2.2.2). By controlling the number of HB 
acceptors and HB donors a good balance between water and fat solubility is obtained. Finally, the 
candidate is ready for clinical trials, at which point no more modifications to the structure are 
==PAGE:13==
Page 2 of 54 
 
carried out, as otherwise clinical trials would have to be started again. A scheme of the drug 
discovery and development process can be seen in Figure 2.1.

2.1. Forward and reverse pharmacology 
As mentioned above, there will be two main strategy for drug discovery. If the exact target is not 
known the strategy of choice will be forward pharmacology also known as classical 
pharmacology or phenotypic drug discovery (PDD). For PDD a compound library is tested in a 
biological system often such as isolated cells, tissue or a whole animal. The hits are compounds 
that show a desirable therapeutic effect. By knowing the active compound, the target is then 
identified (Figure 2.2). 5

The reverse pharmacology, also known as target-based drug discovery (TDD), is when the 
target of interest is known. Here the compound library will be tested directly on the specific target,

2.2. The three phases of drug action 
Drugs are typically processed by our bodies according to the three phases of drug action. The first 
phase, pharmaceutical phase, is only applicable to drugs taken orally, as its deals with how the 
drug comes from the e.g. tablet and into dissolution in the body. The second phase, 
pharmacokinetic phase, deals with the different actions that the body carries out on the drug

the target and the biological response. 
2.2.1. Pharmaceutical phase 
Approximately 80% of drugs are taking orally, and most of them as a tablet. The drug needs to 
dissolve before it can be absorbed in the gastrointestinal (GI) tract and then reach the systemic 
==PAGE:15==
Page 4 of 54 
 
circulation. The tablet first disintegrates into smaller pieces before the drug can be dissolved 
(Figure 2.3). If the drug is taken as a liquid, it will already be in a solution and ready for absorption 
when it reaches the GI tract. 
 
Figure 2.3: Dissolution of a tablet. When a drug is administered orally as a tablet, the tablet first have to disintegrate so 
the drug can get into dissolution. Reproduced from McCuistion et al.8. 
 
A tablet typically contains 5-10% of the drug, 10% of compounds that ensure the disintegration, 
and around 80% of fillers, glidants, and binders. The formulation of a tablet can give the tablet 
different properties, e.g. fast or slow disintegration for controlling the realise of the drug 8,3.  
2.2.2. Pharmacokinetic phase 
The pharmacokinetic phase of a drug is as important as the pharmacodynamic phase. Even a very 
potent drug will not be a useful medicine if it cannot reach its site of action. The first step of 
pharmacokinetic phase is absorption. The drug needs to be absorbed in the GI tract, through the 
gut wall and be brought into the systemic circulation. This means that the drug should not be too 
hydrophilic, as it cannot cross cellular membranes, but also not too hydrophobic, since it must be 
soluble in the blood. Dr Christopher A. Lipinski therefore studied the World Drugs Index database, 
and in 1997 he published an article in Advanced Drug Delivery Reviews with the rule of five, also 
known as Lipinski’s rule of five: 3,9 
• 
A molecular weight less than 500 Da 
• 
No more than 5 hydrogen bond donor groups 
• 
No more than 10 hydrogen bond acceptor groups 
• 
A calculated log P value less than 5  
 
Even if a drug meets all the rules, not all of the administered dosage is likely to reach systemic 
circulation. The percentage of the drug that does reach the systemic circulation is called the 
bioavailability, and can defined as: 
“The relative amount of an administered dose that reaches the general circulation and the rate 
at which this occurs”10 
==PAGE:16==
Page 5 of 54 
 
There are several things that influence the bioavailability e.g. the route of administration, where 
for an intravenous (IV) injection it will be 100%, and less for the oral route, the absorption from 
the GI tract can be affected by other drugs or food, and then there is the first-pass hepatic 
metabolism which can be changed due to liver diseases 8,10.  
Once a drug is absorbed into the blood stream, it gets distributed throughout the body very quickly, 
and then to the tissues and organs. Pores between the cells in the tissues, make it possible for most 
drugs to enter the tissue without being absorbed by the cells. However, these pores are not big 
enough for the plasma proteins to leave the blood, which makes a protein-bound drug less available 
for its site of action. If the target for a drug is in the brain, the drug must be able to cross the blood-
brain barrier. Unlike other capillaries, those in the brain have no pores, but the cells are sitting 
tight to each other, and are also coated with a layer of fatty cells. Therefore, the drug may not be 
to polar as it must dissolve through the cells and the fatty layer to reach the brain. In the other 
hand, polar drugs will have no side effect on the central nervous system (CNS) because they cannot 
cross the blood-brain barrier 8,3. 
The last two steps in the pharmacokinetic phase are metabolism and excretion, which are tightly 
related. The purpose of metabolism is to ensure a better excretion.  Most of the enzymes that 
metabolize drugs are located in the liver, and their type of metabolism are divided into two phases. 
Phase I metabolism is mostly carried out by the non-specific class of enzymes called cytochrome 
P450 which add polar groups to the drug, as polar molecules are more likely to be excreted when 
they pass through the kidney. Cytochrome P450 are haem proteins and introduce oxygen to the 
drug by splitting molecular oxygen and use NADPH as an oxidation agent (Figure 2.4).

It is important to know all the metabolites of a drug, and test them individual for biological activity, 
to know possible side effects of a drug. Some drugs lose their biological activity when they 
undergo metabolism, and some change their biological activity. A drug can be designed so it first 
reaches its active structure after metabolism. These drugs are called prodrugs. Prodrugs can have 
the advantage that they are absorbed more easily in the GI tract than the active form, which they 
get converted to when they have got into the systemic circulation 3. 
2.2.3. Pharmacodynamic phase 
The pharmacodynamic phase describes the way in which drug interact with its target to give a 
biological response. Drugs can give more than one response, the primary response which is 
desirable and the secondary response (side effects), which can be desirable or undesirable. An 
example is the drug Benadryl, whose primary effect is to treat symptoms of allergy, but displays 
side effects including drowsiness. This can be desirable if the drug is taken in the evening but 
undesirable during the day 8. 
To give a biological response, the drug must interact with its target which often will be a receptor 
or an enzyme. The place where a drug interacts with its target is called the binding site. Knowing 
the binding site will make it easier to design drugs with better efficiency. As the binding of a drug 
to its binding site is rarely covalent, hydrogen bonding, electrostatic interactions and van der Waals 
(VDW) interactions predominate. The binding will be an equilibrium between bound and unbound

2.3. Fragment based drug discovery 
Fragment based drug discovery (FBDD) is based on screening smaller molecules, which typically 
only form a small part of the final drug. The advantage of FBDD is that the number of possible 
molecules is less than for bigger drug-like molecules, simply because there are less atoms in the 
molecules which gives less possible combinations. 
“Fragments are usually defined as having less than 20 non-hydrogen (or ‘heavy’) atoms.” 4 
In addition to this definition, the rule of three (RO3) is widely used as guideline for synthesising 
or selecting good fragments for screening (section 3). As fragments are smaller they will also have 
fewer functional groups, and their interaction with the target will be weaker. These weak 
interactions make the binding more difficult to see, which typically requires the screening method 
to be very sensitive 3,4. On the other hand, the chance that a fragment fits into the binding site of 
the target is greater, and therefore a better chance for getting a hit.  
Good fragment-based hits only make productive interactions with their targets, which is another 
point why FBDD is so interesting. Even if the fragment only makes few interactions with its target, 
a big part of the molecule will be involved, making the ligand efficiency (LE) very high. LE is 
defined by the free energy of ligand binding, divided by the number of heavy atoms in the ligand 
(Figure 2.10). By this measurement, even fragments with weak binding interaction can have a 
great LE, as long as the size is equally small. LE can be used to compare the strength of binding 
of molecules of different sizes 11,12.

When the hit fragment is identified it often needs to be extensively modified to become a lead. 
There are three main strategies for carrying this out. The first is fragment growing, where the 
chemist adds more atoms and functional groups to the fragment, to increase its binding affinity 
and activity. Here, carrying out a SAR analysis is an important tool to choose which groups should 
be added to the fragment. The second strategy is fragment merging, where two or more fragments 
are joined to make a compound with binding properties from both fragments. The last strategy is 
fragment linking, used for fragments that bind in different sites on the target. The fragments are 
linked together to become one molecule. Identifying suitable linkers without interfering with 
fragment binding can be challenging. A good linker should not change the orientation of the 
original fragments, as otherwise these will not be able to bind to the target simultaneously. When 
the first fragment is identified, screening for a second binder may be carried out in presence of the 
first binder, to ensure that the fragments bind to different sites (Figure 2.11) 3,4.

2.3.1.  NMR screening 
Nuclear magnetic resonance (NMR) can be used both for identifying the hit and optimization of a 
hit to a lead. To identify a hit, differences in relaxation time can be used. The target will typically 
be a protein (receptor or enzyme) which has a short relaxation time after the given impulse, 
whereas small molecules including fragments typically have longer relaxation times. By delaying 
the detection time, only the fragment will be observed. If binding occurs, the small molecule 
becomes a part of the protein and therefore loses its signal. This method is very sensitive, but there 
==PAGE:21==
Page 10 of 54 
 
is also a chance of getting false positives 3. For hit to lead, a  technique known as SAR by NMR 
is carried out by identifying which atoms of the target change signal, when the fragment binds to 
it 4. 
2.3.2. Thermal shift assay 
In a thermal shift assay, a hit is identified by a change in the unfolding temperature of its target 
protein. A dye with a low fluorescence signal in polar conditions e.g. aqueous solution, but high 
fluorescence signal when bound to hydrophobic groups, is used for detecting this change. At 
physiological temperatures, a protein will be folded with the hydrophilic groups exposed to 
solution, resulting in no binding of the dye. As the temperature increases, the protein will start to 
unfold, making hydrophobic parts available for the dye to bind to, resulting in an increase in 
fluorescence. The temperature where half of the maximum fluorescence is observed is the melting 
temperature Tm, which will be greater if a ligand is bound to the protein, due to stabilization of the 
fold structure (Figure 2.12) 13.

Lucet et al.14.

3. Library synthesis 
A library is a collection of compounds that is used for drug discovery. A fragment library will be 
in the range of 500 to 2000 compounds 4. Libraries can vary a lot in terms of size and structure of 
the compounds, depending on the target or assay of interest, or the scaffold(s) that a chemist may 
want to screen for a given biological activity. Fragment-based libraries are not typically assembled 
for a specific target type, as they are predicted to bind to many kinds of targets. Several sets of 
rules or guidelines have been outlined for the design and construction of a fragment library. The 
most famous and widely applied is the RO3: 15 
• 
A molecular weight less than 300 Da 
• 
No more than 3 hydrogen bond donor groups 
• 
No more than 3 hydrogen bond acceptor groups 
• 
A calculated log P value less than 3 
• 
No more than 3 rotatable bonds 
• 
A polar surface area not greater than 60 Å2 
 
The criteria relating to rotatable bonds and polar surface area (PSA) are not universally applied. 
Additionally, when new fragments are synthesised it is important that they have high purity, good 
stability and most importantly high solubility because most screening methods used require  
relatively high fragment concentrations (>100 µM) 4. 
3.1. Natural products in drug discovery 
Nature has shown to be a great source of bioactive compounds 16. The main argument for using 
natural products (NPs) in drug discovery, is that evolution has already designed these compounds 
to have a specific function in the organism that they came from, and there is a good chance that 
they will also interact with similar targets in the human body. NPs have already shown great results 
clinically, as 28% of all approved drugs are NPs or derivatives thereof 17. There are many research 
groups working on designing and synthesising libraries based on NPs and derivates thereof. 
Another advantage of NPs is their high content of sp3-hybridized carbons, which gives a highly 
3D structure. Until now, many fragment libraries are based on more sp2 rich compounds which 
give them a more flat, 2D structure. Biological targets that a drug typically interacts with have a 
3-dimensional binding site, therefore it is predicted that more sp3 rich compounds like NPs may 
deliver better hits  34,17,18. 
==PAGE:23==
Page 12 of 54 
 
NPs can be used for FBDD in different ways. The simplest method is to use the NPs directly as 
fragments without further modification. Vu et al. which already had a library of NPs, filtered it in 
silico to get a library of NP fragments. Their criteria were inspired of RO3:  molecular weight ≤ 
250 Da, calculated log P < 4, HB donors ≤ 4, HB acceptors ≤ 5, rotatable bonds ≤ 6, and polar 
surface area < 4%. After the filtration they got 331 NP fragments 19. Some of the typical structures 
(Figure 3.1) and their physicochemical properties (Figure 3.2) are illustrated in the two following 
figures:

4. Prins cyclization  
A way to access new NP-derived compounds is to use the Prins cyclization reaction. By using this 
reaction new ring systems and stereocentres will be formed. This gives the opportunity to build 
complex molecules, starting from more simple starting materials including small NPs. 
The reaction is a ring formation between an allylic, homoallylic, or bishomoallylic alcohol 21 and 
an aldehyde or ketone 22 activated by an acid. Both a Lewis and a Brønsted acid have been used 
20,21. The number of carbons between the alcohol and the carbon-carbon double bond, will decide 
the size of the resulting ring. 
First the carbonyl is activated towards nucleophilic attack by the acid. The alcohol then acts as a 
nucleophile in a condensation reaction, to give an oxonium ion. The ring is formed by a 
nucleophilic attack from the carbon-carbon double bond to form a carbocation. The final 
elimination step reforms the double bond, however the elimination is not always regioselective 
and therefore can give rise to more than one product 23a, 23b, and 23c (Scheme 4.1). 
 
Scheme 4.1: Mechanism for the Prins cyclization. The general mechanism for the Prins cyclization reaction, shows 
how the new ring system is formed from an allylic alcohol and an aldehyde or ketone in the presence of an acid.

4.2. Spirocyclic products 
The product in Scheme 4.5 is a dispirocyclic compound, where the three-dimensional structure 
will be set by the ring systems. Another way to get spirocyclic products is to use a cyclic ketone 
as the carbonyl in a Prins cyclization (Scheme 4.6).  
 
Scheme 4.6: Spirocyclic product. By using a cyclic ketone in the Prins cyclization reaction, the product will be a 
spirocyclic compound. Adapted from Jacolot et all. 20. 
 
One of the advantages of spirocyclic compounds is their non-planar structure, as the two rings will 
be twisted towards each other because of the shared sp3-hybridized carbon. The 3D structure makes 
these kinds of products interesting for FBDD, as discussed in section 3.1. The spirocycle will also 
increase the solubility of the compound which is another important property in FBDD 26. 
ETX0914, 38 (Figure 4.1) is an example of a spirocyclic drug which has entered clinical trials for 
the treatment of gonorrhoea. Another example is rolapitant, 39 which was approved in 2015 for 
the treatment of delayed-phase chemotherapy-induced nausea and vomiting 26.

5. Project outline 
FBDD can be a powerful tool to discover new hits. Fragments often have a high LE as most of the 
molecule will be participating in binding to a target protein. For the design of new fragments, we 
would like to make compounds with a highly 3D structure, to meet the 3D structure of a target 
protein. One way to increase 3D character of a fragment library is to make spirocyclic compounds 
via the Prins cyclization reaction. 
The purpose of this thesis was to synthesize new spirocyclic compounds via the Prins cyclization 
reaction starting from natural, commercially available allylic and homoallylic alcohols (Figure

6.9. Synthesis of final products 
Using the optimised conditions, it was possible to synthesise some spirocyclic products following 
the Prins cyclization reaction with isopulegol 24 and cyclic ketones. Initially tetrahydro-4H-pyran-
4-one 65 was used as the cyclic ketone (Table 6.7, entry 1). All three regioisomer products fulfil 
the RO3 with a molecular weight of 236.18 Da, no HB donors, two HB acceptors, a calculated log 
P value of 1.85, 1.94 and 1.72 respectively, and no rotatable bonds. The reaction gave only the two 
products 66b and 66c which were separable. 
When using tetrahydro-4H-thiopyran-4-one 67 as the ketone (entry 2), all three products 68a-c 
were observed in addition to an uncharacterizable fourth product, none of which were separable. 
This product also fulfils the RO3, they differ from 66a-c by having a molecular weight of 252.15 
Da and a calculated log P value of 2.57, 2.66, and 2.44 respectively. However, as this was an 
inseparable mixture of four compounds, this will not be useful in the context of FBDD and full 
characterisation was not carried out. 
The last product was obtained by using cyclopentanone 69 as the ketone (entry 3) which gave a 
mixture of 70a and 70b, however separation by column chromatography was not possible. These 
products are of less interest as they only have one heteroatom, which makes them less functional 
and less polar. The log P is in the high end, however aside from this they do fulfil the RO3: 
molecular weight of 220.18 Da, no HB donors, one HB acceptors, a calculated log P value of 3.19, 
3.28 and 3.05 respectively, and no rotatable bonds.

7. Conclusion  
The Prins cyclisation from natural, commercially available allylic and homoallylic alcohols with 
aldehydes and ketones was attempted. All reactions where myrtenol 40 or nopol 41 were used as 
starting materials did not give the desired product. Instead, the alcohols seemed to decompose 
under the acidic conditions to give several decomposition products that could not be characterised 
and did not undergo the Prins reaction. 
Subsequently isopulegol 24 was used as the natural homoallylic alcohol as several Prins 
cyclization reactions starting from this compound had been reported. However, the reaction turns 
out to give three regioisomeric products, which had not been well described for similar 
transformations in the literature. By varying the reaction temperature, solvent and acid, the 
selective formation of the kinetic or thermodynamic product was attempted. This turned out to be 
rather challenging. Another challenge was that the three products were typically not separable by 
flash column chromatography despite several attempts. Only in the reaction of isopulegol with 
tetrahydro-4H-pyran-4-one 65 was it was possible to separate the thermodynamic product from a 
second regioisomer (the kinetic product was not formed).  
The best conditions for the Prins reaction using isopulegol and a cyclic ketone were found to be 
triflic acid (2 equivalents) in CH2Cl2 at 0 °C. 
Attempts to convert the regioisomeric products to only one isomer by hydrogenation, were not 
successful.  
Two final products were separated and fully characterized, whereas the other products were a 
mixture of two or three regioisomers.  
8. Future Work 
The conditions for the Prins cyclization reaction with isopulegol 24 and cyclic ketones could be 
further optimized. However, many conditions have already been tested. The next step for this 
project will be to synthesise more spirocyclic derivatives from isopulegol from the different cyclic 
ketones listed in Figure 5.2. Heterocyclic ketones are of particular interest as these will increase 
the functionality and solubility of the products. The obtained products will be part of a NP-derived 
library and will be employed in FBDD screens in the future.

9. Experimental 
9.1.  General methods 
Commercially available reagents were used without further purification and all solvents were of 
HPLC quality. All reactions were run under a N2 atmosphere and were monitored by thin layer 
chromatography (TLC). 
Analytical TLC was conducted on Merck aluminium sheets covered with silica (C60). The plates 
were either visualized under UV-light or stained by dipping in a developing agent followed by 
heating. KMnO4 [3 g in water (300 mL) along with K2CO3 (20 g) and 5% aqueous NaOH (5 mL)] 
and cerium molybdate [Ce(NH4)2(NO3)6 (2.5 g), (NH4)6Mo7O244H2O (12.0 g), and H2SO4 (25 
mL) in water (225 mL)] were used as developing agents. Flash column chromatography was 
performed using Merck Geduran® Si 60 (40-63 µm) silicagel. 
All new isolated compounds were characterized by 1H-NMR, 13C-NMR, IR, HRMS (ESI), and 
optical rotation (side products were not characterized). For the recording of 1H-NMR and 13C-
NMR a Bruker Ascend with a Prodigy cryoprobe (operating at 400 MHz for proton and 100 MHz 
for carbon) was used. A few NMR spectra were acquired on a Bruker Avance 800 MHz system 
equipped with a 5mm TCI cryoprobe. The chemical shifts (δ) are reported in parts per million 
(ppm) and the coupling constants (J) in Hz. For spectra recorded in CDCl3, signal positions were 
measured relative to the signal for CHCl3 (δ 7.26 ppm for 1H-NMR and δ 77.16 ppm for 13C-
NMR). IR analysis was performed on a Bruker Alpha FT-IR spectrometer. Analytical RP-UHPLC-
MS (ESI) analysis was performed on a S2 Waters AQUITY RP-UPLC system equipped with a 
diode array detector using a Thermo Accucore C18 column (d 2.6 µm, 2.1 x 50 mm; column temp: 
50 °C; flow: 1.0 mL/min). Eluents A (0.1% HCO2H in H2O) and B (0.1% HCO2H in MeCN) were 
used in a linear gradient (5% B to 100% B) in 2.4 min and then held for 0.1 min at 100% B (total 
run time: 2.6 min). The LC system was coupled to a SQD mass spectrometer. Analytical LC-
HRMS (ESI) analysis was performed on an Agilent 1100 RP-LC system equipped with a diode 
array detector using a Phenomenex Luna C18 column (d 3 µm, 2.1 x 50 mm; column temp: 40 °C; 
flow: 0.4 mL/min). Eluents A (0.1% HCO2H in H2O) and B (0.1% HCO2H in MeCN) were used 
in a linear gradient (20% B to 100% B) in a total run time of 15 min. The LC system was coupled 
to a Micromass LCT orthogonal time-of-flight mass spectrometer equipped with a Lock Mass 
probe operating in positive electrospray mode.

9.2. Procedures 
General procedure: To a stirred solution of (-)-isopulegol 24 (100 mg, 0.65 mmol), cyclic ketone 
(0.65 mmol) and dry CH2Cl2 (2 mL), a solution of triflic acid (115 µL, 1.30 mmol in 2 mL dry 
CH2Cl2) was added dropwise at 0 °C. After completion of the reaction as determined by TLC, 6 
mL of saturated aq. NaHCO3 solution was added to the reaction mixture and extracted with ethyl 
acetate (3 x 6 mL). The combined organic layer was washed with brine (1 x 12 mL) and dried over 
anhydrous magnesium sulphate and then concentrated by rotary evaporator. The product was 
purified by flash column chromatography using 1% ethyl acetate in hexane as eluent.
