Abstract
Increasing urbanisation in the Arctic region accompanied by growing industries call for city
expansions and new infrastructure facilities concerning all means of transportation. Until
recently, engineers have relied on the favourable foundation conditions posed by stable
permafrost, as frozen ground has en increased compressive strength due to the support
from the ground ice. However, the rapidly changing arctic environment necessitates asôÙ
sessments of future ground conditions when designing projects in the Arctic.
Soil is a porous medium consisting of grains and fluids in different phases occupying the
pore space. The complexity of the soil matrix increases as the fluid changes phase, e.g.
when pore water freezes or thaws as it does every summer in the seasonally thawed
active layer above the permafrost table. The thickness of the active layer layer depends
on the thermal properties and of their changes with temperature.
As permafrost is expected to recede drastically on a global scale over the coming cenôÙ
turies due to climate changes, the active layer depth will increase, endangering existôÙ
ing constructions and challenging future projects. Knowledge of the future geotechnical
ground properties through geothermal modelling is thus of great importance within cold
climate engineering. In this project, a method of determining the thermal properties of
permafrost soil vital for such a model was investigated, as well as a range of other exôÙ
periments to accurately describe the soil matrix were conducted. To determine relevant
physical parameters the samples were subject to experiments in frozen intact state, durôÙ
ing temperature controlled stepôÙwise thawing where each step was held sufficiently long
for the cores to obtain stable temperature, as well as in crushed dry state.
During temperature controlled testing the moisture content was measured, and heat imôÙ
pulses were emitted in the permafrost cores, which resulting temperature signals were
measured and used to determine the thermal properties. These were estimated by iterôÙ
ations where a theoretical temperature evolution curve, based on the assumptions of a
perfect cylinder of infinite length in both directions, was matched to the measured temôÙ
perature curves at different initial temperatures.
A range of classification experiments were also conducted to provide input information on
salinity, ice content, grain size distribution and unfrozen water curves needed as input for
the iteration.
As a validation of the method a reference experiment on glass beads with known thermal
properties was conducted. However, the thermal properties of the glass beads provided
by the manufacturer were not obtained from the measurements during temperature conôÙ
trolled testing. A series of secondary experiments were made to identify the source of
error and optimise the experimental setup for future research within this topic. The reôÙ
sults of this project suggest that the assumption of an infinitely long cylinder is invalid with
the current setup and that it was impossible to match the theoretical temperature curve
with the measured temperature curve in frozen state.

1
Introduction
Permafrost is defined as ground ôÙ including both soil and rock ôÙ that stays at a temperature
below zero degrees for two or more consecutive years. This definition is solely based on
temperature and does not consider the phase of pore water in the ground. Fine grained
soils will have a remarkable unfrozen water content even at several degrees below the
freezing point due to capillary and electrical forces acting on the water and the grains.
Furthermore, if solutes are found in the pore fluid, the freezing point will be depressed
depending on the concentration (Andersland and Ladanyi 2004). This allows the ground
to contain significant amounts of liquid water well within the boundaries of the definition
of permafrost, which influences its static as well as thermal stability.
The stability and strength of bedrock is mostly independent on its temperature, and perôÙ
mafrost in soils are thus more interesting to engineering related projects. Consequently,
this project is only related to permafrost in soils. Frozen soil has a higher compressive
strength as ice acts as a bonding agent, which has been an advantage used in frozen
ground engineering for decades. However, constructions on permafrost also encompass
a danger, as the structures will be more vulnerable to a deepening of the seasonally
thawed top soil layer called the active layer. This means that structures within the perôÙ
mafrost areas will experience increased settlements as the thaw depth increases and
exceeds the foundation depth due to the climate changes the world is currently experiôÙ
encing. As the soil thaws, so does the ice trapped in it and the soil loses its bonding agent
and thus the extra strength it provided, thereby weakening the foundation of the structure.
The permafrost region is ordered in four groups. The continuous permafrost zone deôÙ
notes the area where permafrost is found in a continuous layer everywhere in the ground.
When patches with unfrozen soil occur, it is considered discontinuous permafrost. When
permafrost occurs at sporadic locations in an otherwise unfrozen region, it is considered
sporadic. In some areas a few relatively small isolated patches of permafrost are found in
what is called the isolated permafrost zone. Together the permafrost zone currently covers
a quarter of the land area of the northern hemisphere (Chadburn et al. 2017) (Biskaborn
et al. 2019), making this a relevant research topic as the temperature in the Arctic has
increased by 0.6ô¯C per century over the past 30 years (Schuur et al. 2015).
Not only does thawing permafrost induce higher maintenance costs for buildings and conôÙ
structions, in the near future it also poses a safety hazard and challenges the distribution
of supplies to the scarcely populated Arctic region with only four towns with more than
100,000 inhabitants (Chadburn et al. 2017).
Most present predictions of the future thawing of permafrost are made to understand the
processes behind global warming and are thus on a large scale not considering local
conditions (Hjort et al. 2019), which is necessary to further increase the resolution and
precision of the models and make them suitable for engineering purposes. In order to obôÙ
tain a valid local model, it is imperative to describe the soilãs energy budget, accounting for
physical properties of the soil at the location as well as the local climate (L. H. Rasmussen
et al. 2018).
The focus of this project is on determining the thermal properties of permafrost cores exôÙ
tracted from Ilulissat in Greenland. To obtain these properties in frozen and unfrozen state
an experimental, temperature controlled approach is applied. By letting the temperature

Literature Review
In this chapter the physical properties and mechanisms concerning the thermal regime of
permafrost is investigated through a literature study. The basic definitions and conditions

2.1
Ground temperature
It follows directly from the zeroth law of thermodynamics, that any system not in thermoôÙ
dynamic equilibrium with its surroundings will exchange energy with the environment to
obtain it. The energy balance of any thermodynamic body is thus given by the summation
of energy gains and losses. For soil this is a quite complex system, but for simplicity, the
temperatures of a homogeneous soil can be described by a sinusoidal curve depending
only on the ambient temperature, both on daily and annual time scale (Andersland and
Ladanyi 2004). Due to the thermal conductivity and heat capacity of the soil, the temperôÙ
ature at depth will not be equal to the temperature at the surface but rather increasingly
shifted and damped with depth. This means that if the surface temperature is highest
on a specific day during summer, the temperature at depth will reach its maximum later,
depending on the depth and the thermal diffusivity of the soil (Andersland and Ladanyi
2004). These very idealised mechanisms are visualised in Figure 2.1, where the damping
and phase shift of the temperature curves due to thermal properties of the soil can be obôÙ
served. Note that the temperature amplitude at depth, z is given by Az, and it decreases
by increasing depth as seen in Figure 2.2 (Andersland and Ladanyi 2004).

As previously mentioned this is a simplification, and in reality the temperature of the soil
is not only depending on the ambient air temperature but on the local climate as well
as the surface vegetation and terrain. Less cloud coverage induces an increased heat
exchange from radiation during winter, and strong winds will lead to greater heat loss due
to convection, both of which will reduce the temperature (Foster and Fye 1973). The heat
balance is obviously also influenced by the vegetation, as trees offer lee and shade for the

2.2
Frozen soil
Soil that stays at a temperature blow 0ô¯C for at least two consecutive winters and the
intervening summers is commonly referred to as permafrost soil or perennially frozen
ground. However, permafrost is a broader term including soil with no water content as well
as bedrock (Andersland and Ladanyi 2004) (Biskaborn et al. 2019). Although thawing of
permafrost in rocks also may cause instabilities through frost erosion and landslides that
could also be relevant for civil engineering projects, this report will focus on the four and
three phased systems described in Figure 2.4 containing water in solid and/or liquid form,
gas and solid soil particles.
Due to seasonal temperature fluctuations the top soil in permafrost regions will to some
extend thaw during the Arctic summer and refreeze during autumn. The layer of soil
from the surface to the thaw depth is called the active layer, and it may vary from a few
centimetres in the colder permafrost zones to about a metre or more in the warmer zones
(Andersland and Ladanyi 2004). Because of the limited heat conductivity and capacity of
the soil, the temperature evolution at depth follows that of the surface with some delay
and damping as shown in Figure 2.3
As illustrated in Figure 2.3 the top part of the permafrost soil will experience annually
fluctuating temperatures until the depth of zero annual amplitude (ZAA). From this point
the ground temperature will increase with depth depending on the geothermal gradient.
Frozen soil is a four phase system containing solid soil particles, which includes organic
as well as mineral matter, and its pore space is occupied by ice, unfrozen water or a
gas, such as air. Despite the fact that the temperature of the soil is below the freezing
temperature some amount of the bound water will remain liquid, and the mass of water
in the frozen state thus accounts for both frozen and liquid water. In a closed system,

2.3
Inverse problem
Determining the thermal properties of soil is a complex problem as the soil is composed
of numerous different minerals and materials with different physical properties. One of
the approaches for doing so is by inversion. Finding the thermal properties as an inverse
problem means that the effects are measured and used to determine the cause. Using a
specific heat sensor emitting a heat pulse as further explained in Section 4.1, the standard
assumption is that the specific heat capacity is inversely proportional to the height of the
temperature rise, and the thermal diffusivity is related to the time it takes for the heat pulse
to pass the temperature sensor. The thermal conductivity may then be computed as the
product of the diffusivity and the specific heat capacity (East30Sensors 2021). However,
this assumption does not hold for soil in frozen conditions, as it does not consider the
phase shift where the emitted heat is countered by the latent heat of fusion, which flattens
out the temperature signals.

The dielectric permittivity, commonly denoted öç, is a physical property grounded in the field
of electromagnetism which describes the ability of a material to hold an electric charge,
and it is a quantity consisting of a real and an imaginary part. The real dielectric permitôÙ

magnetic field orthogonal to that of the electrical. The real dielectric permittivity is the most

electrical wave moves through the medium, among others the water content will influence
the frequency, impedance, amplitude and propagation velocity of the wave. Changes of
the real dielectric permittivity are thus directly related to the change in moisture content of
the medium in question. To ease comparison of the real dielectric permittivity of different
substances, the ratio between the dielectric permittivity of the substance to that of air,
called the dielectric constant, Ka, is used. Water, for instance, has a dielectric constant
of 80, while the typical value for dry soil is typically around 2 ôÙ 4.

6.4
Discussion on secondary experiments
The secondary measurements of the thermal properties did not match those provided by
the manufacturer, but the discrepancy on thermal conductivity might again be due to the
geometry of the glass beads as proposed in Section 5.6. It is difficult to assess whether the
obtained thermal properties are more valid than those provided by the manufacturer, but
the inversion results indicate that the value obtained from the secondary heat capacity
measurement is more accurate, which is further implied by the table value of the heat
capacity of glass shown in Table 5.6 from H. E. Hansen, KjerulfôÙJensen, and Stampe
(1997).
Running the inversions on the data sets on saturated glass beads in the cylindrical specôÙ
imen holder on the POM base and in the bucket observed in Table 6.6 showed a quite
good agreement between numerical and analytical solutions. Very good fits of the inverôÙ
sion curves were obtained in unfrozen state, but became more problematic when ice was
introduced to the system. Observing the inversion curve of the saturated glass beads in
the cylindrical specimen holder with POM base in Figure A.44 in Appendix A.6 shows a
very bad fit, and heat capacity obtained from the numerical inversion is far from the target
value.
It is noted that all obtained values for the thermal conductivity in dry state correspond quite
well to that obtained from the secondary thermal conductivity measurement with the CôÙ
Therm System. When water is introduced in the system, both data from the POM base in
unfrozen state and from the bucket in frozen as well as unfrozen state lead to values rather
close to that of the manufacturer. This indicates that the low thermal conductivity obtained
in dry measurements originates from the geometry of the material. The introduction of
water allows more accurate estimation of the true thermal conductivity provided by the
manufacturer, because it does not act as an insulator between the grains, as it conducts
heat far better than air.
With regards to the heat capacity, no inversion results lead to the value from the manufacôÙ
turer, but they are all rather close to the value obtained from the secondary heat capacity
measurement with the Micro Calorimeter, which indicates that this is a reasonable value.
Even though the results obtained from the inversions from the dry and saturated unfrozen
bucket data sets show slightly lower values on heat capacity than that obtained from the
secondary measurement, they are within a reasonable range. In frozen saturated state,
the results from the bucket setup is very close to the thermal conductivity from the manôÙ
ufacturer and the heat capacity obtained from the secondary measurements. Observing
the inversion fit in Figure 6.11 shows that the fit is not perfect, but the trend and shape of
the curves is rather good. It is no perfect solution, which the obtained parameters a and
b show. These values are far from those obtained from the Hydra Probe measurements.
However, the fact that the obtained thermal properties are within the expected range and
that the inversion curves are generally good, indicates that it indeed is possible to obtain a
valid result on thermal properties with the right experimental setup. It is also indicated that
the bucket setup with the SH sensor placed in the centre of the material is representing
the assumptions behind the inversion algorithm far better than the cylindrical specimen
holder, even though the aluminium base is substituted with a less conductive POM base.

8
Summary & conclusion
8.1
Summary
Seasonal frost as well as permafrost are characteristics for cold regions and an important
condition to consider when doing civil engineering in the Arctic. Frozen soil has a high
compressive strength, which is an advantage for the foundation of structures, but as arctic
temperatures increase by up to three times the global mean, substantial changes to the
soil conditions will occur in the near future.
The active layer is very sensitive to temperature changes, and to estimate the heat flow
in the soil and thus the evolution of seasonal thaw depth and ground bearing capacity, the
thermal properties of the soil in frozen and unfrozen state must be determined. This is of
high interest for arctic communities, where an increased urbanisation calls for town and
infrastructure expansions in permafrost zones.
In this project permafrost cores have been extracted from a marine sedimentary deposit
in Ilulissat, Greenland. Due to the transportation time and the time constraints on this
project, these samples have not been investigated in this project, but rather similar cores
from previous yearsã field work in the same area. However, the participation in the field
work has given insight in the procedure of permafrost core drilling and inôÙfield handling
of the samples. The permafrost cores investigated in this project have been examined
through a series of laboratory experiments, and the methods to determine the thermal
properties have been presented and evaluated by comparing to a reference experiment
on glass beads where the thermal properties were already known.
The experimental part of this project was conducted at the laboratory facilities at DTU
in Lyngby and included a temperature controlled testing of the material while measuring
temperature signals using specific heat sensors as well as TDR/FDR sensors to measure
the temperature evolution and the moisture content of the samples.
Given reasonable starting guesses, the thermal properties of the grains were assessed
through inversions based on a theoretical link between heat capacity and thermal conducôÙ
tivity given the assumption of a oneôÙdimensional heat flow through the side of an infinitely
long cylinder. The testing procedure was evaluated by comparing the obtained results of
the parameters describing the unfrozen water content to those obtained from the hydra
probe measurements, and by comparing the thermal properties of the glass beads in the
control experiment to the values provided by the manufacturer.
The thermal properties of the grains in the permafrost cores obtained through these exôÙ
periments were not within a reasonable range, and the control experiment on glass beads
also failed to reproduce the values from the manufacturer. To indicate sources of error, a
sensitivity study was conducted, where it was found that it was impossible to match the
measured temperature evolution with the theoretical inversion curves, even when changôÙ
ing the input parameters on porosity and thermal properties of water.
To validate the table values on the thermal properties provided by the manufacturer, secôÙ
ondary measurements on thermal conductivity and heat capacity of the glass beads were
conducted. The thermal conductivity found from this testing was underestimated, most
likely due to the spherical structure of the grains, and the table value was regarded more
precise. For the heat capacity however, the measured value was lower than the table
Determining thermal properties of permafrost in marine sedimentary deposits in Ilulissat
79
==PAGE:97==
value, and since it agrees better with table values from literature and since the measuring
method is trusted, the measured value is trusted.
The reason for the diverging data acquired from the experiment was further investigated
through a series of secondary experiments on glass beads, to which end a new cylinder
base for the specimen holder made of POM was fabricated. The POM base replaced the
highly conductive existing cylinder base of aluminium to eliminate it as a potential source
of error. Two different setups where then used during a second temperature controlled
experiment on glass beadsëƒ one using the same cylindrical specimen holder as in the initial
experiment, but replacing the aluminium base with the less conductive POM base, and
one where the sensor was placed in the middle of the glass beads in a bucket. The results
obtained from this experiment were then compared to the secondary measurements of
the thermal properties of the glass beads in order to determine whether the reason for the
discrepancy lay with the theory or the setup.
8.2
Conclusion
The results on unfrozen water content obtained in the saturated glass beads experiment
suggest that the pore water contained in the glass beads behaves similar to what was
expected for soils of similar grain sizes. From this it is observed that glass beads can be
a reasonable substitute for soil, regarding pore water behaviour, e.g. when conducting
such reference experiments as in this project. However, it was also found that the FDR
hydra probes were easier and more reliable and practical to use than the TDR sensors.
The moisture curves obtained from the measurements of the dielectric permittivity agreed
well with theory, both for the experiments on saturated glass beads and the permafrost
cores.
It was further found that the investigated method of determining the thermal properties
of a material could not be verified through the reference experiment on dry glass beads,
because the spherical geometry of the glass beads resulted in an underestimation of
their thermal conductivity. However, with the introduction of liquid water in the system the
obtained thermal conductivity of the glass beads corresponded to the value provided by
the manufacturer regardless of the specimen holder base being aluminium or POM.
As the obtained values on heat capacity in unfrozen state from the second temperature
controlled experiment matched the value obtained through the Micro Calorimeter experôÙ
iment on DTU, the heat capacity of the glass beads can be determined using this setup
with reasonable accuracy, if the aluminium specimen holder base is replaced by the POM
base. Using the aluminium base leads to a too high heat capacity. However, in frozen
state reasonable thermal properties are only obtained in the setup where the SH sensor
is placed in the centre of the material as in the setup using the bucket.
This finding implies that the underlying assumption of an infinitely long cylinder is invalid
with the initial setup, even when replacing the base with a less conductive POM base. This
comprises a major problem when investigating thermal properties of permafrost cores, as
these must be investigated in intact state and the SH sensor used in this project thus
cannot be inserted in the centre of the core.
These results were further confirmed by the measurements conducted on the permafrost
cores during temperature controlled measurements, as the resulting thermal properties
exceeded reasonable ranges of both heat capacity and thermal conductivity and did not
show similar results in frozen and unfrozen state.

Even though it has been possible to obtain reference values of glass beads by inverôÙ

8.3
Recommendations for further work
For further work within this research area it is recommended to investigate whether the
cylindrical setup could be improved if the specific heat sensor with 3 cm long pins used
in this study were replaced with an SH sensor equipped with longer pins allowing temôÙ
perature measurements at a larger distance from the bottom of the sample. In this way
the setup might better represent the assumption of an infinitely long cylinder, which would
make it possible to obtain the correct values of thermal properties by inversions. It is
recommended to conduct these experiments using a cylinder base of POM rather than
aluminium and to determine the a and b parameters describing the moisture content by
measuring the dielectric permittivity and use the results obtained from these measureôÙ
ments as initial guesses for the inversions.
Alternatively, an inversion algorithm based on theory with assumptions more aligned with
the actual experimental setup should be determined.
