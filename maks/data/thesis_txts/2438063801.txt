Abstract
In commercial fisheries, regulations concerning discard and by-catch rates in EU waters
have been introduced. These seeks to reduce the effective amount of fish wrongfully
being caught and discarded. The company ATLAS MARIDAN is currently developing
a small cost-effective AUV to aid fishermen in parametrising a detected school of fish
before it is being caught. For the fishermen this enables facts based on-site decisions
seeking to drastically reduce the amount of by-catch and maximise the yield of limited
fishing quotas.
In this thesis, the development of a low-cost state-of-the-art camera
system utilising artificial intelligence for fish detection, localisation and species classifi-
cation on board the AUV in (near) real-time is described.
The underlying algorithm, called OFDNet, is based on current state-of-the-art deep learn-
ing architectures for object detection and have been successfully modified and trained
for the purpose of performing detection and localisation of fish in underwater images
and furthermore classify detected fish into three class categories; herring, mackerel and
“other species”. For training such algorithms, an image database of fish has been ac-
quired by visiting Danish aquariums with a developed underwater camera setup.
OFDNet has been successfully integrated on a Raspberry Pi with an Intel Movidius Neu-
ral Compute Stick attached. This achieved a performance in terms of processing speed of
7.81 FPS which is shown to be an increase of 1001 % compared to a vanilla Raspberry Pi.
For testing OFDNet outside the scope of good conditioned aquarium environments, im-
age material from the Weser river, Bremen, and Baltic Sea off the coast of Rügen have
been obtained. In this data, the visibility is about 30-50 cm and the overall image quality
is poor compared to aquarium data. In a test dataset, OFDNet successfully detects and
localises 44.8 % of the fish and classifies 23.1 % of these into the correct class category.
A retrained model containing a very sparse representation of the previously unknown
environments showed to successfully detect and localise 66.7 % of the fish and classify
89.7 % of these into the correct class category.
Furthermore, this thesis proposes simple means to extend the capabilities of the system
to perform fish school tracking by utilising the location output from OFDNet and a
Kalman filter.
The final software package has been developed as a highly scalable
RESTful API. An Android tablet application have been developed to store data, and
facilitate data visualisation and statistics to the end user after a completed mission by
the AUV.

Resume
I kommercielt fiskeri indenfor EU er et regulativ omhandlende bortskaffelse og bifangst
af fisk blevet indført. Dette har til hensigt at reducere den mængde fisk der fejlagtigt
bliver fanget og bortskaffet. Virksomheden ATLAS MARIDAN arbejder i øjeblikket på
udviklingen af en lille omkostningslav AUV til at bistå fiskere med at parametrisere en
opdaget fiskestime før denne bliver fanget. Dette muliggør en faktabaseret beslutning på
stedet med henblik på drastisk at reducere mængden af bifangst og maksimere udbyttet
af begrænsede fiskekvoter. I dette projekt beskrives udviklingen af et omkostningslavt
state-of-the-art kamerasystem ved hjælp af kunstig intelligens til detektering og lokalis-
ering af fisk i billeder samt klassificering af de detekterede fisk i klassekategorier. Dette
gøres ombord på AUVen i (nær) real-tid.
Den underliggende algoritme, kaldet OFDNet, er udviklet baseret på undersøgelser af
nuværende state-of-the-art arkitekturer til objekt genkendelse gennem dyb læring. Disse
er blevet modificeret og trænet til formålet; at udføre detektion og lokalisation af fisk i
undervandsbilleder og derudover klassificere detekterede fisk i tre klassekategorier: sild,
makrel og “andre”. Til træning af sådanne algoritmer er en database med fisk blevet
etableret ved at besøge danske akvarier med en udviklet undervandskamera opsætning.
OFDNet er med succes blevet integreret på en Raspberry Pi med en Intel Movidius
Neural Compute Stick tilsluttet. Dette opnåede en endelig præstation i form a processer-
ingstid på 7.81 FPS, hvilket er påvist at være en forøgelse på 1001 % sammenlignet med
en selvstående Raspberry Pi.
For at teste OFDNet udenfor rammerne af det godt betingede akvariemiljø er “real-
world” billedmateriale blevet indsamlet. I disse data er sigtbarheden i vandet omkring
30-50 cm og den overordnede billedkvalitet er dårlig sammenlignet med akvariedataen. I
et testsæt kan OFDNet med succes detektere og lokalisere 44.8 % af de tilstedeværende
fisk og ud af disse er 23.1 % blevet klassificeret korrekt. En udvidet model med en lav
repræsentation af de tidligere ukendte miljøer viste med success at kunne detektere og
lokalisere 66.7 % af de tilstedeværende fisk og ud af disse er 89.7 % klassificeret korrekt.
Ud over dette foreslår denne afhandling enkle metoder til at udvide systemets muligheder
til at udføre fiskestime “tracking” ved brugen af lokalitetsoplysningerne fra OFDNet og et
Kalman filter. Den endelige softwarepakke er udviklet som et højt skalerbart RESTful
API. En Android tablet applikation er udviklet til at visualisere data og statistik til
slutbrugeren efter en fuldført mission med AUV’en.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter will introduce the project of developing an underwater state-of-the-art
camera system for detection, localisation and classification of fish and fish species to be
used in intelligent fishing operations. This includes the motivation for carrying out this
work, problem statement and project description. Finally, the overview of the thesis
structure and the list of personal contributions of this work are given.
1.1
Motivation
ATLAS MARIDAN ApS is a company situated at the harbour of Rungsted Kyst. Since
the establishment in 1992 the company has specialised in developing, designing and
integrating autonomous underwater vehicles.
This has mainly been focused towards
larger vehicles (6-8 meters) for underwater campaigns and surveys with high require-
ments for navigation, sensing suite, communication and durability. ATLAS MARIDAN
is owned by the German company ATLAS ELEKTRONIK which in turn is owned by
ThyssenKrupp. In total ThyssenKrupp employs about 160 000 workers across multiple
industries.
During the summer of 2017, a new project was proposed with a new line of a smaller and
cheaper AUV since it became apparent that a market for such vehicles exists. The scope
included both the design and development of a technology platform and mechanical de-
sign of this new small cost-effective AUV. The project is supported by ThyssenKrupp
Garage - an in-house start-up accelerator - and is officially referred to as the oXeanpedia
project [57]. The overall objective of this vehicle is to be an easy accessible surveying
vehicle that will enable marine research applications to be more economical and environ-
mental friendly as well as exploring the applications within commercial fishing. Most
focus is currently attended towards the latter object with respect to reducing by-catch
and discards in commercial fisheries.
By-catch and discards refers to the portion of a catch of fish which is not retained on
board during fishing operations and is returned to the sea, often dead or dying. To main-
tain a sustainable aquaculture and force commercial fishing to strive for a cleaner catch,
a new set of regulations have been introduced to the industry in 2015. The “Discarding
and the landing obligation” [12] presents two options for commercial fisheries regarding
by-catch and discards. Either the fishermen must use their limited quota for by-catch
==PAGE:28==
2
1 Introduction
causing them to abandon all fishing activities when used up, or they must invest time
and money towards cleaner fishing effectively reducing the amount of by-catch and dis-
cards. In EU waters, the discard and by-catch rates are estimated to be in the range of
15% to 40% in recent EU reports [10] and worldwide estimations quantifies to as much
as 20 million tons of marine life annually.
To help reduce by-catch and discards for fishing operations, ATLAS MARIDAN proposes
the aforementioned small cost-effective AUV to aid commercial fisheries by inspecting
and parametrising a detected school of fish before it is hauled aboard. A sketch of the
proposed concept is illustrated in Figure 1.1. Here a fisherman has detected a school
of fish on his on board echo sounder.
No information of this specific school of fish
other than echo returns and guesswork based on experience is available. Not knowing
expected by-catch rates or possible discards, the proposed AUV is deployed. As shown
in the sketch, the AUV is then to descent into the water column where the fish are
detected and move towards/circulate the school of fish while gathering and processing
data from on board cameras and processors. When the AUV emerges, the results are
streamed to the fisherman. This effectively allows the fisherman to make facts based
on-site decisions to substantially minimize the risk of by-catch by making sure necessary
data and information of that specific school of fish is made available to him. Directly
correlated with by-catch reduction is maximization of exploitation of quotas as well as
maximizing the yield of the main catch. Other than being a benefit for commercial
fishing, this also seeks to sustain the aquaculture in EU waters by taking a step towards
greener fishing.
1.2
Problem statement
In order to acquire and process underwater footage, a camera system is needed. Such a
camera system should be able to obtain quality images in low-light conditions and have
the means of an algorithm and processor capable of processing the gathered data. This
algorithm should be able to detect and localise fish present in the images and furthermore
classify detected fish into class categories to generate valuable information of by-catch.
Highest priority species include herring and mackerel. Other fish species included in the
by-catch regulative such as sprat, cod and salmon among others are also of interest but
of lower priority for the algorithm and are not prioritised in this work. Furthermore, to
realise the “hunter-mode” as sketched in Figure 1.1 the AUV should be equipped with
some means to identify and track a school of fish from a longer range using the data
obtained from the cameras.
Since the fisherman will be eagerly awaiting the results of a dive, a time dependency
on the data processing is introduced. The data should be immediately available when
the AUV emerges. This places the processing load towards the AUV itself and it must
therefore be capable of processing the acquired data on board during a mission. The
==PAGE:29==
1.3 Project description

data is not needed to be processed in real-time, but in a timely fashion such that enough
samples produce a general overview of the fish school distribution. However, for fish
school tracking the algorithm should be capable of running in near real-time. Other
restrictions are introduced by available space and power capacity on board the AUV. I.e.
the possible physical size of the camera system including processor is limited and it may
not be possible to utilise standard highly efficient GPUs for processing. In summary the
entire system resulting from this work should be able to integrate well into the already
existing AUV platform for both interfacing and cohesion.
Currently a system as described above does not exist. The conditions for underwater
video/image acquisition are usually poor in EU waters rendering the data both unpre-
dictable and cumbersome to process using traditional image processing. However, due
to the advances within deep learning for visual recognition and optimised embedded
platforms in the recent years, a statistical data driven solution might be beneficial for
this specific task.
1.3
Project description
Following from the previous section, the main purpose of this project is first and foremost
to investigate the possibilities to develop an algorithm or model capable of detecting and
==PAGE:30==
4
1 Introduction
localising fish in images and furthermore classify detected fish into class categories: her-
ring, mackerel and “other species”. The approach taken is based on current state-of-the-
art methodologies within deep learning and artificial intelligence for visual recognition.
Secondly, methods for deploying the developed algorithm or model in near real-time on
a small AUV should be explored. Here constraints on size, processing power and power
capabilities are limiting factors. Finally, the results of processing data from a completed
mission with the AUV should be presented to the end user in an easy interpretable
fashion.
A secondary and lower prioritised objective of this work is to investigate the possibility
to extend the developed system to carry out fish school tracking. However, being a sec-
ondary objective most focus is put towards the development of the fish detection system.
The project description, as stated above, is elaborated in the following bullet points:
• Obtain a fish database consisting of images and/or videos of fish with a proposed
low-light camera system. Acquisition is focused towards herring and mackerel.
• Process acquired fish database with bounding box and class annotations in prepa-
ration for training deep neural networks.
• Investigate, model and develop deep convolutional neural networks for fish detec-
tion, localisation and classification based on current state-of-the-art architectures.
• Propose simple means to extend the application to facilitate fish school tracking
using the optical sensors.
• Scale the proposed models for near real-time inference and explore the possibilities
for deployment on embedded processors.
• Design and integrate a complete scalable production level RESTful software pack-
age for the entire system on the AUV.
• Design an Android tablet application to facilitate the visualisation of the results
of an AUV mission.
• Evaluate all the above by sea-trial or simulated data.

Thesis structure

discussion of which technologies are pursued is presented.

the final neural network. This seeks to test robustness, generalisation and quality of
the developed algorithm in very poor, low-visibility and low-light conditions previously
unexplored.

1.5
Personal contributions
A summary of the main contributions achieved during this project and thesis work is
given in the following:
• An underwater camera system has been identified and well tested. The camera
system includes external lights supported by a developed light-intensity controller.
• An underwater camera-tube has been developed and built to support easy acces-
sible data acquisition and testing.
• A valuable image fish database mainly focused towards herring and mackerel has
been collected. This is of high value for the current and future work both for

CHAPTER 2
Analysis
In this chapter the current state-of-the-art of related research is presented and discussed.
The advantages, drawbacks and thoughts of a fish-finding vehicle is presented along with
how such a vehicle would be used and which sensors it would need to include. Finally, the
pursued approach on an underwater fish detection, localisation and classification system
as well as fish school tracking is presented along with the capabilities and limitations of
these.
2.1
State-of-the-art
In previous research [44, 13, 16, 52] the challenge of detecting fish in underwater images
or video sequences is described by a similar workflow. Traditional image processing is
utilised to detect fish in an image by background subtraction or other forms of segmenta-
tion, noise removal, image transformations and binary mask/contour estimation. Some
of these publications propose methods utilising unsupervised clustering techniques to
obtain and separate the position and area of fish in the images as the final detection
step instead of blob/contour estimation.
For fish species classification [8, 40, 51, 44, 16], the proposed methods also resolve around
a similar approach. From the previously detected areas in the image sought to encap-
sulate a single fish, linear features are extracted such as histograms, contour shapes,
gradients, raw pixel values and Fourier transformations. These features are in the pre-
sented references commonly used to train different variations of linear machine learning
classifiers to obtain a model capable of predicting the specie or type of the detected fish.
In more recent publications [37, 45], the approach to classify fish into species is based
on deep neural networks. In [45] a multilayer neural network is utilised as classifier for
processing already extracted features from a fish image. These features are based on
known parameters such as size and shape of the fish. In [37] current state-of-the-art
convolutional neural networks (CNNs) are utilised as both feature extractor of an input
image as well as classifier for these extracted features.
Finally, [32, 34, 33] proposes methods for the entire process of detecting and localising
fish in images as well as classifying detected fish into their respective species by utilising
state-of-the-art object detectors based on deep regional convolutional neural networks
(R-CNNs) for both region proposals and classifications.

In related research fields such as visual recognition for autonomous cars and mobile
robots, similar methods are used for detecting road signs, lanes, other cars and objects.
Current state-of-the-art models too resolves around CNNs for both object detection and
classification. A well-known architecture for this is called Faster R-CNN [43], of which
the work in [32, 33] is based upon. This is currently among the highest achieving models
for precision in object detection. Other mentionable publications on this subject include
Single Shot MultiBox Detectors [36].
Most of the referenced publications for underwater fish detection and classification is
developed for non-intrusive environments, i.e. the camera system is statically fixed to
a position. Most of the data presented in these publications is generally of good qual-

classification is based and proved upon an already available large-scale fish dataset [27]
which mainly contains good quality footage of tropical fish species and does not target
specifically chosen species outside of this scope.
The limitations of such automated systems for fish detection and classification are com-
monly scalability and reliability. Traditional image processing often fails to generalise
outside the scope of which it has been developed and is therefore challenged when in-
troduced to new environments, noise and harsher conditions. Large-scale data-driven
models seeks to eliminate the effects of such variations in data but in turn usually
requires very large annotated datasets and puts a much larger demand on processing
power.

As discussed in Section 2.1, underwater detection and classification of fish does present
some challenges and limitations. One of the key issues is the quality of the underwater
footage. In good conditions such as tropical waters, it is entirely possible to obtain high
quality images. In such waters the light conditions are good even at lower depths, the
visibility is good and the water is in general free of particles, clutter and marine snow.
However, for fishing operations in the Baltic or North Sea this is not the case. In some
conditions the visibility is limited to as little as 30-40 cm. In addition to this, most water
will be highly affected by marine snow which only amplifies when lighting is introduced.
Examples of two images obtained at approximately 1.5 m depth in the Baltic Sea are
shown in Figure 2.4. Here external light is introduced since no natural light reached this
depth. Looking at the images reflections of marine snow is clearly seen as well as the
image looks somewhat out of focus and blurred. Some shapes, contours and objects can
be visually identified even though the quality of the images is generally poor both due
to visibility and motion-blur. In such conditions traditional image processing techniques
are difficult to develop. A generalised algorithm applicable in both good as well as bad
conditioned environments is hardly achievable. Furthermore, the classification task of
the detected fish should consider many unreliable factors such as extensive motion blur,
light conditions, varying angles and rotations. These are all effects that needs to be
considered and manageable by a such an algorithm.

Even though deep learning is investigated and deemed necessary for the applications

Lighting
As shown in Figure 3.1, two artificial light sources are integrated into the AUV. These
seek to aid the cameras in conditions where natural light is no longer available or the
amounts of these are insufficient. The lights used in this version of the AUV are Lumen
Subsea Lights supplied by Blue Robotics and is shown in Figure 3.9. The internals of
the underwater light are based on a high-efficient Cree XLamp MK-R LED which is
capable of outputting more than 1500 lumens at 15 W. This is equivalent or brighter
than most car headlights.
Given the 135° beam angle of the light and the typical underwater conditions in EU
waters, some challenges and complications by using external light might be introduced.
This is due to the underwater conditions with respect to visibility, particles, marine snow
and general underwater clutter. These will cause high-intensity reflections in the camera,
given that the light illuminates directly in front of the camera and thereby decrease the
quality of the data obtained.
To minimize the effect of this, the lights should be placed at a sufficient distance from
the cameras. For this specific AUV, with the small size in mind, the lights should simply
be placed as far away as the mechanical design constraints allows while still illuminating
in the desired direction. For the bow section, the light is placed on top of the camera
and retracted a few centimetres to compensate for the beam angle such that the camera
dome will not be directly illuminated by the light. The portside light is placed in the
space available in the bow section of the AUV. Here a distance of 10-15 cm is obtained
to the camera.

4.3.2

As the process of annotating data for object detection is a long and cumbersome process,
an automatic method for performing most of the work is developed. The proposal is
to have a neural network do the heavy lifting of bounding box annotations and then
manually verify these bounding boxes and assign class labels. The assignment of class
labels can too be mostly automated, since only two fish tanks are of mixed species and
even in these mixed fish tanks the major part of the fish are mackerel.

tional neural network (R-CNN) known as Faster R-CNN [43]. More specific details on

4.4
Data augmentation
Working with a sparse dataset, such as the one obtained for this project, poses the
challenge of not being able to have the neural network becoming invariant to rotations,
translations, viewpoint or illumination given the lack of examples of this in the dataset.
This will lead to lower precisions, overall performance and might result in overfitting.
During the data acquisition it is attempted to vary the conditions in the best possible
way to address this issue. In addition to this, a very effective way of overcoming many
of these challenges is by augmenting the obtained data. This seeks to transform and
create variations of the already obtained data and thereby extending the dataset with
what the neural network will think is new distinct, unique images. Essentially a much
larger dataset is created from the sparse dataset obtained.
Augmentations such as rotations, flipping and translations are performed and generated
at random during training time of the neural network.
As such a more generalised
representation of the fish in the database is generated.

5.1.1
Definition of image classification and object
detection
Image classification is among the most well-known problems in visual recognition. Clas-
sification tasks seeks to classify an input image into one of many different categories. An

example of this is shown in Figure 5.1 where an input image of a mackerel is correctly
classified with a 98% confidence. This works well for classifying images into certain
categories.
Another situation is when an input image contains multiple objects, e.g. a school of
fish or an image from a mixed species aquarium. Classification models might be able
to classify that such an image contains fish, however it lacks further capabilities to
differentiate between different fish in the image and provide their location. This is the
exact definition of object detection. Given an input image, object detection will output
a set of bounding boxes and a set of corresponding class labels and confidences. An
example of this is shown in Figure 5.2 where an input image contains multiple fish of
different species. An object detection model is then able to predict a bounding box
encapsulating each detected fish and to predict a class label for this bounding box at
a given confidence. Such functionality is needed in this work to satisfy the objectives
of detecting and localising fish and furthermore classify detected fish from underwater
images.
5.1.2
Object detection methodology and theory
To introduce the working concept of object detection, the most intuitive form of this
can be described as shown in Figure 5.3. Here an input image is processed with sliding
windows. Sliding windows is a set of pre-defined windows in different scales and aspect
ratios that crops out one window at a time. The set of windows will evaluate the input
image by sliding a pre-defined amount of pixels window by window. Each window is
fed to a feature extractor which can be of any form linear/non-linear. These features
are then used in a classifier which again can be of any form linear/non-linear. From
this, each processed window will obtain a class label, e.g. background, herring, mackerel
or “other species”. Many of these windows will overlap essentially containing the same
object. To compensate for this, non-maximum suppression (NMS) is applied to identify
the bounding box that best encapsulates the object. In order to explain the concept of

Feature extractor
A feature extractor comes in many different types, sizes and depths which all relies on
convolutional layers. Excellent detailed in-depth explanations of these can be found
in [38] and [29] and will only be presented in very general terms here. Convolutional
layers are particularly well-suited for images since the architecture allows the network
to take the spatial structure of the image into account. As shown in Figure 5.5 this is
done by having a set of learnable filters or kernels evaluate the input image and each
feature map by convolutions. These filters are typically of the pixel size F = 3 × 3 or
less common F = 5×5 and extends in depth through the full depth of the input volume.
A feature map refers to the volume generated by a convolutional layer.
Each convolution in the network follows with an activation function (non-linearity). This
is to decide if the neuron will fire. Currently, ReLU (rectified linear unit) is the activation
function most commonly used in neural networks. ReLU computes the function

(5.3)

known and still commonly used activations are the Sigmoid and Tanh activation.

layer. A pooling layer seeks to reduce or downsample the spatial size of the feature
representation to reduce the amount of parameters and computations in the network.

State-of-the-art and investigated architectures
As previously mentioned this work focuses on expanding and modifying the capabili-
ties of well known state-of-the-art deep learning architectures and therefore the object
detection pipeline shown in Figure 5.5 is not directly applicable. However, with the
material presented in the previous sections it becomes easy to understand how these
object detectors work.
One deviation of the presented pipeline is a simple trick that describes one of the greatest
advances within object detection. This is to swap the order of the sliding windows and
feature extractor to reduce the computations drastically by only evaluating the input
image in the feature extractor once instead of one time per window. Instead the sliding
windows, or anchors as they will be referred to in the papers, are evaluated on the feature
map. Also, as previously stated, most architectures use a regression head for bounding
box refinements in addition to the classification head. This is to achieve higher precision
in encapsulating objects in comparison to using the standard sized anchors as the final
output.

the-art object detectors. From here two architectures are identified and chosen for this
work. The most accurate is stated as being Faster R-CNN [43] with Inception ResNet
V2 [53] as feature extractor. The best trade-off and commonly recommended architecture
is stated as being a single shot multibox detector (SSD) [36] with MobileNet [21] as
feature extractor. Faster R-CNN is, in addition to being the most accurate model, also
the slowest and largest of models and will be used in this work to form a baseline model
as reference for accuracy. These two models will be briefly introduced in the following

Faster R-CNN
Faster R-CNN is the third generation of regional convolutional neural networks first
introduced as R-CNN in [18]. Later came Fast R-CNN [17] and finally the architecture
which is applied in this work. As seen in the architecture of Faster R-CNN in Figure 5.8
the network consists of what can be divided into three modules; feature extraction, re-
gion proposals and final output.
What is new here compared to the previously discussed object detection methodology,
is the use of a region proposal network. The task of this network is in its essence simple;
it proposes regions in the input image that might be interesting and therefore might
contain an object. This produces class-agnostic boxes using a set of pre-defined anchors
(earlier introduced as sliding windows) and concludes with non-maximum suppression.
The remainder of the network crops the proposed region from the feature map (RoI pool-
ing) and passes it through a classification head for box class prediction and a regression
head for box refinements.
Details related to training Faster R-CNN as the baseline model and the choice of hyper-
parameters for this are presented and discussed in Section 5.2.1.
Single Shot Multibox Detector
SSD consists, in contrary to Faster R-CNN, of a single network which at the same time
predicts bounding boxes as well as box labels. This is inspired by MultiBox [54] which
is a technique used for fast class-agnostic bounding box predictions. This eliminates the
computational overhead from the region proposal network and is therefore at its core
much faster. The architecture of the network is shown in Figure 5.9 and is in the paper
[36] originally presented using the VGG-16 [49] architecture as feature extractor. This
could however be any feature extractor, and for this project the MobileNet architecture
is used as feature extractor.
As with Faster R-CNN, a set of predefined boxes are used to evaluate the feature map.

5.2.2
Optical Fish Detection Network
In short “OFDNet”. This is the proposed name for a single unified neural network pos-
sibly capable of achieving (near) real-time inference even on embedded processors. For
this purpose, the SSD architecture is explored. As previously discussed, this architecture
is often recommended as the “sweet-spot” in the speed/accuracy trade-off. As can be
seen in Figure 5.9, the SSD architecture produces output in six different layers using
3 × 3 convolutions. The classification output in all six layers are modified such that the
network complies with the 3 + 1 classes used in this work.

handles different object scales in the input image and seeks to mimic the effect from
handling the input image at different scales. The scale of the default boxes for each

5.2.3
Cascaded FishNet and classifier
From the labelling task in Chapter 4, it was found that a network trained on a very
sparse dataset produced quite impressive results in the task of predicting fish regions in
the input image. Leveraging this obtained knowledge at a higher scale, the approach
taken here is to train the SSD architecture with a MobileNet feature extractor to per-
form class-agnostic object proposals. The proposed name for such a network is FishNet,
as it simply predicts areas in input images containing fish. The region proposals from
FishNet are fed into into a self-contained image classifier which can be modified in ar-
chitecture, size and scale to obtain high-accuracy classification while maintaining a high
throughput. This approach can be visualised as shown in Figure 5.17. Here an input
image is fed into FishNet which proposes regions in the input image that might contain
fish. The classifier utilises this information to crop out the proposed region in the input
image and predicts a class label for this region.
FishNet is trained in a similar fashion as OFDNet presented in Section 5.2.2. The results
are shown as loss and precision in Figure 5.18. Here it can be seen that a final AP of 0.59
is obtained. Both the localisation and classification loss resembles the results obtained
for OFDNet in Section 5.2.2.

5.3
Visualisation and inspection of trained
networks
In this final section tools to visualise and inspect trained neural networks are investigated.
This is to introduce some means overcome a commonly taken “black-box way of thinking”
when discussing how neural networks function during runtime. These methodologies
present ways to peer into the neural networks, diagnose attentions and assess whether
the network is over/underfitting. The tools used for this purpose are based on source
code made available in [6] and [31].
5.3.1
Filter activation maximisation
Visualising the learned filters in the network reveals which types of input patterns or
features maximises the output throughout the different filters in the network. Some
filters from the feature extractor are shown in Figure 5.20. From here it can be seen
that the early filters mostly match for colours and directional patterns. Further into
the network the filters evolve from looking for simple patterns to much more complex
patterns and abstract features. Should filters have failed to learn, the visualisation of
such a filter would mostly identify as noise. An important observation of the filters is
that some of these seem identical, but rotated. This is due to the previously discussed
rotation variance in convolutional neural networks.
To extend this approach to a more extreme case, the question of a neural network could
be turned around such that given a fully trained network an input image is generated
based on what input would maximise the activation of a given output class. Doing this
for the three classes used in this work generates the abstract artist-ish images shown in
Figure 5.21. After starring at these for a while it does not take much convincing to start
identifying shapes of fish, fish heads, eyes and fins.
5.3.2
Saliency & activation maps
Saliency maps was first introduced in [48] and seeks to investigate how output values
changes in the network with respect to a small change in the inputs. Given an input
image this provides some means to inspect that the correct pixels or regions are used
to determine an output category and hence verify that the network has not overfitted
to water colours, marine snow or similar. A saliency map is computed for an output
category with respect to an input image and is shown for each output category in Fig-
ure 5.22 d, e and f. The areas investigated are shown in Figure 5.22 a, b and c, and
are areas proposed and detected by OFDNet and further expanded by 30 pixels in each
direction. The visualisation of this generates an image reminiscent of edge detection and
verifies that it is indeed the fish in the input images which affects the output class.

Fish School Tracking
As a second, lower prioritised, objective of this project, a method to perform fish school
tracking is proposed. In the initial phase of the AUV mission only a reference position
for a previously echo-sounder detected fish school is given. By the time the AUV arrives
to this position, the fish school might have moved. For this purpose, some basic method
enabling fish school tracking is desired.
Given the visibility of the water allows for it, the forward-looking camera system is
utilised to first estimate the centre position of a visually detected fish school. This is
based on utilising the location output from OFDNet presented in Chapter 5. Secondly
a method for filtering and tracking the position estimation in 2D is proposed. The main
purpose for this method is to smooth and estimate the output of the centre-estimation
due to inconsistent detections.

neural network is heavily optimised towards GPUs and is clearly the bottleneck of the

on embedded processors. This work is carried out and presented in Section 7.2.

The Camera Module initiates, configures and interfaces the cameras.
The Image Processing Module pre-processes an image to prepare it for entering

As previously discussed, Intel have made a Neural Compute Stick available which es-
sentially is a hardware accelerator for AI applications with a USB interface. The NCS
is developed to support and accelerate only a limited set of operations at this moment.
These are mostly well-known classification models in both the TensorFlow and Caffe.
To deploy any model on the NCS, the Intel Movidius SDK [24] is needed. This is used
to compile a trained model to a graph that can be deployed on the NCS. The SSD Mo-
bileNet architecture used in this work is initially not supported by the NCS and would
therefore not be able to be converted and deployed on the NCS. To overcome this issue
a few steps needs to be taken as shown in Figure 7.3. From investigations it has been
found that the operations used in the Caffe implementation of the SSD architecture
from [9] is almost all supported by the NCS by making small changes in the source code
for the NCS SDK. Hence, the model can be retrained in the Caffe framework using the
referenced implementation, compiled to a NCS graph and finally be deployed on the
Raspberry Pi and Intel NCS for optimised inference. The detailed instructions for this
process is described step-by-step in Appendix C.

7.3
Scaling with REST API
To deploy and scale the deep learning model to production level standards, the concept
of a REST API [15] is adopted to the software package. For the purpose presented in this
work, a RESTful application seeks to split up and de-attach the deep learning inference
from the rest of the system and interface this part using only HTTP requests to a web
server. This introduces easy scalability of the system. With OFDNet encapsulated in a
web server, the inference could also take part on a different hardware device than where
the cameras and the remaining operations of the system are performed.
Additional
camera clients could easily be introduced or removed as everything can be written as
stand-alone applications where the only operation needed is taking an image, post it to
the web server and interpret the results.
As mentioned, another possibility could be to relocate the inference from the vehicles
to a much larger and faster hardware platform located on the ship or even in the cloud.
This single hardware platform or cloud service could then support an entire fleet of
AUVs. Without changing a single line of code. This would of course remove the optical
fish school tracking abilities from the vehicle, but would in the same time allow for post-
processing much larger datasets. However even for inference locally on the AUV, this
type of production architecture is considered as best practice and will be followed.

CHAPTER 9
Results
In this chapter the capabilities of OFDNet are tested and evaluated by new data. This
is to detect and localise fish in harsh real-world environments and to classify the de-
tected fish into their corresponding class categories. Two obtained datasets are jointly
evaluated by quantitative metrics introduced in Chapter 5 and the effect of retraining
OFDNet with a sparse representation of the new environments is investigated.
During the project, two opportunities came to obtain real-world data. First the camera
mock-up tube was utilised in the Weser river at the ATLAS ELEKTRONIK’s test fa-
cilities in Bremen to investigate the presence of fish. Second, the AUV participated in
sea trials with local fishermen on Rügen in fishing operations for herring by fish traps.
During the Rügen trials, the AUV recordings were supplemented by GoPro recordings
from a statically fixed point inside the fish trap.
9.1
Weser fish inspections
The conditions in the Weser river was poor. The visibility was about 40-50 cm, the
water was full of clutter, marine snow and particles aggravating the image quality. The
top light of the camera mock-up tube was turned on as otherwise it would be completely
dark at the approximately 1.5 m depth. Some samples of the obtained and evaluated
data are shown in Figure 9.1. Prior to this, OFDNet has never seen any data from a
distribution similar to this w.r.t. visibility and overall conditions of the environment.
However, the network is able to detect and localise some, not all, fish in the images.
In Figure 9.1a and b, the fish are correctly detected, localised and classified as not a
herring and not a mackerel. In Figure 9.1c fish are detected and localised, however one
of the detected fish are falsely classified as a herring. In some samples the network fails
to identify any fish, even though humans by visual inspection recognises at least two or
three fish as shown in Figure 9.1d.

9.4
Summary
In this chapter the results from testing OFDNet with real-world data obtained in the
Weser river as well as in the Baltic Sea have been presented. These showed that the
network was able to perform both detection, localisation and classification of fish in
previously unknown environments. However, these results also showed samples of fish
where the network failed to detect them while being visually identifiable.
In a test
dataset 44.8 % of the fish were correctly detected and out these 23.1 % were correctly
classified.
The tested environments differ largely from the environments used for training, since
the overall image quality and visibility of the real-world data are magnitudes lower than
what is represented in the training set. To explore how well the network learns from
new environments, OFDNet was retrained with an extension of the original dataset by a
sparse representation of the real-world environments. In total 17 samples were randomly
selected from the sea-trials data and introduced to the dataset. This showed that the
performance in the harsh environment increased and the classifications of the detected
fish where mostly correct. In the same test dataset as before, 66.7 % of the fish were
correctly detected and out of these 89.7 % were correctly classified.

CHAPTER 10
Conclusion
The main objective for this work has been to explore and develop methodologies for un-
derwater fish detection and localisation, and fish species classification in (near) real-time
using optical sensors on a small low-cost AUV. This involves an investigation of hard-
ware possible to integrate on the AUV while satisfying requirements for data acquisition
and data processing. This must at the same time comply with restrictions introduced
by the AUV with regards to size and power. The approach taken on the algorithms for
such a system is based on current state-of-the-art deep learning architectures for object
detection. A second objective has been to propose simple means to further extend the
capabilities of the detection system to perform fish school tracking.
The aim and motivation to pursue research and system development in this direction is
explained in Chapter 1, and further supported by an analysis in Chapter 2.
For underwater optical sensing small low-light cameras from Blue Robotics have suc-
cessfully been investigated and tested. Each camera is supplemented with an external
lighting source along with a developed light-intensity controller.
This secures a cor-
rect amount of lighting at any given time. Two camera systems have been integrated
into the AUV for forward looking and side looking data acquisition in the form of images.
Using the chosen cameras, a dataset containing three class categories have successfully
been acquired. The class categories include herring, mackerel and “other species”. The
dataset has been acquired by developing an easy accessible underwater camera mock-
up tube. This is used on travels to three different aquariums in Denmark. From the
collected dataset, the fish localisation and class category have been annotated using
bounding boxes and class labels, respectively. In total 13 124 image files have been pro-
cessed in which 4593 fish have been annotated. The annotation process has been carried
out both by hand and by a sparsely trained neural network for automatic annotation.
The collected dataset has successfully been utilised in training state-of-the-art object
detection architectures expanded and modified for the purpose of fish detection, locali-
sation and fish species classification. The final proposed model for runtime inference on
embedded processors, OFDNet, reached a final mAP of 0.55. In comparison, a baseline
model reached a final mAP of 0.65. OFDNet is shown on evaluated samples to deliver
good performance even though it achieves a lower mAP. Tools for inspecting and visual-
ising the trained neural network have been successfully applied to verify generalisation
and fitting of the network in regards to feature attention, class activations and learned

filter kernels.
The chosen hardware device for AUV integration consists of a Raspberry Pi with the
low-cost Intel Movidius Neural Compute Stick. By retraining and recompiling the in-
ference graph (OFDNet) to a custom format, the performance of the complete OFD
system reached 7.81 FPS, which is a 1001 % increase from a vanilla Raspberry Pi. In
comparison a NVIDIA Jetson TX2 was investigated and through optimisations reached
a performance of 36.63 FPS, however by the cost of approximately a factor ten in price.
For the complete OFD system software package a highly scalable RESTfull API have
been developed in Python. This encapsulates OFDNet as an inference server and has
the capabilities to perform run-time data management. This successfully ensures a pro-
duction level environment in which essentially the inference could be performed in the
cloud supporting multiple clients at the same time, without the need to change any code.
To store and present the results from a completed mission with the AUV, an Android
tablet application has been developed. After a dive is completed and the AUV is within
WiFi range on the surface, the application connects to the AUV and transfers the data
to the tablet. The tablet processes the results and presents the end user with the statis-
tics of by-catch, sensor data and image data in an easy interpretable fashion.
In order to test the capabilities of OFDNet outside the scope of aquariums, real-world
data from the Weser river in Bremen and from the Baltic Sea at Rügen have been
acquired and processed. This data represents the expected conditions at sea in terms
of visibility, motion blur, marine snow and general clutter and differs largely from the
original acquired dataset from the aquariums. In the collected test dataset, OFDNet
successfully detects 44 % of the fish present in the samples, and from this classifies 23.1 %
of the detected fish into the correct class category.
To prove a major advantage of using data-driven models over traditional image process-
ing algorithms, 17 randomly sampled images of the real-world data have been intro-
duced in the training dataset to generate a very sparse representation of the previously
unknown real-world environment. By retraining OFDNet and processing the collected
test dataset, the model drastically increases its performance and now detects 66.7 % of
the fish and furthermore classifies 89.7 % of these into the correct class category.
A simple method for fish school tracking have been proposed, developed and tested on
a video-snippet acquired at the aquariums. This successfully retrieves the location data
of each detected fish from OFDNet and from this calculates the estimated centre po-
sition of a fish school. Furthermore a Kalman filter is applied to dampen fluctuations
in the centre position estimation which are caused by inconsistent fish detections. The
Kalman filter is shown to furthermore estimate the position of the fish school when no
measurements are available based on a motion model for velocity.

10 Conclusion
105
Final remarks
Both OFDNet and the proposed method for fish school tracking are limited by the vis-
ibility of the water, which for the Baltic and North Sea is often very poor. In order
for the cameras to be able to successfully capture a fish school at a longer range, a
visibility of at least a few meters should be available. This does not seem achievable,
hence another solution should be investigated for fish school tracking as presented in the
following Chapter 11.
OFDNet is shown to deliver good performance and is furthermore shown to generalise
even in harsh unknown environments, but the overall results of OFDNet is too depending
on the effective visibility of the cameras. In poor conditions, the obtained images are
furthermore highly affected by motion blur and other image degrading factors. More
real-world data for OFDNet is obviously necessary, and an exclusion of some sea states
must simply be realised as not operational for the system, given its current state.

CHAPTER 11
Future Work
A new version of the AUV is already decided on as shown in Figure 11.1. Version 1.3 will
feature a single forward looking camera system. To compliment the camera system, a
forward looking sonar is to be integrated. Further investigations and research will focus
on fusion of the sonar data with the image data to enable fish size measurements.
Another purpose of the forward-looking sonar is to replace the optical fish school track-
ing. A forward looking sonar achieves much better results in terms of range and is not
affected by poor visibility in the water.
A “big data” database is to be developed for the purpose of continuously extending the
currently used dataset. Much more real-world data is needed in order to increase the
performance of OFDNet. More sea trials is needed to both acquire new data, and to
investigate a recommended diving behaviour and hardware configuration such that the
AUV is enabled to position itself within visibility range of the fish rather than scaring
them away.
