Abstract
Advances in technology at reduced length-scales has led to an increasing interest in designing
and characterizing nanosystems with advantageous thermoelectric properties both for fun-
damental understanding and for device applications. Thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects in capacitively
coupled quantum dots have received increasing attention, and concepts such as Maxwell‚Äôs
demon, refrigeration, and energy harvesting has been studied in such systems.
In this thesis we study thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects in a system of two capacitively coupled
quantum dots in a three-terminal conÔ¨Āguration suggested in literature, where the Coulomb
inter-dot coupling mediates a heat Ô¨āow between the active (where a bias can be applied) quan-
tum dot system and the passive (unbiased) quantum dot system without particles tunneling
between the two systems.
Whereas most theoretical studies of such a system describe the thermoelectric behaviour
due to single-electron tunneling processes, less attention has been given to higher-order multi-
electron tunneling processes. To this end, we apply a master equation approach with transition
rates and heat transfer rates derived from the T-matrix formalism up to second order in
the tunneling Hamiltonian. Applying a regularization scheme, we evaluate the second order
cotunneling rates analytically under certain assumptions, and in more general cases we suggest
a numerical approach.
Synthesizing on literature in the sequential tunneling regime, we characterize the system‚Äôs
ability to transfer heat between the two quantum dot systems in terms of a thermal factor
which cools down the hotter system and heats up the colder system, and a demon factor
which can cool down the biased system, where the eÔ¨Äect from the latter can be increased with
asymmetric couplings to the leads. As an example, we consider a graphene-based device, and
as an outlook we discuss the bias dependence of the electric current under optimized cooling
conditions.
We Ô¨Ānd that cotunneling processes contribute to a signiÔ¨Ācant quantitative correction to
the sequential cooling power in the biased system in the region of interest of maximum cooling
power. The increased contribution from cotunneling processes for suÔ¨Éciently large energy-
independent bare tunneling rates quenches the cooling eÔ¨Äect in the case of uniform tempera-
ture in the system, and can change the behaviour qualitatively as compared to the sequential
result. With asymmetric lead couplings, the cooling power can be increased but we highlight
the necessity for including higher-order processes to make improved quantitative estimates.

Introduction
As advances in technology has driven electronics closer to the nanoscopic scale, theory has de-
veloped to describe transport phenomena on a more fundamental level in the Ô¨Āeld of quantum
transport or mesoscopic physics. The word mesoscopic implies that the region of interest is
the intermediate (meso) length scale between the microscopic world of individual atoms and
fundamental particles and the macroscopic world of large organisms and structures. In other
words, it is the regime where classical objects start to show quantum phenomena [1].
Quantum conÔ¨Ānement is the immediate consequence of moving to smaller length scales
where boundary conditions become increasingly important and give rise to discrete energy
levels.
Small structures where electrons are conÔ¨Āned in all three spatial dimensions with
delta-function like density of states are called quantum dots. Coulomb interaction between
electrons in such structures become important and gives rise to a charging cost of adding
electrons to a quantum dot. Whether electrons are actually conÔ¨Āned in the quantum dot and
feel this Coulomb interaction depends on how easily they can tunnel to the outside world.
As an estimate, the tunnel junction between a quantum dot and a metallic lead (see e.g.
Fig. 1.1) can classically be considered as a capacitor C and a resistor R in parallel with a
discharge time ŌĄRC = RC = C/G, where G is the conductance. The lifetime of the state
of an electron on the dot, which have paid the charging energy EC ‚ąľe2/2C, is of the order
of the discharge time. The energetic uncertainty, ‚ąÜE, associated with the dot state satisÔ¨Āes
the Heisenberg uncertainty principle and should be much smaller than the charging energy
‚ąÜE ‚ąľ‚ĄŹ/ŌĄRC ‚Č™EC or G ‚Č™e2/2‚ĄŹ‚ąľ2e2/h ‚Č°GQ, where GQ is the quantum conductance.
Hence, in this so-called weak tunneling regime, electrons are conÔ¨Āned long enough to actually
feel the charging energy. If an electron does not come with enough energy to enter the dot,
transport through the quantum dot is Coulomb blockaded [1].
1.1
Thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects
The high degree of electronic control in Coulomb blockaded systems make them interesting
from not only a fundamental point of view but also from an engineering point of view. Of
particular focus in this thesis, such systems provide a highly interesting platform for studying
thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects, which receive increasing attention with the ongoing miniaturisation of
technology. One central issue in the miniaturisation of electronical devices is heat generation,
and a major Ô¨Āeld of interest is how to control the heat Ô¨āow or take advantage of the heat Ô¨āow
in such nanosystems.
In solid-state devices, heat is generally carried by phonons and electrons. In this study
we focus on electron transport, and on how to control the Ô¨āow of heat by not just controlling

A tunnel through the quantum dot, the extraction of thermally excited electrons cools the

In 1867, Maxwell thought of an experiment to illustrate a violation of the second law
of thermodynamics, that no process is possible whose sole result is cooling a colder body
and heating a hotter body [16]. The principle is illustrated in Fig. 1.2(b) showing a vessel
containing particles with diÔ¨Äerent velocities. At uniform temperature the average velocity of
an ensemble of particles is uniform, however, individual particles move with diÔ¨Äerent velocities.
A wall is placed in the vessel to split it into a left chamber and a right chamber, with the two
chambers initially being at uniform temperature. Maxwell thought of a creature, the demon,
being able to observe the particles and only allowing fast particles from the left chamber to
enter the right chamber and slow particles from the right chamber to enter the left chamber
by opening and closing a small hatch in the wall. In this way, the temperature increases in
the right chamber and decreases in the left chamber [17]. This idea of cooling a system by
applying some kind of feedback has been explored in the system in Fig. 1.2(a) [10, 11]. The
particular process is illustrated in Fig. 1.2(c). Here the upper dot and lead is interpreted as a
demon system which can perform a feedback on the lower system. If the upper dot is initially
occupied by an electron (top left Ô¨Āgure), only thermally excited electrons can overcome the
Coulomb-interaction barrier and tunnel to the lower dot, thereby decreasing the electronic
temperature in the lead from which the electron came. When an electron has entered into the
lower dot, the demon performs a feedback to trap the electron and decrease its energy (top
right Ô¨Āgure) before the electron continues into the right lead (bottom right Ô¨Āgure). In this way,
the lower system is cooled down without exchanging particles with the upper system. We will
not dive into the particular Maxwell‚Äôs demon interpretation2, but from an engineering point
of view, the fact that both leads in the lower conducting system cool down simultaneously by
exchanging energy with a Coulomb-coupled system is an interesting proof of concept.

4.5
Concluding remarks
We have derived the sequential tunneling and cotunneling transition rates and heat rates
relevant for the particular system introduced in Ch. 2. Following a standard regularization
procedure, we can evaluate cotunneling rates analytically under certain assumptions. Firstly,
the analytical result is limited to the case of uniform temperatures.
We can study some
interesting situations where this is satisÔ¨Āed, however, when studying thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects
we would like to be able to calculate cotunneling rates in the general case of non-uniform
temperatures. Secondly, to ensure well-deÔ¨Āned integrals, the allowed energy-dependence of
the bare tunneling rates is limited. In particular, when studying the electric current the con-
tribution from local cotunneling limits us to the case where the product of the bare tunneling
37
==PAGE:54==

rates corresponding to the reservoirs involved in the process is maximum of second order in
energy. However, when considering heat currents, there is an additional energy contribution
such that the product of the bare tunneling rates can be of maximum Ô¨Ārst order in energy.
Furthermore, the bare tunneling rates should be analytic, which in the case of e.g. graphene
is not valid near the Dirac points.
To Ô¨Ānd a pragmatic solution to this issue, a numerical procedure is suggested where one
keep a small but Ô¨Ānite ő∑ and subtract the contribution which would be eliminated in a rigorous
regularization procedure of the analytical integral. A comparison with analytically calculated
cotunneling rates showed that the error in this approach is small and the relative diÔ¨Äerence
between the analytical result and the numerical result considered in Fig. 4.6 is of the order
10‚ąí5‚ąí10‚ąí3 for ő∑/U = 10‚ąí4. A comparison between the numerical approach and the analytical
expression valid in the case of a small temperature diÔ¨Äerence between the two subsystems also
showed fairly good agreement.
In the case of tunneling through a single non-interacting level, we could compare the result
to the Landauer-B√ľttiker result. When including cotunneling we found a good agreement
between the master equation approach and the Landauer-B√ľttiker result.
We therefore suggest to use the numerical procedure in cases where the analytical expres-
sion does not apply. We will explicitly write when we use the analytical expression and when
we use the numerical approach.

5.3.2
Cotunneling processes and the lift of Coulomb blockade
Whereas the sequential current is blocked when the electrochemical potential of dot D is
outside the bias window, this is no longer the case when including cotunneling processes.
In a cotunneling process, an electron can virtually occupy a higher-energy state and tunnel
through the system as long as the total energy is conserved after the tunneling event.
The electric current including cotunneling processes is plotted in Fig. 5.3(a) and clearly
shows that cotunneling processes lift the blockade of the system in regions where the sequential
contribution is vanishing in agreement with Ref. [22]. This is also illustrated in Fig. 5.3(b)
where the total current and the contribution from sequential tunneling only is plotted as
function of straight line paths between points marked in Fig. 5.3(a). Along the black curve,
local cotunneling processes contribute to the background current until the level of dot D
enters the bias window along the red curve, whereafter cotunneling gives a small negative
correction to the dominant sequential contribution. Along the orange curve which runs along
the degeneracy line, ‚ąÜE01,10 = 0, between the two triple points, also nonlocal cotunneling
processes start to contribute. We quantify this discussion below.
It is important to note that Fig. 5.2 and Fig. 5.3 show the low temperature regime where
the contribution from sequential tunneling in the centre of the stability diagram is suppressed
to clearly identify the contribution from cotunneling. However, for higher temperatures se-
quential tunneling processes also contribute in the centre of the stability diagram, and this is
highly important in the study of inter-dot mediated thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects which we discuss
in the next chapter.

5.5
Concluding remarks
In this chapter, we introduced the overall thermoelectric behaviour of the system by intro-
ducing the stability diagram and studying the contribution from sequential tunneling and
cotunneling processes to the steady-state electric current and heat currents. We found that
cotunneling processes can contribute signiÔ¨Ācantly near the stability vertex in the center of the
stability diagram, where in particular local cotunneling and nonlocal cotunneling contribute.
This is an important observation, since although we did not discuss the heat current in the
center of the stability diagram in Fig. 5.6(a), it is quite clear that the system shows a non-
trivial behaviour in this region. As we will see next, this is the region of interest when studying
inter-dot thermoelectric phenomena. Hence, we might expect that cotunneling processes could
contribute to these phenomena.

6.3.5
Concluding remarks
We conclude that cotunneling gives a signiÔ¨Ācant decrease in the cooling power compared to
the sequentiel tunneling result in the region of interest of large cooling power. Hence, the
contribution from cotunneling is indeed relevant to make improved quantitative estimates of
the cooling power in this system.
The increased contribution from cotunneling processes, for suÔ¨Éciently large bare tunneling
rates, quenches the cooling eÔ¨Äect in the case of constant bare tunneling rates and uniform
temperature in the system. In this case, for large bare tunneling rates, the behaviour can
be qualitatively diÔ¨Äerent than expected from an analysis with sequential tunneling only. E.g.
when increasing the tunneling rate in subsystem 2, from a sequential analysis one anticipates
an increased cooling power, however, for large tunneling rates there is a trade-oÔ¨Äfrom a
decrease in cooling power from cotunneling.
With asymmetric couplings to the leads, the cooling power can in principle be increased
further. However, the analysis of a graphene-based system showed that in practice it might
be diÔ¨Écult to achieve suÔ¨Écient asymmetric couplings to the leads, to Ô¨Ānd a signiÔ¨Ācant en-
hancement of the cooling eÔ¨Äect, without some other Ô¨Āltering mechanism between the leads
and the dot.
We emphasize that when the bare tunneling rates become suÔ¨Éciently large, level-broadening
and the contribution from even higher-order processes start to contribute, and one should be
careful with the perturbation approach. We do not consider the behaviour beyond ‚ĄŹő≥ > kBT.

Conclusion and outlook
In this thesis, we have studied thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects in a system of two capacitively coupled
single-level quantum dots in a three-terminal conÔ¨Āguration, where the Coulomb inter-dot
coupling mediates a heat exchange between the (possibly) biased quantum dot system (sub-
system 1) and the unbiased quantum dot system (subsystem 2) without particles tunneling
between the two subsystems.
Similar systems have caught increasing attention in recent
literature [10, 11, 12, 13, 14, 15, 38].
In particular, we have presented a study of the contribution from higher-order cotunneling
processes. To this end, we applied a master equation approach with transition rates and heat
rates derived using the T-matrix formalism up to second order in the tunneling Hamiltonian.
To evaluate the cotunneling rates, we applied a regularization procedure and discussed two
approaches to calculate the rates. In the case of uniform temperature and with restricting
assumptions about the energy-dependence of the coupling to the leads, an analytical expres-
sion can be found. To generalize to the case of non-uniform temperatures and more general
couplings to the leads, which is necessary in thermoelectric studies, we suggested a numerical
approach which agreed with the analytical result in the case of uniform temperature and with
the Landauer-B√ľttiker result in the case of tunneling through a single non-interacting level
between leads with diÔ¨Äerent temperatures.
In Ch. 5, we presented the overall thermoelectric behaviour of the system. In particular,
we introduced the stability diagram and discussed the steady-state electric current and heat
currents in relation to the stability diagram. We identiÔ¨Āed that both local cotunneling and
nonlocal cotunneling processes can contribute signiÔ¨Ācantly near the stability vertex in the cen-
ter of the stability diagram. Since this is the region where inter-dot mediated thermoelectric
eÔ¨Äects occur, the contribution from cotunneling can be relevant in such studies.
This was explored further in Ch. 6, where we focused on the heat exchange between the
two subsystems mediated by the inter-dot coupling. We synthesized on ideas from literature
[12, 38] and characterised the heat exchange in terms of a thermal factor, which sends heat
from the hot subsystem to the cold subsystem, and a demon factor, which can cool down the
biased system; an eÔ¨Äect which can be increased with asymmetric couplings to the leads (bare
tunneling rates).
We found that in relevance to energy harvesting where a temperature diÔ¨Äerence between
the two subsystems is used to generate a current in subsystem 1, due to energy-dependent bare
tunneling rates, cotunneling can in the low temperature regime contribute to the generated
electric current.
We focused, particularly, on the Maxwell‚Äôs demon cooling eÔ¨Äect discussed in [10, 14], and
found that cotunneling processes give a signiÔ¨Ācant quantitative decrease in the cooling power
in the biased system compared to the sequentiel tunneling result in the region of interest of

large bare tunneling rates, quenches the cooling eÔ¨Äect in the case of constant bare tunneling
rates and uniform temperature in the system. In this case, for large bare tunneling rates,
the behaviour can be qualitatively diÔ¨Äerent than expected from an analysis with sequential
tunneling only. E.g. when increasing the tunneling rate in subsystem 2, from a sequential
analysis, one anticipates an increased cooling power. However, there is a trade-oÔ¨Äfrom a

However, the asymmetry of the lead couplings was too small to contribute

As an outlook, we considered an idealized conÔ¨Āguration of the system where the electric
current shows diode characteristics, which cools down when a current Ô¨āows in the system by
exchanging heat to the passive system.
Outlook
Following this study, the next points will be of special interest in future work.
Comparison to experiments
A natural next step of the cotunneling analysis would be to compare to experiments. Hope-
fully, the procedure in Ch.
4 could be used to make improved quantitative estimates of
experimental results of thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects in capacitively coupled quantum dots, and in
particular, experimental studies of systems which utilize the energy-dependence of the cou-
pling to the leads. At the current stage, Maxwell‚Äôs demon cooling eÔ¨Äect has been studied
experimentally in a system with metallic dots in Ref. [10]. We chose to study a system of
two single-level Coulomb-interacting quantum dots to discuss some generic aspects of inter-
dot thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects. However, it would be interesting to apply the methodology and
compare to similar experiments as in Ref. [10].
Evaluation of cotunneling rates
One of the challenges in the project was to Ô¨Ānd a way to evaluate regularized cotunneling
rates in the general case of non-uniform temperatures in the leads and with general energy-
dependence of the couplings to the leads. We suggested a numerical approach, but ideally we
would like to Ô¨Ānd an analytical solution valid in this more general case.
Alternative systems
As an outlook in Ch. 6, we discussed a particular conÔ¨Āguration where the electric current could
show a diode-like behaviour which under forward-bias delivered heat to the passive subsys-
tem. Although an idealized situation, in future work it would be very interesting to continue
in these Ô¨Āelds, and consider alternative systems which could show interesting thermoelectric
properties.

Project plan
The aim of this thesis is to study thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects in capacitively coupled quantum dots.
The initial project plan is outlined as follows
‚ÄĘ Study recently published literature on thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects in capacitively coupled
quantum dots in the sequential tunneling regime
‚ÄĘ Synthesize on literature and models to set up a generic model allowing a study of these
thermoelectric eÔ¨Äects in the sequential tunneling regime
‚ÄĘ Derive transition rates in the cotunneling regime and evaluate these using complex
analysis, and study the physical contribution from cotunneling
‚ÄĘ Depending on the time available, consider further studies of the system
25 July 2016: The project has been carried out in accordance with the initial project plan.

to be more challenging than anticipated (that is of course part of doing research). Therefore,
much time has been used on trying diÔ¨Äerent approaches to calculate these integrals. However,
we found an approach with time left to study aspects on how cotunneling contributes to
thermoelectrics in capacitively coupled quantum dots.
