Abstract 
Digitalisation is one of the major trends changing society, business and production as the integration 
of advanced digital technologies change the internal processes and structure of the organisation 
and its environment. This study aims to develop a recommendation for companies wanting to 
initiate a Digital Transformation. To investigate this phenomenon, a deductive case study has been 
conducted in collaboration with two large manufacturing companies with the aim of understanding 
the connections between Organisational Design, Digital Maturity and Change Management. Semi-
structured interviews were conducted with 17 participants and analysed using a theoretical coding 
framework.The analysis discovered the importance of planning and coordinating the Digital 
Transformation on all Managerial Levels and account for the connection between this 
transformation and the Organisational Design. As a result, this study recommends a coordinated 
portfolio strategy meant to align the transformation with the Digital Vision as well as Organisational 
components. This recommendation will support the initiation of Digital Transformation in 
manufacturing companies based on the connections between the three theoretical areas while 
accounting for the common obstacles identified throughout this study

Introduction 
“Going digital is quickly becoming common practice—or at least a common 
mandate—at many companies. Most executives have high expectations for 
the business benefits of digital initiatives, […] the results suggest that 
digitalisation promises seem more of a hope than a reality.” 
 – McKinsey, 2017 
 
Digitalisation has been identified as one of the major trends creating changes for society, business 
and companies due to the adoption of digital technologies in the organisation or in the operational 
environment (Parviainen et al., 2017). The digital trend was ignited in the 1950s, and gradual 
advancements happened through the next decades, until the 1990’ies when the World Wide Web 
digitalised and radically changed the approach for how we could work, shop, bank, travel, educate, 
govern, manage our health and enjoy life (Press, 2015). The last twenty years has resulted in a digital 
race where all organisations must be digitally tuned to stay relevant for their customers and the 
need for a digital transformation is more evident now than ever.  
A major aspect of digital transformation is known as Industry 4.0, which changes the world of 
manufacturing and the economic environment (Rajnai et al., 2018). The term industry 4.0 was 
coined in 2011 to describe the advantages given by integration and communication of advanced 
technologies in industrial manufacturing and its supporting processes (Acatech, 2017). Furthermore, 
these advantages are reinforced when industry 4.0 is integrated with the rest of company's 
functional areas such as service, logistics, planning etc. The digital transformation of a company’s 
functional areas and their interaction with each other can be described using digital maturity 
models. However, despite the seemingly numerous advantages provided by this transformation and 
the rough implementation plan provided by maturity models many companies struggle when trying 
to advance their digital maturity as well as implement and utilize the advantages provided by 
Industry 4.0 (McKinsey, 2017).  
In general, the term “maturity” refers to a “state of being complete, perfect, or ready” and implies 
some progress in the development of a system. Accordingly, maturing systems increase their 
capabilities over time regarding the achievement of some desirable future state (Schumacher et al., 
2016). The development of said systems happens through the digital transformation projects, with 
distinct motives and focus on specific outcomes.  
==PAGE:13==
 
 
2 
 
As organisations adopt new technologies into the strategic framework, there are generally two 
motives behind; to automate or to informate. When organisations want to automate, they deploy 
information technologies for cost effectiveness and efficiency. According to a McKinsey research 
from 2017, 40 percent of all Danish working hours could be automated with current technologies, 
and some occupations can even be completely replaced by machines (McKinsey, 2017). To informate 
refers to the motives of promoting innovation and growth through new technologies and data 
(Zuboff, 1985). To simplify the concepts one can say that automating tends to replace human work 
with algorithms and robots, while informating strives to enhance the employees’ competencies 
through technology, effective data usage and learning (Ala-Laurinaho et al., 2019).  
The exploitation and integration of digital technologies often affect large parts of companies and 
even go beyond their borders, by impacting products, business processes, etc. (Matt et al., 2015). 
The impacted components mentioned by Matt et al. are parts of an organisation’s design. Therefore, 
digital transformation projects can result in drastic changes to the core aspects of an organisation. 
Accordingly, aside from current established aspects of change management, managers must be 
aware of the impact and changes created by digital transformation projects. 
This study aims to develop a recommendation for managers responsible for initiating digital 
transformations projects. Figure 1 presents where this study fits in. The theoretical areas 
Organisational Design and Change Management are well established through extensive research. 
However, the connection between the latter and new Digital Maturity theory during digital 
transformations is not yet fully established.

“How can large manufacturing companies initiate, structure and plan the development of their

•

• 
“What are the characteristics of a framework meant to initiate, structure and plan the

o “What are the characteristics of an Organisational Design meant to support this 
framework?”

Literature review 
Organisational Design 
The first theoretical topic reviewed in this study is the area concerning Organisational Design. For 
any type of project where new technologies are introduced, and old routines are changed, different 
internal aspects of the company will be affected. Therefore, a systems approach is necessary to 
understand how technological changes from digitalisation projects are connected to internal 
organisational components. 
The Star Model  
To assess the case companies’ internal design aspects in this study, the Star Model framework will 
be used. The model was developed by Jay R. Galbraith, who has long been considered the father of 
Organisational Design. He founded Galbraith Management Consultants in 1978 to help companies 
manage the conflicting demands of matrix organizations, as well as solve strategic and organisational 
design challenges across corporate, business unit, and international levels. (“Jay Galbraith - Galbraith 
Management Consultants | International Organization Design,” 03.02.2020) 
The Star Model framework for Organisational Design is the foundation on which a company bases its 
design choices (Galbraith, 2011). This framework will be utilised to highlight the internal aspects of 
the case companies and how these might impact or be affected by the implementation of new 
technologies. The Star Model consists of five major components in which managers can control 
policies that will affect the behaviour of employees; Strategy, Structure, Processes, Rewards and 
people. When all major components are in alignment, the organisation is at its most effective 
(Galbraith et al., 2001). Galbraith and Amy Kates have extended the Star Model to include a sixth 
component Capabilities (Kates & Galbraith, 2007). Changes in any of the six components will affect 
the effectiveness of the others. According to Galbraith, this especially concerns the strategy which 
needs to be fully supported by the other five components and vice versa.

Strategy 
The strategy creates the foundation of any Organisational Design. Strategy can be perceived as a 
cluster of managerial decisions and actions to help an organisation achieve a set of defined goals. It 
also covers the vision, mission, goals and values to be pursued by the company (Galbraith, 2011). 
Vision refers to a long-term ideal situation or goal for the company. The mission declares what the 
organisation seeks to achieve from its activities in the short term through tangible goals. Depending 
on how the organisation defines its strategy, the policies and focus of the other aspects in the 
Organisational Design will, according to Galbraith, be affected. A change in strategy might involve 
incorporating new technologies into the organisational environment for competitive advantages.  
People 
The people component concerns all human-resource aspects of the organisation. This area covers 
the acquisition of talent, training of employees and rotation of work. It also covers the practice of 
knowledge sharing among employees, which focuses on feedback on performance to support the 
learning and development of competencies (Galbraith et al., 2001). According to Donald Hislop, this 
learning and development can be characterized into three types; Via formal training, via the use of 
interventions in work processes and via day-to-day work, and reflection on these (Hislop, 2013). The 
competencies of the employees are an essential parameter to ensure alignment with the other 
==PAGE:18==
 
 
7 
 
design components. If new IT technology is being implemented, the employees might need training 
to acquire additional or new competencies to continue their work. A balanced Organisational Design 
can translate individual competencies into organisational capabilities through processes, strategies 
and structures. An aligned design ensures that these three components integrate the competencies 
efficiently. 
Rewards 
The reward system within an organisation has the purpose of motivating employees to achieve both 
personal and organisational goals. Managers can control the reward system with policies concerning 
bonuses, salaries, promotions etc. These rewards can help managers enhance the performance of 
the employees and hence strengthen internal processes (Jones et al., 2015). Rewards can be based 
on individual performance and team-based performances and can be delivered both in the form of 
monetary values and intrinsically motivating recognitions. Intrinsic motivation can be a result of 
informating project benefits, where employees develop their competencies and find their work 
exciting and challenging. Furthermore, as described in the ability “Creating purpose” by Pernille 
Kræmmergaard, the incentive of employees can be enhanced by mastering the aspects of this 
ability.  
According to Greg Kesler, there are two predictable impacts of not aligning the reward system with 
changes in structure and processes: 
• 
Individual performance targets compete with the goals of the strategy. 
• 
Roles and accountabilities are confusing or continue to be aligned around the old 
organisational design. (Kesler et al., 2012) 
Therefore, it is important to monitor and evaluate the reward system to make sure that employees 
remain satisfied and productive.  
Processes 
The processes can be defined as either vertical or lateral (horizontal). Vertical processes refer to the 
decisions made regarding budgeting and resource allocation. Information and needs from the 
different departments are used as input when these processes are carried out. Having too many or 
too slow vertical processes can be defined as bureaucracy within the organisation. The lateral 
processes refer to the daily workflow within the departments. These processes can be related to 
customer orders, product development, production operations and others. How the vertical and 
lateral processes are carried out heavily depends on how the organisation is structured and which 
competencies the employees possess. The internal processes can be changed when new technology 
==PAGE:19==
 
 
8 
 
is adopted by the organisation. As will be described in the Digital Maturity model section, different 
levels of Digital Maturity require specific changes to the lateral and vertical processes, and in some 
cases, the development of new processes. As a result, the Digital Maturity of an organisation should 
be dependent on the design of the processes component and vice versa.  
Structure 
The structure of the organisation refers to how authority and power are placed internally. This 
component covers how departments are organised and how the span of control is defined within 
these departments. Departments can be defined based on different dimensions. The most common 
ones are products, functions, processes, markets and customers. The structure also covers the 
specialisations included in each department. When new strategies are implemented, the structure of 
the organisation is essential to enable decision-makers access to necessary resources and 
competencies. According to Jones et al., the structure of the organisation defines how cross-
department collaboration is conducted. In this study, two different approaches were identified and 
are described below. 
Cross-department collaboration 
Traditional: 
As described in the previous section, different dimensions can be used to divide departments in an 
organisation, but these departments should also be able to collaborate efficiently. A key factor for 
having a smooth cross-department collaboration is the hierarchy of authority (Jones et al., 2015), 
defining how an organisation’s chain of command is designed. Traditional organisations are designed 
to have a certain number of managerial levels, to define the organisational distance from the top 
CEO to the blue-collar worker. Altogether, this defines whether the organisation has a tall or flat 
decision structure, which can affect the ease and effectiveness of cross-department collaboration. If 
the design of the organisational hierarchy is tall, top management can counter-balance this by 
decentralising authority (Jones et al., 2015). Providing a decentralised authority should ensure 
lower-level managers and employees can make relevant decisions for how to utilise resources. It 
should be noted that too much decentralisation can create challenges with employees working 
towards their own goals instead of aligning with the organisational strategy (Jones et al., 2015). 
Agile:  
An agile organisation is defined by how it is structured to facilitate flexibility and speed when 
reacting to changes in its environment (Jones et al., 2015). The employees of an agile organisation 
typically have more individual responsibility and fewer constraints on what projects to pursue and 
how to pursue them (Acatech, 2017). The cross-department teamwork is dynamic as employees will 
==PAGE:20==
 
 
9 
 
regularly have to change tasks and switch between working with different teams to do so. They will 
still be assigned to a specific department but will be completing their tasks as part of a temporary 
goal-oriented team. The capabilities needed to create an agile organisation is described by both 
Acatech and Pernille Kræmmergaard and involve capabilities such as Flexible Communities, Decision 
Rights Management and Agile Management. This will be elaborated in the Digital Maturity section.  
Capabilities 
Strategic capabilities are defined as “the capabilities of an organisation that contributes to its long-
term survival or competitive edge” (Johnson et al., 2015). This statement indicates that having 
control of your organisational capabilities is crucial for ensuring long-term survival in the market. 
Therefore, organisational capabilities are a necessity to consider when adapting your organisational 
design to a new strategy or digital transformation. According to Johnson et al., strategic capabilities 
are comprised of two components: Resources and Competencies. Resources are assets that the 
organisation can utilise, while competencies refer to how the assets are used. Capabilities should be 
considered dynamic, as the organisation will have to develop and fulfil future needs for capabilities 
when the organisational environment changes (Johnson et al., 2015). Therefore, an assessment and 
awareness of capabilities are needed to ensure that the organisation is well equipped for future 
changes. Lastly, it is important to understand that the planning of any organisational changes is 
affected by the availability of resources and competencies. 
Culture and human behaviour 
According to Jay Galbraith, the outcome of Organisational Design is culture and human behaviour. 
As a result, all major components of the Star model will impact the culture and behaviour within an 
organisation. If changes are made to any of the components, managers should consider the impacts 
on human behaviour and assess whether the current culture is prepared for said change. To do this, 
the term organisational culture must be defined.  
Huczynski and Buchanan define organisational culture as: “The collection of relatively uniform and 
enduring values, beliefs, customs, traditions and practices that are shared by an organisation’s 
members” (Hislop, 2013). According to Jones et al., the different organisational values can be 
maintained and transmitted throughout the organisation by the managers. If management can 
adopt these values and communicate them to the employees, they can help nurture and shape the 
desired behaviour. This is easier for start-ups, as the manager is most likely also the founder. For 
larger companies changing organisational culture is a much tougher task, as the culture is based on 
years of experience, ways of working and values adopted across many employees. A term for this 
kind of cultural challenge is called Path Dependency (Hayes, 2014). This term refers to employees 
==PAGE:21==
 
 
10 
 
and managers getting locked into a behavioural pattern. If a certain way of behaving has been 
proven successful in the past, this pattern is rarely questioned, and it can be difficult to motivate 
employees to change these traditions and practices. 
These behavioural patterns can hinder the willingness to change of employees (Acatech). This 
principle from Acatech can indicate how much work managers must put in to motivate employees 
during a change initiative, which is mainly done through communication and knowledge sharing 
(Hodges, 2016). Organisational culture and behaviour can have an impact on how knowledge sharing 
is shaped within an organisation. There are in general five characteristics of organisational culture 
that affects communication and knowledge sharing; knowledge sharing is regarded as a norm, 
employees have a strong sense of collective identity, employees have a high level of trust of each 
other, organisational processes are regarded as fair and that employees trust management (Hislop, 
2013). Therefore, culture and behaviour can be a key driver or obstacle when implementing change.

Digital Maturity 
Industry 4.0 
Technological advancement is changing the organisational landscape. A desk study conducted by the 
European Union's regional development fund has analysed the opportunities for industry 4.0 in the 
North Sea area. In the EU study, Industry 4.0 has been described as a three-part concept:  
“First, Industry 4.0 is also about new business models, new organisational structures, and new ways 
of collaborating. Second, Industry 4.0 is about innovations and technological (IT) possibilities. Third, it 
is also related to new skills, further training of the existing workforce, and new courses/educations.” 
(EU INTERREG, 2018).  
According to the EU research, Industry 4.0 will transform traditional production relationships 
between suppliers, producers and customers as well as between workers and machines (EU 
INTERREG, 2018). 
Considering the impact on the Danish market, research states that automation will have a significant 
effect, creating both challenges and opportunities. It is still important to bear in mind that there are 
differences in the automation potential within the Danish market (McKinsey, 2017). Areas like 
manufacturing have a potential for automation, as there are many manual routine activities where 
several of the technological trends from Industry 4.0 can be relevant. Lastly, the McKinsey research 
concludes that even though there is a high potential for automating jobs through technical solutions, 
this is not done easily. Different areas should be assessed to determine the level of implementation 
and its pace compared to where the organisation is currently (McKinsey, 2017). This assessment 
could be conducted by using Digital Maturity models. 
Digital Maturity models  
An organisation with the desire to improve their digital capabilities and infrastructure would benefit 
from defining their Digital Maturity level. This can be done to understand how organisational 
strategy affects the current maturity, both related to obstacles in the present state and possible 
internal strengths supporting a desired future. It is also crucial when planning the development of 
Digital Maturity to have a structured approach as to how the changes will affect departments, 
processes, culture, capabilities etc. This study will utilise two different Digital Maturity models; 
Pernille Kræmmergaard’s Digital Maturity Model and Acatech’s Industrie 4.0 Index. The two chosen 
Digital Maturity models complement each other well. Kræmmergaard’s maturity model has a more 
strategic and longterm view of how to develop a company's Digital Maturity but lacks a more 
practical and thorough description of how to achieve a specific maturity level. The Acatech study 
==PAGE:23==
 
 
12 
 
focuses mainly on how digitalisation and information systems can help modernise factories, but it 
also describes how this digitalisation affects, and should be supported by, a parallel change in the 
structural and functional areas of the company. However, the Acatech study focuses on the benefits 
of the factory digitisation, which is only a part of the digital maturity process. Therefore, the two 
models make up for the lack in its counterpart.  
Kræmmergaard’s Digital Maturity Model 
This Digital Maturity model is a descriptive organisational model used to outline the different 
maturity levels companies can operate within. Furthermore, it focuses on the development of 10 
abilities that, according to Kræmmergaard, are essential for any company seeking a digital 
transformation.  
The author of this model, Pernille Kræmmergaard, is the CEO and founder of the Digitalisation 
Institute of Denmark, with more than 20 years of research in organisational and IT management. She 
is an external evaluator for the Danish National Audit Office, she has the title of Digital Sage and is a 
former professor at Aalborg University. 
The outcome of the Digital Maturity model by Kræmmergaard is the ability to answer the following 
initial questions:  
• 
What effects do IT and the internet have on organisations and their employees? 
• 
How do we adapt to new ways of working affected by IT?  
• 
Do we need new capabilities and approaches to navigate and plan strategy in this future 
environment? (Kræmmergaard, 2018)

The generations are presented with regards to different areas, such as outcome, the focus for the 
company in the current state and the culture and mindset towards digitalisation. Other additional

The general concept of the Generations Model is that the organisation must incrementally go 
through each step to evolve into the next level of maturity. Therefore, all companies start at 
Generation 1, generating basic IT competencies and from there develop into a more advanced 
integration with IT. Overall, the scope for initiating a digital transformation will influence which 
generation level the organisation can aim for. If the digital transformation projects have a focus on 
automation, the organisation should be aiming for the first generations. Is the ambition to reach the 
higher generations (3 & 4), then the focus should be a combination of automating and informating 
purposes when scoping the projects (Ala-Laurinaho et al., 2019). 
Generations 
Generation 1 
As described in figure 4, Generation 1 is the initial stage when digitalising an organisation. The focus 
is on the support of existing business operations with IT solutions. IT is implemented to replace 
manual handling and is considered a success if the solutions function without any breakdowns and 
perform stably. At this generation level, it is common that organisations have no joint IT system 
throughout the entire company, but these are instead bought/developed by individual departments 
only suited for their specific needs. 
Generation 2 
An organisation in the second generation has moved beyond the goal of automating manual 
processes and now has the goal of integrating systems and internal processes for standardisation 
and optimisation of work processes. It is important to have systems that can communicate across 
many departments in the organisation in this generation. Therefore, general IT systems are needed 
to replace some of the more individual niche solutions implemented in Generation 1. This can 
potentially result in more redundant or time demanding ways of working compared to the earlier 
generation, but the standardisation of internal processes should intentionally lead to better 
coordination and reduction of costs.  
Generation 3 
The process of evolving into the third generation is, according to Kræmmergaard, the most 
demanding change in the Digital Maturity theory. In Generation 3, the understanding of technology 
and its use has been expanded. There is now a profound focus on digitalising work processes and the 
development of new services and products. The changes for evolving the company into Generation 3 
==PAGE:26==
 
 
15 
 
demands a cultural change and identification and acquirement of new competencies. New internal 
cooperation structures have to be installed to ensure cross-functional collaboration between 
departments in the organisation. Importantly the old mindset of daily operations is no longer 
sufficient, and IT is no longer a separate department providing solutions for the company. Instead, IT 
must be a part of the management mostly on a tactical level, formulating decentral strategies 
(kræmmergaard, 2018).  
Generation 4 
According to Kræmmergaard, Generation 4 is a stage not yet seen in the industry. The fourth 
generation is a pin-pointer of how she considers tendencies within the industry and how these will 
evolve. Compared to Generation 3, the fourth generation not only focuses on experimenting and 
improving existing products and services. A company placed in the fourth generation is searching for 
new business models, exploiting the advantages of new digital infrastructure to fulfil the changing 
needs of the customer. The internal changes needed for reaching this maturity level are extensive. 
The company is expected to adapt to new Organisational Designs where external partners are 
closely integrated into a beneficial relationship/ecosystem for utilising the data streams for insight.  
Abilities  
As described earlier, Pernille Kræmmergaard has through her experience and research identified 10 
abilities instrumental for any organisation seeking to implement a digital transformation. For this 
study, some abilities will be more relevant than others, but as Kræmmergaard describes, they are all 
important when pursuing a major organisational change within the digital area. 
1. Digital foresight  
Digital foresight is an ability mostly needed at a strategic level to assess how the competitive 
landscape is developing and how the organisation is positioned. To have the right competencies in 
this area, there should be a high focus on rethinking the current business model, defining how the 
company will be relevant for customers and partners in the future. 
2. Focus on the end customer 
Pernille Kræmmergaard describes that the changes created by digital transformations are highly 
impactful. The ability of understanding and utilising digital solutions to fulfil customer needs is an 
important factor for succeeding in a digital transformation. 
3. Ecosystem Minded 
Digitalisation has the potential to create opportunities that a traditional hierarchical organisation will 
have difficulties to utilise efficiently. According to Kræmmergaard, it will be beneficial for 
==PAGE:27==
 
 
16 
 
organisations to enter a partnership as an ecosystem. A beneficial partnership should challenge the 
concept of the traditional value chains, by cooperating with organisations from other sectors, 
customers and, potentially, competitors. To have the ability to work “Ecosystem Minded”, the 
company must understand where it should be positioned within these partnerships, as well as 
considering the technological and organisational relations with other companies. 
4. Ambidexterity 
To be ambidextral in a transforming organisation, it is crucial to have a dual transformative mindset. 
This is the ability to utilise and improve the current business operations, alongside a focus on 
creating change. This ability is instrumental for succeeding in a digital transformation. The 
management must be aligned in order to make the appropriate allocation of resources so that the 
development of new solutions do not compromise the daily operations. For succeeding within 
ambidexterity, both lateral and vertical processes are important to consider. The organisation must 
align its vertical processes to ensure sufficient resources are allocated to specific initiatives. The 
lateral processes are likewise important when new transformative initiatives have been selected.

The explorative management paradigm has a different approach. This paradigm is based on 
Generation 3 & 4 of the Generations Model. Digital technology should not only optimise and 
improve but create a radical change where new innovative solutions are the focus. 
5. Agile 
The next ability described by Kræmmergaard is the need to be agile within the organisation. Agile is 
not only a method for innovation and development; it is also an organisational mindset. In a digital 
transformation, it is important for the organisation to adapt quickly and be proactive to changes. 
There should be a focus on establishing agile development teams. These teams need the liberty to 
work independently to make relevant decisions without a slow chain of command. As the 
organisational demands in Generation 3 and 4 increases, a greater need for radical change and 
development will require a mastery of the agile ability. A general pre-requisite for this is the need for 
understanding the cross-department collaboration, and more specifically, Flexible Communities. 
These communities can be established to excel as an agile organisation. Flexible Communities will be 
described further in the Acatech section. 
6. Capability awareness 
After defining the abilities presented above, it is important to analyse which capabilities are needed 
for the managers and employees to fulfil these new organisational demands. For this analysis, 
Capability awareness is required. An organisation striving for a digital transformation should 
understand the need for attracting, retaining and developing the needed capabilities. There should 
be an understanding of securing the abilities in the company to excel both internally and with 
partners. Lastly, a close collaboration between HR and the IT-department is essential to ensure the 
right resources are allocated for capability development, knowledge sharing and learning.  
7. Creating Purpose 
The ability to create purpose for the employees is not only a matter of improving the work 
processes, but several factors need to be taken into account. The reason for initiating a digital 
transformation is an important factor to consider, as the focus for initiating the digital 
transformation projects should support how the organisation defines the change strategy and vision. 
The conformity between mission, vision, strategy and physical settings are essential for creating a 
purposeful work environment. Aligning the digital, social and physical elements of the infrastructure 
are furthermore important for having goals, culture and incitement in balance. This creates 
expectations for the manager, who proactively should ensure these elements are utilised fully by the 
==PAGE:29==
 
 
18 
 
employees. This ability should furthermore enhance the reward structure of the Organisational 
Design. 
8. IT-mindset 
The IT-mindset is focused on how IT should support and realise the organisation’s strategies. This 
ability is connected with Generation 3 and 4 in the Generations Model, as it is important to 
understand that IT is no longer only an effectiveness tool. For the more digital matured 
organisations, IT should now be recognised as an integrated part of top management's strategic 
planning. The management of IT should be clearly defined, as independence in different 
departments could result in silo structures where strategy and infrastructure are misaligned. 
Altogether this ability is connected to the culture of the organisation, ensuring that, if managed well, 
a focus on IT-mindset should enhance the culture for further digital advancement. 
9. Data Driven 
The ability to be Data Driven is about collecting and analysing big amounts of data for creating 
valuable insights. To excel in this ability, the organisation must be aligned concerning what kind of 
analysis is needed and what data should be collected for generating these insights. Furthermore, this 
ability is important to define new processes within the organisation and possible partnerships with 
other companies.  
10. Management of Transformations 
The ability to manage a transformation process is important when communicating the future vision 
and purpose of the change. Management must be clear in their strategy and messages to ensure the 
employees are on the same track and act as expected. It has to be accepted that uncertainty is a part 
of a transformation process and management cannot plan everything in detail. A Chief Digital Officer 
(CDO) could be defined in the company to support the change. The CDO has the responsibility for 
driving innovation and leading the search for the right transformative capabilities, but it is important 
to notice that the role of the CDO is mostly a position for the transformation process. For the long-
term perspective, the responsibility should not be allocated to one employee, but divided among 
several people. This should create a sense of cohesiveness where many employees have the 
possibilities to make the necessary decisions. 
Critical Reflections 
Within the presented theory and maturity model by Pernille Kræmmergaard, different areas are 
relevant to consider. The maturity model is, according to the author, designed to describe an entire 
organisation and not only a single department in the company. It should be questioned whether this 
==PAGE:30==
 
 
19 
 
model can describe the whole organisation in practice or if it only is applicable from a theoretical 
point of view. This theory has a limited perspective on the specific infrastructural requirements 
needed for lifting an organisation to the next maturity level. This needs to be further explored in 
additional theory. 
The abilities described by Kræmmergaard has an important connection with Change Management 
theory, as the development of these abilities are most likely to succeed if the organisation is 
mastering Change Management practice effectively. The literature by Kræmmergaard does not 
describe concrete implementation approaches thoroughly, and some of the abilities are difficult to 
acquire as an individual manager/employee. These abilities are more minded as a collective 
capability for the company seeking a transformation.  
The book used as the foundation for this section regarding the maturity model by Kræmmergaard 
has not yet been cited on literature databases such as Scopus. A reason for this can be, that it is 
written in Danish and was published in 2018. This has a natural effect on the credibility of the 
theory, but several of the chapters in her book are based on a paper regarding digital leadership in 
LEGO. This paper is co-written by Kræmmergaard and has been cited several times on Scopus. 
Altogether, this take on Digital Maturity by Pernille Kræmmergaard has different useful areas 
concerning the needed abilities for evolving in Digital Maturity. The challenges for this theory lie 
within the high focus on business model development and the company's future product innovation. 
A focus on specific implementation approaches for digital transformations has not been prioritised 
and leaves a gap in the research area. Therefore, other Digital Maturity models should be explored 
for additional insight. 
Industrie 4.0 Maturity Index – Acatech 
The Acatech Industrie 4.0 Maturity Index is one of several publications by the German Academy of 
Science and Engineering, also known as Acatech. Acatech was established in 2008 with funding from 
the German government with the goal of: 
“National Academy of Science and Engineering is the voice of the technological sciences at home and 
abroad. We provide advice on strategic engineering and technology policy issues to policymakers and 
the public. We fulfil our mandate to provide independent, evidence-based advice that is in the public 
interest” (Acatech, 2019) 
The mission of the academy is to conduct studies and support project collaborations with external 
experts from academia and industry with the goal of improving their digital and technological 
capabilities. The Industrie 4.0 Maturity Index study focuses on exploring the benefits and 
==PAGE:31==
 
 
20 
 
requirements for implementing information-based technologies in companies. The study establishes 
a model that provides practical guidance to companies on how to develop an Industrie 4.0 
implementation strategy which fits the company's current capabilities as well as its strategy. 
Maturity Index 
The maturity index model is based on six stages, starting at the basic requirements for industry 4.0 
to full implementation. The stages are grouped into two phases, namely Digitalisation and Industry 
4.0. Each stage builds on the previous stage, and the requirements and benefits for each stage are 
described.  
Stage one: Computerisation  
The first step in the transformation process is computerisation since this provides the basis for the 
coming steps. This step involves the implementation of different information systems across the 
company. To begin with, these systems are used in isolation of each other. According to Acatech, 
this stage delivers benefits such as cheaper manufacturing as well as higher quality products. 
Stage two: Connectivity 
The second stage involves connecting the different information-based systems implemented in the 
previous stage. For old production equipment, sensors can be used to track products as they move 
through the production line. This step provides the company with production data which is crucial in 
the following step. 
Stage three: Visibility 
The information systems installed in the two previous stages now provide the company with a large 
amount of data which can be used to keep an up-to-date digital model of the factory. This is referred 
to as the companies’ “Digital Shadow”. This data allows the company to make decisions based on 
real-time data. However, creating this Digital Shadow can be problematic as data can be stored in 
decentralized silos providing multiple sets of information and no single source of “truth”.  
Stage four: Transparency 
The fourth stage is focused on creating the digital shadow of the company's current processes and 
areas. It is important for the company to understand why different situations occur; this can be done 
using a root cause analysis. The data captured in the two previous stages can be extremely helpful in 
this analysis. However, it can be next to impossible to analyse the huge amounts of data using 
current methods; therefore utilising automated data analysis tools is required.  
 
 
==PAGE:32==
 
 
21 
 
Stage five: Predictive capacity  
To further develop and benefit from the digital shadow created in the previous stage companies can 
develop their predictive capacity. This predictive capacity is based on the digital shadow, and the 
company can start to simulate different future scenarios. This stage depends on the quality of the 
previous stages as the accuracy of the forecasts and recommendations relies on the validity of the 
digital shadow. This information is then combined with knowledge of how different processes 
interact with each other to create a digital simulation model referred to as a “digital twin”. 
Stage Six: Adaptability 
This last stage involves using the digital twin and knowledge about dependencies between different 
actions to automate decision making. The system should be able to make decisions on what has and 
hasn’t worked in the past and what has created the greatest benefits.  
Depending on the current state of the organisation, progressing through these levels could involve 
small adaptations/changes to the organisation, or it could require a radical change to the design of 
work processes, culture, structure or multiple of these. Therefore, the goal and scale of the change 
define the type of change.  
Structural Areas 
The Acatech model breaks companies' structure into four distinct areas, namely Resources, 
Information Systems, Culture and Organisational Structure. Each of these areas are measured on two 
principles that determine how digitally mature a company is. Each principle consists of several 
capabilities that should be developed to expand the principle. The development of the structural 
areas can provide a guideline and act as triggers for change initiatives. (Acatech, 2017) 
 
 
==PAGE:33==
 
 
22 
 
Resources  
Resources are defined as the tangible resources of the company; this includes physical materials, 
human workforce, machinery and equipment. Developing this area using the Digital Maturity index 
is focused on the two principles Digital Capability and Structured Communication. Figure 6 shows the 
development of the two principles. Each ring represents a stage in the Acatech model, and the 
development of the capabilities in each principle should, in theory, advance the company through 
these stages.   
 
Figure 6: Structural Areas - Resources 
Digital Capability 
To develop the first principle, the organisation must be able to gather and analyse data and have 
employees able to implement the correct decisions based on that data. This is done through the 
gradual implementation and improvement of three capabilities. The first capability provide digital 
competencies focuses on either training or hiring employees with the competencies needed to not 
only analyse gathered data but use that data to make decisions that can have a short, medium- and 
long-term effect. The second capability automated data acquisition pertains to the gathering and 
communication of information. It is already common for many companies to have sensors installed 
on equipment as this is used to monitor the stability and technical processes. However, in addition 
to this, the sensors should be able to communicate with each other as well as monitor the quality of 
products produced. This will help when working with the “digital shadow” of the company. Lastly, 
the decentralized (pre-)processing of data involves the installation of smaller processing units on 
the sensors themselves to save time. The abilities Data-Driven and IT-mindset from Kræmmergaard 
share several similarities with the capabilities within the Digital Capability principle.  
Structural communication 
The enormous amount of data collected through the development of the previous principle requires 
that the organisation also develops a structured and efficient way of communicating the 
information. Development of the efficient communication capability helps ensure that employees 
==PAGE:34==
 
 
23 
 
do not waste time waiting and searching for information. It requires the implementation of systems 
and processes that trace communication so that the employee can quickly understand the context of 
the message received and communicate or give access to the relevant information. To avoid 
overloading users with the amount of information gathered, the organisation is required to develop 
and implement task-based interface design. Employees should not be required to constantly 
monitor and analyse all parts of the company. Therefore, it’s important to develop automated 
systems that understand when to make the employees aware of issues that could arise as well as 
communicate information using visual tools such as figures and graphs. The employees should only 
be presented with information that is relevant to their specific task. 
Information systems  
Information systems are technical systems that store the company's data. The configuration of the 
information system is critical as it needs to be accessible and well-structured so employees can use 
the data to support decision making. The development of information systems is based on two 
principles, namely Information Processing and Integration. 
 
Figure 7: Structural Areas - Information Systems 
Information processing 
The information processing principle is developed through the implementation and improvement of 
four distinct capabilities. Firstly, the automation of data analysis involves the continuous analysis of 
data where the cause and effect of different systems is determined. More mundane analysis should 
be constructed as to be automated so that the user can quickly acquire and act upon the 
information. This capability is essential as the large amounts of data can be difficult to analyse using 
current tools, as mentioned previously. Secondly, the contextualisation of data involves the use of 
visual tools such as 3D models, graphs and diagrams to help support decision making. Furthermore, 
it is important that the company develops the ability “System of engagement” which, according to 
Acatech, means that employees receive the right information at the right place at the right time. This 
overlaps with the third capability Task-specific user interfaces which simply is that the information 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
24 
 
received is formatted so that it best suits the needs of the user and the task they perform. Lastly, it is 
important to build a resilient IT-infrastructure to store all the captured information. This IT-system 
needs to reliably gather, store, transfer and process all the data as well as store backups. This is 
something that needs to be continuously monitored and improved to meet the needs of the 
company.  
Information integration 
The second principle is improved through four capabilities. The Horizontal and vertical integration 
of IT-systems throughout the organisation is the first of these. It involves the implementation or 
improvement of IT-systems throughout the company. This IT-system should be integrated within all 
company processes to ensure that there is a single source of truth in the company. There should not 
be conflicting data sources so that users have two views of the same process. The last three 
capabilities are more on the technical side of IT-systems but involve the continuous improvement of 
IT-security by following certain standard and IT-governance, which is a maintenance and data 
quality assurance tool. Lastly, a standardised data interface is used to support the first capability as 
this will ensure that all information is logged and viewed through a single source. The abilities IT-
mindset and Data-driven of the Kræmmergaard model have very similar traits to this principle. 
Organisational Structure 
Organisational Structure refers to the changes the organisational structure requires to transform the 
company into a learning and agile company. The area is measured on the two principles Organic 
Internal Organisation and Dynamic Collaboration in Value Networks. This ensures that both the 
internal and external factors of the organisation are considered.  
 
Figure 8: Structural areas - Organisational Structure

An Organic/Agile internal Organisation 
The organic/agile internal organisation is defined as employees having fewer constraints on how 
they work and who they work with. The environment is well suited for companies with a highly 
skilled workforce. Steps towards creating such an organisation involve the promotion and creation of 
flexible communities where cross-department teamwork, either as task or goal-oriented teams, is 
the norm. It is important to create systems for cross-department communication as well as identify 
employee skills and make overviews available to facilitate the creation of the teams. To further 
benefit from the agile organisation structure, it is important to find the balance in decision rights 
management, as some decisions can and should be made by employees with the necessary 
information; strategic level decisions should be left to higher management. These two capabilities 
support the development of the last two capabilities, motivational goal systems and agile 
management. With the implementation of temporary cross-department teams, it is necessary to 
have temporary project leaders for the teams. This does not remove the need for department heads, 
but it does change their role as they are less involved in employee day-to-day tasks and are more 
focused on facilitating team creation and managing competencies. Furthermore, it is important to 
establish goals that motivate and directs employees towards the company's overall goals and values. 
According to Acatech, one-dimensional goals such as increasing the number of products produced 
tend to result in local optimisation and “silo mentality”. Creating multidimensional goals and ways to 
measure success will focus employees on increasing process efficiency and continuous 
improvement. This principle shares many similarities with the abilities “Agile”, “ambidexterity” as 
well as “Capability awareness” from Kræmmergaard. 
Dynamic Collaboration in Value Networks 
The second principle refers to the external value chains of the company; this includes customers and 
suppliers of goods or expertise. The first capability is to focus on customer benefits and revolves 
around matching company strategy and competencies with the ability to provide the right customer 
benefits. This can be done using a value proposition canvas, but it is also important to consider how 
the digital transformation can provide additional benefits to the customers. This could be the 
addition of information about the product such as how to avoid breakdowns, running it efficiently 
etc. The second capability Cooperation with the network pertains to the management of companies 
that are a part of the external network. This could be transportation, suppliers of raw materials, 
outsourced production and external consultants. It is important to create a tool to manage these 
companies and create an overview of their roles and competencies. This allows the company to 
quickly adapt to situations and match problems with the correct competencies. The abilities

“Ecosystem minded” and “Focus on end customer” described by Kræmmergaard are almost identical

Culture 
The company culture and employee behaviour are key when implementing any change initiative. 
This is especially true when implementing radical and impactful changes associated with Digital 
Maturity. Simply implementing the changes, previously described, is not enough if employees do not 
trust the systems enough to use and rely on them. According to Acatech, the transformation of the 
corporate culture can be encapsulated using two key principles, namely, the Willingness to Change 
and Social Collaboration.

The Willingness to change 
According to Acatech, the willingness to change can be described as: 
“To what extent are employees willing to continuously review and adapt their own behaviour in 
response to a changing environment” – Acatech, 2017 
This is not only during the change initiative but also that employees should continuously examine 
their own and corporate environment to identify opportunities and need for change. They should 
then initiate the relevant procedures themselves. The development of this principle is based on five 
capabilities. Firstly, Recognizing the value of mistakes can be a powerful tool to identify possible 
change projects and help continuously improve processes. It is important to nurture a culture that 
focuses on understanding the causes of mistakes, rather than identifying who to blame. This allows 
the company to develop the necessary solutions to avoid that specific mistake in the future. An 
openness to innovation is also important, as the benefits of new technologies and approaches might 
not be apparent initially due to a lack of understanding or proof of concept. Therefore, a willingness 
to take chances and experiment with the different solutions provided by emerging technologies is 
crucial. The third capability data-based learning and decision-making focuses on convincing the

creating a procedure for managing and developing the competencies of the company’s employees. 
This is essential because of the rapid pace of technological development and the environment 
surrounding the company. The ability to quickly identify the needed competencies and which 
employees are best suited to acquire these competencies can be beneficial to the company. The last 
capability shaping change is somewhat related to the previous capability, as it involves shaping the 
change initiatives by assigning employees with the correct competencies to implement the changes. 
The overview given by the previous capabilities can assist in this process. Abilities by 
Kræmmergaard, such as “Creating purpose” and “Management of transformation” both share 
similarities with the principle described and could be used to further evaluate the organisation. 
Social Collaboration 
The Second principle Social Collaboration is aimed at improving the speed and quality of knowledge 
sharing in the organisation. This is done through the development of three capabilities. According to 
Acatech, the rapidly changing environments that surround companies requires companies to make 
and implement solutions faster without impairing the quality of the solutions. The capability 
democratic leadership style involves the development and adoption of a more flexible decision-
making process, as well as giving employees more leeway when making decisions. The second 
capability is the adoption of open communication throughout the company. This includes providing 
procedures that allow employees to not only get access to explicit information through data 
communication systems but also implicit knowledge. Therefore, it is important to cultivate an 
environment where the sharing of relevant knowledge is supported, and the “us and them” 
mentality is abandoned, making way for the open sharing of information. The last capability 
confidence in processes and information systems is similar to previous capabilities with the goal of 
building trust around information systems. However, this capability focuses on involving employees 
in the development of said information systems and processes, making sure that they feel confident 
in how data is collected and analysed. This will help ensure that employees use the systems and 
processes. 
The development of the cultural aspect of the Acatech model should help create a culture and 
organisation not only more motivated and willing to change but also better able to identify and act 
upon opportunities.

Change Management 
Digitalisation is a complex process that can require changes to an entire organisation and the 
internal work environment. To support these changes and the complex process, a process approach 
within the Change Management theory can be utilised.   
Managing change is an essential task in any larger business. The change must be defined, 
communicated, planned, organised, directed, implemented, monitored, controlled and measured 
(Hodges, 2016). However, the importance of change management is often underestimated, resulting 
in “quick-fixes” where the organisational requirements and implications are neglected. The 
importance of managing changes can be seen in the statistics, as up to 60 per cent of change 
programmes fail to achieve the targeted outcomes (Hayes, 2014). To assist project managers the 
process model developed by John Hayes can be used as a guideline. 
Conceptual framework for Leading and Implementing Change 
The John Hayes conceptual framework describes how leading and implementing change can be 
broken down into seven core activities, as seen in figure 11 (Hayes, 2014).

Recognising the need for change 
For companies to stay competitive, it is important that they recognize and adapt to changes that 
could affect their supply of inputs or demand of outputs (Hayes, 2014). Companies can generally be 
grouped into two different groups, the proactive and reactive. Proactive companies actively search 
for potential threats and opportunities, prepare for destabilising events and actively develop and 
implement changes that provide them with a competitive advantage. Reactive companies only 
respond when the need for change is clear and pressing.  
The earlier the need for change is recognized, the more options are available to the managers. 
According to Hayes, if managers are constantly reacting to changes, they will have less time to: 
1. Plan the change: It takes time to carefully plan the change and account for the different 
variables.  
2. Involve people in the change: Involving many people in the change can improve the 
diagnosis and quality of the change as well as create a sense of commitment and ownership. 
3. Little time to experiment: Proactive organisations will have time to not only experiment with 
different creative solutions but also to make and learn from mistakes. (Hayes, 2014) 
Recently almost all change models assumed that change was a continuous incremental change. 
However, this has been the exception, not the norm. Most changes were transformational where 
companies experienced long periods of equilibrium where the organisation is frozen in a specific 
state with no to small changes occurring. The organisation would then experience a period of 
revolution triggered by either external or internal factors (Hayes, 2014). 
Type of change 
Change can be grouped into four types, depending on whether it is proactive or reactive and 
incremental or transformational. Each type has implications for how the change is managed and the 
options available to managers. 
 
Figure 12: Type of Change (Hayes, 2014) 
==PAGE:42==
 
 
31 
 
Tuning typically occurs during periods with no immediate need to change. Tuning changes involves 
exploring better ways of supporting the strategic vision and goals of the company, improving 
internal policies, methods and procedures with the aim of reducing cost, time, etc. 
Adaptation is an incremental but reactive change in response to external demands. This could 
involve a company reacting to a competitor reducing the price of their products. Adaptation changes 
involve doing the same but more effectively to remain competitive.  
Reorientation involves a major shift in focus for the organisation. This shift is typically initiated in 
anticipation of future possibilities or threats. The goal of such a change is to ensure that the 
organisation will remain effective and competitive in the future, as well as aligning it with the future 
strategic situation. Since the need for change has been anticipated, the change process can be 
structured and carefully planned to be a gradual, continuous process. Time can be spent on 
formulating the strategy and solutions needed as well as convincing relevant stakeholders in the 
organisation of the necessity of the change.  
Re-creation is a major reactive change which transforms the organisation quickly across all its basic 
elements. Such changes are inevitably destructive towards the day-to-day procedures and can be 
disorienting. Involving and gaining commitment from employees can be difficult due to the radical 
change and timeframe.  
Each of these different types of changes has different effects on the organisation and culture as well 
as having varying scopes. Therefore, it is important to take the type of change into account when 
planning how to implement it. Recognising the need for change is not only relevant for Top 
management but also equally important for employees. Therefore, a crucial element in any change 
project is how to motivate and gather support from employees.  
Motivation to change 
Motivation may be defined as psychological forces that determine the direction of a person’s 
behaviour in an organisation, a person’s level of effort, and a person’s level of persistence in the face 
of obstacles (Jones et al., 2015). Motivation is essential for managers, as it explains why employees 
behave in certain ways; why are they providing a max amount of effort? and why are they being 
persistent towards known or unknown obstacles in their work? Understanding what motivates 
employees can help appreciate their reactions to change, whether they are positive or negative. 
(Hodges, 2016) 
According to Jones et al., there are two sources of motivation; extrinsic and Intrinsic. Extrinsic 
motivation refers to the obtainment of materials, rewards or to avoid punishment. Extrinsically 
==PAGE:43==
 
 
32 
 
motivated employees will improve performance to acquire bonuses, higher salaries, a high social 
status etc. These are consequences of their performed work and not part of the work itself. 
However, intrinsically motivated employees are motivated by the work process. They find their work 
meaningful; they can feel a sense of accomplishment, and they are interested and/or challenged by 
their work. There are three key intrinsic motivators according to Daniel Pink; Autonomy, mastery 
and purpose (Pink, 2011). 
Autonomy 
Employees who are provided with a high degree of responsibility towards their own work and job 
tasks can feel a great sense of autonomy. These employees have the freedom to make decisions on 
the methods and circumstances of their own work. Autonomy can motivate employees as they feel 
empowered and trusted by management. According to Hodges, the latter can be compromised by 
change initiatives if the employees’ autonomy level will decrease. Hence managers need to be aware 
of how the change initiative will impact the organisational processes and job tasks to keep 
employees motivated.   
Mastery 
When employees can develop professionally during their day-to-day work, they can feel a sense of 
mastery and motivation. The employees can, with hard work and practice, develop their skills and 
improve performance. If this is not the case, the motivation will decrease, and employees might look 
for other job opportunities, hence change managers need to cope for this motivation factor.  
Purpose 
Employees are motivated when their work feels meaningful and impactful. When managing changes, 
communication regarding purpose is key. Making sure employees are aware of their role in the 
change, their importance to the change, and how the change will benefit their work will provide a 
sense of purpose. 
Ownership of change 
It is important that the manager and employees feel ownership for the change. This can be achieved 
when employees are involved and empowered in the change process. An example is when 
employees and managers collectively create different alternatives for change initiatives through 
workshops and meetings. This will allow employees to voice their concerns, needs and goals 
regarding the change.  
 
 
==PAGE:44==

Diagnosing what needs to be changed 
The organisational diagnosis is the research of internal functions and processes with the goal of 
generating improvement recommendations. This phase mainly focuses on describing and explaining 
models and theories that can aid the user when diagnosing the company. This includes models such 
as Galbraith’s star model, technology assessments and different maturity models. Furthermore, the 
diagnosis phase is also used to help create an overview of possible projects and should also prioritise 
which of these projects to focus on and allocate resources accordingly. In the assessment of possible 
projects, it is essential to include an analysis of the required capabilities (Hodges, 2016).  
Building capabilities 
For an organisation to go through a smooth transition project and deliver the desired benefits, the 
right internal capabilities are required. To identify whether these are present or should be acquired a 
capability audit can be conducted. 
Employees may doubt their own competencies in terms of technical skills and adaptation. If a new 
work system is implemented, they need to have confidence in how it is used and how it will improve 
their working routines. Aside from technological and adaptational competencies, managers can also 
assess capabilities within the organisation in terms of readiness to change.  
The capability audit for needed training analysis recommended by Julie Hodges asks the following 
questions: 
1. High-level objectives (What are the critical capabilities we need to develop?) 
2. Target audience (Who and how big is the audience?) 
3. Proposed delivery method (Most appropriate delivery approach such as a workshop on the 
job) 
4. Key resource requirement (People, technology, the infrastructure required for development 
and delivery) 
5. Timing (When will training and development be needed to support implementation plans?) 
6. Budget (How much will it cost to develop and deliver?) 
These questions can be set up in a table to form a capability matrix consisting of all needed 
capabilities and training. This can create an overview for managers, making it easier to plan and 
prioritise training and development of employees.   
Planning and preparing for change 
To plan the change initiative, Hayes recommends utilising a planning framework developed by John 
Oakland. He is chairman of Oakland Consulting plc and Emeritus Professor of Business Excellence 
==PAGE:45==
 
 
34 
 
and Quality Management at Leeds University Business School. Oakland has developed a framework 
for planning a change initiative. He uses this framework in his consulting work with many different 
organisations. It is called Oakland’s Figure of Eight Framework and can be seen in figure 13. 
 
Figure 13: Oakland's Figure of Eight 
The framework consists of two focus areas; Readiness to change and Implementing change. The first 
area starts with incidents triggering a need for change; this can be incidents such as employee 
complains, shifts in customer demand, new technology etc., but also a part of the development of 
structural areas mentioned by Acatech. These triggers occur before the planning phase begins and is 
used as input for the recognition of the need to change phase. Managers will need to make sure that 
the change is led and directed in a proper way by defining roles and responsibilities and plan the 
communication during the implementation of the change. This step will be based on how the 
organisational culture is shaped and involving people with the appropriate competencies. The 
organisation will then need to nurture a readiness to change by making sure recipients understand 
the need and priorities for the change, which Oakland refers to as Planning, but since this model is 
used for planning a change, this step will be referred to as Priorities.   
The second focus area concerns the planning of change implementation. Management needs to plan 
for which processes will be affected and make sure new ones fit in with the process architecture of 
the organisation. Enough resources will need to be allocated to the change and systems should be in 
place to control the implementation. The change should drive behaviour, that can align with the new 
processes integrated into the organisation. Oakland only touches briefly upon the behavioural 
aspect, but a change in behaviour will be impacted by the motivation to change of the employees 
affected by the initiative. Therefore, this can be one of the most challenging aspects of the planning 
framework.

Unforeseen incidents are likely to occur that can affect different areas of the plan. Oakland defines 
seven symptoms of poor planning. Each one is a result of poor planning for one of the areas in the 
framework. Symptoms of poor planning for the readiness for change are presented in figure 14.  
 
Figure 14: Readiness to change symptoms (Oakland, 2013) 
If the change initiative is not triggered with a sense of urgency or employees do not understand the 
need for change, it can lead to a lack of action, and the change will be prolonged or fail completely. 
Without proper direction and leadership, the change may be urgent but will never get initiated to a 
greater extent. If the planning of the change is insufficient, wrong priorities and directions may 
result in a failed change process.  
Symptoms of poor planning regarding the implementation of change are shown in figure 15.  
 
Figure 15: Implementing the change symptoms (Oakland, 2013) 
If priorities and attention are not given to how processes will change, the project team may waste 
time on non-value adding activities. Similarly, priorities must be made regarding resource allocation, 
or the project team may be paralyzed and frustrated by the lack of progress. If the performance of 
new processes is not controlled by integrated systems, the project could be driving blind without 
performance indicators. The desired behaviour of employees should be nurtured to prevent 
resistance towards the implementation of change. As mentioned, the planning for behaviour area 
can be complex. To support this area of Oakland’s Planning Model, Knoster’s Model for Managing 
Complex Change can be utilised. This is presented in figure

The model consists of six elements required for an effective change; Vision, consensus, skills, 
incentives, resources and action plan. If any of the elements are neglected, a corresponding 
behavioural outcome will occur: 
• 
A lack of vision, which can be an expression of a desirable direction or future state, can lead

• 
If consensus is neglected, which includes collaboration and agreement on ideas, purpose,

• 
If the necessary skills and knowledge required is not identified, employees may feel a sense

• 
Employees who are not motivated and feel the incentive to change may show signs of

•

• 
Without an action plan that directs the change towards future goals, the symptom can feel 
like a treadmill where processes are done as usual without change.  
Knoster’s model can support leaders, as they often may sense what is wrong, but do not understand 
how to resolve or determine the root of the problem (Knoster, 1991). The model is simple but can be 
used as a checklist by project managers to ensure that employees aren’t demotivated or show 
resistance towards the change initiative. 
When the change has been planned for, the implementation of the change initiative can begin.  
Implementation 
The implementation involves the realisation of the planning and diagnosis into actual change. The 
implementation can be a one-off procedure in cases where the change is a small incremental 
change. However, if the change is more complicated, this phase can become iterative where small 
==PAGE:48==
 
 
37 
 
steps are taken in what is thought to be the right direction. There is then a continuous loop with the 
previous phases where feedback from the implementation is used in the planning and diagnosis. This 
can result in it being difficult to separate the different phases, as stated by John Hayes. In such a 
case monitoring and analysing feedback becomes important. 
Sustainment 
A challenge faced by many companies is making sure that once projects have been completed, the 
changes are maintained. Often changes are only maintained a short while after implementation 
before reverting back to the old work processes, structure etc. As stated by Hayes, much of the 
groundwork for sustaining a change is created in the earliest and latest phases of the project. The 
early impressions generated among employees affected by the change can be difficult to change 
once the implementation is underway. 
Communication 
Motivation and outcomes of change depend on how the change is communicated. Nothing will 
demotivate employees more quickly than the feeling of being “kept in the dark” during a change 
program, hence why knowledge sharing is essential during change initiatives (Galbraith et al., 2001).  
To increase engagement from employees, it is essential to remove fears, concerns and doubts 
through a high level of transparency in the communication. There are different means of 
communicating change, such as emails, presentations at meetings, videos etc. But according to 
Hodges, the most impactful communication is engaging in dialogue with employees. It is through 
dialogue that the manager can become aware of employees’ concerns and ideas. It can be used 
when motivating employees to change.  
Making employees motivated and recognise the need for change is a way of creating commitment to 
the change. If the change includes a change in employee attitude or a drastic change in work 
processes, creating commitment should be the aim. Another approach is instructing employees that 
they must change, creating compliance. This approach might be enough if the initiative will change 
minor aspects of the processes or systems. The latter can be dangerous as employees who are not 
committed to the change may revert to their old ways of working after the change is implemented.

Research Paradigm 
The nature of the gathered data is solely qualitative. The results of the study emerge through the

Data collection method 
When deciding which data collection method to choose, it is important to understand what kind of 
research question that has to be analysed and solved. The quantitative data collection method is 
focused on presenting frequencies and percentages of a larger representative population to 
understand the issues in question (Trost & Jeremiassen, 2010). The qualitative interview method 
should be used when the issues of investigation are concerning the understanding of human 
experiences, also known as the phenomenological approach to answering the desired research 
question (Kvale & Brinkmann, 2015). 
==PAGE:56==
 
 
45 
 
According to Trost & Jeremiassen, qualitative data is commonly perceived with scepticism from 
managers and decision-makers, as this kind of data often relies on a limited representation of the 
company/population. Quantitative data, on the other hand, has been more appreciated by 
managers, as they can be presented with a key-number or a specific percentage to act upon. In 
relation to the quantitative method, it should be mentioned that numbers and statistics only have 
value if they are based on a large and diverse population. If not, the results from a quantitative study 
could be heavily biased, and the method does not have the possibility to ask additional investigating 
questions for strengthening the data, as the qualitative method provides. 
After defining the research paradigm for this case study, a clear connection to the data collection 
method should be visible. The phenomenological paradigm has an explicit link to the qualitative data 
collection approach, where interviews have been chosen as the main tool for data collection. As this 
study is based on personal experiences and opinions from involved employees at the two case 
companies, quantitative methods such as questionnaires would not have been as suitable as 
interviews (Kvale & Brinkmann, 2015). 
Interviews 
The data collection and analysis approach was based on Steinar Kvale’s seven stages approach 
(Steinar Kvale, Svend Brinkmann). The first stage focus on defining the scope of the interviews by 
exploring why the study is being conducted, researching and generating knowledge about the topics, 
determining who to interview and lastly how to conduct the interviews. The second stage involves 
designing an interview structure or questionnaire based on the findings of the first stage. The 
questions should be targeted at acquiring the previously defined knowledge area. Furthermore, it 
can be beneficial to design multiple questionnaires depending on who you interview. The third stage 
is the facilitation and completion of the interviews themselves, which is followed by the fourth stage 
transcription. In the fifth stage, the interviews are analysed using a coding framework that can be 
formulated using several different theories or methods. The sixth and seventh stages pertain to 
validating and communicating the results of the interviews.  
For this study, interviewees have been divided into two different categories; Managers and 
Employees/Users. All interviewees have interacted with the digital transformation, providing this 
study with in-depth information on as many aspects as possible. The division into the two interview 
categories was done to ensure that data was collected from both the managerial perspective when 
planning for the digital transformation projects and from the employee's perspective of a changing 
working environment.

questions have been formulated and were used to create the framework for the interviews. There 
was also planned for time to explore issues or topics that would arise during the interviews.

Interview setting 
Most of the conducted interviews were done via Skype since many of the interviewees were located 
in other countries. The skype calls made it convenient to reach the intended people. However, there 
are some limitations to these kinds of interviews; there is a lack of physical communication, and it 
can be harder to interpret if the interviewee feels comfortable with the questions (Kvale & 
Brinkmann, 2015). The face-to-face interviews were also typically longer, as a meeting room was 
booked for an hour with each interviewee, and the physical communication made it easier to ask 
more follow-up questions.  
Each session started with a presentation of the study and the relevance of the topic to their project. 
The interviewees were informed that the interview would be recorded and transcribed 
anonymously. For the opening questions, they were asked to briefly describe their role within the 
company and the project. The purpose was to make the interviewee feel comfortable and 
understand the context and how they could contribute to the study. A comfortable interviewee is 
essential when the topic could get personal regarding their opinion and attitude towards the change 
project (Kvale & Brinkmann, 2015).  
The opening questions were part of a general interview guide, which was designed separately for 
Managers and Employees/Users.  
Interview guides 
An interview guide is a script that structures the interview process more or less strict (Kvale & 
Brinkmann, 2015). For this study, a semi-structured approach to the interview guides was chosen, to 
allow a discussion if interesting and/or unexpected topics come up during the interviews. The 
interview guides can be seen in Appendix E. Two main areas of interested was defined;  
1. Strategy & decision making leading to project initiation. 
2. Collaboration & communication during the change project.

during the process and is not a strict sequence of questions. The questions within the first main area 
will cover the strategy and purpose of the project, where the focus will lie on information regarding

Coding & analysis 
Coding approach 
The coding of the gathered qualitative data was initialised with a deductive approach, where codes 
were defined based on preliminary theoretical research (Miles et al., 2014). The process of coding 
was conducted iteratively while data was collected; hence the coding structure has been dynamic 
throughout the data collection and data processing.  
According to Miles et al., a provisional coding approach is appropriate for qualitative studies that 
build on previous research. As a result, this approach is suited for the purpose of the analysis within 
this study, where the collected data will be compared to the themes, trends and paradigms within 
the researched theory. The provisional codes derived from theory will be expanded, revised or 
modified through the analysis of the conducted interviews. The themes, obstacles, enablers etc. 
derived from the analysed data will create new codes, change relations within the theoretical codes 
and potentially define new relations between the codes. The result of the updated coding model can 
confirm claims, suggest changes or define areas for further research within the theoretical 
framework.

Reliability & Validity 
Reliability concerns whether the findings of the research can be repeated by conducting the same 
study again. This factor is not as relevant in this study, as in a phenomenological study, it is difficult 
to measure whether the results align. However, it is still relevant to consider whether similar 
observations can be made during different occasions and by different researchers.  
Validity describes whether a study accurately represents a situation. According to Collis & Hussey 
research errors, faulty research procedures, poor samples, inaccurate or misleading measurements 
can erode the validity of a study. Since the nature of this study is phenomenological, where the goal 
is to describe and understand a phenomenon accurately, the validity of the research is crucial.  
Lastly, generalisability determines whether you can apply the results of the study to other cases. It 
includes the argumentation of the extent to which the findings of the study can be applied based on 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
61 
 
the sample used in the analysis. In a phenomenological study, it is argued that it is possible to 
generalise based on very few cases if the cases in question capture the essence of the phenomenon.   
The validity, reliability and generalisability of the gathered data and findings will be examined in the 
analysis and discussion chapters.

Case background

“The first 6 months he went around in the organisation and asked the employees what they think

The pilot in Denmark helped prove the viability of the technology, and it was therefore included in

The design of Service processes has created several challenges. The high degree of autonomy within

the non-value adding processes had been a positive experience for the users, but making everyone

that they did not have the time to test the new tool properly. Meanwhile, the Service division has no 
system for capability development (6+3). If this was in place, it could be a priority in the daily work 
for employees to set aside time for testing and development of competencies for the new tool and 
processes.  
Culture 
Through the interviews with managers and users for Case 1, it became apparent that the 
Organisational Design choices have resulted in a specific work culture. The design promotes a 
culture that is heavily focused on cost-saving. This is a result of the recent shortage of full-time 
employees, and a focus on standardising the processes. However, the Service design has also 
promoted an end customer focus and a high degree of autonomy. This has led to a mixed attitude 
from users towards the new Service configurator tool, as it saves time and improves customer 
service while standardising the work processes.  
The new silo-structure design and specialised workforce promote a mentality where employees are 
reluctant to openly communicate and collaborate across departments. This could challenge the 
Service strategy of standardising across departments and countries. The Strategic decision of only 
aligning certain main systems (ERP, CAD etc.) has created a culture where multiple departments can 
compete on providing the same software solution (such as the Service configurator). The 
infrastructure is not aligned on these systems, and departments can freely choose to go their own 
way if deemed beneficial. As a result, the Service configurator needs to be marketed and sold to 
regional service departments (Final users). 
Another cultural challenge for the Service division is the regional differences. It became clear that 
the UK Service managers and users have been very positive towards the new initiative. However, the

Key findings of Organisational Design 
Through the analysis of the two different cases, it can be concluded that the main design driver is 
the strategy component. As visualised by the connections from the Organisational Designs, the 
strategy has an influence on all other design components, recognising that all synergies and 
challenges identified in the designs are created by the overall strategy. 
As described by Amy Kates and Jay Galbraith, the organisational culture and human behaviour is a 
result of the 6 design components. From this analysis, it became clear that culture is difficult to 
change. The first case showed that a change in strategy affected the structure as infrastructural 
systems and clear procedures for cross-department collaboration were not aligned. Case 2 
presented the opposite picture, as the strategy “Digital Journey” ensured a clearly aligned 
Organisational Design where the majority of components supported the strategy. This has resulted 
in a culture nurtured by the aligned components ensuring a receptiveness towards the desired 
organisational strategy. 
The implications of the Star model that culture and human behaviour can be derived from the 
overall design is clearly verified in this analysis. It can be concluded that a cultural change cannot be 
made without analysing the strategy and ensuring an overall alignment of all components.

Key findings of Change Management 
Recognise the need for change 
During the analysis of the Recognise the need for change process focus was on motivation. The two 
cases varied in how employees are motivated. However, it was clear that the cultural aspect of the 
Organisational Design and Digital Maturity influenced which types of motivation dominated.  
The motivation to perform related to the current situation is derived from the processes design in 
the Organisational Design. Processes of Case 1 can have a motivational effect by providing a high 
degree of autonomy, while processes seen in Case 2 can be demotivating due to repetitive and 
unchallenging tasks. As a result, employees in Case 2 may be more motivated or inclined to change; 
the opposite can be the situation for Case 1.  
Diagnosis 
Stakeholders from both cases provided information related to the new Diagnosis codes. It became 
clear through the analysis that the approach to the diagnosis phase is vastly different between the 
two cases. The approach is influenced by the Organisational Design and Digital Maturity principles. 
This was especially the case for the diagnosis of Capabilities, Suppliers and Pilot testing: 
• 
Capabilities - Case 1 with an ad-hoc approach and no initial capability assessment. Case 2 
with a structured capability assessment and training of employees as a part of the overall 
strategy. 
• 
Suppliers - Case 1 with a focus on the cost of collaboration, while Case 2 focuses on 
collaboration and capability development with suppliers.  
• 
Pilot testing - For Case 1 pilot testing is a result of limited resources and used to strengthen 
the business case. Case 2 has pilot testing as an integrated part of the overall strategy; 
develop capabilities and define standards through three pilot tests.   
Both cases emphasise the importance of diagnosis and development of Business cases. The creation 
and validation of Business cases will be an even greater priority for Case 1 when the new silo 
structure is implemented.  
Planning 
Through the analysis of the planning for each case, it became apparent that the strategy of each 
company had resulted in vastly different approaches to planning. The project in Case 1 started out 
with a wide scope which aimed at implementing and standardising a tool across multiple divisions at 
once. This happened due to the One-Strategy adopted during the project initiation period. However, 
once this strategy was changed, resources were redirected and accessing different divisions became 
==PAGE:148==
 
 
137 
 
increasingly difficult, leading to frustration among project members. In Case 2, the strategy is to test 
technologies in smaller environments using pilot tests before upscaling the technology and making it 
a standard solution. This allowed the project managers to determine benefits, missing competencies 
and needed resources more accurately before launching a full-scale implementation. 
Communication 
Through the analysis of the communication within each case project, it was clear that commitment 
from relevant management had been secured by the project managers; UK Service Management in 
Case 1 and Top management in Case 2. This is likely a result of the focus on development of business 
cases and that both projects include pilot testing. However, the knowledge sharing practice varies 
greatly between the two cases, as a result of different approaches to communication: 
• 
Case 1 – No clear strategy for collaboration and creation of purpose. As a result, the 
approach to communication is shaped by the organisational culture with a silo mentality. 
The process indicates an ad-hoc approach to knowledge sharing.  
• 
Case 2 – A clearly defined bottom-up strategy and promotion of Willingness to change has 
shaped the approach to communication for commitment.  
In conclusion, the strategy and cultural aspects of the Organisational Design and Digital Maturity 
influences the overall approach towards Change Management. The two cases are clear examples of 
how different a change initiative can be approached based on strategic design choices, cultural 
principles and human behaviour.

Limitations of this study 
Case Selection 
As described in the methodology, this study was affected by the long and time-consuming process of 
finding case companies relevant to the subject of interest. The research approach had to be 
modified multiple times to accommodate the changing number of cases. This became necessary as 
companies claimed their interest in participating in the study, but ended up not responding when we 
tried to arrange the interview dates. All of this resulted in a lot of wasted time in the initial stages of 
the study, and a need for re-defining the involvement of the case companies already interviewed. 
Additional interviews were performed with both companies, and specific cases were chosen as the 
practical foundation for the analysis. As it was not planned from the beginning to have specific 
project cases from these companies, a longer period of time was needed to identify a relevant case 
at company 2. This issue resulted in a compressed timeline for interviews at Case 2 and limited 
possibilities for validation of our findings at a second round of interviews. 
Lastly, this study was conducted throughout the fall of 2019 and the winter of 2020. This had a 
negative effect on our data gathering process, as the Christmas holiday season and annual end-of-
year reporting resulted in busy schedules for relevant interviewees. As other companies were 
supposed to have been interviewed at this stage of the project, both case company 1 and 2 were 
rarely available in the last months of 2019, and the final data gathering had to be delayed into the 
beginning of the new year. 
Different factors were identified as possible limitations regarding the selection of cases finally 
chosen for the study. 
==PAGE:157==
 
 
146 
 
Firstly, both case companies are large engineering organisations, with a deeply embedded focus on 
the technical capabilities of the company. Throughout the interviews, it has been seen that these 
companies have a clear focus on how to handle the technical issues arising in a digital 
transformation project, but the management of human aspects of a change varies in quality. This has 
resulted in our analysis and solution being minded for these types of large old engineering 
companies. It could be expected that the study would have turned out differently if the case 
companies had been SME’s, where the technical competencies might not be as embedded in the 
company as seen in Case 1 & 2. 
The next factor is the difference in the historical background of the two case companies. This has 
been seen to have a clear effect on the cases used for the analysis. Case company 1 has had a global 
strategy of acquiring companies relevant to its product portfolio. This has resulted in large 
differences in IT systems and culture between branches acquired through several decades. Case 
company 2 has a different story. The company has expanded its size by building new production 
sites and sales sites from scratch rather than buying whole companies. This has ensured a more 
aligned infrastructure and culture for employees globally. These differences have had a significant 
influence on the current state of the company and the results found in our analysis. This is an 
important factor to consider, as it might have an impact on the generalisability of the final 
recommendations of this study. 
Another limitation of the study is regarding the perspective of each case. Case 1 is a project within 
one department and Case 2 is a program where we have been allowed to dive into a specific project. 
Considerations should be made whether best practices from Case 2 can be implemented directly in a 
project like Case 1. However, this does provide insight into the management in different managerial 
levels and thereby define the actions needed for managers in different roles on project, program 
and perhaps even portfolio levels. Furthermore, this study has been able to analyse the impacts on a 
project that are a part of a program versus a standalone project.  
Lastly, both cases are projects currently in the pre-implementation phases. This has created a 
limitation for our study, as no tangible data has been available for the implementation and 
sustainment phases. Therefore, the analysis and recommendations provided from the study can only 
address and support the initial phases of a change project. 
 
 
==PAGE:158==
 
 
147 
 
Interview approach 
Another aspect that has affected the study is stakeholder interviews. For Case 1, the interviewees 
were chosen based on availability and the preliminary interviews. During the last minutes of the 
interviews, the interviewee recommended other stakeholders that could provide useful information; 
these were all related to the same product application and country. After the analysis, an issue in the 
cross-department collaboration was identified, hence why interviews with the other applications 
and countries could have provided useful information. If this issue was acknowledged earlier, these 
interviews could have been conducted, but time constraints on this study did not allow for that. 
Likewise, an interview with the Head of Service could have provided information on the vertical 
processes within Service, but he was too busy to participate.  
As mentioned in the methodology, a decision was made to change the interview approach. As a 
result of interviewees expressing insecurity and not being able to answer questions sufficiently, the 
interview guide was sent in advance to managers in Case 2 involved later in the data collection 
process. The amount of information gathered from these interviewees increased and provided 
greater insight for the analysis. However, the interviews were often initialised with a longer 
presentation from the employee, as they already knew what information we were seeking and could 
prepare their answers and, in some cases, a presentation. This made it difficult to follow the 
interview guides, but the interviewer made sure that all questions in these guides were covered, so 
information across interviews, regardless of approach, could be used for our analysis. One could also 
argue, that sending the questions beforehand while giving the interviewee more time to prepare 
information, also allowed them to determine what information to show and what not to show, 
resulting in a more biased view of Case 2. 
Due to the nature of the qualitative data approach, the opinions of the interviewees will colour the 
gathered information. Similarly, our opinions within the group will affect how the gathered 
information is interpreted and analysed. To limit the impact of the bias from interviewees, all 
interviews included the same questions as mentioned earlier. The information was also 
systematically coded and analysed for patterns. As a result, outliers were not included in the 
analysis, unless they were deemed significant for the project progress within each case. 
Furthermore, the bias of our own opinions was also limited by the systematic approach described in 
the methodology section. The coding was based on the gathered theories instead of an open-coding 
approach. This allowed for a deductive coding process, where all group members were aligned on 
how each theoretical code should be interpreted.  
==PAGE:159==
 
 
148 
 
Despite the systematic data collection and analysis approach, bias can never be completely 
removed. The included theories chosen for data coding were all chosen by the group members; 
other theories, such as a maturity model developed by Gartner, could have been used and would 
maybe alter the study outcome. The analysis is also a combination of what challenges were 
indicated by the theory and how the group members interpreted the analysed data. Even though all 
models used for analysis were analysed and discussed among the group members, it is impossible to 
be entirely unbiased.  
Related to the bias of the interviewees, the information from Case 2 seems mainly positive. It is 
difficult to determine the reason for this. However, all interviewees were asked if they could provide 
insight into the challenges and obstacles they had encountered during the project; the challenges 
mentioned were all of a minor impact. Furthermore, the project is not deemed “highly sensitive” to 
the company; if this was the case, interviewees could have been inclined to be overly positive about 
the project due to personal or managerial investments. The group has critically analysed the Case 2 
data to look for areas where theory indicates mistakes, but the outcome is still a smooth project that 
has met the project goals and involves positive participants.  
The validity of the interview approach can also be discussed. The validity is enhanced by the number 
of interviewees included in the study; they represent a broad spectrum of organisational layers, 
responsibilities and backgrounds. The result is an extensive dataset with information regarding the 
Service Configurator Project and the Factory of the Future program (including the AGV project). An 
aspect that can limit the validity is a lack of validation interviews due to time constraints. A 
validation interview was conducted with one manager from Case 1, as well as an interview with a 
Digital Transformation Architect from a third company. Ideally, follow-ups should have been 
conducted with all interviewees, to make sure that the transcribed interview was correct, and that 
the interpretation of their quotes does not fall too far from reality. However, all interviewees were 
asked whether they were interested in receiving the transcribed interview, and they all declined. The 
main conclusions of the study were discussed with a couple of the interviewees from Case 1. If more 
time was allocated to the study, additional validation, follow-ups or interviews would have been 
conducted.  
To summarise, this study would argue that the generalisability of our research is high for 
organisations within the boundaries of the mentioned limitations. However, the weaknesses 
described throughout the limitations should be considered when using the recommendation as we 
did not have sufficient data and time to validate the performance outside the limitations.

Data limitations 
The Acatech stages described in the literature review focus on the development, implementation 
and use of information systems in factory production. While informative and useful to an 
organisation planning to use such technology, the systems described were not part of the projects of 
Case 1 and 2. In Case 2, the stages are a part of the overall strategy, as described by the Program 
Director, but the information gathered was not sufficient to accurately analyse its impact on the 
organisation. This is not to say that aspects of the stages model are not applicable in other areas of 
the organisation. Aspects, such as digitisation and analysis of connectivity between non-production 
work processes, can lead to greater insight into the cause and effect of specific processes. 
Furthermore, the ability to use the Digital Shadow described to test improvements to the production 
in simulations is extremely valuable.  
While the stages have not been applicable to our study, the descriptions of the structural areas and 
their development have been extremely useful in providing a framework for the assessment of the 
two organisations.  
Through this assessment and the Kræmmergaard Generational Model, it also became apparent that 
the parts of the organisations analysed spanned from the first to the third generation. Much of the 
information, problems and results from the two cases pertain to these specific generations. As such, 
we are able to describe these generations and understand the actual transitions. The fourth and last 
stage is not included in the scope of our recommendation as we simply do not have any information, 
besides theoretical on the subject.  
Interview challenges versus theory 
Most of the theories used in this study have been verified through the analysis and cases. Only in 
minor areas of the theory, this was not the case. 
One area where differences from theory were identified was regarding knowledge sharing in Case 1. 
It was seen through the interviews that no formalised knowledge sharing system was set in place for 
securing a systematic approach to communication and knowledge sharing. According to Hislop, an 
organisation missing these kinds of systems will miss out on essential organisational learning. 
Currently, this has not been a problem as the scope of involved people in Case 1 is limited. However, 
according to Galbraith et al., participants will be demotivated during a change initiative if they are 
kept in the dark; this might be the result when the project is scaled up to cover the entire userbase 
within global or additional departments.  
==PAGE:161==
 
 
150 
 
The second area where differences between the theory and Case 1 were identified is in regard to the 
Organisational Design. As described, case company 1 has been undergoing different organisational 
changes through the last five years. Latest, the company has gone from the described One-strategy 
to a more divisional and silo-based structure. According to the Digital Maturity theory, this is the 
wrong approach, as an organisation striving for advancement in maturity should remove 
departmental silo structures and promote agile work teams. For Case 1, abandoning the One-
strategy could possibly create a positive outcome for the company, as more structure and defined 
responsibilities are desired at the moment. In the future, a need for changing this departmental 
strategy will be necessary for achieving long term goals of Digital Maturity, but in the short-term, the 
solution will create the desired benefits, as mentioned by Project Manager 1.  
Changes for theory 
The study made use of three distinct theoretical areas to support and conduct the analysis. 
However, through the analysis certain changes had to be made to the theory to account for lack of 
information and overlap between theories. Furthermore, certain modifications to the theory were 
made based on practical findings. 
Change Management 
Throughout the analysis of Change Management, the process model for change by John Hayes has 
been the main framework. The theory provided great insights into change practice, and the gathered 
practical data verified many of the theoretical codes and connections. However, the two cases also 
indicated major issues with the Change Management approach in the Hayes model. Hayes does 
mention that the Diagnosis phase is not a one-off process and that it happens throughout the 
change initiative. However, the model is still a process model, and the phases are presented as 
sequential. The analysis of the two cases in this study indicates that the Change Management model 
from Hayes could be optimised by defining the phases with a different approach. This is a result of 
the following reasons: 
• 
Recognise the need for change - Motivation to change is a result of the current work process 
and how activities within the change initiative motivate employees to change these 
processes. As a result, this should not be considered as an initial phase, but rather a result of 
the Organisational Design and Change Management activities during later “phases”.  
• 
Diagnosis – As Hayes mentions, the diagnosis is not a one-off phase. The two cases also 
indicate that the diagnosis of what needs to be changed is a longer process. There are 
different ways of approaching the diagnosis; Managers from Case 2 and the Digital 
==PAGE:162==
 
 
151 
 
Transformation Architect mention that on-going technology assessments and testing with an 
iterative approach are necessary. 
• 
Planning – The planning model that Hayes suggests is the Oakland model, which has been 
used in the analysis of this study. The model includes areas such as Triggers and Need for 
change which arguably are events or results of diagnosis prior to the planning phase. As 
mentioned, the diagnosis in this study indicated a big focus on pilot testing. These tests need 
to be planned for. As a result, the planning phase should not be a one-off phase before the 
implementation, but rather an iterative process throughout the change.  
 
To cope with these issues, this study will recommend changing the first three phases of the Hayes 
Change Management model to a joined iterative process. The Digital Transformation Architect 
mentions that waterfall process models like Hayes’ are great when developing physical products or 
initiatives where the end-users’ opinions are irrelevant. He goes on to say that a more agile and 
iterative process is preferable when developing something new where the user experience is 
essential. This study will argue that digital transformation projects like Case 1 and 2 will mostly 
implement new processes where the users’ acceptance and understanding should be a high priority. 
As a result, this study recommends changing the Hayes Change Model to a more iterative process 
combining the first phases.  
This study will also suggest changes to the Oakland Planning Model. As mentioned, the first two 
areas Triggers and need for change should not be part of the planning, but rather inputs from other 
activities. It was also clear during the literature review and analysis that some of the areas within the 
planning model were too simple. The area that Oakland calls Planning was revised during this study 
to focus on priorities. Furthermore, having a “planning” area within a planning model was deemed 
redundant and confusing. It was also clear that having an area for “behaviour” is essential but needs 
greater attention. As a result, this study suggests using other tools to support the areas within the 
Oakland model, such as the Knoster’s Model for Managing Complex Change, which has supported 
this study. Another aspect that needs to be revised is the Systems & Control area of the Oakland 
model. It was clear from the two cases that this area is not easily planned for by the project 
manager. It is rather a result of the Organisational Design and the motivation to change of the 
affected employees. This was clear from Case 1, where the project manager has no way of ensuring 
the sustainment of the tool. This is a result of the overall strategy where the focus of defining 
company-wide standards is on main IT systems. As a result, employees can freely use other tools if 
management within their own smaller division deems it beneficial, which can result in an internal 
conflict of interest. This area of the planning model should be moved to a higher level and should 
==PAGE:163==
 
 
152 
 
not be part of the responsibility of the Project Manager, who should rather focus more on the 
Leadership & Direction, Priorities, Processes, Resources, while managing Behaviour.  
Digital Maturity 
While most aspects of the Digital Maturity models were useful and beneficial to the study, others 
needed revision and changes to fit in. The Generational Model presented by Kræmmergaard implies 
that the transition between generations is done in leaps. This might not be the intention, but this is 
the result of the design. Our study indicates that the transition is a slow incremental process, and 
this should be reflected in the design of the Digital Maturity model. This will be elaborated upon in 
the recommendation. One of the key issues with Kræmmergaards Digital Maturity assessment 
theory was its vagueness in some areas. There is no ranking of abilities as to which are important in 
each Generation, and while it was mentioned that abilities could influence each other, it is not 
expanded upon. Furthermore, it is stated by Kræmmergaard that the abilities are based on her 
extensive practical experience. This was not apparent in the descriptions of the abilities, as she 
would describe a certain ability and its merits without providing a practical guideline for said ability. 
This approach to the abilities results in them seeming like a list of abilities to develop without 
actually giving any indication about how and when to do so. The solution to this was comparing 
abilities with the capabilities of the Acatech structural areas and thereby grouping abilities into 
areas. Each of these areas is structured so that individual capabilities benefit from the development 
of other capabilities in the same structural area. This resulted in abilities like IT-mindset and Data 
driven being grouped into the principle Digital capability. As Acatech had a practical example about 
how to develop its capabilities, this was deemed beneficial. This also allowed us to combine the 
abilities into the assessment of each structural area.  
The problem of evaluating the importance of certain abilities in each generation was partially solved 
by the Organisational Design analysis as well as the Digital Maturity assessment. The assessment 
allowed us to identify where the companies were placed in the Generation Model. Furthermore, 
through the assessment and the Organisational Design analysis, we could evaluate the impact of 
certain abilities and capabilities in the organisation at that specific generation.  
Abilities, such as the Agile organisational structure in Case 2, had a positive impact on the success of 
the program as well as the project. This was only possible due to the overall digital strategy 
supporting this structure, as well as support from the Director of Transformation. It also became 
apparent, that it was not the case that a single ability was developed and implemented in a single 
generation; abilities and capabilities were gradually developed across multiple generations. The 
==PAGE:164==
 
 
153 
 
Director of Transformation was crucial in this process, as it is his job to direct the development of 
the abilities and capabilities and make sure to maintain the momentum of each project. 
Through the Case 1 analysis, it became clear that the Organisational Design, namely structure and 
strategy, had a significant impact on how the project had progressed and the issues it was facing. 
When using the process models of Hayes and Oakland, it was therefore surprising that the models 
did not seem to take the organisation surrounding the project into account. This study recommends 
that Project Managers can and should plan for how the Organisational Design affects their project. 
However, it is clear that some of the issues were due to Organisational Design changes that they are 
not in a position to anticipate and plan for. This reaffirms the need for a CDO or an organisation that 
can perform a similar role as the Director of Transformation from Case 2; to make sure that the 
environment that the project managers work in is stable and that they can access the resources they 
need. 
Bigger picture 
A discussion point in the literature is whether there really is a need for a CDO and if the 
responsibilities of a CTO/CIO overlap with the CDO’s. This would make a CDO redundant as they 
would be fulfilling an existing role. This study would argue that, while a CTO/CIO’s role is to develop 
technologies that improve work processes or new products, the CDO’s role is to plan how to manage 
the disruptions created by said technologies. This is also described by Digital Transformation 
Consultant Doctor Mark Baker in “Digital Transformation”.  
“The truth is that transformation falls outside the CTO’s remit. The roles of the CTO and CIO are to 
keep things running stably […] CTO/CIO’s are responsible for sustainment and development of 
technologies, while the CDO provides a plan for development of disruptive technologies.” (Baker, 
2015) 
We would also argue that a digital transformation includes the development of abilities and 
capabilities which are disruptive to components of the Organisational Design. It is the CDO’s role to 
plan and manage this transformation to make sure that the changes cause as little disruption as 
possible. Another aspect of the CDO’s role is exemplified in Case 2, where the Director of 
Transformation was responsible for creating an overview and structure for cross-department 
collaboration. It is his job to make sure that Project Managers have access to an overview of the 
company's competencies and that they can use these. The competencies are not necessarily 
internal; Case 2 shows an example of how suppliers were used to develop the competencies to 
operate and maintain an AGV internally. Similarly, data-science competencies were developed 
internally using external suppliers. However, as discussed with the Program Director at case 
==PAGE:165==
 
 
154 
 
company 2 and the Warehouse & Logistics Manager the question about whether it is worth 
becoming experts in a system should be considered. While it is important to have a basic 
understanding of new technology, for example the AGV, it is questionable whether there is a need to 
become experts in the use of it. Other organisations have already invested resources and time in 
developing their expertise, and it might be worth utilising their expertise instead of developing it. 
However, the basic understanding of the technology is crucial since it reduces the chance of 
suppliers “overcomplicating” the implementation.  
The CDO should act as a facilitator or enabler for the transformation; their role is to make sure that 
the development of capabilities and associated technologies is managed appropriately and with a 
focus on the end-user. Furthermore, it is important to make sure that the projects developing said 
capabilities are aligned, as to benefit from the connections described in the analysis. A Program or 
portfolio management approach could prove beneficial in this case.  
This study would argue for the importance of thoroughly planning the transformation, based on 
observations in Case 1 as well as theory from John Hayes. He describes how the reactive 
transformation “re-creation” can be extremely disruptive to organisational structure and processes, 
as well as creating confusion and resistance among the affected employees. This can be seen in Case 
1 where the structural, cultural and process changes created by the One-Strategy were not planned 
well enough, and the strategy seems to have been a reaction to external events rather than a 
strategy which was proactively identified.  
Considering two of our main theoretical areas Organisational Design and Digital Maturity it was 
evident that the connection between these is important for executing a Change Management 
strategy. More precisely, it was seen through the analysis that the organisational strategy defines 
the digital capabilities/abilities, many of these having a strong connection to Change Management 
areas such as Diagnosis, Planning and Communication. It is therefore important to recognise that the 
strategy decides which abilities are in focus for the organisation and how the infrastructure for this 
will be aligned. 
As it was seen in the analysis, the strategy is a crucial component for the design, where it affects all 
other components. Hence, it is important to understand the benefits or potential issues arising when 
making decisions regarding the Organisational Design. Therefore, it has been seen in the cases that if 
the strategy in the Organisational Design accommodates the digital transformation, by setting up a 
foundation for the development of Digital Maturity abilities, the transformation has been less 
disruptive. This could be executed by implementing a CDO, as described earlier, and give him the 
power to establish programs for different digital project areas, and further define a portfolio 
strategy together with top management to support this.  
==PAGE:166==
 
 
155 
 
Dr Mark Baker describes the potential of a portfolio approach when defining the digital strategies: 
“One of the main strategies that the CDO can apply when planning road maps is a portfolio strategy, 
where a mixture of approaches is tested in parallel.” (Baker, 2015) 
Dr Baker goes on to describe how a change in mindset is needed for the organisation to ensure the 
implementation of a full digitalisation strategy; different projects are tested simultaneously and 
there is an open culture for developing new ideas. 
When considering the approach for developing the digital strategies, major differences between the 
two cases were identified. For the recommendations of our analysis, it was clear that a digital 
transformation is not possible to be executed overnight. A key element is the need to understand 
that a transformation process is dependent on changes in several components from the 
Organisational Design. Therefore, small incremental steps are necessary to ensure long-lasting 
results. This was stressed by the Director of Transformation in case company 2, stating that small 
gradual changes are required for achieving a desired outcome. The opposite example has been seen 
in Case 1, where an overly ambitious and rushed One-strategy resulted in failure and the need to fall 
back to an old divisional strategy with limited possibilities for testing and development of new 
solutions. 
To support the long-term incremental process, this study recommends organisations focusing on 
pilot testing. Case 2 had a clear strategy of pilot testing, and it was analysed as having a highly 
positive impact on the project progression. To further support this, the Digital Transformation 
Architect and Dr Mark Baker underline the importance of technology testing. The purpose is to verify 
the quality and safety of the prototype, exploring new possibilities and new technologies as well as 
analysing the viability of integration into existing processes. This can minimise the disruptive impact 
of new technologies, as they are tested and configured for users and operations prior to 
implementation. The tests can also help facilitate capability assessment as it provides a real-time 
picture of the current and needed capabilities. This was clear from the Case 2 approach, where the 
AGV project was initialised to learn how to operate AGV’s and how to facilitate an AGV project. The 
Program Manager from Case 2 and the Digital Transformation Architect both mention that these 
tests can create company-wide standards; or “Smart Copies”. These standards can help facilitate 
cross-department communication, as they provide a common platform or standard for further 
configuration. As an example, the AGV would run three pilot tests resulting in a standard AGV 
specification, and other factories can afterwards use the standard AGV template and add features 
that fit their specific product group or infrastructure.  
==PAGE:167==
 
 
156 
 
The pilots can also work as the input and framework for promoting business cases. The importance 
of developing a business case has been clear throughout the analysis. A business case can provide 
commitment from top management and can facilitate the acquisition of needed resources. The 
approach to business cases can vary, but this study suggests a focus on organisational capability 
development, which requires a higher willingness towards risks and less focus on short term profits. 
Ideally, the business case will include both monetary gains and long-term capability development.  
This study suggests a high focus on user engagement through pilots and capability development. As 
mentioned, the Digital Transformation Architect suggests an agile work process with a focus on user 
engagement whenever the user experience is essential. This study acknowledges that digital 
transformation projects are typically focused on changing the internal work processes, where the 
main user is the worker who’s daily processes will be affected. As a result, there is a need for 
organisations seeking to develop digital transformation projects to treat the process as if they were 
developing and testing a product for a customer; testing new processes/technologies using sample 
groups, iterative development cycles, user feedback etc.  
This situation would promote collaboration between the R&D department and the Chief Digital 
Officer, to make sure the integration of the new product features fit a desired digital portfolio 
strategy.

Recommendation 
Approach 
The design process of the recommendation was inspired by the IBM Loop. The Loop strives to 
support organisations envision their future through a continuous cycle of observing, reflecting and 
making. (“Learn the Enterprise Design Thinking Framework - Enterprise Design Thinking,” 
03.02.2020). The tool targets managers who want to understand their customers/users and design 
tangible solutions to meet their needs. Likewise, this study aims at understanding the challenges and 
needs of the included case companies, and with this design a recommendation for managers.  
 
Figure 94: The Loop by IBM  
Our design process did not strictly follow every aspect of the framework, but the continuous process 
and considerations recommended in each phase were extremely beneficial and well suited to the 
study. This is reflected in the design of the solution, as in the preliminary stages, it was decided to 
only develop a solution for project managers on an operational level. However, through 
observations and a better understanding of the context of the cases, it became apparent that this 
would not address several key issues identified through the analysis. Multiple iterations of this 
process were repeated before concluding that all three managerial levels had to be addressed in the 
design of the recommendation. 
Principle 
The recommendation aims to address each of the managerial levels described by Jones et al. This 
study recommends an agile process for managers working with digital transformation projects, 
including typical innovation aspects such as technology assessment, development and testing. This 
process will be supported by aspects from Change Management to secure the support and 
motivation of employees who will be affected; user engagement, competency development, etc. To 
==PAGE:169==
 
 
158 
 
make this a reality, aspects from the Organisational Design and Digital Maturity will support the 
projects through a digital portfolio strategy.  
Figure 94 presents an overview of this study’s recommendation principle. Based on the analysis, the 
solution design was divided into three managerial levels; Strategic (Portfolio), Tactical (program) and 
Operational (project). The described CDO is responsible for managing the portfolio and formulating 
and maintaining the long-term strategy. Program managers are responsible for the alignment of all 
projects within the program and project managers have the responsibility for the execution of 
projects approved by the program manager.  
Detailed responsibilities of the associated managers on each level are defined and will be further 
explained in the coming sections. Digital Maturity capabilities related to the different managerial 
levels are presented at the bottom of figure 95. The goals of each managerial level should include 
the development of associated capabilities as the presence and development of associated 
capabilities should support the implementation of the solution principle. The responsibility of 
developing the capabilities has been allocated based on the analysis of the two cases. Some 
capabilities should be developed across multiple levels.  
 
Figure 95: Recommendation principle

The recommendation principle will suggest a new approach to digital maturity assessment compared 
to the generations model by Pernille Kræmmergaard. In figure 96 the new approach is presented, 
showing both the slight alteration between the generational staircase and our new approach.  
 
Figure 96: Digital Maturation approach 
The static picture that an organisation is situated at a specific level until they develop capabilities to 
reach the next is rigid and does not precisely reflect the reality seen in the two cases. 
As a result, the new approach presented in figure 96 shows how an organisation should define a 
strategy for how to develop their digital capabilities. These strategic goals should then be achieved 
by a long-term incremental process where the portfolio strategy will support the transition. 
Developing the capabilities through small steps, the organisation will gradually evolve their Digital 
Maturity and can set new and more advanced goals as the process develops. The implication of this 
recommendation is an approach focused on Digital Maturation (continuous development) rather 
than Digital Maturity (static levels and end goals).  
Therefore, programs that direct the development of an organisation's Digital Maturation need 
commitment and leadership from a CDO and the top management strategy, resulting in a structured 
portfolio approach.

Leadership  
The project manager should define the vision and purpose of the pilot project and make sure this is 
communicated to all participants. The project team(s) will be formed based on required 
competencies, and roles and responsibilities should be clearly defined. Involving the users as part of 
the project team with defined responsibilities is recommended. This will ensure that the user’s

managers of the users should also be included as they will need to approve project participation.  
Priorities 
The project manager will define the scope of the pilot tests. During this process, the goals related to 
the tests will be specified. It is recommended to involve the users when defining the goals. As seen 
in Case 2, the involved factory manager and project manager had different but synergising agendas 
and goals related to the AGV pilot project, which improved the incentive for collaboration.  
Processes 
It’s important to analyse the core changes to the work process provided by the new technology/tool. 
During this analysis, a definition of the technical configuration is essential, to make sure the new 
initiative fits the current work environment. This was clear from Case 1, where the main motivation 
to change was a result of a well-configurated tool with features supporting the daily work of the 
users. Analysing the core changes should be conducted in collaboration with the users, as they can 
provide useful insight.  
Resources 
Finally, the project team needs to plan for the required resources. During this process, the need for 
collaboration with suppliers should be assessed. To support this, the internal capabilities are 
analysed with the goal of identifying any gaps between the current and needed resources. The 
planning of resources is based on the defined pilot scope and should be discussed with all project 
team members and users involved.  
The outcome is an action plan for the pilot testing of new technologies and tools. The pilot testing 
will provide feedback from participants to the Project Manager, who will update the action plan 
accordingly. The process is iterative with continuous communication between the pilot testing 
participants and the project team. The learning from the pilot testing can be used for additional tests 
within the project or knowledge sharing among the project/program managers.  
When the pilot testing is complete, a business case and specifications for a standard/smart copy is 
defined. A part of the business case the defined pilot goals results are presented to top 
==PAGE:177==
 
 
166 
 
management. When the business case and standard template have been validated and approved, 
the template can be used for broader implementation; for additional lines in the factory, other 
product applications etc. An assessment of the internal capabilities can determine whether the 
implementation should be continued within the program or moved to the involved department (for 
example, the Service division).  
The recommendation presented above requires that Top management is willing to dedicate the 
resources, personnel and time needed to initiate, plan and maintain the Digital Maturation strategy 
described above. The strategy will not change the organisation overnight, and Top management 
needs to acknowledge that Digital maturation is a slow incremental process and includes learning 
from mistakes.

Conclusion 
The importance of Organisational Design has throughout this study been exemplified through two 
case studies. It has become clear that digital transformation projects have a significant connection 
with core aspects of the organisation such as people, processes and structure. This study aimed to 
develop a recommendation for managers responsible for initiating digital transformation projects. 
The overall research question was formulated as the following: 
“How can large manufacturing companies initiate, structure and plan the development of their 
Digital Maturity?” 
To answer the overall research question, three main theoretical areas were reviewed; Organisational 
Design, Digital Maturity and Change Management. Initially, the Organisational Design for each case 
was analysed to gather information related to the components of the Star Model developed by 
Galbraith & Kates, 2007. This led to the Digital maturity assessment, which was conducted using the 
Digital Maturity models developed by Acatech and Pernille Kræmmergaard. Input from the 
Organisational Design supported the assessment in each case. Lastly, the analysis of Organisational 
Design and Digital Maturity was used to systematically understand the effect of these areas on the 
process model from John Hayes’ Change Management theory. Altogether, the results of this study 
have pushed the boundaries of knowledge with regards to Digital Maturity and its interplay with the 
two associated areas. 
Data was collected from Managers and Employees from two large manufacturing companies. To 
utilise the data for a structured analysis approach, all interviews were transcribed and coded with a 
deductive approach based on the three main theoretical areas and their connections. This process of 
identifying and analysing connections between theoretical codes provided the basis for the 
recommendation and answer to the research question: 
This study resulted in a recommendation of how to approach and structure the development of 
Digital Maturity through change initiatives. Based on the analysis and results of two distinct cases, 
Digital Transformation Projects are likely to accomplish the desired goals when they are initiated as 
part of a well-supported portfolio strategy. To ensure alignment between the portfolio and the 
surrounding organisation, this study recommends an Organisational Design aimed towards 
promoting agile work processes, openness to innovation, capability and competency awareness etc. 
Furthermore, due to practical implications of the two cases, the recommendation includes a revised 
version of John Hayes’ model for change, aiming at supporting project managers in the initiation of 
==PAGE:179==
 
 
168 
 
digital transformation projects through pilot testing, user engagement and cross-department 
collaboration. 
The following sections will describe the process and outcomes of the analysis & results and 
discussion chapters leading to the final recommendation.  
Analysis & Results 
This section will sum up the process and result of systematically analysing the Organisational design 
of each company to determine whether this can provide insight to the Digital Maturity assessment 
and thereby determining the generational placement of each company. Thereafter, the Digital 
Maturity assessment and the Organisational Design analysis will be used to determine their effect on 
the case project from a Change Management perspective. The insight gained from the analysis of 
connections between these three areas is the basis for the recommendation principle presented 
afterwards. 
Organisational Design 
An analysis of the design of the two included cases was conducted to answer the question of how 
Organisational Design can provide insight for a Digital Maturity assessment. The scope was to 
analyse the involved department for each case project and the surrounding organisational 
components that have had an impact on the project. The analysis revealed two very different 
designs, where Case 1 was characterised by a silo-structure with a lack of knowledge sharing systems 
for cross-departmental collaboration. Furthermore, the infrastructure was not aligned on secondary 
IT systems such as the Configurators; the strategy focuses on standardisation and alignment on main 
systems such as ERP and CAD. This resulted in a culture with a silo-mindset and an environment 
where departments freely can choose which secondary systems they prefer to integrate, leading to a 
wasteful competitive approach to IT systems development. In Case 1 the main misalignments 
stemmed from major changes in the overall strategy within the company. On the contrary, the 
design of Case 2 showed synergetic alignments between many components as a result of a strategy 
focusing on innovation, an agile work environment and digital maturity development. The design 
was characterised by cross-department collaboration, technology testing and development 
processes and capability awareness. The design resulted in a culture that promotes innovation and 
collaboration.

Digital Maturity 
The two distinct Organisational Designs were used as the groundwork for a Digital Maturity 
assessment of each case. To conduct the assessment, the capabilities and abilities of Pernille 
Kræmmergaard’s and Acatech’s digital maturity models were utilised. The maturity “compass” from 
the Acatech model was used as the framework, where each case was assessed in three out of the 
four structural areas. These areas consist of associated capabilities, and each case was ranked on 
these capabilities based on the information from the interviewees and the Organisational Designs. 
The cases were ranked on whether a capability was an integrated part of the design (high), 
conducted with a systemic approach (medium), conducted with an ad-hoc approach (low) or none. 
For example, Case 2 scored high on Openness to Innovation as the design has integrated processes 
for innovation within disruptive technologies and a strategy that is invested in development of 
digital maturity.  
A current digital maturity level was determined for each case, as well as a maturity vision based on 
the assessment and the coded data. The Digital Maturity assessment for Case 1 can be seen below.

Change Management 
The two case projects providing practical insight for this study are the Service Configurator Project 
and the AGV Project. To analyse the process within each case project, the change management 
process model from John Hayes was utilised. Each project was in the early stages prior to a broad 
implementation, hence why only the first three phases of the model could be assessed in this study.  
During the analysis of the Recognise the need for change phase focused on motivation. The two 
cases varied in how employees are motivated. However, it was clear that the culture and processes 
components of the Organisational Design heavily influenced how employees are motivated to 
==PAGE:182==
 
 
171 
 
perform and to change during the projects. However, no major challenges were identified related to 
motivation. The next phase Diagnosis was also analysed.  
It became clear through the analysis that there is a high variance in the approach to the diagnosis 
phase between the two cases. The approach is influenced by the Organisational Design and Digital 
Maturity principles. This was especially reflected in the diagnosis of Capabilities, Suppliers and Pilot 
testing: 
• 
Capabilities - Case 1 with an ad-hoc approach and no initial capability assessment. Case 2 
with a structured capability assessment and training of employees as a part of the overall 
strategy. 
• 
Suppliers - Case 1 with a focus on the cost of collaboration, while Case 2 focuses on 
collaboration and capability development with suppliers.  
• 
Pilot testing - For Case 1 pilot testing is a result of scarce resources and used to strengthen 
the business case. Case 2 has pilot testing as an integrated part of the overall strategy; 
develop capabilities and define standards through 3 pilot tests.   
Both cases emphasise the importance of diagnosis and development of business cases. This will be 
an even greater focus for Case 1 when a new silo structure is implemented. The final phase used for 
the analysis was planning. To support the analysis, the planning model from John Oakland was used, 
as recommended by Hayes.  
Through the analysis of the planning for each case, it became apparent that the strategy of each 
company had resulted in vastly different approaches to planning. The project in Case 1 started out 
with a wide scope which aimed at implementing and standardising a tool across multiple 
applications and countries at once. However, due to major changes in the strategy, resources were 
redirected and accessing different applications became increasingly difficult, leading to frustration 
among project members. Project scope had to be changed to a UK pilot project due to problems 
related to resources and a lack of ambidextrous capabilities; employees were busy with daily 
operations. In Case 2, the strategy was to test technologies in smaller environments using pilot tests 
before upscaling the technology and making it a standard solution. This allowed the project 
managers to determine benefits, missing competencies and needed resources more accurately 
before launching a full-scale implementation. 
An assessment was also made on how communication was conducted in both case projects. It was 
clear that both project managers had secured the commitment from top management through their 
==PAGE:183==
 
 
172 
 
communication approaches. However, big differences in the knowledge sharing between the project 
team and users were identified: 
• 
Case 1 – No clear strategy for collaboration and creation of purpose. As a result, the 
approach to communication is shaped by the organisational culture with a silo mentality. 
The process indicates an ad-hoc approach to knowledge sharing.  
• 
Case 2 - Clearly defined strategy of bottom-up and promoting Willingness to change has 
shaped the approach for communicating for commitment.  
In conclusion, the strategy and culture aspects of Organisational Design and Digital Maturity highly 
influence the overall approach towards Change Management. The two cases are clear examples of 
how different a change initiative will be approached based on strategic design choices, cultural 
principles and human behaviour. 
The Hayes and Oakland models were useful during the analysis with many relevant aspects. 
However, it was clear that the models could be optimised to realistically reflect how the two projects 
have been managed: 
• 
Recognise the need for change - Motivation to change is a result of the current work process 
and how activities within the change initiative motivate employees to change these 
processes. As a result, this should not be considered as an initial phase, but rather a result of 
the organisational design and change management activities during later “phases”. 
• 
Diagnosis – As Hayes mentions, the diagnosis is not a one-off phase. The two cases also 
indicate that the diagnosis of what needs to be changed is a longer process.  
• 
Planning – The Oakland model includes areas such as Triggers and Need for change which 
this study argues are events or results of a diagnosis prior to the planning phase. As 
mentioned, the diagnosis in this study indicated a big focus on pilot testing. These tests need 
to be planned for. As a result, the planning phase should not be a one-off phase before the 
implementation, but rather an iterative process throughout the change.  
 
To cope with these issues, this study will recommend changing the first three phases of the Hayes 
Change Management model to a joined iterative process. The Digital Transformation Architect 
mentions that waterfall process models like Hayes’ are great when developing physical products or 
initiatives where the end-users’ opinions are irrelevant. He goes on to say that a more agile and 
iterative process should be preferred when developing something new and where the user 
experience is essential. This study will argue that digital transformation projects like Case 1 and 2 will 
mostly implement new processes where the users’ acceptance and understanding should be a high 
==PAGE:184==
 
 
173 
 
priority. As a result, this study recommends changing the Hayes’ Change Management Model to a 
more iterative process combining the first phases. 
Results compared to theory 
The new connections identified in each area supported further validation of previous findings as well 
as theoretical concepts. Through the analysis of Organisational Design, it became apparent that the 
structural elements played a crucial role in developing and maintaining specific cultures. This was 
further established by the identified connection between the different structures of each 
organisation and their corresponding Digital Maturity cultures. Furthermore, as stated in the Digital 
Maturity theory, the ability to be ecosystem minded can help alleviate the need for resources and 
provide competencies not found internally in the organisation. This connection was identified and 
verified by the use of suppliers in Case 2. This also allows the company a higher degree of 
ambidexterity as suppliers can provide resources to perform extra tasks during the transition period. 
Lastly, the theoretical assumption that an agile organisation with cross-department collaboration 
and efficient systems for team creation improves companies' ability to quickly react to changes in its 
environment was proven through the coding. 
Notably, Case 2 has mostly verified theoretical codes and connections, and the project has been 
successful in reaching the project goals. On the contrary, Case 1 has experienced challenges as a 
result of decisions related to the Organisational Design, which have heavily influenced the project. 
This further validates the approach by Case 2 as being close to best practice. As a result, the 
recommendation in this study is heavily inspired by the Organisational Design and Digital Maturity 
approach in Case 2 combined with the mentioned changes to the Change Management models.  
Recommendation 
The design of the recommendation in this study is inspired by the IBM Loop design process. The 
recommendation is designed for three managerial levels; Strategic (Portfolio), Tactical (Program) and 
Operational (Project). It includes the creation of a digital transformation portfolio managed and led 
by a CDO. It is important that the CDO is either part of or has access to the board, as he will act as 
the link between the transformation portfolio and the rest of the organisation. The CDO is 
responsible for conducting the preliminary assessment and building the portfolio accordingly. The 
number and goals of programs will differ depending on the findings of the preliminary digital 
assessment. In parallel with this assessment, it would be beneficial to start creating an overview of 
the organisation’s current and needed competencies. This will support the planning of which 
competencies to develop and acquire and help project managers by providing an overview of the 
==PAGE:185==
 
 
174 
 
competencies available to them. Furthermore, the CDO is responsible for ensuring alignment 
between programs as well as the Organisational Design. It is important to facilitate cross-
department collaboration as the transformation can affect the processes and structure surrounding 
departments; involvement in certain projects will ease the resistance and disruption caused by the 
transformation.  
Lastly, as seen in the analysis, Organisational Design can either hinder or support this 
transformation. While some of the responsibility for aligning programs with the organisation lies 
with the program managers, the CDO is ultimately responsible.  
The Portfolio strategy and the vision of an agile organisation need to be seen as a key organisational 
strategy. Otherwise, employees will not be inclined to work across departments, as seen in Case 1. 
However, it’s important that the strategy is supported by design choices in the other components of 
the Organisational Design. The structure should promote cross-department collaboration between 
current departments and the independent portfolio department/unit. Within Capabilities, the new 
department will facilitate the digital transformation by providing central support for technological 
innovation and capability assessment, which will promote capability awareness and development 
across the organisation.   
The cross-department collaboration should be facilitated by processes such as knowledge sharing 
systems, which can be implemented by defining standard systems/protocols and promoting 
connectivity. Furthermore, the strengths of a diverse workforce need to be harnessed. Both cases 
were characterised by an older specialised workforce, these employees are needed for knowledge 
and experience, but the organisations should also try to acquire younger technologically interested 
personnel. The analysis identified that these employees are more positively inclined towards 
changes. Furthermore, the continuous development of the workforce is needed for the development 
of digital maturity. This can be supported with a focus on intrinsic motivation with personal goals 
and development.

At a program level, the Program Manager is responsible for assessing the organisations’ current 
capabilities and competencies in the program’s specific area. The needed competencies should 
either be developed internally or acquired through hiring of new personnel. It can prove beneficial 
to use external partners to help develop these competencies, as seen in Case 2, where suppliers 
were used to develop competencies within data science. The assessment can then be compared to 
the goals of the program and projects should be initiated to develop the program. This assessment 
requires that the program manager has in-depth and up to date knowledge on the technological 
potential of their assigned program. Furthermore, exercises such as process mapping can in 
collaboration with relevant departments help identify potential possibilities related to the program. 
The program manager is also responsible for aligning projects within the program.  
The project manager has several general responsibilities besides the managing of individual projects. 
While many of the ideas for projects are generated at a program level through process mapping, 
bottom-up exercises and from the goals of the program the project manager should be encouraged 
to actively identify possibilities not identified previously. The project manager is also responsible for 
planning the pilot projects and collecting and analysing feedback. This feedback should be used to 
improve the action plan for further pilots (if further pilots are needed) and in the final standard 
implementation template. Lastly, projects which have a direct effect on employee's day-to-day work 
should actively identify and include relevant employees in the project. This could either be done by 
having employees from relevant departments be a temporary part of the project team or at least by 
having continuous communication with them about the development of the project.

Leadership  
The project manager should define the vision/mission of the pilot project and make sure this is 
communicated to all participants. Project team(s) will be formed based on required competencies, 
and roles and responsibilities should be clearly defined. Involving the users as a part of the project 
team with defined responsibilities is recommended. This will ensure that the user’s feedback and 
insight is included and can be motivating due to a sense of purpose and ownership. Department 
managers of the users should also be included as they have the overview of competencies and will 
need to approve project participation.  
Priorities 
The project manager will define the scope of the pilot tests. During this process, the goals related to 
the tests will be specified. It is recommended to involve the users when defining the goals. As seen 
in Case 2, the involved factory manager and project manager had different but synergising agendas 
related to the AGV pilot project, which improved the incentive for collaboration.

Processes 
It’s important to analyse the core changes to the work process provided by the new technology/tool.  
This was clear from Case 1, where the main motivation to change was a result of a well-configurated 
tool with features supporting the daily work of the users. Analysing the core changes should be 
conducted in collaboration with the users, as they can provide the necessary insight.  
Resources 
Finally, the project team needs to plan for the required resources. During this process, the need for 
collaboration with suppliers should be assessed. The internal capabilities are analysed to support 
this, to recognise any gaps between the current and needed resources. The planning of resources is 
based on the defined pilot scope and should be discussed with all project team members and users 
involved.  
The outcome is an action plan for the pilot testing of new technologies and tools. The pilot testing 
will provide feedback from participants to the project manager, who will update the action plan 
accordingly. The process is iterative with continuous communication between the pilot testing 
participants and the project team. The feedback from the pilot testing can be used for additional 
tests within the project or knowledge sharing among the project/program managers.  
When the pilot testing is complete, a business case and specifications for a standard/smart copy is 
defined. A part of the business case is the defined pilot goals to present the test results to top 
management. When the business case and smart copy have been validated and approved, the smart 
copy can be used for broader implementation. An assessment of the internal capabilities can 
determine whether the implementation should be continued within the program or moved to the 
involved department. The implementation may plan for the same areas described in this model, but 
it is not a part of the scope in this study. 
The recommendation presented above requires that Top management is willing to dedicate the 
resources, personnel and time needed to initiate, plan and maintain the Digital Maturation strategy 
described above. The strategy will not change the organisation overnight, and Top management 
needs to acknowledge that Digital maturation is a slow incremental process and includes learning 
from mistakes. 
Limitations of this study 
Different limitations have been considered throughout the study. Firstly, changes to the scope when 
finding case companies created a limitation for our project. Half of the intended companies declined 
to participate; this resulted in a constrained time frame because these turndowns did not happen 
==PAGE:189==
 
 
178 
 
until a substantial part of the project duration was gone. This forced us to quickly rescope the 
approach for the study and created other limitations in regard to the interviews. 
A large number of our interviews were conducted in the period after we had rescoped the study and 
chosen to focus on the two cases presented in this paper. Because of the limited timeframe for 
setting up interviews with the two companies, it was not possible to include all relevant 
stakeholders. Even though many interviews were conducted at the two companies, stakeholders 
such as the Head of Service for the UK in Case 1, could not be included due to a busy schedule. The 
possibility of interviewing stakeholders from other departments/programs connected to our two 
cases would also have been interesting to investigate to further verify and validate the 
recommendation. 
Lastly, a diverging approach for how to handle the interview guides has also created a minor 
limitation for the study. The approach of how to handle the interview guides shifted through the 
data collection process, as some interviewees had the guide sent beforehand. This resulted in 
interviewees providing significantly more information, but a substantial part of the interview was a 
presentation rather than a traditional question-and-answer session. It was checked through the 
interviewee presentation that all topics from our interview guide were covered, but 
methodologically this has created a limitation for how the data was presented for us. 
Future research 
Throughout this study, several aspects have been covered using the three main theoretical areas 
supported by the two case projects. However, some aspects could not be covered due to the nature 
of the cases or the scope of this study.  
As mentioned, this study was not able to analyse the project in the last two phases of John Hayes 
planning model namely, Implementation and sustainment. For future research, it would be 
beneficial to analyse these two phases as well. Questions such as how to sustain the projects after 
implementation, who is responsible for broader implementation, how to support the broader 
implementation should be examined. However, this study would argue that a lot of the groundwork 
for the implementation and sustainment phases are laid in the preliminary stages worked with in 
this study.  The outcome of the model described previously is to create a template for broader 
implementation. Furthermore, as discussed with the case companies, a key source of sustaining 
projects is including and engaging the user in the project from the start. This creates a sense of 
ownership with the user and is less likely to be neglected after implementation. Furthermore, 
systems and control are a vital part of the sustainment and analysis indicated that it is a part of the 
==PAGE:190==
 
 
179 
 
Organisational Design. These aspects are taken into account throughout the recommendation and 
should, therefore, contribute to the sustainment.  
As described previously, the analysis indicated that the two cases were in the first, second and third 
generations of Digital Maturity. As such, this study does not have empirical data to analyse the last 
two generations. For future research companies in the fourth or in a transition to the fourth stage 
should be analysed as well. The fifth stage is largely theoretical at this moment as, according to 
Pernille Kræmmergaard, no companies have reached this maturity stage yet.  
This study has recommended a solution at three different managerial levels; Strategic (portfolio), 
Tactical (program) and Operational (project). The research has primarily been conducted at the 
operational level, with interviews with several Project Managers and users related to the project. As 
a result, the recommendation can provide guidelines on how the pre-implementation project can be 
structured to support the project manager. Information has been gathered on Strategic and Tactical 
levels regarding the importance, main purpose and benefits of the portfolio strategy. However, this 
study cannot provide practical guidelines on these levels and is an area that future research can 
investigate.  
A part of the solution recommends a set of digital capabilities related to each managerial level. 
These are based on which manager has the responsibility and power to make changes that can 
improve said capabilities. However, this study can’t provide a practical guideline on how to develop 
all of the digital capabilities. Some of the capabilities will be developed and reflected through parts 
of the recommended pre-implementation project model; the ones related to the Organisational 
Design, portfolio and program levels should be further researched for practical guidelines. 
Furthermore, the significance of the digital capabilities can be researched further. The significance is 
likely heavily dependent on the project type and organisational design, but this study is unable to 
conclusively determine the varying significance of different capabilities.  
The initial scope of this study aimed at analysing three to four different case projects but ended up 
with two. If more cases were part of the research, the outcome and recommended solution could 
have been further validated, and the generalisability of the study could have been improved. As a 
result, further research can use some of the analysis framework in this study to validate the 
outcome.The two cases are both large manufacturing companies. Further research can apply the 
analysis in a different setting. This can be large non-manufacturing companies or SME’s. This study 
has argued that the challenges experienced in a large company with many engineers are not related 
to the technology, but rather human aspects; this may not be the case in SME’s. Future research 
should explore the implications of digitalisation within SME’s.

180 
 
To sum up, this study has investigated the impacts of Organisational Design and development of 
Digital Maturity on digital transformation projects using a structured analysis approach. The analysis 
of two large manufacturing companies has resulted in a recommended Organisational Design, 
including a portfolio strategy for the development of digital capabilities and technologies. The 
recommendation includes an updated Change Management model for project managers responsible 
for the pre-implementation phase, with a focus on planning, user engagement and pilot testing. To 
validate this recommendation further, future research can be conducted within different areas; 
other company types such as SME’s, different managerial levels and Organisations ambitiously 
aiming for higher levels within Digital Maturity. This should increase the generalisability of the 
recommendation for managers and companies striving for competitive advantages in a complex 
digital world.

Learning objectives  
For this study to be ambitious and self-critical, the following 10 learning objectives have been 
formulated. This has been based on the learning objectives for master theses by DTU (Thorp et al., 
2010) and on Benjamin S. Bloom’s six taxonomy levels; Knowledge, Comprehension, Application, 
Analysis, Synthesis and Evaluation (Bloom et al., 1956). 
1. Conduct a literature research to identify and select relevant theories, creating a base for a 
theoretical framework.  
2. Understand the balance between people and processes during digital transformation 
projects, ensuring a holistic view of the case study. 
3. Prepare and conduct interviews at different organisational levels with relevant stakeholders. 
4. Construct a data analysis framework based on literature and gathered practical information. 
5. Apply a systematic view to interpret a phenomenon (Digital transformation) across different 
production companies. 
6. Transcribe and code interviews to systematically produce relevant qualitative data.  
7. Conduct a within-case analysis to understand the practical implications of the phenomenon 
and report findings.  
8. Develop a practical framework to apply the findings to organisational change practice. 
9. Manage the project to ensure a smooth transition of phases, handling of stakeholders and 
completing and maintaining milestones as planned.  
10. Evaluate the project course and the developed recommendation.

Project note 
This chapter describes some of the practical issues encountered by group through the study period. 
1. Generally, the work process in the group has been very good, we have been working 
together for almost six years so no group contract was deemed necessary. This has not been 
an issue. We are also aware of our weaknesses and strengths as individuals and would 
allocate tasks accordingly. However, all important analysis, discussions and figures were 
done in plenum to ensure consensus and insight from all members. 
2. As mentioned in the report, two companies who initially showed interest in working with us 
declined after a certain period of time. We had been waiting with conducting our analysis 
until we collected all the data. In hindsight we should have conducted the analysis parallel to 
the data collection as to avoid the situation we found ourselves in where we only had 2 
months to conduct the analysis, discussion and conclusion. 
3. The issue above was however mitigated by the fact that we had been discussing and writing 
down notes throughout the study period. So, despite not actually having conducted any 
analysis on the interviews as of the declines from the 3rd and 4th case company, we did have 
a substantial amount of notes and thoughts about the subjects and cases. This was actually a 
constant throughout the study period, many days would generally start out with discussions 
about certain findings and analysis conducted the day before and this resulted in many 
changes throughout the project process but ensured a consensus and improved analysis, 
discussion and recommendation.  
4. The last two months of the project we were extremely pressed for time as mentioned above. 
To solve this, we planned the long-term goals of each month and in the start of each week 
we would evaluate how far we were and what needed to be done to not miss the deadlines. 
This worked extremely well even though the work hours became quiet long. The ability to 
complete and mark tasks and milestones as completed gave a sense of progression 
throughout the last two months. So even though we were working a lot we felt that we were 
actually making very good progress. 
5. We were also very lucky that two of the group members worked as student assistants at the 
case companies. The contrast between the collaboration with these companies and the 
companies who declined was significant. Contacting and setting up meetings was a lot of 
easier and interviewees actually seemed interested and open to questions and discussion. 
This was evident after the declines from the 3rd and 4th case companies as we quickly had to 
find a second case and this was only made possible through the efficient collaboration with
