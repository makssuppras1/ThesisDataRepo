Abstract
This project presents the characterization of bolted connections in pultruded FRP
proﬁles subjected to bending moments, based on an experimental study on FRP I-
shaped beams spliced at mid-span with bolted web and ﬂange connections. Twenty-
one, four-point ﬂexure tests on spliced beams with bolted connections on the web,
and fourteen tests on spliced beams with bolted connections on the ﬂanges are
performed. Detailed description of the load-deﬂection response of the spliced beams
is presented and the inﬂuence of geometric parameters in the connection stiﬀness
and strength is included.
It is found that friction force between the connecting
steel plates and the GFRP pultruded elements highly inﬂuence the response of the
stiﬀness and strength of the connection. In the web connected spliced beams, it is
noticed that changes in the geometric distribution of the group of bolts can lead to
increments of the connection stiﬀness and spliced beam strength. The results show
that the characteristics failure modes related to double-lap single-bolted connections
presented in technical literature and the related geometric ratios recomendations are
applicable.

Introduction
During the last years, the use of the ﬁber reinforced polymer (FRP) materials in
the structural engineering ﬁeld has increased. Initially, their use was mainly related
to research and laboratory environment. However, with their increasingly successful
applications in actual structures and the development of codes and speciﬁcations, the
FRP composites are turning into a daily used material in the structural engineering
ﬁeld with the appropriate characteristics to complement the traditional materials
as, concrete, steel or wood.
Nowadays, the composite materials have three main uses in the structural ﬁeld: 1)
Reinforcement for new concrete structural members, 2) Strengthening of existing
structural members and 3) FRP pultruded proﬁles for new structures. The extent
of this work is focused on the use of FRP pultruded proﬁles for new structures.
The low self-weight of the FRP materials represent the main characteristic of pul-
truded members in structural applications. On the contrary, their characteristic low
elastic modulus (compared to steel elements), and brittle behaviour represent their
biggest disadvantages. The above is clearly observed in the use of pultruded ele-
ments in frame structures, since usually the deﬂection limits are reached before the
stresses surpass the strength of the elements. Accordingly, the stiﬀness of the pul-
truded elements and the connections become dominant parameters in the structural
design due to their inﬂuence on the deﬂections.
Several investigations have studied the behaviour of bolted connections in pultruded
structures, the most of them focus on lap-bolted joints subjected to tensile forces
and beam to column joints. Investigations on lap-bolted joints under tensile forces
resulted in an adequate comprehension of the main parameters that aﬀects the
joints behaviour including a reasonable understanding of the failure modes in this
type of joints. As a result, initial drafts of code-like design guidance based mainly
on geometric parameters have been developed. Coelho and Mottram [1] reported
an extensive review on this topic. The useful ﬁndings in this subject are included
in the present report in order to analyse the test results.
Regarding the beam to column joints, a number of experimental investigations have
been reported in the literature, the most of them use FRP materials for the columns,
the beams and the connection angles. Coelho and Mottram [1] presented a review
of experimental research on beam to column bolted and bonded joints between
pultruded GRP proﬁles.
The review includes results of connection stiﬀness and
failure modes. The principal conclusions mention the limitations on the connections
response due to the use of FRP proﬁles as connecting plates and identify the failure
of these elements as the two of the most common ultimate failure modes. However,
the conclusions also indicates the possibility of obtaining appropriated semi-rigid
1
==PAGE:7==
beam to column connections that allows to increase the static loading within the
serviceability limit state (SLS) deﬂection limits.
An analogous work was done by Turvey and Cooper [2]. The authors reported a
review of moment tests on beam to column bolted joints between pultruded GRP
proﬁles. The review analyse the connections stiﬀness and ultimate moment and
rotation within similar categories of joints based mainly on the type of connections
(web, web and ﬂange, and high stiﬀness, and high strength joints).
The main
conclusion highlight the variation range of the results in similar categories and the
resultant need of further testing with identical parameters in order to improve the
understanding of joints behaviour.
As it is stated above, the most of the experimental studies on bolted connections
in pultruded FRP proﬁles under bending moments have been focused on beam to
column joints. However, the complexity of this type of joints due to the interaction
of the diﬀerent parts of the connection, added to the inﬂuence of other parameters as
bolt hole clearance, bolt torque, and geometry ratios, can diﬃcult the comprehension
of the connections behaviour.
A sign of this is the high variation of the results in experimental studies that can
be related to a limited understanding of the connection behaviour for identifying
the source of the deviations. In addition, the limited information on how an speciﬁc
parameter inﬂuence the behaviour of each part of the connection and how this
part inﬂuence the entire joint response, can be considered knowledge gaps in the
connections behaviour.
According to the above, the current project is focused on characterize the behaviour
of bolted connection under bending moments, based on the analysis of the response
of spliced beams, and identify how the response is aﬀected by the diﬀerent param-
eters related to the connection. It is expected, the ﬁndings of this project can be
useful for improving the understanding of more complex bolted connections.
To this end, pultruded FRP I-shaped beams spliced at mid-span with bolted web and
ﬂange connections are used in a simply supported single span four-point bending
test.
The spliced beams are connected with steel plates, whose thicknesses and
dimensions are selected to ensure, at some extent, the behaviour of the connection
is mainly controlled by the response of the FRP pultruded element. The inﬂuence
of diﬀerent parameters in the connection as end distance, bolt-to-bolt distance, bolt
hole clearance, and bolt torque is analysed, and their eﬀect in vertical stiﬀness of
the spliced beam and failure mode is studied.
Since there are limited research literature on composite beams with bolted spliced
joints, the results of the actual project are conceptually validated mainly using the
investigations of lap-bolted joints subjected to tensile forces.

Analytical considerations
2.1
Pultrusion
Is a manufacturing process used in the composite material industry in which rein-
forcing ﬁbers are impregnated with resin and then are pulled through a steel die
with the desired cross-section. The die is heated in diﬀerent locations of its length
during the pulling process, which generates the curing of the resin and creates the
pultruded composite elements.
This manufacturing method allows producing continuous straight elements with a
constant cross section that can be cut in any desired length. There are a number of
structural proﬁles that can be produced using this method, for instance I-sections,
angles, ﬂat-proﬁles and tubes.
The raw materials required in this transformation procedure can be divided into two
groups, the ﬁbers system and the resin system.
2.1.1
Fiber system
The ﬁber system conforms the reinforcing of the composite material. It is composed
by a number of small ﬁlaments that are the responsible for the mechanical proper-
ties of the pultruded elements. The ﬁbers are named by their constitutive material,
for example glass ﬁber, carbon ﬁber, and aramid ﬁbers; and their application in the
structural engineering ﬁeld depends on how their mechanical and physical character-
istics are suitable for the requirements generated by the loading and environmental
conditions.
The glass ﬁbers are the most commonly used ﬁbers in the structural engineering
ﬁeld and are used in the vast majority of the FRP pultruded structural proﬁles.
These ﬁbers are classiﬁed according to their chemical and physical characteristics
and they are identiﬁed by a letter nomenclature that represents the glass grade.
It is important to say the E-glass ﬁber is the most commonly used in the structural
applications due to its high electrical resistivity. However, has some aspects that
need to be taken into account when used: Its sensitivity to the moisture and its
susceptibility to creep rupture and lose of strength under sustained stresses. The
ﬁrst one can be controlled by ensuring a proper ﬁber protection by means of an
adequate election of resin and coat system.
These ﬁbers can be acquired in diﬀerent forms and their use in the pultrusion process
is deﬁned according to the characteristics they give to the ﬁnal pultruded element.
For example, a light-weight mat is adequate for using in the outer layer of the
3
==PAGE:9==
pultruded elements, since allows a high volume fraction of resin, which contributes
to obtain a ﬂat surface with good chemical resistance. The most common forms of
the glass reinforcing ﬁbers are: Strands form on a spool (roving or tow), mat, fabric
and veil.
The rovings are strands conformed by groups of continuous ﬁlaments that are used
as reinforcement in the middle zone of the cross-section of the pultruded elements.
Due to its unidirectional form, the rovings are aligned in the longitudinal direction of
the structural shapes, accordingly, this reinforcement is the main responsible for the
longitudinal mechanical properties (axial direction) of the pultruded elements. Due
to its low properties in the direction perpendicular to the ﬁlaments, it is required to
use mats or fabrics in the pultrusion process (with the rovings) in order to ensure
adequate mechanical properties in the transverse direction of the resulting pultruded
elements.
The mats are conformed by continuously ﬁlaments positioned in random directions
and bonded with resin.
Because of the random arrangement of the ﬁbers, it is
considered that a composite laminate whose reinforcement is a mat is an isotropic
material. According to this, the mats are used in the pultrusion process in order to
increase the transverse mechanical properties of the plate-like parts of the pultruded
elements.
The glass ﬁber fabrics are used in the pultrusion elements in order to ensure mechan-
ical properties in diﬀerent directions, that can not be provided by the rovings. This
reinforcement is the responsible of providing the transverse mechanical properties
to the pultruded elements.
2.1.2
Resin system
Part of the FRP composite material responsible for maintaining the reinforcing ﬁbers
bonded together in the desired direction. They can be divided into two main groups
the thermosetting and thermoplastic polymers. The thermosetting polymers have
strong atomic bonds so after the curing process, the shape of the composite material
cannot be changed by heating the resin. On the contrary, the thermoplastic polymers
have weak bonds so they can be reformed after cured by reheating the composite
element.
The polymer resin are considered an isotropic material with good insulating proper-
ties and acceptable ﬁre ratings when mixed with ﬁre retarding additives. However,
it is important to mention, like the glass ﬁber, the polymer resins can present creep
and relaxation.

2.2.1
Geometric parameters
The orthotropic characteristics and brittle behaviour of the composite materials
added to the complexity of the stress distribution around the bolt holes, lead to a
lack of analytical methods for describing the response of bolted connections in GFRP
pultruded elements. Accordingly, and also due to the high inﬂuence of the geometry
in the joints behaviour, it is usual to deﬁne relationships between the geometric
parameters in order to ensure speciﬁc response characteristics of the connection, for
instance, the failure mode.
This relationships have been traditionally determined based on experimental inves-
tigations that evidence the critical geometric parameters and establish their speciﬁc
values for reaching a desired mechanical performance of the connection. Figure 1
summarize the most important parameters deﬁned as:
• longitudinal end distance e: Is one of the most important parameters in bolted
connections in pultruded composite materials. e highly inﬂuences the connec-
tion strength and, when it takes low values, is related to cleavage in-plane
failure mode of the GFRP elements, as it is described in section 2.2.2.
It
is also related to the ductility of the connection and the magnitude of the
structural fracture load.
• width w: As e, inﬂuences the connection strength and failure mode. When it
takes low values, is related to net-tension failure of the GFRP elements.
• hole diameter d0: Is the basic geometric parameter used for the deﬁnition of
the geometric ratios. It inﬂuences the connection strength and behaviour. It
is directly related to the bolt diameter, so aﬀects the connection stiﬀness. It
deﬁnes the bolt hole clearance so can highly inﬂuence the diﬀerence between
the damage load and the structural fracture load as it is shown in section 2.2.5.
• lateral edge distance e2: Is geometrically related to w so has high inﬂuence
in the connection strength and failure mode. It is usually related to the net-
tension failure and in some cases, to the cleavage failure as it is explained in
section 2.2.2.
• pitch and gauge length sh, sv: According to Coelho and Mottram [1], there is
not enough quantitative guidance for describing the eﬀect of these parameters
in the connections behaviour. However, in the case of sv, its inﬂuence in multi-
bolted connections strength can be easily identiﬁed since it is related to the
connections eﬀective width thus to the net-tension failure mode.

Figure 2: Typical failure modes of double-lap single-bolted pultruded GFRP connection,

6
==PAGE:12==
• Net tension failure: is described by a crack in the perpendicular direction of
the tension force, generated by insuﬃcient cross sectional area of the element
due to the bolt hole. Is a brittle failure mode and in the case of single-bolted
connection, is related mainly to low values of w.
• Cleavage (or splitting) failure: is described by a crack propagation in the
longitudinal direction of the tension force, from the bolt contact point to the
end of the pultruded member. Is a brittle failure mode and is related mainly
to low values of e. Can also present a crack in the perpendicular direction
from the bolt hole to the side edge of the pultruded material, generated after
the longitudinal splitting due to stress concentration around the bolt hole in
this loading state.
• Bearing failure: is described by local crushing and delamination of the GFRP
material at the bolt contact region. It is considered a ductile failure mode
because it develops gradually and allows to identify the failure occurrence
before the ultimate failure so is normally preferred over the other failure modes.
However, it requires an appropriate combination of w and e values in order to
occur.
• Shear-out failure: is described by two cracks in the longitudinal direction of
the tension force along planes starting from the bolt hole edge to the end of the
pultruded member. Is a brittle failure mode and is related to low values of e.
Can also occur after a bearing failure of the connection due to the longitudinal
displacement of the bolt and the crushed pultruded material. Is also a frequent
failure mode in multiple row bolted connection, with the cracks propagation
between two bolt holes aligned in the loading direction.
The occurrence of an speciﬁc failure mode depends on the geometry of the con-
nection, thus researchers and manufacturers traditionally have recommended ratios
between the geometric parameters of the connection in order to generate the bearing
failure mode and avoid the potential occurrence of brittle failure modes.
Rosner and Rizkalla [4] completed an experimental program composed of 102 ten-
sion tests on double-lap single-bolted connections in GFRP specimens. The authors
varied the following geometric ratios of the connection: w/d0, and e/d0. The speci-
mens were tested until reaching the failure and the failure mode was analyzed as well
as the load-displacement response. The results of the experimental program were
summarized in graphs relating the geometric ratios, the ultimate failure loads and
the failure type. Figure 3 taken from the work of Coelho and Mottram [1] presents
the results for two thickness of the GFRP element (in the ﬁgure, C: Cleavage, B:
Bearing, NT: Net-tension).

2.2.3
Behaviour of double-lap single-bolted connection in GFRP pul-
truded elements
Figure 4 shows the typical load-displacement response of a double-lap single-bolted
GFRP connection under tensile load. The response is characterized by a large initial
bolt displacement for low loading with a non-linear load-displacement relationship.
This initial behaviour is related to the ﬁtting of the connection parts and is highly
inﬂuenced by the bolt hole clearance.
After the bolt contacts the edge of the plates and the connection parts adjust com-
pletely, the load-displacement relationship is characterized by a linear region whose
gradient is deﬁned by Cooper and Turvey [5] as the initial stiﬀness of the connection.
In the case of the bearing failure mode, the linear region is interrupted at a speciﬁc
loading value deﬁned by Woo et al. [3] as the local failure load. At this point the
GFRP connection suﬀers a sudden drop in the load, or a signiﬁcant reduction of
the stiﬀness that is characterized by an irreversible bearing damage, so Cooper and
Turvey [5] deﬁne this load as the damage load.
After the local failure load (or damage load) the load-displacement relationship is
described by an unstable behaviour related to a progressive damage of the composite
material, until reaching the failure of the connection. The loading related to the
failure of the connection is is deﬁned by Woo et al. [3] as the structural fracture load
and by Cooper and Turvey [5] as the failure load.
It is important to mention, in the case of brittle failure modes (net-tension, cleavage
or shear out) the maximum load is deﬁned as the failure load, and the concept of
local failure (or damage failure) is not used.

2.2.4
Symmetry of the connection
The inﬂuence of non-symmetric conditions in lap-joint connections in GFRP pul-
truded elements can be observed in the response of Single-lap joints. Turvey [6] and
Turvey and Godé [7] developed an experimental investigation on the ultimate load of
pultruded GFRP composite single-lap single-bolted connections under tension. The
eﬀect of the geometry of the connection was analyzed in a set of 45 tests varying
the ratios width to hole diameter w/d and end distance to hole diameter e/d. The
results were presented in forms of graphs showing the average connection resistance
for the diﬀerent ratios under analysis. In addition, the experimental investigation
also showed a complex failure mode related not only to the displacement of the joint
components within the plane of the specimens as occurs in symmetric double-lap
conﬁgurations, but also to rotations of the bolt about the plane of contact between
the plates of the connection. This behaviour is generated by the eccentricity of the
load path.
2.2.5
Bolt hole clearance
Woo et al. [3] studied the eﬀect of the bolt-hole clearance in the failure mode of
pultruded FRP double-lap single-bolted connections under tension. Several sizes
of bolt-hole clearance where studied in a range from 0 mm to 3mm, using e that
ensures the occurrence of bearing type failure mode. The results showed for bolt
hole clearances values between 0 mm and 3mm the local failure load (damage level)
decreases as the bolt hole clearance increases. In the same range, the structural
9
==PAGE:15==
fracture loads did not show a clear trend.
In the same way Yuan et al. [8] developed experimental investigation on the con-
nection strength of double-lap bolted connections varying the bolt hole clearance
within a range from 0 mm to 6,4 mm. The results showed a substantial reduction
in the connection strength for the local failure load, which agreed with the results
obtained by Woo et al. [3].
2.2.6
Bolt threads
Hizam et al.
[9] evaluated the eﬀect of bolt threads on the strength of double-
lap single-bolted connections of pultruded GFRP elements studying 70 connections
specimens. The authors compared the strength of connections under tension using
bolts with threaded and smooth shanks as fasteners. Reductions in joints capacity
was observed when using threaded bolts in contact with the GFRP bolt-hole edge
due to severe damage of the ﬁbers and delamination of the GFRP pultruded material
in the contact zone.
2.2.7
Bolt torque
The inﬂuence of the bolt torque conditions in the behaviour of GFRP pultruded
beam with spliced joints have been studied by Turvey and Cerutti [10]. Fifteen
ﬂexure tests (3 point ﬂexural tests) on simply supported GFRP wide ﬂanges beams
with single-lap and double-lap bolted spliced joints in the mid-span were tested.
GFRP pultruded plates bolted to the ﬂanges of the GFRP beams were used to
created the spliced joints and the connection bolts were torqued to 3 Nm, 20 Nm,
and 30 Nm. The tests were developed for the serviceability limit state conditions,
which means the tests ﬁnished when the mid-span deﬂection of the beams reaches an
speciﬁc value. The results showed the short term transverse stiﬀness of the spliced
beam increase linearly with the bolt torque regardless the use of single or double-lap
bolted joints.
2.3
Forces distribution in bolted connections with multiple
bolts
The distribution of forces acting on the bolts of the connection are calculated based
on the theory of elasticity applied to plates loaded by pure bending moment and
connected by a group of bolts.
10
==PAGE:16==
Due to the thickness and elastic modulus of the materials it is considered the steel
plates and the bolts are rigid compare to the pultruded GFRP structural proﬁle,
thus the rotation between the steel plates and the GFRP parts is deﬁned by the
GFRP deformations.
According to the above, the following assumption are deﬁned:
• The GFRP proﬁles rotate around the center of rotation of the bolt group that
connect them to the steel plates.
• The force acting over a bolt is perpendicular to a straight line between the
bolt center and the center of rotation of the bolt group.
• The deformations at the bolts locations in the GFRP proﬁles, can be consid-
ered as a small deformation case.
• The center of rotation of the bolt group is located at its centroid.

Experimental investigations
In order to analyse the behaviour of bolted connections in GFRP pultruded struc-
tural proﬁles under bending moments, a four-point bending test is selected for testing
the GFRP pultruded I-shaped beams with bolted splice joints in the middle point.
The characteristics of this kind of test and bolted connection allows to study the
beams behaviour under vertical loads while the connection located in the middle
span is subjected only to bending stresses.
3.1
General description of the test setup
As it is mentioned previously, a four-point ﬂexural test is selected for testing the
GFRP pultruded spliced beams. Figure 6 presents a general description of the test
12
==PAGE:18==
assembly including the location of the loading points, the spliced joint, and the
measurement devices.
Figure 6: General description of the test setup
The beams vertical deﬂections are measured at six points along the beams length,
three on each GFRP pultruded element (left and right) following a symmetrical array
about the beams mid-span plane. The three measurement points on each pultruded
element are referenced according to their locations as middle point, intermediate
point, and loading point. The deﬂections are obtained using six LVDTs supported in
a rigid steel element below the GFRP spliced beams which allows to place the LVDTs
tips in contact with the bottom ﬂange of the beams. The LVDTs measurements
δi,j are identiﬁed based on the location along the beam (middle point-i = mp,
intermediate point-i = ip, and loading point-i = lp) and on the corresponding
pultruded element (left-j = l, and right-j = r).
The load is applied by an electro-mechanical universal testing machine provided
with a 100 kN capacity load cell connected to a steel beam that transfers the load to
the top ﬂanges of the pultruded GFRP spliced beam using circular steel rods. The
even distribution of the load between the two GFRP elements of the spliced beam in
a deformed condition is ensured by connecting the load cell to the steel beam with
a set of hemispherical steel elements that allows the loading beam to rotate. Figure
7 presents an image of the actual test setup.

3.2
Spliced beams
As it is mentioned previously, the spliced beams used in the tests are formed by two
GFRP pultruded proﬁles connected at the mid span by bolted joints.
Two types of spliced joints are analysed in this project, the ﬁrst deﬁned only by
bolted connections in the web and the second by bolted connection in the ﬂanges.

the potential failure mode of single-lap joints related to out of plane bolt rotations
as is explained in section 2.2.4, according to the studies by Turvey [6] and Turvey
and Godé [7]. This kind of behaviour in beams under transverse forces can lead to
instability phenomenon as lateral torsional buckling.

3.2.3
Drilling process and bolt holes clearance in the GFRP pultruded
material.
Based on the studies by Yuan [8] and Woo [3] explained in the section 2.2.5, it is
decided to drill the bolt holes in the GFRP proﬁles with a clearance of 0,1 mm,
which corresponds to a tight-ﬁt clearance (no-clearance) situation.
This ensures
an appropriate performance of the connection for the local failure load (damage
level) and avoids potential decrements in the connection strength related to large
hole clearance. In addition, using low bolt hole clearance reduces the undesirable
relative movement between the parts of the connection in the spliced beams that
highly inﬂuence their vertical deﬂection, mainly in the initial loading stage of the
tests.
17
==PAGE:23==
According to the above, plastic templates are used for marking the bolt locations
on the GFRP proﬁles before the drilling process. In this way, the precision of the
drilling process increases and the possibility of alignment problems of the bolt-holes
between the parts of the connection is reduced.
3.2.4
Bolts and connection characteristics.
In the joints bolted in the web, 10 mm diameter steel bolts are used as fasteners,
while in the case of bolted connections in the ﬂanges, 6 mm diameter steel bolts
are selected. In both cases, the connections are provided with washers between the
contact of the steel plates with the bolts head and with the nuts.
In both joints type, the bolts are selected with smooth shanks for avoiding the
contact between the GFRP bolt holes and the bolts threads. As it is explained
in section 2.2.6, this contact can lead to reductions in the connection strength as
presented by Hizam et al. [9].
Two levels of bolt torque are analysed in the project, in order to identify the bolt
torque eﬀect in the behaviour of the connection. The ﬁrst one, named pin-bearing
condition torque, is related to the hand tightening of the bolts. The second, named
snug-tight condition torque, is achieved when a person use full eﬀort and an ordinary
spud wrench to tension the bolt (according to the AISC deﬁnition).
The speciﬁc bolt tension is not measured in the tests, however, the same bolts
installation procedure is followed in the entire set of tests in order to reduce any
potential change in the bolt tension conditions between two tests.

3.3.3
Loading characteristics
In the entire set of test it is used a displacement controlled loading ratio of 2
mm/min, in order to avoid sudden high increments of the load, which can occur
mainly in the last part of the tests, after the damage load is reached and the stiﬀ-
ness of the spliced beams is reduced.
In the case of beams with bolted connections in the ﬂanges, the described loading
condition is constant during the entire test duration. However, in the case of the
beams with bolted connections in the web the load is applied up to 3 kN where two
loading and unloading cycles are applied. After reaching 3 kN again in the second
loading cycle, the loading ratio of 2mm/min is again kept constant until the failure.

4.2
Analysis of the spliced beams symmetry
The symmetry analysis of the spliced beam deformed shape allows to identify not
only imprecision in the test set-up but also changes in the beams behaviour related
to the diﬀerent stages of the connection failure development. In addition, this study
can be also used as a tool for concluding which is the most appropriated way of
using the deﬂection data obtained from the tests.
Any error in the location of the beams supports, loading points, and measurement
devices is directly reﬂected in a non-symmetric response of the deformation of the
beam. In a similar way, any diﬀerence between the mechanical properties and con-
nections conditions of the left and right GFRP proﬁles generates diﬀerences between
their deﬂections.
Due to this, the variable Sratio is deﬁned as the ratio between the deﬂection of the left
part of the spliced beam and the corresponding deﬂection in the right part (δi,l/δi,r).
In the case of perfect symmetry, Sratio takes a constant value of 1.

Region 1: This region can be related to the behaviour of the steel plates and GFRP

GFRP is high enough to avoid, at least partially, their relative sliding. The Kvert
of this zone are the largest of the curve, which means the bending stiﬀness of the
connection is also the largest. The Kvert analysis presented in section 4.3.2 give
signs of this behaviour.

Kvert in this region and the corresponding to the loading leg of the cycling load

zone. This show the deﬂection behaviour for both zones is mainly controlled by the
same parameters, even though the loading and deﬂection ranges are diﬀerent.
Due to the above, in order to analyse the inﬂuence of the friction, three tests were
developed for diﬀerent connection conﬁgurations under pin-bearing condition torque
(hand tightening) that aﬀects directly the friction. The results are presented in the
section 4.3.4.
Region 2: This region can be related to a continuous sliding between the steel
plates and the GFRP that reduces progressively the bending stiﬀness (EI) of the
connection. Due to the bolt holes clearance used in the steel plates, and the precision
of the holes drilling in the GFRP, this sliding can be activated when at least one of
the bolts settle against the bolt holes edge in the steel plates. The P −δ response in
this region is leaded not only by the friction between the faces of the steel plates and
the pultruded elements, but also by the friction generated by the contact between
the bolt with the GFRP and the steel bolt holes.

1 and Region 2 is similar to the initial zone of the load-displacement response of
single-bolted connections.
Region 3: Once all the bolts touch the steel plates, and all the parts of the con-
nection settle, the P −δ response can be approximately described again by a linear
relationship. Kvert is lower than the one of the ﬁrst region and can be said that is
leaded mainly by the stress-strain relationship around the bolt holes in the pultruded
elements. This can be presume taking into account the magnitude of the bolt forces
acting over the GFRP and each steel plate, the GFRP and steel thicknesses, and
the elastic modulus of both materials. This region is related to local deformations of
the GFRP around the bolt holes that can reach a local failure, but without reaching
a global failure or a total instability of the connection.
Based on the behaviour of double-lap single-bolted connections described in section
2.2.3, this region can be related to the linear zone presented in Figure 4. Accordingly,
The loading value related to the end of this region has a similar meaning to the
damage load for double-lap single-bolted connection described in section 2.2.3.
Assuming deﬂection limits in the Serviceability Limit State about L/200 = 6mm,
in all the tests with four bolts connection (for each GFRP element - eight in total),
the limit deﬂection occurs in this region. This means, the connections behaviour
related to Regions 1, 2, and 3 controls the fulﬁllment of the deﬂection requirements
in SLS.
Due to the explained above, understanding the connection behaviour in this Region
is crucial for estimating the spliced beams response in the SLS, that in the most of
29
==PAGE:35==
the cases is the leading requirement of the beams design.
Region 4: This region can be related to the occurrence of cracks in the GFRP
material located around the bolt holes that trigger the beginning of the global failure.
After the cracks appear, as the bolt forces increases, the size and length of the
cracks rises and generates stress concentrations in the connection that result in
deformations increments.
If after the appearance or enlargement of a crack the
connection has the capability of resettling and creating a new loading path through
the bolts and the GFRP material, the connection is able of taking additional load
without sudden changes in geometry or material properties, and its behaviour can
be approximately linear again. At some loading point, the stresses on the GFRP are
again higher than the resistance of the local material and the process repeats. In
consequence, it can be said the number of linear-like behaviour zones within Region
4 is directly related to the connection capability of creating new loading paths, which
is known in structural systems as internal redundancy.
For the purpose of identifying the main parameters that inﬂuence the connection
internal redundancy, the number of linear zones in Region 4 (deducted from Table
6) are compared with the geometric parameters of the connections. This comparison
show the inﬂuence of the ratio e/d.
For connections with the same number of bolts, as e/d reduces the number of linear
zones in Region 4 tends to reduce. C7 is an example of this trend since it has the
same connection conﬁguration than C1 and C8, and a lower e/d ratio (e/d = 2), but
does not have linear zones in Region 4, while C1 and C8 have 1 linear zone. This
trend is expected since, as is explained in section 2.2.2, for e/d<4 a brittle failure
modes is expected and, in the case of C7 this kind of failure mode interfere with the
redistribution of loads. This is also observed in the conﬁguration C5.

5
Conclusion
The connection response is conceptually characterized by the P −δ charts of the
spliced beams. Diﬀerent behaviour regions are deﬁned based on the P −δ charts and
each region is controlled mainly by an speciﬁc parameter of the connection. The
regions with linear P −δ responses can be associated to approximately constant
values of the rotation stiﬀness of the connection.
It is shown the ﬁrst behaviour region is highly inﬂuenced by the friction (bolt torque)
between the steel plates and the FRP elements. In the case of bolted connections in
the web with two bolts per each pultruded element, large deﬂections (> L/200) can
occur in the ﬁrst region, which is a disadvantage due to the deﬂection limits related
to the SLS.
The behaviour Region 3 is related to an approximately linear P −δ response leaded
mainly by the stress-strain relationship around the bolt holes in the pultruded el-
ements.
This region is of important interest as it is likely to be related to the
fulﬁlment of the SLS deﬂections requirements. In addition, its upper loading value
can be deﬁned as the damage load of the connection. According to the P −δ charts,
the vertical stiﬀness of the spliced beams, in this region is not highly aﬀected by the
friction.
The number of linear zones in the P −δ curves after the Region 3, can be related to
the ability of the connection to redistribute the forces once the initial crack occurs.
As it is mentioned in the section 2.2.2 this capability is inﬂuenced by the ratio e/d
(in order to avoid brittle failures) and by the number of bolt columns that can create
alternative load paths in the connection (internal redundancy).
The bolt hole clearance and bolt torque can inﬂuence the general behaviour of the
spliced beams since control the initial response (displacements and rotations) that
highly inﬂuence the later behaviour of the connection, mainly the deﬂection in SLS
design conditions.
Regarding the spliced joints with bolted connections in the webs, the results of the
vertical stiﬀness of the spliced beams in the behaviour Regions 3 and 4 present
considerable variations that can be generated due to the inﬂuence of the beam
rotation at the beginning of the linear-like behaviour zone. However, the general
trend analysis show the inﬂuence of bolt group geometry in the vertical stiﬀness
since the average value for connections of Group 3 (the group with highest bolt row
separation) results in stiﬀness about 39% larger than those related to the Groups 2,
using the same number of bolts.
As it is expected, the vertical stiﬀness of the spliced beams with ﬂange bolted connec-
tions is larger than the corresponding to spliced beams with web bolted connections.
48
==PAGE:54==
The main parameters that inﬂuence the vertical stiﬀness of the spliced beams and
the connection behaviour are the length and the thickness of the steel plates.
In the case of spliced joints with bolted connections in the webs, it is possible to
conclude the spliced beam failure is controlled by the connection strength.
The
failure modes obtained in the tests agree with those of double-lap single-bolted
connections presented in the technical literature and the corresponding geometric
ratios recommendations seems to be applicable.
However, in the case of joints with bolted connections in the ﬂanges, for several
tests, elastic instabilities (lateral torsional buckling and local buckling of the web)
occurs before the failure of the connection, this can be due to the reduced restraints
in the web of pultruded elements created in the connection.
The inﬂuence of the bolt hole clearance in the connection response, is an important
parameter to be studied since directly aﬀects the initial stiﬀness of the connection.
The bolt torque and friction eﬀect on the connections behaviour requires a better
understanding since, can improve the connection stiﬀness and strength. However,
special considerations should be taken into account, since the increment of normal
force through the use of bolt torque can generate changes on the long term behaviour
of the connection due to the creep suceptibility of the GFRP material.
