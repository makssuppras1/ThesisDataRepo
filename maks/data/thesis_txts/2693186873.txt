Abstract
Computer assisted navigation of a marine vessel requires reliable information
about surrounding objects at sea.
The project will investigate methods for
analysis of data from W-band radar units that operates in the 75-79 GHz
range and covers distances up to 110 m with cm resolution. Such radar units
are developed for use in cars and for traﬃc surveillance, and the manufacturer
has no experience with use at sea. The project investigates the hypothesis that
these radar units could be useful to help determine a known docking area and
ﬁnd structures and objects in a harbour area that should be avoided, such as
piers, mooring poles and other marine craft. The thesis employs methods for
analysis of clusters and transforms that are meant to ﬁnd geometric shapes to
investigate to what extent the W-band radar units could be used to assist as
sensor for docking and manoeuvring inside a harbour area.

Sammenfatning
Computer assisteret navigation af et skib kræver pålidelig information om an-
dre fartøjer og objekter i dets omgivelser. Projektet vil undersøge metoder
for analyse af data fra MM-bånd radarenheder som opererer i 75-79GHZ om-
rådet og dækker afstande op til 110m med cm opløsning. Disse radarenheder
er udviklet til brug i biler og traﬁk overvågning men fabrikanterne af radaren-
hederne har ingen erfaring med brugen af disse til søs. Projektet vil undersøge
den hypotese at disse radarenheder kan være brugbare som en hjælp til at
bestemme kendte steder hvor skibe kan ligge til kaj og ﬁnde strukturer samt
objekter i havneområder som skal undviges, så som moler, fortøjningspæle og
andet marinefartøj. Specialet anvender metoder til analyse af cluster og trans-
formationer, der er beregnet til at ﬁnde geometriske former for at undersøge,
i hvilket omfang, MM-bånd radarenheder kan bruges som sensor til at ﬁnde
pladser hvor skibet kan lægge til kaj og manøvrering inde i et havneområde.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
This chapter is the introduction to the exploration of the usage of W-band
radars at sea. How the system performs at sea in terms of object localisation
and detection based on data from two W-band automotive radar used for guid-
ance/navigation at sea on an autonomous level. The chapter will include the
motivation behind the project as well as the problem statement and project de-
scription. Furthermore the scope and methodology of the project are explained
and ﬁnally, an overview of the thesis structure is presented.
1.1
Motivation
ShippingLab is a non-proﬁt innovation project, which targets to develop an
autonomous and environmental friendly ship in Denmark. The autonomous
ship has to be compliant in all aspects of autonomous navigation.
For decades ships have been sailing between the same destinations going
back and forth in the navigational waters around the world. Often ships sail in
a beeline from point A to point B. This is a tedious process which is expensive
and could result in unawareness or unfocused behaviour and could probably
be solved with autonomous navigation at sea. As an aid to the captain in case
of the aforementioned unawareness to guide or alarm if the vessel is going oﬀ
course or an obstacle is present or passing. Even guidance with manoeuvring
in harbours and spaces with higher density of obstacles.
In order to help with navigation and the situational awareness; a system
comprised of diﬀerent sensors could be implemented on a platform.
These
sensors could help with tracking distances and speeds of surrounding obstacles
and/or objects. Determine the size and shape of the obstacles in lightings where
the captain most likely does not have the visuals to determine such objects or
obstacles with bare sight.
==PAGE:20==
2
1 Introduction
Other than being an eﬀective help to navigators at sea, this also seeks to
sustainable navigation since the likelihood that something will go wrong during
a voyage is to be minimised if the system is fault tolerant.
1.2
Problem statement
In order to acquire and process data from voyages, a platform system consist-
ing of sensors should be implemented. Luckily the ShippingLab project has
already an installation of the aforementioned system with sensors and cam-
eras explicitly for thus purpose. The system has two W-band radars which are
normally used in automobiles, to test the capability of these radar units this
project is to test the possibility of those sensors at sea. Since the limitations
at sea are that the distances are greater between objects than those on the road.
Radar sensors eliminate the issue with lighting thus allowing for constant
awareness regardless if the sun is up or not.
X-band radars are utilised at
sea regardless, however the resolution of these radars are unmatched with the
resolution of w-band radars. Since the manufactures of said radars have no
experience with the sensors at sea the usage for these radar units at sea is a
new territory and should be explored.
Currently the exploration of W-band radar usage at sea is limited at should
be investigated. With the advances in clustering and image processing for visual
recognition, detection and optimised sensors in the last decade, a system for
collision avoidance, navigation and, localisation of piers, mooring poles and
other marine craft could be beneﬁcial for navigation at sea.
1.3
Project description
Following from the previous section the purpose of this project is to utilise
the two available radars and investigate the possibilities of these at sea. The
units are two W-band radars operating at 75-79 GHz range with centimetre
resolution.
Radars omit echos and those are returned if an object has a reﬂective sur-
face, these echos are represented in a point cloud. The two W-band radars
have not only an azimuth but also an elevation and velocity information. From

that the echo point cloud will have three dimensions and information regarding
velocity, so four dimensions if strictly looking at the available data. This will

Information regarding location and shape. Trying to match the shape of

object detection. Furthermore, utilising the information regarding speed the
case where two or more objects might be indistinguishable the speed informa-
tion could separate the two or more objects by creating a class or distinction
between objects that are mobile or stationary.
The cloud data provided by the radar is used to deﬁne the project goals:
• Cluster diﬀerent objects in radar echos.
• Location and shape of diﬀerent objects should be estimated from radar
echos.
• Estimate dimensions (geometric length) and poses of objects detected.
• Relative speeds and distances of each object in the radar range.
The shapes that are to be found are limited to lines, circles and arbitrary shapes
that do not fall under any speciﬁc category.
Location should be given by the objects centre, dependent on the objects
shape. If the object is a line it should be deﬁned as three points, the centre
and the end points. Figure 1.1.a shows the line deﬁnition. The pose of the line
is deﬁned by the distance to the observer to the closest point on the line. The
bearing from observer to the line should be estimated every frame thus allow-
ing for the observer to constantly have the knowledge of the position of said line.

1.4
Method
For clustering of the data, three diﬀerent clustering algorithm will be inves-
tigated, namely the K-means cluster, Hierarchical cluster and Density Based
Spatial Clustering of Application with Noise also known by its acronym DB-
SCAN.

For detection of diﬀerent shapes the Hough Transform is tested. Hough
Transform is an algorithm with many branches this includes ﬁnding lines, cir-
cles, ellipses and/or arbitrary shapes in images. The Hough Transform(s) are
normally used in high quality images where features are easy to spot for the
human eye, incomparable to that of radar echo which can be hard for humans
to identify objects purely based on the point cloud.
Moreover, the project investigate diﬀerent methods how to diﬀerentiate be-
tween objects. One of these are image moments (HU moments), which classiﬁes
objects using moments which in turn are rotation and translation invariant [1].
Since the data has a third dimension the project expands on the concept of
image moments which are used on images in 2D, to Zernike moments which
are used in 3D point clouds [2]. These moments allow for selective pattern
recognition of the created clusters, thus allowing for the distinguished feature
extraction.
The method for extracting location and shape of an object is generated by
clustering algorithms. This should allow for pattern recognition. This project
will investigate which methods of object recognition, based on the shapes pro-
vided by the clustering, could be feasible for this project.
One method of
feature extraction are moment invariants, also known as HU moments [3]. Al-
though HU-moments was initially based on 2D objects, further research has
shows that HU moments can be used on N-dimensional data (in theory). Di-
mension, pose, relative speed and distance should be estimated by utilising
mathematical methods.

ROS
The recordings of the voyage from the radars were stored in a ROS Bag. Smart-
Micro has a build in ROS driver for their radar units and this software is used
to record data in a given timeframe/recording session. ROS is an open-source,
meta operating system for robots in the lines of other robot frameworks such
as CARMEN, Microsoft Robotic Studio and among others [5]. ROS services it
what you would expect from other operating systems services such as low-level
device control (this includes setting up the range control given in Table 2.2),
hardware abstraction, package mangement, message-passing between processes
and implementation of commonly-used functionality. ROS have several usages
in robotics, it can be used to manipulate robots, establish communication be-
tween robots, signal processing, mapping, etc. but for this project is it purely
used for data acquisition.
ROS is built on three concepts there is the,
1. Filesystem Level
2. Computational Graph Level
3. and the Community Level
The ﬁrst two are main concepts of ROS while the Community Level is what the
name suggest a community of ROS users where they can exchange knowledge
and software including the oﬃcial ROS wiki website [5].
3.2.1
ROS Filesystem Level
The Filesystem level covers primarily ROS resources such as,
Packages: Packages are the primary unit for software organisation in ROS.
ROS packages contains runtime processes (nodes), ROS library, datasets, ﬁles
for conﬁguration or anything which is useful for organisation.

design behind ROS is that it ought to be modular at a ﬁne-grained scale; usu-
ally, robot control systems are comprised of many nodes. For example one
node controls the range of the radar, onde node controls the frequency of the
radar and another node controls the radar transformation (TF) e.g transfor-

shape or number, in such a way that they are easily diﬀerentiable.
Clustering is commonly known as an entire collection of clusters. Several
types of clustering have been suggested. Each has speciﬁc features and beneﬁts
and areas of disadvantages. The clustering method are comprised of various
types:

• Hierarchical clustering

b) Divisive hierarchical clustering

In the case where the data is represented as a graph e.g
the points follow the concept that each node is closer to at least one node in its
cluster than to any other node. An important example is contiguity-based

Clusters of dense region of points located in a low density
region. The low density region which is not a part of any clusters are usually

DBSCAN
Establishing that the radar data is mostly the density based and of arbitrary
shape the third option for this project is the DBSCAN. DBSCAN is an acronym
for Density Based Spatial Clustering of Application with Noise.
The DBSCAN algorithm was ﬁrst introduced in 1996 by M. Ester et al. [9].
Density-based clustering ﬁnds regions of higher density that within a space of
lower density. DBSCAN works with any distance functions among the most
common is the Euclidian distance. The DBSCAN algorithm has two tuning
parameters. Firstly, ϵ or Esp which is the maximum radius of a point in the
point cloud for it to be associated with other points. Secondly, MinPts which is
the minimum amount of points in the ϵ radius for it to be considered a cluster.
Points outside clusters are considered outlier and/or noise. But some deﬁnitions
has to be deﬁned because there are diﬀerent type of points.
Deﬁnition 1: (Eps-neighbroheed of a point) The Eps-neighborhood of a
point p, denoted by NEsp(q), is deﬁned as
NEsp(q) : {p ∈D | dist(p, q) ≤Esp}
From the literature the ﬁrst approach was: if each point in a cluster has at least
a speciﬁed MinPts number of points in the Eps-neighborhood distance within
a single point. However, this approach is naive because a point in clusters are
not that simple. There are two kinds of points in a cluster. A border point,
which is located at the very border of the Eps-neighborhood distance from the
center point or core point. Additionally a point that is not a border point or a
core point must be a noise point. Thus, the three points can be deﬁned as,
• Core points a point is a core point if it has at least MinPts within Esp
from it self.
• Boder a border point has at least one Core point at a distance Esp.

• Noise is a point that is neither a core nor a border point. It has less
than MinPts in a distance Esp within it self.
By deﬁnition a border point contains notably less points within the Eps-

every point p in a cluster C there is a point q in C such that p is within Eps-

Deﬁnition 4: (density-connected) A point p is density-connected to a

density-reachable from o wrt. Eps and MinPts.

5.1
Hough Transformation
The Hough transform (HT) [10] is a location technique used on images to
ﬁnd shapes. Three of the major uses have shown to be line, circle and ellipse
extraction. The Hough Transform was initially invented for machine analysis
in 1959 and later patented by Paul V.C. Hough in 1962 [10]. The HT was
implemented by Duda et al. [11] to ﬁnd lines in images and has since been
improved and extended greatly [12][13][14][15].
The HT technique is based
on a gathering of evidence. For instance, if the feature is a line in an image,
the HT would cast a vote for each feature, where a line is found. Then, the
accumulated space (Hough space) is the mapping of image line points onto the
Hough space.

increases as well. Since the circumference of the ellipse is not being voted like
it is with CHT where each intersection with the circle gets voted on. With
ellipse it is the centre, minor and major axis and the orientation of the ellipse
is voted on. The Hough transform can also be used to ﬁnd any given shape in
an image. This is called the Generalised Hough Transform (GHT).

The result of this work has been presented.
The tested methodologies was
compared to the three selected scenes with camera footage.
First the DBSCAN algorithm was tested on 2D and compared to the 3D, the
diﬀerence in clusters showed a small diﬀerence which was the cause of manip-

is used in 3D thus, giving slight variation in the clusters. The 2D DBSCAN
showed promise in ﬁnding objects that where obviously separated, but some
issues when objects where relatively close to each other. This is a result of
choosing the most optimal Esp. This was especially true in scene 3.
Secondly the PHT was tested on the data, ﬁnding the canny edges of the
points in the plot showed that on a pixel level the algorithm might have been
performing better since it made multiple lines in areas where there should only
be one line. This could have been avoided by transforming the point cloud into
a pixel-map, however for the purpose of ﬁnding connecting lines between the
points that where separated by a predeﬁned distance the algorithm performed
very well. The algorithm has a parameter that determines the distance between
points before it has to create a line, this showed that determining the distance
is a factor which has to be accounted for by either determining the width of
the observing ship in order to correctly disregard points that are further apart
than that of the observing ship thus allowing for passage between objects that
would be considered safe.
Thirdly the CHT was also tested and was performing as expected. If there
was an indication that a circle was present in the point cloud, whether i was
obvious or not, the algorithm drew a circle because it found one. Here the
threshold had to be set relatively low, since the only real circular object (which
was not small docking poles) was the mooing pier in scene two. Since radars
does not receive echos of the entirety of an object but only the surface facing
the radars, the threshold had to be set low in order to ﬁnd objects that resem-
bled circles. The limitations with CHT is the aforementioned threshold and
the user has to specify the radii which are desired to ﬁnd.
Lastly the CvH was implemented, it was used in combination with the PHT
in order to get the enclosure of objects that wasn’t recognised otherwise. The
algorithm could be implemented on each of the clusters found by the DBSCAN

7.1
Conclusion
The main objective for this thesis has been to explore and develop methodolo-
gies for detecting objects in radar echo point clouds by using W-band radars,
which only has been tested by manufactures in cars and traﬃc surveillance, at
sea. For testing the sensor platform system was setup on a ship and was voy-
aging between the docking piers in Faaborg harbour. The two W-band radars
was set to record on a structure where they where mounted 90 degrees from
one another on the railing of the ship which was approximately 1.7m above sea
level. The radars was recording in short range mode, meaning that they where
projecting a straight beam in the range 0.15m to 19.3m with ±65◦covering in
total 220◦FoV with an overlap of 40◦.
Available for comparing radar echos with camera images was a single HikVi-
sion RBG camera recording in the ahead direction, covering (according to the
data-sheet) 109.2◦, thus unfortunately does not cover the combined radar FoV.
This was accounted for by taking frames from the camera approximately 4
seconds before the radar image allowing for visual comparison of the objects of
interest. The camera was mounted on the top of the ship’s bridge.
The ﬁrst approach of this work was to compute everything from the avail-
able 3D radar echo point cloud. The data playback showed scenes of clearly
visible navy vessels, sailboats, mooring piers and docking stations. But from

further analysis of the data in 3D showed that the true heights of the objects
was incoherent with the real values, this is not measured but since the hori-
zontal axis showed maximum values of -2m and 2m, it can be assumed that
objects where taller than that. This corresponds to the radars elevational FoV
are ±7.5◦the information about objects heights was then invalid information.
Thus, the analysis was done in 2D.
From that starting point the DBSCAN algorithm showed prominent re-
sults compared to K-means clustering and Agglomerative Hierarchical cluster-
ing methods since it could cluster an arbitrary amount of cluster and not forced
to stick with a predeﬁned value for K. Throughout the three scenes objects
that where separated when observing the camera footage, where separated with
the DBSCAN algorithm, except a few cases where objects where visibly close to
one and another. As could be seen in scene 1 with the docking pole behind the
sailboats rear, and with the pier that the navy vessel was docking up against
in scene 3. Overall the performance of the DBSCAN algorithm fulﬁlled the
purpose of this work. Thus, the ﬁrst project goal was satisfactory.
When taking a closer look into the available data, here the topic is the
radial speed, it shown that objects radial velocity was depended on where the
object was positioned compared to own ship. This information is useful when
determining the relative speed at which objects move. By inspecting the radial
velocity it was shown that negative values indicated that objects where mov-
ing away, and positive values was for objects moving closer, thus the relative
velocity of every object was then found as was one of the project goals.
The implementation of the two diﬀerent Hough Transforms was well suited
for the purpose of this project, they where both able to detect their respective
shape and did it too a standard that was acceptable. Where the geometric
length and pose of each detected shape was given, thus fulﬁlling the second
project goal. The implementation of the convex hull, was decided to be imple-
mented since the PHT created several lines in areas with high density echos.
This allowed for a more complete estimate of the objects shape, since it convex
enclosed all the lines. The issue with this implementation is that if line where
detected far apart then, by applying convex hull on the entirety of the end
points for each line, would create polygons that does not match with any given
object.

7.2
Future work
Some of the future works have been addressed throughout the thesis and will
be addressed again in this section. Since most of the issues arose from not
being able to extract each individual cluster in the DBSCAN implementation,
thus an implementation of the algorithm where it is possible to process each
