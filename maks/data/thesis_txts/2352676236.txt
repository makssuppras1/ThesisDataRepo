Abstract 
The “Copenhagen Teknik- og Miljøforvaltningen (TMF)” has proposed to the Technical 
University of Denmark (DTU) - Management Engineering Department - a collaboration 
aiming at facing the miss-use of public street bins that are adopted by some 
householder as domestic garbage dumping site.  Streets and public areas of some 
districts of Copenhagen become dirty, regardless the efforts and resources devoted by 
the Municipality to keep clean the urban environment. Bins are stuffed with shopper 
bags plenty of un-sorted trash and even surrounded by them. The observed districts 
are lived by acculturated people, wealthy households, well-feeling with the 
environmental protection issues. Moreover, the domestic waste collection system is 
well running.  This evidence makes not easily understandable the householders’ bad 
behavior and negligence in keeping clean their habitat and pushed thinking at behavior 
design disciplines as a possible way useful to reduce the habit of the street bins’ miss-
use.  
The present thesis carried out a field research to size the problem and applied selected 
behavior design concepts to re-design the street bin function. This latter has been 
assumed as a part of a system involving several actors and thus able to implement 
functions, not only to collect trash.  The bin is designed to convey messages to citizens 
and to raise the sense of ownership of commons. Citizens are meant not to be passive 
actors, but also able to convey messages to the Municipality through IT devices. 
Persons in charge of street cleaning are not only meant as mere workers carrying out 
their service, but part of the society through the possibility to provide information to 
the administration of streets and public areas status. Officers from the Municipality are 
involved in formulating messages labeled on the bins and take care of citizens’ 
suggestions and claims to improve the habitat. The bin is also physically modified 
according behavioral and ergonomic needs. The designed system takes origin mainly 
from the sustainable design theory and in particular from the socially responsible 
behavior theory. These set of concepts are expected to both inducing the sustainable 
outputs of the bin use and fostering the responsibility of the user in respect of his or 
her behavior.

Foreword 
Some districts of Copenhagen suffer a miss use of public street bins. Often these bins 
are stuffed with waste coming from houses or assumed as a dumping site, thus 
surrounded by bags plenty of trash. Houses, buildings and condominium are provided 
with containers to collect domestic garbage, but appear to be disregarded by some 
householders who prefer to simply leave trash into public bins, which conﬁgures an 
evidence of a bad behavior. Of course, the public street bins are located in the street 
to collect “handy trash”, as street food packaging, for tourists, commuters and citizens. 
Both location and size of the street bins are obviously devoted to this “daily” walking 
use.  
The “Copenhagen Teknik- og Miljøforvaltningen (TMF)” has thus proposed to the DTU 
Management Engineering Department to cope with such a miss-use of public bins and 
with the problem of city districts becoming dirty, regardless of efforts and resources 
devoted to street cleaning.  
The direct and responsible involvement of citizens in keeping streets clean has been 
assumed of utmost importance by the Municipality and the DTU that, therefore, aimed 
at approaching the problem through the behavior design discipline.  
This thesis aims at designing proper devices that are expected to progressively change 
the bad habit of dumping domestic waste into public bins. Behavior design concepts 
and theories, as well as technical solutions adopted elsewhere, have been studied and 
applied in order to limit such a “bad behavior”. The purpose is to activate  the  
mechanism  by  which  the mind  reacts  to visual  and mechanical  stimuli,  translate all  
this  information  into  an eﬃcient way  to  prevent citizens from  lit  trash on the  
“street”  thus  mitigate  the bad  habits. 
The behavioral design study is limited to the “bad behavior” of Copenhagen’s citizens 
dumping non-separated domestic waste in the street bins.  The design thus focuses on 
the public street bins. So to do, external factors causing  the miss-behavior have been 
only touched upon, including reasons why garbage are  not  properly  separated at 
home for their proper dumping into  plastic, metal, glass, paper and organic fraction 
==PAGE:8==
 
8/85 
 
condominium containers. This specific set of information lacks of ad-hoc studies 
specifically carried out for Copenhagen; it requires most likely further investigations to 
be carried out by researchers other than designers.  
The design exercise took into account contribution coming from the ergonomic design 
science in addition to behavioral studies analyzed to frame the concept-idea.

1 Introduction 
1.1 
Aim of the thesis 
The study aims at hampering householders to dump their domestic garbage into street 
bins and or around them. As of consequence - as a side effect - domestic garbage is 
expected to be progressively dumped into house or condominium proper containers 
and thus separated by type of material. An overlook of reasons behind the bad 
behavior is part of the study as well, within the limits of an industrial design exercise 
focusing on the “street device” i.e. the bin, is carried out by the thesis. Moreover, 
specific studies analyzing reasons why householders do not separate garbage at home 
are not available for Copenhagen. Definitely, the study touches upon the 
understanding of the mechanism by which the mind reacts to visual and mechanical 
stimuli and aims at translating this information in an efficient way to prevent or 
mitigate people from lit trash on the street.  
As the title suggests, the project is going to focus on the householders waste and its 
disposal. Of course the problem is not only related to the presence of big bag of 
rubbish inside of small public bins, but to address a correct garbage disposal inside the 
houses.  
In conclusion the specific objectives of this thesis are:  
 Recognize elements causing householders’ bad behavior in order to understand 
how and where to operate. 
 Review behavior and ergonomic discipline in order to understand which 
theories, methods and tools can be applied to orient behaviors towards a 
correct dumping. 
 Elaborate an intervention proposal for the specific problem raised by the 
Copenhagen Municipality. 
 Figure out possible results linked to the implementation of the intervention.

2 About behavior science 
This chapter presents a review of literature supporting the design of the specific 
product elaborated by the master thesis. 
2.1 
Behavioral model 
The concept adopted for the “behavior”,  intended as the “the manner of conducting 
oneself” (Merriam Webster),  follows the ABC model described by Raymond G. 
Miltenberger (Miltenberger et al. 2008) who configures it as the chain of 3 phases or 
attitudes: 
Antecedent – Behavior – Consequence 
In the specific case dealt with by the thesis, the antecedent is configured by the need 
for a household to dump domestic garbage. The behavior is the action to dump 
garbage either properly into suitable containers or not properly into public street bins. 
The consequence configures a set of possible events touching upon many subjects as 
the city community (Pieters 1991) as well as the individual households’ health and 
environment, but also the global environment (EU DG Environment 2010);  
(http://www.carbonfootprint.com/recycling.html )  
2.1.1 Antecedent and consequence 
The ABC model has been adopted in order to identify the strategies to be implemented 
in order to facilitate the progressive change of behavior of Copenhagen citizens 
towards the proper dumping of their domestic garbage into suitable containers 
according the waste separation directions. By this framing, the “antecedent” strategy 
focuses on the prevention of the bad behavior (Abrahamse, et al  2005). In this master 
thesis, the bad behavior to be prevented, i.e. the antecedent, is defined as: non-
separated garbage dumping into public street bins. The strategy will focus on 
technological and awareness-increasing / educational interventions, according to the 
classification proposed by D. Lilley (Lilley et al. 2005), in particular for the design 
solution adopted by the proposed product as suggested by Lilley: “identify ways of 
reducing unsustainable behaviors through improved product design”  
==PAGE:13==
 
13/85 
 
The “consequence” strategy cannot be narrowed as framed with the “antecedent” 
strategy as suggested by Abrahamse with feedbacks and rewards and by Lilley with 
technical solutions. In fact, the consequences induced by the bad behavior are in the 
case studied quite wide-ranging and affect various aspects of the human community, 
including habitat, health and economy. However, both strategies will aim at the 
behavioral change by increasing awareness and also thoughts or educational points of 
view. This is since the “bad” behavior has apparently no justification. Suitable 
containers exist; directions how to separate domestic waste are clear and available; 
public bins are evidently not fitting with domestic bags plenty of mixed garbage. Finally 
the problem could not be limited to the provision of correct information, but should be 
widen with the need to involve unspecific householders into a wider understanding of 
environmental damages and economic losses caused by the irregular waste dumping. 
Which would be the correct information to be conveyed? This is the question that the 
strategy facing the consequence has to develop. By taking into account the analysis 
elaborated by Dolan (Dolan et al. 2012), who classifies the information provided to 
human beings in order to affect their behavior, it could be assumed that, in the case 
studied, neither incentives and norms, nor ego related motivations configure effective 
messages, but those that can stimulate the sense of commons ownership. The 
concepts elaborated by the authors of “salience”, “priming”, “affect” and 
“commitments” can help elaborating the effective messages. Finally these concepts in 
the Dolan analysis configure the awareness increasing and educational messages. In 
particular salience is defined by them as the “attention drawn to what is novel and 
seems relevant to us” and affects us, as per our emotional associations powerfully 
shaping our actions, making evidence to aim at a consciousness building messages. The 
intervention will foresee a device conveying educational messages regarding resources 
gains and economic / environmental losses related to materials un-recycled material.  
2.2 
Inertia 
The need to elaborate an intervention focusing on awareness increasing messages has 
many justifications and is supported by various concepts elaborated not only in the 
behavioral science.   An important concept taken into account by the case studied is 
==PAGE:14==
 
14/85 
 
the “inertia”. A second and more effective concept in addressing the “antecedent” 
strategy refers to the “Socially responsible behavior”. 
The Inertia is meant as the strong desire to stick with your current habits which put 
people into an “automatic pilot mode, in which they are not actively paying attention 
to the task at hand” (Thaler & Sunstein 2008). Also in the transport science, for 
instance, the inertia concept is applied to identify travelers having the tendency to 
continue on their current paths, irrespective of messages and directions provided by 
the advanced information system (Srinivasan & Mahmassani 2000). In the 
understanding of Richard H. Thaler and Cass R. Sunstein the inertia configures one key 
factor pushing human beings in making choices and in keeping individuals behaving in 
the same way and draw the attention to the power of the nudge in addressing 
behaviors (Thaler & Sunstein 2008). A small stimulus can address the inertia to behave 
in a more proper way.  In the case studied, the inertia can be identified with citizens 
having the habit to go out from home with the bag of non-separated garbage and thus 
“inertial” obliged to dump it into the street bin. This “powerful” bad habit could be 
counteracted by addressing a nudge building awareness on the social loss caused by 
the irregular dumping. 
2.3 
Priming 
Priming is first indicated by Karl Lashley in 1951 as “temporary internal activation of 
response tendencies” (The SAGE Encyclopedia of Social Science Research Methods 
Michael Lewis-Beck, Alan E Bryman). In the present report priming is defined as the 
influence of a specific object (often a piece of text, a picture or a physical object) on a 
target to activate a subconscious response that corresponds with the priming stimuli. 
“Priming activates existing lingering concepts and is meant to create a temporary state 
of internal readiness. Priming is a passive cognitive process that does not require 
motivation or intent from the perceiver” (Bargh 2000). 
2.4 
Sustainability 
Sustainability is a term that has been described in numerous ways by many different 
people, companies and organizations. The EPA (United States Environmental 
==PAGE:15==
 
15/85 
 
Protection Agency) gives a description, which is adopted in this project: “Everything 
that we need for our survival and well-being depends, either directly or indirectly, on 
our natural environment. Sustainability creates and maintains the conditions under 
which humans and nature can exist in productive harmony that permits fulfilling the 
social, economic and other requirements of present and future generations”. 
2.5 
Socially responsible behavior  
The “Socially responsible behavior” is currently and highly present in the field of the 
“sustainable design”. This discipline is not really meant as a design that requires the 
lower as possible intake of primary resources as energy, water, raw material and so 
forth to manufacture a product, but rather that will really affect “sustainable” changes 
in the users’ behavior (Tromp et al. 2011). Actually, the sustainable design aims at 
producing products users’ behavioral changes beneficial for the society in the sense 
that these latter allow a reduction of primary resources intake. In the case studied, the 
users are citizens, thus the socially responsible behavior focuses on keeping the city 
clean and supporting the recycling of second raw materials. Finally, the strategy 
follows the principle for which the behavior is the goal aimed at by both users and 
society. According to the “Socially responsible behavior” remarked by Nynke Tromp, 
the intervention should address both the individual and societal concerns.  
Both the need to overcome the “inertia” and to stimulate the “Socially responsible 
behavior” can be implemented by generating a “new framing” that is a new way to 
understand the urban environment and neighborhood habitat.   Stephen Wendel very 
well explains “how changing small aspects of our environment, like the framing of 
questions, can radically affect our behavior” (Stephen Wendel 2014). This approach 
suggest to focus the  intervention on the street the bin only, by creating also aesthetic 
objects designed to increase the awareness by telling stories regarding the value of 
materials composing waste, environmental gains and economy value chains.   
2.6 
The need for a correct dumping  
In this outlook, the environmental awareness increasing effort, the environmental 
sustainability concept and the sensible economy gain achieved by recycling waste 
==PAGE:16==
 
16/85 
 
configure the scientific reference as well as the field for composing messages. To this 
regard the “Report on the Environmental Benefits of Recycling” edited by the “Bureau 
of International Recycling (BIR)” is worth quoting (Grimes et al. 2008) . With regard to 
the awareness increasing, among others, the Anja Kollmuss & Julian Agyeman 
overview is also to be taken into account to this regard (Kollmuss 2010). The process of 
acquaintance is described by them through framework and diagram substantially 
based on knowledge, locus of control, attitude and sense of responsibility.  
A paper from Peter Tucker and David Speirs tells about people's attitude towards 
recycling and composting of the household waste (Tucker 2003). While the advantages 
of waste reduction are well known, people are not well informed of the disadvantages. 
The paper includes citation from several researchers; according to them the reasons of 
bad behavior is to be found in the narrow understanding of the problems, in addition 
to an overall negligence of the population, constraints and barriers and even shortages 
for storage. According to the authors, a positive thinking is a key factor than can 
effectively influence the extent by which an individual feels that by acting alone, 
he/she can make a difference (Ellen 1994) and (Boldero 1995). 
The study carried out by J. Thøgersen, presenting a Behavioral science frame of 
reference, reports a study carried out to analyze the behavior of Danish people with 
regard to recycling and makes evidences of the strong need to aim at an information 
campaign (Thøgersen 1994). The result of the research shows that effective 
informative policy has reduced the amount of generic waste and encourage a recycle 
behavior, even if is not always accepted by citizens, mainly because of the additional 
cost and troubles. 
2.7 
Behavior and Social control 
In his article “Designing ‘moralized’ products”, Jap Jelsma states that designers need to 
take moral responsibility for the actions which take place with the use of products 
manufactured by them.  Actually, the response to this observation is the design for 
behavioral change that acknowledges this responsibility and seeks to put ethical 
behavior and goals higher on the agenda (Wikipedia 1, Behavioral change). Behavioral 
==PAGE:17==
 
17/85 
 
change is stimulated by actions and services associated with any design. However its 
effectiveness depends also on the social and physical environment where the designed 
product is providing services or actions. To this regard, the theory of the “broken 
window”, elaborated by Philip Zimbardo more than 30 years ago on vandalism, 
provides an example of the role that social and physical urban environment can play in 
orienting behaviors (Wikipedia 2).  Zimbardo demonstrated that the vandalism actions 
on the same abandoned car were different if the car was located in deteriorate urban 
environment (Bronx - NY) or in a well-kept environment (Palo Alto California).  It can 
be argued that the social control acted as agent leading the behavior. However it can 
be also noted that also the environment led the behavior, but for the case studied and 
the task that the designer is facing, it is worth noting that both factors – i.e. social 
control and environment - probably have been determinant to influence the behavior. 
The question for the designer thus can be summarized in the relationship between 
social and physical environment and social control on citizens’ behavior.   
2.7.1 Sustainable design   
The paper edited by Kristina Niedderer brought some aid to designers specifically that 
which develop the model of the “Mindful Design as a Driver for Social Behavior 
Change” (Niedderer 2013). She observes that in the sustainable design practice - that 
actually is close to the environmental gains intended as better social performances - 
the social context and the nature of social interaction are rarely considered. Moreover, 
she notes that “individual and social needs are often competing and that both need to 
be managed and negotiated”. The social control can promote bad behavior as the case 
of alcohol abuse in Western societies, but according to the Mindful design” can be 
enhanced towards positive behavioral change by the awareness increasing. In light 
contrast with the persuasive and coercive design (Tromp et al. 2011), the mindful 
design does not require to achieve through a clear educational context, but by giving 
to the user the choice on how to act, thus “requiring the user to take responsibility”.  
The example brought forward is the traffic junction in the Netherlands, “where the 
increasing number of signs did not reduce the rate of accidents, but taking away all 
signs did improve the situation”. Pedestrians and bikers acted better in absence of 
==PAGE:18==
 
18/85 
 
prescriptive guidance and over warnings since they were directly involved in their 
safety and the same was for drivers. The author underlines that the “explicit nature of 
this design intervention is not based on the forceful nature of any addition, but on the 
disruptive nature of the omission, which causes conscious awareness and reflection”.  
This consideration can address the designer in considering the need to facilitate the 
social control on the correct use of a product by configuring the bad behavior as an 
omission that produce damages to the living environment and more broadly to the 
community welfare and economy.  
The matter raised by Kristina Niedderer: “individual and social needs that are often in 
competition” is also taken into account by the sustainable design model.  Also this 
model is explored by many authors and is useful to address the designer toward 
possible interventions taking into account the social control as design element. As 
described before, the sustainable design is meant both as a strategy aiming at 
manufacturing products beneficial for the society (e.g. allowing a reduction of primary 
resources intake) and as a strategy aiming at generating a “Socially responsible 
behavior” (Tromp et al. 2011). This latter understanding has been further developed 
into the theory of the design for a “Social innovation” that aims at manufacturing 
products that can facilitate political and societal change (Chick 2012) (Emilson et al. 
2011). Anne Chick in her article “Design for Social Innovation: Emerging Principles and 
Approaches design” reports on the possibility to address social and environmental 
issues to policy makers and communities.  The paper states that “There is growing 
consensus that design is a mode of innovation that provides a set of skills, tools and 
methods that can guide people to new social innovative solutions or improve existing 
ones”   
Both the “Socially responsible behavior” and the “Design for social innovation” 
strategies ground the design intervention that would leverage the social control in 
order to change the bad behavior of dumping home garbage towards the correct 
disposal of sorted waste into proper houses containers.  To this regard and in general 
to the social innovation issues, see also the Book of Social Innovation (Murray et al. 
2010).

19/85 
 
2.7.2 Aesthetic  
Philip R. Ross and Stephan A. G. Wensveen in their paper “Designing Behavior in 
Interaction: Using Aesthetic Experience as a Mechanism for Design” demonstrate that 
the design of products and systems requires a link and a reflection to the aesthetic that 
goes beyond the “traditional static form aspects”. The theory grounds on the 
interaction of human beings with used object or devices, which play each other 
reciprocal roles. The interaction is described as interplay in which the aesthetic can 
have a key role since involves the whole person and personal emotional status. In few 
words, the authors demonstrate that the experience of beauty can change emotional 
status and is also in relation with social skills and behavior. They conclude that we can 
experience the beauty in a social interaction with objects (Ross & Wensveen 2010). It 
can be argued that in the case studied any recall to a clean and beautiful environment, 
where it is worth living, can help in addressing a non-hurting behavior.    
2.8 
Behavior and technical factors 
A behavior that is strengthened by an operator is called “operant behavior”. The 
operant behavior needs an agent that can act as a “reinforcer” (Miltenberger et al. 
2008). By this understanding, the bin-person in charge of the bins cleaning, who could 
grade the proper use of the bins by neighborhood citizens, can be assumed as the 
“reinforcer” (McLeod 2015) who can cause a progressive change in the behavior.  
Desmet and Hekkert draw the attention to the instrumental interaction and non-
instrumental and even non-physical interaction between users and product in use 
(Desmet & Hekkert 2007). With non-instrumental interaction, the authors refer to “the 
interactions that do not directly serve a function in operating a product”, but  mentally 
and involving the user, for instance by teaching or generating habits.   
Technology plays an important role in the study of the behavioral science. Extract from 
handbook of evidence of Nirbhay N. Singh (Singh 2016) explains how to use devices to 
test reactions to stimuli. The case study is meant to assist kids with disability, but 
technically can be extend to any individual. 
==PAGE:20==
 
20/85 
 
The technical design of the bins shall take into account the two concepts of the of the 
“weak general” interaction style and “strong specific” product (Jensen et al. 2005). The 
weak general interaction aims at generic actions that can be can be used for anything 
as, in the case studied, the aesthetic, the messages convened and so forth.  The ‘strong 
specific’ products aims at a tailored design by which inputs are ruled by the 
functionality as it is, in part,  the case of the product being designed.  
2.9 
Functionality matching 
The concept of “functionality matching” has been largely studied and adopted by many 
designers and addresses clearly the need to join the functionality of the product with 
consumers or users behavior.  As described by Rodríguez and Boks (Rodríguez 2005) 
the functional design discusses the option to better adapt a product to the habitual 
use of consumers and thereby try to minimize negative side effects between delivered 
functionalities and desired functionalities. A mismatch between delivered 
functionalities and desired functionalities is obviously not sustainable. However 
“Redundant functionalities can generate adverse impact. Missing functionalities does 
not cope with the problem aimed at and can trigger unwanted behavior, with 
subsequent unsustainable effects”, as is the case of trash dumped even aside the bin. 
2.10 Nudging  
To discuss correctly interventions needed to reduce the irregular dumping practiced by 
householders, some consideration on the nudging theory can help framing the product 
and possible results aimed at.  
As well discussed by Charlotte Gram Hartlev and Christine Boysen Durazo in their 
master thesis on behavioral design, the nudging concept lacks of operational outputs 
but provides indications on the possible quality of an intervention (Hartlev & Durazo 
2015).  Actually this vagueness of the nudging theory is confirmed by Mari Gloppen 
Hunnes who surveyed and analyzed several articles and books discussing nudging 
(Hunnes 2014), including the huge reference text “Nudge – Improving Decisions about 
Health, Wealth and Happiness” edited  by Richard Thaler and Cass Sunstein (Thaler & 
Sunstein 2008).  
==PAGE:21==
 
21/85 
 
Major concerns with regard to the quality and the effectiveness of nudging are with 
the social and physical environment that influence messages conveyed by 
interventions. This means that nudges can be ineffective in the absence of a favorable 
cultural environment, thus they fail to achieve due to people’s “own automatic 
thinking and pre-set biases” (Selinger & Whyte 2011). In other words, when people 
make decisions, they are exposed to the social and physical environment where they 
live, also temporarily.  To this regard Theresa Marteau - who actually deeply studied 
the possible role of nudging in the public health as a tool to prevent unhealthy 
behaviors like fatty foods -  notes: “nudging is at best a fuzzy set intended to draw 
attention to the role of social and physical environments in shaping our behavior and 
not to inform a scientific taxonomy of behavior change interventions” (Marteau et al. 
2011).  
Of course Dr Marteau refers to public health issues and not to facilities and services 
use, however her note introduces an additional concern related to the “time” during 
which the nudge is effective.  To this end, some examples are worth discussing and 
useful to argue the case studied.  The smaller size of glasses in the bars/ pubs is 
proposed to reduce the alcohol intake; to make stairs, not lifts, more prominent and 
attractive in public buildings would nudge physical activities (Bonell C 2011);  the 
reduction of  dishes size  in a canteen is proposed to nudge smaller quantity of food 
intake for lunch and so forth (Selinger & Whyte 2011).  
Provided that the product in these described 3 cases is designed to nudging a healthier 
behavior, the question is if such a conduct would endure also outside the social and 
physical environment where it is induced, for instance also at home for alcohol or food 
intake.  It can be argued that nudge works better if a social control is also active, i.e. 
when people feel watched.  The conclusion is quiet important for the case studied 
since as many authors state, the understanding of causes of biases or miss behavior do 
not help much in designing nudges (Selinger & Whyte 2011). The conceptual position is 
referring to challenges that “choice architects” have to face and is well described also 
for what concerns its ethical consequence by  the authors in the article “Is there a right 
way to nudge? The Practice and Ethics of Choice Architecture”.   
==PAGE:22==
 
22/85 
 
Actually the authors support the thesis of C Gram Hartlev and C Boysen Durazo 
(Hartlev & Durazo 2015)  by stating that “Thaler and Sunstein do not provide anything 
like a formula, technique, or method for creating and implementing successful nudges 
(…)”, which can help designers. The authors quote also researches of Rizzo and 
Whitman describing that “choice architects” may project their values on others, which 
can result in unwanted behaviors (Rizzo & Whitman 2008) . The example brought 
forward is described by Trevor Pinch regarding the sound warning with a female voice, 
rather than a visual signal, in the German highways to nudge a lower speed: “It turned 
out to be totally ineffective as German male drivers paid little attention to a female 
voice telling them to slow down! A male voice was found to be much more effective” 
(Pinch 2010).  The reported conclusion is that such a nudge promotes sexism in 
Germany. It also to be argued for the case studied if the sound warning could be 
considered a nudge.   
Nudge strategy is however very well watched by public authorities since is expected to 
achieve results without imposing norms, taxes, bans and related prescriptive policies 
(Rainford & Tinkler 2011). However nudging requires attentions to be effective. In the 
case studied, the effort is to convey a mixture of messages by the intervention so as to 
address educational aspects, i.e. to ground a common environmental culture and 
enhance social control on values shared by the involved population. This conclusion is 
supported once again by the “Ethics of Choice Architecture” vision prosed by E. 
Selinger and K. White who conclude their analysis stating that the nudge shall have a 
semantic invariance. In other words, the perception of meanings conveyed by 
messages must have, as far as possible, the same understanding for people living in 
the town, disregarding age, welfare, race, religion, gender, education and occupation.  
The attention to the need of the semantic invariance of messages, graphics, pictures, 
symbols, icons and so forth is taken into consideration in the design of the intervention 
proposed by this thesis.

3.2 
Waste treatment  
The incinerator facility was opened in 1970, just outside Copenhagen; it turns the 
garbage into energy. Over the years, it has been improved; now it has an installed 
power of 26 MW and can provide district heating to 140,000 inhabitants of the capital. 
The plant has significantly lower emissions than a coal plant of comparable power and 
==PAGE:26==
 
26/85 
 
processes - by recycling or converting into electric power - nearly the 97% of the 
Copenhagen garbage. Only the 3% of the Copenhagen waste ends up in landfills.  
A huge crane and a robotic system select and load waste.  The garbage is transferred 
and incinerated in 4 furnaces. The water is then heated with high pressure steam 
boilers and subsequently pumped through a gas cleaning system which eliminates the 
most dangerous polluters, ensuring clean outlet emissions. The steam generates 
electricity by a turbine, while the recovered condensation heat is transferred by pipes 
to the district houses heating system. The solid by-products coming from the 
incineration process, ashes and slags are stored for subsequent reuse operated by the 
construction companies that buy them directly at the plant. 
Wastes therefore do not end up in landfills, but generate energy and heat for 
buildings. Any slag is reused as far as possible (Svendsen 2012  – Environmental 
Department City of Copenhagen, Denmark), 18 December 2012, conference organized 
by “Confservizi Cispel Toscana, ATO Toscana Centro, ISWA Italia” in collaboration with 
the Chamber of Commerce of Florence. 
The urban area of Copenhagen, actually 4 municipalities counting about 550,000 
inhabitants, over an area of 88 km2, produces 820,000 t/year of waste.  National laws 
regulate the waste classification and processing, while municipal laws regulate the 
operational aspects, related to domestic waste collection and processing handled by 
the municipalities. The recycling system it is privately run. The use of the landfill has 
gone by now near-zero, amounting to only 2%. Out of the remaining 98%, the 58% is 
recycled and a 40% incinerated. Two old incinerators are located in the outskirts of 
town (5 Km from the center) built in the 70s, but are soon supplemented / replaced by 
the  new large and futuristic plant from 560,000 t / year i.e. the Amager Bakke 
incinerator. This latter has been equipped with the best technologies available in terms 
of fume treatment, with the complete liberation from the plastic component in the 
dumped material and a futuristic design integrated into the city skyline.

4 Behavioral problem 
This chapter defines the subject and scope of the study.  
The bad behavior performed by some citizens in the studied districts, described in 
paragraph 1.1, it is not immediately understandable since it happens in a social 
background where there is a general environmental awareness and the garbage 
collecting system is modern and well running. In these districts the wastes are sorted 
in house, which are generally well served by the collection system. These 
considerations force enquiring the reasons behind the bad habit to dump domestic 
garbage into street bins and  make evident the need to adopt behavior design 
concepts in order to try coping with it.  
The identification of reasons leading behaviors and classification of single actions let us 
understand where to intervene in order to modify the behavior of the single citizen. 
4.1 
Causes of the problem 
First of all, it is necessary to try deepen causes leading citizens to a non-proper use of 
the public street bins, since motivations are not evident. 
It can be argued that the bad behavior could have several causes, from the incomplete 
information received by citizens with regard to rules, permissions or bans to the lack of 
awareness about economy, environmental gains of the correct waste collection and 
treatment, including environmental depletion or health risks related to street disposal 
and also lack of motivation in respecting commons and urban habitat decorum.  
In addition to the hypnotized informative default, structural constraints and habits 
could play a significant role. Among other, the in-house space to store dedicated 
containers for plastic, metal, glass, paper and humid fraction could be not enough or 
pleasant. Moreover, the condominium containers could be uncomfortable or far away 
so that the dumping into street bins could be comfortable and faster.      
Some of these aspects have been enquired and summarized in the field work section. 
==PAGE:30==
 
30/85 
 
4.2 
Focusing on the bin 
Actually, the problem related to waste collection and treatment is quite huge and goes 
up to packaging system, involving not only householders, but also industries and so 
forth.  The complexity of the waste treatment system can be coped with a variety of 
possible solutions, ranging from the products manufacturing to the houses trash 
containers, type, sorted waste possibilities, location, collection timing and so forth.  
The present thesis focuses on the street bin only that is the final object of the 
operational chain, i.e. the site where domestic garbage is irregularly dumped.  
To this regard, nowadays the Copenhagen Municipality has not adopted strong and 
systematic actions to prevent and hamper the domestic garbage disposal into the 
street bins. Since the problem does exist and is easily detectable (bins full of not 
separated waste, garbage littered on the sidewalk, dirty pavements), the Municipality 
asked the DTU to study effective solutions to be applied. 
4.2.1 Some bins’ solutions   
The problem is not a peculiarity of Copenhagen, many other towns’ administration 
cope with such a bad behavior providing or attempting solutions as far as possible in 
coherence with the urban and socio-economic background. For instance in Rome 
(Italy), heavy street bin cast iron manufactured with small openings are placed down 
town and mainly in proximity of monuments, residential zones or shopping areas. The 
small opening and the heavy locked container make not possible dumping voluminous 
bags.  This technical solution is a typical behavioral related intervention and performs 
the function of the “strong interaction” with the user (Jensen et al. 2005), as well as 
that described by the need to have an object matching the “functionality” (Rodríguez 
2005). 
Other solutions, as those adopted in Berlin, foresee a series of 4 bins in one device 
labelling the kind of waste to be dumped (plastic, paper, glass, organic…) also for small 
street trash as the street food packaging in order to perform as well a clear 
“functionality matching” with the purpose meant by the bin to compel users in sorting 
even street food packaging (Fig.  2).

Also the “artistic” design has been adopted for the street bin with the aim to decorate

The described “Bilbao” solution relies on the “weak interaction” model described by 
Jensen, but aims at fulfilling the task to involve citizens in keeping the urban habitat 
clean. The aesthetic and the good sight of the urban area aim at making the citizen 
more responsible of his or her action and are expected to enhance the social control 
on the correct use of the bin. This design option follows the “social responsible 
behavior” set of theories discussed mainly by architect and psychologists. To this 
regard, among others, Nynke Tromp and Kristine Niedderer can be quoted (Tromp et 
al. 2011) (Niedderer 2013).   
4.2.2 Technical solutions per se are not enough  
Citizens could be persuaded to correctly use street bins by adopting technical solutions 
(aesthetic, size, shape, operational modes), however the effectiveness of these 
technical solutions depends also on external factors as compelling norms (e.g. fines), 
information, awareness, sense of ownership and social control, which are addressed 
by the behavioral design effort. In other words, the shape and the aestetic of the bins 
per se could not be efefctive in solving the problem. Actions aiming at modifying the 
citizens’ behavior are to be designed together with the physisc moulding of the bin. 
Behavior design disciplines allow advising operational solutions aiming at taking into 
account emotional, rational, functional and not only technical aspects. In addition to 
this behavioral design constantly adresses on the need to estabilsh communication 
channel between users and the device or the service in use. See also Bjorn  Appelqvist 
(Appelqvist 2007) and the ergonomic discipline.  
The present project focuses mainly on these factors complementary to the bin, rather 
than proposing a “hardware” reshaping of all street bins in Copenaghen, which can be 
carried out by the Municipality at any time.

houses.

7 Intervention idea 
The intervention conceptualization process resulted in the intervention-idea fully is 
described in this chapter. It is however useful to recall objective and functions of the 
intervention being designed. The intervention idea has been selected according to 
criteria established by the Designer. These criteria selected may be physical factor as 
well as economical and cognitive, but the main goal of the designer is to project 
according to behavioral science.  
7.1 
Features of the intervention 
The intervention-idea shall have important features according to the pertinence to the 
problem. In order to finalize a successful design the product or the system should be:   
- 
Affordable: too keep low the cost of the bins upgrading. 
- 
Easy application: the intervention affects an already existing facility system, 
rather than redesign the entire device. 
- 
Accessible: must be accessible to all kind of users. 
- 
Friendly interaction: the system interface is easy to be spotted and 
understood; in this sense, the interface should act as efficient trigger. 
- 
Behavioral means: the new systems should activate the users’ psychological 
mechanisms, instead of being coercive; a solution of this kind it is preferable, 
because there is evidence that is more effective. 
- 
Social function: the system shall involve all subjects working together, since the 
goal of the system is to push users toward a correct behaviors.

7.2 
Objective of the intervention 
The intervention aims at contributing to the prevent Copenhagen’s citizens bad 
behavior consisting in dumping domestic waste into public street bins.  
The product is designed to undertake 3 functions strictly correlated: 
1.  Discourage citizens to dump un-sorted domestic waste into street bins, rather than 
separate waste at home and dump them into condominium containers according to 
their typology.  
2.  Involve citizens in keeping tidy up and clean the habitat where they live and raise 
the responsibility of people living in the neighborhood. 
==PAGE:57==
 
57/85 
 
3.  Provide educational information in order to increase the awareness of citizens, 
while providing administrative information in order to convey right directions to 
citizens.  
7.2.1 Function 1 
According to Ninke Tromp (Tromp et al. 2011),  the design of a product aimed at 
changing the behavior should generate 4 influence:  coercive, persuasive, seductive, 
and decisive. Of course, each influence requires elaborating a strategy that depend on 
the socio-economic environment and urban context,  as well explained by Selinger & 
Whyte (Selinger & Whyte 2011) 
The Function 1) “discourage citizens dumping domestic waste into street bins” is 
meant to be achieved by elaborating a strategy fulfilling the coercive and decisive 
influence.  
This strategy focuses on the physical design of the product that should hamper 
dumping voluminous bags, but allows dumping only small size garbage, as street food 
packaging. The result is meant to be achieved by operating with the “technical 
factors”, according to the instrumental interaction need proposed by Desmet and 
Hekkert. The authors focus also on the key function of the non-instrumental 
interaction expected to be carried on the product (Desmet & Hekkert 2007). According 
to this, the instrumental interaction configures the physical design of the bin, whereas 
the “non-instrumental” will focus on the aesthetic and “telling” functions of it.  The bin 
should become a “design object” that is worth keeping tidy up. 
The technical design of the product shall take into account the 2 concepts of the of the 
“weak general” interaction style and “strong specific” product (Jensen et al. 2005). The 
weak general interaction aims at generic actions that can be can be used for anything 
as, in the case studied, the aesthetic, the messages convened and so forth.  The ‘strong 
specific’ products aims at a tailored design, by which inputs are ruled by the 
functionality, as it is the case of the locked cover of the bin.  
Finally, the product shall follow the concept of “functionality matching” (Rodríguez 
2005),  largely studied and adopted by many designers. In the case studied, the 
==PAGE:58==
 
58/85 
 
concept addresses the need to join the functionality with users’ behavior.  As 
described by Rodríguez and Boks it shall match the correct well-fitting functionality 
that in the street bin case is the small-size of openings allowing garbage dumping.  
7.2.2 Function 2 
The function 2 “Involve citizens in keeping tidy up and clean the habitat where they 
live; raise the responsibility of citizens” is touched upon by many theories and is meant 
to be achieved by elaborating a strategy fulfilling the seductive and persuasive 
influences of the Tromp’s model.  
Function 2 is of key importance and should try to put in place and develop the idea of 
getting the users more and more responsible of their actions. The sustainable design 
(Tromp et al. 2011),  (Thorpe 2010), the social innovation design as described by Anne 
Chick, (Chick 2012) and the mindful design model presented by  Kristine Niedder 
(Niedderer 2013) make evident 2 aspects. First of all it is possible that design would 
provide tools and methods able to guide people to social solutions, secondly that the 
raised responsibility of the user will be a clear and key complement to the educational 
and awareness increasing function.  
The expected results coming from an increasing responsibility of householders will 
consist in an increased social control on the use of the street bins and in the increased 
exigence to keep urban environment clean. This results would configure and generate 
the common cultural and physical background where educational and awareness 
increasing function (i.e. the 3rd  function) can better operate to be effective. In this 
understanding,  by adopting also the visionary but very useful approach proposed by 
Ann Thorpe, the re-design of the bin could enable building a more supportive and 
liable community, respectful of common goods and more educated with 
environmental gains or losses (Thorpe 2010). 
The mindful approach suggested by Kristine Niedderer aims at employing the 
awareness of social norms, emotions and moral values to question users and to enable 
changes in thinking and perceiving where they live and damages they are producing 
whit the irregular dumping of not separated domestic waste.  
==PAGE:59==
 
59/85 
 
According to Miltenberger, a behavior can be strengthened by an operator called 
“operant behavior”. The operant behavior needs an agent that can act as a 
“reinforcer” (Miltenberger 2011). By this understanding, the bin-person in charge of 
the bins cleaning, who will award the use of the bin within the neighborhood, can be 
assumed as the “reinforcer” (McLeod 2015).  
7.2.3 Function 3 
The function 3 delivers educational information in order to increase the awareness of 
citizens, while providing administrative information in order to convey right directions 
to citizens. It is meant to be performed elaborating  a strategy fulfilling the decisive 
and persuasive  influences of the Tromp’s model.  
According to E. Sellinger and K. White the nudging action requires a semantic 
invariance to be effective. This means that messages conveyed by the bin shall be clear 
to any inhabitant, irrespective of education, race, believing, wealth and so forth. As of 
consequence, the function 3 is also important in order to generate a “common” 
cultural background of the neighborhood.  Therefore, the need of a correct dumping 
and for the sustainability in the understanding of Anja Kollmuss & Julian Agyeman, the 
economic gains of waste recycling, the positive thinking engendered by a correct 
management of own garbage in the view of Peter Tucker,  Pam Scholder Ellen and  
Janet Boldero will be taken into account (Ellen 1994), (Boldero 1995). The design 
would participate to a strong information campaign whose need is also well described 
by J. Thøgersen (Thøgersen 1994).

8 Prototype 
8.1 
Desired effects 
This project aims creating awareness and foster citizens towards a correct disposal of 
domestic waste. We look forward to a more responsible behavior of citizens who 
should stop filling the street bins with domestic garbage. The concept it is a systems 
composed by two elements, a set of “labels” to be applied on the side of the bins and a 
webpage or App to support the transition of the message.  
The system is meant to be applied abroad relatively large area of the city, by applying 
labels on several bins. The system can be effective only if applied over periods e.g. 
weeks or months. The label and the correspondent bins are mapped, and weekly 
checked to see if improvement occurred. 
An important role is played by the bin-person, he is in charged to inform the 
municipality about the situation of the area. This task is accomplished as weekly 
routine while cleaning the bins. This grading doesn’t apply all over the city 
simultaneously, but only to the area which has been decided to apply the system. 
Nevertheless, the system can be apply to different areas at once, in order for the 
system to work, furthermore the citizen are encouraged to use the app to inform the 
municipality about potential area of application. 
Yet a scoring of the bins is made, the designer can update the map of the neighbor on 
the webpage www.cleanercity.com showing the score of each bins and the average 
score of the all neighbor. The goal is to inspire sense of shame or pride into the 
inhabitant, in addiction the site aim to inform of the existence of this behavior and 
promote new solution or offer assistance to the users. The system has also the 
secondary goal to make the citizen feel part of the system and be rewarded from living 
in a more pleasant neighborhood.  
The messages have immediate effect on the users. In fact, users throwing the 
household waste find a big “label” applied on the side of the bin composed by a logo -  
to visually communicate the message - a small slogan catching the attention and 
making the message clearer and the information to access the webpage. There are 
==PAGE:68==
 
68/85 
 
three types of logos and changing slogan. This feature aims to trigger the user in a 
direct way, he or her will be able then to act consciously to change his or her behavior.  
A worker or a designer is in charge to apply the “label” on the scheduled bins. After a 
week checking and scoring the bins, a comparison is made to see if improvements 
have occurred. In case of sensible difference between the first week and the second 
one, the “label” will change to positive in case of an improved behavior or to negative 
if the bad behavior persists. The results are updated on the webpage for each area of 
interest.  
8.2

8.2.1 Cover of the bin

8.2.3 Background images 
The combination of the signals and the background picture has a double function. The 
information conveyed by the signals are immediately understood, the attention of the 
user is caught by the blue square and by the info-graphic. They clearly fulfill the 
semantic invariance need. On the other hand, the background has the side effect to 
trigger the unconsciousness of the user. When the user is approaching the bin, his or 
her mind reads the cleanness and neat picture on the background. Both aesthetic has 
an important role and clean beautiful landscapes, as well as amazing environment, 
where it would be worth staying, have the power to discourage detrimental actions 
e.g. lit garbage on the ground. The purpose of these images is to discourage the bad 
behavior by triggering a sense of guiltiness in dishonoring such a beautiful 
environment. In other words an image of an untouched beach, a wild forest or a clean 
green park operates at the subliminal brain level of and encourages a correct behavior 
(Fig.  22 and Fig.  23). This design element grounds on the assumption of the “broken 
window theory” grounding on the Zimbardo research (Wikipeida 2) and takes into 
account the conclusions of  P R Ross  and S Wensveen on the social role of beauty and 
aesthetic in the design (Ross & Wensveen 2010)

8.3 
Testing phase 
The testing phase needs to last some weeks or months to be effective. For practical, 
logistic and legal reason, this long test phase has not been applied to test the designed 
prototype. However, the entire process how to carry out the test is described in this 
paragraph, since it is an important part of the design.  
A testing phase has been planned, it foresees two distinct actions. First of all a 
checking phase to test the ergonomic effectiveness of the concept; secondly if the 
whole system runs in the sense that actors interact smoothly, effectively and 
continuously.  
In order to check the ergonomic power of the concept, the designer needs to observe 
how the users interact with the bins.  This will carried out by recording them with the 
hidden camera. Otherwise an isolated group of testing persons will be exposed to the 
poster and then interviewed. The camera recordings show the reactions of the users 
and other citizens approaching the bins, then it is possible to understand how effective 
is the poster in achieving its purpose. 
By this way, it would be possible to check the functionality of the entire system. The 
aim of the system is to facilitating and communicating essential information, which 
need to be clear and as simplest as possible. In this sense a daily updating of the 
related website needs to be done.

9 Conclusion  
 
To conclude, the solution of the problem brought forward by the bad behavior of 
disposing domestic garbage into street bins instead of using the specific facilities, must 
be learnt as far as possible in the widest panorama of interventions. This tangible 
undesired phenomenon is wrongly assimilated to the social and cultural context of 
Copenhagen city, which is well known to care much environmental issues. 
The project has evolved from the initial idea of a design of a single device to the design 
of a more complex system of actions, with the main goal of involving all the actors 
participating to the behavioral chain: the citizens, public administration, workers who 
clean the street and the bin itself. In this sense the street bins become a “talking” 
agent, able to provide information and interact with others.  
The project focus on two aspects configuring the key features of the work.  The first 
aspect is the system that allows creating awareness and receiving feedbacks on the 
bins ‘ use. The aim of the system is to promote the sense of ownership of the citizens 
toward their city and raise the pride of the neighborhood in contributing to the city 
wellness. The second aspect is configured by unconscious information received from 
the special graphic on the bins. The behavioral theory suggest that design of products 
could create an innovative way of intervention in order to stimulate users towards a 
more correct behavior without physical constrains. In this case the graphic of the bins 
stimulates users thinking at the action being carried out. in the sense that it pushes 
considering that such an action could be wrong. In other words, the user is the 
responsible or something  clearly wrong and there is nothing else preventing the user 
to go further with his or her bad action, except his sense of guiltiness.  
In addition to the subliminal stimuli raising the doubt of guiltiness, posted messages 
aims also at raising the awareness of citizens with regard to the waste of resources and 
economic outputs caused by the irregular dumping of materials excluded by the 
recycling value chain.  
 
==PAGE:82==
 
82/85 
 
The final design foresees making operational a communication system between the 4 
set of actors involved in the behavior chain of action: citizens, municipality, cleaning 
operators in charge and the bin The leading strategy the project is the belief the raising 
sense of responsibility of everyone positively affects the entire community.
