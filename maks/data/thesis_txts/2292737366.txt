Abstract 
The yeast Saccharomyces cerevisiae serves as a model eukaryotic organism and used in the 
field of biotechnology as a key cell factory for production of insulin, biofuels and chemical 
compounds. Yeast as a production organism has several advantages compared to other mi-
croorganisms such as bacteria and filamentous fungi. It is generally regarded as safe, easy 
to cultivate and relatively simple to genetically manipulate with modern engineering tech-
nologies. A broad variety of background strains are available for industrial use of which 
S288c and CEN.PK are the two most commonly applied in biotechnology. Comparative anal-
ysis studies show significant phenotypical and genetic variations between the two strains, 
and in addition also different production yields of certain heterologous compounds. Choice 
of background strains as cell factory is likely to play an essential role in obtaining high pro-
duction yields of heterologous compounds.  
The aim of this study is to perform comparative quantitative analysis of de novo heterolo-
gous polyketide 6-methyl salicylic acid (6-MSA) and 5-methyl orsellinic acid (5-MOA) pro-
duction in two different CEN.PK and one S288c S. cerevisiae strain. This is achieved by intro-
ducing the polyketide synthase encoding genes 6MSAS from Aspergillus niger and mpaC 
from Penicillium brevicompactum, respectively. In addition, PPTase encoding gene npgA 
from Aspergillus nidulans necessary for activation of polyketide synthases is introduced with 
6SMAS and mpaC. Identification and quantification of compounds and synthase gene tran-
scription is performed by HPLC and RT-qPCR analysis. 6-MSA is considered a model polyke-
tide and expression levels may provide information regarding aromatic polyketide synthesis 
suitability of CEN.PK vs. S288c. 5-MOA synthesis is the first step in the mycophenolic acid 
(MPA) synthesis pathway. MPA is an immunosuppressant used in organ transplant surgery. 
This study serves as preliminary work for integration of whole MPA pathway. 
Analysis of 6-MSA and 5-MOA production and gene transcription levels in the constructed 
strains could not be determined in this study. Comparative evaluation of heterologous 
polyketide expression in the three background strains was not established.

Resumé  
Gæren Saccharomyces cerevisiae er en eukaryot modelorganisme, og anvendes inden for 
bioteknologi som en central cellefabrik til produktion af insulin, biodiesel og kemiske forbin-
delser. Gær har som produktionsorganisme adskillige fordele i forhold til andre mikroorga-
nismer, såsom bakterier og filamentøse svampe. Den er generelt betragtet som sikker, nem 
at kultivere og relativ enkel at manipulere genetisk med moderne genteknologier. En bred 
vifte af baggrundsstammer er tilgængelige til industriel brug, hvoraf CEN.PK og S288c er de 
to mest almindeligt anvendte i bioteknologi. Komparativ analysestudier viser signifikante 
fænotypiske og genetiske variationer mellem de to stammer, som derudover også har for-
skellige produktionsudbytter af visse heterologe molekyler. Valg af baggrundstamme som 
cellefabrik kan sandsynligvis have en væsentlig betydning for at opnå optimal produktions-
udbytte af heterologe molekyler.  
Formålet med dette studie er at udføre en sammenlignende kvantitativ analyse af de novo 
heterolog produktion af polyketiderne 6-methylsalicylsyre (6-MSA) og 5- methylorsellinsyre 
i to forskellige CEN.PK og en S288c S. cerevisiae stamme. Dette opnås ved at hhv. indføre de 
polyketidsyntase kodende gener 6MSAS fra Aspergillus niger og mpaC fra Penicillium brevi-
compactum. Derudover indføres det PPTase kodende gen npgA fra Aspergillus nidulans, som 
er nødvendig for aktivering af polyketidsyntaserne, sammen med 6MSAS og mpaC. Identifi-
kation og kvantificering af polyketiderne samt af syntasegen transskription udføres ved 
HPLC og RT-qPCR analyse. 6-MSA betragtes som et modelpolyketid, og ekspressionsni-
veauer kan give oplysninger om aromatisk polyketidsyntese i CEN.PK og S288c. 5-MOA er 
det første trin i mycophenolsyre (MPA) syntesen. MPA er et immunsystemhæmmende stof, 
som gives til organtransplantations patienter. Dette studie fungerer som forarbejde til inte-
gration af hele MPA syntesevejen. 
Analyse af 6-MSA og 5-MOA produktion og gentransskriptionsniveauer i de konstruerede 
stammer kunne ikke fastlægges i dette studie. Komparative vurderinger af heterolog poly-
ketidproduktion i de tre baggrundsstammer kunne ikke etableres.

1 Introduction 
1.1 Biotechnology  
Utilization of microorganisms and biomolecular processes to develop new products and 
technologies for human purposes is the essence of biotechnology. The most elementary 
form of biotechnology relies on fermentation and has been used for thousands of years to 
produce cheese, beer, wine and bread. Modern biotechnology assimilates knowledge from 
chemistry, microbiology and life science to precisely modify and manipulate living organisms 
for improvement and construction of new products and processes. It is a large area of study 
and includes many other fields such a bioinformatics, bioprocess-, metabolic- and biochem-
ical engineering. Application of biotechnology in agriculture, medicine and industrial pro-
cesses have proven to be an efficient and environmentally friendly approach to increase 
yields and substitute chemically intensive practices [1]. These fields are respectively referred 
to as Green, Red and White biotechnology [2].  
Agricultural applications have provided sustainable crops by not only increasing yield and 
nutrition values, but also decreasing environmental impact by reducing the use of pesti-
cides. Biotechnological applications in medicine have opened the possibilities of gene ther-
apeutic vaccines and tailored / personalized medicinal treatment grounded on individual 
genetics. 
Industrial biotechnology use genetically modified microorganisms as living cellular manu-
facturing plants for production of different biological and chemical compounds, such as bi-
odegradable plastics, “green” chemicals and renewable biofuels [3]. Many of the biotech-
nological processes are designed to compete and substitute current industrial chemical pro-
cesses by being cheaper, more environmentally friendly and having a higher productivity 
==PAGE:14==
2 
 
level [1]. Fermentation technology and biocatalysis are at the base of industrial biotechnol-
ogy and use bioreactors to create a controlled setting where raw material (such as glucose 
and other carbohydrates) is converted into high-value chemicals or biological products [3].  
Bacteria, fungi and yeasts are all used as cell factories and have different production benefits 
and disadvantages. Some microorganisms contain innate metabolic pathways that synthe-
size compounds of interest and only need to be modified to increase expression levels. It is 
desirable to use microorganisms that already have the pathway since it involves the least 
amount of genetic, expensive and time-consuming work. However, not all organisms thrive 
in laboratory conditions and production yields may suffer immensely [1]. Introduction of a 
de novo synthesis pathway in organisms such as the bacteria Escherichia coli or the yeast 
Saccharomyces cerevisiae that are easier to genetically manipulate, cultivate and harness 
may provide a feasible alternative. 
 
1.2 Yeast in biotechnology 
Yeasts are unicellular eukaryotic microorganisms and have been used in production of alco-
hol and foods for millennia. The yeast S. cerevisiae is one of the most well studied eukary-
otes and serves as a model organism for understanding eukaryotic systems (Figure 1) [4]. 
Availability of complete genome sequence and receptivity to metabolic engineering through 
genetic modification technology have made S. cerevisiae widely used in the field of biotech-
nology [4] [5]. Some of the most prominent examples of industrial yeast applications are 
production of bioethanol, detergent-enzymes and insulin [3] [6]. 
 
==PAGE:15==

Figure 1: Electron microscope picture of Saccharomyces cerevisiae of from [7]. 
 
S. cerevisiae has several advantages as a production organism over bacteria, filamentous 
fungi and mammalian cells. It is relatively easy to genetically manipulate with the advances 
in modern technology compared to other eukaryotes and can be grown to a high cell density 
on simple media [8]. Yeasts are able to perform many post-translational modifications that 
are eukaryote specific such as glycosylation, disulfide bridge formation and proteolytic al-
teration [9]. S. cerevisiae as a cell factory is more preferable for production of eukaryotic 
proteins than bacteria, since bacteria are not capable of performing eukaryote specific mod-
ifications and in many cases produce inactive or misfolded proteins [10]. Mammalian cells, 
such as Chinese Hamster Ovary cells (CHO), have more complex expression systems than 
yeasts, however, they may contain viral or oncogenic DNA and are usually much more ex-
pensive to cultivate, both money- and time wise [8] [10]. 
S. cerevisiae is nonpathogenic and generally regarded as safe (GRAS) for production of con-
sumable food and pharmaceutical products [4]. Comparatively, Escherichia coli has toxic cell 
wall pyrogens, whereas many filamentous fungi are known to produce secondary metabo-
lites that are toxic to humans and require extensive purification and tests to be performed 
[8].  
==PAGE:16==
4 
 
Certain complex post-translational modifications of heterologous proteins cannot be per-
formed by yeast, making it inadequate and less preferable for production of these proteins. 
Modifications include some types of glycosylation and phosphorylation, in addition to prolyl 
hydroxylation and amidation [11].  
A broad variety of S. cerevisiae background strains are available for industrial use of which 
S288c and CEN.PK are the two most commonly applied in biotechnology [12] [13]. Diverse 
strains were developed for different analytical purposes prior to becoming platforms for 
metabolic engineering [14] [15]. Microarray and comparative genomic studies of CEN.PK 
and S288c reveal significant phenotypical and genetic differences [16] . Many of the genetic 
variations are located in basic metabolic pathways such as carbohydrate, amino acid and 
lipid. These differences could very likely affect expression levels of certain pathways making 
one or the other strain more suitable for synthesis of a specific group of compounds. This 
was demonstrated by Tomas Strucko when integrating a de novo vanillin-β-glycoside syn-
thesis pathway in both CEN.PK and S288c. S288c vanillin-β-glycoside yield was 10-fold higher 
than CEN.PK when grown under the same standard conditions [17].  
 
1.3 Aim of this study 
Choice of S. cerevisiae background strains in the scope of designing new cell factories to 
produce heterologous compounds, may play a crucial role for obtaining optimal high pro-
duction yield. The aim of this study is to perform comparative quantitative analysis of de 
novo heterologous polyketide production in three different background strains by introduc-
ing the genes 6MSAS and mpaC.

1.5 Polyketides 
Polyketides (PK) are a class of complex molecules with a wide range of structural and bioac-
tive diversity found in bacteria, fungi and plants. Fungal polyketides have a variety of appli-
cations and play a key role in intra- and interspecies signaling [22]. Many of the approxi-
mately 10,000 known polyketides exhibit medicinally relevant properties such as antibiotic, 
anticancerous or antifungal activity [23] [24]. Chemical synthesis has in many cases proven 
to be an inviable method for production of polyketides due to their high levels of structural 
complexity. [25].  
Formation of polyketides is catalyzed by a group of multifunctional enzymes or enzyme com-
plexes referred to as polyketide synthases (PKS) and are classified into three types: I, II and 
III. Type I is further subdivided into modular (mPKS) and iterative (iPKS). Type II and III are 
both iterative PKSs. IPKSs are further subdivided into non-, partially- and fully reducing. 
Polyketides are formed by a series of chain extension steps in which an acyl starter unit 
(often acetyl-CoA) is condensed with extender units (often malonyl-CoA) [24]. 
Type I iPKSs are the main focus in this study and will be covered in more detail than type II 
and III. Type I iPKSs are typically found in fungi and consist of a single multifunctional poly-
peptide with similar active site catalytic properties to the mammalian fatty acid synthases 
[26]. The PKSs contain a core domain of ketosynthase (KS), acyltransferase (AT) and acyl 
carrier protein (ACP). KS catalyzes extender unit condensation facilitating chain elongation. 
The AT is responsible for transfer of starter and extender unit onto ACP, which carries the 
PK chain between the active sites. Additionally, reducing PKSs have β-keto processing do-
==PAGE:19==
7 
 
mains: ketoreductase (KR), dehydratase (DH) and enyol reductase (ER), whereas non-reduc-
ing PKSs have starter unit ACP transcylcase (SAT) and product template (PT) domain [24]. 
SAT determines starter unit (which can be an alternative to acetyl-CoA) and PT catalyze cor-
rect PK backbone folding. Terminal thioesterase (TE) catalyzes the cyclisation and release of 
the synthesized polyketide. Figure 2 depicts a schematic representation of the reducing and 
non-reducing iPKS domains. 
 
 
Figure 2: Schematic representation of reducing and non-reducing iterative polyketide synthases domains. KS: keto-
synthase, AT: acyltransferase, DH: dehydratase, ER: enoyl reductase, KR: ketoreductase, ACP: acyl carrier protein, 
SAT: starter ACP trancyclase, PT: Product template domain, TE: thioesterase 
 
In type I iPKSs chain extension is facilitated by iterative use of active sites, unlike modular 
type I PKSs.  Many of the known aromatic and clinically used compounds are synthesized 
through type I iPKS [22]. Variation of starter unit, number of iterations, folding pattern and 
reduction levels make this class of compounds too complex for prediction using bioinfor-
matics tools. Moreover, the synthesized polyketides can be further modified through e.g. 
oxidation, reduction, O-methylation and more by tailoring enzymes [24]. 
Type I mPKSs are typically found in bacteria and consists of multiple KS, AT and ACP domains. 
Additionally, KR, DH and ER can be present within these domains. Unlike iPKSs each active 
site in each domain is only used once. The number of modules determines PK chain length. 
Type II and III PKSs are mostly found in bacteria and plants, respectively, however, some 
type III PKSs have also been discovered in fungi [27]. Type II PKSs are made up of an enzyme 
complex consisting of multiple monofunctional polypeptides, whereas type III has two KS 
homodimeric domains but no ACP domain.

1.6 PKS in yeast for heterologous compounds 
A majority of natural polyketide producing organisms are slow growing and only produce 
low levels of polyketides. Furthermore, they are often challenging to manipulate genetically 
making them undesirable for strain development [23]. These traits make such organisms not 
suitable for high-level production and thus industrial use. Overproduction of polyketides 
could be achieved by construction of de novo pathways in a more generic host such as S. 
cerevisiae by transferring and expressing genes from known pathways. Previous studies 
have shown successful high levels of heterologous polyketides production in S. cerevisiae 
through metabolic engineering [28].  
Yeast as a production organism for heterologous polyketide compounds encounters multi-
ple challenges that need to be taken into account. A majority of natural polyketides syn-
thases are expressed from large GC rich genes in organisms with large GC rich genomes. 
Since S. cerevisiae has a low GC content of approximately 38% making the codon usage bias 
towards AT, tRNA insufficiency might become an issue for high-level production [23]. Codon-
optimization and in some cases endogenous tRNA supplementation may be a necessity. An-
other challenge is posed by the fact that S. cerevisiae is not able to perform a specific post-
translational modification of the ACP domain due to unsuitable innate yeast phosphopan-
tetheinyl transferase (PPTase). However, heterologous expression of bacterial or fungal 
PPTase in yeast has been shown to eliminate this problem [28] [29]. Some acyl-CoA precur-
sors, such as methylmalonyl-CoA, are not produced in yeast. Endogenous availability of ac-
etyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA polyketide building blocks could become insufficient for produc-
tion of higher complexity polyketides. Overexpression of innate acyl-CoA or introduction of 
heterologous acyl-CoA pathways might be necessary for high level yields [23]. Lastly, introns 
from heterologous genes must be eliminated to ensure correct expression and functional 
PKSs. Nevertheless, it is still in many cases, as previously discussed, desirable to use yeast as 
a cell factory for high production levels.

1.7 6-methyl salicylic acid 
6-MSA is an aromatic compound widely used as a model polyketide. It is known to be pro-
duced in many Aspergillus and Penicillium species, and serves as a precursor for the myco-
toxin patulin and antibiotic yanuthone D [22] [30]. 6-MSA is synthesized by the PKS 6-MSA 
synthase (6MSAS) and is made up of one acetate and three malonate ketides [24]. 6MSAS 
consists of a KS, MAT (malonyl-acetyl transferase), DH, KR, and ACP domain in the afore-
mentioned order [31]. 
6-MSA is synthesized in three chain extension cycles that each vary in keto group reduction 
levels (Figure 3). Initially, starter unit loading is facilitated by transfer of acetyl to the KS 
domain from acetyl-CoA followed by loading of extender unit malonyl transfer from malo-
nyl-CoA to the ACP domain. KS catalyzes the condensation of acetyl and malonyl units com-
pleting the first cycle of extension. Secondly, another malonyl unit is transferred to the ACP 
domain and chain extension is proceeded by condensation. The newly formed keto group 
undergoes reduction and dehydration prior to completing the second cycle of extension. 
Reduction of the keto group requires nicotinamide adenine dinucleotide phosphate 
(NADPH) cofactor facilitation [24].  A third extension cycle is undergone prior to dehydra-
tion, cyclisation and enolisation resulting in the completion and release of aromatic 6-MSA 
from the PKS.

Genetic and physiological diversity amongst S. cerevisiae strains influence the expression 
levels of different metabolite pathways. Knowing which strains is ideal for production of a 
certain type of compound may have a great effect on production yields. Expression of 6-
MSA in different S. cerevisiae strains could provide an indication of heterologous aromatic 
polyketide synthase suitability amongst said strains, since 6-MSA is regarded as a model 
polyketide. Previous studies have shown that S. cerevisiae does not have a suitable PPTase

heterologous co-expression of PPTase [28] [29]. In this study ASPNIDRAFT_44965 (6MSAS) 
from Aspergillus niger and PPTase from Aspergillus nidulans are used [22] [29]. 
 
1.8 Mycophenolic acid  
Mycophenolic acid (MPA) is a fungal secondary metabolite found in several Penicillium 
strains (such as P. brevicompactum and P. stoloniferum) with a diverse spectrum of bioac-
tivity including antibacterial, antifungal, antiviral and immunosuppressant [32] [33]. MPA is 
the active compound in several drugs used by patients undergoing organ transplant surgery 
to reduce organ rejection risk [32].  
The immune system is designed to reject foreign cells / organs by action mechanisms of the 
B and T lymphocytes [33]. These lymphocytes are heavily dependent on proliferation to gen-
erate an efficient immune response. DNA synthesis is a key part of proliferation and requires 
purine and pyrimidine monophosphate building blocks. Formation of purine monophos-
phate is either achieved through de novo biosynthesis or by a “salvage” pathway [34]. In the 
salvage pathway free purine bases or nucleosides are converted back to nucleotide mono-
phosphates by hypoxanthine phosphoribosyltransferase (also known as hypoxanthine-gua-
nine-xanthine phosphoribosyltransferase) [33]. In the de novo biosynthesis pathways both 
adenosine monophosphate (AMP) and guanosine monophosphate (GMP) are derived from 
inosine monophosphate (IMP) precursor. IMP conversion to xanthosine monophosphate 
==PAGE:24==
12 
 
(XMP) by IMP dehydrogenase (IMPDH) is considered the first committed and rate-limiting 
step in GMP synthesis (Figure 4) [35].

B and T lymphocytes lack the salvage capability and depend exclusively on the de novo path-
way for purine synthesis. MPA is a potent inhibitor of IMPDH and efficiently hinders lym-
phocyte proliferation [33]. IMPDH inhibition by MPA is only temporary, making it a very 
suitable and high value immunosuppressant.  
MPA biosynthesis mechanism in P. brevicompactum proposed by T. B. Regueira et al. is de-
picted in Figure 5 [32] . The polyketide 5-methyl orsellinic acid (5-MOA) is the first precursor 
for MPA and is synthesized by non-reducing PKS MpaC from acetyl-CoA and malonyl-CoA 
building blocks. Expression of several genes (mpaA, mpaDE, mpaH and mpaG) located in a 
cluster with the mpaC gene results in enzymes which convert 5-MOA into a phthalide inter-
mediate prior to addition of farnesyl group and oxidative cleavage to form MPA. MpaC is 
proposed to contain a methyltransferase domain (MT) involved in C5 methylation, in addi-
tion to non-reducing PKS SAT, KS, PT and ACP domains. [32].  Catalysis of cyclization and 
release of 5-MOA is predicted to be performed by cyclase/thioesterase domain in MpaC.

Only few gene clusters encoding enzymes involved in MPA synthesis have so far been iden-
tified. Furthermore, gene-targeting technology is often non-existent or inefficient in MPA 
producing organisms and severely challenge optimization of MPA production [37]. Heterol-
ogous expression in yeast could provide a viable option. In addition to the previously de-
scribed benefits of using S. cerevisiae as a production organism, it also has a natural re-
sistance to MPA allowing for high production levels that could exceed lethal levels in other 
organisms [38] [39]. S. cerevisiae contains four highly homologous IMPDH genes (IMD1-
IMD4) of which Imd2 is resistant to MPA [40].  IMD1 is an inactive pseudogene, while both 
IMD3 and IMD4 encode MPA sensitive IMPDHs. Growth of S. cerevisiae does not require 
supplementation of guanine in presence of MPA.

out npgA (PPTase encoding gene) from A. nidulans.

1.9 Experimental strategy 
The aim of this study is to investigate if production levels of the polyketides 6-MSA and 5-
MOA vary when heterologously expressed in the three background strains; CEN.PK113-11C, 
sScSGG and TS173. This was achieved by constructing a total of nine different S. cerevisiae 
strains by introducing 6MSAS (along with npgA PPTase gene) and mpaC (with and without 
npgA) in the three background strains. High Performance Liquid Chromatography (HPLC) 
and Real-Time Quantitative PCR (RT-qPCR) were used for comparative analysis of produc-
tion yields.  
Strains were constructed by implementing a yeast gene expression platform, involving Ura-
cil-Specific Excision Reagent (USER™) cloning technique to create a transformation vector 
for site-specific gene integration [41]. 
 
1.9.1 Yeast gene expression platform and USER™ cloning 
USER™ cloning technique can be implemented to fuse one or more PCR amplified DNA frag-
ments into plasmid cloning vector. This technique is based on complementary overhang cre-
ation and hybridization of PCR fragments and cloning vector. These overhangs are also re-
ferred to as USER™ tails [42]. The basic principle of USER™ cloning is depicted in Figure 6. 
PCR primers containing a uracil residue near the 5’ end facilitate 8 bp single strand fragment 
overhangs at the 3’ end. These overhangs are generated by treatment with uracil DNA gly-
cosylase and DNA glycosylase-lyase endo VIII (USER™ enzyme mix) catalyzing uracil excision 
[43]. Plasmid cloning vector digested with specific restriction and nicking enzymes result in 
single strand overhangs complimentary to the PCR fragments. This allows for hybridization 
of the USER™ tails. Subsequently the construct can be transformed into competent E. coli in 
which the host ligase enzymes catalyze ligation of vector plasmid and inserted fragments. 
The USER™ construct contains an OriE.coli and antibiotic resistance gene for selection and 
amplification in E. coli [43].

1.9.2 High Performance Liquid Chromatography 
HPLC is a technique that allows for separation of different compounds in complex mixtures. 
It is often coupled with a Diode Array Detector (DAD) and Mass Spectrometer (MS). HPLC-
DAD-MS schematic black-box representation is depicted in Figure 8. HPLC is carried out by 
administration of a solvent-sample mixture (mobile phase) through a column (stationary 
phase) under high pressure. The inner column composition is made up of solid core uniform 
particles surrounded by a porous layer [44]. Properties of the porous layer play a key role in 
separation of the compounds by interacting with and retaining each molecule differently. 
The delay by which each molecule elutes from the column is referred to as the retention 
time (RT) [45]. Features such as affinity, charge, polarity and size may be used for chroma-
tography separation. Separated compounds can be identified by comparing obtained RT to 
known RT for molecule determined by HPLC applying the same solvent and stationary phase. 
Mobile phase pH and temperature affect RT and must be regulated to achieve viable results 
[44]. 
 
 
Figure 8: Schematic black-box representation of HPLC-DAD-MS. Sample data is obtained as chromatogram, UV-spec-
trum and mass-spectrum from HPLC, UV-DAD and MS, respectively. 
 
==PAGE:30==
18 
 
Distinguishing single compounds solely based on RT may prove insufficient due to condi-
tional sensitivity affecting RT. UV-DAD and MS provide additional analysis techniques for 
separated compounds in eluate. UV-DAD measures the unique UV light dispersion at differ-
ent wavelengths of each compound and generates a spectrogram. However, UV-DAD only 
detects compounds with conjugated double bonds [46]. MS measures mass to charge ratio 
(m/z) of compounds after electrospray ionization and provide both a qualitative and quan-
titative analysis [47]. 
In this study, HPLC-DAD was used to verify 6-MSA and 5-MOA production in constructed 
strains. 
 
1.9.3 Real-Time Quantitative Polymerase Chain Reaction 
RT-qPCR is a technique used for identification and quantification of specific amplified DNA 
fragments. It can be used to determine relative transcription levels by using cDNA templates 
obtained from mRNA. RT-qPCR relies on a fluorometer integrated with a thermal cycler to 
obtain DNA melting curves. This is achieved by monitoring fluorescence of dsDNA binding 
dye (e.g. SYBR® Green) for each PCR cycle and plotting as a function of temperature [48]. 
Fluorescence intensity is directly correlated with PCR product levels and can be used for 
quantification. Intensity decreases as the temperature rises above the PCR product specific 
dissociation temperature resulting in unique DNA melting curves. These curves can be used 
to distinguish between desired PCR products from unspecific fragments (such as primer di-
mers). GC-content, disposition and amplified fragment length all affect the DNA melting 
curve [48]. 
Housekeeping genes are constitutively expressed under normal growth conditions and are 
required for basic cellular function maintenance. A relative quantification technique using 
internal housekeeping reference genes enables fold-difference determination and accom-
modate nonspecific variations, such as quality and quantity of cDNA used [49] [50].

2.7 Spot assay 
A spot assay was performed to according to protocol described in (Appendix 4). Yeast strains 
were inoculated in liquid YPD and incubated at 30°C overnight. Overnight cultures were di-
luted with MilliQ water to obtain an optical density of 0.2 at 600 nm (OD600 = 0.2) in a total 
volume of 1 mL. Liquid YPD was used as blank measurement. A dilution series with a dilution 
rate of 1 to 105 was made for each of the strains. Dilution mixture with respectively added 
(200 µg/mL) 5-MOA or MPA was transferred onto YPD plates (with printed wells). Acetoni-
trile was added in equivalent volume as 5-MOA. Dilution series with no added compounds 
was used as control. Plates were incubated for 30°C for 3 days.

3 Results and Discussion 
In this study three S. cerevisiae strains (CEN.PK1113-11C, sScSGG and TS173) were chosen 
as background strains for investigation of heterologous expression of the two polyketides 
synthase genes 6MSAS and mpaC. ASPNIDRAFT_44965 (6MSAS) from A. niger and mpaC 
from P. brevicompactum were transformed into background stains using a yeast gene ex-
pression platform. 6MSAS requires PPTase activation and was introduced with the PPTase 
encoding npgA gene from A. nidulans. mpaC was introduced with and without npgA.  HPLC 
and RT-qPCR were used for comparative analysis of product expression.  
In this section, results of strain construction and quantitative analysis are presented and 
discussed. 
 
3.1 Spot assay 
MPA is an immunosuppressant compound used in organs transplant surgery. It is a potent 
inhibitor of the IMPDH enzyme, which catalyzes the first committed step in GMP biosynthe-
sis. T and B lymphocytes do not have a salvage pathway and rely on IMPDH for GMP purine 
synthesis and proliferation. Additionally, IMPDH is important for proliferation of many or-
ganisms. S. cerevisiae contain four copies of IMPDH of which IMD2 product shows resistance 
to MPA. A spot assay was performed on the three background strains to evaluate MPA sen-
sitivity. Furthermore, spot assay using 5-MOA was also performed to investigate for any 
growth limiting effect. 5-MOA solution contained acetonitrile. Spot assay using acetonitrile 
was conducted to exclude any interference in 5-MOA setup. Addition of no compounds was 
used for control. Spot assay results are shown in Figure 9.

Concentration levels of 200 µg/mL MPA was in previously studies shown to be growth in-

3.2.1 Isolated fragments for transformation vector  
6MSAS, mpaC, npgA genes and pTEF1 promoter were amplified by PCR. gDNA from A. niger

overhang creation. Figure 10 depicts amplified fragments along with USER™ tails. npgA and 
pTEF1 fragments both have a tail complimentary to 6MSAS and mpaC.

3.5 Sequence analysis  
Plasmids pM, pNM and pN6 were sequenced and compared to expected sequences using 
CLC Genomic Workbench 7 (CLC bio). Only the genes of interest were sequenced i.e. mpaC 
in pM, npgA and mpaC in pNM, and npgA and 6MSAS in pN6.  
Sequencing data of pM and pNM indicated four large conflicts present within the mpaC gene 
(Figure 14a). MpaC was amplified from pDom7 plasmid and supposed to be introns-free,

splicing mechanism recognizes heterologous P. brevicompactum introns present in mpaC. 
Introns may contain stop-codon or result in misfolded proteins. Effect on MpaC synthase 
expression and functionality in yeast could not be verified in this study.

3.6 HPLC-DAD 
HPLC-DAD is a widely used analytical technique for separation, identification and quantifi-
cation of different compounds in a solution-mixture. Evaluation of 6-MSA and 5-MOA pro-

tained from 48h cultures prepared in high glucose YPD media (40g/L). The three background

detectability. Reverse-phase (RP) HPLC was applied with polar stationary phase containing 
phenyl and hexyl functional groups, and acetonitrile mobile phase. This technique displays 
a high range of polarity and is very well suited for separation of small molecules [45]. 6-MSA 
retention time of 1.42 min and 1.17 min for 5-MOA were based on standard with concen-
trations ranging from 1 to 300 mg/L. Chromatogram of standards displayed uniform narrow 
peaks. Expected UV-spectrums are depicted in Figure 16.

3.7 RT-qPCR 
Real-time qPCR is a technique that can be used for identification and quantification of PCR 
amplified DNA fragments. In this study, RT-qPCR was performed for indirect quantitative 
transcriptional analysis by using cDNA template constructed from purified RNA. Specific pri-

within the housekeeping genes ALG9 and TAF10 were used. ALG9 and TAF10 expression 
levels are reliably stable during normal growth conditions and have been evaluated in sev-
eral S. cerevisiae strains [50]. Housekeeping gene expression provide a method for relatively 
quantifying PCR product levels and neglecting quality of template DNA. RT-qPCR data results 
are shown in (Appendix 15) and contain melting temperatures, quantity and relative fluo-
rescence unit (RFU) values. Figure 17 depicts summarized melting peak curve obtained from 
RT-qPCR on background and constructed strains.

4 Conclusion and future perspectives 
The aim of this study was to investigate and compare de novo heterologous polyketide ex-
pression in three different yeast strains. This was achieved by introducing the two polyke-
tide synthase encoding genes ASPNIDRAFT_44965 (6MSAS) from A. niger and mpaC from P. 
brevicompactum in the S. cerevisiae background strains CEN.PK113-11C, TS173 and sScSGG. 
6MSAS was introduced along with the PPTase encoding npgA gene from A. nidulans, 
whereas mpaC was introduced with and without npgA. USER™ cloning techniques and yeast 
gene expression platform were used for strain construction. Restriction and sequencing 
analysis was used to verify constructed vector plasmids used for yeast transformation. Se-
quencing data confirmed the presence of npgA and 6MSAS in pN6 transformation vector. 
npgA could not be identified in pNM, however, HindIII and NotI restriction analysis produce 
fragment sizes consistent with presence of npgA in pNM. Furthermore, sequencing data in-
dicated introns in mpaC gene, and were subsequently verified by HindIII restriction analysis. 
Introns were unexpected, since mpaC was obtained from plasmid allegedly containing a 
coding sequence version of the gene. PCR confirmed inserts in integration site 2 on chromo-
some XII in all constructed strains. Additionally, PCR also verified presence on mpaC in all M 
and NM strains. 
Presence of 6MSAS and npgA need to be verified in constructed strains and not only trans-
formation vectors. This could either be achieved by performing PCR using gDNA as template 
and primer set designed to amplify each gene specifically, or by gDNA for sequencing anal-
ysis. Furthermore, intron-containing mpaC is not desirable for downstream strain construc-
tion of MPA synthesis pathway. Introns could affect gene expression resulting in low or non-
functional MpaC. Presence of introns could also explain lack of detection in HPLC-DAD anal-
ysis. Re-construction of M and NM strains may be necessary, depending on new chemical 
and transcriptional analysis. 
==PAGE:62==
50 
 
HPLC-DAD analysis was unable to detect 6-MSA and 5-MOA compounds in constructed 
strains. Unusable chromatogram and UV-spectrum results were caused by high media back-
ground noise. Mass spectrometry might have been able to detect 6-MSA and 5-MOA within 
the YPD media background by identifying possible mass adducts of the compounds, such as 
[M + H]+ or [M + Na]+.  However, this technique was not implemented in this study. A mass 
spectrum provide additional information applicable to distinguish between compounds with 
similar retention times, and should be considered coupled with future analysis (HPLC-DAD-
MS). 
Cultivation of yeast in media more suitable for HPLC analysis (i.e. resulting in less noise) was 
not viable in this study. All background and constructed yeast strain contain multiple aux-
otrophies such as his, ura and several others. Supplementation accommodating all strain 
auxotrophy was not a feasible option. However, prototroph strains can be constructed by 
sexually back crossing to wild type variant of the background strains. This could be achieved 
by crossing ScT strains with S288c, and ScC and ScS with CEN.PK110-16D [17]. Elimination of 
auxotrophy allow use of Delft or other defined minimal media for cultivation of constructed 
strains [41] [57]. These media display marginal interference on chromatograms with no 
peaks at retention times for both 6-MSA and 5-MOA [58]. An approach using prototroph 
strains enable detection at lower concentration levels and provide a better tool for com-
pound identification. 
Batch and chemostat cultivations of prototroph strains may further increase 6-MSA or 5-
MOA yields by providing optimized growth conditions increasing the overall biomass and 
metabolite production. 
6MSAS, mpaC and npgA transcription identification and quantification could not be deter-
mined by RT-qPCR. Data showed no 6MSAS transcription in any of the strains, whereas npgA 
and mpaC transcription was indicated in both background and constructed strains. This was 
suspected to be the outcome of unspecific primer binding. Regular PCR using the same 
qPCR-primer set from this study on cDNA or gDNA of background strains should be per-
formed as further investigation to asses fragment sizes of PCR products. Amplification of 
==PAGE:63==
51 
 
fragments larger or smaller than expected 160-200 bp would indicate primer binding to 
other DNA templates than npgA and mpaC. Experimental re-design using new primers and 
higher quality cDNA may be sufficient in achieving usable results. 
Northern blotting is a technique used for detection of specific RNA (or mRNA) and can be 
used as an alternative to verify gene transcription in this study. The procedure is based on 
RNA separation by size through gel electrophoresis and subsequently transfer to diazoben-
zyloxymethyl (DBM)–paper. RNA is covalently coupled to DBM groups, and appropriate 
treatment is necessary for elimination of any free diazo groups prior to detection.  Hybridi-
zation using specifically designed 32P-labeled DNA probes containing complementary se-
quences to whole or part of RNA sequence of interest can be detected by radiography [59]. 
Northern blotting is a highly sensitive technique with low background. However, it does not 
produce directly measurable quantitative results as RT-qPCR. 
In conclusion, transformation vectors constructs were confirmed, and inserts were detected 
in expected site on chromosome XII in constructed strains.  However, transcription levels of 
inserted 6MSAS, npgA, and mpaC genes and product synthesis of 6-MSA and 5-MOA could 
not be verified in this study. Comparative evaluation of heterologous polyketide expression 
in the three background strains could not be established.

Appendix 3 – Illustra™ GFX™ Gel Band Purification Protocol 
 
Illustra™ GFX™ PCR DNA and Gel Band Purification Kit protocol is used to purify DNA frag-
ments from agarose gels. Carry out all centrifugations at 16000x g. 
1. Transfer excised gel band to Eppendorf tubes (1.5 ml) 
2. Add 10 μl Capture Buffer type 3 pr. 10 mg excised gel band and mix by inversion. 
(minimum of 300 μl total) 
3. Incubate sample at 60°C until gel is dissolved. 
4. Transfer up to 600 μl sample mix to assembled GFX MicroSpin™ column and collec-
tion tube and incubate for 1 min at room temperature. 
5. Centrifuge sample for 1 min and discard flow-though. 
6. Add 500 μl Wash Buffer type 1 (with ethanol) to column. 
7. Centrifuge sample for 1 min and discard flow-though. Repeat this step. 
8. Transfer column to new collection tube and elute with 50 μl MilliQ H2O. 
9. Incubate sample for 1 min at room temperature. 
10. Centrifuge sample for 80 s and store flow-though.

Appendix 5 – GenElute™ Plasmid Miniprep protocol 
 
GenElute™ Plasmid Miniprep Kit (Sigma-Aldrich™) was used for extraction of plasmids from 
bacteria. Carry out all centrifugations at 16000x g. 
1. Centrifuge 1-5 ml overnight culture and discard supernatant. 
2. Resuspend pellet in 200 μl Resuspention Solution. 
3. Add 200 μl of Lysis Solution and mix gently by inversion (6-8 times) until sample is 
clear. 
4. Add 350 μl of Neutralization Solution and mix gently by inversion (4-6 times) to stop 
lysis of bacteria. 
5. Centrifuge samples for 10 min. 
6. Prepare binding column by addition of 500 μl Column Preparation Solution and cen-
trifuge for 1 min and discard flow-though. 
7. Supernatant from step 5 is transferred into prepared binding column and centrifuge 
for 1 min. Discard flow-though. 
8. Add 750 Wash Solution (with ethanol) to binding column and centrifuge for 1 min. 
Discard flow-though.  
9. Centrifuge sample for 2 min and discard flow-through.  
10. Transfer binding column to new tubes and elute with 100 μl sterile H2O.  
11. Centrifuge for 1 min and store flow-though.
