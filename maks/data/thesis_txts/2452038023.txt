Summary
With the increase of demand for Internet of Things products, both for general con-
sumers and in the industry, more products enter the market. This leads to compa-
nies focusing on fast development of products while paying less attention to security.
Security-by-Contract (SxC) is a framework for enforcing policies of a network onto
IoT devices. Using the concept of fog computing, fog nodes have a central role in
this framework. They can monitor network traffic, enforce the policies and perform
low latency operations. The communication of the network devices can be enforced
by SxC, but it is also important that communication between the devices local to
the network is enforced. This introduces the notion of Device Based Access Control,
where the type of a device defines the communication that can occur. However, when
a device is installed on a network, the fog node must learn the device behaviour and
its device type through a profiling stage.
The work of this thesis focuses on implementing a proof of concept profiling stage
for IoT devices. Focus is put on detecting the type of the device. The thesis contains
an analysis of the various approaches for implementing the overall profiling stage and
how to classify the device type. Based on the analysis, the profiling stage is imple-
mented and thorough testing of the device type classification is completed. Based
on testing and analysis, the thesis aims to optimise the classification of the device
types.
Legal issues regarding the implementation are taken into account and the
implementation is thoroughly discussed.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
The Internet of Things (IoT) is a rapidly expanding market [Sun+10].
The IoT
domain is massive, leading to a wide variety of products, either used for entertainment,
home security, health monitoring and many other tasks [Owe+16]. Given the large
variety of applications, there are many IoT device manufacturers which produce many
different kinds of devices. Some of the producers are well established and have large
customer bases, but the competition is nonetheless very high. This leads to many
producers focusing on providing cheaper alternatives than the more popular devices
available. More well established companies might focus on bringing new devices to
the market as soon as possible. This generally leads to the problem that many of the
companies decide to focus less on the security of their devices [DGM18].
The lack of focus on security when developing IoT devices can lead to several issues.
It is not uncommon to find devices with poor access control leaving them exposed to
unauthorised access from potentially malicious parties. The lack of encryption, the
usage of weak encryption algorithms, poor access control and weak authentication
are a few of many vulnerabilities that often are present in IoT devices [Jin+14].
A popular example showing the severity of the security challenge for IoT devices is
the Mirai malware [De +18], which was used to perform the largest scale Distributed
Denial of Service (DDoS) attack ever recorded.
In such a case, access control is
important. The IoT devices should only be able to communicate with a specific set
of relevant entities. This is relevant for communication with the internet, but also for
devices locally.
1.1
Security-by-Contract and Device Based Access
Control
To address the problems created from the expanse of the IoT market, one can consider
the Security-By-Contract (SxC) concept. It is a concept previously documented for
mobile code [Bie+09], web services [DM07] and also suggested for the IoT domain
[DGM19] using Fog Computing. Policies dictate the kind of behaviour that can be
exhibited by devices on a network, whereas contracts specify the behaviour of the
individual devices on the network. Matching the contracts with the policies can be
done to ensure that devices live up to the requirements of the network and if not, their
usability can be limited. This can ensure that communication going out or coming
==PAGE:14==
2
1 Introduction
into the network corresponds with the policies. However, attacks do not necessarily
have to come from other entities on the internet, but can also happen locally, which
is why communication between devices on the same network also should be enforced
through policies.
This, however, produces the issue of how to moderate the communication between
local devices. The idea of Device Based Access Control (DBAC) is then proposed,
where communication between devices is dictated by their device types. As an ex-
ample, it would make sense that a mobile device can communicate with a lamp for
turning it on. However, it would likely not make sense that the lamp can communi-
cate with security related devices, such as cameras or motion sensors. This approach
requires that the device type is known when a device is installed on a network.
1.2
Fog Computing for IoT Security
For the SxC concept to work, it must be possible to detect the intended behaviour
of a device, and also to identify what type of device it is, thus enabling DBAC.
This is called profiling and requires computational power local to the network. Fog
computing comes into the picture, which can potentially be used to give more or less
instant profiling results on devices on the local network. A fog node can thus provide
the functionality required to apply SxC and DBAC to a local network.
Besides
profiling, this also includes tasks such as monitoring of network traffic, routing and
matching of SxC contracts versus the SxC policies [DGM19].
1.3
Contribution of the Thesis
The main contribution of the thesis is to develop a proof of concept for profiling of
devices. This is to be used in conjunction with the SxC concept. The focus is on clas-
sifying device types using text classification and natural language processing (NLP)
and also extracting information about the devices for contract creation. Contract
creation is however out of scope. This involves investigating information extraction,
various classification algorithms, data extraction for device classification and overall
profiling workflow. Generalisability of the classification is of big importance, i.e. the
device classification should work regardless of the device’s protocol stack and on pre-
viously unseen devices. The focus is primarily on general house devices and industrial
devices.
To summarise, the contributions can be outlined as the following:
• An investigation and analysis of how information can be extracted from devices
for profiling.
• An elaboration on machine learning and NLP.
• An analysis on various text classification algorithms and NLP techniques.

1.4
Goals of the Implementation
For the implementation there are some goals that ideally should be met at the comple-
tion of the thesis. The goals focus on the classification of device types and information
extraction. While the profiling implementation is critical for the security of the SxC
framework, the security is not the focus of this thesis, but rather implementing device
classification and optimising it for accuracy. The following are the main goals:
• Device Classification: The implementation should be able to accurately identify
the device type of the device being profiled.
• No Classification: The implementation should be able to identify when it is not
possible to determine the type of a device.
• Information Extraction: The implementation must be able to extract informa-
tion from the device to be used for SxC contract creation.
The following are the main non-functional goals:
• Speed: The device detection and information extraction should not take more
than a couple of seconds.
• Modularity: The device detection should not depend on a specific method for
device information retrieval and should be usable with different methods for
profiling devices.
• Generalisability: The solution should be generalisable.
This means that it
should be able to work on many different types of devices regardless of do-
main, i.e. industry IoT devices or household IoT devices, and also on devices
beyond those used to test the solution.

2.1
Internet of Things
The internet of Things (IoT) is the result of the evolution of wireless communi-
cation and thus internet connectivity throughout the last decades.
There is no
clear cut definition of the Internet of Things, however the term was first defined
by Kevin Ashton, known for his work with RFID. Generally, the Internet of Things
can be described as the embedding of internet connectivity and computational ca-
pability onto many different types of devices, or things. This can be devices found
in many places such as households, offices or factories. It can be hardware such as
thermostats, doorbells or speakers and sensors such as motion sensors, cameras or
health related devices [REC15].
Devices can thus be defined as being any equip-
ment with communication capabilities and potentially sensing, triggering, capture
of data, data processing, etc.
Things can be defined as any physical or virtual
object which can be integrated into communication networks [13].
Some relevant
properties of IoT are Interconnectivity, Heterogeneity, Scale and Resource Constraint.
==PAGE:20==

• Interconnectivity: refers to the property that any device or thing can be inter-
connected with many different types of devices or entities in the communication
infrastructure.
• Heterogeneity: is the property that the IoT devices are heterogeneous, i.e. there
are many devices based on different hardware, using different protocol stacks
and networks.
• Large Scale: The scale of the number of devices that communicate and needs
to be managed is very large. Given the number of internet enabled devices
in today’s households, it is only expected to increase greatly.
Furthermore
communication will not only be triggered by humans, but also by the devices
themselves putting even more demand on the management of the devices and
their communication.
• Resource Constraints: refers to the fact that many of the devices in IoT are
either embedded devices or devices with specific functionality meaning that
their hardware is simplified for performing a specific task.

consider a four layered model as can be seen in Figure 2.1. The model has similarities

layers in the OSI model.

The Application Layer contains the applications, which provide functionality
based on the access and communication to devices in the device layer.

vice Support and Application Layer contains entities that support the application
layer. This can be middleware providing data processing and storage, cloud comput-

2.1.2
Security Challenges
Each of these layers provide different security challenges.
For all layers, authori-
sation and authentication of communication are important whilst also maintaining
confidentiality of data and protection of integrity. In this thesis the focus is primarily
on access control in the lower layers, i.e. the network and device layer. This involves
management of devices and their communication both locally and when communicat-
ing through the internet.
The fact that IoT devices tend to be resource constrained is also a challenge for se-
curity. This means that devices which have limited computing power and in general
constraints on energy, time, memory, etc. In some cases, resource constrained de-
vices will have issues implementing encryption complex enough for today’s standards
leading to the use of lightweight encryption algorithms [Jin+14].
2.1.3
Use Cases
There are many different use cases for IoT depending on the setting. The following
is an non-exhaustive list of examples [REC15]:
• Homes: where devices can be used for controlling and monitoring of the indoor
environment, can provide security, entertainment and more.
• The Human body: where wearable or even ingestible devices can be used for
maintaining and monitoring health.
• Offices: where devices can provide energy management, security or productiv-
ity improvements.
• Factories: where devices can be used to monitor manufacturing processes,
optimise processes, replace repetitive jobs, etc.
• Vehicles: where devices can be used to monitor position, speed and direction.
• Cities: where traffic monitoring can be used to regulate traffic lights, or the
city environment can be monitored.

2.2
Fog Computing
Fog computing is an architecture that has emerged due to massive growth of IoT. IoT
devices have different requirements, where some devices are safety critical and require
low latency to work optimally, whereas others do not. Furthermore the large amount
of devices also require vast amounts of data processing. Fog computing is combined
with cloud computing to bring hardware, called fog nodes, closer to the smart end-
devices directly used by the consumer [Jor+18]. Hence a fog layer is created between
the end-devices and the centralised services. The proximity of this layer allows for
lower latency to the end-devices, but also providing local computing resources and
connectivity to the centralised services if needed.
The fog node is the vital part of the architecture. Fog nodes are either physical
or virtual components. Examples of physical components can be gateways, routers,
servers, etc. Virtual components can be virtual switches, virtual machines, cloudlets
(local datacenters), etc. Fog nodes have location awareness in terms of its location
within the system to provide functionality to end-devices which are in its proximity
[Cis15].
In the case of Fog Computing, the following are important characteristics which
separates it from other architectures: Contextual location awareness, geographical
distribution, heterogeneity, interoperability and federation, scalability [Jor+18]:
• Contextual location awareness: The fog nodes have awareness of their location
within the system and knowledge of the latency cost of communication with
other nodes further away from the fog node.
This allows the fog nodes to
provide the lowest possible latency to the end-devices, which is much lower
than if the end-devices were communicating with a cloud service.
• Geographical distribution: Compared to the cloud, which is centralised, fog
computing, takes a more distributed form by deploying fog nodes at different
geographical locations.
• Heterogeneity: Fog computing is heterogeneous since it allows for communica-
tion with and data processing of many different types of devices or entities with
different protocol stacks.
• Interoperability and federation: Since fog computing spans over a large part of
the network, there must be cooperation with different providers. This means
that the components of the architecture must be interoperable and that different
parts of the architecture must be spread out over the domain.

• Scalability: Fog computing is adaptive when considering clusters, where elas-
ticity, resource pooling, changes in data-load and variability in the network
condition is supported.
• Real-time interactions: Fog computing uses fog nodes for real time computation,
whereas heavier tasks can be offloaded to the cloud.
Additionally, it can also be considered that fog computing is best suited for wireless
communication, where support for mobility also is possible.

2.2.2
Security Challenges
If security is considered for each layer, there are many issues that are similar to
those described for IoT. In the device layer, the IoT devices could be tampered with
which can lead to data leakage. The IoT devices can be used for spoofing attacks to
send fake data, or to spoof identities. IoT devices can also be used as the source for
distributed denial of service (DDoS) attacks.
In the fog layer, where the fog nodes exist, data confidentiality, integrity and
availability is important. Similarly to the device layer, tampering attacks, spoofing
attacks and DDoS attacks can also affect this layer. Additionally, since the fog node
==PAGE:24==
12
2 Theory: Internet of Things, Fog Computing and Access Control
might also allow for end-device communication to the cloud layer, access control is also
an important challenge, as this can be used to prevent IoT/end-devices from being
the source of DDoS attacks. The scalability of the fog computing architecture can
lead to congestion and lack of resources due to devices connecting and disconnecting.
The cloud layer generally has many of the same security challenges as for cen-
tralised services. These are issues such as sniffing of communication, code injection,
session hijacking, denial of service and poor access control.
2.3
Security-by-Contract and Fog Computing
The emergence of IoT has seen a large increase in devices placed close to users. The
lack of security in many of these devices potentially leads to rogue devices or poor use
of cryptography. Ideally, these issues should be prevented, but currently IoT device
consumers rely on the producers of these devices to live up to security requirements.
They also have to trust in the behaviour of the IoT devices that are installed on local
networks [Dra+09].
The overall idea of Security-by-Contract (SxC) is not new and has been applied
to other domains [DM07; Bie+09], but can also be shown to have its place in IoT
through the use of fog computing. This can allow for removing the need of trust as
specified above. As will be considered in the following, the SxC framework requires
computing and data processing power. This should ideally be local to the network,
since low latency is a must for the functionality that SxC provides. This is why the fog
computing architecture is relevant, where the functionality related to the functioning
of the local network is placed in a fog node.
The removal of trust occurs by ensuring that devices live up to requirements that
are defined for the local network specifically. This can be requirements of what pro-
tocols the devices are using or which devices can communicate with each other. It
would make sense to only allow devices using proper encryption practices to function,
and also to enforce access control of the devices on the local network. Access control
can be considered for communication to the internet, but also internally. For com-
munication with entities in the network, it makes sense to only allow what is needed
for normal functioning of the device, but internally there might be many different
devices that can interact and it can be hard to predefine without knowing the specific
set up of the local network.
2.3.1
Components and Workflow
There are two major components of the SxC framework: contracts and policies which
are used to enforce the desired behaviour on a local network.
• A contract is associated with a specific device and describes the behaviour of
the device. This can be information about the protocol stack of the device, what
==PAGE:25==
2.3 Security-by-Contract and Fog Computing
13
entities outside of the local network the device needs access to, the direction of
the communication with different entities.
• A policy is associated with a platform, in this case a local network, where
requirements are defined for the devices running on the network. This can be
requirements on the protocol stack, access control requirements for external or
internal communication, i.e. similar types of requirements as specified by the
contracts.
The contract must be defined by the developer of the IoT device or a trusted
third party. After having created the IoT device and the software for it, the contract
is created. Based on this contract a proof-of-compliance is equipped for the device,
which binds the software produced for the IoT device with the contract that was
created. The software, the contract and the proof-of-compliance is then signed with a
private key, thus binding everything together with a digital signature. This produces
trust that the device will work as the contract specifies, but not trust in the device.
This also depends on policies that are defined.
The policies are installed on a fog node placed in a local network. IoT devices will
connect to the fog node providing their contract. This initiates a workflow, where
proof-of-compliance is checked and the contracts are matched against the policies to
see if the device should be allowed to run. There are however cases when the proof-
of-compliance is not valid, where either the device is run without care of security, the
device is run but the policies are enforced by the fog node in real time, or the device
is not run at all. The same three outcomes can occur if the contract does not match
the policy [DGM19].
Lastly, if a device that connects to the fog node does not support the SxC frame-
work, then instead of discarding that device, a profiling stage can occur which at-
tempts to identify the type of the device and its behaviour. This profiling stage is
not currently defined for SxC. The idea of the workflow can be seen in Figure 2.3.
2.3.2
Profiling
Profiling allows the framework to create contracts for devices that do not support
SxC and thus cannot provide a contract for the fog node. If profiling is successful,
then, as depicted in Figure 2.3, the matching of the contract created in the profiling
stage and the policy can be done. The proof-of-compliance does not need to be done,
since the contract was created locally and the profiling step is trusted.
However, it is important that the contract creation in the profiling stage is authen-
tic and that the information extracted about the profiled device is correct. Tampering
with the profiling stage could lead to a device running with elevated privileges, using
sub optimal encryption or leaking information to unwanted services.
Sufficient information needs to be retrieved about the device. Essentially, as much
information as possible should be retrieved. This can be the type of the device, what
services and resources the device needs to access, what protocols are being used for

this, etc. For the extend of this thesis focus is on detecting the type of the device

2.4
Device-Based Access Control
There exists many different types of access control models with varying complexity.
On the more complex side there is Attribute Based Access Control (ABAC), where
subjects request to perform actions on objects. These requests are accepted or denied
based on attributes of the subject, the object and environment conditions such as
time of day, location, threat level, etc. Policies define the rules that apply based on
the attributes defined. This has a quite high complexity when implemented in large
enterprises [Hu+14]. A less complex model is Role Based Access Control (RBAC),
which is generally applied in large enterprises. The model grants or accepts operations
based on the role of the subject where the subject can have several role assignments.
These roles can be authorised for different operations and based on the subjects active
role and the rules defined for the roles access can be granted or denied [FK97].
Device Based Access Control (DBAC) has a similar structure to that of RBAC,
but its intended use is for IoT devices. For DBAC, there are defined several device
types. Each device on a local network is therefore through their SxC contract or
through profiling assigned a device type. The policies defined for the local network in
SxC then specify what types of devices can communicate with each other. Examples
could be:

• Device type Speaker cannot initiate communication with device type Media
Player.
• Device type Lamp cannot communicate with device type Speaker.
While DBAC is not as specific as other models, the idea is suitable for the IoT
domain, where there can be many of the same device types in a household. The
connections can be dynamic, i.e. many of the devices may connect and disconnect
frequently or a device device might be replaced with another device of the same type.

3.1
Initiating the Profiling
For the implementation, the profiling should be triggered somehow. As there is not
a usable implementation for SxC onto which the profiling can be extended onto it is
necessary to implement an initiation of the profiling work flow. Two approaches are
considered: manually scanning the network or integrating profiling into the network
setup of the device.
3.1.1
Scanning the Network
Scanning the network can be done with any network scanning tool. For this case nmap
is considered, which is a popular tool for scanning both local and remote networks.
The fog node could therefore perform scanning at defined intervals to detect new
devices on the network. This would then lead to the profiling of the device.
Advantages
Nmap is very configurable and has a variety of different functionalities,
including the possibility of doing OS detection of devices on the network [nmaa]. OS
detection could be used to provide extra information for the profiling stage. Nmap
is also versatile in what output it can provide making it simple to chain together the
discovery of the device and the profiling.
==PAGE:30==
18
3 Analysis of the Profiling Approach
Disadvantages
Nmap has to be run at timed intervals. This means that devices
might not be detected instantly and profiling will then be delayed. Ideally, the device
discovery should occur as soon as the device connects to the local network. Nmap can
flood the network with unnecessary traffic, as many of the devices that are already
discovered will be probed by nmap.
3.1.2
Integrating Device Discovery into the Network Setup
This option requires that the device triggers the profiling stage when it is set up
and attempts to connect to the network. This removes the need for the fog node
to perform device discovery. This can be done for IP devices by using the DHCP
protocol, which is a prevalent protocol for assigning IP addresses to devices [Dro97].
When a device is connecting to a network, it provides a set of options to the
DHCP server in a DHCP request. This functionality could be integrated into the
fog node, such that it is in charge of dealing with DHCP requests and thus acts as
a DHCP server.
The options are parameters which specify what information the
connecting device needs from the DHCP server to correctly function. This can be
that the connecting devices requests a subnet mask, a list of routers on the same
subnet, the domain name, the IP address of a DNS, etc.
Furthermore, DHCP allows the definition of custom options [LDRa], which allow
extra information to be passed as part of the DHCP request. An example is that it
is possible to specify the URL of the MUD file describing the connecting device as
part of the request.
Advantages
This approach does not require that the fog node performs any actions
to detect new devices on the network, besides handling incoming requests for new
devices connecting to the network. This means that there is no excessive network
traffic and that profiling can then occur as soon as a device connects to the network.
Disadvantages
The fog node is required to handle DHCP requests giving it more
workload and may require additional computational power.

This needs to be information about the behaviour of the device and information that
can be used to determine the type of the device. The behaviour of the device must
be extracted, such that it can be used for contract creation. For general consumer
devices (IP devices) the focus is on Manufacturer Usage Descriptions (MUD), but
alternatives will also be considered. For industrial devices several domain specific
protocols have been considered, but the focus is on BACnet. Lastly, as an alternative

Manufacturer Usage Descriptions is a file that contains various information about

From this model, there are various important fields:

MUD files are not used in practice as the IETF specification is

Previously MUD profiles have been generated by

In the same article they explain the process, but also released 28 generated MUD
profiles generated using their tool1. These files can be relevant for use in this thesis.
Another article describes how MUD profiles can be deconstructed into flow rules

Lastly, an article describes what MUD profiles lack in their specification [Sin+19].
They focus on specifying IP-addresses, ports, protocol numbers and such in the ACLs,
but not any characteristics of the physical layer. This article then focuses on learning
the physical layer properties of a device, such as packet length, frequency and more.

Advantages
MUD profiles describe the expected behaviour of IoT devices in an
easily interpretable way. This makes it easy to extract the behaviour in the form
of ACLs, which, besides describing IP-addresses, relevant ports and protocols to be
used, also describe devices of specific manufacturers that can be communicated with.
There are various fields, such as the systeminfo field, that describe the device which
is relevant for determining the device type. Furthermore there is also support for
DHCP options, which allow devices to send their mud URLS to the DHCP server,
allowing an easy initiation of the profiling stage.
Disadvantages
The MUD profiles describe port numbers and also protocol num-
bers. The protocol numbers specify what Transport level protocols are being used
for the communication, whereas the port number can be used to infer higher level
protocols, such as HTTP, but this doesn’t give assurance that the specific higher
level protocols are actually being used. Furthermore, many of the fields that can help
describe the type of the device are optional, meaning that determining the type of
the device using MUD relies on the descriptiveness of the optional fields.
3.2.2
Industry Devices
For industry devices there are various protocols used to communicate between devices.
These protocols allow for different types of industry devices to communicate mainly
on a local network and not externally over the internet.
The focus here will be
on utilising BACnet, as many of the protocols allow for some sort of information
extraction about the device and its capabilities. However, alternatives will also be
considered. BACnet is the most popular protocol having a market share above 60%
[Inta].
3.2.2.1
BACnet
BACnet is used for communication between industry or building automation devices
primarily on Local Area Networks. It also offers support for inter-network commu-
nication using IP [Intd], but it is mostly used locally [Swab], i.e. on top of ethernet.
Besides allowing for communication between various devices, it can also be used to
query devices for information. In the BACnet language, devices consist of objects.
Objects are virtual entities that describe different properties of the device. The de-
vice object is the most general object, which describes general properties about the
device, such as the manufacturer, information about the model and also what other
objects that are defined for the device. The following are examples of fields in the
device object that relevant [Swab]:
• Vendor_Name: The name of the producer of the device. Mandatory field.
• Model_Name: The name of the model. Mandatory field.
==PAGE:34==
22
3 Analysis of the Profiling Approach
• Object_List: Contains all objects defined by the device. Mandatory field.
• Device_Type: Describes the type of the device. Optional field.
• Description: A brief textual field describing the device. Optional field.
The other objects can describe what data the device can take as input, or return as
output, thus describing the capabilities of the device. An example can be the Analog
Input Object seen in Table 3.1. In the Description field it can be seen that this object
represents the analog input that the device provides, which is outside atmospheric
temperature. The current reading of the pressure can be seen, as well as more detailed
information [Intb]. It is possible to identify local BACnet devices through ”Who-Is”
messages, where devices respond with information regarding themselves. Devices can
be further queried with ”Read Property” messages, where all the properties for a
device can be extracted [Swab].
Advantages
The advantages of using BACnet as the primary protocol for the proof
of concept profiling is that it has built in device discovery and devices can easily be
queried for information about their capabilities. Many of the fields that describe the
type of device in the device object are mandatory fields, however how explicit they
are can vary. The information is easily obtainable and interpretable by a fog node.
Disadvantages
BACnet does provide some security measures [Swaa], but the in-
formation that can be extracted about the device is not security oriented. This makes
the retrievable information usable for detecting the type of the device, but using it for
SxC contract creation can be hard. BACnet devices are not always ”plug and play”
making it harder to integrate it into a profiling workflow without requiring manual
intervention from a user.
3.2.2.2
LonWorks
An alternative to BACnet is LonWorks. LonWorks is a proprietary communciations
protocol built on top of physical and data link layers created by Echelon Corporation,
also the developer of LonWorks. For any device to work with LonWorks, it requires
a custom chip produced by Echelon Corporation. LonWorks shares similarities with
BACnet, since data is described in the form of objects. LonWorks calls these SVNTs.
Devices consist of functional blocks, which is the part of the device that performs tasks
when it receives or sends messages. The network can be seen as consisting of many
functional blocks interacting together. Each functional block is defined by a functional
profile, which can be thought of as an interface or template for the functional blocks
[09a]. The SVNTs are specified by the functional profiles and examples can be:
• SNVT_volt_kilo: Electric voltage in kiloVolts.
• SNVT_res: Electric resistance in Ohmns.

Modbus
Modbus is another protocol commonly used for communications between industrial

3.2.3
Monitoring While Enforcing
Regardless of domain, one option is to monitor the network traffic of a device. In
terms of the profiling, the device could be quarantined by the fog node such that it is
allowed to run, but the policies stored on the fog node are enforced upon the device.
The network traffic could then be used to generate ACLs similar to those in MUD
[HHS18], to be used for contract creation. Furthermore the network traffic could be
used to classify the type of the device.
Related Work
Previous work shows how network traffic can be used to classify a
device based on its network traffic. This requires collecting months of network traffic
to train the classification models and then observing the target device for enough
time to gather enough network traffic to classify it [Siv+18]. This would require the
device to be running under enforcement of the SxC policies [DGM19].
Advantages
This method does not require the device to have a MUD profile speci-
fied, or require the device to be running a certain protocol like BACnet, thus making
the solution very general. This method therefore has a wide applicability.
Disadvantages
Since this method requires learning the intended behaviour from
the network traffic of the device, there is no knowing if the device has been maliciously
affected before being installed on the network.
There will be no reference as to
whether the behaviour of the device is valid, however it is still possible to ensure that
the behaviour lives up to the SxC policy. Some malware can detect if the device is
sandboxed, which makes this solution less viable [DGM19].

3.3
Classification of the Device Type
To classify the device type, various methods were considered. Either probing the
device for information, performing web scraping based on the contents of e.g. a MUD
profile or queried objects from a BACnet device and using it for text classification or
performing classification based on the network traffic of the device.
3.3.1
Device Probing
Device probing was considered due to nmap supporting OS detection [nmaa]. This
is done by probing a device by sending TCP, UDP and ICMP packets. The replies
obtained from sending these are used to extract data which is compared with data of
operating systems maintained in a database [nmab]. The accuracy of this approach
is not always the best and relying on this solely for detecting the device type could
potentially make the SxC profiling insecure, due to the inaccuracy.
Another method which is similar to this, is DHCP fingerprinting, which was men-
tioned earlier. This is used for device detection based on the DHCP options provided
by the device. This could also be considered as an addition to the device classification.
Additionally, it is advantageous that this is a relatively quick process, which makes
it suitable for the profiling stage.
3.3.2
Web Scraping, Web Crawling and Text Classification
Considering the information that is retrievable from MUD profiles and BACnet de-
vices, it is possible to obtain text to be used for text classification. In the case of
MUD, it contains optional fields which specify information about the device type.
Furthermore, the URLs in the ACLs in the MUD file can also be considered as a
starting for web crawling. These URLs could then be crawled and scraped to obtain
text for the classification. For BACnet the information could be retrieved from the
Device Objects or, as an example, the Analog Input Object in Table 3.1 and thus text
classification could be performed. This task would be very fitting for a fog node, as
the fog node could perform the crawling and scraping, and lastly, the classification
could be done locally in the fog node. This process has a low latency and the accuracy
would depend on the classification model and how it has been trained. The downside
of this approach is that it requires additional information about the device. Taking
consumer devices as an examples, they must have a MUD profile specified or some
other means of providing a textual representation of the behaviour of the device to
work. A fallback if this is not possible could be doing classification based on network
traffic.

3.3.3
Classification based on Packet Inspection and Network
Traffic
Lastly, this approach relates to what was mentioned in terms of monitoring while
enforcing policies. The network traffic of the device would be collected, while it is
sandboxed and being enforced by the fog node and the SxC policies for the network.
This would then be classified by a classification model, which has been trained on
many different IoT devices, such that the classifier would generalise and could be
applied to many different types of IoT devices. As a part of this, packet inspection
would be relevant as it could be used as part of the classification to determine the type
of device. As an example, the protocol could give away that it is a low power device
if it uses the MQTT protocol. The advantages and disadvantages are similar to those
in section 3.2.3. No external information is needed about the device, such as a MUD
profile, however the classification would not be instant and requires sandboxing.
3.4
Chosen Approach
Based on the analysis and literature review that was done prior to the beginning of
the thesis [Tho18] and the considerations mentioned in this chapter, it was chosen to
focus on Text Classification for device type identification. This is due to the possibility
of instant classification of the device type and that it would fit into the role of the
fog node, since it requires internet connectivity to obtain text for the classification.
Furthermore, MUD would be used as the method for gaining information about device
behaviour and to perform text classification for the general consumer devices. This
was also chosen since it is an under development standard. This would support the
argument that the proof of concept profiling has applicability in practice. It was also
decided that DHCP fingerprinting would be added to the profiling to provide the
most information as possible about the device. For industry devices, the information
extraction and device classification would be based on BACnet, which is already in
use by a large majority of the industry. Therefore, successfully implementing the
proof of concept would also demonstrate the applicability in the industry domain.
Furthermore using both MUD and BACnet would make it easy to obtain information
about the devices. For BACnet, the structuring of the data into objects makes it
easy to interpret and query for device behaviour. For MUD, the MUD file is easy

4.2
Approaches to Machine Learning
Machine learning can be thought of as computers learning from experience [Mit97],
with experience referring to data. This data is known as training data, denoted by
X. A training dataset containing N different samples is defined as:
X = {X1, X2, ..., XN−1, XN}
(4.1)
Each of these samples, Xi, are feature vectors, which, as is implied, are vectors of L
features x [Moz10]. A feature is simply some standalone measurable property.
Xi = {x1, x2, ..., xL−1, xL}
(4.2)
The specific use of this training data varies by the different machine learning ap-
proaches. The three main ML approaches supervised-, unsupervised-, and reinforce-
ment learning, are introduced in order to provide an insightful introduction to ML,
despite this projects primary focus on supervised learning.
4.2.1
Supervised Learning
In supervised learning, each sample is paired with a specific expected output value y.
The training dataset therefore also contains:
Y = {y1, y2, ..., yN−1, yN}
(4.3)
In this sense, the training of the models are supervised due to the fact that each
sample X of the training set is associated with an output y. The algorithm will infer

some function, g, for mapping samples from the input space X to the output space
(or feature space) Y through these pairs of samples and expected outputs:
g : X →Y
(4.4)
If the output space of the learning function is discrete then the model is said to be a
classification model. However, if the output space is a continuous variable, then the
model is considered as being a regression model [HSM17]. The focus of this report is
mainly on classification models and thus supervised learning.
4.2.2
Unsupervised Learning
In the case of unsupervised learning, the training dataset no longer consists of pairs,
i.e. y as defined earlier is nonexistent. The goal in this approach is then for the model
to be able to infer information about the structure of the dataset X. This can be
information such as determining which kinds of observations are related to each other
(clustering) or the uniqueness of an observation (density estimation). Determining
these things are not as clearly defined as in supervised learning, since there is no
matching y pair to guide the training of the model [HSM17].
4.2.3
Reinforcement Learning
Lastly, reinforcement learning is the act of training an agent (e.g. a robot) to perform
some actions in order to gain some reward. It can be understood as if any action or
overall goal has some reward signal assigned to it. The agent thus wishes to maximise
reward. It must therefore be trained to predict as accurately as possible which actions
provide the most reward. This approach can be seen as a kind of supervised learning,
since the agent needs to estimate some reward based on an action, however, this is
not the case, since the training data does not contain pairs like in supervised learning
[HSM17].

4.4
Feature Extraction
After completing the pre-processing of the text, it is time to represent the text in
a way that can be understood by the classifier. That is, the document should be
represented as a feature vector. The following are some well established methods
that can be used to represent text as a feature vector.
4.4.1
Bag of Words
Bag of words is a basic representation of text, where the text is represented as a
multiset (i.e.
the same word can occur several times).
This is implemented by
splitting the text into its various words and counting each of the words. Some example
text could be:
==PAGE:46==
34

'How did it taste like? Like food.'
'How did it feel like? Not like food.'
'How did it sound like? Like the wind.'
The unique words here are:
How, did, it, taste, feel, sound, like, not, the, food, wind
Each sentence can then be presented as a vector, which can be interpreted as a feature
vector, where each number represents the count of each unique word the text:
'How did it taste like? Like food.' = [1,1,1,1,0,0,2,0,0,1,0]
'How did it feel like? Not like food.' = [1,1,1,0,1,0,2,1,0,1,0]
'How did it sound like? Like the wind.' = [1,1,1,0,0,1,2,0,1,0,1]
This representation is simple and the features are in this case the occurrence of each
considered word. It does not reveal anything about the structure of the sentences,
or take into account the fact that one text might be twice as large as another text.
These are issues that the following representations do take into account [HGS10].
4.4.2
N-gram
N-gram models are quite similar to bag of words. They can either be word or character
n-grams and are bags of either n words or n characters. These n words or characters
must be distinct. An example could be the following sentence:
'How did it feel like? Not like food.'
which in this specific instance can be deconstructed to the following word 2-gram:
How did, did it, it feel, feel like, like Not, Not like, like food
Bag of words are essentially word n-grams, where n = 1. The n-grams can thus
be represented by vectors as described for bag of words. For n > 1 it is clear that
more information is kept about the structure of the sentences, whereas any infor-
mation of sentence structure is lost using bag of words. If character n-grams are
considered, the feature space will generally be smaller, since the amount of combina-
tions of possible n-character grams will be smaller than that of n-word grams. They
are also more general, since words vary from language to language, unlike characters
(for languages using the Arabic alphabet). Character grams have also been shown to
perform well when it comes to tasks like spam filtering, authorship attribution and
speech recognition [Gia+12].

4.4.3
Term Vector Models and TF-IDF
Lastly, term vector models can be considered. In this model, a text document is
represented by a vector, v, with size |W|, where |W| is the size of the set of possible
terms.
v = (w1, w2, ..., wW−1, wW)
(4.5)
Like for n-grams, terms can be the count of characters or words, but more advanced
statistics can also be used as terms. The information about each term can either be
binary (does the term exist) or multi-valued (i.e. counting the term frequency). This
model is therefore a more general way of describing and modelling what was covered
above in terms of bag of words and n-grams. However by changing the weights (i.e.
how the values for each term are decided), some different characteristics of the text
documents can be noticed [Gia+12].
Term Frequency-Inverse Document Frequency (TF-IDF) is a computationally ef-
ficient statistic that describes how valuable a word is for a document by considering
all of the remaining documents in the corpus2. The weight (w) for a specific term (t)
in a document (d) in a corpus (D) would be the following:
wt,d = tf(t, d) · idf(t, D)
(4.6)
tf in this case is just the term frequency, i.e. the frequency of which a term occurs
in a specific document d. This is advantageous compared to only using bag of words,
since the frequency can be more representative than directly counting the number of
occurrences of a word. The term frequency can be normalized in terms of document
length. This alleviates the issue of texts being in different sizes, however in some
extreme cases where a text is very short, the frequency can be disproportionate. idf
however is the Inverse Document Frequency, which is defined as follows:
idf(t, D) = log
N
|{d ∈D : t ∈d}|
(4.7)
N presents total number of documents |D| and |{d ∈D : t ∈d}| is the number of
documents in which the term d occurs in. idf thus weighs terms that do not occur
very frequently in the corpus higher due to the logarithmic transformation. Words
that are unique to documents and occur frequently within those documents will score
higher, whereas common words which appear in all the texts will weigh less. tf −idf is
therefore effective in ensuring that the overlapping terms that occur in the documents
are not what is defining the document, i.e. making the document stand out. This
tends to lead to better performance [BB09].

Feature learning is a new and considerably more advanced concept than the various
methods previously described. Instead of doing a lot of pre-processing to clean a

text and manually defining the features of a text, it is possible to do feature learning.
Feature learning is another method for turning text into feature vectors, however it is
not static like term vector models and using TF-IDF. As this has not used extensively
in the thesis, but has only been shortly considered, this will be a brief explanation.
The issue with simply using term vector models is that words lose their semantics.
In a term vector model, consider the words ”car”, ”cow” and ”chicken” which are

An example is doc2vec [VM14], which is an extension of an already existing feature

vectors of the words in a text, where the context around the words are taken into
account. The feature vector of a word is then the average value its own feature vector
and the feature vectors surrounding it.

Multi-class Support Vector Machines
There are various methods to allow for multi-class classification by SVMs. The most
common way of handling the issue is by creating many different binary SVMs and then
combining them together to obtain the desired multi-class support. One-against-all
is a method where k SVM models are created, one for each class. Let 1 ≥i ≥k, then
for i-th model, it is trained with class i as being the positive class, whereas all the
other classes are the negative class. This therefore creates k decision boundaries and
SVMs, and when classifying, the classification is only valid if one of the SVMs accepts
the input, while the remaining do not. One-against-one works by creating k(k −1)/2
classifiers, where each classifier is trained with two different classes of the dataset.
There is thus a decision function for each combination of classes. Classification occurs
by voting. Each of the k(k−1)/2 classifiers has a vote, i.e. based on the classification
input the classifier votes on either of the two classes in which it has been trained on.
In the end, the class with the most votes, also known as the majority vote, becomes
the classification [HL02].

4.7.6
Classification and Regression Trees
Classification and Regression Trees are a tree-based framework. The tree models split
the input space into different partitions. Each partition of the input space can then
be represented by a model which is responsible for the predictions in that part of
the input space. In terms of classification, a particular area can belong to a specific
class. In terms of regression, each area can be assigned a different regression model.
Consider the example in Figure 4.6. Given a new input x, it can first be considered

The region of x1 ≤v1 can be divided into whether or not x2 ≤v2, namely area A and
==PAGE:59==

B. For the subregion x1 > v1, the same procedure is followed leading to regions C,

seen in Figure 4.7, hence the naming of the framework [Bis06]. These tree models are
Figure 4.7: The two-dimensional partitioned input space of Figure 4.6 represented as

simple and easily understood by humans and can be understood as a series of binary
decisions. A simple example could be classifying some animal into male or female. A
splitting criterion could then be ”is the height above 80 cm?”, another could be ”is
the age above 14?”. Each leaf of the binary tree would then represent either male or
female.
Training the tree model is thus the task of finding the best amount of splits,
which input variables are used at a split and also the values of the split criterion,
all to get the lowest possible error. The precision of the predictions of the tree is
measured by the sum-of-squares error function. This is simply the sum of the square
of the difference between the predicted values and the training labels given for the
dataset. During training this needs to be minimised. When training the tree model,
minimising the sum-of-squares error function can be computationally infeasible due
to the large number of possible ways of structuring the tree. This is why a greedy
optimisation approach is often used.
The greedy optimisation approach starts with a tree containing a single root node.
This root node covers the whole input space. The approach gradually grows the tree
by adding a pair of leaf nodes one at a time. Each of these leaf nodes correspond
to another region of the input space, where a choice of input variables have to be
==PAGE:60==
48
4 Theory: Natural Language Processing, Machine Learning and Text Classification
made and also the value of the threshold of the variable to split at. This can be done
by a brute-force search, where the choice of variable and threshold with the lowest
sum-of-squares error is chosen.
However, the tree can be split infinitely many times, so there must be some criteria
for when to stop adding splits to the tree. This is generally done by using a stopping
criterion, where the amount of data points associated with a leaf node determines
whether or not to continue splitting. This usually ends up producing a large tree, for
which pruning is done. Pruning is a merging of two leaf nodes such that complexity
of tree is decreased, but the performance of the model remains more or less the same
[Bis06].
One way of pruning is by cost complexity pruning. In this method, m trees are
constructed: T0, ..., Tm. T0 is the full tree that was created during training and Tm
is the tree with only the root. Tree Ti is created from Ti−1 by reducing each sub tree
t in Ti−1 into a single node and for each t the cost complexity trade off is computed.
The cost complexity is a measurement of the increase in error for each of the removed
nodes. The sub tree t that produces the lowest cost complexity is used to produce
Ti. Once all m trees are produced, the tree with the lowest training error is chosen
[HSM17].
4.7.7
Random Forests
The idea with Random Forests is that several trees are trained and used in an ensemble
to vote on classification or, in the case of regression, the mean output value. Each
tree of the ensemble is created by taking equally sized bootstrap samples (i.e. random
sampling with replacement) and training the trees based on that. Training happens
by recursively selecting a certain amount of variables at random from all the variables,
finding the best split for the variables and splitting the current node. When all trees
are created, predictions can happen. The overall approach can be seen in Algorithm
1 [HTF01].

5.1
Comparison of Feature Extraction
Previously, Bag of Words, N-grams, TF-IDF and Feature Learning was covered. TF-
IDF is of course not a means of feature extraction, but can be used in conjunction
with the other methods for extracting features. Most of the main points that can be
considered when comparing the methods have been mentioned in the description of
each method, therefore this is a brief summary.
5.1.1
Bag of Words
Advantages
The advantages can be summarised as the following:
• Works well with a smaller dataset: Bag of Words doesn’t require any training,
unlike feature learning, leading to usable results with less training data.
• Works well with a domain specific dataset: If the classification is done in a
specific domain, which is the case for device classification, it means that there
is less variability in the semantics of texts. This alleviates some of the following
disadvantages.
==PAGE:66==
54
5 Analysis and Creation of the Classification Pipeline
Disadvantages
The disadvantages can be summarised as the following [HGS10]:
• High Dimensionality: The dimensionality of the vectors will be high, because
the size of the vectors will equal the size of the vocabulary for all the texts in
the training set. This is space consuming.
• Sparse: Due to the high dimensionality, the vectors are likely to be sparse. This
means that for many of the texts only a portion of the vocabulary will be used,
meaning that a majority of the vector values will be 0s.
• Loss of Locality Information: Because bag of words simply counts the words in a
text, any information about the structure of the sentences and the surrounding
terms will be lost.
• Loss of Semantic Relationship: Due to the loss of locality information any
semantic relationship between the words is also lost. Sentences that contain
similar words, but have completely different meanings, will not be differentiated.
5.1.2
N-Grams
The N-Gram can be considered an extension of the Bag of Words and therefore many
of the following points are related to those listed above [TM18].
Advantages
• Works well with a smaller dataset: This argument is the same as for Bag of
Words.
• Keeps Locality Information: Some information about surrounding words can be
maintained given N > 1.
• Keeps Semantic Relationship: Can keep the semantic relationship of surround-
ing words.
For N = 2, the words ”dead end” would retain their meaning,
compared to simply counting each word.
Disadvantages
• High Dimensionality: The dimensionality is higher than that of Bag of Words,
since the combinations of words have to be taken into account.
• Sparse: This still has the downside of having sparse matrices.
5.1.3
TF-IDF
TF-IDF has to be considered when used together with either Bag of Words or N-
grams, as it is not a standalone method, but rather a weighting added to each term
of the vectors created by Bag of Words and N-gram models.

Classification Algorithm Comparison
It can be difficult to compare one algorithm to another and confidently say that one
is better than the other. Therefore when possible, advantages and disadvantages will

other published articles.
The Naive Bayes classifier is a simple classifier and its advantages lie in the fact
that the classifier is easy to implement, it works well for text classification, it is

computationally efficient and does not require a large dataset to see good results.
However, the classifier assumes that features are independent of each other, which is
rarely the case in real life [HGS10].
Logistic regression is shown to theoretically have a lower asymptotic error rate
than Naive Bayes. It is also the case that Naive Bayes tends to perform a little better

Support vector machines are very useful for text classification, as they perform

quite well enabling them to perform well on new data.
Lastly, classification and regression trees are very easy to understand and can
reduce the complexity of the classification problem. However the trees are also prone
to overfitting. If an error is made for a decision plane higher up in the tree, then the
error can propagate down to the bottom of the tree. Random forrests however reduce
the chance of overfitting, since classification is averaged over results from all trees in
the forest [HGS10].

removed. Also, no n-grams are used, meaning only the individual terms are counted.

scored higher, thus lowering the score of words that appear in many of the other text,

6.1
Generic Implementation
It is desired that the implementation is as generic as possible, i.e. that it can be
used to classify many different types of devices, regardless of what protocol stack
the devices are based on.
As previously mentioned, it was chosen to utilise text
classification as the tool for identifying the device type, since it could be implemented
in a general way. Figure 6.1 shows the overall procedure of profiling devices. What
should be considered, is that the red steps are considered non-generalisable, i.e. these
depend on the implementation and the protocols that the device uses. Therefore it is
not possible to make a general implementation in those cases. As an example, devices
using MUD will allow for information extraction through the MUD file. However for
BACnet devices, it would be necessary to query the devices through the BACnet
protocol to extract information for classification.
Therefore the difference in the
protocol leads to different information extraction implementations. The green step,
text classification, is the general step, which works regardless of device protocol stack.
It should be noted that the Text Classification and Extract steps do not need to be
successful. Ideally, they should be successful if the classification fails, but this does
not mean that the profiling should stop. Ideally, the profiling implementation should
recognise when classification is not possible.

6.3
Initial Implementations
The very initial implementation used only URLs in the Access Controls Lists (ACLs)
of the MUD profile for device classification. Each URL would be scraped and the
text would be appended together with all other scraped text. This text would then
be classified. This showed poor results, only classifying 25% of all the MUD capable
devices.
It was then chosen to add Bing as a fallback, if classification could not occur
based on the URLs extracted from the MUD profiles.
The sequence diagram for
this can be seen in Figure B.1 of Appendix B. It was initially the idea that these
URLs in the MUD profiles could be used for web crawling and text extraction could
then be based on crawled websites. However, these URLs were hard to crawl since
there were hardly any links to follow. Therefore the initial implementation relying
on extracting text from URLs in the MUD files showed poor results, as a lot of the

URLs were backend resources providing no relevant HTML resources. Therefore the
Bing search engine was added as a fallback for text retrieval and later Google was
added as well. Furthermore, the dataset that was used for training the models in
chapter 5 was expanded and the new dataset was used for testing the implementation
with the changes mentioned above. The new training dataset also performed better
with SVMs rather than logistic regression which showed best results in the initial
pipeline testing. This lead to the use of SVMs for the remaining implementations.

accurate classifications, only the best obtained text from the search engines would be

device, i.e. the Parameter Request List. This list contains DHCP option numbers4,

NTP IP address, DNS server IP address, etc. To further ensure that the fingerprinting

which describes the devices configuration are sent. This is needed since the Parameter
List can be identical for other devices, so more information is needed. This is executed
in Message 5-5.1 of Figure 6.3. The result gives the name of the device, but also a
scoring of the accuracy of the fingerprinting. This step is completed to provide extra

6.7
Profiling Discussion
The initial implementation specifically for MUD in section 6.3 used the URLs in
the ACLs of the MUD file for scraping. The idea was that the URLs could be the
starting point for a web crawler, which could crawl the URLs and find relevant pages
to scrape. This would thus be the ”Retrieve information about device” step for MUD
in Figure 6.1. It was however quickly found out that it would be infeasible to crawl
the URLs and also the URLs themselves provided little usable text for classification.
Many of URLs linked to different NTP services or other backend resources required
for the functioning of the devices. The lack of text retrieved from these URLs lead
to the described implementation in section 6.4, where the ACLs were only extracted
for future contract creation and instead Google’s and Bing’s search APIs were used
to provide URLs which could be scraped. Only the best obtained text from a URL
would be used.
The classifier used for device classification was also used to determine the best
texts that were extracted from Google and Bing and their relevance. There are other
algorithms for determining the relevance of a text, but given the available training
data it made sense to use the same model. Using a search engine will already attempt
to provide the most relevant results, so trying to do this on top of that would be
superfluous.
In terms of BACnet, the profiling was implemented such that the devices were
simulated and then they were queried for their objects. This would then be used for
text classification, but it is not in line with what was envisioned, where the device
itself would initiate the profiling.
Generally, BACnet devices aren’t installed and
automatically connected to the network. Rather they are installed, turned on and
then an administrator configures the device through BACnet specific software. So it
is not straight forward to automatically initialise profiling for BACnet devices, unless
the BACnet communication is running on top of IP.
Lastly, a PDF to text converter was added, when realising that most of the re-
sults returned from Google and Bing when searching for industrial devices was PDF
resources. This meant that little text could be extracted, since only HTML pages
were scraped initially.

classification and not the overall profiling. The novelty in the thesis is the classi-
fication of the device and not the remaining information extraction.
The DHCP
fingerprinting is an already existing service, and extraction of ACLs from MUD files
or the BACnet objects is not important to test. Furthermore, to fully test the MUD
profiling using DHCP fingerprinting, proper configuration of the different devices is
needed. Ideally, testing should occur with real devices, since information like MAC
addresses, the parameter request list and DHCP vendor details are required. Testing
with real devices was not accomplished for this thesis. This is also why the testing of
the speed of the implementation only involves the steps related to device classification
and not the full profiling implementation. Lastly, the results of the classification are
analysed to identify how the solution could be improved.

7.5
MUD Results Discussion
The overall accuracy, rate of No Classification and scoring can be seen in Table 7.10.
Compared to the testing of the initial implementation in section 6.3, the accuracy
of the classification in the MUD profiling implementation in section 6.4 is lower.
Instead of using all the retrievable texts and combining them into one, the newer
implementation only used the best describing text. This introduced edge cases, such
as the one for the Chromecast in section 7.2.2.1. The idea of only using the best text
is sound, because it ensures that more noisy texts will not be taken into consideration
by the classification. However, it is clear that accuracy has dropped, therefore the
edge cases must be handled.
When considering the results obtained for MUD on the dataset made only for
general consumer devices, the overall accuracy of 83% is acceptable. The reduction
of the accuracy (75% down from 83%) is expected, due to the addition of more
categories. It would however still be desirable with a higher accuracy, such that the
generalisability of the solution could be shown. It should also be noted that the test
dataset is not that big, so to be completely sure about how general the solution is, a
bigger dataset with more categories would be desirable.
It is also important to note that if the accuracy and the percentage of ”No Classi-
fication” are added together, then for both MUD tests 87.5% of the results are usable.
If this profiling is supposed to be used in a real world setting, then the results must
be very accurate. However, if the profiling can successfully identify when it does not
have enough data to accurately classify the device, then the result is still usable. It is
better to have a profiling stage that can provide usable, reliable results, rather than
possibly returning wrongful classifications.
With the addition of new categories, some conflicts occur. As an example the
IndustryEnvironmentSensor class is very similar to the HouseEnvironmentMonitor
class. Both classes contain texts about devices that monitor the environment, but
the difference is that the texts for the industry class generally contain more technical
language and are aimed towards people with technical knowledge. Generally, there
are not issues with misclassification due to this, but the classification score might be
lowered for classification within these two categories.
If the classification of the Chromecast Ultra mentioned in section 7.2.2.1 is con-
sidered, it was classified as a Speaker when it should have been a MediaPlayer. It is
an understandable misclassification, since the devices in the MediaPlayer class typi-
cally can communicate with other types of output sources, such as TVs and Speakers.
However, if the extracted links are considered in Table 7.11 the scraping works well.
A lot of the texts are predicted as describing a MediaPlayer, but unfortunately a
single text has the highest score and is classified as being a Speaker. In this specific
case, it would be advantageous if the other high scoring texts, which belong to the
true class of the device could be included. One could consider instead of only choosing
the highest scoring text, to use a cumulative score instead. This means summing up
the scores for each predicted class and using the class with the highest cumulative
score as the prediction. In this specific case it would lead to a correct classification

CHAPTER 8
Improving the
Implementation
This chapter focuses on improving the implementation of which the tests of chapter 7
was based on. Based on the discussion and proposed improvements of the previous
chapter, attempts to improve the solution are implemented. These attempts are then
tested and the results are analysed. This includes trying to modify and clean the
training dataset to remove irrelevant text. Different ways of scoring the classification
are considered by taking into account the classification of all of the retrieved texts
for a device. This is done to avoid some edge cases shown in chapter 7.
8.1
Cleaning the Training Dataset
In order to avoid the issues of misclassification between similar classes, the texts in the
training dataset have been revised. Many of the scraped web pages contain sections
with reviews about the product, links to other parts of the website, descriptions
about other products and more. These parts of the texts do not describe the device
specifically, but it could still be considered relevant, since it helps describe the type
of website you would retrieve when searching about that type of device. This was
the logic in keeping it in the first place, however as these types of websites reoccur as

Classification of the Chromecast Ultra (section 7.2.2.1) and the Touchplate Light
Controller (section 7.3.2.1) was not possible. In both cases one text had a higher
score than than the other texts and this lead to a wrong classification. However there

the devices. The specific case with the Touchplate was already handled correctly in
the previous improvement simply by cleaning the dataset. However, the Chromecast
was still classified incorrectly. One way to handle this specific case could be through
cumulative scoring. Instead of basing classification on the retrieved text with the
highest score, each score would be accumulated to its class and the class with the
highest accumulated score would be the classification result. This was implemented
and tested using the cleaned dataset, which was used in the previous section.
After trying this, it showed poor results in the cases where there were many noisy
texts. This meant that in these cases there were a few texts correctly describing the
device, but a lot of noisy text which accumulated score lead to a wrong classification.
Therefore it was chosen to square the scores, such that higher scores would be further
distinguished from lower scores.
This would avoid cases where noisy texts would
affect the classification. The same threshold for discarding noisy text was used, but
to account for the squaring then classification treshold was the square of the old score.

CHAPTER 9
Legal Issues
This chapter describes legal issues regarding the implementation described in chap-
ter 8. Many of websites used for creating the training dataset and for classifying the
devices describe in their Terms of Service (ToS) that text scraping is prohibited. This
is a problem, because the implementation relies on scraping to provide its functional-
ity. Therefore, a solution is proposed that to the best knowledge does not break any
ToSs. This is then tested similarly to previous chapters and the results are analysed
leading a final attempt at improving the solution.
9.1
Legal Analysis
While the solution using the CSS method works well, there is the issue that many
websites prescribe that the use of any kind of ”robot”, ”scrape”, ”spider” etc. to ac-
quire or copy any content is prohibited. This means that if it is detected that scraping
is being performed, the website owners might decide to blacklist the IP address of
the scraping agent. Performing scraping while the ToS specifically mentions not to
is undoubtedly unethical, but whether or not it can lead to direct legal consequences
is not certain.
There have been many previous legal cases with different outcomes making it hard
to comment on the legality of web scraping [BB18]. An example is a case between a
start up company hiQ and LinkedIn. hiQ was providing its services by using scraped
data from LinkedIn, which was publicly available, i.e.
information that could be
obtained without creating a LinkedIn account. The case revolves around whether
data that is available to the public should be allowed to be scraped freely.
The
initial outcome is that hiQ should be allowed to freely scrape the public data which
is LinkedIn holds, however the case is not finished as of now [Har18].
There are several cases where the scraping party did not win.
One case was
between Associated Press (AP) and Meltwater, where Meltwater was crawling AP’s
websites to extract large portions of text only to republish a significant portion of the
texts. Another one was between Ticketmaster and Riedel Marketing Group (RMG)
where RMG scraped Ticketmaster’s site to find tickets that they could buy and resell.
Both of these cases ended against the favour of the scraping side, where the main
issue was breach of copyright laws.
==PAGE:114==
102
9 Legal Issues
Laws also tend to change from country to country, thus leading to different rulings
on these types of cases. The previous cases all occurred in the US, but there have
also been other cases in the EU which have ended in favour of scraping.
Besides copyright, other cases also focus on whether or not the ToS of a website
is a legally binding document or not. Generally either websites include their ToS as
a browserwrap, i.e. the terms are placed as a small link in the bottom of the website,
or a clickwrap, i.e. the user is prompted and asked to specifically accept the terms
for using the website. A case between Ryanair and Billigfluege.de GMBH ended in
favour of Ryanair, when Billigefleuge had been scraping Ryanairs website for flight
prices even though the website explicitly requires the user to accept their ToS through
clickwrap [Ire].
Overall, it can be difficult to determine the legality of scraping.
If a browser
presents their ToS through browserwrap, it can be argued that the ToS is not a
legally binding document, since the user hasn’t explicitly given acceptance. It is clear
that copyright laws are well in place and that republishing scraped data is against
the law.
In the case of the profiling of devices, it can be hard to tell if copyright laws are
breached, since the dataset for training the models does not need to be published and
any scraped text used for classification is not stored in any way. Furthermore the act
of scraping websites for text can break the ToS of various websites, but whether or
not this can lead to legal action is questionable. It is more likely that the scraping will
be blocked. Due to the uncertainty regarding the legal issues, it is chosen to produce
a solution which is not legally conflicting. This is also an ethical choice, knowing that
many websites specify that scraping is not allowed, even though one could argue for
the legality.
9.2
Solution Based on Snippets and a Revised Dataset
The solution so far used a custom dataset that had been created manually. Further-
more, Google’s Custom Search API and Bing Web Search API was used to search
for websites related to specific keywords. The URLs retrieved from the APIs were
then scraped to perform classification. To avoid any ToS issues, the dataset used
for training the classification models has been redone, such that any text from any
website that disallows web scraping is removed from the dataset. Any texts retrieved
from websites that mention nothing about scraping are kept in the dataset. To make
up for the removed texts, new texts are found from websites that allow scraping or
do not explicitly disallow it. Furthermore, instead of using the URLs retrieved from
Google and Bing, text snippets that describe each URL are used for classification
instead. Therefore instead of using all text that can be scraped from a website, only
the snippet is used. Both Bing and Google return text snippets together with the
resulting URLs.
These changes allow for a solution that is almost identical to the solution using
the CSS method described earlier. The only difference is in the training dataset and

CHAPTER 10
Discussion
The results obtained from testing the device classification are promising but there are
limitations to the results and also potential issues with the current implementation.
The limitations are mostly due to the training and testing data sets, whereas the
potential issues relates to the use of search engines and the extraction of optional
fields from the MUD files. This and the improvement attempt will be discussed.
Implementation
Regarding the BACnet implementation, it is not optimal that the profiling is initi-
ated by querying the BACnet devices. Ideally, the profiling should be enabled by
the BACnet device when it gets installed as is the idea with the MUD profiling im-
plementation.
BACnet supports DHCP on BACnet/IP networks and therefore it
could be possible to integrate the BACnet profiling into the initialisation of a device
through DHCP. However, the lower layer protocols, which BACnet is based on, vary
depending on the use case and therefore there would have to be different profiling
implementations depending on the lower layer protocols.
Improvement Attempts
It can be discussed whether or not the improvements
done to the implementation made any significant difference compared to the initial
implementation. The initial implementation simply combined all obtained text into
one large text and then performed classification. The final implementation removed
text below the scraping threshold and accumulated scores for each class. While the
accuracy improvement is only minor, it was shown that the amount of noisy text that
was considered for classification was reduced. This is an advantage because it can
lead to more consistent results and as was shown, increased accuracy. It did however
produce edge cases that weren’t necessary to consider for the initial implementation.
Performance
Search Engines Affecting Performance
The classification accuracy obtained for
the final version of the implementation is an improvement. The initial implementation
was based on general consumer IoT devices and used MUD URLs as a starting point
for crawling and scraping. Search engines were only used as a fallback, but it was
==PAGE:130==
118
10 Discussion
quickly discovered that basing the text extraction on MUD URLs was ineffective.
The implementation therefore ended up relying on search engines for providing the
texts used for device classification.
There are pros and cons regarding this.
The
pros are that relevant text can be extracted regarding the devices, which improves
the classification performance and thus less work will have to be done by the fog
node. The fog node will not have to do any cumbersome task to obtain text for the
classification, but can instead just query some services. The cons are that there is a
dependency of the external services to obtain text. If the availability of these services
is reduced, the profiling and classification will be slowed down and in the worst case
it cannot be completed. The results obtained from the search engines also affect the
classification accuracy heavily. If poor results are returned, then the accuracy can
drop. There is also variability in the results obtained from the search engines. The
classification accuracy obtained now could be different in the future due to different
results and texts being obtained from the search engines. It could therefore be argued
that more consistent results and a potentially faster solution could be obtained if the
classification did not rely on external services. An alternative could be solely using
the MUD files and the queried BACnet objects as the training and test data for the
text classification. However, the information contained in these is limited and also
more MUD files and BACnet object data than what has been gathered for the thesis
would be needed to test this.
Optional Fields In MUD Files
Another part of the implementation that can
make the classification uncertain is the search terms used to query the search engines
for the MUD implementation. The systeminfo field is an optional field, which means
that the specification for MUD does not require that systeminfo is defined for the
MUD. If it is not defined, then the device cannot be classified, and thus the functioning
of the MUD profiling relies on the manufacturers to fill out systeminfo. Furthermore
the field is non-normative, it does not have to follow a certain structure. Anything
can be written as long as it is less than 60 characters. This means the descriptiveness
and also the preciseness of the text will impact the classification performance. For
BACnet, some of the fields used as search terms are optional, but the majority of
them are not, so the same issue cannot occur to the same extend.
Terms of Service
Even though the potential legal issues forced the implementation
to change, it was still possible to obtain good results and in the end further improve
them. However, this can make the further development of the implementation harder.
Acquired text for training of the classification models will have to be from websites
not having a stance against scraping. Furthermore, websites cannot be scraped to
obtain text for the classification of the devices, making it hard to replace the use of
search engines for text extraction. Given the results, it is not necessary to replace
the search engines, but the functionality that Google and Bing provide can change
over time making it necessary at some point.

Dataset
Training Dataset
In the end, the training dataset was small.
There were 12
categories and each category had between 5000 and 10000 words. Some categories had
more text than others, but overall the dataset was nearly balanced. There were some
issues due to classes being similar. As an example, the MediaPlayer and Speaker class
caused trouble classifying the Chromecast device, which is a MediaPlayer, but this
was solved by changing how the individual texts retrieved for classification were scored.
In Figure 10.1 and Figure 10.2, it is the clear that there is an overlap in the words
of the MediaPlayer and Speaker category. There is an overlap on a few words, most
notably ”home” and ”google”. There were also issues between the HealthMonitor and
IndustryEnvironmentSensor categories, due to overlaps in terminology occurring in
the texts. The training dataset should be increased to see if this can help separate the
categories. There were 10 categories for general consumer devices and 2 categories for

industrial devices. It was problematic finding enough data to populate more industrial
categories, but it is still desired that there are more industrial categories such that
the generalisability can be shown with more certainty. The training dataset could
also be further cleaned to solve issues similar to that of the Wemo devices. Texts in
the ElectricControl category contained Wemo devices, and thus the word ”Wemo” in
their texts. This caused a Wemo motion sensor in the test dataset to get wrongly
classified. This is a lot of manual labour and was only done for this specific case,
but ideally this should be solved for all possible cases. When increasing the size of
the dataset and cleaning it, the performance of the various classifiers should also be
reevaluated, since the classification accuracy might scale differently depending on the
classification model. This could lead to a change in what classification model should
be a part of the pipeline.
Testing Dataset
The testing dataset for MUD contained 24 devices, whereas for
BACnet there were 9 devices.
This is a very small set of devices to test against,
because it cannot necessarily prove that the classification is generalisable, i.e. that
it will also perform well on classifying new and unknown devices. Additionally, there
are only two categories for industrial devices. This made sense based on what devices
could be simulated and the data that was available, but this also makes it harder to
tell how well the classification generalises beyond different domains. There should
be many different categories in both the industry domain and the general consumer
domain. There are also some categories that are better represented in the test dataset.
As an example, there are more cameras and house environment monitors than there
are media players. There is only one media player, and having only one will make it
hard to evaluate whether the classification can differentiate between classes that are
similar to each other. It can also be the case that the classifier is good at classifying
the device types that occur often in the training dataset, but not those that almost
do not occur. Overall, more devices are needed for testing.
Evaluation of Goals
Finally, it is possible to evaluate the implementation with the goals that were specified
in the introduction.
• Device Classification (Partly accomplished): The final implementation was
able to classify MUD capable devices at best with an accuracy of 92% and
BACnet devices with an accuracy of 89%. This is partly accomplished, due to
some incorrect classifications.
• No Classification (Partly accomplished): Out of the two failing classifica-
tions for MUD, one was a ”No Classification”. For BACnet, the only failing
classification was a ”No Classification”. It was therefore partly accomplished to
identify when classification is not possible.

• Speed (Accomplished): The final solution used on average 2.5 seconds for
classifying each MUD device and 7.6 seconds for classifying BACnet devices.
This is sufficient for a practical use case and thus the goal is achieved.
• Modularity (Accomplished): The classification is shown to work with devices

• Generalisability (Partly accomplished): Based on the results, the classifica-

Usability Within SxC Framework and DBAC
Based on the results that were obtained, the implementation does have its place in the
SxC framework. If the accuracy of the classification can be maintained for even more
devices, then it can have a practical use. The ability to identify when classification
cannot occur (i.e. ”No Classification”), is required in the practical use case. It is very
important that devices do not get misclassified as this could lead to potential security
issues, where devices can get elevated access rights. The fact that it can be hard to
obtain either a correct classification or get a ”No Classification” all of the time is a
potential security risk, but the results obtained here are promising.
Even though the profiling, especially for MUD, has been implemented such that
it is supposed to work with real devices, the testing was only done for the device
classification and not the whole profiling stage. Ideally, to further show the usability
in the practical use case of SxC, real devices that have been setup with MUD profiles
should be tested. For industry devices, a BACnet network including devices should
be set up such that BACnet profiling can be tested without simulating devices.
In terms of the speed, the solution is fast. The BACnet classification is a little
slower than MUD, but this is because the devices have to be simulated and testing
on real devices would likely lead to a faster classification.

CHAPTER 11
Conclusion
In this thesis, a proof of concept implementation for profiling of devices for the SxC
framework was done. While profiling also involves creating SxC contracts for devices,
the focus of the implementation was mainly on classifying the type of the device being
profiled. Furthermore the implementation also extracted some information from the
device, which can be useful for contract creation in future work. Identifying the type
of the devices gives rise to the idea of Device Based Access Control, where access
control within a local network, e.g. a household, can be based on the types of the
devices.
To design the implementation, an analysis was presented. This involved research-
ing how to initialise the profiling, how to classify the devices and how to extract
information from the devices. Based on this analysis it was chosen to use Manufac-
turer Usage Descriptions for the profiling of general consumer IoT devices and use
the BACnet protocol for the profiling of Industry IoT devices. It was also chosen to
perform text classification to identify the device types.
Various feature extraction methods and text classification algorithms were anal-
ysed and tested to find the best classification pipeline to perform device classification.
The implementation evolved over several iterations, each iteration focusing on testing,
analysing results and improving the solution.
The best obtained results for classification were 92% for MUD capable devices
and 89% for BACnet devices, however it was shown that the accuracies could vary
slightly. For MUD and BACnet just one device could not be classified (0.42% and
0.11% respectively of all the devices). Based on the best obtained results, this means
that 96% of the device classifications for MUD are usable and for BACnet all the
classifications are usable, i.e. not incorrect classifications.
The overall goals for the implementation were met and even though the obtained
results were good, additional testing should be done. Only 24 MUD enabled device
and 9 BACnet devices were tested and there was some overlap between some of the
device categories. Some device types were not well represented in the testing dataset
and there were only 2 industry device categories. Overall, this suggests the need for
future work.

11.1
Future Work
The following is a summary of future work:
• More Device Categories: More categories of devices could be tested to make
a more realistic coverage of the devices that exist in the general consumer and
industry domain.
• Expand Training Dataset: The training dataset could be expanded to ensure
that the individual categories are better represented. If more categories are
added, this will also require more training data. The imbalances in the training
dataset could also be reduced.
• Further Dataset Engineering: The dataset could be further improved by en-
suring fewer overlaps between the device categories and less ambiguity between
the texts in the different categories.
• Reevaluation of Classification Algorithm: Expanding the datasets should
be followed up by a reevaluation of the performance of the various classification
algorithms, in case the performance might scale differently with more data.
• Expand Testing Dataset: The testing dataset could be expanded to get a
better idea of the classification performance. The categories of the devices in
this dataset should be evened out, as there are some device types that are not
well represented currently.
• Testing Profiling Workflow: The current testing focuses solely on the clas-
sification of the device types. In future work, the whole implementation of the
profiling workflow could be tested rather than just the device classification.
• Testing With Real Devices: For MUD capable devices, real devices could be
set up with MUD profiles for testing. For BACnet, the implementation should
be changed such that it can work without having to simulate devices and thus
testing should be attempted on real devices on a real BACnet network.
