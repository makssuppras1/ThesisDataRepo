Abstract
Reconstructing unknown wave sources from measurements of their emitted wave
ﬁeld has many applications in science and engineering. In this thesis we treat the
problem of localizing a moving point source using only measurements of a scalar
wave emitted from the particle itself.
In the ﬁrst part of this thesis we determine the forward wave solution given the
source trajectory and intensity. We use this solution to prove a number of regu-
larity results for the wave ﬁeld. We also prove a uniqueness result for the recovery
of an unknown trajectory and intensity in the inverse problem.
In the second part we develop a Bayesian inference method for reconstructing the
unknown trajectories and intensities of a moving source. The method is based on
Gaussian process prior distributions for all unknown functions and Markov-chain
Monte Carlo sampling of the posterior distribution. We show how to condition the
prior distribution to incorporate system-speciﬁc knowledge such as starting points
or velocities.
This model is then demonstrated through a number of reconstruction problems.
We successfully reconstruct a simple constant-velocity trajectory, a circular tra-
jectory, and a more complicated trajectory together with their intensities. We
also show how the reconstruction can be improved using prior knowledge of the
system behaviour. Lastly, we show how the developed method can also perform
reconstruction of two simultaneous moving sources.

Chapter 1
Introduction
The problem of recovering the properties of wave sources from measurements of
their radiated ﬁeld arise in many applications in science, engineering and medical
ﬁelds. Examples of such problems are found in e.g. the propagation of sound,
electromagnetic and seismic waves [1].
In this thesis we consider a particular
type of such a problem where the source is comprised of moving point particles
with unknown trajectories and emission intensities. Many physical systems can be
described by such a model. A few examples include moving speakers [2], mobile
polution sources [3] sound simulation systems [4] and underwater sonar systems [5].
However, localization and tracking of potentially multiple moving sources is a
challenging task, in particular in the presence of ambient noise [2,6]. At present,
only a few papers have been published on the moving point source problem [6–12]
and most reconstruction methods rely on strong assumptions such as requiring
the source speed to be much slower then the wave-speed [7], knowledge about
several derivatives of the measurement data [8], or simplifying assumptions such
as constant source acceleration [9] or known intensities. In this thesis we aim to
develop a method capable of reconstructing simultaneously the position and the
emission intensity of a single point source moving at an appreciable fraction of the
wave speed using only wave ﬁeld measurements.
The reconstruction methods for particle trajectories found in the literature can be
categorized into two tyepes of methods [6]. The ﬁrst category consists of algebraic
methods where the problem is reduced to algebraic equations of parameters of the
unknown functions. The second category contains more standard optimization
methods based on minimizing some objective functional. A common example is
1

the least-square method, where the underlying PDE is solved while the estimated
functions are varied in some controlled way. The ﬁrst approach has been used
both for stationary sources [8,13] and has recently been applied the moving source
problem [6]. It has the advantage of being computationally eﬃcient since it does
not rely on iterative computations of the forward solution. However, the method
must be speciﬁcally constructed for a certain type of problem and may not gen-
eralize easily if the problem assumptions are changed. Approaches of the second
type have been used in the context of stationary sources [14–16]. While it is less
eﬃcient, since we have to iteratively solve a PDE problem, it oﬀers a larger degree
of ﬂexibility and versatility.
The method we consider is this thesis is best described as the second kind. How-
ever, instead of simply optimizing an objective functional, we instead take a
Bayesian approach. The Bayesian approach to inverse problems has seen a surge
of interest in recent years [17,18]. Inverse problems are often ill-posed and require
some amount of regularization to yield stable solutions.
This ﬁts conveniently
into the Bayesian framework where such regularization can be provided by prior
knowledge. Similar methods to the ones we consider in this thesis, have already
been used succesfully in e.g. the context of X-ray transforms [19] and conductivity
estimation, see e.g. [20] and references therein. A Bayesian framework has also
been applied in the context of moving sources [2], however in a very speciﬁc setting
of tracking multiple speaker in reverberant environments using parametric models.
The method we consider in this thesis aims to determine the distribution of the
unknown functions based on measurement data. The distribution of functions is
based on nonparametric Gaussian process priors and we evaluate statistical es-
timators for the unknown source properties through Markov-chain Monte Carlo
sampling. This approach oﬀers a number of advantages compared to both the
algebraic and the more standard least-squares approaches. Firstly, we can infer
other quantities than the simple point-estimates oﬀered by a least-squares method.
Examples include the mean of the data-conditioned distribution and distributional
quantiles. Secondly, incorporating additional prior knowledge into the reconstruc-
tion scheme becomes a trivial task as we shall see examples of how simple prior
information can greatly aid the reconstruction. To the best of our knowledge this
type of approach has not yet been used in the context of moving sources.

Chapter 2
The Forward Problem
In this chapter we consider the forward problem where we are given the source
trajectory p and intensity q and wish to determine the resulting wave ﬁeld u. We
ﬁrst solve the forward problem in the Fourier domain and then ﬁnd the time-
domain solution by an inverse Fourier transform. Then, we prove a number of
regularity results for the obtained solution. Lastly, we discuss how to implement
the solution numerically.

proﬁle q : [0, T] →R. The resulting scalar wave ﬁeld u can then be described by

Chapter 3
Uniqueness result for the inverse problem
In this chapter we consider the following inverse problem: Given measurement of
the wave ﬁeld over some surface in space, can we uniquely reconstruct the unknown
source trajectory and intensity?
We here present one of our main theoretical
results, which is a uniqueness guarantee for such a reconstruction.
We ﬁrst mention that several uniqueness results exist for inverse source problems.
We here mention two that concern moving sources and are therefore relevant for our
purposes. The recent work by Hu et al. [11] gives a number of uniqueness results
including a scalar uniqueness result for a distributed source when the intensity is a
known positive function and the trajectory lies in a plane. The proof in [10] gives
a uniqueness and stability result for interior detection points but with a constant
intensity of emission. The uniqueness result we give here resembles the ﬁrst of
these but with a number of key diﬀerences: We assume a point source and remove
the assumptions of given positive intensity and the trajectory being restricted to a
plane. In addition, we also obtain uniqueness for the unknown intensity function.

Bayesian inference for moving sources
When constructing models for physical system, we often have the choice between
using data-driven models and knowledge-driven models. As the name suggests,
data-driven models are based on data from measurements of the system while
knowledge-driven models are based on knowledge of how the physical system be-
haves, e.g. the underlying physical laws. Data-driven models rely on real behavior
of the system, but can be prone to various problems such as overﬁtting or insuf-
ﬁciently available or noisy data. Meanwhile, knowledge-driven models can fail to
capture important features since it is not based on data.
Bayesian data analysis is an attempt to combine these two approaches. In the
Bayesian framework, we apply prior knowledge of the system behavior and then
ﬁt the model to observed data. Bayesian data analysis can be divided into three
steps [22].
1. Constructing a full probability model. This includes specifying distributions
for all known and unknown quantities of interest.
2. Conditioning on observed data. In this step we infer the posterior, i.e. data-
conditioned probability distribution for any quantities of interest.
3. Evaluating the model ﬁt and calculating quantities of interest from the ob-
tained posterior distribution.
In the general problem of Bayesian inference we are given some data D and a
model M for our data. We then infer the posterior probability density P(M|D)

a balance between system-speciﬁc knowledge and data-based knowledge. The pres-

4.1
Gaussian processes
To be able to consider inference directly on the function spaces, we can use a
Gaussian process (GP) to describe a distribution over functions. A GP is a non-
parametric model deﬁned as a stochastic process in which every ﬁnite collection of
function values has a multivariate normal distribution. Since a realization of a GP
corresponds to a random function, it will be a natural choice as a prior distribution
over our unknown trajectories and intensities.
24
==PAGE:33==
4.1. GAUSSIAN PROCESSES
If a random function f : R →R has a GP distribution, we write
f ∼GP(m, k),
(4.3)
and the distribution is completely characterized by its mean function m : R →R
and covariance function k : R2 →R.
The mean function m is a parametric
function that represents an initial guess of the latent target function. By choosing
a speciﬁc type of mean function, e.g. a linear function, we force the GP to center
around this mean function while still allowing deviations. This is a way of reducing
dimensionality, since the posterior can concentrate close to the mean function, but
to an extent supported by the available data. The covariance function k speciﬁes
the covariance between the function values at any two points, i.e. k(t, t′) gives the
covariance of the function evaluated at t and t′. It controls the smoothness of the
realizations from the GP and the degree of regression towards the mean function.
We can understand GPs as an expansion in suitable basis functions where the
coeﬃcients are normally distributed.
In fact, all linear basis function models
f(t) = wTφ(t) with a Gaussian prior over the weight vector w ∼N(w0, Σw)
correspond to a GP with mean function m(t) = w0φ(t) and kernel function
k(t, t′) = φ(t)TΣwφ(t′). We thus have a method for translating from weight space
view to function space view [22].
The deﬁning property of a GP is that every collection of function values are nor-
mally distributed. Writing t = (t1, . . . , tn) for a vector of sampling times and
f(t) = (f(t1), . . . , f(tn)) for the corresponding function values, we have
f(t) ∼N(m, K).
(4.4)
Here, N(m, K) is the multivariate normal distribution with the mean vector m =
(m(t1), . . . , m(tn)) evaluated at the sampling times and covariance matrix K. The
covariance matrix K is often called the Gram matrix and its elements are simply
evaluations of the covariance function, i.e. Kij = k(ti, tj). In the following section
we discuss possible choices for the covariance function.

Markov chain Monte Carlo (MCMC) is a general and very powerful method which

visited more often than regions with low density. We can thus obtain samples of
the desired distribution, by recording states from the chain.

the chain and the new sample. Lastly, the acceptance probability is computed
using only the log-likelihood instead of the log-joint density.
Intuitively, since
our samples are based on the prior we can avoid drawing samples that have very
low prior density and thus reject less samples. We will use the pre-conditioned
Crank-Nicolson method to sample from the posterior distribution.
Usually the most computationally expensive step in MCMC algorithms is solving
the forward problem to evaluate the log-likelihood. Even in our case where we have
an analytic, although implicit, solution available, the evaluation of the forward map
is the bottleneck of the MCMC algorithm. In more severe cases, one can employ
local approximations of the forward map [28], enlist the help of a neural network
as was recently demonstrated [29] to help with this problem or attempt to exploit
geometric properties of the posterior [30].

4.3.3
Assessing convergence
To trust the estimate from a sampling-based algorithms such as MCMC we need
ways to measure the degree of convergence to a stationary distribution. In the
case of MCMC we have a many available tools to do this and we here discuss some
options that we use in this work.
Inference from simulation draws can generally suﬀer from two issues [22]. The ﬁrst
is if the simulation has not run long enough for the sample to accurately reﬂect
the underlying distribution. The second is the within-chain correlations, which is
not necessarily a problem since we do not care about the ordering of samples, but
which reduces the eﬀective number of samples. In the following we discuss tools
to assess whether our samples suﬀer from these issues.
Warm-up and multiple initial guesses
The most obvious way to assess convergence is to monitor the evolution of some
representative quantity during the sampling process. If we do this for multiple
initializations of the algorithm, we can get an idea of whether it has reached a
stationary distribution. Figure 4.4 gives an example of this, where the x-coordinate
of the starting point of a trajectory reconstruction modelled by a GP is shown for
four diﬀerent initial guesses. The ﬁgure indicates that all four runs reach the same
stationary distribution, independently of their initial starting points, after roughly
2000 iterations.
If some of the runs reached diﬀerent stationary distributions,
this could indicate a multimoded posterior distribution, but this is not the case
here. This can also give an indication of how many samples should be discarded
when performing inference from the samples. This discarding of samples is usually
referred to as warm-up. As a general rule of thumb we always discard the ﬁrst half
of the samples as warm-up, which is always more than suﬃcient for our purposes.

guarantee that the estimated mode from our samples is close to the true poste-

Other estimators
As discussed, the posterior mode gives a more volatile estimation of the latent
functions than the posterior mean. However, if we take the average of the posterior
mode from each computed chain of samples we can reduce this volatility and we
will see examples later of how this estimator can result in excellent estimations
of the latent functions. The idea behind this is that the average of these modes
gives a better estimate of the true mode of the posterior.
More sophisticated
approaches would be to average over e.g. the 10 % of the samples with highest
posterior density, ﬁnd a weighted sample average or even ﬁt some type of Gaussian
to the peak of the distribution. This idea is illustrated in Fig. 4.6 where the 5
samples with highest density are averaged to obtain an averaged sample mode,
which is much closer to the true mode than the sample mode. In this work we
restrict ourselves to the chain-wise modes and means as well as the modes and
means averaged over the chains.
Finally we do note that the use of Bayesian
inference with GP priors for inverse problems is still new. In some cases statistical
guarantees exist for certain estimators in these types of problems [19,20] but this
is, to the best of our knowledge, not the case for the problem we consider here.

Chapter 5
Numerical reconstruction results
In this chapter we present our numerical results for the reconstruction method.
Recall that the goal is to reconstruct the unknown source trajectory and source
magnitude (intensity) from wave ﬁeld measurement data. We consider four diﬀer-
ent examples for reconstruction with two diﬀerent intensity proﬁles. We also look
at how the reconstruction method is aﬀected by noise in the measurement data.
Then, we consider an example that illustrates how the choice of measurement sur-
face aﬀects the reconstruction. Finally, we attempt to reconstruct simultaneously
the trajectory and intensity of two point sources.
For each of the cases, we focus on a particular aspect of the reconstruction pro-
cedure.
We start with a simple case of a straight line with constant intensity
and focus on how to choose suitable hyperparameters and understand their inﬂu-
ence on the reconstruction. For the second case we consider a circular trajectory
and introduce a more complicated intensity proﬁle. We also consider the eﬀect of
noisy measurements. In the third case, we consider a longer and more complicated
trajectory and compare with a reconstruction that uses a smaller measurement
surface. We also show how conditioning on prior information can improve the
reconstruction. Lastly, we show how the algorithm is capable of reconstructing
multiple trajectories and intensities at once.

Chapter 6
Concluding remarks
In this ﬁnal chapter we outline some ideas for future work and end with a conclu-
sion.
6.1
Future work
The work presented in this thesis oﬀers many opportunities for future research.
We here highlight a few ideas for future directions. Firstly, it would be interesting
to test the method we have developed here on a broader class of trajectories and
intensities.
Are some types of trajectories particularly diﬃcult to reconstruct?
This would also encourage us to explore the parameter space of the method further.
We could for example move the sensors further away or change the geometry of
the measurement surface entirely and consider diﬀerent types of additive noise. In
addition, we could test various types of conditioning to explore how they aid with
the reconstruction.
There are several ways we could improve our Bayesian inference scheme. Firstly, it
would be advantageous to automate the selection of hyperparameters and perhaps
include them in a fully Bayesian way where we also assign them prior distributions.
They could then be included in an MCMC algorithm as part of the inference pro-
cess. Secondly, we could consider other kernel types such as the Mat´ern kernel that
we discussed in Chapter 4 for less smooth functions. Thirdly, we could consider
other estimators than the posterior mean and mode and try to ﬁnd estimators that
69
==PAGE:78==
CHAPTER 6. CONCLUDING REMARKS
better reconstruct the true trajectory. In relation to this last point, it would be
ideal if we could obtain some statistical guarantees for the convergence of certain
estimators such as obtained for others problem [19,20].
Finally, it would be interesting to further generalize our method to the case of
multiple moving sources. On the theoretical side we could attempt to address when
we have uniqueness and stability for this case. On the numerical side, we could
further test our method and develop strategies to provide consistent reconstruction.
This could also involve some type of conditioning, e.g. the reconstruction problem
could become signiﬁcantly easier if the starting points were provided.
6.2
Conclusion
In this thesis we have considered the problem of reconstructing the unknown tra-
jectory and emission intensity of a moving and radiating point source from its
emitted wave ﬁeld. We ﬁrst derived the solution to the moving source problem
through a frequency-domain analysis. We then used both the frequency and time-
domain solutions to prove a number of regularity results for the solution. This
included its square-integrability and smoothness.
We then moved to the inverse problem of determining the unknown source func-
tions from gathered wave data. We ﬁrst proved a uniqueness result for the un-
known trajectory and intensity under weak assumptions on these functions. Then
we covered basic theory on Gaussian processes and used these as a basis for our
Bayesian probabilistic model. To sample from the resulting data-conditioned pos-
terior distribution over the unknown source functions, we described a numerical
MCMC algorithm that has recently been used for similar problems.
The numerical results based on this Bayesian approach included reconstructing the
unknown source functions in four diﬀerent examples. In each example we high-
lighted some feature of the reconstruction process such as how to choose suitable
hyperparameters, inclusion of noise in the measurement data, the eﬀect of shrink-
ing the measurement surface and how to use prior knowledge to aid the reconstruc-
tion. The ﬁnal numerical experiment featured two simultaneously radiating point
sources. We demonstrated how the Bayesian method we have developed could also
provide an accurate reconstruction for this case, but also how the posterior can
exhibit local maxima which have to be dealt with. In total, the Bayesian method
presented here seems to provide a ﬂexible and promising framework for this type
of inverse problem.
