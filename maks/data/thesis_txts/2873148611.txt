Abstract
Significance: Conventional imaging tools such as Magnetic Resonance Imaging (MRI)
and Computed Tomography (CT) have been used for studying the brain for many years.
However, due to the lack of cellular resolution, these systems are limited to macroscopic
imaging of the brain. In order to achieve cellular resolutions, newer optical imaging techôÙ
niques have been developed. One such technique is lightôÙsheet fluorescence microscopy
(LSFM) and is explored in this thesis. In conjunction with propagation invariant beams,
the LSFM promises to achieve deeper penetration into the tissue, while having cellular
resolution, being fast and with minimal photodamage.
Aim: To design, construct and characterize a compact twoôÙphoton based Bessel LSFM
for functional imaging of the brain.
Approach: The system uses an axicon based Bessel beam generation approach, which is
then used to illuminate the sample. The whole system has been constructed on a 30 x 30
cm2 optical breadboard, thus achieving compactness.
Results: The microscope is able to generate a Bessel lightôÙsheet that has a comparable
DOF to the theoretical DOF. The system also shows twoôÙphoton excitation when used
with fluorescein. However, the same nonlinear relation could not be shown for fluorescent
beads.
Conclusion: The system offers promise and potential. However, system optimization is
the key to fully utilize the potential of the system.
Keywords: LightôÙsheet fluorescence microscopy, Bessel beams, system optimization,
brain imaging.

Introduction
1.1
Background and Motivation
The human brain is one of the most complex parts of the body with an estimated 86 billion
neurons [1] and governs all physiological and psychological processes. A dysfunction in
the brain, therefore leads to neurodegenerative diseases. For instance, according to the
World Health Organization (WHO), an estimated 57 million people suffer from dementia,
of which 60 to 70 % of the cases are due to Alzheimerãs alone [2] and 8.5 million people
suffer from Parkinsonãs disease [3]. Therefore, understanding the brain and its functions
has been a core focus for neuroscientists. Diagnostic tools such as Magnetic Resonance
Imaging (MRI) and Computed Tomography (CT) have been used by researchers [4] to
study brain. However, these methods suffer from poor resolution and studying the brain on
a cellular level becomes challenging [5]. This can be overcome by using optical methods
such as Optical Coherence Tomography (OCT) [6], MultiôÙPhoton Microscopy (MPM) [7]
and LightôÙSheet Fluorescence Microscopy (LSFM) [8].
LSFM has gained a lot of attention amongst researchers, especially in the last 20 years,
due to its ability of being fast, while providing excellent resolution and causing less phoôÙ
todamage [9, 10]. This has been possible by converting the illumination light into a thin
sheet of light. This sheet of light also provides excellent optical sectioning. However, the
traditional lightôÙsheets suffer due to diffraction. Hence with the inclusion of unconvenôÙ
tional beam profiles, which maintain their focus over longer axial lengths, such as Airy
and Bessel beams, LSFM has been able to provide access to extended depths inside the
brain tissue, making it far more attractive. [11, 12, 13, 14].
In this thesis, one of the propagation invariant beams, which is the Bessel beam has been
incorporated on to a LSFM. The system has been designed and built in a way that its
footprint is compact. The system is also transferrable from one lab to another making the
microscope advantageous for collaborative research.

2.1
Fluorescence and Fluorescence Microscopy
Luminescence, also termed as ãcold lightã is the spontaneously emitted light from a maôÙ
terial whose electrons have been excited through nonôÙthermal processes. These modes
of excitation could be through chemical reactions (chemiluminescence), electronôÙmatter
(electroluminescence) or photonôÙmatter interactions (photoluminescence) [15]. Here, let
us focus on photoluminescence as it is the light emitted by a material due to the absorption
of photons. Depending on the timescale of emission rates, photoluminescence is classified
into two categories: fluorescence, with rapid decay and phosphorescence, with delayed
decay [16].
Fluorescence was first recorded by NicolûÀs Mondarez as early as 1565 when a medicine
made out of the wood Lignum nephriticum fluoresced with a blue colour [17]. However,
the full understanding of the concept of fluorescence only happened centuries later with
the fundamental principles of fluorescence being very clear now.
A detailed pictorial representation of absorption and emission was provided by Aleksander
Jablonski, a Polish scientist. Figure 2.1 shows a Jablonski diagram which explains the
transitions that occur in a material subjected to singleôÙphoton excitation. The singlet elecôÙ
tronic states are represented by S0, S1 and S2 with each of these having multiple vibrational
states which are represented by 0, 1, 2 and so on. The vertical lines represent the transiôÙ
tions. When a molecule absorbs a photon of energy hö§a, it is excited from the ground
singlet state (S0) to one of vibrational states of the excited states (S1 or S2). From here,
a nonôÙradiative process called as internal conversion relaxes the molecule to the lowest
vibrational state of S1 and does not contribute to any photon emission. From S1, the
molecule could undergo either radiative relaxation or nonôÙradiative relaxation through inôÙ
ternal conversion to the ground state. If the relaxation process is radiative, then the emitted
fluorescence has a lifetime of the order of 10ã7 to 10ã10 seconds. If the molecule underôÙ
goes spin conversion while being in S1 state, then it will enter the triplet state denoted by
T1 through a process called intersystem crossing. Relaxation from T1 to S0 leads to phosôÙ
phorescence emission whose photons have lower energy compared to the photons emitted
via fluorescence [16, 18, 19].
The radiative transition between S1 and S0 results in the emitted photon being longer in
wavelength with respect to the absorbed photon, this has been termed as Stokes shift and
shown in Figure 2.2.
Using the physical principles of singleôÙphoton excitation, researchers have been able to
advance the field of microscopy through the invention of new microscopy techniques such
as WideôÙfield Fluorescence Microscopy (WFM) and Confocal Laser Scanning Microscopy
(CLSM). A brief account of these techniques, their advantages and disadvantages is given
below.
In a WFM, the light typically from a lamp is passed though an excitation filter and is then
reflected by a dichroic mirror. The filtered light is then allowed to illuminate the sample

of fluorophores outside the focal plane, often referred to as outôÙofôÙfocus excitation. This
results in collection of fluorescence from regions located within and beyond the focal plane
resulting in images being blurry with reduced contrast and poor resolution [23].

of a lamp as in WFM. This technique involves raster scanning a tightly focused spot size
of the laser over the sample and collecting the fluorescence from each points. Here, both
the excitation and emission pathways are focused on the same area of the sample at the
same time. In addition, a pinhole aperture is placed before the detector. This pinhole
acts by rejecting the light coming from the outôÙofôÙfocus planes, thus improving the optical

The evolution of LSFM began in the early 20th century [34] and has gone through many

Fluorescence Biomarkers
Having discussed the fundamentals of fluorescence microscopy from both quantum meôÙ

imaging of biological tissue is worth doing here.
The term biomarker as defined by the European Medicines Agency is an indicator which
can be measured that tells us regarding the underlying physiological or pathological proôÙ
cesses, or the response to a treatment, without including qualitative measurements such as
the individualãs feelings [56]. In terms of fluorescence microscopy, these biomarkers are
used as indicators of certain biological processes and are measured by labeling the tissue
with fluorescent molecules or fluorophores. Fluorophores are mainly classified into two
categories based on their origin: intrinsic and extrinsic [16].
Intrinsic fluorophores, often referred to as endogenous fluorophores are molecules that
are present naturally in the cells and tissues. These fluorophores give rise to a pheôÙ
nomenon known as autoôÙfluorescence. Molecules such as nicotinamide adenine dinuôÙ
cleotide phosphate (NADPH), flavin adenine dinucleotide (FAD), tryptophan and porôÙ
phyrins have shown to autoôÙfluoresce when excited using the right wavelengths.
On the contrary, extrinsic fluorophores, also called as exogenous fluorophores are exterôÙ
nally applied to the tissue to make them fluoresce. For instance, dyes such as fluorescein
and rhodamine have been used to label cells to study the movement of lymphocytes [58].
Green fluorescent proteins (GFP), which are usually extracted from the Jellyfish Aequorea
victoria have been used as fluorescent indicators. Further, encoding indicators genetically
based on GFP, such as GCaMP and TIE2ôÙGFP have been used in studying the calcium
[41] and vascular dynamics [59] in mice biology.

is a convolution between the two. Now, the systemãs intensity PSF is given by the product
of the PSFs of the illumination and detection systems and is expressed as
|hLS|2 = |hill|2 û |hdet|2

From this, we can say that if the lightôÙsheet is thick and is not focused tightly, then it
degrades the quality of the overall system PSF |hLS|2. In a similar way, if the detection
system is poorly aligned, or if the system is not optimized correctly, then it will still lead
to a degradation of the overall PSF.
Hence, the optimization of the detection system with respect to the lightôÙsheet is essential.
Such an optimization was tried at DTU.
Even though, the optimization did not result in a lightôÙsheet having a thickness comparable
to the theoretical value, it still stands as evidence that aligning the detection objective
perfectly will help in achieving better focus, thus reducing the thickness. Unfortunately,
such an optimization could not be carried out at KU due to lack of time.
Another part of this is, even if the detection objective is focused perfectly on the lightôÙ
sheet, the |hLS|2 could be still affected if the lightôÙsheet inherently is not focused tightly.
A slight misalignment or divergence in the system could hamper this focusing and thus
the overall PSF.
Interestingly, the slope of the logôÙlog plot for fluorescein excitation yielded a result of
approximately 2. This could be explained due to a relatively high twoôÙphoton cross section
of fluorescein, with ü2 lying between 30 and 60 GM [63]. The beads, however, have a
low twoôÙphoton cross section and thus their excitation becomes even harder.
As a final attempt to make sure that the beads were indeed fluorescing and had not been
bleached, imaging was attempted under a multiôÙphoton system. However, the imaging
could not be done, possibly due to a misalignment in the multiôÙphoton system and this
theory could not be proven.

As part of the continued work, more and more optimization of the system is required to

6
Conclusion
In this thesis, a compact twoôÙphoton Bessel lightôÙsheet fluorescence microscope was sucôÙ
cessfully designed and built. The initial goal of the thesis was to develop a system which
could be useful in the functional imaging of the brain, with excellent resolution and minôÙ
imal photodamage, while also having a small footprint.
The construction of the system began at DTU with the incorporation of an axicon, which
converts the incoming Gaussian laser beam into a Bessel beam. The excitation and deôÙ
tection pathways of the system were constructed with careful engineering and most of the
system was fit within a 30 x 30 cm 2 optical breadboard. The breadboard also served as
an essential tool during the moving of the system.
The microscopeãs performance was determined using several key measurements, such as
determining the power loss throughout the system, lightôÙsheet DOF and thickness, verifiôÙ
cation of twoôÙphoton excitation in fluorescein using power series measurements and imagôÙ
ing of fluorescent beads. Although twoôÙphoton excitation was observed in fluorescein, the
same nonlinear behavior was not observed using while using the beads. The reasons for
not observing such a behavior could be due to misalignment between the excitation and
detection systems or the inherent poor performance of the illumination pathway. This puts
an emphasis on system optimization and thus with precise alignment, the improvement in
the overall system PSF could be observed.
In summary, the compact twoôÙphoton Bessel lightôÙsheet fluorescence microscope holds
potential and promise, and with proper system optimization, the system is capable of
achieving its goals and being an indispensable tool in neurovascualar imaging.
