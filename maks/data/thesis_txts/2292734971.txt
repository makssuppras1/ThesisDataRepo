Abstract
In this thesis an effort is made to design a Radio over Fiber Cloud Radio Access
Network Fronthaul for 5G mobile networks. For this purpose, current 16 QAM
LTE OFDM signal is generated at 60 GHz of frequency through two methods.
First by using quadrature modulator and second heterodyne method. The
performance of the system is studied by measuring bit error rate and error
vector magnitude of the received signal in each system configuration. As can
be seen from the results in the first method due to extremely high frequency of
signal launched into the fiber, the received signal is highly distorted whereas in
the scond method which is heterodyne, as the up conversion process in taken
place through a nonlinear event at the receiver, signal received with bit error
rate of zero and error vector magnitude of 7.922 %. Such dissertation helps
mobile network operators to make better decisions on deployment scenarios
and strategies.

CHAPTER 1
Introduction
Generally, the ultimate goal of the Internet and communication networks is to
provide access to information when it is needed – needless to say, in whatever
format one or a device needs it in [1]. As a matter of fact, Wireless commu-
nication is now the fastest growing sector of the telecommunications industry
[2]. Global mobile data traffic will skyrocket in near future such that, for in-
stance, in 2017 data traffic of smart phones will exceed 9.4 exabytes (6 × 1018
bytes) per month, and when combined with the traffic generated by laptops,
tablets, and machine-to-machine communications, the overall demand should
reach more than 12 exabytes per month.
Taking this trend into account, the impact on mobile networks is clear.
As the demand for capacity in mobile broadband communications increases
dramatically every year, wireless carriers should be prepared to support up
to a 6-fold increase in total mobile traffic by 2020 [3] driven by hundreds of
thousands of data applications, video streaming, cloud computing on smart-
phones and tablets, etc. [4] [5]. Such exponential growth of mobile data traffic
cannot be supported by the “business-as-usual” evolution of the existing com-
munication systems [5] and requiring researchers to seek greater capacity and
to find new wireless spectrum beyond the 4th Generation of Wireless Systems
(4G) standard [6] since spectral efficiency for the Long Term Evolution(LTE)
standard is approaching the Shannon limit which is demonstrated in figure
1.2 [7].
Hence, devising a strategy pushing at the boundaries of state-of-the-art
technologies is strongly felt in order to rise networks and communication sys-
tems’ capacity to satisfy such this huge user and devices demands.
5th Generation of Wireless Systems (5G) could make the dream of real
wireless world or called “WWWW: World Wide Wireless Web” come true.
5G technology includes all types of advanced features which make 5G technol-
ogy most dominant technology in near future such that consumer has never
experienced the utmost valued technology making use of mobile phones within
very broad bandwidth with no more limitation to access and zone issues in one

CHAPTER 2
Overview of the 3GPP
Long Term Evolution
Physical Layer
In this chapter we introduce and investigate the technology of LTE standard,
challenges, and requirements for transmitting the desired bit rate, and tech-
nologies that have been used for cellular such as Orthogonal Frequency Divi-
sion Multiplexing (OFDM) [12].
LTE is a 3rd Generation Partnership Project (3GPP) standard that has
brought many technical benefits to cellular networks. LTE is designed to meet
carrier needs for high-speed data and media transport such that provides for
an uplink speed of up to 50 megabits per second (Mbps) and a downlink
speed of up to 100 Mbps [12]. Bandwidth is scalable from 1.25 MHz to 20
MHz [12]. This has suit the needs of different network operators that have
different bandwidth allocations, and also allow operators to provide different
services based on spectrum [12]. LTE has also improved spectral efficiency in
3th Generation of Wireless Systems (3G) networks to 2 (bits/s)/Hz, allowing
carriers to provide more data and voice services over a given bandwidth [12].
With growing users demand on information, it becomes impossible to trans-
fer information higher than a particular rate. The reason lies in the fact that
Intersymbol Interference (ISI) occurs if symbol period becomes very smaller
than channel delay spread [13]. In order to overcome to ISI, complex equaliza-
tion is required at receiver which is not worth to do it since equalization com-
plexity is proportion to square channel impulse response length [13]. Therefore,
engineers were considering increasing symbol duration, yet maintaining in the
high symbol rate which yielded to come up with a scheme called Multi-carrier
modulation(MCM) which is explained in the following section.

2 Overview of the 3GPP Long Term Evolution Physical Layer
2.1
Multicarrier Transmission
MCM is a form of data transmission in which data stream is divided into
several bit streams, each of which has a much lower bit rate; needless to say,
longer symbol duration, which these sub-streams are used to modulate several
carriers.
(a)
(b)
Figure 2.1: Intuitive modeling and comparison of single carrier transmission (a) [14] and
multi-carrier transmission (b) [15].
The first systems using MCM were military analog High Frequency (HF)
radio links in the late 1950s and early 1960s [16][17].
In MCM data is
first serial-to-parallel (S/P) converted then modulates M parallel sub-carriers
through which symbol duration increases on each sub-carrier by a factor of
approximately M such that it becomes significantly longer than the channel
delay spread [13].

2.1.2
Multipath Nature of Wireless Signal Propagation
It is obvious that wireless signal reaches the receiver from multiple paths
which causes ISI. In order to eliminate the remaining impact of ISI, the next
key operation in the generation of an OFDM signal is the creation of a guard
period at the beginning of each OFDM symbol by adding a Cyclic Prefix
(CP) generated by duplicating the last G samples of the IFFT output and
appending them at the beginning of the OFDM symbols. Therefore, if the
part of the signal is to be distorted it will be a repetitive part which will be
ignored by the receiver anyway. To avoid ISI completely, the CP length G
must be chosen to be longer than the longest channel impulse response to be
supported.

2.2
Orthogonal Frequency Division
Multiplexing Access (OFDMA)
When it comes to real world implementation of the MCM system using OFDM
described above, there is a problem has to be solved as the system mentioned-
above is assumed that a single user receives data on all the sub-carriers at any
given time, while in the real world implementation there are multiple users
demanding data simultaneously [13]. Therefore, OFDM scheme should be de-
veloped in order to meet the multiuser requirement. Hence, OFDMA scheme
for mobile communications was first proposed in [19] based on multicarrier
Frequency Division Multiplexing Access (FDMA), where each user is assigned
to a set of randomly selected sub-channels [13]. OFDMA realizes the OFDM
transmission to benefit from multi-user diversity. By employing OFDMA tech-
nique the total system spectral efficiency increases considerably compared to
single-user OFDM systems as adaptive user-to-subcarrier assignment can be
performed based on feedback information about the frequency-selective chan-
nel conditions from each user [13]. OFDMA can also be used in combination
with Orthogonal Time Division Multiplexing Access (TDMA), such that the
resources are partitioned in the time-frequency plane – i.e.
groups of sub-
carriers for a specific time duration. In LTE, such time-frequency blocks are
known as Resource Blocks (RBs), Figure 2.8 depicts such an OFDMA/TDMA
mixed strategy as used in LTE.
The ultimate goal of OFDMA is to satisfy the expectations of as many
users of the system as possible, considering the QoS constraints. This is done
by the eNodeB [13].
ENodeB, also known as Evolved Node B is the hardware element at air
interface of LTE which is connected to the mobile phone network that commu-
nicates directly with User Equipment (UEs). eNodeB allocates the RBs and
transmission powers for each subframe in order to optimize a function of a set
of performance metrics, for example maximum/minimum/average through-
put, maximum/minimum/average delay, total/per-user spectral efficiency or
outage probability [13].
The generic function of a resource scheduler, as shown for the downlink
case in Figure 2.10, is to schedule data to a set of UEs on a shared set of
physical resources.
One of the fundamental characteristic of wireless channels is the fading
effects arising from the mobility of the UEs in a multipath propagation en-
vironment, and from geographical variations in the surrounding environment.
==PAGE:38==

2 Overview of the 3GPP Long Term Evolution Physical Layer
Figure 2.8: Example of resource allocation in a combined OFDMA/TDMA system [13].
for a multi-user system, significantly more information can be transmitted
across a fading channel than a non-fading channel for the same average signal
power at the receiver. This principle is known as multi-user diversity. With
proper dynamic scheduling, allocating the channel at each given time instant
only to the user with the best channel condition in a particular part of the
spectrum can yield a considerable increase in the total throughput as the
number of active users becomes large [13]. In a nutshell, OFDMA is a scheme
which implements user-to-RB allocation based on the information gathering
from users which is called channel estimation in order to use the available
spectrum as efficient as possible.

duces the cavity size by 76.8%. Liao et al. presented a novel planar aperture
antenna with differential feeding, which maintains a high gain and wide band-
width compared with conventional high gain aperture antennas. The proposed
aperture antenna element has low cost, low profile, compact size, and is also
good in gain and bandwidth. Zwick et al. presented a new planar superstrate
antenna suitable for integration with mmWave transceiver integrated circuits,
which is printed on the bottom of a dielectric superstrate with a ground plane
below. Two designs for the 60 GHz band achieve over 10% bandwidth while
maintaining better than 80% efficiency [11].

4.1.2
mm-Wave Frequency and Bandwidth
What is sought for in RoF systems is to generate a mm-wave signal at the
desired frequency with the required bandwidth and carry the signal without
modification. In this context, mm-wave should be taken into account as the
sum of all spectral components of the signal which is to radiated or received
wirelessly by the BS within the desired band. Depending on the system ar-
chitecture, the mm-wave signal may include an mm-wave carrier and one or
multiple data channels. Two different bandwidths should be contemplated:
electrical and optical. Thus, in the electrical domain the bandwidth of the
mm-wave signal is defined as the difference between the maximum and mini-
mum frequency components, which should not be confused with the channel
bandwidth. In the optical domain, the bandwidth depends on the maximum
mmwave frequency in the electrical domain, fmax, and whether Optical Sin-
gle Side Band (OSSB), or Optical Single Double Band (ODSB) modulation
scheme, is used. Each subsystem of an RoF system has to be designed to
fulfill mm-wave frequency operation and bandwidth requirements. With high-
speed Uni-travelling-carrier photodiodes (UTC-PDs) which can provide 3 dB
bandwidths up to 300 GHz and relatively mature ODN components the down-
link transmission technique is typically the main factor limiting the maximum
frequency [10].

4.1.3
RF Output Power and Signal Power
In RoF systems where mm-wave carrier is transmitted, signal power which is
considered as the power of the modulation sideband transporting the informa-
tion, is a fraction of the total power and must be specified. The signal power
is computed as the power over a reference bandwidth that covers most of the
modulation signal. The ratio of the carrier power to the signal power can
be understood as the energy efficiency of the transmitted signal. A common
criterion to assess it at a particular frequency is the 1 dB compression point,
that is, the power at which the transfer characteristic drops by 1 dB with
respect to the ideal linear transfer function. It is worthwhile noting that the
power relation between the carrier and the signal is determined at the CS and
remains almost unaltered through the ODN and the BS [10].
4.1.4
Spectral-Purity and Frequency Accuracy of
mm-wave Carrier
Both spectral-purity and frequency accuracy are figures of merit of the mm-
wave carrier quality and are related to the phase stability such that the two are
responsible for short-term phase fluctuations while the latter is related to the
long-term stability, respectively. The most desired indicator of performance
for spectral purity or linewidth, is single side band phase noise measurement.
Phase noise is typically expressed in units of dBc/Hz, representing the noise
power relative to the carrier contained in a 1 Hz bandwidth centered at a
certain offset from the carrier. Beating of optical tones at the BS in the pho-
todetection process yields to generation of mm-wave in RoF systems. Hence
the phase noise of the generated signal depends on the correlation degree of
the beating tones. In order to achieve the frequency accuracy of the mm-wave
generated at the BS plus to maintain the optical signal within its correspond-
ing optical channel in the ODN, temperature of the optical source has to be
precisely controlled at the CS. In case of existence an independent oscillator at
the Mobile Terminal (MT), high spectral-purity and frequency accuracy are
required. Higher mm-wave frequency accuracy allows reduction of the guard
band between channels and, therefore, higher spectral efficiency is obtained
[10].

4.1.5
Transmission Integrity
The transmission integrity can be assessed employing Bit Error Rate (BER)
or Error Vector Magnitude (EVM) metrics. Some researchers measure the
transmission integrity of an RoF system at the MT, considering the wireless
transmission effects and the MT architecture while others, are concentrated
only on the effects of the RoF land network by measuring signal integrity
at the output of the Photodetector (PD) at the BS. Therefore, transmission
integrity analysis can be separated into two stages, which correspond to the
two different physical channels that the signal traverses to get MT from CS
and vice versa: optical and wireless [10].
4.1.5.1
Optical channel
Non-linearities, noise, and undesired phase modulation or chirp are respon-
sible for the link performance degradation in the optical domain. The main
sources of noise in the optical path of the RoF link are PD shot and thermal
noise, the Amplified Spontaneous Emission (ASE) of the optical amplifiers,
and the Relative Intensity Noise (RIN) of the light source. Main reason of fre-
quency chirp and fiber induced dispersion and increment of the output noise
power the beating of the different optical components at the PD. Non-linear
effects include clipping distortion and third order intermodulation distortion
caused mainly by the nonlinear modulation. As a general rule, non-linearities
limit the maximum power generated, transported, or received, while noise es-
tablishes the minimum power boundary for a given BER or EVM. Nonlinear
effects in the RoF transmission at the CS are caused by the non-linear behav-
ior of the laser if direct modulation is used or by the External Modulator (EM)
non-linearities, in case of external modulation. In addition, direct modulation
introduces higher RIN and stronger frequency modulation than external mod-
ulation. The nature of fiber imposes non-linearity on the signal while in the
active ODN optical amplifier gain compression is responsible for that. Fiber
chromatic dispersion is a serious problem even over relatively short spans for
conventional double sideband modulation. Its effect produces different phase
shifts of the signal sidebands, and when these sidebands are mixed with the
optical carrier in the PD at BS, the output signal is reduced depending on
the mm-wave frequency and the fiber link length. In the worst case scenario,
for fiber dispersion-induced signal cancellation effect, the detected signal am-
plitude can go down to zero level.

domains. In this way, non-linearities and fiber dispersion suppose an addition
power penalty to the SNR that only considers the noise [10].

CHAPTER 8
Conclusion
In this report we talked about LTE OFDM theory, technology, and existing
challenges and proposed a solution which might solve the issue regarding in-
creasing demand for traffic. The proposal was exploiting the mm-wave in order
to support future traffic demand which could also be considered for future 5G
mobile networks. Therefore, we talked about the characteristics of mm-wave
in terms of propagation which is very directional and propagation loss is very
sensitive to frequency and temperature; by which the higher is the tempera-
ture, more loss has to be suffered by the wave, impact of ambient elements
on mm-wave which is very sensitive to blockages , and diffraction capability
which is very low. Some existing challenges in system design and interference
management and spatial reuse such as designing of components integrated
circuits and antennas for mm-wave communications and interference manage-
ment and spatial reuse have been investigated. Hence, we assessed the overall
performance of RoF system through addressing its figures of merit including
cost, simplicity, bandwidth, output power, scalability, power consumption,
etc. such that these capabilities have made RoF systems a prominent option
for future 5G mobile networks as as one part of the whole transmission sys-
tem. Thus, C-RAN architecture which is a novel mobile network architecture
by which network deployment, operation cost, and energy consumption gets
reduced, plus, network’s mobility and coverage performance as well as energy
efficiency gets improved was investigated. Cost reduction stems from simpli-
fying the RUs by leaving the RRHUs on the site and move the rest to the
corresponding BBU which is also in favor of required time for installation and
maintenance and site renting price. Cost reduction originates from significant
decrements of power consumption as lower BBU capacity is needed to support
same number of sites. Radio performance gains and better performance in mo-
bility are enabled by CoMP implementation thanks to the very low latency
BBU pool. In the following chapter we will introduce and present the concept
of fronthaul and its characteristic when an RoF signal is transmitting through.
The C-RAN architecture makes it easy to realize high-speed inter-cell coordina-
