Abstract
Investigation and detection of action potential in cells network has always represented a rele-
vant topic in electrophysiology. Since its ﬁrst detection in 1971 by Bernstein, electrical based
techniques has represented a natural way for detecting, measuring and understanding in a
qualitative way these electrical signal characterizing biological systems like neural networks.
Nowadays, owing to recent development of micro/nano fabrication methods able to produce
small scale electrodes, state of the art technologies like micro electrodes arrays (M.E.A)[17]
has ensured high incorporation with biological equipment, enhanced sensitivity and spreading
analysis out to larger system of cells, without compromising their physiological conditions [18].
Thus, the possibility to study not only the action potential itself but to investigate communi-
cation networks between cells that are at the base of our fundamental functionalities, is now
a reality.
On the other side, yet, despite the mentioned advantages, the following factors limits Electrical
detection of action potential:
1. The required contact between cells and electrodes still deﬁne an invasive strategy for
interfacing investigation devices with biological system. Besides, even if it could be
limited by exploiting high bio-compatible materials, a not perfect control of the cells
growth yields to low integration and control levels between the electrical and biological
system.

1.1 | The Action Potential
Animal electricity was discovered for the ﬁrst time during experiments of Luigi Galvani in
the 1791, when he observed for the ﬁrst time frog muscle contractions by supplying current
through a metal wire connected to the muscle (Figure 1.1). Besides, the same contraction
occurred when the leg nerve and the muscle were simply connected through a metal conduc-
tor, or when the nerve was directly brought into contact with a skinned part of the muscle
leading therefore to the conclusion that He ”animal electricity” was present in the nerve and
the resulting contraction was induced by the ﬂow of electricity through the conductor. Hence,
with the birth of the Electrophysiology, which purpose is the study of electrical properties of
biological cells and tissues, the investigation along with a greater interpretation of the electri-
cal behavior occurring among cells, has lead to characterize this latter in two main diﬀerent
states. In fact, even if not excited, cells are always polarized, keeping a determinate nega-
tive potential (resting potential), whose value of some mV, deﬁnes an equilibrium status. In
contrast, alteration from this value due to the presence of excitation stimuli of the cell under
particular circumstances, generate transmission of deﬁnite electrical signals through the whole
cell.
These electrical signals, despite always involved in the overall response of the body to a
given stimulation, like occurred during the Galvani experiment, is mainly responsible for the
transmission of the action regarding the contraction rather than the motion itself. Thus, this
charge based inter-mediation between cells, described as we will see by a characteristic pulse,
is able to deliver the required information enabling our ability of performing movements or
thinking, from which its deﬁnition of action potential (AP). In more details, the action po-
tential in physiology is deﬁned as the variation in the electrical potential associated with the
passage of an impulse along the membrane constituting a cell of a muscle or nerve. [2].

1.1.1 | Bernstein’s Membrane Theory
So far, the action potential has been introduced in its concept, covering the main aspects
of its role as communication tool for cells as well as a fundamental reasoning of its spike
form. This subsection, aims to introduce and describe the general idea behind the formation
of action potential, providing a clear view on the charge environment and processes at the
base of its generation and propagation throughout the tissue. The Bernstein’s membrane
theory comprehensively explains this mechanism by arguing that the liquid inside a cell, such
as a nerve or muscle ﬁber, is a solution with a high concentration of potassium ions (K+)
called intra-cellular medium. In contrast, the equivalent outside the cell, which we will call
the extra-cellular medium , is essentially salty water containing mainly sodium (Na+) and
chloride (Cl−) ions, and relatively few K+ ions. Bernstein hypothesized that the membrane
is permeable to potassium from which the diﬀusion of highly concentrated K+ ions in the intra-
cellular medium, move these toward the extra-cellular medium, where they are less present.
Since ions carry a positive charge, this movement creates an excess within the extra-cellular
side of the membrane.
Thus, by taking int account that charges of the same sign repel
each other, the resulting electrical force across the membrane, opposes further movement
of ions by diﬀusion. A very small displacement of K+ ions is therefore suﬃcient to create
an electric ﬁeld, directed inwards, that completely opposes diﬀusion, establishing a condition
called the electrochemical equilibrium. From a mathematical perspective, the properties
of the membrane ﬁeld are such that it can be written as:
⃗E = −∇V
(1.2)
where V is a scalar potential. Thus, the electric potential is higher outside than inside the
cell. As a main result of its theory, Bernstein deﬁnes from these observation a quantity called
the membrane potential, deﬁned by the diﬀerence:
Vm = Ve −Vi

1.1.3 | The Characteristic Curve of Action Potential:
The action potential appears in a pulse and it is composed by 4 separated phases, each of
them associated with a particular event yielding to a variation in the transmembrane potential
mediated by the primary Ion Pumps interfacing the extra-cellular and inter-cellular solutions.
Going into the details, all these steps can be labelled as: Resting potential, depolarization,
re-polarization and Hyperpolarization (See bottom panel ﬁgure 1.6):
1. Resting State: In the resting state of a nerve cell membrane, both the sodium and
potassium gates are closed and across the membrane, equilibrium concentrations are
kept constant. Besides, while inside the cell the K+ concentration is higher, outside the
composition has a major contribution form the Na+. The generated voltage or electric
potential presents inside the cell when referred to the outside is typically about -70mV,
although this diﬀers signiﬁcantly from cell type to other cell type. Although the changes
in electric potential across the membrane during an action potential are suﬃcient for
achieving purpose, the actual changes in the concentrations of the Na+ and K+ ions
are very small. Furthermore, The Na+/K+ pump is a dominant contributor to the

resting state or equilibrium of the cell. In fact, it is responsible for maintaining the large
excess of Na+ outside the cell and the large excess of K+ ions inside, describing an
active transport process which makes use of the ATP as energy source energy.
2. Depolarizaion: When a stimulus is received some Na+ ion pumps pass to their open
state, and the diﬀusion inﬂux of Na+ ions starts to drive the inner potential to more
positive values. Despite the cells are sensitive to external stimuli, these do not always
cause the potential to rise to the threshold level. Thus, the cell will tend to settle back
down to an equilibrium state. In contrast, if the stimulus response is suﬃcient to drive
the potential from -70 mV up to -55 mV, the process is not stopped. At such threshold,
there is therefore no turning back and the cell will ﬁre into the full action potential.
Once the threshold is reached, more sodium gates become open and Na+ ions ﬂood
into the cell, raising the voltage rapidly. This transition is said to be ”voltage gated”
and occurs rapidly since the Na+ ions are driven by both the concentration and the
voltage gradients. The rapid rise period characterizing the interior cell potential from
its resting state of about -70 mv to a value the ﬁnal +30 mV occur on the order of
milliseconds, deﬁning the phase called depolarization. At this point, The open sodium
gates continue to transport Na+ ions into the cell, guiding the depolarization and raising
the potential of the cell inner membrane. However, in an undeﬁned moment, the K+
gates start opening, but this process is slower than that of the Na+ gates, so that the
depolarization has time to come to completion. The depolarization continues until the
interior cell potential goes positive, reaching 30mV. As a consequence to the interior
cell potential change through neutrality to positive values, the sodium gates close, in
such a way to conclude the depolarization phase.
3. Repolarization: The drop in the interior cell potential as a result of the open K+
gates is called re-polarization. This re-establishes the initial cell ”polarization” wherein
the inner side of the membrane is negatively polarized with respect to the outside.
4. Hyper-polarization: Hyper-polarization is the name given to the period of overshoot
of the interior cell potential to values more negative than the normal rest state. One
inﬂuence in this phase is the fact that the Na+ gates remain closed and the lack of
Na+ mobility across the membrane causes the K+ process to proceed toward the value
of -80mV, which would occur if only potassium were present. While hyper-polarization
might seem to be counterproductive, it is actually important in the transmission of
information. Hyper-polarization prevents the system from receiving another stimulus
during this time, or at least raises the threshold for any new stimulus. Part of the
importance of hyper-polarization is in preventing directionality of any stimulus already
sent, avoiding other cells from triggering another action potential in opposition. In
other words, hyper-polarization assures that the signal is proceeding in one direction.
Besides that, during the this stage, the K+ gates close and the Na+ gates remain
closed. Hence, equilibrium potential is obtained owing to action of the Na+/K+ pump
and the K leakage channel, allowing the cell membrane to approach its rest state.

1.2 | What Makes Action Potential Interesting
Among the diﬀerent actions potentials studied in biology, the neural action potential cover a
primary role as investigation system in electrophysiology. In fact, the neurons constituting the
nervous systems are speciﬁcally designed for communication and, even if we know a lot about
the individual mechanism involved in neural transmission, our knowledge about how individual
neurons respond as a consequence of certain stimuli as well as for groups of neurons work-
ing together to produce the complex behaviors observed everyday, in animals and humans, is
still limited. Neural circuits operate at the mesoscale, between molecular-level interactions
and behavioral or cognitive output. Hence, mapping the neural networks is the key for under-
standing how the brain is able to give shape to human consciousness, cognition, and emotions.
Besides that, the greatest advantages that neuronal circuits can provide to biological sys-
tem are ﬂexibility and adaptability, or in other words, the ability to respond to varied and
novel situations, and adjust to a wide range of diﬀerent and changing external stimuli [21].
For example, animals and human can rapidly perform image segmentation, decomposing a
complex picture of multiple overlapping objects into its constituents, even when the objects
are novel, in contrast with computer programs that can either require large amounts of time,
or miserly fail in trying to segment initial problem. Clearly, these are regimes where biology
excels, but machines fail, supporting in this way the many eﬀorts as well as the great in-
terest and motivation, for both science and engineering, to understand what unraveled rules
and characteristics allow biological systems to be changeable in an adaptive way and to use
them for designing devices equipped with these remarkable features in order to outperform
the current limits presents in technology, as it has been done with the introduction of artiﬁcial
Neural Networks or more general Machine Leaning algorithms for computer vision or Image
recognition methods that are also part of this project.
Hence, in order to understand a neural circuit, the determination of connectivity patterns
between its inputs and outputs is a requirement.
In addiction, nervous systems become
clearly unique in their communication properties only at the tissue and organ level, in which
billions of cells may work together as an intricately organized, interconnected circuit. In this
picture, the action potential traveling from synapse to synapse through axons deﬁnes the

the logical 0 and 1 in electronics and digital information processing. Thus, the ability to de-

present among the neurons, would let us the possibility to keep track of its dynamic and to
identify particular reaction given an input stimuli, a strategy known as (encoding nerve) or
the other way around (decoding nerve)[21].

1.3 | Machine Learning: A brief Introduction
The analysis and interpretation of complex schemes describing phenomena or interaction usu-
ally involves high number of variables requiring huge amount of data in order to be able to
extract the necessary amount of information for their proper understanding. Consequently,
the time-demanding processing of the gathered data needs to be optimized, feeling necessity
for automated methods, which is what Machine Learning provides.
Generally, we refers to Machine Learning as the set of methods implemented and adopted
for detection and prediction of pattern in data, allowing devices to perform decision making
under uncertainty. Based on this purpose, the designed methods and algorithms use statistical
techniques for enabling computers with the ability to identify and learn progressively and to
enhance their performance on a speciﬁc task, without being programmed a priori.
The way with which the data are interpreted and used in Machine Learning applications can be
subdivided in two main classes: i) learning from input data for running prediction of behaviors
(Supervised Learning), or ii) to exploit data-set for describing and recognize similarities
among these latter (Unsupervised Learning).

2.1.1.1 | Sarcoplasmoic Reticulum:
The sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR), constituting muscle cells (cardiomyocyte), is a membrane
structured components similar to the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) contained in other type of
cells. While the ER synthesizes molecules, the this Reticulum stores calcium ions (Ca2+)
and pumps them out into the myocyte cytoplasm (also called sarcoplasm) when the muscle
ﬁber is stimulated. Calcium ion levels are kept relatively constant, with the concentration
of calcium ions within a cell being 100,000 times smaller than the concentration of calcium
ions outside the cell (extra-cellular medium)[23]. Such proportion is required because small
increases in calcium ions within the cell are easily detected and can bring about important
cellular changes. In fact, greater amount of this kind of ions within the cells membrane can
lead to hardening (calciﬁcation) of certain intracellular structures, including the mitochondria,
leading to cell death. Therefore, it is crucial that calcium ion levels are controlled tightly, and
can be released into the cell when necessary and then removed from the cell.
Depending on its position inside the cell, the SR is described as peripheral (or superﬁcial)
when it is close to the plasma membrane and central (or deep) when it is located away from
this membrane [23]. The peripheral SR has been associated with Ca homeostasis, which gov-
erns the myocyte excitability by releasing Calcium ions, and also for interactions with plasma
membrane ion channels. In contrast, the central SR has been suggested to be more directly
involved in contraction by supplying Ca to the myoﬁlaments, representing the fundamental
units of sarcomeri, responsible for the cellular contraction. Thus, this intracellular membrane
system plays an important role in determining whether or not contraction occurs, by regulating
the concentration of intracellular calcium.

2.1.2 | The Excitation-Contraction coupling (ECC) in Car-
diomyocyte Cells
Excitation-contraction coupling (ECC) is the linking process of the action potential triggers
with a myocyte contraction and its subsequent relaxation.
The Key steps in cardiac excitation-contraction coupling can be summarized in 5 points:
1. Step 1: Action potentials traveling along the sarcolemma and down into the cellular
system, depolarizes the cell membrane.
2. Step 2: Voltage-sensitive dihydropyridine (DHP) receptors (VGCC L-type calcium
channels) open to permit calcium entry into the cell during phase 2 of the action
potential.
3. Step 3: Calcium inﬂux triggers a subsequent release of calcium that is stored in the
sarcoplasmic reticulum (SR) through the RyR calcium-release channels, increasing in-
tracellular calcium concentration from about 10−7 to 10−5 M.
4. Step 4: Free calcium binds to thin Myosin ﬁlaments inside the sarcomere. The result
of these changes is a movement (”ratcheting”) between the myosin heads and the actin,
such that the actin and myosin ﬁlaments slide past each other, shortening the sarcomere
length. Ratcheting cycles as known as Cross-bridge cycle occur as long as the calcium
concentration remains elevated.
5. Step 5: At the end of step 2, calcium entry into the cell slows down and calcium is
gathered by the SR by means of an ATP-dependent SERCA calcium pump, lowering
down the calcium concentration and removing calcium from the myoﬁlaments binding
sites. To a quantitatively smaller extent, calcium is transported out of the cell by the

2.2 | Cell Culture
2.2.1 | Introduction to the Cell Culture:
Production and preparation of HL-1 samples for investigation of action potential activity has
been achieved by growing cells under application of cell culture protocols. In gernera, Cell
culture is one of the major tools used in cellular and molecular biology, providing excellent
model systems for studying the normal physiology and biochemistry of cells indeﬁnitely. From
a practical prospective, it consists in removing cells from an animal or plant and their subse-
quent growth in a favorable artiﬁcial environment provided by opportunely designed laboratory
[9]. These conditions vary for each cell type, but generally consist of a suitable vessel with a
substrate or media that supplies the essential nutrients like amino-acids, carbohydrates, vita-
mins, minerals, growth factors, hormones as well as gases (CO2, O2), and regulates at the
same time the physio-chemical environment (pH buﬀer, osmotic pressure, temperature). The
cells used in cell culture can be either collected from the tissue and broken up in a enzymatic
or mechanical way before cultivation, or they may be derived from previous cultured cells
(cell line) whereby artiﬁcial conditions have already been established. Among all the major
advantages of using cell culture for applications in Cell-biology studies and Characterization,
the consistency and repeatably of results that can be obtained from using a batch of clonal
cells represents the most attractive one[9].
Normally, cultured cells divide only a limited number of times before losing their ability to
proliferate, which is a genetically determined event known as senescence. These cultures are
referred as ﬁnite due to their limited ability to sustain several duplication cycles. However,
some cell lines become immortal through a process called transformation, which can occur
spontaneously or can be chemically or virally induced. The culture can be classiﬁed depending
on the cells source in:
1. Primary Culture: refers to the stage of the culture after cells insulation from the tissue
and proliferated under the appropriate conditions until they occupy all of the available

mixed with the cell in order to uniformly distribute these letter throughout the adhesive
base of both T-25 ﬂask and culture dish.

2.3.1 | Introduction to Calcium Imaging
Concentration of Calcium ions (Ca2+) mastering both the contraction as well as the action
potential mechanisms through the E.C coupling, has been monitored and analyzed inside the
cardiomyocyte by using calcium imaging technique. This latter consists in collecting emitted
ﬂuorescence signal from the cells which is induced by binding compounds called Fluorophores
to Ca divalent particles inside the intra-cellular media. In fact, Fluorophores, also refered as
calcium indicators, has the ability to change their ﬂuorescence properties when bonded to
Calcium ions present in the cytoplasm. However, this signal is often weak for two reasons: i)
it contributes only to a small percentage of the whole cell’s emission spectra, ii) not all the
Calcium ions are eligible and therefore involved in the bonding process[32]. Selection of the
used has been done by taking into account that each indicator shows distinct characteristics,
and therefore has to chosen depending on its application or experimental system. Generally,
there are three main groups of Calcium Indicators:
• Organic Dyes: Synthetic organic dyes, such as ﬂuorescein, were the ﬁrst ﬂuorescent
compounds used in biological research. Derivatives of these original compounds have
been produced to improve their ability to keep unaltered the sample physiology (pho-
tostability) and solubility. The small size of these ﬂuors is a beneﬁt over biological
ﬂuorophores for bioconjugation strategies because they can be crosslinked to macro-
molecules, such as antibodies, without interfering with proper biological functions. A
wide variety of dyes with characteristic excitation/emission spectra commercially avail-
able for any ﬂuorescence application[32].
• Biological Fluorophores: While bioluminescence has been known for millennia, the
ﬁrst use of a biological ﬂuorophore for research applications occurred in the 1990s, when
green ﬂuorescent protein (GFP) was cloned from the jellyﬁsh Aequorea victoria and used
as a gene expression reporter [ﬂuorophore].The beneﬁt of these types of ﬂuorophores is
that expression plasmids (a small DNA molecule) which can be introduced into either
bacteria, cells, organs or whole organisms, to drive expression of that ﬂuorophore either
alone or fused to a protein of interest in the context of the biological processes studied.
The use of ﬂuorescent proteins can be time consuming, and expressing large amounts
of light-producing proteins can generate reactive oxygen species and induce artiﬁcial
responses or toxicity. Additionally, the size of the ﬂuorescent protein can change the
normal biological function of the cellular protein to which the ﬂuorophore is fused, and
biological ﬂuorophores do not typically provide the level of photostability and sensitivity
oﬀered by synthetic ﬂuorescent dyes like quantum dots.
• Quantum Dots: Quantum dots are nanocrystals with unique chemical properties that
provide tight control over the emission characteristics of the ﬂuor (sample under inves-
tigation). Quantum dots were developed in the 1980s and since the 1990s have been
increasingly used in ﬂuorescence applications in biological research. Quantum dots are
nanoscale-sized (2-50 nm) semiconductors that, when excited, emit ﬂuorescence at a
wavelength based on the size of the particle; smaller quantum dots emit higher energy
than large quantum dots, and therefore the emitted light shifts from blue to red as

a whole tissue, whose detection can be diﬃcult to be captured during high magnitude optical
transmission observations, the concentration value used for plating cell in petri-dishes at the
end of the cell splitting (see section (2.2)) must not be too high.

3.1 | Fluorescence Microscopy
3.1.1 | Principles of Fluorescence:
Physical processes involving excitation in electronic structure of materials, whose relaxation
results in generation of a photon, can be divided in two macro-categories: Incandescence,
where the energy altering the electronic structure equilibrium is provided thermally, or Lumi-
nescence, when the stimulation occurs by other types of input source. This last group includes
the emission of light as a result of a chemical reaction (Chemiluminescence), crystallization
(Crystalloluminescence),electric current passed through a substance (Electroluminescence),
mechanical action on a solid (Mechanoluminescence) and photo-luminescence which involves
absorption of photons. As far as the photo-luminescence is concerned, the particular interac-
tion between matter and the absorbed light, its energy levels conﬁguration deﬁning electronic
structure properties of the same as well as the vibrational states, lead to three diﬀerent optical
events: Fluorescence, Phosphorescence and Raman Scattering[34].
While Raman scattering, when considering light in its particle nature, results from inelas-
tic light scattering inside the target material [34], Fluorescence and Phosphorescence can be
distinguished by looking at the diﬀerent relaxation mechanism occurring from the electron oc-
cupying an higher energy level, excited by an absorbed photon, to its ground state. In a more
Empirical picture, with Fluorescence we deﬁne a photo-luminescence phenomena character-
ized by the emission of light by a substance, occurring after the absorption of electromagnetic
radiation, which has in the most cases a longer wavelength and therefore lower energy than the
absorbed radiation (Stokes Shift). Generally, Fluorescent materials relax nearly immediately

when the radiation source stops, in contrast to phosphorescent materials, which continue to
emit light for some time after. However, as already mentioned, an evaluation of the relaxation
processes within the electronic structures is necessary for understanding the main diﬀerences
behind these two processes. This can be addressed by introducing the Jablonski’s diagram,
which describes most of the relaxation mechanisms for electronic states of a molecule and the
transitions between them.
3.1.1.1 | The Jablonski’s diagram
In molecules, the energy of electrons constituting the atoms can assume only discrete values
called energy levels given by the quantum nature of the system and other contribution pro-
vided by the vibrational motion and electric interaction between the other electron and nuclei
surrounding them. The Jabloski diagram is a graphical representation of these energy levels,
whereby electrons in thermodynamic equilibrium lies in a ground state level (see ﬁgure (3.1))
and when excited by energies greater than the gap dividing two levels, the electron is then
promoted moving to an higher one. In the case of energy provided by an external incident
photon we refer to the excitation process as optical absorption, whereas the consequently
release of light in order to the return the electron in its ground state as radiative optical
transition, as known as Emission.
Furthermore, speciﬁc position of energy levels are given by two contributions: the electronic
state deﬁning the electron and its properties like spin, and the electron-molecule interaction
accounting the vibrational state of the latter. Hence, despite sharing the same electronic
state, diﬀerent energy levels can diﬀer in their associated vibrational state of the molecule
(see ﬁgure(3.1)).
As far as the electronic state is concerned, the spin orientation plays a crucial role in deter-
mining the occurrence of a Fluorescence or phosphorescence phenomena. For understanding
its contribution to the whole electronic structure conﬁguration, it is worth adopting the fol-
lowing example. For simplicity, let us consider a system where two electrons with anti-parallel
spin lay in the same ground state. The excitation of one of them to a higher energy level
without involving a spin ﬂip will not aﬀect the total spin (S) of the two electrons system,
therefore assuming S=0 there will be only a possible single conﬁguration of the total spin for
the diﬀerent available excited state energy, or in other word the electron can access to only
singlet states preserving the total spin number S=0. Instead, if the total spin of the system
changes due to a ﬂip of the promoted electron, a variation in the total value of spin (S=1)
will result in three diﬀerent conﬁgurations of the total spin for diﬀerent energy values called
triplet states. In fact, S can be either anti-parallel (coincident with singlet state) or parallel
for two diﬀerent orientations. While in general Singlet to Singlet relaxation as well as Triplet
to Singlet one are characterized by radiative emission, electrons keeping the same electronic
state but varying in the vibrational state will not result in emission of a photon, leading to a
non-radiative optical transition[34].
Hence, Fluorescence events can be described as excitation of an electron from the ground
energy level to an excited vibrational singlet state due to the absorption of a photon, where
the relaxation does not occur directly by optical radioative event, but rather through some
dissipation of energy by successive transition in close diﬀerent vibrational excited energy level,
represented by interaction between the molecule with the environment. As a consequence,

3.1.2 | Microscopy Setting:
Based on the Stokes shift principle, describing Fluorescence as a physical process where the
absorbed light of a material is emitted at a longer wavelength, microscopy set-up designed
for observing ﬂuorescence signal are conﬁgured in order to illuminate a sample, deﬁned by a
particular absorption spectra, with a excitation light and to selectively collect only a portion
of the emitted signal focused in the range of wavelength characterizing the emitted photon
involved in a ﬂuorescence transition process[35].
Normally, a classical microscope conﬁguration for collecting ﬂuorescence photons includes
the following components:
• Illumination Source: Illumination Source plays partially the important role of providing
photons within the spectral range of wavelength required for stimulating the occurrence
of ﬂuorescence photo-luminescence. Furthermore, as it will be discussed in the next
sections, diﬀerent types of light source are chosen by not considering only physical
aspects of the photon/sample interaction, but also possible provoked damage to this
latter, a scenario mainly present when dealing with biological samples.

• Excitation Filter: With Excitation ﬁlter we refer to an optical band-pass ﬁlter whose

the incoming electromagnetic radiation from its source by matching the ﬂuorescence ab-

3.1.3.1 | Photo-Blenching:
Photo-bleaching is a dynamic process where ﬂuorochrome undergoing to excitation light expo-
sition, lose their ability to ﬂuoresce. The degradation of ﬂuorophores is a signiﬁcant problem
in live cell imaging, where the involved high-intensity illumination can reduce the emission
ability of a ﬂuorophore or can render it completely unable to ﬂuoresce.
In microscopy applications, as explained in the second Chapter, ﬂuorochrome molecules are
chemically bound to targets of interest (such as DNA, RNA, protein, or other cellular com-
ponents) where the chemical micro-environment is very complex, normally diﬀering from one
specimen to another, and often very diﬃcult to control.Thus, re-radiation of ﬂuorescence and
thus reduce the signal intensity. More generally, owing to its much more longer time-scale,
Photo-bleaching is considered a distinct physical/chemical light-induced event if compared
with decay processes like Fluorescence and Phosphorescence, even if they partially share the
same physics.[38]
In fact, by recalling the fundamental theory of ﬂuorescence introduced at the beginning of this
chapter, photo-luminescence principally involves absorption of photons, subsequent excitation
of electrons from their energy ground state, and depending on the relaxation mechanism,
occurring either from an excited singlet state (S∗) or triplet (T∗) one to the ground singlet
state (S), we can distinguish between ﬂuorescence (ﬁrst case) and phosphorescence (second
case) respectively. Thus, based on the ratio of occurrence of the two light-induced phenom-
ena, and since both the processes can occur inside the same electronic structure, ﬂuorescence
material will be characterized by more S∗to S transitions (Ks) and phosphorescence with T∗
to S (Kt ratio). These decays does not necessarily involve ﬂuorophore decomposition, while
on the other hand, photo-bleaching is the process in which the total number of ground state
molecules is depleted via permanent photo-chemical destruction when ﬂuorescence molecules
are either in the singlet or the less probable triplet excited state. Photo-bleaching is therefore
the cumulative eﬀect of ﬂuorophore loss from each excitation-emission cycle over time, and
the rate of photo-bleaching is a function of the excitation intensity. Those bleached molecules
can no longer participate in the excitation-emission cycle.

a triplet dye molecule and an oxygen molecule (D-O mechanism).
The triplet state plays a fundamental role in both the reactions due to its longer electrons
de-excitation time to the ground state, allowing more time for ﬂuorophores to interact with
molecules or other indicators. Furthermore, the two events compete in the overall photo-
bleaching and, where the switch between D-0 and D-D mechanisms depends on the relative
concentrations of dye ([F]) and of oxygen ([O2])[39], since the constant rate of reaction at
a given temperature lead to a probability governed only by the concentrations of the react-
ing molecules. Hence, the dye fading behavior provoked by its chemical destruction can be
categorized in two regimes:
• D-D regime: In this circumstance we have [F]/[O2] ¡ 0.2. Majority of photo-bleaching
is governed by Dye to Dye interactions provided by more freely moving and diﬀus-
ing ﬂuorescein in solution (or close proximity of ﬂuorescein molecules, in the case of
immobilized ﬂuorescein).
• D-O regime: Dye to Oxygen reactions are principally responsible for the dye degrada-
tion, occurring for [F]/[O2] greater than 0.2.
To sum up, the general Photo-bleaching is the irreversible decomposition of the ﬂuorescent
molecules in the excited state because of their photo-oxidation with molecular oxygen and
interactions involving other excited ﬂuorophores before emission. Some of this energy will
inevitably be channeled into chemical reactions that result in ﬂuorophor breakdown. There
are a number of proactive ways that scientists can protect ﬂuorescent proteins and dyes
from fading and preserve ﬂuorescence signal, without sacriﬁcing cell health. Among these,
it is wroth mentioning that a thorough deoxygenation of the sample environment may not
be completely advantageous with respect to the prevention of photo-bleaching, because a
complete removal of oxygen increases the triplet state lifetime, leading to the accumulation
of electrons in the triplet state dye molecules, favoring the bimolecular reactions via the D-D
mechanism [39].

3.1.5 | K¨ohler Illumination System:
Optimal image clarity, contrast and resolution are required features characterizing a proper
illumination system, deﬁned as the set of components that includes the light source/s and
other optical elements arranged in order to focus the emitted light from to the sample plane of
the microscope. At the base of its design, the designed illumination system had to satisfy the
following conditions: i) Light from the source should be concentrated onto the sample plane
where the specimen is placed, ii) The system should create a uniform and grainless illumination
across the Field of View (FOV) (the region of the sample visible from the acquisition system).
However, both uniform distribution and focusing of the light on the sample were not easy
achievable.
Intuitively, focusing a lens or a combination of lenses, usually by involving a
collector lens whose geometry allows to collect and focus emitted light from diﬀerent incident
angles. However, even if this solution would in principle present a straight strategy, it has
been seen that, when the light was focused on the specimen from the used extended source, it
created an image of this latter on the sample plane. In this case, the image of the light source
was formed on the sample plane, since being extended source, they had a ﬁnite dimension
unlike ideal point source (inﬁnitesimally small).
So any attempt at focusing the light from such a source will result in an image of the physical
involved emitting device. This focusing and the formed image results therefore in a not uni-
form illumination throughout the FOV and a superposition of light source and sample images,
where the ﬁrst one appears in the background of the second one when viewed through the
eyepiece or a camera.
In contrast, point-like source of light cannot be created, and despite the possibility to reduce
the size of a light-source, the light emitted would be so little to be of any practical use. In
fact, point source could be emulated using a coherent source of light like a LASER, but since
wideﬁeld illumination from a coherent source will give rise to mutual interference of a set of
wavefronts (speckle patterns) in the Field of Illumination (FOI), the intensity distribution in
the FOI will be Gaussian and still not really uniform. Thus, in order to allow an extended
source to work as an illumination source, concentration of the light without the need to focus
the source was required, a solution that has been provided by adopting a technique called
K¨ohler Illumination system.

single sensor. Besides, pixel reaching saturation, if not well isolated from adjacent ones,
can lose part of charge that pass in other pixels, an issue usually referred as blooming

side, is represented by the brightest signal not producing , when acquired, saturation
of the camera pixels (complete white picture). Mathematically, the Dynamic range can

of capabilities translates into high speed, high-resolution biological images, even in low

3.2.3 | Detection Noise:
During the components selection process occurred through the design of the dual acquisition
microscope, all the previous introduced parts like the illumination system and the acquisition,
has been opportunely decided by also considering the minimization of the resulting noise on
the readout cells images. With noise we generally refer to an ensemble of diﬀerent processes
that lead to undesirable random variation of brightness or color information occurring during
the acquisition of an image, which obscures the desired information. More precisely, image
noise is to some extent an aspect of electronic noise since it can be produced by the sensor
and circuitry composing a digital camera like CCD and CMOS. The impact and the type of
dominant noise vary for diﬀerent sensor types. In general, one can classify camera noise into
three major classes according to their source:
1. Read Noise: It describes the amount of noise generated by electronic system of the
camera, as the charge present in the pixels is transferred to the this latter. It is a
combination of all the noise generated by system components which convert the charge
of each photodiode pixel into a signal for conversion into a digital unit (analog digital
unit (ADU) or grey-scale value). As a rule of thumb the read noise can be reduced
by reducing the pixel readout rate, deﬁning how fast charge can be read out from the
sensor (unit: MHz). Since this determines the frame rate of the camera, read noise has
to be taken into account for fast experiments like high-speed time-lapse of living cells.
The unit of read noise is e- and is independent of integration time. Read noise together
with the dark noise can be used to decide if a particular camera is suitable for low light
ﬂuorescence application or not. Furthermore, since read noise is added to each pixel as
it is read out, it is applied uniformly across the camera as the data is read out. A lower
read noise is always desirable, it improves the ability to detect very weak signals that
would otherwise have been hidden below the noise background. In addiction, limiting
the read noise also allows for a higher dynamic range, making the diﬀerence in signal
levels much more accurately detectable.
2. Dark Noise: also known as dark current - is a fundamental noise present in the sensor.
Dark noise is caused by thermal energy in the silicon randomly generating electrons in
pixels. Dark noise builds up in pixels with exposure time. It is expressed in electrons
per pixel per second. In applications, Dark noise represents a negligible term for fast
imaging with short exposure times. In contrast, it comes to long exposure times e.g.
one second or more for weak ﬂuorescent signals, this noise type can become a major
issue. Among the diﬀerent strategies that can be implemented for reducing dark noise,
cooling the sensor is a primary solution, which halves the dark current with every 8
degrees of cooling.
3. Photo-Shot Noise: Photon shot noise is results from the uncertainty in counting the
incoming poisson distributed stochastic photons reaching the camera sensor photodiode
pixel. Hence, it is not introduced by the sensor itself. In most analysis, photon shot
noise is by far the most signiﬁcant contributor to noise, whose value can be estimated
by considering its mathematical description as a poisson process whereby the noise is
obtained from the calculation of the count of signal photoelectrons square root.

3.3 | Microscope Environment Control:
Microscope stages do not provide natural environment for living cells which tend to die after a
short period of time under usual laboratory conditions. This limiting factor make questionable
the relevance and robustness of the produced analyses as reliable outcomes obtained from
an organism/organ/cell cultured in a non physiological environment. It is therefore neces-
sary to conﬁgure microscopy set-up for ensuring optimum sample environment in terms of
temperature, pH, nutrient supply at the specimen level. Conditions that can be recreated by
adopting speciﬁcally designed Incubating systems and temperature control technologies for
live-cell imaging applications.[40]
3.3.1 | Temperature Control:
Both biological and experimental considerations has to be taken into account when we want to
upgrade a microscope set-up for supplying heat aiming to establish the required temperature
for preserving cells alive during analysis. In fact, while from a biological assessments it is
possible to estimate the right range of temperature to set in a region close to the microscope
stage holding the sample, discussion accounting the heat transmission among the set-up
components in order to install and optimize heating source for providing such conditions at
the microscope stage are not of less importance.[37]
3.3.1.1 | Biological Point of View:
Temperature is a very important parameter as it can aﬀect the complete cells’ phenotype
deﬁning their behavioral characteristics as well as leading to reversibility in the cell-division
cycle when exposed to temperatures between 0 and 22◦C [41]. Furthermore, at the membrane
level, temperature variations do not only aﬀect transmembrane proteins such as receptors but
also the medium surrounding the cells, formed by both the cytoplasm and the extracellular
electrolytes. In fact, the biomolecules (lipids) constituting the phospholipid bilayer together
with the plasma and the intracellular membrane behave diﬀerently depending on the temper-
ature conditions. At high temperatures, the bilayer is in a non-lamellar hexagonal phase while
at very low temperatures it becomes a very ordered structure acting like a gel(see ﬁgure(3.6)).
Its diﬀusion coeﬃcient and the ﬂuidity are therefore very low where the rigidity goes up as the
temperature decreases. At the optimal cell temperature, for values usually higher than 20◦C,
the membranes behave more like a liquid (less order and structured), allowing the diﬀusion
and the ﬂow in and out of the cell [42]. At molecular level, if temperature is lower than the
needed one, the dynamic processes of the cells tend to occur at slower rate [41]. This can also
be related with the stability of certain proteins that might denaturalize (loss of functionality)
if they are not under the correct temperature conditions [42].

at the experiment site is mandatory since: i) ambient temperature at the sample location
might change even if the room has climate control, because the equipment themselves gets
warmer with the time and thus create a temperature gradient to the sample [43], ii) heat sink,

3.3.2 | Incubating Systems:
Incubating system are more complete tools allowing to provide not only ideal temperature
condition within the sample under investigation, but at the same time to set other necessary
parameters like pH and CO2. Furthermore, Incubating system adapted for installation in mi-
croscopy set-up are not only designed in order to maintain cells in a healthy state, but to allow
at the same time the living cells to be observed with the highest possible resolution. Depend-
ing on the applications, compatibility with the used microscope set-up, we can distinguish
three diﬀerent solutions: Air-Stream incubators, Stage-top incubators and full Microscope
enclosures.[13]
3.3.2.1 | Air stream incubators:
The simplest way to control temperature is by blowing warm air across the specimen. designed
on this purpose, Air stream incubators are able to provide pre-warmed air of a particular
mixture of gases, allowing high temperature control. Since based on a simple working principle,
home built solutions can be assembled without great loss in accuracy. In contrast, while
this design has the advantage of being compatible with virtually any microscope stage and
specimen, it is diﬃcult to precisely control temperature at the specimen due to a not complete
insulation of the microscope stage area. In addition, heat ﬂuctuations and vibration negatively
inﬂuence focus leading to focus drift. The Positioning of the incubator requires the output
tube providing the warm air to be at 15 to 30 cm from the specimen with an angle of
declination of about 30 degrees from horizontal, lowering down the power requirements and
giving also higher temperature stability.
3.3.2.2 | Stage-top incubators:
Heated stage incubators are available from many manufacturers and are growing in popularity.
These range from relatively simple heated inserts for existing microscope stages to more com-
plex incubation chambers that combine temperature and gas control and also allow perfusion
(passage of ﬂuid through micro-tubes system) of diﬀerent types of media. A general problem
with this design is that the central hole in the heated plate through which the specimen is
observed is not heated. For long working distance air objectives, transparent, warmed bot-
tom plates are available. However, this does not work with high NA oil objectives, which
are thermally coupled to the coverslip by the immersion oil. The objective acting as a heat
sink will especially cool the spot that is currently under observation, generating temperature
gradients of unknown magnitude. To alleviate this problem, stage-top incubators are often
used in combination with previous mentioned objective heaters. A troubling aspect of this
design is temperature ﬂuctuations and gradients generated in the objective. Form a practical
point of view, it is worth noting that heating the front lens to 37◦C will require a much higher
temperature of the heating collar and since glass and metal have very diﬀerent thermal ex-
pansion coeﬃcients, temperature gradients and repeated heating and cooling will negatively
aﬀect optical performance and lifetime of compound objective lenses.

Data Handling
Acquisition of high quality data are always required for guaranteeing their implementation in
Deep Learning algorithms, whereby beating motion and Calcium ﬂuorescence signal will be
used as labelling and training data. However, despite the high accuracy in both application
of cell culture protocol as well as precision in the selection of microscope components for the
set-up assembling, a not perfect control of the cells conditions favors undesired deviation in
their behaviors, resulting in weak contractions, that could be diﬃcult to detect by the machine
learning network. Thus, application of post-processing algorithms can represent an useful tool
for partially overcome the problem, leading therefore to an easier analysis and usage of the
gathered data. In this chapter, two computer vision methods for motion magniﬁcation (The
Eulerian magniﬁcation Method) and image stabilization (Lucas-Kande algorithm for Image
Stabilization) will be described and explained in their principle. Afterwards, the application
to HL-1 data collected from the transmission camera will be processed for both beating
motion magniﬁcation and stabilization of background vibration introduced by the usage of
high objective lens magniﬁcation and absence of vibration-less laboratory environment.
4.1 | Eulerian Magniﬁcation Method
As it has been analyzed in the previous Chapters, one of the most critical aspect when
dealing with biological samples, is to properly guarantee ideal environmental as well as bio-
logical condition allowing the cells to grow in a natural way without encountering deviation
in their morphology and physiological behavior when positioned under the microscope set-up.
Although this issue can not be overcome completely bu limited by applying proper culture
protocol or even by designing and upgrade set-ups with incubating systems, there still may
happen that some sample or a part of it, do not show own speciﬁc characteristics. By consid-
ering for instance the case of investigated HL-1, bad development of cells component owing to
inaccurate preparation can lead to weak beating motion diﬃcult to be detected by the camera
system. Furthermore, even if in presence of healthy specimens, beating dynamics can vary in
intensity throughout the sample due to not evenly distributed cells on the petri-dish coverslip,
entailing a diﬀerent biological collective communication inside the same sample from which
the beating motion may be aﬀected.

Hence, in order to optimize the individuation of HL-1 contractions with the scope to ob-
tain more reliable and useful measurements that has to be provided as labelling data for
performing and applying supervise learning on the system, a post processing strategy called
Eulerian Magniﬁcation Method is applied on the recorded data in order to enhance or even
revealed hidden motion among the observed cells.
Inspired by the Eulerian picture of ﬂuodynamics, where properties of a voxel of uid, such as
pressure and velocity, evolve over time, the Eulerian approach to motion magnication do not
explicitly estimate motion, but rather exaggerate motion by amplifying temporal color changes
at xed positions. This results, in comparison with previous attempts based on unveiling im-
perceptible motions in videos by following a follow a Lagrangian perspective (trajectory of an
object is tracked over time), with a less computationally expensive approach where artifact-
free output are also easier to get. The basic implementation of this algorithm for magnify
motion is to take a standard video sequence as input, and applies spatial decomposition, since
these latter might be magnied diﬀerently due to diﬀerent signal-to-noise ratios, and then fol-
lowed by temporal ltering to the frames.
In practice, this means to consider the time series of color values at any spatial location (de-
ﬁned by the camera pixels) and amplify their variation in a given temporal frequency band of
interest, where the desired motion is supposed to be present. Thus, the protocol starts by rst
decomposes the input video sequence into diﬀerent spatial frequency bands (spatial decom-
position). Afterwards the same temporal ﬁltering is performed to all the diﬀerent bands, a
step required to isolate only the interested motion. Finally, the ltered spatial bands are then
amplied by a given factor α, added back to the original signal, and collapsed to generate the
output video.
Both the choice of temporal lter and amplication factors can be tuned to support diﬀerent
applications including also magniﬁcation of low-amplitude motion rather than only modulation
of color variation.

4.3.2 | Post-processing Stabilization:
The discussed Lucas-Kanade Method for stabilization has been applied under the software
environment imageJ [46] in order to reduce background vibration detected by the high mag-
nitude objective lenses and provided by a non perfect vibrations insulation of the used optical
table wherein the entire set-up has been assembled on. The stabilization is performed by
loading a video as an image stack. One of the picture composing this latter is then taken as
an initial reference, also known as template.Hence, estimation of the best geometrical trans-
formation required for aligning all the stack’s slices to the chosen template. In this selection
step the Lucas-Kanade algorithm is applied. The whole protocol compares each image of the
stack separately and it corrects each slice of the recording one by one subsequently, changing
in each iteration the template image. In conclusion, when the alignment with the template
has occurred, the stack is updated according to the following formula:
T ∗= a ∗T + (1 −a) · A,
(4.21)
where T ∗is the the new template, T the old one, A the processed image undergoing to
the geometrical transformation and ”a” deﬁnes the template update coeﬃcient, a quantity
that can be set externally. It uses the currently shown slice in an image stack as the initial
reference, or ”template”. Hence, the template is moved to the new stabilized image which
is then used as new template. As it is possible to observe in ﬁgure(4.4), the main feature
making the post-processing stabilization method interesting lies in its ability to ﬁlter only the
vibration without aﬀecting motion within the picture. This has been pointed out by recording
HL-1 beating motion in a noisy background. By applying the algorithm, the resulting output
does not show attenuation of the dominant peaks describing the contraction, halving only
the background vibration noise.
In conclusion, the algorithm can be used together with
the Eulerian magniﬁcation method for enhancing the quality old data-set used for running
classiﬁcation by means of deep learning codes.

Results Discussion:
The designed dual acquisition set-up assembled for performing live cell imaging on HL-1 cell
line for detection of action potential revealed general good performances during the performed
tests and measurements used for characterize the HL-1. In particular, starting from consid-
erations on the illumination system, LED proved to be a straight and fast solution, providing
at the same time narrow band-width and high stability throughout the time. Nevertheless,
despite the low contrast, good quality staining as well as adoption of Kohler method has
given nice nice distribution of the light and uniform emission of ﬂuorescence photons from
the sample. However, compared with similar works based on application of ﬂuorescence mi-
croscopy methods, the quality of the resulted value of F/F0 are remarkably higher and lower in
terms of noise [29]. This suggests therefore the needed to upgrade the microscope for further
enhancement of the ﬂuorescence imaging quality, by mainly working on the illumination and
acquisition hardware.
As far as the acquisition system is concerned, selection of the used CMOS camera has been
done only by assessing parameters such as Quantum eﬃciency or full well capacity and more-
over since it has a general better temporal resolution (due to higher acquisition frame rates),
larger ﬁelds of view due to larger chip sizes, better spatial resolution due to smaller pixel sizes
(that can more properly digitally sample the images produced by high numerical aperture
(NA) objective lenses [Nyquist sampling]) and better intensity quantiﬁcation due to a higher
dynamic range. However, as reported in, EMCCD represents an ideal solution for ﬂuorescence
microscopy due to their remarkable ability to detect low signal intensities with low levels of
illumination light power [47] which despite loss in the ﬁnal image resolution, would permit
less illumination with a resulting reduction of photo bleaching.
Nevertheless, the choice of using Cal-520 has organic dye for calcium imaging has revealed
to be the best solution since in regards to other characterization work performed on both
HL-1 or other type of cells, these letter have been involved mainly for studies concerning the
cell morphology rather than for calcium activity tracking, making therefore strong stability to
quenching a minor advantage [48]. Furthermore, as it has also been reported in [49], Cal-520
outclass in performance other green emitting indicators mainly for the obtained higher con-
trast and stability [49]. Whilst even red indicators would have been in principle considered,
wider availability of LED in the range of the blue/green light has favored the choice of the
discussed green one.
On the other hand, the readout obtained from the optical transmission side has ensured
high resolution of the image whereby the only drawback has been pointed out by focus drift
phenomena, emerged from the absence of proper microscopy environment control system.
Besides, compatibility between the immersion oil of the objective lens with the polymer based
cover-slip has been taken into account, avoiding unwanted detachment of material, which

Conclusions:
The project aimed to study, design and assembly a microscope set-up for dual acquisition
imaging of action potential in cardiac cell line HL-1, for Machine Learning applications. This
particular type of cardiomyocyte has been chosen due to the particular stability and preser-
vation of phenotype when they undergo to repetitive culture protocols as well as for their
spontaneous contractions in absence of external stimuli. Hence, recorded electrophysiological
data concerning they activity detected in both optical transmission and ﬂuorescence signal
form, are gathered with the purpose to use them as training data-set for developing an au-
tomated method of action potential detection based on simply recording cells motion under
classical optical microscopy methods, which it would be i n principle able to overcome inva-
sivity problems of state of the art methods of investigation currently used to map activity and
communication throughout cellular networks.
Whilst the project began by considering also a possible introduction of electrical sensing
based on micro electrodes array M.E.A, the complex design and time demanding procedure
for setting up the required electronics aﬀected the decision to adopt, as ﬁnal solution, only
optical-based analyses techniques. In addiction, post processing data like the Eulerian magni-
ﬁcation method or the Lucas-Kanade algorithm for stabilization have been used for favoring
enhancement in the quality of the data. On the other side, developed competences by working
independently in cell culture laboratories helped the general understanding of the fundamental
biology characterizing the samples, which has revealed to be a key feature for optimizing the
overall quality of the experiment and data reliability as well as for increasing the ﬂuorescence
signal. Both the microscope design, application of culturing protocols and experiment struc-
tured for characterizing HL-1 and testing the mounted set-up, has required understanding and
analysis of both microscopy and cell biology concepts, which has been reported in this work
as an introductory theoretical background giving the necessary information to the reader for
either interpreting obtained results, getting insights about proper selection and evaluation of
optical hardware components as well as ability to independently assess quality of the sam-
ple and knowledge of its visible biological dynamics when undergoes to microscopy observation.
Among the ﬁnal results, we can assert to have successfully assembled the microscope by
properly selecting every components of the system in order to make it operative for running
either supervised or unsupervised learning codes on collected training data. In particular, clear
enhancement of ﬂuorescence has been obtained by modiﬁcation of the used staining protocol,
passing from F/F0 values of 2.73 dB to 26.17 dB.

Outlook:
From the obtained results and limitations arisen during the testing of the assembled set-up in
the current condition, upgrades principally involving optical components has to be addressed
in order to ensure: i) longer time-windows for recording cellular activity, ii) higher SNR ratio
capable to resolve more details of the HL-1 structure, making easier the association between
the contraction in the optical transmission with the corresponding ﬂuorescence signal. Among
these, adoption of coherent light may lead to work in a range of wavelengths that can be pre-
cisely tuned for matching with high accuracy the speciﬁc excitation of the ﬂuorophore dye, at
the cost, yet, of a greater quenching and photo-damage due to the higher intensity of the laser.
furthermore, time-lapse microscopy may be improved by adopting two solutions: a) by per-
forming interval ﬂuorescence recordings while keeping track of the cell status from their obser-
vation under their dynamics recorded in optical transmission, b) introduction of microscopy
incubating systems. As far as the incubating system is concerned, the best solution would
be provided by complete coverage given by cage incubators, since able to get full control of
temperature humidity throughout the entire set-up. However, even if compatibility issue with
the current conﬁguration could be overcome by following a solution like that one reported in[],
stage-top incubators gives faster achievement of working condition and can be easily installed
onto every stage.
In terms of optical resolution, current readout acquired by the 60x times objective lens are
deﬁned by poor edge deﬁnition, making the HL-1 diﬃcult to be distinguished when disposed
within cluster of other cells. Hence a straight solution would be to upgrade the optics in order
to run diﬀerential interference contrast (DIC) imaging. This indeed would represent a straight
solution since it would require only the insertion of two Wollastone prisms which has to be
mounted next to the already positioned polarizers presents in the set-up [50].
Last but not least, upgrade of current used manual stages to motorized solution will be a
primary requirement for testing automatized investigation provided by the application of Ma-
chine Learning codes, allowing the system to scan independently the cultured area of the
sample hosting the cells. Among the diﬀerent possibilities like piezo-stages or integrated mo-
torized stages, a strategy concerning installation of x-y actuators on the knobs of the used
stage would guarantee a precise and straight solution.
In conclusion, the necessity to perform all these upgrades is well reasoned by the presence of
the same correlation principle between ﬂuorescence and optical microscopy (calcium pulses
with contraction) that has encouraged the usage of HL-1 as the investigating system for ac-
tion potential by means of Machine Learning algorithms. In fact, it has been demonstrated
that, at the moment of its excitation by the stimulation of action potential variation of the
