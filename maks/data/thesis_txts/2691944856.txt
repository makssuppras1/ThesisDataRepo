The non Gaussianity of wind turbine loads
Abstract
Recent studies have shown that wind fluctuations tend not to follow Gaussian distributions, al­
though, when designing wind turbines, it has always been assumed so to simplify the complex
calculations and to reduce the time spent solving the computational models. It is a common
belief that this assumption can result in an overestimation of the fatigue loads that the structure
suffers and that consequently, in some scenarios, this could end up in a reduction of the tur­
bine lifetime. Even though studies have been focusing on whether the wind follows or not the
Gaussian distributions, the real consequences that this hypothesis would imply have not been
explored yet.
The aim of this project is to demonstrate that wind fluctuations are indeed non­Gaussian and
how this non Gaussianity varies with the different wind parameters. To fulfill this purpose, the
analysis is carried out with a 10­minute sample since it can be assumed that during this time
period the wind maintains a constant average speed. The turbulence increment is the parameter
used to represent the probability density function and to study the type of distribution.
Afterwards, the fatigue loads on the blades and the tower structure are analyzed, and several
comparisons are made. In particular, loads are studied according to different wind parameters.
Once their evolution is known, the samples are then classified between the ones following a
Gaussian distribution and the ones that are not, and a comparison is made in between the
damage equivalent load that each sample presents. The result of this analysis is that both
groups are characterized by the same regression curve, although the Gaussian samples tend
to converge more into this curve, while the non­Gaussian samples have more spread values.
Thus, it is concluded that no evidence of an overestimation of the fatigue loads has been found.
Keywords
Wind, Gaussian distribution, Non Gaussian distribution, wind profile, speed, direction, turbu­
lence intensity, wind turbine loads, atmospheric stability, statistical moments.

Resumen
Estudios recientes han demostrado que las fluctuaciones del viento no suelen seguir distribu­
ciones Gaussianas, aunque en el diseño de los aerogeneradores siempre se ha supuesto así
para simplificar los complejos cálculos y reducir el tiempo de resolución de los modelos com­
putacionales. Es una creencia común que esta suposición puede dar lugar a una sobreesti­
mación de las cargas de fatiga que sufre la estructura y que, en consecuencia, en algunos
escenarios, podría terminar en una reducción de la vida útil de la turbina. Aunque los estudios
se han centrado en si el viento sigue o no las distribuciones gaussianas, todavía no se han
explorado las consecuencias reales que implicaría esta hipótesis.
El objetivo de este proyecto es demostrar que las fluctuaciones del viento son efectivamente
no Gaussianas y cómo esta no Gaussianidad varía con los diferentes parámetros del viento.
Para cumplir este propósito, el análisis se realiza con una muestra de 10 minutos, ya que se
puede suponer que durante este periodo de tiempo el viento mantiene una velocidad media
constante. El incremento de la turbulencia es el parámetro utilizado para representar la función
de densidad de probabilidad y estudiar el tipo de distribución.
Posteriormente, se analizan las cargas de fatiga sobre las palas y la estructura de la torre, y se
realizan varias comparaciones. En particular, se estudian las cargas en función de diferentes
parámetros de viento. Una vez conocida su evolución, se clasifican las muestras entre las que
siguen una distribución Gaussiana y las que no, y se realiza una comparación entre la carga
equivalente al daño que presenta cada muestra. El resultado de este análisis es que ambos
grupos se caracterizan por la misma curva de regresión, aunque las muestras gaussianas tien­
den a converger más en esta curva, mientras que las no Gaussianas presentan valores más
dispersos. Por lo tanto, se concluye que no se ha encontrado evidencia de una sobreestimación
de las cargas de fatiga.

INTRODUCTION
Nowadays, renewable energies are a key aspect of the transition to a zero­emission world.
Among these energies the wind, solar and the hydraulic are the industries more developed and
that most of the countries are either introducing on their electrification system. [34]
Within the wind energy sector, one of the parameters that are essential to increase the percent­
age of utilization is the meteorological forecasting. Increasing the accuracy of the prediction
systems will allow an improvement on the amount of wind energy generation. It is well known
that wind is defined as an stochastic and unpredictable flow, that depends on many factors,
including, speed, direction, turbulence (short term fluctuations), ground obstacles, wind shear
profiles, and long term fluctuations. It is crucial to defined in detail all these parameters before
choosing a location and designing the wind turbine, as even a small error can end up on larger
effects and a poorly optimized wind farm, causing financial losses and/or structure damages.
[4]
An aspect that needs to be taken into account is that turbines are outside structures, hence, they
are exposed to all weather conditions and, in addition, as they require high winds, they will be
subjected to great stresses, questioning the integrity of the structure, yet it is of vital importance
to make an accurate forecast of the wind evolution in a specific location. This study of the wind
forecast can also be extrapolated to more outside structures as bridges, buildings, airplanes...
The main fact about wind is that it is continuously changing, so rapidly that it is impossible for
a wind turbine to follow and modify its parameters so precisely. Additionally, and due to its
complexity, when it comes to analysing the wind conditions, some simplifications are done in
order to simplify the complex maths and make the design and the construction of the device
more available. One of the most important simplifications is assuming that the increments that
wind fluctuations have follows a Gaussian distribution.
Moreover, recent studies have analyzed the fluctuations of wind, and most of them achieved the
conclusions that these time increments did not converge with Gaussian distributions. On the
other hand, there are no research about the implications that it would have on the structure. The
fact that the wind does not follow a Gaussian probability distribution will imply that a scenario
that could occur once every 500 years, actually happens once a day or once a week. It should
be noted that from the beginning, although an error in the calculations is being assumed, given
that a safety coefficient is always taken into account, the structures are capable of withstanding
the estimated time. But, in any case, this error can mean a significant increase in the loads,
fatigue life and therefore, a decrease of its lifetime expectancy. [4]
Furthermore, the wind peed spectrum can be measured with different lengths. Three ranges
can be divided: the macro meteorological range that goes from one day to one year, the spectral
gap which covers from 10 min to 1 hour and finally the micro scale where data is analyzed from
0.1 seconds to 10 min. These patterns are quite important when it comes to forecasting on
the short and long term, for example, the long term is necessary to know whether to install the
turbine or not in that site, while the short one is more focused on the forces that each component
will be facing. This project will be focus on the micro scale where 10 min data will be examined.
[4]
Many models have tried to simulate the wind distribution in order to study its behavior, here,
the Mann model, the Kaimal model, von Karman model and Kolmogorov 1941 models can be

As it was explained on the introduction, the wind is characterized for being a stochastic and
unpredictable flow which is caused by difference of the atmospheric pressures, difference on
temperature and due to the Coriolis effect caused by the rotation of the planet. For all this, the
wind profile changes horizontally, vertically, with the distance and with short interval of time,
making it more difficult to forecast.
The wind speed and direction are the main parameters that define the flow. on a macro scale, its
distribution can be described with a Weibull function, composed with a shape factor k dependant
of the topography and a scaling factor A. [12]

On a macro­meteorological scale, the wind speed is affected by three parameters, the diurnal

From a micro­meteorological scale, the turbulence is the most important variable. This effect

On this scale, the wind shear can also be found. It is a phenomenon where the wind profile
increases with height, and taking into account that wind turbines can achieve high altitudes and
that the turbulence also increases with height, it is a parameter that will be studied deeply.
1.1.2
Background
The spectral models that are currently being used for simulating wind conditions with the aim
of studying new materials of the turbine or new devices, generate a Gaussian wind, thus they
do not represent extreme wind increments as met in gusts. [31]. Inside the wind simulations,
several studies can be found such as the Kaimal model, the von Karman model and the Mann
model, all of which take as base for their calculations Gaussian distributions.
Between all these models, the Mann model is the one that has a better representation of the
turbulence of the wind since it calculates the u­w correlation. Moreover, it is able to represent
the turbulent eddies since it considers a space of wave vectors composed of three components
k = (k1, k2, k3) being the spatial directions the longitudinal, transversal and vertical, respectively.

Each component is inversely proportional to the length scale of the eddy, therefore indirectly
inversely proportional to both the mean wind speed and the simulation time, since the length is
defined as L = U · T [31].
On the article [31] a new model is presented by Friedrich and Kleinhans. On this paper, it is
proven how the modelling methods that exist generate Gaussian wind distributions, while if real
data is measured, this one shows a non Gaussian tendency. In addition, the fatigue loads were
compared between the old models that follow a Gaussian distribution and the new model for
wind modelling that adjust better to these increments. Thereby, it is demonstrated how the tails,
which are direct consequence of the increments, influence on the fatigue loads and therefore,
should be taken into account.
This new model from Friedrich and Kleinhans [31] proves that from the equation of Navier­
Stokes, the wind simulation for non Gaussian distributions can be directly derived.
The main point of turbulent eddies is that they can have several sizes and while mixing, several
stresses may appear creating fatigue loads on the structure, way bigger if compared to laminar
flows. The Navier Stokes equations describe the flow behavior using the continuity equation.
Furthermore, this equation becomes more complex to solve when there are changes in the flow,
like the density, the energy and the temperature. Here, more equations need to be calculated
in order to obtain the velocity vector and the pressure of the flow.
Since the Navier Stokes equation become more complex, it is impossible to solve them in an
analytical way, therefore computational methods need to be applied. In order to simplify the
maths, the numerical methods divide the field in parts, and solve them independently.
As for the turbulent flow, it is characterized with higher Reynolds numbers, thus the wind speed
and pressure variation from one point to the next one is also higher and have a great dependency
on the time. Here, maths become more complex, since the equations need to be solved for
smaller spatial parts in order to analyze correctly the flow, which directly imply more complex
computational methods and more time to solve them.
Here, the Large Eddy Simulation (LES) is useful since it is a computational model for simulating
the turbulent flows. It is able to model both big scales and small ones in a more simpler way.
Furthermore, it follows the reasoning that the frontier has more influence on the eddies and
gusts than the information located inside the mesh. Therefore, as it does not solve the equations
directly, it simplifies the maths. [32]
Other mathematical method that is used for small range turbines is the Reynolds Averaged
Navier Stokes (RANS), where the computational part takes less time since it is less complex.
This models assumes that the variables can be divided in two components, the mean value
and the fluctuate part. Here the Reynolds average is applied, simplifying all the Navier Stokes
equations to their mean values. This method is less accurate compared to LES, but still useful
for a first approach. [32]
To sum up, it can be concluded that models tend to apply Gaussian distributions to wind in
order to simplify the numerical models, where, although the maths are done with mathematical
models, without some assumptions, the model will take a long time to process the restrictions
and calculate the parameters.

turbine. This aspect is of vital significance since when designing a new wind turbine, the loads

The main problem is that wind is stochastic and cannot be predicted. Normally, and with the

• The study of current wind models and their accuracy respect reality, as these methods

• Comparison between loads, fatigue, shear and turbulence, for both a model based on

• Data analysis of the measurements taken from both lidar and mast devices and a com­

Weibull distribution
When designing a wind turbine, one of the principal aspects that need to be known, apart from
the optimal wind direction, is the range of wind speeds where the wind turbine will move on and
which values are the ones most likely to have. This is one of the principal aspects as the energy
that can be extracted depends of the wind speed cubed.
If the wind is measure during a long period of time, it can be observed that it is likely to have mod­
erate and fresh winds, being the stronger winds more unlikely. All this variation usually follows
the so­called Weibull distribution. A Weibull distribution is a generalized gamma distribution
with both shape parameters equal to k.
As it was mentioned on the paragraph above, the wind is characterized for having moderate wind
speeds, therefore, its distribution is not symmetrical, it is right skewed, as it is more unlikely to
have stronger forces.

Mast measurement
The met mast is a tower made of steel or concrete where meteorological instruments are in­
stalled along its structure. As it is a large structure, several devices can be installed at different
heights, allowing to study the wind variation with height. On the case of study, the met mast is
composed with four stations: 31 m, 44 m, 57 m and 70 meters. The one located at 44 meters
is the one similar to the hub height, therefore, it will be the one of more interest for the project.
From fluid mechanisms theory, it is known how a fluid (in particular air) varies with height. The
air varies with height due to friction with the earth’s surface, once a limit distance is achieved
the speed is no longer affected by this parameter, thus it follows an exponential distribution,
increasing its value with height until a limit. This dependency with height is also affected by the
roughness of the terrain and with the obstacles that surround the turbine. On Figure 2.10, the
typical wind profile has been represented. It can be seen that depending on the characteristics
of the environment, the shape of the distribution changes.

The induction force is the responsible for the decrease of speed as the rotor is approached.
According to Newton’s third law, for every action force, there is an opposite reaction force. If
extrapolated to aerodynamics, the incoming wind is creating a force on the blades called lift,
which produces a reaction one, called thrust, opposing the incoming wind. This opposing force
will be more noticeable moving upstream to the rotor and the consequence is a decrease of the
wind speed on the so called induction zone. [15] [16]
Figure 2.16: Effect of the induction force from the rotor [15]

the kinetic one cannot be neglected as it is very dependant of the wind speed. On the other

Turbulence
In order to analyze the turbulence, it is necessary to analyze the wind variation with time as

can be decomposed into two factors, the average mean wind speed and the fluctuating part.
With the aim of studying the wind variation, it is necessary to analyze the fluctuating factor that
represents the turbulence. [1]
For doing so, the wind increment will be defined, where the mean speed is of no interest and
is deleted from the equation. Then, the increment is defined as the difference between the

be assumed that the velocity remains approximately stationary over this time period, and so an

3.4
Loads
Due to the surface roughness, the wind speed is variable, stochastic, and presents turbulence,
therefore when it finds an obstacle on the way it may cause vibration. The pressure caused by
the wind is proportional to the square of the velocity and must be calculated for the exposed
surfaces. When the free flow of the wind is obstructed by a fixed object, it has to deflect to go
around it, which produces a gradient of pressure around the object. The air particles hit the
windward side, producing a pushing force. Meanwhile on the downwind side, the flow trails
separate from the object, causing a suction pressure. [16]
The two forces add up to a drag force, creating a gradient of pressure between both faces.
Sometimes, if the incoming flow has a high pressure or speed, the wind can separate from the
obstacle, generating asymmetrically vortexes that can cause vibrations. [16]
When structures block the wind flow, the kinetic energy of the wind is reconverted into the
potential energy, which causes wind loading. The effect of the wind depends on the density and
velocity of the air, angle of incidence, shape and stiffness of the structure.
All the forces that are applied to the turbine from the external environment result not only in a
decrease of the efficiency to convert wind energy into power, but also in an extra stress to the
materials. When it comes to designing a turbine, this last aspect is of vital importance since the
cost of its construction must be amortized and if the components do not last for a certain period
of time, the project will not economically viable. [16]
The loads that a wind turbine suffer all over its structure are the following: [33]
• Static loads
• Steady loads: rotation (centrifugal force)
• Cyclic loads: due to wind shear, blade weight and yaw motion
• Impulsive loads: when blades are in front of the tower (short duration)
• Transient loads: start and stop process
• Stochastic loads: due to turbulence
• Resonance induced loads: excitation near the natural frequency of the structure.
• Fatigue loads: due to fluctuating winds.
Normally breakdowns are caused by fatigue cracks, extreme wind conditions, inadequate use
of the control system, a defective safety control system or the worst possible scenario that is the
lost of power connection to the generator which will cause an acceleration of the whole system
causing the blades to start spinning uncontrollably (since the rotor speed is squared according

Fatigue definition
Most of the materials that exist can withstand a load when it is applied a certain number of
times, but if this load is applied more than once for short periods of time, so the component has
time to recover its position and then applied again, achieving a cycled pattern, then tiny cracks
start to grow on the material due to fatigue loads. As more cycles take place, this tiny crack will
become bigger, ending up on a break down of the component.
Usually, this property is tested with the material itself on a laboratory, and then when designing
the blade and the tower, the materials are chosen according to the properties that best adjust
to the require conditions. Furthermore, once the compound has been chosen, also the compo­
nents of the wind turbine will be tested, as there is a mixture of different compounds. With all
this, the Wohler curve for each component (also called S­N curve) can be plotted and yet the
number of cycles before break down for an specific applied force can be known. On this case,
those curves cannot be reproduced with the data available for the project, thus, in order to study
this effect, the damage equivalent load will be calculated and compared. For obtaining this last
parameter, the rain­flow counting algorithm will be applied.

damage load for fatigue is determined. With this method, the stress range and the number of
cycles will be calculated. [21]

3.5
Turbulence intensity
In general, the design of wind turbines is done by assuming almost an uniform laminar flow, in
order to simplify calculations. However, on the atmospheric phenomena, wind turbulence can
be defined as one of the most unpredictable conditions since it is the outcome of eddies and
vertical currents that result in an irregular and unstable flow. This continuous changes of the flow
produces an abnormal increase on loads, an increase on the structure’s fatigue, wake effects,
noise propagation and power performance effects. For all this, on this chapter the impact that
the wind turbulence has on the structure and on the energy obtained will be defined since is

crucial when it comes to designing an efficient and an operational turbine.
The wind turbulence depends on four causes. The first one is due to the friction with the ground
surface. Here, if the flow encounters an irregular ground or any obstacles (both natural or
artificial) some eddies will be formed as a consequence of the flow surrounding the obstacle or
when passing through it, affecting the stability of the flow. This cause is known as mechanical
turbulence.
The second factor that affects the turbulence is the thermal variable. It is well known that warm
air tends to rise in order to cool down, while colder one goes down on the atmosphere in order to
heat up. Therefore, depending on the surfaces, the ground can be heated more quickly, result­
ing in a faster movement of the cooler­warmer flow which implies an increase of the turbulence.
The third factor to take into account is the frontal turbulence that occurs when the warm air
is lifted by the sloping frontal surface where the friction between the two opposing air masses
produce turbulence in the frontal zone.
Lastly, any change in the wind direction or speed produces a wind shear (either horizontal or
vertical) and if it additionally contains temperature inversions, the stability is more affected. The
temperature inversion depends on the sun position, as during night the Earth’s surface cools
down, while during day it heats, creating a surface­based inversion.
To conclude, there are many parameters that affect the stability of the flow and are the cause of
its complexity. These variables are the wind speed variation, the wind direction, the atmospheric
stability and the thermal variable. More research has to be done between the wakes and the
atmosphere conditions with the aim of understanding better the influence of turbulence.
Individual turbulent motions are unpredictable, although some of their properties can be studied
from an statistical point. A way of measuring the turbulence is with a parameter known as
turbulence intensity (TI) defined as the ratio between the standard deviation of a 10 min sample
and the mean wind speed of the same sample. This measures the amount of wind that does not
remain laminar. Thus, if the TI achieves a high value, the wind is fluctuating rapidly, meanwhile
the steady winds will be characterized with lower TI factors.

Recent studies have shown that this parameters has a great influence on the structural behavior
of the wind turbine, since the higher the value of TI, the bigger the power losses are, as there
is an increase on the loads that the blade needs to endure. Additionally, a high TI implies a
continuous change on the loads for both tower and blades, resulting in the appearance of cyclic
loads that can cause more fatigue failure of the structure.
In addition, this parameter greatly affects the power that the turbine generates. The power
output increases with TI at lower wind speeds but when reaching the rated power, an increase
on TI implies lower power generation, since the great amount of instability reduces the speed of
the wind. Gottschall and Peinke [23] suggested that the wind power can be decomposed into
two parts, a deterministic one and a stochastic one. With this, the power curve resembled more
to reality. Nevertheless, there was still a certain amount of error, being overestimated during
unstable regimes and underestimated during stable ones. [23]

small error can be neglected and not taken into account.

take the same measures, where the first one is located at a distance of 130 meters from the

Fitting a probability distribution
As it has been studied during the whole project, the main objective has been comparing the real
distribution that wind posses against the Gaussian one, since when designing wind turbines
and developing new improvements on the structure, or on the operative system, for simplicity,
the wind is taken as a normal distribution although it is not correct. Therefore, the main purpose
of this project has been studying the effect of different wind parameters and its effect on the
structures loads.
But once it was demonstrated that it does not follow a Gaussian distribution, a search for a new
fit that behave more as the current flow was carried out. For this, all known distributions have
been analyzed and a few were selected in order to study if they could fit the profile. Thus it was
concluded that the t location scale was the one that best fit the actual profile.

4.7.1
TI diurnal effect
As it was studied on the theory chapter, the turbulence intensity is affected by several param­
eters, in between them, the diurnal cycle at the boundary level. This layer of the atmosphere
is the lower one and its behavior is directly affected by the changes on the surface. Wind tur­
bines, although they are taller, they are impacted by all possible changes. Furthermore, since
the turbine studied is also low power, and it is located only 44 meters from the surface, it can
be more influenced by all these variables in comparison to other devices.
Apart from all the variables aforementioned, the diurnal cycle effect has been studied below.
This parameter has been analyzed since 1980 where the boundary layer was studied by Carlson
and Stull (1986) and Mahrt (1998) [24]. The cycle is as follows, during the night, the earth starts
cooling since there is no solar radiation, then the formation of eddies also lowers down, having
the wind less energy and decreasing both the turbulence and the speed of it, resulting in a more
stable flow. On the contrary, during daytime, the radiation heats the surface making thicker the
boundary layer and favoring the formation of big size and strength eddies which makes the wind
to mix and rise up to higher heights in order to cool down. It should be highlighted that overnight
there is an increase on the wind vertical gradient near the surface since the radiate cooling at
the surface decouples the wind. [24]
On this section, this effect has been examined, where the TI factor has been classified in two
groups, night and day. Below the results of the diurnal cycle are represented. It should be
noticed that in Denmark, the number of daylight hours varies in great measure with the season,

hours has been adjust to the month in particular.

this effect. The second alternative was the control system where it was observed that the pitch
control system tended to suddenly increase the pitch angle in about more than 5◦for just a
couple of seconds, leading to a momentary drop or increase of the tower loads.
Usually, the purpose of the pitch control system is to adjust the blades angle so the active power
obtained from the wind is limited to the maximum that the rotor can handle, while for speeds
below rated the adjustment is just slightly to obtain the best aerodynamic position. For this
reason, for speeds below rated, the pitch tends to remain almost constant and with a slight
negative angle so the aerodynamic that the blades have is the optimal one, while for speeds
above rated the pitch goes to positive values with the aim of losing part of the energy that can
be extracted. For doing so, when the aerodynamic position of the blade is not the optimal one,
the formation of eddies happened and the flow is detached from the blade.
Furthermore, due to the high force of the wind, this angle variation must be made slow, in order to
avoid fatigue over strain. As it was observed, for the case of study, this system is characterized
with sudden increases and drops of around 5­6 degrees when the speeds are below rated.
Moreover, even for above rated, where it is correct that the pitch varies on a large scale, the
variation from lower angles towards higher ones is not done gradually but with drops. This may
result in unstable loads, and yet more fatigue damage can be caused at the whole structure
which could also lead to an early deterioration of the turbine, a reduction of its lifetime.
In addition, from the control system and electrical point of view, every time the signal process
receives a signal of increasing the pitch angle, in order to modify it, it must send other signal
with a higher voltage so the control system can work. With this, the motor receives contradictory
signals in a short period of time, suffering also fatigue consequences.

CONCLUSION
Throughout the project, results have been obtained and several conclusions have been gath­
ered. In this chapter, all the conclusions drawn will be summarized.
It is recalled that the main objective of this research was to prove that the wind turbulence does
not follow a Gaussian distribution as it is normally assumed when designing the wind turbine.
In recent studies, it was thought that this assumption alter and provided an error that could be
appreciated when studying the fatigue loads that the wind turbine was suffering. Nevertheless,
this error did not have any influence, such as, a sudden break down of the turbine since this de­
vice is design with a high security coefficient, large enough to ensure the stability of the structure
during the estimated half­life. But still, they aim was to prove that the Gaussian assumption will
imply higher loads, and that in the long term, it could affect the structure, lowering its lifetime.
Before analyzing the loads, the wind main parameters were analyzed, in order to study the type
of distributions that the fluctuations had over time. Here, the speed, the direction, the shear
effect and the turbulence intensity were studied, firstly, in an individual form, secondly as a
whole. The following conclusions were drawn.
To begin with, the speed parameter had a high influence on the type of distribution that the
wind turbulence present. Here it was observed that lower speeds were characterized with high
Gaussianity even for low time increments studied. Where as speed increases, the percentage
of samples that were facing Gaussianity decreases linearly, achieving a minimum value of cases
the closer to the speed rated. The new distributions were characterized with quite heavier tails,
which implied that there were scenarios that were more likely to happen and that were not
considered with the Gaussian one. The explanation for this behavior comes from the stability of
the flow, whereas for low speed samples, the flow is more stable and can be more predictable.
Meanwhile, as the velocity increases, the flow becomes more unstable, creating situations that
before were more unlikely to happen, for this reason the tails become heavier. Finally, for
high speeds, the stability does not play a role, since the flow is mixing and balancing by itself,
therefore, the tails tend to narrow its scale.
On the other hand, the direction parameter was studied. In here, it was observed that depending
on the direction the wind came from, the pdf behave one way or another. For example, when the
sample was located on the ideal region (289◦) the wind tend to achieve Gaussianity for higher
time increments, while when the wind came from the sector where the trees obstacles were
(80◦) the pdf converge more towards normal functions. This was due to the turbulence that the
trees created. Following the same reasoning as with wind speed, the trees were increasing
the instability of the wind although the speed was being decrease. This instability originated
more eddies and more turbulence, but the mixing part balanced the effect and for this reason,
the distributions where more Gaussian for this sector, since the probability for having unusual
situations was dropping. Nevertheless, this does not imply that the loads that the structure suffer
are not higher for the 80◦sector, since being the turbulence quite higher, the correspondent
loads were rising more than usual.
Furthermore, it was concluded that the turbulence intensity that characterizes the flow, had
also a big influence on Gaussianity, since the more laminar the flow was, the more Gaussian
the pdf was, while for intermediate values the lower percentage of samples following normal
distributions was found. Once again, if the turbulence was too large, the non Gaussianity was
lost due to the turbulence equilibrium.

CHAPTER 5. CONCLUSION
Once it was demonstrated that the wind tends not to converge towards Gaussianity until high
time increments, it was decided to look for a more suitable function that could adjust better to
the data and that was still simple to use. Here, the main purpose was to find distributions with
heavier tails and with a third parameter called shape. After conducting a study with 6 different
functions, it was concluded that both the logistic and the t location scale (t students family) fitted
better with the data, where both of them where mainly characterized with heavier tails.
Finally, the loads were studied. Here, due to the cyclic state of the tangential loads, and the long
time series that all the loads presents, the rain flow counting algorithm was used to calculate
the damage equivalent load and be able to make a comparison between several sets of data. It
was concluded that the initial conclusion was wrong, since when comparing the values from the
samples that present Gaussianity against the ones that do not, it was seen that they both sets
follow the same regression, the same pattern, this is, they vary with the same ratio with wind
speed, where it was expected that the non Gaussian ones present quite higher loads below
rated.
Even, it was observed that above rated, the non Gaussian samples tended to achieve values
quite lower than the Gaussian ones. The only difference that could be appreciated was that all
the Gaussian samples had their load values close the regression curve, while the second group
was more spread, having values quite higher or quite lower than the regression curve. The only
problem were this could have any effect is when forecasting the loads for the turbine, since it is
always expected to have the same loads, while the non Gaussian ones cannot be forecast so
precisely.
Lastly, it should be highlighted that as the measurements analyzed were obtained from two
different devices, the mast and the lidar, that were located at different positions and that measure
on a different way, a comparison between both of them was done. It was concluded that the
mast was more accurate for analyzing the turbulence and the fluctuations of wind, since the lidar,
by taking the average of the four points of measure, was losing part of the small fluctuations.
Therefore, its distribution behave more easily and sooner as a Gaussian distribution.

FUTURE PROGRESS
The project is intended to serve as a guide for future work related to the study of wind conditions,
specifically for the modelling and designing part where an accurate forecast of wind is needed.
To begin with, and as it was demonstrated during the research, two statistical distributions were
discovered to fit better into the wind characteristics. Since during the designing part, the Gaus­
sian function is always assumed, it is proposed to design the turbine with one of these two dis­
tributions, to verify if the parameters of the structure vary greatly, and although these functions
are not as simple as the Gaussian, taking into account that they are based on it, the modelling
method should be able to process the new calculations.
Other topic of great interest would be a deeper study of the loads, where since it was concluded
that the samples classified as non Gaussian were characterized with more spread values of
loads, it would be interested to try and forecast these values, where depending on the time
series that the wind presents the loads obtained will be higher or lower, since it was seeing that
a non Gaussian wind speed did not directly imply a non Gaussian distribution of the loads, and
vice versa. Therefore, it would be interesting to study this evolution of loads and velocities, and
try to find a correlation that could help on finding a better forecast of the loads.
The third idea presented as future progress could be the analysis of how much does the blades
movement affect to the measurement taken by the lidar mounted on the nacelle. This is, it is
recalled that the lidars, since they are located on top of the nacelle, are affected by both the rotor
induction effect but also by the constant movement of the blades interfering on the measures.
The idea would be to installed several lidars on the ground, so they would be measuring the
same space (point) as the one mounted on the nacelle. This way, a comparison between both
methods could be carried out, and the effect of the blades over the lidar and over the nacelle
could be better understand.
Moreover, with several anemometers or lidars located at different distances from the rotor, the
induction rotor effect could be also studied.
It should be noticed that wind energy is one of the most developed renewable energies, thus,
the main objective is not to keep researching on the structure, but rather to investigate about
wind conditions and how the efficiency of the turbine can be improved. A precise forecast of
the wind conditions is essential on this type of projects since is the main resource of energy.
