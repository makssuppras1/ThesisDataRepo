Implementation of BroadR-Reach communication and SOME/IP Protocol on
Altran’s Muxlab 6
by Nicolas MINOT
The automotive industry currently faces a major evolution in its networks design.
Today a data network in a car is based on the calculators. This means that each
functionality is based on one calculator, and are connected to each other using slow
but reliable communication protocols such as CAN or LIN.
However, the current trend is to provide to the car user more and more infor-
mation about the car itself, its travel, and to facilitates its driving experience (using
reversing camera, GPS...). To be able to provide this features, it is necessary to equip
vehicles with higher data rate networks, but also to change the network structure:
the multiplication of features also multiplies the computing power demand, increas-
ing the number of calculators.
A possible answer to this problem is the use of Ethernet adapted to automo-
tive industry called BroadR-Reach. This technology provides 100 Mbits/s Ethernet
communication over a unshielded single twisted pair cable in Full-duplex. In other
words, it provides fast and reliable communication using light and cheap cables.
Ethernet based protocols will also provide an efﬁcient and reliable communica-
tion between calculators, allowing them to share features depending on the actual
need at a particular moment. This is called a service based Network.
Yet, before it can be implemented to cars, constructors need tools to test the
changes in their vehicles networks. Altran’s Muxlab 6 is an automotive calcula-
tor simulator and is currently used on vehicles during their development. It now
supports BroadR-Reach in addition to all other previous networks and is delivered
with SOME/IP protocol, which is a Service Based protocol. The implementation is a
success, and the Muxlab 6 will be used in the future to help automotive constructor
develop and test their networks in their vehicles, including BroadR-Reach.
BroadR-Reach numerous qualities make it a very promising technology for ve-
hicles networks, its use is already spreading. The implemented protocol, SOME/IP,
is likely to be used, but this not yet sure. Indeed constructors need to make choices
between several protocols, and SOME/IP could or could not be one of them. This
work shows the feasibility of its implementation and indicates its pros and cons.

Chapter 1
Introduction
Electronics in cars grow faster for a few years now. The number of ECUs (Elec-
tronic Control Units) in a typical car exceeds today 100. All these ECUs need to be
connected to each other to perform correctly, requiring more and more cabling. Fur-
thermore, the demand in data rate is also increasing with the use of cameras, GPS.
Indeed video stream requires more data than information.
To tackle this issue, car manufacturers deployed several networks over their car
(such as LIN, CAN or MOST), each one for a speciﬁc use. The multiplication of net-
works increases the complexity of the system to such an extent that they are thinking
about changing the network structure.
For now, networks in vehicles are ECU-based, meaning that each functionality
of the car has its own ECU. However, processors are getting powerful enough to
process several features at a time. This is especially the case if we consider that all
features are not used at the same time on a car.
The idea would then be to create a Service-based network, a service representing
a feature of a car. A service would not necessarily be associated to one particu-
lar ECU, and could be started anywhere on the network. Some information would
however be provided to one ECU (a sensor would be connected to only one ECU), a
protocol is then needed to transfer the information of the sensor to the right service,
independently of the service location. A higher data rate will also be needed because
of the mobility of information and services. The data rates provided by the actual
networks such as CAN or LIN are less than 10 Mbit/s, whereas the Ethernet proto-
col that is used to connect our computers to the Internet can provide 10 Gbit/s for
the optical ﬁber. Adapting this technology to the automotive industry could solve
the problem of data rate.
The aim of this Master Thesis is to introduce the technologies that have been de-
veloped to solve these problems.
The work was realized in the Altran company, world leader in innovation con-
sulting and advanced engineering. The Altran group achieved in 2017 a turnover of
2.9 billion euros. It now has more than 45,000 employees in more than 30 countries.
The group has been involved in many sectors: aerospace, automotive, defense, en-
ergy, rail, ﬁnance, life sciences, telecoms, etc.
The agency in Annecy where the thesis was written is a design ofﬁce with mainly
two activities. The ﬁrst one is to develop a personalized solution to a client’s prob-
lem. This could include more or less complex hardware, PC software or embedded
software. The other main activity is to develop, produce and sell their own products.

2.1
Automotive networks
The increasing demand in electronics, camera and infotainment media in vehicles
requires the automotive industry to ﬁnd efﬁcient solutions. This indeed induces an
increasing amount data to transfer in the vehicle, that is to say more cable, more
weight...
The actual networks that are used on the vehicles will be described here.
2.1.1
CAN Network
A CAN Network is a bus with at least two nodes. All the nodes receive all data
emitted on the bus. The CAN protocol avoid collisions on the bus and settle an
emission order in case of conﬂict depending on a priority that is set to each data. If
a node starts emitting, the other nodes listen and wait for the emission to be over to
start emitting. If several nodes start emitting at the same time, the message with the
higher priority wins and the other nodes stop emitting and wait again for the bus to
be free.
FIGURE 2.1: CAN Network structure from [10]
==PAGE:18==

The main inconvenient of this type of network is that only one node can send
data on the bus at a time. This means that data can be delayed depending on its
priority and on the priority of the other messages that need to be send at a moment
in time. That is why a CAN bus is loaded to only half of its capacity in real conditions
to avoid problem with low priority messages.
2.1.2
LIN Network
The LIN network is a broadcasting network with one master and up to 15 slaves.
The master sends messages that can be answered by up to one slave. A scheme of
this network is given on Figure 2.2. The data rates is limited to 20 kbit/s, with frames
FIGURE 2.2: LIN Network structure
rate of 2, 4 or 8 bytes. However, there are guaranteed latency times.
This Network is less expansive than a CAN Network, but data are limited and
can circulate in only one way: from master to slave to master. This is used in part
of vehicle where speed is not important in criticality is low, such as seats or mirrors
controls.
2.1.3
Ethernet Network
An Ethernet Network doesn’t work in the same way. It is called a Packet Switching
Network. The network doesn’t form a loop like in CAN Network, packets are auto-
matically directed to the destination through Switches. The communication is done
point to point that is to say ECU to ECU in our case: that is called Unicast. ECUs
that are not concerned by the message will never receive it, and will not be aware
that it was send on the Network.
However it can happen that a message should be received by several ECUs. It
would then be necessary to send one message for each receiver, which is not opti-
mum. To answer this issue, Multicast is used. An address is deﬁned for a Multicast
group, and each message send to this address will be received by all the member of
this group. It is also possible to send Broadcast message which will be forwarded to
all nodes connected to the network.

This type of network allows to transfer more data (several packets to several
ECUs can be send in the same time). However it requires more material (Switches).
Switches are also a potential failure source: if one switch doesn’t work it can shut
down all communication on the system. On a CAN Network, if an ECU goes down,
the other can continue to communicate, only the information from this ECU are lost.
2.1.4
BroadR-Reach
The BroadR-Reach network is part of the Ethernet networks, and more details about
it are given here.
From [24] BroadR-Reach was created in 2011 by Broadcom Limited. It corre-
sponds to the IEEE 802.3 standard 100BASE-T1.
BroadR-Reach technology is based on unshielded single twisted pair cable. The
Ethernet protocols allows a data rate of 100 Mbits/s, and the cabling system is lighter
than the 100BASE-TX used on PCs requiring two twisted pairs.
FIGURE 2.4: Single twisted pair cable
The connectivity is not deﬁned in the BroadR-Reach standard, therefore several
technologies are used. D-E9 Connector is one of them, used by the company Vector.
They use two or four pins of the connector, enabling the possibility of two BroadR-
Reach connection over one D-E9 Connector. However, the use of a decoupler cable
is then needed to isolate each BroadR-Reach connection over one single twisted pair
cable.
Technica-Engineering uses the MQS technology, allowing to connect only one
BroadR-Reach single twisted pair cable, with only one unconnected pin.
Intrepid Control System uses the nanoMQS technology allowing them to connect
up to six 100BASE-T1 PHY’s to one connector.

My work was based on the Altran’s Muxlab product and the Vector VN5640
product, using both of them the D-E9 Connector. Therefore, I will not address the
other connectors further in this thesis.
This technology realizes simultaneous transmit and receive (i.e. full-duplex) op-
erations. To do so, Broadr-Reach requires a master/slave conﬁguration. The master
will communicate it’s clock to the slave in order to have synchronous operation on
both sides of the Ethernet wire. This standard allows reduced connectivity costs and
cabling weight for automotive manufacturers because only one twisted pair cable is
needed. Furthermore, the cable is unshielded, so of a lower quality but also lower
price.
Several uses for BroadR-Reach as described in [10] are possible. The simplest
use would be to transfer other signals like CAN or LIN frame over Ethernet. The
high bit rate of BroadR-Reach (100 Mbits/s) would allow to forward several CAN
or LIN channels over only one BroadR-Reach channel. Furthermore Ethernet frames
are easier to process on a computer (which doesn’t support CAN or LIN protocols.)
Another use of the high speed of BroadR-Reach would be to implement a higher
level protocol over Ethernet to process data. Possible protocols will be detailed in
the following part.
2.1.5
Ethernet protocols
In this part, the Internet protocol Suite or TCP/IP will be detailed. It provides a
whole set of layers and protocols to organize data transfer through Ethernet. It is the
current technology used for Internet.

Link Layer
The Link layer is the Medium Access Control (MAC). The MAC is connected to
the physical layer (PHY) using a media-independant interface (MII). The MAC will
encapsulate the higher level data by adding the destination MAC address, the source
MAC address and the type of Network Layer that is used: in our case it is IPv4. The
MAC is also responsible for forwarding the packet to the PHY.

2.1.6
Network Layer
The Network layer that interests us is IPv4. This is the internet layer that is actually
used for most internet applications. It is quite complex, and only few parts of the
header will interest us. The ﬁrst one is the Length (in green on 2.9). It corresponds
to the total length of the IPv4 header and the data that follows. The other interesting
part is the protocol. It corresponds to the code of the transport layer that is used in

To answer the problem of automotive communication, choices of technology have

First the hardware that will be chosen is BroadR-Reach. It indeed has a lot of

3.7
SOME/IP-SD Communication
As previously seen, a service can provide methods calls, but also events sending.
The SOME/IP-SD protocol uses the notion of eventgroup to organize events. Events
will be regrouped in an eventgroup, and other services can subscribe to this event-
group. This means that they would receive all events that are included in this event-
group, and only these ones. It allows to separate unrelated events, so that it avoids
clients to receive too many events that they are not concerned with. It also avoids
to subscribe to each event individually, which might be tedious in case of numerous
but rare events.
The IP address corresponding to a service is not known. The SOME/IP-SD pro-
tocol allows to share it with the other nodes connected to the network that might
need to communicate with it at some point.
Therefore, all nodes using SOME/IP will join a multicast group where all SD
messages will be exchanged. Each service provider will send an Offer Service mes-
sage with the services that can be provided and all information needed to contact
this service (IP address and port). Services will then send a unicast message Sub-
scribe to each service they are interested in describing to which eventgroups or ﬁelds
they want to subscribe. Finally the provider sends an Subscribe Acknowledgment to
conﬁrm the subscription is correct and taken into account. (see Figure 3.6).
FIGURE 3.6:
SOME/IP-SD communication Scheme starting from
server
The provider can also send a Subscribe Nack. It means that the information that
the client sends in its Subscribe message are not coherent with the information the
service can provide. Therefore, the client is not subscribe to the service, and shall
resend a Subscribe message with hopefully correct information.

The scheme can also start from the client side if it has started before the server.
The client will send a multicast Find Service for each Service it wants to be subscribed
to. Servers will answer this message with a unicast message Offer Service to the client
precising the corresponding offered services. The following of the communication

Offers have to be send cyclically to be maintained. Indeed, a Time To Live (TTL)
is present on each message. If the TTL expires before a new Offer message is re-
ceived, the connection is considered as lost between the server and the client. The
client shall send a new Find Service to subscribe again.
The SD standard also deﬁnes messages when one service stop. The service provider
has to send a StopOffer Service when the service is not provided anymore.to explicitly
indicate that the service stops.
When a service doesn’t want to be subscribed to an eventgroup, it has to send a
StopSubscribe Eventgroup message.
All this messages stop cyclically and explicit StopSubscribe allow to detect any
anomaly in the network communication. Even if data are lost with a communica-
tion loss, the application will know and could be programmed to act accordingly. It
allows to counteract the lack of robustness of UDP communication.

The ﬁrst bit is the reboot ﬂag, the second and third ﬂag should be set to 1, and
are used for compatibility with previous versions.
The reboot ﬂag allows to detect the reboot of the server or of a client. When the
communication starts, reboot ﬂag is set to 1. Then the communication goes on, and
each message that is sent or received is identiﬁed by a Session ID that increments

3.7.5
SOME/IP-SD routine
From [4], a communication routine as to be implemented to be informed as fast as
possible on any change on the situation of the network and to show regularly that
everything is working properly. The routine is divided in three phases.
• Phase 1 after boot: wait phase. The server do not emit any messages during
a certain time that can be user deﬁned. This phase is made to wait for other
nodes, clients and servers, to start.
• Then comes the repetition phase. The server sends all needed Offer Service and
Find Service and then wait for a user deﬁned period. After this wait, messages
are sent again and the wait time is doubled. These tasks are repeated for a user
deﬁned number of time before entering the next phase. However, if an Offer
Service corresponding to one of the Find Service is received, this phases stops
and the following phase is triggered for this service: the main phase.
• In the main phase, no more Find Service are emitted, and Offer Service are
emitted cyclically. Subscribe and Subscribe Acknowledgement are sent, re-
sponding to the corresponding message type. An example given in [4] allows
a better understanding of the routine 3.11.

Chapter 5
Conclusion and future work
5.1
Conclusion
One of the actual problem of the automotive industry is to provide an increasing
amount of data to the user, to help him drive in more safely conditions or to make
him enjoy his ride. The solution described in this thesis offers one answer to this
problem. The use of BroadR-Reach allows a much higher data rate than the actual
automotive networks with low-cost cabling, without a loss in quality.
The protocol that comes with it, SOME/IP, facilitates the integration of features
to the network. Indeed, the network doesn’t need to be designed as a ﬁxed and
unchangeable network, but in the contrary as a modular network, where features
could be added by a simple software update. There is a clear distinction between
hardware that is to say the ECUs (where calculation are made) and features (calcu-
lation that should be made).
5.2
Future Work
The implementation described in this document has been tested only with two con-
versing ECUs. This is a good base, but will certainly need modiﬁcation with a more
complex system. Protocols improvement could be considered, such as the use of
TCP instead of UDP for reliable and secured communication.
Concerning the Internet layer IPv4, ﬁxed IP addresses are assigned to each ECU.
There is a protocol DHCP that would allow to dynamically assign IP addresses to
the nodes of the Network. This protocol would add even more ﬂexibility to the net-
work, and could also be a must have in case of an important number of nodes. Ipv4
might also arrives to the end of its lifetime because of the lack of free IP addresses.
Furthermore car manufacturer might want to be able to connect the vehicle to the
internet. That would of course need a highly secured network, and that would also
require the network to be compatible with IPv6 addresses that should be used on
Internet in a few years.
On a less technical point of view, the BroadR-Reach technology seams powerful
enough to be used on vehicles. Another similar technology is also being develop to
perform 1000 Mbits/s on the same cables.
However the SOME/IP protocol might not be chosen by car manufacturers. This
is of course a powerful protocol allowing a well optimized use of the high data rate
of BroadR-Reach, it requires however a lot of changes in the automotive network
design. Therefore, car manufacturer might be reticent to use this protocol at ﬁrst.

They could prefer the use of AVB or DoIP, that offer new features without modifying
existing networks. It means that BroadR-Reach would be used in addition to actual
existing networks, and would not replace it.
