II 
Abstract 
Efficient mixing is essential in stirred tank reactors (STRs), where it affects mass and heat transfer, 
pH control, and nutrient distribution. Inadequate mixing can lead to gradients in these parameters, 
reducing process efficiency and product quality. Although global mixing times are commonly 
reported, they fail to capture local inhomogeneities such as dead zones or slow-mixing compartments. 
Understanding both local and global mixing behavior is therefore essential. 
 
This project investigates the use of image analysis to determine both local and global mixing times 
in a 180 L STR under aerated and non-aerated conditions. The method is based on a decolorization 
using bromothymol blue as a pH indicator. Acid was added to induce a color change from blue (base) 
to yellow (acid), which was recorded using a high-speed camera (25 fps). Mixing times were 
determined by analyzing changes in greyscale pixel values. Experiments were conducted at agitation 
speeds of 75, 100, and 150 RPM, and gas flow rates of 0, 0.25, 0.39, and 0.5 vvm in deionized (DI) 
water with and without 0.05 M MgSO₄·7H₂O. Additional experiments were performed in tap water.  
 
The image analysis method was validated against both pH and conductivity measurements. The pH 
method significantly overestimated mixing times, while conductivity measurements showed 
agreement with image analysis. Results showed that mixing time decreased with increased agitation 
and without aeration, and with aeration, mixing time decreased. Local mixing distributions indicated 
air accumulation at the top of the tank and recirculation zones around the impellers. The middle 
compartment showed the longest mixing time. Gas hold-up increased with agitation speed and gas 
flow rate, and the presence of MgSO₄·7H₂O increased gas hold-up without affecting mixing time. In 
tap water, neither image analysis nor the pH method proved reliable due to buffering. 
 
Overall, image analysis proved to be a useful method for the determination of both global and local 
mixing times.

III 
Resumé  
 
Effektiv blanding er afgørende i en omrøringstank (STR), da det sikrer god masse- og 
varmeoverførsel, pH kontrol og fordeling af næringsstoffer. Utilstrækkelig omrøring kan føre til 
gradienter i disse parametre, hvilket reducerer proceseffektivitet og produktkvalitet. Selvom globale 
blandingstider almindeligvis bestemmes, formår de ikke at fange lokale inhomogeniteter, hvor 
blandingen er langsom eller  utilstrækkelig. Det er derfor vigtigt at forstå både den globale men også 
lokale blanding.  
 
Dette projekt undersøger brugen af billedanalyse til at bestemme både lokale og globale 
blandingstider i en 180 L STR både med og uden tilførsel af luft. Metoden er baseret på en 
decolorization, hvor bromthymol blå benyttes som pH indikator. Syre tilsættes for at opnå et 
farveskift fra blå (basisk) til gul (sur). Dette optages med et højhastighedskamera (25 fps). Herved 
kan blandingstiden bestemmes ved at analysere ændringer i gråskala pixels værdier. Forsøgene blev 
udført ved omrøringshastigheder på 75, 100 og 150 RPM og gas flow på 0, 0.25, 0.39 og 0.5 vvm i 
DI-vand med og uden tilsætning af 0.05 M MgSO4·7H2O. Der blev desuden udført forsøg i postevand.  
 
Billedanalysemetoden blev valideret med både pH- og konduktivitetsmålinger. pH metoden viste 
samme tendens i blandingstider, men med markant større værdier, mens konduktivitetsmålinger viste 
god overensstemmelse med billedanalyseresultaterne. Resultaterne viste, at blandingstiden faldt med 
øget omrøringshastighed uden tilsætning af luft. Ved tilsætning af luft fald blandingstiden ved alle 
omrøringshastigheder. Lokale blandingstider viste luftophobning i toppen af tanken og omkring 
impellerne. Det blev bestemt at den midterste del af tanken havde den længste blandingstid 
sammenlignet med toppen og bunden. Gas hold-up steg med både omrøringshastighed og gas flow, 
og tilsætning af MgSO4·7H2O øgede gas hold-up, men havde ingen påvirkning på blandingstiden. I 
postevand viste hverken billedanalyse eller pH-metoden sig at være pålidelig grundet buffereffekt.  
 
Samlet set viste billedanalysemetoden at være en brugbar metode til bestemmelse af både globale og 
lokale blandingstider.

Chapter 1  
1. Introduction  
Efficient mixing is a fundamental requirement in many processes within the chemical and 
biochemical industries, where it plays a critical role in ensuring optimal mass and heat transfer, 
nutrient distribution, and pH control. In such systems, mixing time is a key parameter, as it 
significantly influences process performance and product quality. (Zak et al., 2022) 
Mixing in stirred tank reactors (STRs) is among the most complex and least understood processes in 
both chemical and biochemical engineering. It has been reported that inadequate mixing accounts for 
over 60% of production issues (John Villadsen, 2015). Insufficient mixing can lead to gradients in 
pH, oxygen, or nutrients, resulting in heterogeneous conditions that affect cell viability, yield, and 
reproducibility. 
A deeper understanding of mixing behavior is therefore crucial, particularly in gas-liquid systems 
where gas is dispersed into the liquid phase. Parameters such as gas hold-up, oxygen transfer rate, 
and mixing time are closely interrelated and influenced not only by reactor geometry and flow 
structures, but also by operational conditions such as agitation speed and gas flow rate. Since oxygen 
is often a limiting factor in aerobic fermentations, the ability to achieve and maintain sufficient gas-
liquid mass transfer is a critical aspect of STR performance. (Rosseburg et al., 2018) 
While global mixing time is commonly used to evaluate reactor performance, it does not capture 
spatial variation. Slow-mixing zones and compartmentalization may persist even when global 
measurements suggest complete homogenization. Consequently, assessing both global and local 
mixing times allows for a more complete understanding of the hydrodynamics within the reactor.  
However, despite extensive research on STRs, detailed insight into local mixing distributions remains 
limited, especially at larger scales. This project develops an image analysis method to quantify local 
and global mixing times in a 180 L STR.  The method uses bromothymol blue as a pH indicator, with 
the resulting color change captured in real-time using a high-speed camera. The overall objective is 
to assess whether image analysis can reliably determine both global and local mixing times, while 
also providing insight into flow structures and inhomogeneities within the STR.

1.1  Scope 
The scope of this project is to investigate local and global mixing times in a 180 L stirred tank reactor, 
under both aerated and non-aerated conditions. For this purpose, an image analysis method is 
developed and subsequently validated with the pH method and the conductivity method. The image 
analysis method provides a more detailed understanding of local mixing behavior in the STR across 
different operating conditions.   
 
The experimental work includes systematic variations in agitation speed and gas flow rate to evaluate 
their impact on mixing time and gas hold-up. These parameters are critical in STRs, as they influence 
both hydrodynamics and mass transfer. 
 
Additionally, experiments are carried out in deionized water, with and without the addition of 0.05 
M MgSO4·7H2O, to investigate how coalescence affects mixing and gas dispersion. This comparison 
is intended to improve the applicability of the results to industrial processes, where salts are 
commonly present. Limitations of the methods were also explored under buffered conditions using 
tap water.

2. Theory of Hydrodynamics  
Mixing plays a central role in chemical and biochemical industries, where it is used in everything 
from the production of chemicals, enzymes, and foods to life-saving drugs. Effective mixing ensures 
uniform distribution of components, efficient heat and mass transfer, and optimal reaction conditions, 
which are all crucial for achieving high product yield and quality. In bioprocesses, particularly in 
aerobic fermentations, mixing becomes even more critical due to the need for adequate oxygen 
transfer and the sensitivity of biological systems to environmental fluctuations. This requires a 
thorough understanding of hydrodynamics, including flow conditions, gas dispersion, and oxygen 
transfer. (John Villadsen, 2015; Stanbury et al., 2017) 
 
Figure 1 illustrates the relationship between hydrodynamic parameters and their importance for 
oxygen transfer. Due to the low solubility of oxygen in water, the availability of dissolved oxygen 
limits aerobic processes. Therefore, efficient gas-liquid mass transfer is essential, making 
hydrodynamic factors such as mixing, bubble dispersion, and interfacial area critical to sufficient 
oxygen supply and optimal process performance. The different elements of the figure will be 
described in this chapter, where their individual and combined effects on process efficiency will be 
explained. (Garcia-Ochoa and Gomez, 2009)    
 
Figure 1: Relationship between OTR, kLa, and hydrodynamic parameters at several levels. Inspired from (Garcia-
Ochoa and Gomez, 2009) 
==PAGE:14==
 
 
Page 4 of 108 
An optimal understanding of the hydrodynamics of fermentations is essential for improving process 
performance, ensuring uniformity, and enhancing productivity. As illustrated in Figure 1, 
hydrodynamics influence oxygen transfer through three main factors: Physical properties (e.g. 
viscosity, density, surface tension), geometric parameters (e.g., reactor and impeller dimensions), and 
operational conditions (e.g., agitation speed, gas flow rate). These variables determine power input 
and affect essential parameters such as gas hold-up, bubble size, and residence time, which affect the 
overall oxygen transfer rate. By optimizing these elements, mixing efficiency and gas-liquid mass 
transfer can be improved. This results in higher yields and better product quality.  
 
 
2.1  Gas-Liquid Mass Transfer   
Oxygen transfer plays a significant role in aerobic processes. The rate at which oxygen is transferred 
from the gas to the liquid phase is of crucial importance, as the process can be affected by oxygen 
deficiency. Therefore, it is essential to examine how the oxygen transfer rate can be optimized. In 
aerobic processes, oxygen molecules must overcome various transport resistances before they can be 
utilized by the cells (Tsao and Lee, 1975). As illustrated in Figure 2 below, the oxygen transport from 
the interior gas bubble to the cell can be described in eight distinct steps. 
 
 
Figure 2: The individual steps involved in oxygen transfer from the interior of the gas bubble to the cell (Doran, 2013).  
[1] In the first step, oxygen is transferred from the interior of the bubble to the gas-liquid interface. 
This step is relatively fast. [2] Then, there is movement across the gas-liquid interface. The gas-liquid 
interface itself contributes an insignificant resistance. [3] In the third step, oxygen diffuses from the 
gas-liquid interface into the fermentation liquid. This occurs through the relatively stagnant liquid 
film, which presents a significant resistance to oxygen transfer. [4] Oxygen is then transported 
through the well-mixed liquid to a less-mixed area around the cell. In a well-mixed reactor, 
==PAGE:15==
 
 
Page 5 of 108 
concentration gradients in the liquid are minimized, and mass transfer resistance in this region is low. 
[5] Diffusion then occurs through the relatively stagnant liquid film surrounding the cells. Since 
individual cells are significantly smaller than gas bubbles, the liquid film around each cell is much 
thinner than that around the bubbles. Therefore, this resistance can generally be neglected. However, 
if the cells form clusters, the liquid film resistance can be significant. [6] Movement then occurs 
across the liquid-cell interface. Resistance at the liquid-cell interface is negligible. [7] If the cells are 
clustered, oxygen diffuses through the solid mass to reach the individual cells. [8] Finally, oxygen is 
transported through the cytoplasm to the reaction site. Oxygen transfer resistance in this step is 
negligible due to the very short distances involved. (Doran, 2013; Nielsen and Villadsen, 1994) 
The biggest resistance to oxygen transfer is the liquid film surrounding the gas bubbles. This step is 
the rate-limiting factor for the entire process and therefore determines the overall mass transfer rate. 
The magnitude of the individual mass transfer resistances depends, among other factors, on the 
composition of the liquid and the bubble size. (Caşcaval, 2019) 
2.1.1 Film Theory    
As mentioned above, the term "film" refers to regions where two phases are in contact. This applies 
to liquid-liquid mass transfer, solid-liquid mass transfer, and gas-liquid mass transfer. The focus here 
is on gas-liquid mass transfer, as this is the process studied in the project. The film theory was 
introduced by (Whitman, 1962) and is based on the idea that two films are formed when two phases 
come into contact. In gas-liquid mass transfer, these films consist of a liquid film and a gas film. As 
illustrated in Figure 3 below, a gas film and a liquid film are present at the interface between the gas 
and liquid phases. 
 
Figure 3: Gas-liquid mass transfer at the phase boundary.(Doran, 2013)  
==PAGE:16==
 
 
Page 6 of 108 
According to film theory, turbulence dissipates at the phase boundary. As a result, mass transfer 
through the two stagnant films occurs only by molecular diffusion. Diffusion through the gas film 
proceeds at a rate proportional to the difference in the concentration of the dissolved substance in the 
gas on the inner and outer sides of the gas film. Diffusion through the liquid film is governed by the 
difference between the concentration of the dissolved substance in the liquid at the interface and its 
concentration on the opposite side of the liquid film. This concentration difference across the films 
represents the driving force for diffusion to occur. (Doran, 2013; Rodriguez, 1924) 
For substances with low solubility in the liquid phase, such as oxygen in water, the main resistance 
to mass transfer occurs in the liquid. This is due to slower diffusion rates and limited solubility, which 
hinders molecular transport. Conversely, for highly soluble substances, the greatest resistance will be 
in the gas phase. In this case, the focus is on oxygen in aqueous solution. While all of the substance 
must diffuse through both the gas and liquid films, the resistance of the gas film can be neglected, as 
it is insignificant compared to that of the liquid phase. Equation 1 gives the mass transfer rate through 
the liquid boundary layer: (Rodriguez, 1924)

2.1.2 Gas Hold-up   
Gas hold-up is an important parameter in an aerated STR, where efficient gas-liquid contact is 
essential. Gas hold-up refers to the relative volume of gas that is present in the gas-liquid volume in 
a reactor and affects both gas transfer and the hydrodynamics of the system. If the gas hold-up is too 
low, the production rate in most aerobic processes will decrease. On the other hand, a high gas hold-
up should also be avoided since the gas fraction determines the maximum volume in a specific reactor, 
as it displaces productive liquid. Therefore, a thorough understanding of gas hold-up is necessary to 
optimize process conditions and maximize productivity in chemical and biochemical processes. 
(Hofmeester, 1988a; Kastanek et al., 1972)

Page 7 of 108

used to determine either the overall gas hold-up or the local gas hold-up within a specific area of the 
reactor. Gas hold-up 𝜀1 is defined as: (Doran, 2013)

Several factors affect gas hold-up in a STR, including gas flow rate, agitator design, power input, and 
liquid properties. These factors are described later in the report. The size of the gas bubbles directly 
affects the gas hold-up.

Three main processes determine the bubble size distribution. This includes bubble formation, bubble 
break-up, and bubble coalescence. Bubble formation happens when gas, through a sparger, is 
dispersed into a liquid phase, where the gas breaks up into discrete gas bubbles. Bubble break-up and 
bubble coalescence are two opposing processes. Figure 4 shows how bubble break-up and bubble 
coalescence affect the bubble size distribution:  
 
 
Figure 4: Factors influencing the dynamic bubble size distribution in a bioreactor. (Nielsen and Villadsen, 1994)  
 
As Figure 4 shows, bubble break-up increases the number of gas bubbles. On the other hand, bubble 
coalescence decreases the number of gas bubbles. Agitation speed and gas flow rate are the primary 
influences on the efficiency of bubble breakup.  Bubble break-up occurs as a result of the competition 
between surface tension, which stabilizes the bubble, and inertial forces, which cause destabilization.

2.1.4 Residence time 
In gas-liquid systems, the mass transfer coefficient of the liquid phase (𝑘/) is a crucial factor for gas 
transfer. The mass transfer coefficient describes how quickly gas molecules diffuse from gas bubbles 
to the liquid phase. One parameter that affects this is the residence time, which describes how long 
gas bubbles remain in contact with the liquid. Residence time refers to the time a gas bubble is in 
contact with the liquid phase before rising to the headspace and leaving the system. In aerobic 
processes, the oxygen supply is crucial for the growth and metabolism of microorganisms. (Garcia-
Ochoa and Gomez, 2009)  
 
A sufficient residence time ensures that the gas bubbles remain in the liquid long enough for oxygen 
to be effectively transferred to the liquid. If the residence time is too short, the gas bubbles leave the 
liquid before sufficient mass transfer occurs, which can lead to oxygen limitation. On the other hand, 
a too long residence time can also be problematic. If the gas bubbles remain in the liquid for too long, 
it can result in foaming or unwanted accumulations of gas hold-up zones which can affect the 
efficiency of the bioreactor. Therefore, it is necessary to optimize the residence time to balance 
efficient oxygen transfer and avoid disruptions. The size of the bubbles (mentioned in the section 
above) plays a crucial role since small bubbles move slower than large bubbles and thus remain in 
the liquid for a longer time. The longer the gas bubbles remain in the liquid, the longer the oxygen 
has to dissolve. (Panjipour et al., 2021)

describes the volumetric gas flow passing through the liquid per unit cross-sectional area of the 
reactor and can be calculated as:

2.3  Fluid Properties  
 
The fluid properties play an important role in power consumption, bubble size, and consequently 
mass transfer within a STR. Key parameters such as viscosity and surface tension influence the 
movement of substances within the liquid, the rate at which they dissolve, and the efficiency of the 
mass transfer from one phase to another.  
 
Viscosity  
During fermentation, viscosity increases as a result of rising cell density and the production of 
extracellular compounds. Additionally, changes in microbial morphology can influence the 
rheological properties of the broth. These developments are commonly observed during fermentation 
and contribute to a gradual thickening of the medium. The resulting increase in viscosity of the liquid 
has a major impact on gas-liquid mass transfer, as the viscosity affects both the size of the gas bubbles, 
gas hold-up, and thus kLa. When the viscosity of the liquid increases, it causes the liquid boundary 
layer around the bubbles to become thicker, which increases the resistance to gas-liquid mass transfer. 
In liquids with high viscosity, the bubbles are generally larger compared to the bubble size in less 
viscous liquids. In addition, an increase in viscosity will dampen the turbulence in the liquid, which 
will give a more frequent tendency for bubble coalescence and a less intensive bubble breakup. As 
viscosity increases, gas-liquid mass transfer tends to become less efficient. This reduction in transfer 
efficiency can pose a significant limitation in aerobic fermentations, where a sufficient oxygen supply 
is critical. To counteract these effects, higher agitation speeds or increased gas flow rates are often 
applied, though these measures can increase energy consumption and mechanical stress on cells. 
Consequently, controlling viscosity is an important consideration in scaling and operating 
fermentations efficiently. (Mahiout and Vogelpohl, 1983) 
 
Surface tension  
Surface tension is also an important parameter in gas-liquid systems, as it affects bubble formation, 
size, and stability. It is a consequence of intermolecular cohesive forces and is the reason why small 
gas bubbles in water take up a spherical form. Surface tension acts to stabilize the bubble, maintaining 
the spherical shape.  
In pure aqueous systems, bubbles tend to spontaneously coalesce upon contact. However, the 
presence of salts affects the coalescence behavior of the liquid. With increasing salt concentration, 
the proportion of film formation during bubble collisions gradually increases and can approach 100%, 
thereby inhibiting coalescence. At the same time, higher salt concentrations lead to a monotonic 
decrease in bubble size. This inhibiting effect of salts is related to both their valence and the surface 
tension gradient to salt concentration. In fermentations, various salts are present in the medium as 
essential nutrients and buffering agents, and these can influence bubble behavior through their effect 
on surface tension. Furthermore, the presence of dissolved substances with coalescence-inhibiting 
properties can enhance mass transfer. This is primarily due to increased gas hold-up and a larger total 
bubble surface area, which promotes gas-liquid contact. (Firouzi et al., 2015; Srivastava and Mishra, 
2011)

2.4  Reactor Design  
Reactor design plays a crucial role in chemical and biochemical processes, significantly impacting 
the efficiency and quality of the product. In bioreactor design, the focus is on optimizing efficiency 
while ensuring that desired parameters are achieved at a low cost, resulting in a high-quality product. 
The type of reactor used depends on factors such as the source of cells, the production scale, and the 
product. Various types of reactors are designed. Examples are bubble column bioreactors, airlift 
bioreactors, wave bioreactors, and STR (Doran, 2013). For this project, the reactor type is a STR,

2.5  Mixing    
Mixing is a fundamental aspect of reactor operation, as it ensures proper distribution of gases, 
nutrients, and cells throughout the liquid phase. The most common method involves mechanical 
agitation using an impeller. The efficiency of mixing depends on several factors, including stirrer 
configuration, speed, and gas flow rate. These factors influence not only the flow patterns inside the 
tank but also the volumetric mass transfer coefficient. By optimizing mixing and gas dispersion, the 
oxygen transfer rate inside the reactor can be significantly improved.  
 
The following sections will describe how different flow patterns and gas dispersion dynamics impact 
overall mixing performance.  
 
2.5.1 Flow patterns  
In a stirred tank, both radial and axial flow can occur. Radial flow moves outward from the impeller 
toward the tank walls, while axial flow moves vertically, either upward or downward. Additionally, 
rotational flow, which circulates around the stirrer shaft, may also be present. Figure 9 shows typical 
flow patterns for radial and axial mixing (Zlokarnik, 2001):

3.1. Gas bubble size distribution 
Bubble size distribution (BSD) plays an important role in determining the efficiency of gas-liquid 
mass transfer in aerated STRs. Since oxygen transfer from the gas to the liquid phase is often the 
limiting step in aerobic processes, it is essential to understand the size and distribution of bubbles for 
optimal reactor performance. The Sauter mean diameter (𝑑2) is commonly used to characterize BSD 
and is particularly important in estimating the interfacial area available for mass transfer. (Wilkinson 
et al., 1993) 
 
Several techniques exist for measuring BSD, which can be broadly classified as non-intrusive and 
intrusive methods. One of the most widely used non-intrusive methods is high-speed photographic 
imaging. (Mahmood et al., 2023) employed this method to determine the Sauter mean diameter in 
water at different air flows in a 5.6 L reactor.

impacting the hydrodynamics of the reactor. However, this method has its limitations. One major

3.2. Gas hold-up  
Gas hold-up is a critical parameter in characterizing fluid dynamics and mixing in aerated STRs. It 
determines, together with the bubble size distribution, the interfacial area available for gas-liquid 
mass transfer, which is often the limiting factor in aerobic processes. As such, gas hold-up not only 
influences oxygen transfer but also plays a central role in the productivity of fermentation processes.  
Despite its importance, relatively little information is available on gas hold-up in actual fermentation 
broths. One major reason is that gas hold-up depends strongly on the physicochemical properties of 
the broth, such as ionic strength, viscosity, and biomass concentration. These properties vary not only 
between different microbial strains but also during fermentation. Moreover, gas hold-up is seldom 
measured in small-scale laboratory fermentations, where vessels are rarely completely filled and 
liquid levels fluctuate due to aeration. As a result, reliable experimental data under realistic 
fermentation conditions remain limited (Hofmeester, 1988).  
 
One of the most widely used and straightforward methods for determining global gas hold-up is the 
Volume expansion method. In this method, the liquid height with and without aeration is measured, 
and gas hold-up is calculated as a ratio between the volume with aeration and without.  
 
Figure 15: Measuring global gas hold-up. (a) Liquid height without aeration. (b) Liquid height with aeration. (c) 
Liquid height with aeration and foam layer (Hofmeester, 1988).  
(Karimi et al., 2013) employed the volume expansion method to investigate gas hold-up in a stirred 
tank bioreactor with a working volume of 1.77 L. Their study focused on comparing single and dual 
impeller configurations and demonstrated that the number of impellers has a significant impact on 
gas hold-up. Specifically, an increase in the number of impellers led to higher gas hold-up. 
Additionally, they observed that gas hold-up increases with increasing gas flow rate, consistent with 
trends reported in earlier studies. Although the method is simple to use and widely applied, it also has 
several limitations. The liquid surface is often unstable due to agitation, gas bubbles, and foam 
formation (especially in viscous media), making visual measurements of liquid height imprecise 
(Hofmeester, 1988).   
==PAGE:34==
 
 
Page 24 of 108 
To improve measurement precision, (Meng et al., 2002) developed a new inclined tube method 
(modified volume expansion method). In this technique, a transparent, U-shaped glass tube is 
mounted externally on the reactor to measure changes in pressure and liquid level caused by aeration. 
The U-bend prevents gas bubbles from entering the tube, resulting in more stable and accurate 
readings compared to direct visual observation. The method is particularly suitable for laboratory-
scale applications, where precise control and observation are required.  
 
While global gas hold-up provides an overall picture of gas in a reactor, gas distribution can vary 
significantly throughout the reactor volume. Local gas hold-up offers deeper insights into 
hydrodynamics, flow regimes, and mixing behavior within STRs. Several advanced techniques have 
been developed to measure local gas hold-up.  
 
One such method is the use of optical probes. A single-point optical fiber probe has been developed 
by (Mueller and Dudukovic, 2010) to accurately determine local gas hold-up under a range of 
operating pressures, temperatures, and fluid properties. The probe detects changes in refractive index 
as bubbles pass across its tip. However, it has been observed that probe orientation relative to flow 
direction in STRs can lead to underestimation of gas hold-up values, indicating that precise alignment 
is critical for accurate measurement.  
 
In this project, the volume expansion method is used to determine the global gas hold-up. This method 
is selected due to its simplicity and suitability for the experimental setup. Despite its limitations, it 
provides consistent data under the given experimental conditions and serves as a practical method for 
the determination of the global gas hold-up.  
 
 
3.3. Mixing time   
Mixing time is one of the most essential parameters when characterizing the performance of mixing 
processes in STR. The mixing time under unaerated conditions has been extensively studied and well-
documented in the literature. In contrast, data on mixing times under aerated conditions are relatively 
limited, because of the insufficient measurement methods. The presence of gas introduces more 
complex two-phase fluid dynamics, making studies more challenging. In aerated vessels, it has been 
reported that the mixing time can be both longer and shorter compared to unaerated. (Otomo et al., 
2003b)  
 
One of the earliest approaches to determining mixing time was using visual methods based on 
decolorization. (Blakebrough and Sambamurthy, 1966) applied this method to a 5 L aerated STR and 
observed an increase in mixing time under aerated conditions compared to unaerated, at constant 
impeller speed. However, the method was based on subjective observation with the naked eye, 
making the results difficult to interpret and reproduce.  
 
==PAGE:35==
 
 
Page 25 of 108 
To improve objectivity, (Einsele and Finn, 1980) utilized a pH probe to track the time required for 
neutralization following the addition of acid or base. They reported that increasing the gas flow rate 
at a fixed agitation speed led to longer mixing times. However, the accuracy of the results was 
compromised by the slow response time of the pH probe and its sensitivity to the presence of gas 
bubbles, which often distorted the readings.  
 
(Zhang et al., 2014) in their study “Effects of pH probe lag on bioreactor mixing time estimation”, 
systematically investigated the influence of probe response time on the accuracy of mixing time 
measurements. The experiments were conducted in a 3 L STR at three agitation speeds using a Mettler 
Toledo InPro3253 pH probe. Their results demonstrated that the lag introduced by the pH sensor 
could significantly distort the measured mixing time, not only in laboratory setups but also at pilot 
and manufacturing scales:  
 
Figure 16: Effect of probe response time on the measurement of mixing time at all bioreactor scales.(Zhang et al., 
2014). 
The study showed that at an apparent mixing time of 60 seconds, the lag could result in up to 15% 
error in the estimated value. When the mixing time was 40 seconds or less, the error increased with 
deviations exceeding 27%. These findings show the importance of considering response times when 
using sensors for experimental estimation of mixing time.  
Conductivity-based methods have also been explored as a more quantitative alternative. (Cooke et 
al., 1988; Pandit et al., 1989) measured mixing times under aerated conditions using a conductivity 
probe. Although the method provided numerical data, the reliability was significantly affected by 
bubble interference, as the gas bubbles disrupted the electrical contact between the electrodes and the 
liquid. A later study from 2003 introduced an improved version of this technique by enclosing the 
probe in a protective cage to shield the electrodes from bubble-induced disturbances. This 
modification allowed for more stable and consistent measurements under aerated conditions and 
==PAGE:36==
 
 
Page 26 of 108 
represents one of the more promising advancements in this area. (Otomo et al., 2003b; Pinelli et al., 
2001)  
More recently, researchers have begun to adopt advanced image analysis techniques to evaluate 
mixing times with higher precision. An example is a study by (Rosseburg et al., 2018) which 
investigated mixing in a 15,000 L acrylic glass reactor using a decolorization method based on 
phenolphthalein. Mixing was studied in water both with and without aeration. A CMOS camera 
operating at a sampling rate of 1 s-1 was used to capture the decolorization, enabling a quantitative 
assessment of the global mixing time. Figure 17 shows the determined global mixing times as a 
function of Reynolds number for different superficial gas velocities:  
 
Figure 17: Mixing time as a function of Reynolds number in the acrylic glass reactor for different superficial gas flow 
rates and fitted correlations. (Rosseburg et al., 2018)  
In addition to quantifying mixing, the study provided valuable visualization of flow patterns at a large 
scale. The results indicate the importance of the local inhomogeneities on a large scale.  
Similarly, (Fitschen et al., 2021) employed the decolorization method in combination with modern 
image acquisition techniques. The experiments were conducted with a working volume of 2.8 L of 
water in a STR without aeration. Automatic image evaluation was used to determine the global but 
also local mixing times. The representation of the mixing time was obtained by evaluating the gray-
scale value during the experiment for each pixel in the image. An example of the representation of 
the local mixing time distribution is shown in  Figure 18:

3.4. Summary  
Based on previous studies, it can be concluded that agitation speed and gas flow rate are key 
parameters influencing bubble size distribution, gas hold-up, and mixing time, all of which 
significantly affect process and product quality, as well as mass transfer efficiency. Based on findings 
from the literature, several questions have emerged and will be investigated in this project. However, 
some of these questions will remain open for further investigation.  
 
1. Is it possible to determine the local mixing time in a 180 L STR both with and without aeration 
using image analysis? Furthermore, it is investigated if the mixing time results obtained from 
image analysis align with those determined by the pH method.  
 
2. How is mixing time and gas hold-up affected by agitation speed and gas flow rate? The mixing 
time is determined both with and without aeration.  
 
3. How does the addition of MgSO4·7H2O affect mixing time and gas hold-up? 
 
4. How does the flow in the STR affect mixing time in different compartments?

4.2  Determination of mixing time 
To enable the determination of local mixing times through real-time measurements, an image analysis 
method is developed and subsequently validated. The pH method is used for validation, as the 
experimental setup already involves pH changes within the tank. However, due to the known 
limitations of pH sensors, particularly their slow response time, reported by (Zhang et al., 2014) and 
shown in Figures 23 and 24, the accuracy of this validation approach is limited. Therefore, the 
conductivity method is briefly employed for further validation of the image analysis method.  
 
The experiments conducted with image analysis and the pH method are described in the following, 
and the conductivity method is presented in section 4.2.3.  
 
In the experiments with image analysis and pH methods, 180 mL of bromothymol blue is added to a 
180 L STR to enable observation of a color change. Bromothymol blue is yellow at pH < 6, and blue 
at pH > 7.6 (VWR Chemicals, n.d.). Before the experiment starts, 2 mL NaOH (2 M) is added to the 
tank, so the pH increases to 8.5, and the solution gets blue. The experiment begins when 4 mL of HCl 
(2 M) is added from the top of the tank through a small tube extending to the bottom. Due to high 
water pressure, a pump is required to discharge the HCl at the bottom. The flow rate of acid added to 
the tank is 1.0 mL/s.  The pH is measured with two pH probes, one at the top of the tank and one at 
the bottom. The color change is recorded with a Phantom high-speed camera. It enables the 
determination of both global and local mixing times through automatic image evaluation. The tank is 
divided into three sections (top, middle, and bottom), as it is not possible to capture the entire tank in 
a single recording. A Chemical Risk Assessment for the conducted experiments is shown in Appendix 
B.  
 
In sections 4.2.1 and 4.2.2, the two methods are further described.  
 
4.2.1 pH method    
The pH method is used to determine the global mixing time. For the measurements, a Mettler Toledo 
pH probe (InPro3253i SG/120) is placed at the top of the tank, and a Mettler Toledo pH sensor 
(InPro3100i/UD/120) is positioned at the bottom. The setup allows monitoring of pH changes both 
in the top and bottom of the tank, allowing determination of the mixing time when acid is added. The 
mixing time is defined as the time required for the pH in the top and bottom of the tank to reach 
within ±5% of the final steady state value. It is measured as the time from the moment a drop in pH 
is observed at the bottom of the tank until the pH of both sensors stabilizes.  
 
Principle of a pH sensor  
A pH sensor determines the hydrogen ion concentration in a solution, indicating its acidity or 
alkalinity. It consists of a pH-sensitive glass electrode and a reference electrode, which together 
generate a voltage corresponding to the pH value. The type of pH electrode used in this project, 
including its internal and reference system, is illustrated in Figure 21:

4.2.2  Decolorization method  
The decolorization method is used to determine both the global and local mixing time in the tank. 
The disadvantage of the decolorization method is the subjective determination of the mixing time. 
Therefore, a high-speed camera is used, allowing for automatic image evaluation to determine the 
mixing time.   
 
Image Representation  
There are two types of images: monochrome images and color images. A monochrome image has 
gray-scale values with the lowest number being black and the highest number being white. In color 
(RGB) images, each pixel consists of three values representing red, green, and blue. In this project, 
the images are monochrome. Therefore, only monochrome images will be described.  
 
In digital image analysis, an image is fundamentally a matrix of numerical values, where each value 
represents the light intensity captured by a camera sensor at a specific location. These values are 
referred to as ‘pixel values’, and the full image can be described in a matrix with rows and columns 
corresponding to the vertical and horizontal axes of the image. Figure 25 shows a typical matrix for 
a monochrome image:  
 
Figure 25: Matrix for a monochrome image. Inspired by (Phantom Cameras - Vision Research, 2024).

resolution. For a video composed of images, this results in a matrix of images, where each image 
itself consists of a matrix. The number of grayscale levels depends on the bit-depth of the image. Bit-
depth is the number of possible gray-scale values or light levels in the image. Bit-depth is given by 
two to the power of n (Bit-depth = 28), where 𝑛 is the number of ‘bits per pixel’. For a 12-bit image, 
the bit-depth is 4096. It gives 4095 different possibilities of gray-scale values. (Phantom Cameras -

5.1.1  Global mixing time image analysis method    
The mixing process from blue (basic) to yellow (acidic) was recorded with a Phantom high-speed 
camera. Based on recorded cine files, Equation 20 was used to normalize the average grayscale

6. Discussion 
 
6.1 Validation of image analysis method  
The results obtained for global mixing times using both image analysis and the pH method exhibit 
similar overall trends. However, the mixing times measured with the pH method (Figure 38) are 
significantly higher than the mixing times determined with the image analysis method (Figure 36). 
Across the operational settings, the measured mixing time differs from 10.1 to 42.9 seconds. This 
discrepancy prompts a critical assessment of which method provides the most accurate representation 
of the actual mixing time within the STR. It remains uncertain whether the actual mixing time is 
better reflected by the values obtained through image analysis, those measured using the pH method, 
or whether it lies somewhere in between.  
 
To further validate the image analysis method, additional experiments were conducted using the 
conductivity method (Figure 39), which is a well-established method in the literature (Ascanio, 2015). 
Table 7 presents mixing times at 150 RPM without aeration, determined by image analysis, pH, and 
conductivity methods:    
 
Table 7: Mixing times determined with image analysis, pH, and conductivity method at 150 RPM without airflow. 
 
 
 
 
The results in the table above show good agreement between image analysis and the conductivity 
method, while the pH method deviates significantly. The high standard deviation observed in the 
conductivity measurements may be attributed to the probe limitations, particularly its unsuitability 
for continuous data acquisition (Section 4.2.3). Nonetheless, based on these findings, the image 
analysis method appears to provide reliable measurements of the global mixing time.  
 
Mixing time under aerated conditions was also investigated using the conductivity method. However, 
this method becomes unreliable under aerated conditions (Figure 40). The probe signal is heavily 
affected by gas bubbles, which cause erratic fluctuations indistinguishable from actual changes in ion 
concentration. These observations are consistent with previous findings reported by (Pandit et al., 
1989),who were similarly unable to determine mixing time using the conductivity method in the 
presence of air. Enclosing the probe in a protective cage, as proposed by (Otomo et al., 2003), may 
help reduce bubble-induced interference and enhance measurement stability under aerated conditions.  
Each of the applied methods has its own advantages and limitations, which must be considered when 
evaluating their reliability and applicability. For the pH method, one major drawback is its sensitivity

6.2  Global mixing time  
The trends observed in the results of the global mixing time (Figure 36) are in good agreement with 
those reported in Figure 17, in which (Rosseburg et al., 2018) determined the mixing time in a 15 m3 
STR. As only a limited number of data points are determined, additional experiments will be 
necessary to confirm the observed trend. However, the initial results show the same tendency.  
 
Since fermentations are typically conducted under aerated conditions, the buoyancy-driven flow 
induced by the gas phase must be considered. To characterize the flow regimes (Figure 11), the gas 
flow number (Equation 13) and Froude number (Equation 14) are calculated for the different agitation 
speeds and gas flow rates applied in this project:   
 
Figure 51: Flow map showing the transition between different gas-liquid flow regimes in a STR. Green, orange, and 
blue points indicate the gas flow number and Froude number for the experimental conditions used in the project.   
 
As shown in Figure 51, the experimental conditions (the green, orange, and blue points) result in 
different flow regimes, including flooding, loading, and complete dispersion. The flow map also 
illustrates that regime transitions can occur during scale-up, even at constant power input and gas 
flow rate per volume. While Figure 51 was developed for a STR with H/D = 3, the tank used in this 
study has a higher aspect ratio (H/D = 3.8), which may explain that the shortest mixing times in this 
study were observed under flooding according to the flow map.  
 
To assess whether flooding was present under certain conditions, visual observations were made at a 
gas flow rate of 0.25 vvm at agitation speeds of 75, 100, and 150 RPM:  
==PAGE:71==
 
 
Page 61 of 108 
 
Figure 52: Different flow regimes observed at 75 RPM, 100 RPM, and 150 RPM at a gas flow rate of 0.25 vvm.  
Although interpretation is inherently subjective and better supported by real-time observation, distinct 
differences were observed (Figure 52). By changing the agitation speed, a trend similar to Figure 10 
is observed. At 75 RPM, the impeller appeared unable to effectively disperse the gas, with bubbles 
rising almost undisturbed, indicating a flooding regime. At 100 RPM, the impellers captured and 
redistributed the gas, pushing it downward, which suggests operation closer to the loading regime. At 
150 RPM, vigorous gas dispersion and full entrainment beneath the impellers are observed, indicating 
the complete dispersion regime. At a gas flow rate of 0.25 vvm, the shortest mixing time was observed 
at an agitation speed of 100 RPM (Table 3). This indicates that the shortest mixing time is achieved 
within the loading regime.    
 
According to (Rosseburg et al., 2018), large-scale bioprocesses often operate in the transition between 
the loading and flooding regime, where mixing behavior becomes highly sensitive. In this region, 
increasing agitation speed does not necessarily shorten mixing time. In some cases, it may reduce 
large-scale circulations caused by rising bubbles, thereby prolonging the mixing time. This underlies 
the need for careful regime control during scale-up and supports the nonlinear behavior in the 
determined mixing times (Figure 36).  
 
The global mixing time results show that the shortest mixing time is not necessarily achieved at the 
highest agitation speed (Table 3). For a gas flow rate of both 0.39 vvm and 0.5 vvm, the fastest mixing 
time is achieved at the lowest agitation speed used in the experiments. However, increased agitation 
speed leads to a higher gas hold-up (Table 5), indicating a greater volume of dispersed gas within the 
==PAGE:72==
 
 
Page 62 of 108 
STR. This enhances oxygen availability and is expected to improve kLa, which is critical for aerobic 
fermentation processes. These observations emphasize the need to balance competing process 
demands: While rapid mixing helps minimize concentration gradients, sufficient oxygen transfer 
must also be maintained. The optimal agitation speed should therefore ensure both effective mixing 
and adequate oxygen supply, while also considering factors such as energy consumption and shear 
stress.

6.5  Effect of MgSO4·7H2O 
Experiments were conducted in both pure DI water and DI water with 0.05 M MgSO4·7H2O to 
investigate how gas hold-up and mixing time are affected by MgSO4·7H2O addition. The results 
indicate that the addition of MgSO4·7H2O affects gas hold-up (Figures 49 and 50), while mixing time 
remains unaffected under the same operating conditions (Figure 46).  
 
When MgSO4·7H2O is added to DI water, it suppresses bubble coalescence. At the concentration 
applied in this project, the solution exceeds the transition threshold, defined as the point at which 50% 
of the bubbles exhibit coalescence (Firouzi et al., 2015b). As the presence of MgSO4·7H2O reduces 
bubble coalescence, the average bubble diameter consequently decreases. As described in section 
2.1.4. smaller bubbles rise more slowly than larger ones and thus remain in the liquid for a longer 
time. This extended residence time leads to an increased gas hold-up and explains the observed 
change in gas hold-up when MgSO4·7H2O is introduced. Similar effects have been reported by 
(Ribeiro and Mewes, 2007), who demonstrated that the presence of electrolytes in water brings an 
increase in the gas hold-up.

Since no change in mixing time was observed with the addition of MgSO4·7H2O, this indicates that 
as long as the flow in the tank remains turbulent (Section 4.1), variations in gas hold-up and bubble 
diameter do not affect the mixing time under the conditions used in this project.    
 
6.6  Tap water challenges  
The resistance to pH changes in tap water, shown in Figure 48, is primarily due to the presence of 
bicarbonate (𝐻𝐶𝑂3
D) ions in tap water, which act as a buffer system. The reaction below illustrates 
the buffering of hydroxide ions: (Casey Burnett, 2018)  
 
        Reaction 3:  
𝐻𝐶𝑂3
D + 𝑂𝐻D →𝐶𝑂3
4D + 𝐻4𝑂 
 
 
This reaction reduces the effective concentration of OH- in the solution, limiting the rise in pH upon 
base addition. A similar buffering effect is observed when acid is added to the tank. Bicarbonate 
neutralizes protons via Reaction 4:  
 
    Reaction 4:  
𝐻𝐶𝑂3
D + 𝐻K →𝐶𝑂4 + 𝐻4𝑂 
 
 
This reaction consumes hydrogen ions, thereby mitigating the pH drop.  
 
To reach the bromothymol blue transition range (from pH>7.6 to pH<6.0), a total of 200 mL 2 M 
HCl must be added. Even at higher acid concentration, substantial volumes are required to overcome 
the buffer system.  
 
However, the addition of such large volumes introduces challenges. Notably, it significantly increases 
the total liquid volume, potentially affecting the mixing time. In the setup used in this project, acid is 
injected through a pump to the bottom of the tank, capable of delivering only 1 mL per second. 
Consequently, the acid addition may last several minutes, much longer than the expected mixing time 
(25-110 seconds). This temporal mismatch prevents accurate measurement of mixing time using the 
decolorization method.  
 
Furthermore, when large amounts of acid and base are added, the overall volume increases 
significantly, which can potentially influence the mixing time. For safety reasons, it was not feasible 
to introduce acid from the top of the tank, even though this approach could have allowed for a faster 
addition of acid.  
 
Due to the factors mentioned above, it was not possible to determine the mixing time in tap water 
using the pH method and the image analysis method, which represent the primary methods applied 
in this project. Due to the significant buffering capacity of tap water, alternative methods may be 
more suitable for determining mixing time in tap water.

7. Conclusion  
The project aimed to investigate whether image analysis can be used to determine both local and 
global mixing times in a 180 L STR, both with and without aeration. Experiments were conducted at 
agitation speeds of 75, 100, and 150 RPM, and gas flow rates of 0, 0.25, 0.39, and 0.5 vvm in DI 
water with the addition of 0.05 M MgSO4·7H2O. Some experiments were also conducted in pure DI 
water, and the method was tested in tap water. The most significant conclusions are listed below:  
 
1. All experiments were conducted using a color change from blue to yellow (base to acid), as 
the reverse transition can prolong mixing time measurements due to the formation of H2CO3 
and its reaction with NaOH under aerated conditions (Reaction 2).  
 
2. Global mixing times measured with the pH method showed a similar trend to those obtained 
by image analysis, but significantly higher mixing times were observed with the pH method 
across all operating conditions (Figures 36 and 38). The conductivity method showed good 
agreement with image analysis (Table 7).  
 
3. Based on the global mixing time results, increasing agitation speed reduced mixing time under 
non-aerated conditions. Aeration decreased mixing time across all agitation speeds. (Figure 
36)  
 
4. Local mixing time distributions were successfully visualized with the image analysis method. 
Gas accumulation was observed at the top of the tank during aeration (Figure 44), and at 150 
RPM and 0.5 vvm, air seems to recirculate around the impellers (Figure 45). 
 
5. The middle compartment showed the longest mixing time compared with the mixing times of 
the top and bottom compartments (Figure 41). This is attributed to the limited axial flow of 
the Rushton turbines.  
 
6. The addition of 0.05 M MgSO4·7H2O increased gas hold-up (Figures 46 and 47) without 
affecting mixing time (Figure 46).  
 
7. Determining gas hold-up in pure DI water and DI water with 0.05 M MgSO4·7H2O by the 
volume expansion method, the results show increased gas hold-up with increased agitation 
and gas flow rate. (Figures 49 and 50)  
 
8. Neither image analysis nor pH method proved reliable in tap water due to bicarbonate 
buffering, which hindered detectable pH and color changes (Figure 48).

8. Future work  
The image analysis method developed in this project has demonstrated reliable results for determining 
both local and global mixing times in a 180 L STR under various operating conditions. However, the 
experiments in the project were limited. To get a more comprehensive understanding of how agitation 
speed influences mixing time under aerated conditions, additional experiments are necessary. This 
project applied a maximum power input of 0.32 kW/m3, whereas industrial aerobic fermentations 
typically operate within the range of 1.0 kW/m3 to 1.5 kW/m3. Experiments at higher power per 
volume would therefore improve the relevance of the findings to industrial-scale processes.  
 
In the project, it was observed that the middle compartment exhibits the longest mixing time 
compared to the top and bottom compartments of the STR. As impeller geometry has a strong 
influence on flow patterns, future work should investigate how alternative impeller types affect flow, 
mixing efficiency, and gas distribution within the STR. 
 
It is a well-known phenomenon that viscosity tends to increase during aerobic bioprocesses, among 
other due to increasing biomass or viscous product formation, and it can significantly affect mixing 
behavior. To better replicate industrial fermentations, experiments with higher viscosity must 
therefore be conducted. Simulating this change using viscosity-enhancing agents would allow for 
examination of how increased viscosity affects mixing time, flow patterns, and gas hold-up in the 
reactor. The image analysis method used in the project relies on a pH-induced color change. It is 
therefore essential to select a ‘thickening agent’ that meets the following criteria:  
 
- 
The substance must be pH stable, so that its viscosity remains unaffected by pH changes 
 
- 
It must not react with either NaOH or HCl.  
 
These requirements are crucial to maintain accurate and reliable mixing time measurements at 
increased viscosity.  
 
In addition, combining mixing time analysis with bubble size distributions (BSD) could provide 
valuable insight into the gas-liquid mass transfer. Using a shadowgraphic endoscope, it would be 
possible to measure the BSD at various positions within the 180 L STR. Figure 55 shows the STR 
with openings on the side, where the shadowgraphic endoscope can be inserted to enable local 
measurements of the gas bubbles.  
==PAGE:78==
 
 
Page 68 of 108 
 
Figure 55: (A) The STR equipped with side openings (blue caps) at various heights, where the endoscope can be 
inserted. (B) Schematic illustration of the shadowgraphic measurement setup. (C) An image captured with the 
Shadowgraphic endoscope, showing the gas bubbles and their motion within the liquid phase.  
Based on the observations presented in Previous work Section 3.1, it would be relevant to investigate 
the following questions:  
- 
How is the BSD affected by different measurement positions within the 180 L STR with a 
liquid height of 1.5 meters?  
- 
Is there a correlation between local mixing times and local BSDs?  
 
In this project, gas hold-up was only determined globally, assuming uniform distribution across the 
entire volume. However, the local mixing time distributions indicate air accumulation at the top of 
the tank and around the impellers, suggesting that local gas hold-up is significantly higher in these 
regions. For future work, it would be of interest to investigate local gas hold-up inside the STR and 
examine how it influences both mixing time and bubble size distribution. The local gas hold-up can 
be measured using the shadowgraphic endoscope (Figure 55B). By analyzing captured images (Figure 
55C), the local gas hold-up can be estimated based on the ratio of black (air) to white (liquid) pixels 
in the image. This could be compared to the mixing times determined in the top, middle, and bottom 
compartments of the reactor. Such an investigation could provide a more comprehensive 
understanding of the interplay between gas dispersion, flow dynamics, and mixing efficiency.
