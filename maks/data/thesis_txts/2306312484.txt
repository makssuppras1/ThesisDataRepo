Abstract 
 
Due to the fluctuating character of energy produced from renewable sources, a large 
penetration of renewables in the energy supply is expected to lead to important issues in 
the coming years, with respects to security of supply and balance of the heat and power 
grids. One of the proposals formulated as a solution to this problem is demand-side 
management, which implies a larger flexibility of energy loads in order to adapt 
production fluctuations. 
This thesis focuses on demand-side management potential for the heating grid in 
residential buildings. In this work, the possibility to increase the flexibility provided to 
the heat network through specific building design is investigated. The role of the 
different building parts in thermal flexibility is assessed through a parameter variation 
on a building model. The different building designs are subjected to perturbations in 
heating schedule and flexibility is evaluated through four different indicators. 
The thermal flexibility achieved is found to be a result of heat storage within the 
building structure, but to be strongly influenced by outdoor conditions and occupants’ 
activity. The thermal transmittance of the building envelope appears to be the main 
factor influencing thermal flexibility. The importance of window design, in particular the 
size, U-value and orientation, is underlined due to its critical influence on solar gains and 
heat losses. It is eventually observed that the building thermal mass has a secondary 
influence on the evaluated indicators: its variation only affects thermal flexibility if the 
thermal resistance of the envelope is sufficient. 
By improving the investigated building design according to the obtained results, a 
stabilization of indoor temperature is observed leading to better indoor comfort, also 
when assessed in response to a periodic perturbation in heating schedule.

Chapter 1 
Introduction 
1.1 
Framework 
The European Union has set itself the goal to reduce its greenhouse gas emissions by 80 
to 95% in 2050 with respects to their 1990 level (European Commission). In order to 
achieve this goal, different scenarios have been evaluated in the Energy Roadmap 2050 
[1], which all conclude to the necessity of a rapid and massive shift of part of the energy 
production towards renewable energy sources. The share of these sources should 
represent at least 55% of the gross energy consumption in 2050. 
One of the major challenges of the next decades is therefore to handle an increased 
penetration in the energy grid from renewable sources such as wind or solar power, 
which production is inflexible and highly dependent on weather conditions, without 
jeopardizing the security of supply. 
In the last years, a lot of attention has been given to the notion of demand side 
management as a solution to this issue. This concept was first introduced in the 1980s 
and qualified by Clark Gellings in 1985 [2] as the possibility to influence the end users’ 
load profile in order to serve the production side. Making the energy demand more 
flexible would permit to adapt production fluctuations and thus guarantee a better grid 
security. Among the demand side management strategies are energy savings policies, but 
also strategies aiming at shaving demand peaks or displacing consumption in time in 
order to avoid imbalances between demand and supply. 
According to the International Energy Agency, the building sector represents one third 
of the global energy consumption, including both heat and electricity [3]. This sector is 
required to improve its energy efficiency in the near future, moving towards low-energy 
or zero-energy buildings, comprising decentralized renewable energy production [4]. 
Demand side management in grid-connected buildings would thus permit to reduce the 
energy footprint of the building stock, while providing flexibility to the energy grid as a 
whole [5]. For this goal to be achieved, buildings need to be integrated in a larger 
network which energy demand can be controlled via double-flow information between 
the utilities and the production units, namely a smart grid. 
1.2 
EnergyLab Nordhavn 
EnergyLab Nordhavn is a smart city research project based in Nordhavn district, in the 
north part of Copenhagen. The project runs from 2015 to 2019 and is integrated in the 
general urban development plan of Nordhavn, which is expected to host and provide 
work for 40,000 new occupants within the next 50 years. The project gathers several 
companies and research groups and aims at making part of Nordhavn district a full-scale 
demonstration neighbourhood regarding energy efficiency and intelligent city operation. 
The public description of the project states: “The project focuses on a cost-effective 
future smart energy system that integrates multiple energy infrastructures (electricity, 
thermal, transportation) and provides an intelligent control of subsystems and

components – providing necessary flexibility for efficient utilisation of renewable energy” 
[6]. Several questions are being investigated and experiments are carried out on-site. One 
of the focuses is the optimisation of electricity supply, transportation and consumption, 
via new proposals for grid design and the implementation of demand control systems 
with the aim of a larger adaptability of appliances and HVAC systems to the grid

The present study is linked to the activity of the Working Package 3 entitled “Smart

1.3 
Problem statement and goals 
This Master’s thesis focuses on the design phase of nearly zero energy buildings (NZEB). 
Indeed, the need for a building to provide flexibility to the grid should be addressed in 
the earliest phases of the process. The structure of the edifice itself can act as a thermal 
storage system, while the heat diffusion system, as well as the ventilation system used, 
have their role to play in the way energy is used during the building operation and in 
the part of it that can be displaced in time according to the grid requirements. 
The goal of this study is to understand how a building’s design features can influence its 
contribution to the flexibility of the energy system in which it is integrated. 
Focus will be set on passive flexibility strategies, in particular heat storage in the 
building structure. A parameter variation will be carried out on a model of a dwelling, 
investigating the role of the different building components, their dimensions, materials, 
position, in the flexibility potential of the building regarding its heat load. 
The main outcomes of this thesis will be the following: 
• 
A set of indicators quantifying heat flexibility in a building relatively to indoor 
comfort and heating demand; 
• 
An understanding of the heat storage processes in a building and a comparison of 
the role of the different building components in this process, thus in flexibility; 
• 
An assessment of the impact of external factors, such as outdoor conditions and 
occupants’ behaviour, on flexibility.

Chapter 2 
Background 
2.1 
Smart cities 
In 2014, 54% of the world population was living in cities, and this share is expected to 
reach 66% by 2050 [7]. UN Habitat estimated that urban areas represent 75% of the 
global energy consumption, and it has been shown that energy demand in cities grows 
faster than their population [8] – this increasing energy consumption leading to more 
greenhouse gas emissions in the atmosphere and an acceleration of global warming. 
In the last decades, sustainable development principles have been introduced in city 
planning. According to the United Nations, a sustainable city must ensure social and 
economic development as well as a thorough environment management, in a context of 
fair urban governance [9]. These four pillars are supposed to guide the growth of cities 
across the world, leading to different actions depending on their development level and 
their history. 
At the forehead of sustainable city models, the concept of smart city was born. 
According to the Smart Cities Council [10], a smart city “uses information and 
communication technology 
(ICT) 
to 
enhance 
its 
livability, 
workability 
and 
sustainability” by “collecting, communicating and crunching” data. All aspects of city 
management are complemented and possibly optimized by interaction with ICT: 
transportation, energy, waste, domestic appliances, health [11]. Broader definitions are 
reviewed by Giffinger [12], and organised in six areas: smart economy, smart mobility, 
smart environment, smart people, smart living and smart governance. Similarly, Caragliu 
et al. [13] define as smart a city where “investments in human and social capital, 
traditional (transport) and modern (ICT) communication infrastructure fuel sustainable 
economic growth and a high quality of life, with a wise management of natural resources, 
through participatory governance”, and considers that the last years have included in the 
concept of smart city much more than the simple integration of information technology. 
As highlighted by De Jong et al. [14], the expression of “smart city” has been largely 
mixed with a multitude of other appellations such as “knowledge city”, “eco-city” or even 
“sustainable city”. Besides adding several dimensions to well-being in an urban 
environment, this confusion in terms highlights the vision of information technology as a 
solution for achieving environmental sustainability. 
2.2 
Energy flexibility – definition and mechanisms 
2.2.1 
Energy security of supply and demand side management 
One of the key challenges in the development of smart cities is the optimization of 
energy networks. In Denmark, the Energy Strategy 2050 aims at a fossil-free energy 
sector by 2050. In the report published by the Danish government in 2011 [15], the 
means of this strategy are explained, including an increasing penetration of renewable 
energy technologies and a high electrification of the economy, in particular the 
5 
 
==PAGE:20==
Chapter 2 
Background 
 
transportation sector. The unpredictability of energy sources such as wind turbines or 
photovoltaic cells therefore induces an important challenge in terms of security of supply: 
how can the energy networks ensure stable supply to all consumers regardless of the 
variations in consumption and production levels? 
Since energy production cannot be fully controlled, the demand profile has to be known 
and optimised: this idea is called demand side management (DSM) and is one of the key 
concepts to be dealt with in smart cities. DSM is described by Gellings [2] as the notion 
of influencing the customer’s use of electricity in ways that will produce desired changes 
in the load shape, namely changes in the time pattern and level of the load. 
Energy demand side management is enabled by an intensive knowledge of the status of 
both the grid and the appliances themselves – provided by ICT – and an ability to 
control them. This second part of the process can have several drivers. Direct control via 
smart devices linked to the grid is one of the options (applied to domestic devices such 
as washing machines or refrigerators, or to electrical vehicles). [16] Another possibility is 
the introduction of incentive mechanisms, in particular driven by a demand-based, 
dynamic price for electricity with lower power prices during low-load hours, encouraging 
customers to shift their consumption from peak hours to off-peak hours [17]. 
Several strategies can be adopted to achieve this goal [2]: among them, reduction of the 
overall load (energy savings), reduction of the peak load (peak shaving), displacement of 
the peak load to low-load hours (load shifting) or increased flexibility of the load so that 
it can respond to production fluctuations (flexible load shape). While the first two rely 
on a limitation of energy use, the last two imply a change in load schedule. The rest of 
this work will assume that no more energy consumption reduction can be achieved and 
that the challenge lies in the displacement of part of the load from one period to the 
other. This strategy, used either to reduce demand peaks (load shifting) or to adapt 
production fluctuations (flexible load shape), will in the following sections be called 
flexible load operation of buildings, or simply flexibility strategies. 
2.2.2 
Electrical and thermal flexibility in buildings 
When speaking about buildings, two forms of flexibility have to be distinguished: 
electrical flexibility and thermal flexibility. Electrical flexibility is the most studied and 
arises from serious issues, due to the high variability of electricity production from 
renewable sources and the risks of grid imbalance [18]. Demand side management 
relatively to electricity therefore relies on electrical savings, on different forms of 
electrical storage and on changes in operation schedules of appliances. Thermal flexibility 
is the ability to reduce or shift thermal loads, namely heating, cooling and domestic hot 
water loads supplied by non-electrical sources, typically through district heating. Heat 
plants such as the CHP plant that is to be used in Nordhavn District don’t present the 
same unpredictability as power sources; therefore, match between supply and demand is 
granted in normal operation. Lowering and flattening the heat demand curve mainly 
benefits the plant operation: it allows a more constant operation schedule and can on the 
long run permit to reduce the plant capacity.

==PAGE:21==

However, thermal flexibility can be a solution to solve electrical load shifting challenges. 
Indeed, on a longer term, the aim of EnergyLab Nordhavn is to couple heat and 
electricity power production in order to guarantee a better global security of supply. One 
of the means is to equip buildings with heat pumps or electrical heaters that can replace 
or complement the district heating system in case of surplus in the electricity 
production: the extra electricity would be used for heating purposes, which relieves the 
grid and permits a lower production from the CHP plant [6]. Therefore, it becomes 
essential to adapt the heating schedule to the electrical grid balance, and try to shift 
both thermal and electrical loads to periods of low electrical consumption. This concept 
is called Dual Demand side management and has for instance been assessed by Müller 
through a simulation of a city district in Germany [19]. 
Information about the power balance has to be retrieved several hours in advance so that 
coordinated instruction can be given to the buildings to bring forward or to delay the 
planned energy use. The energy production and consumption of the district have to be 
known with accuracy a number of hours in advance. 
The EnergyLab project includes forecasting of wind power production based on weather 
data; it must therefore be possible to predict to a certain extent the expected energy 
production. But trying to anticipate demand is likely to be a more reliable approach 
than forecasting production: indeed, residential power and heat consumption follow a 
daily pattern, which can be predicted with some certainty on the basis of collected data 
(depending on the season, the location and the day of the week). Thus, an important 
objective for flexible operation of buildings is to “flatten” the residential demand curve, 
by moving part of the peak consumption to the off-peak hours (load shifting). In this 
way, important imbalances between renewable electricity production and global demand 
could be avoided. The second level of flexible building operation is to adapt consumption 
to forecasted variations of renewable power production (flexible load shape), which 
requires a more important adaptability to change in the appliances’ functioning. 
Both these objective can be achieved by several means. Some loads are flexible in time; 
they can therefore be displaced either through an automatic control taking orders from 
the grid, or by the user: incentive mechanisms, in particular differentiated price systems, 
exist that prove to have an effect on load shifting [20]. On the contrary, some loads are 
not flexible in time, which means that the corresponding energy load has to be retrieved 
from the grid when convenient but stored and used at the planned moment. In the rest 
of this work, focus will be set on this second category of loads. 
2.2.3 
Energy storage 
For continuous energy needs such as heating, cooling or ventilation, limiting the energy 
intake from the grid during certain hours implies the use of a storage system able to 
discharge energy during this critical time without directly creating a load on the grid.  
Storage systems can be found in different forms: thermal storage (the most widely used 
system being the hot water tank), storage of electricity (for example in batteries) or 
conversion of electricity into heat (through heat pumps for instance). Heier [21] 
distinguishes two categories of storage processes: active and passive. Passive storage

==PAGE:22==

systems concern exclusively thermal storage and are defined by their ability to naturally 
charge up or discharge according to the temperature difference between the storage 
medium and its surroundings, whereas active storage systems are activated or 
deactivated by devices such as pumps or fans, or by a control signal (in batteries for 
example). 
2.2.3.1 
Passive systems 
De Gracia et al. [22] differentiates two passive heat storage techniques: sensible heat 
storage and latent heat storage. 
Sensible heat storage refers to the capacity of a material to retain heat after being 
subjected to a temperature gradient. The quantity of energy stored, the starting point 
and duration of delivery depend on the thermodynamic properties of the material as well 
as the temperature evolution of the surroundings. An application is the use of the 
building thermal mass as short-duration heat storage. Indeed, some construction 
materials such as concrete show a high storage potential: the floors, internal and external 
walls could therefore absorb thermal energy during heating periods and release it when 
the air temperature gets lower, which opens interesting opportunities in terms of 
reduction of energy peaks by shifting heating loads. Prospects of heat storage in thermal 
mass will be reviewed in detail in Section 2.3. 
The idea of using latent energy as a storage means has become increasingly popular 
among researchers and is very well documented in literature. When a solid material 
becomes liquid, it absorbs some energy (latent heat) that can be delivered again if the 
material gets back to solid state. The same phenomenon happens during evaporation and 
condensation processes. Phase change can therefore be considered as a form of heat 
storage, and could have an interesting regulation function in a building: phase change 
materials (PCMs) could act as a cooling system by absorbing extra heat and as a heating 
system by releasing it when the indoor temperature drops [23]. Applying this concept to 
energy storage in buildings nevertheless creates a number of constraints. The material to 
be used needs to be adapted to the conditions of the building: its phase change must 
occur around the same temperature range that is likely to be found indoors, and heat 
intake and restitution must be ensured around the desired setpoints. Some studies focus 
on applications in buildings where PCMs are included in the walls; the phase changes 
that are examined are from solid to liquid (fusion) and from liquid to solid 
(solidification). Kuznik et al. [24] draws up a list of the PCMs that have been studied for 
integration in the building envelope: among them are organic materials such as paraffins, 
fatty acids and polyethylene glycol, and inorganic materials (salt hydrates). One of the 
main challenges is the containment of liquid substances in the building components. The 
three most widely spread techniques are simple impregnation of a porous material with 
the PCM (with the risk of some leakages), encapsulation of PCM into polymer micro-
capsules that will then be included in the building material, and creation of a building 
material by solidifying a mixture of PCM and a chosen supporting material (this 
technique is called shape stabilization) [22] [24]. Even though this topic has raised a lot 
of research interest in the last years, the industrial applications are at their very 
beginning.

2.2.3.2 
Active systems 
The most common active storage system known is the electrical battery. On a small 
scale, installed into domestic electronic devices, it allows a large mobility during the use 
and makes the impact on the grid only punctual (the charging moment). On a large 
scale, the possibility of using large domestic batteries able to store energy for a 
household for several hours has been studied for many years, but their cost efficiency and 
their environmental impact are discussed [25], and the effect of repetitive charging and 
discharging on their lifetime is also found to be an issue [26]. 
Batteries included in electric vehicles (EVs) are situated on an intermediate scale. With 
regards to the volume that this market is expected to represent in the future (over 35 
million vehicles in the world in 2022 according to the International Energy Agency [27]) 
and to the fact that the actual use of the vehicles represents a small fraction of the day, 
these batteries offer a certain potential for grid balancing. While connected to the grid, 
they could indeed be used as a buffer to prevent imbalances between production and 
demand: this concept is known as Vehicle-to-Grid technology (V2G). The coupling 
between the EV fleet and the grid can be achieved via an optimisation algorithm; 
different solutions have been studied and are reviewed by Tan et al. [28]. However, this 
technology faces some challenges, among which battery degradation and social 
acceptance due to the owners’ fear of finding their vehicle insufficiently charged when 
they need it. 
Active heat storage can be performed in many different ways. According to Heier [21], 
the main two storage mediums in a building are the HVAC system and the building 
structure. Hot water tanks are the most common example included in the first category. 
This technology makes use of the high heat capacity of water in order to store heat, in 
particular as domestic hot water storage to respond large variations in DHW demand – 
but they can also be used as storage for space heating. Hot water tanks are usually a 
central element in combined systems including thermal solar cells, as described by Lund 
[29]. Heat storage in the ventilation system by use of phase-change materials is also being 
investigated, in particular for cooling applications [30]. Active heat storage in the 
building structure refers to what is called thermally activated building systems, or 
TABS. These systems use the building elements (walls, floor, ceiling) as a storage 
medium and charge or discharge them according to the needs [21]. Floor heating systems 
embedding water pipes or heating cables in the floor slab, making use of its heat 
capacity, are an example of TABS. Microencapsulated phase-change materials can also 
be added to a floor heating or ceiling cooling system, on the condition that the 
fluctuation range of the indoor temperature remains within the melting range of the 
material [31]. 
Thermo-chemical storage can also be used in active storage strategies. This technology 
makes use of endothermic and exothermic reversible reactions: some chemical 
components release thermal energy when they combine (exothermic reaction) and absorb 
energy when they split (endothermic reaction). Just as PCMs, these components provide 
a form of heat storage that can be charged and discharged by combining and separating 
the two parts [21]. This technology is however not yet implemented at an industrial scale 
and faces important practical challenges [22].

2.3 
Heat storage in the thermal mass – theory and 
applications 
This work focuses on the impact of design parameters on a building’s flexibility 
potential. The point here is therefore not to evaluate the efficiency of incentive-based 
strategies on the load pattern; modules such as heat pumps or hot water tanks will not 
be integrated either in the building system since their own behaviour could eclipse the 
influence of building design and fabric itself. Components such as phase-change or 
thermochemical materials could be studied; however, the present work applies to a 
building under construction and the mentioned technologies are at the moment not 
sufficiently mature to be exploited at a commercial scale. Heat storage in the building 
thermal mass is thus the target in the present study. In order to understand its 
mechanisms and potential, it was investigated first the materials’ performance, and later 
the wall dynamic behaviour and its applications. 
2.3.1 
Physics involved 
2.3.1.1 
Properties of materials 
During a normal operation day in a building, a wall exchanges energy with its 
surroundings in different ways: by convection with air at its surface, and by radiation 
processes. Depending on the temperature difference between the two sides of the wall, 
this energy absorbed can be transported through the wall thickness by heat conduction. 
Moreover, the wall materials have the ability to store heat, which impacts the duration 
of the heat transfer process. Depending on their properties, they will be able to charge 
up with the energy absorbed by the wall and to release it when the temperature of the 
surroundings changes. 
Different physical properties of materials are embedded in this process, the most 
fundamental of which are thermal resistance and thermal capacity. 
Thermal resistance (expressed in m²K/W) is the ability of a material to resist heat 
transfer. In building science, it is used to report the degree of insulation of a building 
material; a large thermal resistance allows a large temperature difference between the 
two sides of a wall. Thermal resistance is an extensive parameter: it depends on the 
quantity of matter contained in the resistive object. The corresponding intensive 
property of a material is the thermal conductivity λ (measured in W/mK).

Thermal capacity, also called thermal mass, is the ability of a material to store energy: it 
is measured in J/K and physically represents its ability to absorb heat when changing 
temperature. An increase in temperature is a form of energy storage, but it is also the 
driving process for heat transfer: therefore, a wall showing an important temperature rise 
after an energy intake (for instance via radiation from the sun) is likely to release the 
energy absorbed as soon as it comes in contact with a material with a lower temperature 
(air for example). A performing storage medium will thus be characterised by a small 
temperature response to energy absorption, i.e. a large energy intake for each rise in 
temperature of 1 Kelvin, namely a large heat capacity.

2.3.1.2 
W all dynamic response 
As explained in Section 2.3.1.1, the density, specific heat capacity and thermal 
conductivity of a material determine its ability to store and release energy. When the 
theory is applied to real multi-layer walls submitted to heat gains and fluctuating 
temperature on both sides, the quality of a wall in terms of heat storage is more complex 
to assess. Moreover, if the goal of using heat accumulated in walls is to redistribute 
demand peaks or to adapt energy production drops, not only the quantity of stored 
energy must be known, but also the dynamic response of the envelope to a stimulation 
and its evolution in case of changed surrounding conditions. 
In [34] some theory can be found on the dynamic component of thermal storage. The 
focus is on a wall consisting of both a layer with a high thermal mass (on the inside) and 
an insulation layer (on the outside), subjected to a sinusoidal heat wave from inside 
representing the daily temperature fluctuation. This configuration is very common and is 
close to the actual wall structure in the examined building. A large part of the heat 
transferred through the accumulating layer will be reflected at the interface with the 
insulation, modifying the profile of heat stored within the thickness of the heavy layer. 
This document highlights the existence of an optimal accumulating layer thickness where 
the heat stored in the wall is theoretically maximised. This concept is also demonstrated 
by Ma et al. [35] by calculating the heat accumulated by a homogeneous internal wall as 
a function of its thickness when submitted to a sinusoidal surface temperature change; it 
is shown in particular that concrete is able to store a larger amount of energy in a given 
thickness than materials such as wood, and that energy storage increases up to a larger 
thickness than for wood. The inclusion of the dynamic response of a wall leads to a new 
definition of thermal capacity, transformed in “effective thermal capacity” varying with 
the wall thickness. This indicator is also used by Biddulph et al. [36] and calculated 
through an RC-model of a wall combined with a Bayesian analysis; for a 30-cm brick 
wall covered by 1 cm gypsum on the inside, it was estimated that the effective thermal 
mass as seen from the inside (in terms of energy effectively released to the space) was the 
equivalent of the first 4 cm of the wall. 
Orosa et al. [37] assess the inertia of a building with respect to its time constant, defined 
as the ratio between its total heat capacity and its total heat loss factor. It gives an idea 
of the response pace of a building to a step change in outdoor temperature, by balancing

2.3.2 
Application of thermal mass to indoor comfort and temperature 
stability 
Many studies have been carried out demonstrating the ability of heavy walls to stabilise 
indoor temperature and limit temperature fluctuations. Asan [39] compares different 
building materials dedicated to be used in external walls, relatively to their response in 
terms of inner surface temperature to a sinusoidal temperature at the outer surface. Two 
parameters are used to describe the output wave: the decrement factor and the time lag.

The decrement factor is calculated as the ratio between the amplitude of the output 
wave (internal surface temperature) and the amplitude of the stimulation wave (external 
surface temperature). The time lag is the delay observed between the maximum of the 
output wave and the maximum of the input wave, which corresponds to the time it 
takes for the heat wave to propagate from the outer surface of the wall to the inner one. 
Predicting these two parameters is very useful to assess the sensitivity of the wall to 
outdoor conditions and its ability to act as a thermal buffer, of interest in the design of 
passive houses. Similarly, Wang et al. [40] study the possibility of designing a thermally 
autonomous building – in which thermal comfort is maintained without assuming any 
HVAC system – by relying on thermal storage in the building envelope (internal and 
external walls, floor, ceiling). The operative temperature variation (ΔTop) is measured for 
different outdoor temperature steps. The floor and ceiling slab thicknesses appear to be a 
crucial factor in the reduction of indoor temperature swings: changes in ΔTop due to a 
change in slab thickness are found up to 61.5% of ΔTop – internal and external wall 
thicknesses have a smaller but noticeable impact. Thermal mass therefore has an 
important influence on temperature stability. This effect is also highlighted by Orosa et 
al. [37] who compare the indoor temperature fluctuations in two schools submitted to the 
same exterior conditions, one having stone walls and the other one brick walls with an 
insulation layer in the middle. As mentioned in Section 2.3.1.3, the time constant is 
calculated taking into account both thermal capacity and thermal resistance; the results 
show much lower temperature fluctuations in the brick school (better insulated so having 
a larger time constant) than in the stone school, even though the latter has the heaviest 
walls. Both parameters must therefore be taken into account. Minimization of infiltration 
is also shown to greatly improve indoor thermal stability. Moffiet et al. [41] compare the 
indoor thermal stability of six buildings with different wall types, light or heavy, 
insulated or not, by varying the relative position of the insulation and the accumulative 
layer. It is found that external insulation and internal thermal mass guarantee a better 
stabilization of the indoor temperature, heat being stored more efficiently in the wall. 
These different findings illustrate the contribution of thermal mass to indoor comfort in 
buildings, showing its ability to attenuate indoor temperature swings. 
2.3.3 
Application of the building thermal mass to flexible load 
operation 
Some researchers have already studied the possibility of reducing heating and cooling 
demand during some periods using high thermal mass walls, without jeopardizing indoor 
comfort. Back in 1982, a pilot test was carried out in Sweden by Österlind [42] with the 
aim of assessing the ability of several buildings to face a heat shortage during several 
hours. Measurements carried out in two of the buildings for shortage durations shorter 
than 24 hours show a drop in indoor temperature of less than 3 degrees, which was 
considered acceptable. Johansson et al. [43] measured the energy consumption and indoor 
temperature consecutive to heat load reduction events (short, long and intermittent); it 
was shown that even though indoor temperature variations are larger than without heat 
reductions, reduction events of up to 2.5 hours still lead to comfortable levels of indoor 
temperature. With the aim of achieving peak load shifting, Wolisz et al. [44] investigated  
overheating periods of different durations before a predicted peak load and a reduction in

heating load during the peak, applied to an uninsulated massive brick building. The 
building’s cooling down response was observed: even though the energy released to the 
space from the thermal mass was not sufficient to cover the entire energy demand of the 
building, a significant fraction of the peak heat load could be avoided (around 25% 
during the first two hours). The heat conduction through external walls due to the 
absence of insulation is one of the reasons brought up by the authors to the fast heat

2.4 
Impact of design parameters on flexible load operation – 
literature review 
The aim of this work is to assess the impact of various design parameters on the energy 
flexibility of the building. Even though several flexible load operation experiments have 
already been carried out, few of them specifically evaluate the influence of some 
characteristics of the building on its flexible behaviour. As seen in the previous sections, 
the impact of different wall properties on their ability to store energy is well documented 
in literature [34][35][37][39][40][41] and their importance for load shifting was also 
specifically studied [47]. Some researchers pay closer attention to the role of windows and 
solar gains [37][40][47], again more with respect to thermal buffering than flexibility. 
Research on design optimisation for energy savings demonstrated the interest of careful 
window orientation and building compactness for the energy performance of the building, 
but literature about their contribution to flexibility potential is very rare [47][48]. 
Identically, little specific information was found on the influence of internal wall features 
and position on energy flexibility. 
2.4.1 
Wall design 
The aim of Section 2.3 was to understand the material properties and physical 
phenomena allowing thermal storage in walls. The combination of thermal capacity and 
thermal resistance of building materials was found to be a key parameter in 
understanding heat transfer and storage in the building envelope. The dynamic 
behaviour of walls can be described through indicators such as the time constant and the 
effective thermal capacity. The wall, floor and ceiling thicknesses [34][35][40], infiltration 
rate [37] and the layer arrangement [34][41] proved to have a decisive importance on the 
ability of the building to store and deliver energy. The capability of high thermal mass 
envelope to attenuate temperature swings from outside and to delay them was retrieved 
from literature, and understanding the nature of the materials used – therefore knowing 
the time lag and decrement of the envelope elements – appears as a reliable way to 
control the influence of outdoor conditions on the thermal comfort indoors [37][39].

It has to be kept in mind that flexible load operation relies on reaction to indoor 
conditions and time span of absorption/release of heat, whereas buffering concerns the 
response to an exterior temperature change. However, since the wall design parameters 
evidenced in Section 2.3 rule the static and dynamic behaviour of thermal storage walls, 
they are the first that should be investigated when assessing the factors likely to 
influence energy flexibility in buildings. A broad parameter analysis concerning different 
wall properties (interior walls thickness, external wall insulation thickness, brick 
thickness in the external walls, roof insulation thickness) was carried out by Reynders 
[47] in order to assess their impact on the storage capacity and storage efficiency of the 
building envelope. It is found that insulating buildings reduces their storage capacity 
since the heating load can be reduced (the storage capacity is calculated as the amount 
of heat that can be added to the structural mass, thus it is dependent on the heating 
capacity), but storage losses are also greatly reduced. Wall thickness affects the storage 
capacity and efficiency for long changes in heating schedule; for shorter perturbations, 
walls with different thicknesses give similar responses. 
2.4.2 
Glazing design and solar gains 
Wang et al. [40] studied the influence of the glazing-to-external-wall ratio on thermal 
stability after a drop in outdoor temperature, by correlating this to the concrete slab 
thickness in walls. It was found that for outdoor temperature drops smaller than 10°C, a 
glazing-to-wall ratio up to 42% doesn’t significantly affect thermal stability and no 
addition of concrete in the walls is necessary for indoor temperature to remain in an 
acceptable range. On the other hand, for glazing-to-wall ratios higher than 42% and a 
temperature drop of more than 18°C, fluctuations in indoor temperature exceed the 
comfort range, including with thicker concrete walls. Window design must therefore be 
carried out carefully taking into account the sensitivity to the outdoor conditions. In the 
previously mentioned parameter analysis, Reynders [47] considers the existence of a large 
glazed surface as an impediment for flexibility, since increasing the glazing-to-wall ratio 
decreases the thermal mass of the facade. Moreover, solar gains permit to reduce the 
heating demand, therefore reducing the storage capacity according to the indicator used 
in that study. However, in Orosa’s study [37], solar gains allow the buildings to retain 
heat during a longer period of time. 
2.4.3 
Geometry parameters 
Very few studies could be found regarding the influence of building structure on its 
flexible operation, such as the compactness of the building, the orientation of the main 
windows, the position and number of internal walls, etc. Such parameters are usually 
studied with regards to their impact on energy consumption. Compact buildings are 
usually recommended for energy savings purposes (e.g in [49]) and are sometimes 
discussed [50]; impact of compactness on energy storage is briefly assessed by Reynders 
[47] and considered to reduce storage capacity and efficiency as it reduces the internal 
wall thermal mass – due to the mathematical definition of the internal wall-to-floor ratio. 
This parameter needs to be isolated from its dependencies for its effect on flexibility to 
be thoroughly evaluated.

Several studies demonstrate the influence of window orientation on the building energy 
performance because of its impact on heating, cooling and lighting consumptions (for 
example [34][35][50]). Whether or not window orientation impacts energy flexibility is 
however very little documented. Indirect indications are given by Zhu et al. [48], who 
studies two prototype houses without windows (one with high thermal mass and the 
other without) and measures the inner and outer temperature of the four walls. The 
analysis shows that the South wall of the house with poor thermal mass is the most 
sensitive to changes in outdoor conditions; reducing the inertia of the South facade by 
including windows may therefore not perform very well in terms of thermal buffering 
capacity. 
As seen in the previously cited studies [47][40], internals walls have a certain influence on 
thermal flexibility since they contribute to the overall thermal mass. Their area and 
thickness are therefore likely to impact the building storage potential. However, even 
though most of the studies include solar gains in the energy balance of external walls, 
little research seems to have been conducted on the transmission of solar gains through 
the windows and their storage in internal walls – just as in the floor. Walls and floor 
areas positioned close to the windows are likely to receive direct solar gains, to 
accumulate it and to release it when no more direct heat from the sun is available, which 
could participate in the thermal flexibility potential of the building. The extreme version 
of this concept is the Trombe wall or solar wall (a review of related studies is presented 
by Saadatian et al. [53]), constituted of a high thermal mass wall covered by a glazing 
pane; solar energy received on the glazed side is stored in the wall mass and later 
delivered to the space. Detachable insulation is sometimes added to avoid heat losses in 
absence of solar gains. 
2.5 
Measuring and quantifying flexibility 
In the previous sections, different energy storage systems were reviewed. Integrated in 
buildings, they could permit to shift energy consumption from the moment where it is 
produced to a moment where it is convenient for the energy system as a whole. Buildings 
could therefore help provide flexibility to the grid – and the extent to which they could 
be useful for the system depends on their utilisation of these energy storage techniques, 
but also on the period and duration of use. In order to optimize building design with 
regards to flexible load operation, some indexes have to be set quantifying the flexibility 
degree of a building. 
It is first important to focus on the energy-consuming appliances themselves: which of 
them offer the possibility of changes in load scheduling and to what extent? Valsomatzis 
[54] proposes several indicators to measure the flexibility potential of the load operations 
themselves. Their adaptability is first assessed with respect to two basic properties: the 
allowable time span within which they have to be performed, and the range of power 
needed for their operation. From these two indicators, some are formed through 
mathematical combinations, giving the opportunity to rank different operations with 
regards to their flexibility potential. These criteria are adapted to flexible load shape 
approaches: even if no predefined load shifting pattern is known, some loads can be 
displaced at any time regarding their flexibility potential.

Then, the demand side management opportunities of the building as a whole have to be 
evaluated. Several ideas have been put forward in literature, focusing on different aspects 
of flexibility. 
Changes in load schedule can be expressed in terms of capacity: Oldewurtel et al. [55] 
define the power shifting capability as the maximum power shift that is obtained as a 
response to a pulse – in this case, in electricity price, but this indicator could be adapted 
to have as an input a sudden indoor temperature change for example. This indicator 
does not qualify the real-time ability of a building to help grid balancing, but it gives an 
idea of the maximum contribution that it can bring on a punctual solicitation. 
Some studies start from a basic energy consumption pattern and implement a flexible 
load operation strategy. Evaluating the performance of this strategy sometimes includes 
a flexibility assessment in terms of shifted volume. De Coninck et al. [56] define a 
flexibility interval as a time period within the experiment duration, and the negative and 
positive flexibilities as the amounts of energy respectively deduced and added from the 
reference load pattern during the whole flexibility interval. This information permits to 
assess the time response of the system to the implemented change and can be used for 
both load shifting and more general flexible load shaping aims. Masy et al. [20] evaluate 
the space heating load shifting potential of buildings equipped with heat pumps. Peak 
and off-peak consumption hours are defined based on a power price signal, and the 
percentage of the load shifted from peak to off-peak time zones is calculated over a 
delimited period. This flexibility indicator thus directly includes a mention to the grid 
balance; it does not reflect the ability of the building design to respond any solicitation 
but its conclusions may vary depending on the moment when consumption is needed or 
prohibited. However, relating this indicator to the position in time and intensity of the 
solicitation could permit drawing the profile of the building’s flexibility potential hour 
after hour. 
The same article includes a second flexibility indicator, built from the energy grid point 
of view. The price incentive mechanism applied reflects the day-ahead market price of 
electricity to the consumer, leading to high prices when the demand is high and/or the 
production of renewable energy is low. The cost of the electricity supplied to the heat 
pump is therefore an indicator of the influence of the heating load on the grid: the higher 
the price, the more the load is a burden for the energy system. The authors thus define 
their flexibility indicator as the position of the average price of the heat pump’s power 
between the highest and the lowest available prices. Flexibility is equal to 1 if the heat 
pump always consumes at times where the price is the lowest – which means that the 
heating system constantly has a positive influence on the grid balance and its flexibility 
is optimally used. Following the same idea, Reynders [47] reviews different studies 
putting in place a control of the building load scheduling based on a minimisation of 
energy prices (assuming a varying time-of-use price scheme). Flexibility can in these 
cases be quantified as the financial savings achieved by changing the consumption 
pattern. 
The extra cost brought by flexibility strategies is also discussed in literature. Making use 
of storage systems during load-shifting strategies implies some energy losses, which can

3.1 
Approach and preliminary choices 
In this section, the aim of this study and the reasons behind the choices made along the 
process are described. The flexibility topic is large and complex, and this work focuses on 
some specific aspects of it, that are detailed further here. 
General aim 
The general goal of this work is to analyse the contribution that residential buildings can 
make to energy flexibility at the scale of a district, and to understand to what extent a 
building’s design features impact this contribution. In other words, the aim is to find out 
which parameters of a building can help make its energy consumption more adaptable to 
the grid requirements, while guaranteeing indoor thermal comfort to the occupants. 
Electrical or thermal flexibility? 
The first question that had to be answered is the following: which grid is targeted in this 
work? Since the focus here is on design considerations, it implies to consider only passive 
demand side management strategies, and therefore to focus on thermal flexibility: while 
the heating demand is directly affected by the building features, the building 
components that can alter the electricity consumption have very specific properties that 
go beyond the scope of this project. However, as explained in the background section, in 
a system where heat and power are coupled as Nordhavn district intends to be in the 
future, a better flexibility of the heating grid has direct impact on the electrical grid 
stability. 
Target of the flexibility assessment 
Presently, the area of interest is alimented by a district heating network, which does not 
present the same variability in demand and regulation mechanisms as the electrical grid. 
In particular, no differentiated heating prices have been set yet, which makes it difficult 
to assess flexibility from an economical point of view by calculating the financial savings 
achieved by load shifting in the context of a price-based control, as in the previously 
cited studies by Masy et al. [20] and Reynders [47]. Since the focus is on residential 
buildings, the target here will rather be to assess flexibility in terms of comfort 
preservation for the occupants. 
Choice of the scale 
The next choice that had to be made is the scale at which this study takes place. In 
order to isolate the impact of building parameters from other factors, it was chosen not 
to exceed the scale of a building to lead this parameter variation. Moreover, in order to 
be able to get a thorough understanding of the obtained results, and for reasons of 
simulation ease, the study was finally performed on a single apartment of a chosen 
building in Nordhavn district. This choice reduces the opportunities to vary the overall 
geometry of the building, which would have been of interest in the present study, but it

orientation of the chosen apartment is nevertheless addressed during this study. 
Choice of the heating control strategy 
The aim of this study is not to provide the district heating operator with information 
about the control strategy to be adopted. It is rather meant to provide an estimation of 
the effect of different parameters on flexibility; what is of interest here is the difference 
between the abilities of several designs to soften the impact of a given change in heating 
pattern on the occupants’ comfort. 
Moreover, the alterations in heating schedule required by the district heating operator 
can be extremely variable and can be needed at any moment of the day, depending on 
the overall heating demand but also on the state of the grid, on a possible plant failure 
or even on the balance of the electrical grid, in case the two systems are interconnected. 
It is therefore hazardous to set up a control schedule and use it as input for a flexibility 
assessment, since the obtained result may be very dependent on the schedule profile. 
It was thus chosen to use some fundamental signal as an input, namely a complete heat 
cut-off, and observe the changes in the building’s reaction. The impact of a preheating 
period or of successive heat cut-offs is not evaluated in this study. 
3.2 
General methods 
The chosen apartment, located in a residential building in construction in Nordhavn 
district, was modelled with the building performance simulation software IDA ICE 4.7, 
which allows an important precision in the design as well as a very complete analysis of 
the obtained results. 
Definition of the assessment method 
The next step was to define a method to assess flexibility, which includes two elements: 
a protocol, and a set of indicators quantifying the apartment´s flexibility potential. 
Flexibility can be defined here as the ability to adapt to constraints on the heating 
power schedule without compromising the occupants’ comfort. Therefore, the flexibility 
assessment pattern used was the following: a change in heating schedule is performed 
and the evolution of indoor comfort is assessed. 
The building system was subjected to a total heat cut-off at a certain instant t0, which 
corresponds to a worse-case scenario among the different possible heating controls 
strategies. The exact time of this cut-off is likely to significantly alter the results. Thus, 
six different possibilities for the cut-off moment were evaluated in each of the cases, 
spread out over 24 hours in order to obtain a flexibility profile throughout a day. 
This protocol was inspired from theoretical studies aiming at experimentally observing 
the time constant of a building, which gives an indication of the combined effect of 
thermal resistance and heat capacity on the indoor temperature profile after a change in 
heating power. It was thought at first to evaluate the building time constant according 
to IDA ICE and use it as a flexibility indicator. However, the time constant as defined is

a basic and theoretical indicator, and its evaluation method implies to omit all time-
dependent gains and losses – it can therefore not be used as such to give information 
about the actual ability of a building to store heat in given conditions. Some new 
indicators were set up quantifying the building flexibility in more complex conditions, 
including solar gains and a variable occupancy profile. Two flexibility indicators were 
thus defined based on the same idea: measuring the speed of the temperature drop after 
heat is cut off, since temperature is the variable of indoor comfort that is mainly affected 
by a change in heating schedule. Their construction and the details of the protocol are 
explained in Section 3.3. 
On top of this comfort analysis, it was investigated if a change in design can have an 
impact on the heat consumption pattern following an alteration in the heating schedule. 
In particular, cutting heat off in a building for a certain period in order to reduce a 
consumption peak could create a peak at another moment, which can in turn threaten 
the grid balance if it does not match a production peak, or call for extra production 
capacity. It was therefore investigated if the building design can have an impact on the 
height of this peak by setting up a schedule including a heat cut-off for several hours, 
and measuring the height of the peak and the consumption profile. These two indicators 
as well as the details of the schedule are also described in Section 3.3. 
Parameter variation 
Once the protocols and the four indicators were set up, different design parameters were 
chosen for further analysis of their impact on flexibility, most of them on the basis of the 
literature reviewed. Their variation range was also documented and values were chosen 
for each of the parameters spread over the whole possible range. 
Different versions of the original apartment were created in IDA ICE, corresponding to 
the range of the investigated parameters. Every model included a change in the value of 
one single parameter. In each of these models, the six cut-off schedules and the schedule 
used for the peak power analysis were set up. Given the important number of 
simulations, the data retrieving process had to be automatized. The version handling 
option of IDA ICE was used, by creating a tree where all the different models were 
arranged. A Matlab code was then created, retrieving the data of interest from the data 
series ordered in the IDA ICE result folders, calculating the indicators and arranging 
them in an Excel file, from which they were converted into graphs and analysed. The 
details of the process can be found in Appendix 2.

3.3 
Choice of the flexibility indicators 
Four different indicators were defined to quantify the flexibility potential of the building. 
Two of them are related to the ability of the building to retain heat in case of a heat cut-
off. The other two indicators are related to the change in heat consumption pattern after 
a perturbation in heating supply. These two sets of indicators were measured after two 
different types of experiments. 
3.3.1 
Temperature drop indicators 
The first protocol, used to calculate the temperature-related indicators, was the 
following. Each simulation was carried out from January 19st to January 25th. The 
simulation is initialized under normal operating conditions for heating: constant heating 
with a setpoint of 21°C for operative temperature. This setpoint is higher than average, 
but it is used as a substitution for a preheating phase. On Monday, January 19th, heating 
is cut off at time t=t0. It is kept off until the end of the simulation period. Heat cut-offs 
concern the connection to district heating, so they apply to both the floor heating and 
the heating of the supply air in the air handling unit. In order to get a profile of the 
flexibility potential of the building along the day, t0 is varied between six different hours 
of the day: 
• 
5:00. With the goal of cutting off the morning heating peak, heat supply could be 
cut off in residential buildings early in the morning so that priority is given to 
heating up office buildings before employees arrive. 
25 
 
==PAGE:40==
Chapter 3

• 
7:00. In the case of electrically provided heating, cutting heating off when 
occupants wake up would permit to reduce the electrical morning peak. In the 
case of heat supply from district heating, a cut-off at 7 could also participate in 
the heat prioritization between offices and dwellings. 
• 
10:00. Heat could be cut off after the occupants leave the place. 
• 
16:00. Heat could be cut off before the occupants come back home in order to 
reduce the late afternoon peak. 
• 
19:00. It could also be interesting to cut heating off a bit later in the evening so 
that the evening peak is reduced with a lower risk of having to turn it on again 
before the night. 
• 
22:00. Heat could be cut off at night to make room for other uses: charging of 
electrical cars for example, in the case of electrically provided heating. 
Two measurements were made on each of the simulations, corresponding to the first two 
flexibility indicators. The measurements were carried out on two rooms: the living room 
and the double bedroom (oriented North-West), which showed to be the coldest. The 
complete description of the building model will be done in Chapter 4. 
In this whole study, the operative temperature was chosen to be investigated rather than 
the air temperature, since emphasis is placed upon the occupants’ comfort. 
The first output is the time (measured from t0) after which the indoor temperature drops 
below the lower bound of the occupants’ acceptability range. In this work, this minimum 
acceptable temperature was set to 20°C. As Reynders [47] shows, it is expected that the 
occupants’ acceptability impacts the flexibility potential of a building, since a narrower 
comfort range means less room for flexibility strategies to alter the indoor temperature. 
In this study, it was chosen not to assess this impact. 
This indicator gives a concrete indication of the possibilities that the building offers in 
terms of heat retention and could be used by the district heating operator to know how 
long the building can be independent from the heating network – keeping in mind the 
assumptions regarding influential factors, such as the heating setpoint and the outdoor 
temperature. The theoretical formulation of this first indicator is shown on Equation (9).

3.3.2

In order to calculate the third and fourth flexibility indicators, another protocol was set

during the night, and offices during working hours, with a small advance: heating of 
offices starts two hours before employees arrive and heating of dwellings is set back one 
hour before the occupants come home. In this scenario, the indoor temperature is not of 
interest, but focus is set on the heating power.

The BR15 standard sets the setpoint for heating at 20°C, which corresponds to the 
lowest thermal comfort limit. It is also required to remove heat from the rooms which 
temperature exceeds 26°C. The setpoint for heating was however set to 21°C to mimic a 
slight preheating in order to be able to assess the time spent above the comfort threshold 
of 20°C. The base acceptability pattern for occupants was based on the two boundaries 
from the BR15 standard: 20 and 26°C. Assuming a wider temperature tolerance (for 
example with a lower bound at 18°C, which is the lower limit for Category III in DS/EN 
15251 [64]) would permit to offer a larger flexibility to the grid – but in the framework of 
this study, the users’ acceptability was not investigated. Moreover, it was assumed that 
occupants open the windows in reaction to a rise in indoor temperature. This behaviour 
was modelled by a thermostatic controller leading to a progressive opening of the 
windows, starting when the operative temperature reaches 24°C. The window is totally 
open if the indoor operative temperature exceeds 26°C. This thermostat was coupled 
with the occupancy pattern of every zone, so that windows can be open only when

A dynamic simulation was chosen: in this configuration, the program makes an initial 
guess and reaches convergence while simulating the days from the 1st of January to the 
18th of January, then starts the actual simulation from the 19th of January, day of the 
cut-off. This startup phase of 18 days was chosen because it showed a good convergence 
of the results while testing the model under conditions that could give a predictable 
response.

5.2.3 
Conclusions 
The main conclusions drawn from this reference study are the following. 
• 
The apartment as currently designed shows an important ability to retain heat in 
case of heating cut-off. 
• 
The living room globally offers a better potential for flexibility, as its 
temperature can be maintained above comfort levels for a longer time and has a 
slower decrease pattern. 
• 
The cut-off time mainly impacts the time above 20°C and not significantly the 
temperature drop speed. However, cut-offs performed in the morning and cut-offs 
performed in the afternoon or evening are likely to show different flexibility 
results. 
• 
Cutting heat off for part of the day seems to have little impact on the energy 
consumption: it cannot really be considered as an efficient energy saving strategy. 
• 
Distinct power peaks are observed after the cut-off period that could jeopardize a 
flexibility operation.

indicators, as seen in Figure 33. In particular, the South orientation gives much better

room, and the heat gained in the bedroom is lost towards the door to the main space.

different window distribution would give different results.

5.4 
Parameter combinations 
An extra analysis was carried out in order to understand the impact of some design 
features on the results given when varying some other parameters. In particular, having 
proved that the internal thermal masses (concrete layer of internal and external walls) 
have little impact on flexibility in the conducted study, it was investigated whether this 
result had any dependency on other design assumptions, such as the glazing area. 
5.4.1 
Glazing-to-wall ratio and external wall concrete thickness 
The question here was to understand if the concrete layer of the external wall had any 
influence on flexibility at all, or if this result was due to the large glazing-to-external-
wall ratio minimizing the wall surface and consequently its thermal retention potential. 
The glazing-to-wall ratio and the concrete layer thickness of the external wall were 
simultaneously changed in order to create one model for each set of values. For 
readability reasons, only one cut-off was performed, one indicator was calculated and the 
results are only shown for the living room. However, the trend showed to be similar for 
other cut-off times and in the bedroom. The time before the indoor temperature drops 
below 20°C was assessed in each of the cases for a cut-off performed at 7 AM. The 
flexibility results of the living room are presented in the form of a 3D surface plot shown 
in Figure 37.

5.5 
Main conclusions 
5.5.1 
General observations about the indicators 
Several observations can be made regarding the behaviour of the chosen indicators when 
performing the parameter variation. 
Overall energy consumption 
In all design configurations, shutting down heat for some hours of the day does not lead 
to any remarkable increase or decrease in energy consumption. Regardless of the heating 
power level in normal operation, the height of the peaks after the end of the cut-off 
period compensates the decrease in consumption due to the cut-off. The total energy 
consumption is therefore definitely not impacted by the flexibility strategy in that case. 
However, several studies show that when a pre-heating period is performed before a cut-
off, the storage losses occurring during that phase lead to a global increase in energy 
consumption [44][55][67][68]. 
Dependency on the cut-off time 
In the majority of the cases, the time spent in the comfort temperature range shows a 
much higher dependency on the cut-off time than the average temperature drop speed 
over the first 48h after the cut-off. This is due to the fact that indoor temperature varies 
significantly during the day due to the successive internal and external gains; therefore 
the cut-off time has an important impact on the time spent before the 20°C threshold is 
reached. In the calculation of the average drop speed, the daily variations are somehow 
erased since the average is calculated over two days. Since the temperature profiles 
following different cut-offs are similar, the drop speed does not vary much. It has also 
been noticed that the dependency on the cut-off time decreases when the average drop 
speed is calculated over more days. 
Difference between the two temperature drop indicators 
Apart from a difference in dispersion due to different cut-off times, the two temperature-
related indicators show similar trends for all cases. This proves that the change in 
duration of the comfort period when varying a parameter is actually due to a variation 
in temperature drop speed, and not only to a change in the initial temperature 
conditions due to the design change. Moreover, it was often noticed that the temperature 
droop speed had a smooth and definite trend, clearly showing the dependence on the 
studied parameters, while the first indicator had more irregular variations due to the 
daily fluctuations of indoor temperature.

5.5.2 
Impact comparison of the different parameters 
In order to compare from a mathematical point of view the influence of all the 
investigated parameters on flexibility, the relative change (RC) was calculated in the 
value of the indicator between the two extreme points corresponding to the two extreme 
design versions for each of the parameters. RC is expressed as follows, with y the output, 
here the flexibility indicator. 
𝑅𝑅𝑅𝑅= ∆𝑦𝑦
𝑦𝑦𝑏𝑏𝑏𝑏𝑏𝑏𝑏𝑏
 
This impact indicator does not take into account the variation in input leading to this 
change in output, as for example the influence coefficient IC which is the ratio of RC 
and the relative change in input. It was chosen not to include any mention of the input 
variation, for the following reason. The parameters which impact on flexibility is here 
compared all have different units and different variation ranges. Therefore, assessing the 
change in output relatively to a given change in input for each parameter and comparing 
them does not make much sense. As an example, if varying parameter A by 20% gives a 
50% change in flexibility while varying parameter B by the same 20% gives a 30% 
change in flexibility, one cannot conclude that parameter A is more influential, because 
varying parameter B by 20% could be a very important change (if the variation range of 
B is very narrow), while it might represent a very small step for parameter A. 
Since in this study, the different values chosen for the design parameters cover more or 
less the whole range of possibilities, it was chosen to simply compare the difference 
between the flexibility values given by the two extreme design cases. The interpretation 
of the data obtained is thus the following: how much change in flexibility is at maximum 
achievable when using the whole variation range of the considered parameter? 
The relative change in flexibility when varying the different parameters was assessed 
using three out of the four flexibility indicators, the change in heating energy 
consumption having proved to be very little influenced by design changes. The three 
indicators were evaluated for the living room for the six different cut-off times and the 
relative change between the minimum and maximum values (corresponding to the 
maximum and minimum values of the design parameters) calculated for each of them. 
The highest of the six values obtained was retrieved and plotted in Figure 39. The 
abbreviations used to name the design parameters are explained in Table 6.

From this summary and from the single parameter graphs described previously, the 
following conclusions can be drawn. 
• 
In the current conditions (in particular with the current glazing-to-wall ratio), 
the concrete thickness of the external wall (EXCT) shows little impact on 
flexibility; however, it has been shown that a lower window share would increase 
the impact of this concrete layer on flexibility since the volume of concrete would 
be increased and the losses decreased. A similar conclusion can be drawn for the 
thickness of the internal walls (INCT), even though the impact on flexibility 
seems slightly higher. On the contrary, the material used in internal walls 
(INMA) is the parameter showing the smallest impact on the temperature drop 
pattern. The thickness and material of the walls shows absolutely no impact on 
peak power: in general, the peak power is not influenced by heat storage at all. 
• 
The thickness of the floor slab (FLCT) shows more impact on flexibility than the 
thickness of the walls, since the presence of floor heating makes the floor slab the 
primary energy storage device. However, for floor thicknesses above 12 
centimeters, the impact on flexibility remains constant. 
• 
The position of the insulation layer (EXIP) does not show a very important 
impact on flexibility. This proves again that under the current conditions, the 
concrete layer of the external walls does not have a big role to play in heat 
retention: whether it is located inside or outside is not critical as long as the 
insulating properties of the wall are preserved. 
• 
The heat transfer coefficient of the external walls is of critical influence on 
flexibility: insulating the external wall (EXIT) can multiply by up to 4 the time 
spent in the comfort temperature zone, and can reduce the temperature drop 
speed by up to 63%. The relative height of the power peak can moreover be 
reduced by 69%. The impact of increasing the insulation thickness is particularly 
important for poorly insulated apartments; for high levels of insulation, 
improving the insulation level even more shows a relatively lower impact. 
Similarly, increasing the U-value of the windows (WUVA) strongly degrades the 
flexibility potential of the apartment, dividing by up to 4 the time spent in the 
comfort zone when going from a window with a U-value of 0.75 W/m2K to a 
window with a U-value of 2.5 W/m2K. 
• 
The size of the windows (GTWR) has an important role to play in the flexibility 
question: in particular, the temperature drop speed can be doubled when 
increasing the glazing-to-external-wall ratio from 0.1 to 0.5 without changing the 
window performance. However, smaller windows induce a higher consumption 
peak after a cut-off period. 
• 
The position (POSI) and orientation (ORIE) of the apartment are also 
influential. South-oriented windows allow a 50% increase in time spent in the 
comfort zone relatively to North-oriented windows, and can divide the relative 
increase in consumption peak by 5. Moreover, having the apartment located on a 
corner with windows facing another direction than South can divide by 3 the 
duration of the comfort period. However, locating the apartment on the East 
corner seems to minimize the heating power peak in case of a cut-off for some 
hours.

5.5.3 
Thermal resistance versus thermal capacity 
One of the main results highlighted by this parameter variation is the importance of the 
envelope thermal resistance. Indeed, in all cases where it was decreased (larger windows, 
higher glazing U-value, thinner insulation layer), the flexibility potential of the 
apartment was also greatly diminished. 
In order to verify this observation, all the investigated cases were gathered and the first 
two flexibility indicators were calculated after a cut-off at 7 AM and plotted over the 
UA-value of the apartment, giving a point for each of the different design cases. The 
results can be seen in Figure 40. 
The impact of UA-value on the chosen flexibility indicators appears very clearly: the 
lower the UA-value, the longer the heat retention, and the slower the temperature

5.6 
Compliance with the time constant theory 
As explained in the methodology section, the idea of using the building time constant as 
a flexibility indicator was not carried further because of the difficulty to include in the 
time constant theory variable gains and losses. It is however valuable to confront the 
obtained flexibility results with this theory and understand if the results could have been 
predicted by calculating the time constant of the building. This time constant was 
therefore calculated using the formula expressed in Equation (3), with the correction 
from the standard: the time constant is the ratio of the equivalent internal heat capacity 
against the total heat transfer coefficient (including the envelope, infiltration and 
ventilation losses). 
The time constant was calculated for all the investigated design versions and the results 
obtained for the flexibility indicators are plotted against the time constant, for a cut-off 
at 7 AM and at 7 PM in the living room. 
It must be recalled that the time constant is a performance indicator of the building 
itself: the role of gains is not embedded in its construction, but in the way this time 
constant is used to predict indoor temperatures. Moreover, the time constant theory does 
not give a response for variable gains which variation does not follow a defined 
mathematical profile (step, sinusoid, etc.). Therefore, no equation permits to link, in the 
current indoor and outdoor conditions, the value taken by the chosen flexibility 
indicators with the time constant of the corresponding design version. As an example, in 
case the gains are constant, the time spent above 20°C should vary linearly with the time 
constant, while the temperature drop speed should decrease exponentially when the time 
constant increases. The results for the temperature drop indicators are displayed in 
Figure 42.

6.3 
Remarks 
The assumptions regarding the outdoor conditions and the occupancy schedule prove to 
have a great impact on the obtained results. The values of the indicators presented in 
this study can therefore not be directly used as such, for example to set up a heating 
schedule: an assessment of the indoor and outdoor conditions must be done beforehand. 
The main output of this work is rather the impact of the assessed design parameters on 
the calculated indicators, in relative terms. 
It is therefore also valuable to know if the indoor and outdoor factors impact in the same 
way all the design cases: it is for example expected that the cases with larger windows 
react more strongly to solar gains than cases with a smaller glazed surface. The impact of 
outdoor conditions and occupancy was therefore evaluated on a number of cases in order 
to figure out if some patterns could be drawn.

7.1 
Methodology 
Design modifications 
On the basis of the flexibility results obtained in the conducted study, some design 
improvements were proposed for the existing apartment. 
The floor slab was thickened from 6 to 12 centimeters, which showed to increase the 
duration of the comfort period by up to 20 hours in the living room. The glazing-to-wall 
ratio was reduced from 0.42 to 0.3, which proved to provide 24 more hours of comfort in 
the living room. Moreover, the results detailed in Appendix 7 show that this glazing-to-
wall ratio leads to optimal performances in case of large solar gains in winter. 
In order to provide the users with an access to the terraces while reducing the area of the 
windows, the high windows of the double bedroom and the living room were replaced by 
a glazed door and a window starting 0.62m from the floor level, as shown in Figure 50.

Daylight analysis 
In a first phase, a daylight analysis was carried out on this new version of the apartment 
in order to evaluate the daylight loss experienced when reducing the window size for 
thermal performance reasons. Indeed, the conducted analysis shows that the best 
flexibility results are achieved with very low glazing-to-wall ratios; however, windows 
have a clear role in the occupants’ visual comfort. A compromise therefore needs to be 
found. 
The daylight factor was thus calculated in the living room and the three bedrooms 
according to the BR15 guidelines: its value was evaluated on a grid starting 0.5m from 
the walls and including a point every 0.5m. The measurement plane was set at a height 
of 0.8m, which is the requirement for daylight calculations in office buildings – no 
specific method exists for residential buildings. The tool used for this analysis was the 
daylight calculation module of IDA ICE 4.7, which integrates the professional version of 
VELUX Energy and Indoor Climate Visualise.

Thermal comfort assessment through a representative year 
In a second phase, the modified apartment was compared to the original version on the 
basis of a yearly simulation under realistic weather conditions. The Test Reference Year 
climate file for Copenhagen provided by IDA ICE was used in this simulation. The 
heating system was considered in normal operation and the heating setpoint was lowered 
to 20°C, which corresponds to more realistic conditions than the setpoint of 21°C used to 
simulate a slight preheating phase in the previous study. 
The goal of this additional study is to observe the behaviour of a building with 
important heat retention potential under fluctuating outdoor conditions. How fast does 
indoor temperature change? Is there a higher risk of overheating in summer? 
Flexibility assessment under realistic weather conditions 
Finally, in a third phase, a heating pattern was set up for the winter months and applied 
to both versions of the apartment, the goal being to observe the behaviour of the 
apartment subjected to periodic changes in its heating supply and to realistic 
stimulations from outdoors. 
No information was provided from the district heating suppliers of Nordhavn district 
about the type of heating control they want to apply. Moreover, few studies focus on 
demand response in heating networks, and those who do use either a punctual signal 
such as a total cut-off, or make use of model-predictive control strategies based on 
heating prices or CO2 emissions [69][70]. No fixed daily pattern was found in literature. 
The pattern that was applied in this study was thus drawn out of some hypotheses and 
trials. 
It was assumed that the district heating operator applies a prioritization strategy 
between offices and dwellings. Early in the morning, priority is given on preheating the 
offices rather than heating up the dwellings. It was chosen to operate this shift at 4 AM 
with the idea of distributing the heating phase in the offices over a long time to avoid 
creating a peak. In the residential buildings, the heating setpoint is reduced to 19°C from 
4 AM instead of ordering a heat cut-off: in well-insulated buildings like the present one, 
the result is an actual shutdown of heating, but decreasing the setpoint still permits to 
provide some heat to a minority of the building stock which would otherwise experience 
very low temperatures. 
The setpoint is kept at 19°C in the dwellings until 11 AM, after which heat supply in 
offices can be reduced – due to the presence of employees and possibly of solar gains – 
and the heating setpoint in dwellings is set to 20°C again. This phase occurs at the 
moment where heating demand is the lowest in residential buildings: the resulting 
heating peak is thus expected not to be too important. The heating setpoint is then 
lowered to 19°C again at 5 PM, around the time where the working occupants usually 
come home, since the gains from the occupants and equipment showed to contribute 
importantly to heating up the space. The setpoint is then raised to 20°C at 8 PM, when 
the heating cycle of offices is completely over, and kept at that level until the next 
morning. This schedule was applied to every day from Monday to Saturday, while no 
constraints were set on heating on Sundays. It was experimented on both the reference

7.5 
Conclusions 
Reducing the size of the windows has a certain impact on daylight penetration into the 
space, in particular if the windows have to be split in order to ensure access to the 
terraces. A deeper window design analysis should be done to figure out if other positions 
and shapes for the glazed surface could provide a better daylight intake. Visual comfort 
is to be seen as an important consideration that impacts the occupants’ well-being in the 
same way as thermal comfort. The present study was however made under the 
assumption that a reduction of the window size was possible, and compared the 
performance of the existing apartment with an apartment comprising a thicker floor slab 
and a smaller glazing-to-wall ratio. 
When assessing the response in terms of temperature of both apartments under realistic 
outdoor fluctuations during a representative year, it is made clear that the modified 
version provides a more stable indoor temperature in all rooms. Up and down peaks are 
limited, and temperatures are globally higher in winter and cooler in summer than in the 
existing building, which leads to overall better comfort conditions through the year. 
Moreover, the improved stability of indoor temperatures leads to a lower need for 
heating energy and permits to reduce the overall peak power level.  
A daily pattern for the heating setpoint was then experimented on both buildings. The 
modifications brought to the apartment lead to a better tolerance to the performed 
change in heating schedule in terms of comfort in the living room, and permit to prevent 
temperature from dropping below 20°C in the bedroom. The results in terms of stability 
of the heating power profile are more satisfying in the modified version, the new design 
allowing reducing to some extent the height of the heating power peaks. 
These results permit to verify to some extent the analysis conducted in Chapter 5 by 
extending it to more representative weather conditions and on a longer time span. The 
apartment modified on the basis of the obtained results shows a better potential for 
retaining heat in the spaces, thus limiting energy consumption and peak power. The 
modifications also show, to some extent, a positive impact on the response to constraints 
on heating power, mainly in the living room and South-oriented spaces.

Chapter 8 
Discussion 
 
This study is a theoretical work that aims at understanding the compared influence of 
design parameters on flexibility. Assumptions and simplifications were used in order to 
be able to isolate the influence of the investigated parameters from other factors. Thus, 
the results cannot be used as such to dimension a heating control strategy for example, 
since they have been obtained in a context that can significantly deviate from reality. 
The purposes of the obtained flexibility results are rather to show the evolution of the 
flexibility potential when varying a parameter and to observe the difference in impact 
between them – the results have to be read in relative terms, the trend being more 
interesting and reliable than the level itself. 
The most important assumption concerns the external parameters that have been 
discussed in the sensitivity analysis. The assumption made of a constant outdoor 
temperature, with no wind and identical solar gains day after day was chosen to serve 
the purpose of a theoretical investigation and not to reflect fully realistic conditions. As 
concluded from the sensitivity study, the results obtained are not to be used in absolute 
terms, since the outdoor conditions and occupancy profiles have such an important 
influence on them. The analysis carried out in Chapter 7 aims at observing the impact of 
some of the considered parameters on flexibility in more realistic outdoor conditions and 
helps drawing a more general picture – even though the results are still subjected to 
some specific assumptions. 
The occupants’ behaviour and preferences are also an important factor of divergence 
between modelling and reality. Defined user profiles have been implemented in this 
study, with a fixed daily occupancy schedule, automatic controls for window opening and 
shading, and a constant temperature comfort range between 20 and 26°C. In reality, the 
presence of occupants is unpredictable and the moment and frequency for opening the 
windows or using the shadings can be uncertain and driven by other concerns than 
indoor temperature and light (presence of wind, of noise, desire for natural ventilation, 
etc.). Moreover, the flexibility results would be very much impacted by the personal 
tolerance of the users to high or low temperatures, reducing or increasing the need for 
heating. The focus here was on design parameters rather than on occupant behaviour 
itself; however, an interesting complement to this work for future investigations could be 
to implement a stochastic user profile and observe the variation in results. 
The flexibility quantification protocol also embeds its own assumptions. In particular, in 
order to give a result as general as possible, the building was initialised with a normal 
heating pattern during 18 days and a setpoint at 21°C. In reality, changes in the heating 
schedule are likely to happen several times per week or even per day, which obviously 
modifies the charging level of the thermal mass and the air temperature of the space 
when a new cut-off happens. Moreover, preheating strategies are likely to be performed 
and the heating setpoint modified. The same holds for the calculation of the peak power 
ratio, which is made considering a cut-off between fixed hours and which results could 
vary when considering another time period. The flexibility results will be very dependent

on the heating control strategy – but the first step in understanding the behaviour of the 
different building parts is to submit them to a simple signal and observe their effect on 
thermal comfort in the zones. From this study can be drawn some conclusions about the 
role of each of the building features in heat retention phenomena, which can later on be 
used to predict their reaction to more complex control patterns, such as the one 
investigated in Chapter 7 and many other types of constraints. 
Another important question regarding this study is to know whether or not the findings 
in terms of relative impact of building parameters on flexibility can be applied to other 
buildings. Even though most of the design features of the apartment have been 
investigated, the results are still only valid for the given building type embedding its 
own assumptions, in particular the geometry of the apartment, its position in the 
building, the shape of the building itself, the distances between the walls, the shape and 
position of the windows, etc. All these parameters were not investigated within the 
parameter variation for prioritization reasons, but they may lead to interesting outcomes 
and help making the whole analysis applicable to a wider range of buildings. Another 
element on which the given results depend grandly is the HVAC systems: floor heating, 
constant air volume ventilation, are choices that impact flexibility, but were not within 
the scope of this work. 
However, even though the choice of the apartment constitutes an important bias, the 
phenomena observed fall within building physics concepts, and are generally applicable. 
Among these concepts: the balance to be found between solar gains and heat losses when 
modifying the window size, the predominance of the impact of heat losses over thermal 
mass for heat flexibility questions, the increased role of thermal mass when losses are 
low, the role of orientation, the factors influencing peak levels: these findings can be used 
to predict the behaviour of other building types and evaluate their flexibility. 
As mentioned previously, if this work was to be conducted further, it would be relevant 
to complete the present analysis with considerations on a larger scale: the overall design 
of the apartment, its dimensions, the influence of its depth and external-surface-to-floor-
ratio could be studied. As mentioned in the literature review, very few studies assess the 
impact of compactness on flexibility, as well as the position of internal walls with respect 
to the sources of heat (the windows in particular). Moreover, the present analysis showed 
the importance for flexibility of the orientation and position of the apartment in the 
building: flexible design could be thought from the point of view of the building as a 
whole, and the possibility of synergies between different apartments subjected to 
different conditions due to their position could be contemplated. Eventually, it would 
have been of great interest to be able to couple this study, which is entirely based on a 
simulation model of the apartment, to actual measurements in the investigated building. 
The edifice still being under construction, it was impossible, but this aspect could be the 
topic of a further study. The influence of the occupants and of the outdoor conditions 
would have been made clearer and the evaluation of indoor comfort more detailed by 
distributing questionnaires to the occupants. 
As a last remark, even though it was tried to set up a variation range for the parameters 
that would be a realistic reflection of the possibilities, theoretical interest prevailed over

for the only reason that water has an important volumetric thermal capacity, and 2-

penetration depth limit of concrete.

Chapter 9 
Conclusion 
 
The present study deals with design of buildings as a tool to improve their ability to 
retain heat and thus adapt changes in heating schedule, the end goal being to provide 
energy flexibility to the heating network. The main objectives of this work were: to 
develop a tool to assess heat flexibility in a building; to understand which of the building 
parameters influence its flexibility potential and in which way each of them contributes 
to it; to assess the influence of uncontrollable parameters on flexibility, such as the 
outdoor conditions and the occupants’ behaviour. 
Complete heat cut-offs at different moments of the day were applied to an IDA ICE 
model of an apartment located in Nordhavn district and its flexibility was assessed with 
respects to the speed of the temperature drop. The peak in heating power after a cut-off 
period was also studied and used as another tool to evaluate adaptability to a changing 
heating pattern. 
It was found that the design parameters with the most influence on the temperature 
drop in the inhabited space after a heat cut-off are those impacting the heat losses 
through the building envelope. A building with less than 10 centimeters insulation in the 
external walls is for example very sensitive to a heat cut-off; this sensitivity decreases 
when approaching higher insulation levels but this parameter remains the one showing 
the largest impact on flexibility. The U-value of the windows has a linear impact on the 
temperature decrease speed and its variation can change the duration of the comfort 
period from simple to quadruple. Heat retention time strongly decreases when the 
glazing-to-external-wall ratio increases, showing that the losses due to a larger glazed 
surface impact more the indoor temperature than the increased solar gains when the 
window gets larger. Placing the apartment on a corner of the building, thus introducing 
a larger glazed area, identically shows negative effects on flexibility. 
The orientation of the apartment shows a moderated but clear impact on heat retention: 
any other orientation than the South one reduces by around 40% the time in the comfort 
range after a cut-off. 
As to the thickness and position of the heat storage layers, their impact is less important 
than expected. Positioning the concrete layer of the external walls on the inside gives a 
slightly better result than other configurations, but the difference is not striking. The 
layer which thickness has the most significant influence on flexibility is the floor slab, 
since it embeds the heating pipes. The internal walls’ thickness comes second in terms of 
influence, and the thickness of the concrete layer of the external walls shows the least 
influence, in particular for early cut-offs. The material used for the internal walls mass 
shows almost no impact on flexibility. It was also shown that increasing the floor slab 
thickness to more than 12 centimeters does not lead to any flexibility improvement, and 
the same conclusion can be drawn for external walls with more than 15 centimeters 
concrete. 
91 
 
==PAGE:106==
Chapter 9 
Conclusion 
 
An important finding is that the higher the UA-value of the envelope, the larger the 
positive impact of thermal mass on the flexibility result. In particular, when reducing the 
glazing-to-wall ratio, the impact of both internal and external wall thicknesses gets more 
significant. The first step in ensuring flexibility is therefore to guarantee that the 
envelope shows a sufficient thermal resistance. Thermal storage mechanisms come in a 
second place. 
Performing a heat cut-off from 7 AM to 4 PM does not lead to any significant change in 
energy consumption that could be correlated to any design change. However, such an 
operation creates a heat power consumption peak around 4 PM that is influenced by the 
building design. The relative height of this peak with respects to the peak power in 
normal conditions is not impacted at all by thermal storage in the building structure, 
but is greatly influenced by the thermal resistance of the envelope, namely by the 
insulation thickness and U-value. However, according to the present calculation methods, 
the main influence on the peak power seems to come from the presence of solar gains: 
the peak gets twice higher than normal when the main façade is oriented North, East or 
West, while it is only 20% higher than normal when the apartment is oriented South. 
Similarly, while smaller windows guarantee a better heat retention, they also lead to an 
important increase of the peak power (up to 50% higher than in normal conditions). 
A sensitivity analysis was then carried out assessing the robustness of the obtained 
results with respects to the simplifications made for the uncertain factors that are the 
outdoor temperature, solar gains, occupants’ number and activity. The temperature drop 
following a cut-off in the reference apartment is greatly influenced by solar gains and 
outdoor temperature: as an example, the duration of the comfort period in the living 
room can be multiplied by 3 from a case with no solar gains to a case with a large solar 
gains profile, and by 4 when temperature goes from -12 to 0°C. This result makes the 
previous results impossible to apply as such to a building operated in real conditions: the 
values of the indicators have to be pondered by the bias of outdoor conditions. 
As to occupancy, an unoccupied apartment retains temperature above comfort levels 
around 40% less time than an apartment occupied with a family of 4, and working 
occupants give a 30% decrease in duration of the comfort period with respect to a 
holiday situation. The heat gains from occupants and appliances show some significance 
in the flexibility results, which makes the prediction of the flexibility potential difficult, 
since forecasting the occupants’ behaviour embeds a large share of error. 
It was moreover shown that the versions of the apartment with the poorest thermal 
performance were those which flexibility potential was the least sensitive to outdoor 
conditions, which means that internal or external gains can only improve flexibility if the 
envelope shows sufficient resistance performances. 
On the basis of all these results, the improvements that can be proposed for the existing 
building are not numerous, the reference case being close to optimal. However, a thicker 
floor slab, increasing from 6 to 12 centimeters thick, shows an interesting potential in 
prolonging indoor comfort after a cut-off and also showed to slightly reduce the heating 
power peak. On the contrary, the dimensions of the external walls seem overrated in 
terms of flexibility performance: if not needed for structural reasons, their thickness 
92 
 
==PAGE:107==

could be reduced. Moreover, the large size of the windows is an impediment for 
flexibility due to thermal losses: the glazing-to-floor ratio could be decreased from 0.42 to 
0.3 in order to guarantee better performances, without increasing the peak level. 
However, the choice of the glazed surface also embeds a lot of aesthetical considerations, 
and daylight has an important impact on well-being indoors. A solution to improve 
flexibility while keeping the same glazing-to-wall ratio would be to use thick curtains or 
blinds that would be drawn during the coldest and the hottest periods, keeping 
respectively heat or freshness inside – but the efficiency of this system depends on the 
occupants’ behaviour. 
A version of the apartment with smaller windows and a thicker floor was investigated for 
a yearly simulation under realistic weather conditions. The new version showed smaller 
temperature fluctuations than the original version, with lower temperature peaks in 
summer and a globally higher temperature in winter. This resulted in an overall better 
indoor comfort all year long. A daily schedule was set for the heating setpoint, with the 
goal of distributing heat supply between offices and dwellings in Nordhavn district. The 
modifications brought to the apartment permit to slightly minimize the degradation of 
indoor comfort in the spaces by limiting temperature drops. 
 
This work has permitted to understand the mechanisms that lead to intake and 
retention of heat indoors, which give the district heating operator the possibility to apply 
restrictions on heat supply when best for the system as a whole. The importance of an 
optimal building design is now obvious, and the role of the main building components 
has been clarified. Some more investigations dealing with different building shapes and 
indoor space organisation could complete this work, and help forming a set of guidelines 
for an optimal building design with focus on heat flexibility. By being able to take 
advantage of all surrounding sources of heat and to resist changes in both outdoor 
conditions and heat supply, the building stock could on the long term gain an active role 
in the energy grid in which it is integrated. This change will require some decades to be 
carried through. However, the present years are the starting phase of an exciting process 
towards new urban energy systems, taking advantage of the potential of all stakeholders 
to provide security of supply while optimally operating the installed capacity, with on 
the long run the aim of a shift towards more fluctuating, decentralized, carbon-neutral 
sources.

Appendix 1 
Parallel study – time constant 
 
This investigation was carried out as a preliminary study in order to figure out if the 
time constant could constitute a performing flexibility indicator. Eventually, the goal 
was to determine the accuracy of the time constant theory when calculated on a complex 
building. Indeed, the theory is demonstrated for a very simplified case: a simple zone, 
initially at equilibrium with the outdoor conditions – which implies no variation in solar 
gains or internal gains, no sudden window openings nor changes in ventilation flowrates, 
etc. Calculating the time constant of a real building in operation is therefore likely to be 
unprecise.  
The first step of this evaluation was to calculate the time constant with its different 
methods on a very simplified building model simulated on IDA ICE and evaluate the 
differences. The idea was to make the building more and more complex, until obtaining a 
result for the Nordhavn building. The idea was finally abandoned, the time constant 
theory being too simplified for the targeted applications and its improvement by 
numerical methods fastidious. This appendix presents the first step of this study. 
1 Model description 
In order to find a valid method to measure the time constant on a complex building 
modelled in IDA ICE, a shoebox building was first created with the following properties: 
• 
Single zone with no partition walls: dimensions: 2.5×4×2.6m 
• 
No windows 
• 
Two external walls facing North and South – 0.25m concrete, total 13m2 
• 
All other surfaces are internal – 0.15m concrete, total 40.8m2 
• 
No ventilation nor heating system 
• 
No thermal bridges 
• 
No infiltration 
• 
No occupants nor internal gains 
• 
Outdoor conditions: no solar gains, no wind, relative humidity 60%, sky 
temperature equal to the air temperature.

Theoretical calculation 
The total heat capacity of the building as well as its transmission heat loss coefficient 
(there are no ventilation and no infiltration) were calculated based on the properties of 
the materials and the building time constant was calculated from Equation (4). 
However, the way internal walls are treated in IDA ICE had to be considered in the 
calculation. The walls oriented East and West as well as the ceiling and floor are 
internal, which means they are only in contact with the building body and not with the 
outside. They are thus considered adiabatic by the program, just as if they were 
separating two rooms with the exact same conditions. Therefore, half of their thermal 
mass is considered belonging to the other side. Considering the whole internal thermal 
mass in Equation (4) leads to an overestimation of the building time constant: only half 
of the internal thermal mass has to be inserted into the equation.

Experimental calculation: 63% method 
The outdoor temperature was in a first phase constant and the building was left in 
equilibrium with the outdoor environment, with an indoor temperature equal to the 
outdoor temperature. The outdoor temperature was then suddenly dropping and the 
indoor temperature in the building was measured during the transition phase. Since no 
additional gains were considered, the air temperature in the building ended up being 
equal to the final temperature outside. 
The time series of the indoor temperature was retrieved. Knowing the starting 
temperature T0 and the final temperature T∞, the time at which the indoor temperature 
satisfies Equation (7) was found – it is equal to the time constant. 
Experimental calculation: logarithm method 
The same drop in outdoor temperature was experimented and the series of the indoor 
temperature retrieved. ln( T(t) −T∞)  was then calculated and plotted over time. A 
linear trendline was added and the time constant of the building was calculated from the 
slope of the trendline, according to Equation (8). If the trend is indeed linear, this 
method is the one reflecting the actual time constant of the building.

Appendix 2 
Data retrieving 
 
1 IDA ICE version tree 
In order to be able to retrieve the results automatically through Matlab, the different 
simulations had to be thoroughly organised in IDA ICE. 
Each parameter was given a four-letter code; the list can be found in Table 6 (Section 0). 
Each version of the parameter was assigned a number (from 1 to the number of 
investigated values for the parameter), and was added the mention of the cut-off time 
after an underscore dash. As an example, the simulation investigating the effect of a 10-
centimeter thick insulation layer in the external walls for a cut-off at 4 PM was called: 
EXCT-3_16 
EXCT being the code for the insulation thickness, 10 centimeters being the third 
investigated value (after 0 and 5 centimeters) and 16 the cut-off time. 
The simulation investigating the overconsumption and peak level was called using the 
same nomenclature for the parameter code and version, and ending with ‘_over’: for 
example, for the same parameter and version as previously, the code would be: 
EXCT-3_over 
All these simulations were performed using the version handling function of IDA ICE. A 
tree was created, with at its base the reference model. Children versions were created by 
changing the design but without performing any cut-off: these are called by their 
parameter and version coded, such as: EXCT-3. The versions with the different cut-offs 
and overconsumption calculation are defined as grandchildren versions. Part of the tree, 
showing some children and grandchildren versions, is seen in Figure 61. The columns 
show an overview of the evolution of different quantities over the simulation time, such 
as the operative temperature of different rooms, the relative humidity in some rooms, or 
the supply and return temperatures of the HVAC system. 
The advantage of this system is that a modification in a parent model is automatically 
reproduced in the child version. It was therefore easy to correct some mistakes in the 
original model, or carry out some experiments by changing a whole branch of the tree at 
once. Moreover, one command is enough to launch all the simulations one after the 
other, which represents a precious gain of time given the important number of 
simulations to be performed. 
After the simulations were performed, the results were all automatically stored on the 
computer drive in a folder named after the simulation, thus using the same code.

Appendix 5 
Reference model – lighting strategy 
 
Artificial lighting 
No standards could be found about lighting requirements in residential buildings. DS-EN 
12464 (Part 1 - Lighting in indoor workspaces) [71] gives some recommendations for 
different types of spaces and activities in office buildings. Even though lighting needs are 
dependent on user preferences, indications can be retrieved from this document and 
adapted. In particular, the illuminance level required at 0.8m (working plane) in 
canteens, bathrooms, lounge areas and common rooms for students is 200 lux; zones 
where focus is necessary are advised to be provided with an illuminance level at 
workplane height of 300 lux and above depending on the task effectuated. An 
illuminance of 100 lux at floor level is required in circulation areas. For the simulated 
apartments, illuminance levels were thus set to 200 lux in the rooms, living room, 
bathrooms and depot room, and to 300 lux in the kitchen corner. 
The BR15 regulation gives as a guidance value a luminous efficacy of at least 50 lm/W 
for domestic luminaires, as well as an automatic daylight-controlled lighting system if 
achievable. Luminous efficacies for different luminaire types were retrieved and it was 
chosen to implement compact fluorescent lamps with an efficacy of 60 lm/W. The LEDs 
used in the kitchen have an efficacy of 65 lm/W. In order to calculate the necessary 
power in every room, the following formula was applied:

Daylight control of artificial light 
Artificial lighting is assumed to be turned off when daylight is sufficient: this command 
was performed with an IDA ICE macro. The controller used is based on the thermostat 
model, with an illuminance setpoint at workplane height and a deadband of 100 lux. The 
setpoint for all rooms and the living room is 250 lux while it is 350 lux in the kitchen 
zone. The system is also linked to the occupancy schedule as well as to a day-night 
schedule: if the time is between 7AM and 11PM, if the occupants are present in the 
room and if daylight is providing less than 300 lux in the room (respectively 400 lux in 
the kitchen), light turns on. If daylight provides more than 200 lux (respectively 300 
lux), if the occupants are not present or if it is night, light is turned down. 
Shading control 
Shading was assumed to be manually drawn by the occupants in reaction to a too high 
daylight level. The shading type was chosen from IDA ICE database and is called “Light, 
tightly woven internal drape”. It was chosen for its likelihood in the case of an apartment 
building, instead of systems such as blinds inserted in-between the panes or dark drapes. 
It was also considered average in terms of light reduction and thermal transfer 
limitation; its properties are detailed in Table 13. Using this type of curtains permits for 
example to reduce the overall solar gains (g-value) by 56%.

The control used relies on a proportional controller based on the illuminance level, with 
a deadband situated between 1500 and 2000 lux. Below 1500 lux, the users are assumed 
to keep the blinds open; above 2000 lux, considered as a discomfort threshold, they are 
completely closed. In-between these two boundaries, the occupants are supposed to draw 
the curtains proportionally to the illuminance level. This model is not completely 
realistic since it assumes that the occupant has perfect information of illuminance levels 
and a constant sensitivity to it. However, it reflects the overall blind drawing mechanism 
and presents an interesting performance in terms of computation time. 
Measurement of illuminance in IDA ICE can be made at the first occupant (the user can 
place it in the room) or as an average over the room. This parameter can only been set 
once for all necessary daylight measurement. Ideally, daylight-dependent control of 
artificial lighting should rely on a measurement of average illuminance in the room, while 
drawing blinds should be a reaction to a punctual excessive illuminance where the 
occupant is standing – probably close to the windows. A compromise was therefore found 
by placing the occupant in the center of the room and using the illuminance 
measurement at his position to control both lighting and shading.

Appendix 7 
Specific study: GTW ratio of 0.3 
 
In the sensitivity study carried out in Section 6.4, it was found that the flexibility results 
of an apartment with a glazing-to-external wall ratio of 0.3 were in general more 
sensitive to solar gains than expected. This impact of solar gains was calculated by 
simulating the building with no solar gains and large solar gains, and by calculating the 
relative difference of the flexibility outputs. 
In order to understand this phenomenon, it is necessary to look into the temperature 
profiles for different glazing-to-wall ratios when cutting heat off. 
Figure 62 shows the operative temperature in the living room for the first week after a 
cut-off at 7 AM, for the 5 buildings with different glazing-to-wall ratios (GTWR). The 
low curves correspond to the case no solar gains are considered, and the high curves to 
the case of large solar gains. 
When focusing on the cases without solar gains, it is made clear that the smaller the 
glazed area, the smaller the losses and thus the slower the temperature drop. The five 
curves are ordered according to the glazing-to-wall ratio. 
However, when looking at the cases with large solar gains, the order is perturbed. It is 
seen that the smaller the glazing-to-wall ratio, the more regular the temperature profile: 
little gains around noon and little losses at night. On the contrary, a large glazing-to-
wall ratio means a very high temperature peak around noon and a very fast temperature
