Abstract
Verification of the actual operation of software systems is unquestionably important from
a business perspective. Any deviations and discrepancies in business processes can
have a significant impact on aspects such as costs generated by the company or cus­
tomer satisfaction with services provided. Therefore, the detection of the previously men­
tioned inconsistencies can have a significant impact on a company’s wider success, or
lack thereof.
This thesis proposes a solution that makes it possible to generate a business process
model on the basis of real­time events generated by the process­aware information sys­
tem (PAIS). To achieve this, it uses the Split Miner process discovery algorithm, streaming
data handling methods: Sliding Window, Lossy Counting and Lossy Counting with Bud­
get, and streaming process mining environment ­ Beamline. By appropriately adapting
and connecting the aforementioned, the thesis extends the set of available methods for
online process mining with a new solution ­ Online Split Miner. Moreover, experimental
results performed on various types of event logs are reported.

Introduction
Developing a series of steps to achieve a given goal has been known to humanity for
centuries. Without a shadow of a doubt, in ancient Rome, those responsible for making
critical decisions had to do a lot of thinking to properly carry out the actions that led to the
creation of such power.
In the era of industrial growth, despite the diversity of the goals of different organisations,
humankind is still trying to develop a set of steps to enable their organisations to achieve
their goals. Such a set of steps, activities, or tasks is called a process. The notion of
the (business) process has evolved, and it isn’t easy to unequivocally give credit for the
emergence of this concept to one person.
Undoubtedly, one such person was Frederick Taylor. In [1], he explained how the indus­
trial process quality could significantly improve by applying a scientific approach. The
main principles he listed describe the need for the scientific study of tasks by managers,
scientific selection and training of workers, cooperation between workers and people in
charge, and worker motivation. In [2], Peter Drucker pointed out how organisations can
benefit from distributing responsibility among workers and managers, understanding the
customers’ needs, and decision­making based on strategic planning. The list of people
who have contributed to shaping and understanding business processes is long, and it is
impossible to mention everyone who has had a hand in it.
However, despite many guidelines, executing processes optimally is still a significant chal­
lenge for many organisations. In particular, in the era of digitisation and the presence of
computer systems in most industries, given that software engineers often lack an ade­
quate understanding of the business domain, this challenge becomes even more difficult.
Process mining [3, Chapter 2] is a set of techniques, methods, and tools supporting build­
ing, understanding, verifying, and extending business processes based on the data gen­
erated by business process­aware information systems (PAIS). This data is a set of event
logs [3, Chapter 5]. An event log describes the actual, not hypothetical, course of busi­
ness processes. The standard (offline) approach in process mining is based on analysing
static files containing historical data that serve as the input for the selected process mining
methods.
However, finding all kinds of irregularities when the institution overseeing the business
processes in question does not exist or when those processes have changed will not
bring any value.
Online (streaming) process mining is built on top of offline process mining. The main dif­
ference between this and offline approaches is that the data is provided in real­time. In this
attitude, an event stream [4] [5] consists of an unbounded sequence of data frames, each
corresponding to one business event. Then, these events are processed by a selected
process mining method, which updates its results based on newly observed events.

continuous optimisation and analysis of business processes. As the availability of real­
time data increases, so does the scope for using streaming process mining. Since the

Background and problem statement
This chapter introduces the domain of the problem ­ process mining. Section 2.1 de­
scribes the essence and the structure of event logs. Section 2.2 presents popular ways
to present a business process model. The selected algorithms that discover these models
are described in 2.3. Section 2.4 discusses metrics for assessing the quality of a given
process model. The core part of the domain problem ­ streaming process discovery is
discussed in Section 2.5, including state­of­the­art analysis. The problem statement is
formulated in Section 2.6, including the research questions of this thesis.
Process mining techniques can be split into three categories
• Process discovery ­ the main goal of this group of techniques is to, based on the
event data, construct a process model describing certain business processes.
• Conformance checking ­ allows for comparison between observed behaviour and
the modelled or discovered behaviour, represented as a process model.
• Process enhancement ­ a set of techniques that focuses on analysing business
processes recorded in an event log to make it more efficient.

Before getting started with process mining, one should read the Process Mining Manifesto
[7]. It is a reference point for process mining researchers and developers, created by a
group of scientists specialising in the area of process mining. By formulating guiding prin­
ciples and challenges, it creates a common understanding of process mining foundations,
goals and limitations.
2.1
Event logs
An event log contains structured information on business processes and is crucial in the
context of carrying out process mining operations. Organisations often use and develop
more than one system to achieve their goals. As an example, let’s consider some hypo­
thetical e­commerce organisation that, apart from using the central system to carry out
purchases/sales, uses other systems for invoicing, customer relationship management
and shipping products to customers. Each system may have its own database and emits
its own business event. Therefore, the information about the processes is distributed
across many data sources. To perform any process mining operation, it is required to
extract the data from these sources and, based on that data, create an event log that may
serve as an input allowing process mining.
The single event recorded in the event log has the following structure:
• Case ID ­ an identifier of the process instance, allows for grouping related events.
• Activity ­ what task/job has actually happened.
• Timestamp ­ when was the particular event observed.
It may also contain some extra information about any associated data, such as information
about whether the entity responsible for executing the activity is a human or what was the
total duration of the activity.
The preparation of an appropriate dataset is a major challenge. Inconsistencies between
traces, incomplete traces, and duplicate records are problems that can significantly affect
the final results obtained when working with such data. For this reason, after extracting
an event log from relevant data sources, in order to get reliable data, the event log can
be filtered and cleaned. There are many ways to tackle the data quality issue, many of
which are explained in [8].
2.2
Process model
There are many ways to represent a process model ­ that is, a business process de­
piction ­ such as Transition Systems, Petri Nets, Workflow Nets, Yet Another Workflow
Language (YAWL), Business Process Model and Notation (BPMN), Event­Driven Pro­
cess Chains (EPCs), Casual Nets, Process Trees [3, Chapter 3]. Although at first glance,
it may seem that mapping one notation to another is pretty straightforward ­ after all, they
are all meant to be used to describe process models ­ each notation is different, has dif­
ferent advantages and disadvantages, and mapping between the notations may be very
complicated.
When the need arises to switch from one notation to another, it is very important to make
sure that they are trace equivalent. Two models are trace equivalent if both of them allow
for the same execution sequences. Verification of trace equivalence is not a trivial task and
often requires employing additional techniques, such as model checking [9]. However, it
is important to note that even though the models may be trace equivalent, they may have
a different structure.

In this section, the following discovery algorithms are discussed: Alpha Miner, Heuristics
Miner and Split Miner. In addition to these, there are many other algorithms such as Fuzzy
Miner [10] or Inductive Miner [11].
2.3.1
Alpha Miner
Alpha algorithm [12] is one of the earliest process discovery algorithms. As is often the
case with first solutions for particular problems, it is not a robust solution, and may not
perform well when applied to use in a real­world situation. However, it is the foundation on
which subsequent solutions have been developed and gives a correct view on the subject
of process discovery.
The Alpha algorithm takes an event log as an input; as an output, it returns a Petri Net
Split Miner process discovery technique in streaming process mining environments
5
==PAGE:14==
of the mined model. Before having an overview of what the Alpha algorithm does, the
concepts of the ordering relations between tasks should be presented.
• Direct succession ­ Task A is in direct succession relation with Task B if, at least
in one case, Task B occurs after Task A, with no other tasks between them.
• Causality ­ Task A is in causal relation with Task B if Task A is in direct succession
relation with Task B, but Task B is not in direct succession relation with Task A.
• Parallel ­ Task A is in parallel relation with Task B, if Task A is in direct succession
relation with Task B, and Task B is in direct succession relation with Task A.
• Choice ­ Task A is in choice relation with Task B, if Task A is not in direct succession
relation with Task B, and Task B is not in direct succession relation with Task A.
The main steps of the Alpha algorithm are:
1. Create the set of all transitions Tw, where each activity execution in the event log
corresponds to one transition.
2. Create the set of the initial transitions Ti ­ transitions that, in at least one trace, are
executed first.
3. Create the set of the final transitions To ­ transitions that, in at least one trace, are
executed last.
4. Create a set of pairs Xw = (A, B), where A and B are subsets of Tw and each
transition in A is in causal relation with each transition in B, while all the transitions
in A, and all transitions in B are in choice relation.
5. Create a set of pairs Yw = (A, B) by retaining the maximal sets from Xw.
6. Create a set of places Pw for each pair in Yw; moreover, create one place for input
and one for output.
7. Create a set Fw of relations between already created places Pw and transitions
Yw, additionally create the relations between input place and transitions from Ti,
analogically for output place and To.
8. Create a Petri Net based on Pw, Tw, Fw.
Even though the original version of the Alpha algorithm is able to mine a large class of
models, it has some limitations (short­loops, non­local dependencies) [Chaper 5][3] that
in many cases may completely disqualify it from use in certain environments.
2.3.2
Heuristics Miner
Heuristics Miner is the process discovery algorithm that uses the ordering relations intro­
duced with Alpha Miner. The main principle of the Heuristics Miner is to count frequencies
of ordering relations between the tasks from the event log in order to tackle the noise. It
also uses two new relations that were not used by the Alpha Miner:
• Short loop ­ Task A forms a short loop with Task B if, at least in one case, Task B
occurs after Task B, and after Task B, Task A occurs again.
• Long distance ­ Task A is in long­distance relation with Task B if Task B occurs after
Task A, with one or more tasks occurring between them.
The main steps of the Heuristics Miner are:

1. Create the dependency relation matrix of causality relation between all tasks ob­
served in the log.
2. Create the set of non­observable tasks ­ AND/XOR splits and joins, based on the
Causal Matrix [13].
3. Update the Causal Matrix with extra dependencies based on long­distance relations.
Herictis Miner can produce a noise and low­frequent behaviour­free model. This is what
makes it more robust when compared to Alpha Miner. Heuristics Miner details and eval­
uation results can be found in [14] [15].
2.3.3
Split Miner
Split Miner is the last process discovery algorithm discussed in this section. As with pre­
vious algorithms, as an input, it gets the event log. Then, in six steps it creates a BPMN
process model. Its main goal is to create a simple model while maintaining the confor­
mance metrics balanced. According to [6], the majority of process discovery techniques,
when applied for real­world logs, for example, Heuristics Miner produce large, unintel­
ligible and behaviourally incorrect models. On the other hand, other methods, such as
Inductive Miner, produce behaviourally correct models that are poor in terms of precision.
A description of concepts such as precision is in Section 2.4, together with a description
of the other conformance metrics.
The previously mentioned steps of this algorithm are as follows:
1. Create Directly­Follows Graph (DFG), detect self­loops and short­loops.
2. Detect concurrent tasks in DFG
3. Filter previously created DFG by pruning concurrent and infrequent edges. Then,
using a specially designed Dijkstra algorithm [16] adaptation, for each node in Pruned
DFG (PDFG), keep only the best incoming and outgoing edges unless the edge fre­
quency occurrence is above the predefined threshold.
4. Discover splits based on direct succession and concurrent relations.
5. Discover joins based on Refined Process Structure Tree (RPST) [17] of the current
model. Depending on each single­entry­single­exit (SESE) node (SESE fragments
are nodes of RPST) content establish the type of split gateway.
6. Minimise OR joins usage by analysing the flow in the current model.
These steps broadly describe the Split Miner algorithm presented in [6]. However, the
authors of the Split Miner decided to take steps in order to improve the original Split Miner,
and as a result, they came up with Split Miner 2.0 [18]. The main improvements of Split
Miner 2.0 are revised concurrency detection that uses start and end timestamps, and
enhancement of the split discovery step by allowing inclusive decisions (OR­splits).
2.4
Conformance checking
The process model is beneficial; after all, it gives insight into the procedures taking place
in the organisation and is an entry point for possible optimisations. Nevertheless, the
situation becomes complicated when the process model is nowhere near its actual course.
Such a model does not carry all the features that business people care about, which
makes its value negligible. In such cases, conformance checking is very helpful.
Conformance checking helps to determine how the process model reproduces the actual
course of events, in this case, represented in the event log. It is a set of tools that allows

The motivation for employing conformance checking has its root in corporate governance,
risk, compliance, and legislation (Sarbanes–Oxley Act (SOX), Basel II); ISO 9001:2008
that requires modelling of the operational businesses; the desire for business alignment
(ensuring that the information system is aligned with the business process) and auditing
(evaluating the business process). These regulations and guidelines do not demand that
organisations check conformance at such a fine­grained level as the level of business
events. However, the link to conformance checking is clear.
There are four quality criteria that describe the process model:
• Fitness ­ the model should allow the behaviour observed in the event log
• Precision ­ the model should not allow the behaviour not observed in the event log
• Generalization ­ the model should generalize the behaviour observed in the event
log
• Simplicity ­ the model should be as simple as possible
It is a big challenge to find a well­balanced process model, as these criteria are conflicting
with each other (Fig. 2.6). As it can be seen in Fig. 2.5a, for the presented model, which is
also called the Flower Model, the fitness is very high because it is possible to replay every
possible trace of the log containing the activities included, but it has a bad fitness score.
On the other hand, as presented in Fig. 2.5b, the model that allows the execution of only
a limited set of sequences has a high fitness and precision score, but it lacks simplicity
and generalization.
A detailed description of the conformance checking, including the evaluation for different
types of process models, can be found in [19] [20].

2.5
Streaming process discovery
Although the classical approach to process discovery can be very fruitful, situations arise
in the current world where we need to know what the process model is ad hoc and the
event log is not prepared. Suppose a situation where, due to a suboptimal business pro­
cess, an organization loses customers or money overnight. In a case where the company
goes bankrupt, from the management’s perspective, identifying the shortcomings of a
particular process may not be helpful at all.
Such situations can be prevented by streaming process discovery, also known as online
process discovery. While the classical process discovery approach focuses on static
event log files as input, the streaming process discovery processes event streams [4] [5]
characterised by a very high throughput.
Event stream is an unbounded data stream in which each data frame contains the infor­
mation stored in a single event observation in the classical process discovery approach.
According to [21] [22], there is a short list of assumptions that can give a better under­
standing of the streaming process discovery environment:
• Data frame is small ­ it has a small number of properties.
• Process discovery algorithm should be able to process an infinite amount of data
without failing.
• Data frame has a controlled number of possible class labels for classification.
• The number of resources (memory) used to run the process discovery algorithms is
significantly smaller than the incoming data (over a reasonable period of time).
• The algorithms should work within certain performance boundaries.
• Data streams are stationary or evolving.
There are four main concepts for handling event streams in terms of process discovery
[23]:
• Window Models ­ store a ”window” of most recent events; the window serves as an
input for offline process mining technique.
• Problem Reduction ­ employing selected stream processing techniques reduces the
problem of streaming process discovery into a well­studied problem, for example,
a frequency counting problem. The algorithms that face the frequency counting
problem are for instance Lossy Counting [24], or Lossy Counting with Budget [25].
• Offline Computation ­ split the streaming process discovery problems into smaller
sub­problems and check if some of these can be handled without considering the
aspect of streaming data.
• Hybrid Approaches ­ a combination of the above approaches.
Streaming process discovery is not as mature as its offline counterpart. Nevertheless,
considerable progress has been made toward the development of this branch of process
mining over the past years.
In [26], Andrea Burattin and Alessandro Sperduti propose an adaptation of Heuristics
Miner so that it can be used to work with the event stream. The authors propose three
adaptations, one based on Sliding Window [4, Chapter 8], the other one based on Lossy
Counting, and finally, the Lossy Counting with Budget.

speed up the search when the cost of comparing the entire key is large, to get the se­
quential patterns of events from the stream [29]. StrProm tracks the directly follows rela­
tionship and updates prefix­trees using Lossy Counting with Budget. The process models
are generated from prefix trees once a decaying mechanism that adjusts the importance
of events based on the time of their occurrence is applied.
In [30], an online extension of the block­structured process discovery algorithm [31] was
introduced. The algorithm creates blocks of a partial event log and recognizes blocks that
have changed using matrix composition. However, the assumption that the partial event
log contains only complete traces limits this method’s usability spectrum.
In 2017, in [32] Sebastiaan J. van Zelst, Boudewijn F. van Dongen, and Wil M. P. van
der Aalst proposed the S­BAR (Stream­Based Abstract Representation) architecture as
a mechanism for keeping an updated representation of the event stream. The S­BAR
architecture is a generic architecture that allows for the adoption of several classes of
existing process discovery techniques in the context of event streams. The following
algorithms: Alpha Miner, Heuristics Miner and Inductive Miner, were presented in collab­
oration with the S­BAR architecture. Here, the problem reduction to frequency counting
was performed using Lossy Counting, Space Saving [33], and Frequent [34].
To examine any online process mining solution, an appropriate environment is needed.
The most popular open­source software tools for process mining, such as ProM 2 or
PM4Py3 were not created with a focus on streaming process mining. Nevertheless, they
still offer many plugins and packages that support it. However, the goal of the community
developing these tools is the entire spectrum of tools related to process mining, not only
those associated with the streaming branch.
In 2022 Andrea Burattin developed the Beamline framework [23] that mainly aims to facil­
itate the prototyping and development of streaming process mining algorithms. Beamline
is an open­source framework4, built on top of Apache Flink [35], allowing its users to imple­
ment the process mining techniques, benefitting from pre/post­processing event transfor­
mations included in Apache Flink, extended by process mining transformations provided
by Beamline such as process­aware event filters or flat­mappers. All of the available
Apache Flink connectors5 can also be plugged into Beamline. Moreover, Beamline also
supports replaying logs coming from static code (Java String6) or event log files (XES7 or
CSV), and changes their static nature to streamed data, making it easier to develop new
methods or test already implemented ones. Other software systems, e.g. databases,
may consume the results of the processing performed by Beamline.

3.4
Streaming data handling
As described in the previous chapter, there are four main concepts for handling event
streams. The proposed solution supports the Sliding Window, Lossy Counting and Lossy
Counting with Budget.
Depending on the variant, the data these variants keep has a different structure. Sliding
Window keeps track of a set of recently observed events. Lossy Counting and Lossy
Counting with Budget monitor two types of data structures:
• Lossy Counting Relations/Lossy Counting Budget Relations ­ data structure storing
tuples R = (A, B), where A is the activity directly followed by activity B. The Lossy
Counting estimated frequency is based on the number of occurrences of a directly
follows relation between the two activities.
• Lossy Counting Cases/Lossy Counting Budget Cases ­ data structure storing tuples
C = (a1, ai−1, ai, S1, S2) where a1 is the first activity observed within a case, ai−1 and
ai are successively second to last and latest activity observed within a case. Based
on ai−1 and ai, whenever the new event is observed, it is possible to determine if
the case contains any short loops or self­loops and if that is the case S1 (self­loops)
and S2 (short loops) sets are updated. The Lossy Counting estimated frequency is
based on the number of events observations within a single case.
Sliding Window variant is configured by the window length parameter. Lossy Counting
approach is configured by the maximum approximation error parameter. Lossy Counting
with Budget method has two budget parameters instead of one. The first one is called
cases budget ­ the maximum number of tracked Lossy Counting Budget Cases. The
second one ­ relations budget is the maximum number of tracked Lossy Counting Budget
Relations.
3.5
Process discovery
The selected process discovery method is the Split Miner discovery algorithm. However,
to enable the application of this technique in the streaming environment, the algorithm,
instead of the event log, should take as input any output produced by the node responsible
for handling streamed event data.
As described in the previous section, the observed streaming data has two formats ­ one
for Sliding Window and another one for Lossy Counting and Lossy Counting with Budget.
Compatibility with the Sliding Window is pretty straightforward. The event log, which is the
input for the offline version of Split Miner, is a set of all events of the particular business
process. Therefore, if the Sliding Window keeps track of a certain number of these events,
the process discovery algorithm behaves as if it was an event log.
In the case of Lossy Counting and Lossy Counting with Budget, the situation is different.
The frequency­counting­based methods provide a set of properties as the starting point
of the process discovery.
• Starting activities ­ set of initial activities from the data tracked by Lossy Counting
(Budget) Cases.

• End activities ­ set of last activities from the data tracked by Lossy Counting (Budget)
Cases.
• Self­loops ­ combined set of self­loops from the data tracked by Lossy Counting
(Budget) Cases.
• Short loops ­ combined set of short loops from the data tracked by Lossy Counting
(Budget) Cases.
• Relations ­ set of directly following relations from the data tracked by Lossy Counting

and ”Short­loops detection”, are skipped in this case. The process discovery starts with

4.4.1
Sliding Window Split Miner
In the case of Sliding Window, the situation is trivial, as the data collected by the Sliding
Window serve as the entry point for DFG creation. The entire procedure is presented in
Algorithm 1. Whenever a new event is observed, if the maximum window size is reached
the oldest event is removed from the window, and the newly observed one is added. Then,
if the number of events is a multiple of the refresh rate, a new model is generated.
4.4.2
Lossy Counting Split Miner
With Lossy Counting the situation is different. As mentioned in the conceptual descrip­
tion, this method does not keep track of the chronological sequence of observed events,
thus there is a need to provide information about starting activities, end activities, self­
loops, short loops and directly following relations before launching the process discovery
algorithm. Therefore, the procedure is adjusted in the following way, as presented in the
Algorithm 2. Whenever a new event is observed, the current bucket is calculated based
on the number of events processed so far, and the bucket width calculated based on the
approximation error given as an argument. The new event is then added to the LC Cases
(Lossy Counting Cases) structure ­ Algorithm 3. On condition that the LC Cases holds
the information about the case with an ID equal to the new event’s ID, it means that it
is possible to add a new dependency observation to the LC Relations (Lossy Counting
Relations) ­ Algorithm 4. Next, if the periodic cleanup condition is met, both LC Cases and
LC Relations are cleaned up using the standard Lossy Counting approach ­ Algorithm 5
and 6.

Evaluation
This chapter discusses the evaluation results for the proposed solution. Section 5.1 intro­
duced the configuration used for running the experiments. The performance metrics such
as memory and processing time are discussed in Section 5.2. Section 5.3 analyses the
conformance metrics for the produced models. The experiments were run using a set of
artificially generated logs for 12 process models with trace and event noise levels of 0.1,
0.1 and 0.5, 0.51 and a real event log for a loan application process, provided by a Dutch
Financial Institute containing 262200 events in 13087 cases.
5.1
Solution configuration
For each experiment, the solution was configured in the way presented below, unless
specified otherwise.
• Sliding Window Split Miner (SW)
– Real event log ­ window length: 5000; concurrency threshold: 0,4; frequency
percentile: 0,6; refresh rate: 100.
– Artificial event log ­ window length 250; concurrency threshold: 0,4; frequency
percentile: 0,6; refresh rate: 10.
• Lossy Counting Split Miner (LC)
– Real event log ­ max approximation error: 0.00018; concurrency threshold:
0,4; frequency percentile: 0,6; refresh rate: 100.
– Artificial event log ­ max approximation error: 0.005; concurrency threshold:
0,4; frequency percentile: 0,6; refresh rate: 10.
• Lossy Counting with Budget Split Miner (LCB)
– Real event log ­ cases budget: 4875; relations budget: 125; concurrency
threshold: 0,4; frequency percentile: 0,6; refresh rate: 100.
– Artificial event log ­ cases budget: 150; relations budget: 100; concurrency
threshold: 0,4; frequency percentile: 0,6; refresh rate: 10.

5.4
Discussion
The solution was evaluated under two general criteria ­ performance and model quality. In
terms of processing time, all three adaptations of Split Miner showed very similar results.
The memory consumed by each adaptation is controllable by the solution user. In the
case of the Sliding Window and Lossy Counting with Budget, the memory consumed is
defined upfront. In the case of Lossy Counting, the situation is less intuitive, as it is not
that explicit how much memory will be used for the selected approximation error setting.
The second criteria that determines the overall quality of the solution are conformance
metrics. In terms of fitness, there was no difference significant enough, on which basis
it could be concluded that the models produced by the particular method are superior in
this aspect. However, after the follow­up experiments, it can be deduced that the methods
based on frequency counting are more robust. Regarding generalization, the scales were
tipped in favour of frequency counting by evaluating the real event log. The generalization
values were on a simmilar level for the artificially generated logs, with simmilar distribution.
The next conformance dimension discussed was precision. Frequency counting adapta­
tions produce models for artificial logs with precision values closer to 1, which could be
observed on the distribution charts. Combining that knowledge with the precision val­
ues calculated for the real process models, where there was a clear difference in favour
of frequency­counting­based methods, makes Lossy Counting and Lossy Counting with
Budget clear winners over the Sliding Window. In terms of simplicity, it’s hard to say

Evaluation of research questions

RQ2: Can the solution produce process models from event streams for various

All solution variants show a processing time of about 10 microseconds per model. The
memory used by the solution is fully user­controlled. The quality of the models produced
varies depending on the memory allocation.

6
Conclusion and Future Work
This chapter concludes the learnings acquired while working on the project. Section 6.1
focuses on what has been done and how it was achieved. Section 6.2 describes what
can be done to improve and expand the usability spectrum of the solution.
6.1
Conclusion
The main goal of this project was to expand the spectrum of tools available to perform
process discovery in a streaming process mining environment. The solution develop­
ment was an iterative process, where each iteration brought the solution closer to its final
version while ensuring that everything that has been developed so far is still correct and
no malfunctions were introduced.
To prepare a solution, available technologies and algorithms were used, the fusion of
which made it possible to achieve a solution that is the main objective of this project. The
first decision was to select the process discovery algorithm that has no streaming adap­
tation ­ Split Miner. The development of the algorithm required re­implementation in the
target streaming process mining environment ­ Beamline. To connect the algorithm with
the selected environment, it was necessary to implement the ways of handling streaming
event data ­ Sliding Window, Lossy Counting and Lossy Counting with Budget. By con­
necting and adjusting the process discovery algorithm, the environment, and the ways of
handling event data in such a way that they could work together, it was possible to create
a solution that was the primary goal of this project.
To prove the usefulness of the solution, it had to be evaluated. The evaluation has con­
firmed that the solution can discover useful process models from an event stream. It has
also shown how the solution’s outcomes depend on its initial configuration, describing the
advantages and disadvantages of each available variant.
As a result of this project, the spectrum of the available tools for performing streaming
process discovery has been extended with a new method.
6.2
Future work
The core node of this solution is the process discovery algorithm ­ Split Miner. As dis­
cussed in Section 2.3, the initial version of the algorithm has been improved with better
concurrency detection and enhanced split discovery. These improvements could be con­
sidered as the improvement of the Split Miner adaptation implemented in this project.
In the current solution state, the process discovery algorithm runs from scratch every time
it generates a process model. Therefore, another improvement to the current state would
be to consider the calculations made to generate the previous model when creating a new
one.
The ways of handling event data are not limited to Sliding Window, Lossy Counting or
Lossy Counting with Budget. The proposed improvement is to extend the available vari­
ants with new event stream data handling algorithms, such as Count­Min Sketch [37] or
Sticky Sampling [24].
The last proposed improvement is to enhance the set of data sources, as the selected pro­
cess mining environment ­ Beamline ­ supports many different streaming data sources.
