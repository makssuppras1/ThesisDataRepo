Abstract
The integration of advanced computational methodologies into the analysis of high-dimensional tissue imaging
data presents a significant opportunity to enhance the accuracy and efficiency of biological research, particularly
in understanding complex cellular interactions within varied pathological contexts. This thesis explores the
implementation of two pioneering algorithms, REDSEA (REinforcement Dynamic Spillover Elimination) and
UTAG (Unsupervised discovery of Tissue Architecture with Graphs), which aim to improve the annotation
and classification processes of tissue slide images. By mitigating issues such as signal spillover and enhancing
unsupervised cellular clustering, these algorithms facilitate a transition from subjective interpretation to
objective, quantifiable data analysis.
This study not only serves as a reproduction test for REDSEA and UTAG but also as an enhancement of
their functionality through their combined application to two different tissue samples: a urothelial carcinoma
and a lung cancer sample. The results indicate that the effectiveness of these computational tools can be
substantially influenced by the quality of the input data, with a notable improvement in output when applied
to low-quality data where spillover effects are more pronounced. This underscores the necessity for high-quality
data acquisition and meticulous preprocessing to ensure the reliability of the results. Additionally, while these
tools offer substantial improvements in the precision of cellular phenotyping and clustering, their performance
varies significantly based on the specific conditions and characteristics of the tissue samples analyzed. This
research contributes to the fields of digital pathology and personalized medicine by providing enhanced tools
for accurate tissue analysis, ultimately aiming to improve diagnostic processes and therapeutic outcomes.
Future work should focus on further validating these algorithms across different imaging modalities and
exploring their efficacy in broader diagnostic and research applications.

INTRODUCTION
1
Introduction
The detailed analysis of images of tissue slides is crucial in advancing our shared understanding of cellular
functions and their interactions within various biological and pathological contexts.
Traditionally, the
evaluation of these slides has been heavily reliant on manual annotation processes. These traditional methods
are not only labor-intensive but also prone to significant rates of error, necessitating the development and
integration of more automated and sophisticated technological solutions (Rizzardi et al. 2012, Rakhlin
et al. 2018). This thesis proposes a comprehensive analytical framework that incorporates two pioneering
computational algorithms, REDSEA (REinforcement Dynamic Spillover EliminAtion) (Bai et al. 2021) and
UTAG (Unsupervised discovery of Tissue Architecture with Graphs) (Kim et al. 2022b). The integration of
these algorithms aims to significantly enhance the accuracy and efficiency of image analysis of tissue slides,
transitioning from subjective interpretations to objective, data-driven insights. This thesis primarily focuses
on implementing a workflow that combines REDSEA’s spillover compensation capabilities with UTAG’s
unsupervised clustering approach to improve the accuracy and efficiency of high-dimensional tissue imaging
analysis. In this regard, this thesis serves both as a reproduction study of the two algorithms and as an
expansion of their capabilities through their combination.
1.1
Challenges in High-Dimensional Tissue Imaging
Advancements in imaging technologies, such as Imaging Mass Cytometry (IMC) (Giesen et al. 2014), Multi-
plexed Ion Beam Imaging (MIBI) (Angelo et al. 2014) and Cyclic Immunofluorescence (CyCIF) (Gerdes et al.
2013), have revolutionized the ability to analyze biological tissues. These technologies enable the simultaneous
measurement of numerous biomarkers on a single tissue slide, providing a rich, multi-faceted view of cellular
behaviors and interactions. However, the increased amount of data obtained from these high-dimensional
imaging techniques highlights another challenge, notably signal spillover (Nguyen et al. 2013, Chevrier et al.
2018). This phenomenon occurs when the emission from one marker overlaps into the channel of another, and
can also manifest spatially within the same channel, leading to misinterpretation of cellular markers. Such
issues are particularly prevalent in densely packed cellular environments like tumors or inflamed tissues and
severely complicate the accurate segmentation and classification of cells. Cell segmentation is the process
of identifying and delineating individual cells within the image, which is crucial for accurate analysis and
annotation.

1.2
REDSEA: Addressing Signal Spillover
To address the challenge of spillover, this thesis utilises the REDSEA algorithm, which is designed to correct
signal contamination between adjacent cells in multiplexed images. By dynamically compensating for the
lateral spillover based on the proportion of the shared boundary between cells, REDSEA significantly enhances
the clarity and quality of multiplexed images. This improvement in image quality facilitates a more precise
attribution of signals to their correct cellular origins, thereby enabling more accurate cell-type classification
and enhancing the overall reliability of phenotypic assignments. The adoption of REDSEA thus represents
a pivotal advancement in the field of digital pathology, potentially transforming the landscape of cellular
imaging.
1.3
Advancements through UTAG
Building upon the enhancements provided by REDSEA, the UTAG algorithm introduces a refined method
for the unsupervised clustering of tissue cells. Rather than relying solely on phenotypic markers, UTAG
organizes cells into distinct groups based on both their spatial proximity and feature levels. This dual approach
allows UTAG to analyze tissue architecture and cellular interactions within a multiplexed imaging context
comprehensively. By leveraging spatial information alongside cellular features, UTAG facilitates a deeper
exploration of cellular microenvironments and enables the identification of novel cellular interactions and
phenotypes, providing crucial insights into the dynamics within cellular ecosystems.

1.4
Objectives and Significance
The primary objective of this thesis is to implement and critically evaluate the effectiveness of the REDSEA
and UTAG algorithms in refining the classification and analysis of multiplexed tissue images. By automating
the correction of signal spillover and enhancing the granularity of cellular clustering, this research aims to
minimise the reliance on manual annotation, streamline processing times, and boost the reproducibility of
tissue analyses. The broader implications of this work are substantial, with the potential to drive significant
advancements in personalised medicine and targeted therapies. More accurate and detailed tissue analyses
could lead to a better understanding of the cellular compositions of various diseases, thereby enhancing
diagnostic precision and therapeutic outcomes.
2
Methods
2.1
Introduction to the Methods
This thesis utilizes computational methodologies to tackle prevalent challenges in high-dimensional tissue
imaging, specifically targeting the classification of cellular data derived from tissue slide images. The approach
incorporates two algorithms, REDSEA for spillover compensation and UTAG for unsupervised clustering,
each selected for their robustness in handling complex imaging data. The integration of these techniques
seeks to enhance the accuracy of cellular phenotyping and classification, crucial for detailed analyses of tissue
samples.
2.1.1
Justification for REDSEA
REDSEA addresses the prevalent issue of signal spillover in multiplexed tissue imaging — a challenge
particularly notable in densely packed cellular environments like tumors or inflamed tissues. This algorithm
dynamically compensates for signal contamination between adjacent cells by considering the shared boundary,
effectively enhancing the specificity of cell-type annotation. Although several methods exist for spillover
compensation (Miao et al. 2021, Chevrier et al. 2018) REDSEA was selected for its robust application across
various imaging scenarios. It is particularly effective even when initial cell segmentation is imperfect, enhancing
the accuracy of signal attribution to their correct cellular origins. This is crucial in multiplexed imaging,
where intricate tissue structures and overlapping cells often complicate accurate cell segmentation. REDSEA’s
demonstrated effectiveness across multiple imaging modalities, including MIBI and CyCIF, and its ability to
deliver improved outcomes even with suboptimal segmentation, render it an optimal choice for this study,
where precision in cell-type classification is paramount.

2.1.2
Justification for UTAG
Following the signal correction by REDSEA, UTAG employs an unsupervised learning algorithm to cluster cells
based on their phenotypic markers. This process is crucial for deciphering tissue architecture and for single-cell
analysis without prior knowledge of marker distributions. The implementation of UTAG is instrumental in
advancing the analysis capabilities from mere cellular identification to a comprehensive exploration of cellular
interactions and organization within tissues, such as understanding the tumor microenvironment.
Together, these algorithms embody the core computational strategies adopted in this thesis to push the
boundaries of what can be achieved with current tissue imaging technologies, aiming to convert the vast
amounts of data generated into meaningful biological insights.
2.1.3
Workflow Overview
The imaging data is first compensated for spillover using REDSEA to ensure the accuracy of signal attribution.
The clustering process within the combined framework leverages the capabilities of UTAG, which incorporates
the PARC (Phenotyping by Accelerated Community Partitioning) algorithm (Stassen et al. 2020). PARC
utilizes elements from both the HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World graphs) (Malkov et al. 2020) and
Leiden algorithms. Initially, PARC uses HNSW to construct an efficient graph that represents phenotypic
and spatial relationships between cells. This graph is then processed using the Leiden algorithm to detect
robust communities or clusters.
Following the initial community detection, PARC implements a unique pruning step. This involves the
removal of spurious edges within the graph to focus on the strongest and most biologically relevant connections,
thereby reducing redundancy and enhancing computational efficiency. The clustering criteria are based on the
strength of connections (Euclidean distance) and community density (Jaccard similarity), ensuring that the
clusters are both statistically significant and biologically meaningful.
Figure 2 illustrates the comprehensive data processing workflow utilized in this study to analyze high-
dimensional tissue imaging. The workflow initiates with the acquisition of raw imaging data that includes
both the features of interest in the tissue and a segmentation mask delineating individual cell object. This
segmentation is crucial as it sets the foundation for accurate feature extraction and subsequent analyses. The
raw features undergo a critical refinement process via the REDSEA algorithm, which compensates for any
spillover effects, ensuring that the signals attributed to each cell are as precise as possible. Post-compensation,
these features are normalized and scaled relative to cell size, which standardizes the data, making it suitable
for uniform analysis across varying cell sizes and densities.
Subsequently, this prepared data is fed into the UTAG algorithm, which executes unsupervised clustering.
This sophisticated analysis stratifies cells into clusters based on similarities in their phenotypic markers
without prior knowledge of the marker distributions, thus highlighting the inherent cellular architecture of the
tissue samples.

2.2
Data Collection
2.2.1
Types of Tissue Samples Analyzed
The primary focus of this project has been on analyzing kidney cancer tissue samples (Ohara et al. 2021).
These samples have been selected due to their availability and extensive use in previous scientific studies, which
have made their IMC images publicly accessible. This choice facilitates comparative studies and validation
against established datasets.
2.2.2
Imaging Techniques Used
The tissue samples were imaged using IMC (Giesen et al. 2014). IMC is a high-dimensional tissue imaging
technique that allows for the simultaneous measurement of multiple biomarkers, providing a detailed cellular
composition of the tissue at a resolution of approximately 1µm2 per pixel (Kim et al. 2023). This technique is
particularly advantageous for complex tissue environments, such as those found in cancerous tissues, due to
its ability to clearly delineate cellular boundaries and phenotypic expressions.
In IMC, antibodies specific to target proteins or biomarkers are conjugated to stable, heavy metal isotopes.
Tissue sections or cell samples are prepared on slides, and the metal-tagged antibodies are applied. These
antibodies bind to their specific targets within the sample. Once the antibodies have bound to their targets,

the sample is placed under a specialized laser microscope. The energy from the laser vaporizes the tissue at
the targeted spots, turning the tissue and the bound metal tags into a plume of ions (Koch et al. 2011). This
process is called laser ablation. The precision of laser ablation allows for targeting sub-cellular, facilitating
detailed analysis at an exceptionally granular level.
The ionized metal tags are then sucked into a mass spectrometer through a carrier gas stream. In the
mass spectrometer, these metal ions are separated based on their mass-to-charge ratios. Because each metal
tag corresponds to a specific antibody (and thus a specific antigen), the mass spectrometer can quantitatively
measure the abundance and distribution of each target within the sample. The data collected from the mass
spectrometer is used to create detailed images that show the distribution of each target across the sample.
Each pixel in the image corresponds to a specific point on the sample where the laser ablated the tissue, and
the intensity of each metal signal in the pixel indicates the abundance of the corresponding antigen at that
point. One of the significant advantages of IMC is its ability to simultaneously detect at least 40 targets
(Milosevic 2023) in a single scan without the spectral overlap that occurs with fluorescent tags. This is because
the metal isotopes used have very distinct mass/charge characteristics, allowing the mass spectrometer to
easily distinguish between them. The drawback of this technique is the destruction of the sample during
analysis and significant signal spillover due to the limited precision of the laser ablation.
2.2.3
Data Preprocessing Steps
Tissue images for this study were partly sourced from open datasets (Kim et al. 2022a, Ohara et al. 2021,
Rendeiro et al. 2021, Rashid et al. 2019), complete with corresponding cell segmentations available in TIFF
format. This study does not delve deeper into the segmentation of the tissue slides into cell objects. Correct
segmentation of cells is a critical and complex process that could merit a dedicated study on its own,
highlighting the intricacies and methodological innovations required (Petukhov et al. 2022).
Additionally, a high-quality IMC image of lung cancer tissue was provided by Standard BioTools™.
Unlike the open-source data, the cell segmentation for this image was not done manually but by using an
implementation of the U-net algortihm (Ronneberger et al. 2015). The U-net implementaion is embedded in
the Visiopharm ® software, and has previously been proven to result in reliable cell segmentation (Tison et al.
2024).
From the cell data, faulty feature channels were removed as part of the preprocessing lest they skew the
entire process and inhibit the following clustering. Faulty feature channels are channels with large amounts of
noise or channels that have no designated biological meaning. In the case of IMC data this could be channels
where a metal ion has no associated antibody.
The REDSEA algorithm was applied to correct for any signal spillover between adjacent cells. This
correction process involves calculating centroids and adjusting signal intensities to compensate for potential
overlap and misattribution of signal to neighboring cells.
Data normalization, an essential step in the preprocessing pipeline, was particularly focused on adjusting

2.3
Algorithm Setup
2.3.1
Parameter Settings and Configuration Details
The successful application of the REDSEA and UTAG algorithms depends heavily on the precise configuration
of their parameters, which are tailored to the specifics of the imaging data and the computational environment.
For REDSEA, key parameters include the threshold for determining signal spillover, the size of the structuring
element used for defining cellular boundaries, and the sensitivity settings for detecting signal overlap. These
parameters are critical for accurately identifying and compensating for spillover between adjacent cells.
REDSEA Parameters: The REDSEA algorithm begins by identifying the boundary between cells
through a precise method involving a search around each boundary pixel using a 3x3 pixel grid. This
meticulous approach is crucial for accurately calculating spillover corrections across cellular interfaces.
Spillover compensation for each cell is then executed by adjusting the total boundary signals based on the
extent of shared perimeter, utilizing the signals gathered along these boundaries. The methodology employed
for defining which pixels to include in the boundary region is depicted in Figure 3, providing a visual overview
of the boundary determination process integral to the spillover correction mechanism.

2.4
Implementation of REDSEA
2.4.1
Theory and Design
The REDSEA algorithm is designed to address a significant challenge in multiplexed tissue imaging: signal
spillover. Signal spillover occurs when the emission from one cell’s marker overlaps into another, leading
to potential misinterpretation of cellular data. This is particularly problematic in densely packed cellular
environments, such as tumor tissues, where accurate segmentation and identification of individual cells are
crucial for diagnostic and research purposes.
REDSEA’s primary purpose is to enhance the clarity and accuracy of multiplexed imaging data by
dynamically adjusting the signal attributed to each cell based on the detection of overlapping signals. We can
assume that the observed intensity for a given marker in a cell is a combination of the true signal from that
cell and unwanted spillover from its neighbors. By correcting these signals, REDSEA ensures that the data
more accurately reflects the true expression levels of markers within each cell. Since REDSEA is unsupervised,
good spillover reduction performance relies on two assumptions: That the signals from the feature channels
to be corrected are higher within the cell compared to the spillover region outside, and that signals to be
corrected are generally uniformly distributed around the cell.
2.4.2
Step-by-Step Compensation Process
The process involves several key steps to ensure accurate spillover correction:
1. Signal Extraction: Initially, the raw signals are extracted from the imaging data where each pixel’s
intensity is captured for various markers. These raw signals are indicative of both the target cell’s
marker expression and the unintended spillover from adjacent cells.
2. Boundary Detection: Using a predefined structuring element (such as square or diamond shapes),
REDSEA analyzes the boundary pixels of each cell to determine the extent of overlap or proximity to
neighboring cells. This step is crucial for mapping the areas where spillover is most likely to occur.
3. Spillover Estimation: The algorithm quantifies the spillover by comparing signal intensities at the
identified boundaries against the average intensities within the cell’s core. This comparison helps to
estimate the amount of signal spillover from adjacent cells.
4. Signal Correction: Based on the estimated spillover, the algorithm adjusts the signal intensities
for each cell. This correction is done by subtracting the estimated spillover contributions from the
cell’s total observed signal, thereby refining the signal to reflect more accurately the marker expression
intrinsic to the cell.

2.8
HNSW (Hierarchical Navigable Small World graphs)
2.8.1
Theory and Design
The Hierarchical Navigable Small World graphs (HNSW) algorithm (Malkov et al. 2020) enhances approximate
k-nearest neighbor searches by structuring data into navigable small world networks (Malkov et al. 2014). This
approach utilizes a graph-based, multi-layer structure which facilitates efficient searching across hierarchical
layers of proximity graphs. These layers are constructed to capture varying scales of neighborhood proximity
among data points, enhancing search efficiency and precision.
2.8.2
In-Depth Exploration of HNSW Algorithm
The Hierarchical Navigable Small World (HNSW) algorithm constructs scalable, efficient proximity graphs that
enhance the speed of approximate nearest neighbor searches. It incrementally builds its graph by placing each
new element into the structure with a layer determined by an exponentially decaying probability distribution,
as outlined in Equation 2.4. This method distributes elements across different scales, optimizing both storage
and the speed of search operations. Each layer functions independently, enabling rapid traversal from coarser
to finer resolutions, thus accelerating the search process significantly. This section further explores the
mechanics and underlying principles of the HNSW network construction to provide a deeper understanding of
its operation and advantages over traditional methods.

yet unexplored element in W. This process repeats until all elements in W are evaluated, utilizing two
priority queues to manage and update the list efficiently.
3. Transition to Construction Phase: Upon reaching the target layer l, the construction phase begins.
Here, the ef parameter is increased from 1 to efConstruction to enhance the recall of the greedy search,
ensuring a more exhaustive connection of the inserted element to its neighbors. These neighbors then
become candidates for establishing connections to q.

2.9
Leiden
The development of the Leiden algorithm is deeply rooted in the ongoing research addressing the limitations
of previous community detection methods, particularly the resolution limit issue highlighted in earlier studies
(Fortunato et al. 2007, Traag et al. 2011). The resolution limit problem refers to the inability of certain
algorithms, like those based on modularity optimization, to detect smaller communities within large networks.
This limitation can lead to significant challenges in accurately interpreting the underlying structure of complex
systems.
The Leiden algorithm addresses these challenges by incorporating a more sophisticated handling of
community sizes and interconnections, allowing for a more nuanced detection of community structures that
are free from the constraints imposed by the resolution limit. This approach not only improves the accuracy
of community detection but also enhances the algorithm’s utility in practical applications across various
scientific domains.
2.9.1
Algorithm Overview
The Leiden algorithm (Traag et al. 2019) represents a significant advancement in community detection
algorithms, building upon the foundational concepts of the earlier Louvain method (Blondel et al. 2008).
The primary goal of the Leiden algorithm is to identify high-quality partitions in a network, which involves
dividing a network into communities or clusters in a way that maximizes the density of intra-community
edges while minimizing the density of inter-community edges.

One of the key advantages of the Leiden algorithm over the Louvain method is its ability to generate more
connected, well-defined communities. Unlike Louvain, which might produce disconnected communities under
certain conditions, Leiden ensures that each identified community is a connected component, enhancing the
algorithm’s robustness and the interpretability of its results. Additionally, Leiden tends to be faster and can
reach a higher modularity in less time due to its refined approach to moving nodes between communities and
exploring the partition space more effectively.
2.9.2
Cluster Optimization
The optimization capabilities of the Leiden algorithm are crucial for achieving the desired resolution and
accuracy in clustering tissue data. The algorithm includes parameters such as the resolution parameter and
the number of iterations, which can be finely tuned to adjust the granularity of the resulting clusters. The
resolution parameter, in particular, controls the size of the communities, with higher values leading to smaller,
more detailed communities, while lower values result in larger, more generalized groupings.
The cluster optimization process in the Leiden algorithm involves three key phases: local moving of nodes,
refinement of the partition, and aggregation of the network based on the refined partition. The local moving
phase resembles the first phase of the Louvain method but incorporates advanced strategies to efficiently
handle node movements.
Phase 1: Local Moving of Nodes
Initially, nodes are moved locally to optimize a predefined quality
function. This is done by evaluating the gain in modularity that can be achieved by moving individual nodes
between communities. The decision to move a node is based on a heuristic that seeks to maximize the quality
function, often leading to significant improvements in community compactness and separation.
Phase 2: Refinement of Partition
After the local moving phase, the partition is refined to enhance the
connectivity of the communities. This refinement is not merely a repetition of the initial phase; instead, it
involves a sophisticated analysis of the network structure to identify potential improvements. Nodes might be
repositioned within their communities to strengthen the internal connectivity, ensuring that each community
forms a cohesive subgraph. This step is critical as it addresses the major drawback of the Louvain method,
which could leave communities internally disconnected.
Phase 3: Aggregation of the Network
In the final phase, an aggregated network is constructed where
each node represents a community from the refined partition. This aggregated representation allows the
algorithm to operate on a higher level of abstraction, focusing on the interactions between communities rather
than individual nodes.

by PARC are both accurate and meaningful, reflecting true phenotypic variations rather than mere artifacts
of data density or sampling biases.
Through this detailed integration and enhancement of the data structuring and community detection
processes, PARC significantly contributes to the UTAG framework’s ability to analyze complex biological
data effectively.
2.11
Integration of REDSEA and UTAG
2.11.1
Workflow Integration
The integration of the REDSEA and UTAG algorithms is critical for ensuring that the spatial and phenotypic
corrections made by REDSEA enhance the accuracy of the subsequent unsupervised clustering performed by
UTAG.
The workflow begins with the application of REDSEA to compensate for signal spillover in high-dimensional
tissue imaging data. This step is essential for clarifying the signal from each cell and ensuring that subsequent
clustering reflects true phenotypic differences rather than artifacts from overlapping signals. Key parameters
such as element shape and size, and specific channels for normalization are configured to optimize the correction
process.
Following signal compensation, the corrected data is structured into formats suitable for UTAG analysis.
This involves scaling the compensated signals by cell size and ensuring that the data is free from cells with
insufficient information. These preprocessing steps are crucial for maintaining the quality and reliability of
the data fed into UTAG.
The preprocessed data is then encapsulated into an AnnData object, which is a data structure widely used
in computational biology for storing annotated data matrices (Virshup et al. 2021). This object includes not
only the normalized cellular data but also metadata such as ce ll centroids and phenotypic channels, which
are essential for the spatial analysis of tissue structures.

This step is supported by specifying various parameters like the resolution for clustering and the normal-
ization mode, which influence the granularity and accuracy of the detected clusters.
2.11.2
Implementation
The integration relies on a variety of computational tools that handle data processing, analysis, and visualization.
Python is used extensively for scripting the workflow, utilizing libraries such as Pandas (McKinney 2010) for

The AnnData framework is instrumental in managing complex data structures that arise from tissue
imaging studies. It provides a flexible container for matrix data with annotations of cells, genes, and proteins,
facilitating easy access and manipulation of data layers necessary for comprehensive analysis.
Python scripts streamline the integration of the REDSEA and UTAG algorithms, managing data transition
through all stages—from TIFF image processing to clustering and result visualization. This implementation
illustrates the application of REDSEA adjustments and subsequent data preparation for UTAG clustering,
emphasizing the utility of software tools in handling complex computational biology workflows.
The full workflow is documented in (Angen 2024).

4
Discussion
4.1
Impact of Poor Data Quality
The results presented in Section 3 reveal that the effectiveness of the combined workflow of REDSEA spillover
compensation and UTAG unsupervised clustering is contingent on the quality of the input data. While the
application of REDSEA has the potential to enhance the outcome of UTAG by minimizing the effects of
spillover, the enhancement is more pronounced when applied to data of particularly low quality. The adage
"garbage in, garbage out" holds true in computational biology, where the quality of the input data critically
influences the reliability and reproducibility of the results (Kilkenny et al. 2018). This observation underscores
the necessity for high-quality data acquisition and preprocessing to ensure that subsequent analyses might
yield meaningful and reliable interpretations.
4.2
Comparative Analysis of Clustering Outcomes
4.2.1
Urothelial Carcinoma
The clustering results of the Urothelial carcinoma sample, as presented in Figure 15 and Figure 16, particularly
after spillover compensation, revealed distinct expression patterns that allow for potential identification of
specific cell types within the tissue based on biomarker expression.
Before spillover compensation, a universal expression of αSMA across all clusters was observed, which is
atypical as it is a lineage marker for fibroblasts and smooth muscle cells. This suggests an overgeneralization
of expression profiles due to uncorrected spillover effects.

Comparative Analysis of Clustering Outcomes
4
DISCUSSION
After spillover compensation, the expression of features becomes more delineated across clusters:
• αSMA is localized predominantly to clusters 3 and 4, indicating these clusters likely contain fibroblasts
or smooth muscle cells (Shinde et al. 2017).
• E-cadherin and Pan-CK, epithelial cell markers, are highly expressed in clusters 0, 5, and 11,
highlighting regions where epithelial cells are abundant (antibodies-online.com 2024). This pattern
suggests these clusters may represent either intact urothelium or carcinoma cells.
• CD45, an immune cell marker, shows specific expression in clusters 4, 8, and 12, indicating these
clusters may contain lymphocytes or other leukocytes (Nakano et al. 1990).
• Granzyme B, found almost exclusively in cluster 7, indicates the presence of active immune response
cells, such as cytotoxic T cells and NK cells (Shi et al. 2000).
• CD31 is an endothelial marker that shows elevated levels in clusters 4 and 10, suggesting these
clusters include blood vessels, which are essential for supporting a possible tumor microenvironment
(Vanchinathan et al. 2015).
• CD16 is observed in cluster 12, pointing to potential interactions between NK cells or phagocytes and
tumor cells (Orange 2002).
• CD68 is a macrophage marker, predominantly found in clusters 5, 7, and most intensely in 9 post-
compensation, suggesting significant macrophage activity within these clusters (Holness et al. 1993).
• Vimentin is observed in cluster 4 with lower levels in clusters 3 and 11, indicative of mesenchymal cells
or fibroblasts undergoing ageing (Liu et al. 2015, Sliogeryte et al. 2019).

The significance in this context might be interpreted as how well the observed data points (or nodes in a

The clustering is performed with the Leiden algorithm as described in Section 2.9 which employs modularity
optimization. Modularity is a measure of the strength of division of a network into clusters. A higher modularity
value indicates a configuration where there are denser connections between nodes within clusters than between
nodes in different clusters.
In the context of clustering the same dataset with and without spillover compensation, the variation in
the number of resulting clusters raises significant considerations regarding the methodology and its impact on
the analysis.

• Improved Data Quality: spillover compensation can refine the dataset by removing outliers or correcting

4.3
Handling High-Quality Data
When comparing the impact of spillover compensation on high-quality data, it’s important to consider the
specific characteristics and variability inherent in such data sets. High-quality images typically exhibit
fewer artifacts and less noise, which means that the cells and their markers are more distinctly visible and
differentiated without the need for extensive preprocessing. For high-quality data sets, which inherently exhibit
less noise and clearer distinction between markers, the application of spillover compensation can sometimes
lead to overfitting. This situation can obscure biological nuances critical for accurate cluster analysis. Thus,
while REDSEA’s application is vital in data sets with significant spillover, its utility should be evaluated
carefully against the backdrop of data quality to avoid potential misrepresentations of biological variations.
In the lung cancer sample, applying spillover compensation resulted in a general decrease in mean correlation
scores after compensation. This suggests that while spillover compensation can improve clarity in datasets
where spillover is a significant issue, as observed in urothelial carcinoma sample, its use in high-quality datasets
might lead to overfitting. In such cases, the algorithm could overemphasize minor discrepancies that are not
biologically relevant.
This observation underscores the necessity of a balanced approach to data preprocessing in computational
biology. It emphasizes that the utility of algorithms like REDSEA must be evaluated contextually, with
consideration given to the initial data quality and the specific goals of the analysis. Understanding when and
how to apply such corrections can significantly influence the outcomes of complex image analyses, particularly
in high-stakes fields like cancer research where precise cellular characterization is essential.

Effective channel selection is crucial for optimizing clustering outcomes in high-dimensional tissue imaging.
The decision on which channels to include or exclude significantly impacts the accuracy and reliability of the

analysis. This section explores the implications of removing channels with high noise levels or those irrelevant
to specific analytical goals, considering both the advantages and potential drawbacks of this practice.
Arguments for excluding channels often center around the benefits of reducing noise and avoiding misclas-
sification. Channels that predominantly contain noise can skew a clustering algorithm’s ability to accurately
segment and classify cell types. By eliminating these channels, the dataset becomes more focused on biologi-
cally relevant signals, enhancing the clarity and specificity of the analysis. Additionally, certain channels,
such as those measuring DNA content, might exhibit high expression levels not necessarily indicative of cell
type or state. Small variations in these channels, possibly due to technical artifacts, could lead to significant
changes in how cells are clustered. Removing such channels can prevent these discrepancies from affecting
the overall results, thereby streamlining the analysis process, reducing computational load, and focusing on
channels that provide the most insight.
However, there are compelling arguments against excluding channels. Excluding channels might lead
to the loss of potentially useful information. While some channels might initially appear to contain noise
or irrelevant information, they could hold subtle cues about cellular states or environments that are not
immediately apparent. Removing these channels outright might result in the loss of valuable data that could
contribute to a more nuanced understanding of the tissue being studied. Furthermore, selectively removing
channels based on their perceived relevance or noise level could introduce bias into the data analysis. This
approach assumes prior knowledge of what is or is not important in the dataset, which could skew the results
and lead to biased conclusions. Moreover, by tailoring the dataset too closely to idealized conditions through
the removal of challenging or noisy channels, there is a risk of overfitting the analysis to ’clean’ data. This can
make the algorithm less robust to real-world data variations, potentially reducing its applicability to broader
datasets or other types of samples.
Given these considerations, the decision to include or exclude certain channels should be made carefully,
with a clear rationale based on the specific objectives of the study and the characteristics of the data. In cases
where the impact of excluding channels is uncertain, sensitivity analyses could be performed to assess how the
exclusion affects the outcomes of the study. This approach allows researchers to make informed decisions
about channel selection, balancing the need for clarity and noise reduction against the risk of losing valuable
biological insights.
