Abstract 
The energy and economic performance of a solar-driven multi-generation system were 
investigated. This system uses heat as the energy carrier between its different plants and is 
henceforth called Thermal Multi-Generation System (TMGS). It is composed by a Concentrating 
Solar Power plant based on an Organic Rankine Cycle, a Multi-Effect Distillation plant for seawater 
desalination and a Vapour Absorption Refrigeration plant to produce low-temperature 
refrigeration. Its desalination and refrigeration plants are optimised so that their unitary 
production cost is minimised. This optimisation consist of a design phase where the component 
sizes are calculated, and an operational phase where the off-design performance is determined 
throughout a year. This system is then compared to a multi-generation system that supplies the 
same products but uses electricity as the energy carried between its different plants. It is henceforth 
called Electric Multi-Generation System (EMGS), and its constituents are a Photovoltaic, Reverse 
Osmosis and Vapour Compression Refrigeration plants. No optimisation is carried out for this 
electric system. Additionally, the local tariffs for water and electrical energy are used to put the 
economic performance of the multi-generation systems into the local context. 
This work uses the environmental conditions of the coastal city of Antofagasta, located in the 
Atacama Desert in northern Chile. The TMGS has capacities of 1.5 MWel, 1435 m3/day, and 775 
kWth for electricity, fresh water and cooling, respectively; whereas for the EMGS they are 2415 kWel, 
852 m3/day and 450 kWth, respectively. The total annual production of each system reaches 4900 
MWhel of electricity, 311000 m3 of fresh water, and 4000 MWhth of refrigeration at -30°C. After the 
optimisation procedure, the following unitary costs are found: the Levelised Cost of Electricity 
reaches 0.283 $/kWhel and 0.062 $/kWhel for the thermal and electrical systems, respectively; 
whereas the local grid tariffs reach 0.11 $/ kWhel. The Levelised Cost of Water is equal to 2.061 $/m3 
and 1.031 $/m3 for thermal and electrical systems, respectively; whereas the cost from the water 
distribution network is 1.97 $/m3. Lastly, the cost of cooling is 0.076 $/kWhth and 0.081 $/kWhth for 
the thermal and electrical systems, respectively. 
These results show that the TMGS is not cost-competitive to produce electricity and desalination, 
as their prices are higher than those of the electric grid and water network. On the other hand, the 
electrical system is economically feasible with regards to these commodities because their costs are 
approximately 55% and 52% of the prices from the electric grid and water network, respectively. 
Regarding the cooling production, both multi-generation systems have similar costs, with that 
from the electrical system being only 6% higher than that from the thermal system. 
From a technical point of view, the TMGS is able to operate without external energy sources other 
than solar energy, allowing it to operate in remote locations in island mode. The EMGS is also able 
to operate in this way, but the results aforementioned are found with a connection to the electrical 
grid available and might drastically change without it.

1 Introduction 
1.1 
Background 
The impact of human population and economic activity exerts increasing strain in the climate and 
ecosystems society relies upon, both globally and locally. As a consequence, it is necessary to 
develop technologies that allow better utilisation of energy sources, either being of renewable or 
fossil origins. The importance of this approach is only highlighted by the fact that by the year 2040, 
total energy demand worldwide is expected to rise by 28% [1], while greenhouse emissions must 
be significantly reduced to mitigate the effects of climate change. 
This paradigm shift must be addressed considering the opportunities, challenges, energy demands 
and constraints of specific locations and technologies. Desert areas have a set of unique 
characteristics that make them attractive places to develop and test renewable energy systems, 
mainly because of their high availability of solar energy. Likewise, economically developed, desert 
zones such as the Persian Gulf present relatively high energy consumption per capita [2] due 
primarily to energy-intensive processes such as desalination and refrigeration processes, which are 
needed to support their population and their economies. 
Examples of populated places with high solar irradiance, shown in Figure 1 [3], are the Middle East 
and North Africa region, Australia, southern Africa, southwest United States, northern Mexico as 
well as northern Chile, along with neighbouring areas of Argentina, Bolivia and Peru. 
 
Figure 1. Direct Normal Irradiance world map [3]. 
In this work, the coastal city of Antofagasta, located in the Atacama Desert in northern Chile, is 
selected as the geographical context to carry out this work, due mainly to its favourable solar 
resource. Moreover, water scarcity is a severe problem in the region not only by its climatic 
conditions but also due to the water demand of the mining industry located inland, whose 
economic activity is the main motor of the Chilean economy. The city’s location within the Atacama 
Desert, as well as the selected location for the system, are shown in Figure 2. 
Regarding the solar resource, the Atacama Desert has the highest irradiance in the world, where 
certain places have Global Horizontal Irradiance (GHI) and Direct Normal Irradiance (DNI) values 
slightly over 2700 kWh/m2-year and 3700 kWh/m2-year, respectively [3]. As a result, the installed

Given the capacity of operational and approved PV plants, the electricity market might likely 
become saturated for them in the future if no electrical storage is possible, considering that the total 
electrical installed capacity within the Atacama Desert is approximately 8900 MW [5]. As a result, 
several studies in the literature have focused on hybrid CSP + PV plants so that their capacity factor 
could be increased [6]. A real example of this is the 110 MW Cerro Dominador CSP plant [7], which 
is located 120 km from Antofagasta. It has a storage capacity of 17.5 full-load hours and is 
complemented with a 100 MW PV plant. While the CSP is currently under construction, the PV 
plant is already operative. 
Regarding water scarcity, Antofagasta has relied on desalination technologies for a long time. The 
first plants were built in the late 19th century using solar stills or coal [8]. Nowadays, the city of 
approximately 400000 inhabitants is served in 85% by Reverse Osmosis (RO) plants at a capacity 
of 650 L/s [9], whereas their coverture is expected to reach 100% within the next decade. 
However, most of the water demand comes from the productive processes of the mining industry. 
Chile produced 28% of copper, 21% of molybdenum, 63% of iodine and 21% of lithium of the world 
production in 2017 [10], most of them extracted inland of Antofagasta. As a result, several reverse 
==PAGE:20==
3 
osmosis plants have been built to supply these mines, some of them located 150 km inland and up 
to 3000 meters above sea level. Mata-Torres developed a study of a hybrid CSP + PV coupled with 
a desalination system and suited for mining purposes [11]. 
On the other hand, no information about representative cooling demand or built refrigeration 
systems has been found for Antofagasta, as these systems are privately installed for own 
consumption and in small scales, unlike electricity or freshwater. An advantage of Antofagasta 
compared to other arid places is the fact that air temperatures rarely reach 30°C, due to the 
moderating effect of the cold oceanic Humboldt Current [12]. 
Another effect of this current is high fishing productivity along the coast of the Atacama Desert 
[13]. Antofagasta has some fisheries, although most of this industry is based in other coastal cities 
in the region, such as Iquique. Therefore, this work assumes cooling production to be used in 
generic fish processing facilities, which might require from -18°C to -29°C cooling depending on 
the product and intended shelf-life [14]. 
In this local context, all systems currently in use to produce cooling and freshwater are electricity-
driven, requiring a connection to the grid to operate. Despite the significant investment in solar 
energy in the last years, 77.2% of the generation in the administrative Antofagasta Region still 
comes from either natural gas or coal power plants as of December 2018 [5]. 
This problem of heavy reliance on fossil fuels could be partially overcome by using CSP plants not 
only to produce electricity to further produce cooling and freshwater by conventional means, but 
also by integrating heat-driven refrigeration and desalination plants into the structure of the CSP 
plant. Such an arrangement is called a multi-generation system and allows higher energy 
utilisation and potential investment savings. Examples of desalination systems driven by heat 
sources are Multi-Stage Flashing (MSF) and Multi-Effect Distillation (MED), whereas Vapour 
Absorption Refrigeration (VAR) and adsorption systems are some examples of heat-driven 
refrigeration systems. Section 2.1 presents a more detailed review of some of these technologies. 
1.2 
Scope and objectives 
The research question of this work is: How does a CSP-driven multi-generation system with electricity, 
cooling and seawater desalination as products perform, both technically and economically, considering the 
local context, and how does it compare to a PV-driven system or other alternative solutions in the area? 
The following sub-questions support this question: How do the design parameters in the MED and 
VAR plants affect their economic performance considering their operation throughout the year? Are there 
optimal design conditions considering their impact on capital and operating costs? These questions are 
answered by pursuing the following specific objectives: 
 
To perform a techno-economic analysis of a heat-driven multi-generation system with a 
two-tank thermal storage. In this setup, electricity, fresh water and cooling are produced 
by an Organic Rankine Cycle, MED and VAR plants, respectively. 
 
To perform a comparative analysis of an electricity-driven multi-generation system where 
electricity, fresh water and cooling are produced by PV, RO and Vapour Compression 
Refrigeration (VCR) plants, respectively.  
In the heat-driven multi-generation system, henceforth called thermal multi-generation system 
(TMGS), the MED and VAR plants are integrated as bottoming cycles into a given design of a CSP 
plant. It is composed of a parabolic trough solar field, two-tank molten salt storage and an Organic 
Rankine Cycle (ORC) power block. On the other hand, because of the variability of the PV electrical 
==PAGE:21==
4 
output, a connection to the electrical grid is allowed in the electrical multi-generation system 
(EMGS) to reduce the required design capacity of these RO and VCR plants and to better represent 
the operation of a real system on the given context. 
Due to the several plants required to be modelled, this work characterises the performance of 
individual components within each plant from adequate sources and correlations found in the 
relevant literature, but no further detail of the inner component layout is analysed. Additionally, 
the economic analysis is addressed from a technical standpoint, where the capital and operating 
cost are included, but no private feasibility appraisal is carried out. Therefore, factors such as taxes, 
financing, risks, product distribution, permits, and future market perspectives are not considered. 
1.3 
Methodology 
The performance of the CSP plant is calculated by the author’s co-supervisor Postdoc. Researcher 
Nishith Desai, using the models developed by him [15], [16], given the environmental conditions 
of the location of interest of this work. A workflow diagram of this work is shown in Figure 3. From 
this point, the performance of the MED and VAR plants is addressed from a thermodynamic 
modelling approach using the Engineering Equation Solver (EES) software [17], where each 
component is modelled based on energy and mass balance equations. 
 
Figure 3. Workflow diagram of the present work. 
Specifically, an on-design model is developed first, whose inputs are design conditions while its 
outputs are variables such as component sizes. Subsequently, off-design models are developed to 
assess the annual performance of the each design, where the component sizes are fixed and the 
environmental conditions change between time steps so that freshwater or cooling production, as 
well as the heat and electricity consumption, are found. The annual performance of the system is 
evaluated through a series of hourly time steps, each following a steady-state approximation. 
After that, an optimization procedure is run to find the optimal array of design conditions which 
maximises their economic performance. This array of design conditions is separated into fixed 
parameters, which are held constant throughout the procedure, and decision variables, which are 
modified for each case. These design variables are simulated in the on-design models for both MED 
and VAR plants, and their results are passed to the off-design models. Once the annual results are 
calculated for each set of decision variables, a simplified economic model is included to assess the 
cost of production of freshwater and cooling by using the Levelised Cost Method. In this way, 
optimal design conditions with the minimum production cost can be found. 
On the other hand, the PV, RO and VCR systems are also modelled in EES, but they are represented 
by simplified models. However, no optimization is carried out for the EMGS case. Lastly, the 
production costs of water and electricity are compared to market prices found in the location of 
interest, to put them into the local context.

1.4 
Outline 
This work is organised into six chapters; the first, Introduction, describes the background of this 
work related to the location and its characteristics. The objective of this work and the types of 
systems to be analysed are presented, while the methodology to achieve such objective is detailed. 
The second chapter, Multi-Generation Systems, offers a general description of the technologies 
included in this work and an overall description of fundamental concepts, as well as how these 
technologies are related within the multi-generation system. Furthermore, a review of previously 
published literature on renewable multi-generation systems is presented.  
In the third chapter, System Modelling, a detailed description of the models developed in this work 
is illustrated. On-design models are detailed with their fixed design parameters and decision 
variables. Later, a description of the off-design models, as well as their economic parameters, are 
presented. 
The fourth chapter, Results, summarises and discusses the performance of each system given the 
optimised design conditions. Additionally, capital and operating cost are presented, leading to the 
final cost of production of each commodity. 
The fifth chapter, Discussion and Future Work, briefly discusses the main results of this work in the 
light of possible future improvements in methods, assumptions or constraints or system layout. 
The last chapter, Conclusions, summarises the main results, conclusions and learnings obtained 
from this work.

2 Multi-Generation Systems 
2.1 
Literature Review 
Multi-generation systems refer to systems capable of producing several products simultaneously, 
such as electricity, heat, cooling, freshwater or chemicals from a single or multiple energy sources 
by integrating several processes in such a way that waste streams from one process can supply 
others [18]. In this way, energy utilisation is improved by reducing the energy requirements 
compared to stand-alone systems [19][20], costs are reduced due to pooling between processes and, 
as a result, the overall cost of the products can be reduced even though the cost of some individual 
products might increase. As an example, Hoffman et al. [21] found that the Levelised Cost of 
Electricity (LCOE) in tower CSP plants is increased by 8.8% if used for cogeneration. 
Traditionally, the primary energy sources of multi-generation systems were fossil fuels or biomass. 
However, increasing attention is being directed in the literature towards the study of multi-
generation systems using renewable energy sources. One factor supporting this interest is the 
falling prices of PV and CSP plants [22]. The latter, when equipped with storage, might become a 
key technology in desert areas as its dispatchable base-load nature allows them to compete with 
coal or nuclear plants [22]. Although its cost is significantly higher nowadays, it has been falling 
considerably in well-suited areas such as the United Arab Emirates, where electricity bids have 
reached values as low as 0.09$/kWh [23]. 
However, even when large scale CSP plants are considered, the intermittent availability of 
renewable energy sources drives the need to study hybrid systems using two or more 
complementary energy sources. Calise et al. [24] investigated the annual performance of a hybrid 
system powered by geothermal and solar sources producing electricity, freshwater, district cooling 
and heating. In this specific case located in southern Italy, solar energy only plays a support role, 
as the solar fraction is just 14.6%. Similarly, Patel et al. [25] analysed the performance of a solar-
biomass system to produce power and heat via an ORC power block and cooling through a cascade 
absorption-compression refrigeration plant. In this case located in Jaipur, India, solar energy also 
only plays a support role accounting for a solar fraction of 0.179 from Fresnel Collectors. 
An example of a built multi-generation hybrid system is the Aalborg-Brønderslev CSP project [26], 
which produces district heating and electricity from a PTC field and biomass sources, utilising an 
ORC power block. The use of ORC systems is advantageous in CSP plants because it operates with 
lower temperatures which better match those of certain collecting devices, such as PTC, Linear 
Fresnel collectors [27] and other new technologies such as the polymer foil collectors studied by 
Desai [15]. 
The main drawback of PV plants in multi-generation systems is their inherent uncontrollable 
power output. As a result, these systems require a back-up grid connection if available, the use of 
expensive battery arrays or fossil fuel generators, or a reduced capacity factor, which increases the 
cost of the products. Astolfi et al. [28] optimised a PV-CSP-Diesel-Battery system to produce 
electricity and RO-MED systems for desalination in a small island considering variable loads and 
rolling horizon forecasting. Their results show that the inclusion of CSP Plants allows for 
penetration over 75% of renewable sources, the RO plant increases the grid flexibility due to its 
capacity of providing demand response and reducing battery usage, while the MED plant benefits 
from cheap low-temperature heat. Although just a prototype, an example of PV-RO system is 
==PAGE:24==
7 
found in Easter Island, where a RO plant with a capacity of 4 kW and 1 m3/h was installed in 2015 
as proof of concept [29], [30]. 
2.1.1 
Desalination Technologies 
Regarding desalination processes, Reverse Osmosis is the most mature technology [31] and has the 
highest installed capacity [32] with a 65% world share as of 2014 [33]. This technology works by 
passing salt water through a semi-porous membrane which lets the water go through while 
retaining most of the salt content. However, a considerable pressure difference between both sides 
of the membrane is required to overcome the osmotic pressure. As a result, the energy 
requirements for seawater are between 2 and 4 kWhel/m3 when energy recovery devices such as 
turbochargers and Pelton turbines are included [33]. These components recover energy in the form 
of pressure from the brine reject to reduce the pumping power of feed water. However, pressure 
exchangers are being used in new RO plants due to their higher efficiency and reliability. Du et al. 
[34] have shown that their use can reduce the cost of water by 38%. The working principle of this 
component is summarised in Figure 4 [35]. 
 
Figure 4. Pressure exchanger working principle [35]. 
Multi-Stage Flash (MSF) and MED processes are the leading technologies among thermal 
desalination processes, with 21% and 7% of the global installed capacity, respectively [33]. Unlike 
RO plants, MSF and MED plants require minimal pre-treatment and reduced electricity 
consumption [33]. However, they require thermal energy in the order of 80 kWhth/m3 [33], which 
makes them interesting options to use residual heat from thermal power plants. In this context, 
MED plants have certain advantages compared to MSF such as lower electrical consumption, lower 
risk of corrosion and scaling problems and lower operating temperature [36] [37]. 
In MED plants, salt water is evaporated through consecutive effects with decreasing pressure and 
saturation temperatures. An external heat supply boils a fraction of the feed water in the first effect. 
As a result, vapour with minimal or no salt content and concentrated brine are produced. 
Depending on the plant configuration, either pressure differentials or pumps move the brine 
between effects, where it is further evaporated by the vapour produced in the previous effect and 
the resulting condensate is practically salt-free. Several configurations of MED plants exist 
depending on how the feed water moves through the system, represented in Figure 5. 
==PAGE:25==
8 
In a backward feed configuration, the feed water moves from the last to the first effect through 
increasing temperature, and pressure and thus pumps are required between effects. Its 
disadvantages are increased pumping power and a higher risk of scaling because the higher 
temperature occurs where the brine salinity is highest [38]. In a parallel feed configuration, the feed 
water is split between effects without circulation across effects, i.e. the brine is rejected after passing 
through the effect. Its advantage is the simplicity of construction, but the distillate recovered per 
unit of feed water is lower [38]. In a forward feed configuration, brine is injected into the first effect 
and moves through the system in the same direction as the vapour. This means that in the first 
effect when the temperature is highest, the salinity is lowest; as a result, this configuration can 
operate at higher temperatures [38]. 
 
Figure 5. MED process configurations [38]. 
Besides RO and MED plants, other desalination processes exist, but they have not made their way 
to widespread commercial applications. One of them is humidification-dehumidification, in which 
salt water is heated and passes in direct contact with air, which is humidified. This hot, moist air is 
then put in indirect contact with cold salt water and therefore a freshwater stream is produced by 
moisture condensation. An example of a multi-generation system using this process is evaluated 
by Ghaebi et al. [39], where a geothermal source feeds the system to produce power, cooling, 
district heating and freshwater. 
2.1.2 
Refrigeration Technologies 
Regarding refrigeration systems, most of the multi-generation systems use vapour compression or 
vapour absorption cycles, whose basic diagrams are shown in Figure 6. In the former, a refrigerant 
is boiled in the evaporator while it takes heat from the fluid to be cooled; this refrigerant is then 
==PAGE:26==
9 
directed into the compressor, where its pressure is raised and as a result, also its temperature. In 
this point, the refrigerant temperature is higher than the ambient of the heat sink, and therefore it 
condenses in the condenser. Finally, the temperature and pressure drop as the refrigerant passes 
through the throttling valve, thus closing the cycle. On the other hand, the compressor is replaced 
in the absorption cycle by a thermal compressor, which consists of an absorption/desorption process. 
In this process, a solution made of a refrigerant and an absorbent circulates between the absorber 
and the desorber. The liquid solution is pumped from the low temperature in the absorber into the 
desorber, where an external heat supply boils the volatile part of the solution, i.e. the refrigerant. 
This refrigerant flows then to the condenser, while the remaining solution flows back to the 
absorber. In this component, the refrigerant from the evaporator can be reabsorbed into the 
solution if heat is removed, thus closing the cycle. 
 
Figure 6. Basic diagram of vapour (left) and absorption (right) compression cycles. 
VCR is widely used due to its suitability to different temperature lifts depending on the refrigerant 
used and its high performance, with Coefficients of Performance (COP) usually well above 1. 
Concerning VAR cycles, few suitable refrigerant/absorbent pairs exist, being the most used 
Water/Lithium-Bromide and Ammonia/Water. Although their COP is lower compared to those of 
VCR cycles, e.g. usually in the order of 0.7-1.2 for lithium-bromide systems, the electricity 
consumption is reduced by approximately 90% [40]. More importantly, VAR plants could achieve 
essential energy savings if coupled with residual heat sources. 
Vapour absorption plants are considered not only to provide refrigeration but also to improve the 
performance of MED plants while working as heat pumps. Alelyani et al. [41] analysed a VAR-
MED system in which the NH3-H2O VAR plant produces cooling, but the heat rejected in the 
condenser works as the heat supply of the MED plant. The results show that the cost of cooling 
decreases by 44% while the cost of water increases by 19%, compared to separated systems. 
Esfahani et al. [42] analysed a combined VCR-VAR-MED system, where the VCR plant produces 
cooling using water as refrigerant, but its condenser is replaced by the absorber of the Li-Br/H2O 
vapour absorption heat pump. As a result, steam from the desorber serves as the heat source for 
the MED plant. The results show that this combined system can save approximately 57%, 6% and 
26% in electric usage, heat consumption, and total costs, respectively.

2.1.3 
Multi-Generation System Assessment 
The integration of several plants within a multi-generation process requires methods to assess their 
performance and to optimise their design parameters depending on the objectives of the designer 
[36]. As a result, some authors use energy of exergy efficiencies as the primary benchmark [19], 
[20], [32]; others use exergy costing techniques [31], [43]–[46]; others use simplified or levelised 
costs [15], [25], [28], [42], [47]–[49]; while others acknowledge the trade-off between objectives and 
perform multi-objective optimizations [18], [50]–[55]. 
El-Emam et al. [18] analysed a system capable of producing power, refrigeration, hydrogen and 
fresh water via ORC, VAR, electrolysis and RO plants, respectively. The design conditions were 
optimised using a genetic algorithm to maximise exergy efficiency and minimise the total cost rate 
simultaneously. This study does not address its off-design performance. 
However, off-design modelling must be considered for solar systems, as their design conditions 
do not reflect the actual operating conditions through significant portions of the year. As an 
example, Calise et al. [24] examined the off-design performance throughout the year of a solar-
geothermal system producing power, heating, fresh water and cooling via ORC, MED, and VAR 
plants, respectively. However, no details on which equations govern the off-design model of the 
systems are given. Moreover, no assessment on the unitary price of each commodity is determined 
even when the total investment cost is calculated. This latter point is addressed in a related study, 
where Calise et al. [48] calculated the exergy cost variability throughout the year, with the 
conclusion that the system is not cost-competitive compared to conventional stand-alone options. 
Summarising key aspects of the literature review that concern this work: 
 
Most CSP-driven multi-generation systems are compared to stand-alone options, such as 
using diesel generators or gas burners, but no study has been found where heat-driven 
plants are compared to electricity-driven plants run from PV plants. 
 
Few multi-generation systems have been found where refrigeration is provided by 
ammonia-water VAR plants [39], [41], [56], [57]. Only Mehrpooya et al. [56] analyses a 
system with temperatures suited for fish processing facilities, with an evaporating 
temperature of -29°C. However, no comparison with other types of refrigeration plants is 
carried out. 
 
Only Calise et al. [24] studied the off-design performance of both VAR and MED plants 
but, as previously mentioned, no details of these models are described.

2.2.1 
Thermal Multi-Generation System 
The layout of the TMGS is shown in Figure 7 and is composed of a CSP plant, a MED plant for 
seawater desalination and a VAR plant for refrigeration. The scope of this work considers the 
modelling of the last two plants, which are attached to the condenser of the CSP plant as bottoming 
cycles in a series configuration through a secondary circuit. 
This secondary circuit is required primarily due to safety issues to avoid potential contact between 
the ORC fluid and water in the MED plant in case of leakages on the heat exchanger. Additionally, 
it eliminates the need for a specialised design in the ORC condenser to allow the heat transfer to 
the bottoming cycles and thus permits the procurement of standard ORC power blocks, reducing 
engineering and equipment costs.

Figure 8 shows the layout of the MED plant herein considered, which corresponds to a feed-
forward configuration. This plant is linked to the secondary circuit, hereafter called supply circuit, 
in points A and B and transfers the supply heat to the first effect. This heat turns seawater into 
water vapour which is passed down consecutively between effects. Except for the last effect, each 
effect has a preheater that uses part of the latent heat of the vapour to preheat the incoming 
seawater before it is introduced into the first effect. Additionally, each effect has a flashbox, where 
the condensed vapour from the current and previous effects is collected. The condensate from 
previous effects flashes when entering this component, as effects have a decreasing pressure. 
Finally, a condenser is used to remove the latent heat of the vapour produced in the last effect. 
Within this work, the pre-treatment, post-treatment, and distribution of water are not modelled, 
but the energy consumption of the main pumps and the pre-treatment process are considered.

Although the existence of an auxiliary circuit further reduces the evaporating temperature for a 
given required temperature within the facilities, it is included due to the risk of ammonia-leakages 
in closed spaces and more importantly due to the risk of contaminating the food products [58]. 
Assuming that the required temperature in the facilities is -23°C and a temperature difference in 
the heat exchanger within the facilities equal to 7°C, the temperatures of the auxiliary refrigeration 
circuit in points N and M are considered equal to -28°C and -30°C, respectively. 
As a result of the low evaporating temperature and the need to reduce the ORC condensing 
temperature, this double-lift cycle is favoured instead of a more efficient one-stage cycle. Tyagi [59] 
studied the cut-off temperature of one-stage ammonia-water chillers, which is defined as the 
minimum generator temperature the cycle can operate. From that work, it is observed that a one-
stage cycle requires generator temperatures above 110°C to operate under the conditions required 
in this work, which not only heavily penalises the performance of the CSP plant but also does not 
allow to integrate it with the MED plant without modifying the CSP plant design. 
The double-lift configuration works by splitting the absorption/desorption process into two parts, 
which allows an intermediate cooling in the upper absorber. Seawater is used as the cooling 
medium because its temperature is lower and more stable than the air temperature. This results in 
a reduction in the maximum condensing temperature required, which is a design parameter of the 
model. This work assumes a retrofit of existing fishing facilities, and therefore any variability in 
the refrigeration supply is covered by existing vapour compression refrigeration systems.

configuration links two single-stage cycles through a cascade heat exchanger, thus allowing a 
higher COP compared to a single-stage cycle due to a reduced pressure ratio in each cycle. Also, it 
allows the use of different refrigerants exploiting their advantages at different temperatures. 
Cascade systems with ammonia and carbon dioxide as the refrigerant in the top and bottom cycles 
respectively are well suited for low-temperature applications such as fishing processing facilities

provided, e.g. at -30°C and -10°C from the bottom and top cycles, respectively. However, this 
approach is not included in this work. Additionally, the CO2 cycle operates at low temperatures 
and, as a result, high pressures and trans-critical cycles are avoided [58]. 
More importantly, these two refrigerants are considered natural and sustainable refrigerants. The 
Global Warming Potential of CO2 and NH3 are equal to 1 and 0, respectively; while their Ozone 
Depletion Potential is equal to 0. Economically, these refrigerants are cheap, and, in the case of CO2, 
there is no necessity for recovery and disposal [58].

3.3.2 
Multi-Effect Desalination Plant 
A modular modelling procedure is chosen, where each effect is a self-contained subsystem 
composed of an evaporator, a preheater and a flashbox. Thus, it is possible to model any arbitrary 
number of generic effects, N, without modifying its inner structure just by connecting the outputs 
to inputs of neighbouring effects. However, the first and last effects are slightly different, so they 
are modelled as a modified version of the generic effect, as explained in sections 3.3.2.2 and 3.3.2.3. 
The following assumptions are used [70]: 
 
Quasi-steady state operation, i.e. each time step is significantly longer than required to 
reach equilibrium. Start-up and shut down procedures last less than 20 minutes [71], while 
for most of time steps conditions vary much more smoothly.  
 
Thermal losses and vapour leaks to the environment are neglected because operation 
temperatures are low, and the plant is assumed to be well insulated. 
 
Complete condensation is assumed within the evaporator tubes. 
 
Pressure drops are neglected, so no vapour temperature drop occurs between components. 
 
Non-equilibrium allowances are neglected, which means that fluids in the evaporator and 
flashbox reach thermodynamic equilibrium before leaving the component.   
 
From the last two points, it follows that temperatures in the flashbox and vapour side of 
the evaporator are identical. 
 
The distillate has no salt content. 
 
Non-condensable gases are removed by the venting system, with maintains vacuum 
conditions. This system is not modelled.

5 Discussion and Future Work 
This work has analysed the thermodynamic and economic performance of two solar-driven multi-
generation systems. The first system uses heat as the primary energy carrier between plants, while 
the second uses electricity. Several topics are analysed, being the first ones the context and general 
assumptions, which define the main design layout and considerations of both systems. The 
modelling procedure is second, which aims to represent the most critical variables and phenomena 
affecting their energy performance. The economic aspect is the third one, which defines the unitary 
cost of production of each commodity and ultimately define their feasibility. 
In this work, one of the fundamental assumptions is that the CSP and PV plants share the same 
location to analyse the performance of both systems with an identical solar resource. Although the 
CSP plant is required to be close to the coast so that the heat can be transported to the MED system, 
the EMGS is not subject to this limitation. Figure 2 shows that the solar resource is higher inland, 
up to 16% in fact, and the PV plant location can be optimised while the RO plant can still be located 
close to the coast. This setup would add higher costs in transmission lines or fees for grid usage, 
but the increased energy production could certainly pay for it. 
The lack of optimisation in the EMGS also contributes to an unfair comparison to the TMGS. The 
addition of such a procedure would further highlight the cost-effectiveness of PV-RO systems 
compared to water market prices and also clarify the product cost difference between VAR and 
VCR plants. On the contrary, the TMGS lacks a back-up plant to keep it running throughout the 
night as it has been done with the EMGS. The lack of such a back-up worsens the economic 
performance of the MED and VAR plants.  
A key aspect to include is the development of a design that allows the MED and VAR plants to 
operate independently from the ORC and thus increases their capacity factor. One solution might 
be to include a low-temperature storage system fed either directly from a designated solar array or 
integrated into the supply loop. Essential criteria in the latter option are that the storage material 
should work at temperatures around 90°C and have a high energy density. Molten salts do not 
comply with the first criterion, while hot water requires larger volumes and temperature glides 
that might importantly decrease the performance of all plants. 
The high electricity cost from the CSP plant also increases the levelised costs in the TMGS, but its 
impact could be reduced by considering a hybrid CSP/PV plant. The PV plant supplies cheap electricity 
during the day, while the CSP shifts part of its generation to night-time and thus increases the 
capacity factor of the bottoming cycles and sells electricity at hours with higher market prices. 
The low refrigeration temperatures are also a design criterion that diminishes the cost-effectiveness 
of the VAR system, although deliberately. This criterion is chosen to test and compare their 
performance even in highly constrained designs, where a VCR plant would be though as the best 
option. However, it is observed that cooling produced by the VAR plant is cheaper than that from 
the VCR plant, although not significantly and only by 6%. As previously stated, a more exhaustive 
cost analysis is suggested in this case. All in all, it is possible that NH3-H2O or even H2O-LiBr VAR 
plants are highly cost-effective if the required cooling temperature is raised. Another option is to 
develop a cascade system that uses H2O-LiBr VAR and CO2 VCR plants as topping and bottoming 
cycles, respectively. 
Another assumption that could be modified to better represent the system operation in real cases 
is to include a variable cooling load, either from environmental conditions or from operating 
==PAGE:73==
56 
schedules of the fish processing facility. This condition would require a complete off-design 
modelling for the VCR plant on one side, and a way to control the VAR production on the other. 
Also, the performance of the VCR is negatively impacted by the assumption of a fixed condenser 
temperature. On the light of the final results, it is recommended to allow this temperature to follow 
the changes on the ambient temperature to reduce the compressor pressure ratio, increase the COP 
and reduce the operating costs, which contributes to 90% of the LCOC. 
Regarding desalination systems, a sensitivity analysis on the effect of the supply temperatures from 
the CSP plant to the bottoming cycles is of great interest. A change in this temperature modifies 
the performance of the CSP plant and the bottoming cycles in opposite ways. Depending on the 
market prices of each commodity, an optimal temperature exists where the total profit of the 
system is maximised. In fact, Ortega-Delgado et al. [104] affirm that further increases in the supply 
temperature for the MED plant are possible up to 125°C if pre-treatment processes such as nano-
filtration are included. This type of analysis is not carried out because it requires several 
simulations of the CSP plant and this task had a high computational time. 
Several improvements could have been included in the plant modelling, particularly in the MED 
plant. First, pressure and temperature losses should be included, as the MED model is shown to 
overestimate the distillate production compared to an experimental setup. Additionally, the 
dimensioning of each heat exchanger would provide more accurate values of their overall 
conductance, which directly impacts the size of these components and hence their cost. 
The modelling of each component also solves another problem present throughout this work, the 
off-design modelling of the heat exchangers. Their performance is well known in the case of single-
phase to phase-change heat transfer as long as the type of flow does not change, i.e. from turbulent 
to laminar. However, assumptions have been made for cases when no heat transfer process 
dominates that would not be required if the components were fully modelled. Unfortunately, such 
a calculation would drastically increase the computational time and the available decision 
variables of an optimisation procedure like the one performed in this work. 
This work also neglects transient phenomena that affect system performance. For instance, no 
penalty on the production is included for start-ups or load changes, unlike the CSP plant model. 
In practice, transient effects are not negligible because the MED and VAR plant must go through 
start-up procedures every day, and they can take up to one-third of the simulation time step, as 
pointed out in the MED model assumptions. 
The last area of this work is the economic assessment, which can be improved in several ways. 
Firstly, the cost of equipment for MED and VAR plants is based on correlations with reference 
values almost 20 years old in some cases and, therefore, they could not be accurate anymore. 
Moreover, freight and import costs from the manufacturer to Chile has not been considered. A 
solution to this problem is to obtain quotes of real equipment with Delivered-At-Place (DAP) costs. 
Even more importantly, no specific details on the other capital expenses such as infrastructure, 
installation and construction have been found. Project developers rarely share this information 
publicly, as it is sensible company information. 
Two other analyses can be carried out to improve, contextualise and build upon the results 
presented in this work. The first is a private economic assessment with varying market prices, 
while the second is a sensitivity analysis on the capacity of each plant to study the effect of 
economies of scale on the unitary product cost.

57 
6 Conclusion 
The analysis presented in this work focused on the economic and energy assessment of a thermal 
multi-generation system composed of a Concentrating Solar Power plant based on an Organic 
Rankine Cycle power block, a Multi-Effect Distillation plant for seawater desalination and a 
Vapour Absorption Refrigeration plant to produce low-temperature refrigeration. The latter two 
plants are optimised to minimise the unitary cost of their products. The thermal system is then 
compared to an electrical multi-generation system based on Photovoltaic, Reverse Osmosis and 
Vapour Compression Refrigeration plants. The thermal system is modelled in a series of different 
design parameters, and those results are passed to an off-design model which determines the 
hourly system performance. Then, the setups of design parameters with lower unitary production 
cost are selected and compared the results of the electrical system, in which no optimisation is 
carried out. 
The city of Antofagasta, Chile, is chosen as the context to carry out this work, and a power block 
of 1.5 MWel is selected as the fundamental dimensioning parameter for the thermal system. Its 
desalination and refrigeration plants have a capacity of 1435 m3/day and 775 kWth, respectively. 
On the other hand, the capacities of the electrical system are 2415 kWel, 852 m3/day and 450 kWth, 
for the power, desalination and refrigeration plants, respectively. Over a year, both systems are 
able to produce approximately 4900 MWhel of electricity, 311000 m3 of fresh water from sea water, 
and 4000 MWhth of refrigeration at -30°C. The capacity factor of the entire thermal system is slightly 
lower than 60%; on the contrary, it reaches only 31% for the photovoltaic plant and 100% for the 
electric desalination and refrigeration plants. 
Regarding the unitary production costs, the Levelised Cost of Electricity is 0.283 $/kWhel for the 
thermal system and 0.062 $/kWhel for the electrical system, whereas the local grid tariffs reach 0.11 
$/ kWhel. The Levelised Cost of Water reaches 2.061 $/m3 in the thermal system and 1.031 $/m3 in 
the electrical system, while the cost from the water distribution network is 1.97 $/m3. Finally, the 
cost of cooling from the thermal system is 0.076 $/kWhth, as opposed to 0.081 $/kWhth from the 
electrical system. 
In respect of the optimisation procedure, a set of design parameters is found that minimises the 
unitary production cost of the Multi-Effect Distillation and Vapour Absorption Refrigeration 
plants. The optimal decision variables for the former plant are nine effects, a temperature difference 
in the condenser of 4°C, and a minimum temperature difference in any preheater equal to 3.5°C. 
Conversely, the optimal decision variables for the refrigeration plant are a seawater temperature 
rise of 6°C, and temperature differences in external and internal heat exchangers equal to 3°C and 
4°C, respectively. On one side, the cost of water varies from 3.86 to 2.06 $/m3 among the different 
combination of decision variables; on the other side, the cost of cooling ranges between 0.100 and 
0.076 $/kWhth depending on the set of decision variables chosen. 
From these results, it is concluded that the thermal multi-generation system performs well from a 
technical point of view, being able to produce power, desalination and low-temperature 
refrigeration without external support at rather high but feasible prices if no other alternatives are 
available. However, it is not a cost-effective way to produce electricity and desalination on the 
context found in the city of Antofagasta.  
The cost of electricity is approximately 2.5 times higher compared to that supplied by the electrical 
grid. Nevertheless, it might be profitable from the revenues generated by payments from available 
capacity found in the Chilean electricity market, but a detailed economic assessment is necessary 
==PAGE:75==
58 
to elucidate it. The cost of water is similar to that paid by consumers of the distribution network 
and, as a result, the thermal system is not economically attractive, considering that post-treatment 
and distribution costs must be added. 
The electrical system is economically feasible with regards to power and fresh water production, 
as their costs are approximately 45% and 48% lower than the tariffs of the electrical grid and water 
network, respectively. As a result, this solution is economically feasible as long as distribution costs 
are lower than those values. By extension, the electrical system outperforms the thermal system 
based on power and fresh water costs, although it requires a connection to the electrical grid and 
without it these results would drastically worsen. 
With regards to cooling, the electrical system is 6% more expensive than the thermal system. 
Therefore, it is concluded that both options are economically feasible, considering that the systems 
currently installed in the area are vapour compression systems running on electricity from the grid, 
which is more expensive than that from the photovoltaic plant. However, the thermal system 
requires a back-up system as its production is not controllable in the current system design. Due 
to the similar cooling costs between the two systems, it is recommended to perform a more detailed 
analysis to define with technology is actually the most cost-efficient in this context.
