Abstract
In 2002 it was discovered that various heat-treated starchy foods had high concentrations
of acrylamide. This started an extensive investigation to see how acrylamide eﬀected con-
sumers. During resent years additional food groups has been discovered to contain concerning
concentrations of acrylamide. In November 2019 a statement by the European Commission
was published, stating which additional foods should be tested and regulated in terms of
acrylamide.
Amongst these foods is vegetable fries, whose acrylamide concentration and
possible mitigation techniques is investigated in this master thesis. The vegetable fries inves-
tigated is made from carrot and sweet potato and the setting has been made to be similar
to that of a at home oven-baking. Baking at 160◦C, 175◦C and 190◦C, for times resulting
in a moisture content of ∼40 g water/100 g product was conducted. The acrylamide anal-
ysis was done using LC-MS/MS, with a method developed and validated at DTU National
Food Institute. The acrylamide formation was found to follow an exponential curve and can
be used to predict the acrylamide content at diﬀerent times at the three diﬀerent tempera-
tures. The acrylamide concentration resulted in a health risk to consumers (calculated using
MOE). Some mitigation techniques were explored and two which could be done at home
by consumers was investigated. Blanching the vegetable fries and pH alteration with acetic
acid before baking was conducted and most vegetable fries became ‘safe’ to eat (calculated
using MOE). The colour formation, hardness and crispiness were measured, none to little
correlation between these physical parameters and acrylamide concentration was found.

Introduction
Acrylamide was found in foods for the ﬁrst time in 2002 and was recognised as a serious public
health issue (Tareke et al. [2002]). Since then, various types of food have been examined for
acrylamide and regulatory precautions has been implemented in order to limit our dietary
exposure to acrylamide (EFSA-Panel [2016]). In November 7th 2019, the EU Commission
released a recommendation for surveillance of the presence of acrylamide in certain foods
(EU-Commission [2019]), this recommendation is for company managers, who manufactures
and market these foods. The recommendation entails a list of foods that should be mea-
sured for acrylamide, including vegetable fries (EU-Commission [2019]). Vegetable fries is a
relatively new food and therefore not as thoroughly examined as regular fries. It is a new
discovery that vegetable fries might contain acrylamide, like potato fries. Therefore, stud-
ies examined acrylamide in vegetable fries are very limited in the literature. Against these
backgrounds, this projects aims is to investigate the acrylamide concentration in oven-baked
vegetable fries and subsequently to mitigate acrylamide formation in these foods.
While a wide variety of vegetables can be made into fries, the 6 months period of this project
limits the number of investigated vegetables to two diﬀerent kinds. From a study examined
diﬀerent process contaminants in vegetable crisps (Breitling-Utzmann and Hankele [2019]),
it was very clear that carrot crisps showed alarming concentrations of acrylamide. Therefore,
carrot has been chosen as one of the vegetables to examine in this study. In the same study
by Breitling-Utzmann and Hankele, three other types of vegetable crisp contained higher con-
centrations of acrylamide than potato namely beetroot, parsnip, and sweet potato. Sweet
potato was chosen, ﬁrstly because of its macro nutrient proﬁle being similar to the one of
regular potato, but also since it has become quite a trend to make and eat sweet potato fries
as a substitute to regular fries.
The fries will be prepared in an oven using a conventional recipe found online, mimicking the
steps a consumer would do at home (Bauer [2020]). This is because making fries at home has
become increasingly popular, but the needed precautions to minimize acrylamide formation
6
==PAGE:8==
might not be implemented during at home cooking. Either because these are unknown or
because the needed information has not been conveyed to the consumer. After sample prepa-
ration, the acrylamide concentration in carrot and sweet potato fries will be explored using
an LC-MS/MS (Tandem Liquid Chromatography Mass Spectroscopy) analytical method de-
veloped and validated at DTU National Food Institute by Kit Granby.
Following the initial study of the acrylamide concentration of carrot and sweet potato fries,
diﬀerent mitigation techniques will be explored. These mitigation techniques will be based
on previous studies regarding mitigation of acrylamide formation in diﬀerent potato prod-
ucts. These mitigation techniques include, immersion in water, blanching, citric acid and
acetic acid immersion and antioxidant addition. These mitigation techniques is explored,
since it would be something the consumers could do at home. Two mitigation techniques will
be chosen to be conducted in the laboratory, and the reduction of acrylamide formation in
sweet potato and carrot fries will be investigated.
The risk associated with eating the vegetable fries without and with applied mitigation tech-
nique, will be estimated and discussed. The risk will be expressed using the risk assessment
term Margin of Exposure (MOE). From this the best suited mitigation technique(s) will be
discussed. Furthermore other risk decreasing choices will be discussed.
During the experiments the physical properties hardness, crispiness and colour will be reg-
istered in order to see any correlation between these and the amount of acrylamide in the fries.
The overall laboratory work has been limited due to the Covid-19 lockdown appearing simul-
taneously with the course of this Master thesis. Any additional laboratory work which could
be of interest in connection with this thesis is listed and commented in section 10 (Future
work).

3.1
Acrylamide
Acrylamide (AA) is an organic chemical which in April 2002 was found to be naturally present
in a wide variety of commonly consumed food products (Tareke et al. [2002]). At ambient
condition, AA is a white crystalline organic salt without odour. It is known to be carcinogenic
in animals (Tareke et al. [2000]) and a probable human carcinogen (IARC [1994]). Scientists
agree that higher doses of AA are toxic to both humans and animals, aﬀecting the nervous
system (Arvanitoyannis and Dionisopoulou [2014]).

The Swedish National Food Authority found

AA is formed in foods by the Maillard
browning reaction between naturally occur-
ring compounds, mainly reducing sugars (glucose, fructose, saccharose (sucrose)) and the

==PAGE:10==
amino acid asparagine (Stadler et al. [2004]). An illustration of the Maillard reaction can be
seen in ﬁgure 1. According to several studies, it has been determined that it is mainly the
amino acid asparagine and a reducing sugar (glucose, fructose, saccharose (sucrose) that con-
tributes to the formation of AA. Additional factors have been found to aﬀect the formation of
acrylamide such as initial concentration of the precursors, their ratio, heating temperature,
time of processing, pH and water activity of the heated product (Hedegaard et al. [2008]).
Since AA ﬁrst was discovered, many measures to reduce it in consumer products has been
developed and implemented. The easiest way to minimise AA is to make sure the food is not
cooked excessively (Arvanitoyannis and Dionisopoulou [2014]). Another way to minimise it
is the limitation of the precursors (reducing sugar and amino acid). In order to minimise
asparagine, the enzyme asparaginase has been employed and shown promising AA mitiga-
tion. Asparaginase converts asparagine into aspartic acid, so it no longer can be part of the
Maillard reaction. The way asparaginase most often has been applied, is by immersing the
food product in an asparaginase solution (Pedreschi et al. [2014]). In order to minimise the
reducing sugar (glucose and fructose) diﬀerent mitigation techniques can be used. The tech-
niques include soaking and blanching, which have shown varying results due to diﬀerences
in the products examined, nevertheless, it has shown signiﬁcant reduction in the acrylamide
concentration (Pedreschi et al. [2006]). Lowering of pH before frying has also been used, and
citric acid and acetic acid has shown considerably reduction of AA in French fries and potato
crisps (Jung et al. [2003]) and (Kita et al. [2004]). The mitigation techniques mentioned are
explored further in section 3.6, with the exception of asparaginase, since it is not a mitigation
technique which can be used by the consumer yet.

3.2
Tandem Liquid chromatography - Mass spectrometry
For the quantiﬁcation of the acrylamide concentration in the vegetable fries, Tandem Liquid
Chromatography - Mass Spectrometry (LC-MS/MS) was used. Chromatography is deﬁned
as a “Separation process in which the sample mixture is distributed between two phases in
the chromatographic bed, either column or plane” (Meyer [2010]). Two phases are present
during the separation, the stationary phase and the mobile phase. The stationary phase
depends on the analysis which is conducted and can be a solid, porous and surface-active
material in small particle form or a thin ﬁlm of liquid coated on a solid support or column
wall. The mobile phase is a liquid and will always be a liquid when using liquid chromatog-
raphy (Meyer [2010]). The mode of separation used for the experiment in this thesis is the
one called reverse-phase. It is a mode often used by laboratories for the chromatographic
separation of acrylamide (Wenzl et al. [2003]). The mixture which is separated is introduced
at the top of the column and then washed through by the eluting liquid, thereby separating
the mixture (Lindsay [1992]). The rule of thumb when using reverse-phase chromatography
is that “nonpolar compounds are eluted later than polar compounds”. The stationary phase
should be very non-polar and the mobile phase should be relatively polar (Meyer [2010]).
A simple illustration of the HPLC (High Pressure Liquid Chromatography) system can be
seen in ﬁgure 2. The system consists of a reservoir where the solvent/mobile-phase/eluent
is located, the solvent is delivered via a pump. There is a device/injector that introduces
the sample into the system, either manually or automatically. The solvent and sample move
through the column, a detector(s) and lastly, the mass signal is generated in a data system
(K. Robards [1994]).
10
==PAGE:12==
Figure 2: Illustration of the LC-MS system (K. Robards [1994])
Tandem mass spectroscopy was used for the detection and quantiﬁcation of acrylamide in this
project. The sample leaves the HPLC system and enters the MS/MS system as an electro-
spray, using electrospray ionisation (ESI). A capillary needle where the liquid sample leaves,
makes sure the liquid transforms into a ﬁne mist consisting of highly charged liquid droplets.
From here on the solvents used is evaporated, freeing the sample molecules. Coulomb ﬁssion
happens multiple times creating many smaller, more stable droplets, these particles is then
carried by an electric ﬁeld into the ion analyser (quadrupole) (Williams and Ian [2008]). The
mode of action in the quadrupole was multiple reaction-monitoring mode (MRM), where a
transition from a precursor ion to a product ion is monitored, this mode of action has a high
selectivity. In ﬁgure 3 is an illustration of the quadrupole mass spectrometer used. In the
ﬁrst quadrupole is the precursor ion separated into product ions, these are collided with a gas
in the Collision Cell (second quadrupole), a product ion speciﬁc to the molecule of interest
is monitored in the third quadrupole (Wenzl et al. [2003]).

Figure 3: Illustration of a quadrupole (Yost and Fetterolf [1983])
Large amounts of data is often collected from LC/MS-MS runs, therefore it is usually coupled
on-line to a computer controlled data system. The computer system is used to manipulate
and interpret the data. To quantify a molecule in the sample, the sample can be spiked with
a known quantity of a deuterated analogue, the relative signal sizes permit a quantitative
estimate of the amount of molecule present (Williams and Ian [2008]).

In this system, colour is deﬁned as a point in a
three-dimensional space in relation to coordinates L*, a*, and b*, see ﬁgure 4. Whereas L*

the colours blue (-b*) and yellow (+b*) (Saláková [1986]).

3.5.1
Toxicokinetic - ADME
Figure 6: Metabolism of acrylamide into the
metabolite glycidamide via CYP2E1. And the
metabolism of acrylamide and glycidamide
into mercapturic acid via GSH conjunction
(Katen and Roman [2015]).
Acrylamide (AA) is absorbed through the
gastrointestinal tract (when the intake is
through food) and it is widely distributed
to the tissue (Sörgel et al. [2002], Tritscher
[2004] and Pedreschi et al. [2014]). Because
of its high-water solubility, AA is easily dis-
tributed in the body, this includes to the
milk and placenta of women (Sörgel et al.
[2002], Tritscher [2004]). AA is metabolized
in the liver, where it follows two main path-
ways:
it is either metabolized into glyci-
damide via CYP2E1 or it is conjugated with
glutathione (GSH) mediated by glutathione-
S-transferases, this is illustrated on ﬁgure 6
(Sörgel et al. [2002], Tritscher [2004], EFSA-
Panel [2016] and Pedreschi et al. [2014]). Glycidamide is more reactive towards DNA and
proteins than AA and it is considered to cause the genotoxic and carcinogenic eﬀects, since
DNA adducts is formed. Before AA or glycidamide can be excreted it is ﬁrst detoxiﬁed
through conjugation with glutathione (GSH), here the GSH adducts are converted into mer-
capturic acids. Afterwards these are excreted through urine and serves as biomarkers of
acrylamide exposure (EFSA-Panel [2016]).
3.5.2
Toxicodynamic
When Acrylamide (AA) ﬁrst was discovered, it was found to have neurotoxicity eﬀects in hu-
mans after occupational exposure (FAO/WHO [2002]). These eﬀects were found in Swedish
tunnel workers, whom experienced side eﬀects which included skin peeling on the hands,
tingling, and numbness. Some experienced more severe side eﬀects which included staggered
14
==PAGE:16==
gait and nervous dysfunction (FAO/WHO [2002]). AA is known to cause several eﬀects in
animals, mainly male rodents, which is often used as test animals. These eﬀects include
neurotoxicity, reproductive toxicity, genotoxicity and carcinogenicity (Tritscher [2004], Pe-
dreschi et al. [2014] and Sörgel et al. [2002]). The carcinogenic eﬀect of AA happens when
AA has been metabolised to its metabolite glycidamide which can bind covalently to DNA.
A DNA-adduct can induce mutations and cell transformation, which may lead to tumour
formation. When administering AA in various doses, speciﬁc organs has been found to de-
velop tumours (Pedreschi et al. [2014]). In both male and female rodents thyroid tumours
has been found. Male rodents also develop testicular tumours and female rodents develop
breast tumours (FAO/WHO [2002]). Since AA has been found to have carcinogenic eﬀects
in rodents, AA is classiﬁed as a probable human carcinogen by IARC (IARC [1994]).
3.5.3
Dose-response modelling
Since acrylamide (AA) is a genotoxic compound it is not possible to establish a tolerable
daily intake (TDI). Instead margin of exposure (MOE) is used for the risk assessment. A
MOE above 10,000 for compounds which is both genotoxic and carcinogenic tells that it is
considered of low health concern (Pedreschi et al. [2014]). Before MOE can be calculated a
reference-point is needed. Bench-mark dose level (BMDL) is the value in which a small dose
corresponds to a low, but measurable change in response. This value is found from cancer
studies in animals, since human studies so far have not been adequate for dose-response
assessment (EFSA-Panel [2016]). When assessing the risk of tumours experts have advised
to used BMDL10 values of 170 µg/kg bw/day, which is derived from an experiment on tumours
in the Harderian gland on mice. This tissue is a sensitive target to detect compounds that
are both genotoxic and carcinogenic (EFSA-Panel [2016]).

Analysis method
To analyse the concentration of acrylamide in the vegetable fries, the following method has
been conducted. The method has been tested at the EU Reference Laboratory for Processing
Contaminant, which DTU National Food Institute is designated to host. The procedure used
is as following:
◦Store samples at -18◦C
◦Homogenise samples with a blender
◦Weigh 3 g sample into a plastic centrifuge tube (50ml) (log exact weight).
◦Add 150 µL ISTD d3-acrylamide 10 µg/mL to each plastic centrifuge tube (50ml)
◦Add 450 µL acrylamide 10 µg/mL to spiked samples (spiked with 1500 ng/ml)
◦Weigh 30.0 mL Milli-Q-Water (log exact weight) and extract using Ultra-Turrax 10,000/12,000
rpm for 2 min. (use setting no. 4)
◦Centrifuge samples for 20 min. at 3500 rpm
◦Transfer 1.9 ml of the upper water extract in the sample to Eppendorf tubes
◦Freeze samples in freezer overnight or approx. 30 min.
◦Centrifuge samples in mini-centrifuge, 10 min. at 14,000 rpm
◦Transfer 1 mL of the upper water extract in the sample to a plastic tube for SPE cleanup
(Gilson Acpec (500st./pcs. Ref 55.526, 5 mL, 75*12 mm, PP (Polypropylene)))
◦Run SPE cleanup with ISOLUTE Multimode 300 mg/ml from Biotage, which are special
mixed mode anion/cation exchange and reverse phase. The samples is collected in HPLC
vials with build-in ﬁlter (mini-UniPrep/Cat. No.: UN203NPUPP)
◦Put the ﬁlter in the vial, press it down, mix. The samples are now ready for LC-MS/MS

Discussion
8.1
Acrylamide concentration
A comparison between the alternative vegetable fries and regular potato fries (industrial)
acrylamide concentration is in order. Just to see which option is the safest to eat. Two stud-
ies has been found which states the mean acrylamide concentration in oven-baked potato fries.
In Normandin et al. [2013] the fries were prepared according to manufacturer’s instructions,
this is assumed to be at 175◦C-180◦C for about 20 minutes. The acrylamide concentration
Normandin et al. [2013] found in oven-baked potato fries was 358 µg/kg, from EFSA-Panel
[2016] another acrylamide concentration of potato fries was found to be 257 µg/kg, this value
is the mean value from various studies, uncovering the acrylamide concentration in potato
fries. It is also assumed that the potato fries in EFSA-Panel [2016] was baked at 175◦C-180◦C
for about 20 minutes. In order to compare the industrial potato fries with home-made sweet
potato fries one temperature has been selected, 175◦C. Knowing that the formation happens
exponentially, it is possible to calculate the acrylamide concentration after 20 min, using the
equations from ﬁgure 28 and ﬁgure 26. The equations is:
Sweet potato fries at 175◦C
Y = 0.45x −2.10
Inserting 20 (min) into x’s place results in a Y value (natural logarithm of the acrylamide
concentration) at 6.9. Reversing this from the natural logarithm (ln), results in the acry-
lamide concentration being 992 µg/kg.
Carrot fries at 175◦C
Y = 0.5x −3.83
Doing the same for carrot fries results in a acrylamide concentration at 478 µg/kg.
From these equations, it is clear that sweet potato fries has a higher acrylamide concentration
than regular potato fries made from the industry. The carrot fries has a bit higher acrylamide
56
==PAGE:58==
concentration than the regular potato fries made from the industry. One reason why home-
made vegetable fries are having a higher acrylamide concentration, than regular potato fries
made by the industry, might be because the industry is subject to strict regulations. These
regulations are based on previous experiments where high concentrations of acrylamide was
found in the potato fries. The regulations are then put in place to ensure a decrease in
acrylamide concentration happens at the production site. As of right now, the benchmark
concentration for French fries is set by the EU to 500 µg/kg (European Commission [2017]).
A comparison between the acrylamide concentrations found in this study and the acrylamide
concentrations others may have found, would also be in order. Unfortunately, it was not pos-
sible to ﬁnd any studies looking at the acrylamide formation in oven-baked sweet potato and
carrot fries. Studies looking at the acrylamide formation in fried sweet potato fries (Truong
et al. [2014]), sweet potato and carrot chips (Breitling-Utzmann and Hankele [2019]) was
available, but a direct comparison would be too uncertain to draw any conclusions from.
Nevertheless, this study has found high acrylamide concentration in both sweet potato and
carrot fries. These high concentrations poses a clear health risk to the consumers and some-
thing should be done in order to reduce the acrylamide concentration and thereby the risk.
8.2
Acrylamide formation
The acrylamide formation was found to follow an exponential curve, making it possible to
calculate the acrylamide concentration at diﬀerent times for the 3 investigated temperatures,
as shown earlier. It is also possible to calculate, at which temperature and time a ’safe’
acrylamide concentration would occur. If for example, the consumers would like to bake
their sweet potato fries at 190◦C because they think it might result in a better end product.
Assuming only adults with an average consumption would consume the fries. Then the time
needed to reach an acrylamide concentration at ∼18 µg/kg, calculated in section 6.6.2, could
be calculated using the equation from ﬁgure 26, shown below:
57
==PAGE:59==
Sweet potato fries at 190◦C
Y = 0.5x −1.1
Inserting ln(18 µg/kg) into Y’s place and solving for X results in a time at ∼8 min.
In order to ensure a ’safe’ end product for adults with an average consumption when baking
at 190◦C the sweet potato fries must not be baked for longer than 8 min. If instead using
175◦C as the baking temperature the baking time would be twice as high at 16 min.
For the application of the mitigation techniques a temperature of 175◦C and a time of 22 was
used. The theoretical acrylamide concentration can be calculated based on the equations in
ﬁgure 28 and ﬁgure 26 and this value can be compared with the value found. The theoretical
acrylamide concentration sweet potato fries would have when baked at 175◦C for 22 min
is 2440 µg/kg, this value is somewhat consistent with the average acrylamide concentration
found at 2012 µg/kg. The theoretical acrylamide concentration carrot fries would have when
baked at 175◦C for 22 min is 1299 µg/kg, this value is not consistent with the value which
was found to be 326 µg/kg. A possible explanation for the found value being lower than the
theoretical value could be that the acrylamide precursors where used up. When these are
absent, no more acrylamide is formed. At which acrylamide concentration the precursor have
run out require more experiments at longer times, this additional experiments would show
that the acrylamide formation at some time would become logarithmic and have a shoulder,
where the maximum acrylamide concentration can be read. Another possible explanation
could be because diﬀerent carrots was used in the ﬁrst study compared with the ones used
in the second study. 3 months passed between the ﬁrst study and the second study and the
carrot matrix could have changed, due to climate, season, brand, species etc.
Knowing the acrylamide concentration at diﬀerent temperatures and times has been shown
to be a useful tool, when wanting to ensure a ’safe’ end product for the consumers. It enables
the industry to guide the consumers correctly, when informing about the required baking
temperature and time on their packaging.

the question is then if it would be possible to make the consumers conduct any of these
mitigation techniques at home when making home made oven-baked vegetable fries. In order
to motivate the consumers to conduct safety measures when cooking at home, it is important
to educate them about the risk, or else they will might not consider the mitigation step as
an important part, but rather as a unnecessary time consuming step.

8.3.1
Impact of pH on the kinetics of acrylamide formation
In order to explain the lowering of acrylamide formation when lowering the pH, a previous
study by Jung et al. [2003] is used. Jung et al. [2003] was the ﬁrst to investigate the reduction
of acrylamide formation by lowering pH in a food system. The formation of acrylamide is
already known to be dependent on the pH. The ﬁrst step of the acrylamide formation in
the Maillard reaction is the formation of a Shiﬀbase, this happens via nucleophillic addition
of the alpha-amino group of the asparagine to the partially positive carbonyl carbon of the
dicarbonyl compound. Lowering the pH would convert the nucleophilic free nonprotonated
amine (-NH2) into a nonnucleophilic protonated amine (-NH3+), eﬀectively blocking the
nuclerophilic attack.

8.6
Uncertainties
Preparation before baking
Even though vegetable of the same brand and species was selected, it could be possible that
diﬀerences within the vegetables occurred. For the sweet potatoes this diﬀerence could be
the farmer, since it was not stated. This means that the sweet potatoes could come from
diﬀerent soil conditions, causing a change in the composition of the sweet potato. For the
carrot diﬀerences occurred within the carrot, since carrot has a thicker part in the middle.
Also, the carrots was bought while refrigerated. The time-span they have been refrigerated
was unknown. Refrigeration of vegetables promotes sugar generation, and the longer it has
been refrigerated, the more sugar is formed (Bartz and Brecht [2003]).
During the experiment a big batch of vegetable was cut and seasoned, but only 20 fries was
baked at a time. This caused some fries to stay in the bowl for a long period of time. This
resulted in water excreting from the vegetable, which may eﬀect the acrylamide formation in
the fries, since precursors might be leeching out with the water.
Preparation before analysis
The baked fries did not gain a cohesive colour all over. It is suspected that more acrylamide
is formed, where there is a darker colour. Homogenisation was diﬃcult and a completely
homogeneous sample was not possible, with the used equipment. This resulted in samples
where colour diﬀerences was very clear. When moving the homogenised samples to the test
65
==PAGE:67==
tubes, some might have gotten more brown parts than others, which equal higher concen-
tration of acrylamide. Random sample collection was done in order to avoid any bias across
samples.
Consumption data
The consumption data used in this study was extracted from EFSA-Panel [2016]. This data
included the consumption of ’French fries and potato fried’ from various studies, from Euro-
pean countries and from mostly recent time periods. Uncertainties may occur because it is
uncertain whether the consumers is completely substituting potato fries with vegetable fries,
or if they only substitute a certain amount. It was not possible to ﬁnd recent consumption
data of sweet potato and carrot, and it was impossible to ﬁnd any consumption data of
vegetable fries.

Conclusion
The acrylamide concentration in homemade vegetable fries made from sweet potato and car-
rot and the current consumption of ’French fries and potato fried’ is so high that it results
in a carcinogenic risk to the consumers.
The formation of acrylamide in vegetable fries made from sweet potato and carrot hap-
pens exponentially, making it possible to predict the acrylamide concentration at a certain
time/temperature.
There is no correlation between the lightness value L* and the acrylamide concentration in
both sweet potato fries and carrot fries.
A small negative correlation between hardness (Force[N]) and the acrylamide concentration
was found, this is arguably because of the fact that both hardness and acrylamide concen-
tration is aﬀected by the increase of time/temperature.
No correlation between crispiness and acrylamide concentration was found in this study.
Most of the mitigation techniques are able to suﬃciently reduce the acrylamide concentration,
alternatives to the mitigation techniques is lowering the consumption, lowering the acrylamide
concentration by decreasing the temperature or lowering the time the fries are baking.

Future work
Since the work in the laboratory was limited due to the Covid-19 lockdown, a section con-
cerning any additional considerations and laboratory studies has been made.
To make a direct comparison between the acrylamide concentration in the vegetable fries and
regular potato fries, potato fries should have been included in this study, and underwent the
exact same procedures. The comparison conducted in the discussion is based on assumptions
and therefore not necessarily true.
At the beginning of the study, the plan was to conduct a study to examine the precursor
concentrations (glucose and asparagine) to investigate which would be the limiting factor.
When the limiting factor is known, it is easier to narrow the mitigation techniques to ﬁnd
the ones resulting in the highest reduction in acrylamide concentration.
The mitigation technique acetic acid for 15 min and 30 min, almost showed the same value.
It could be interesting to see at how low a time a large reduction still is seen. This is be-
cause the plan with the mitigation techniques is to have at home cookers conduct them, and
they would be interested in the mitigation technique which required the least eﬀort and time.
The physical eﬀect was examined on the vegetable fries without applied mitigation technique.
Investigating the physical eﬀect on the vegetable fries with applied mitigation technique could
be interesting, to see if the mitigation had a positive or negative eﬀect. During the laboratory
work a diﬀerence in colour between vegetable fries which was treated as, control, blanching
and acetic acid, was visually seen. Also, the organoleptic properties could be of interest,
especially for the vegetable fries which has been immersed in acetic acid, since it might result
in an oﬀ-ﬂavour.
