Abstract 
 
Industrial and economic espionage is a growing problem for both governments and private 
companies. In recent years a lot of money has been invested in protecting against the cyber treat. 
But the treat of a physical breach in our ever increasingly complex socio-technical world is still 
alive and well.   
In this thesis we investigate the feasibility of using model checking of socio-technical systems to 
detect and quantify the probability of successfully breaching an organizations physical 
infrastructure and stealing valuable assets.

1 Introduction  
 
Industrial and economic espionage is becoming a bigger and bigger problem for both governments 
and companies. In 2014 the Washington think tank Center for Strategic and International Studies 
estimated that espionage and cybercrime cost the world economy $445 billion US dollars [1].  For 
private companies the theft of trade secret can result in great financial loss inflicted by wasting large 
amounts of resources on R&D without gaining the benefits of the research. Furthermore, in some 
cases the theft can even lead to losing market share to the culprit [2].   
 
Industrial and economic espionage happens in one of two ways. A disgruntled employee might 
choose to leak trade secrets for financial gain or to simply punish the company. The second way is a 
competitor or foreign government wishes to obtain the trade secret to advance their technological 
and financial position [3].  The two most prominent ways that an outside organization or 
government engages in industrial and economic espionage today are through cyber espionage and 
by using an insider.  Cyber espionage entails the least risk for the attacker, since it is relatively easy 
to hide the real source of the attack while being able, if successful, to gain reasonable good 
intelligence on the target. Using an insider entails greater risk of exposure for the attacker but has 
the potential to yield the best intelligence on the target, due to the insider’s knowledge of the inner 
workings of the target.  
 
A lot of attention in terms of both research and investment in infrastructure and organization in 
recent years has focused on the cyber threat. This is of course for good reasons as previously stated. 
However, there is a third way organizations can engage in industrial and economic espionage. This 
is by physically breaching the offices, data centers and buildings of the target to steal the desired 
information. This physical breach can be conducted solely by outsiders or it can be aided by 
insiders. One should not underestimate how successful and impactful a physical breach can be. This 
is illustrated by the case of Professor Willian Davidon leading a small group of people in 
conducting a break-in at a FBI office, which led to the successful theft of secret documents [4]. It is 
generally accepted that there is a tradeoff between usability and security [5]. It is therefore 
imperative to take into consideration the benefit, in this case how much harder, in terms of cost and 
success probability, it is for an adversary to succeed versus the cost incurred to the defending 
organization. To conduct a security analysis of an organization we need a model that captures the 
structure and workings of the organization and here enter socio-technical systems.     
 
Socio-technical systems describe the interaction between humans, technology and the environment 
of the workplace. All modern organization can be modeled as socio-technical systems. In purely 
technical systems, we need only worry about the internal processes of the machines and their 
mutual communication. While in socio-technical systems we need also to take into consideration 
the interaction between people, location and physical objects. The people aspect makes a security 
analysis of such a system tricky indeed.

Example  
 
We will briefly introduce a very small example, which is used throughout this report to illustrate 
and facilitate the understanding of the concepts and techniques we employ. The example is a very 
simple socio-technical physical system.  
We imagine that we have a building consisting of three locations; a reception that functions as the 
entrance to the building, a janitor’s room and an office.    
The janitor’s room is connected to the reception via a lockable door and this is also the case for the 
office. We imagine a scenario where we have two human actors. One of them is an intruder and the 
other is an office worker. As the intruder enters the reception, the office worker is hard at work in 
the office. This of course entails that the initial location of the office worker is the office, while the 
initial location of the intruder is the reception. The objective of the intruder is to come into 
possession of a secret document located in the office. The problem for the intruder is that the door 
to the office is locked and the office worker is in the office and in possession of the key to the 
office. As we make our way through this report we will model this example and conduct an analysis 
of the model to detect and quantify possible attacks.

2 Socio-technical System Model  
 
Organizations consist of people and technology that interact with one another to fulfil the goals of 
the organization. Socio-technical systems try to describe this complex interaction between humans, 
machine and organizational structure. The term socio-technical systems was first coined by Eric 
Trist and Fred Emery [7]. Socio-technical systems theory originates from Trist’s efforts in the years 
immediately after World War 2 to describe the change from one system of coal mining, which 
emphasized autonomous work groups to a more mechanized system drawn from factory procedures.       
With the advent of computers socio-technical systems are as relevant as ever.  
 
2.1 Security in Socio-technical Systems  
 
We are now going to take a brief look at some of the work that has been done to analyze the 
security of socio-technical systems. Gunnarsson [8] and Probst and Hansen [9, 10] analyzed the 
security of a system by using static program analysis methods. The analysis highlights the 
credentials that an actor needs to reach specific locations and access specific data. Actors can move 
around the modeled building and can acquire credentials and data. The system is modeled in 
acKlain, which is a calculus of the Klaim family [11]. Lenzini [6] focusses on analyzing the security 
of physical systems with actors, locations, doors and objects. Actors are giving behavioral 
specifications that are constructed from basic actions. A special actor called the intruder is not 
bound by a behavioral specifications and is free to do as he pleases. Actions have a cost and 
probability of success. They characterize their model as socio-technical physical system. Security 
analysis of the system is done by model checking. The work done in this thesis is inspired from the 
above. We take a step further by implementing a tool that can be used to model STPS and translate 
the model into PRISM code. We also investigate the feasibility of the methods used.       
 
 
2.2 Socio-technical Physical System Model 
 
In the physical space lots of human to human and human to object interaction occurs. People move 
around the physical space in varying patterns. They lock and unlock doors as they move around. 
They are also responsible for moving objects in the space. It is with other words the human actors 
that are the initiators of change in the system. Human behavior is not deterministic and it displays a 
certain amount of stochastic characteristics. That is why any model that tries to capture a system 
with humans as central figures must take into consideration this randomized variation in behavior. 
With this in mind, we will in the following set out to model the basic elements of a STPS.  
 
A STPS consists of physical space with objects and people. It is structured in locations that are 
accessible via lockable doors. People perform actions in this space. They lock and unlock doors. 
They enter and exit rooms. They take and drop objects. Each action happens  
non-deterministically, conditionally and probabilistically and has a cost.

2.3 Labeled Transition System 
 
In computer science we often use transition systems to model the behavior of a system of interest. A 
transition system is a directed graph where the nodes are states and the edges are transitions. A state 
describes information about a system at a giving moment. A transition models the change from one 
state to another. We add labels to the transitions to indicate what triggered the change of state in the 
modeled system.  
 
We model the evaluation of a STPS by means of a labeled translation system 〈𝑺, 𝑆0, Γ , ⟹〉, where: 
 
 𝑺 is the state space. 
 𝑆0 is the initial state. 
 Γ is the set of labels. 
 ⟹⊆𝑺× Γ × 𝑺 is the transition relation.

Labels are tuples of the form Γ ⊆Σ𝑙× ℝ+ × [0,1], where: 
 
 Σ𝑙 is the transition names. 
 The non-negative real numbers in ℝ+ donate the cost of the transition. 
  [0,1] is the probability of the transition.  
 
The names in Σ𝑙 indicate the action being performed, the actor or intruder performing the action as 
well as the objects and subjects of the action. 
 
Σ𝑙= {𝑚𝑜𝑣𝑒(𝑥, 𝑙, 𝑙′), 𝑙𝑜𝑐𝑘(𝑥, 𝑑, 𝑜), 𝑢𝑛𝑙𝑜𝑐𝑘(𝑥, 𝑑, 𝑜), 𝑑𝑟𝑜𝑝(𝑥, 𝑙, 𝑜), 𝑡𝑎𝑘𝑒(𝑥, 𝑙, 𝑜),

2.4 Evaluation of the running example 
 
To illustrate the evaluation of our model let us imagine the following scenario in the running 
example. The intruder enters the reception with the intent of breaking into the janitor room. The 
intruder has good intelligence that the janitor’s uniform is stored there. By wearing the uniform, the 
intruder wishes to blend in and not seem out of place. The intruder then proceeds to wait until the 
office worker leaves the office and enters the reception. The intruder bumps into the office worker 
and in the process steals the office key from the office worker. The intruder uses the key to enter the 
office and steal the secret document. Let us try this scenario with the model of the running example. 
In the following, we only show the elements that are of interest in every state.

3 Model Checking  
 
Model checking is a formal verification technique for verifying the behavior properties of a giving 
system. The verification is done through systematic inspection of all states of a suitable model of 
the system.  
The strengths of the model checking are: 
 
 It is completely automatic.  
 It is a general verification approach that can be applied to a wide range of applications. 
 It supports partial verification (properties can be checked individually).  
 It has a sound and mathematical foundation. It is based on theory of graph algorithms, data 
structures and logic [12]. 
 
The main weakness of model checking is the state space explosion problem. The number of states 
needed to model a system can easily exceed the amount of available computer memory.   
 
3.1 Models  
 
A prerequisite for doing model checking is to have a model. We will now take a brief look at some 
models that support model checking.  
 
3.1.1 Transition system 
 
A transition system is a tuple 𝑇𝑆= 〈𝑆, 𝐴𝑐𝑡, ⟶, 𝐼, 𝐴𝑃, 𝐿〉 where  
 
 𝑆 is a set of states. 
 𝐴𝑐𝑡 is a set of actions. 
 ⟶⊆𝑆× 𝐴𝑐𝑡× 𝑆 is a transition relation. 
 𝐼⊆𝑆 is a set of initial states. 
 𝐴𝑃 is a set of atomic propositions. 
 𝐿 : 𝑆⟶2𝐴𝑃 is a labeling function.  
 
In this transition system if a state has more than one outgoing transition the next transition is 
selected nondeterministically. Similarly, when the set of initial states contain more than one state 
the start state is selected nondeterministically.         
  
The labeling function 𝐿 relates a set 𝐿(𝑠) ∈2𝐴𝑃 of atomic propositions to any state 𝑠.  
𝐿(𝑠) is the set of atomic proposition 𝑎∈𝐴𝑃 that are satisfied by state 𝑠. The state 𝑠 satisfies the 
propositional logic formula Φ if the evaluation induced by 𝐿(𝑠) makes the Φ true; that is: 
 
𝑠⊨Φ    iff    𝐿(𝑠) ⊨Φ 
 
This transition system model does not satisfy our requirements since it does not take into 
consideration probabilistic selection.

3.1.2 Markov Chain     
 
Markov chains behave as transition systems with the only difference that the nondeterministic 
selection of transitions is replaced by probabilistic selection. 
 
A Markov chain is a tuple 𝑀= 〈𝑆, 𝑷, 𝑖𝑖𝑛𝑖𝑡, 𝐴𝑃, 𝐿〉 where  
 
 𝑆 is a finite, nonempty set of states. 
 𝑷∶𝑆× 𝑆⟶[0,1] is the transition probability function such that for all states 𝑠: 
 
∑𝑷(𝑠, 𝑠′) = 1
𝑠′∈𝑆
 
 
 𝑖𝑖𝑛𝑖𝑡∶𝑆⟶[0,1] is the initial distribution, such that 
 
∑𝑖𝑖𝑛𝑖𝑡(𝑠) = 1
𝑠∈𝑆
 
 
 𝐴𝑃 is a set of atomic propositions. 
 𝐿 : 𝑆⟶2𝐴𝑃 is a labeling function. 
 
The transition probability function 𝑷 specifies for each state 𝑠 the probability 𝑷(𝑠, 𝑠′) of 
transitioning from 𝑠 to 𝑠′.  
The value 𝑖𝑖𝑛𝑖𝑡(𝑠) specifies the probability of state 𝑠 being the initial state. The states 𝑠 with  
𝑖𝑖𝑛𝑖𝑡(𝑠) > 0 are the possible initial states.  
The use of the labeling function 𝐿 and atomic propositions is the same as for transition systems.  
 
Markov chains does not satisfy our requirements since it does not allow nondeterministic selection.

3.1.4 Markov Reward Model   
 
A Markov reward model is a Markov chain/Markov decision process in which transition are 
augmented with natural numbers that can be interpreted as a reward or cost. The idea is that 
whenever a specific transition occurs the reward or cost of the transition is earned or paid.  
 
A Markov reward model is a tuple 〈𝑀, 𝑟𝑒𝑤〉 where  
 
 𝑀 is a Markov decision process with transition probability function 𝑷. 
 𝑟𝑒𝑤∶ 𝑷⟶ℕ is a reward function that assigns a non-negative integer reward to each 
transition. 
   
Markov reward models fit our requirements perfectly and of course in our case we interpret rewards 
as costs. With a model now in place we need a way to specify the properties we wish to verify. 
 
3.2 Probabilistic Computation Tree Logic     
 
Probabilistic computation tree logic PCTL is a branching-time temporal logic. A PCTL formula 
formulates conditions on a state of a Markov chain. A state either satisfies or violates a PCTL 
formula.  
 
3.2.1 Syntax of PCTL  
  
PCTL state formula over the set 𝐴𝑃 of atomic propositions are constructed according to the 
following grammar: 
 
Φ ∷= 𝑡𝑟𝑢𝑒 |𝑎|Φ1⋀Φ2|¬Φ|ℙ𝐽(𝜑)  
 
where 𝑎∈𝐴𝑃, 𝜑 is a path formula and 𝐽⊆[0,1] and 𝐽≠∅ is a non-empty interval.

PCTL path formula are formed according to the following grammar:  
 
𝜑∷= OΦ|Φ1 ∪Φ2|Φ1 ∪≤𝑛Φ2 
 where  Φ, Φ1 and Φ2 are state formula and 𝑛∈ℕ. 
 
3.2.2 Satisfaction Relation for PCTL

be PCTL state formula and 𝜑 be a PCTL path formula.  
The satisfaction relation ⊨ is defined for state formula by:  
 
                                                            𝑠⊨𝑎            iff      𝑎∈𝐿(𝑠),

Given a path 𝜎 in 𝑀, the satisfaction relation is defined by: 
 
𝜎⊨OΦ               iff      𝜎[1] ⊨Φ,

3.3 Mapping STPS Models into PRISM Programs  
 
PRISM is a probabilistic model checker that supports the analysis of Markov decision processes. 
Models are inputted to the tool by writing descriptions in the PRISM language. Properties that are 
to be verified are writing in the PRISM property specification language, which is based on temporal 
logic. It incorporates probabilistic computation tree logic and supports costs/rewards properties.    
PRISM performs probabilistic model checking to automatically analyze such properties. PRISM’s 
probabilistic model checking is based on exhaustive search and numerical solutions [13].   
By using PRISM, we can automatically analyze STPS models.   
 
3.3.1 The PRISM Language   
 
The PRISM language is a simple state-based language. The fundamental components of the 
language are modules and variables. A module is composed by local variables and commands. The 
value of the local variables constitutes the state of the module. The behavior of a module is 
described by a set of commands. A model is composed by a number of modules that can interactive 
with each other. The global state of a model is determined by the state of the local variables in all 
modules. The values of local variables can only be changed by commands specified in the module.  
 
 
==PAGE:24==
23 
 
A command takes the form: 
 
[action] guard → prob_1 : update_1 + … + prob_n : update_n; 
 
A command consists of three parts: 
 
1. The optional action name that is used for synchronization with other modules.  
 
2. The guard is a predicate over all variables in the model. 
 
3. The updates describes a transition the module can undergo if the guard is true. A transition 
is specified by assigning new values to the variables in the module. Each update is also 
assigned a probability, which will be assigned to the corresponding transition.  
 
 
A module consists of three components; a name, a list of variable definitions and a list of 
commands.  
A module has the following form: 
 
                                                              module 〈𝑁𝑎𝑚𝑒〉 
                                                                    〈𝑉𝑎𝑟𝑖𝑎𝑏𝑙𝑒 𝑑𝑒𝑓𝑖𝑛𝑖𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛𝑠〉 
                                                                    〈𝐶𝑜𝑚𝑚𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑠〉 
                                                              endmodule 
 
PRISM supports integer and Boolean variable types. Integer variables can take values from a finite 
range [𝑎, 𝑏]. Boolean variables can take values true and false.  
When we define an integer variable, we specify its name, its range of values and its initial value. 
As an example:  
 
𝑥∶[0. .5] init 2; 
 
𝑥∈{0,1,2,3,4,5} is an integer variable with initial value 2. 
  
When we define a Boolean variable, we specify its name and its initial value. 
As an example:  
 
b ∶bool init false; 
 
b ∈{𝑡𝑟𝑢𝑒, 𝑓𝑎𝑙𝑠𝑒} is a Boolean variable with initial value false.

Each object in the STPS model is mapped to a module in PRISM. The location of the object is 
modeled by an integer variable 𝑖𝑜 that is assigned the value that corresponds to the objects location.   
The value of 𝑖𝑜 can only be changed when an actor or the intruder performs a drop action. The

𝑙→𝑖.    
 
The commands are mapped from the actions that modify the object. In the case of objects only the

3.3.4 Door Mapping 
 
Each door in the STPS model is mapped to a module in PRISM. The state of the door is modeled by 
a Boolean variable 𝑙𝑜𝑐𝑘𝑒𝑑 that has the value true if the door is locked and the value false if the door

set of commands in the door module. For a door Δ we have the following mapping to PRISM code: 
 
Δ = 〈𝑙, 𝑙′, 𝑙𝑜𝑐𝑘𝑒𝑑𝑑, 𝑘𝑒𝑦𝑑〉⟶ module Δ lockedΔ  : bool init 𝑙𝑜𝑐𝑘𝑒𝑑𝑑; 〈𝑐𝑜𝑚𝑚𝑎𝑛𝑑𝑠〉 endModule 
 
The commands are mapped from the actions that modify the door. In the case of doors there are 
three types of actions ( lock, unlock, lockpick) that can modify its state.  
For each lock action 𝐿𝑜𝑐𝑘(𝑙, Δ, 𝑜, 𝑝) performed by an actor 𝜃 that modify door Δ we have the 
following mapping to PRISM code: 
 
Case 1: Probability 𝑝= 1: 
 
𝐿𝑜𝑐𝑘(𝑙, Δ, 𝑜, 𝑝) ⟶[LockΔ𝜃] lockedΔ = false −> (lockedΔ′ = true); 
 
Case 2: Probability 𝑝< 1: 
 
𝐿𝑜𝑐𝑘(𝑙, Δ, 𝑜, 𝑝) ⟶[LockΔ𝜃] lockedΔ = false −>  𝑝: (lockedΔ′ = true) + 
(1 −𝑝): (lockedΔ′ = false); 
  
where [LockΔ𝜃] is the action name designating that door Δ is being locked by actor 𝜃, lockedΔ is

3.3.7 Cost 
 
In PRISM we can specify the reward associated with a transition in the rewards … endrewards 
construct. Reward items describing the reward associated with a transition are of the form. 
 
[𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛] 𝑔𝑢𝑎𝑟𝑑∶𝑟𝑒𝑤𝑎𝑟𝑑;  
 
The interpretation being that transitions from states that satisfy the guard 𝑔𝑢𝑎𝑟𝑑 and are labelled 
with the action 𝑎𝑐𝑡𝑖𝑜𝑛 acquire the reward 𝑟𝑒𝑤𝑎𝑟𝑑. Just to clarify we interpret rewards as cost in 
our model.  
 
Let us illustrate with our running example: 
 
We set the cost of performing a lockpick on the janitor’s room door to 100 and probability of 
success to 0.5. 
 
𝑠0

5 Analysis  
 
We will now demonstrate the analysis of STPS models. The running example will be the model we 
will analyze. Once we have demonstrated the analysis we will turn our attention to analyzing the 
feasibility of our approach. 
      
 
5.1 An Analysis of the Running Example  
 
The first step in the analysis is to input the model into our tool. This is done by writing the models 
specification (see 4.2).   
 
The next step is to translate the model to a corresponding model in PRISM. The output of this 
translation can be seen in Appendix A.   
 
In section 2.4 we conducted an evaluation of the model were the intruder was able to successfully 
obtain the secret document within 10 steps. The condition the intruder had to fulfill for obtaining 
the secret document was to be in possession of the janitor uniform and office key. Let us use 
PRISM to quantify the probability of the intruder obtaining the secret as described in section 2.4. 
Let us specify the property in the PRISM property specification language.  
 
Pmax=?[F=10 (JanitorUniformIntruder = true) & (OfficeKeyIntruder = true) & 
(SecretDocumentIntruder = true) & (intruderLocation = 0) &(OfficeWorkerLocation = 1)])]     
 
In this property we ask what is the maximum probability that within 10 steps the intruder will be in 
the office alone and in possession of the secret document, janitor uniform and office key. 
Let us go to PRISM and verify this property.  
 
  
 
We get that the maximum probability of the intruder stealing the secret document within 10 steps is 
0.1.

6 Conclusion  
 
We have produced a tool that is able to specify a socio-technical physical system model and 
translate the model into a corresponding PRISM model. We have shown that by using PRISM to 
conduct model checking on the model we can detect and quantify the probability of attacks. We 
have shown that this approach has its limitations. It is only possible to conduct model checking on 
relatively small models. If a model reaches a certain size, we run into the state space explosion 
problem. 
More features can be added to socio-technical physical system model. This could be modeling the 
cyber domain or just simply adding more physical actions. Regardless, the program that we have 
designed can easily be extended as more features are added to the model. One should just have in 
mind that as we increase the complexity of the model we substantially decrease the size of models 
that can be model checked.
