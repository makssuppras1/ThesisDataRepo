Abstract 
The aim of this study was to investigate the hue-heat hypothesis which suggests that CCT has 
an impact on the thermal sensation. If confirmed, the association between thermal sensation 
and CCT could become relevant, as the energy potentially could be reduced without 
compromising occupant comfort. The investigation was carried out in a climate chamber, 
designed to accommodate four people. The chamber was installed with new controllable LED 
lighting technology, which could gradually change the correlated colour temperature (CCT), 
thus making warm and cool colours easy to modify in order to manipulate thermal perception. 
Three types of experiments were conducted; experiment type 1 to investigate the ‘pure’ effect 
of CCT at the ideal comfort temperatures for cooling- and heating season (22 °C and 24.5 °C, 
respectively), experiment type 2 to investigate if a PC monitor would dominate the perceived 
lighting from the LED fixtures, and experiment type 3 to investigate the possible different 
perception of light for people over 30 years, as eyesight changes with age. The immediate effect 
to different CCTs was investigated for all experiment types, and the effect of exposure to the 
same CCT for 30 minutes was investigated for experiment type 1 and 2.  
There was not found an association between thermal sensation and CCT for any of the 
experiments. However, results at 22 °C for experiment type 1 indicated tendencies of an 
association. These results suggested that a total difference in operative temperature of 1.8 °C 
(22 °C ± 0.9 °C) could be achieved when adjusting the CCT. The effect of an exposure to the 
same CCT for 30 minutes showed that the immediate effect of thermal sensation vanished over 
time.  
A significant association was found between perceived lighting and CCT for experiment type 
1. The results showed that the lighting was perceived brighter, preferred darker and more glare 
was experienced with increasing CCT. The results from experiment type 2 demonstrated that 
the lighting from a PC monitor dominated the perception of lighting from the LED fixtures. 
The results from experiment type 3 confirmed the theory stating that the colour vision at low 
wavelengths (blue light) is diminished with age. 
The results highly indicated that in order to find and association between thermal sensation and 
CCT, there had to be an association between the perceived lighting and CCT.

1 Introduction 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:20==
 Colour temperature of Lighting and Comfort 
 
2 
 
 
 
The hue-heat hypothesis suggests that colour has an impact on the perception of temperature. 
Earlier studies have shown that there is indeed a connection between the correlated colour 
temperature and the perception of ambient temperature. The aim of this project is, also by using 
experiences from earlier studies, to test the hypothesis. The hue-heat hypothesis originates from 
the: “… universal tendency to speak of green or blue as “cool” colors, and red or orange as 
“warm”.” (von Allesch, 1925). Since 1926 (Mogensen et al 1926), colours have been variated 
in different ways to test the hypothesis. With the use of new controllable LED lighting 
technology, it is now possible to gradually change the correlated colour temperature (CCT) in 
practice. Thus, warm and cool colours can easily be modified to manipulate thermal perception. 
When designing a building today, two factors are especially relevant; the energy consumption 
and the indoor environment. Today, energy consumption in buildings accounts for roughly 40 
% of the total energy consumption in Denmark. The energy is primarily used for heating, 
ventilation and lighting (Energistyrelsen, n.d.). Besides the energy consumption, a good indoor 
environment is important as it improves occupant satisfaction, which results in fewer sick 
leaves and complaints, and increases productivity (Wargocki, 2016).    
The purpose of testing the hue-heat hypothesis was primarily to understand whether CCT of 
lighting could influence the perception of warmth (Mogensen et al 1926). But with an increased 
focus on both lowering the energy consumption in buildings and the relation between occupant 
comfort and productivity, combined with the new possibilities that LED lighting provides, the 
hypothesis has again become relevant due to the potential of reducing the energy use without 
compromising occupant comfort.   
1.1 About the project 
This master thesis is part of a larger project with the project number ‘PSO 348-030’ and project 
title ‘Warm or Cool? Lights influence on thermal comfort’. The project is made in collaboration 
with AAU/SBi, DTU Civil Engineering department, and DTU Photonics Engineering 
Department and is funded by ELFORSK, which is Danish Energy Associations research- and 
development program that funds 25 million kr. each year for projects having a potential for 
energy efficient solutions.

1.2 Previous bachelor- and master theses  
Kasper Støttrup and Vibeke Christensen, two engineering students finishing their bachelor 
degree in Civil Engineering at DTU, took part in the initial part of this project. This initial study 
took place from August 2016 until January 2017 and helped create a basis for how the lab

choosing which LED luminaries should be used in the test room, and made sure that the rest of

phase; the lab studies. 
As part of their Master Theses (Architectural Engineering) at DTU, three engineering students; 
Michelle Kühl, Marie Rosenbeck, and Nanna Sofie Gudmandsen took part in, and carried out 
the first real lab studies for this project. Similar to the present Master Thesis, their theses were 
divided into five phases; 1. literature studies, 2. planning of experiments, 3. carrying out the 
experiments, 4. processing of results, and 5. analyses, discussion and conclusions. The details 
of these five phases will be described later in this section of the report. The results of their 
experiments will be outlined in section 3. Previous studies. Due to their results, it was decided 
to do another round of lab studies, with slightly different climatic- and lighting conditions. 
1.3 This Master Thesis 
This master thesis was made in the period from 28th of August 2017 to the 12th of February 
2018. The investigation of the hue-heat hypothesis for this master thesis included experiments 
with and without a PC. The effect of ‘pure’ colour temperature has been proven, but as 
everybody uses a PC today, it was investigated if the lighting emitted from a PC monitor would 
dominate the lighting from the ceiling fixtures and thereby diminish the effect of the CCT. 
Immediate effect and the effect of longer exposure time to the lighting was investigated. Longer 
exposure times to the same CCT was conducted to investigate whether or not the subjects

would adapt to the lighting, both thermally and visually over time. As these experiments were 
conducted with DTU students, experiments with employees from DTU over the age of 30 were 
also conducted to see the possible different perception of light, as the eyesight of people change 
with age. Lastly, it was investigated to see if the CCT impacted the thermoregulation of the 
subjects or if the felt temperature was purely perception, done by measuring core-/skin 
temperature after being exposed to the same CCT (either high or low) for a longer period of 
time.  
Therefore, the hypotheses being investigated in this report are: 
 The thermal environment is perceived warmer at low CCT than at high CCT. 
 The lighting is perceived brighter for increasing CCTs. 
 When using a PC, the light from the PC monitor will reduce the effect of the CCT.  
 Longer exposure time will give a more neutral voting. 
 Core- and skin temperature are not affected by the CCT. 
 The air feels stuffier at low CCT and will therefore be deemed less acceptable. 
 The perception of the lighting will change with age. Thus, the CCT will not affect the 
perception of temperature. 
This master thesis has been divided into the same 5 different phases as the former master thesis 
with lab studies: 
Phase 1. Literature studies 
First of all, literature such as publications describing earlier studies and theory dealing with the 
subject were read in order get an understanding of the hue-heat hypothesis, and to learn what 
type of experiments had already been done in order to set up a realistic set of experiments and 
confirm or reject the hypotheses. The literature study allows ‘carrying on’ from former projects, 
avoiding wasting time on types of experiments that have already been done and did not work - 
and in this way having the best background for preparing the experiments. 
Phase 2. Planning of experiments 
The planning of the experiments was mostly based on the former lab study with some minor 
changes. The test room was designed, temperature and lighting settings were decided, 
designed, and tested to make sure they worked properly. It was decided how many and what 
kind of test subjects should participate in the experiments, how they should be recruited, and 
how many experiments should be carried out in order to get the best results. An experimental 
protocol and questionnaires/performance tests for the experiments were developed.

Phase 3. Experiments 
The subjects comprised 36 students and 9 employees from DTU, Lyngby. The experiments 
took place in week 44, 45 and 46 and thus lasted almost three weeks. Based on the experimental 
protocol, the experiments were carried out with time precision down to the minute. Using a 
walkie-talkie, the subjects were told precisely when to fill out the questionnaires and when to 
start the performance tests. During the experiments, different parameters were measured in

The indoor environment has an impact on the occupants’ health and productivity – and as 
people spend 90 % of their lifetime indoors, a good environment is important. Many parameters 
have an influence on the indoor climate, and these are essential to take into consideration in 
order to achieve a good indoor environment. In this section, the theory concerning the thermal 
and visual environment will be elaborated. Furthermore, regulations and standards for these 
will be described.  
This project aims to examine the hue-heat hypothesis, where the main hypothesis states that 
different CCTs may have an impact on the perception of temperature. Therefore, the main focus 
in this section is on the theory concerning the thermal- and visual environment. As daylight 
was neglected in the experiments, no theory concerning this will be included. 
2.1 Thermal Comfort 
ASHRAE standard 55 (2013) and ISO 7730 (2005) define thermal comfort as ‘‘the condition 
of the mind in which satisfaction is expressed with the thermal environment’’. However, this 
definition contains a clear subjectivity that is further expressed by Djongyang et al. (2010): “... 
it correctly emphasizes that the judgment of comfort is a cognitive process involving many 
inputs influenced by physical, physiological, psychological, and other factors”. Thus, the 
perception of thermal comfort depends on people’s subjective sensation and some cognitive 
processing of a multitude of sensory stimuli, even in the same environment. Due to these 
individual differences, it is not possible to define a thermal indoor climate that will satisfy 
everyone. Fangers climate chamber experiments from 1968 indicate that the highest achievable 
satisfaction rate is 95 % meaning that even when most people assess an indoor environment as 
neutral, 5 % consider it as unsatisfactory (Fanger 1970).  
 Human Thermoregulation 
The body temperature is normally relatively stable and varies within one or two degrees from 
37 ℃. This is due to the thermoregulation mechanisms that balance the amount of heat 
produced in the body with the heat lost from the body (Havenith 2005). The control for the 
temperature regulation is handled by the hypothalamus, which is a small centre in the brain. It 
responds to both internal and external stimulation by sending signals to activate physiological 
processes including sweating, shivering and blood flow regulation. An increase in body 
temperature will activate the sweat production in order to decrease the temperature by 
evaporation or reduce the blood flow to the extremities such as hands and feet. Oppositely a 
decrease in body temperature will activate shivering, to increase the heat production, or

 Prediction of thermal comfort 
The first condition to achieve thermal comfort is to maintain the heat balance for the body as a 
whole and, secondly, to avoid local thermal discomfort (Fabbri 2015). Six parameters influence 
the thermal sensation and are used to quantify thermal comfort. These parameters are divided 
into two categories, environmental parameters and personal parameters. The four 
environmental parameters include air temperature, air velocity, relative humidity and mean 
radiant temperature, and the two personal parameters include clothing insulation and activity 
level i.e. metabolic rate (ISO 7730 - 2005). The activity level is quantified as a metabolic unit 
(1 met) which corresponds to 58.2 W / m2 and the clothing insulation is quantified as a clothing 
unit (1 clo) which corresponds to 0.155 m2 ∙ C / W. The standard values for sedentary work are 
1.2 met and for the clothing insulation, the standard values are 1 clo and 0.5 clo for heating and 
cooling season, respectively.  
The most commonly used model to predict thermal comfort for the body as a whole is drafted 
by Ole Fanger. It is based on climate chamber experiments conducted in 1968 and is called the 
predicted mean vote (PMV). The PMV expresses the expected mean value of the thermal 
sensation of a large group of persons exposed to the same conditions, characterized by the 
above-mentioned six parameters. The index quantifies mean thermal sensation on a 7-point 
thermal sensation scale ranging from -3 to +3 where -3 is cold, 0 is neutral and +3 is hot. From

2.2 Artificial Lighting  
 Light  
Light is described as both waves and particles, thus called the wave-particle-dualism. The 
particle theory of light explains the emission and absorption while the wave theory explains 
the wave propagation (Tsangrassoulis 2004). 
The electromagnetic spectrum is the spectrum of radiation ranging from X-ray to radio waves. 
The wavelength region of 380 nm to 780 nm is called visual light, seen in Figure 2-2. It is 
distinguished from the rest of the electromagnetic spectrum due to the responses from the 
human visual system.  
 
Figure 2-2: The electromagnetic spectrum (Tsangrassoulis 2004). 
 The human visual system 
The human eye is capable of understanding and distinguishing light, colour and shapes. This 
is achieved by a sophisticated visual system that absorbs energy and transfers it to brain by 
electrical impulses. When the light wave travels through the eye, several processes are initiated. 
A few but important processes are; that the pupil area is adjusted to admit the right light level, 
the lens is either fattened or flattened in order to focus on an object and that photoreceptors on 
the retina absorb and convert the light to electrical impulses that are passed to the brain through 
the optic nerve (Boyce 2014). Figure 2-3 shows a sagittal section of the human eye.    
 
Figure 2-3: Section of a human eye (Tsangrassoulis 2004).

There are four types of photoreceptors that are divided in two groups, the rods and the cones. 
The rods are 1000 times more sensitive to light than cones, hence are responsible for night 
vision only. The colour vision is enabled by the cones, which are trichomatic, meaning that 
there are three types of cone receptors, red green and blue (Boyce 2014). When exposed to a 
colour change the visual system will make a chromatic adaptation. The chromatic adaptation 
takes less than 200 ms at moderate light levels (Boyce 2014).

2.2.4.3 Colour quality  
The colour quality of a light source is described with the colour rendering index or the Ra-
value. It is a measure of the light source ability to accurately reproduce an objects colour and 
is a comparison between how the light source renders the colour compared to daylight. The 
index ranges from 0 to 100 where low values corresponds to poor colour rendering and high

 Optical changes  
Vision depends among other things on the amount of light or the wavelengths that reach retina. 
Optical changes come with age, such as the ability to focus on an object or the ability to see 
contrasts. The changes begin in the early adulthood and the form and magnitude increase with 
age. Especially two factors of the optical changes may affect the impact of the hue-heat 
hypothesis, which is a diminished colour vision particularly at low wavelengths and a reduced 
absolute sensitivity to light.  
The sensitivity to light is connected with the amount of light that enters the eye. The pupil is 
the adjusting mechanism, meaning that the pupil size increases or decreases at low or high light 
levels, respectively. With age, the ratio of the maximum to minimum pupil area decreases, 
since the maximum pupil diameter decreases. Thus, elderly are less able to compensate for low 
light levels and are therefore less sensitive to light. Figure 2-6 shows the maximum and

 Visual discomfort  
Light installation is, in addition to guaranteeing that people can see, designed to ensure visual 
comfort. Visual comfort is often explained as the absence of visual discomfort. Except for 
insufficient lighting the most common causes of visual discomfort is uniformity and glare, 
which will be explained in the coming sections.  
2.2.6.1 Uniformity  
Exposure to a total uniform light distribution is undesirable, since lack of contrasts can affect 
the perception of distances etc. In addition, areas with high illuminances adjacent to a work 
desk is e.g. likely to cause distractions for the employees, this is explained by a non-uniform 
space. The uniformity is defined as the ratio of the highest to the lowest illuminance on a plane. 
Studies conducted in windowless rooms have shown that when the uniformity ratio drops 
below 0.7 a considerable increase, from approximately 15 % to 60 %, of people find the lighting 
unreasonable (Boyce 2014). 
2.2.6.2 Glare 
Glare is also a non-uniform illumination just more extreme in the luminance variation. The 
discomfort from uniformity builds over time, whereas the discomfort from glare is apparent 
immediately. Normal indications of glare are behaviour such as blinking, shielding the eyes or 
looking away when a light source of high luminance is present (Boyce 2014). Glare is 
quantified by Unified Glare Rating (UGR).

The first ones to investigate if there was a connection between colours and thermal perceptions 
were Mogensen & English (1926). They wanted to investigate if the apparent warmth of an 
object could influence the perceived temperature when touching the object. In order to test this 
they used six different coloured papers (green, blue, yellow, orange, purple, and red), wrapped 
around rheostats with the same and constant temperature of 42 ℃. The 25 participants of this 
experiment were asked to touch the different colors (two at a time), and tell which one felt the 
warmest without knowing that they actually had the exact same temperature. The results 
showed that there was no connection between colours and thermal perception as green 
(normally considered a ‘cool’ colour) was judged to be the warmest and purple (normally 
considered a ‘warm’ colour) was judged to be the coldest. 
After Mogensen & English failed to prove the hue-heat hypothesis using a tactile method, a 
number of different approaches were investigated in the 20th century. Houghten et al. (1940) 
and Greene & Bell (1980) used a method with colour treatment of surfaces. Both Berry (1961) 
and Fanger et al. (1977) used artificial lighting to examine the hypothesis. The “warm” colours 
were obtained by using plastic foil on fluorescent lighting tubes and blue fluorescent tubes 
were used for the “cold” light. Berry (1961) used five colours, two colours considered “warm”, 
amber and yellow, and two considered “cold”, blue and green, and a white light. Fanger et al. 
(1977) only used blue and red light. Bennett & Rey (1972) experimented with the use of red, 
blue and clear filtered goggles. 
The conclusion for both experiments with coloured surfaces was that no correlation was found 
between the perceived temperature and the colour of the environment. Houghten et al. (1940) 
used an ambient temperature on 71.5 ℉  (≈22 ℃), but suggested that further studies should 
be conducted using temperatures on the boundaries of the thermal comfort zone. Interestingly, 
Greene & Bell (1980) did try to test the hue-heat hypothesis with temperature conditions at the 
boundaries; 18 ℃, 22 ℃, 29 ℃ and 35 ℃, but without any correlation. They argued that a 
reduction in thermal comfort caused the thermal environment to dominate the perception. 
Therefore they proposed that more of the visual field should be coloured. The methods with 
goggles and artificial lighting exposed the subjects to a total coloured visual field, but only 
Fanger et al. (1977) confirmed the hypothesis. Bennett & Rey (1972) discussed whether the 
failure to find a correlation could be caused by other parameters, such as luminance, that were 
not sufficiently controlled, a point that was also articulated by Houghten et al. (1940) in terms 
of temperature. Furthermore, both Bennett & Rey (1972) and Berry (1961) were critical about 
the thermal evaluation methods suggesting that a more sensitive method had to be used.  
==PAGE:41==
3. Previous studies                  
 
23 
 
The method used by Fanger et al. (1977) was very sensitive in terms of evaluating the thermal 
environment. Every 10 minutes the subject was asked whether a warmer or colder ambient 
temperature was preferred. This process was repeated until the subject was satisfied with the 
temperature and did not request further changes all while being exposed to either blue or red 
lighting.  The result of the experiment was that the subjects preferred a 0.4 ℃ colder ambient 
temperature when exposed to red light than when exposed to blue light. Although a correlation 
was found, Fanger et al. (1977) commented that it is not a practically applicable method since 
the colours used were too extreme.   
Since 1980 the exploration of the hue-heat-hypothesis seemed to have stalled, but in recent 
years, from 2014 to 2017, at least eight new studies have been made to investigate the 
connection between colours and thermal perception. These studies are mostly based on the 
methods used by Fanger et al. (1977) using different colours of light. Until 2016 these studies 
continued to use an intermittent change of colour, meaning an abrupt change from one colour 
to another. These studies include Winzen et al. (2014), Vitali et al. (2014) and Albers et al. 
(2015). Vitali found no connection to the hypothesis, but Winzen and Albers concluded that 
the hypothesis could be confirmed. Albers had results to a certain degree similar to Fanger et 
al., and concluded that it could still be a big topic with regards to energy savings. Three other 
studies focused on CCT. Baniya et al. (2016) used gradually changing CCT with constant 
illuminance without finding any connection to the hypothesis. Huebner et al. (2016) also used 
constant illuminance but with CCT at either 6500 K or 2700 K (the extremes) and had results 
which supported the hypothesis. The latest study on the hue-heat hypothesis conducted by Kühl 
(2017) and Gudmandsen & Rosenbeck (2017) was carried out at DTU in the spring 2017. The 
temperature conditions for the experiment were constant at 19 ℃, 22 ℃ or 27 ℃, based on 
Houghten’s suggestion to investigate the boundary of the comfort zone, while the illuminance 
was kept at 1000 lux on the work plane with CCT changing from either 6200 to 2700 to 6200 
K or 2700 to 6200 to 2700 K. At the boundary of the comfort zone the correlation between 
temperature and colour failed to appear, but at 22 ℃ a quite significant difference was found. 
The difference between the subjects thermal evaluation in blue - (6200 K) and red light (2700 
K) corresponds to approximately 2 ℃ in operative temperature; thus the hypothesis was 
confirmed. 
The last two of these eight studies draw similarities to Mogensen & English (1926), applying 
colours to objects. Ho et al. (2014) applied different colours to the hands of the test persons 
(blue or red) after having applied the colour to an object - an experiment which actually showed

Physiological measurements in earlier studies 
In some of the earlier studies, the subjects participating in the experiments had their 
temperature (core- and/or skin) measured to see if there was an association between CCT and 
their temperature, or if feeling warmer at red light and cooler at blue light was just perception. 
Houghten et al (1940) measured the subjects oral and skin temperature to see if there were any 
change when watching an illuminated screen in white, blue, and red light for 7 hours. The 
results did not show any significant changes in temperature. Fanger (1977) measured the skin 
and rectal temperature at different colours of light (white, blue, and red). He concluded that 
there was no significant change in skin or rectal temperature between the results for coloured 
light and white light.

4 Methodology

In this section, the methodology used to investigate the hue-heat hypothesis will be elaborated. 
Students and employees were used as subjects in experiments carried out in a climate chamber 
with controlled thermal conditions and LED lighting with changing CCT. The subjects’ 
perception was evaluated during the experiments with questionnaires, performance tests, and 
human temperature measurements. 
4.1 Experiment type 
The laboratory experiment was conducted under two thermal conditions with a constant 
operative temperature at either 22 ℃ or 24.5 ℃, which corresponds to the ideal operative 
temperature for the heating and the cooling season, respectively (ISO 7730-2005). The 
temperatures were chosen based on the findings from Kühl (2017) and Rosenbeck & 
Gudmandsen (2017) who found an effect on the thermal perception of CCT at 22 ℃ whereas a 
relation between CCT and the perceived thermal sensation was unnoticeable at the boundary 
of the comfort range. While the operative temperature was kept constant at either 22 oC or 24.5 
oC, a lighting animation with changing CCT was run. To balance out a possible hysteresis 
effect, 50 % of the subjects experienced a lighting animation where the CCT gradually 
increased from low-to-high going from 2700 K to 6100 K (LH), and the other 50 % experienced 
the opposite and decreasing animation from high-to-low (HL). The lighting animations started 
and ended at the same CCT to eliminate bias caused by a noticeable difference in CCT when 
entering and leaving the room. The chosen CCT was 3700 K so the time to fade to each extreme 
(2700 K and 6100 K) was the same. In each experiment there were two long waits, one at 2700 
K and one at 6100 K, meaning the CCT was constant at these values for 40 minutes and one 
short wait at a constant CCT of 4000 K for 10 minutes. Based on the minimum requirements 
from the standards for lighting stated in section 2.2.7 the illuminance for the lighting 
animations was set to approximately 500 lux on the tables. The lighting animation and settings 
are documented in section 4.4.2.  
Additionally, the experiment sought to investigate whether a possible association between CCT 
and thermal sensation would be diminished when bringing the experiment closer to a realistic 
work environment. Firstly, it was investigated whether a possible effect of the HHH is an 
immediate effect to a given CCT and/or if a longer exposure time would increase, diminish or 
retain the effect, i.e heat balance would dominate the cognitive processing. This was 
investigated by the length of the waits; two questionnaires were filled out at a long wait, the 
first questionnaire when the CCT reached the extremes and the second after a constant exposure 
time of 30 minutes. Long waits dealt with both an immediate- and longer exposure time effects 
==PAGE:47==
 
4. Methodology 
 
 
29 
 
whereas short waits only dealt with the immediate effect. Secondly, office workers spend most 
of their workday by computers, which make the dominant light exposure the “cool lighting” 
from the monitors (CCT = 5600 K for the monitors used in the experiment). By introducing 
monitors in some of the experiments, the effect of light from the monitor on subject perception 
could be compared with the experiments without monitors. Thirdly, since vision changes with 
age the experiment aimed to investigate whether different age groups would perceive the 
controlled environment differently (Boyce 2014).  
For ease of communication in this report, the three types of experiments will be referred to as: 
Type 1. Paper experiment 
In this experiment, the subjects were students. They should fill out the questionnaires and 
performance tests with pen and paper. The paper was dull and yellowish in order to reduce the 
risk of glare. When they were not filling out questionnaires and tests they had the opportunity 
to read a newspaper, or do Sudoku/crosswords – also on paper without risk of glare. This 
experiment lasted 2 hours with long waits (40 minutes) at the extreme CCTs. Two operative 
temperature levels were used, 22 and 24.5 °C. 
Type 2. PC experiment 
As for the paper experiment, the subjects participating in the PC experiments were students. In 
this experiment, the test subjects should fill out the questionnaires and performance tests on a 
PC. They were encouraged to use the PC in the periods when they were not filling out 
questionnaires/performance tests. This experiment also lasted 2 hours with long waits (40 
minutes) at the extreme CCTs. The PC experiments were carried out at a operative temperature 
of 22 °C. 
Type 3. Senior experiment 
This experimental condition was also carried out with paper questionnaires/performance tests. 
The operative temperature was 22 °C and the test subjects were employees from DTU, Lyngby 
with a mean age of 44 years. Since the experiment was carried out in their lunch break, the 
duration of the experiment was only 1 hour. Only short waits of 10 minutes were used.

As illustrated in Figure 4-3, there was a total of four heating coils installed in the ventilation 
system, one central heating coil in the control room and one local heating coil before the supply 
diffuser for each climate chamber. This made it possible to control the temperature individually 
in the three climate chambers. The ventilation system had a total of seven temperature sensors 
(T1-T7). T1 was installed right after the central heating coil. T2, T3, and T4 were installed

damper regulated the airflow for each in the chambers. F1, F2, and F3 estimated the airflow 
for each climate chamber.

heating coil. From a control cabinet, the supply air, exhaust, and local heating coil could be 
turned on or off. The desired values for supply air (F1-F3) and inlet temperature (T2-T4) for 
the different rooms were controlled by the computer in the control room.

4.5 Subjective measurements  
Questionnaires, performance tests, and skin/core temperature were used to ‘measure’ the 
subjects during each experiment. The various methods will be elaborated in this section. 
 Questionnaire  
During each experiment, the subjects were asked to fill out five rounds (experiment type 1&2) 
or three rounds (experiment type 3) of identical questionnaires containing 20 questions (see 
Appendix I). The questions were defined in compliance with DS/EN 10551 (2001) and were 
based on different subjective judgement scales. All scales that focused on the subjects’ 
perception of temperature and lighting consisted of evaluative scales, preference scales, and 
perceptual scales. The questionnaire also consisted of questions dealing with other indoor 
environmental aspects such as air quality, alertness, and noise in order to assure that it covered 
a broad spectrum with regards to indoor environment. These questions were also added in order 
to make sure that the subjects would not be able to guess the purpose of the experiments based 
on the questionnaire. 
==PAGE:64==
 Colour temperature of Lighting and Comfort 
 
46 
 
 
 
 Performance tests 
Two different performance tests were used in order to measure each subject’s level of 
concentration under different lighting settings. The performance tests used were the Tsai-
Partington test and the Baddeley test. For experiment type 1 and 2, the subjects should do five 
rounds of the tests and for experiment type 3 they should do three rounds.  
4.5.2.1 Tsai-Partington test 
The Tsai-Partington is a 1-minute test where the subject should draw lines from one number to 
the next in increasing order without lifting the pencil during the test. For example if the lowest 
numbers are 7, 8, 16 etc., the subject should draw a line from ‘start’ to ‘7’ to ‘8’ to ‘16’ and 
continue from there in order to get the test correct. If the subject misses a number during the 
test, for example draws a line from ‘7’ to ‘16’ and misses the ‘8’, the subject should just leave 
the ‘8’ behind and continue. The test should be compiled with 20 random numbers between 0 
to 100 and is different in the Tsai-Partington tests given each question round during each 
experiment. An example of the test can be seen in Figure 4-18. 
 
Figure 4-18: Example of a Tsai-Partington test. 
4.5.2.2 Baddeley test 
The Baddeley test is based on grammatical transformation (Baddeley 1968) – each subject has 
3 minutes for the test. The test contains 64 sentences, where the letter sequence AB or BA ends 
==PAGE:65==
 
4. Methodology 
 
 
47 
 
each sentence. The sentences tell the order of these last two letters and the subject then has to 
decide whether the sentence is true or false. If for example the order of the letters is BA, a 
correct answer could be B precedes A, A follows B, B does not follow A etc. and would be 
false if the order of the letters is AB. In order to create the test, six boundary conditions should 
be complied with in terms of different combinations of sentences (Baddeley 1968): 1. Positive 
or Negative, 2. Active or Passive, 3. True or False, 4. Precedes or Follows, 5. A or B mentioned 
first, 6. Letter pair AB or BA. The different Baddeley tests given in each experiment had a 
different random order. The subjects were told to work as quickly as possible, and complete as 
many sentences as possible during the 3 minutes given, marking TRUE or FALSE on each 
sentence without leaving blank spaces along the way until the time was up. An example of the 
sentences for the Baddeley test can be seen in Figure 4-19.

5.4 Noise 
The sound pressure level in the climate chamber was measured by Christensen & Støttrup 
(2017) to be 48 dB (A). A damper was installed before the inlet in the ventilation system prior 
to these experiments and the sound pressure level has been reduced to 44 dB (A). The sound 
pressure level in the climate chamber is higher than the design values which, according to 
DS/EN 15251, are 35 dB (A) for small offices/conference rooms, and 40 dB (A) for landscaped 
offices/office cubicles.

7 Discussion 
 
 
 
==PAGE:118==
 Colour temperature of Lighting and Comfort 
 
100 
 
 
 
This master thesis examined if there was an association between CCT and thermal sensation. 
In this section the results of the questionnaire responses, core-/skin temperature measurements, 
and performance tests have been discussed, as well as the experimental design. Furthermore, 
considerations concerning future investigations have been made. 
7.1 Questionnaire responses 
 Thermal environment 
The p-value for the association between CCT and the thermal perception (p = 0.062) was just 
above the significance level (5 %) for the immediate effect. However, the null-hypothesis was 
not rejected as an association was found between CCT and the related questions on thermal 
comfort (Q2) and the preferred temperature (Q3). The investigation of Q1, Q2 and Q3 showed 
that the responses clearly depended on time. As argued, the descending voting in Q1 appeared 
to be caused by residual metabolic heat. The intersection, after 40 minutes, between the slopes 
for the different functions (CCT dependent) showed a counterbalancing effect, which indicated 
an association between CCT and the thermal sensation. The finding of a decreasing metabolic 
heat is in congruence with earlier studies from climate chambers, concluding that “… subjects’ 
average thermal sensation decreases steadily during the first 2 h, even during exposure to 
constant temperatures.” (Knudsen et al. 1989). This suggests that the acclimatisation of 30 
minutes was too short as the subjects’ steady-state thermal sensation was not reached. The 
responses given on the 9-point scale for Question 1 corresponded to a difference of 0.4 scale 
units between the extreme CCTs. Using the Berkeley Comfort Tool (Hoyt, et. al 2013), this 
again corresponded to an operative temperature difference of ± 0.9 °C. The finding is in 
accordance with earlier studies by Fanger et al. (1977), Huebner et al. (2016), Kühl (2017), and 
Gudmandsen & Rosenbeck (2017). 
Earlier studies investigating temperature conditions on the boundaries of the comfort zone 
showed no association between CCT and the perceived temperature, and argued that further 
investigations with ideal comfort temperatures should be conducted (Greene & Bell 1980, Kühl 
2017, Gudmandsen & Rosenbeck 2017). Therefore, the investigation was carried out under 
two thermal conditions; 22 °C and 24.5 °C for heating and cooling season, respectively. The 
tendencies found at 22 °C between CCT and the thermal related questions were not found at 
24.5 °C. This could indicate that humans are less susceptible to sensory stimuli at 24.5 °C than 
22 °C which may be caused by simulating cooling season conditions in the winter, since the 
subjects’ thermal expectations could depend on the actual season. This implies that the 
physiological influences dominate the psychological.   
==PAGE:119==
7. Discussion 
 
101 
 
 Visual environment 
From previous studies, it was argued that in order to document an association between CCT 
and thermal sensation the lighting design should be extreme in illuminance and/or colour 
(CCT) (Fanger et al. 1977, Christensen & Støttrup 2017), e.g. Kühl (2017) used an illuminance 
of 1000 lx. A more realistic lighting design, with illuminance of 500 lx, was investigated in 
this study. This corresponds with the minimum requirement of illuminance on a work plane as 
stated in DS/EN 12464 (2011). In addition, the possibility of simulating wallwashers to get 
maximum exposure was examined but neglected due to the UGR. Even with these non-extreme 
lighting conditions, a clear association between CCT and perceived brightness (Q10), preferred 
brightness (Q12), experienced glare (Q15) was found. The lighting was perceived brighter, 
preferred darker and more glare was experienced with increasing CCT. This is in accordance 
with findings by Kühl (2017) and Gudmandsen & Rosenbeck (2017). When introducing a PC 
monitor these associations did not appear. The perception of brightness (Q10) was constant 
between ‘bright’ and ‘very bright’ over the entire CCT range, which is explained with the 
dominating light from the PC monitor. This argument is further emphasised by the preferred 
lighting (Q12) and experienced glare (Q15), which for both questions was voted constant for 
all CCTs; for Q12 between ‘neither brighter nor darker’ and ‘slightly brighter’ and for Q15 
close to ’no glare’. The perception of brightness for subjects over the age of 30 years is in 
accordance with the theory that colour vision at low wavelengths (blue light) decreases with 
age, as the perceived brightness was voted brighter with increasing CCT until 4019 K, after 
which the lighting was perceived equally bright with increasing CCT.  
Since significant results especially on the thermal - and air quality perceptions failed to appear 
it could indicate that the lighting conditions were not extreme enough to affect the sensory 
responses 
 Atmospheric environment 
The statistical analysis did not show an association between the CCT and the perceived air 
quality. However, the mean votes indicated that the air quality was perceived fresher with 
increasing CCT. This is in accordance with findings from Winzen et al. (2014) and Kühl 
(2017), Rosenbeck & Gudmandsen (2017) who argued that the “... air quality is perceived as 
being higher and thus fresher in blue light.” (Winzen et al. 2014). At 22 °C the air quality was 
perceived fresher for all CCTs compared to 24.5 °C. At a neutral thermal sensation, the PAQ 
strongly depended on not only the chemical composition of the air, but also the temperature 
(Wargocki 2004), which explains this difference in PAQ with temperature. Converting the 
==PAGE:120==
 Colour temperature of Lighting and Comfort 
 
102 
 
 
 
acceptability of the air quality to PD, a difference between the CCT extremes of 3.2 % was 
found, which corresponds to a ventilation rate reduction of 13.5 l/s/person.  
 
When introducing PC monitors, the air was generally perceived fresher. The mean votes for 
the perceived air quality were almost constant between ‘neutral’ and ‘slightly fresh’ for all 
CCTs. This could be explained by the evaluation of the perceived brightness which was voted 
constant between ‘bright’ and ‘very bright’. Conversely, the air quality was accepted the least. 
This could be due to sensory pollution caused by the desktop computers. The mean PD over 
the two CCT extremes with and without PC monitors at 22 °C was calculated to 29 and 7 %, 
respectively. This is in accordance with a related study, which showed that “The presence of 
PCs increased the percentage of people dissatisfied with the perceived air quality from 13 to 
41 % …” with PCs emitting 3.4 olf (Bakó-Biró et al. 2004).  
 Symptom intensity  
There was a significant association between the CCT and the symptom intensity in at 24.5 °C. 
This is in accordance with related studies dealing with occupant responses related to thermal 
environment (Kolarik et al. 2009). In addition to the CCT dependency, the symptom intensity 
depended on time, meaning that longer time in the chamber resulted in higher intensity of the 
symptoms. 
 Exposure time 
The immediate perception of the visual environment (Q10, Q12 and Q15) seemed to diminish 
over an exposure time of 30 minutes with constant CCT. The subjects adapted to the conditions, 
thus voting more neutral after 30 minutes [2] than at the immediate response [1]. As the 
perception of the thermal environment (Q1) seemed to be weakly associated with the CCT, the 
vanished effect of the thermal perception over the 30 minutes exposure corresponded to the 
decreasing effect of the perceived lighting (Q10). 
7.2 Physiological measurements  
There was no association between measured the core-/skin temperature and CCT which is in 
accordance with findings of Houghten et al. (1940) and Fanger et al. (1977). 
The observations at 22 °C for experiment type 1 for the thermal perception (Q1-Q3) decreased 
(perceiving the thermal environment colder) with time, which suggested that the heat balance 
or residual heat affected the subjects’ thermal sensation. Further investigations showed that the 
==PAGE:121==
7. Discussion 
 
103 
 
measured skin temperature on the hand decreased with time and was associated with the 
thermal sensation. This was supported by the statistical analysis of the skin temperature on the 
hand, which showed an association with the time and the thermal responses. No association 
was found between CCT and the measured core- or forehead skin temperature. That there only 
was found coherence with the hand temperature could be linked with the fact that the thermal 
responses to cold sensations are more pronounced at the extremities (hands and feet) (Odia & 
Aigbogun 1988). 
7.3 Performance tests 
The Baddeley test was recommended to be used for future experiments by Kühl (2017) as it 
supposedly is sensitive to environmental changes. However, no association between the 
performance of the Baddeley test and CCT was found in any of the experiments.  
The Tsai Partington test also did not show any association between CCT and performance. The 
results showed that the difficulty of each individual test had higher influence than the CCT, 
even with the attempt of creating equally difficult tests. Hence, the test was not sensitive 
enough. 
7.4 Experimental design 
In this section, the experimental design has been discussed in terms of the different 
experiments, the lighting animation, and the applied questionnaire.  
 Experiment types  
For all the experiments, the experimental design sought to balance out a possible hysteresis 
effect by including two lighting animations; one going from 2700 to 6100 K (LH) and vice 
versa (HL). Since the number of LH and HL animations was not evenly distributed, as intended, 
the mixed effects model (MEM) needed to account for that by including the interaction between 
time an CCT in the data processing. The direct effect of CCT on the thermal perception could 
have presented itself more clearly if the time effect did not have to be included in the analysis.  
After the experiments, several subjects told that the experiment was too long. In retrospect, 
sitting on a chair during 2 hours (+ ½ h acclimatisation) without moving could have had an 
effect on focus and concentration, which would affect the completion of the questionnaire. The 
experiments could either be shortened or small physical exercises could be implemented during 
the experiment. This could also have a positive effect on the decreasing metabolic heat rate.

104 
 
 
 
A series of experiments were carried out with 36 student subjects with an equal distribution of 
gender. The desired clo-values typical for the cooling (clo = 0.5) and heating seasons (clo = 
1.0) were not entirely achieved. The subjects were told what kind of clothing they should wear 
in the experiments. The calculated values were based on assumptions and on the values given 
in DS/EN 9920 (2009). More uniform clo-values would have been optimal, and could be 
achieved by giving the subjects uniforms prior to the experiments.  
 
A second series of experiments were carried out with a PC monitor. The responses in this 
experiment at 4019 K were not valid, as a programming error in the third questionnaire given 
in the experiment resulted in the disappearance of almost all answers except two. The influence 
of the light from the PC monitor on the perception of brightness was clearly shown, even 
though only 14 subjects participated in the experiment with an uneven distribution of gender 
(more men).  
 
A third series of experiments were carried out with 9 employee subjects from DTU with ages 
ranging from 32 to 55 years. Even though the results from the experiment were in accordance 
with the theory suggesting a diminished colour vision with age at low wavelengths. The age 
dependency could become clearer if the subjects were divided into groups with a 10 years age 
span. Since the experiment was carried out in the subjects’ lunch break, the duration of the 
experiment could only last 1 hour.  It would have been optimal to have as many subjects as in 
the first series with same duration in order to directly compare the perception of light for lower 
age with higher age.  
 The lighting animations 
The lighting animations were designed to accommodate two criteria; the artificial lighting 
should correspond to a realistic work space and the CCT range should be as wide as possible 
based on Fanger et al. (1977) findings with extreme colours. The illuminance level of 500 lx 
resulted in a considerable difference in perceived brightness between the two extreme CCT 
levels. This may have caused the subjects to be aware of the changing light, thus be biased 
when responding to the questionnaire. Secondly, as the CCT went to the extremes there was a 
small flickering of the light. This was caused by either the cold-white - or warm-white diodes 
to switch off at the extremes. Even though the flickering was small it was noticeable and again 
this could increase the attention to the light and thereby cause bias.  
==PAGE:123==
7. Discussion 
 
105 
 
 Questionnaire  
At the introduction meeting, the subjects were asked to read the questionnaire and ask questions 
in case of incomprehensible formulations. Since the questionnaire was written in English, and 
only very few had English as native language, there could still be some language barriers. 
In regards to previous studies, the sensitivity of the evaluation method (questionnaire) was 
discussed by both Bennet & Rey (1972) and Berry (1961). They argued that noticeable results 
would require a very sensible evaluation scale. In order to meet this suggestion, continuous 
scales were used for the questions regarding perception of thermal -, atmospheric -, and visual 
environment. Furthermore, the 7-point thermal sensation scale was extended to a 9-point scale. 
A detection during the experiment was, that the filling in of the questionnaire became faster 
during each experiment. This could imply that the questionnaires were answered by 
automation, and that the focus on evaluating the perceived indoor environment at the specific 
moment was decreased. It was emphasised to the subjects that the whole scale could be used, 
but this automation seemed to cause the subjects to use it as an ordinal scale by marking the 
transitional points i.e. neutral, slightly warm, etc. Thereby the sensitivity was decreased. To 
avoid this automation it should be considered to have either fewer questionnaires or fewer 
questions per questionnaire.   
In order to process the data, the questionnaire responses were typed in manually on a computer. 
This manual process required lots of time and concentration, hence some mistakes might have 
happened. In the data processing phase this was examined by investigating outliers in the 
observations, only few errors were found and corrected.   
7.5 Future investigations/considerations 
Earlier investigations carried out by Fanger et al. (1977) and Kühl (2017) found an association 
between CCT and the thermal sensation but both commented that a more realistic lighting 
design should be used as the extreme colours (high illuminance) were not a practically 
applicable. Since this study failed to find a significant association between CCT and the thermal 
sensation at a more realistic lighting design of 500 lux, the effect from wallwashers should be 
investigated. Wallwasher have the ability to illuminate the walls, thus high illuminances in the 
field of vision can be achieved. 
The challenges in the transition from lab to field studies includes both the number of and 
monitoring of the variables. The only variable in the climate chamber experiments was the 
CCT. In field studies, the temperature, the illuminance, the air change rate etc. may be 
uncontrollable variables. This challenge was addressed by Hougten et al. (1940) and Bennett 
==PAGE:124==
 Colour temperature of Lighting and Comfort 
 
106 
 
 
 
& Rey (1972) who stated that the association between the CCT and thermal sensation failed to 
appear due to uncontrollable variables.  In addition, the field study may introduce a new and 
important variable, which has not yet been implemented in the experiments; the daylight. The 
influence of daylight, as well as the PC monitors, will be included in the field studies which 
will take place at DTU Photonics in Risø. The influence from daylight will affect the lid office 
environment where the visual field is dominated by the PC monitors. As the results for the 
perceived brightness indicated, the light from the PC monitor dominated the visual perception. 
Thus, the interaction between the PC monitor and the artificial lighting could be used as sources 
of exposure.   
 
 
 
 
 
==PAGE:125==
 
8 Conclusion 
==PAGE:126==
 Colour temperature of Lighting and Comfort 
 
108 
 
 
 
The purpose of this master thesis was to investigate if correlated colour temperature (CCT) has 
an impact on the thermal perception. Physical responses to different CCTs was examined as 
part of the investigation. The investigation was carried out in a climate chamber, designed to 
accommodate four people at a time. LED lighting fixtures which gradually could change the 
CCT between cool and warm colours was installed in the climate chamber. A series of 
experiments with physiological measurements included were carried out: 
- 
The effect of ‘pure’ CCT was investigated at the ideal comfort operative temperatures 
for heating and cooling season (22 °C or 24.5 °C) for student subjects. 
- 
PC monitors was implemented in order to investigate if the light coming from the 
monitors would dominate the perceived lighting coming from the LED fixtures. 
- 
As the eyesight of people changes with age, subjects with ages over 30 years 
participated in order investigate a possible different perception of light. 
 
There was not found any significant association between the CCT and thermal sensation in the 
experiments. Yet, the result of the immediate effect at an operative temperature of 22 °C 
showed tendencies of an association. This result indicated that the thermal sensation was 
associated with the CCT, as 2674 K was voted the warmest and 6087 K the coldest. A 
difference of 0.4 on the PMV scale was found for the mean votes at the extreme CCTs. This 
could be converted into a difference in operative temperature of 1.8 °C (22°C ± 0.9 °C), 
meaning that the same thermal sensation should be felt in a thermal environment with 21.1 °C 
and a CCT of 2674 K as an environment with 22.9 °C and a CCT of 6087 K. However the 
results for the subjects’ thermal sensation after 30 minutes of constant CCT exposure at either 
2674 K or 6087 K showed that the immediate effect vanished over time.  
 
There was found a clear significant association between the CCT and the perceived lighting for 
the immediate effect. The lighting was perceived brighter, preferred darker and more glare was 
experienced at higher CCTs than at lower CCTs. These results suggested that the illuminance 
level could be increased for the low CCTs and decreased for the high CCTs, but as there does 
not yet exist a calculation method for this, and as brightness is a personal preference, further 
research needs to be made in order to evaluate how much the illuminance could be changed for 
different CCTs. When exposed to a constant CCT for 30 minutes at either 2674 K or 6087 K, 
the results showed that the subjects got used to the lighting over time, as the effect of the 
perceived brightness decreased over time.  
==PAGE:127==
8. Conclusion 
 
109 
 
When PC monitors were included there was not found any association between the CCT and 
the perceived lighting as the subjects generally voted the lightning to be equally bright for all 
CCTs. This result highly indicated that the lighting from the PC monitors dominated the 
lighting from the LED fixtures. Further research is needed in order to find an association 
between CCT and perceived lighting for environments including a PC monitor.  
For subjects with ages over 30 years there was found an association between the CCT and the 
perceived lighting, though not as clear as for younger subjects (students). Even though only 9 
subjects over the age of 30 years participated, the theory stating that the colour vision at low 
wavelengths (blue light) diminishes with age was confirmed, as their perception of brightness 
flattened out for CCT at 4019, not considering 6087 K (blue light) more bright.  
 
The experiments with PC monitors and subjects over the age of 30 years did not show an 
association between thermal sensation and the CCT (even though both experiments were 
carried out with an operative temperature of 22 °C). The results highly indicate that an 
association between the CCT and the perceived lighting is needed in order to find an association 
between thermal perception and CCT.
