7 Abstract
Breast cancer remains a leading cause of cancer death among women worldwide, leaving 
conventional therapies insufficient despite decades of research in the area. Immune therapy has 
excelled as a potent therapeutic strategy utilizing or enhancing the immune system to efficiently 
fight several cancer types. Breast cancer was originally considered immunologically silent but 
recent studies are encouraging further research of breast cancer immunogenicity. As the epitope 
landscape in breast cancer is minimally described it is essential to identify T cell targets to develop 
competent antigen specific immune mediated therapies.  
 
This project investigates four breast cancer associated proteins aromatase, prolactin, NEK3 and 
PIAS3 as possible shared tumor associated antigens via a reverse immunology approach. 147 
peptides derived from the protein sequences have been in silico predicted and experimentally 
confirmed as HLA A*0201 and HLA B*0702 binders. This peptide library was assessed for 
potential breast cancer related epitopes via screening for CD8 T cell recognition in peripheral blood 
samples from breast cancer patients and healthy donors. A novel high-throughput DNA barcode 
labelled pMHC strategy was utilized that enabled detection of all 147 specificities in a given sample 
simultaneously. In a cohort of 27 breast cancer samples a total of 91 peptide specific CD8 T cell 
responses were observed. Within 10 healthy donor samples screened with against the same 
peptide library 24 responses were detected. For HLA B*0702 restriction, significantly more 
responses were observed in breast cancer patients than healthy donors, whereas HLA A*0201 
restricted responses did not have this distinction.  
Furthermore, in vitro MACS enrichment of pMHC specific T cells was applied within 11 breast 
cancer samples. Screening of these samples led to finding of 21 peptide specific T cell responses, 
of which 6 were detectable in the same donors both before and after enrichment.  
 
CD8 T cell responses were observed against a range of peptides from proteins aromatase, 
prolactin, NEK3 and PIAS3 demonstrating that these are certainly targets for T cell reactivity. 
However, more studies are necessary to establish any of the peptides as true T cell epitopes. 
Functional testing of peptide specific T cell cultures and demonstration of antigen processing and 
presentation is crucial in the validation process. If any breast cancer associated T cell epitopes are 
discovered within these four proteins, they might be of great value in immune therapeutic 
applications offering novel approaches to breast cancer treatment.

 1.
 Aims of the study 
The aim of the conducted study was to examine T cells from breast cancer patients for reactivity 
against peptides derived from four proposed tumor associated antigens (TAAs); aromatase, 
prolactin, NEK3 and PIAS3, restricted by the HLA A*0201 and HLA B*0702 molecules.   
 
Sub aims:  
 
1. High-throughput screening of peripheral blood samples from a cohort of breast cancer 
patients and healthy donors for the presence of spontaneous CD8 T cell responses against 
a library of 147 proposed breast cancer associated peptides via barcode labelled peptide-
MHC multimers.  
 
2. Enrichment of peptide-MHC specific CD8 T cells from breast cancer patient samples via 
magnetic activated cell sorting and subsequent screening for the presence of CD8 T cell 
responses, as in aim 1.  
 
3. Further expansion of peptide-MHC specific CD8 T cells from breast cancer patient samples 
further via fluorescence activated cell sorting and functional testing of these specific 
cultures to assess cytotoxicity along with processing and presentation of potential tumor 
associated antigens.

11 2.
 Introduction 2.1
 Fundamentalprinciples of the immune system 
The human immune system is multifaceted and has the ability to recognize and eliminate 
pathogens and cellular transformation through several mechanisms. Physical barriers such as skin 
and mucosa provide an initial line of defense against threats from the environment. If these barriers 
are breached, a pathogen will face more specialized mediators of protection, e.g. the body’s 
repertoire of leukocytes. Traditionally, the immune system is divided into two branches: innate and 
adaptive. Innate immunity is characterized by its ability to distinguish non-self, e.g. pathogens, as 
foreign via its repertoire of pattern recognition receptors (PRRs) such as Toll-like receptors (TLRs) 
upon encounter with conserved pathogen associated molecular patterns (PAMPs). Innate immune 
defense mechanisms are initiated rapidly upon encounter of non-self and thus provide an 
immediate barrier to infection, attempting to clear threats by recruiting relevant cells and producing 
important effector molecules, e.g. cytokines and chemokines [1]. Innate immune responses are 
generic and do not induce any kind of memory for future re-encounters. However, the type of 
innate immune response that is mounted prominently shapes the adaptive immune response that 
will follow, as this depends profoundly on the signature of cytokines produced by the initial innate 
phase [2].  
 
Antigen presenting cells (APCs), especially dendritic cells (DCs), are important in bridging innate 
and adaptive immune responses. DCs take up and degrade pathogens and their associated 
antigens at the site of infection upon binding to PRRs on the DC cell surface. Antigen carrying DCs 
will travel with the lymph to lymph nodes, where they present antigens to naïve lymphocytes; the 
key players in adaptive immunity [1][3].  
 2.2
 Adaptive immunity 
Upon antigen presentation and co-stimulatory signals from DCs, lymphocytes will mature and 
proliferate, giving rise to an antigen specific immune response. Adaptive or acquired immunity 
develops gradually throughout life depending on exposure and takes longer time to mount 
compared to innate immunity but will, due to induction of specific and persisting memory cells, 
respond very rapidly and effectively upon re-exposure [3]. 
 
B lymphocytes (B cells) and T lymphocytes (T cells) comprise the main mediators of adaptive 
immunity, embodying humoral and cellular defenses, respectively. B cells contribute to eradicate 
extracellular threats via the secreted form of the B cell receptor (BCR): antigen specific 
immunoglobulins that facilitate neutralization, opsonization, and activation of the complement 
system. In contrast, T cells have the inherent capacity to detect and react towards intracellular 
changes and additionally to control and shape B cell responses, as will be outlined in the next 
sections [4].

 2.2.1
 T cells and their development
T cells are a family of cells originating from the bone marrow and maturing in the thymus, 
expressing a T cell receptor (TCR) for antigen recognition and the associated co-receptor CD3 [5]. 
Two main types of T cells are significant, distinguished by their expression of surface co-receptors 
CD4 or CD8. CD4 T cells are characterized as helper T cells (TH), responsible for orchestrating 
other cells to engage their effector functions. Several subtypes of TH cells exist, each with different 
purposes and with cytokine secretion profiles that help to shape immune responses according to 
the danger that is sought eliminated [6]. Examples are follicular TH cells providing help for B cells in 
antibody production, TH1 cells assisting CD8 T cells against intracellular infections and malignancy, 
and TH2 cells assisting with protection from extracellular parasites [6][7].  
CD8 T cells are identified as cytotoxic T cells reacting to changes of intracellular origin, be it viral 
infections or cancer cells, with a collection of cytolytic proteins released upon recognition of target 
cells [4].   
All T cells depend on processing and presentation of antigens by the major histocompatibility 
complex (MHC) for their TCR to recognize. The majority of T cells bear a hetereodimeric TCR 
consisting of a TCR-α and a TCR-β glycoprotein chain, each having a variable and a constant 
region (Figure 1), of which the variable region functions as antigen-binding site. Unlike the secreted 
BCR which recognizes antigens in their native form, the TCR is anchored in the T cell membrane 
and recognizes processed and short peptides only when they are complexed with an MHC 
molecule [8][9].  
 
Figure 1 – Schematic representation of the T cell receptor structure. TCR-α and TCR-β chains, each with a 
variable (V) and constant (C) region make up the heterodimer anchored to the cell membrane. Variable regions 
are responsible for TCR recognition of T cell epitopes. Co-receptors are left out for simplicity. Figure made by 
the author.  
 
The TCR-α and TCR-β chain loci contain several segments of genes termed variable (V), diversity 
(D) and joining (J) that make up the TCR variable region. Somatic recombination of VDJ segments 
gives rise to an immensely diverse TCR variable region repertoire, which in turn enables 
recognition of a multitude of antigenic peptides [10]. In theory, more than 1015 different T cell 
receptors are probable for each individual, providing potential detection of practically all foreign 
peptides [7]. However, not all combinations of TCR-α and TCR-β chains create functional antigen

receptors. In the thymus, intensive screening of the T cells takes place and most clones will never 
mature fully and reach periphery. Thymic stromal cells present self-MHC molecules to the 
developing T cells, and only those who are able to recognize self-MHC molecules will be positively 
selected for survival. However, T cells reacting too strongly with the self-MHC molecules undergo 
negative selection by apoptosis [11]. After recombination of the TCR, T cells are double positive for 
the CD4 and CD8 co-receptors but after selection in the thymus, T cells will mature into CD4 or 
CD8 single-positive cells, which specifies their future effector functions [12].  
 2.2.2
 Major histocompatibility complex
The major histocompatibility complex comprises a family of genes encoding cell surface molecules 
responsible for antigen presentation. MHC molecules facilitate interactions in-between leukocytes 
as well as leukocytes and additional cells in the body. In humans the MHC region is referred to as 
human leukocyte antigen (HLA) and located on chromosome six, with two regions being 
particularly important for diversity of antigen presentation: MHC class I and class II [13]. Of these, 
MHC class I is expressed on all nucleated cells in the body, whereas class II is only expressed on 
APCs. Several factors contribute to the extensive diversity of antigen presentation. Polygeny of the 
MHC means that several genes are encoded in the locus (HLA-A, -B, and –C for class I, HLA-DP, -
DQ and -DR for class II), polymorphism implicates that several alleles exist for each gene, and 
lastly, co-dominant allele expression ensures equivalent expression of variants inherited from both 
parents. These factors lead to each individual expressing 6 different MHC class I molecules and 6 
or 8 MHC class II molecules . Heterozygous individuals are thus able to mount a broader immune 
response than homozygous and the high degree of diversity in antigen presentation is a desirable 
feature at a species level as it provides better protection against pathogens [14].  
 
MHC class I consists of two polypeptide chains, the polymorphic α chain and a smaller, non-
covalently attached β2-microglobulin (β2m). Four domains make up the structure of the MHC class 
I, with α1 and α2 forming the peptide binding groove while α3 spans the membrane (Figure 2A). The 
binding groove is closed at the ends, which constrains the length of peptides able to bind to 9-11 
amino acids [15][16].  
MHC class II is similar in overall structure to MHC class I, and consists of two non-covalently linked 
polypeptide chains, α and β, both spanning the membrane. The peptide binding groove is formed 
by the α1 and β1 domains in an open shape that allows longer length peptides of 10-18 amino acids 
to bind (Figure 2B) [15][16].  
Figure 2 – Structures of MHC molecules. (A) MHC class I molecule consisting of a polymorphic α chain (violet) 
non-covalently attached to β2m (blue), which is non-polymorphic. (B) MHC class II molecule consisting of 
polymorphic α chain (violet) and β chain (blue). Figure made by the author.

As mentioned previously, MHC antigen presentation is essential for cell mediated immunity [16]. 
MHC class I is responsible for antigen presentation to CD8 T cells and MHC class II to CD4 T 
cells.  For the remainder of this thesis attention will be on MHC class I and CD8 T cells.  
 2.2.3
 MHC class I antigen processing and presentation
Proteins of intracellular origin will be presented by MHC class I in the form of peptides. This 
includes self-proteins, tumor antigens, and antigens from viruses replicating in the cytosol [16]. The 
process from protein to peptide-MHC (pMHC) presentation is outlined in Figure 3, and begins with 
intracellular proteins being degraded by cytosolic proteasomes into peptides. These peptides are 
then transported into the endoplasmic reticulum (ER) by transporters associated with antigen 
processing (TAP), where they will be trimmed by an aminopeptidase to a length suitable for binding 
to MHC class I molecules. In the ER, newly synthesized MHC class I molecules are retained and 
stabilized by chaperone proteins, until the arrival of a fitting peptide. Once the peptide has bound to 
the MHC molecule, the complex is stable and will depart the ER for presentation at the cell surface 
to CD8 T cells [17].  
 
Figure 3 - MHC class I processing and presentation pathway, where proteins of cytosolic origin are degraded by 
proteasomes to peptides and transported by TAP to the ER lumen where peptides are loaded on to empty MHC 
class I molecules. Peptide:MHC complexes depart the ER to perform peptide presentation at the cell surface to 
CD8 T cells. Adapted from [18]. 
 
Also noteworthy is cross presentation, which allows peptides from extracellular origin that would 
normally be restricted to MHC class II presentation to be presented on MHC class I molecules as

15 
cytosolic peptides do. This feature facilitates a CD8 T cell response against pathogens that do not 
normally infect APCs and thus do not end in the cytosol, giving rise to effective cellular immune 
responses. Cross presentation is highly dependent on the presence of interferons (IFNs) in the 
milieu [19].  
 2.2.4
 Antigen recognition 
The variable region of the TCR contains three hypervariable loops in each chain termed 
complementary determining regions (CDRs). The CDR1 and 2 contribute to the recognition and 
interaction of the TCR with the MHC molecule while CDR3 is responsible for interaction with the 
peptide displayed by the MHC. The TCR:pMHC interaction, known as signal 1, is strengthened and 
stabilized by contact of co-receptors on the T cell, recognizing invariant sites on the cognate MHC 
molecule on the APC. MHC class I is co-received by the CD8 co-receptor and the binding is 
required to mount an effective CD8 T cell response [5]. The T cell co-receptor CD3 mediates 
intracellular signaling upon antigen recognition via immunoreceptor tyrosine based-activation 
motifs (ITAMs) present on cytoplasmic tales of the CD3 receptor subunits. Phosphorylation of 
ITAMs initiates a signaling cascade inside the T cell, leading to its activation or priming [4].  
Signaling solely based on signal 1 is not sufficient for activation of T cells. The cell surface protein 
CD28 acts as a co-stimulatory receptor for naïve T cells, binding to co-stimulatory ligands (B7.1 
and B7.2) on APCs, providing signal 2 for T cell activation [9]. Lastly, T cell stimulatory cytokines 
such as interleukin (IL)-2 provide signal 3 for activation and are necessary for T cells to proliferate 
and differentiate into effector cells. Clonal expansion of antigen specific T cell clones follows, and 
mature T cells will reach periphery to exert their effector functions and give rise to a pool of specific 
memory T cells [7].  
 
The cytotoxicity of mature CD8 T cells upon antigen encounter can be indirect with release of IFN-
γ and tumor necrosis factor-α (TNF-α), mediating caspase activity and induction of apoptosis. 
Cytotoxicity can also be direct, with binding between Fas-ligand on the T cell and Fas death 
receptor on the target cell or via release of lytic granules from the T cell containing granulysin, 
perforin, and granzyme B, eventually killing the target cell. Upon recognition, an immunological 
synapse is formed between the cytotoxic T cell and the target cell, ensuring specific killing while 
avoiding healthy cells [4].  
 2.2.5
 Antigen specific T cell detection and characterization
Knowledge of the interaction between specific T cells and pMHC complex has potential 
implications for the development of both diagnostics and therapeutics. This interaction can be 
investigated experimentally by fluorochrome labelled pMHC complexes and flow cytometry. The 
affinity of the TCR:pMHC interaction is very low, which originally posed a hindrance for ex vivo 
detection of antigen specific T cells with MHC molecules [20]. However, Altman and colleagues 
discovered in 1996 that multimerization of recombinant biotinylated pMHCs with a streptavidin (SA) 
core, forming a tetramer complex, provided sufficient avidity to monitor specific T cells by 
==PAGE:16==
 
16 
 
fluorescent labeling and flow cytometry analysis [21]. This was a breakthrough in experimental 
immunology, and has considerably advanced the field of immune monitoring from detection of 
antigen responses in bulk populations of cells, as with e.g. Enzyme-Linked Immunospot (ELISpot), 
to detection of distinct T cell populations based on their cognate epitope [22].  
Since the development of the MHC tetramer, other multimeric forms of higher valency have been 
established [23]. For relevance of this project is the dextramer, consisting of a fluorescently 
labelled polysaccharide dextran backbone with several SAs attached that allows for 
multimerization of 10-20 biotinylated MHC molecules. The higher number of MHCs increases the 
avidity of the interaction with a specific T cell, which may improve detection of antigen specific T 
cells, especially those with low affinity to their cognate epitope [24].  
 
Advances in polychromatic flow cytometry and development of novel fluorescent markers have 
further enabled the characterization of antigen specific T cells. Several surface and intracellular 
markers can be detected simultaneously with antigen specificity on a single cell level, allowing 
distinction of T cell differentiation, activation state, and functionality. Brought together these 
advances facilitate thorough monitoring of T cell mediated immune responses, which is of great 
interest in many disease settings such as infections, autoimmune disorders, and, particularly 
important for this study; cancer [22]. 
 2.3
 Tumor immunology
Cancer is a collective term for malignancies characterized by genetic alterations which lead to 
transformed self-cells exhibiting unlimited proliferation and tissue invasion [25]. Cancer is 
associated with increasing age due to accumulation of mutations during the lifetime of an individual 
nad novel studies also point towards immune ageing, the principle of immune cells becoming 
impaired over time [26][27].  
 
Prior to the development of cancer, a complex interplay takes place between loss-of-function 
mutations of tumor suppressor genes and gain-of-function mutations of oncogenes [28]. Malignant 
transformation occurs as result of an evolutionary process, which results in most cancers sharing a 
number of common traits. Hanahan and Weinberg in 2000 and 2011 published their Hallmarks of 
Cancer, describing the essential characteristics gained by cells in order to progress to malignancy. 
These include 1) ability to resist cell death and sustain proliferation, 2) inducing invasion, 3) 
metastasis and angiogenesis, and 4) evasion of immune recognition as some of the hallmarks that 
contribute to altered self-cells becoming cancer cells [26][29]. Development of cancer is thus a 
result of several steps towards malignant transformation, all contributing to the abnormal profile of 
tumor cells.  
 
However, the characteristics that enable cancer cells to proliferate, spread, and persist can also 
make them noticeable to the immune system. Immune surveillance has the intrinsic capability to 
detect foreignness such as cancer, and it is broadly acknowledged that the immune system can 
take part in elimination of cancer cells [30][31]. Thus, tumor reactive immune cells are of great 
==PAGE:17==
 
 
17 
interest for their potential applications as therapeutics. Tumor infiltrating lymphocytes (TILs) have 
proven to be a positive prognostic factor in most cancers, and especially the intratumoral presence 
of cytotoxic T cells improves overall survival [32].   
 2.3.1
 Cancer immune evasion 
Immune recognition of cancer can lead to tumor regression but this process simultaneously puts 
an evolutionary pressure on tumor cells, resulting in what is known as immunoediting of cancer 
[31]. Overall, three phases are considered to be part of the immunoediting process: elimination, 
equilibrium and escape [33] (see Figure 4). Throughout the elimination phase, cells of the immune 
system attempt to eradicate tumor cells upon detection and have the ability to keep the cancerous 
growth under control. But the selective influence by immune cells favors the malignant cell mutant 
variants that persist. Thus, an equilibrium phase can be reached where less immunogenic cells 
have evaded elimination, and the constant pressure from immune cells continuously shapes the 
tumor. Eventually, immune sculpting results in less immunogenic and thus less recognizable tumor 
cells with the ability to evade immune destruction completely [30]. This results in the tumor being 
able to escape immune recognition, where immune evasion is inevitable and the tumor can grow 
intensely [34].  
 
 
 
 
 
 
Evidently the interconnection between immune cells and cancer cells is intricate and many-sided 
[30]. Tumor cells can evolve to create an immune suppressive microenvironment that enables 
tumor escape of immune recognition [35]. Factors that inhibit immune detection and support 
immune suppression in the tumor vicinity include recruiting suppressive immune cells such as T 
regulatory cells and tumor associated macrophages [36][37]. Production of suppressive cytokines

and deficiencies in intracellular antigen processing also contribute to cancer evasion of immune 
responses, as malignant cells will be invisible to CD8 T cells when MHC expression is obstructed

 2.3.3
 Immune therapy 
With immune therapy, the aim is to harness the specific targeting capacity of the immune system 
therapeutically. Within the past decade immune therapy has emerged as a powerful treatment 
option in several cancer types, especially melanoma [54][55]. Immune specific targeting of cancer 
cells has the potential to circumvent the common adverse effects of conventional therapies such as 
chemotherapy and radiation, but implies the risk of other adverse events of autoimmune character. 
The use of immune therapy also implies generating a lasting memory response, which can reject 
future relapse.  
 
Different immune therapeutic strategies exist, characterized as active when the idea is to induce or 
boost own immune responses in the treated subject, whereas passive immune therapy involves 
transfer of extrinsic cells or effector molecules. Immune therapy can further be antigen-specific, 
giving rise to tumor antigen-targeted therapies or induction of a more general immune response 
[56]. Increased insight into new TAAs may thus aid in the formulation of novel effective immune 
therapeutic strategies against cancer.   
 
Attractive strategies within cancer immune therapy entail, among others: 1) adoptive T cell transfer 
of autologous or allogeneic tumor specific T cells after in vitro expansion or TCR engineering [42], 
2) use of checkpoint inhibitors, such as anti cytotoxic T lymphocyte associated protein 4 (CTLA-4) 
and anti programmed death 1 (PD-1) monoclonal antibodies, removing the brake imposed on T 
cells by APCs or tumor cells [57], 3) various vaccines such a DCs loaded with tumor associated 
antigens or peptide vaccines with adjuvants to specifically induce tumor targeted immune 
responses [58]. It is further speculated and investigated how immune therapies in combination with 
conventional cancer therapies might offer augmented tumor targeting. An example is radiation in 
combination with checkpoint inhibitors, as radiation has the ability to induce the release of tumor 
associated antigens and thus induce specific adaptive immunity. Therefore, combination with 
checkpoint inhibitors should give rise to a group of tumor specific and activated T cells [59].  
 2.3.4
 Breast cancer
Breast cancer is a group of diseases characterized by tumor growth in various regions of breast 
tissue. Breast cancer remains the leading cause of cancer incidence and second cause of cancer 
mortality among women worldwide, as one in eight will be diagnosed during their lives [60]. Despite 
==PAGE:20==
 
20 
 
research and advances in therapeutics and diagnostics for breast cancer established therapies are 
rendered insufficient, and novel approaches are necessary [61]. Breast cancer frequently 
metastasizes to bone, brain, liver, and lung, therefore, as with most other cancer types, early 
detection remains essential for effective breast cancer therapy [62]. 
 
Inheritance of high-risk susceptibility genes such as mutated BRCA1 and BRCA2 (breast cancer 
genes 1 and 2) and mutated tumor suppressor p53 increases the lifetime risk of breast cancer by 
several fold [63]. Breast cancer comprises biologically variable diseases originating mostly from 
transformed cells of milk ducts (ductal carcinoma) or milk-producing glands (lobular carcinoma 
[64]). Breast cancer cases are regularly diagnosed by distinct subtypes based on differential 
expression of the following molecular markers: human epidermal growth factor receptor 2 (HER2), 
estrogen receptor (ER), progesterone receptor (PR) and proliferative marker Ki67 [65][66]. The 
subtypes differ in incidence, prognosis, and treatment options, as targeting therapies such as anti-
HER2 antibodies are available [67][68].  
 
Despite traditionally considered an immunologically silent malignancy, recent and ongoing studies 
are encouraging further research of the immunogenicity of breast cancer, and its applicability in 
immune therapeutic settings [69]–[71]. Vaccines and checkpoint inhibitors are currently 
investigated for their ability to induce immune-mediated elimination of breast cancers [70][72][73].   
The T cell epitope landscape in breast cancer is still minimally described, thus it is essential to 
identify suitable T cell targets in order to develop competent cell-mediated immune therapies.  
 
In this project, four potential breast cancer TAAs are investigated for presence of CD8 T cell 
epitopes: aromatase (ARO), prolactin (PRL), NEK3, and PIAS3, outlined in the following. These 
proteins have been selected to find a repertoire of shared TAAs preferentially overexpressed in 
breast cancer.  Aromatase
  
Aromatase is a key enzyme in estrogen biosynthesis, as it converts androgens into estrogens. 
Normal tissue expression is mostly in breast tissue, adipose tissue, ovary, and placenta [74]. 
Estrogen has mitogenic effects and is highly involved in the pathogenesis of several cancer types, 
namely endometrial, ovarian, and breast cancer [75]. In breast cancer aromatase expression from 
malignant epithelial cells, immature adipocytes, and vascular endothelial cells acts as an autocrine 
and paracrine growth stimulant. The excess aromatase expression in malignant breast tissue is a 
result of augmented activity of aromatase promoters, some of which are not normally utilized in 
breast tissue (see Figure 5) [75][76]. The role of aromatase is breast cancer is further evidenced by 
the fact that aromatase inhibitors are approved and marketed for breast cancer therapy [77].  
 
 
==PAGE:21==

 Prolactin, NEK3 and PIAS3 
 
Prolactin is a hormone responsible for inducing milk production in mammalian breast tissue. Under 
normal conditions, prolactin is normally expressed in breast tissue, placenta, endometrium and 
thyroid gland, but augmented expression is seen in malignant breast tissue, where it contributes to 
increased proliferation, angiogenesis, survival, and motility of cancer cells [78]. The involvement of 
prolactin in breast cancer development has been acknowledged for decades [79].  
Binding of prolactin to the prolactin cell surface receptor initiates an intracellular signaling pathway, 
involving several signaling proteins (see Figure 6). Never In Mitosis A-Related Kinase 3 (NEK3) 
and protein inhibitor of activated STAT3 (PIAS3) are two of these proteins, both shown to be 
upregulated in breast cancer. NEK3 is a serine-threonine kinase, normally expressed in a wide 
variety of tissues with kidneys and muscles (heart, skeletal and smooth) having the highest 
expression. NEK3 is particularly involved in motility and survival of breast cancer cells [80]. PIAS3 
expression is normally seen in various brain tissue, fallopian tubes, placenta and bone marrow and 
is augmented in breast cancer and a collection of other cancers, supporting its role as an possible 
anti-cancer target [81]. 
 
No T cell epitopes have so far been described within aromatase, prolactin, NEK3 or PIAS3, thus 
identification of any would mean establishing these as novel TAAs [82]. If identified as TAAs, 
aromatase and prolactin would very likely be classified as differentiation antigens due to restricted 
tissue expression of these. NEK3 and PIAS3 have a much broader tissue expression profile, and 
these would very likely be classified as overexpression antigens.

 2.3.5
 Discovery of TAAs in breast cancer
Researchers from the Ludwig Institute for Cancer Research, Belgium, have proposed a set of 
certain criteria that must be assessed for identified TAAs in order to be viewed as containing true T 
cell epitopes [82]. The requirements entail: 1) Isolation of human T cell clones or lines that 
recognize the peptide, 2) Verification of the recognized peptide, 3) Identification of the HLA that is 
presenting the peptide, and 4) Evidence proving the peptide is processed from whole protein 
sequence by tumor cells and presented to T cells by given HLA.  
 
Throughout this study, it is investigated if any of the peptides from the four proposed TAAs 
aromatase, prolactin, NEK3 or PIAS3 meet these demands, so they can be characterized a novel 
breast cancer associated TAAs.  
A reverse immunology approach is applied, a process with several steps [41][70]. First, selection of 
proteins based on knowledge of tumor expression, followed by prediction of HLA binding peptides 
via in silico screening methods and experimental validation of actual HLA binders. Then follows 
identification of T cells recognizing these peptides in patient samples. Lastly, isolation of these T 
cells and analysis of reactivity towards cells with relevant HLA expression of investigated peptides 
evaluates validity of the TAAs.   
 
Prior to the work conducted in this thesis, members of the SRH research group (Sofie Ramskov 
Andersen and Rikke Sick Andersen) have selected the four proteins aromatase, prolactin, NEK3 
and PIAS3 based on breast cancer associated expression. Via in silico prediction and in vitro 
validation a total of 147 HLA A*0201 and B*0702 restricted peptides have been determined for 
further T cell recognition screenings.

 Experimental strategy2.4.1
 pMHC multimers to detect antigen specific T cells 
MHC molecules are stable only when associated with a peptide and will degrade if the binding 
groove is left empty, a feature utilized to create MHC monomers carrying specific peptides of 
choice via UV-mediated peptide exchange [84]. This technology comprises MHC class I molecules 
complexed with conditional, UV-liable ligands (p*) and the presence of an excess of rescue 
peptide, i.e. a peptide of choice predicted to bind stably to the given HLA type. Exposure to 366 nm 
UV light causes the conditional peptide ligand to fall apart, leaving the MHC binding groove empty 
and requiring stabilization by the added rescue peptide. Thus, UV-mediated peptide exchange 
enables high throughput generation of several pMHC specificities in parallel, which will be done for 
the 147 breast cancer related peptides in this study. MHC monomers are routinely produced in the 
laboratory as described by Hadrup et al. [84] by members of the SRH group.  
 
To be able to differentiate several T cell specificities in given sample, a combinatorial encoding 
strategy of pMHC tetramers can be applied, as described previously by Andersen et al. [85]. With 
this strategy each pMHC specificity is encoded by two fluorescent labels. Upon T cell staining both 
pMHC tetramers bind to the same T cell giving a unique two-color combination that can be 
analyzed by flow cytometry. This approach minimizes background and detection of false positives. 
Furthermore, combinatorial encoding of pMHC multimers allows simultaneous detection of a 
considerably increased number of specificities simultaneously compared to using single color 
staining for each pMHC.  Dependent on the available fluorescently labelled SA 20-30 specificities 
can be screened for at once. However, as described previously an individual has an extensively 
diverse TCR repertoire and an immense amount of peptides potentially presented to them in the 
context of MHC. Combinatorial encoding technology of pMHC multimers has considerably 
enhanced the amount of TCR:pMHC interactions that can be investigated at once. But the total 
amount of immune interactions detectable by this method is diminished by the need to divide the 
sample into several portions, depending on the pMHC library size. Patient sample material is often 
sparse and combined with the particularly low frequency of tumor recognizing T cells, the splitting 
of a sample into portions can impede the detection of such rare populations.  
 
A novel technology has recently been developed and validated allowing screening for T cell 
recognition of >1000 pMHC specificities simultaneously [86]. The innovative technology uses 
pMHC and unique DNA barcodes multimerized together on a dextran backbone. Each pMHC 
specificity is thus coupled to a given DNA barcode. This allows a so-called “one pot” approach that 
does not require dividing of sample into smaller portions. Cell sorting, PCR amplification, and high 
throughput sequencing of pMHC multimer binding T cells reveals the relative frequency of each 
DNA barcode, which is related to the size of the antigen-responsive T cell population. The DNA 
barcode labels used for each pMHC specificity are products of two partly complementary 
oligonucleotide-sequences Ax and By after annealing and elongation (see Figure 7). Annealing 
==PAGE:24==
 
24 
 
and subsequent elongation of the oligo A and B gives rise to a unique AxBy DNA barcode of 138 
bp in length.  
 
 
This study seeks to apply the novel technology of high-throughput screening for antigen responsive 
CD8 T cells to simultaneously screening for T cells recognizing any of the 147 HLA A*0201 or 
B*0702 restricted peptides in breast cancer and healthy donor peripheral blood mononuclear cell 
(PBMC) samples. This approach is significantly advantageous as the available patient material is 
limited. In search for populations of antigen responsive CD8 T cells both direct ex vivo pMHC 
multimer screening will be conducted along with a multiparallel pMHC enrichment step.  
 2.4.2
 Enrichment of antigen specific T cells 
T cells of a given antigen specificity are typically of rather low frequencies which makes them 
difficult to distinguish from non-specific cells. Cancer specific T cells can occur as particularly rare 
populations that are often impossible to detect in samples directly ex vivo [23]. By utilization of 
pMHC tetramers, epitope specific T cell populations can be isolated via magnetic activated cell 
sorting (MACS) as a means of specific in vitro enrichment. This facilitates an increase in frequency 
of specific populations and enhances detection of cancer specific T cells.  
 
Aiming to achieve T cell cultures of even higher specificity fluorescent activated cell sorting (FACS) 
was utilized. After MACS enrichment the cell cultures still contain a considerable amount of 
unspecific cells. With fluorescently labelled combinatorially encoded pMHC tetramers antigen 
specific CD8 T cells can be specifically sorted based on a set of fluorescence sorting parameters.  
 
It was attempted to obtain breast cancer specific CD8 T cell cultures will be attempted achieved via 
FACS and utilize these in functional assays to investigate cytotoxicity upon antigen encounter and 
antigen processing and presentation. These are crucial steps to determine the status of the 
investigated peptides as true CD8 T cell epitopes.

 3.1.1
 Isolation of human peripheral blood mononuclear cells
Isolation of PBMCs from buffy coat samples was achieved by the use of density gradient 
centrifugal separation with lymphoprep (Axis-Shield PoC) and leucosep tubes (Greiner bio-one). 
Lymphoprep is an iodine-containing media that facilitates a density gradient able to separate 
distinct blood components [87].   
 Protocol
15 ml lymphoprep was transferred to a leucosep tube and centrifuged at 1000 g for 30 seconds, to 
allocate lymphoprep across the porous barrier. The buffy coat was diluted 1:1 with RPMI 1640 
(Gibco) and transferred to the leucosep tube above the barrier. Centrifugation for 10 minutes at 
1000 g with slow acceleration and no break led to separation of PBMCs from erythrocytes and 
granulocytes. Thus, the PBMC layer above the erythrocytes and below the plasma could carefully 
be isolated and washed twice (for the remainder of materials and methods, all washes entail 
centrifugation at 1500 rpm for 5 minutes and subsequent removal of supernatant) in RPMI 1640. 
PBMCs were further resuspended in fetal calf serum (FCS; Gibco) and 10% dimethyl sulfoxide 
(DMSO; Sigma-Aldrich) and cryopreserved at -150°C.

 HLA tissue typing
Breast cancer patient samples from the Mambio protocol were provided with unknown HLA types.  
Therefore, a flow cytometry based approach was applied to distinguish samples positive for HLA

 Protocol 
Cells were thawed in RPMI + 10% FCS (R10), washed once in R10 and once in FACS buffer (PBS

 3.4.3
 UV-mediated peptideexchange Protocol
Biotinylated p*MHC monomers of designated HLA type and DMSO-dissolved peptides were 
thawed on ice, and mixed to a final concentration of 100 µg/ml p*MHC monomer to 200 µM peptide 
in a 384 well plate, one well for each peptide specificity. Plates were then exposed to 366 nm UV

tetramers (section 3.5) or DNA barcode labelled SA-conjugated dextran backbone, generating 
dextramers (section 3.6).  
 
 3.5
 Combinatorial encoding of pMHC multimersProtocol 
UV exchanged monomers (see section 3.4.3) were centrifuged at 3300 g for 5 minutes and each 
specific supernatant was split into two new wells for multimerization of pMHC complexes with each 
their SA-conjugated fluorochrome. SA-conjugated fluorochromes were centrifuged at 10000 g for 5 
minutes and added to pMHC containing wells in a final concentration of 0.018 mg/ml followed by 
30 minutes of incubation at 4°C. Lastly, 28 µM D-biotin (Avidity Bio200) was added to each well, 
incubated for 20 minutes at 4°C where after tetramers of the same peptide specificity were mixed 
1:1, and stored at -20°C in 0.5% BSA and 5% glycerol.

 3.6.4
 Sequencing and analysis 
High throughput sequencing of amplified and purified PCR product was carried out by Sequetech 
(California, US) on a 316 Ion Torrent chip. Adapters for Ion Torrent sequencing were introduced 
with forward and reverse primers during PCR. The resulting sequencing data in the form of a 
FASTA file was analyzed by the web based tool ‘Barracoda’, version 1.0 (cbs.dtu.dk). pMHC 
responsive T cells were identified based on the number of reads for an individual pMHC-
associated barcode relative to the complete pMHC multimer library (baseline sample). Thus, 
determining the presence of a specific population entails it being significantly augmented 
(expressed by a p-value<0.001) compared to reads for the same specificity in the baseline sample. 
Furthermore, the random 6 nucleotide UMI regions present on each barcode allows for a clonality 
reduction where it is assessed what number of unique DNA sequences originate from each pMHC-
associated barcode. This revealed the number of specific pMHC multimers that interacted with T 
cells in the given cell sample and reduced any bias introduced by PCR amplification [86].

 T cell staining with pMHC multimers
In this study T cell staining with pMHC multimers followed a similar protocol, regardless of the 
multimer type. Differences between staining with combinatorially encoded pMHC tetramers and 
barcoded dextramers are outlined in the protocol, but were limited to use of wash buffer, amount of 
multimer added and number of washing steps. Protein kinase inhibitor dasatinib was utilized to 
increase staining with both pMHC tetramers and dextramers, by preventing down-regulation of 
TCRs from the T cell surface [24][88].  
 Protocol 
Cells for pMHC multimer staining were thawed and washed twice in R10. Cells were counted and 
1-2*106 cells were transferred to wells of a 96-well plate, washed additionally once in FACS buffer 
(tetramers) or barcode-cytometry buffer (barcoded dextramers) and resuspended to a volume of 20 
μl. Hereafter 50 nM Dasatinib was added to each well and incubated for 30 minutes at 37°C. 
pMHC multimer panels were centrifuged at 3300 g for 5 minutes and the required amount of each 
multimer was collected: 1 μl of each pMHC specificity for tetramers and 3 μl of each specificity for 
barcoded dextramers.  
If the total volume of the collected barcoded dextramer panel exceeded 100 μl the multimers were 
concentrated to approximately 80 μl per staining on a size exclusion spin column (Vivaspin 20, 
cutoff: 100 kDa, Satorius), pre-saturated with 2% BSA and washed twice with PBS before addition 
of panel. 
Collected and concentrated pMHC multimer panels were transferred to eppendorf tubes and spun 
again for 5 minutes at 3300 g and added to cells, followed by incubation at 37°C for 15 minutes. 
Cells were then stained with 20 μl antibody mixture containing dump-channel FITC-conjugated 
antibodies: anti-CD4 (CD4 T cell marker; BD), anti-CD14 (macrophage marker; BD), anti-CD16 
(NK-cell, macrophage and neutrophil marker; BD) anti-CD19 (B cell marker; BD), and anti-CD40 
(antigen presenting cell marker; Serotec), with anti-CD8 PerCP antibody (Invitrogen) and 0.1 μl 
near-IR-viability dye (NIRVID; Invitrogen). After incubation at 4°C for 30 minutes, cells were 
washed twice with FACS buffer (tetramers) or three times with barcode-cytometry buffer (barcoded 
dextramers). Cells were fixated 50 µl 1% PFA, and stored for up to seven days before acquisition 
on LSR Fortessa or FACS Aria Fusion II.  
 3.8
 MACS enrichment
For this study, PE-labelled pMHC tetramers were created via UV-exchange (section 3.4.3) with the 
147 confirmed HLA A*0201 and HLA B*0702-binding peptides from aromatase, prolactin, NEK3 
and PIAS3 proteins. Magnetically labelled anti-PE microbeads and passage through a magnetic 
field column enables retention of pMHC tetramer recognizing cells and further expansion of these 
in culture (see Figure 10).  
==PAGE:34==
 
34 
 
 Protocol
 
Cells for enrichment were thawed in R10 with 40 U DNase/ml (Stem Cell Technologies), 100 µl 
MgCl2 and 100 µl MnCl2 (R10 + DNase), washed twice and resuspended in 100 µl R10. Tetramer 
panels were spun at 3300 g for 5 minutes and 1 µl of each specific PE-tetramer was collected and 
mixed before addition to the cells. After 1 hour of incubation at 4°C, cells were washed twice in 
R10 and resuspended to a volume of 80 µl. 20 µl of anti-PE magnetic microbeads (Miltenyi Biotec) 
were added to each sample followed by incubation for 15 minutes at 4°C. Cells were washed twice 
in R10 and resuspended in 500 µl R10 + DNase before applying on top of a pre-washed MS 
column (Miltenyi Biotec) placed in a magnetic field of a suitable MACS separator over a collection 
tube. The unbound negative cell fractions were washed away three times by adding 500 µl R10 on 
top of each column. Hereafter, columns were removed from the magnetic field and magnetically 
labelled specific cells were flushed out by use of a plunger (Miltenyi Biotec) into a collection tube 
with X-vivo 15 media (Lonza) and 5% human serum (HS; Sigma-Aldrich). The negative cell 
fractions were irradiated at 15 Gray (Gy) for 15 minutes to achieve autologous feeder cells for each 
sample. Subsequently, cells were washed and counted and the specific cells were mixed with the 
feeder cells in a 1:10 ratio (5000 specific cells to 50000 autologous feeder cells) in 96 well plates. 
Lastly, 11000 pre-washed anti CD3/CD28 Human T Activator Dynabeads (Invitrogen Dynal) were 
added to each well along with culture medium consisting of X-vivo with 5% HS, 100 U IL-2/ml and 
23.8 U IL-15/ml. Cells were cultured for two-three weeks and media changed at least twice weekly, 
until specific T cells responses were analyzed by barcoded MHC multimers.

 3.10
 Vital-FR cytotoxicity test 
The vital FR-cytotoxicity test is a flow cytometry based cytotoxicity assay. Direct assessment of 
cytotoxicity of CD8 T cells can be achieved by observing survival of fluorescently labelled target 
cells relative to a differentially labelled internal control population without specific antigen [89]. For 
this project, T2 cells pulsed with peptide were utilized as target cells to calculate specific lysis by 
pMHC specific CD8 T cells. 
 Protocol 
T2 cells of correct HLA type were thawed and washed twice in RPMI 1640. 500 µl containing 1*106 
cells was added to two separate tubes, one for each fraction of target cells. Cells in one tube were 
stained with 10 µM CFSE (Carboxyfluorescein succinimidyl ester; Invitrogen) while the other was 
stained with 5 µM FR (Far red; Invitrogen) at 37°C for 5 minutes. To terminate staining, 20% FCS 
was added to each tube and they were washed twice in R10, followed by resuspension in 500 µl 
R10.  
Cells were pulsed with 10µM specific peptide (CFSE, true target cells) of choice or control peptide 
(FR, control target cells) for 1 hour at 37°C. Cells were washed twice and resuspended in R10 + 10 
U IL-2/ml. True target cells and control target cells were mixed 1:1.  
 
Effector cells were thawed and washed twice in R10, followed by resuspension in R10 + 10 U IL-
2/ml. Target and effector cells were mixed in wells of a 96-well plate in chosen effector:target cell 
ratios, and incubated for 24 hours. Post incubation, cells were acquired on LSR Fortessa and 
specific lysis was calculated as (formel).

 Discussionand perspectives5.1
 Barcode labelled pMHC multimers as a novel screening technology
Throughout this project a novel high-throughput pMHC multimer technology was utilized to screen 
samples for antigen specific T cell responses. The technology implements the use of a unique 
DNA barcode coupled to each pMHC specificity in a given library, which allows screening for more 
than 1000 T cell specificities simultaneously [86]. This drastic increase in complexity compared to 
conventional flow cytometry based pMHC multimer screenings allows for increased detection of 
antigen specific T cells even in small biological samples. Furthermore, high order pMHC 
multimerization with the dextran backbone combined with a lower dependence of fluorescence 
separation due to the co-attached DNA barcode may assist in the detection of more low affinity T 
cell responses than conventional methods. This is in part due to the fact that less stringent flow 
cytometry fluorescence gating criteria are applied so that even pMHC multimer binding cells that 
are barely separated from the pMHC multimer negative population are sorted and thie pMHC 
associated barcodes are sequenced, allowing recovery of DNA barcodes related to low affinity 
specificities. In disease settings such as cancer and autoimmunity where T cell epitopes are mostly 
of self-origin the detection of low affinity T cell responses is particularly important. Barcode labelled 
pMHC screening therefore has applicability in these diseases for assessment of T cell mediated 
immune reactivity. For these reasons, barcode labelled pMHC multimer screening was an ideal 
technology to screen for T cell responses against a library of shared TAA peptides in breast cancer 
patient samples.   
 
The readout of the barcode labelled pMHC screening is a P-value for each pMHC specificity in 
each sample, calculated based on reads of the associated barcode. This offers no distinction of 
whether the antigen specific T cell population found is representing a small population with high 
affinity for its cognate pMHC or a larger population with lower affinity for its cognate pMHC. 
Additionally it should be noted that high-throughput screenings tend to have some degree of false 
positive detection [90][91]. This could theoretically also apply to barcode labelled pMHC multimer 
screenings of high-throughput nature. Knowledge of false positives is crucial and should be taken 
into consideration as a high false positive rate would imply that some of the found responses are in 
fact not true responses. However, data from initial studies regarding this assay show very precise 
correlation between CD8 T responses found by barcode labelled pMHC and by flow cytometry 
based pMHC tetramer screening [86]. If a high degree of false positives were generally 
encountered with barcode labelled pMHC screening it would have been apparent by these 
comparisons.  
 5.1.1
 CD8 T cell responses against breast cancer peptides 
In this study a cohort of 27 breast cancer patient samples and 10 healthy donor samples were 
screened for T cell recognition of 147 A*0201 and B*0702 restricted peptides from four proposed 
==PAGE:48==
 
48 
 
breast cancer TAAs. The breast cancer patient samples investigated contained relatively few 
PBMCs. Low cell numbers would have been problematic if samples were to be screened with 
conventional flow cytometry based pMHC tetramer screening as it would require that samples were 
divided into several portions in order to screen for 147 pMHC specificities. However, due to the 
application of DNA barcode labelled pMHC screening all 147 pMHC specificities could be screened 
for simultaneously in one sample. Further advantageous to this screening method is the possibility 
of detecting low affinity T cell responses as the investigated library consists of peptides of self-
origin.  
 
In a direct ex vivo screening a total of 91 responses were detected against breast cancer 
associated peptides in 27 breast cancer patient samples and 24 responses detected in 10 healthy 
donors. The responses found in breast cancer patient samples were considered spontaneous as 
patients had received no prior treatment before sample was taken. It was anticipated that there 
would be considerably more breast cancer peptide directed T cell responses in patient samples 
compared to healthy donors. A χ2-test revealed that responses restricted to B*0702 were 
significantly more present in breast cancer patient samples than healthy donors. For A*0201 
restricted responses there was not a significantly higher amount of responses found in breast 
cancer patient samples than healthy donors. The healthy donor responses against proposed 
breast cancer antigens were mainly found in two healthy donors (20 responses out of 24) and were 
A*0201 restricted. As the investigated peptides are of shared or self-origin it is not unlikely to find T 
cells recognizing these in healthy donors, but it would add more value to the antigens as potential 
TAAs if responses were preferentially found in patient samples compared to healthy donors. 
Healthy donor samples were obtained from the blood bank and we have no knowledge of these 
donors except the HLA types at four digits. Hence it cannot be excluded that some of these donors 
may have or may have had breast cancer at some point, giving rise to a repertoire of breast cancer 
antigen responsive T cells.  
 
The HLA typing of breast cancer patient samples was done by flow cytometry and the results for 
B7 typing were considered uncertain. Stimulation with IFN-γ could have been applied to see 
whether it would induce upregulation of MHC class I expression in investigated samples and thus 
enable easier distinction of HLA B7 expression. It was problematic to determine whether any 
responses against B*0702 restricted peptides were not matching the HLA type of the donor and all 
significant B*0702 restricted responses were thus considered as HLA matches. A further 
complication is the fact that typing was done on HLA supertype level, implicating that we only 
obtained knowledge of a sample as e.g. HLA A2 positive. It is therefore unknown whether the HLA 
A2 positive samples are in fact HLA A*0201 positive or instead positive for another member of the 
A2 supertype family such as HLA A*0205 or A*0206 [92]. It has been decided to send the samples 
for typing by gene sequencing of the HLA locus to determine the true HLA types at four digits. It 
will indeed be interesting to see these results and how the four digit HLA types correlate with 
responses found in breast cancer patient samples.

After in vitro MACS enrichment of pMHC reactive CD8 T cells in breast cancer samples, the 
barcode labelled pMHC screening strategy was again applied to detect responses against 147 
breast cancer associated pMHC specificities. 11 breast cancer patient samples were attempted 
enriched via MACS, and resulted in 21 breast cancer peptide responses detected. Only 5 out of 11 
enriched samples contained responses but for the samples with no responses detected after 
MACS enrichment there were in most cases no detected responses directly ex vivo either. 6 out of 
21 detected responses were found in the same donor both after enrichment and ex vivo. The 
finding of these responses in the same samples twice acts to confirm the responses. The 
responses present after enrichment are also interesting, as these represent populations of specific 
CD8 T cells that were below detection limit ex vivo but have been enriched above threshold via 
MACS. Conversely, a number of responses were found in samples ex vivo that did not show up as 
significant after enrichment. Some specific cells may have died in culture and for low frequent 
responses there is a substantial stochastic variance. Chances of re-finding low frequent responses 
after enrichment are thus much lower than for high frequent responses. Furthermore, it cannot be 
excluded that in some cases the finding of said responses ex vivo were in fact background or 
unspecific binding that appeared significant. A second ex vivo screening of all breast cancer 
samples will be performed to distinguish responses found from background. Several of the 
enriched responses were of very convincing significance and in ongoing studies it will be attempted 
to confirm the CD8 T cell responses by flow cytometry screening with fluorescently labelled pMHC 
multimers. If successful, these cultures may be sorted by FACS to obtain highly specific T cell 
populations that can be utilized in functional assays. Functionality assessment will provide 
necessary steps in the evaluation of the peptides as true CD8 T cell epitopes and the proteins 
aromatase, prolactin, NEK3, and PIAS3 as tumor associated antigens.

and MHC ELISA. The inclusion of intermediate MHC binders was prioritized due to the shared 
characteristics of the antigens and the previously mentioned tolerance of T cells reacting strongly 
to self-peptides. The CD8 T cell responses discovered in this study were directed against a wide 
variety of the 147 peptides. There appeared to be somewhat a tendency of lowest P-values 
corresponding to high affinity peptides, but not consistently. Interestingly, some intermediate 
affinity peptides also had low P-values and the second most significant response found overall was 
detected against an intermediate peptide. These findings underline the importance of having an 
appropriate selection strategy of peptides for reverse immunology screening approaches 
depending on the origin of the investigated TAAs. As this project inspects shared antigens the 
inclusion of poorer MHC binding peptides was crucial and worthwhile as some CD8 T cell 
responses would not have been noticed without the inclusion of these.

50 
 
Throughout the conducted study aromatase, prolactin, NEK3, and PIAS3 are evaluated as having 
potential as TAAs, based on the fact that T cell reactivity towards their derived peptides was 
observed in the peripheral blood of breast cancer patients. The presence of responses in healthy 
donor samples was, however, also frequent. This is disadvantageous to the applicability of these 
TAAs in a therapeutic setting, as it points towards the expression of said antigens in both healthy 
and diseased individuals, increasing risk of adverse effects. A larger cohort of breast cancer and 
healthy donor samples is to be screened. Since the majority of responses in healthy donors was 
found in only two out of ten donors it can be speculated if an increased healthy donor cohort would 
contain limited significant responses to breast cancer antigens.  
 
The knowledge of shared tumor associated antigens is advantageous as these can be utilized 
therapeutically to benefit broad groups of cancer patients. Nevertheless the shared nature of these 
antigens implies a substantial risk for autoimmune adverse effects if they were to be targeted in a 
therapeutic setting. Destruction of healthy tissue expressing shared antigens is a major risk and 
depending on the tissue or organ affected, such adverse effects can have devastating or fatal 
consequences. However, for cancer therapies a certain degree of adverse effects can be tolerated 
as the prognosis for cancer patients, particularly after metastasis, is often poor and mortality is 
high. Therefore it is of relevance which tissues are impacted by adverse effects. As aromatase and 
prolactin would very likely be classified as differentiation antigens due to restricted tissue 
expression, any adverse effects would also be limited to these tissues i.e. breast, placenta, and 
endometrium. But for potential overexpression antigens NEK3 and PIAS3, tissue distribution is 
much broader. NEK3 having high expression in kidneys and heart muscle and PIAS3 being 
expressed in various parts of the brain implies risks of lethal adverse effects. The potential adverse 
targets for aromatase and prolactin are of less essential character and might be acceptable if these 
antigens were targeted therapeutically. For an immune therapy approach a suggestion could be 
that T cell recognized peptides from aromatase and prolactin could be utilized in the generation of 
a breast cancer vaccine. A combination therapy with checkpoint inhibitors might induce a 
synergistic anti-tumor effect, with the induction of unhindered cancer recognizing CD8 T cells.   
 
 5.3
 Future and ongoing studies
To truly validate any peptides as true CD8 T cell epitopes proof must be provided for processing 
and presentation of whole protein to peptide displayed in the context of MHC. Furthermore, 
evidenced is required that specific CD8 T cells exhibit cytotoxic capacity upon recognition of its 
target pMHC on the surface of cells. During this study T cell cultures specific for any investigated 
peptides were not successfully isolated. This is to be pursued again with the enriched mambio 
breast cancer samples as some of these contained significant T cell responses against breast 
cancer peptides. The vital-FR cytotoxicity test will be utilized with the virus specific T cells isolated 
from healthy donor 18 in section 4.7 to investigate cytotoxicity of breast cancer patient T cells. 
Instead of T2 cells pulsed with peptide to give surface expression of relevant pMHC, K562 cells will 
be used. K562 cells are a leukemic cell line with a defective MHC expression, that can be stably

transduced with HLA A*0201 or B*0702 and transfected with whole protein mRNA from aromatase, 
prolactin, NEK3, or PIAS3 to investigate processing, presentation and recognition in the final 
epitope validation steps.    
As antigen-responsive CD8 T cells were detected within HLA A*0201 and B*0702 restrictions used 
in this study it is reasonable to consider that there will also occur reactive T cells towards other 
HLA restrictions. The study could therefore be expanded to include additional HLA types and the 
predicted binding peptides within the four antigens. 
 
Additionally, an agreement has been made to receive more breast cancer patient PBMC samples 
from collaborators. The patient cohort will thus be increases by screening of these samples with 
the 147 breast cancer peptide barcode labelled pMHC multimer library for recognition directly ex 
vivo and after enrichment, to increase the patient cohort. In parallel, more healthy donor samples 
will also be screened. For some of the mambio samples there have been taken bone marrow 
biopsies. As breast cancer cells are known to frequently metastasize to the bone marrow it will be 
of great value to screen these for pMHC responsive T cells and compare with the peripheral blood 
screenings.

 6.
 Conclusion
A novel high-throughput DNA barcode labelled pMHC strategy was utilized to successfully screen 
for CD8 T cell responses directed against a library of 147 breast cancer associated peptides 
restricted to HLA A*0201 and HLA B*0702. The method was advantageous for the conducted 
study as it permitted simultaneous detection of specificities in samples of limited quantity. 
Furthermore the DNA barcode labelled pMHC strategy had the potential to detect lower affinity T 
cell epitopes than conventional flow cytometry based screening methods, a preferable trait when 
screening for responses towards antigens with shared expression profiles.  
A total of 91 peptide specific T cell responses were uncovered in 27 breast cancer patient samples, 
distributed on 50 different peptides. In 10 healthy donor samples 24 responses were detected, 
distributed on 27 different peptides, however the majority of these were found in just two healthy 
donors. HLA B*0702 restricted peptide responses were significantly more represented in breast 
cancer patients compared to healthy donors. For HLA A*0201 restricted peptide responses, this 
difference was not significant. MACS enrichment of 11 breast cancer samples from the cohort 
resulted in discovery of 21 peptide specific T cell responses. 6 of these were found in the given 
sample both ex vivo and post enrichment, providing additional verification of said responses.  
 
Within peptides derived from aromatase, prolactin, NEK3, and PIAS3 targets of T cell reactivity 
were observed. Final validation of any of these peptides as true breast cancer associated T cell 
epitopes is relevant and necessary to establish the applicability of these potential tumor associated 
antigens.
