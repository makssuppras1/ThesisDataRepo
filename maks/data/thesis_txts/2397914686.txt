2 Abstract 
Having challenging ways to gain knowledge concerning quantities of organic waste fraction from 
commercials, dining places, public institutions, service companies and industries, it was only successful to 
gain data for one childcare institution, one dining place, one accommodation and fish processing industry, 
for the majority of attempts to gather knowledge for quantities of organic waste ware failures. However, 
many had provided roughly personal visual estimations, that briefly showed uncertain amounts of organic 
waste they produce.  
The amount of biomass was estimated to 4020tonnes from households, solid fish waste of 20.1 tonnes from 
fish processing industry, and wastewater sludges from the city and industries based on the water 
consumption. Total biomass was estimated to 24.732tonnes/years. Using this biogas plant was designed for it 
bioreactor volume of 3303m3 and feedstock to 471m3. The plant is thermophilic condition to make the 
solution also destructing pathogens.  
Economic briefly analysis showed the biotechnological solution of organic waste is economic viable and 
potentially could produce 4 jobs in Nuuk and the plant might be having net present value of 22.5 million 
DDK for 19 years.

3 Introduction 
Solid waste is a resource, different mineral resources are existing within the solid residuals in the waste. But 
for many locals in Greenlandic settlements the waste is a mass of which has earned its original use and is 
discarded. That includes organic fraction of the waste; this fraction is biomass originated from vegetal, and 
animal substance residuals from forestry, municipal waste and especially from agricultural. This fraction is 
biodegradable, meaning it can be treated with biotechnology, an application similar to the natural 
degradation processes in the nature. 
The life on earth needs nutrition to exist and one of these elements within the nutrition is the element of 
phosphorous. Where ever we are on the planet, whether we are on the vast oceans and the depths within, 
whether we are on the great plains, mountains, tropical forest, tundral regions, delta regions and cities in 
every part of civilization - there is need for nutrition and phosphorous to thrive and to exist. 
The current megatrend for the usage of P (phosphorous) is unsustainable for the long term (Cooper, et al., 
2011) but there are several solutions to recycle this element among others and it is through biotechnological 
treatment. The future solutions for this trend are assessed in (Koppelaar, et al., 2013) and the solutions are 
mainly through increasing recycling application. 
With the growing population the needs for resources well grow (United Nations, 2015), that consequential 
means elements as P within fertilizers among other elements will be an important resource in agricultural 
economy (Cordell, et al., 2009). There is an opportunity here that Nuuk and possibly larger Greenlandic 
towns can produce high-value fertilizers, as they are getting more and more of reuse and recycling schemes.  
Greenlandic waste management is still on the way to mitigate and still to become better and develop. But one 
of the important aspects is the economy part that the activities almost needs to have scales of economy to 
operate and to evolve. With respect to the megatrend of the population, phosphorous element and the future 
economies of agricultural, there are potential possibilities to produce biogas, biodiesel, ethanol, high-value 
fertilizers and compost in Nuuk by introducing biotechnological treatment in the waste management system 
that potentially might give economic returns.  
The waste management of Greenlandic municipalities is by law framed by (Naalakkersuisut, 1993a) together 
with (Naalakkersuisut, 1993b), (Naalakkersuisut, 1994) and (Naalakkersuisut, 2011). The more practical 
aspects for the frames of the laws are the executional plans from (Grønlands Hjemmestyre, 2008). The 
Greenlandic waste management history, system and potential challenges is also described in (Eisted, et al., 
2011a; Eisted, et al., 2011b; Eisted, et al., 2011c). Although one of the claims from these are source 
separation of bio-waste do only pose few environmental benefits (Eisted, et al., 2011c), however this claim is 
merely based on tunnel composting, a type of reactor composting that also needs fresh air continuously and 
on the outputs only one marketable product is compost. Other outputs from this solution are leachate that 
needs to be disposed elsewhere. In this paper other biological treatment technologies will be introduced that 
can produce biogas, biodiesel, ethanol and high-value fertilizers. It will be stressed to make brief 
economically analysis of the possible solutions introduced. 
The managerial and administrative goals for the waste management in Sermersooq municipality at current 
state based on afore mentioned legal frames, and these schemes for the Sermersooq municipality is described 
in (Komuneqarfik Sermersooq, 2011). The plan has 7 objectives: which are identical and analog to goals 
defined in (Grønlands Hjemmestyre, 2008). 
1. Strengthening planning, administration and operation 
2. Increasing information efforts 
3. Establishing recycling stations and ably solutions for hazardous waste 
4. Improvement of the operation of landfills

4 Waste management in Sermersooq Municipality and Nuuk 
Sermersooq municipality is situated in Greenland that includes former municipalities Nuuk, Paamiut, 
Tasiilaq and Ittoqqortoormiit, see Error! Reference source not found. in Appendix A. The municipality is 
biggest in Greenland around 531.900 square kilometers, more than 12 times larger than whole Denmark. The 
total population for the municipality is 22600 inhabitants, 6.8% lives in Paamiut region, 12.9% in Tasiilaq 
region and 1.7% lives Ittoqqortoormiit region. There are several villages in throughout the municipality and 
constitutes 5.2% of the total population. However, 78% of them (17600 in year of 2017) lives in Nuuk, the 
center of the municipality and the capital of Greenland in which is the focus in this paper. 
The total household waste generated is estimated to 3.005.800kg each year based on unit generation for 
households in Greenland obtained from (Eisted, et al., 2011b) (133kg/inhabitant/year), this is however might

waste (475.121kg) from Tasiilaq, Paamiut and Kapisillit to incinerate the total transportation distance is 
1884km the distances are to be found in  are in total shown Figure 8 in Appendix A – Municipal overview. 
The villages and outer districts Tasiilaq and Ittoqqortoormiit are hunting and fisherman societies, their unit 
rate generation of household waste might be differing, this also includes waste compositions. 
The estimated total bio-waste is 1.2 million kg household waste alone, 114tonnes in Tasiilaq, 82tonnes in 
Paamiut and 21tonnes in Ittoqqortoormiit, in Nuuk organic fraction was 4019tonnes in 2016 four times

4.3 Source segregation of organic waste 
There is development in Copenhagen, to source segregate organic kitchen waste (Kristiansen, 2015), to 
generate biogas as they are expecting Copenhagen households to produce 57.000ton biowaste.  
There can be however constrains and challenges to segregate organic waste as described in (Vaz, et al., 
2015), mainly collection collapse due to collection truck was old and political disengagement and motivation 
for the workers were dominating constrains, linking these to Sermersooq municipality, it is not excepted 
collection vehicles to collapse and the motivators to source segregate waste fractions in the waste 
management system in Sermersooq are political and consulting agencies within the municipality. 
Economical aspects in terms of separate collection of biodegradable waste has been briefly studied in 
(Gomes, et al., 2008), the study was taking on a municipality in Portugal with 28.000 inhabitants, it was 
concluded costs for separate collection of whole biowaste does not necessarily be higher then traditional 
solution of unsorted collection.   
With the worldwide development to segregate organic waste, Sermersooq municipality and hopefully the rest 
of Greenland in the future would be gradually taking part, the question is the participation of the main 
producers – inhabitants. Thinking about (Monrad, 2015; Monrad, 2017), (Jeremiassen, 2016), (Lundgaard, 
2013) and establishment of waste knowledge center, the municipality has a good starting point to segregate 
organic waste fraction.  
5 Data collection methods 
As it was stated in Christensen (2010) the unit generation rate for waste from institutional and commercial 
wastes are based on very little information available concerning quantities and composition of waste. The 
unit generation rates are only taken account for the number of employees, for example concerning dining 
places: the amount of produced waste and produced food is directly linked to the number of customer of each 
dining place. The contacted dining places, public institutions and commercials is to be found in the separate 
excel file: data_collection in the sheet named: waste_producers 
As it was not possible to travel to Nuuk to collect needed data for amounts of organic waste from the 
institutional entities in Nuuk, restaurants and dining places, hotels and estimations from commercials. It was 
decided to collaborate with interested workers in these different places in Nuuk. It was furthermore decided 
to simplify the measurement part to only measure mass of the organic waste as these workers needs to make 
the measurements beside their daily work.

5.1.1 Ideal data collection method 
If it was possible the ideal data collection for the organic residuals would have been making direct 
measurements from the actual producers in the city of Nuuk for a defined duration, with one type of 
weighting device, by using one device is to avoid flaws in the measured masses as two different electronic 
weighting devices might have small differences in their results. Having a defined duration of measurement 
scheme was to make sure the data could be used to estimate a generation scheme of the residuals like as for a 
year or any defined duration.  
Other important variables in the ideal situation is to collect data for how many workers and clients the 
producer (institutions, commercials and restaurant) had. As example for institutions for child care, one needs 
to know how many children were at the child care institution and how many workers that day for each 
measurement. 
Having data for amount of mass, duration and numbers of clients and workers, one could have defined 
generation rate of organic residuals from this type of institution. Furthermore, as shown in section 6 page 10 
the mass balance of residual categories can be measured, knowing amount of these one might help to 
determine what to expect in terms of the most abundant component (lignin, cellulose, hemicellulose and 
others) might imply type of pretreatment or final treatment.  
Since the author cannot travel to Nuuk, it was decided to take contact to institutions, commercials, 
restaurants and hotels in Nuuk, this implies several flaws and barriers that is resulting in simplification of the 
measured data to only the mass.  
5.1.2 Barriers and challenges  
There are several barriers to collect data through the local workers in Nuuk. First challenge is for the 
workers; what beneficial do they have from being participating to measure their organic waste? This is an 
important concept What’s in it for me? Are the benefits higher than the cost for the worker that undertake the 
extra task to measure weight of the organic waste? The answer is simply no. When getting “in their head” by 
seeing their way; there is more to be done;  
• 
Categorizing the bio-waste as done in (Engström, et al., 2004)  
• 
Extra material as bags for categories – extra cost 
• 
Extra workload - Lost time by doing the extra work 
• 
Measuring the each of the category 
• 
Deriving the number of clients, costumers or guest for each day 
• 
Procurement of weighting device 
It was clear that there was need for greatly simplification of the asked work to be done. Therefore, it was 
decided to ask for to measure only the mass of organic waste each day for a duration of either a week or 
month. If they haven’t a weighting device, ask them to give their best visual estimation. 
One thing that could motivate them is the natural human curiosity, that it might be interesting to know how 
much residual there are each day or a week for the services or goods they sell. The other thing is their natural 
being as people, that people wants to help other people for something good, cleaner city, to being part of 
something good – save the planet etc.

Studying amounts of BOD, COD TTS and effluent flows are widely different. 
Total suspended solids (TTS) are residual matter in the wastewater the values are dry weight for each unit of 
liter wastewater. FOG is an abbreviation of Fat, Oil and Grease in the wastewater, but this is not taken in 
consideration as it is assumed to be part of BOD. 
BOD is an abbreviation for Biochemical Oxygen Demand, a parameter of the amount of oxygen, under 
aerobic conditions used by microbial organism in their metabolism to oxidize organic matter (to decompose 
the waste) in the wastewater (Nazaroff, et al., 2001). This parameter is to give estimate the amount of 
organic matter that are available for microorganisms.  
Chemical Oxygen Demand is another parameter to indicate all or total organic matter in the wastewater, it is 
normally determined by experiment or it can be calculated by the knowledge of organic matter consisting 
carbon, hydrogen and oxygen and assuming these fully oxidizes and possible sulphur and nitrogen are 
assumed to be in reduced form (H2S and NH3). 
TTS are in range of 14-20.000mg/L in Table 6, other studies have yield TTS up to 30.000mg/L for herring 
processing, it is depending om the process, but processing operations had shown BOD 1-75kg/tonnes 
product and filleting process between 12.5-37.5kg/tonnes product (Tay, et al., 2006).  
It is assessed that TTS of 245mg/L from halibut to represent the others, more than 98% of the total fish 
processed in Nuuk are halibut and cod both are within the range. Concerning BOD, it is assumed 3-35kg/ton 
raw material in (KANUKOKA, 2005b) in this paper it is used 20kg per raw material, BOD from filleting 
processes is not considered. COD is assumed to be 30kg/ton raw fish material. The results are 113.8tons a 
year for BOD and 170.7ton a year. TTS is calculated to 1.4ton/year. 
Different biological treatment techniques both aerobic and anaerobic were reviewed in (Chowdhury, et al., 
2010) and it was concluded in the article biological treatment to be best option to treat wastewater from fish-
processing with treatment by anaerobic followed by aerobic to be the optimal solution.

6.9  Biochemical methane potentials 
Biochemical Methane Potential (BMP) is theoretical maximum available in a unit of organic matter or 
substrate. The value can be expressed in two ways; L CH4/g COD (STP), in standard temperature and 
pressure (t = 0℃, 1atm). This is based on COD [g-O2/L]. The other way to determine BMP is by using the 
content of volatile solids (VS) [g-VS/L] or [%], L CH4/g VS (STP), this is organic content in the waste. VS 
is determined experimentally by drying samples measure the weight and furnaced the samples at high 
temperature approximately 550℃. The needed variables are ash, dry matter and total mass of matter to 
determine VS and TS. Which are known for fishing wastes.  
The organic fraction in household waste is assumed to contain only carbon, hydrogen, oxygen, nitrogen and 
sulphur. The methods to estimate the biogas potential needs to have knowledge of the chemical and physical 
conditions of the organic matter; chemical compositions, moisture and mass distributions.  
There are theoretical stoichiometric methods to calculate methane biogas potential; Buswell & Muller and 
Boyle equations. Buswell & Muller uses glucose chemical formula CaHbOc, this was modified by Boyle, to

7 Biotechnology applications 
Biotechnology it is an evolving technology in terms of recycling resources, but the technology has been 
existing for a long time, in China since 7000BC, in Egypt since 3400BC and gradually spreading to the 
European continent.  
In agricultural countries like Denmark, France, England etc. have been improving their reuse and recycling 
techniques of the residuals from the agricultural activities. Example pig manure, cow manure and vegetables, 
wheat straw etc. are digested in an anaerobic condition or composted to reuse as fertilizers in their crop 
production. In these days biomass is being in focus as source for fertilizer, as it contains crucial life 
supporting ortho-phosphorous as it is in terrestrial means a diminishing resource. But also, more important is 
seen as source for energy production.  
In this paper the purpose for the technology is however to recycle biomass, produce biogas and energy, 
digester and fertilizers, gaining knowledge and generation of possible professions. The advantages for using 
energy from biomass is it is renewable energy, less overall pollutions, less consumption of fossil fuels, 
utilization of waste, recycling the important fertilizers, it is contributing for export and economy. The 
disadvantages is however high emission of PAH compounds and odour, energy from biomass is more 
expensive from fossil fuels.  
7.1 Biomass  
Biomass is organic material mainly origins from vegetation and animal origin, examples; vegetable oils, 
wheat straw, trees and branches, wood chips, saw dust, or municipal and industrial waste. The lifecycle for 
biogenic carbon (biomass), biomass from living tissue (phytoplankton in the oceans and plants on the land)

reuses the CO2 in their growth. Biomass is in another word stored solar energy. 
7.2 Conversion technologies of biomass 
There are different forms to convert the biomass to energy both either as source for power production and 
heat, the more modern way is combined power and heat (CPH) energy production. There are two ways to 
convert biomass into energy, the first is direct use, the other as secondary energy form.  
Direct energy 
Direct use is by direct combustion of the biomass like trees, branches and garden waste in the incinerators.  
This type of direct usage has existed since the humanity learnt to make fire for heat and food processing.  
The secondary forms 
The secondary form energy form is the biomass are thermochemical, mechanical and biological conversions. 
Thermochemical is gasification and pyrolysis, both is in high pressure and temperature conditions, pyrolysis 
is by cracking the biomass to produce charcoal and oil and gas, which are combusted, gasification is biomass 
turns into combustible gasses. Mechanical extraction is by pressuring out the oils from plants like rapeseed 
oil and using extracted oil for combustion or further processing to heighten the heating value of the oil. 
Biological energy form 
This technology is by using microbial organisms like different bacteria; extreme thermophilic bacteria 
Bacillus, Clostridium and thermoanaerobacter produces biohydrogen, methanogenic archaea 
methanosarcina, methanobacteriales methanosaeta convert the biomass into biogas, yeast contains 
S.cerevisiae bacteria and yeast have been used for centuries for alcohol brewing they turns biomass into 
ethanol.  
There are four categories for biomass: 
1. Agricultural resources; sugar crops, potatoes etc. oil crops, sunflowers, rapeseed etc. wood crops. 
Algae is aquatic biomass 
2. Animal residues; manures from pig, cattle and litter from poultry, fish farming residues 
3. Forrest biomass; wood and wood residues 
4. Industrial residues and waste; municipal solid waste like organic fraction, food processing waste like 
from fish processing industries and sewage sludge 
The interest here the fourth category and most abundant in Greenlandic context, fish processing residue 
resource and municipal solid waste. Other grouping exists.  
7.3 Biomass chemistry 
For all within biomass categories especially goods from agricultural production is the biomass contains 
lignin, cellulose, hemicellulose, carbohydrates, proteins and lipids. (Vassilev, et al., 2010) and (Clark, et al., 
2008). 
Lignin is the bearing structure of plants, giving the mechanical strength and defensive wall against pathogens 
for the plants. Lignin is insoluble in water and difficult for many microbial organisms to digest.  
Cellulose is unbranched glucose polymers as straight chain polymers inside the plants, it can be chemically 
broken down to glucose units. Hemicellulose is sugar polymer, compared to cellulose it has more random 
and branched structure and in smaller sizes than cellulose and it is made up by many different sugars like

7.4.5 Physical-chemical parameters 
The chemical composition of the cellular material is important for microorganisms as these determines 
available nutrients for the microbials in their metabolism. Nitrogen, hydrogen, oxygen and carbon are main 
ingredient for organic materials. Sulphur (sulphide) and ammonia is necessary to form amino acids as it is 
use by reaction form simpler materials (synthesis). 
7.4.5.1 
Ammonia inhibition  
Ammonia is an inhibitory compound for anaerobic fermentation. This is well known for animal manure 
contains large concentration of ammonia as it was shown in laboratorial analysis in (Jéglot, et al., 2017) free 
ammonia is inhibitor especially for methanogenic bacteria. Free ammonia will be increasing in concentration 
when pH and temperature are increasing.  
7.4.5.2 
Temperature 
Temperature during anaerobic digestion is crucial for level of control. Anaerobic digestion in mesophilic 
(30-40℃) and thermophilic (50-60℃) temperatures are mostly known and experimented (Angelidaki, 
2017b). Thermophilic is having   on control having can have report advantages compared with mesophilic; 
Residence time is shorter, better destruction of pathogenic organisms (important part), faster or better 
degradation of LCFAs, main products formation is higher than biomass formation, improved solubilities and 
availabilities of substrates. The disadvantages are; degree of instability for the anaerobic processes is larger, 
higher demand for process energy and larger risk of ammonia inhibition. It is indicated high loading or short 
retention time gives larger biogas yield for thermophilic compared to mesophilic.   
7.4.5.3 
pH parameter 
The pH is a parameter that determines biomethane formation, methanogens have a narrow optimum pH 
condition, between pH of 7 and 8, most approximately (5.5 – 8.5) (Angelidaki, 2017b; Al Seadi, et al., 2008). 
pH is also influencing dissociation of ammonia, sulphide and acids which are important for the anaerobic 
processes. VFAs (volatile fatty acids) can also affect the pH by decreasing pH if VFAs accumulates (Al 
Seadi, et al., 2008).  
7.4.5.4 
Substrate inhibition 
Lipids and proteins in high concentrations in sudden feeding with these can inhibit anaerobic processes as, 
LCFAs can accumulated from lipids. Protein degradation also forms ammonia and thus inhibition effect. 
However, biogas plant can have adapted to high concentrations of lipids and higher gas yield be obtained. 
The size of the particles in the substrate can also play a role (Angelidaki, 2017b). 
7.4.5.5 
Solid content  
Dry matter contents is also important as this can affect the mobility of the microbial bacteria. Based on (Al 
Seadi, et al., 2008) should be <50%. One study (Safari, et al., 2017) have concluded optimal TS at 7% 
(7.02%) for thermophilic condition. This well be used in the designing the volume of bioreactor.

7.4.6 Hydraulic retention time 
The hydraulic retention time together with the temperature are determining factors for the size of the 
bioreactor. The hydraulic retention time for thermophilic conditions are rather shorter compared to 
mesophilic conditions as the temperature determines also the yield of biogas and destruction rate of 
pathogens. In a study from (Moset, et al., 2015) and from (Al Seadi, et al., 2008) the hydraulic retention time 
was optimal between (12-20) days for thermophilic conditions. Therefore 20 days was used in the designing 
of the bioreactor.  
7.4.7 Efficient stirring in the bioreactor is needed 
The mixing rate inside the reactor is important to secure substrate homogeneity and avoidance of 
accumulation of sludge, solid particles or sand in the biogas plant system. The stirring time was found to be 
3.12min/day in (Safari, et al., 2017), but lastly optimal adjustment would depending on the substrate and 
fermentation process inside the bioreactor.  
7.4.8 Process imbalance 
A fine balanced for anaerobic digestion processes is the key for high biogas production, when imbalances 
occurring there might be accumulation of hydrogen or other intermediates. Four dominant reasons for 
imbalance are; hydraulic overloading, organic overloading, toxic materials and sudden changes. 
Hydraulic overloading; This occurs if diminishing efficient reactor due to bad stirring and accumulation of 
sludge or sudden addition of substrate.  
Organic overloading; Is occurring due to addition of substrate to the bioreactor faster than microbials can 
degrade the added biomass 
Toxic material; occurs through added compounds or formation of toxic compound during the anaerobic 
processes. High contents of LCFAs can be inhibitory effect in the processes.  
Sudden changes; Occurs when feed rate is sudden increases thus affecting digestion processes.  
Balance is important, increasing of organic acids and CO2 would lead to decreasing pH value while increase 
and accumulation of ammonia would imply to pH increasing. pH value is a viable and cheap parameter to 
determine system imbalance.

8 Discussions & conclusions 
The idea for this paper was based on incinerated quantities of households in Nuuk for 2016. The organic 
fraction was sit on focus in a report written in the course 12132 – LCA-modelling of waste management and 
systems at DTU, 2016 January (Hammeken, 2016). But after careful consideration the LCA part was not 
conducted, the reasons are; firstly, the outcomes for the analysis are showing biotechnological solution is 
possibly economical viable and beneficial, secondly for the Greenlandic context job making is important, the 
idea for self-sustaining country is important.    
The goal to obtain any data for biomass quantities in to make supportive knowledge to heighten and optimize 
estimation for the biomass and solution possibilities in Nuuk. The approach was for starters through research 
in scientific papers and reports and through companies in Nuuk, the approach was though challenging as the 
author could not be in Nuuk with direct interaction with the companies and making actual measurements. 
The attempts to make different workers in Nuuk to make measurement were in many occasions failure, only 
two companies successfully made measurements of organic waste they produce. More had though made 
visually estimations, and these were used to assess the estimated amounts of bio-waste.  
Household waste generated in Nuuk was 9391733kg, compared to unit generation rate from (Eisted, et al., 
2011b) of 133kg/inhabitant/year, it was only 25% of measured amounts, this might be due to the “household 
waste” also included commercial and industrial household alike waste, but this cannot be confirmed at this 
point. However, using composition of waste from (Eisted, et al., 2011b) the organic fraction is 4020tonnes 
per year, here arises question is this also including organic waste from commercials and industries in Nuuk?  
To overcome this, it was stressed to assess carefully the visually estimates and measured organic waste to 
represent other companies alike. Example it was successful to gain data from one childcare institution 
Paarsisoq, the results were unit generation rate of organic waste for child care institution in Nuuk 1.84-
6.66kg/week gained through a two month of measurements, this is valid to use for estimation of organic 
waste generation in childcare institutions in Nuuk and Greenlandic context as this is closest to realities, by 
using unit generation rate from (Christensen, 2010), these were making overestimations in comparison to the 
measured amounts.  
Dining places, public schools were based on visual estimate made by the workers in the school and dining 
places, the results used to estimate the amount was using the lower end of the estimates. Like schools are 
estimated to produce 7.4-14.8 tonnes per year for further calculus lower end are used.  
Sømandhjemmet in Nuuk have provided measurement of a one week and unit generation this was resulting 
in unit generation of organic waste of 4.5-20.3kg/week, as there are no other alternatives it was assessed to 
be best estimation for other accommodations in Nuuk. Nasiffik also providing valid data.   
Healthcare and commercials were challenging, and no other alternatives were gained the estimate are only 
for further calculus for and cannot be used in other contexts, there is too much uncertainty.  
Fish processing waste research in scientific papers had given highly wide estimated amounts of BOD, COD 
and TTS and thus very uncertain and therefore was not used in further estimation of biogas potential. 
Measured amounts are needed to gain useful data to estimation of biogas potentials. 
Throughout the estimations only scientifically valid and useful data to use in other Greenlandic towns are 
data gained from childcare institution and Sømandhjemmet Nuuk and Nasiffik a dining place. 
 Biogas estimated is the closest author could come to, based on, several and highly uncertain estimates and 
thus rather weak foundation. That being said, the estimation was using combined data from known amounts 
like wastewater and water usage. For better estimation several compositional analyses are needed.  
The choice of thermophilic biotreatment is assessed to be best option in terms of governmental and 
municipality policies and plans to be more sustainable, the technological terms is also better way as
