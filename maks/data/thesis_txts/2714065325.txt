Abstract 
This thesis studies the possibility that, during the operation of a switch in a high-speed railroad 
line, a ballast particle gets stuck between the switch blade and the stock rail due to the "ballast fly" 
phenomenon so that, by bending the switch blade, the point machines can carry out the locking 
and detection mechanism usually, and therefore, that the ballast particle remains undetected and 
the gauge narrowing that it causes can produce a risk on the operational safety on the line. For this 
purpose, on the one hand, the mechanical behavior of the ballast has been studied to know how it 
behaves against the compression of the point machine. On the other hand, the gap produced has 
been quantified by means of a structural analytical model based on a real-life high-speed turnout 
used in Denmark. The risk it creates has been evaluated, as well as possible solutions to reduce or 
eliminate this risk.

1. Introduction 
1.1. 
Background 
Despite being hundreds of years old, the railway is still one of the primary means of transportation 
today. Since its standardization during the industrial revolution, it has been the primary means of 
transporting people and goods between production, urban, and port hubs. However, since the 
beginning of the 20th century and the development of other means of transport such as road 
vehicles and airplanes, railroads have lost ground in the field of short- and long-distance passenger 
transport. 
 
However, during the second half of the 20th century, passenger transport by rail underwent a 
revolution with the arrival of the high-speed railway (HSR). Countries such as Japan, France, the 
USA, Spain, Italy, Germany, the UK, experimented with technologies to allow commercial use of 
passenger transport at speeds exceeding 200km/h. This technology was quickly implemented in 
densely populated corridors and large urban nodes over distances ranging from 150 to 800 km [1]. 
 
Since the end of the 20th century, HSR has experienced an enormous growth mainly due to 
globalization and the challenge of climate change. Several countries, such as China, Spain, USA... 
have invested heavily in this means of transport. In 2010, 895 million passengers traveled by HSR 
globally, compared to the 3,300 that traveled during 2019, representing a 368% growth over a ten-
year period [2]. 
 
1.2. 
Motivation 
The element known in railways as turnout is responsible for allowing a train to change tracks either 
in a convergent or divergent direction smoothly. For this purpose, variable profile rails called 
switch rails are moved using either a manual or automatic device, which in contact with the fixed 
rails, called stock rails, allow a train to remain on the same track or to change to a new track either 
to its left or its right. 
 
The railroad turnout has two distinctive parts: the switch (left, Figure 1) and the crossover (right, 
Figure 1). The switch is the moving part of the turnout, and the one that is subject of study in this 
paper. Its purpose is to link the wheels with the rails corresponding to the direction to be taken. 
The crossover is the part in which the two directions are definitively separated/joined, preventing 
the flanges from hitting the rail being crossed, using the track known as the "frog".

The turnout has been in service ever since the inception of the railways, with minimal changes in 
its functioning. However, with the advent of high-speed rail, it is necessary to design turnouts with 
longer switch blades to accommodate the increased curve radius required for high speeds. This 
length increase requires the use of several point machines to ensure that the switch blade is placed 
in the correct position to allow the turnout, which is usually located at distances of more than five 
meters. This, together with the phenomenon known as "ballast flying" caused by the under pressure 
generated by the train running at high speeds, causes pieces of ballast to shoot out of the 
substructure and eventually end up between the switch rail and the stock rail. Generally, the 
position it occupies creates a circumstance in which the point machines are not locked, and 
therefore the movement authority does not allow trains to run on the switch. However, it may be 
the case that, due to the flexibility of the switch rail, it bends and allows the point machines to be 
locked even when there is an object in between. Such a scenario, which would remain undetected, 
can lead to a derailment, an intolerable risk in commercial transport at high speeds. The aim of this 
project is to investigate this phenomenon, trying to find out the circumstances under which this 
phenomenon may occur, as well as the behavior that the ballast may present.

2.1. 
Introduction 
The following is a review of the existing literature on the topic in question. Section 2.2 will focus 
on the phenomenon known as "ballast flight" and how this can lead to the situation under study. 
Section 2.3 will focus on the railway turnout, its functionality, its various components, etc. Section 
2.4 will cover everything that exists on gauge narrowing. Finally, section 2.5 will deal with the 
Point Load laboratory test and its usefulness in providing ballast strength values. 
 
2.2. 
Ballast flight 
2.2.1. Background 
Since the inception of the railway, and with the use of sleepers to maintain the gauge, the problem 
arose that the sleepers, acting as the only point of load on the ground, constantly suffered from 
stability problems in relation to the ground. For this reason, ballast emerged as an intermediate 
material between the ground and the track, with the main purpose of distributing the loads and 
ensuring greater stability. It comes from the crushing of high-density bedrock (mainly quartzite, 
ballast, and granite), with a defined granulometry (between 31.5 and 50 mm in Denmark) [4] as 
well as high resistance to abrasion. Among its other functionalities, it has the ability to attenuate 
the dynamic effect of loads on the sleepers in horizontal displacements since the sleeper-ballast 
interface prevents this (traditionally, due to the interlocking of the rocks with the wooden sleepers, 
now being replaced by the weight of the concrete sleepers). Furthermore, the ballast allows the 
alignment of the track to be placed very precisely, as well as permitting readjustments through the 
tamping process; its use prevents the growth of vegetation around the route, and its geometry 
makes it ideal for water drainage, as well as reducing vibrations on the ground.  
 
However, the disadvantages of ballast are the high cost of transport (in Denmark, the ballast comes 
from Norwegian quarries) and maintenance (due to the tamping and wear of the ballast). 
Additionally, on high-speed lines, a phenomenon known as "ballast flight" is caused [5], [6]. 
2.2.1. Definition and formulation 
Ballast fly is the phenomenon where ballast particles are lifted above the ballast bed due to a

2.2.2. Mitigation measures 
Among the options used to mitigate ballast flight, the most commonly used is the reduction of the 
ballast profile on the sleeper. In one study [8], the profile height was reduced by 0.04 m above the 
sleeper, producing a noticeable improvement, especially at high speeds. This is because the 
reduction in height reduces the aerodynamic force on the ballast. Another less common solution is 
the use of polyurethane on the ballast surface so that the ballast is bonded together. This measure 
is capable of eliminating ballast fly-off at speeds of up to 385 km/h [18]. This measure is not 
affected by tamping, as it is carried out at a sufficient depth.

The focus of this paper is on the switch rail device, where the problem under study is developed. 
It mainly consists of three elements: 
 
 Stock rails are those whose position is fixed. They go on the outside, and their section is 
constant 
 
 Switch rails (or switch blades) are those whose position is mobile. They go on the inside, 
and their section is variable. 
 
 Point machines are devices operated by electric, hydraulic, or pneumatic mechanisms, 
generally operated remotely, which have three functions: to move the switch rails laterally, 
to block them, and to detect that they are correctly positioned. 
 
 
2.3.2.1. 
Switch blades and stock rails 
The aim of the switch rail device is to transfer the train from one set of tracks to another in a 
smooth way, minimizing maintenance costs and maintaining user comfort. Switch blades are used 
for this purpose. With a slightly curved layout and a variable section, the aim is to ensure that the 
wheel support point transitions between the stock rail and switch blade. This transition on 
conventional railways is short, being only centimetres, but on high-speed railways, where the 
turnouts are designed to be used at design speeds of over 200km/h, these transitions are usually 
several meters long. The contact surface between the two rails is flat to ensure a smooth transition.

The total length of switch blades, from the tip to where they cease to be movable, is several meters 
in length, being between 20 and 30 meters for high-speed railways. Their variable sections are

2.3.2.2. 
Point machines 
The point machines perform three functions: moving the switch blades between their two 
configurations, locking them in place, detecting that their position is correct, and communicating 
with the interlocking system. Normally, in conventional railway, 1- or 2-point machines are 
required, whereas in high-speed switch rail devices, up to 8-point machines can be necessary due 
to the length of the switch blades [19]. These are arranged between 5-6 meters apart, determined 
by the stiffness of the switch blade and other auxiliary elements.  
 
These mechanisms usually have a linear movement, in the case of electromechanical, electro-
pneumatic, and electro-hydraulic actuators. For example, in the case of electromechanical 
actuators, an electric motor generates a displacement by means of a connecting rod or worm gear 
system. The force they use for their purpose does not exceed 10kN, and the maximum force they

There are also two types of point machines depending on how they should behave in the event of 
a train run-through[21] (Figure 10): 
 
 Trailable point machines move each switch blade separately, using two rods. Generally, a 
force greater than that of the point machine is applied (such as that generated by a train 
forcing its way), and the lock is broken, allowing the train to re-rail safely. This mechanism 
is generally non-destructive, allowing the machine to be put back into operation 
immediately, but as a consequence, it can reduce the life of the device. This type of point 
machine is often used on conventional railways, where speeds are lower, and safety 
standards are lower. 
 
 Non-trailable point machines move both switch blades in tandem, using only a single rod. 
In the event of failure, there is a sacrificial element that breaks when a specific force is 
reached (several times greater than that permitted by the machine), rendering the point 
machine inoperable without damaging the mechanism itself. As a consequence, it is 
necessary to repair and replace the sacrificial element, which can lead to the line being 
temporarily out of service. This type of point machine is commonly used on high-speed 
lines, where safety standards are higher.

2.3.3. Other components 
2.3.3.1. 
Stretcher bars 
The stretcher bars are elements that are placed joining both switch blades to provide greater rigidity 
to the set of switch blades and ensure the distance between them [22]. Their function is to reinforce 
the work of the point machines, as they transfer the force exerted on one switch blade to the other. 
This system is useful to reduce the use of point machines or where the optimal function is not 
adequate, especially in the trailable ones.  
 
 
Figure 11. Stretcher bar (white element) in a railway switch. 
 
2.3.3.2. 
Detection devices 
 
Detection devices are used to detect the position of the switch blades in relation to the stock rails 
at a given point. They are usually placed between point machines and can be either laser detection 
systems placed on the stock rails or hydraulic/pneumatic pistons.

The FAKOP solution 
Fahrkanten Optimierung (optimizing the active rail edge), abbreviated to FAKOP, is a system that

The CATFERSAN system is based on the surface machining of the straight stock rail in a

2.4.1. Wheelset and track dimensions 
The nominal rail gauge (between rail axles) used on high-speed lines in almost all countries is 
1500mm. Following the geometry of the rail head, when the rail head is 14mm below the top of 
the head, this is the point at which the wheel comes into contact with the rail. The distance 
measured between the two points of both rails is what is known as the standard width, which is 
1435mm, with different tolerances according to the regulations of each country. Another important 
distance to consider is the flange gauge, which is measured from the point on the wheel geometry 
that is 10 mm below the top of the head of the rail. This gauge has a maximum value of 1426mm, 
although it can vary up to 1410mm [30]

2.5. 
Point Load Strength test and UCS determination 
When you have samples of crushed rock, such as ballast, and you do not have access to the original 
source to take samples easily analyzable in most laboratory tests (cylindrical, rectangular), the 
Point Load Strength Test developed by the International Society for Rock Mechanics (ISRM) 
commission on testing methods in 1985 [32] provides a test applicable to the irregular lump shapes 
that ballast is characterized by.  
 
The Point Load Strength test is intended as an index test for the strength classification of rock 
materials. It may also be used to predict other strength parameters with which it is correlated, for 
example, uniaxial tensile and compressive strength. The machine for the test consists of two steel 
truncated cones that apply a concentrated load (P) on the same axis until the sample crushes. The 
machine is calibrated, records the pressure used, and keeps the highest one (the one before breaking 
point).

calculations:

 D = Distance between tips in the point load device 
 W1 = Shortest width of specimen  
 W2 = Longest width of specimen

3. Objective and methodology 
3.1. 
Objective 
The objective of this thesis is to study the gauge reduction caused by a ballast particle located 
between the switch blade and the stock rail in conditions not detected by the point machine to 
determine how much it can compromise the safe functioning of a high-speed line and to propose 
solutions for the elimination of this risk. The project works with various disciplines such as 
mathematics, geometry, structural analysis, and rock mechanics. 
 
This work assumes that although the probability of occurrence of the phenomenon under study is 
very low, the consequences for passenger safety can be catastrophic, incurring material and human 
damage. Therefore, it is considered that such a risk should be eliminated. 
 
Due to accessibility status, data from the Danish high-speed rail network, managed by the public 
company Banedanmark, will be used. More specifically, the data will be based on the Copenhagen-
Ringsted line, the only completed high-speed railway in Denmark, completed in 2019 and 60 
kilometres long with a maximum operating speed of 250km/h, both because of its proximity and 
because it is the only line of these characteristics in operation at the time of this study. Therefore, 
Danish railway rules and standards will also be used wherever possible. However, this does not 
prevent this study from being extrapolated to other high-speed lines with similar characteristics. 
 
3.2. 
Methodology 
To carry this task, and with the means available, it has been decided, first, to assess the mechanical 
behaviour of the ballast particles used on a high-speed line to check whether the force generated 
by the point machines is sufficient to crush them and, if not, to find out what force is necessary for 
this purpose. For this, a representative sample will be obtained, and Point Load Strength tests will 
be carried out in order to obtain its compressive strength values (UCS) and compare them to the 
geometrical characteristics of the ballast particles and the point machine throwing force. Secondly, 
based on these results, an analytical-structural model to simulate the mechanism of the switch will 
be created, where all the elements of the set (switch blade, point machines, friction, ballast) are 
integrated and whose function will be to carry out several tests with different sizes and positions 
of the ballast on the model, and to establish its effect on the correct operation of the locking system, 
as well as the safe operation of the railway line. 
 
With the results obtained, measures will be proposed to eliminate any risk that this problem may 
have on the safety of railway operations. The limitations that this study has on the approach to the

5.2.1. Simplifications and hypothesis 
The following simplifications and assumptions have been made for the model: 
 
1. The geometry of the switch blade when it does not receive any load will be considered to 
be straight. 
 
2. The section with the smallest inertia dimensions will be used to model the switch blade 
(thus, the values achieved will be more adverse than those expected in the real model). 
 
3. The influence of friction on the model will be neglected (thus, the values achieved will be 
more adverse than those expected in the real model). 
 
4. The influence of torsion on the model shall be neglected. 
 
5. Any vertical movement on the switch blade shall be neglected (so that the model acts in 
2D dimensions). 
 
6. Deformation in the direction of the switch blade (stretching) shall be neglected.

5.4.5. Results analysis on rail gauge narrowing 
As can be seen from the tests performed, the gauge narrowing produced by an undetected ballast 
particle can generate a gap between the switch blade and the stock rail of up to 21 millimetres if 
tolerances are used. This means that, at the point where the ballast is, there is a rail gauge of 
1435+10-21 = 1424 millimetres, so the real narrowing on the standard gauge is 11 millimetres. 
This distance may pose a risk of derailment and is not acceptable by Banedanmark maintenance 
standards (which establish a maximum reduction of 2 to 5mm over the standard gauge). 
5.4.6. Solutions 
Two solutions have been adopted on the model: the reduction of the point machine force and an 
increase of the inertia: 
 
 In the first case, a large gap reduction can be achieved, from 17 to 8mm if the point machine 
force is reduced by half (21 to 12mm if tolerances are taken into account). Therefore, if the 
FAKOP of 10mm is added, the gauge would be +-2mm above the standard, which is 
tolerable according to the regulations. The problem with this solution is that if the force 
available to the point machines is reduced, it is possible that false positives may occur (for 
example, in a normal situation, the machines may not be able to close the mechanism 
because they do not have enough force). To avoid this type of situation, it is necessary to 
carry out a detailed study of the force required by the point machines to locking the 
mechanism in most cases. 
 
 In the second case, the reduction is less considerable, going from 17 to 12mm if the point 
machine force is reduced by half (21 to 16mm if the tolerances are taken into account). 
Therefore, if the FAKOP of 10 mm is added, the gauge would be -2-6 mm above the 
standard, which may not be tolerable according to the regulations. This analysis also

Out of the analysis carried out, there are two other solutions which add external elements to the 
system and are widely used in most high-speed systems in the world: 
 
 Point detectors, which simply perform the same detection function as point machines, and 
based on the model, if placed between two point machines (as in our model, the PM3 and 
PM4), would reduce the span by half, greatly limiting the gap that can be produced by an 
undetected ballast particle located between a point machine and a point detector. On the 
other hand, they increase the price of the turnout and also the price of maintenance. 
 
 Stretcher bars are bars that are fixed between the two switch blades and whose purpose is 
to increase the transmission of deformations between them, thus making the assembly more 
rigid. For example, if one of these elements is placed between two-point machines (as in 
our model, the PM3 and PM4), it would transmit the stresses to the other switch rail and 
therefore distribute the force of the point machines, reducing the gap produced by an 
undetected ballast particle. It is, therefore, a cheaper and less maintenance-intensive 
solution than the point detector but has the same problems of increasing inertia.

6. Conclusion 
Throughout the thesis, a review of turnouts on high-speed railways has been carried out, with 
special emphasis on the switch rail device, which is the most technically complex part of the 
turnout.  
 
Afterward, an analysis of the mechanical behaviour on a ballast sample used for Danish high-speed 
lines was carried out in order to know if it is superior to the force exerted by the point machines 
and, if so, to see how it behaves in case of breakage. It has been shown that the force required to 
break a ballast particle is considerably higher than the force that the point machines are capable of 
delivering and, therefore, the breakage factor of the ballast in the event of gauge narrowing due to 
undetected ballast particles has not been considered. 
 
Finally, a structural-analytical model of a switch rail based on one of the turnouts used in the 
Danish high-speed network has been carried out, on which several simulations and a study of 
solutions have been performed. The worst position for a ballast particle in the real case has been 
analyzed: between point machine PM3 and 4, and based on this, it has been calculated that an 
undetected ballast particle can cause a gap of between 17 and 21 mm depending on the tolerances 
for locking and detection, which is a reduction over the standard gauge of 7 to 11 mm, which is 
not tolerable for Danish standards. 
6.1. 
Conclusions and recommendations 
The conclusions of this thesis are set out below: 
 
 The mechanical strength of the ballast is high enough to resist the force generated by the 
point machine. 
 
 The gauge narrowing due to an undetected ballast particle is capable of producing 
significant gauge reductions, which, if left unaddressed, could lead to a risk of a train 
derailment, leading to material and human damage. Such a risk is unacceptable on high-
speed rail and must therefore be addressed. 
 
 Based on the set of simplifications and assumptions made, there is a possibility that this 
risk could be mitigated by reducing the force supplied by the point machine coupled with 
greater inertia in the switch blades (and, therefore, greater stiffness). However, further 
studies are needed to assess whether these measures are sufficient for the safe operation of 
the line.

6.2. 
Limitations and future work 
As this is a highly technical subject, and there is not much existing literature on it, and much of 
the technical information obtained is not accessible on the web because it belongs to companies 
and manufacturers, several hypotheses and simplifications had to be made in order to arrive at the 
results obtained. Although the author believes that these results should not make much difference, 
a more detailed analysis of the problem is needed. 
 
Future work on the problem is needed to achieve more accurate results. Among the possible actions 
to be carried out would be to carry out simple compressive strength tests (UCS) with several 
samples from different sources and on samples extracted in the quarry and with techniques that 
give accurate results (and not correlation tests). As for the analytical model, it is necessary to obtain 
a three-dimensional profile of the switch blade and to be able to use this profile by means of finite 
element modeling (FEM), in which the effects of torsion, friction, and reaction forces at the total 
switch blade/stock rail interface can be calculated. It is also necessary to study in more detail how 
the force supplied by the point machine varies over cycles and time so that this information will 
be used to achieve a force limitation that reduces the risk of gauge narrowing. All this is 
accompanied by empirical analysis using actual deviations and measuring whether the model is 
fulfilled.
