Abstract
The Roll-on/Roll-off (RoRo) shipping industry is a significant part of the global shipping net-
work. This thesis will focus on optimisation at the operational level of planning, namely the
actual stowage of cargo. This thesis introduces the RoRo Ship Stowage Problem (RSSP) with
ballast water and shifting cost minimisation for a multi-deck and multi-port problem, utilis-
ing a grid representation of the ships. A non-linear solver is used in minimising the ballast
water such that the ship is seaworthy i.e. within stability, shear force and bending moment
limits. The cargo shifting cost is minimised with a shortest path formulation to decrease the
time spent loading and unloading the ship. Multiple heuristics are applied to a set of generated
problem instances, herein the Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search, the TABU search and a
Greedy Randomised Adaptive Search Procedure, each utilising the solution methods for cargo
shifting and ballast water optimisation to solve the combined problem. Multiple cargo booking
list instances, simulating the real world stowage problem, are created for three different sized
RoRo ships. The results from the combined problem of the RSSP show that the ALNS method
and the TABU search provides comparable performances. For the individual sub-problems, the
ALNS performs better at minimising shifting cost and ballast water volume as well as the overall
objective whereas the TABU search is better at generating feasible solutions. The findings in
this thesis is a step in the direction of enabling automated stowage plan generation for the RoRo
shipping industry, but the results and assumptions still needs to be verified in a realistic setting.
Keywords: Maritime Transportation, Roll-on/Roll-off, Shipping, Optimisation, Metaheuris-
tics, ALNS, TABU Search, Stowage planning, Ship stability, Cargo shifting, Shear force and
Bending moment, Ballast water optimisation

Chapter 1:
Introduction
The shipping industry is an important vein of the current global economy. Today, the shipping
industry accounts for more than 80% of the global trade according to the United Nations (UN)
(2022)[1]. This means that the majority of the goods that consumers buy have touched a ship
at some stage on their journey to the customer. The shipping market has seen huge growth but
also increased competition since the 2008 recession. The market was shaken during the COVID
pandemic but progressed into a period of growth within shipping due to high demand for goods.
This led to high shipping prices and long lead times at ports, largely due to congestion[1]. All
these events and circumstances signify great potential for optimisation in all parts of the journey
of a ship.
The shipping market is segmented in multiple ways, but mainly by the type of cargo. The four
main categories are bulk, container, liquid and other cargo. The latter includes Roll-On/Roll-Off
(RoRo) shipping. A RoRo ship carries cargo on wheels e.g. cars, trucks and heavy equipment.
RoRo ships carry ramps to load and unload cargo and are thus not in need of expensive port
infrastructure in the way that container shipping employs quay cranes etc. RoRo shipping is
often in competition with container shipping as they can carry some of the same cargo. This
means that RoRo shipping companies have to compete with huge and highly eﬀicient container
shipping companies. Optimisation within RoRo shipping is therefore highly sought after as that
would help create a competitive advantage, especially in optimisation of planning.
The main planning problems in the shipping industry can be divided into three categories:
Strategic, tactical and operational planning[2]. The time frames associated with the three cate-
gories are long, medium and short-term planning, respectively. Strategic planning handles the
overall shipping strategy that the company wants to follow whereas tactical planning is about
determining routes and timetables. Operational planning is the day-to-day handling of the ship
e.g. loading/unloading.
This thesis will focus on the operational planning for RoRo shipping. One of the major chal-
lenges for operational RoRo planning is to create good stowage plans i.e. the way the cargo is
placed on the ship. Creation of good stowage plans is a crucial task as it determines the amount
of cargo that can be carried, the time spent loading and unloading cargo and how much ballast
water is needed to stabilise the ship.
A shipping route often consists of multiple port calls. For each of these ports, a booking list
determines which cargo should be shipped. A good stowage plan will include as much cargo
as possible with the available deck space from the booking list. The time spend loading and
unloading at each port is then a crucial aspect of a stowage plan, as it represents an implicit
cost for the RoRo companies. As cargo has to be driven out of the ship, a clear path for driving
through the ship is needed. If no clear path is available, the only solution is to move other cargo
that is in the way. The act of temporarily moving the cargo to make space for other cargo to be
loaded or unloaded is called shifting, and the time lost can be represented as the shifting cost.
Thus a good stowage plan also minimises the shifting cost.

Chapter 1. Introduction
A ship needs to be seaworthy to embark on a journey, which means that there are certain sta-
bility requirements that need to be met, such that ship does not capsize. Ships employ ballast
tanks to correct the position of the ship and counteract both inside and outside forces that might
make the ship unstable. A good stowage plan takes this into account, minimising the amount of
ballast water needed by strategically placing cargo and distributing the weight evenly across the
deck space. Minimising ballast water is crucial as carrying excess weight increases fuel costs and
thereby also carbon emissions. Another reason to distribute the weight evenly is to maintain
the structural integrity of the ship. Concentrating all the heavy cargo in one place, puts a lot of
stress on the hull of the ship, increasing the risk of structural failures. The two main concerns
are the shear forces created by weight pushing down, and the buoyancy lifting the ship, as well
as the bending moment as a result of this. A good stowage plan also takes this into account, as
it distributes weight such that the structural limits of the ship are not compromised.
The creating of stowage plans is currently a manual task, but industry experts believe that
the industry is ready for automated stowage plan generation, according to the business paper
by Spaniol et al. (2022), [3]. Stowage plan generation can be seen as a Operations Research
problem and have seen academic interest since the 2010’s. Prior research shows that this is an
NP-hard problem and commercial solvers have trouble solving it as a MIP model [4–6]. This
thesis will therefore investigate how the RoRo ship stowage planning problem (RSSP) can be
solved using heuristics while taking stability, ballast water, shear force, bending moment and
shifting cost into account. Thus the two-fold research question for this thesis is as follows:
How can the complete stowage problem for RoRo ships be solved using a metaheuristic approach,
while ensuring stability requirements are met for vessels that contain multiple decks and travel
between several ports?
and,
Can heuristics solve realistic instances of the RoRo ship stowage problem to provide good and
feasible stowage plans within a reasonable time frame?
The thesis will try to answer those questions in the following chapters. In chapter 2, the existing
literature of RoRo stowage planning and similar problems will be described.
It will outline
what has been the subject of research within RoRo stowage planning and set the stage for this
paper. Chapter 3 will describe the theory of ship stability. This is required understanding when
modelling ballast water, shear force and bending moment. In chapter 4, three mathematical
formulations will be presented. Each of these formulate a part of the complete stowage planning
problem. Chapter 5 will describe and explain the methods used for solving the RSSP. This also
includes the representation of the problem. Chapter 6 will present and comment on the results
obtained by the study, and a sensitivity analysis will be performed to provide insights about the
model. The findings from this thesis, the assumptions taken and the future work yet to be done
will be discussed in chapter 7 before a final conclusion is presented in chapter 8. The data used
in this thesis has been provided by DFDS in the form of stability booklets, detailing the ships
that the stowage plans will be made for.

Chapter 2:
Literature review
The study of RoRo stowage planning is quite new in academic terms as the first paper on it
was released in 2011. Since then multiple papers have been released on the topic due to the
growing demand for optimised stowage plans. The purpose of this literature review is to study
prior findings and motivate why this thesis is relevant. The review will first cover the RoRo
stowage planning literature and then present literature on similar problems within the container
shipping industry. Finally, the relevant ship stability and metaheuristic background literature
to understand and model this version of the RSSP is introduced.
The first paper on RoRo stowage optimisation Optimization of stowage plans for RoRo ships was
written by Øvstebø et al.(2011) [4]. It introduces the RoRo Ship Stowage Problem (RSSP) with
a lane representation of the decks and its mathematical formulation and presents the problem
as NP-hard even in its simplest forms. The presented model can handle some aspects of ship
stability, mandatory and optional cargo, several decks and multi-port shipping with shifting
cost minimisation. The paper solves the problem using a MIP formulation and a squeaky wheel
heuristic combined with a local search, showing that a heuristic approach is suitable for the
RSSP by benchmarking it against a commercial solver. In a related paper by Øvstebø et al.
(2011), Routing and Scheduling of RoRo ships with Stowage Constraints[7], they employ the
TABU search heuristic to solve the problem. The problem in question deals with not only the
stowage of cargo but also time windows for said stowage. The paper shows that the TABU
search heuristic is well-suited for both the RSSP and the RSSP with time windows. Øvstebø et
al. (2011) worked under several assumptions concerning the layout of the ship. This limits the
adaptability of the model and some of these assumptions are mitigated in later research.
Not much research was published until Hansen et al. (2016) published the paper, 2D-packing
with an Application to Stowage in Roll-on Roll-off Liner Shipping [8]. This paper researches the
RSSP seen as a 2D Packing Problem or 2DRSSP. The model handles single-deck configurations
and thus does not calculate ship stability. It employs constrained optimisation and presents
several MIP formulations with which to solve the problem i.e. minimisation of shifting costs.
They show that the 2DRSSP can be solved using a commercial MIP solver, but only for for
small instances, further emphasising the need for heuristics when expanding the problem. Puisa
(2018) published Optimal stowage on RoRo decks for eﬀiciency and safety [9], highlighting an
aspect of the problem not extensively researched before, the transportation of dangerous goods.
In addition, they do a discretisation of a grid representation of the ship and thus create slots
for each vehicle size. Puisa (2018) proposes a MIP that maximise revenue while taking aspects
like fire safety on deck into account.
Zhang et al. (2020) introduces A Two-Phase Stowage Approach for Passenger-Cargo RoRo Ship
Based on 2D-KP: Coping With Complex Rotation and Safe Navigation Constraints [10]. The
paper focuses on maximising cabin area utilisation with the possibility of rotating cargo and safe
navigation. Usually, cargo is placed in longitudinal lanes from the aft to the bow of the ship
and as such, allowing cargo to rotate and be placed perpendicular to the direction of the ship
potentially allows the deck space to be more optimally utilised. They employ three different
algorithms to solve the problem based on swarm intelligence. The algorithms are compared to
a MIP model solved with a commercial solver. Results show that the commercial solver is not
able to solve realistic instances of the problem, whereas the swarm intelligence-based heuristics

The latest literature on the subject was made by
Parvarsi et al.(2022) - RoRo Ships Stowage Planning: Using a Novel MIP Model and Stability
Constraints [6]. This paper introduces a mathematical model that encompasses the following
aspects, multi-deck, multi-port, optional/mandatory cargo and stability. The paper suggests
that a MIP can potentially solve the problem for small instances and the authors mention that
heuristics should be developed to solve real-life instances.
The paper, Stowage Planning with Optimal Ballast Water by Beizhen et al.(2020) [11] proposes
both an integrated approach to ballast water optimisation where loading computers are used
as well as a MIP model to optimise ballast water. Beizhen et al. (2020) suggests potentially
large reductions in the amount of ballast water used, which would lead to great savings in fuel
consumption and CO2 emissions. The paper only considers a single cargo size, while also con-
sidering refrigerated cargo that can only be placed in designated spots. When modelling the
problem, Beizhen et al. (2020) linearises stability equations and discretises ballast water tank
filling levels for the MIP model by allowing tanks to be either empty, 50% filled or full.
The literature on the subject is limited, and thus several aspects of the problem can still be re-
searched, which could potentially ease the way RoRo stowage planning is done today. Through-
out the years the problem has been explored from different angles, with several assumptions
and limitations in place. Both MIP models and heuristics have been used to solve the problem.
It seems that MIP models generally perform well for smaller instances, but once the problem is
scaled to a more realistic size MIP solvers begin to struggle and the use of heuristics becomes
the preferred or advised tool for further work. Given the complexity of the entire RSSP, most
papers try to keep complexity manageable with the aforementioned assumptions. Thus, this
thesis aims to collectively apply all core aspects of the RoRo stowage problem, shifting, stability
and ballast water, in one model.
To gain a further understanding of how both MIP models and heuristics are used to solve prob-
lems in the shipping sector one can also search outside the RoRo area. While the use of both
MIP formulations and solvers, as well as heuristics are not prevalent within the RoRo shipping
industry outside of academia yet, it is within the field of container shipping.
With regards to optimisation in similar areas as RoRo shipping, the container shipping industry
is ahead of RoRo shipping. One of the earliest optimisation papers on container shipping was
published by Lai et al. (1995), called Shipping Container Logistics and Allocation [12]. The
study employed heuristic search methodologies to minimise operating costs. While the meta-
heuristics used by Øvstebø et al. (2011) for RoRo ships were far more advanced, the research
was also conducted 16 years later. This speaks to the maturity of the area of optimisation within
container shipping and inversely of the area of RoRo shipping. Much of the literature in the
RoRo area builds upon the earlier research conducted within container shipping [4, 5, 7, 13].

Chapter 2. Literature review
Homayouni and Fontes (2018) published the book Metaheuristics for Maritime Operations [14],
which is a collection of papers and methodologies detailing the use of heuristics and metaheuris-
tics to solve complex problems in the maritime industry. While RoRo optimisation is mentioned
in the book, most of the work focuses on the problems in and around container shipping, such
as routing problems as well as berth space allocation and quay crane placement at container
terminals. While routing can be applied to the RoRo shipping industry, most of the research
presented has been done with regard to container ships. This further emphasises that optimisa-
tion within the container industry has been extensively researched, in comparison to the RoRo
shipping industry.
In working with ships, knowledge of their design and the physics at play when large forces are
involved is also needed. The book Skibsteknik 2 - Stabilitet & Manøvrering (Ship Technique 2
- Stability and Manoeuvring) by Frey et al. (2007) goes into great detail regarding the physics
of objects in bodies of water and is used as a manual for navigators both in training and in
practice [15]. Also, the book Ship Design and Performance for Masters and Mates by Barrass
(2004) provides knowledge of the intricacies of large vessels [16]. This thesis bases its knowledge
of stability and ship physics on these pieces of literature.
In addition to the earlier literature, knowledge about the metaheuristic algorithms used in this
thesis consists of two main pieces of literature. The first is the Handbook of Metaheuristics
- Third Edition by Potvin and Gendreau (2019) [17] (originally published in 2010) and the
second, Search Methodologies - Introductory Tutorials in Optimization and Decision Support
Techniques by Burke and Kendall (2005) [18]. Both works provide a great foundation on heuris-
tics and metaheuristics applied in solving optimisation problems.
In this paper, the goal is to extend the prior research done by creating a model that accounts
for a combination of aspects tackled by the different research papers and solve it using multiple
relevant metaheuristics [5, 7].

Chapter 3:
Theory
In this chapter, the theory needed to fully understand the RSSP and its facets will be described.
The chapter focuses on the physics of the ship as they are an integral part of understanding the
constraints and measures that must be taken to ensure a ship’s seaworthiness. As such, theory
regarding the ship stability, shear force and bending moments as well as correcting measures
will be discussed, providing an understanding of the RSSP as well as stability and ballast water
which are some of the focal points.
3.1
Ship stability
To ensure the seaworthiness of the ship during its journeys, the stability of the ship needs to be
considered when creating stowage plans. Placing too much heavy cargo on one side might cause
the ship to heel. Similarly, too much cargo in the bow or stern of the ship can cause trim by bow
or trim by aft respectively. A lot of heavy cargo placed midships can cause sagging of the ship’s
structure, inversely heavy cargo in the aft and the bow can cause hogging. Thus, certain stability
limits must be met in all three physical dimensions and weight must be dispersed throughout
the ship. This section will cover some of the core aspects that decide seaworthiness in more
detail. Fig. 3.1 provides an overview of the dimensions of the problem, illustrated on a RoRo
ship.

gravity across all three dimensions of the ship. If these values are within the limits defined for
the ship, it will ensure a stable vessel. First, the general definition of calculating centers of
gravity on the different space dimensions will be defined.

3.1.2
Stability parameters
By applying the general formulation for each of the directions of a ship, longitudinal, transverse
and vertical, a stability parameter is defined. These are longitudinal, transverse and vertical
centers of gravity, or LCG, TCG and VCG respectively. The LCG of a ship is determined as
the distance of the center of gravity from the aft perpendicular (AP) of the ship. It is used
to determine if the ship will maintain a trim by bow or trim by aft. TCG is measured on a
ship as the distance of the center of gravity from the longitudinal center line (LCL) of the ship
represented by negative values to the port side and positive if the center of gravity lies starboard
of the LCL. The TCG value is used to determine if the ship will maintain a heel during sailing.

While both LCG and TCG are just as important as the VCG, the VCG has some extra con-
siderations related to it. The VCG helps ensure that the ship will always return to an upright
position after being affected by outside forces as long as the center of gravity lies below the
metacenter of the ship. The metacenter of a ship is determined as the theoretical intersection
of a vertical line drawn through the center of gravity and the keel of the ship, and another line
drawn through the center of buoyancy in the direction of the upward force of buoyancy when
the ship is tilting to a side. If the metacenter, when the ship rolls, becomes lower than the
center of gravity, the vessel will be unstable as the center of buoyancy and center of gravity
are now working together to rotate the ship and the ship will not roll back [15]. Thus having a
low VCG is good for preventing rollovers, as the distance to the metacenter will be large, but
if the VCG value is too low, the ship will be very ”stiff”, which can result in an uncomfortable
sailing experience and cargo damage. An illustration of the metacenter in relation to the center
of gravity and the center of buoyancy can be seen in fig. 3.3.

3.1.3
Buoyancy
Archimedes’ principle states that ”a body immersed in a fluid experiences an upthrust equal
to the weight of the fluid displaced”. This is fundamental to the equilibrium that ensures that
ships can stay afloat. The buoyancy defined by the weight of the fluid displaced is then:
∆= ▽∗ρ
(3.11)
Where ▽is the displaced volume of water, and ρ is the density of water, in this case, seawater.
The displaced volume ▽is then dependent on the weight of the vessel. The Center of Buoyancy
(CB), i.e. the geometric center of the displaced water is an important metric in calculating the
trim and heel of the ship. The CB can also be split up into the three axes of the vessel.
3.1.4
Trim
The trim of a ship is the difference between the draught of the bow and the aft. Equal draughts
means the trim is 0, thus the ship is at an even keel.
Trim Lever = ∆(LCG −LCB)
(3.12)
The ship lies horizontally in the longitudinal plane when the trim lever is equal to zero. Finding
the differences between the LCG and the longitudinal center of Buoyancy (LCB) tells whether
the ship is leaning towards the aft or bow, with a positive value meaning the ship is leaning
towards the bow.
3.1.5
Heel
If the TCG of the ship does not lie on the LCL of the ship, the ship will assume a heel towards
the side where the TCG lies. This may not pose an issue during sailing for small degrees of heel,
but larger values will compromise ship stability, especially during rough seas and/or high winds
and therefore should be kept within specified limits.
3.2
Shear forces and bending moment
While a ship floats, the upthrust supplied by the buoyancy is equal to the total downward force
of gravity acting on the mass of the vessel. This equality is true for the forces as a whole, but
the displacement and weight of the ship are not equally distributed throughout the length of
the ship. This creates load differences, as some areas of the ship will have a surplus of weight,
while others will experience an excess of buoyancy. These differences in forces will manifest as
shearing forces, and bending moments in subsections of the ship.

First, the weight curve is drawn for the ship, which is done by plotting the weight distribution
of the ship against its length, split into smaller segments based on the frames of the ship i.e.
subsections of the ship of roughly 0.5-1 meter depending on the ship. For each of the frames,
the combined weight is divided by the length of the frame, giving the average weight per length
unit of the frame, denoted in tons. The buoyancy curve is then drawn by plotting the buoyancy
of the ship, against the length of the ship that is in the waterline. The average buoyancy is then
calculated per frame, also denoted in tons.
The load curve is drawn as the difference between the weight and buoyancy curves. This details
where there are large imbalances, whether it’s negative due to excess weight, or positive due to
a larger upthrust for the frame.
The gravitational force acting upon the weight of the ship and the buoyancy counteracting this,
creates a shearing force through the ship. This acts upon the hull of the ship, parallel to the
cross-section of the hull, along the vertical axis. The shear force is found by taking the integral
of the load curve, resulting in a positive shear force acting in a downward direction. The ship
has defined limits for what shear forces can be withstood throughout the length of the ship
before shear failures in the hull occur.
The bending moment is the sum of moments about the section to either of the sides. It is found
by taking the integral of the curve for the shear force up until the given section. Hogging of
the keel is then a result of a positive bending moment while sagging of the keel is the result of
a negative bending moment. To counteract misaligned shear forces and the resulting bending
moment, the weights on the vessel must be equally dispersed. This can be done with the loading
of ballast water and strategically placing cargo.

3.3
Ballast water
To ensure the stability of a RoRo ship, proper dispersed loading of cargo might not be enough.
As a certain weight is needed for large ships to be seaworthy, the weight of cargo going between
two ports might not meet the weight requirements of the given ship. Also, the weight might be
distributed in an inappropriate way which creates excessive shear forces and bending moments,
or instability. To remedy this, vessels are equipped with ballast tanks. The ship draws water
from the ocean to fill the tanks which will, in turn, affect the gravitational moment exerted on
the ship dependent on the tank’s placement.

Chapter 4:
Problem formulation
The purpose of this thesis is to create a holistic model for solving the RSSP. The motivation
to pursue this problem is given by the current high demand for optimisation of RoRo stowage
planning and the sparse amount of established research and papers on the subject compared to
container shipping. To do this, prior findings in the literature must be included and expanded
upon to fit the RSSP defined in this thesis.
Previous literature has often simplified the problem and solved only elements of the combined
problem. The concrete problems handled in this thesis are as follows: The model should be
able to create a stowage plan for a RoRo ship with multiple decks and for a complete route
that contains multiple port calls. All cargo on the booking list has a revenue associated with
carrying them and they can be classified as either mandatory or optional cargo. Mandatory
cargo must be placed on the ship and complete its journey to constitute a feasible solution. All
cargo should be placed such that the amount of shifting needed on the route is minimised. The
model should be able to do this while keeping the ship seaworthy and minimising the amount of
ballast water required to do so. The seaworthiness of the ship in this thesis is defined as being
within the LCG, TCG and VCG limits for the ship as defined in the theory section, and by all
sections of the ship being within their shear force and bending moment limits. Finally the total
weight limit for each deck must not be exceeded on any journey. Thus, the overall objective is
to create a complete stowage plan for a whole route while maintaining the stated constraints
and maximising the revenue from the route.
To achieve this objective, the problem can be observed as three sub-objectives. The first sub-
objective is to maximise the revenue from the cargo through eﬀicient utilisation of the stowage
area. The second sub-objective is to minimise the need for cargo to be shifted after it has been
initially placed on the ship. The third sub-objective is to minimise the amount of ballast water
required to maintain the ship’s seaworthiness. This is done through optimal placement of cargo
as well as utilising the ballast tanks eﬀiciently, thus minimising the cost of carrying the excess
ballast weight.
By combining these three sub-objectives into a unified model, it is the goal
that it will produce feasible and useful stowage solutions reliably, which require minimal human
interaction.
In general, optimisation with multiple objectives can be hard as they will often be in contra-
diction with each other. In the case of the RSSP an example is that maximising revenue may
lead to higher shifting and an increase in the volume of ballast water needed. This highlights
the requirement to balance the objectives correctly and make the best trade-offs between sub-
objectives. In addition, the balancing of the problem requires the sub-objectives to have the
same units such that they can be weighted correctly.
Prior research shows that solving the individual sub-objectives in conjunction can be hard with
a MIP solver and they have therefore typically resorted to using heuristics to solve the problems
[4, 5]. The proposed solution method for the RSSP in this thesis will therefore utilise heuristics
to create solutions for the combined problem.

Chapter 4. Problem formulation
The combined problem has many aspects to it and is thus very complex to model in a completely
realistic manner [4]. Therefore, creating a representation of the problem necessitates multiple
assumptions.
• Ballast tanks are assumed to be cubic. This ensures that stability calculations can be
made linearly based on the filling ratio of the tanks.
• The free surface effect is ignored with regard to the ballast tanks. The free surface effect
states that liquids have a tendency to slosh within their container which creates a dis-
placement to the side of a tank [15]. This can potentially cause ships to capsize if not
handled correctly, but requires modelling of fluid dynamics which is out of scope for this
thesis and as such is ignored.
• The trim and heel of the ship are both assumed to be zero in the calculations of shear
force and bending moment, since it would introduce more complexity and require buoyancy
values for each combination of heel, trim and displacement.
• No real cargo booking list have been supplied, fictional booking lists have been created to
test the solution methods. The problem is only solved for when the ship in question is at
port. This means that stability is calculated with the assumption that the ship is located
in still water with no outside forces.
• The stability of the ship not accounted for during loading and unloading as it is assumed
that the loading personnel ensures the seaworthiness of the ship throughout this process.
• It is assumed that cargo can not be rotated, i.e. placed perpendicular to the direction of
the ship, as this is not typically done when loading RoRo ships.
This concludes the primary assumptions taken. The limitations that these assumptions introduce
and ways to mitigate them will be detailed in chapter 7.
To summarise, the primary objective of the problem is maximising revenue from the given
booking list, while minimising shifting and ballast water and ensuring the following constraints
are met.
• All mandatory cargo must be loaded onto the ship, ensuring compliance with the require-
ments of the stowage plan and the cargo contracts.
• The ship must be maintained within all stability bounds e.g. LCG, TCG and VCG limits
on each journey of the collective route.
• The ships shear and bending values must be within the limits of the ship.
• The total weight of the cargo on a deck must not exceed its allowable limit, as determined
by the ship’s design and capacity.

Chapter 5:
Method
The method chapter will detail the methods and implementation considerations used to solve
the RSSP. First, the way the problem is represented will be described in section 5.1. Then
the methods used in solving the stability part of the ballast water problem will be presented
in section 5.2, then the shear force and bending moment part of the ballast water problem in
section 5.4, and finally the shifting problem in section 5.3. The subproblems for handling ballast
water, shifting cost and shear force and bending moments are presented before the placement
subproblem. In practice, these are solved during or after the execution of the placement problem
as the subproblems are dependent on the placement of cargo. With the methodologies pertaining
to the subproblems in place, the solution methods used to solve the placement problem and in
turn, the problem in its entirety will be detailed from section 5.5 and forward.
5.1
Problem representation
Part of the problem representation is the defined way of showing and memorising where on the
ship a piece of cargo is placed or where it can go. In prior research, three different methods have
been presented: lanes, slots and grids [4, 5, 11]. Lanes and slots are simple in the sense that
they have a predefined area where the cargo can be placed. This enables a quick way to find an
area to place a piece of cargo. These methods are less flexible for different size cargo as the lanes
and slots need to be predefined. The grid representation offers the greatest flexibility in terms of
freedom to place cargo wherever compared to its counterparts. With a grid approach, the size of
the grid cells can be tuned to the size of the cargo, the layout of the ship or a mixture of both to
find the best solutions. This method is computationally harder as it requires checks of multiple
grid cells to place a piece of cargo. This thesis will use the grid representation as flexibility is
preferred to accommodate multiple cargo types with different sizes and it can be more flexible in
terms of placing cargo for optimal shifting and ballast water minimisation. The generated grid is
modelled after measurements of the ships from their corresponding stability booklets. The grid
representation of a ship is a 4-dimensional matrix, with the first two dimensions representing
the rows and columns of the grid, the third dimension representing the decks and the final
dimension representing what port on the route the ship is about to leave from and thereby its
journey number. This is illustrated in fig. 5.1 showing the dimensions and fig. 5.2 showing the
grid representation of a large ship.

The cells of the grid representation can have three different value types placed in them. A ”-1”

this 4-dimensional matrix.
Furthermore, the distances to the reference point of the ship used to calculate the LCG, TCG
and VCG of each cell are calculated and stored in a similar representation, one for each of the
ship’s axes. These matrices only contain 3 dimensions (rows, columns and decks). The port
dimension is not needed in this context, since the distances of each cell does not change going
from port to port, i.e. on a journey.
Lastly, another three-dimensional version of the matrix (rows, columns and decks) is used for
containing static information about the properties of the ship at that grid cell. This matrix
contains information about where the ramps are located on each deck of the ship, where the
entries to those ramps are and where the entry/exit to the ship is located. This is needed to
determine the exact path the cargo needs to take through the entire ship when being loaded or
unloaded.
In addition to the matrices that contain information about the grid cells, multiple data structures
are implemented to handle smaller aspects of the problem. The most noteworthy of them are
presented here. First, two 1-dimensional arrays are used to store information about placed and
unplaced cargo e.g.
cargo ID, cargo type and where it is going.

a 1-dimensional array of structs that stores the location of placed cargo is maintained. This
enables an eﬀicient way to reference and find placed cargo instead of searching through the
entire solution placement matrix. Apart from cargo, the ballast tanks, other storage tanks as
well as shear and bending values are also represented using data structures. The tanks have
a placement with centers of gravity and a capacity along with a volume which is the decision
variable that denotes how much water is in a tank. Shear and bending data structures contain
the limits for shear force and bending moment as well as the values calculated per frame for the
solution in question.
5.2
Ballast water
When solving the ballast water subproblem described in 4.1.3, several considerations must be
taken into account. A challenge in a traditional MIP model would be that the stability calcu-
lations are non-linear. This can be mitigated by solving the problem using heuristics but also
in other ways, such as linearising the stability equations which allows for solving it either itera-
tively or with a MIP. In this case, each tank has an LCG, TCG and VCG value associated with
it, providing information about the tank’s placement on the ship. As with cargo, the center of
gravity multiplied by the weight of a tank provides the added moment from that tank as seen in
eq. 3.1. When calculating the vertical moment of the tanks, the vertical center is approximated
by linearly scaling the VCG with the weight. This is an assumption which introduces errors,
especially in the case of double-bottomed tanks. This will be further explored in the Discussion.
To solve the part of the RSSP that pertains to ballast water, three methods were created
and tested. First, an iterative algorithm that uses linearised stability equations. The stability

5.3
Shifting cost
Another key part of the RSSP is to minimise the number of shifts that need to occur at each
port. In an optimal scenario, this means a piece of cargo only needs to be moved when it enters
and leaves the ship. This problem can be viewed as a graph problem and be solved by finding
the cheapest path in and out of the vessel for all pieces of cargo. This can be solved by modelling
the representation of the ship as a directed graph G(V, E) where each vertex V is a grid cell and
each edge E is a connection between grid cells. A grid cell will be connected to the cell above,
below, left and right. The weights between these vertices will be zero by default thus making
it free to use the edge. The edge weight will increase when a piece of cargo is on the vertex,
as moving through the vertex will now require the placed cargo to be shifted. With this graph
constructed, Dijkstra’s shortest path algorithm [20] can be used to find the cheapest path from
the exit vertex of the vessel to all pieces of cargo in a given port. The sum of all cargo that
has to move in order to let other cargo pass through is then the total shifting cost and will be
the objective to minimise. This extends what Hansen et al. (2020) has detailed for single deck
shifting [5] by expanding it to a multi-deck problem.

5.3. Shifting cost
This method is chosen as it gives the most flexibility at the cost of computational time since it
is necessary to calculate the shortest path for all cargo every time a piece of cargo is placed or
removed. Special cases where multiple pieces of cargo are placed or removed at the same time
are an exception. An alternative method would be to precompute the shortest path from the
exit to all vertices, thus always having the same path to each vertex. The shifting cost could
then be calculated by finding the number of cargoes that need to be moved on that path. This
method would be faster but inflexible as the shortest path cannot change, i.e. making cargo
unable to use a path, which goes around another piece of cargo even if there is space to do so.
The method consists of two steps. First, an initial graph representation of the ships grid cells
is constructed. Second, in each iteration step, this graph is updated when a piece of cargo if
placed or removed and the cheapest path for each cargo is calculated.
5.3.1
Initial graph construction
The initial graph construction starts by initialising a directed graph with N vertices where N
is the number of grid cells in the representation of the ship. The vertices are then connected to
their neighbours if they are not blocked i.e. if the neighbouring cells do not contain ”-1” they are
connected. Lastly, entry locations of the up and down ramps between the decks are connected.
Each vertex of the graph has a vertex id that is stored in a matrix i, j, d to enable referencing
between the grid and the graph. An example of the is illustrated in figure 5.3. This is the base
graph, thus having all vertices connected, but with no edge weights as no cargo has been placed
yet. A grid representation is presented next to a corresponding graph representation for the
small ship in fig. 5.4.

Movement in the graph
Although cargo can span multiple grid cells, all movement is defined by the top left corner of
the cargo as opposed to the bottom left approach from the mathematical model. Functionally
this yields the same results. Defining the move from one corner, simplifies movement as a whole
and allows for finding the shortest path from the exit to a given cell. Moving the cargo through
the graph then requires checking not only if the top left corner can pass through, but also the
rest of the grids that the cargo spans. An example of this can be seen in fig. 5.5.

This means that a piece of cargo with dimensions width W, and length L can use the vertex
i, j, d to pass through if the space from i, j to i + W −1, j + L −1 is empty. In turn, since
a cargo’s movement is measured from its top left corner, this means that cargo with a width
larger than the width of one grid cell can not stand in or move in the bottommost row of grid
cells, effectively removing one row per cargo width.

5.3.3
Delta graph construction
The delta graphs are duplicated from the initial graph C×J times where C is the number of cargo
types and J is the number of journeys. This is because the edge weights on the graph will differ
for each type of cargo and the cargo placed will be different for each journey that the vessel is on.
All graphs need to be updated when a piece of cargo is placed. The edges that need to be updated
will differ depending on which cargo graph is considered. A piece of cargo will affect the edges
of a graph differently as it will be compared to the cargo type that the graph represents. Let’s
consider a piece of cargo c placed at (i, j) to (i + cw, j + cl) where cw and cl is cargo width and
length measured in grid cells respectively. Then the area wherein that cargo needs to be shifted
if another piece of cargo ct needs to pass through will be (i −ct,w, j −ct,l) to (i + cw, j + cl) for
all t types of cargo. This means that the cost of the edges going into this area will be increased
if the cargo is placed and decreased if the cargo is removed. An example is illustrated in fig. 5.6
where two different cargo types are considered of 2 × 2 and 3 × 3 grid cells respectively.
Figure 5.6: An illustration of the cheapest path for different cargo types. It can be seen that
the larger cargo to move has a longer path to the exit around the same placed cargo due to its

5.3.4
Cheapest path
After each step of a cargo placement, Dijkstra’s algorithm [20] is used to calculate the cheapest
path from the exit vertex to all vertices on the ship. Then the individual shifting cost for each
piece of cargo is found by selecting the shifting cost for the top left vertex that the cargo occupies
in its corresponding graph. The total shifting for that journey is found by checking all paths
and ensuring that each piece of cargo that needs to be shifted is only counted once. This is done
for all journeys on a route.

5.4. Shear force and bending moment
5.4
Shear force and bending moment
Shear force and bending moment are included in the model as constraints. They are an addition
to the realistic modelling of the ship, ensuring that the structural limits of the ship is maintained
for a given solution. Shear force and bending moment have been implemented as per eqs. 4.24
and 4.26 in the mathematical formulation. The shear force is a measure of forces acting on the
hull of the ship and is the difference between the displacement of the ship and the buoyancy
that the water exerts on the ship. This is measured per frame, which is a small section of the
ship, typically 0.5 to 1 meter, detailed in the stability booklets.
The weight is found as a sum of the lightship weight, cargo weight and the different tank weights
in the frame. The weight is distributed across the frames, dependent on its location. If a weight
spans multiple frames, which is the case with most if not all ballast tanks and pieces of cargo,
the weight is assumed homogeneously distributed across the area it occupies. The weights are
split across the frames they span, dependent on the percentage of the size of the item which is
within the frame.
The buoyancy per frame is dependent on the ship’s geometry and displacement as per eq. 3.11.
The ships geometry was not available, and thus the buoyancy per frame is found in a buoyancy
table. The buoyancy for a specific displacement was found by extracting several buoyancy tables
from the loading computer, each at a different displacement value. The buoyancy tables were
extracted with different displacement values between the minimum and maximum allowable
weight of the ships. Given a displacement between two of such values, the buoyancy per frame
was then linearly interpolated to fit the displacement of the solution in question, as a linear
relation is assumed. All the extracted buoyancy tables were made with the ship at an even
heel and trim. Solutions with a non-zero heel and/or trim are therefore error-prone, since the
buoyancy is then distributed along the hull under the wrong trim and heel values. The impact
of this will be further explored in the Discussion.
The shear force of a frame is found as the difference between the cumulative sum of the weight
and the cumulative sum of buoyancy of all frames up until the given frame as described in eq.
4.24. The bending moment is calculated from the shear force by taking the integral over the
shear force from the aft perpendicular in each frame, following the calculations of the loading
computer. Since the shear force is calculated under the assumption of even trim and heel, the
inaccuracies introduced by this are present in the bending moment as well. Both shear force
and bending moment are constrained by a set of limits for each. These limits are specific to the
vessel in question, with an upper and lower limit for each frame. When both shear force and
bending moment are within those limits, the constraints hold and the solution is feasible with
respect to shear force and bending moment.
As the method for calculating shear and bending has been described, all of the sub-problems
have been fully accounted for. In the following sections, the initial solution and consequently
the metaheuristics used to solve the RSSP will be detailed.
5.5
Solving the RSSP
With the sub-problems in place, the methods for solving the main part of the RSSP can be
presented. The metaheuristic frameworks used are the Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search
(ALNS), TABU Search as well as the Greedy Randomised Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP).
Both the ALNS and TABU Search are metaheuristics that extend on a Local Search framework.

When performing a local search it is often prudent to create an initial solution such that the
search heuristics has a starting point instead of having to build a solution from scratch [17].
This can be done, either by creating a random initial solution or using a greedy construction
heuristic. The GRASP is one such metaheuristic and is used to create initial solutions for the
ALNS and TABU Search.
The initial solution can be created in different ways e.g. randomly, greedily or through the
Greedy Randomised Adaptive Search Procedure (GRASP). In this thesis, two methods have
been tried: a sorted greedy solution and a GRASP. The GRASP yields better results and is
thus the only one discussed here.
5.5.1
GRASP Initial solution
Implementation of a GRASP inspired by Hansen et al. (2020) [5] has shown promising results.
The version implemented, is described in algorithm 1. The initial solution is created within a
given time limit and always starts from an empty ship and then attempts to construct several
solutions, where the solution with the best objective value is used. The first step of the algorithm
is to find all empty grid cells. For each grid cell it is checked whether it is empty. If it is empty,
the cargo types that are placeable are determined by checking if placing the cargo type in the
empty cell, would intersect with other pieces of cargo or the walls of the ship. With the placeable
cargo types determined, the temporal dimension of the cell is checked, recording which journeys
have empty space originating from the cell for each cargo type. All of the currently unplaced
cargo can then be filtered based on the placeable cargo types and journeys wherein the area is
empty. If there is any unplaced cargo that matches these conditions a restricted candidate list
(RCL) can be created from five fitness measures: revenue, mandatory, stability, center distance
and shifting. These are found for each of the filtered cargo.
The revenue fitness is the revenue that a piece of cargo generates from being placed on the ship.
The mandatory fitness value is one if the cargo is mandatory, otherwise zero. The stability fitness
is approximated by trying to correct for the weight on the opposite sides of the ship. This is
done by finding the mirrored cells in both the transverse and longitudinal direction as well as
the corresponding cell in the inverse quadrant. An illustration of this can be seen in fig. 5.7.
Figure 5.7: An illustration of a ship with four marked grid cells. The red cell marks the cell in
question whereas the three in white are the mirrored cells in all directions.
In each of the cells, the average weight of the cargo placed in that cell is calculated. This average
is then subtracted from the weight that each placeable cargo will put on the selected grid cell.
Lastly, the absolute value of this is taken. If this value is close to zero it means that the weights
of the four cells are close to each other and thus should have an approximately equal impact on
the stability of the ship.

The center distance fitness evaluates the Euclidean distance from a grid cell to the center of
the ship. The weight of the cargo placed in the grid cell is multiplied by the distance such that
heavier cargo is penalised more in the overall fitness function than lighter cargo. The goal of
this is to place heavier cargo closer to the center of the ship, which should create more stable
solutions.
Lastly, the shifting fitness is approximated. The cargo already placed in all of the neighbouring
cells of the potential piece of cargo is determined (top, bottom, left and right). If the neighbours
are being loaded or unloaded at the same ports as the cargo, it will have a higher shifting fitness
value. This is done because cargo that has the same entry and destination ports will not require
each other to be shifted.
These five fitness measures are then normalised between 0 and 1 and added together to a total
fitness evaluation. This is done to give the fitness measures an equal impact. The unplaced
cargo can then be sorted by the total fitness and K best will be added to the RCL. A random
piece of cargo from the RCL is then added to the solution. K is set to five for the heuristic.
Finally, once all the empty cells have been checked and cargo has been placed onto the ship,
a local search is made of the immediate neighbourhood of its solution space. This is done by
swapping two random pieces of cargo and evaluating whether the solution is improved by the
swap. The swap function randomly draws a piece of cargo currently placed on the ship, it then
randomly draws a similar piece of cargo from the entire list of all cargo placed or not. A similar
piece is in this instance defined as the same cargo type with the same loading port and unloading
port. This means that swaps can both introduce new pieces of cargo from the list of unplaced
cargo and swap cargo already on the ship around. When a swap makes the solution objective
better and the solution remains feasible, the swap is saved and used as the solution for further
swap attempts. The whole algorithm is then:

5.5.2
ALNS
The Adaptive Large Neighbourhood Search (ALNS) metaheuristic is an extension of the Large
Neighbourhood Search (LNS) heuristic which in turn extends the Local Search (LS) heuristic.
Neighbourhood heuristics explore the solution space around a given solution by a neighbour-
hood function, all solutions reachable with this function then constitutes the neighbourhood.
Depending on the size of the neighbourhood, the neighbourhood heuristic searches through the
neighbouring area and determines the best solution among them. When the area is small it often
approximates a rapid descend/ascend algorithm and tends to get stuck in a local minimum or
maximum. This can be alleviated by increasing the size of the neighbourhood to theoretically
span several peaks or valleys in the solution space as well as equipping it with tools to move
between them and not get stuck.

5.5.5
ALNS constructors
In this section, the different constructors used in the ALNS will be described.
Shifting constructor
This constructor attempts to insert cargo into the ship in locations where they will cause minimal
shifting to occur. The heuristic finds every empty space on the ship large enough to fit some
or all of the currently unplaced cargo. For each empty space, each unplaced cargo is attempted
inserted one by one and the shifting cost of inserting the cargo is evaluated. Finally, the piece
of cargo which added the least amount of shifting to the solution is inserted. This is calculated
for each empty space or until K pieces of cargo have been placed. This limitation of K cargo
is placed due to the heavy computation of doing an exhaustive insertion using this method.
Therefore the ALNS GRA constructor is executed after the shifting constructor to ensure the
ship has been filled. A K value of 25 is used on all instances in this thesis.

ALNS GRA
The ALNS GRA constructor is a slightly modified version of the GRASP heuristic used to make
the initial solution of the cargo placement. The difference for the ALNS version is that the
heuristic also terminates if there is no unplaced cargo left to place, i.e. line 2 of algorithm 1
is changed to ”while S∅̸= ∅and list of unplaced cargo ̸= ∅do”. Also, the Search Procedure,
normally executed in the GRASP is omitted when the method is used as a constructor, hence the
name, GRA. This constructor is included since the initial solution produced promising results
and might prove useful when iterating through the solution space.
Neighbour constructor
The neighbour constructor works from the principle that by grouping cargo which has similar
loading and unloading ports together, shifting costs can be mitigated. This hypothesis is based
on the fact that cargo with the same loading or unloading port will not cause shifting to occur
between each other. In practice this is achieved by iterating over the empty cells which can
contain a piece of cargo. Then for each piece of unplaced cargo which can be placed in the
empty space, the number of similar cargoes on all four sides of the empty space are counted. The
candidate unplaced cargo which has the potential highest count of similar cargoes surrounding
it is then inserted into the solution. The count scores higher when neighbouring cargoes have
the same loading and unloading port compared to only one of the two. It will insert cargo until
there is no available cargo left.
Longest journey constructor
This constructor attempts to place cargo with very long journeys spanning multiple port calls
into locations on the ship, which are very far away from the entry/exit of the ship. The hypothesis
behind this is that if a piece of cargo with a long journey is placed close to the entry/exit of the
ship it has a higher chance of having to be shifted during its journey since it might be blocking
critical grid cells. In practice, this is achieved by utilising the already generated graph used for
calculating the shifting cost of the solution. The shortest paths to all grid cells are found and
the paths are sorted according to how far away they are from the entry/exit of the ship. The
list of unplaced cargoes is also sorted according to the length of their journeys (unloading port
- loading port) and each cargo in that order is then placed in the first available location in the
sorted grid cell list.
Shortest journey constructor
The Shortest journey constructor works similarly to the Longest journey constructor, but it
attempts to place the cargoes with the shortest journeys as close to the entry/exit of the ship
as possible, the idea being that cargo travelling only a single journey will not need to be shifted
for other pieces of cargo, but might cause a lot of shifting itself if placed far into the ship.
Ballast constructor
One of the goals for the developed model is to place cargo on the vessel strategically to reduce
the amount of ballast water needed to ensure that the stability constraints of the ship are met.
The constructor determines what gravitational dimension and on what journey it differs the
most from the stability limits according to the current solution. The constructor then attempts
to place all of the heaviest currently unplaced cargo to the side which needs more weight to
stabilise the ship while also placing lighter cargo on the opposite side. The stability of the ship
is calculated while ignoring the weight of the ballast water currently in the solution. This way
the cargo can be placed in such a way as to minimise the amount of ballast water needed.

ALNS destructors

Random N cargo destructor
A simple destructor, which removes N random pieces of cargo currently placed on the ship. This
destructor is used to ensure some random movement in the solution space. N is fixed to 25 for
all instances.

Port entry destructor
This destructor draws a port number at random from the range: {1...Nports −1} and removes all
cargo from the solution which enters the ship at the drawn port. The last port is not included
in the drawing pool, since no cargo will be loaded at the final port call.
Port destination destructor
The Port destination destructor works the same as the Port entry destructor, but it removes
all cargo which has a randomly drawn destination port. The range of ports to draw from is
2...Nports, since no cargo has its destination as the first port of the route.
Route destructor
This destructor is a combination of the Port entry destructor and the Port destination destructor
as it first draws a random entry port εport in the range {1...Nports −1} and then a destination
port in the range {εport +1...Nports} and removes all pieces of cargo from the ship which has the
drawn entry and destination ports.
Shifting destructor
This destructor locates the N pieces of cargo, which have the most shifting required to place in
the current solution and removes them from the solution. This is a simple way to remove some
of the cargo causing shifting from the solution, such that the cargo can hopefully be placed in
a more optimal location. N is set to 25 for all instances.
Path destructor
This destructor, as the Shifting destructor, locates the single piece of cargo on the ship which
requires the most shifting overall to place in its current location. It uses Dijkstra’s algorithm to
find the cargo’s path through the ship on both entry and exit. Then the destructor removes every
piece of cargo from the solution which causes shifting for the cargo. This destructor motivates
the ALNS model to make solutions where there is a clear path for cargo to take through the
ship.
Deck destructor
This is a very simple destructor as it randomly picks a deck of the ship and empties it completely.
This can help make large-scale changes to the solution space.
Neighbour destructor
This destructor looks at the immediate neighbours of all pieces of cargo currently placed on the
ship. For each piece of cargo, the destructor counts the number of its neighbours which have
different loading and/or unloading ports from the piece of cargo. The destructor then removes K
neighbours from the solution. This should enable the placement of cargo with the same loading
and unloading port as the cargo in question which in turn should group cargo with the same
journeys. This aids in minimising shifting costs. K is set to 2 for all instances.
Empty space destructor
This destructor attempts to minimise empty space created between placed cargo, which is not
large enough to fit other cargo. The destructor, as the neighbour destructor, looks at all currently
placed cargo and examines each piece of cargo’s immediate neighbours and counts the number
of empty grid cells next to it. It then removes N pieces of cargo which have the most empty grid
cells in its neighbourhood. The hypothesis is that cargo with a lot of empty space surrounding
it is not necessarily placed optimally and its placement could be improved. Some constructors
might make solutions which leave behind a lot of suboptimal empty space between cargo, which
this destructor might help resolve. N is set to 25 for all instances.
Ballast destructor
This works in the same way as the Ballast constructor as it removes heavy cargo from the side of
the vessel, which is currently the furthest from the stability limits and removes light cargo from
the opposing side. The stability calculations are made while ignoring the ballast water currently
placed in the solution. This way the destructor will focus only on how the placed cargo affects

5.5.7
TABU search
In this section, the developed TABU search for the RSSP will be described. TABU search is a
metaheuristic optimisation technique that improves on a local search method by implementing
memory in the search. Concretely, this is done by placing a neighbouring move on a TABU list
after a neighbourhood is evaluated. The candidates on the TABU list cannot be altered until
they have left the TABU list. This means that the TABU search avoids cycling and facilitates
exploration of the solution space [7].
In this implementation of the TABU search for the RSSP, the neighbouring step will reuse the
GRA part of the GRASP presented earlier. The neighbourhood step is implemented by a remove
and a placement step. The removal step will remove the K worst pieces of cargo. The worst
pieces of cargo are found by utilising the fitness function from the GRASP taking the worst
fitness values. The placement step will try to place any unplaced cargo in the best possible
manner using the fitness function from the GRA to determine the best fitness values of cargo.
Each time a piece of cargo is removed it is placed on the TABU list thus forcing the search to
explore more of the solution space. A piece of cargo can removed if present in the TABU list.
After each removal and placement step, the shifting cost and ballast water are evaluated. If this
new solution is better than the current solution the current solution is overwritten with the new
solution.

5.5.8
Feasibility
As the last step of every iteration in every heuristic, the feasibility of the found solution is
verified. To ensure seaworthiness the stability constraints as well as shear force and bending
moment limits have to be accounted for, as described in 5.2 and 5.4. The weight limits for
the decks are checked as per eq. 4.6 in the mathematical formulation, where the weight of all
cargo on the deck is summed, and made sure to be beneath weight limits. The last feasibility
constraint is then purely contractual as opposed to the physical limitations, as it is checked
whether all mandatory cargo is placed. Given that these four feasibility checks are met, the
solution as a whole is deemed feasible.
5.6
General metaheuristic flow
To sum up the method section and the heuristic design, the sub-problems are combined into a
holistic model. The overall design can be summed up in two loops as seen in fig. 5.8. In the
first loop, an initial solution is found as outlined in section 5.5, that is hopefully feasible, as this
improves the solutions of both the TABU search as well as the ALNS. Feasibility is not ensured,
as this loop terminates after a given time limit is met. If no feasible solution is found, the next
loop will start with an empty solution.
The second loop is the main part of the model. This loop has multiple steps, first using the
given heuristic to place cargo, as detailed in the corresponding sections above, 5.5.2 and 5.5.7.
After placing the cargo, the second step is optimising the ballast tanks such that the ship is
stable and uses a minimal amount of ballast water, as described in section 5.2. The final step in
each iteration is then to check the feasibility of the solution as per the feasibility section, 5.5.8,
as well as calculate the combined objective of the three sub-objectives as they are defined in the
mathematical formulation 4.1, 4.7 and 4.16 into eq. 5.20. The solution is then saved if it has
the highest-scoring objective while still being feasible. The second loop also terminates after a
given time limit, returning the solution with the highest objective.

6.1
Test instances
The instances are defined by two main categories: ship and cargo parameters. The ship param-
eters define the ship size, deck sizes, entrance locations, ballast tank sizes and location, the dead
weight location of the ship, and the stability limits of the ship. Three different ships have been
used to test the models: A small ship with 1891 lane meters and three decks, a medium ship
with 4076 lane meters and four decks, and a large ship with 6695 lane meters and five decks. For
each of these vessels, all relevant information has been extracted from their respective stability
booklets. All ships have been modelled with a grid size of 1m × 1m.
The cargo parameters consists of the types and amount of cargo that can to be included in the
stowage plan. Each cargo type has a known width, length and height, and a varying weight.

can also be distributed in different ways on the routes, such that more cargo is loaded and
unloaded at some parts of the route. Finally there is a ratio for the amount of mandatory cargo

6.2.6
Qualitative analysis
One thing is to compare the different heuristics on their objective values as they represent the
best solutions for the heuristics. Another thing is to actually evaluate them in a realistic setting.
To do this, meetings with a software developer working with DFDS have been conducted. This
yielded several insights on the operational process of stowage planing for RoRo ships.
The current process for creating a stowage plan starts when the ship is at port and is ready to be
stowed. A planner will begin by designating spots for cargo in the booking list which is already
at port with crew loading the pieces of cargo simultaneously. This is done in dialogue with the
captain and crew such that large pieces of cargo is placed sensibly. Ballast water is drawn during
the loading process, but until a certain amount of cargo is loaded, the total amount of ballast
water needed cannot be determined. Often, only 50% of the cargo to be stowed will be present
at port when the stowage process is initialised. This creates a level of uncertainty in all parts
of the planning process as sensible placement, potential shifting and ballast water loading will
be hard to estimate before having the full booking list. In the following, several stowage plans
created using the ALNS will be presented and the placement of the cargo will be evaluated in a
qualitative manner.

Sensitivity analysis
Based on the results obtained, a sensitivity analysis of the ALNS heuristic can be conducted
to evaluate its behaviour under alternate conditions. This analysis will examine the effect of
increasing or decreasing the impact of shifting and ballast. Furthermore a detailed investigation
into the stability and ballast aspect of the problem will be undertaken, including an analysis
of the error margins on the calculated stability, and the performance of the different methods
for solving the ballast tanks. This sensitivity analysis will provide insight into the heuristic’s
performance with varying parameters, which is especially important given the uncertain nature
of the input parameters and their impact on the objective function. All tests in this analysis
have been conducted with 8-10 replications instead of five as its been tested on three instances,
and a more precise average is desired.

7.1
Reusing GRASP
The GRASP framework plays a key part in both the ALNS and TABU Search, the main heuris-
tic methods in this thesis, by providing an initial solution as well as by reusing the GRASP
either as a constructor in the ALNS or as a local search heuristic in the TABU search. It is
therefore relevant to discuss its advantages and disadvantages as it is a method with a large
impact on the presented results.
In both cases the Search Procedure (SP) from the GRASP has been omitted, since only adding
and removing cargo has been the goal.
The ALNS and TABU search also employ different
search procedures apart from the GRASP, and running a local search within those frameworks
would be computationally ineﬀicient, why it is omitted. Also, the SP from the GRASP is not
well suited for the general problem because of the structure of the data instances. Many of the
instances contain mixed cargo, either trucks and cars or all types, including bulldozers, paper
rolls and metro wagons. The SP from the GRASP tries to remove a piece of cargo from the
solution and add a similar piece of cargo from the list of unplaced cargoes. When the cargo
types are mixed, the amount of cargo that can be placed in an area similar to a placed piece
of cargo becomes limited. Only cars will fit inside a space where there has been placed a car
already, severely restricting the SP’s ability to swap cargoes. The search procedure could be
extended such that swaps could be made between cargoes of different sizes, if space allows for it.
This would potentially extend the search-space, but requires an advanced swap method. This
could be an interesting addition of future research. As the implemented version of the GRASP
can only swap similar cargo, it does perform well when there is only a single type of cargo, since
the SP can be fully utilised. This further emphasises why the SP from the GRASP is not used
in the ALNS or TABU as most data instances contain several types of cargo.
The GRASP method is highly dependent on the constructed fitness functions, which weight
what cargo to place in into the empty cell currently being examined. These fitness measures
are implemented with equal weighting for this thesis. It could interesting to analyse whether
other weightings could be more beneficial to the heuristic. Furthermore the assumptions that
the measures are based upon could be examined in greater detail i.e. the central placement of
heavy cargo and the mirroring approach to minimise ballast water. This could be interesting
as the fitness measures are utilised in all three heuristics and thus has a large impact on the
performance of every model. An indication of this is that the GRA version of the heuristic used
as a constructor in the ALNS is consistently one of the best performing constructors, exemplified
in fig. 6.6.

7.2. Grid size
7.2
Grid size
Throughout the project, realism has been a central goal of modelling, both in creating heuristics,
solutions and data instances. While this has been the goal, several impediments have presented
themselves. As no real booking lists have been shared, the created cargo is based on assumptions
and expert knowledge from sources in and around the RoRoGreen project, leading to the data
instances described in the Computational study. While many booking lists only contain trucks,
data instances with larger cargo were created to test the heuristics eﬀicacy in placing cargo
that is larger than a truck. This supports the decision to use grid cells in the representation
of solutions, such that it is not confined to slots or lanes. While slots and lanes are generally
used to place cargo the size of trucks, there are ways that would enable one to place larger and
smaller pieces of cargo with those representations. Slots could be made to be the size of the
largest possible piece of cargo with x pieces of smaller cargo fitting into a slot. They could also
be of variable size dependent on the cargo by having several representations of the deck space as
already developed by Puisa et al. (2018) [9], though this would increase complexity dependent
on the amount of different cargoes, as well as potentially create wasted space on the decks.
This is potentially not hard to implement in the framework of this thesis as multiple graphs
are already implemented to solve the shifting aspect of the problem. With lanes some of the
same points apply, in that lanes could be widened to fit the largest piece of cargo, or x smaller
pieces of cargo, though again, this would likely create wasted space. Variable lane width is also
a possibility which also increases complexity. As flexibility was desired, the grid representation
has been the chosen representation of the vessels.
To further accommodate specific types of cargo, the grid size could be been varied, e.g. working
with grids of dimensionality 0.5m×0.5m to better fit cars with dimensions such as 4.5m×2.5m.
The grid dimensions 1m × 1m create wasted space in this regard, but smaller grids also increase
the need for computational resources significantly. There is also the consideration to be made
that cargo needs space in between them to safely complete their journey undamaged by adjacent
cargo. This is in part mitigated by the grid representation in the sense that cargo can only take
up entire grid cells. Thus, cargo with a width and a length in which the quotient between cargo
width/length and grid width/length is not zero would result in automatic spacing between
cargoes. This is however not foolproof as cargo which fits perfectly within grid cells do not have
that space in between them. This could have been mitigated by assuming cargo to be slightly
wider and longer than it actually is. Another limitation in the representation is the support
beams on the decks. These have not been added to the grid representation and as such there are
certain grid cells that are blocked on the vessel though not in the grid representation. This is
a human error in extracting data and should be mitigated in further work. This should not be
hard to implement in the current representation. Lastly, from a practical perspective on loading
of cargo using a grid representation, it would be diﬀicult for the planner and crew to execute a
plan that is done 0.5 meters of granularity.

Ballast tanks and the free surface effect
The ballast water problem is one of the central subproblems of the RSSP. The loading configu-
ration is crucial to a ships seaworthiness as it affects stability but also impacts fuel consumption
and by extension travel time. To model the minimisation of the ballast volume and thus implic-
itly minimise the fuel consumption, the ballast tanks have undergone a simplification in order
to effectively estimate the forces that they exert on the vessel. In the models, all tanks are
assumed to be cubic such that longitudinal, transverse and vertical moment can be assumed to
scale linearly. In reality, some tanks are cubic or very close to, but others are not. Especially
double bottomed tanks are very far from cubic with a shape that often have either concave or

the free surface effect impacts the stability of a ship in moving water, it can also impact the

water it can still heel or have a trim which is not zero. In both cases, the water in a partially

compartmentalise the tanks into smaller subsections using baffles. Finally if one were to try to
mitigate the free surface effect using only existing tanks, one solution would be to force the model
to only fill tanks to a certain low level of fill or to ensure that they are almost full or completely
full as a low degree of fill minimises sloshing up to a point, which is also the case for a high

7.4
Stability, shear forces and bending moment
Along with disregarding the free surface effect, several other assumptions have been taken
throughout the thesis.
First, heel and trim are assumed to be zero at all times.
Second,
this thesis deals with optimising on the operational level and thus the stability calculations are
only assumed to be accurate for a ship in port and not at sea. Calculating stability at sea is
beyond the scope of what is needed to provide feasible stowage plans for this version of the RSSP.
In close link with the stability of the vessel is the shear forces and bending moment that affect
the vessel. While these were approximated fairly accurately, the assumption that both heel
and trim is always zero affect the calculation of the shear forces and bending moment. Since
heel and trim is assumed to be zero, the buoyancy of the vessel was extracted from the loading
computer at heel and trim zero. Ideally, the geometry of the vessels would have been available
such that the precise buoyancy for all positions and displacements of a vessel could have been
calculated to fit the values of the current solution. Alternatively the buoyancy could have been
extracted for different values of heel and trim as well as displacement and interpolated between
trim, heel and displacement such that exact buoyancy for the current solution could be found.
Both methods would be significantly more complex than the method implemented in this thesis.
Trim and heel are not guaranteed to always be exactly zero in feasible solutions, as the stability
parameters have a span that is feasible and thus a solution can be accepted even though its
centers of gravity are not perfectly centered on the vessel. The center of buoyancy would due
to the assumptions always lie at the same position, and thus the calculated buoyancy per frame
would always be the same, no matter the actual heel or trim values. Thus the calculated shear
force and consequently the bending moment will be different with an error margin compared to
the actual values. Therefore, when evaluating the shear force and bending moment of a solution,
both when infeasible and feasible, the shear force and bending moment calculated by the loading
computer is likely to be slightly different from the shear force and bending moment presented
by the model. While this is undesirable, there are methods in place to ensure that the model
is unlikely to break the actual shear and bending constraints, such as deck weight constraints
and constructor/destructor methods that gather cargo together to avoid large sections of empty
space. These error margins cause an excess bending moment. The bending moment in the
calculations would then be higher than the actual values, thus effectively working as an extra
safety margin, at the cost of reaching infeasibility sooner than actually needed.
The shear force and bending moment constraints are largely missing from the computational
study. Through the implicit knowledge knowledge gained from testing and individual runs, it
was observed that the shear forces and bending moments constraints were feasible whenever
the stability constraints were met. Thus they provided less interesting observations and along
with the fact that these calculations are somewhat error-prone, they were withheld from further
study in this thesis. With more time it would be interesting to dive deeper into these constraints
and how they are effected. Ultimately specific constructors and destructors for the ALNS or a
specific fitness measure for the GRASP and GRA could be formulated, which attempt to target
these constraints.

7.5. Cheapest path
7.5
Cheapest path
The other key part of the RSSP described in this thesis is the minimisation of the shifting at
each port. The method to find the cheapest paths through the ship and calculating the shifting
works under many assumptions. First, it is assumed that the cargo moves in accordance with the
grid, thus the realistic movements of the cargo is not taken into consideration as they traverses
a path. Thus, the method might find a cheapest path that it impossible to traverse in real life.
The implications of this problem is not assessed in this thesis and the potential magnitude of the
problem unknown, but should be possible to figure out in collaboration with planners and crew.
The method implemented to calculate shifting cost is computationally heavy for several reasons.
In each iteration it is required that cargo graphs for each cargo type should be updated to fit the
grid representation accordingly and find the cheapest paths. In addition, the method does not
reuse any of the calculated paths and thus it will always recalculate all cheapest paths from the
entrance of the ship. The concrete computational impacts of this is not directly quantified in
this thesis, but code profiling shows that the heuristic does spend a significant amount of time
finding the cheapest paths. Lastly, the total shifting cost need to computed after the cheapest
paths are found to ensure that a piece of cargo is only counted once. In summary, the shifting
cost problem is solved but spends a long time computing and works with several assumptions
which could be mitigated in further studies.
7.6
Objective function and further work
In the search for good solutions, an objective that covers all the areas of the problem that needs
evaluating must be created. In the case of the RSSP with stability, ballast water, shifting and
revenue, the objective quickly becomes an equation with several terms that each affect the total
objective differently. While the problem is a maximisation problem, the terms of the objective
function affects the objective in both a positive and negative direction. The problem maximises
revenue while trying to minimise shifting costs and ballast water costs. Each of these terms
must contribute to the objective using the same unit of measurement, which in this case is an
arbitrary weight. It was not possible to obtain realistic price points for cargo revenue, shifting
cost and ballast water and thus the objective is a sum of arbitrary weights for each objective
term. These weights were created to our best ability, but are not a representation of the real
world.
The first thing that should be done in a further study should be to compare the solutions created
by the heuristic with the solution from a real planner. This would give valuable information
about whether the solution is feasible in a realistic setting. Working with a planner would illu-
minate the gaps between theory and practice, and could guide the focus of future work. Further,
if realistic pricing data on revenue, shifting and ballast water could be obtained it would enable
a more practical aspect of the RSSP as the objective would be closer to a real life scenario.
With such information questions like: Is it worth it to allow shifting of N cargoes in order to
place cargo x? could be answered as well as enabling economic analysis on the results of the
model. This would also require the inclusion of realistic time scales, because the time spent on
each journey is needed to get accurate ballast cost and the loading/unloading time would need
to be included to get accurate shifting cost. This would create an overview of potential areas
where money could be saved should the model provide better solutions, as well as areas wherein
to tune the model, should it perform worse than the status quo. Another way of comparing the
models to a real life scenario would be to have a stowage planner create an initial solution for
the metaheuristics to optimise instead of using the GRASP. This would present an interesting
case as it would be both a realistic booking list and a test in whether the metaheuristics are
capable of improving upon these solutions to e.g. minimise the shifting cost or amount of ballast

The ballast water sub-problem is a key part of this thesis. The results show that the NLP
method for solving the ballast tanks is superior to the other methods. When the solution from
the NLP was compared against a loading computer it showed that sometimes there were still
parameters not included in the models presented, which were deemed unfeasible, e.g. notably
trim, heel and propeller percentage immersion. These calculations are non-linear, but could in
theory be included in the NLP presented and thus be accounted for. Further, the error margin
of the heuristic compared to the loading computer could be quantified. With this data it could
be decided if it is worth to pursue more precise stability calculations e.g. having trim and heel
not equal to 0 or not assume that ballast tanks are cubic.
The shifting part of the problem does have some restrictive assumptions that might be a problem
in a real life setting. If these assumptions could be tested and quantified it would be possible
to decide if the next iterations of the algorithm should incorporate them. A concrete example
would be whether to model realistic movement of a cargo. Investigation other ways of implement-
ing the cheapest path could also be of interest as it is a computationally heavy part of the model.
Lastly, in a real life setting the RSSP problem has a high degree of stochasticity. This is due
to the booking list being only 50% completed when loading of the ship begins. The heuristic
implemented here does not account for that since it assumes that the booking list is complete
when generating a stowage plan. The heuristic could be extended such that it could generate a
stowage plan in multiple phases and account for this. The concrete implementation of this would
be straight forward as it would only require the heuristic to be able to lock cargo in a given
stowage plan such that it cannot be moved. This extension could then be tested to evaluate if
it models the realistic setting in a reasonable way. If this inclusion yield promising results, a
stowage planner could work with the heuristic in a iterative manner to produce stowage plans.
During the development of the heuristics multiple biases have been introduced. One of these
are that the development of the heuristics was primarily completed on the small ship. Therefore
much of the implicit knowledge obtained during this thesis is more representative of the small
ship and its dimensions. A concrete example is how the parameters K and N in the different
constructors and destructors are set to fit the small ship in the ALNS. These should probably
have been tuned to fit the larger instances as well.
In conclusion several different issues and assumptions present in the modelling of the RSSP have
been discussed. There are many interesting possible solutions to these issues, while some still
pose a significant challenge. Though given the complexity of the overall problem there is still
great potential in applying heuristics to solve a realistic combined RSSP.

Chapter 8:
Conclusion
The work of this thesis extends on the prior literature regarding RoRo stowage optimisation,
tackling a combination of aspects previously handled individually. Continuing the metaheuristic
approach from prior literature, ALNS, TABU search and GRASP approaches have been imple-
mented. A comparison has been on generated instances made across three types of ships, with
varying parameters regarding the size of the booking list, amount of port calls on a route, the
distribution of the booking list across ports as well as the ratio between mandatory and optional
cargo. The GRASP proved good at constructing initial solutions as shown in previous literature,
but struggled with optimising ballast water and shifting cost. The TABU search proved better
than the GRASP, producing solutions that on average were better and with a higher chance
of being feasible. Ultimately the ALNS was found as the approach producing the best results.
A sensitivity analysis of the relevant ALNS parameters showed that the heuristic adapted to
changes in cost of the different sub-problems, proving that given realistic costs, the model could
maximise the real revenue of the instance.
The thesis introduced shear force and bending moment limits to the RoRo stowage literature
through extension of the mathematical models and implemented them as part of the metaheuris-
tics. The academic gain of introducing the shear force and bending moment was reduced by
the fact, that the buoyancy calculations were flawed when assuming heel and trim to be equal
to zero at all times. As a result of this, the impact of the placement problem on shear forces
and bending moment was less interesting and therefore not analysed in depth. In future works,
ensuring correct calculations of the distribution of buoyancy on the ship could prove interesting
grounds for research, e.g. specific constructors and destructors for a an ALNS could be devised.
These could precisely target the feasibility constraints imposed on the problem.
In conclusion, there are multiple steps taken to show how the complete RSSP can be solved
using a grid representation of the problem. The ballast water problem, which helps ensure a
stable ship, can be solved using non-linear solvers and the shifting problem for multiple decks
can be solved using a graph approach, utilising Dijkstra’s shortest path algorithm. Furthermore
initial steps were taken to show how the shear forces and bending moment aspect of the prob-
lem can be solved in conjunction with the other sub-problems. The ALNS was proven to be a
good candidate heuristic for solving the RSSP as it targets the individual sub-problems using a
curated combination of constructors and destructors. This, in combination with having access
to a good greedy constructive heuristic, the GRASP, makes for a good combined metaheuristic
for solving the RSSP.
The thesis contributes to the existing RoRo stowage planning literature in a meaningful way.
Although not guaranteeing feasible solutions for all instances, and given a set of assumptions
that simplify the complex reality, the thesis proves that using a metaheuristic approach allows
for solving realistic instances of the RSSP with multiple decks and port calls to a satisfactory
level and in a timely fashion, while ensuring stability requirements are met.
There are still many aspects which need to be implemented, and considerations to be made, in
order to fully enable commercialisation of such an approach in a realistic and safe manner. The
stability, shear force and bending moment requirements need to be upheld for a ship sailing on
