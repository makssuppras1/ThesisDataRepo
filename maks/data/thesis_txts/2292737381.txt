Abstract 
The morphology of filamentous organisms is a crucial factor in submerged cultivations, as certain 
phenotypes can directly impact process productivity. Determining the optimal phenotype for the 
production of a given product, however, can prove quite a challenge, as morphology can be influenced by 
an array of variables – such as agitation, pH, media  and inoculum size and type – and in turn influence 
environmental factors, e.g. viscosity and rheology. As such, the construction of a system capable of 
effectively quantifying the morphology of an organism is absolutely vital in order to achieve optimal protein 
production. 
A semi-automated image analysis procedure was developed, allowing the characterization of filamentous 
growth forms. The physiology and morphology of an Aspergillus nidulans strain, expressing a glucoamlyase-
red fluorescent protein (mRFP) fusion, as well as an overexpressed rabD gene (encoding a small GTPase 
involved in vesicle trafficking from the Golgi to the plasma membrane), was characterized in submerged 
batch cultivation, in order to elucidate the potential correlation between increased protein secretion and 
morphology. mRFP was used as an in vivo reporter of protein secretion, while the Tet-On system was used 
for activation of the rabD gene, induced by the presence of the antibiotic doxycycline. The image analysis 
procedure was utilized to measure the total hyphal area, total hyphal length, number of tips per hyphal 
structure and the hyphal growth unit (HGU). Samples for morphological analysis were extracted from all 
batch cultivations at three time points, deemed to be equivalent to early-, mid- and late exponential 
growth, respectively. 
The morphology and physiology of two recombinant strains of A. nidulans were characterized in submerged 
batch cultivation, in the presence and absence of doxycycline. Both strains exhibited a dispersed hyphal 
growth form, when grown in glucose and complex medium. The NID1602 strain, containing an 
overexpressed rabD gene, exhibited a significant increase in the amount of secreted glucoamylase under 
the effects of doxycycline. Compared to the NID1439 strain, which served as a reference, the amount of 
glucoamylase secreted by NID1602 was slightly higher. Induction of the rabD gene in NID1602 also resulted 
in a lower HGU value, than in the absence of doxycycline, indicating an increased branching density. 
However, the lowest HGU values were obtained by the NID1439 strain, showing no clear correlation 
between branching frequency and protein secretion, under the conditions presented in this study.

1. Introduction 
1.1 Background 
The use of filamentous fungi in industrial processes has become steadily more common in recent years, in 
part due to the constantly growing number of advances in e.g. genetic engineering strategies, expression 
systems and omics tools, as well as their inherent metabolic versatility and potential for efficient protein 
secretion1–3. Some filamentous fungi, most notably those of the Aspergillus genus, have already found their 
place in the industry as efficient producers of organic acids, enzymes, proteins and pharmaceuticals1,3, 
examples of which can be found in Table 1. However, filamentous fungi still fall short in some areas, 
compared to already well-established yeast- and bacterial production platforms, due to their inability to 
consistently express and fold recombinant proteins of non-fungal origin1–4. These issues can possibly be 
attributed to the fact that most of our current knowledge of the fungal protein secretion pathway is based 
on models developed in yeasts and higher eukaryotes5. Although it is generally accepted that the fungal 
secretory pathway does not differ greatly from that of yeast and higher eukaryotes, some significant 
differences still exist, such as the fungal method of apical secretion, as well as the mycelial growth 
phenotype5. However, recent advances in the construction of genetic engineering toolkits, as well as the 
elucidation of greater parts of the fungal protein secretion pathway still, have greatly expanded the 
opportunities for the construction and application of recombinant filamentous fungi in commercial 
processes1–3. For instance, the insertion of machinery encoding multiple genes, involved in a new or 
improved biosynthetic pathway, could prove to be more efficient than “traditional” gene targeting, which 
typically displays a rather low success rate, due to a low frequency of homologous recombination1,3. 
In order to fully utilize the protein secretion machinery of filamentous fungi, the study of fungal 
morphology, in both submerged- and solid-state cultivations, has proven to be crucial, as certain 
phenotypes display a direct influence on process productivity6. The morphology of a given organism can be 
said to be the direct result of the genotype of the organism, combined with the impact of various 
environmental factors, such as process variables in a cultivation; e.g. agitation, pH and media composition 
and other factors, such as inoculum size and type (vegetative, spores, etc.)3,6. Furthermore, fungal 
morphology has been found to have a direct impact on the rheology and viscosity of the cultivation 
medium, thus greatly influencing the mixing and mass transfer of the cultivation3,7. Many filamentous fungi 
are known to exhibit a wide range of morphological forms, ranging from dense pellets to freely dispersed 
mycelium; the former resulting in the lowest productivity, likely due to oxygen transfer limitations7–9. 
Studies have shown that the addition of inorganic micro particles, such as talc or aluminium oxide, to the 
culture medium can greatly decrease pellet formation, thus increasing productivity of the desired product,

possibly due to the increased presence of hyphal tips8,10. Furthermore, several studies, based on the 
characterization of morphological mutants, have reported a direct correlation between branch density, or 
number of active tips, and the efficiency of protein secretion11–13. These recent advances in the 
customization of fungal morphology help to underline the importance of gaining a better understanding of 
fungal morphogenesis and the link between morphology and protein secretion.

1.2 Production of recombinant proteins in filamentous fungi 
In order to fully understand and overcome the obstacles involved in the optimization of protein secretion in 
filamentous fungi, the protein secretion machinery must first be understood. The following section will give 
a general overview of the most important steps involved in the protein secretion in filamentous fungi.  
A schematic overview of the protein secretion pathway in filamentous fungi can be seen in Figure 1. 
1.2.1 
The protein secretion machinery 
The first step of the fungal secretory pathway is initiated by the translocation of a secretory protein into the 
lumen of the endoplasmatic reticulum (ER)5. This translocation can follow one of two pathways, depending 
on the hydrophobicity and amino acid composition of translated signal peptide: the signal-recognition 
particle (SRP)-dependent pathway or the SRP-independent pathway5,19. Once the nascent protein has 
entered the ER, it is subject to various modifications, such as glycosylation, disulfide bridge formation, 
phosphorylation and subunit assembly, facilitated by molecular chaperones and foldases3,5,20. The purpose 
of these modifications is for the protein to fold and mature into its native form. The accumulation of 
misfolded proteins, however, triggers the transcriptional up-regulation of several genes encoding ER 
chaperone and foldases – a mechanism known as the unfolded protein response (UPR)21. Misfolded 
proteins are subsequently either retained in the ER, to be subject to modifications by molecular 
chaperones, or directed towards the ER-associated protein degradation (ERAD) pathway, which serves to 
retrotranslocate misfolded secretory proteins to the cytoplasmic ER, for ubiquitination and, ultimately, 
degradation within the proteasome5,19,22,23.  
Following folding the ER, proteins are directed towards the Golgi apparatus, in which further modifications, 
such as glycosylation and peptide processing, take place3,5,20. The transport to the Golgi is facilitated by 
COPII-coated transport vesicles and SNARE proteins19,24. Improperly folded or glycosylated proteins are 
directed towards the proteasome or vacuoles for degradation19,24. Subsequent to modification in the Golgi, 
SNARE proteins mediate vesicles trafficking of secretory proteins towards the Spitzenkörper (SPK), which 
serves as a vesicle supply center (VSC) – an organizing center to which vesicles accumulate and migrate 
from, in random directions towards the apical cell surface25–27. Following their arrival at the SPK, secretory 
proteins are transported, and fused, to the plasma membrane at the hyphal tip, a process known as 
exocytosis, and are subsequently secreted19,25,26.

1.2.2 
Improving protein secretion in filamentous fungi 
Historically, filamentous fungi have been unable to compete with well-established prokaryotic- and 
eukaryotic hosts, when it comes to the production of recombinant proteins. This can be attributed to the 
low frequencies of transformation obtained in filamentous fungi, as well as potential morphological defects 
and protein modifications due to high protease activity3, the latter resulting in post-secretory degradation 
of valuable products3,18. Furthermore, their protein glycosylation patterns are vastly different from those 
found in mammalian cells, due to a lack of terminal sialyation of glycans, which can result in the protein 
being produced in an inactive/non-functional form3,4. 
In order to improve the protein secretion capabilities of a fungal host, investigation of intracellular protein 
transport can be of interest. The Rab family of proteins consists of various GTPases (chiefly known as Rab in 
A. nidulans, Srg in A. niger and Ypt or Sec in yeast)5,28, which regulate an abundance of steps involved in 
vesicular membrane traffic in both the exo- and endocytic pathways; such as vesicle formation, vesicle 
transport and membrane fusion26,29,30. A number of Rab GTPases are known to be essential, whilst others 
result in significantly stunted protein secretion, if inactivated5,19,28. The A. nidulans rabD (SEC4 in yeast or 
srgA in A. niger, A. fumigatus and also A. nidulans)31,32 is a small GTPase involved in the vesicle trafficking 
from the Golgi to the cell surface, and has been found to be essential in S. cerevisiae5,28,31–33. The Rab family 
of proteins could prove to be interesting gene targets for improved protein secretion. 
==PAGE:13==
6 | P a g e  
 
When attempting to improve the recombinant protein production ability of a filamentous fungal host, 
various limitations may be encountered. Increased flux of proteins through the ER will typically cause ER 
stress, which results in incorrect or delayed folding and misassembly of proteins20, triggering the UPR and 
ERAD-pathway. An approach to counter this response was investigated in A. niger, where several putative 
genes involved in the ERAD pathway were deleted23. Deletion of the derA gene in a strain containing a 
glucoamylase-glucoronidase (GlaGus) fusion resulted in a 6-fold increase in the intracellular amount of 
GlaGus protein. This strategy could potentially be coupled with the overexpression of genes related to 
protein transport, effectively increasing protein flux and export, whilst minimizing ER stress and 
intracellular protein accumulation. 
Manipulation of genes related to morphology could also be of interest, due to the direct link between the 
phenotype of an organism and process productivity. However, unraveling the relationship between 
morphology and productivity still poses a challenge, as indicated by the number of genes involved in 
morphogenesis in A. nidulans alone, which has been estimated to be around 200031. In spite of this, 
progress is being made, and some morphological mutants have been observed to yield increased product. A 
morphological mutant of an α-amylase-producing A. oryzae strain, exhibiting a more densely branched 
mycelium, was found to be more efficient in the production of α-amylase, compared to the wild-type34. 
Comparison of the morphological mutant with a recombinant strain revealed a significantly higher 
production of α-amylase in the morphological mutant, suggesting that the increased branching, resulting in 
a larger number of tips, positively influenced the rate of protein secretion. A different study observed a 
correlation between degree of branching and culture viscosity, with cultures of highly branched A. oryzae 
mutants exhibiting lower viscosity than their respective parental strains13. Furthermore, an increased 
production of α-amylase was observed in one of the recombinant strains, again suggesting a direct link 
between morphology and protein secretion.

1.3 Fungal growth and morphology 
1.3.1 
Taxonomy of fungi 
The term fungus encompasses a large and diverse group of eukaryotic microorganisms, which are typically 
divided into three major groups: multicellular filamentous molds, macroscopic filamentous fungi and single 
celled microscopic yeasts. Traditionally, fungi were classified as members of the vegetable kingdom, due to 
various similarities, such as lack of mobility, mode of nutrition (absorption of nutrients), reproduction by 
spores and general morphological similarities35. Today, however, fungi are classified as an independent 
kingdom, separate from plants, animals, protists and bacteria, and are also found to belong to the group 
Opisthokonta, which includes both the animal and fungus kingdoms36. This radically different classification 
can be attributed to the elucidation of fungal reproduction, as well as their chemoheterotrophic lifestyle 
(the metabolisation of organic compounds in order to gain energy and carbon needed for growth). In 
contrast, green plants are phototrophic autotrophs, meaning that they utilize photosynthesis to produce 
complex organic compounds from simple inorganic molecules. 
Although fungi share their heterotrophic mode of nutrition with animals, the processes involved are fairly 
different. For instance, while animals typically absorb nutrients via ingestion, fungi secrete enzymes in 
order to facilitate extracellular digestion of nutrients, followed by absorption of solubilized breakdown 
products35.  This degradation of nutrients is absolutely vital for the fungus, as larger, more complex 
molecules (such as polysaccharides and proteins) are unable to pass through the fungal cell wall and must 
first be broken down into smaller molecules. Small molecules (simple sugars, amino acids), however, are 
readily absorbed directly across the cell wall and plasma membrane. 
In addition to being heterotrophs, fungi can be subdivided into three additional groups: 1) saprotrophs 
obtain nutrients from nonliving organic matter; 2) biotrophs (e.g. mutualists, obligate parasites, symbionts) 
obtain their nutrients from a living host, without damaging host cells; and 3) necrotrophs (e.g. parasites), 
which infect a living host and kill host cells in order to obtain nutrients36.

The major fungal phyla have generally been characterized according to the morphology of sexual 
reproductive structures, although asexual reproduction, e.g. via dispersal of spores or spore-containing 
structures, is common and also allows for more rapid dispersal than sexual reproduction. The two largest 
phyla in the fungal kingdom are the Ascomycota and Basidiomycota, which form the sub-kingdom Dikarya 
(also referred to as ‘higher fungi’)38. The defining feature of Ascomycota is the mechanism of sexual 
==PAGE:16==
9 | P a g e  
 
reproduction, which is mediated by the ascus – a sac-like sexual structure in which meiotic ascospores are 
formed39, whereas Basidiomycota generate basidia, which produce meiotic basidiospores40. 
Located in between the Ascomycota and Basidiomycota are the Deuteromycota, or imperfect fungi, which 
are known to solely reproduce asexually. Although the fungi belonging to the Deuteromycota lack a sexual 
reproduction cycle, they can still be classified as either Ascomycota or Basidiomycota, by quantifying 
morphological, physiological and phylogenetic similarities to these phyla. Most fungi in the Aspergillus 
genus have no known sexual state (about one-third of the genus have been found to reproduce sexually37) 
and have thus been found to belong to the Deuteromycota, with further classification placing them within 
the Ascomycota. Aspergilli generally rely on the formation of conidia – nonmotile asexual spores generated 
via mitosis. Some members of the genus, however, can either be homothallic or heterothallic, in regards to 
sexual reproduction, the former meaning they are able to self-fertilize, producing conidia with two similar 
nuclei, with the latter species requiring a partner for sexual cross41,42. Unlike many other Aspergilli, A. 
nidulans is known to exhibit both asexual and homothallic reproduction states, depending on growth 
conditions41. Furthermore, A. nidulans also possesses a parasexual cycle, which exhibits a distinct 
advantage when performing genetic analysis, as the resulting heterokaryons and disploids can be used for 
complementation analysis and dominance tests43. 
 
1.3.2 
Fungal growth 
The life cycle of a filamentous fungus typically begins with the germination of a single spore, which is 
produced from the fruiting bodies of the fungus and remains dormant until nutrients necessary for 
germination present themselves. As soon as the suitable conditions for growth have been reached, the 
spore initiates a swelling process, absorbing water and extending in an isotropic manner44. Subsequently, 
the growth of a tubular filament, known as a hypha, is initiated, which grows by apical extension in a 
polarized manner, with a linear extension rate at the apex of the hypha44. As the vegetative growth 
continues, the hypha repeatedly forms several branches, which constitute a mass of hyphal filaments – a 
so-called mycelium (Figure 3)15,44. Proteins and precursors involved in cell wall synthesis are transported in 
vesicles from the ER, along a polarized cytoskeletal network, to the hyphal tips7,44. Here they accumulate to 
form the SPK, to where vesicles accumulate and from which they perform polarized migration towards the 
apical cell surface, fusing to the plasma membrane and producing new cell wall surface25. The SPK 
determines both the tubular shape of the hypha, as well as the growth directionality and rate7,26,44. 
Elongation of a hyphal element leads to septation and formation of hyphal compartments, as well as 
branching into additional hyphae, as soon as a certain length has been reached. It has been suggested that 
==PAGE:17==
10 | P a g e  
 
branching occurs due to an excessive accumulation of transport vesicles within subapical compartments45, 
however, the exact mechanism is poorly understood6. The average branch length that spawns a new 
branch is also known as the hyphal growth unit (Lhgu or HGU), which is defined as the total hyphal length 
(Lth) divided by the total number of tips (N) on a given hyphal structure6,15. The HGU is a direct 
representation of the average length of a hypha required to support tip growth and provides information 
on the density of hyphal branching, as it increases or decreases as branching continues. Low HGU values  
can indicate a high number of growing tips in the mycelium, which might be desirable for optimized protein 
production, due to the apical secretion mechanism of filamentous fungi.

for growth and starts to germinate, forming a branch filament, known as a hypha. The growth of several hyphae results in the 
formation of a dense network, referred to as a mycelium. 
 
1.3.3 
An overview of the genus Aspergillus 
Aspergillus is a diverse genus of filamentous fungi, consisting of about 339 species46, which contains an 
abundance of well-established expression platforms, commonly utilized for the production of various 
metabolites, such as food ingredients, pharmaceuticals, enzymes, antibiotics, organic acids and so on2,3,46. 
Aspergilli are resilient organisms and, as such, are able to withstand a range of wide conditions, such as 
temperature (6-55°C), pH (2-11), osmolarity (0-34% salt), water activity (0,4-1) and oligotrophic- or 
nutrient-rich conditions2,14,41. They are known to secrete a wide variety of enzymes, allowing them to 
degrade a plethora of different compounds; e.g. starch, (hemi-)cellulose, pectin, xylan and proteins, 
allowing for growth on diverse substrates2. In the industry, however, Aspergilli are mainly renowned for 
their innate high titers of enzymes and acids, such as citric acid (up to 200g/L)14 and glucoamylase 
(30g/L)14,41. 
Although the amount of research being conducted towards the development of Aspergilli  as expression 
hosts pales in comparison to other industrial workhorses (such as Bacillus, Pichia, Saccharomyces, etc.) 
recent advances have still been made, such as the whole genome sequencing of the extensively studied A.

Aspergillus nidulans 
The filamentous fungus A. nidulans (previously characterized as Emericella nidulans, due to its possession 
of a sexual reproduction phase) has long since been considered a model organism, and is perhaps the best 
characterized of the Aspergilli, ever since the publication of the genome in 200342,43. Due to the possession 
of both a sexual and asexual reproduction phase, it is possible to obtain conidiation-deficient mutants,

1.4 Filamentous fungi in submerged cultivation 
When utilizing submerged bioprocesses, the morphology of the production host becomes of critical 
importance, as a morphological form can greatly impact the production levels of the desired product. A. 
nidulans displays two distinct growth forms in submerged cultivation, as it can grow either in dense, 
spherical hyphal aggregates, known as pellets, or as dispersed mycelia (Figure 4)15,49,50. Loose hyphal 
aggregates, referred to as clumps, can also be observed, but do not constitute an actual growth form49. 
Hyphal morphology can vary greatly from being simple linear filaments, to highly branched complex 
structures, which can exhibit vastly different lengths and degrees of branching. Pellets and clumps can also 
vary in size, density and surface structure, depending on growth conditions, even within the same host49. 
 
Figure 4 Growth forms of A. nidulans in submerged culture. a) Dispersed mycelia are often observed early on in the cultivation, 
whilst b) mycelial aggregates form after sufficient germination has been attained. c) Pellet formation is not always observed and is 
thought to be related to growth conditions in the culture, such as agitation, and initial spore concentration6.  
Although the formation of pellets generally seems unfavorable, as the growth form leads to reduced 
oxygen transfer, and the fact that only the ‘external’ hyphae are known to secrete products, pellet 
formation can be necessary for higher yields, depending on the process (e.g. the production of citric acid 
from A. niger)15. Conversely, the dispersed mycelia phenotype, which instinctively should result in the 
highest productivity, displays certain disadvantages as well; such as high viscosity, among others. A brief 
summation of advantages and disadvantages of the two growth forms is listed in Table 2. 
Pellet formation has been proposed to be linearly correlated with initial spore concentration, with higher 
inoculum concentrations resulting in a greater number of smaller pellets6,15. An even greater increase in 
inoculum concentration has even been found to induce a completely dispersed mycelial growth form in A. 
niger6,15. Morphological variation can also be observed to change, depending on inoculum concentration, 
with higher concentrations resulting in increased mean main hyphal length, mean total length and

pellets; higher agitation can lead to shearing of outer regions, resulting in a decrease in pellet size and 
circularity6,15.  
Morphology is also directly impacted by various other factors, such as media composition, especially the 
type and concentration of carbon and nitrogen sources, medium pH, metal ion concentration, polymer or 
surfactant compound concentration, temperature, pressure, medium viscosity, as well as various genetic 
factors that influence cell wall composition and surface properties6,15.

1.5 Morphological quantification of filamentous fungi 
The development of a system for the rapid and unbiased quantification of morphology has proven to be a 
significant challenge; simpler growth forms (unicellular organisms, e.g. bacteria and budding yeast) are 
more readily quantified, as they typically exhibit uniform growth and are only present in a single focal 
plane, whereas filamentous microbes present three-dimensional morphology, complicating microscopic 
characterization6. Although several attempts have been made to develop an efficient image analysis 
system6,12,15,51–53, all of  these methods have required some degree of manual input, in order to provide 
accurate and reproducible data. The need for manual operation, however, is likely to lead to biased results 
as the operator typically handpicks the objects and images for analysis. Conversely, the lack of manual 
input in a system can result in inaccurate data, as the error margin grows significantly higher, considering 
that the algorithms or methods applied can typically be relatively cursory. A schematic overview of a typical 
image analysis system can be seen in Figure 5.

1.6 Strain construction 
This section describes the various techniques that were used in this study for the construction of protein 
overproducing A. nidulans strains, in order to ultimately investigate the correlation between protein 
secretion and morphology. 
1.6.1 
USER® Friendly™ cloning 
In order to enhance the protein production capabilities of A. nidulans, a suitable strategy must first be 
conceived. This could typically consist of the construction of an expression vector, using the uracil-specific 
excision reagent (USER) method, in which a single deoxyurical residue (dU) is excised from either end of a 
PCR amplified DNA fragment. Typically, a so-called shuttle vector is used, which bears the advantage of 
possible propagation in two different host species, allowing for transformation in E. coli, prior to the actual 
recipient strain. A typical shuttle vector can be seen in Figure 6.

nidulans. The vector typically consists of a selectable marker for E. coli transformation, insertion sites, promoter region, a cloning 
site for the insertion of a gene of interest (GOI), terminator region and an auxotrophic selectable marker, allowing selection for 
successful transformants.

Initially, vector-specific overhangs are generated by including a single dU in the 5’ end of each primer used

formed on the digested vector and PCR amplicons anneal together, forming a stable hybridization product

1.7 Aim of study 
The aim of this study was to develop an image analysis procedure, which would minimize user input 
required for morphological analysis and, thus, increase throughput. The image analysis procedure was 
applied to quantify the morphology of two A. nidulans strains, both expressing a glucoamylase::mRFP 
fusion, as a reporter system. Additionally, the NID1602 strain contained an overexpressed rabD gene, 
encoding a small GTPase involved in protein transport from the Golgi to the plasma membrane, which was 
induced in the presence of doxycycline, via the Tet-On system. The physiological characteristics and 
morphology parameters (total hyphal area, total hyphal length and HGU) of the two strains were analyzed 
and compared, in order to elucidate the potential correlation between protein secretion and morphology. 
A strain construction strategy was devised for the construction of three recombinant A. nidulans strains, in 
which three genes, related to protein transport in A. nidulans, were to be overexpressed in NID1602. 
Initially, expression vectors were to be generated via USER® cloning, in which the chosen genes are inserted 
into an expression cassette. Finally, the resulting transformants were to be characterized and 
morphologically quantified in submerged batch cultivation.

2.2 Development of a semi-automated image analysis procedure 
A semi-automated image analysis procedure was developed, using the public domain Java-based image 
processing software ImageJ (US National Institutes of Health), the open-source nature of which provides 
extensive possibilities for the implementation of user-made plugins and macros. Some standard functions 
of ImageJ include image segmentation, area calculations, distance measurement, smoothing, noise 
reduction, filtering, etc. The procedure was developed based on a series of initial shake flask cultivations 
(data not included) in preparation for morphological analysis of batch cultivations. 
First, the procedure seeks to minimize background noise by applying a median filter to the image, which 
removes isolated noise pixels below a certain radius, equal to 1 pixel. Subsequently, an automatic local 
threshold is applied, which converts the image to binary (essentially a black and white image) by defining a 
grayscale cutoff point. Grayscale values below the cutoff are coloured black, whilst those above the cutoff 
are coloured white. The Bernsen thresholding method56 was found to be the most precise for the purpose 
of this experiment. 
Following thresholding, a morphological close operation (a dilation operation coupled with an erosion 
operation) is applied to the image, which serves to smooth objects and fill in small gaps, which were found 
to erroneously segment hyphal structures in some cases.  
Subsequently, the analyze particles command is executed, which automatically envelops the now 
separated hyphal structures in regions on interest (ROI) and measures their respective total area. 
Structures with an area beneath a certain cutoff value are excluded, as they are presumed to be 
background noise. 
Finally, the total hyphal length of hyphal structures is found by executing the skeletonize command, which 
removes pixels from the edges of objects until they are reduced to shapes containing only a single pixel in 
width. The total hyphal length is then found by calculating the total amount of black pixels within a ROI. 
A simplified overview of the procedure can be seen in Figure 9. 
 
==PAGE:29==

Figure 9 A schematic overview of the image analysis procedure developed in this study. 
The image analysis procedure was applied to quantify the morphological data obtained from the batch 
cultivations. A given amount of sample was extracted from all batch cultivations at three time points, 
deemed to be equivalent to early-, mid- and late exponential growth, respectively. In order to preserve a 
certain statistical significance, at least fifty hyphal structures were analyzed in any given sample. Only 
isolated structures were analyzed, i.e. a hyphal structure touching another structure/the edge of the image 
was excluded. Furthermore, mycelial aggregates (clumps) were disregarded, as they do not constitute an 
actual growth form, and only true mycelia were included in the analysis (subject to operator bias). 
Samples were stained with Lactophenol Cotton Blue (LPCB) and stored at 4°C prior to analysis.

2.3 Cultivations 
2.3.1 Preparation of spore inoculum 
Fungal spores were propagated on MM, supplemented with 4mM L-arginine, and incubated for a minimum 
of 5 days at 37°C. Conidia were harvested with either sterile milliQ water or sterile 0,9% NaCl solution (only 
used when prolonged storage was necessary) and filtered through miracloth into a sterile falcon tube. The 
spore concentration was determined via the counting of spores in a Neubauer haemocytometer. All 
cultivations were inoculated to a concentration of 109 spores/mL. 
2.3.2 Shake flask cultivations 
Shake flask cultivations were performed in 0,5L Erlenmeyer flasks, without baffles, equipped with cotton 
stoppers. All cultivations were incubated at 30°C with an agitation of 150rpm. 
2.3.3 Bioreactor cultivations 
Batch cultivations were performed in 2L volume glass Biostat B bioreactors (B. Braun Biotech) with a 
working volume of 1,6L. All bioreactors were mounted with two six-bladed Rushton turbine impellers, pH 
electrode, thermosensor, sparger, sampling outlet and membrane port (for inoculation and addition of 
media supplements). CM was applied for all batch cultivations, the composition of which is listed in Table 4. 
Initial batch medium was sterilized in the bioreactors. Doxycycline was added through a 0,45µm pore size 
sterile filter (cellulose acetate) following sterilization. 
To minimize perturbation of cell growth, an automated procedure (ramp) adjusting process parameters 
was implemented. Initially, aeration was set to 0,1vvm, agitation to 100rpm, pH to 3 and temperature to 
30°C. Subsequently, assuming that spores were well germinated, aeration was increased to 2vvm, agitation 
to 800rpm and pH to 5, over an extended period of time. Temperature was kept constant throughout the 
cultivation. pH values were adjusted with 2M NaOH and 2M H2SO4. 
Cultivations were carried out in either duplicates or triplicates. 
A schematic overview of the bioreactor setup used in this study is found in Appendix C (Bioreactor setup).

2.5 Analysis 
2.5.1 Mass of mycelial dry matter 
Biomass concentrations were determined by initially separating mycelium from a sample of culture broth, 
with a known mass, via filtration with a 0,45µm pore size filter paper. Prior to filtration, filters were dried 
for 24 hours at 100°C and subsequently weighed. The culture sample was filtered by vacuum filtration in a 
Buchner funnel and mycelial residue was washed with deionized H2O. Subsequently, filters were dried at 
100°C for 24 hours and weighed. 
2.5.2 Relative fluorescence measurement of mRFP 
Relative fluorescence activity was measured using a Synergy Mx Monochromator-based Multi-Mode 
Microplate Reader (BioTek), using excitation/emission at a wavelength of 584/607nm. 
A given amount of culture sample was filtered by use of a 0,45 µm syringe filter. Subsequently, 200µl of 
culture filtrate was added in triplicates to a 96-well microtiter plate (PS microplate, Greiner bio-one), from 
which fluorescence was measured. Background fluorescence was corrected by subtraction of values 
obtained from measuring a negative control sample. 
2.5.3 HPLC 
For quantification of glucose, culture samples were filtered through a 0,45µm syringe filter (cellulose 
acetate), subsequently frozen and kept at -20°C. Glucose concentrations were detected and quantified by 
refractive index (RI), using an Aminex HPX-87H cation-exchange column (Biorad), with a mobile phase 
consisting of 5mM H2SO4 at a flow rate of 0,6mL/min and 60°C. The glucose concentrations were 
determined by means of a standard curve with known concentration. 
2.5.4 Microscopy 
Morphological images were taken using a Nikon Eclipse E1000 fluorescence microscope mounted with a 
QImaging Retiga EXi film camera (1392 x 1400 pixels) and standard FITC filters. 
All samples were stained with LPCB prior to analysis.

3. Results and discussion 
This section contains a presentation and discussion of results obtained from the experimental work 
performed in this study. Initially, the validity of the image analysis procedure will be confirmed, followed by 
discussion of the physiological and morphological data obtained from batch cultivation of A. nidulans. 
Finally, the progress of the strain constructions will be documented. 
3.1 Development of a semi-automated image analysis procedure 
In order to swiftly and precisely quantify the morphological data obtained from cultivations, a semi-
automated image analysis procedure was created, using ImageJ. The aim of the procedure was to minimize 
the need for user input, thus decreasing operating time as much as possible. An example of the steps 
involved in the procedure can be seen in Figure 10. 
Initially, a degree of background noise was removed from the image (Figure 10, b), followed by separation 
of foreground from background, via the application of a local threshold (Figure 10, c). Subsequently, objects 
were smoothed and gaps closed, via a morphological close operation (Figure 10, d). At this point, ImageJ 
would output the area measurements of the hyphal objects, which the user was required to extract 
manually. Finally, all hyphal objects were converted to pixel-wide shapes (Figure 10, e), from which the user 
had to manually extract the total hyphal length, which is equal to the sum of all pixels in the given hyphal 
object. 
Number of tips was calculated manually by the user. 
 
Figure 10 Image of a stained mycelium, subject to various types of digital processing: a) Original image with no processing applied. 
b) Application of a median filter, removing isolated noise pixels and smoothing objects. c) Thresholding, according to the Bernsen 
method, separating foreground from background, by defining a grayscale cutoff point. d) Application of a morphological close 
operation, which smoothes objects and fills in small gaps. e) Processing by the morphological skeletonize function, which removes 
pixels from the edges of all objects, effectively reducing them to pixel-wide shapes. 
This method was applied to analyze morphological data (total hyphal area, total hyphal length, tip number 
and hyphal growth unit) obtained from batch cultivations, in order to elucidate the correlation between 
morphology and protein secretion, and will be presented in the following chapters.

3.2.3 Summary of physiological and morphological analysis of A. nidulans 
The physiology and morphology of the NID1439 and NID1602 strains were characterized, in the absence of 
and under the effects of 10µg/mL doxycycline. The strains were cultivated in 10g/L glucose and complex 
medium, in which they displayed a consistently dispersed mycelial growth form. Morphological parameters 
were quantified (total hyphal area, total hyphal length, HGU) and biomass and mRFP values were 
measured. An overview of the most relevant values is listed in Table 15. 
NID1602, when induced by doxycycline, is observed to exhibit the highest maximum RFU value of all the 
cultivations. Surprisingly, however, NID1439 (in the absence of doxycycline) attains the lowest average HGU 
value, showing no clear correlation between branching density and protein secretion. 
Hypothetically, when an increased number of hyphal tips are present in the cultivation, it can be assumed 
that the total area, facilitating protein secretion, is also increased. This may not always be the case, as 
observed by Bocking et al., 199913, who did not consistently obtain increased protein secretion, when 
characterizing mutants with an increased branch frequency. 
Although a greater branching frequency can result in the increased secretion of proteins, it is not a given 
that the increased flux of proteins will ultimately result in increased branching. Lack of an appropriate 
amount of secretion sites could possibly result in the intracellular accumulation of proteins, which will in 
turn cause ER stress, resulting in a lower productivity. To fully enhance the protein secretion capabilities of 
a given strain, highly branched mutants could be developed, in which genes related to protein transport 
and export were overexpressed in conjunction.

4. Conclusion 
The utilization of image analysis systems for the precise quantification of filamentous microbial morphology 
is imperative, as, if performed manually; it becomes an extremely laborious and time consuming task. 
Although recent developments in such systems have reduced operating time, whilst increasing accuracy, 
manual operation is not uncommon. 
A semi-automated image analysis procedure was developed in this study, for the analysis of filamentous 
fungal morphology. The procedure proved to be accurate for morphological quantification, albeit rather 
time consuming, as user input was required for quality control and extraction of data. Furthermore, the 
operator was required to make qualitative assessments, in order to differentiate aggregates from actual 
mycelial growth forms, making the data inherently biased. 
The morphology and physiology of the recombinant A. nidulans NID1439 and NID1602 strains were 
characterized in submerged batch cultivation, in the presence and absence of doxycycline. Both strains 
exhibited a dispersed hyphal growth form, when grown in glucose and complex medium. The NID1602 
strain exhibited a significant increase in the amount of secreted glucoamylase under the effects of 
doxycycline. However, compared to the NID1439 strain, the amount of secreted glucoamylase was only 
slightly higher. Generally, the NID1439 strain (in the absence of doxycycline) exhibited the lowest HGU 
values, showing no clear correlation between branching density and protein secretion. Further research is 
needed to draw a clear conclusion, such as the characterization of highly branched mutant strains, also 
containing overexpressed genes related to protein transport and export. 
The strain construction failed, however, due to time constraints. As such, the correlation between 
morphology and protein transport could not be examined further.

5. Perspectives 
To fully optimize the quantification of filamentous morphology, a fully automated system could be 
developed, which completely minimizes the need for user input. Ideally, this system could consist of an 
image analysis platform (basically a microscope connected to a computer) linked to a flow cell, allowing for 
high throughput analysis of morphology, which is not reliant on human interaction. Images could be 
analyzed periodically, in accordance with a set flow rate, utilizing a set algorithm, depending on the growth 
form in question. 
As the growth forms exhibited by filamentous fungi are highly dependent on environmental factors, 
different media compositions and cultivation parameters should be investigated. The influence of organic 
micro particles, which studies have observed to result in a dispersed mycelial growth form, should be 
analyzed, as well as characterization of the pelleted growth form. 
The overexpression of genes other genes related to protein transport could be attempted, followed by 
morphological analysis. Furthermore, genes directly linked to morphology could be investigated, as the 
induction of genes related to e.g. branching rate might result in increased protein secretion.
