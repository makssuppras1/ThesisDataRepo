Abstract
In this thesis, the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide is developed to assist educators in incorporat-
ing makerspaces and technology comprehension into the school curriculum. The research employs
Blessing and Chakrabarti’s DRM as a Type 5 design project, which involves a thorough examina-
tion of the present curriculum design practices and challenges (Descriptive Study I), the iterative
design and refinement of a solution in collaboration with the educators (Prescriptive Study), and a
limited implementation-based evaluation of the guide in practice (Descriptive Study II). This made
sure that, in practice, all the theoretical insights were translated into practical educational solutions
through a structured and iterative methodology.
RQ1: “What are the current practices, challenges, and needs in curriculum development for Danish
basic education, particularly regarding integrating pedagogy, legal requirements (Fælles Mål), and
technology?” – In this regard, an extensive Descriptive Study I, which includes a systematic litera-
ture review and stakeholder analysis, was conducted to address this question. The study revealed
that current curriculum design processes are fragmented; pedagogical planning, compliance with na-
tional standards, and technology integration are handled separately. Additionally, there is no clear
direction on how to incorporate innovative, hands-on teaching methods like makerspace activities
with the mandatory learning objectives and digital literacy goals.
These findings confirmed the
existence of the need for a unified curriculum design framework and, therefore, directly informed
the requirements for the new guide, thus solving RQ1.
RQ2: “What principles and features should an integrated curriculum design guide include to effec-
tively support educators in aligning pedagogical design with Fælles Mål requirements and strength-
ening digital literacy?” – RQ2 was examined in the Prescriptive Study by creating and developing
the curriculum design guide itself. Based on the gaps revealed in DS1, the research stated core
design principles (such as embedding compliance checklists and pedagogical prompts) and incorpo-
rated these features into the Gnist guide. The result is a teacher friendly, complete template that
closely links creative pedagogical planning with curriculum standards and legal requirements. It
offers a simpler format of presenting standard mapping at each step and has separate categories for
digital literacy and makerspace integration. The guide was co-created with a teacher and vetted for
practical usability by experts. These outcomes provided a concrete solution to the needs revealed
in DS1, thus fulfilling RQ2.
RQ3: “How can the iterative development and implementation of an example course serve to eval-
uate the effectiveness and usability of the developed curriculum design guide, and what indicators
demonstrate its success in improving curriculum development processes?” – In order to answer RQ3,
a pilot Descriptive Study II was conducted through using the guide in developing an example course
with a participating teacher. This iterative trial allowed direct assessment of the guide’s usability
and effectiveness in a real-world setting. The pilot results showed that the guide helped the teacher
a lot in coming up with a good and coherent lesson plan that was in harmony with the required
standards. The teacher also gave positive feedback about the guide, stating that it was complete and
easy to use; in fact, the teacher had a higher conformity to Fælles Mål and was very satisfied with
the planning process. These first indicators are consistent with the idea that the guide enhances the
curriculum development process in practice and provide some evidence for its effectiveness, which
forms the basis for future, more extensive evaluations, thus addressing RQ3.
The effectiveness and applicability of the curriculum design guide are demonstrated through its
implementation and feedback from the pilot course creation. The guide was able to fulfill its role
as a curriculum planning tool which helped the teacher develop very specific yet comprehensive
makerspace infused lessons while also making sure that all important parts of a lesson were included

research methods can be used to create real innovations in educational practice.

Motivation
This study has been conducted as part of an Impact Startup spun out from DTU named GNIST
Education ApS. GNIST Education aims to bring modern fabrication tools into an educational set-
ting, combining a state-of-the-art mobile makerspace concept with a comprehensive design guide for
creating educational courses. GNIST Education’s mobile makerspace aims to allow teachers, schools
and educators to include and use modern fabrication tools in day-to-day classroom activities. The
mobile makerspace will enable teachers to use the fabrication facilities when needed, without schools
needing to purchase expensive equipment and hire personnel for equipment maintenance.
Through a course platform, teachers can browse, read and select courses that are created to aid
teachers in effectively teaching their subject and technology comprehension, taking their students
through a modern design process and critical reflections to equip students with 21st-century skills.
The intention behind the mobile makerspaces and courses is that schools run multiple courses in
parallel, with "production weeks" synchronised between courses to allow temporary installation of
the makerspace with high utilization of the fabrication tools.
This master’s thesis aims to design a curriculum design guide based on relevant pedagogy and
didactics that allows educational content creators to safely and effectively create courses that utilize
the GNIST Education mobile makerspaces as an essential teaching tool and method. The Curriculum
design guide is built on a rigorous theoretical foundation to ensure that effective teaching material is
aligned with modern educational design metrics relevant to the GNIST Education business concept.
As this master’s thesis is developed alongside a business, the research scope and assumptions are
impacted. In full transparency, this master’s thesis does not aim to research whether makerspaces are
effective teaching environments. Instead, it assumes that they are and argues for the best approach
to incorporate them as essential and novel teaching tools and not a gimmick.

Use of Artificial Intelligence
In the interest of transparency, this thesis has employed artificial intelligence (AI) tools at certain
stages of the research and development process. Generative AI tools were used to create a quick and
iterative visualization of the design ideas in the stakeholder co-creation workshops to prototype the
design and get instant visual feedback to discuss in the next meeting. This utilization of AI helped
speed up the process of finding design options while making sure that human creativity and critical
thinking were the primary drivers of the process.
All the AI-generated outputs were applied only as preliminary materials: each output was scrutinized
and then either rewritten or refined to make sure that it was accurate, understandable and relevant
to the project’s goals before being incorporated into the final product. Moreover, the interview with
Engineer the Future included in Appendix C was conducted in Danish and then translated into
English with the help of an AI-based translation tool. The translated text was then checked and
corrected for accuracy and coherence by the author. The AI-assisted contributions in this thesis
were used only as supporting tools of the creative process and were not used as credible outputs
in themselves. The human oversight and the rigorous validation were employed at every stage to
maintain the academic rigor and the quality of the final content.

Introduction
1.1
Overarching Problem
Recently the Danish educational system has placed increasing focus on three key elements: digitali-
sation of learning materials, digital literacy and technical educational approaches. The Scandinavian
countries, including Denmark, stand out for their exceptional student preparation methods by em-
phasising teaching creative ability and teamwork with critical thinking abilities along with communi-
cation skills (Ahl, 2024). The Danish government focuses on digital literacy by making Technology
Comprehension (Teknologiforståelse) an essential elementary school course that melds computa-
tional thinking with digital design alongside design processes to let students develop digital knowl-
edge and values along with technology-related critical thinking abilities (Undervisningsministeriet,
2018). Several educational institutions in Denmark have launched makerspaces which support direct
hands-on instruction with contemporary technological production tools (Kjærgaard & Dau, 2018)
(Dau, Hachmann, & von Sehested, 2021). These efforts lead the way in global trends advocating
that education should move beyond conventional learning to develop flexible, digitally competent
students equipped for a complex, fast paced, technology-rich society.
However, while schools have begun to incorporate new instructional approaches into their curricula,
the integration of contemporary teaching instruments into established curricula remains a complex
process.
Makerspace provides students with an opportunity to develop creativity and problem-
solving skills while coming up with practical solutions and working on projects that are not typical
school assignments (Kjærgaard & Dau, 2018). The activities are outside of the bounds of standard-
ised educational programs. According to Aalborg University, a makerspace does induce learning
enthusiasm but the set up is inconsistent with official standards and examinations (Kjærgaard &
Dau, 2018). Real knowledge acquired in the modern world is completely different from the learning
objectives specified by the official national standards. Teachers are facing a difficult situation because
the official curriculum defines certain tasks, although they require the use of flexible technology-based
projects. The integration of modern tools like robotics kits and 3D printers into educational plans
goes beyond simply incorporating new content because it implies the need to reformulate entire ed-
ucational methods and reallocate time and resources within Danish government regulated education
systems.
One central issue is that the existing curriculum frameworks are limited in the amount of guidance
they provide for implementing technology driven, hands on learning principles in the classroom.
Danish teachers are required to follow the national educational standards (the Fælles Mål common
objectives), but these frameworks are rather vague regarding what the students are to learn, not how
to teach it, and definitely in no innovative ways. In theory, the curriculum should support digital
competence, but it doesn’t guide on how a teacher could use a makerspace project to meet a science
or math standard. The unclear guidelines leave teachers in doubt on how to ensure continuity with
the educational standards when trying out new tools, often with only their intuition and experience
to guide them. Research with pioneer teachers in Denmark reveals several structural barriers: tradi-
tional aspects like summative assessments, rigid and strict teacher roles and a classroom culture not
congruent with open open-intended exploration make it difficult to incorporate innovative, design-
based tasks into subject courses (Van Mechelen, Wagner, Baykal, Charlotte Smith, & Iversen, 2021).
Teachers are concerned that engaging in creative, open ended work may mean neglecting some of
the required content or putting students at a disadvantage with tests and exams. Curriculum poli-
cies and supporting material do not yet fully address this gap between ambition, motivation and
practice – they prescribe goals for digital literacy and creativity but provide little examples and
clear pathways for teachers to navigate safely through these tools and material support within the
context of the required curriculum.

This leaves a significant gap in the curricula landscape which this thesis project seeks to fill: there
is a crying need for a clear, easily adaptable and flexible curriculum design guide that could help
in the development of courses in a way that is consistent with the national policies and standards
in Denmark. The gap remains because teachers do not necessarily need, or even want, encourage-
ment to use technology; what they require are understandable and easily implementable frameworks
and tools that demonstrate how they can incorporate 21st-century learning experiences, such as
makerspace projects and coding activities, into their lesson plans without compromising on the edu-
cational objectives. The Danish context, which sees large scale policy initiatives such as Technology
Comprehension (and partly implementation), could benefit from a curriculum design guide as a key
link between curriculum goals and their implementation in the classroom. In this way, a design
guide could offer some guidance, teaching examples, and development checkpoints to ensure that
the alignment with the educational standards is proper, and thus enable teachers to innovate in
their teaching without having to worry about breaking with the mandated requirements. This the-
sis situates itself within that gap. For the purpose of this thesis, the curriculum design guide has
been designed to strike a balance between encouraging innovation and ensuring compliance so that
the educators can incorporate digital literacy and maker oriented pedagogy into their practice with
confidence and without compromising on the educational values.
1.2
Curriculum Design, Digital Literacy, and Co-Creation
To this challenge, theories of curriculum design and established principles of educational innovation
must be drawn on to address the above challenge. with Theoretical work in curriculum design,
scholars have pointed out that effective educational change is not ad hoc, but systematic develop-
ment processes are required. In addition, Nieveen et al. explained that the purpose of curriculum
design is to turn ideas about desirable learning objectives and content into concrete materials and
strategies that can lead to the intended learning outcomes (Nieveen, van den Akker, & Voogt, 2023).
Importantly, this should result in curriculum artifacts that are coherent and endorsed by stakehold-
ers throughout the educational system (Nieveen et al., 2023). In practice, this means that when new
elements, such as digital fabrication or coding, are introduced, the alignment of various elements of
the curriculum (objectives, content, pedagogy, and assessment) must be ensured to ensure coher-
ence (Nieveen et al., 2023) (Saplan & Wardrip, 2024) (Christensen, Hjorth, Iversen, & Smith, 2019).
Jaap van den Akker and others have argued that curriculum components are highly interconnected
and fragile, and, therefore, changing one element, say, technology literacy, is ineffective unless all
the elements of the curriculum are changed (Van den Akker, Kuiper, Hameyer, & van den Akker,
2003) (D. Evalueringsinstitut, 2019) (L. Andersen, 2020). Consequently, a structured development
process – often iterative and research-informed – is required to truly embed innovations. Educa-
tional design research and design-based research are examples of such models: they facilitate the
analysts to develop the f inal curriculum product through a cycle of analysis, design, evaluation,
and revision such that the product is pedagogically sound and practical for teachers and appropriate
to the context. It can be stated that using such structured approaches can prevent the innovation
from being introduced in isolation or from disappearing after the initial interest. The innovation is
made sustainable in the curriculum, and feedback loops and the input of stakeholders are used to
refine it. This theoretical foundation forms the basis of the research approach, which posits that the
development of a curriculum design guide that is effective and quality assurance compatible must
be accomplished through a guided and principled process.
A significant concept in this thesis is digital literacy, which in the current discourses of educational
institutions has grown to encompass much more than the ability to work with computers. Digital
literacy includes all the abilities required for the critical convergence of digital tools, the production
of digital texts, and the fundamental understanding of the technological processes that define our

world. Ole Sejer Iversen and others have argued that education in the modern world should aim
at computational empowerment which means that students should learn how to create and develop
with the help of technology rather than being passive recipients of it (Dindler, Smith, & Iversen,
2020). This view positions students as producers of technology, thereby equipping them with the
knowledge to code, develop digital projects and discuss the impact of technology in society. The
focus on technology comprehension in schools in Denmark reflects this broad understanding: the
curriculum developed for the Technology Comprehension subject has been developed to integrate
computing, design thinking and societal reflective practices (Undervisningsministeriet, 2018) (for
Undervisning og Kvalitet, 2019). The importance of digital literacy in education is also recognized
internationally as one of the key components of 21st-century skills. It is gradually equated with
the need for students to be civically and workforce-ready, as reading and writing were in the past.
However, as mentioned above, incorporating digital literacy into the curriculum is a daunting task.
Schools have to revise traditional subjects and pedagogical approaches to incorporate these new
literacies into them effectively. Research shows that providing students with the opportunity to
create, build, and collaborate with technology can provide significant positive results: for example,
engaging in digital design literacy tasks has been found to enhance children’s agency and teamwork
(Van Mechelen et al., 2021). However, if such activities are not backed up by a well-thought-out
curriculum, they may either get lost in the process or become contrary to and competitive with
traditional teaching strategies. This thesis proposes that digital literacy should be integrated into
curricula in a purposeful manner to ensure that teachers have a map of how to include digital skills
and knowledge into their teaching plans. Based on the current knowledge of Iversen and other re-
searchers, we intend to create recommendations that can help teachers promote digital literacy in a
way that is consistent with the subject matter rather than in opposition to it.
Another strategy that we embrace is the co-creation of the curriculum design guide, which is in line
with the participatory design approach. In this case, co-creation implies the involvement of various
agents – including teachers and other education professionals – in the development of educational
solutions. According to Sanders and Stappers (Sanders & Stappers, 2008), co-creation is “any kind
of collaborative creativity, that is, any kind of creativity that involves two or more people”; thus, the
involvement of different participants in the design process should produce more creative and realistic
solutions (Sanders, Brandt, & Binder, 2010). In the case of curriculum development, the term co-
creation means that the persons who are going to use the curriculum (such as teachers) are involved
in the development of the curriculum. This participatory approach can help to equalize the process
and enhance the quality of the curriculum materials generated (Nasser, 2024). The co-designed
curricula are less top-down and are based on the practical knowledge and experience of teachers
in the field, rather than being imposed upon them. Research on educational change reveals that
such a participation enhances the uptake and applicability: teachers are more likely to embrace and
maintain a change if they have a part in it, and if the design has taken into consideration the actual
situations in the classroom (Nasser, 2024). There are excellent examples of co-design in education
in Denmark. One case study presents how the outcomes of a participatory design project were used
to introduce the Technology Comprehension course in Danish schools (C Smith, Bossen, Dindler, &
Sejer Iversen, 2020). This shows that co-design can be applied on a large scale to influence policy
and curriculum at the national level. In our research, co-creation is not only a topic of discussion
but also a methodological choice. This way, it’s made sure that the guide is relevant and easily
applicable in the real world and will be useful for the target group. This approach is consistent with
the recent trends in curriculum theory that perceives teachers as the co-designers of the curriculum
who can contribute to the improvement of educational frameworks.

Research Methodology
The overall purpose of this project is to develop a structured curriculum design guide that can help
educators and teachers develop effective and inclusive learning materials. Therefore, the general
methodology will be founded in the Design Research Methodology (DRM), proposed by Lucienne
T. M. Blessing and Amaresh Chakrabarti (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). The design process has
been structured with and will be presented in accordance with the four phases of DRM: Research
Clarification, Descriptive Study 1, Prescriptive Study and Descriptive Study 2.
The Research Clarifications dives into the problem space and sets a goal for the curriculum de-
sign guide. Descriptive Study 1 aims to define the domains and design implications of curriculum
development, theoretical and experiential implications hereof, the typology of makerspaces as edu-
cational environments, teaching tools and methods, the national and international laws, regulations,
and standards, and recommendations for educational material development and use, as well as the
emerging need for digital literacy. All design metrics are explored in the context of the Danish educa-
tional system. The Prescriptive Study aims to combine the design metrics and the knowledge gained
from the study in a form of a curriculum design guide. Due to the fact that the curriculum design
guide is being developed through co-creation and education design research, Descriptive Study 2 is
to review and assess the consolidated design metrics, the flow and structure of the curriculum design
guide, and its content in terms of effectiveness and appropriateness. Therefore, the curricula design
research and co-creation approach is used in an iterative design research and iterative validation
method rather than utilizing a large-scale validation of the final design guide.
Therefore, this research project is classified as Type 5 because the study includes a Comprehensive
Study of the Existing Situation (Comprehensive DS-I) – Understanding the problem space, iden-

The DRM is an implementation-oriented, iterative approach that incorporates theory-based research

suited for developing a curriculum design guide because it allows for iterative refinement during the
design process, and it bridges theory and practice, which is perfect for use in this study that does
not seek to test a static hypothesis but instead to develop, test, and refine a functional curriculum
design guide.

The methodological foundation of this project is important for ensuring validity and applicability in
the development of a structured curriculum design guide that fulfills legal, pedagogical and techno-

2.1.1
An Iterative Approach to Curriculum Design Research
This study also has an iterative development process, where progressive refinement is emphasized,
not a traditional, summative validation. Many traditional curriculum design researches adopted a
linear approach that is, learning frameworks and materials are first developed and then validated
through large scale empirical testing. However, this approach is often ineffective in capturing the
ongoing refinements and the expert driven adaptations which are vital in the development of usable
and contextually appropriate educational tools (Nieveen et al., 2023) (Veldhuis, Xiao, Bekker, &
Markopoulos, 2022).
To this end, an iterative research approach is adopted such that an initial framework is proposed
based on the literature review and best practices in curriculum design. Co-creation is employed as
an embedded evaluation and design mechanism, which allows for the immediate and direct feedback
and design responses. The changes and modifications are done progressively during the course of
the study to ensure that the guide is in sync with the compliance standards, pedagogical principles
and the technological practices (Becker & Jacobsen, 2020) (Nieveen et al., 2023). This way, the need
for the initial large-scale summative validation is negated by the feedback and validation mecha-
nisms that are woven into the research and development process. Research in participatory design
and iterative development frameworks reveal that working closely with domain experts during the
development process can produce better, more polished, and more appropriate outputs (Robertson
& Simonsen, 2012a).
2.1.2
The Role of Co-Creation in Replacing Large-Scale Validation
Another important methodological concern is the decision to practice co-creation with a single ex-
pert, rather than with several stakeholders. While traditional research approaches often focus on
large subject samples to enhance the generality of the findings, research in human centered design and
participatory curriculum development suggests that intensive collaboration with a highly qualified
expert may be equally effective in applied educational settings (Spinuzzi, 2005) (Sanders et al., 2010).
The decision to engage with one rather than several participants was based on the depth of the en-
gagement, which would provide more nuanced and contextualised insights that are more actionable.
Key factors in the decision on stakeholder interaction were efficiency and focus, in which partnering
closely with a single individual enabled controlled and detailed improvements.

This research study’s philosophical foundation lies on the assumptions about the existence of the
world, knowledge of the world, and the process of knowing, which shape the way knowledge is gen-
erated, interpreted and applied. In this study which is the development of a structured curriculum
design guide that is not only pedagogically sound but also compliant with legal and educational
standards and integrated with technology in the area of digital literacy, the research philosophy is in
line with a pragmatist epistemological stance, in conjunction with constructivist and interpretivist
philosophy. The study uses a DRM approach for iterative refinement and design validation, while

Situational Mapping
Method Overview
Situational mapping is used to show and discuss the complex of actors, influences, and factors in
the curriculum development context. Based on the situational analysis techniques developed for
qualitative research (Den Outer, Handley, & Price, 2013), it involves creating ‘maps’ of all the
actors (human and non-human) and how they interact. This mapping provides the major human,
institutional, discursive and material elements in the situation of inquiry, and relationships are
explored. Using this method, the study provides a reflexive, big picture of curriculum design which
goes beyond the single perspective and includes other voices in the system (Den Outer et al., 2013).
The main advantage of situational mapping is that it is comprehensive in approach and therefore
encourages the researcher to think about the possible stakeholders in the domain as well as the
context and silent factors such as policies and technologies that may impact the results. A possible
weakness is that the map’s breadth may be overwhelming and crude, and thus requires careful
categorization to produce useful insights.
Application in This Study
The situational mapping was mainly in the Danish curriculum development context. First, key hu-
man stakeholders were identified for further analysis: teachers, students, school leaders, curriculum
planners, policymakers, parents, and industry or community partners. Each of these groups of stake-
holders has its role to play in the mapping process. For instance, as primary implementers, teachers
may provide practical insights, while policymakers ensure alignment with national goals. Research
highlights the importance of involving all these stakeholders, including those directly involved in
instruction (teachers), in successful curriculum development. Therefore, the map focuses on the
teacher and learner interface, with supporting actors (administrators, parents, experts etc.) and
governing bodies surrounding it. Next, non-human and systemic factors were mapped: the national
curriculum framework (Fælles Mål) (E. D. Evalueringsinstitut, 2012), educational laws and regula-
tions, assessment standards, lobbying mechanisms, technology infrastructure (for example learning
management systems) and broader societal trends. Such non-human elements are included in the
way that material and structural factors are seen as being co-constructed in the situation (Den Outer
et al., 2013) e.g. the Fælles Mål policy documents and EU educational directives that determine
what is taught (and are therefore part of the ‘situation’). Figure 3.1 depicts these relationships,
which show, for instance, how Fælles Mål (national standards) affects teachers’ curriculum plan-
ning, how technology platforms function as a means of translating pedagogical intent into classroom
practice, and how feedback from students and parents informs the refinement of the curriculum.
Findings
The situational map showed a highly interconnected landscape. Pedagogical practices are affected
by national standards and assessment requirements; legal compliance (for instance, fulfilling statu-
tory curriculum content) determines the range of content and methods. New technologies (including
digital learning resources and data protection policies) appeared to be both enablers and constraints
– they opened up new pedagogical possibilities but also came with the need to comply with data pro-
tection regulations. External agencies (municipal education authorities, EU frameworks) also have a
influence, for example by requiring inclusion of 21st century skills or key competences in the curricu-
lum. Interestingly, misalignments were seen as possible: for example, teachers are concerned about
the conflict between the innovative pedagogical strategies and the fixed outcomes of Fælles Mål, or
the conflict between the needs of the small school and the goals of the nation. These realisations

Reference Modeling and Benchmarking
Method Overview
Reference modeling in DRM means constructing an abstracted model of the current understanding
or the best practice exemplar of the phenomenon under study (Marxen, Albers, et al., 2012). In
the RC phase, Blessing and Chakrabarti suggest creating an ‘Initial Reference Model’ of the state
of practice and expectations (Marxen et al., 2012). This model is used as a reference to determine
where the gaps are and where the improvements should be made (Marxen et al., 2012). In our study,
reference modelling consists in comparing the Danish curriculum framework with other national or
international frameworks in order to identify the best practices and match the relevant standards.
In other words, Fælles Mål is the baseline reference model that is used to perform a benchmarking
analysis – a type of comparative education research – to determine what characteristics of successful
curriculum frameworks do others have. Benchmarking is a widely used way of enhancing quality
through the adoption of others’ standards and processes; here it is academically underpinned by the
idea that curricula are a manifestation of a country’s educational objectives and social ambitions
(Opertti, Kang, Magni, et al., 2018), which means that comparing frameworks can reveal varying
emphases and novel configurations.
Application in This Study
Firstly, explaining the concept of Fælles Mål and its structure and main characteristics. Fælles Mål is
the common goals that dictate what students are expected to know and be able to do in each subject,
including competence goals at the general level and specific knowledge and skill objectives by grade

levels (E. D. Evalueringsinstitut, 2012). The 2014 reform of Fælles Mål was consistent with the Dan-
ish Qualifications Framework for Lifelong Learning ((Europaea, 2018)) which is in turn linked to the
European Qualifications Framework ((Institute, 2011)) and distinguished between knowledge, skills
and competences (Institute, 2011). This guarantees that the Danish curricula is in conformity with
the Danish and European standards, which focus on learning results and competences. This align-

Findings
The comparison (Table 3.1) confirmed that Fælles Mål is similar to other European curricula in

effective connection between curriculum objectives and assessment in the English framework to guar-

that enable local adaptation (for example, Finland (Lavonen, 2020)) generally have more willing
teachers and innovative practices, while less flexible frameworks present difficulties in implemen-
tation. This finding is consistent with curriculum theory that a good framework should be clear
and explicit yet at the same time flexible in use (Nieveen et al., 2023). Therefore, our design guide
should mimic the stiffness in regulating and ensuring learning goals and the flexibility in educative
approach of the system. Moreover, the treatment of technology was considered: some frameworks
(for example, Singapore’s) include ICT competence and digital citizenship as curricular areas, which
means that our guide should also pay attention to the integration of educational technology. Thus,
reference modelling compared Fælles Mål with international standards and added information to
the Initial Reference Model based on other countries’ practices. These realizations will assist in
guaranteeing that the prescriptive design is relevant to the local context and in line with interna-

Systematic Literature Review
Method Overview
A systematic review of the literature was conducted to gather empirical evidence and theoretical
perspectives of effective curriculum development practices, challenges and existing solutions. The
SLR was conducted in accordance with the guidelines for rigor and transparency, specifically the
Preferred Reporting Items for Systematic Reviews and Meta-Analyses (PRISMA) approach (Zibani,
Rajkoomar, & Naicker, 2022). PRISMA offers a evidence-based checklist and flow diagram for re-
porting systematic reviews (Zibani et al., 2022), which ensures that the search strategy, inclusion/ex-
clusion criteria, and selection process are well described so as to minimize bias in the selection of the
literature. We employed PRISMA to map the information flow through the review phases: iden-
tification of records, screening (e.g., removal of duplicates and obviously irrelevant hits), eligibility
checks via full text reading, and the final inclusion (Zibani et al., 2022). This approach enhances
the methodological rigor by making the review replicable and exhaustive (that is, covering all the
relevant databases and sources) (Zibani et al., 2022). The strength of gleaned from SLR is that it

can synthesize a large amount of evidence to identify consensus and gaps; the weakness is that it
can be time consuming and may fail to capture very new studies if not updated carefully.
SLR Procedure
In line with RC objectives, a definition of the review questions to include:
1. What common challenges and success factors are identified in the literature on curriculum

• Standardization vs. Flexibility - Research further reveals the conflict between fulfilling the

fulfilment for each pillar, but in the combination and presentation of all three.

practices while also solving some of the current problems.

Co-Creation Process with Stakeholders
Method Overview
Co-creation is a collaborative approach where researchers and stakeholders jointly develop solutions,
leveraging collective creativity (Sanders & Stappers, 2008).
In design research, co-creation (or
co-design) sessions involve end-users or experts from the field in the design process, ensuring the
outcome is grounded in real-world experience and needs. Sanders and Stappers (Sanders & Stappers,
2008) define co-creation as “any act of collective creativity, i.e., creativity that is shared by two or
more people” (Baş & Şentürk, 2019), with co-design being a specific instance applied across the full
design process. The strength of co-creation lies in its participatory nature: it breaks the constraints
of individual perspective and brings in diverse insights (Sanders & Stappers, 2008), often resulting in
more innovative and accepted solutions. In curriculum design, involving educators, administrators,
and students in co-creation can democratize the process and improve relevance (Baş & Şentürk,
2019). However, co-creation must be managed to avoid dominance of particular voices and to ensure
the process remains focused on objectives.
Application in This Study
As a way of ensuring continuous development and improvement of the curriculum development
process it was important to integrate teacher’s expertise into the curriculum development process.
This approach was implemented throughout the thesis project in a co-creation manner to ensure

Method Overview
In addition to the forward-looking design activities, a Root Cause Analysis (RCA) was conducted on
the challenges raised, with an aim of identifying the basic reason of the symptoms. RCA is a well-
known problem-solving strategy in the field of quality management and popular tools include the

diagram is a visual tool that depicts the possible causes of a problem in different categories under an
effect or an issue (for Quality, n.d.). It is particularly useful for arranging the possible causes and
identifying the systemic causes (for Quality, n.d.). The Five Whys technique, on the other hand, is a

advantage of Ishikawa diagrams is that they give an overall view of all the possible cause categories

path when in fact there are several cause paths). To this end, we limited the use of Ishikawa to
identify the general categories first, and then employ Five Whys for the important sub-issues within
each category.

Literature Review and Theoretical Foundations
4.2.1
Curriculum Design Methodologies and Pedagogical Foundations
The present curriculum design incorporates principles of different learning theories and pedagogical
models that help to shape the ideal delivery of the learning experience. This section discusses critical
theoretical foundations, such as constructivism (and constructionism), inquiry-based learning, and
multimodal learning, and how they integrate when incorporating makerspace into the curriculum.
Constructivism and Constructionism.
Constructivism as a learning theory is based on the work of Jean Piaget and other theorists and
it state that learning is the process of integration of new experiences into the learners’ cognitive
23 of 109
==PAGE:34==
Transforming STEM Education - Designing School Curricula for Modular Makerspaces
frameworks(Martinez & Stager, 2013).
In contrast to rote or didactic instruction, a construc-
tivist approach transforms students from passive recipients of information into active participants
in meaning-making (Alanazi, 2016). Piaget’s studies of child development led him to conclude that
learning is best described as a process of building mental structures through the engagement of ex-
ploratory and reflective processes. This principle is highly relevant to makerspaces: when students
engage in building, tinkering or creating artifacts, they are effectively “learning by doing,” which
aligns well with constructivism (Alanazi, 2016) (Martinez & Stager, 2013). Kaar & Stary ((Kaar
& Stary, 2019)), Saplan & Wardrip ((Saplan & Wardrip, 2024)) have found in their research in
education that students learn more deeply when they are allowed to actively explore problems and
construct their own solutions than in passive receipt of information.
Papert’s theory of constructionism is a further development of constructivism, with the focus being
made on the production of real artefacts within the learning process. Papert put forward that chil-
dren learn best when they are in the active role of designer and constructor of meaningful products,
saying that “the effective way of learning is when people are active in creating tangible objects in the
real world” (Kynigos, 2015). In constructionist learning environments, including coding activities
or maker projects, learners not only understand information, but also externalize it, that is, make
it tangible and share it (a programmed robot, a 3D-printed model, etc.). It is believed that the ex-
ternalization process leads to reflection and iterative improvement, which in turn enhances learning
outcomes. Therefore, constructionism offers a theoretical underpinning for integrating makerspaces
into the curriculum: making (e.g. building a circuit, creating a prototype) is not only a way to
motivate learners and attract their attention, but also a way to develop hypotheses, receive feedback
and build solid knowledge structures (Kynigos, 2015).
Although makerspaces present excellent opportunities for students to engage in experiential, discovery-
based learning, implementing them appropriately is a complex process. Just providing students with
tools and technology is not enough (Bower, Stevenson, Forbes, Falloon, & Hatzigianni, 2020b). For
instance, scaffolding and facilitation by educators are critical for creating productive and meaning-
ful learning experiences (Becker & Jacobsen, 2020). However, equity and access are also key, and
makerspaces should not perpetuate inequality if used incorrectly (J. J. Ryoo, Kali, & Bevan, 2016).
This includes dealing with possible biases in maker culture and offering culturally relevant making
activities (J. J. Ryoo et al., 2016). In addition, the assessment practices should be in harmony with
the constructivist theory, and thus, move away from conventional methods that may not be able
to capture the learning processes that are involved in making. Thus, for the educational value of
these spaces to be best realized, makerspace activities should be linked to the rest of the curricu-
lum and students should be encouraged to develop their critical thinking and problem-solving skills
(Halverson & Sheridan, 2014).
Inquiry-Based and Experiential Learning.
Another important aspect for the development of the curriculum is the family of inquiry-based and
experiential learning strategies. Dewey and Bruner had also called for learning to be based on in-
terest, doubt, and real-life problem-solving (Bruner, 2009). According to Bruner, students should
be encouraged to construct their own knowledge through discovery and inquiry, not being told ev-
erything as if it had been put before them (Bruner, 2009). Research has shown that inquiry-based
learning can increase students’ motivation and ability to apply concepts, because it contextualizes
knowledge in meaningful tasks (Ge, Koh, & Hu, 2024).
For example, science education studies
report that inquiry approaches lead to improved understanding of scientific concepts compared to
traditional lecture-based teaching, as students engage in the practices of science rather than just
learning about them (Auner, Daugbjerg, Nielsen, & Sillasen, 2018) (Raz Bahat & Eckstien, 2022).
In IBL, teachers guide students by posing questions or setting tasks, and students work to find

==PAGE:35==

solutions. This approach is in harmony with makerspaces: a makerspace task could start with a
driving question (for example, “How can we build a bridge that can hold 5kg?” or “Could we develop
a game to explain fractions?”), and students would have to come up with hypotheses, try out the
materials and structural integrity, and improve their work over time. Such experiences resonate.
This inherently interdisciplinary and student-centred nature of makerspaces encourages the inquiry
mindset across subjects (science, technology, art, even humanities) (Becker & Jacobsen, 2020). The
literature review indicates that blending inquiry with structure — sometimes called guided inquiry
or the “5E model” (Engage, Explore, Explain, Elaborate, Evaluate)—is a recommended practice in
STEM curriculum design (Attewell, 2020). The forthcoming design guide will leverage these insights
by providing templates that include inquiry prompts and reflection activities, ensuring that maker
projects remain anchored to curriculum goals and yield measurable learning outcomes.
Despite recent studies promoting inquiry-based learning in makerspaces and stress the role of stu-
dent exploration and discovery, consistent with Dewey and Bruner’s constructivist theory of learning
(Wiggins & McTighe, 2005), a more detailed discussion is needed for the practical application and
assessment of IBL in these spaces (Saplan & Wardrip, 2024). Although driving questions can be
useful for initiating motivation, they are not always directly connected to the learning objectives
and measurable assessments of the course (Saplan & Wardrip, 2024).
The 5E model has been
widely adopted, but its application in makerspaces needs further explanation (Bower, Stevenson,
Forbes, Falloon, & Hatzigianni, 2020a). How do teachers ensure that students develop conceptual
understanding during the ‘Explain’ stage, for example? Additionally, the multimodal and cross-
disciplinary nature of makerspaces presents both advantages and difficulties for the IBL approach
(Bower et al., 2020a). On the one hand, makerspaces can offer opportunities for inquiry in different
subjects; however, concrete practices that illustrate how this can be achieved, especially in areas that
are not STEM, are lacking. Moreover, the assumption that makerspaces necessarily promote an in-
quiry orientation needs further exploration (Turakhia, Ludgin, Mueller, & Desportes, 2024). Though
the physical environment can be conducive, it is the teachers who are instrumental in nurturing the
culture of questioning, critical thinking, and reflection that is the hallmark of genuine inquiry (Bower
et al., 2020a) (Turakhia et al., 2024). These pedagogical reflections help to enhance the case for IBL
within the context of the active and changing world of makerspaces, and highlight the importance
of onboarding teachers as their importance in curriculum development and implementation, as seen
in the Curriculum Development Domain Hierarchy Figure 3.2.
Multimodal and Multisensory Learning.
Research in the last two decades has recognized that learners are diverse and that learning is most
effective when it is experiential and includes multiple forms of representation and production. Mul-
timodal learning is defined as the use of various channels, including visual, textual, and auditory
kinesthetic, to address the learners and meet the different learning needs or “learning styles” (J. Ryoo
& Winkelmann, 2021). However, the idea of strict learning styles has been refuted; however, every-
one agrees that using various methods of delivering information helps to reinforce the information
and helps the students with different strengths (e.g., some learn best from pictures and diagrams,
others, from hands-on activities, etc.). Meaning is constructed not only through the written word
but also through visual design, spatial arrangement, gesture, and other modalities; therefore, ed-
ucation should encompass the various means of communication (J. J. Ryoo et al., 2016) (J. Ryoo
& Winkelmann, 2021). In a maker space integrated curriculum, multimodal learning is built in:
students may read the instructions (textual mode), watch a demonstration or tutorial video (visual
and auditory modes), do something (kinesthetic mode) or maybe present their work (oral mode).
The above discussed form of rich multimodal engagement may result in better learning. The lit-
erature also proves that modulation enhances storage and generalization of information in learning
(J. Ryoo & Winkelmann, 2021). For example, in geometry, a student may understand properties
25 of 109
==PAGE:36==
Transforming STEM Education - Designing School Curricula for Modular Makerspaces
of shapes better by looking at diagrams and making models of them using a 3D printer or cut out
materials (Attewell, 2020). The makerspace activities are particularly good at providing multimodal
experiences: for example, an electronics activity where students can look at a circuit diagram, hear
an explanation from the teacher, manipulate components and see an LED light up, incorporating
multiple modalities in the transfer of knowledge.
In addition, multimodal design is closely related to accessibility and Universal Design for Learning
(UDL) principles that imply providing multiple means of communication, expression and engage-
ment for all students, including those with disabilities (Rose, 2000). Hence, from a theoretical point
of view, a curriculum that incorporates makerspaces can adopt multimodal learning to enhance the
curriculum to be more effective and accessible for all students. At the same time, the literature sug-
gests that curriculum developers should make provision for the use of visual aids, practical exercises,
stories and group discussions at the same time instead of using only one form of teaching (J. Ryoo
& Winkelmann, 2021). This will be incorporated in the GNIST guide by requiring content creators
to include different types of media (pictures, videos, physical supplies) and types of activities for
every learning objective to enhance learning for a wider number of students.
Multimodal learning, which focuses on the use of various senses in the learning process, is an attrac-
tive idea, especially in the context of makerspaces. However, it is essential to examine the concept’s
practical use and relationship with learning outcomes. The use of different modalities in teaching
can indeed increase the interest and possibly the level of understanding (Seo & Richard, 2021), but
the concept of distinct learning styles is still questionable. However, while it is true that learners can
have some preferences, it is also true that categorizing them rigidly can be unhelpful. A stronger po-
sition is to offer multiple means of representation and action, as with Universal Design for Learning
(Seo & Richard, 2021). Makerspaces are inherently multisensory and thus represent a good oppor-
tunity to apply UDL. However, it is not guaranteed that just offering a variety of materials and
tools will lead to successful multimodal learning. It has to be intentional. Teachers need to think
critically about how the various modalities are connected to the learning objectives and how they
will be able to tell whether the students have learned the material in different modalities (Bower et
al., 2020a).
Constructive Alignment of Pedagogy and Assessment.
In all these pedagogical theories, there is one meta-principle for curriculum design called constructive
alignment (H. L. Andersen, 2010) (Biggs, 1996) (Aldino, 2024), that ensures that learning objec-
tives, instructional activities and assessment methods are coherently linked. In other words, the
learning outcomes should be directly connected to the teaching strategies (activities/resources) and
the learning assessment methods (Aldino, 2024). Thus, a good curriculum is based on well-defined
intended outcomes (sometimes based on national standards, for example, Fælles Mål in Denmark),
and then appropriate teaching strategies are identified and linked to those outcomes, as well as the
assessment tasks (for example, a performance rubric for a given project). The literature highlights
that misalignment can nullify even excellent pedagogical ideas; for instance, telling students to be
creative makers and testing them on factual knowledge sends the wrong signal. To this end, the
developed guide should incorporate alignment checks at each stage in the design guide framework.
The concept of constructive alignment is in harmony with the constructivist and constructionist
philosophies (Biggs, 2012) because it is based on the idea that learners should actively achieve goals
and all elements of the curriculum should support the learner-centered process (Biggs, 2012). In
practical terms, this concept will be used to structure the design guide such. This alignment will
also assist teachers to prove that their innovative maker lessons are meaningful and contribute to the
required competencies and can easily prove learning evidence, an issue that is of concern to many
educators as highlighted in the stakeholder analysis 4.3.

Constructive alignment though good in theory has some practical issues especially in the context
of makerspaces which are fluid. To this end, learning objectives, activities and assessments have
to be properly lined up to guarantee that teaching and testing are appropriately related with the
expected results (Nieveen et al., 2023). However, the open-ended and discovery-based approach of
makerspaces is in conflict with the linear and logical way of constructive alignment (Veldhuis et
al., 2022). Makerspaces are based on the constructivist approach where learning is student-driven
and discovery-based, whereas constructive alignment is based on a teacher-centered approach with
learning objectives and outcomes (Veldhuis et al., 2022). This creates a problem of how to combine
the rigorous learning and the freedom of maker activities. Moreover, learning in makerspaces cannot
be measured by the same means as in a conventional classroom, using tests and quizzes. Despite
the fact that knowledge can be assessed in a conventional way, other aspects, including problem-
solving, creativity, and collaboration, cannot be measured effectively using traditional tools (Saplan

4.2.2
Legal and Policy Compliance Frameworks in Education
Any curriculum design, particularly one involving digital tools and content sharing (as is common
with makerspace projects), must operate within the bounds of educational standards and legal regu-
lations. In the Danish context and the broader EU, three pillars stand out: the national curriculum
standards (Fælles Mål), data privacy laws (GDPR), and accessibility requirements (WCAG).
Alignment with Curriculum Standards (Fælles Mål).
In Denmark, the Ministry of Children and Education sets curriculum goals called Fælles Mål (Com-
mon Objectives) that are binding and are linked to specific subjects in primary and lower secondary
education (UVM, 2023). These standards are the competencies, knowledge, and skills that students
are expected to achieve at certain grade levels in each subject. For instance, in science, Fælles Mål
29 of 109
==PAGE:40==
Transforming STEM Education - Designing School Curricula for Modular Makerspaces
could be that by the end of sixth grade, students should have a basic understanding of electricity and
be able to do simple experiments. These objectives are the “intended curriculum” that all teaching
materials and lesson plans are supposed to address (Nasser, 2024). It is thus very important that
any curriculum design guide ensures that the content created for the purpose of implementation is
directly related to the appropriate Fælles Mål. Not aligning with these could mean that the mate-
rial is not allowed to be used in schools or that it does not contribute to the desired learning outcomes.
The literature on curriculum alignment suggests that standards should be used as a starting point
for design (also referred to as a “backward design” approach in which one works from the end ob-
jectives and then plans learning experiences) (UVM, 2023). In the case of integrating makerspaces,
this means that though we encourage creative, hands-on projects, all projects must be tied to the
curriculum goals they achieve. For example, a maker project that involves building geometric shapes
with a laser cutter should be connected to geometry objectives in mathematics; a coding project
with micro:bits can be connected to computation or science objectives, and so on. There is a finding
that these links help in teacher adoption and administrative support of makerspace activities as
they show that such activities are not just ‘extra’ but are a way of meeting core learning outcomes
(Attewell, 2020). In fact, case studies of schools show that the use of makerspace is most sustainable
when applied as a curriculum support tool and not as a separate club or free time activity (Attewell,
2020). This will be helped by the design guide having sections for listing the relevant Fælles Mål
for each module developed and examples of how maker activities can meet those objectives. The
author wants the alignment to be easy for teachers so that they do not have to guess or work harder
to prove that a makerspace project is relevant to their required syllabus.
Further analysis through interview with two education consultants from Engineer the Future showed
that adherence to Fælles Mål should not be a primary concern, as non-compliance is neither regis-
tered nor enforced upon external educational content (Appendix C 9.2). Implication thereof is that
aligning with Fælles Mål and other educational regulatory goals is of concern in terms of ease-of-
design when aligning learning objectives with proper assessment and teaching methods.
Data Protection and Privacy (GDPR)
The EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) (Regulation (EU) 2016/679) has set a high
standard for privacy and data security across all sectors, including education. As public institutions
that deal with personal data of minors, schools are extremely liable to protect student information
and to make sure that any digital tools or content used are GDPR-compliant (SAASHOW, 2020).
In the context of curriculum design, GDPR considerations might include: getting consent for using
students’ work (if the work includes personal data or identifiable information), making sure that any
online platforms or software that are used in makerspace projects (for instance, cloud-based coding
platforms, project sharing websites) are compliant and secure, and teaching students data ethics (if
applicable). A GDPR-compliant learning design would, for instance, not involve students having
to sign up with personal details on third-party sites without proper vetting, or would de-identify
student data in any research or sharing.
Traditional curriculum guides are not rich in data protection information though this is relatively
new to lesson planning; however, it is now seen as crucial. It has been noted that “GDPR means
that schools are more accountable for the data they collect” and all educational activities that
involve data must be legally justified (SAASHOW, 2020). The consequences of noncompliance can
be severe, from the impact of data breaches on students’ privacy to legal consequences for the
institution (SAASHOW, 2020).
Hence, included in the curriculum design guide are the GDPR
compliance, which is a new but important one.

guide: e.g., "does your project entail collecting student names, photos, or work online?" If yes,

Accessibility and Inclusive Design (WCAG and UDL)
It is both legally and professionally appropriate that all students have access to the learning ma-
terials they need, regardless of their disabilities. Denmark being a EU member, has to adhere to
the Web Accessibility Directive which has provisions on accessibility of websites and digital content
including those produced by schools. The WCAG 2.1 is an international standard that is widely
used to guide the development of web content that can be easily accessed by people with disabilities
(W3C, 2024). Although the WCAG is usually mentioned in connection with websites, its guidelines
can be applied to digital learning materials as well: e.g., alternative text for images (for the use of the
blind and partially sighted using a screen reader), sufficient color contrast, closed subtitling of the
videos for the deaf, correct hierarchy of the information, and so on (W3C, 2024). If the curriculum
design guide recommends the development of online content or multimedia, then these accessibility
recommendations have to be incorporated. Besides, this is in conformity with the educational ob-
jective of inclusion. The interview with Engineer the Future’s educational consultants (Appendix
C 9.2) revealed that whilst inclusion principles (also on digital platform) is important, it should
not be a direct design metric as teachers will adapt material to their student types. This means,
that teachers with impaired student (e.g. special schools) will take "normal" course material and
alter heavily to fit into their classroom. Designing for all student types is neither fruitful nor effective.
The Universal Design for Learning (UDL) framework is compatible with the technical accessibility
recommendations and offers a learning sciences approach to the development of curricula that aims
for learning for all. UDL assumes that curriculum should be developed from the beginning of the
learning process to address the needs of the diversification of students using means of engagement,
representation, and expression (Rose, 2000) (for Teaching Innovation, n.d.). For instance, making
materials available in different formats (text, audio, interactive) and allowing students to present
their learning in different ways (written, oral, practical) can help engage students with different
disabilities or learning styles. There is evidence that implementing UDL not only benefits students
with disabilities but also all students by eliminating unnecessary constraints on learning (for Teach-
ing Innovation, n.d.). The review of the existing curriculum guides did not reveal that they contain
UDL or WCAG checks. However, accessibility is often an add-on or managed by the special ed-
ucation department separately from the general education team. However, as maker activities are
being integrated into the curriculum, we must realize that some maker tools may pose some barriers
(for example, a student with physical disability may not be able to use some of the equipment,
and a student with visual impairment may require technology to help her code during a coding
activity). Teachers experience challenges in modifying maker activities for students with special
needs and accessibility recommendations are missing that can prevent some students from partici-
pating (Seo & Richard, 2021) (Lee, Arnett, et al., 2020). Iterating the expertise from Engineer the
Future (Appendix C 9.2) these teachers are expertly equipped with skills to modify and alter educa-
tional content to fit their students, and whilst designing for clear, concise and inclusive educational
material can be beneficial not only for impaired student, the aim of doing so should be for easing
the transfer of knowledge in general, and not to include every student type into every design desicion.

Other Legal/Ethical Considerations
Beyond the major fields mentioned above, the literature and policy context include several others.
However, one is on intellectual property and copyright: When developing or applying content for
the lessons, teachers are expected to adhere to copyright laws or, where possible, use copyright-free
material. The concept of Open Educational Resources (OER) is increasing, which means that the
teaching materials are freely available and can be used, modified and shared (UNESCO, n.d.). Im-
plementing OER can assist in ensuring that the materials are used correctly and usually encourage
collaboration among the educators. The design guide will promote the use of open licenses for any
content teachers develop (so that they can share it with others) and check that any third-party
resources included in a lesson (photos, code libraries, etc.) are either open license or the school has
properly licensed them. This is not a legal necessity in the sense that one is not required to do so
but using OER is in concordance with the best practices and spirit of the maker movement.
In summary, the framework of compliance identified from the literature is to align with Fælles Mål
(that is, the content is curriculum relevant), to incorporate GDPR compatible practices (to protect
student data and privacy) and to comply with the accessibility and inclusive design principles so that
all students can participate in and enjoy the learning activities. These are fairly specific requirements
that may not be fully met by the current curriculum design models, creating possible gaps which we
identify in the gap analysis below 4.4.

Empirical Stakeholder Analysis
To validate and enrich the findings from literature, we engaged directly with the primary stakeholders
of the curriculum design guide teachers and external course creators.
The aim was to capture
the real-world challenges educators face with curriculum design, particularly regarding makerspace
integration and compliance issues, and to gather user requirements for the guide.

4.3.1
Methods: Co-Creation Workshops and Interviews
To collect qualitative data, co-creation was used in the form of workshops and semi-structured in-
terviews. This approach is in line with the human centered design (HCD) principles that treats
teachers as co-designers of the final product and as experts in what they need. To this end, the use
of multiple formats helped to ensure both idea generation through collaborative work and detailed
feedback.
Co-creation workshops were carried out with a teacher from elementary school. The participant was
a teacher of different subjects (mathematics, physical education) and with low level of experience
with makerspaces to capture the perspective of someone who is open to using a well-developed so-
lution, but doesn’t have the predisposed knowledge to not need it. All the workshops were roughly
2 hours long and were conducted with the help of various interactive activities. For instance, the
teacher was asked to come up with the idea of the ‘perfect lesson planning guide’, to sketch what
a page of such a guide could look like and to describe the problems he encounters when planning
his lessons. A scenario exercise: “Let’s say you are planning to develop a project for your class
using our mobile using our design guide and makerspace, what would you need to do to make it a
success?” This made the teacher define the necessary support (e.g., samples of similar projects, what
objectives it would meet, permission, and safety, etc.). The workshops produced a lot of data in the
form of flipchart notes, diagrams, and post-it clusters of ideas. As a research method, co-creation
did not only give data but also got the buy-in according to Nasser (Nasser, 2024). Some elements
of the co-creation feedback was filtered as irrelevant, as some usecase implications was a matter of

the practicalities of the makerspace and not the course development.
Moreover, semi- structured interviews were conducted. The participants included both the teacher

tants. The interview protocol covered the following areas of inquiry: The current lesson/curriculum

4.3.2
Key Findings: Teacher Needs and Challenges
The stakeholder analysis revealed several consistent themes, which are summarized below:
1. Need for a structured yet flexible template
Teachers wanted something like a ‘plug and play’ curriculum template or guide that they could
use readily when creating a new lesson or project. Typically, they sit down with a blank page or
piece together components of the lesson from a variety of sources (e.g., the national curriculum
website for objectives, a lesson plan from the internet for structure, their own checklist for materials
etc.). These views are also encountered in the discussions on the online teacher forums and include
lack of experience and a systematic approach to preparing teachers to develop educational content
(Bulut, 2025). They didn’t want it to be a strict form that would ‘feel like paperwork’ or limit
their creativity. Therefore, the challenge is to create the guide with clear sections/prompts (so that
nothing important is forgotten) but make it general enough that it can be applied to, for instance,
a third grade art project, or a ninth grade physics project.The co-creation even outlined a potential
template for the guide with parts like “Learning Goals (what are Fælles Mål?)”, “Materials/Tech
Needed”, “Step by Step Activity Plan”, “Checks for GDPR/permissions”, “Assessment Plan”, etc.
This is a perfect place to start for the structure of the guide and represents a clear example of
user-centred design.
2. Difficulty balancing creative freedom with curriculum demands
Teachers appreciate the flexibility of the student-directed projects but are concerned about how to
make sure that while doing these, they cover the curriculum and manage the class. It was pointed
out that projects can easily become unfocused: “The kids get really into building something, which
is amazing, but I worry that it won’t tie back to the specific learning standard I had in mind, or it
could get out of control.” They need ways to make projects disciplined enough to stay on task and
productive, without stifling the innovation. This is in line with the ‘guided’ versus ‘open’ discussion
in the literature (Attewell, 2020). It seems as though what teachers are looking for are specific
ways in which to implement a makerspace project – how to introduce it (perhaps in relation to
previous knowledge), how to establish stages or frequencies, and how to modulate the process rather

necessary to provide students with a very specific product they were to create (‘a game that quizzes
on multiplication’) linked to mathematical objectives, but without prescribing how the game was

In line with the expectations, teachers are not always certain of the legal aspects of curriculum design.

highlighted the requirement of aligning with Fælles Mål: currently they often manually look up the
relevant objectives from the ministry’s website when planning, if they look it up at all.
4. Existing guides or resources are insufficient
Teachers were asked whether they currently employ any familiar curriculum design models or tem-
plates. A few mentioned the ‘didactic relations model’ (a Scandinavian planning model that is based
on the aspects of teaching), and one barely recalled Understanding by Design. However, no one was
employing a formal guide in the everyday planning. They either employ school-based lesson plan

it would actually help to combine them into one process so when I plan a course I’m automatically
following the rules.” This comment is a good capture of the need for integrated design guide. It
also shows that teachers do not want to create more work (for instance, filling a compliance form
separately); they would rather want to have one unified workflow.

challenges. As one teacher suggested, “Show me a couple of shining examples – say, how did someone

research provided general principles, teachers provided context-specific needs and preferences. In
combination, these provide a full understanding of the problem space: there is a requirement for a
curriculum design guide that is not only legally compliant, but also pedagogically sound and easily
implementable in the day-to-day activity of teachers.

ADDIE (Analysis, Design, Development, Implementation, Evaluation): ADDIE is a fre-
quently employed model in the field of instructional design, which was developed in the middle of
the 20th century in the context of training and military education. It presents a high-level process:
First, analyze the purpose or need, then create a plan, create the materials, deliver the products,
and lastly, assess and feed back. Strengths: It is systematic and make one think through the needs
and the evaluation. In terms of pedagogy, ADDIE is neutral and can incorporate constructivist
or traditional approaches easily. Weaknesses: The main drawback of the ADDIE system has been
highlighted by the recent studies – it is rather sequential and rigid (Özkıvanç, n.d.). It does not
encompass the concept of iteration to a significant extent, except for the evaluation phase, which
is a problem in dynamic environments, especially when it comes to technology integration. Of im-

==PAGE:52==

portance, however, is the fact that ADDIE does not explicitly address alignment with standards or
legal compliance; the designer will have to consider these in the Analysis phase, but the model does
not prompt for them specifically. It also has no special guidance for makerspaces or hands-on learn-
ing—again, one could use ADDIE to develop a maker course, but ADDIE itself is context-neutral.
In conclusion, ADDIE provides a general framework but is not a direct way to address the gaps that
have been identified. For example, a teacher using pure ADDIE may still not remember to check
for GDPR because ADDIE does not tell them to.
Understanding by Design (UbD): Developed by Wiggins and McTighe ((Wiggins & McTighe,
2005)) UbD is a framework based on the principle of “backward design.” Designers begin by identi-
fying what they want learners to know, understand, and be able to do (usually based on standards),
then determine what counts as evidence of learning (assessments), and then design learning expe-
riences that will lead to those outcomes. Strengths: UbD promotes alignment with standards and
objectives very strongly; it is suitable for ensuring Fælles Mål alignment because it is literally based
on objectives. It also pushes people to think about assessment at the earliest possible time, which
is consistent with constructive alignment. Because UbD is a process, it is naturally pedagogically
flexible in that it does not prescribe a teaching style, but its implementation tends to favor construc-
tivist approaches since one typically creates meaningful tasks to achieve goals. Many teachers like
UbD because it makes them think about the ‘why?’ and the ‘how do we know students learned?’
weaknesses: In its standard form, UbD does not include particularity on legal compliance or technol-
ogy integration because these were not primary concerns when it was developed. There is nothing
in the UbD template regarding privacy or accessibility, so a teacher could create a UbD unit plan
that is rich from the learning perspective but fail, for instance, to consider that the project they are
assigning requires a website that some students cannot use. Another potential problem is that UbD
can be fairly conceptual for teachers who do not have a lot of structure; it is a framework and not a
framework paired with specific instructions therefore teachers still have to come up with the learning
activities on their own (the framework does not provide them). It is important to note that although
UbD can be used to plan a makerspace project – where one identifies the enduring understandings
and then picks a maker task as the learning experience – UbD does not have built-in features that
relate specifically to iterative design or making as pedagogy. It assumes that the designer will come
up with appropriate activities for the given task. Thus, even though that will include the principle
of focusing on outcomes which is excellent (and which will be use), there is no specific attention to
making or compliance checking.
Universal Design for Learning (UDL): Universal Design for Learning (UDL) is not a curricu-
lum planning model in the traditional sense, but it is a framework for ensuring that curricula are
accessible for all students. The UDL guidelines outlined by CAST are categorized into three aspects,
which include engaging, representing, and acting/expressing (Rose, 2000) (for Teaching Innovation,
n.d.). Strengths: If adopted, UDL can help increase the levels of accessibility and individualization
of a curriculum. It makes the designer think about the different learners first, which would usually
capture many of the WCAG principles and the accommodation requirements. For instance, UDL
would suggest providing choices (text and audio, etc.), something which would be helpful for more
students. It also means that lessons are developed with several possible paths, which is consistent
with the idea of the makerspace where different results or approaches are encouraged. Weaknesses:
UDL is sometimes considered a framework that supports learning content development and not a
full process for developing a unit’s content or linking it to standards. Therefore, a teacher could
employ ADDIE or UbD and incorporate the UDL principles into the process. UDL by itself will not
guarantee that a lesson is properly sequenced or that the right content is covered; it is more about
how to develop the materials than what to teach. Furthermore, UDL does not capture some of the
concerns raised in this course, such as data privacy or legal matters, or at least, not as extensively
as disability rights may. With regards to makerspaces, UDL suggests that maker activities should

be made accessible for all students (e.g., if a student cannot use a drill, then they can design on the
computer, etc.), but it does not assist in incorporating a makerspace into the standards. In other
words, UDL is an important aspect (for accessibility), and our guide will include UDL principles,
but UDL alone is not enough as a curriculum guide.
Didactic Planning Models: In Scandinavia, teachers are usually introduced to didactic relations
models, which require the teacher to consider factors such as objectives, content, learning process,
learner preconditions, teacher preconditions, context, and evaluation, all in relation to each other
(Lund, 2020). Strengths: Such models provide a holistic view. They implicitly encourage alignment
(objectives with evaluation, etc.) and are quite flexible; a teacher can use them for any subject or
approach. They also consider context and learner background, which is good pedagogical practice.
Weaknesses: These models are usually theoretical and not presented as user-friendly templates.
They also often lack concrete prompts for modern requirements (e.g.
a classic model from the
1970s won’t mention "ICT" or "GDPR"). Teachers may consider them too general. The ones we
reviewed show no integration of legal compliance; they assume a mostly pedagogical focus. They
also usually don’t mention specific innovative pedagogies like makerspaces—again, teachers would
have to incorporate that themselves. In practice, few teachers actually write out all elements of such
models for each lesson; they serve more as mental checks. We note that our guide could be seen
as modernizing a didactic model by explicitly adding the technology and compliance dimensions to
the usual pedagogical components. In recent times, some toolkits are telling educators to use design
thinking either to teach students or to create curriculum indeed. Also, there are maker education
frameworks (for instance, Martin’s (2015) principles or the agency by design’s maker capacities).
Strengths: These stress iterative design, empathy in design thinking, and are usually aligned with
the development of 21st-century skills. They can be inspiring and enable educators to move beyond
the confines of their disciplines. A design thinking approach might have teachers prototype and test
their curriculum much the same as a product — this fits well with the DBR concept of iterative
improvement. Weaknesses: Many of these are not very popular or used without certain training.
The ones we saw (including Design Thinking for Educators by IDEO) are more about addressing a
general school issue rather than developing a daily lesson plan. They also fail to incorporate com-
pliance. The maker-specific frameworks like the Maker Empowerment framework are perfect for the
philosophy but not as step-by-step guides. In essence, these new approaches are inspiring but barest
on actual implementation details. Also, they can sometimes ignore the standards’ connection to
content — for instance, a teacher may use a design thinking process to create something interesting
but fail to include some necessary content. There is a requirement to combine the best of design
thinking, which offers the freedom of creativity, with the best of curriculum standards which provide
the much-needed structure.
After examining these, it was found that each existing guide has certain strengths, but none
covers all bases. This comparative insight reinforces our aim: the GNIST guide must combine
the strengths while mitigating the weaknesses:
• From ADDIE/UbD: bring systematic structure and focus on alignment with objectives.
• From UDL: integrate accessibility and flexibility for diverse learners.
• From design thinking: include iteration and teacher/student co-creation, making the guide
more dynamic.
• Crucially, none explicitly handle GDPR or similar compliance, so that is a unique contribution
we will make.

4.6
Designing for Digital Literacy
Digital literacy and technology comprehension are critical competencies for the 21st-century ed-
ucation system where students are expected to use and create digital objects critically (Iversen,
Dindler, & Smith, 2019). The literature shows that effective digital literacy instruction should be
interdisciplinary and include computational thinking, problem-solving, and ethical reasoning in all
subjects (Iversen et al., 2019) (Dindler et al., 2020). This means that the Gnist Curriculum Design
Guide should guarantee that digital literacy is incorporated into other subjects and not offered as
a separate subject, thus complying with national educational frameworks such as Teknologiforståelse.
The most recent pedagogical approaches for use in the classroom focus on practical and student-
centered ways of teaching digital literacy (Van Mechelen et al., 2021) (Iversen et al., 2019). Research
shows that students learn more about digital tools when they engage in computational design, pro-
gramming, and digital fabrication (C Smith et al., 2020) (Dindler et al., 2020). This means that the
guide should support learning processes where students learn through prototyping and reflection,
following the makerspace learning principles. Furthermore, scaffolding techniques should also be
included to help students with different abilities, starting with guidance and progressing to indepen-
dent problem solving (Schaper et al., 2022).
It is not only the technical knowledge that is necessary, but also the ability to analyze digital
products, understand the workings of algorithms, and ponder over the ethical issues such as data
protection and digital rights, and in extension the necessity of teaching children digital citizenship
through systematic discussions of the social aspects of technology in the learning process (Iversen et
al., 2019). This means that the guide should include some time for students to think about their own
technology use and the implications of technology in the society. For instance, including examples
of cases such as algorithmic bias or digital ethics will help the students to be critical consumers
of digital technology. Furthermore, the digital literacy education should include the assessment of
technical performance and critical thinking (Iversen et al., 2019) (Saplan & Wardrip, 2024). The
guide should include some formative assessments including project portfolios, peer evaluations, and
reflective journals to check if the students can use the digital tools effectively. Thus, the adopted
strategies are backed by research, and for this reason, the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide should
ensure that digital literacy and technology comprehension are taught in the way prescribed by the
modern pedagogical theories, for students to become not only technically competent, but also criti-
cally aware of the digital environment.
The integration of digital literacy across the curriculum, making learning student-centered and
hands-on, especially in makerspaces, has great potential (Bekker, Bakker, Douma, Van Der Poel, &
Scheltenaar, 2015) (Hughes, Fridman, & Robb, 2018). If done correctly, it may enhance the under-
standing and interest of the students and at the same time meet the needs of different learners. But
for successful implementation, there is the need to consider teacher training and support, robust
assessment strategies, and equitable access to resources (González-salamanca et al., 2020). Addi-
tionally, this guide is useful in ensuring curriculum coherence, adapting to changing technologies in
the long run, and ensuring that the guide is not too broad yet not too deep (González-salamanca et
al., 2020)).

Pedagogical Gaps and Needs: Educators lack a clear framework that can help them incorporate
new pedagogical strategies such as makerspace into the formal curriculum. However, constructivist
and inquiry-based approaches are appreciated; the teachers are often unable to incorporate them
into their teaching due to the existing standards and time constraints. The results of the DS1 re-
vealed a lack of detailed information on how to link practical, student-led activities to theory and

learning objectives, as well as assessments. It was determined that most curricula do not include
such interesting activities or incorporate them into the curriculum in a way that is not related to
the body of knowledge (Attewell, 2020). There is clearly a need for a guide that shows how to
include making and multimodal learning within units in a standards-based way. Moreover, teachers
require assistance regarding the management of these activities (for example, formatting of projects
and evaluating the results), which is not provided by most of the current guides. In other words,
there is a pedagogical gap between the conventional curriculum organization and the innovative,
student-centered approaches of the 21st century. This gap can be closed by assisting teachers in
creating original (makerspace-based) and standard (aligned to learning objectives) tasks.
Compliance and Legal Gaps: The analysis also shows that legal and policy issues are not be-
ing incorporated into the curriculum planning process. This is a major gap because ignoring these
factors may result in legal consequences such as data protection, accessibility of the materials used
in education, and thus negative impact on students or legal consequences for schools. Teachers are
currently working without the help of simple tools that would help her or him to check whether some
lesson or project that she or he has created is compliant with the GDPR, accessibility standards or
even the school’s regulations. That can result in omissions (e.g., using a object that is impossible to
access or fingerprinting students in a way that is against the law). The gap is not so much the result
of lack of willingness on the part of teachers but the absence of a clear process through which these
factors can be incorporated. Thus, identifying this as a gap means that our design guide will have a
compliance validation component (a new contribution). In other words, DS1 reveals that curriculum
design and legal compliance are separate in practice – our guide seeks to break that separation by
integrating compliance checking into curriculum design.
Lack of a Unified Guide Addressing Both: The last gap that was identified is the lack of a
single framework that would cover both the pedagogical integration of makerspaces and the legal
and standards aspects. For example, UbD is good at alignment but has nothing on privacy, while
the GDPR checklist is awesome at privacy but not much use for lesson planning. This is another gap
in itself – it means that teachers have to learn and use several frameworks at once, and something
will be forgotten.
The DS1 holistic assessment suggests that a clear guide that integrates these aspects would be useful
in current educational practice. We did not find any guide that paid much attention to pedagogical
excellence and strict compliance; this is where Gnist comes in.
Gaps of Usability and Practicality: The DS1 findings besides highlighting content gap, also
reveals a usability gap. It is important to note that teachers have preferences for easy to use, short
and right to the point resources. Theoretical models or verbose guides are not frequently used. This
suggests the need for an easy-to-use format (potentially a template or checklist style) that can be
readily adopted. If the solution is too complicated, the gap remains because busy educators will
revert to simpler habits. The feedback that said things like “please make it plug and play” is a steer
for PS1 to design with the end user in mind at every step. It also means that our evaluation criteria
for the guide in PS1 should include usability metrics gathered from teacher feedback.

some gaps in the literature that our project can help to fill. There is sparse research on systematic
ways of incorporating compliance into the design of learning experiences— our project may be the
first to present a model of this kind, which would combine educational design research with legal
compliance.
Furthermore, makerspace education is studied, but the focus is usually on student
results or justice, not on the development of the curriculum by teachers. Thus, by describing and
analyzing the process of creating and implementing the Gnist guide, it will also generate knowledge

Prescriptive Study
The PS phase’s role in the DRM is to transition from understanding to solution: it uses the re-
quirements stated in DS1 to create a design of a support tool, and to move towards the evaluation
in DS2. These research questions (RQs) were formulated to mirror this progression from problem
analysis to solution development to evaluation. RQ1 investigated current practices and gaps (carried
out in DS1), RQ2 asks what an effective curriculum design guide should contain (which is used to
inform the PS development) and RQ3 is forward looking and asks how to assess the effectiveness of
the guide through an example of a course implementation (to be implemented in DS2). The way
that the inquiry is structured thus, the development of the Gnist guide is closely connected to the
research questions: the design decisions made during PS are in response to the challenges identified
by RQ1, and the objectives of RQ2, while taking into consideration the evaluation criteria of RQ3.
In the project’s DRM implementation, some formative testing and feedback were included during
the development process (via co-creation with a teacher) to fine-tune the guide, but large-scale or
long-term validation was not within the PS purview. This approach is in concordance with the
recommendation made by Blessing & Chakrabarti that repeated consultation with experts during
the product development can be a valid substitute for strict product testing in real-life conditions
if time or financial resources are not sufficient (Blessing & Chakrabarti, 2009). Hence, this chapter
describes how the GNIST Curriculum Design Guide was developed, including the design cycles,
methods used, and decisions made concerning the guide, in light of preparing it for subsequent
evaluation (DS2).

5.3
Design-Based Research (DBR)
In the PS phase, a Design-Based Research approach was used, which means that the development
of the guide was considered as much a design process as it was a research process of the guide itself.
In DBR, the goal is to produce a product (in this case, the curriculum guide) while at the same
time generating knowledge concerning the design process and the educational setting. There were
several features of DBR evident in our approach:
• Empirically grounded design: Decisions were made based on the data collected in DS1 (teacher
interviews, literature review, and stakeholder workshops).
In each instance, it was asked:
What does the data indicate that the guide should aim to achieve? For instance, the DBR-
based research revealed that the teacher required integrated GDPR compliance information,
thus, DBR-wise, that was treated as an experiment condition – what if GDPR controls are
incorporated in the guide, does it solve the problem? The creation was a kind of experiment,
where the hypothesis was that the integrated guide will help to solve the problems identified
in DS1.
• Cycles of design and analysis that are iterative: In Iterative Design and Refinement, it was
described how each iteration of the guide provided a way to test our design hypotheses in
practice (with the teacher) and the ‘analysis’ of what happened each time was what refined
the ‘theory’ of what works. DBR recommends several cycles of formative evaluation; it was
implemented the same by having the teacher work with successive prototypes of the guide and
modifying the design based on her feedback.
• The combination of practical and theoretical findings: The aim was not only to create a
practical product – the guide – but also to gain understanding into how such guides should
be developed. For example, through the DBR process, further knowledge was created about
the type of support the teacher require (e.g., the teacher’s problem with the accessibility was
that he did not know what WCAG is and how to use it, thus, it was important to provide him
with a simple checklist – a design principle that could be applied in other guides, not only in
ours). In writing this chapter, to capture those insights (e.g. “teacher needs clear compliance
documentation for adoption”) as part of the results of the PS. This reflective aspect is classic
DBR: the intervention and the learnings about the intervention are integrated into the cycle
of work.
• Co-creation as part of DBR: Our DBR approach had a unique element: incorporated a co-
creating teacher as a partner in the research. This meant that the line between ‘designer’
and ‘user’ was not distinct, as DBR literature often advises to achieve ecological validity. The
teacher was not a passive subject but an active contributor whose classroom experience was
part of the design experiment. This provided very context specific modifications (for instance,
the teacher suggested a new way of writing learning objectives in student friendly language
which was incorporated both in the guide and generated knowledge on how the teacher translate
curriculum language to make it understandable to students.
In total, applying DBR in PS ensured that the guide was empirically tested at every stage and that
the final product is both practical and theoretical. By the end of PS, the guide was realized, but
also a set of DBR observations (e.g., what design features assisted or distracted teacher engagement)
that increased the credibility of the guide and provided a foundation for DS2’s evaluation.

Compliance-Driven Design
One distinctive aspect of our methodology was compliance-driven design. From the very beginning,
legal and regulatory requirements were not seen as afterthoughts; they were core drivers of the
guide’s design. This approach was necessitated by the DS1 finding that existing curriculum design
practices often overlook or bolt on legal compliance, resulting in fragmented planning. To counter
this, compliance considerations were a built-in part of each design iteration. Concretely, compliance-
driven design meant:
• Starting with compliance requirements: gathering all relevant requirements from Danish ed-

corresponding GDPR note (e.g. ensure consent or anonymity).” These rules were used to shape
the template structure. It’s worth noting that integrating such requirements from the start
is aligned with the principle of “privacy by design” in GDPR – i.e. building data protection
into the planning phase. Effectively applied privacy by design and universal access by design
in the context of curriculum planning.
• Developing a compliance checklist artefact: A key output of this method is the compliance
checklist embedded in the guide. During PS development iterating on this checklist to cover
all necessary points in teacher-friendly language. DS1 research had suggested the value of a

Cognitive Load Reduction and Usability Testing
A key focus during the development was making sure that the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide is
feasible and easy to use. the teacher works in stressful environments and any tool that is too complex
or that poses a high cognitive load is likely to be ignored. This was because cognitive load reduction
was reinforced during the design process to simplify, clarify and minimize the number of steps where
possible. The usability of the guide was tested in a natural environment and the modifications were
made in accordance with the DS1 outcomes and the co-creation process.

Learning Goals (Fælles Mål competencies) for this lesson: Use bullet points or blank lines
for the responses.
Technical terms were kept to minimum or if necessary (e.g., ‘formative
assessment’) they were defined briefly in parentheses. Some examples of the phrases that were
identified by the the teacher during the co-creation as can be simplified included ‘Success
Criteria’ which was simplified to ‘How will you know students learned it?
• Visual layout and organization: The guide template was developed in such a way that it is
easy to use and the workflow is clear, for example, from left to right and from top to bottom.
Some topics were also placed in related groups (for example, materials and setup, objectives
and standards), so that users would not have to look for the information they needed. This
paper contains white space, icons, and formatting features that enable the primary sections
to be quickly identified. This kind of clarity was based on usability research, which shows

parts:Set goals and learning objectives Develop learning experiences and activities Construct
assessments Check compliance with Fælles Mål, GDPR, and accessibility This division of the
work prevents cognitive overload and allows the workers to pay attention to one element at a
time. According to the cognitive load theory, dividing a task into parts helps in the learning
process and problem solving. The teacher also gave her feedback and said that the checkboxes
helped her to feel that the process was structured and doable, when compared to blank lesson
templates that offered no direction.
To establish the usability of the product, think-aloud techniques and post-use interviews were em-
ployed with the co-creating teacher. In think-aloud sessions, participants described how they would
develop a lesson using the guide and the participants provided an auditory verbalization of the
thoughts they had. This uncovered pain points and usability issues. For instance, in the initial
design, learning activities and assessment were combined in one table; when conducting think-aloud
testing, the teacher stated that this was “confusing” and resulted in the development of two separate
sets of columns for learning activities and assessments, respectively.
In its final version, the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide was developed with the help of the usability
testing and cognitive load reduction principles to be simple, understandable, and easy to use. This
usability focus ensures that the teacher can incorporate the guide into their work without the need
for much training, which in turn can lead to a high rate of uptake.
Scenario-Based Testing
It was very important during the iterative development of the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide to
make sure that it is able to support structured course development across all educational settings.
Because the guide plays the role of a decision- support tool rather than a prescriptive template, it was
necessary to assess how well it could induce meaningful reflection and guarantee course completeness
without interfering with the teacher’s autonomy.
For this, scenario-based testing was chosen as the primary evaluation method. This approach mod-
eled how teachers use the guide when creating courses, focusing instead on cognitive tasks than on
actual classroom scenarios. Unlike traditional usability testing, which evaluates how a product is
used in real teaching environments, this method specifically examined:
• The effectiveness of the guide in supporting structured thinking during course development.
• Whether the teacher could identify and incorporate all the necessary course components of the
guide’s structured prompts and checklists.
• How well the guide assisted in achieving pedagogical goals, legal requirements, and the plug-
and-play approach of the GNIST framework.
This method was helpful in focusing the evaluation on the guide’s effectiveness in helping to create
curricula with little or no distraction from such things as classroom dynamics and student interac-
tion, by removing external constraints.

Co-Creation as a Development Process
In the Prescriptive Study (PS) phase, co-authoring with a teacher was an essential part of the de-
velopment process and was employed for iterative improvement, including a series of collaborative
workshops and feedback sessions with an experienced Danish school teacher who was a co-designer
and the end-user of the guide. This co-creation process was not a single event but a cyclical process
of co-creation that was embedded in the development process.
The teacher engaged in multiple sessions at different prototyping stages. First, a kick-off workshop
was conducted to explain the idea based on DS1 findings and to gain the teacher’s general view on
the requirements and desires. In this session, the findings from DS1 were brought back and com-
pared with the professional experience of the teacher. For instance, the discussions on the template
outline developed by teachers in DS1 established that the proposed sections were necessary, thus
confirming the initial structure of the guide.
Right after developing a low-fidelity prototype, there was a practical co-design meeting where the
teacher went through each section of the guide and discussed it conceptually and provided feedback
or ideas right then and there. This session was also a design collaboration moment: if there was
something that was not quite understood, then the solutions were generated together. For example,
the teacher suggested to include an example in the guide on how a certain section will look like when
completed, which was implemented in the next version.
More sessions were done when the medium-fidelity prototype was developed. A major workshop
consisted of the teacher using the guide to develop an actual lesson plan which was later used as
the example course for validation. This process was observed (with the teacher’s permission) to see
how he used the guide, where he might have hesitated, and what he understood from each prompt.
A post-implementation discussion was conducted to allow him to explain the strengths and weak-
nesses of the guide and issues he faced during the interaction. Finally, a de-briefing session of the
high-fidelity version got the teacher’s general opinion and the last minor changes were made.

5.8
Legal and Compliance Considerations
Legal alignment is one of the crucial aspects of the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide. This section
explains how the final design was checked for Danish and European compatibility with the Danish
Fælles Mål curriculum objectives, the EU’s General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR), and Web
Content Accessibility Guidelines (WCAG) for accessible design. Moreover, the guide’s design strat-
egy matches the overall EU legislation and other educational strategies. Compliance was not only
a content decision but a fundamental design strategy (as discussed in Section 3.5), so that teachers
using Gnist are necessarily steered towards complying with legal and policy requirements.
Alignment with the Danish Fælles Mål: Fælles Mål are the national curriculum objectives and
learning outcomes that Danish basic education (Folkeskolen) must meet. The guide is specifically
designed to ensure that lessons or courses created with it are Fælles Mål relevant. The guide’s first
section makes teachers identify the specific Fælles Mål competency targets and knowledge objectives
that the lesson addresses to ensure that the alignment with the official curriculum is clear. This
approach makes the guide follow the backward design principle with a focus on compliance – the
goals start with the mandated ones. Moreover, the guide has a quick reference or a link to the Fælles
Mål documents for each subject (in an appendix or footnote) so that the teachers can easily find the
exact phrasing of the standards. The feature was incorporated after the authors noticed that some
teachers were not quite sure about the precise wording of competency objectives, which is important
for compliance purposes. Therefore, the Gnist guide effectively enforces curricular alignment: the
completion of the guide includes the identification of the appropriate Fælles Mål, which is often
missing in the creative lesson planning process. A comparison with the existing curriculum planning
guides showed that the latter does not include the explicit approach to the standards’ alignment as
a strength of the Gnist guide. Some guides were rather implicit or relied on the teacher’s memory.
Thus, the compliance with Fælles Mål has been integrated into the design of the Gnist guide, which
guarantees the legal requirements for Danish educational quality and readability for children. How-
ever, the Gnist guide is also applicable to the European Union’s outlook on result-based education.
Most of the EU member states have their own national curriculum standards, and the principles
shown in the structure of the Gnist guide can be applied to the same frameworks in other European
countries. Therefore, the Gnist guide shows how compliance requirements can be integrated into the
planning process such that the Gnist guide is a example of how curriculum design tools can combine
legal and pedagogical alignment for users.
Data Protection (GDPR) Compliance: The Gnist Curriculum Design Guide is a GDPR compliant
product that ensures that any learning activity that a teacher plans respects student’s data privacy.
To this end, the guide includes GDPR checkpoints to make teachers think about data protection
when developing the lesson plan. For instance, there is a checklist item that asks whether any per-
sonal data will be collected or shared in the lesson (for example, photographs, student accounts on
websites) and if so, whether there is a valid consent or other protection in place. The insights from
the DS1 study on GDPR in education were incorporated in the guide, including the suggestion that
teachers should consult the data officer of the school if they are not sure. This approach is consistent
with the “privacy by design” principle that has been called for by GDPR, which means that privacy

should be addressed at the beginning of any educational project, not as an afterthought. Also, the

Accessibility (WCAG) and Inclusion: The Gnist guide also ensures that accessibility and univer-

technology. The inclusion of this feature was based on DS1 results, which showed that teachers
said that accessibility issues were not considered often until the last minute. Thus, the guide seeks
to enhance the legality as well as the ideal education for all by shifting the accessibility concerns
to the beginning of the lesson planning process. As in the rest of the EU, there is an increasing

of lesson planning. Moreover, if provided digitally, the guide follows the WCAG 2.1 AA standards,
which includes compatibility with screen readers, logical heading structures, and other accessibility
features. This two-level approach – to make both the guide and the lessons planned with its help
accessible – shows a clear commitment to compliance and accessibility.

was viewed as generally relevant for different teaching situations were gained. The teacher’s ideas

remains both applicable and easily implementable.

to critically challenge the guide in a supportive environment, such that by the end of DS2 we could
be confident that the guide is not only theoretically sound but also practically validated in terms of
usability and compliance.

The iterative tests gave detailed formative evaluation which helped in confirming and refining the
Gnist Curriculum Design Guide. This section presents the findings in the order of the iterations and
the main ideas, explaining what changes were made after every round of feedback. The data showed
that the guide is becoming more practical and easy to use, and by the time of the last test, the

important part of lesson planning. This feedback led to the immediate addition of an “Assessment &

whether the learning objectives have been achieved (for example, through tests, presentations, or
rubrics). This refinement guaranteed that the guide covered the entire teaching process (objectives,
activities, assessment) and that it was consistent with the Backward Design framework that starts
with learning objectives and works back from there. Moreover, the conversation that centered on the
use of the makerspace in a scenario also brought up issues on permissions and safety. Even though
the guide already had a Compliance section, the teacher’s feedback showed that the prompts needed
to be more explicit. Consequently, there was more guidance given to remind teachers to make sure

Implications and Refinements
The validation process in DS2 provided empirical backing for the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide,
so that any revisions made ensured that it is not only compatible with all the criteria but also easy
to use. One of the most important outcomes is that the compliance considerations are now weaved
into the core of the guide rather than being treated as an afterthought. The compliance checklist
was further refined to be simpler and more specific based on the teacher feedback. It includes Fælles
Mål alignment, GDPR/data privacy, and safety/accessibility checks, each with a brief explanation.
This way, the teachers using the guide are being directed towards fulfilling the legal and educational
requirements without having to refer to any other resources. This refinement is a direct contribution
to the solution of the DS1 research problem, which revealed that teachers face challenges in fulfill-
ing external expectations in lesson planning. Thus, incorporating the compliance elements into the
guide means that teachers and schools will be much less likely to miss important learning intentions
or legal steps, such as getting parental permission for data collection on students. The teacher’s
successful use of the checklist in DS2 showed that it is practical and can be easily incorporated into
the workflow, while still ensuring that compliance is achievable without overburdening the planning
process.
The Compliance by Design feature of the Gnist guide is a good example of how regulatory compli-
ance can be integrated into teachers’ work processes in a simple and unambiguous way. The concrete
examples like “No student data shared without consent” under the GDPR section made the checklist
items very easy to understand. This change ensures that compliance is not seen as a separate, ad-
ditional bureaucratic process but as an important part of a structured approach to lesson planning.
As a result of DS2, the Gnist guide has integrated the necessary features for meeting educational
standards and legal requirements into a resource that is not only useful for teachers but also easy

The Gnist Curriculum Design Guide (9.2) is a coherent framework that can be used by educators

the study in the development of effective and practical courses. It is structured into clear segments
that start with the theoretical basis, such as the concept of makerspaces and digital literacy, before
progressing through the process of developing the course, delivering it, and assessing it. The guide is
planned to be a practical tool and not a theoretical one, and it includes some templates, checklists,

The guide is in direct connection with the research questions of this thesis. RQ1, which examines the

scaffolding, and design-based learning, RQ2, which aims to determine the characteristics of a good
curriculum design guide, is demonstrated. These methodologies guarantee that the guide does not
only offer a set of step-by-step planning tools but also supports a reflective and iterative teaching
process. To meet RQ3 on the evaluation of the effectiveness of the guide, it has self-assessment tools.

simplified to easy-to-follow steps that any new or experienced educator can use. It also conforms to
the current global curriculum design principles which include cross-cutting themes, problem-based,
and integration of digital literacy into the curriculum. However, to increase its applicability outside
the Danish setting, it could be useful to provide additional information for other national curric-

on teachers’ comfort in incorporating technology into their teaching, thus suggesting the need for
teacher skill and confidence development programs to accompany its adoption.
In its simplest form, the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide is an effective solution to the problems
posed by educational innovation. Thus, as a systematic approach to the integration of pedagogy,
policy, and technology, it equips educators with the means to develop compelling, standard-based,
and future-oriented curricula that guarantee that students not only master critical knowledge but
also the necessary digital skills of the new century.

8
Discussion, Reflection and Limitations
The purpose of this chapter is to critically evaluate the research and development process of the
Gnist Curriculum Design Guide, reflecting on the methodology and outcomes, and discussing the
broader implications and limitations of the study. The discussion ties back to the original research
objectives and questions, examining how well they were addressed and what insights have emerged.
It also considers the practical effectiveness of the final design guide in the context of Danish education
and outlines limitations and future refinements needed for the guide’s successful implementation.
8.1
Reflection on the Development Process
Within this project, the Design Research Methodology (DRM) framework was adopted as the over-
arching approach. The research was conducted through four classic DRM phases, namely Research
Clarification (RC), Descriptive Study 1 (DS1), Prescriptive Study (PS), and Descriptive Study 2
(DS2), which began with the analysis of the problem and ended with the development and assess-
ment of the solution. This is because the adopted structured approach guaranteed that the design
guide was developed from a deep understanding of the problem domain in the RC and DS1 before
moving to the design and implementation in the PS and DS2. However, in practice, the distinc-
tions between these phases were not always distinct. For instance, some findings from the initial
co-creation workshops that were expected to be used in the PS phase were fed back into DS1, which
fine-tuned the understanding of users’ needs and expanded the knowledge base. This looping is
characteristic of real-world design research and was something that was embraced as part of the
process. The research questions and focus were not set in stone; they were revised as new findings
came in. Although this made the results more relevant, it also demanded the flexibility in project
management and possible adjustment of objectives. The iterative nature of DRM allowed the re-
search to be conducted in a responsive and dynamic manner so that the guide was developed, tested,
and refined in real-world educational settings.
This approach was also based on the iterative co-creation process with the stakeholders, especially
teachers. This participatory design was crucial in creating a guide that is relevant to the actual
users. In this manner, Robertson and Simonsen (Robertson & Simonsen, 2012b)) underscore the
principles of involving end-users in the creation of the project as much as possible. The continuous
integration of teachers in the design process enabled the research team to incorporate the practical
knowledge, experience and issues of the teachers in the content of the guide. For instance, in collab-
orative workshops, teachers highlighted the need for clear examples of how to connect makerspace
activities to learning objectives, which in turn led to the inclusion of concrete lesson plan templates
in the guide. This co-learning and iterative revision process enabled the development team to gain
knowledge and feedback from users and make changes to the design in real time. Therefore, the
evolving prototype of the guide was tested and enhanced several times to produce a final product
that participants owned and had faith in. The co-creation approach also helped to improve the
usability of the guide and the stakeholder buy-in. Teachers who were involved in the development
claimed to be more willing to use the guide in their planning since they saw their ideas implemented
in it. This way, the guide was developed with the participation of teachers, and therefore, it is
practical and applicable for everyday use in the classroom.
Despite its strengths, the selected approach was also not without its problems. The primary dif-
ficulty was probably maintaining the research focus when so many sources were available.
The
initial Research Clarification (RC) phase defined the goals in very general terms, which included
making sure that the curriculum materials for makerspace-integrated curriculum are legally sound,
educationally sound, and easy for makers to use. However, as the project went on, sometimes new
insights would pull the focus in a different direction, i.e., toward scope creep.
The openness of

co-creation workshops can sometimes lead discussions to related, but not directly relevant topics,
such as the general IT infrastructure in schools or the need for teacher training, which is beyond
the scope of the guide. However, these discussions were quite useful and rich, but they could have
jeopardized the specific focus of the research. Managing emerging concerns in relation to the original
research questions was a complex task. In retrospect, it was great to be able to revise the objectives

reasonable course corrections could be made without veering too far off course.
Another challenge was co-creation with academic rigor. Co-creation is a fluid process and partici-
pant led which at times can be at odds with the set expectations of a methodological framework.
For instance, DRM’s Descriptive Study 1 (DS1) is traditionally a rigorous examination of literature
and field studies to define requirements before moving to the Prescriptive Study (PS). However,
in this project, some requirements were identified during the course of stakeholder workshops thus
blurring the distinction between DS1 and PS. This called for a flexible research strategy where the
stakeholder input was considered as form of data as relevant as the literature findings even when the
findings came in out of sequence. The result was a DRM inspired methodology conducted in an agile,
iterative manner. While this approach enhanced the practical relevance and timeliness, it meant
that the research did not adhere to the step by step approach of DRM with methodological purity
an achievement that is elusive in real world design research but one that needs to be acknowledged.
Looking back at the approach, the project is most similar to Type 5 design research as described
by Blessing and Chakrabarti: comprehensive DS1 and PS but limited DS2 evaluation. This ensured
depth of understanding and solution development, but limited time led to a more modest DS2 phase
than is preferable. The trade off between development depth and evaluation breadth is explored
further in the limitations.
The use of an iterative design research process, in conjunction with co-creation, was effective in
addressing the research problem. The structured Design Research Methodology (DRM) framework
provided a scientific backbone, and hence, adopted systematic literature reviews and evidence-based
requirements gathering in DS1. However, the co-design process was also cyclical and helped to keep
the project grounded and practical such that it did not become a purely theoretical exercise. The
process of development, feedback, and refinement of the guide in conjunction with the realities of
the classroom increased the credibility of the guide. This approach is consistent with the current
application of design thinking in education to prototype and test solutions in collaboration with
end-users, teachers, and content creators to produce fit-for-purpose solutions. The research and
development process was a journey of learning for both the researcher and the participants in the
development of the guide that is informed by research and owned by the users. Despite the fact
that there were some challenges in balancing the ideal plans with the real world constraints, these
very challenges provided valuable insights into the practical development of curriculum design tools.
The experience supports the claim that a co-created, iterative and research informed approach can
generate solutions that are at once methodologically sound and practically usable in educational
settings.

One of the strongest arguments in favour of the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide is that it standard-
ises for quality and thus for adherence to educational requirements. By design, the guide weaves in
checks for legal and policy readiness, e.g. for GDPR data privacy, copyright, and national curriculum

and institutional requirements to lower oversight risks. The guide ensures a uniform level of quality
across various courses by ensuring that important aspects like learning objectives, assessment plans,
and inclusivity are addressed consistently. The evaluations of the guide have shown that it has made
content creators aware of the regulatory requirements and has served as a safety net so that certain
critical elements do not get omitted during the curriculum development process. In this way, the
guide is not only a planning tool but also a quality assurance tool. The standardized templates and
checklists ensure that the curricula are documented and reviewed in a similar manner, thus ensuring

Despite the strengths of the standardization inherent in the guide, there are certain limitations.

Moreover, while standardization enhances the quality, it can restrict the creativity of the teachers.
When all the curricula are developed using the same format, there is a chance that educators may
pay more attention to meeting the legal requirements and would not experiment with new and
better ways of teaching. However, some educators may construe the guide’s flexibility as a rigid
structure that would discourage them from trying nontraditional approaches to teaching. The issue
is how to maintain legal sustainability and pedagogical innovation. Although teachers must comply
with certain standards, overemphasizing the compliance aspect may make curriculum development
seem more like a bureaucratic process than a creative one. This could squelch innovation, which is
typically the wellspring of compelling and energetic learning experiences.
Usability and Practical Application
A key claim of the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide is that it is easy to use, in the form of a clear
and simple step-by-step tool that any teacher or curriculum developer can use. The guide provides
clear stages and structured templates that help the user through the process of planning, develop-
ing, and evaluating curriculum content. It is a comprehensive and thorough guide that provides
clear and specific guidance at each stage of the curriculum development process. Teacher comments
have mentioned that its linear approach eliminates confusion in lesson planning since it includes
important elements such as objectives, activities, assessment, and compliance checks, which save
time and avoid omissions. This is especially useful for the less experienced teacher or a newcomer to
the profession who may be unsure about how to incorporate technology and makerspace activities
into their teaching. The guide simplifies the translation of the complex requirements into an easy to
use checklist and workflow and increases the usability and thus the decrease of the cognitive load.
In its step-by-step format, the guide is rather straightforward, and indicates that once teachers have
become familiar with it, they are able to use it on their own without having to consult someone else.
These findings suggest that for the most part, the guide is rather successful as a practical tool for
curriculum design.
While the strengths of the guide are clear, there are potential usability problems that could affect
some users. One issue is the possible training need for teachers who are not confident with structured
curriculum planning. For such persons, the very aspect of the approach that makes it so clear may
at first seem overwhelming, particularly for those who are used to more freeform lesson planning. In
such a case, it may be necessary to provide training or professional development in order to be able
to fully realize the value of the guide. However, on the flip side, too much structure may become
a problem for some users, particularly experienced educators who may view it as too restrictive.

To overcome these challenges, the guide should also encourage creativity and have flexibility built
into it. The guide should also be subjected to periodic compliance checks by institutions that use it
to ensure that it remains a valid quality control tool without restricting pedagogical innovation.

Limitations of the Study
While the project achieved its primary objectives, several limitations must be acknowledged. These
limitations temper the findings and highlight caution areas when interpreting the results or consid-
ering the guide’s broader application.
Limited Sample Size and Stakeholder Representation: A main limitation is the size and repre-
sentativeness of stakeholders involved in co-creation and testing. The teachers and experts who
contributed to the writing, revision and testing of the guide are rather limited in number, and only
one teacher being part of the co-creation, may not be entirely representative. These participants
were passionate about innovation and may have been more inclined to embrace a design guide for
curriculum development than some other teachers, thus introducing potential bias. Since the guide
was developed and fine-tuned with the help of the participants and their feedback and needs, it is
fully compatible with the specific context of Danish schools, but it may not fully reflect the needs of
all the teachers in these schools. However, teachers from schools with limited infrastructure or those
teachers who were less exposed to makerspace were not directly involved. Therefore, other challenges
for example, the need for more basic training or infrastructure support that they may encounter are
probably not well addressed in the guide. Furthermore, all the co-creation participants and intervie-
wees were from the Danish primary education sector. The validity of the guide for use in secondary
education or for an international audience was not investigated. This limited sampling could restrict
its transferability to other participants beyond the given user group. The co-creation approach was
very effective in capturing the participant’s views but at the same time it was not wide-ranging
enough to include a fuller spectrum of educators. It is possible that a group of participants would
have proposed other design choices. For instance, a group might have focused on student assess-

months or years, and student performance improvements. These are beyond the immediate imple-
mentation. Furthermore, the pilot was conducted in a supportive environment, with the researcher
available to assist the teacher. This may not represent a typical teacher’s independent experience
of using the guide. There is a possibility of a novelty effect, which is when teachers and students
respond positively in a trial setting, but regular use may result in declining adherence or unantic-
ipated practical hurdles. The guide was mainly tested in a science/technology focused project. It
has not been tested in subjects such as language arts, mathematics or humanities (except for in the
scenario-based testing) which may have different pedagogical considerations. Because of these test-
ing limitations, the effectiveness claims for the guide must be considered as preliminary. The positive
feedback collected in DS2 needs further corroboration from broader trials. In academic terms, the
evaluation phase was exploratory rather than confirmatory – good for identifying usability issues
and user impressions, but not yet sufficient for statistical validation or broad assertions of impact.
Ensuring Compliance and Interpretation of Standards: This Gnist Curriculum Design Guide is also
important to support legal and educational standards compliance. However, a limitation is the com-
plexity of these standards and their translation into the content of the guide. Laws and regulations
– GDPR and copyright law, and educational standards – national curriculum goals (Fælles Mål)
can be complex and sometimes ambiguous. Since the study had a design orientation, legal experts
or curriculum authorities were not formally involved in verifying the guide’s interpretations of these
requirements. Some of the guidance in the compliance checklist may therefore be oversimplified or
not fully up to date. For instance, the guide offers tips for complying with GDPR in managing
student data, but GDPR is a wide regulation and complete compliance may depend on the par-
ticular school’s policies or IT infrastructure that are not covered by the guide. The compliance
checklist was developed based on official sources (the Danish Data Protection Agency’s guidelines
for schools), but it was not independently verified for legal accuracy. Likewise, the alignment with
Fælles Mål was achieved through identifying learning objectives in the guide’s templates from the
existing curricula. However, they were not checked by pedagogical supervisors, meaning that there
could be some errors. As one of the main objectives of the guide is to offer legally sound compliance
advice, this limitation means that the input of experts may be required on a regular basis. At this
moment, the guide can be considered a guidance document and not as a set of absolute rules. This
means that teachers and schools may still have to verify the compliance with their particular local
regulations or administrators (if they want verified compliance).
Context-Specific Design: The Gnist Curriculum Design Guide was developed especially for the Dan-
ish basic education system and the learning platform and makerspace concept that was part of the
project. This focus was deliberate to manage scope and to ensure relevance, but it also restricts
the immediate applicability of the guide beyond its initial setting. For instance, the inclusion of
teknologiforståelse (technology comprehension) and references to Fælles Mål firmly embed the guide
in the Danish educational context. Other countries’ educators — or even those from other Danish
schools — would have to transform the guide to fit their systems’ standards and nomenclature.
Even in Denmark, the guide makes certain assumptions about the baseline conditions: Schools have
access to mobile makerspace equipment, and there is a digital learning platform to which curriculum

content can be uploaded. Schools that lack these resources may not be able to use parts of the
guide or may have to change them. These assumptions have however been neglected as development
issues, as they are expected business additions to GNIST Education, and not required from school
bodies to acquire. This is the same way that other design interventions, which are targeted to a
specific niche, work—while the solution is effective in the target context, it is not generic. Although

research study. This limitation is particularly significant when considering the usage scale-up of the
guide. Any further use would have to determine whether modifications are required to suit other
settings, e.g.: To align with the curriculum requirements of another country. To extend the guide
to cover secondary education levels. To adapt for schools with different amounts of technological
support. Knowledge of these limitations makes it possible to change future iterations of the guide to
make them more applicable to other settings while keeping its core features for curriculum structure.
Time and Resource Constraints: In the context of a master’s thesis project, this study was limited
in time and by the channel bandwidth of a single researcher. Because of these constraints, several
trade-offs had to be made, and the limitations that resulted from them should be acknowledged. Not
every idea or feature that was coming up during the co-creation could be implemented. For instance,
an initial teacher suggestion was the development of an online tool that could help with some parts
of the guide (for instance, creating a personalized compliance checklist for a given project). But such
tool exceeded the scope of the project, so the guide is still a static document for now. Limitations in

comparisons of student performance, which compared classes using the guide to those that did not
use it.
Such an analysis would have enhanced the claims about the effectiveness of the guide.
Furthermore, the testing phase was rather narrow in scope as it was limited to one subject area
while it could have been applied to a variety of subjects.
Because of time limitations, some parts of the guide perhaps could have been refined and expanded
a little bit more. An example is the accessibility standards section: The guide does present general
guidelines, but a more extensive treatment, including references to the latest Web Content Acces-
sibility Guidelines (WCAG) and more detailed examples, would have been beneficial for this part
as well. Compressed Research and Testing Cycles The compressed timeline also affected the DRM
phases. The DS1 literature review was comprehensive on key topics in Study 1, but time constraints
meant that some relevant studies or case examples may not have been captured. Further, Study 2,
Descriptive (DS2), was a short-term test that was implemented once in a single pilot implementa-
tion, and thus the long-term impact or adoption trends could not be evaluated.

Conclusion
This thesis aimed at creating and assessing the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide that can serve as
a reference when developing curricula for incorporating a mobile makerspace into Danish primary
schools. The results of the research confirm that the guide is able to perform the function for which
it was intended – as a full service curriculum development tool. The creation of the guide was an
iterative process during which the tool was developed and tested with educators to ensure that all
important facets, such as curriculum relevance, pedagogical appropriateness, and legal matters were
addressed. The use of scenarios in the evaluations revealed that the guide enhanced the teacher’s
capacity to come up with detailed and cohesive lesson plans for maker activities by a high degree. He
mentioned that the guide helped to organize thinking and planning and that it ensured that all the
necessary components, including learning objectives, activities, assessment, and compliance, were
considered. The decision support structure of the guide helped to evoke a reflective practice in the
development of the course, making the teacher to actively think through the pedagogical implications
and the integration of technology in particular. However, initial trials showed some weaknesses, for
instance, ambiguous directions and limited applicability across subjects. These realizations led to
changes such as:
• The simplification of the language used in the checklist.
• Clarification of the legal compliance steps to be taken.
• Stresses the adaptability of the tool for use in different subjects.
After the revisions, the final guide was found to be efficient and easy to use. The main result is that
a properly created guide can be a useful tool for curriculum development, which can help teachers
to develop challenging and meaningful learning experiences that are consistent with the standards
using a problem-based approach. The research proves the effectiveness of the guide in addressing the
gap between theoretical ideas of educational change (makerspace implementation) and the real-life
needs of a teacher.
Contributions to Research and Practice
From a research perspective, this thesis contributes to the body of knowledge on the use of design-
oriented research methodologies in curriculum development. It builds on the work on design-based
research in education by showing how a theory-based iterative development process can produce
a real product that can help real teachers in real schools. The guide is based on learning theory,
instructional design, and human-centered design principles and therefore represents how to apply
pedagogical principles to the development of a curriculum tool. Furthermore, the study gives an
empirical view of teachers’ needs and problems when incorporating new technologies into their prac-
tice, such as makerspaces. By describing how the participants engaged with and benefited from the
guide, this research also helps to contribute to the literature on teacher professional development
and curriculum change. Interestingly, the co-creation approach explains the importance of using
practitioners’ input in the educational design. The outcomes support the participatory design re-
search, which claims that the users’ involvement results in more efficient and applicable solutions.
The Gnist Curriculum Design Guide, in practice, is an immediate contribution to the Danish ed-
ucation system.
It is a tool for teachers and curriculum developers to use when incorporating
teknologiforståelse and maker activities into the learning process. The Danish schools work on the
basis of Fælles Mål, the national curriculum framework that sets learning objectives for every subject
(Commission, 2024). This means that teachers have a good deal of freedom in how they teach these
goals, typically using their own materials and approaches. The guide therefore offers a clear struc-
ture and templates that guarantee that newly created courses or projects are always consistent with

national objectives and standards. For example, a teacher using the guide to create a math unit that

Addressing the Research Questions
Three primary research questions guided this research. Each was addressed during the corresponding
study phase, as summarised below:
• Research question 1: What are the current practices, challenges, and needs in curriculum de-
velopment for Danish basic education, particularly regarding integrating pedagogy, legal require-
ments (Fælles Mål), and technology? The first research question was explored in Descriptive
Study I (DS1) through a systematic literature review and stakeholder analysis. The results of
the study show that at the moment, the curriculum design processes are fragmented; pedagogi-
cal planning, Fælles Mål compliance, and technology integration are planned and implemented
separately. This was congruent with the need for a unified curriculum design framework. DS1
also revealed that there is limited information on how to align creative teaching methods with
mandatory standards and digital literacy goals. These insights established a clear baseline and
directly informed the development of the support tool, effectively answering RQ1.
• Research question 2: What principles and features should an integrated curriculum design
guide include to effectively support educators in aligning pedagogical design with Fælles Mål
requirements and strengthening digital literacy? RQ2 was addressed in the Prescriptive Study
(PS) through the design and development of the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide.
In line
with the gaps revealed in DS1, the research formulated core design principles – compliance
checklists and pedagogical prompts – and incorporated these features into the guide. Key
outcomes include: A teacher-friendly and comprehensive tool that links pedagogical creativity
with compliance requirements.
A simplified format of the template that shows standards
alignment at each step.
Special areas on digital literacy and makerspace.
The guide was
co-created with a teacher and received expert review to ensure its practicality. These results
directly answer RQ2 by providing a concrete solution to the needs identified.
• Research question 3: How can the iterative development and implementation of an example
course serve to evaluate the effectiveness and usability of the developed curriculum design guide,
and what indicators demonstrate its success in improving curriculum development processes?
While a full Descriptive Study II (DS2) was not feasible within the scope of the project, RQ3
was addressed through a pilot evaluation of an example course developed with the help of a
teacher. This made it possible to directly assess the usability and effectiveness of the guide
in practice. The pilot results showed that the guide assisted the teacher in creating a good
quality, coherent, and coherent lesson plan that was consistent with the standards and received
positive feedback on its completeness and usability. Initial signs, including higher conformity
to Fælles Mål and high teacher satisfaction, indicate that the guide enhances the curriculum
development process. This offers evidence of its potential to be used in the real world and
addresses RQ3 while Laying the foundation for a future large-scale evaluation.

9.2
Future Refinement and Implementation Considerations
Another important recommendation is to keep on incremental change by performing more extensive
testing. The current development and evaluation was done with a small number of participants
and topics; expanding trials to classrooms trials, grades, and subjects would give more information
on the range of the guide. This could help, for instance, to reveal subject-specific needs or profes-
sional application of the guide’s content and format. For instance, future updates might provide
more hued examples for the humanities subjects; include new modules according to the changing
educational issues (for instance, provide directions on coding if programming is incorporated into
the learning standards). Also, as the national curricula and regulations are updated periodically,
the guide should be in accordance with the most recent Fælles Mål revisions and legal requirements.
Setting up a preservation schedule or an editorial board to check and update the checklists and
references of the guide is also advisable. The other area that needs to be explored is the effect on
student outcomes. As this thesis was concerned with teacher usage and curriculum design quality,
a natural next step would be to examine student results. Future work could examine whether the
lessons created with the aid of the guide result in: Increased student interest, greater learning gains,
better overall educational results than those developed with no such structured support.
These studies would prove the effectiveness of the guide from the learner’s point of view and reveal
where more work can be done to help student-centered learning. To this end, teachers and schools
must be enabled and empowered to incorporate the guide into their planning processes. A key step
is to provide professional development and training sessions, which could be a Seminar or broadcast
(e.g. by GNIST Education) to introduce the guide to educators, or Early followers’ experiences to
create a community of practice based on the use of the tool. This would help to continue the trend
of easy adoption by incorporating the guide into existing digital platforms. For example, if Danish
schools utilize a certain LMS or the Gnist platform, the guide can be incorporated as an interactive
form or checklist. This would make it possible for teachers to use it naturally within their work
process rather than as an additional chore. Furthermore, to increase the availability, the guide can
be available in both English and Danish and possibly in other languages for bilingual programs.
To ensure long-term usability, mechanisms for ongoing user feedback should be established, so that
teachers can offer suggestions or report problems. Therefore, education authorities might consider
supporting the guide as a recommended tool for curriculum planning. Such policy support could
accelerate the uptake and possibly incorporate the guide’s principles into official recommendations
or teacher training courses.
Final Thoughts on the Guide’s Long-Term Impact
The development of the Gnist Curriculum Design Guide is a forward-thinking project that sits on the
cutting edge of educational reform and real curriculum work. More generally, the guide demonstrates
how educators can be ready for the future in the process of teaching. Thus, through a systematic
integration of pedagogical knowledge and practices and real-world conditions (curricular, legal, tech-
nical), it provides a reference for schools tackling the growing complexity of the modern world. The
guide has the potential to become a mainstay in the gradual integration of technology and making
it into the fabric of everyday learning. As Danish schools gradually implement makerspaces, coding
classes and creative technologies, a structured design framework is required to make this adoption
more coherent and in step with educational standards. This could result in a lasting change; an
innovation-led teaching culture, where teaching is planned with as much rigor as it is experimented
with. Over time, as more educators adopt and build on the guide, it has the potential to create a
community of practice where people share improvements and innovations in curriculum design.
