Abstract
Actinic keratosis (AK) is a common precancerous skin condition caused by prolonged
exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation. Photodynamic therapy (PDT) is widely used for
AK treatment, but its e!cacy varies between lesions, and there is currently no standardized
method for predicting treatment response. This study investigates the potential of optical
coherence tomography angiography (OCTA) to monitor PDT e!cacy by analyzing vascular
changes in AK lesions over time.
A longitudinal dataset of OCTA scans was collected from patients undergoing PDT,
and vascular metrics - including vessel area density (VAD), total vessel length (TVL),
mean vessel length, mean vessel diameter, branchpoint density (BD), and mean tortuosity
- were extracted and analyzed. Results indicate that VAD, TVL, and BD peak at Day
2 post-PDT, likely due to an acute inﬂammatory response, before returning to baseline
levels by Day 30. No statistically signiﬁcant di"erences were detected between Baseline
and Day 30 in any of the vascular metrics, suggesting that vascular remodeling may require
a more extended observation period. Mean tortuosity was identiﬁed as a distinguishing
factor between Responder and Non-responder AK lesions, highlighting its potential as a
predictive biomarker for PDT e!cacy. Future research should explore extended follow-up
periods and additional vascular parameters to reﬁne AK classiﬁcation and PDT response
assessment further.

CHAPTER1
Introduction
1.1
Context
Actinic Keratosis (AK) is a common precancerous skin condition caused by chronic
exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation, primarily from the sun. It manifests as rough,
scaly lesions on sun-exposed areas such as the face, scalp, and hands. While many AK
lesions remain benign, some may progress to squamous cell carcinoma (SCC), a type of
skin cancer. The risk of malignant transformation underscores the importance of early
detection and e"ective management of these lesions [8]. AK has a global prevalence rate
of approximately 14%, highlighting its signiﬁcant disease burden [9].
Despite its high
prevalence, diagnosing and classifying AK lesions remains challenging. Traditional clinical
assessment methods rely on visual inspection and in some cases, histopathology, which can
be both subjective and invasive. Additionally, treatment selection is complex, as not all
AK lesions respond equally to therapies, such as photodynamic therapy (PDT). Identifying
biomarkers that can classify lesions and predict treatment response would greatly enhance
clinical decision-making and improve patient outcomes.
A promising approach for non-invasive lesion assessment is optical coherence tomogra-
phy (OCT), an imaging technique that enables high-resolution, in vivo visualization of skin
structures at the micrometer scale. More speciﬁcally, OCT angiography (OCTA) allows
for the mapping of microvascular networks without the need for contrast agents. OCTA
o"ers a novel method for assessing blood ﬂow dynamics post-PDT and identifying poten-
tial biomarkers for predicting PDT response. By providing quantitative vascular metrics,
OCTA has the potential to improve diagnostic accuracy and guide treatment selection.
1.2
OCT and OCTA
For this project, the imaging modality, OCT, was used to collect data.
OCT is a
non-invasive imaging technique that provides high-resolution, cross-sectional images of
biological tissues, with an axial resolution of 1-15 µm and an imaging depth of 1-2 mm in
scattering tissue. Originally developed for ophthalmic imaging, OCT has expanded into
multiple medical ﬁelds, including dermatology, cardiology, and oncology. In dermatology,

1.2.1
How OCT works
OCT functions similarly to ultrasound imaging but uses light instead of sound waves.
It measures the echo time delay and intensity of backscattered near-infrared light. This
imaging modality relies on low-coherence interferometry to generate high-resolution images
of microstructures within the tissue. Low coherence interferometry is based on Michelson
interferometry, and a representation of the key components is shown in Figure 1.1. It
consists of the following:
• A light source, which generates low-coherence light.
• A beam splitter that divides the light into two beams: a reference beam, which is
directed along the reference path, and a sample beam, which is directed toward the
sample.
• The sample path, where the sample beam is focused on the biological tissue (sample).
• The reference path, where the reference beam travels along a known path length,
which can be adjusted. The role of the reference path is to provide a time delay,
allowing the system to measure the echo time delay of the light reﬂected from the
sample.
• The detector, where the light returning from both the sample and reference paths
recombines at the detector via the beam splitter. The detector records interference
only when the optical path lengths of the sample and the reference arm match the
coherence length of the light source.
OCT images are generated from the axial scans (A-scans), which measure the backscat-
tering of light as a function of depth in the sample, as shown in Figure 1.2. By combining
multiple A-scans through transverse scanning, B-scans are generated. B-scans are two-
dimensional, cross-sectional images, and three-dimensional images can be obtained through
raster scans [1].

1.2.2
OCTA
One of the key advancements in OCT technology is OCT angiography (OCTA). OCTA
is an extension of OCT that, based on OCT B-scans, enables the visualization of vascular
networks in biological tissues without the need for administering contrast agents. OCTA
works by acquiring multiple B-scans at the same transverse location and detecting motion
contrast from moving red blood cells within microvessels, allowing for the assessment of skin
perfusion and microvascular networks without external contrast agent injections. OCTA
detects blood ﬂow by analyzing variations in the backscattered OCT signal over time,
distinguishing between static tissue and dynamic blood ﬂow [10, 2]. Figure 1.3 presents
a simpliﬁed schematic representation of the principle of OCTA. The diagram illustrates
signal sampling along an A-scan at ﬁve points. Pixels 1, 2, and 5 correspond to static
tissue, where the OCT signal intensity remains constant over time. In contrast, pixels 3
and 4 are positioned within a blood vessel, where the movement of red blood cells induces
changes in the OCT signal. This di"erence in OCT signaling recorded over time enables
the detection and visualization of vasculature.
By doing multiple B-scans across a region, a volumetric dataset can be generated and
used to produce en face images. An en face projection provides a top-down view of the
microvasculature at speciﬁc depths in the skin.

1.3
Problem statement & objectives
AK is a common skin condition and one of the most frequently diagnosed dermatological
diseases in the older population [9]. However, there is currently no standardized method
to compare AK lesions, making it challenging to consistently assess and monitor lesion
progression and treatment response. Furthermore, despite the widespread use of PDT for
AK treatment, no reliable biomarkers exist to predict treatment outcomes. While OCTA
imaging provides detailed vascular information, its potential role in predicting PDT e!cacy
remains unclear.
This study aims to address these challenges by investigating OCTA-
derived vascular metrics as potential biomarkers for PDT e!cacy and for working towards
a standardized approach for lesion comparison. To achieve this, the study focuses on the
following objectives:
1. To analyze changes in AK lesions following PDT by comparing vascular metrics
generated from longitudinal data collected at multiple time points post-treatment.
2. To identify predictive biomarkers for PDT e!cacy by comparing vascular metrics of
the lesions that responded to PDT to those that did not.
Both objectives will be pursued by comparing vascular metrics generated from OCTA
en face maximum intensity projections (MIPs) using OCTAVA. By achieving these objec-
tives, this project aims to fulﬁll multiple outcomes. Firstly, it seeks to determine whether
PDT leads to measurable improvements that can be detected using OCT and OCTA. Sec-
ondly, it aims to identify biomarkers that can predict the success of PDT in lesions. Finally,
this research contributes to the advancement of OCT and OCTA in clinical practice, as
well as to the broader body of medical knowledge.

CHAPTER2
Actinic keratosis: Pathogenesis, diagnosis, and
treatment
This chapter provides a theoretical foundation for understanding AK, including its
pathogenesis, clinical diagnosis, and treatment options, focusing on PDT. It sets the foun-
dation for exploring advanced imaging techniques, such as OCT, in assessing treatment
e!cacy, which will be discussed in later sections of this thesis.
2.1
Pathogenesis
The human skin comprises three layers: the epidermis, dermis, and hypodermis. The
epidermis is the outermost layer primarily composed of keratinocytes, which play a vital
role in maintaining the skin’s structural integrity and immune defense.
The epidermis
undergoes constant renewal, with keratinocytes proliferating in the deepest layer of the
epidermis (stratum basale) and migrating toward the skin surface to form the stratum
corneum. AK develops in the epidermis and is characterized histologically by atypical,
dyskeratotic, and necrotic keratinocytes [11].
AK lesions are mainly caused by prolonged exposure to ultraviolet (UV) radiation, but
environmental and intrinsic factors inﬂuence the prevalence of the skin lesion. Lesions
typically appear in chronically sun-exposed areas, such as the face, scalp, neck, and chest.
The incidence of AK lesions increases with age and cumulative sun exposure, with higher
prevalence observed in populations living closer to the equator due to UV radiation expo-
sure [12]. Furthermore, the development of AK lesions is more common in individuals with
lighter skin tones, particularly those with Fitzpatrick skin types I and II [12] [9]. There are
several mechanisms underlying AK formation. The processes through which AK arises and
progresses are primarily driven by UV radiation exposure, which triggers a series of molec-
ular pathways at the cellular level. These pathways contribute to inﬂammation, immune
suppression, disrupted apoptosis, and abnormal cell di"erentiation [13, 14]. Figure 2.1 il-
lustrates some of the most common processes behind the initiation and progression of AK
lesions.
UV radiation from the sun consists of di"erent spectral regions with varying wave-
lengths and biological e"ects. UVB radiation (290-320 nm) is one of the primary causes
of AK formation, as it induces DNA damage in keratinocytes in the basal layer of the

epidermis. UVB exposure leads to thymidine dimer formation in DNA and RNA, resulting

signiﬁcant, as this gene typically halts cell division to allow for DNA repair. When p53
is mutated, damaged keratinocytes and potentially neoplastic cells continue proliferating
instead of undergoing elimination [13, 14].
UVA radiation (320-400 nm) is more abundant in the atmosphere and penetrates deeper
into the skin due to its longer wavelength. UVA radiation contributes to AK lesion for-
mation by generating reactive oxygen species (ROS) in the dermis, the layer beneath the
epidermis. These molecules cause oxidative damage to DNA, cell membranes, and pro-
teins, disrupting normal cellular functions. This damage interferes with cell signaling and
communication, promoting a pro-inﬂammatory and pro-tumourigenic environment, lead-
ing to uncontrolled cell growth and increasing the risk of neoplastic transformation. These
processes, combined, contribute to abnormal and unchecked keratinocyte proliferation,
eventually resulting in visible AK lesions observed on the skin surface [14, 3].
2.2
Diagnosis
The presentation of AK lesions varies, but they are usually red, scaly, patches on the
skin that may be thickened and sometimes itchy. The surrounding skin typically exhibits
signs of chronic sun damage, such as elastosis and wrinkles. The appearance of AKs can
di"er signiﬁcantly, and diagnosis is based on a clinical assessment involving both palpation
and visual examination. As a result, diagnosis relies on individual clinical judgment, which
can lead to inconsistencies in lesion identiﬁcation and challenges in classiﬁcation [12, 13, 3].
Histologically, AK lesions present as atypical keratinocytes within the epidermis, char-
acterized by increased mitotic activity and necrotic cells. These keratinocytes exhibit a
loss of polarity and contain large, irregular, dark-stained nuclei with abnormal cytoplasm.
Typically, the atypical keratinocytes are distributed within the epidermis but spare the

While skin biopsies remain the most reliable method for diagnosing AK lesions, they

features of AK, including disrupted skin layers, white streaks, and dots associated with
dense hyperkeratotic areas, and thickened epidermis [15].
2.2.1
Olsen Classiﬁcation Scheme
There is no universally accepted "gold standard" for classifying AK lesions, and treat-
ment is often recommended due to the risk of AK progressing into squamous cell carcinoma
(SCC). One of the most widely used classiﬁcation schemes, and the one used in this project,
is the Olsen Classiﬁcation Scheme, which categorizes AK lesions into three classes: Grade
I (mild), Grade II (moderate), and Grade III (severe) [4]. A more detailed description is
provided in Figure 2.2.
Figure 2.2: Olsen Classiﬁcation Scheme. Generated from [4].
While the Olsen Classiﬁcation Scheme is used in clinical practice, it has been criticized
for its weak correlation with histopathology, low reproducibility, and inconsistency among
experts. These limitations make it unreliable for accurately assessing AK severity or guid-
ing treatment decisions, as it may lead to an inaccurate assessment of lesion progression
risk [16, 17].
2.3
Treatment
As AK has the potential to progress to SCC, early identiﬁcation and treatment are
essential. Furthermore, as AK lesions typically appear in highly visible areas, such as the
face, treatments that yield good cosmetic outcomes are preferred. Field cancerization is
also critical in the development and treatment of AK. Field cancerization describes the
presence of UV-induced genetic mutations in clinically normal-appearing skin surrounding
visible AK lesions. This indicates that the entire a"ected area has undergone carcinogenic
changes rather than just isolated lesions [13]. Because ﬁeld cancerization represents an area
of subclinical change, targeting individual AK lesions alone may be insu!cient. For this
reason, ﬁeld-directed therapies are recommended to address visible and subclinical lesions
[3].
One example of ﬁeld-directed therapy is photodynamic therapy (PDT). PDT is a treat-
ment modality that utilizes a photosensitizing agent, oxygen, and a therapeutic light of a
speciﬁc wavelength to destroy targeted cells. The photosensitizer is applied intravenously
or topically, depending on the target site, and selectively accumulates in neoplastic cells.

2.3. TREATMENT
9
The target area is then exposed to therapeutic light, which excites the photosensitizer.
This triggers one of two reactions:
1. A Type II reaction transfers energy to oxygen molecules, producing reactive oxygen
species (ROS), primarily singlet oxygen.
2. A Type I reaction, where ROS is generated through electron transfer rather than
energy transfer.
The singlet oxygen generated from either Type I or Type II reactions is believed to interact
with lipids and proteins in cell membranes, leading to plasma membrane disruption and
damage to organelles, including the mitochondria, the endoplasmatic reticulum, and lyso-
somes. Secondary e"ects include blood vessel constriction, thrombosis, edema, and immune
cell recruitment [18]. The extent of the treatment response depends on several factors, in-
cluding the therapeutic light’s type, dosage, and intensity, as well as the photosensitizer’s
type, preparation, and application method. When high doses of light and photosensitizer
are applied to well-oxygenated tissue, they trigger cell apoptosis and necrosis, a mechanism
utilized in PDT for AK [18].
Figure 2.3: Mechanism of PDT. Reprinted from [5]
Depending on the target tissue, there are di"erent types of PDT. For AK treatment, one
of the most common approaches is topical PDT, which uses 5-aminolevulinic acid (ALA) or
its methyl ester, methyl aminolevulinate (MAL), as the photosensitizer [19]. The treatment
protocol used in this study is MAL-PDT. MAL-PDT begins with the topical application of
MAL, a prodrug selectively taken up by neoplastic keratinocytes. The cream is typically
applied to the treatment area and left for a speciﬁc time to allow su!cient penetration.
After incubation, the area is exposed to a particular wavelength of visible light, which
activates the photosensitizer.
2.3.1
Cellular Mechanism of Action in MAL-PDT
Once MAL is absorbed into the targeted cells, it is metabolized via the heme biosynthe-
sis pathway to produce protoporphyrin IX (PpIX), a highly photoactive compound. This
process is tightly regulated in normal cells, and PpIX is rapidly converted into heme. How-
ever, in dysplastic cells, this conversion is impaired, leading to an accumulation of PpIX.
When the treated area is exposed to the therapeutic light, PpIX absorbs photons and

Methods
This chapter outlines the methods used to investigate the role of OCT and OCTA-
derived vascular metrics for monitoring the e!cacy of PDT on AK lesions. It details the
study design, including treatment protocols and data collection procedures. The processing
and analysis of OCT-A scans, including the extraction of vascular metrics using OCTAVA,
are described. Additionally, the statistical approaches used to compare lesion responses
and identify potential biomarkers are explained. This section ensures transparency and
reproducibility by providing a comprehensive overview of how the study was conducted.
3.1
Study design and data collection
3.1.1
The Cicaplast Study
Data for this project were obtained from the Cicaplast Study conducted at Bispebjerg
Hospital. The Cicaplast Study is a randomized, intra-individual controlled trial involv-
ing 20 participants, designed to investigate the impact of a commercial cream containing
madecassoside and 5 % panthenol in the post-PDT procedure for AK.
The 20 participants were recruited from the Department of Dermatology at Bispebjerg
Hospital and had been referred for AK treatment. Two symmetrical skin areas within the
same anatomical region - on the face or décolleté - were selected as test areas. A clinical
evaluation identiﬁed the AK lesions, which were then marked, numbered, and graded
according to the Olsen clinical classiﬁcation scheme [4]. To ensure consistency in tracking
the same lesions throughout the study, AK lesions were mapped onto a transparent ﬁlm
template using a permanent marker, documented by digital photography, and selectively
imaged with OCT.
The Cicaplast study was conducted from August 1, 2023, to December 31, 2024. Each
participant underwent clinical evaluation and intervention at ﬁve time points over 30 days.
These time points are illustrated in Figure 3.1 and include:
• Baseline (Day 0): The participant’s AK lesions were mapped, graded, and imaged
using OCT, followed by PDT treatment.
• Follow-up visits on Days 2±1, 7±1, 14±2, and 30±3.

3.1.1.1
Daylight PDT
After selecting, mapping, and imaging the test areas, gentle superﬁcial curettage was
performed across the entire test area. Immediately after pretreatment, the photosensitizer,
Metvix 160mg/g (Galderma Nordic AB, Uppsala, Sweden) was applied to both test areas.
Following a 30 minute incubation period without coverage, participants were exposed to
two hours of indoor daylight (MultiLite, German Engineering, Erlangen, Germany), which
uses light-emitting diodes at wavelengths 415, 585, and 635 nm to match the absorption
spectrum of PpIX. After light exposure, the Metvix cream was removed from the test area
using water.

3.2.2
OCTAVA
All MIPs were processed using OCTAVA (OCTA Vascular Analyzer) to generate quan-
titative vascular metrics. OCTAVA is an open-source toolbox that automates the prepro-
cessing, segmentation, and data analysis of OCTA MIPs [22] [7]. OCTAVA characterized
microvasculature by binarizing and skeletonizing images to extract vascular metrics. The
vascular metrics USD in this project include:
• Vessel area density (VAD)
• Total vessel length (TVL)
• Mean vessel length
• Mean vessel diameter
• Branchpoint density (BD)
• Mean tortuosity
These six vascular metrics were selected for their ability to describe key structural prop-
erties of the microvasculature and were chosen based on previous comprehensive literature
review by Untracht et al. [7]. The six metrics, their corresponding unit, and descriptions
can be found in Figure 3.7.

CHAPTER5
Discussion
This discussion interprets the ﬁndings of this study, in relation to the research objec-
tives and existing literature. By analyzing vascular changes in AK lesions after PDT and
identifying potential biomarkers for treatment e!cacy, this study aimed to address the lack
of standardized lesion comparison methods and reliable predictors of PDT response. The
results provide insight into whether OCTA-derived vascular metrics can serve as objective
indicators of treatment success and lesion progression. This chapter will examine the key
ﬁndings in the context of previous research, along with the strengths and limitations of
this study.
5.1
Interpretation of key ﬁndings
This project aimed to analyze changes in AK lesions following PDT and identify poten-
tial predictive biomarkers for treatment e!cacy. Out of the 42 AK lesions monitored, 35
lesions cleared, representing an 83.33% clearance rate. This aligns with previously reported
clearance rates, which - depending on the lesion severity - have shown complete response
rates ranging from 69% to 93% at the three-month follow-ups. [19, 23] These 35 cleared
AK lesions were categorized by lesion grade and compared with PD skin at Baseline. Vessel
quantiﬁcation revealed a signiﬁcant di"erence in mean vessel length between PD skin and
cleared AK III lesions, with the latter exhibiting shorter vessel lengths, demonstrated in
Figure 4.2c. Additionally, mean tortuosity demonstrated a clear trend of increasing vessel
tortuosity with higher AK grade, as observed in Figure 4.2f. This ﬁnding aligns with a
previous study that also utilized OCTAVA for vascular parameter quantiﬁcation [6].
Among the ﬁve time points at which the lesions were monitored, Day 2 exhibited most
notable changes. On Day 2, VAD, TVL, and BD peaked, as seen in Figure 4.3. This
suggests an immediate vascular response of AK lesions to PDT, followed by a return to
Baseline values, also underlined by the lack of statistically signiﬁcant di"erences in vascular
metrics between Baseline and Day 30 for all test groups (Figure A.8 and Figure A.10). The
increase in VAD, TVL, and BD could potentially be attributed to erythema, which is a
known post-PDT skin reaction. Erythema has previously been investigated by Wiegell et.
al [24], who assessed erythema both visually and objectively and observed a post-PDT
increase when comparing the treatment area before PDT to two days post-treatment.

CHAPTER 5. DISCUSSION
Although the AK lesions were clinically assessed as cleared, the microvasculature does not
appear to undergo signiﬁcant changes - aside from the Day 2 response - from Baseline to
Day 30.
Numerous previous studies have reported clearance of AK lesions following PDT. It
is common to assess treatment outcomes clinically using visual inspection and palpation
and to conduct follow-up assessments at least 3 months post-PDT [20, 21]. Fredman et
al. [25] previously observed changes in vascular parameters post-PDT at 3-month- and
12-month follow-ups. Due to the limited availability of comparable studies, it remains
unclear whether the lack of vascular changes in AK lesion tissue observed in this study
is attributable to the study time constraints or other factors.
These ﬁndings suggest
that a longer observation period may be necessary to fully capture the extent of vascular
remodeling following PDT as it could indicate that microvascular changes may require
more than 30 days to manifest.
Finally, the comparison between Responder and Non-responder AK lesions at Base-
line in Figure 4.4 revealed a statistically signiﬁcant di"erence in mean tortuosity (Fig-
ure 4.4f), with Non-responder AK lesions exhibiting signiﬁcantly higher vessel tortuosity.
This ﬁnding is in line with previous ﬁndings of treatment-resistant AK lesions having a
more disorganized vascular network [6].
5.2
Unexpected results
One of the unexpected ﬁndings in this study was the lack of signiﬁcant di"erences in
vascular parameters between Baseline and Day 30, despite clinical clearance of AK lesions.
The observed return of vascular metrics to Baseline levels by Day 30 suggests that vascular
remodeling may occur over a longer time frame than the study period allowed.
Another surprising result was that vascular metrics did not strongly di"erentiate be-
tween AK lesions of varying severity (Grade I-III) at Baseline. While mean tortuosity
showed a clear trend of increasing with lesion severity, other metrics did not exhibit signiﬁ-
cant variation. This contrasts previous ﬁndings where signiﬁcantly higher vascular density
and complexity were associated with more severe lesions [6]. A possible explanation could
be that the results in this study are made from full area scans (6 mm x 6 mm), instead
of a representative subsection of each scan, as it has been done previously by Fredman et
al.[6]. Another reason for the low di"erentiation in metrics between lesion grades could
potentially be the use of the Olsen classiﬁcation scheme. As mentioned earlier, the ap-
plicability of the Olsen classiﬁcation scheme has been questioned as it might not be very
useful for assessing AK lesions in this setting. Therefore, this could potentially also have
an e"ect on the results, especially in the case of determining biomarkers for categorizing
the AK lesions.

ing and AK treatment monitoring:
• Longitudinal study design: Unlike previous studies that relied on single follow-
up assessments, this study incorporated multiple time points. This approach allowed
for tracking vascular changes and provided a more detailed view of the AK lesion
response to PDT.
• Quantitative OCTA Analysis Using an Open-Source Tool (OCTAVA): The

Limitations
Despite its strengths, this study has multiple limitations that should be acknowledged.
• Small sample size: The study included 20 participants and 42 lesions, with only 5
Non-responder lesions, making it di!cult to draw ﬁrm conclusions regarding predic-
tors of PDT resistance.
• Variability in image segmentation: While OCTAVA provides automated image
processing, the user-chosen settings can inﬂuence vascular metrics. Further, due to
limited experience in image segmentation this presents as a limitation of this study.
• Limited follow-up period: longer term follow-up is necessary to determine whether
OCTA-derive vascular metrics can detect remodeling, and also to make the study
more comparable to previous studies.

CHAPTER6
Conclusion
This study investigated the potential of OCT and OCTA-derived vascular metrics to
monitor the e!cacy of PDT for AK lesions. Analyzing longitudinal OCTA scans it aimed
to identify vascular biomarkers that could predict lesion response and improve lesion clas-
siﬁcation.
The ﬁndings indicate that vascular metrics, particularly VAD, TVL, and BD, exhibit
a sharp increase at Day 2 post-PDT, likely due to an acute inﬂammatory response. How-
ever, by Day 30, these metrics returned to Baseline levels, suggesting that the immediate
response does not translate into signiﬁcant long-term vascular remodeling within the ob-
served timeframe. Additionally, mean tortuosity was identiﬁed as a distinguishing factor
between Responder and Non-responder AK lesions, highlighting its potential as a predictive
biomarker for PDT e!cacy.
6.1
Future perspectives
This study provides insights into the microvascular response of AK lesions to PDT,
demonstrating the feasibility of OCTA-derived metrics for longitudinal tracking of lesion
development. Further research is needed to establish OCTA as a robust tool for AK and
PDT monitoring and treatment response predictions.
Standardized imaging protocols,
improved segmentation techniques, and larger sample sizes will increase the reliability of
OCTA-derived metrics. The Cicaplast study used the Olsen classiﬁcation scheme, which,
while widely adopted, has been criticized for low reproducibility and weak correlation with
histopathology. Future research could explore alternative classiﬁcation models. Further
investigation of how microvascular characteristics relate to lesion grades could enhance
classiﬁcation accuracy.
In addition to the vascular parameters analyzed in this study,
future research could include investigating microvascular ﬂow dynamics using Doppler-
OCT or other ﬂow-sensitive imaging techniques. Another interesting study direction could
be structural skin changes such as epidermal thickness using OCT.
Integrating OCTA into clinical workﬂows could ultimately reﬁne lesion classiﬁcation,
optimize treatment selection, and personalized PDT treatment protocols. These advance-
ments could contribute to a deeper understanding of treatment responses, supporting
