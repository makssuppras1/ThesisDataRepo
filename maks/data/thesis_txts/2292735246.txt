Summary
This project is an experimentally study about heat transfer in pulsating refrigerant flow for
usage in a heat-pump or refrigerants system.
The liquid refrigerant was expanded to the
evaporator pressure in pulses and the evaporator heat transfer was studied, the pulsations
was generated by a solenoid valve where the time fraction between the open- and cycle time
express the opening degree of the valve. By set the opening degree constant and vary the
cycle time the amplitude of refrigerant flow will change and the resulting heat transfer can be
studied. The results were compared with a continuous refrigerant flow regulated by a motor
controlled needle valve. Prior to this study an experimental rig was designed and build but
not initiated, this project contains the experimental start-up phase for the test rig including re-
designs of some components, theory about the control system and methods used for calibrating
the instruments. The first experimentally results for incomplete evaporation were performed
and studied with respect to the heat transfer coefficient and further improvements for the test
rig. The results for the heat transfer was highly depended at the measuring method and further
study about heat transfer for complete evaporation has to be performed for a final conclusion
whether pulsations will enhance the heat transfer or not.

Project description
This project is an experimental study about heat transfer in pulsating refrigerant flow for usage
in a heat pump or refrigerant system. When the liquid refrigerant expands to the evaporator
the pressure will drop below saturation and the refrigerant will start to flash, i.e. boil by other
means than heat addition to the fluid. The expansion process can be a capillary tube, an
electronic expansion valve or a thermostatic expansion. In this study two electronic expansion
valves are compared. The first is a Danfoss AKV valve and the second is a Danfoss ETS valve.
The first works by the principle of flow modulating (on/off pulsating flow) and is controlled
by applying current through an electrical coil that opens the valve. The second valve provides
continuous flow to the evaporator and is controlled by a stepper motor that positions a needle
against the valve body, thereby precisely controlling the continuous flow. The heat transfer
coefficient in the evaporator section will be studied at different pulse frequencies, to see if this
pulsating flow will have a heat transfer enhancement. By increasing heat transfer coefficient
in the evaporator the specific heat transfer surface area will be lower and a smaller evaporator
can be used for the same cooling load.
The project is part of a post doctoral project, where the first prototype of the test rig has
already been designed and build. The operation of the test rig has not been initiated prior
to this project. Therefore, a typical experimental start-up phase including refrigerant charg-
ing, tuning PI-controllers, installing additional sensors and removing noise etc. is part of the
project. A general overview of the system design, are given and an evaluation of the chosen
components, but not the dimensioning or the construction phase, which was done prior to
the current project. In this project, the evaporator test section will be further improved by
re-design. The evaporator is a tube in tube design where refrigerant flows in the inner tube
and secondary fluid flows in the annulus between the two tubes. For centering the two tubes
and mixing the secondary fluid in the annulus for better temperature measurements, a mixing-
block will be designed.
The start-up phase will include the adjustments for the control system, how the PI controls
works and how they regulate the system. When the mixing-block, the control system and the
small adjustments is done, the experiments will be performed. When the experimental part is
done, the pressure transducers, thermocouples and mass flow meter will be calibrated and the

CHAPTER1
Introduction
The energy consumption transforms towards a larger share of renewable energy, whether the
energy comes from solar, hydro, geothermal or wind energy the different technologies has to
be studied for further improvements. The utilization of wind and solar cells has shown an
enormous increase the last decades and seems to continue. National political agreements and
international negotiations seem to set the path for both stakeholders, producers and consumers
for lowering the fossil fuel consumption.
In the light of that the maximum utilization of these fluctuating and in some order not pre-
dictable power sources has to be studied. Countries like Sweden and Norway with a mountain-
ous terrain have succeeded with hydropower for stabilizing the fluctuating part of the renewable
energy system to their baseload. Denmark success with wind turbines, however these systems
need implementation of a buffer system for maximum utilization. The buffer system can etc.
be electric storage (not developed for large scale yet), hydrogen storage or a thermal storage,
where the thermal storage system attempts to be a good solution for places with a highly devel-
oped district-heating network. The electricity produced by fluctuating energy sources can be
utilized by a heat pump system, the heat pump utilize energy from a lower temperature source
and pump it to a higher temperature sink by adding fuel to a compressor. The compressor
is primarily driven by an electric motor and the overall primary energy efficiency depends on
the efficiency for the electricity production. The danish energy agency shows scenarios where
industrial scale heat pump systems will be a part of the future energy system for stabilize an
increased renewable part of the system [1].
Whether the electricity is produced by renewable energy or fossil fuel (in the transition period),
the thermal energy produced by a heat pump has a higher utilization factor than energy pro-
duced by traditional oil burners or electric resistance heaters. The thermal energy is primarily
used for industrial process or space heating, according to the international energy agency (IEA)
is a third of the final energy consumption used for buildings, where a half of it is used for space
heating and cooling as well as hot water [2]. In the light of a huge energy consumption and
growing building areas, these users seems to represent a significant opportunity for reducing
the energy consumption.
For space heating and cooling heat pumps is the predominant technology and exist in various
capacities, form small domestic units to large industrial plants. The heat source can etc. be
the outdoor environment air, ground heat or process waste heat. The standard heat pump

In this project pulses of refrigerant will be evaporated in the evaporator, the pulses is generated
by the expansion valve by open and close the valve. Different types of expansions devises exist
and this project will focus on two of them, a motor regulated needle valve (ETS) and a solenoid
valve (AKV). The motor regulated needle valve has the opportunity to be regulated very pre-
cisely that generates a continuous refrigerant flow to the evaporator. The solenoid valve can
either be open or close, this will generate a pulsating refrigerant flow to the evaporator.
Two-phase flow occurs inside the evaporator when the saturated refrigerant expands to the
evaporator pressure, and the refrigerant starts to boil. The pulsations are expected to change
the flow pattern and thereby increase the liquid-wall contact area, this results in an enhance-
ment for the heat transfer. During the pulsations reserved flow can occur at the closing time,
this results in local pressure drops and thereby increases the local evaporation and heat trans-
fer. If the frequency for the pulses is too high the pulsations will act like a continuous flow
and no changes happens, and if the frequency is too low the wall will dry out and a lower heat
transfer occur.
In this project both the pressure difference across the expansion valve, the mass flow rate
and the pulse frequency will be taken into consideration. For studying the heat transfer a
experimental test rig was build prior to this project and will be described during the next
chapter.

CHAPTER2
The system
The system is designed to simulate the thermodynamically conditions in a standard refrigera-
tion cycle (or heat pump cycle). The working principle for a standard refrigeration cycle can
be seen in figure 2.1 where the cycle is drawn in a P-h diagram. From state 1 to state 2 the
compressor compress the refrigerant vapor. From state 2 to 3 the condenser condense the com-
pressed vapor by release heat to the surroundings. When the condensed refrigerant is cooled
below the saturation temperature the liquid is sub-cooled and will be indicated by Tsc. From
state 3 to 4 the sub-cooled liquid expands to the evaporator pressure below the saturation
pressure and the refrigerant start to flash i.e. boil by heat addition to the refrigerant. From
state 4 to 1 the refrigerant evaporates by heat addition from the heat source. When the liquid
is evaporated the vapor can still be heated to ensure no liquid drops enters the compressor.
The temperature difference between the dew point and the temperature at the suction side
of the compressor is called superheat indicated by Tsh. The test rig is designed to study the
evaporator in a refrigeration cycle by pulsating refrigerant into the evaporator. The degree
of superheat and sub-cooling is an important parameter for a well working refrigeration cycle.
However the phase change in the condenser will not be studied in this experiment, and a pump
can be used instead of a compressor. A pump cycle is less sensitive for pressure changes there
can occur by the pulsating refrigerant flow which is the focus in this project.

Description
The process can be described in a P-h diagram similar to the diagram showed for the stan-
dard refrigeration cycle. The process for the test rig can be seen in figure 2.3 where the state
numbering referred to figure 2.2. At state 1 the refrigerant pump pressurize the refrigerant to
the high pressure at state 2. At state 2 the flow splits to state 3 and 8. From state 3 to 4 the
refrigerant expands to the condenser pressure and enters the two phase region. The receiver
separates the vapor from the liquid refrigerant, the vapor leaves the receiver from the top and
mix with the vapor from the test cycle before it will enter the condenser. The refrigerant enters
the condenser as vapor and condense to liquid at state 7, and will then enter the receiver.
At state 8 the preheater heats the pressurized liquid refrigerant to desired sub-cooling tem-
perature at state 9. From state 9 to 10 the refrigerant expands to the evaporator test section
(ES1) where a part of the evaporation will take place. The refrigerant will then leave the test
evaporator as two phase flow and the complete evaporation will take place from state 11 to
12 in the evaporator (ES2). The refrigerant leaves the evaporator as superheated vapor. For
ensure superheated vapor though a mass flow meter at state 13, the superheater heats the
vapor a few degrees. At stage 6 the vapor mix with vapor from the bypass cycle and enters
the condenser.

Refrigerant velocity in condenser
During the first experiments the evaporation temperature was in the range from 12 to 15 ◦C

at the mass flow rate. Lowering the glycol temperature could solve this, but a lower glycol
temperature means less capacity for the secondary refrigeration cycle. The capacity for the
secondary refrigeration cycle was calculated in EES the results can be seen in Appendix A,
figure A.4. For maintaining a glycol temperature at 1 ◦C the evaporator in the secondary
refrigeration cycle evaporates at -10 ◦C, if the condenser pressure should be decreased 10 ◦C
the resulting evaporation temperature will be roughly -20 ◦C and the capacity will be 1.8 kW.

and the evaporator at the secondary cycle will not make sense and another solution was used.

mixing the vapor at state 13 with the liquid refrigerant at state 4. By doing this the condenser
load will maintain the same but a two phase flow will enter the condenser. The vapor will
condense and the liquid will be cooled to the controlled temperature. The old and new pipe

CHAPTER4
Data acquisition
In this chapter the different data acquisition (DAQ) techniques used during the experiment
will be described. Data acquisition can be performed in many ways, the right method de-
pends at the accuracy needed and then the budget for the experiment or application to be
controlled. The physical quantities measured during the experiment were temperature, pres-
sure (mass/area) and mass flow (mass/time). As shown in chapter 2 section 2.3 these three
quantities can be used for calculating the heat transfer coefficient. General for all the quanti-
ties is the digital or analogue signal that has to be converted, the signals is created by different
electronic sensors used in the instruments. For example will the value of a resistance change as
a function of the temperature, the change in resistance can be converted to a analogue voltage
and logged by a data logger device etc. a computer. If the resistor is in a certain distance away
from the data logger the resistance in the lead will cause a voltage drop due to the resistance
over in the lead and the logged signal has to be corrected for the lead resistance.
The signal used for the DAQ is primarily milliampere to prevent voltage drop over the cables
used for connecting the instruments. Basically for the electronic sensors used in measuring
instruments is the linearity between the input and output. This chapter will describe the ba-
sically functions of the instruments but not the electronic behind the devices etc. amplifying
the output from the sensors in the instruments.

Two types of temperature measuring techniques were used for the experimental set-up. Ther-
mocouples was used for data samples for calculating the heat transfer coefficient and resistive
temperature detectors (RTD) was used for the control system. The difference between the two
types of temperature measurements will be described in the following two subsections.

4.1.2
Resistive temperature detectors
As mentioned at the beginning of this chapter, temperature can be measured form the resis-
tance change over a resistor due to temperature changes. These resistors are available with
various accuracy specifications form different manufactures. The most common type has a
platinum resistor where the resistance is given at 0 ◦C. The resistance is 100 Ωand 1000 Ω
for a PT100 and PT1000 sensor respectively. The resistance will change linear as a function

5.1
Proportional Integral Control
For controlling the system a proportional integral controller was used for each controlled com-
ponent.
The proportional integral (PI) control, is a method for maintain the control of a
varying system. This can be done by a feedback signal from the system, the feedback sig-
nals used in these control systems is voltage for pressure and resistance for temperature. The
feedback is the signal the controller will try to regulate until the desired feedback is obtained.
The feedback signal is desired from the user by entering a set point to the controller. If the
feedback differs from the set point, the control system will act and try to adjust a controlled
component until the set point and the feedback signal is equal. The controlled component can
etc. be a valve, heating element or the speed of a pump and the unit of the output signal for
the controlled component is not necessarily the same as the feedback signal and a percentages
calculation has to be done for defining the working range for both input and output component.

gral controller controls the output by integrate the error over time and adding the integrated
error with a integral gain equation (5.3). A integral controller cannot be used alone, but the
combination of a proportional and a integral controller result in a stable and a fast responding
control system. The proportional part of the controller will speed up the time for the system to
stabilize at the set point. The integral part will slow down the process but remove a constant
steady state error [15].

Calibration
The different data acquisition instruments studied in chapter 4 has to be calibrated to ensure
the validity of the accuracy provided by the manufactures. All the instruments has one input
and one output, for the pressure sensors the input is a fluid pressure and the output is a elec-
tronic signal in milliampere, for the thermocouples the procedure is a little bit different since a
thermoelectric voltage to temperature conversation is taken place in the national instruments
logging module (NI 9214) and the output is a temperature. The logged temperature from the
NI 9214 module is then the input temperature for calibration and the output is a temperature.
For the thermocouples the calibration was done before the experiments since the 8 thermocou-
ples used for wall temperature was glued to the inner pipe and will be difficult to separate after
assembly. The pressure transmitters were calibrated during the 2nd re-design and the mass-
and volume flow meters was not calibrated due to lack of time. For all the calibrated instru-
ments the wiring set-up is the same as used during the experiments so a uncertainty caused
by the logging module or wiring set-up etc. noise will be detected and included in the overall
uncertainty analysis. This chapter will include the different methods used for the calibration.
For detailed view about the instruments used for temperature calibration see appendix B
6.1
Thermocouples
For calibrating the thermocouples a calibration bath was used. A calibration bath is a bath
containing a liquid at a stable temperature. The liquid has to be suitable for the calibrated
temperature range in order not to freeze or boil during the calibration. The liquid used for the
calibration was kerosene, kerosene has a low freezing point and a high boiling point where the
freezing and boiling point is about -40 and 210 ◦C respectively.
The calibration bath has an electric heating element and a two-stage compressor unit for re-
frigeration, both units is controlled by a PI controller with a PT 100 thermometer for ensure
temperature stability. The latest quality test report for the bath shows a stability of 0.005 ◦C
achieved over a period of 15 min. The set point temperature is adjusted at a simple control
unit with a 3 digit LED display, principle for the calibration bath is to keep a stable tempera-
ture in the bath and not the accuracy for the temperature level. The volume of the calibration
bath is 7 liters and the kerosene is stirred, this ensures a large thermal capacity and a uniform
temperature distribution.

For defining a overall uncertainty for the temperature measurement, the uncertainty for each
devise used during the calibration has to be taken into consideration. First the uncertainty
for the thermocouple set-up has to be determined. The thermocouple set-up is the thermocou-

uncertainty for the ice point reference is given to ±0.02 ◦C form the data-sheet [20], and the
uncertainty for the experimental temperature was estimated to ±0.011 ◦C from the previous
section. The uncertainty is calculated with the uncertainty propagation method provided by
NIST [21], this method is implemented in the software EES and will express how a measured
variable with a uncertainty will propagate into the calculated quantity.

CHAPTER8
Discussion
The objective for this project was a experimental start-up phase and to make some improve-
ments for the experimental test rig and perform the first experiments. At the start-up phase
some time consuming problems showed up, at the first refrigerant charge, water was discovered
in the system, and the test rig was discharged and disassembled, all the pipes was cleaned with
acetone and dry compressed air for prevent moisture. However at the far end the system was
running and the first experiments could be performed. The experiments were performed over
some month and improvements were made between each experiment, etc. better control sys-
tems and small re-designs was added to the system. The time schedule can be seen in appendix
E
The pressure drop across the flow meter at state 13 was too high and unfortunately this
flow meter has to be bypassed, and a mass balance for the refrigerant side was not possible.
The Coriolis mass flow meter at state 8 has a working range between 0-5 kg/min and the mass
flow rate for experiments was between 0.15 and 0.45 kg/min, this results in a higher uncer-
tainty for the refrigerant measurement and this component is oversized even for the design
condition. This instrument is not calibrated during this project and the precise uncertainty for
the instrument in the low working range is unknown. However this instrument will not have
an effect on the uncertainty for the results showed for the heat transfer coefficient, since this
calculation is based on the load measured at the secondary side.
The volume flow meter at the secondary side is was not calibrated either, however the volume
flow meter used for the superheater was certificated, where the count number was corrected
0.02 % according to the standard count number and the resulting accuracy drops to 0.48 %
of full scale measurement. For the HTC calculations a uncertainty of 1.0 % of full scale mea-
surements was used for the volume flow meter and the uncertainty for the HTC calculations
seems to be acceptable.
The bypass design with the condenser did not work as expected and the experiments were
performed at a relatively high evaporation temperature compared with the design conditions.
The 2nd re-design was made at the end of the project for performing comparable experiments
with the ETS valve. After the 2nd re-design the evaporation temperature dropped to 5-6 ◦C,
and the pinch point in the heat exchanger was about 1-2 ◦C as the design condition.

8 Discussion
The model in chapter 2 was developed for understanding the pump cycle system and to give
an idea of the expected results, both for the evaporator test section and for the test rig as a
system. The model was later used for boundary conditions for the CFD calculation for study
the temperature variations in the mixing block.
The mixing block did not work as expected and further improvements has to be made be-
fore the evaporator test section can be split in 4 control volumes. During the design of the
mixing block new opportunities showed up, the usage of PVC instead of brass seems to work
well and many of the self-designed components can be replaced by PVC blocks. It will mini-
mize the conduction for these parts, and lowering the heat transfer to the surroundings.
During the calibration the possible uncertainty for the instruments was studied, the uncer-
tainty obtained during the calibration of the pressure transmitters seems to be acceptable one
improvement will be the accuracy for the barometric pressure.
Another institute at DTU
logged the barometric pressure and the information about the accuracy for the longing was
missing. For improve the uncertainty for the pressure measurements another barometric log-
ging method has to be used. However the barometric pressure increasing during the morning
and the time of the day the calibration is performed is a factor for the uncertainty, the baro-
metric pressure gradient was higher for the morning than the afternoon. This results in a
higher uncertainty for the barometric pressure used for the low pressure transmitters than for
the high pressure transmitters.
For the thermocouples the uncertainty for the calibration seems to be acceptable with the
current equipment.

Conclusion
The results showed in this project was the first experiments performed at the test rig for incom-
plete evaporation. The test rig did not work as expected and the results was for a relatively
high evaporation temperature.
From the time varying results showed in subsection 7.1.1 the saturation temperature and pres-
sure shows a time depended variation as a function of the cycle time. At low mass fluxes the
variations was hard to define from the temperature measurements but clear from the pressure
measurements. When the cycle time was increased a larger variation in heat transfer coefficient
was observed.
Subsection 7.1.2 focus on the time averaged results for the heat transfer coefficient and the
uncertainty for the results. By a direct comparison the heat transfer coefficient measured with
the pressure transmitters shows an increase for most of the experiments where the thermocou-
ples shows conflicting results except for one condition.
The small variation at each condition was corrected by normalizing the measurements with
a two phase correlation for continuous flow provided by gungor and winterton (1986). The
normalized results shows 2 conditions where the heat transfer was increased both for thermo-
couple and pressure measurements, however the condition at 150 kg/m2s in mass flux, 45 ◦C
condensing temperature and 1 second in cycle time seems to be notable high and this condition
has to be re-performed for a final conclusion.
The results show an unclear conclusion dependent at the measuring method, and the analyzing
methods used in this study did not show a significant improvement in heat transfer by using
pulsating flow, a study about the complete evaporation has to be performed for analyzing the
pulsating effect on the heat transfer in an evaporator.
From a start-up perspective the system is now running but the mixing block has to be improved,
before experiments for complete evaporation can be performed. An working mixing block will
not change the results showed in this project. However this was for incomplete evaporation, if
the thermal boundary layer is altered this can have an effect at the last or middle section of
the evaporator instead of the first section studied in this project.

APPENDIXE
Time schedule
First refrigerant charge
An experimentally study requires a lot of lab work and the time schedule for the project can
be seen at the next page, the blue boxes express lab work and the black boxes express data
analyzing and thesis work. During the start up phase a problem with water or some undefined
fluid in the system appeared, this results in less capacity for the condenser since water occupied
space for the refrigerant. Before knowing the problem the water in the glycol tank was replaced
with glycol, and the bypass cycle was insulated. The temperature for the glycol tank was set
below 0 ◦C and this result in a blocked condenser and the problem was evident. Furthermore
two flow fronts could be obtained from the sight glasses in the receiver.
Repair the system
The problem was solved by discharging the refrigerant and disassembled the system. All tubes
and instruments was cleaned with acetone and dry compressed air for preventing moisture, at
the same time small changes for the gas bypass and the receiver was made.
For discharging the system a refrigerant recovery system was used, this is a small transportable
compressor system. The unit was damaged by water during the discharging and has to be
dissembled and cleaned as well. All in all a lot of time was used in the lab for diagnose the
problem and fix it before the system was running.
New components and PI tuning
The start-up time for the system to be stable at a condition for experiments was about 2
hours, during the project new controlled components was installed and parameters has to be
tuned, the time consumption for tuning PI controllers was higher than expected. For the AKV
experiments the PI controllers has to be turned off before the cycle time could be changed,
this results in unbalance when the controllers was turned on again, and the system has to be
re-stabilized again before data samples could be started, about 1
/2−1 hour between each cycle
time.
