Abstract
The European Union has set a target to become climate neutral in 2050 through a set of political
initiatives also known as the European Green Deal. As part of the European Green Deal, the
Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) has been developed to improve the qual-
ity of sustainability reporting conducted by companies. However, data suggest that even though
the first set of companies must publish their first annual reports in 2025 for the reporting year of
2024, only very few companies in the state of Denmark are ready and compliant with the new
reporting requirements, suggesting that an implementation gap exists between the intention of
the CSRD and what will actually be achieved. In order to investigate why such a gap exists, a
qualitative case study of the CSRD will be conducted in this thesis, which will be based on pri-
mary data collected through interviews with relevant Danish stakeholders and secondary data
such as hearing results, reports, news articles, etc. This data will then be analysed in two steps:
First, a thematic analysis will be conducted and then the themes found will be analysed using
RRI as an analytical lens, especially drawing on the four dimensions of Responsible Research
and Innovation (RRI). These dimensions are anticipation, inclusion, responsiveness, and reflex-
ivity, as stated in a proposed RRI framework by Stilgoe et al., 2013. Based on these findings,
recommendations will be made on how the future implementation of sustainability legislation
can be improved. This thesis will also discuss the applicability of RRI when analysing the
implementation of sustainability legislation.

Introduction
In the following sections, an introduction to this Master thesis will be given, covering the back-
ground of the thesis, its problem statement, research question, methodology, and its scope.
1.1
Background
Today, the effort to stop the climate crisis has become global and major organisations around
the world have taken it upon themselves to change the course of the current downward spiraling
climate trajectory. This globalisation can be seen through international agreements and com-
mitments made by countries, such as the United Nations’ Paris Agreement (United Nations,
2016) and the European Green Deal (The European Commission, 2019) initiated by the Euro-
pean Union (EU). The goal of the European Green Deal is to make Europe climate neutral by
2050, as well as decoupling economic growth from resource use, conserving and enhancing the
natural capital of the EU, protecting citizens from environmental-related impacts, while making
this transition just and inclusive (The European Commission, 2019, page 2).
Both the European Green Deal and the Paris Agreement are long-term commitments of the
nations of the world to combat climate change and improve sustainability. However, the sus-
tainability landscape is vast and complicated, where several actors have been involved in seem-
ingly ever-increasing environmental and social impacts. Although a long-term target has been
established, it has proven difficult to decide who should take responsibility for what impacts,
especially because improved sustainability has historically been linked with increased financial
costs. These observations above naturally lead to the question of accountability. Who is ac-
tually accountable for the impacts? And who is responsible for initiating and conducting the
clarification process of the interconnections of the impacts of companies?
The above questions have typically been addressed through law, especially in terms of envi-
ronmental impacts. Since its birth in 1972 in the Stockholm Declaration on the Human Envi-
ronment, environmental law has expanded drastically across the globe (United Nations, 2019).
Today, every country has at least one environmental law or regulation and most countries have
formed environmental ministries (United Nations, 2019). The purpose of these initiatives is to
slow and reverse the current environmental path caused by human activity to obtain sustainable

development (United Nations, 2019). However, environmental laws do seem to have one thing
in common: The occurrence of a so-called implementation gap between the requirements of
the environmental law and their implementation and enforcement (United Nations, 2019). The
lack of standards for entities to follow and the failure to ”represent the conditions, needs, and
priorities” (United Nations, 2019, page 3) of the countries into which environmental laws are
imported have historically been evident in environmental law, as has the lack of objective goals
and measures described in the laws themselves (United Nations, 2019). Furthermore, fragmen-
tation in environmental law has led to a high focus on some areas while completely neglecting
others (United Nations, 2019).
1.1.1
The CSRD: Implementing accountability of impacts in the European Union
The EU has taken it upon itself to implement accountability related to environmental and social
impacts in European companies by attempting to map the sustainability landscape in Europe.
This has been done by adding a new and significant branch to the tree of sustainability legisla-
tion: The EU’s Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, also known as the CSRD. Being
a directive, the CSRD ”is a legislative act that sets out a goal that EU countries must achieve”
(”Directive” section, European Union, 2024b). The goal in this case is to make it easier to man-
age the financial risk of sustainability matters for investors in companies, securing a flow of
capital into sustainable companies by making them disclose their sustainability performance.
To decide which companies are sustainable and therefore less risky to invest in, the CSRD seeks
to assign accountability to companies in the EU (European Parliament and Council of the EU,
2022, Section 14), that is, who inflicts what amount of environmental and social impact. Com-
panies in the scope of the CSRD must therefore now conduct sustainability reporting based on
a set of standards related to environmental, social, and governance topics.
In order to define what companies must report, internationally recognised frameworks and prin-
ciples on responsible business conduct, corporate social responsibility, and sustainable develop-
ment (European Parliament and Council of the EU, 2022, Section 45) have been used to create
a set of 12 European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS), which companies must now
follow according to the CSRD (European Parliament and Council of the EU, 2022). Sustainabil-
ity has previously been somewhat voluntary to report on due to a lack of a required minimum
quality of the emissions reporting in the CSRDs predecessor, the Non-financial Reporting Di-

rective (NFRD). Standards are now mandatory and collected into one framework, with the first
subset of companies set to begin reporting on the 1st of January 2024. The CSRD expands the
scope of the NFRD so that it now applies to all large European companies with more than 250
employees, as well as listed small and medium enterprises (SMEs), gradually making sustain-
ability reporting mandatory for more than 49,000 European companies. However, empirical
data suggest that the CSRD is not exempt from the classical tendencies of especially environ-
mental law, that is, it has proven challenging to implement (United Nations, 2019). In 2023,
PwC conducted a sustainability survey with 225 Danish companies as participants, which was
released in January 2024 (PwC, 2023). In general, the survey concluded that the largest amount
of work related to sustainability for Danish companies lies ahead of them and that there exists a
gap between what is intended to be implemented and how far reporting companies actually are
with their work related to the CSRD. The current state of the implementation of the CSRD and
its standards therefore suggest that the implementation gap, which is inherent in environmental
law, has yet to be overcome.
1.1.2
Responsibility in implementation of environmental legislation
The European Union is responsible for implementing accountability through the creation of the
CSRD. However, the above observations suggest that the implementation gap still exists in the
implementation of the CSRD. The obvious follow-up question therefore arises: Why does this
gap exist, and what could have been done to mitigate it? The aforementioned survey mentions
that companies have experienced challenges related to data management and reporting, struc-
turing/governance, lack of necessary competencies, financial cost, and where to focus when
investing. These challenges are the consequences of creating the CSRD, and the focus will nat-
urally shift to the EU in terms of who is to blame for these challenges, as the Union is the one to
have designed and developed the directive. If a gap in the implementation has occurred, has the
EU actually been sufficiently responsible in their imposition of the CSRD in European firms?
To answer these questions, an analysis of the implementation process must be performed. A
natural starting point in such an analysis would be to investigate what has been done to ensure
that implementation can occur, and how the parties affected by the law have experienced the
implementation process. To uncover the reasons for the implementation gap, a methodology
must be used to reveal as many of these reasons as possible, a methodology that can review the
process and highlight where the roots of the implementation gap originate.

A candidate for such a methodology is the principles of Responsible Research and Innova-
tion, also known as RRI. It is a methodology that the EU itself endorses through its Horizon
Europe funding programme for research and innovation (The European Commission, 2023b).
RRI makes considerations regarding the so-called products of science and innovation and its
societal consequences, i.e., it asks its creator to consider what the impacts on society will be if
a piece of innovation is carried out. With the creation of its standards and the novel compul-
sion of disclosure, the CSRD can be considered an innovation within the field of sustainability
accountability and environmental law, but also as a tool to increase the responsibility of compa-
nies, since companies are now required to consider their impacts on society in a systematic way.
If the EU itself encourages scientists and innovators to consider RRI and its principles, it could
be argued that the EU itself should be held accountable to the same standards when making
legislation such as the CSRD. The question then is whether the reasons for the implementation
gap in the CSRD can be explained by gaps in RRI considerations by the EU when developing
the directive.
1.2
Problem statement, research question and methodology
The following section will present the problem statement of this thesis, its research question,
and the methodology used to answer the research question.
Problem statement
Environmental law has historically suffered from implementation gaps, that is, it has proven
difficult to implement and enforce the legislation in question. Empirical data suggest that this
has also been the case with the new Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) is-
sued by the EU, at least for companies situated in the state of Denmark. Since the EU has made
itself responsible for implementing accountability in European companies through the CSRD,
perhaps the reasons for the implementation gap of the CSRD can be found when analysing the
implementation process of the directive. A possible method for reviewing the implementation
process is to analyse it through an RRI framework, which is a way of implementing respon-
sibility in research and innovation. Since RRI considerations are already required to be made
when requesting funding through EU funding programmes and since CSRD is an innovation
within environmental law, the RRI framework as an analytical lens might provide insight into

the reasons why there is a implementation gap with CSRD. These insights might aid in limiting
the implementation gap of future sustainability legislation, which will be crucial in turning the
tides in the global quest towards climate neutrality.
Research question
What insights can the principles of Responsible Research and Innovation give regarding the
implementation challenges of the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) in Den-
mark? Furthermore, what recommendations can be derived from these insights to enhance the
future implementation of sustainability-related laws and initiatives?
Methodology
In order to answer the research question, a scoping interview was first conducted on the 4th
of January 2024. This scoping interview acted as a starting point for what to review in terms
of literature. A literature review was conducted, and this review was used together with the
scoping interview to identify relevant stakeholders related to the research question. A series of
interviews with interviewees belonging to the different identified stakeholder groups was then
conducted, as well as document analysis of secondary data and participatory observations. The
interviews were coded and analysed to identify the themes of the CSRD implementation debate.
The insights from the interviews were then juxtaposed with the development process carried out
by the EU, using RRI as an analytical lens to interpret the findings. The purpose of this is to un-
cover whether there are elements in the development process that would later create challenges
related to the CSRD implementation gap. The dimensions of RRI suggested by Stilgoe (Stilgoe
et al., 2013), namely anticipation, reflexivity, inclusion, and responsiveness, were used for this
analysis, and recommendations on what could be done to enhance the implementation of future
sustainability initiatives were given.

scope of the CSRD, this thesis will focus on the CSRD and its standards (ESRS), but mainly

This contextual overview and review of the existing literature related to the research question

Reporting Directive (CSRD), its content and scope, and its current implementation status in

2.1
The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD)
The Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive is a new piece of legislation created by the
EU. The CSRD in its final form was announced on 22 December 2023, and calls on companies
to improve their sustainability reporting, that is, reporting on data related to environmental, so-
cial and governance (ESG) (Deloitte, 2024). The data that companies must now report on is
spread over more than 1100 data points, both qualitative and quantitative, which are described
in 12 European Sustainability Reporting Standards (ESRS) (Ramboll, n.d.). The ESRS are
delegated acts that support the CSRD (EUR-Lex, n.d.-b). However, it is only data deemed
”material”, meaning relevant/significant for a company, which companies must report on. In
order to decide what is material, each company in scope must conduct a so-called double mate-
rialty assessment, and this decides whether a topic is material based on a financial perspective,
impact perspective or both (Ramboll, n.d.).
The purpose of CSRD is to raise the bar for sustainability reporting, improving both the cred-
ibility and comparability of sustainability information shared by companies (Deloitte, 2024).
This is seen as a stepping stone in order to fulfil the European Green Deal, which goal is to
make Europe climate neutral by 2050 as well decoupling economic growth from resource use,
conserve and enhance EU’s natural capital, protect citizens from environmental-related impacts,
while also making this transition just and inclusive (The European Commission, 2019, page 2).
The CSRD entered into force on 5 January 2024, with the first set of companies set to publish
their first mandatory CSRD statements as part of their annual reports in 2025 for the financial

2.1.1
What is a directive, a delegated act and a standard?
To understand how the CSRD affects the companies that have to adhere to it, it is important to
understand what a directive is and how it is enforced in the EU. A directive is defined as a ”le-
gal act adopted by the EU institutions addressed to the EU Member States” (EU Publications
Office, n.d.), and sets a common goal that must be achieved by all member states (Folketinget,
2024a). A directive is not legally binding directly in a member state until the national authori-
ties have passed it as legislation (Folketinget, 2024a). That also means that each member state
is free to choose which methods they use in order to achieve the goals set out by a directive
(Folketinget, 2024a) - and therefore also that the national legislation related to a directive can
look different from country to country, since a directive acts as a minimum standard (EU Pub-
lications Office, n.d.).
In the case of the CSRD, the EU institution in question is the European Commission, and one of
the member states to which the directive is addressed is the state of Denmark. The CSRD will be
incorporated into different Danish laws, but mainly in the laws related to annual accounting for
companies and auditing (Erhvervsstyrelsen, 2024a). Danish companies within the scope of the
CSRD must therefore follow it in order to be compliant with Danish legislation. The national
bill was proposed by the Danish minister of industry, business, and financial affairs in February
2024 (Deloitte, 2024, page 3), and the Danish Business Authority will oversee the compliance
of the Danish legislation and provide guidance (Erhvervsstyrelsen, 2024b).
The ESRS are so-called delegated acts, that is, ”non-legislative acts adopted by the European
Commission that serve to amend or supplement the non-essential elements of the legislation”
(EUR-Lex, n.d.-a). Delegated acts serve as a way of describing the general application, which
with the ESRS take shape in the form of standards, stating what and how a company should
perform sustainability reporting. Furthermore, a standard is ”set requirements for the perfor-
mance of a product” or a description of ”technical terms in a given area” (Danish Standards
(DS), n.d.). A standard can indicate methods for how to actually perform a specific action -
in this case, the ESRS are standards that contain information on what data a company should
disclose and what other standards they should follow in order to comply with the overarching

directive, the CSRD. The interpretation of CSRD and ESRS is the responsibility of the Court of
Justice of the European Union, whose task is to ensure that ”EU law is interpreted and applied
the same in every EU country” (European Union, 2024a).
2.1.2
How will it all be implemented?
When speaking of the implementation of EU legislation such as the CSRD, there are two steps
that will be addressed several times throughout this thesis. First, as mentioned previously, the
legislation must be implemented or adopted into Danish law, two terms that often are used in-
terchangeably. This is done to ensure that the legislation is legally binding in each member state
and that the authorities can impose its content on the relevant parties, which in this case mainly
refer to the reporting companies. Secondly, companies must also implement the legislation in
their organisation, here referring to the fact that companies must adapt their businesses so that
compliance can be reached, that is, so they can report according to the delegated standards, the
ESRS. As mentioned, the CSRD states the common goals that must be reached, and the ESRS
states the methodology on how to reach these goals. However, this also means that the pro-
cess of becoming CSRD compliant will vary from company to company based on their existing
organisation, current way of working, and the industry to which the reporting company belongs.
2.1.3
Predecessors of the CSRD
As the name of the directive suggests, the CSRD aims to improve the sustainability reporting
of companies, that is the disclosure of data related to sustainability (Deloitte, 2024). However,
this type of reporting is not new, and there have been a series of predecessors that have led to
the creation of the CSRD, which will be addressed in this section.
Due to the occurrence of a series of environmental catastrophes in the 1990s, companies began
to concern themselves with their responsibilities towards society, namely focusing on the im-
pacts their business activities had on society (Khatri and Kjærland, 2023). In particular, the term
Corporate Social Responsibility (CSR) began to gain traction in the aftermath of these events, as
did other international sustainability-related initiatives and frameworks (Khatri and Kjærland,
2023). The purpose of CSR is to ensure that public interests are considered in the decision-
making processes of companies (Ortiz-Martínez et al., 2023), which it does through a greater
focus on the responsibility of companies to society (Mućko, 2021). CSR is a ”self-regulating
12
==PAGE:16==
Master Thesis
DTU Wind
business model that helps a company be socially accountable to itself, its stakeholders, and
the public” (Annesi et al., 2023, page 2), and especially sustainable innovation is one of the
key drivers for CSR, since it can impact future business strategies and competitiveness (Annesi
et al., 2023). CSR places emphasis on the social element of sustainability, which is one of the
three elements of the Brundtland-defined triple bottom line of sustainability, the other two be-
ing economic and environmental sustainability (Ortiz-Martínez et al., 2023).
Adjacent to CSR exists another term, namely non-financial reporting (NFR), which is the re-
porting of company performance in areas not related to its finances. Where CSR describes
the initiatives of a company, non-financial reporting (NFR) gathers and discloses data related
to CSR (Ortiz-Martínez et al., 2023). NFR defines measurements, indicators, and sustainabil-
ity goals so that the CSR performance of a company can be quantitatively assessed to ensure
that the company considers the public interest in its decision-making process (Ortiz-Martínez
et al., 2023). Since 2017, CSR has been reported through the non-financial reporting directive
(NFRD) in the EU, covering the largest companies with headquarters in the EU (Mućko, 2021).
However, the NFRD has been considered somewhat non-mandatory for companies to follow as
there was no disclosed minimum quality level for information present in the directive (Aguado-
Correa et al., 2023). The flexible legislation of the NFRD has led companies to decide for
themselves how detailed their reports should be and what content should be included in them,
depending on the narrative chosen by the company (Mućko, 2021). Furthermore, questions
have been raised about whether the metrics used for CSR measuring are ”suitable, optimal,
and transparent” (Nazarko, 2020, page 6). Therefore, it has not been clear whether the level of
accountability in companies has increased as a result of the implementation of the NFRD and
to what extent reports have been used as a tool for the manipulation of narratives and results
(Mućko, 2021).

der some criticism. The European Commission has, for example, stated that ”there is ample
evidence, however, that the information that companies report is not sufficient. Reports often
omit information that investors and other stakeholders consider important. Reported informa-

2.1.5
Purpose of the CSRD
In 2022, the European Parliament announced the CSRD in a press statement titled ”Companies
to be more accountable for their social and environmental impact”, mentioning that the direc-
tive aims to make businesses more accountable for their impacts while giving investors and the
public easy access to sustainability information, which is comparable and reliable (European
Parliament, 2022). In the CSRD itself, there are also various sections that describe the exact
purpose of the CSRD. Most notably, CSRD is a tool that should help achieve the goals set out
by the European Green Deal, which requires all regions and EU citizens to participate in a ”so-
cially just transition to a sustainable economic system whereby no person and no place is left
behind” (European Parliament and Council of the EU, 2022, Section (1)). In particular, it is
mentioned that the CSRD focuses on the goals of the Green Deal related to redirecting capital
flows to sustainable investments, thus ensuring sustainable growth in the EU (European Parlia-
ment and Council of the EU, 2022).
Furthermore, it is intended to make it easier for stakeholders to manage the financial risk of
”climate change, resource depletion, environmental degradation, and social issues” by having
reliable information regarding sustainability available to them (European Parliament and Coun-
cil of the EU, 2022, Section (2)). In addition, the CSRD has also been created to prevent the
continuation of undertakings using different frameworks and standards to report on sustainabil-
ity, stating that such fluctuation will lead to ”significant increases in terms of cost and burden
for reporting undertakings and for users of such information” (European Parliament and Coun-
cil of the EU, 2022, section (15)). It is also stated that using different frameworks and standards
in different countries could undermine the internal market of the EU, making information less
comparable between member states (European Parliament and Council of the EU, 2022, Sec-
tion (13)).
The CSRD justifies the need for intervention by declaring an ”absence of policy action”, and
that this absence is expected to increase the ”gap” between what sustainability information is
available and what users will require in order to make investment decisions where sustainability-

2.1.7
Danish status on the CSRD
Due to the novelty of the CSRD at the time of writing, companies are still working to implement
the reporting standards of the directive, with the first mandatory reporting year being 2024 for
the largest European companies.
In 2023, PwC conducted a sustainability survey with 225 Danish companies as participants,
which was released in January 2024 (PwC, 2023). 3 percent of companies with more than
250 employees and 4 percent of companies with less than 250 employees reported that they
were fully prepared and compliant with the new CSRD reporting requirements (PwC, 2023).
53 percent of companies with more than 250 employees and 25 percent of companies with
less than 250 employees reported that they had taken the mandatory first step of completing a
double materiality assessment (PwC, 2023), which determines which of the sustainability topics
in the ESRS the company should report on (PwC, 2024a). 73 percent of larger companies and
65 percent of smaller companies reported that data management and reporting was the biggest
challenge for them when working with sustainability, with structuring/governance (60 percent
and 29 percent), lack of necessary competencies (50 percent and 63 percent), the financial cost
(30 percent and 35 percent) and where to put focus when investing (both 22 percent) also being
major challenges. The survey therefore suggests that the implementation of the CSRD is still
underway in Danish companies.

2.2.1
What is RRI?
Responsible Research and Innovation (RRI) is a term that first appeared under the synonym
”responsible innovation” in 2003 in the US Act: 21st Century Nanotechnology Research and
Development Act (Stahl, 2013). In Europe, the term started to gain traction after the Nether-
lands Organization for Scientific Research, NWO, promoted it during a series of projects in
2009 (Stahl, 2013). The EU then picked up RRI in Horizon 2020, a European research frame-
work with a budget of 70 billion euros that runs from 2014 to 2020, and in its continuation,
Horizon Europe, which runs until 2027 (The European Commission, 2023b).
In 2011, Dr.phil. Rene von Schomberg (at the European Commission, Directorate General for
Research) offered the following definition of responsible research and innovation: ”Responsi-
ble Research and Innovation is a transparent, interactive process by which societal actors and
innovators become mutually responsive to each other with a view to the (ethical) acceptability,
sustainability and societal desirability of the innovation process and its marketable products
(in order to allow a proper embedding of scientific and technological advances in our society)”
(Schomberg, 2012, page 9), which has since become one of the most cited definitions of RRI
(Karner et al., 2016). Schomberg was concerned with ensuring that society gained the ”right”
impacts of science and technology (Schomberg, 2012, page 1) - and for judging what can be
deemed a ”right” impact, he derived so-called normative anchor points from the Treaty on the
EU (Schomberg, 2012). These anchor points require that a certain product must be ethically
acceptable, sustainable, and socially desirable (Schomberg, 2012, page 10).
Despite Schomberg’s definition of RRI, there is yet to be an agreement on an internationally
recognised definition of RRI (Chen et al., 2022). The definitions of RRI vary depending on
the context, and the exact nature of RRI is still an ongoing discussion in the scientific field
(Stahl et al., 2019). Despite the many angles and perspectives of RRI, the main themes that
reoccur are that research and innovation should align with societal needs, that anticipation and
responsiveness are required to address ethical, environmental, and societal concerns, and that
stakeholders should be engaged in investigating the elements of RRI (Stahl et al., 2019).

2.2.2
The need for RRI
Science and knowledge have held a high status and importance to society, which traditionally
has led to some distancing between science and social obligations and political accountability
(Stilgoe et al., 2013). However, the status of science and innovation is being questioned more
and more frequently, due to the realisation that they lead to both positive and negative conse-
quences. To handle the development in technological prowess, a broader societal reflection on
the consequences of technology, science, and innovation has since followed (Stahl, 2013). The
need for this reflection has also been considered by the EU through the continued inclusion of
responsible research and innovation initiatives (Griesdoorn et al., 2023).
The need for RRI stems from the fact that emerging technologies seldom have agreed struc-
tures and rules that govern them, making them fall into what is known as an ”institutional void”
(”The idea that institutions can fail to support markets”, Dieleman et al., 2022), due to their
unproven and untested nature, furthering the need for anticipatory governance. The need for
anticipation and foresight has increased due to the growing prowess of technology, especially
when addressing the societal consequences of science and innovation due to society’s own in-
ability to mitigate the negative consequences of technology without interference (Stilgoe et al.,
2013).
As a result, society has started to think in a forward-looking way when it comes to address-
ing responsibility related to science and innovation, also called accountability and/or liability
(Stilgoe et al., 2013). Due to the complex and coupled nature of science and innovation, scien-
tists, research funders, innovators, and others are also required to have not only an individual
responsibility, but also a collective political responsibility (Stilgoe et al., 2013). This is because
while these actors individually may not be irresponsible, they can together create the so-called
”organised irresponsibility” (Stilgoe et al., 2013, page 1569).
2.3
An RRI framework
In relation to the CSRD, the European Green Deal lists a number of topics where the conse-
quences of human activity have escalated to a critical degree, such as greenhouse gas emissions,
air and soil pollution, loss of biodiversity, increasing use of chemical pesticides, fertilisers, and

antibiotics, etc. (The European Commission, 2019). The immediate action requested by RRI
is to improve anticipation, but it is perhaps easier said than done to ask society to know what it
does not know. To help operationalise the principles of RRI, notably Jack Stilgoe (University
College London), Richard Owen (University of Exeter Business School) and Phil Macnaghten
(Durham University) have worked on developing a framework for RRI to address this issue. In
their paper, Developing a framework for responsible innovation (Stilgoe et al., 2013), they de-
signed an RRI framework for the UK Research Councils, but their reflections were also intended
to be an input in a broader discussion on European research policy (Stilgoe et al., 2013). The
framework addresses specific methods for how to review responsibility in research and how
to actually include it in governance in a more practical manner than any pre-existing studies
offered. The Stilgoe et al., 2013 paper first reframes responsibility to also include anticipation,
gaining knowledge of possible consequences, and creating the capacity to respond to these
found consequences (Stilgoe et al., 2013).
Stilgoe et al., 2013 suggest four dimensions of responsible innovation: Anticipation, reflexivity,
inclusion and responsiveness (Stilgoe et al., 2013). The purpose of including exactly these di-
mensions is to ensure a deliberate inclusion of considerations regarding ”uncertainty, purposes,
motivations, social and political constitutions, trajectories and directions of innovation” in the
innovation process (Stilgoe et al., 2013, page 1570) - this is done to ensure that conventional
focus of governance on products and their technological risks is expanded. The four dimensions
will be elaborated on in the following subsections.
2.3.1
Anticipation
The call for better governance anticipation comes from the political, environmental and aca-
demic, and is derived from the often unforeseen consequences of new technologies and risk-
based estimates that often fail to provide warning of future impacts of new technologies (Stilgoe
et al., 2013). ”What if” questions are at the heart of anticipation, as is the ability to distinguish
prediction and anticipation due to anticipation’s acknowledgement of the complex and uncer-
tain nature of science and society (Stilgoe et al., 2013). Stilgoe et al., 2013 lists a range of
techniques for performing a successful anticipation: Foresight, technology assessment, hori-
zon scanning, scenarios, vision assessment, and socioliterary techniques. Stilgoe et al., 2013
also list the following factors that can affect anticipation: Engaging with existing imaginaries,

autonomy, and reluctance to anticipate.
2.3.2
Reflexivity
Stilgoe et al., 2013 define reflexivity as ”holding a mirror up to one’s own activities, com-
mitments and assumptions, being aware of the limits of knowledge and being mindful that a
particular framing of an issue may not be universally held” (Stilgoe et al., 2013). Instead of the
self-critique that scientists carry out themselves, reflexivity is a public matter due to it needing
to be responsible - it is created by connecting external values to the scientific work (Stilgoe
et al., 2013). According to Stilgoe et al., 2013, reflexivity places moral responsibilities on sci-
ence in public and makes a connection between the moral and role responsibility of scientists.
Techniques for reflexivity include: Multidisciplinary collaboration and training, embedded so-
cial scientists and ethicists in laboratories, ethical technology assessment, codes of conduct, and
moratoriums. Factors affecting reflexivity are: Rethinking moral division of labour, enlarging
or redefining role responsibilities, reflexive capacity among scientists and within institutions,
and connections made between research practice and governance.
2.3.3
Inclusion
Stilgoe et al., 2013 emphasises that inclusion today has started to involve members of the wider
public instead of solely so-called ”expert top-down policy-making” to ensure that an upstream
dialogue takes place. The inclusion of lay knowledge is also an important aspect in inclusive
governance, as is the realisation that ”the technical is political, the political should be demo-
cratic, and the democratic should be participatory” (Stilgoe et al., 2013, page 1572). Stilgoe
et al., 2013 argues that if the public is not included, the existing relations in power are reinforced
and the understanding of the public is decreased (Stilgoe et al., 2013). Due to fear that public
participation could end up being a means of closure, the level of intensity (how early the public
is included), openness (how diverse the group is), and quality (the gravity and continuity of the
discussion) are criteria for the inclusion of the public (Stilgoe et al., 2013). Stilgoe et al., 2013
list the following techniques for inclusion: Consensus conferences, citizens’ juries and pan-
els, focus groups, science shops, deliberative mapping, deliberative polling, lay membership
of expert bodies, user-centred design and open innovation (Stilgoe et al., 2013). Factors af-
fecting inclusion are: Questionable legitimacy of deliberative exercises, need for clarity about,

2.3.4
Responsiveness
The responsiveness dimension of the RRI framework addresses ”adjusting courses of action
while recognising the insufficiency of knowledge and control”, i.e., to act and to answer (Stil-

respond to improved inclusion, anticipation, and reflexivity, which are the three other dimen-
sions of the RRI framework. Stilgoe et al., 2013 mentions the constitution of grand challenges
and thematic research programmes, regulation, standards, open access, and other mechanisms
of transparency, niche management, value-sensitive design, moratoriums, stage-gates and al-

The factors that could affect responsiveness are strategic policies and technology ‘roadmaps’,
science-policy culture, institutional structure, prevailing policy discourses, institutional cul-
tures, institutional leadership, openness and transparency, intellectual property regimes and
technological standards (Stilgoe et al., 2013).

2.4.1
Implementation gaps of sustainability related legislation
Sustainability can be divided into environmental, social and economic aspects, which was orig-
inally defined by Brundtland (Ortiz-Martínez et al., 2023). When searching for research re-
lated to the implementation of sustainability legislation, environmental issues (especially cli-
mate change) seem to be the most significant part of the research. The UN has for example
recognised in their 2019 report, Environmental Rule of Law (United Nations, 2019), that there
typically exist implementation gaps when making new environmental legislation - this means
that there is a gap between what was intended with a piece of legislation and what actually ends
up being implemented. The UN names that rule of law, meaning that all are accountable to the
same set of laws, is vital to close this gap, especially when addressing environmental issues
(United Nations, 2019). With successful environmental rule of law, ”well-designed laws are
implemented by capable government institutions that are held accountable by an informed and
engaged public lead to a culture of compliance that embraces environmental and social values”
(United Nations, 2019, page 1).
Both the number of countries with environmental legislation and the number of institutions have
increased since the birth of environmental legislation in 1972 through the Stockholm Declara-
tion on the Human Environment (United Nations, 2019), and it is widely believed that to for-
mulate and execute environmental policies and legislation, high-quality institutions are needed
to support political decisions (Omri and Boubaker, 2024). However, the UN acknowledges that
environmental legislation too often ”exists mostly on paper because government implementa-
tion and enforcement is irregular, incomplete, and ineffective” (United Nations, 2019, page 3),

environment ministries are typically ”under resourced and politically weak in comparison to

There is therefore a recognition that while environmental legislation have developed greatly,
there still are some structural and political explanations to why an implementation gap in envi-

that neglecting sustainable development is not only a matter of environmental impacts but also
of social ones.

2.4.2
Implementation gaps of EU policies and legislation
The realisation that a gap exists between the ambition of the Paris Agreement (limit global
warming to 2 degrees in 2100) and the actual trajectory that the world is on, has initiated the
aforementioned European Green deal, which is the key transformation narrative and policy
framework made by the EU in order to reach climate neutrality (Perino et al., 2022). In addition
to the European Green Deal, the EU package, Fit-for-55, has been initiated to close the gap
between the ambition of the Paris Agreement and actual political and legal initiatives in the
EU (Perino et al., 2022). The Fit-for-55 package was presented in 2021 and its purpose was
to overhaul existing climate policy instruments (Perino et al., 2022). Based on the Fit-for-55
package, a paper named Closing the Implementation Gap from 2022 listed several factors that
could explain why successful implementation of such an initiative can be hard to achieve:

2.4.4
Research on the GDPR
A recent and widespread piece of legislation made by the EU is the General Data Protection
Regulation, GDPR, which was approved in 2016 (Smirnova and Travieso-Morales, 2024). The
GDPR ”seeks to revolutionise how organisations perceive and manage data”, but has nev-
ertheless ”posed organisations with numerous challenges” (Smirnova and Travieso-Morales,
2024, page 327). In a systematic literature review from 2024 (Smirnova and Travieso-Morales,
2024), the challenges found related to implementation were:
• Technical complexity, that is challenges related to redesigning systems to comply with
the GDRP and data management.
• Legal compliance, that is, challenges related to legal complexities and legal uncertainty.
• Organisational challenges, that is a lack of knowledge, expertise and experience, cost
concerns, and challenges related to process adaption (Adaptation of internal processes
and establishing new procedures).
• Regulatory challenges, such as dealing with third parties to erase private data, complexity
of the GDPR regulation, lack of awareness and understanding of the GDPR requirements,
and incomplete or vague guidelines and little support from authorities.
In addition, a crucial point made was that ”while large companies can easily hire new personnel
to handle compliance, small companies tend to rely on their own efforts to interpret the legis-
lation and ensure compliance”, making implementation increasingly difficult for smaller com-
panies (Smirnova and Travieso-Morales, 2024). Furthermore, there has been reported criticism
on the timing of guidance and that ”inadequate information provision by government author-
ities” has decreased the level of implementation of GDPR (Smirnova and Travieso-Morales,

Sustainability reporting
A scientific article related to sustainability reporting addresses the role of auditors in terms of
sustainability reporting (Liu et al., 2023). The paper states that auditors are ”poised to bridge the

demanding level) once the current capacity and technical ability of the market for assurance
is more mature (European Commission, 2021). Auditors are therefore identified as the key to
sustainability reporting (Liu et al., 2023).

2.5
Identified gaps in knowledge
The literature review has resulted in a number of key insights. It was identified that limited
research has been conducted on the implementation of the CSRD, but also that there is an im-
plementation gap related to the CSRD, suggesting that there has not yet been found a way to
implement legislation so that it is realised as intended. This means that the current way of pro-
ceeding when developing legislation is insufficient and that new methods should be adopted to
fix these inherent issues. As the RRI framework by Stilgoe et al., 2013 was also intended to be
applied to policy development, this framework could help to discover new insights that could
help mitigate the implementation gap.
However, RRI and its operationalisation is not well developed in terms of sustainability matters
(Ligardo-Herrera et al., 2018) or policy making (Bidstrup et al., 2024), which means that there
exists a gap in how to actually use RRI to analyse legislation such as the CSRD. The findings of
this review of the literature suggest that the framework of Stilgoe et al., 2013 can be used and
that a starting point should be to include stakeholders in the legislation, such as reporting com-
panies, developers, and authorities. The literature revolving implementation gaps in legislation,
CSRD anticipation, other EU legislation such as the GDPR, and research on sustainability re-
porting have given suggestions to the reasons why the CSRD implementation gap exists.
This thesis will therefore close two gaps of knowledge: First, it will collect information from
stakeholders that has not yet been presented in other research, and second, it will attempt to
operationalise RRI in relation to policies and legislation using the framework of Stilgoe et al.,
2013. Finally, the results of the combination of RRI and CSRD will be made into recommen-
dations, as recommended by Michalak et al., 2023.

Methodology
The methodology section of this thesis will describe the research design, that is, how this thesis
aims to collect and analyse data to answer the research question proposed in this thesis. The

3.1
Research design
This thesis addresses the implementation gap commonly found in environmental legislation
through a qualitative case study of the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive, as de-
scribed in the guide Essentials of Qualitative Interviewing (Olson, 2011, page 147). The case
study approach was chosen as this allows the investigation of CSRD implementation to be un-
derstood in the broader context of implementation gaps in sustainability-related legislation and
policy making. Since the CSRD at the time of writing is currently being implemented in compa-
nies across Europe, the case study will give a snapshot of the current state of the implementation
of the CSRD, which will likely be subject to change as the implementation matures. Implemen-
tation gaps, especially in environmental legislation, are, based on the literature review, not novel
and, more importantly, not solved. In order to bring new insights to the table when analysing
the CSRD, an additional feature of the thesis is to apply an analytical lens to the collected data,
namely Responsible Research and Innovation (RRI). From the literature review, it was found
that the framework by Stilgoe et al., 2013 has not yet been applied to the implementation of
environmental legislation, and with this thesis, the objective is that such a framework might
reveal new and interesting insights to the implementation of environmental and sustainability
related legislation.
The reason for the qualitative approach to answering the research question can be found in the
chosen RRI analytical lens. In terms of determining the methodology that could be applied, it
seemed likely that expert and lay knowledge was needed to understand the interconnections of
the problem statement, as the CSRD affects many different stakeholders with different roles in
society. As the problem at hand most likely could not be narrowed down to a single entity, but
rather to a chain of structural causality that crisscrosses between several stakeholders involved,
a methodology that could reveal the conflicts between the stakeholders would be required. The

dimensions of the RRI, as suggested by Stilgoe et al., 2013, require qualitative data related to
the research question to be collected. The Stilgoe et al., 2013 framework seemed reasonable
to apply to the initial problem statement for a number of reasons. Firstly, the obstacles that
typically constitute an implementation gap (or gap in rule of law) in environmental law ”are
not technical or financial, but political and human” (United Nations, 2019, page 3). This sug-
gests that a qualitative analysis framework that investigates the political and human elements of
environmental legislation could reveal the insights needed to explain the implementation gaps
that seem inherent in environmental legislation (United Nations, 2019).
The problem statement evolves around the CSRD, which can be classified as an innovation in
environmental and climate legislation, since the EU has created a set of ESG reporting standards
that apply across the entire EU, something which has not been done before. The innovative na-
ture of the CSRD and its introduction into society has naturally led to consequences for the
stakeholders it will affect. With the desired outcome of the CSRD being full compliance with
its standards, an implementation gap can be seen as an undesired and negative consequence
of creating a directive such as the CSRD. This is what the RRI principles evolve around: The
undesired consequences for society that emerge from research and innovation and how to con-
sider them. The RRI framework could therefore be used to explore and make sense of the
implementation of the CSRD, which will be attempted in this thesis.

4.1
Thematic findings
Based on the thematic analysis, four themes have been found in the different interviews related
to the challenges that impact the CSRD implementation gap. These themes are the inherent
complexity and difficulty of the CSRD/ESRS, rollout complications, first wave advantages and
second wave disadvantages, and lack of incentives. This section will explore and unpack the
overall themes and corresponding challenges and present the findings.
4.1.1
The inherent complexity and difficulty of the CSRD/ESRS
As mentioned previously, to comply with the CSRD, companies must report on the data de-
scribed in the sustainability reporting standards, the ESRS, which have been created by EFRAG.
While the purpose of the CSRD is to get companies to disclose that they are sustainable and
thereby attract investment capital to them, several stakeholders have mentioned a series of chal-
lenges related to difficulties in actually executing the reporting. This section describes the chal-
lenges related to the practical steps that a company must undergo in order to set up reporting
compliant with the CSRD. The challenges described therefore have a practical nature and are
related to the actual understanding of the directive and its standards, data management, setting
up the organisation to conduct the reporting, clashes with previous ways of working, and the
shear size of the task at hand.
Understanding the sustainability reporting landscape
The first element of reporting on the standards is actually understanding what is required in order
to be compliant. The CSRD is an attempt to ”formalize and streamline” sustainability report-
ing (EFRAG/DI), with each ESRS referring to a specific area related to environmental, social
and governance topics (ESG) - in order to report on ESG, there exists ”a myriad” (EFRAG/DI)
40
==PAGE:44==
Master Thesis
DTU Wind
of different standards and standard setters, which companies previously have reported on se-
lectively, but the ESRS now describe exactly which standards to follow in order to become
compliant. The sustainability reporting landscape consists of many abbreviations referring to
technical terms and has been labelled as an ”alphabet soup” (EFRAG/DI), which has led to
the area being complicated to understand for the inexperienced. Due to the combination of so
many technical elements, the EFRAG experiences challenges with companies not being able to
understand the CSRD on an ”European level” (EFRAG/DI), suggesting that this challenge is
widely present among companies.
ROCKWOOL, Nordkysten and Ørsted, the three companies interviewed for this thesis, have
all raised concerns regarding understanding issues at different levels. When asked about the
intricacies in sustainability reporting, the Nordkysten interviewee explained that it is a common
occurrence that when attempting to understand an element of sustainability reporting, they of-
ten discover that it is interrelated to something else, which quickly increases the time spent on
understanding the topics drastically (Nordkysten). Nordkysten has therefore had ”a more or
less difficult time” making it clear to themselves what the directive actually is about and what is
required in terms of implementation efforts (Nordkysten). Furthermore, the Nordkysten inter-

4.1.2
Rollout complications
The previous section described the challenges that arise due to the way the CSRD and ESRS
are structured and written, and is mainly focused on the work and tasks that companies must
carry out to comply with the directive and its standards. However, additional challenges have
occurred due to how the CSRD and ESRS have been rolled out into society. Therefore, this
section will describe the experienced challenges that have occurred due to more external cir-
cumstances, such as the given implementation time, the level of support for the reporting com-
panies, and the readiness of the market to actually implement the legislation.
Market-driven nature of directives
In order to understand some of the challenges later presented, it must first be disclosed how the
CSRD and the ESRS are supposed to actually be implemented. The CSRD is, as mentioned, a
directive, meaning that member states individually create national legislation that ensures that
the stated goals of the directive are achieved. However, the CSRD itself does not describe
how this should be done but only what must be achieved. Furthermore, the ESRS only list
the required data points, that is, the data related to each sustainability topic that a company
must disclose and which other standards a company must use - the standards do not define
the specific calculations or how the tasks should be carried out or what organisational changes
a company should make. Therefore, the CSRD and its standards are what stakeholders refer
to as ”market-driven” (Lagerberg, Danish Business Authority), that is, the market itself must
figure out a so-called best practice in terms of how to conduct reporting in the best way to
deliver the data required by the ESRS. The exact way to structure the reporting is therefore up
to the reporting companies themselves, who must figure out how to set up their organisation
so that the relevant data can be collected - this can naturally be done in a multitude of ways
since companies are not organised and structured in the same way. Especially the stakeholders
with more oversight, that is, the EFRAG/DI and the Danish Business Authority, endorse this
approach. The Danish Business Authority interviewees state that this is ”common practice”
and that ”it will be difficult for an authority to come and say that this is how you should do
it”, since the companies themselves are ”the ones who know best in terms of how to do it in
the best way for them and for their industry”. The EFRAG/DI interviewee also mentions that
this market driven approach gives ”better regulation than where you go out in advance and say,
with unwavering certainty, that it is the right thing to do” (EFRAG/DI).

Short development time
While the Ørsted interviewee agrees that there are upsides to this method, since it gives com-
panies some ”leeway” in terms of developing the best practice together with other companies,
the market-driven approach in combination with short time limits is, however, the main rea-
son for a number of challenges that stakeholders experience when working with the CSRD.
The short time limits have been a part of every step related to the creation and implementa-
tion of the CSRD, from the development of the 12 ESRS to the time given before companies
have to publish their first new annual reports. Once the European Commission decided that
the CSRD would become a reality, the EFRAG was given 14 months to develop the 12 stan-
dards - the EFRAG interviewee described the process as ”intense” and compared the process
with the development time frame of Global Reporting Initiative, where 14 years were spent
developing standards of a similar scope. Furthermore, EFRAG developed the standards during
the peak of the COVID-19 pandemic, which means that online coordination of work efforts
resulted in ”very long meetings” and ”not necessarily the most efficient decision-making pro-
cess” (EFRAG/DI).
The CSRD was published on 14 December 2022 (European Parliament and Council of the
EU, 2022) and adopted into Danish law on 2 May 2024 (Line Berg Madsen, 2024), a process
described by the Danish Business Authority interviewees as ”rash”. The Danish Business Au-
thority had to ”rush to adopt it in national legislation” (Danish Business Authority) due to a
short transposition deadline (6 July 2024, European Parliament and Council of the EU, 2022),
meaning the deadline by which member states must have adopted the CSRD into national legis-
lation. This would have allowed member states about a year and a half to implement the CSRD,
which is shorter than the normal time period of 2-3 years (Folketinget, 2024b).

The haste of the CSRD and ESRS can also be seen in the time given before reporting efforts
had to begin, which has been labelled as short by several stakeholders. There are several conse-
quences to this, one for example being that companies have had relatively short time to develop
best practices, meaning that they are not ”matured naturally” (Danish Business Authority) and
are somewhat ”forced” (PwC). Guidance on how to actually do reporting was also not devel-
oped by the time companies would have to start preparing to meet the new requirements. The

Auditor differences
If a company cannot find answers to their questions internally, two options are left to pursue. Ei-

the introduction of newly developed terms. An example given is the mandatory double mate-

leaves auditors to rely on their own understanding of the standards when providing guidance,
which means that executive boards and management in companies are currently asked to ”make
decisions based on advisor input, without precedent for anything” (Lagerberg Consult).

arguing what the correct interpretation is, because no one has the definitive answer, but report-

long time debating whether they (auditor) are right or we are right”, which slows the imple-

First wave advantages and second wave disadvantages

the new type of sustainability reporting. As mentioned previously, the CSRD is rolled out in
three different waves: First, the largest corporations will do reporting starting in 2024 and then
smaller corporations will follow suit in 2025, and the smallest in 2026. However, larger compa-
nies have some clear advantages over smaller ones, because they typically have more resources
and a better organisational structure to deal with EU new legislation, leaving the playing field
uneven when it comes to resources, competencies, and experience when working with EU di-
rectives such as the CSRD. These challenges will be described in the following paragraphs.
Advantages of large companies
Ørsted and ROCKWOOL are two large corporations that have to carry out reporting for the

4.1.4
Lack of incentives
As stated, the CSRD is resource intensive to implement, and the implementation has also been
required to be completed in a relatively short time period. Therefore, the incentive to actually
perform high-quality CSRD reporting must be evident to stakeholders in order to succeed with
the implementation. However, several interviewees address concerns related to why CSRD re-
porting should even be conducted in the first place, and these concerns will be addressed in the

Insufficient market demand
As mentioned, the CSRD is market driven - besides the industry developing best practices, the
market must also demand reporting of high quality for it to be worthwhile to develop by the
reporting companies. The Lagerberg Consult interviewee mentions that in the construction in-
dustry, it is life cycle assessment requirements that currently matter the most, and ”not yet the
CSRD”. In the public sector, the interviewee has experienced that sustainability demands, while
first being a requirement in tenders, will be ”negotiated away before entering into a contract”,
still leaving room for actual market demand to drive the CSRD implementation further. The
Ørsted interviewee also comments that in the market, the ”price element” is most important, as
competitors still battle with each other to win bids on various tenders for projects. The Nord-
kysten interviewee also explains that while great efforts are made to, for example, collect CO2
emissions data for their services, ”there isn’t really anyone (...), who has asked for it yet”.
The financial cost of CSRD
Increasing financial costs are also associated with the implementation of CSRD. The intervie-
wees report that there is ”no doubt about” that their accounting expenses will increase for the
reporting of ESG, as more and more data are required to ensure. Assuring data, that is, getting
the data approved by an auditor, is only one element of the estimated costs of implementing
the CSRD. The EFRAG/DI interviewee explains that current evaluations made by the Danish
Business Authority estimate initial implementation costs related to CSRD to be 10.8 billion
DKK, while after the implementation, the administration costs per year will be 7.5 billion DKK
(Dansk Erhverv, 2024) - which companies ”in the end” will have to pay for. The EFRAG/DI
interviewee then states that society must consider ”whether it is worth the investment”, since
many other sustainability initiatives could have been purchased for this significant amount of

money. The Ørsted interviewee also worries that the CSRD costs will end up adding a pre-
mium to sustainability, potentially making it less feasible for companies to actually pursue it as
a business strategy.
Lack of advantage acknowledgement
Although the cost of implementing the CSRD might be easily measurable in monetary terms, the
advantages of the CSRD can, however, be more difficult to estimate, leaving a general lack of
acknowledgement of the upsides of the directive and its standard. The EFRAG/DI interviewee
describes that while the Danish Business Authority has calculated only the costs (referring to
annual costs of 7.5 billion DKK), the European Commission estimates annual costs to be closer
to 1 billion DKK, when also including the benefits generated by the CSRD. The EFRAG/DI
interviewee mentions that they have struggled with measuring the benefits, since they are ”just
very difficult to measure”, again referring back to the fact that it is challenging to measure the
monetary value of, for example, biodiversity and human working conditions, which is needed
in order to combine them with financial costs. One advantage mentioned by, for example, the
PwC interviewee is that ”companies that are run with an eye for sustainability” often perform
better financially and are in general managed more efficiently, based on ”academic studies” -
but that companies actually need to believe in sustainability in order to actually incorporate it
in a financially profitable way. However, this has proven difficult to do when looking at the
CSRD implementation. The Danish Business Authority interviewees have seen that companies
in general ”voice concerns” about the CSRD, not recognising that it can be a ”advantage”
for them. This advantage comes from the fact that the Danish Business Authority expects that
”Danish companies will be better off than the rest of Europe” once it is revealed who has the
best sustainability performances, which will be made evident through CSRD reporting. The
Ørsted interviewee also expects this to be an advantage for their company, as they expect their
sustainability efforts to be ”valued in the selection process” when bidding for projects, as it
now will be more clear ”who is really the best” in terms of sustainability. So, while advantages
exist, the debate about the CSRD mainly focuses on ”burdens and how unmanageable it is and
how difficult it is” (Danish Business Authority).

An incentive for doing CSRD reporting could also be potential punishments from authorities for

Contradictory purposes
If a company has not been able to identify advantages of implementing the CSRD in their busi-

difficult for some to see why companies should put great effort into CSRD reporting, both time

data, the CSRD might therefore have a somewhat profit-reducing nature due to high imple-

companies exist. The PwC interviewee addresses this point by listing the three elements of a
”purposeful corporation” - that is, making a product which is demanded by the market, making
the product in a profitable way, and also doing so while not harming others. However, many
companies exist based on only the first two elements and not the third, making it contradictory
for them to implement initiatives that make it harder for them to make a profit. The Lagerberg
Consult interviewee has experienced that some customers say that since neither themselves nor
their customers are interested in sustainability, the bare minimum reporting should simply just
be conducted to ”comply with the legislation”, without seeking to improve sustainability at all,
focusing more on finances instead. So, while the financial aspects of corporate reporting still
dominate the sustainability reporting in terms of importance, it will be difficult to incentivise

Geopolitical uncertainty
A final remark on the lack of incentives from the interviewees is related to the current geopo-

this era is not defined as a time with transparent sustainability reporting and ”where everything

in relation to what happens when the world changes”, leaving them with ”little time” to work

their sustainability reporting now. The EFRAG/DI interviewee adds that the process of devel-

which at some point ”over matured” due to the acceleration of climate changes, leaving the EU
to ”push the button” on the CSRD at an unfortunate time. The timing of the CSRD was certainly
”not an EFRAG decision” (EFRAG/DI), since companies currently face external threats while
also attempting to comply with a variety of new EU regulations (EFRAG/DI). The PwC inter-
viewee also worries that the conflict in Ukraine has left Europe at a ”critical time” in terms
of deciding whether to ”pause” the European Green Deal and ”continue the work when the
war is over”, potentially losing momentum in terms of implementing transparent sustainability
reporting in Europe, which could be hard to regain at a later time.

RRI lens
The themes found in the previous sections can be summarised as follows:
• Due to the complex nature and the novelty of the CSRD and the ESRS, many companies
struggle with understanding how to actually tackle the implementation of the directive.

Master Thesis

• A short implementation time combined with the market driven implementation approach
has meant that there currently is a lack of guidelines and no developed best practice for
CSRD implementation.
• First wave companies have several advantages over second and third wave companies,
since they typically have the organisational strength, resources and competencies to work
with directives such as the CSRD.
• There is in general a lack of incentives for some companies to do high quality reporting - a

In the following section, the findings will be analysed and interpreted using RRI as an analytical
lens. This will be done by looking at the challenges described by the interviewees and seeing
which dimension of RRI they belong to, while also taking the development process of the CSRD
and ESRS into account, ultimately seeking an explanation for why these challenges ended up
occurring. This section will attempt to answer the first part of the research question of this
thesis, namely What insights can the principles of Responsible Research and Innovation give
regarding the implementation challenges of the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive
(CSRD) in Denmark.
4.2.1
Anticipation
Anticipation is, as mentioned, about the efforts made in order to foresee the impacts and con-
sequences of a piece of innovation. In hindsight, some of the challenges ended up occurring
due to a lack of anticipation, although an effort was made to discover them through the impact
assessment published in April 2021 prior to the development of the CSRD and ESRS (The Euro-
pean Commission, 2021). The assessment describes the anticipated impacts discovered by the
EU Commission, and is based on stakeholder consultation, external studies, studies carried out
on the behalf of the commission, open public consultations, etc. (The European Commission,
2021, Annex 1). The assessment explores the impacts of possible policy options that could be
used to address the challenges identified with the predecessor of CSRD, the NFRD (The Euro-
pean Commission, 2021, Section 5). The impact assessment can be used to explore whether the
58
==PAGE:62==
Master Thesis
DTU Wind
EU Commission has actually anticipated the current status and challenges of the implementa-
tion. If not, then this would mean that there is a risk that implementation gaps occur for which
there is no developed strategy to mitigate them.
Anticipated negative impacts
The impact assessment was carried out before the development of the CSRD and the ESRS,
and the assessment is therefore not actually based on what companies would eventually end up
reporting on. This is in itself somewhat problematic, since it would be hard for the respondents
to predict future challenges based on legislation that has not yet been developed. The only nega-
tive impacts found for the reporting companies were an ’increase in administrative burden due
to detailed reporting requirements” and ”significant costs” in relation to the assurance level
(The European Commission, 2021, Section 6), which is somewhat surprising compared to the
multitude of challenges addressed by the interviewees for this thesis.
The ”increase of admin burden due to detailed reporting requirements” seems to not cover the
challenges related to implementing the reporting in companies in sufficient detail. That it is
difficult to understand the sustainability reporting landscape, practical implementation difficul-
ties, data management difficulties, and the shear size of the task at hand is not evident at all,
and seems to be clumped into the ”admin burden”, which is much less nuanced than what the
interviewees have described for this thesis. Furthermore, the fact that many organisations need
to be overhauled to actually accommodate the reporting is also not addressed. That these chal-
lenges were not anticipated in great enough detail can therefore perhaps explain why the burden
anticipated by the EU is smaller than what actually ended up being required (EFRAG/DI).
Macro-level focus
In terms of actually addressing how companies are expected to work with the directive and fu-
ture standards, the assessment is rather bare-boned and, in general, focuses more on macro-level
consequences to ”society at large” (The European Commission, 2021, Section 6.1.3) rather than
on how the directive is received by those who are actually going to implement it. Reporting
companies only play a minor role in the assessment, meaning that the challenges related to how
companies actually implement the CSRD would be difficult to discover. Since the expected in-
crease in the cost of CSRD, as found by the Danish Business Authority (Dansk Erhverv, 2024),

Misjudgment of advantages and drivers
Some of the advantages listed also seem to miss the mark compared to what interviewees have
been stating. For example, the assessment lists ”clarity about which information to disclose”
as an advantage for the reporting companies, where instead the information has proven to be
unclear and difficult to interpret, due to the novelty of the directive. The impact assessment also
addresses that the third wave companies ”do not have the technical expertise nor resources nec-
essary to prepare reports in accordance with state-of-the-art, sophisticated standards” (The
European Commission, 2021, Section 5.2.1), suggesting that a voluntary standard should be
made for them instead, but there is no recognition of the potential challenges for second wave
companies.
Ironically, many of the challenges found related to the CSRD are actually listed as drivers for
why the directive exists, and as a problem with the CSRD predecessor, the NFRD. For exam-

suggested lack of incentives (The European Commission, 2021, figure 1), and ”complexity and
uncertainty about what to report”, ”difficulties of getting necessary information from suppli-

(The European Commission, 2021, figure 1). It may therefore seem that the CSRD did not fix
the problems of the NFRD, since they are still being mentioned by the interviewees.
Combination of market imperative and short implementation time
On a more external level, the consequences of the combination between the short implemen-

that must develop what good reporting looks like (Danish Business Authority) were not ad-
dressed in the assessment, even though it seems like something bound to fail. Because how
can something develop naturally in the market when insufficient time is given to let the mar-
ket come up with the best solutions? As was stated by the Lagerberg Consult and EFRAG/DI
interviewees, consultancies have ended up creating solutions because they can be sold to the
reporting companies. The fact that the strongest incentive to develop best practices lies with

Implementation method and need for guidance
To uncover the challenges found in this thesis before the directive was actually created, several
elements should have been evaluated to uncover the actual challenges that ended up occurring.
It is for example remarkable that no evaluation of implementation process options has been
addressed, since the challenges related to the chosen market driven approach are some of the
main reasons for the current implementation gap. The assessment only states that ”national
supervisors” such as the Danish Business Authority and DI could need additional resources in
their supervision efforts (The European Commission, 2021, Section 6), but not that sufficient
guidance should be developed before asking companies to perform the very novel CSRD re-
porting. As the lack of guidance is a major problem for several stakeholders, a plan on how to
actually help answer the many questions that have risen seems like an absolute must, but this
has, as seen, not been a priority to roll out beforehand.

The development of the ESRS
In addition to the development of the directive itself, the ESRS were also developed by in-
volving different stakeholders. The EFRAG interview described that the standards were devel-
oped through a task force consisting of different smaller teams called work streams, where the
EFRAG attempted ”to be balanced in the composition of the different groups”. The PwC in-
terviewee was part of the task force and described that it consisted of ”environmental represen-
tatives, investor voices, trade union representatives, human rights experts, etc., which resulted
in a ”well-composed group, which, in general, cooperated very well”. So, while the EFRAG
seemed to have succeeded in bringing in a diversified group of stakeholders, there were other
elements of the process that ended up being problematic. The EFRAG interviewee especially
addressed that ”missing support” from the Commission affected the development of the stan-

you think about how big a burden and how big a change you are really asking for”, referring to
that the Commission, for example, did not provide assistance to EFRAG with additional staff
while also requiring the standards to be developed in 14 months.
The designated development time meant that the task force would have to work on standards
during the COVID-19 pandemic, where international group meetings and sessions were difficult
to carry out. For example, the PwC interviewee addressed that standards had to be developed
in parallel by different work streams since there were not enough resources to do it in steps.
Furthermore, the conceptual framework of the entire set of standards was developed simultane-
ously with the topical standards, meaning that the work streams started working on the specific
elements of the reporting before knowing how their area would interconnect with the others
(PwC). The consequence of this was that coordination would be conducted by the leaders of
each work stream, who met in a cross-functional group, in which the other participants in the
work streams could not participate, preventing them from assisting and providing feedback for
the other standards. In addition, the COVID-19 pandemic could also have prevented important
stakeholders from participating in the development process because they had to focus on how
to navigate the challenges of the pandemic in their own organisation.

4.2.3
Reflexivity
The reflexivity dimension is related to the ability to reflect over one’s perception of a topic,
while also including public opinions and letting it shape one stance and viewpoint instead of
only relying on self-criticism. Since the CSRD and ESRS are passed as legislation, the definite
meaning of the written standards comes down to how the law will be interpreted. Although
developers may therefore have had an initial idea of what the correct interpretation of their leg-
islation is, the right way is ultimately decided through the judicial system. This means that if
the way the law is interpreted by stakeholders is unknown to developers, exactly how the leg-
islation will be challenged later on might result in a legal and definitive interpretation that the
developers did not intend, which could offset some of the expected benefits of the directive.
Different perceptions
To start with, the CSRD has been made in order to help the EU reach the European Green Deal’s
2050 objectives and is in general concerned with macro-level development (European Parlia-
ment and Council of the EU, 2022, (1)) - the interviewees, however, address many challenges
that are more concerned with the ”here and now”, for example, how the directive should be
implemented in their organisation this year. The Union and the stakeholders operate on dif-
ferent timescales, where the stakeholders are required to focus on annual reporting rather than
the effects the CSRD will have on society over the next decades. This illustrates how the two
sides actually view reality differently, simply because they have different objectives and scopes
in their work. Reporting companies are also expected to be more concerned with their own
activities rather than the cumulative impacts of all European companies, again illustrating that
the EU and the reporting companies have different priorities, which therefore creates a need for
balancing the differences out if a high level of reflexivity is to be reached.
The suggested lack of incentives also illustrates the differences. The fact that most companies
operate with the purpose of generating profits, more macroeconomic benefits do not appear to
be of interest to most companies if they come with decreased financial performances, which
still is a main driver in the market today (Ørsted). As there is no real reason to implement the
reporting from a business perspective, other than satisfying the wants of the EU, the reporting
standard might end up falling short in terms of what the EU and the Commission actually de-
sired when the CSRD was created. In general, the listed challenges, such as insufficient market
64
==PAGE:68==
Master Thesis
DTU Wind
demand and lack of recognition of advantages, suggest that the proposed benefits, like improved
biodiversity and increased transparency, are simply not that important for reporting companies
if they cannot be used to increase profits. This suggests that there is a gap in understanding
in what reporting companies want and what the EU wants, and that a more business-orientated
approach is needed to really spread good reporting out into society and not just in large compa-
nies, who have the luxury to focus on sustainability more intensely.
Underestimation of culture
In addition, the specific ways of reporting in each country is somewhat overruled by the CSRD,
referring to the aforementioned cultural clashes that exist in reporting. It is naturally hard to de-
velop legislation that fits the specific norms and traditions in each country, but it is important to
understand them and perhaps ensure that the legislation is flexible enough to accommodate these
differences, which is a suggested challenge by the EFRA/DI and Ørsted interviewee. Since the
ESRS, for example, is a compromise based on the negotiations and discussions of the task force
that helped develop the ESRS, it is simply impossible to ”please everyone” (EFRAG/DI) who
will be affected by the legislation.
Different ways of working with sustainability
Furthermore, the fact that many companies experience difficulties in understanding the ESRS
and lack the competencies to implement the CSRD reporting in their organisation also reflects
that there is a difference between how sustainability is worked with and perceived between
developers and stakeholders. If developers expect companies to be more capable in terms of
working with sustainability than they actually are, the developed legislation might not suit the
level of competencies and resources available for companies to spend on the implementation,
hence why challenges have occurred with completing the CSRD implementation. Although
the EFRAG and the European Commission are concerned with formal legislative formulations
of their intentions, the reporting companies interviewed for this thesis did not have the same
background and prerequisites to fully understand what was meant by every element of the CSRD
and ESRS while also viewing the legislation from a business-influenced standpoint. This gap
exemplifies that the ”sender” and ”receivers” do not necessarily speak the same language, and
since guidance and a best practice are yet to be developed, adequate translation from one side to
the other seems somewhat difficult to achieve during the first waves of CSRD implementation,

since the interlink is currently missing.
4.2.4
Responsiveness
When passing legislation as far-reaching as the CSRD, it is expected that revelations regarding
how things should have been done differently will occur once the directive enters into force.
Responsiveness is therefore related to how new knowledge on the three other dimensions is
handled and used to improve an innovation. This section will therefore address the challenges
mentioned by stakeholders in terms of responding to the problems that has risen because of
the CSRD. In terms of the directive itself, there are various examples in which the feedback
of stakeholders directly influenced the final piece of legislation. For example, the European
Commission announced in February 2024 that the adoption of sector-specific ESRS would be
postponed by two years, with the decision being based on ”the concerns of businesses” and an
understanding of that ”the pace of change can be challenging for many companies” (European
Commission, 2024).
Inflexibility of judicial systems
Most of the changes made have occurred prior to the standards becoming delegated acts simply
because it is no longer possible to easily edit or change the standards. The EFRAG/DI inter-
viewee explains that if one wants to make changes to the standards now, these changes ”must
then go through a new legislative process, and past the Parliament and the Council, who can
make objections if they disagree with the changes that come as a whole package”. This is of
course somewhat the norm when it comes to judicial systems, since, for example, the Danish
Parliament also requires a piece of legislation to go through several steps, ministries, and com-
mittees before it is fully processed (The Danish Parliament, 2024). The judicial system, which
is part of rolling out directives, is simply less flexible and allows less responsiveness than what
is the norm for innovations in an RRI perspective. Although solutions to reporting of the CSRD
and ESRS are developed by the market itself, the content and framework of the directive and
the standards are somewhat set in stone. As the EFRAG/DI interviewee mentioned in relation
to that market driven implementation provides ”better regulation than where you go out in ad-
vance and say, with unwavering certainty, that it is the right thing to do” (EFRAG/DI), the
content of the standards have still been rolled out with that exact ”unwavering certainty” that
was attempted to be avoided in the first place.
66
==PAGE:70==

DTU Wind
Everything all at once
It was also decided that all 12 standards were to be implemented all at once, which resulted in
the aforementioned ”huge task” (Ørsted) that the companies have had to face in reporting year
one. Since the content of the standards are now fixed, responsiveness is naturally reduced as it
is not possible to adjust the trajectory compared to if the standards were rolled out gradually.
If, for example, the first two standards regarding the double materiality assessment had been
rolled out first, the EU and the EFRAG would have been able to respond and make changes to
the other standards based on the learnings from the first part of the implementation, but that has
not been possible due to the chosen way of implementation.
Bureaucratic distance
The stakeholders seem to be aware that the EU Commission itself is not able to provide an-
swers to the questions that may have occurred. The ROCKWOOL interviewee addresses that
they ”don’t have time to go to the Commission and ask simple questions for clarification and
then wait several months for a reply when I (ROCKWOOL) need to implement it by the end
of the year”. Furthermore, the EFRAG/DI interviewee addresses that an EU ”interpretation
body” would be ”formal and slow” and questions whether such a body ”will be able to make a
decision within 14 days” that matches the practices in the different member states (EFRAG/DI).
Furthermore, the EFRAG/DI interviewee also addressed that it would be challenging to decide
on the structure of a formal interpretation body placed within the authorities of each member
state, since they would most likely also interpret the legislation differently. This is a prob-
lem since EU legislation is supposed to be interpreted ”with as few differences as possible”
(EFRAG/DI), but this is nonetheless what is happening today where each stakeholder seems
to be making an interpretation individually. There is therefore a bureaucratic distance between
reporting companies and the Commission, since there is no national authority to bridge those
two together. This therefore also limits how responsive it is possible to be, since individual
companies such as ROCKWOOL simply need answers quickly to meet deadlines, and since the
commission is not geared to address simpler questions, but only the ”general things” (ROCK-
WOOL) that apply to all.

Although there is this bureaucratic distance, this does not mean that stakeholders have no feed-

viewing comments and feedback is not a process that the Commission ”has a history of doing
routinely”. The legislative element of the ESRS therefore creates a major hindrance in actually
adapting to the feedback received. All the challenges mentioned by the interviewees for this
thesis will therefore have to be addressed in a way that is different from actually changing what
should be reported on and how the standards are written. For example, the EFRAG made an
additional effort to make official guidance documents (EFRAG/DI). Three guidance documents
are currently under development, and they are related to double materiality assessment, how to
work with supply chains, and an overview of data points (EFRAG/DI), all topics that have been
deemed challenging by the interviewees of this thesis; however, the EFRAG/DI interviewee
also states that the EFRAG does not have the resources to actually support the implementa-
tion process itself but only can develop guidance material, and that implementation is now the
responsibility of each individual member state, and not the EFRAG nor the EU Commission.

5
Discussion and Recommendations
Below, an overview of the insights found using RRI will be given to answer the first part of the
research question, that is, what insights can the principles of Responsible Research and Inno-
vation give regarding the implementation challenges of the Corporate Sustainability Reporting
Directive (CSRD) in Denmark?:
• Anticipation was made through an impact assessment on the CSRD but the effort made
was found to be somewhat insufficient. First, the assessment is based on the lacks of
the NFRD and not the CSRD itself, a decision that could make anticipation increasingly
difficult. The assessment did not anticipate the size and nuances of the burden imposed
on stakeholders, simply addressing these as an anticipated increase in administrative bur-
dens, which has led to an underestimation of implementation costs. This can be explained
by the chosen macro-level focus rather than placing attention on the actors that are actu-
ally going to implement the CSRD. There were also misjudgments of the advantages of
the CSRD, where many of the identified advantages actually have ended up being chal-
lenges for the reporting companies - this has also been the case with the drivers of the
CSRD. The consequences of combining market-driven implementation and a short im-
plementation period were also not anticipated. Finally, the need for fully prepared guid-
ance was underestimated, which has created many of the challenges of the stakeholders
interviewed for this thesis.
• In terms of inclusion, the outreach efforts made by the Commission and the EFRAG
could have been improved. A significantly lower than anticipated number of participants
actually provided answers and comments to the impact assessment - many companies
were also not aware of the development of the CSRD, which could have led to only the
most resourceful companies to participate in the discussions, leading to an incomplete
understanding of what companies really think of the CSRD. Basing conclusions on sur-
veys is also problematic, as surveys are less nuanced and rich compared to qualitative
interviews. An outreach event hosted by the EFRAG underlines this claim, as partici-
pants could only answer polls with predefined answers as well as only being able to raise
questions allowed by a moderator. Inclusion was also limited due to the short time given
for development and the lack of resources received by the EFRAG in the development
phase. The development phase itself was also limited in its inclusion due to online work-
69
==PAGE:73==
Master Thesis
DTU Wind
ing conditions where members of the developing task force were divided into teams that
only communicated through a cross-functional team.
• The reflexivity of the development was influenced by the different perceptions between
the EU and the reporting companies. Companies operate on a different timescale com-
pared to the EU, and therefore might not be able to see the advantages of the CSRD as
clearly as the EU, who focus more on macro-level results. Therefore, incentives for good
CSRD reporting have been misunderstood, as the financial purpose of companies to in-
crease profits clashes with the goals and aims of the CSRD. Since the CSRD applies to
all member states, the Danish reporting tradition was not adequately taken into account,
as the legislation fits better into a central European style of reporting. Finally, the CSRD
does not take into account that reporting companies may not have had experience working
with sustainability at this level, which is evident in the lack of competencies addressed
as a challenge by some interviewees - the different ways of working with sustainabil-
ity highlight that the EU does not necessarily speak the same language as the reporting
companies, which has increased the implementation gap further.
• Responsiveness is limited by a number of factors. The fact that the CSRD and the ESRS
are part of a complex and inflexible judicial system means that there is little agility in
terms of changing legislation once it has been adopted. Since the content of CSRD and
the ESRS cannot be easily changed, the fact that all standards were rolled out all at once
prevents step-wise learning. Furthermore, there exists a bureaucratic distance between
the EU and the companies, which means that simple answers cannot be answered quickly.
This therefore becomes the task of auditors, who are currently challenged in terms of pro-
viding a correct interpretation of the ESRS, since the European legal complex means that
interpretation must be made through the EU judicial systems, which could take some
years. Lastly, the feedback system is currently limited to the EFRAG collecting feed-
back and then sending it on to the Commission, who will have to evaluate, which the
Commission does not have a history of doing routinely.

5.1
Interpretation of findings
The following section will address the findings in the context of the existing research presented
in the review of the literature. This will be done in order to address the level of novelty of the
findings and to validate them. Furthermore, the methodology developed for this thesis will be
discussed.
5.1.1
Comparison to past implementation processes
The most obvious parallel to draw, as there is no current peer-reviewed research on the im-
plementation of the CSRD itself, will be to the challenges found in the implementation of the
GDRP. Interestingly, many of the challenges found for organisations in the implementation of
the GDPR (Smirnova and Travieso-Morales, 2024) are also present in the implementation of the
CSRD. Challenges related to implementing the legislation in terms of setting up systems and
procedures and the lack of knowledge and expertise are present for both pieces of legislation.
Perhaps most notable is the fact that the timeliness of the guidance and the lack of support from
the authorities were problems in both cases. The increased difficulties for smaller companies
compared to larger ones were also present in the GDPR implementation, and the level of the
task at hand was extensive and time consuming for the GDPR and the CSRD. This therefore
suggests that these challenges are somewhat inherent to EU legislation and that a structural issue
exists within the EU to actually successfully implement the legislation it imposes on companies.
Furthermore, the challenges addressed related to implementation gaps in EU policies and leg-
islation (Perino et al., 2022) were also present in the challenges mentioned by the interviewees.
The coordination difficulties, legislative complexity, and how to balance costs and benefits also
proved challenging in the implementation of the CSRD. However, many stakeholders appeared
to understand the need for improved sustainability reporting legislation, which means that dif-
ferences in political ideologies were not a challenge that has reoccurred with the CSRD.
When it comes to sustainability legislation in general, the challenges listed by the UN (United
Nations, 2019) are also present in this case, as there is certainly an implementation gap. The

focus of macroeconomic development has, as mentioned, created a distance between the EU
and the reporting companies, and the fact that the EFRAG has been under-resourced in terms
of getting the required funds to develop the CSRD and ESRS as good as possible is something
that has occurred in especially environmental legislation before - and which has caused gaps in
implementation before as well.
These comparisons therefore suggest that the CSRD implementation process is another chapter
in the book of implementation challenges related to EU and sustainability legislation - and that
measures have not been taken to correct these errors as they are still present in the way legislation
is implemented today. Furthermore, Michalak et al., 2023 comment that ”academia’s impact
on policy making is not fully efficient” therefore also still rings true, as does the fact that it was
possible to anticipate many of the arising challenges, and to formulate a strategy to mitigate
them.
5.1.2
Enabling the market to improve implementation
With the current setup of letting the market develop best practices, it seems obvious that the
stakeholders who will help with implementation should be well prepared for this task. Auditors
were identified to especially play a key role in implementation (Liu et al., 2023), as they serve as
the first line of guidance for companies in terms of answering interpretation questions related
to implementation. However, the interviewees suggested that the auditors do not seem to be
ready to take on this task. The EFRAG/DI interviewee stated that it is hard to educate and train
people in standards that simply have not been developed yet, referring to the ESRS, and this
will naturally be a challenge since the auditors are the ones that have been indirectly appointed
as drivers of the implementation process. One can therefore wonder why the key drivers of the
implementation have not been sufficiently assisted in taking on this task, especially when also
considering the lack of guidance that has been available to the auditors.
5.1.3
RRI considerations
The existing literature makes interesting points in relation to how responsibility in policy mak-
ing can be operationalised. Especially the fact that co-creation and co-implementation should be
aimed at, and that authorities should lead the creation of synergies between stakeholders (Bid-
strup et al., 2024) seems like something that has somehow been neglected in the implementation

of the CSRD. As the CSRD has been imposed on some stakeholders, this ”co” element of imple-
mentation is simply not present; so far, each company has developed implementation processes
on their own without much help from authorities, making the implementation individual and
uncoordinated within the different industries. The Danish Business Authorities mentioned that
efforts to coordinate ”benchmarking, exchange experiences, etc., and agree on some standards
for what constitutes good reporting” would be beneficial, but also that they were uncertain
whether it was their responsibility or the companies. RRI in policy making states that the au-
thorities should lead this process, so making the responsibility of such an initiation clear could
have reduced confusion and resources spent on the implementation.
Furthermore, it is stated that authorities should promote the culture of public engagement (Bid-

anticipated responses on their outreach efforts in terms of its impact assessment of the CSRD
suggests that public participation was deficient. If, for example, the Danish Business Author-
ities promoted and even incentivised participation in the development of legislation, perhaps
some challenges could have been mitigated. In addition, a detailed analysis of the needs of the
companies would also have been beneficial in terms of determining how implementation could
be rolled out to cause as little harm to reporting companies as possible.
In relation to sustainability, research on RRI is still somewhat limited, as sustainability has
recently been added to the concept of RRI (Ligardo-Herrera et al., 2018). However, it is sug-
gested that integration in core management, stakeholder management, a life cycle perspective,
and open access databases can help actually operationalise RRI in sustainability matters. In
terms of integrating RRI in core management, a question was raised during the EFRAG pub-

and CSRD (EFRAG, 2024b). The EFRAG responded that they were not familiar with RRI,
which suggests that although RRI is part of the EU funding programme, Horizon Europe (The
European Commission, 2023b), other EU actors might not be familiar with the term and its
principles. A first step in improving the implementation process could therefore be to actually
consider RRI principles when developing legislation. Furthermore, it can be debated how well
stakeholder management was actually executed during the development, as companies have
been somewhat clumped together to instead be represented by industry associations like Dan-

5.1.4
Intentions of the CSRD
Finally, it would be appropriate to discuss the actual intentions and purpose of the CSRD. Based
on the description of the CSRD (European Parliament and Council of the EU, 2022), it is clear
that the aim is to improve sustainability throughout the EU, but also that the CSRD intervenes
with existing ways of working in reporting companies to achieve this goal. One might there-
fore ask whether all the challenges listed by the interviewees are simply the price of increasing
the morality of companies so that meaningful change can occur. And therefore also that the
implementation of the CSRD is supposed to be challenging and is supposed to force compa-
nies to move away from how they have worked with sustainability previously. As mentioned,
companies still focus first and foremost on maximising profits, but if this is not done in a sus-
tainable manner, other EU stakeholders such as the population and society as a whole might
suffer. The CSRD can in some ways been seen as an RRI tool itself, because it attempts to
reduce the societal consequences of the way companies are operated, as well as increasing their
morality and accountability. The question therefore is whether sympathy should even be given
to companies, since they are responsible for many sustainability related impacts - for example,
80 percent of global CO2 emissions stem from just 57 companies (Smithsonian, 2024), clearly
showing that sustainable development has not been achieved by the market itself. This does,
however, not mean that actions should not be taken in terms of mitigating the impacts of the
CSRD - but challenges that may be perceived as negative for reporting companies could also
be seen as signs that the CSRD actually works in terms of changing the way sustainability is
handled in the industry.

5.2
Recommendations
Based on the findings of this thesis and the previous discussion of the findings, recommen-
dations on how to improve responsibility when implementing future legislation and policies
related to sustainability will be given in the following.
5.2.1
Recommendations for anticipation
In terms of anticipation, there are several areas that can be improved in terms of making the im-
plementation process less challenging for stakeholders and thus mitigating the implementation
gap. First, since many challenges found in the GDRP implementation process also occurred in
relation to CSRD, research on other implementation processes could be applied at a deeper level.
By adequately considering past implementation processes, it can quickly be learnt what the an-
ticipated challenges are, which means that a plan for dealing with these challenges can be made
early on. Furthermore, the importance of having sufficient and timely guidance ready would
mean that the immediate first-year implementation would be less straining, as there would at
least be something to refer to for the stakeholders. This would especially be relevant if the type
of legislation is novel; therefore, it would make sense to evaluate just how much of a change
the legislation would actually demand, as this would also somewhat clarify the level of support
needed.
Efforts to actually measure the burden of the legislation should also be made with a heightened
focus on those directly affected by it. In order to do so, the implementation phase of a piece of
legislation should be given equal value to the expected macroeconomic affects, as successful
implementation with as little of an implementation gap as possible is what determines if the
long-term effects of the legislation will actually be realised. A suggestion on how to do this
would be to think about the legislation as a life cycle, where there are different phases that
require different actions to mitigate future challenges. Putting an increased focus on implemen-
tation and performing additional actions to support it would also mean that additional funding
would be required. To ensure that the transition and development of legislation is able to actu-
ally encompass the dimensions of RRI, sufficient resources must therefore be allocated in order
76
==PAGE:80==
Master Thesis
DTU Wind
to actually make a piece of legislation responsible towards its stakeholders. The anticipation
recommendations can be summarised as follows:
• Learn from insights from previous implementation, especially from academia.
• Have guidance set up before implementation starts.
• Consider the novelty of the legislation when evaluating the burden put on the subjects of
the legislation.
• Evaluate the implementation process carefully, so that focus is not solely put on macroe-
conomic consequences.
• Increase the importance of the implementation phase through the adoption of a life cycle
perspective.
• Ensure that the implementation process is properly funded.
5.2.2
Recommendations for inclusion
For inclusion, adjustments can also be made that will improve responsibilisation. A first step
could be to involve external stakeholders in the development process that will play a key role in
driving the implementation forward. As the developer will most likely not be in charge of every
part of the implementation, it is vital to ensure external drivers have the proper support and are
involved in the development process. This would mean that their role is properly understood,
leading to a more efficient way of supporting the implementation.
Furthermore, if legislation targets companies, it is important to understand that companies vary
in terms of structure and way of working based on the industry to which they belong. One can-
not simply divide companies into large ones and SME’s since their readiness to comply with a
piece of legislation is dependent on what is the norm of their industry. For example, companies
could instead be classified based on how ready they are to implement the legislation in their
organisation.
It is also important to collect as much feedback in as good a quality as possible while develop-
ing a piece of legislation. To get as many participants as possible to join in on the discussion,
77

Master Thesis
DTU Wind
authorities in the individual countries that the legislation is aimed at, can play a vital role in pro-
moting participation amongst stakeholders, so that they are actually aware that the participants
can help improve the legislation. Incentives for participating could also be given to stakehold-
ers so that those who normally do not participate can have a say in the development. Finally,
collecting feedback through surveys can mean that nuances are lost, so an emphasis on qualita-
tive feedback and open discussions that are not moderated would be beneficial to conduct. In
summary, the recommendations given for improving inclusion are as follows.
• Identify who will help drive the implementation and ensure that they receive sufficient
support.
• Do not just divide companies into large ones and SME’s - consider that more than two
types of companies exist, and that the type of industry they belong to also plays a central
role.
• Consider getting authorities to promote and perhaps incentivise participation in develop-
ment processes.
• Facilitate more qualitative discussions, reducing dependence on surveys.
5.2.3
Recommendations for reflexibility
Some points can also be raised in terms of reflexibility. A crucial aspect to understand is that
the interests of the subjects of the legislation typically vary compared to the interests of the leg-
islator. It is important to understand the incentives of stakeholders in terms of complying with
the legislation, as some might be inclined to put in the minimum effort if they cannot identify
benefits from the legislation. In cases where the market is expected to develop solutions itself,
there should be clear incentives that fit the purpose of why a stakeholder exists. This would
lead to the development of true best practices, as those who know the most about how directly
affected stakeholders work and operate would have incentives to develop best practices. If
best practices are developed by the subjects of the legislation themselves, this would also mean
that they would be less vulnerable to consultancies with inadequate competencies to assist with
compliance.
Developers should also be aware that legislation is part of often complex judicial systems, where
changes are not easy to make once a piece of legislation has been adopted. Considerations of

legal barriers and whether or not they will influence the implementation gap should therefore
be made. Finally, traditions and norms related to the field of the legislation at hand should
also be carefully evaluated - these cultural variations could lead to the creation of ripple ef-
fects, which could cause additional consequences that can be hard to identify from afar. The
recommendations relating to reflexivity can be summarised as follows:
• Consider that the purposes, interests, and incentives of those subject to the legislation
might be different than those of the legislators.
• Be aware that the flexibility of judicial systems is limited and that changes cannot be
easily made once legislation has been adopted.
• Consider national differences when developing legislation.
5.2.4
Recommendations for responsiveness
For responsiveness, it should be considered that due to the inflexibility of judicial systems,
legislation is difficult to change after it has been adopted, making responsiveness difficult to
achieve. It could therefore make sense to ensure that legislation is rolled out in steps instead of
all at once, as this allows for actually making changes based on feedback from the operational
phase. This can prevent legislation from solely being based on anticipation, but instead taking
into consideration how stakeholders actually end up working with the legislation. This would
also allow some practices to have been developed for some areas of the legislation earlier instead
of having everything developed all at once. Furthermore, instead of letting every individual
stakeholder develop their own way of complying with the legislation, coordinated efforts for
each industry should be initialised early on so that stakeholders can benefit from the findings
of others. Coordination must be led by authorities who should be adequately instructed on
how to guide this effort. There should also be created a suitable feedback system where these
procedural learnings can be collected and analysed so that they can help shape the next parts of
the legislation to be implemented. The recommendations for responsiveness can be summarised
as follows:
• Roll out legislation stepwise so that learnings can be made and applied continuously.
• Ensure that the feedback system for the legislation is set up properly so that learnings can
be collected.

Conclusion
This thesis attempted to answer the following research question: What insights can the prin-
ciples of Responsible Research and Innovation give regarding the implementation challenges
of the Corporate Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD) in Denmark? Furthermore, what
recommendations can be derived from these insights to enhance the future implementation of
sustainability-related laws and initiatives? The conclusion of the thesis work will be presented
below.
6.1
Themes found from interviews
Through interviews with eight interviewees belonging to 7 different stakeholder groups and
the analysis of secondary data, four themes related to the challenges in the implementation of
the CSRD were found. The complex nature and novelty of the CSRD and its reporting stan-
dards, the ESRS, the short implementation time combined with a market-driven implementation
process, a number of advantages for larger companies compared to the many disadvantages of
smaller companies, and a general lack of incentives are the general themes, which represent the
challenges addressed. The challenges were then analysed using the dimensions of Responsible
Research and Innovation through the application of the framework suggested by Stilgoe et al.,
2013 These dimensions are anticipation, inclusion, reflexivity, and responsiveness. This anal-
ysis demonstrated that there were areas of concern related to all four dimensions in terms of the
development process of the CSRD and the ESRS.
The approach chosen by the EU Commission failed to anticipate the size of the burden imposed
on stakeholders, underestimated implementation costs, and did not consider the need to have de-
tailed guidance ready at the beginning of the implementation process. The anticipated benefits
of the CSRD were also misjudged, and instead many of these were mentioned as challenges for
the interviewed companies. A focus on macro-level results and too little focus on the reporting
companies and the implementation phase itself could have caused the lack of anticipation.
In terms of inclusion, the outreach effort by the European Commission and the EFRAG had
shortcomings. Limited participation due to unawareness of that the CSRD was underdeveloped
has made it difficult to collect nuanced inputs from reporting companies. Since there were no
81
==PAGE:85==
Master Thesis
DTU Wind
real incentives for companies to participate, only resourceful companies would be expected to
have participated. The fact that the EFRAG relied on surveys over qualitative interviews may
also have restricted the collection of nuanced input.
Different perceptions, interests, and ways of working with sustainability between the EU and
reporting companies laid the ground for a lack of reflexivity in the development process. The
reporting companies do not necessarily share the perspectives of the EU, so the advantages
expected by the EU were not identified by the interviewees representing the reporting compa-
nies. The fact that reporting tradition is different from country to country would also be hard to
consider in standards such as the ESRS, which has further increased the implementation gap;
in addition, the experience and expertise within the reporting companies have not yet reached
the required level to work efficiently with the new legislation, which has made implementation
increasingly difficult.
The inflexible judicial system to which the CSRD and ESRS are subject has decreased the level
of responsiveness, due to it being difficult to change the legislation once it has been adopted.
By implementing all 12 ESRS at once, stepwise learning has been prevented. Furthermore,
there exists a bureaucratic distance between the EU and the reporting companies, meaning that
they instead reach out to their auditors to get answers. Auditors do, however, struggle with
interpreting the ESRS, as there are no real predecessors to these standards and since guidance has
been untimely. Finally, the current feedback system is also lacking in terms of being responsive.
6.2
Discussion of findings
The challenges addressed resemble those found in the GDPR implementation process. Lack of
expertise, difficulties in setting up systems and procedures, and insufficient guidance were chal-
lenges present in both the CSRD and GDPR implementation. This suggests that there might be
structural issues with the way the EU develops its legislation. In addition, coordination efforts,
legal complexity, and difficulties in balancing costs and benefits were found for both pieces of
legislation.
Auditors were expected to be some of the main drivers of the implementation, but their work
has proved challenging due to a lack of guidance, leaving them unprepared to address the many

Master Thesis
DTU Wind
interpretation questions that have risen because of the CSRD and ESRS.
In terms of Responsible Research and Innovation, co-creation and co-implementation should
be considered when roling of legislation and policies, but these are not present in the implemen-
tation process of the CSRD. Companies handle implementation individually, but it is recom-
mended that authorities lead efforts to actually coordinate implementation between stakehold-
ers. The participation of stakeholders should also be encouraged by the authorities, which has
not occurred. This is evident through the lack of participation of the companies in the develop-
ment of the CSRD and ESRS. A focus on stakeholder management, which considers companies
individually and not just as part of industry associations, could mitigate the implementation gap.
The intentions of the CSRD were also discussed. As the CSRD is a way to impose accountability
for societal impacts, one should perhaps consider with whom to actually sympathise with in this
matter. Companies are responsible for many impacts related to sustainability, and the market has
not been able to adjust its course, leading to the adoption of the CSRD. That challenges occur
can be viewed as the cost for companies for not having been able to improve sustainability
sufficiently, which has caused many consequences for the rest of the EU’s stakeholders, such
as the population, environmental matters, and society as a whole. However, further qualitative
data must be collected from the EU itself to answer how companies are perceived, which was
not possible to secure during the thesis period.

the findings and their discussion and were categorised into the four dimensions of Responsible

plementation process more carefully and increase the focus on the implementation by adopting
a life cycle perspective, learning from insights of previous implementation processes, ensuring
proper funding of the implementation process, having guidance ready before implementation
starts, and considering how novel a piece of legislation is when evaluating its burden on its
subjects. For inclusion, getting authorities to promote and incentivise participation, identifying
stakeholders that drive the implementation and support them sufficiently, facilitate qualitative

For increased reflexivity, it should be considered that the purpose and incentives might differ

the judicial systems should also be taken into account. In addition, national differences should
also be considered when developing legislation that covers several countries. To improve re-

way. The feedback system should also be set up efficiently so that comments from stakehold-
ers can be collected and considered. Authorities should also be an important driver when it
comes to coordinating industry efforts in terms of developing a methodology to comply with
the legislation.
6.4
Final remarks
This thesis sought to explore how Responsible Research and Innovation could be applied to
uncover insights related to the challenges of the implementation of the CSRD. A plethora of
challenges and themes were discovered, and the level of complexity of the CSRD suggests that
a study of a wider scope could be conducted, which would result in even deeper insights. As
the application of Responsible Research and Innovation in relation to this topic is novel, the
methodology was developed gradually throughout the thesis period.
Based on the findings of this thesis, one thing remains clear: The implementation gap of leg-
islation such as the CSRD exists and will continue to reoccur if legislation is developed and
implemented in the same way as with the CSRD and the GDPR. Most people, including the
interviewees in this thesis, want implementation to succeed, and an implementation process
that mitigates challenges as much as possible is vital in reaching the long-term political goals
established by institutions such as the EU. However, if the structural issues that lead to im-
plementation gaps are not addressed, the same challenges might occur over and over again.
This thesis provided concrete recommendations based on its findings on how to change the
development and implementation of legislation and policies, with Responsible Research and
Innovation being the cornerstone of these recommendations. Whether or not Responsible Re-
search and Innovation is the most efficient way to uncover how and what changes should be
