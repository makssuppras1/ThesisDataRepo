Abstract
The present study analyzes a techno-economic analysis of different charging solutions
for Battery Electric Multiple Units (BEMU) within local non-electrified railway lines in
Sjealland. The Danish government has set an ambitious goal of reducing the levels of
greenhouse emissions by 70% until 2030. The electrification of the major railway lines is
the most popular and widely implemented solution, and it has undoubtedly less environ-
mental impact than the operation of diesel rolling stock. However, the high investment
costs as well as the complexity of these systems make them appear unattractive and
insufficient solutions when it comes to local railway lines. The deployment of BEMUs
in short sections of the railway network is a scenario worth investigating. The present
study examines different charging alternatives from a spatial and economic point point
of view and it investigates the most efficient solution for the short and the long term
period.

1
Introduction
1.1
Problem description
It is widely known that a dominant factor contributing to the increase of greenhouse gas
emissions is derived from the transport sector. For this reason, the ambitious goal of
creating sustainable and environmentally friendly cities and societies, is partially based
on radical changes within both the private and the public transportation systems around
the world. The aim of creating a sustainable transport system, is inextricably linked
with the replacement of fossil fuels with alternatives that make use of renewable energy
sources that have minimum or zero impact on the environment.
Traditionally, the electrification of the rolling stock has been the most popular and widely
implemented alternative within the railway industry for transitioning into a greener mo-
bility. Thus, in the scope of creating a sustainable future that will eradicate the carbon
footprint generated by the transport sector, the rising of EMUs in combination with
the elimination of the diesel rolling stock is gaining more and more popularity over the
last years. Among the developed countries, it is the vast majority that chooses to elec-
trify their main railway lines while at the same time they are seeking for sustainable
and environmentally friendly solutions regarding their secondary local ones. Although
electric rolling stock has proved to be more energy efficient with an undoubtedly less
catastrophic impact compared to diesel trains, both the complexity and the high invest-
ment cost of the required infrastructure constitute a major drawback of such kind of
alternatives. However, the emerging state-of-the-art technologies such as hydrogen fuel
cells or lithium-ion batteries have been under the spotlight in the recent years with the
use of battery trains in railway lines especially in situations where their operation is
proven to be more cost efficient compared to electrification alternatives. In this way, the
construction of overhead line equipment is for many countries justified and implemented
mostly in main lines with increased traffic demand.
At the moment, less than 60% of Denmark’s railway network has been electrified in
major arteries of the country while diesel rolling stock is currently operating in local
lines that connect small towns. Over the last few years, the concept of the Battery
Electrical Multiple Units (BEMU) has already been introduced into the railway market
for several countries including Denmark, which implies the beginning of a new era.

==PAGE:11==
1.2
Motivation
It is well known that the transport sector still dominates in the rise of C02 emissions,
leading politicians into implementing new measures and policies aiming to the eradica-
tion of those harmful emissions while at the same time seeking solutions for transitioning
into a greener mobility.
Under this spectrum, in 2020, the Danish government established, as part of the SDGs,
the objective of eliminating greenhouse gas emissions by 70% until 2030. Hence, a deci-
sion was made of purchasing environmentally friendly trains with lithium-ion batteries,
that would replace the diesel ones on the local non-electrified lines. According to the
above, the obligation of creating a sustainable transportation network together with
the complexity and the high investment cost of electrifying railway lines, triggered the
detailed investigation of the operation of Battery Electric Multiple Units in secondary
local lines on the railway network in Denmark.
Thus, the scenario and the techno-economic analysis of operating BEMUs in such lines
is examined thoroughly in this thesis and it will be proven to be both a realistic and a
feasible alternative compared to electrifying the whole network. Furthermore, a more
detailed analysis was performed regarding the technical part of the establishment of such
an investment and its benefits while the upcoming challenges that can emerge of such
an investment were examined and evaluated thoroughly.
The current situation in Denmark together with the growing market of BEMUs are
both contributing factors that motivated the current thesis, which examines a techno-
economic study for charging infrastructure solutions in three secondary non-electrified
railway lines.
The methodology and the results acquired from this thesis could be
used for relevant studies concerning secondary-local railway lines within the railway
network of Denmark and could motivate transportation authorities into implementing
such alternatives while at the same time reaching closer to the sustainable development
goals.
1.3
Learning Objectives
In particular, the main objective of this project was to perform a techno-economic as-
sessment of BEMU in secondary non-electrified lines currently operated by diesel rolling
stock. The thesis mainly focused on the investigation of the efficient positioning of the
charging infrastructure within the northern part of Sjealland. Different ways of charging
were analyzed so as to achieve the most efficient and effective solution. The benefits
10
==PAGE:12==
and the drawbacks of each alternative were presented and an economic assessment was
accomplished. Hence, three railway lines were chosen, namely the Hornbækbanen, the
Lille Nord and the Gribskovbanen whereas the adjacent railway lines were also consid-
ered when proposing the final solution. One or more alternatives were found, and a
comparison of the different scenarios was presented. Secondary objectives:
• Study of operational lifetime of chosen battery system.
• Gather the operational and the performance data of the chosen lines that are
currently operated by diesel rolling stock.
• Define different BEMU alternatives, regarding the charging options as well as the
efficient positioning of charging infrastructure and compare them with each other.
Research objectives:
• Classify Electrification alternatives (OHLE and OHLI).
• Investigate the sufficiency of the capacity of the used transformer station to supply
power to the examined area.
• Define charging options (Static or dynamic)
• Determine infrastructure components for the necessary charging infrastructure of
battery trains.
• Cost estimation and comparison of the alternatives in terms of the total cost of
the charging infrastructure.
1.4
Organization of the thesis
The thesis consists of six chapters. The first chapter presents the problem researched, the
motivation of analyzing such a project and the learning objectives. Chapter 2 demon-
strates the remarkable points of the electrification systems and it gives an overview of
the battery electric multiple units as well as the current situation in Denmark regarding
that innovative means of public transport. Within this chapter, the examined area is
also presented and the operational characteristics of the lines are described thoroughly.
Chapter 3 introduces the methodology used for performing the calculations of the power
and as an extent of the energy consumption. In chapter 4, the different scenarios for
charging infrastructure options are illustrated and analyzed. In the next section of the
thesis, an economic assessment of the alternatives followed, including all the necessary
aspects to be used when constructing a charging station. Besides the cost assessment,
other factors were also considered in order to conclude to a final proposal. A sensi-
tivity analysis followed where the most critical factors of the cost estimation changed,
aiming to investigate how the final cost will be affected. A third sensitivity analysis
11
==PAGE:13==
was accomplished considering an increment in the running resistance so as to include
the wind resistance that can be generated in more extreme weather conditions. Finally,
the conclusion of this study is presented followed by a brief discussion regarding a few
untouched issues and further recommended research.

2
General background
2.1
Electric Multiple Units
The first step towards the decarbonization of the railway sector relies on the electri-
fication of major lines as well as the replacement of diesel rolling stock. Today, the
deployment of electric multiple units (EMU) is considered as the most preferable op-
tion, in terms of environmental, passenger service as well as operational efficiency. In
other words, electric trains are cleaner, lighter and undoubtedly more cost efficient. One
of the most predominant factors that differentiates EMU over diesel trains, is the higher
power-to-weight ratio, which enables them to accelerate and decelerate faster. There-
fore, total travel times can be reduced significantly and thus, ensuring an effective and
an efficient service. From an environmental perspective, there is no doubt that electric
traction is superior than the diesel one. In particular, electric trains emit 20-30% less
carbon emissions per passenger mile than diesel ones, and the generation stations use
special assemblies so as to control and eliminate the GHG emissions. This is of the
utmost importance, especially in Denmark, where the production of electric power is
carried out from non-fossil sources, and more and more renewable energy sources are
being developed and used under the spectrum of SDGs. Furthermore, modern electric
units are using the regenerative braking function in which the traction motors act as
generators by sending current back into the supply system and consequently saving a
significant amount of energy. From an economic point of view, EMUs abound over diesel
rolling stock, mainly due to the reduced manufacturing, maintenance and leasing cost.
Specifically, electric trains are approximately 20% cheaper to lease and to be manufac-
tured whereas due to their light mass, they cause on average 13% less wear to the tracks.
As a result, the track maintenance costs are typically 33% lower. Finally, the levels of
comfort of electric trains are rather high since the absence of diesel engine causes less
vibrations [15].
Banendanmark has set an ambitious target to electrify the majority of the Danish rail-
way network until 2027 under the Electrification Program that is currently being im-
plemented over the whole country. The Danish Parliament’s funding for that project is
reaching 12 billion DKK. Until the target year, 820km of the state railway have been
electrified, which in total comprises of 1362km of railway track since most sections are
double tracks. As of today moment, 1756km of the railway network is electrified, namely
the section from Øresund to Padborg via Storebælt and the lines Tinglev-Søndenborg
as well as København-Helsingør [3]. In addition, all trains that are running on non-
electrified lines currently in denmark, are powered by diesel engines. That is to say and
13
==PAGE:15==
for all the aforementioned reasons, the major railway lines are going to be electrified in
the upcoming years.
However, that is not the case when it comes to secondary, smaller sections that are
currently operated by diesel rolling stock. Electrifying these parts is not a desirable
scenario since the capital cost of the equipment as well as the complexity of such a
system is relatively high, making this solution unattractive. Thus, battery electrical
multiple units can be a rational and a realistic alternative solution especially for the
secondary, short lines.
2.1.1
Electrification systems
Throughout the last century, Europe has undergone a vast transition and upgrade from
the traditional diesel to electrified railway systems, albeit at different times for each
country. Thus, it was only natural that this transition comprised various discrete re-
alizations in terms of the technology of the applied electrification system. A collective
overview of the various specifications of each system in different parts of Europe is given
in figure 1. This classification entails the definition of the voltage, the contact system,
as well as the type of applied current.
Figure 1: Electrification systems in Europe [18]
As expected with every new technology application, in its early stages, electrification

When it comes to railway systems in Denmark, three main specifications are employed.
The predominant electrification system is the 25kV AC at 50Hz. Some suburban trains

2.1.2
DC current system
Up to the present time, the best type of motor widely used in railway systems utilizes
direct current. The DC motors are way more efficient than the AC since their manufac-
ture is rather simple, they occupy small space and they are highly serviceable. Back to
the beginning of the 20th century, DC current systems were used for the electrification of
the railway lines. Railway engineers based this decision to the fact the they were seeking
to achieve the best operating motor conditions. This is why DC systems were employed
within several countries, so as to use the same type of current with the motors. The
main downside of low voltage DC current systems is the requirement of higher amount
of current so as to be transmitted effectively. That to be achieved, a large cross section
15
==PAGE:17==
of contact wire should be used ( > 400-800mm2) [19] whereas the substations must be
placed in near proximity (15-20km for 1500V and 35-40km for 3000V)[2]. Although the
construction of a DC system is rather simple, the increased weight of the overhead line

2.2
Railway power supply systems
Electricity is supplied to trains through four stages:
• Power generation
• Power transmission
• Power feeding
• Power collection
First of all, the power generation takes place at an electricity generating source, usually
a power station. Then, it is transmitted from the national grid to feeding points or else
converter substations which are located next to transmission lines and near the track. In
Denmark the high voltage transmission grid is 132kV and 400kV in Sjælland (Eastern
Denmark) and 150kV and 400kV in Fyn and Jylland (Western Denmark). The overhead
contact line system does not have the same voltage as the power supply network and
therefore a converter is required. Specifically, the substations convert the three-phase
high voltage to one-phase and reduce it to 25kV so it can be transmitted to the OLE and
finally give power to the train through the pantograph. Although transmitting electricity
at high voltages is more economical, it is true that larger clearances are required for safety
purposes, that need more expensive equipment as well as more complex construction.
Thus, it has been found that 25kV is the optimum voltage for main line trains and this
is why it is used in several countries, including Denmark.

• The electrical system
• The traffic system
• The control system
The electrical system includes the feeder stations, the railway transmission grid, the
generators and storage systems as well as the overhead line equipment. Regarding the

the aforementioned two systems.

2.3
Battery Electric Multiple Units
The concept of battery trains is not new in Europe.
Germany was the pioneer by
introducing such trains at the end of 19th century.
Specifically, in 1896, Palatinate
Railway manufactured the first battery train with lead batteries on board that were
charging with DC current, having battery capacity of 12,6khW and offering a catenary
free operation with a total range of 4km. Over the last decades, modern rechargeable
batteries with higher density and the ability to be charged with AC current in less than
10 minutes have been developed and used by several manufacturers. Today’s BEMUs
are equipped with pantographs and batteries and they can both operate under the
catenary or perform a catenary free operation (CFO) in battery mode. The latter can
be considered as the fundamental advantage of battery rolling stock and it is the reason
why BEMUs have been characterized as an attractive solution for short railway lines.
Figure 6: BEMU Talent 3 Bombardier [39]
In particular, the catenary free operation, as its name indicates, benefits from the fact
that it avoids the construction of the complex overhead equipment which is necessary
for electric trains to operate. The absence of the overhead line equipment can be a
significant cost saving especially for the short term period.
Moreover, maintenance
costs are relatively low since the batteries require only an occasional replacement of air
filters. CFO is also important for challenging spots such as underpasses or tunnels where
there are limitations regarding the clearance, making the construction of the catenary
even more demanding. Besides that, CFO is suitable for tracks under maintenance or
those facing problems with electromagnetic compatibility.
Manufacturers are constantly developing and increasing the range of BEMUs in CFO and
today’s latest models can drive up to 120km. This parameter is rather critical and for
20
==PAGE:22==
that it has been increased significantly over the past few decades whereas it is expected
to be even higher in the future. The batteries should not be discharged completely
and therefore charging must occur at regular time intervals. Today’s technology allows
charging via the pantograph from the overhead catenary line, which should have a
sufficient total length.
BEMUs can be a suitable alternative for partially electrified railway lines along the net-
work. Within the electrified sections, the trains are using the energy from the catenary
whereas in the non-electrified sections it utilizes the energy that is stored in the battery.
The figure below illustrates simply the typical operation mode of a BEMU running in

territory, the train is constantly fed by electric energy by the catenary.
Figure 7: Typical operation mode of a BEMU [11]
The charging operation is based on the standard voltages that are already used, meaning
that there is no need for extra electrical equipment. The charging stations supply power
to the trains according to the standard electrification voltages. The AC 25kV 50Hz used
in Denmark allows for 80A current which is lower than the limit of 150A, over which
the pantograph would start burning.

2.3.2
Different battery technologies
The battery comprises of anodes, cathodes as well as of an electrolyte and its role is
the conversion of chemical energy into electrical. Throughout the past few decades,
different battery technologies have been used as energy storage solutions. Several types
of batteries using different materials have been developed and they have been used by
railway manufacturers that are seeking for the most efficient possible solutions. The
comparison and the evaluation of these technologies is based on four parameters namely
the energy and power density, the life cycle as well as the usability of the battery’s
density. The most common battery solutions that have been used in the railway sector
are listed below:
22
==PAGE:24==
i) Lead acid batteries:
The first operating battery trains in Germany were equipped with lead acid batteries
that were charging during night with DC current. Their low density as well as the short
life cycle led the manufacturers searching for more efficient solutions[20].
ii) Nickel-cadmium (NiCd) and Nickel-metal-hydride (NiMH):
Nickel-cadmium (NiCd) were introduced into the railway industry offering higher energy
density and longer life cycle compared to lead acid batteries. However, their major dis-
advantage was the ”memory effect” issue which does not allow them to fully charge their
batteries and exploit their high energy density. Asides that, cadmium is a rather toxic
heavy metal which can cause serious environmental damage. For all the aforementioned
reasons, NiCd batteries were replaced by nickel-metal-hydride (NiMH), that offer higher
capacity and primarily they do not suffer from the memory effect issue [20].
iii) Lithium-Ion (LiIon):
The most dominant battery technology in today’s railway industry is the one of lithium-
Ion batteries. Providing the highest energy density among all today’s battery technolo-
gies, low self-discharge rate as well as a relatively low cost per Wh, makes them the
most attractive rechargeable energy storage option up until now. Common lithium-Ion
cells that are used in conventional vehicles consist of graphite anodes and metal oxide
cathodes [20]. Although they excel over other technologies, they have some drawbacks
that have to combat. Their inability to manage any thermal overload, may result to
combustion and as an extend to lower life cycle. To address this issue, other materi-
als have been used as anodes instead of graphite such as lithium titanate or titanium
niobium oxide [16]. For instance, the latter material is under development so as to be
used on the Siemens Mireo Plus B in the future, providing a significant high capacity, a
relatively short charging time and increased range in catenary free operation.

Environmental aspects of BEMUs
The amount of CO2 emissions derived from the railway network in Denmark was mea-
sured at 247.000 tonnes in 2019, including all passenger and freight trains. This number
is expected to be reduced down to 70.000 by the end of 2030, within the scope of the
Electrification Programme introduced by Banendanmark (BDK) [3]. Large projects re-
garding the electrification of the major railway lines in Denmark have already started.
However, that is not the case when it comes to secondary local railway lines where inno-
23
==PAGE:25==
vative, eco-friendly solutions should be investigated. The replacement of diesel rolling
stock with BEMUs will prove to be beneficial from an environmental perspective.
Diesel operation is admittedly outdated; its energy utilization is quite poor. It is well
established that the operation of diesel rolling stock is lacking in terms of environmental
impact mitigation and therefore battery trains will provide a valuable relief in that
aspect. The fact that renewable sources of energy are gaining an increasing foothold
throughout the world in the production of electricity, is the cornerstone of a battery
train’s more green approach. There is a clear trade-off in the energy efficiency of those
two technologies. For example, it is certain that battery rolling stock overall mass is
greater than that of a conventional electric train, which results in a greater energy
consumption. At the same time, however, they collect braking energy more efficiently,
without major losses, hinting a clear possibility for energy consumption equalization.
Current assessments have shown a 2-3 times energy efficiency deficit between diesel and
battery trains. Essentially, this leap in performance amounts to a environmental gain of
minus 2 kg of emitted CO2 for every kilometer traveled, by simply replacing diesel with
battery rolling stock.
The environmental footprint of the implementation of BEMU’s charging infrastructure
is not considerable but nonetheless inevitable. The construction of overhead line equip-
ment, including the masts, the concrete foundation as well as cable works, will have a
negative impact to the environment. According to BDK’s report [3], a total amount of

3
Methodology
Taking all the aforementioned into consideration, it can be concluded that BEMUs are
able to run both under catenary and perform a catenary free operation (CFO) while
providing numerous advantages. To achieve the CFO, it should be ensured that the
batteries are charged adequately, being able to cover the necessary range. The ultimate
goal of this chapter is the determination of the energy requirement.
Therefore, the
power consumption was calculated first and then by multiplying it with the time, the
respective consumed energy was computed.
In general, there are three methods of estimating the power consumption:
1. Considering the TSI recommendations
2. The Simple calculation method
3. Simulations
The technical specification for interoperability (TSI) provides the basis for several Euro-
pean standards in terms of energy consumption that must be fulfilled by every country.
The simple calculation method uses preliminary data and considers basic assumptions
in order for the procedure to proceed. In particular, it involves the estimation of the
average demanded power for supplying a railway line and it is based on the approxima-
tion of power in relation to the train’s movement situation. Finally, the third method
requires knowledge of a priori information in the form of relevant data, regarding the
infrastructure or the traffic demand in which a software simulation tool is used giving
accurate results. For the purpose of this project, it was decided that the simple calcu-
lation method, which gives results with a 10% deviation from those derived from the
simulations, is sufficient for the calculations of power and energy consumption.
3.1
Assumptions
To apply the simple calculation method properly, a series of assumptions were considered
and they are listed below.
• The operation of the BEMU is seen as an extension of the electrified line to a
non electrified line between Helsingør and Gilleleje, and between Helsingør and
Hillerød.
• Helsingør station will be used as a charging spot for each different alternative.
• The gradient of the line is negligible and it does not affect the calculations.
• The wind resistance is not considered.
32
==PAGE:34==
• The calculations apply for an ambient temperature of °5 C.
• The BEMUs operate in accordance with the timetable, provided by the Lokaltog
that currently applies for diesel rolling stock.
• The trains stop at every possible station within the line.
• The operating BEMUs are the Siemens type “Mireo”, reaching a maximum speed
of 140km/h, with a range of 120km.
• The trains are equipped with lithium-ion batteries with a total weight of 9,5 tonnes.
• The trains have 3 axles, weighting 20 tonnes each.
• The track curves as well as the gradient of the line are negligible and do not
generate substantial resistance forces.

3.3
Train dynamics
The motion of a rolling stock is achieved when the tractive effort overcomes the resistance
forces while at the same time is lower than the adhesion force [2]. In particular, the
resistance forces generated throughout the motion of a train are the following:
• Running resistance
• Track resistance
• Acceleration resistance
3.3.1
Running resistance
The running resistance is a rather important factor when analyzing the energy consump-
tion of a train. In principal, it is widely used to assess the train performance as well
as to create speed profiles. The energy consumption can be attributed to four energy
losses that are derived from the running resistance, the gear, the traction circuit as well
as the brake [8]. Figure 19 illustrates the energy breakdown of energy consumption of
a local and a rapid train. The running resistance is of the utmost importance when
investigating power supply alternatives since it constitutes the main source of energy
consumption. Specifically, energy losses derived from running resistance for a local train
can reach up to 37%, according to the figure below..

3.3.2
Track resistance
The track resistance depends on the geometry of the track, and it is equal with the
sum of the resistances caused by the curves and the gradients of the line. The former
37
==PAGE:39==
is generated along the curves where there is also friction between wheel flange and rail,
known as flange resistance as well as wheel slippage. The latter represents the force
that is generated when the train runs in an inclined track and it is caused by gravity
(Newton’s law). The inclination of the railway tracks is insignificant and considering
the fact that the maximum height in Denmark is 126 meters, the gravity resistance was
assumed to be negligible [2].

The replacement of the diesel rolling stock with more environmentally friendly multiple
units is about to take place within the next few years. Thus, an extension of the existing
overhead line (OLE) section or a construction of a completely new overhead equipment
would be required to be used as charging infrastructure. The efficient positioning of the
charging station is a rather important issue that can ensure a long term cost reduction
and in general, a more efficient and effective operation of the battery multiple units.
There are two major electrification alternatives (see figure 22) that differentiate from
each other according to the positioning of the overhead line equipment. In particular,

The peak values of the graph represent the transient loads that are generated due to the
simultaneous operation of multiple units. The peak loads that may occur throughout a
day are not considered for the examination of the substation’s capacity and therefore the
important factor here is the average value of the power. Transient loads are described as
significant changes in the magnitude and the value of power. In general, the transformers
are designed to compensate for the increased heat which is generated by such overloads,
by using standardized cooling systems. In other words, transformer manufacturers are
following guaranteed temperature limits in order to control any sudden increase of the
heat. Thus, they are not considered for the rating of the substation’s capacity.

4.2
OHL Extension
The increased waiting time at Helsingør as well as considerations for the future operation
of the battery rolling stock across all the local railway lines, motivates the decision
makers to consider the most effective solutions. Although the scenario of not adding
any extra infrastructure is the simplest and the most cost efficient one, at least for the
short term period, future works and changes in traffic should be considered as well. The
extension of the existing overhead line equipment (OHL-Extension) does not require a
new converter substation since it can be fed by an existing one. Thus, it can be deemed
as an attractive alternative that should be examined thoroughly.
Having said that, it was decided to investigate two different cases of overhead line
extension. The first case (OHLE-Gilleleje) suggests the extension of the catenary only
towards the town of Gilleleje. Consecutively, trains will be able to charge while running
and as a result, the increased dwelling time of 17 minutes at Helsingør will be avoided
or decreased as much as possible.
The second option (OHLE-Hillerød) entails the extension of the wires towards both
Gilleleje and Hillerød considering the future operation of battery trains in other adjacent
local railway lines. The implementation of the aforementioned alternatives necessitates
dynamic charging of batteries or, in other words, charging while driving. In both cases,
the required length of the overhead line equipment should be calculated based on the
speed of the rolling stock and on the time needed for charging.

5
Economic assessment of the alternatives
An economic assessment of the alternatives was followed aiming to compare and examine
thoroughly the aforementioned scenarios.
The comparison was based mainly on the
required infrastructure costs of each alternative. Regarding the necessary infrastructure,
a price catalogue involving all the used components was considered.
Figure 31: Price catalogue [34]
• Feeder stations: Substations or else feeder stations convert the three-phase AC
current into one-phase and distribute it to the overhead line equipment. They
are also used for addressing emergency situations (i.e fallen OCS conductor) by
cutting of the supply.
• Neutral sections: They are described as short insulated ”dead” sections of over-
head line, dividing the catenary into different parts that are fed from another
substation and they are connected to different phases. The neutral sections are
also known as ”dead zone” due to the fact that there is no flow of current and
therefore, they should be placed far from the stations and areas that the train holds
still. They are placed at the feeder points as well as in their between distance.
• Switches and section insulators: Switches and section insulators are necessary
when electrifying a track in order to ensure that in case of maintenance or a failure
in the OCS system, that track can be cut off while maintaining the traffic in the
adjacent tracks.
• Overlaps: The overhead line should be divided into sub elementary sections called
tension lengths, reaching a maximum length of 1,5km. The connection between two
tension lengths takes place within an overlap which is schematically depicted in 32.
64
==PAGE:66==
This installment includes two twin cantilevers, two balance weight terminations
for keeping the wire stretched as well two back-ties for supporting the masts. It
was assumed that they are placed every 1,5km.
Figure 32: Construction overlap [38]
• Immunization: Magnetic field is generated in the area between two conductors
and therefore there is a high risk of interference with the signalling and telecommu-
nication systems that exist along the line. As a result, immunization components
are used along the track to prevent this problem.
To perform the cost estimation of each alternative, the following assumptions were con-
sidered:
• The existing railway substation is located 5m far from the track, and therefore the
feeding cable which connects it with the track was set equal to 5 meters.
• Construction overlaps are placed every 1,5km, at the connection of two tension
lengths. One construction overlap consists of two twin cantilevers, two balance
weight terminations and two back-ties.
• Stations with two tracks consist of 2 sectionizers and 2 switches whereas those
with only one track do not include any sectionizer or switch.
• The immunization system is placed along the electrified line and therefore it has
the same length with the OCS-system.
5.1
Do nothing scenario
The ”do-nothing” scenario is undoubtedly the most cost efficient compared to the other
alternatives due to the fact that the main expenses rely mainly on the purchase as
well as the maintenance of the battery trains. In particular, the range of 120km that
battery trains can travel in catenary free operation is sufficient to cover round trips
in the examined area where maximum distances reach 99km. However, it has several
drawbacks as it requires to amplify the dwelling time at Helsingør up to 6 minutes in

order to complete a full charging, resulting in an increase of the total travel time. Thus,
it is considered as an unattractive option.
Besides that, Hillerød constitutes a terminal station for the adjacent local railway lines
namely 960R (Griskovbanen/Hillerød -Tisvildeleje) and 920R/E (Frederiksværkbanen)
and their connection can be seen in the figure 33 below.

6
Conclusion
The present study conducted a techno-economical assessment of different charging so-
lutions of battery electric multiple units. The analysis started by examining the oper-
ational characteristics of the lines and the existing timetables which are operated by
diesel rolling stock. A methodology was followed aiming to estimate the consumed en-
ergy for each trip, considering a worst-case scenario in terms of energy consumption,
of stopping at each possible station. Then, different solutions regarding the positioning
of the necessary infrastructure for charging BEMUs were investigated. The decision of
implementing battery rolling stock in the northern part of Sjealland necessitates the con-
sideration of numerous aspects besides the total needed infrastructure cost. The analysis
started with the do-nothing alternative which does not include the construction of any
additional infrastructure to be used for charging. In contrast, the existing electrification
station at Helsingør can be used to supply power and charge the BEMUs. Although this
scenario is the most economical and the investment costs rely mainly on the purchase
and the maintenance of the new rolling stock, the analysis showed that there should be
an increment on the waiting time at Helsingør to achieve a complete charging. Moreover,
the fact that this option is applicable only for the three examined lines since the other
two adjacent railway lines cannot be served, made this option unattractive.
Hence, the investigation continued with the possibility of an extension of the already
existing OCS towards Gilleleje. This option solves the problem of the increased dwelling
73
==PAGE:75==
time at Helsingør and it combines both static and dynamic charging.
The cost as-
sessment for that scenario resulted in a low cost investment which can be efficient and
applicable for the “triangle area” (Helsingør-Gilleleje-Hillerød). However, similarly with
the first case, the adjacent railway lines cannot be served and thus extra charging spots
may be needed in the long term. The final two alternatives suggest the construction of
an “island” that can be used as charging station either at Gilleleje or at Hillerød. The
case of placing a station at Gilleleje was inspected first in terms of total infrastructure
cost and the benefits as well as the drawbacks were discussed. A charging ”island” there
can be used by other means of public transport such as electric cars, buses, bicycles or
the ferries that commute between Helsingør and Helsingborg daily. This scenario serves
effectively the three lines while an adjustment of the timetable may be needed for the
trains of the line 950R. The trains running at 920R/E should be able to charge within
it in order not to interfere with the other lines. Finally, the construction of the new
regional hospital at Sjealland as well as the difficulty to find an effective option to serve
all the five lines, led to the scenario of building a charging station at Hillerød station.
The charging time follow the current timetable and all the northern part of Sjealland
can be served without any further adjustments. The major downside of this project
is the increased infrastructure cost which was derived from the requirement of a new
converter substation to “feed” the track. However, this construction will benefit at a
greater extent the surrounding area and will enhance the transport connections. For all
the aforementioned reason, this was considered as the best solution and it is the one
proposed to be further analyzed in the future.

7
Discussion and further research
The calculations of determining the amount of energy consumption for each scenario were
based on several assumptions and preliminary data. In particular, they were performed
for the ambient temperature of 5°C. In lower temperatures the capacity of the battery
decreases resulting in a shorter driving range in catenary free operation. In addition to
that, in lower temperatures, the auxiliary power takes higher values since the cooling as
well as the air conditioning systems operate on their best performance aiming to retain
the temperature. Five years ago the manufacturers of Bombardier released the Talent
3 having a range equal to 80km. Today, Siemens has introduced trains type “Mireo”
that can reach 120km in CFO. This number is expected to be increased even more the
upcoming decades making the construction of additional overhead equipment obsolete.
Implementing a new system to the existing local railway lines, may require, at least
for the long term period, some adjustments on the timetable aiming to achieve the
most efficient and effective performance. In other words, none of the aforementioned
parameters should remain “frozen”; on the contrary, this solution would require further
find tuning and optimization of all related aspects. Thus, a further research concerning
the adaptation of the timetable for the proposed solution could be conducted.
Furthermore, a future research that could be done is the investigation as well as the com-
parison of the aforementioned alternatives according to the environmental perspective.
Given the shear magnitude of this project, an environmental assessment to complement
the overall impact and efficiency of the application, could be considered. The construc-
tion of new charging facilities affects the natural landscape of the area as well as the
flora and the fauna.
