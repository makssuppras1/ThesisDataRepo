Abstract
Riblets, longitudinal grooves inspired by shark skin denticles, are a well­known passive
flow control and have been studied for their effects on turbulent flows, in particular drag
reduction and turbulence suppression. This thesis investigates these effects through a
combined approach of high fidelity large eddy simulations (LES) and calibrated turbulence
models (RANS). The riblet geometry studied is the one proposed by [1] as the best trade­
off between drag reduction and mechanical resistance.
Large eddy simulations (LES) are used to resolve near­wall turbulent structures over
trapezoidal riblets, successfully reproducing the drag­reducing behavior reported in prior
experimental and numerical studies. A smooth­wall baseline is first simulated to deter­
mine reference wall quantities, which are then used to non dimensionalize the riblet ge­
ometry. With a wall resolved setup and strict near­wall resolution criteria, the LES cap­
ture the key physical mechanisms behind drag reduction, yielding a 5% decrease in drag
coefficient, consistent with the literature, though slightly underestimated due to domain
constraints.
Based on the LES results, the calibration strategy of Aupoix et al. [2] is applied to the
Spalart Allmaras RANS model, enabling low cost simulations of riblet induced drag re­
duction. The calibrated model, with an optimized coefficient Cv1 = 11.1, reproduces the
drag reduction observed for the Bechert et al.[1] riblet design on smooth walls, though
certain riblet effects remain less accurately represented.
A significant breakthrough of this work consists of the direct comparison of LES and cali­
brated RANS under identical flow conditions, bridging the gap between high fidelity sim­
ulation and practical engineering tools. The methodology provides a solid basis for future
large domain studies and experimental validation, with potential applications to industrial
scale drag reduction in wind energy, aerospace and automotive.

1
Introduction
The goal of this Master Thesis is to numerically investigate the impact of riblet surfaces
on a turbulent boundary layer, with the goal of demonstrating that a properly designed
riblet geometry can lead to a reduction in wall shear stress and, consequently, drag. In
addition, this work also aims to combine, within a single project, the numerical analysis
of a turbulent boundary layer over riblets, conducted via large eddy simulations, with a
strategic modeling approach for drag reduction using well­known and reliable RANS tur­
bulence models that are also less computationally demanding. All of these tasks will be
done adopting the same boundaries, initial conditions, and physical flow properties for all
the different parts of the project. This ensures a direct and reliable comparison between
the components of this process.
Riblets are microstructured surfaces inspired by the dermal denticles of fast­swimming
sharks and have been experimentally shown to provide drag reduction by aligning near­
wall turbulent structures and inhibiting cross­stream momentum exchange. In particular,
the trapezoidal geometry—characterized by its sharp grooves and well­defined spacing—
has proven to be one of the most effective designs in reducing skin friction drag by up to 8%
in turbulent flows over flat plates (Bechert et al. [1]). Advancements in the understanding
of riblets and their effects on turbulent boundary layers are essential to promote their
wider application in industrial fields. The drag reduction achievable through this type of
passive flow control can be beneficial in various sectors, both to reduce fuel consumption
in aeronautical, automotive industries, etc., and to improve the efficiency of, for example,
wind energy production.
The first part of the study focuses on conducting Large Eddy Simulations (LES) of a turbu­
lent channel flow over such riblet surfaces, with the aim of reproducing and validating the
drag­reducing behavior observed in the cited experiments and numerical analyses. The
numerical domain and boundary conditions are inspired by previous works such as Choi
et al. [3], which presented Direct Numerical Simulations (DNS) of riblets in a fully devel­
oped channel flow, and Jahanmiri et al. [4], who used LES to investigate drag­reducing
surfaces in wall­bounded turbulence.
Prior to riblet implementation, a baseline flow configuration over smooth walls is simulated
to extract key flow quantities such as the friction velocity Uf, the wall shear stress, and the
dimensionless wall distance y+. These values are necessary to non­dimensionalize the
riblet geometry, since optimal riblet spacing and height are typically defined in wall units
(e.g., s+ ≈15−20, h+ ≈8−15).
The geometries, both with riblets and smooth, are constructed using the CAD software
SolidWorks and imported into STAR­CCM+, the commercial CFD tool used throughout the
project. Mesh generation and refinement near the wall are critical for resolving the near­
wall structures in LES; for this reason, a wall­resolved LES approach is adopted with grid
spacing and timestep selected to satisfy y+ < 1 and Courant number constraints.
In the second part of the project, Reynolds­Averaged Navier­Stokes (RANS) models are
calibrated and tested to evaluate whether such lower­cost models can reproduce the ef­
fects observed in LES. Since RANS models inherently rely on turbulence closure as­
sumptions and averaging, their ability to capture small­scale drag­reduction mechanisms
is limited. Therefore, the calibrated RANS models are compared against the high­fidelity

==PAGE:14==
LES results not only in terms of global drag metrics, but also by examining flow structures
and turbulence statistics near the wall.
The ultimate objective of this two­step process is to determine whether RANS models,
once properly calibrated using LES data, can provide a reliable and computationally ef­
ficient alternative for predicting riblet­induced drag reduction in industrial applications.
Given the growing interest in passive flow control and energy efficiency in the wind energy
industry, this study contributes to bridging the gap between experimental observations,
high­fidelity simulations, and practical turbulence modeling strategies.

2
State of the Art
2.1
Overview of Previous Work
After the oil crisis in the late 1970’s, many different solutions for aerodynamic drag re­
duction were investigated to improve the fuel consumption of cars, airplanes, and ships.
Among these strategies, riblets were of great interest, and their effect on a turbulent
boundary layer has been studied both experimentally (Walsh [5]) and numerically (Choi
et al. [3]). Walsh, at NASA Langley Research Center, studied a flow in a closed circuit
wind tunnel and showed that riblets with s+ < 30 and h+ < 25 consistently present a
net drag reduction. This project took into account two different riblet shapes, respectively
V­shaped and U­shaped longitudinal grooves. The experimental results from Walsh [5]
indicate that riblets with dimensions of s+ = h+ = 12 and h+ = 8, s+ = 16 can lead to
drag reductions up to 8%.
Choi et al. [3] compared direct numerical simulations for different sizes of V­shaped riblets
with the experimental data carried out by Walsh [5]. The simulations consist of a flow in a
closed channel with all smooth surfaces except for a ”ribleted” bed. Two different values of
spacing were studied, respectively s+ = 40 and s+ = 20, and each one of these sizes was
studied with both a 90° and a 60° ridge angle. The aim of this project was also to better
understand the mechanism of drag reduction. The results showed that only those with
s+ = 20 were drag­reducing configurations, and a 5­6% drag reduction was calculated in
the case of a 60° ridge angle, showing a good agreement with experimental data.
The mechanism underlying drag reduction was identified as the restriction of streamwise
vortices in the near­wall region, leading to a reduction in momentum transport and wall
shear stress. The effect was found to be confined to the inner boundary layer, where
velocity fluctuations and Reynolds shear stresses were reduced in drag­reducing config­
urations. When the riblet spacing was sufficiently small (s+ = 20), streamwise vortices
were prevented from entering the riblet valleys, limiting their interaction with the wetted
surface and reducing the downwash of high­speed fluid. This resulted in a decrease in tur­
bulent intensities near the riblets and an overall reduction in shear stress. Conversely, for
larger spacings (s+ = 40), vortices fit within the riblet valleys, exposing a greater surface
area to sweep motions and negating the drag­reducing effect. These findings confirm that
optimal riblet performance relies on a balance between vortex size and riblet spacing to
ensure minimal interaction between the downwash motions and the wetted surface. Ad­
ditionally, the reduction in all three components of vorticity fluctuations in drag­reducing
configurations indicates that riblets disrupt the near­wall turbulence regeneration cycle,
further contributing to drag reduction. The dominance of ejection events away from the
wall and the localized suppression of sweep events near the riblets reinforce the idea
that riblets act primarily by altering the turbulence structure in the immediate wall vicinity,
rather than modifying the outer flow.
Goldstein et al. [6] extended the study of riblet­induced drag reduction using direct nu­
merical simulations but adopted a different approach. Instead of explicitly meshing the
riblets, they employed the immersed boundary technique, introducing a spatially localized
body force to mimic the effect of the grooves. This method offers flexibility in modeling
complex geometries and incurs minimal computational overhead, though its accuracy re­
lies on properly imposing the force field to replicate flow­riblet interactions. Goldstein et
al. [6] investigated the effect of riblets on turbulent flow by imposing a damping force

on the spanwise velocity fluctuations at locations corresponding to riblet crests. Their
simulations tested the hypothesis that riblets contribute to drag reduction by suppressing
cross­flow fluctuations. The results supported this idea, showing that damping spanwise
motions alone could reproduce key effects observed in real riblet flows, suggesting that
this mechanism plays a fundamental role in the drag­reducing behavior of riblets.
The experiments conducted by Bechert et al. [1] further improved the drag reduction by
studying an oil flow over a surface with riblets with an adjustable geometry. This particular
feature made it possible to do a systematic experimental optimization. In this project, it
was found that blade­shaped riblets lead to optimal drag reductions of up to 9.9%; how­
ever, for greater durability of the riblet structures, trapezoidal grooves are preferred. A
good compromise between the structural resistance of the riblets and their performance
is obtained with a ridge angle of 45°, for which a drag reduction slightly lower than 8%
is measured. In these experiments, the optimum normalized spacing between one crest
and the other is s+ = 18, while the riblets height, normalized with respect to the spacing,
is h
s = 0.5.
The study of large eddy simulations (LES) is another useful instrument for understanding
the mechanism of restriction of streamwise vortices and suppression of spanwise velocity
fluctuations. Jahanmiri et Bahraini [4] compared LES to the DNS conducted by Choi et
al. [3], finding a good agreement both in quantitative and qualitative aspects of the flow.
The LES, when conducted on a flow over riblets with spacing s+ = 20, presented a drag
reduction around 7%. The riblets used for this project were V­shaped riblets with the same
geometry of the best­performing configuration from Choi et al. [3].
As it was previously mentioned, the direct numerical simulations conducted over riblets
have highlighted that only the inner layer of the boundary layer accounts for the effect
of riblets. A few riblets’ heights above the surface, the flow becomes independent of
the position relative to the riblet crests and troughs, restoring a canonical boundary layer
structure. As a result, the logarithmic and wake regions remain largely unaffected, expe­
riencing surface modification only through changes in wall friction. Moreover, simulations
that resolve the flow at a riblet scale are computationally very expensive and it is not pos­
sible to run them for a full­scale industrial application of riblets, such as a turbine blade or
an aircraft wing.
These observations led Aupoix et al. [2] to the intuition of modeling the effect of riblets
on drag reduction using the Reynolds­Averaged Navier­Stokes (RANS) equations. They
introduced modifications to baseline turbulence models to account for the velocity shifts
associated with riblet­induced drag reduction. This approach allowed for the estimation of
drag reduction rates based on riblet configuration, spacing, and height. The core idea of
this project is to simulate, by modifying turbulence models such as Spalart­Allmaras and
k­ω models, the upward shift of the logarithmic region, caused by the reduction of skin fric­
tion. Naturally, the modified turbulence models cannot capture the flow at the riblet scale
but only reflect the riblets’ effects at a certain distance above the riblet crests. The project
offers a strategy to model eddy viscosity models, meaning that these approximations are
not really able to reproduce the flow physics inside the riblets.
The main idea is that to capture the decrease or increase in skin friction, the momentum
transfer—and consequently the eddy viscosity—must be reduced or enhanced in the wall
region. In the Spalart­Allmaras model, the constant Cv1 is responsible for controlling the
model behavior in the near­wall region. As it gets increased, the end of the buffer layer
and the logarithmic region are moved upwards whereas the slope of the logarithmic re­
gion is, of course, preserved. For what concerns the k­ω models, to capture the increase

in friction over rough surfaces, turbulence in the wall region can be modified by reducing
the wall value of ω, which in turn lowers turbulence dissipation based on the roughness
scale. For smooth walls, where ω tends to infinity at the wall, it cannot be raised to sim­
ulate skin friction reduction. Given the near­exact nature of the turbulent kinetic energy
transport equation, altering the ω equation proves simpler. In the wall region, the primary
components influencing the ω equation are the dissipation term (sometimes referred to as
the sink term) and viscous diffusion. Thus, a practical approach is to damp the sink term
in the ω transport equation through a correction function, which results in an increase in
ω and dissipation. This, in turn, lowers eddy viscosity and leads to reduced skin friction.
Aupoix et al. [2] concluded that their models, to be fully validated, require a wider range of
both experimental data and direct numerical simulations to be compared. However, both
the modified models capture well the effect of skin friction reduction.
The study of riblet­induced drag reduction has evolved from experimental investigations to
advanced computational approaches. Experimental results demonstrate that riblets with
appropriate spacing and height can reduce skin friction by limiting near­wall vortices and
suppressing spanwise velocity fluctuations. Numerical simulations, including direct nu­
merical simulations (DNS) and large eddy simulations (LES), confirm these mechanisms
and provide detailed insights into the interaction between riblets and turbulent structures.
To model riblet effects within Reynolds­Averaged Navier­Stokes (RANS) frameworks,
modifications to turbulence models, such as Spalart­Allmaras, k­ω, and k­kl, have been
proposed. These approaches capture the upward shift of the velocity profile and effec­
tively estimate drag reduction, although further validation across diverse riblet geometries
and flow conditions remains necessary.
2.2
Physical Principles of the Problem
The analysis of DNS provides valuable insights into the physics of the turbulent boundary
layer over a surface with riblets. Firstly, the work of Choi et al. [3] highlights that, in
the s+ = 20 configuration, Reynolds stresses are significantly reduced near the riblets.
This phenomenon has led to the hypothesis that riblets reduce momentum transport by
inhibiting cross­flow motion.

The proposed drag reduction mechanism suggests that, for riblets with spacing below
a certain threshold, streamwise vortices remain confined above the riblets, while only a
limited portion of the riblet surface is exposed to the downwash of high­speed fluid they
induce. In drag­reducing configurations, high wall­shear rates are concentrated only at
the riblet tips. When the spacing between the riblet tips is small enough, the majority of
streamwise vortices remain above the riblets, as their average diameter exceeds the riblet
spacing, leaving only a small portion of the riblet tips exposed to the sweep they induce.
This confinement of streamwise vortices weakens both the downwash and ejection mo­
tions typically induced by these vortices, leading to a reduction in turbulent momentum
transfer toward the wall. Consequently, a general reduction in wall­shear rate ∂u
∂y is ob­
served across most of the riblet region. This suppression of near­wall turbulence modifies
the velocity gradient in the viscous sublayer, effectively increasing its thickness compared
to a smooth wall. The upward shift of the logarithmic velocity profile further confirms this
behavior, as the eddy viscosity and turbulent mixing are reduced in proximity to the ri­
blets. Overall, these effects illustrate how riblets passively alter the turbulent boundary
layer structure.
Garcia­Mayoral et Jiménez [7] explained this mechanism from another, equally interest­
ing, point of view. The flow, in the outer region of the boundary layer, exhibits uniform
shear. Near the wall, this uniformity is disrupted by the presence of the riblets, which, if
s+ is sufficiently small, do not affect the flow outside the inner region of the boundary layer.
Since, in the region where the riblets are confined, viscous effects dominate, linear equa­
tions can approximate the equations of motion, and the riblet geometry is uniform in the
x direction, then the problem reduces to two uncoupled two­dimensional sub­problems in
the z–y cross­plane.
The first one is the longitudinal flow of u, driven at y+ ≫1 by a streamwise shear

Garcia­Mayoral et Jiménez [7] stated that, following the considerations of Luchini et al.

perceived by different velocity components. When the protrusion height is positive, the
spanwise motion induced by streamwise vortices is effectively weakened, limiting their
interaction with the wall. This attenuation of momentum transfer reduces turbulent mixing
in the viscous sublayer.
Goldstein et al. [6] wanted to verify that the drag­reducing effect of riblets is a result of the
damping of the cross­flow velocity fluctuations. In this project, DNS is performed using
2 different riblet designs. The conclusion of this project was that, in agreement with the
discussion done previously, the damping of the spanwise velocity fluctuations is the most
important and explanatory effect of riblets.

In conclusion, the review suggests that riblets influence near­wall flow through a combi­
nation of mechanisms, which can be summarised as the alteration of the near­wall turbu­
lence production cycle and the inhibition of turbulence mixing, as well as the “expulsion” of
coherent vortical structures from the region dominated by viscous effects. These mecha­
nisms lead to a reduction in wall shear stress—except at the riblet crests—an upward shift
of the velocity profile in the logarithmic layer, and, above all, a reduction in drag, provided
that riblet geometries and dimensions are appropriately matched to the flow.

Channel Design and Flow Parameters
3.1
Channel and Riblets Design
The computational domain employed in this work is a turbulent channel flow, bounded by
a no­slip bottom wall, a symmetry plane as the upper boundary, and periodic interfaces
in both the streamwise and spanwise directions, with the former fully developed and the
latter internal. The domain size is chosen to capture the essential near­wall turbulent
structures while remaining computationally efficient.
Unlike the approach of Choi et al. [3], the channel dimensions used in this LES are signif­
icantly smaller than the full boundary layer thickness. This reduction is justified by the fact
that the effect of riblets on the flow is primarily confined to the inner region of the boundary
layer. The objective is to accurately resolve near­wall coherent structures with minimal
computational cost.
In the present study, the channel dimensions are expressed in wall units, inspired by the
configuration used by Choi et al. Specifically, the domain spans L+

The physics continuum was defined as a segregated, turbulent, and incompressible flow,
unsteady with simple algorithm and 5 inner iterations per time step. A k­ω turbulence
model was adopted for these simulations. This choice ensures accurate resolution of the
near­wall region, as the k­ω formulation is known to perform well in the viscous sublayer,
especially for low values of y+. Specifically, the simulations employ the Shear Stress
Transport (SST) variant of the model, which blends the standard k­ω model near the wall
with the k­ε model in the outer region of the boundary layer.
The turbulent kinetic energy k and specific dissipation rate ω are governed by two transport
equations, which are coupled with the Navier–Stokes equations. In addition, synthetic
turbulence was imposed on the domain as an initial condition, with the initial value of
0.5. Typically, a fully developed boundary layer presents a lower turbulence intensity, in
a range from 0.03 to 0.05; the initial condition has been set to a much higher value to be
sure of having a fully turbulent flow after a few iterations. Wall treatment is not needed,
since the near­wall resolution is very high. In fact, wall y+ results to be equal to 0.5545,
and thus small enough to accurately resolve the viscous sublayer without the need for
wall functions. This ensures that the near­wall gradients, including the wall shear stress
and velocity profiles, are directly captured by the numerical solution. In addition to the
turbulence model, the γ–Reθ transition model was activated.
Time discretization was handled with care to balance temporal accuracy and computa­
tional cost. The timestep was chosen to keep the maximum Courant number well below
unity near the wall, and generally below unity, ensuring stability and accuracy of the so­

4
LES Analysis
In this chapter of the project, the flow will be analyzed through Large Eddy Simulations
(LES): in one case, the lower boundary of the channel will be covered with riblets, while
in the other simulation the lower boundary will remain smooth, as in the simulations dis­
cussed in the previous chapter.
These simulations will be used to accurately analyze the effect of riblets within the inner
region of a turbulent boundary layer. The objective is to investigate the reduction of skin
friction, as well as the statistical flow quantities and its qualitative characteristics.
The first step is to define the riblet design, based on the flow parameters that have already
been identified. Subsequently, the CAD model of the channel with the ribleted bed surface
will be created, which will then be imported into STAR­CCM+, where the LES will be
performed. Finally, the analysis of the results will be carried out.
4.1
Riblets Design
The riblet design used in this project is the one, among those experimentally studied by
Bechert et al. [1], that provides the best compromise between shear stress reduction and
structural strength. It consists of a trapezoidal profile with s+ ≈18, h/s = 0.5, and a ridge
angle of 45◦. Figure 4.1 shows the riblets’ design and its proportions.
Figure 4.1: Bechert et al. (1997) riblets design
The dimensions, in millimeters, correspond to a s ≈0.32 mm, and h ≈0.16 mm. This pro­
file has been sketched in SolidWorks and subsequently extruded along the entire length
of the channel’s lower boundary. The result is a flow bed with 9 riblet crests and 9 valleys.
4.2
LES Set­up
The simulations were carried out using Large Eddy Simulation (LES) with a WALE subgrid­
scale model, employing a segregated flow solver under the assumption of constant den­
sity and incompressible flow. An implicit unsteady formulation with a second­order time
discretization scheme was applied, with a time step of 2.5 × 10−5 s. The choice of such a
small time step is necessary to guarantee a Courant number below 1.
Regarding the boundary conditions, the upper boundary was set as a symmetry plane,
while periodic boundary conditions were applied to the lateral boundaries in the spanwise
direction. The inlet and outlet were modeled as periodic fully developed interfaces, with a

a no­slip wall, representing either the smooth surface or the ribleted surface, depending
on the specific case under analysis.
Synthetic turbulence was applied as an initial condition to accelerate the transition to a
fully developed turbulent state, with a turbulence intensity of 0.5 and a turbulence length
scale of 2.5 × 10−4 m. To allow turbulence development, the maximum number of eddies,
was raised to 100000.
The mesh was generated using the trimmed cell mesher, with a base cell size of 1×10−4 m.

4.3

Before moving on with the analysis of the flow and its characteristics, it is important to
evaluate the accuracy of the large eddy simulations performed. There are different criteria
to study the performance of a LES, and it is essential to comply with these criteria to ensure
sufficient LES accuracy without approaching the computational cost of a DNS. The core
idea behind LES is to resolve the turbulent flow down to a certain scale, and then model
the remaining, filtered­out part below that scale in the sub­grid scale (SGS). Therefore,
the mesh must be sufficiently refined to allow at least a portion of the turbulent structures
to be directly resolved.

study. The first metric adopted is the LES_IQ index proposed by Celik et al. (2005),

reduction that can be obtained by adopting a surface with riblets, and particularly those
proposed by Bechert et al. [1]. In the LES analyzed so far, it has been possible to im­
plement a monitor of the shear drag coefficient at the lower boundary, which is either a
smooth or ribbed surface. In addition, a plot over time of the monitor has been created, so
that a mean value over a sufficiently long interval could be extracted. STAR­CCM+ calcu­
lates the drag coefficient after defining a reference area and a reference velocity. Defining
a reference velocity for the flows analyzed so far is complicated by the fact that we are
dealing with turbulent flows and because the free stream velocity is not reached, given
the small height of the channel used. However, it was possible to determine a maximum
average velocity for both simulations, which in the flow over the riblets is slightly higher,
as was also mentioned in the previous section. Furthermore, it should be remembered
that the increase in wetted surface leads to a significant increase in drag; for this reason,
the coefficients obtained from the simulations, before being compared, were multiplied by
the respective wetted surface, reported in Table 4.1.
After these calculations, the result consists of a drag reduction equal to 5%. If compared
to the experiments of Bechert et al. [1], who obtained approximately a 8% reduction using
the same riblet design, the drag reduction achieved in this project is lower, but it has to be
said that the aforementioned experiments have been conducted using oil and not water.

RANS Calibration
In this chapter of the project, the goal is to modify the turbulence models in such a way as
to obtain, through a RANS simulation, a result as close as possible to the one obtained
through the LES simulation over a surface with riblets. This operation is very important
because it opens up the possibility of simulating the effect of riblets with a coarser grid
and drastically lower computational costs. Before doing that, it is important to highlight all
the main outcomes observed by analyzing the flow over riblets in a LES.
The main characteristics of the flow over riblets are the upward shift of the normalized
velocity profile, a significant reduction of the turbulent kinetic energy, both in its peak
and mean values, a decrease in the peak value of turbulence intensities and Reynolds
stresses, an upward shift of the region where turbulence is most intense, and, as a con­
sequence of these features, a reduction of the drag coefficient around 5%.
To simulate these effects, it is necessary to calibrate the turbulence models by adjusting
the constants that are accessible in STAR­CCM+. First, in order to verify whether an
RANS simulation is sufficient to reproduce the flow characteristics identified through the
LES, an RANS simulation was carried out using the riblet­equipped channel. This was
done through a simulation with SST k­ω, a two­equation turbulence model available in
STAR­CCM+ that was also used in the previous RANS in the present project. Since it
was proven in Table 3.2 that the flow was mesh independent, the resolution of the mesh
was chosen to be the one that was called ”very coarse”, to reduce the computational cost
of the simulations.

6
Conclusion and Future Perspectives
At the conclusion of the project, it can be said that the analysis of the turbulent boundary
layer over riblets has yielded good results. The numerical analysis of the flow confirms
all the characteristics found in the literature on the subject. In particular, the capabil­
ity of large eddy simulations to successfully represent, above a riblet­covered surface,
the physical mechanisms contributing to the reduction of the drag coefficient has been
demonstrated. Large eddy simulations have been proven to have an excellent discretiza­
tion, which allowed, near the riblet­covered wall, the numerical resolution of almost all
turbulent structures. This led to defining a drag reduction of 5%, a realistic and consis­
tent result if compared with the consulted literature, although, given that the wall normal
dimension of the profile was kept very small for computational cost reasons, the drag
reduction value is slightly underestimated.
Furthermore, from the obtained data, it was possible to formulate and test the strategy
proposed by Aupoix et al. [2] for calibrating the Spalart–Allmaras model, with the aim
of simulating, on a flat surface, the reduction in skin friction coefficient produced by the
riblets and, consequently, the reduction in drag coefficient. The innovative aspect of this
thesis is the direct comparison, within the same project, between the results of highly
accurate simulations, such as LES, and those of calibrated RANS, adopting the same
flow conditions and boundaries. This comparison led to establishing an optimum value
of the coefficient Cv1, which controls the model behavior in the near­wall region. The
value of Cv1 = 11.1 has been found to be perfect to represent, on a smooth wall, the
drag reduction achieved by the Bechert et al. [1] riblet design. The other effects of riblets,
however, haven’t been depicted accurately.
This project lends itself to being continued and enriched by an experimental analysis. It
would indeed be very interesting to carry out a simulation, using the calibrated RANS
model, in a domain much larger than the one considered in this project, given the enor­
mous computational advantage of such simulations. This would make it possible to sim­
ulate the flow in a domain identical to an experimental channel, such as the Flume 5
available at the Technical University of Denmark, and to compare the results with those
obtained experimentally. The present work can therefore serve as a reference for col­
lecting experimental data with a deeper understanding of the phenomenon, and for inter­
preting any observed drag reduction with greater knowledge. If the experimental analysis
showed good agreement with the result obtained from the calibrated RANS model, this
could represent a turning point in riblets’ research, enabling the calculation of drag reduc­
tion achieved by riblets even in realistic industrial scenarios.
