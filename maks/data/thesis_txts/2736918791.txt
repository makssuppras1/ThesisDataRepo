Abstract
This thesis presents an implementation of the Multi­State Constraint Kalman Filter (MSCKF)
in the context Visual Inertial Odometry (VIO) for the purpose of UAV navigation in GNSS
denied environments. The MSCKF method is renowned for its computational efficiency
and robustness in fusing visual and inertial measurements to estimate the UAV’s pose.
This research aims to enhance the UAV’s navigational accuracy by integrating monocular
camera data with inertial measurements, overcoming the limitations of traditional navi­
gation systems that rely heavily on GNSS. Additionally, the low cost of camera and IMU
sensors makes this approach even more attractive, providing a cost­effective solution for
reliable UAV navigation in complex and constrained scenarios.
In this work, we focus in the implementation of the MSCKF framework on various datasets
provided by the KITTI benchmark suite, in order to assess its performance in a variety of
different environments, examine its accuracy, robustness and overall performance, under
different parameters. These parameters include various feature detectors, outlier rejec­
tion algorithms, and triangulation methods, as well as the minimum number of poses in
which a feature must be detected, and finally the baseline distance between consecutive
camera measurements. The findings demonstrate accurate evaluation of the position and
orientation of the sensor platform, when compared with the ground truth measurements,
validating the implementation of MSCKF for the purpose of accurate navigation and lo­
calization in GNSS denied environments.

1
Introduction
1.1
Unmanned Autonomous Vehicles
Unmanned Autonomous Vehicles (UAVs), encompassing a broad spectrum of air, land,
sea, and underwater vehicles, have seen a dramatic increase in both development and
application over the last decade [1]. These vehicles are designed to operate without direct
human intervention, utilizing a combination of sensors, and advanced algorithms to navi­
gate and perform complex tasks. The use of UAVs is extended to a plethora of industries
ranging from agriculture and environmental monitoring to public safety and emergency re­
sponse and even exploration of remote environments [2], [3]. The recent improvement of
relevant sensor technologies, such as radar, LIDAR, cameras and IMUs, as well as their
low­cost, high accuracy and reduced computational complexity have greatly contributed
to the surge of UAV usage.
In any of the aforementioned uses of UAVs a crucial aspect of the system is to be able
to accurately track its position and orientation within its environment through the usage
of onboard sensors. Up until recently GNSS was the main sensor responsible for the po­
sitioning and orientation of the platform. However, there are many situations that deem
the GPS unreliable, whether the platform must be operated in an environment where the
sensor is inaccurate (e.g. urban areas due to multi­path interference) or completely unus­
able (GNSS denied environments e.g. indoors, underwater or underground, space) [4].
Furthermore, GNSS receivers present an accuracy within a few meters unless more ex­
pensive receivers (RTK) are used. Furthermore, the usage of a single sensor, increases
the instability and unreliability of the navigation system, since a sensor malfunction results
to the failure ofthe entire system. In addition the update rate of GNSS is considered low
and presents a slight delay [5]. Due to the aforementioned limitations, many alternative
solutions have been developed, that focus on the integration of information from multiple
sensors, thus making the system more robust and versatile to different situations. Ex­
amples include integration of GNSS and IMU data and combination of visual and LIDAR
data, one of the most notable being visual inertial navigation systems (VINS) [6]. Cam­
eras tend to perform well if provided with high quality images, however it is prone to fast
motion, illumination changes and camera distortion. IMUs tend to produce high frequency
estimations of position and orientation, but their performance is greatly compromised due
to drift in both gyroscopes and accelerometers, as well as sensor bias, temperature and
noise. The combination of these two sensors creates a cumulative system where each
part complements each other’s weaknesses, leading to a more accurate reliable and ro­
bust outcome [6]. Furthermore, the relative low cost of commercial IMU sensors and
lightweight cameras, make the implementation a cost efficient alternative to GNSS based
navigation. Finally the computational complexity allows for real time implementation mak­
ing this alternative an ideal choice for substituting a GNSS based navigation system in
GNSS denied environments. [7]
1.2
Visual Inertial Navigation
Visual Inertial Navigation is divided in two main subcategories. Visual Inertial Odometry
(VIO) and Visual Inertial simultaneous localization and mapping (VI­SLAM). In VIO the ob­
jective is to retrieve the pose (position and orientation) of the moving platform as it moves
in 3D space, by utilizing the information provided by the visual (cameras) and inertial (IMU)
Visual Inertial Navigation for UAVs in GNSS denied environments
1
==PAGE:8==
sensors. In the case of SLAM. the purpose is to also utilize the position of surrounding
points of the environment to create a map of the surrounding environment as the plat­
form is moving along with each trajectory. The inclusion of feature points associated with
the surrounding environment increases the accuracy of the pose estimation at the cost of
computational complexity [8]. An efficient way to decrease computational complexity (for
both VIO and VI­SLAM) was proposed by Mourikis and Roumeliotis [9], by implementing a
modified version of the Extended Kalman Filter (EKF), the Multi­State Constraint Kalman
Filter. This iteration utilizes geometric constraints imposed to a static feature observed by
multiple points of view. A main advantage of this method is that it does not need to store
the position of the 3D feature point, maintaining computational complexity to a minimum.
Since its release, this approach has been implemented in a variety of different situations
and is considered a staple in the design of visual inertial navigation systems.
In this work we approach the problem from the perspective of a monocular camera, mean­
ing a single camera sensor is mounted on the platform, instead of using a configuration
of stereo cameras. While the usage of stereo cameras results to higher accuracy, it also
comes with additional expenses and computational complexity. In many cases the differ­
ence in performance does not justify for the cost and computational complexity imposed
by the stereo configuration [10]. Furthermore, the use of stereo configuration, leads to
a decline in the accuracy of triangulation, as the distance between the center of the two
cameras is affected by the conditions of the measurement accuracy and climate changes
[11]. Monocular visual inertial odometry is considered a relative low cost, efficient method
that provides robust pose estimation. The main drawback this approach suffers from is the
scale ambiguity introduced, due to the loss of one dimension when projecting the three­
dimensional world onto a two­dimensional image plane. Furthermore, moving points in
the camera’s field of view can be misinterpreted as motion of the sensor platform leading
to errors in the pose estimation [12]. However, one of the main advantages of this im­
plementation is that the MSCKF algorithm leverages past pose estimates to compute the
current state, thus ensuring a more robust and accurate pose estimation.

General Overview

concept of visual aided inertial navigation. The goal of the system is to track the position
of the recording platform with respect to the global coordinate frame.

the position and orientation calculated from the IMU by utilizing visual information from
multiple past instances.

leading to more accurate estimates over time. In the case described in this work, the

2.3
Feature Correspondance
A core aspect of visual inertial odometry is the incorporation of visual information to our
predictor­corrector structure (MSCKF) in order to correct the measurements of the IMU
that are prone to drift. This visual information comes in the form of features tracked across
the image sequence captured by the UAV. These features are points tracked across mul­
tiple frames of the image sequence and their observations can constrain the pose esti­
mation of the IMU. The 3D position of the features with respect to the world coordinate
frame is computed, through the process of triangulation, and is then used to update the
state of the MSCKF. To that end, there are two main approaches that can be followed;
feature matching and feature tracking. In this work, feature matching was chosen due to
the robustness of the results it yields and its superior overall performance.
2.3.1
Feature detection
We will begin with feature detection, since it is used on both of the aforementioned meth­
ods. Feature detection is related to the process of locating points of interest in an image
based on several characteristics (e.g pixel intensity, neighboring pixels , etc).
Those
points of interest are often corners or edges, due to their unique and distinct patters com­
pared to smooth regions of the image. To that end, there are many feature detectors
developed over the years, such as the Harris corner detector, the Canny edge detector or
the Shi­Tomasi corner detector. The aforementioned algorithms are examining the pixel
intensity of a region in the image and based on a calculated score they detect relevant
features. Although their accuracy and computational complexity is adequate for the pur­
pose of this work, they have one major drawback that prohibits their use in the context
of visual inertial odometry. The detectors mentioned are rotation invariant, meaning that
even if the image is rotated the same points of interest can be located, but they are not
scale invariant. It is obvious, that in the context of visual inertial odometry, both of these
characteristics are necessary, since features will be detected both in different rotations as
well as different scales. A detector that satisfies those criteria is the Scale Invariant Fea­
ture Transform (SIFT) algorithm and the algorithms that derive from it (e.g SURF, ORB).

Orientation Assignment
Each keypoint is assigned an orientation, based on local image properties, with the pur­
pose to achieve rotation invariance. An orientation histogram is formed from the gradient
orientations of sample points within a region around the keypoint. The orientation his­
togram has 36 bins covering the 360 degree range of orientations. Each sample added to
the histogram is weighted by its gradient magnitude and by a Gaussian­weighted circular
window. The highest peak in the histogram is taken and any peak above 80% of it is also
considered to calculate the orientation. This process creates keypoints with the same
location and scale but different orientation, that contribute significantly to the stability of
matching.
Image descriptors
The final step is to extract from each selected keypoint a region (descriptor) that is dis­
tinctive and remains invariant to illumination changes and camera 3D viewpoint. Below
we present a schematic illustration of the image descriptor computation :
Figure 2.8: Image descriptor [18]
Image gradient magnitudes and orientations are sampled around the keypoint location,
using the scale of the keypoint to select the appropriate Gaussian window to smooth the
image. In order to achieve orientation invariance, the coordinates of the descriptor and
the gradient orientations are rotated relative to the keypoint orientation. The end result is
a 16x16 neighbourhood around the keypoint. It is divided into 16 sub­blocks of 4x4 size.
For each sub­block, 8 bin orientation histogram is created. So a total of 128 bin values are
available, for each keypoint, that form its respective vector. Finally the resulting vector is
nomralized to unit length to account for illumination changes and values higher than 0.2
are discarded. Finally the vector is renormalized to unit length to account for non­linear
illumination changes.[17]. Image descriptors are extracted after the keypoint localization
in the form of a vector that contains useful information about the neighborhood of the
keypoint, thus allowing for identification irregardless of changes in rotation and scale.

An alternative to SIFT is the Oriented FAST and rotated BRIEF (ORB) algorithm. It is more
attractive for real time applications (VIO included), since the computational complexity is
smaller, although this obviously affects performance. Orb is based on the FAST keypoint
detector and the BRIEF descriptor. FAST is based on comparing the intensity of the pixel
in question with 16 neighbors in a circle around it. If two or more consecutive neighbors
have value higher than an upper threshold or lower than a low threshold, the pixel under
test is considered a feature. Finally non­maximal suppression is applied in order to com­
pute a corner response function similar to the process followed in SIFT.[19]. BRIEF is
essentially a binarization of the SIFT descriptor resulting in a vector much more attractive
towards real­time applications. First the vector is compressed to reduce its dimensional­
ity (PCA,LDA) and convert the floating point numbers to binary strings (LSH). One main
advantage of BRIEF, is that it computes the binary strings without needing to compute
the descriptors [20]. ORB combines those two methods and modifies them accordingly to
account for the rotation invariance that is not guaranteed. Specifically for FAST, ORB in­
troduces a measure of cornerness, similar to the Harris algorithm, and adds a method that
computes the orientation of said corners, based on the intensity centroid measure. ORB
also steers a descriptor computed by BRIEF to the orientation of its respective keypoint,
thus accounting for the rotation invariance. One distinct property of BRIEF, is that each
bit feature has a large variance and a mean near 0.5. However, once aligned with the
keypoint orientation, it no longer retains this characteristic and its distribution becomes
wider. High variance enhances a feature’s distinctiveness as it yields different responses
to varying inputs. Additionally, it is beneficial for the tests to be independent, as this allows
each test to add unique value to the outcome. To achieve this, ORB conducts an iterative
search among all possible binary tests to identify those that exhibit both high variance and
average values near 0.5, while also being uncorrelated. This optimized method is termed
rBRIEF. [21]

pair of images. This process, translates to the comparison of descriptors of the respective

There are many matchers that can be employed, the simplest one, being the brute­force
matcher. It essentially compares the descriptor of a feature in the first image with all of the
descriptors of the features in the second image, based on some distance calculation. The
pair with the smallest distance is considered a match. The distance metric ranges from
L1­norm to L2­norm (preferably) for SIFT descriptor and Hamming­norm for binary based
descriptors (eg ORB). A good addition to the brute­force matcher is the process of cross
check. It essentially constraints the matcher to identify the matches with value (i, j) such
that ith descriptor in the first image matches with the jth descriptor in the second image
as the best match and vice­versa, thus ensuring,two features in the image pair match with
each other. An alternative to this method that also ensures cross­matching is the imple­
mentation of Lowe’s ratio test, described in [17]. For each keypoint descriptor in the first
image, the algorithm finds the k closest matching descriptors in the second image, using
a distance metric like L2­norm. For each match, the ratio of the distance to the nearest
neighbor and the distance to the k nearest neighbors is calculated. A ratio threshold of
0.75−0.8 is advised although it can be modified according to the application. This thresh­
old implies, that the distance to the closest match should be significantly smaller than the
distance to the k closest matches, for it to be considered a good match. Matches with
ratio above the specified threshold are discarded. This approach essentially filters many
false matches, that the distance metric cannot distinguish.
2.3.3
Outlier rejection
Feature matching comes down to essentially locating points of interest across a sequence
of frames. However, it is usually accompanied by a number of false matches, due to the
nature of the algorithm. Specifically the detectors (SIFT, ORB, etc) are not entirely in­
variant to illumination changes and camera 3D viewpoint. Furthermore, particularly noisy
images and repetitive patterns make the algorithm prone to the correspondence of false
matches.

In the concept of visual inertial navigation, it is crucial that the EKF update based on
the measurement model is accurate. Essentially, since we use visual information to cor­
rect the inertial measurements, features tracked across frames, should ideally contain no
outliers. To that end, the method of Random Sample Consensus (RANSAC) was em­
ployed to mitigate the number of potential outliers to a minimum. The basic principle of
RANSAC, is that it uses a small initial dataset (in our case, randomly select a subset of
the total matches between two images) and incorporates more and more data changing
the model parameters accordingly. Specifically, we assume a model that requires a min­
imum of n data points to define its free parameters, and a set of data points larger than
the number of free parameters. Then randomly select a subset of the total data points
initiate the model. Said model is used to determine the subset (consensus) of the re­
maining data that lie withing the error tolerance of the model. If the consensus exceeds
a predetermined threshold, then we use the consensus to compute a new model. If the
consensus lies below the predetermined threshold, a new subset is randomly selected
and the above process is repeated. If, after a fixed number of iterations, no consensus
exceeds the threshold, the model is computed based on the largest consensus.[23] In
figure 2.11 a simple example of applying RANSAC to a set of data points, in order to find
the line with the best fit, is presented. The process begins by randomly selecting two
points (minimum number required to define a straight line) and compute the straight line
between them. Based on a predetermined threshold it is decided whether or not the rest
of the points satisfy the model computed. This process is repeated for a number of trials
or until the best result is achieved. In the case described in this work, RANSAC selects
a minimum number of feature matches between two images. This number, depends on
the transformation model being used. Fundamental Matrix and Homography matrix were
both considered as potential models.

3.2
Data description
Data are available in their raw recordings (unsynced­unrectified), as well as calibrated

2. Inertial data : Each measurement contains 30 different values corresponding to

contains its 3D coordinates , along with its corresponding reflectance.

achieve adequate triangulation results. The odometry dataset contains of 22 stereo se­
quences, saved in png format, with ground truth trajectories for evaluation.

After successfully tracking features across multiple frames, we store them based on the

of the MSCKF. Based on the aforementioned feature structure, if features have moved

simply augment the covariance to include all past poses. Finally we calculate the errors
between the estimated position and orientation of the camera sensor and the respective
ground truth poses.
3.5
Feature Correspondence
The first step to implement the MSCKF framework is to detect optimal features in subse­
quent frames and associate them with each other in order to find common correspondence
as described in section 2.3. Since, the implementation by OpenCV does not provide a di­
rect way for matching features for more than two frames, the approach had to be modified
in order to include multiple camera poses. Specifically the approach that was followed is
described as follows :
1. Detect keypoints and descriptors in every image and assign them with a unique ID
2. Perform pairwise correspondence by matching descriptors
3. Perform outlier rejection on matching features
4. Check unique IDs. If a feature is discarded at least once, remove it from sequence,
else store it along with its unique ID
5. Iterate through the remaining paired features
6. If two pairs contain a common ID, merge them together
7. Store each feature along with the images it appears in as well as its respective
coordinates
8. For every stored feature perform triangulation as described in 2.3.4 and extract its
3D position with respect to the world coordinate frame

5
Discussion
The implementation of visual­inertial odometry (VIO) using both inertial and visual data
has yielded promising results and a few areas that require further refinement. The integra­
tion of inertial with visual information aimed to successfully combine, data from sensors,
that complement each other, while at the same time mitigate their weaknesses. Specif­
ically the inertial sensor presents short term accuracy, but is prone to noise and error
propagation, while the visual sensor is characterised for its rich information and long term
stability, but is also dependant to the surrounding environment. The results of our imple­
mentation highlight several key aspects worthy of discussion.
A very crucial part of the implementation depends on the accurate feature localization.
To that end the SIFT detector was implemented, since its performance is by far superior.
In addition, an appropriate outlier rejection filter must be implemented in order to discard
faulty features. In this work it was decided to utilize RANSAC with the Homography matrix
as the underlying model, since it yields better results than the Fundamental matrix. Fur­
thermore, an accurate triangulation method must be used, in order to accurately calculate
the 3D location of tracked features. Due to the insufficient performance of linear triangula­
tion, we have decided to implement a Gauss­Newton optimization method, to accurately
calculate the 3D position of tracked features with respect to the world coordinate frame.
Another important topic, is the number of features tracked across multiple poses. An ad­
equate number of features ensures that the measurements are sufficiently rich to provide
accurate updates to the state estimate. Furthermore, the impact of noise or erroneous
measurements from any single feature is reduced. This improves the robustness of the
filter against noise and outliers. An adequate number of features helps in continuously
correcting the drift, keeping the estimates accurate over long durations.
From the above, we can infer that there is a trade­off between the number of features
tracked and their quality. In this study, we successfully identified the optimal balance be­
tween feature quality and quantity through a systematic process of trial and error. Specif­
ically, it was decided to use a lenient threshold during the RANSAC implementation, thus
ensuring feature quantity, while at the same time we chose to process every 5th image
to ensure better geometrical representation, stability and distinctiveness, thus ensuring
feature quality.
Regarding the EKF update step, it was chosen to focus only on features that move out
of the camera’s field of view. Many implementations of the MSCKF algorithm, also per­
form an update when a feature exceeds the maximum number of poses it is allowed to
be tracked in. This constraint is imposed to account for computational efficiency and real
time considerations. In this work, we chose to ignore this aspect, since we are mainly
concerned with accuracy, meaning that in a real­world scenario, our algorithm could yield
poorer results.
The modelling and initialization of the gyroscope and accelerometer, bias and noise play
a crucial role in the accuracy of the MSCKF framework. The purpose of the MSCKF is
to produce accurate positional and rotational measurements by accounting for the biases
and noise processes that are inevitably included in the inertial measurements.
In addition to this, the model does a fairly good job of estimating the position and ori­
entation of the recording platform, while at the same time mitigation the noise and bias
propagation, in a variety of environments and for different kind of conditions, thus solidi­
fying its accuracy.
Finally, we should note that our model lacks in robustness, since the hyperparameters

that affect its performance were chosen based on measurements taken by ground truth
dataset and following additional refinement to achieve optimal results. For real world appli­
cations, additional steps must be included, such as state management and state pruning,
in order to remove from the update step any poses where no feature moves out of the
field of view.

6
Conclusions
6.1
Summary
The work presented, implements the design of a visual and inertial navigation system,
that leverages multiple past poses of recurring visual features in order to compensate for
the noise and bias propagation introduced by the inertial sensor. After investigating the
standard implementation for the monocular case, an accurate feature correspondence
protocol was designed, followed by an optimal outlier filtering method. The method of
linear triangulation combined with the Gauss­Newton optimization was implored for accu­
rate feature position estimation and state correction. The EKF update steps triggers only
based on the features that move out of the field view and the bias and noise estimated was
based on measurements obtained by the ground truth poses. The final step constitutes of
the propagation of noise and covariance using the standard equations, followed the state
correction. The algorithm was tested on a variety of different environments to accurately
assess its performance. All tests yielded promising results confirming the validity of the
MSCKF framework in GNSS denied environments.
6.2
Path forward
While the results provided by the algorithm are promising, there are a lot more additions
that can be implemented to fully optimize the performance and use of MSCKF frame­
work. The main addition, is to include the IMU propagation equations based either on
their analytical or numerical derivation. This step will solidify the validity of fusing visual
and inertial data, with the purpose of accurate position and orientation estimation. Fur­
thermore, the algorithm was tested on datasets provided by the KITTI benchmark suite.
Further testing on different and more challenging datasets that include even more dy­
namic environments with non­static features or environments that are characterized by
the absence of features, could provide a sense of the limits of the current implementation.
In addition, state management and pruning can also be investigated in order to provide a
more computationally efficient and accurate approach designed for real­time implementa­
tion, as well as explore the contribution of camera poses that do not trigger EKF updates,
to the accuracy of the algorithm. Another interesting implementation would be to apply
the current framework to unsynced and unrectified data, in order to account for the fact
that IMU provides a much bigger volume of data with respect to the camera. Finally, the
ultimate step is to implement and test the algorithm on a real UAV, which will include an
appropriate hardware setup, performing the relevant sensor calibration, data acquisition,
parameter tuning field validation.
