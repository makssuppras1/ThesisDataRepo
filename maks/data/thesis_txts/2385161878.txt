Abstract
The aim of this thesis is to replicate the experimental research of solitary waves by Sumer et
al. (2010), using the open source computational ﬂuid dynamics programme OpenFOAM.
A standard OpenFOAM solver is modiﬁed to simulate the ﬂow of a solitary wave.
The boundary layer, generated by the propagation of a solitary wave, is studied by means
of direct numerical simulation solving incompressible Navier-Stokes equations. (Sumer et
al., 2010) A numerical convergence analysis is carried out for one of the tests to ﬁnd a
suitable CFL number for the simulations.
The experimental research done by Sumer et al. (2010), showed coherent structures in the
wave boundary layers, and turbulent spots for high Reynolds numbers. The simulations
of the tests are carried out in a one dimensional domain with high accuracy i.e. low time
steps, then two- and three dimensional simulations are hot started from a given time step
where the pressure- and velocity proﬁles are mapped to a mesh in two or three dimensions.
The tests in the laminar regime are replicated and the solitaryFoam solver is validated with
respect to laminar theory. The vortex tube structures found in Sumer et al. (2010) are
replicated and studied in detail in two and three dimensional ﬂow domains, the emergence
of the vortex tube structures in the simulations are occurring at approximately the same
time step as the experiments.
Tests in the transitional regime are simulated in three dimensional domains with the
interest of simulating a sudden turbulence spot. Several runs are carried out of Test 15,
which is the test with the highest Reynolds number from Sumer et al. (2010). In these
simulations the disturbance in the ﬂow is seen at a later time, because it has not been
possible to simulate the occurrence of sudden turbulence spots, with the current numerical
setup in OpenFOAM. Flow resistance analysis of the simulations are compared to the test
from Sumer et al. (2010).

Chapter 1
Introduction
1.1
Background
Studies of the boundary layers beneath a solitary wave have been done by Vittori &
Blondeaux (2008) where a numerical study showed the appearance of turbulence in the
boundary layer. In their simulations, the occurrence of turbulence appeared behind the
crest of the wave.
Later experimental studies done by Sumer et al. (2010) a oscillating water tunnel was
used to study the boundary layer beneath a solitary wave. Sumer et al., (2010) could
identify 4 diﬀerent ﬂow regimes, i) laminar regime, ii) laminar with vortex tube regime,
where the boundary layer ﬂow experiences a regular array of vortex tubes near the bed
over a short period of time during the deceleration phase. iii) transitional regime cate-
gorized with turbulence spots revealed by single/multiple dense spikes in the bed shear
stresses. iv) turbulent regime, where the ﬂow is turbulent over the whole period. A fully
turbulent boundary layer could not be replicated with the experimental setup.
Sumer et al’s experimental research conﬁrmed the instability in the boundary layer be-
neath beneath a solitary wave. Coherent structures in form vortex tubes as illustrated in
ﬁgure 1.1 and in the tests with higher Reynolds numbers turbulence spots were seen.
Figure 1.1: A video frame illustrating the vortex tubes in side view at ωt = 105.7◦.
U0m = 43.9 cm s−1, T = 9.2 s, Re = 2.8 · 105. Figure 8 from Sumer et al., (2010).
A numerical study of the laminar with vortex tube regime was done by Scandura (2013),
where Scandura showed that the vortex tubes cause the destabilisation of a second vortex

Research questions
In this master thesis the boundary layer beneath a solitary wave is studied. DNS are
done with a computational ﬂuid dynamic programme OpenFOAM. The ﬁrst concern is
to replicate the tests, done by Sumer et al. (2010). In the interest of: i) Identifying the

structures of the vortex tubes. iii) Simulating and studying a turbulence spot in a three
dimensional domain.
The numerical study is done by using the open source programme OpenFOAM to solve
incompressible Navier-Stokes equations. A standard OpenFOAM solver pimpleFOAM is
modiﬁed, which satisﬁes Navier-Stokes equations conservation of momentum, with a ﬁrst
order solution in time and a second order solution in space. Unlike the numerical study by
Scandura (2013) no additional perturbations have been implemented in the OpenFOAM
model.

Direct Numerical Simulations
DNS solves Navier-Stokes equation without any turbulence model, meaning that the whole
range of the spatial and temporal scales of the turbulence, are all resolved within the com-
putational mesh. This sets a high resolution requirement for the mesh within the turbulent
boundary layer, leading to a high cost of computational power/time.
DNS is a useful tool to study turbulence, because it is possible to obtain information,
that on experimental basis is diﬃcult or impossible to obtain. E.g. in the experimen-
tal research by Sumer et al. (2010) where the solitary wave is cut oﬀat approximatly
ωt = 100◦for all tests due to experimental constraints, and the experiments where also
restricted from testing higher Reynolds numbers.

The Euler method is a ﬁrst order numerical method, solving diﬀerential equations with
a given initial value. The error at a given time is proportional to the step size, ∆t/∆x.
Solving a nonlinear equation with a small error margin using the Euler method, requires
small steps of space and/or time. The Euler method in OpenFOAM is hard coded to be
the implicit Euler method also know as backwards Euler method (CFD-Direct, 2017). The
implicit Euler method is solved with ﬁxed point iteration, and if the sequence converges,
with a given tolerance, the method takes its limit as the new approximation.
A Crank Nicholson second order time can be used with the entry CrankNicolson followed
by a coeﬃcient. Where 1 corresponds to pure Crank Nicholson solution and 0 correspond
to pure Euler method, any number between 0 and 1 is a blend between the two methods.
Second order time schemes have not been used in this thesis. Increasing the order of the
time scheme increases the computational cost. A previous master thesis project by Malte
V. Eisum (Eisum, 2016) on the same topic as the current project, showed that using a
second order time scheme increased the computational cost, and increased the stability
in the model. In the interest of having some perturbations in the model to replicate the
experimental results (Sumer et al., 2010) a ﬁrst order time scheme is considered to be
suﬃcient, and solving the governing equations in a ﬁrst order time scheme can be a source
of perturbations.

The linear solver GAMG (generalized geometric-algebraic multi grid) is used to solve the
pressure in the Navier-Stokes equations. GAMG uses the principle of generating a quick
solution on a mesh with a small number of cells, mapping this solution onto a ﬁner mesh,
using it as an initial guess to obtain an accurate solution on the ﬁne mesh. GAMG starts
with the mesh speciﬁed by the user, and coarsens/reﬁnes the mesh in stages. Other entries
to the GAMG solver are set to default (cacheAgglomeration = true, nCellsInCoarsestLevel
= 10, agglomerator = faceAreaPair, mergeLevels = 1). cacheAgglomeration is a switch
specifying caching of the agglomeration strategy.
nCellsInCoarsestLevel approximates
mesh size at the most coarse level, in terms of the number of cells. agglomerator is the
process of deﬁning the coarser grid involves agglomeration, i.e. the combination of several
nodes, control volumes or coeﬃcients from the original grid. Here OpenFOAM developers
(CFD-Direct, 2017) recommends using faceAreaPair method. And lastly, mergeLevels is
a keyword that controls the speed at which coarsening or reﬁnement is performed; the
default is 1, which is safest. If a simple mesh is used the speed can be increased by coars-
ening/reﬁning 2 levels at the same time by setting it equal to 2.
The tolerance is set to an order of 10−6 for both the pressure and velocity calculations,
with a relative tolerance of 0.01. Meaning the solver will start iterating from the initial
residual, and stop iterating when the ﬁnal residual falls below 0.01 in diﬀerence. The
absolute tolerance is the relTol under the entries UFinal and pFinal. Setting this value
to zero, forces the solution to converge to the solver tolerance at each time step, iterat-
ing until the ﬁnal value reaches a precision in the order of the tolerance, i.e. 10−6. The
tolerance represents the level at which the residual is small enough so that the solution is
deemed suﬃciently accurate.

5.1
Freestream velocity and bed shear stresses
The freestream velocity and bed shear stresses are compared to the experimental results
from Sumer et al. (2010) and the analytical solution (Liu et al., 2007). Since previous
results show that the one dimensional simulations follow the analytical solution perfectly,
the one dimensional simulations in OpenFOAM are used to represent the analytical solu-
tion. This has been done for convenience since all the tests have been carried out in a one
dimensional domain with high accuracy.
It is seen on ﬁgure 5.2 that the free stream velocity follows the analytical solution very
well for test 5,6 and 7, but in test 8 and 9 at approximately ωt = 270◦the free stream
velocity diverts from the analytical solution. This is due to a vortex tube being lifted
from the bottom wall and reaching the upper boundary of the domain, hence disturbing
the free-stream velocity. This is considered not to have a signiﬁcant eﬀect on the results,
because it is at a late stage of the wave, and it does not eﬀect the results close to the
bottom wall, i.e. bed shear stresses.
Sumer et al.
(2010) found that the bed shear stresses in the vortex tube regime was
typically an increase followed by a strong decrease, which is related to the emergence of
vortex tubes illustrated in ﬁgure 5.1, with the points A and B. Figure 5.1 is ﬁgure 9 from
(Sumer et al., 2010) which illustrates the bed shear stresses and the vortex tube, where
A is the bed shear stress between two vortex tubes and B is directly beneath a vortex tube.
The ensemble averaged results from the experiments represents measurements at the same
positions in the U-tube through-out several runs of the same wave. The reason why the
ensemble averaged from the experiments give similar results in test 5, 6 and 7 are unknown
to the author. The vortex tubes are emerging at the same spot each time, with an increase
followed by a decrease, through-out several diﬀerent tests. This could have something to
do with the physical setup and/or physical environment of the U-tube. To ﬁnd a similar
position on the bottom wall in the simulations in OpenFOAM, the maximum bed shear
stress is found (which corresponds to point B) from the whole bed. The position on the
bed where the maximum bed shear stress is found, varies from test to test. The position
where the maximum bed shear stress is reached are illustrated with the black solid line
in ﬁgure 5.2. Another main diﬀerence in this comparison, is that the simulation is run
a single time and the calculations of the bed shear stresses is in a single point on the
bottom wall, whereas the ensemble averaged measurements from Sumer et al. (2010) is
the averaged bed shear stresses on a line in the transverse direction over several runs. In
a numerical simulation with the exact same input parameters is likely to give the exact
same results.

creased, at test 5, ωt = 97.2◦and test 9, ωt = 68◦, as expected.
Another note is that the clockwise rotational motion is of greater magnitude relative to the
counter clockwise rotational motion with increased Reynolds number. This is illustrated
with the thickness of the positive vorticity (blue layer) close to the bottom, as it decreases
with increasing Reynolds number.
Given the fact that the vortex tubes will occur at an earlier phase with increasing Reynolds
number, the upstream velocity close to the wall will naturally have less time to develop to
a counter rotational motion, hence the smaller positive vorticity with increasing Reynolds
number. This also increases the asymmetry between the vortex pairs.
If you look closer at test number 8 in ﬁgure 5.4d it is clear that the vortex tubes are
very similar in size, compared to the other tests. This is probably because that the do-
main size and the vortex tube sizes are proportional, and it should be noted that this is a
coincidence. All the other test show small gaps in between the vortex tubes and/or bigger
asymmetry in the vortex tubes. The cause of this asymmetry between vortex tubes in the
same test is because the domain size and vortex tubes sizes are not proportional, and due
to the fact that the left and right boundaries are cyclic.

5.4
Numerical convergence analysis
The accuracy of the calculations is dependent on the steps in space and time. In this sec-
tion, a numerical convergence analysis has been carried out in a two dimensional domain
for test 9, to check the eﬀects of increasing/decreasing the time steps by changing the CFL
number.
A numerical convergence analysis of the one dimensional domain can be seen in appendix
B, where it was found that the error in the simulations were accumulating in the accel-
eration phase of the wave. If a one dimensional test was simulated with high accuracy
and then the two dimensional domain hot started from a given time step, this error in the
acceleration phase could be signiﬁcantly reduced.
The computational cost of simulating test 15 in a three dimensional domain is very ex-
pensive, the simulation time of one period of test 15 with a CFL = 1 is approximately 250
hr on 80 processors. That gives CPU time·p ≈19, 800 processor hours, hence the interest
of increasing the CFL number in the more critical tests.

Chapter 8
Conclusion
The solitaryFoam solver created in OpenFOAM has been successfully validated in terms
of laminar theory, and using the current setup in OpenFOAM, the simulations of Reynolds
numbers within the laminar regime are showing good agreement with the analytical solu-
tion from Liu at al. (2007).
The simulations of the tests in the laminar with vortex tubes regime illustrated simi-
lar results to that of (Scandura, 2013) of the vortex tubes, where a vortex pair emerges
close to the bottom wall, and these vortex pairs are causing the vortex tube to be lifted
from the bottom wall. The points A and B show a decrease followed by a strong in-
crease in bed shear stresses from the experimental measurements in test 5, 6 and 7 are
clearly seen replicated by the simulations in OpenFOAM, and the vortex tube structures
are seen for all the test in the laminar with vortex tube regime. Test 8 and 9 showed
more violent spikes in the bed shear stresses, after the emergence of vortex tubes, indi-
cating that the ﬂow is more turbulent in these test than the other three in the same regime.
The domain size of the simulations was seen to be a signiﬁcant factor in when the two
dimensional structures of the vortex tubes are replicated, where small gaps between the
vortex tubes are seen in the simulations.
Three dimensional simulations showed clear structure of the vortex tubes, and simulating
in a three dimensional domain also showed a small delay in maximum and minimum bed
shear stresses indicating a small delay in the occurrence of turbulence, presumably because
of allowing perturbations in the transverse direction, hence slowing down the emergence
of vortex tube structures. This diﬀerence of ≈5◦between 2D and 3D simulations was the
same for test 8,9 and 15.
The current setup of the solitaryFoam solver was not able to simulate a sudden turbulence
spot, as seen in the experimental results from (Sumer et al., 2010). Increasing the pressure
tolerance and allowing the inaccuracy of the pressure calculations to serve as perturbations
in the simulations did not show an earlier occurrence of turbulence, nor any indication of a
turbulent spot. The simulation with increased pressure tolerance broke down but that did
not have any signiﬁcant eﬀect on the results because it occurred at a late stage of the wave.
The friction coeﬃcients in the acceleration stage and deceleration stage is seen to fol-
low laminar theory (Liu et al., 2007) for all the tests in the laminar and laminar with
vortex tube regime. The phase diﬀerence in the acceleration stage also showed all tests
following the laminar theory, but the phase diﬀerence in the deceleration stage indicated

that tests 7, 8, 9 are diverting from the laminar solution, hence located in the transitional

The source of the perturbations in the simulations is somewhat unknown. Increasing the
order of the time scheme to Crank Nicholson does add stability to the calculations. But
it cannot be concluded that the source of the perturbations is solely from solving the time
scheme in a low order. It could be a combination of several numerical schemes, it has not
been the primary goal of this thesis to pinpoint the source of the perturbations. There
are arguably to many unknowns in the current numerical setup to identify a single source
of perturbations.
8.1
Recommendation for future work
Increasing the stability in the numerical model, would be recommended using a higher
order time advancement scheme. It is unknown to the author if the higher order Runge-
Kutta time schemes have been hard coded in OpenFOAM, but using a Crank Nicholson
second order time scheme would add more stability to the model. And disturbance can be
added with perturbations in the bottom wall, with utilities such as typoSet in OpenFOAM.
Adding velocity ﬂuctuations to the mapped velocity proﬁle at a given time step is also
a possibility, this could work as a kick that could induce the occurrence the a sudden
turbulence spot.
The computational resource available is a signiﬁcant factor in simulations using direct
numerical simulations of Navier-Stokes equations. And increasing the time scheme to a
higher order Runge-Kutta time scheme will naturally increase the computational cost of
these simulations.
