ABSTRACT
Magnetic Resonance Spectroscopy (MRS) is as a noninvasive method to study in vivo metabolism.
Conventional thermal MRS can provide information on steady state metabolism over a long
measurement time. Additionally, dissolution dynamic nuclear polarization (dDNP) MRS with
enhanced sensitivity can measure short-term metabolic kinetics. This thesis aims at designing
and evaluating a novel pyruvate tracer with combined labelling of 2H and 13C. The 2H and 13C
labelling of pyruvate follow distinct metabolic paths and work on diﬀerent time scales. It was
examined whether complementary metabolic information can be gained from measuring the
thermally polarized 2H and hyperpolarized (HP) 13C signal from the same tracer.
The optimal labelling of the pyruvate tracer was investigated by spectrum simulations at
varying ﬁeld strengths, T1 measurements and mapping of the possible metabolic pathways. An
enzyme model system was developed to evaluate the tracer. To compare the kinetics of the
two methods within three minutes recording, an irreversible reaction with a full conversion of
pyruvate to lactate catalyzed by lactate dehydrogenase was designed. Multiple mathematical
models were tested to get the best estimates of the reaction rate for a reliable quantitative com-
parison. The tracer was further evaluated in a neuron model system to test it under physiological
conditions. Thereby, the tracer was studied in vitro in neurons from healthy NMRI mice.
The best tracer design was [1-13C1,U-2H3] pyruvate with qualities of a long T1 for hyperpolar-
ization and no demands of decoupling. The results of the enzyme model system were successful.
The pyruvate and lactate peaks were detectable from both the 2H and HP 13C signals at a magnetic
ﬁeld strength of 11.2T. The temporal resolution of both signals were high enough to measure
the dynamics of the process within three minutes. Similar reaction rates were estimated by a
simple ﬁrst-order model for the 2H signal and a one-way kinetic model for the HP 13C signal.
With the neuron model system it was possible to measure dynamics of the HP 13C signal within
two minutes of recording. Detecting 13C peaks of pyruvate, lactate and bicarbonate originating
from cellular activity unfolds the opportunity to measure aerobic and anaerobic metabolism. For
a dynamic proﬁle of the 2H signal, a larger concentration of cells and longer repetitions times
of minimum 30 minutes will be required. Pyruvate and lactate peaks from the 2H signal were
measured nicely within a time range of 1.5 hours.
The feasibility of studying brain disorders related to aberrant metabolism applying the
[1 −13 C1,U-2H3] pyruvate tracer is promising. Both MRS methods have already been used
for human studies separately. With the same pyruvate tracer, steady-state metabolism can be
measured from the 2H signal and real-time dynamics from the HP 13C signal. Expanding the
mathematical models applied in the project will give rise to the opportunity of modelling absolute
reaction rates for quantitative comparisons of metabolic ﬂuxes.

INTRODUCTION
1
In the introduction, the project will be presented from the motivation behind in large perspective and
narrowing down by going through the state of art of the methods applied and ﬁnally zooming in on
the aim and objectives of the projects.
1.1
problem statement
Aberrant metabolism is a prominent factor in the origin of various brain diseases [32]. The most
prevalent neurodegenerative disease is Alzheimer’s disease (AD). It is characterized by progressive
hypometabolism and impaired mitochondrial bioenergetics. This leads to an accumulation of
β-amyloid and is coupled with oxidative stress. Al together it evokes a shift in the metabolism
from aerobic glycolysis towards being dominated by the ketogenic fatty acid β oxidation path-
way. This is vicious circle resulting in degeneration of white matter with time [56]. Cognitive
impairment especially linked to memory loss is the main symptom revealing an AD diagnosis.
Late measurable biomarkers of the disease are abnormal deposition of β-amyloid plaques and
accumulation neuroﬁbrillary tangles of Tau protein. However, decades before at a stage of mild
cognitive impairment altered cerebral glucose metabolism can be detected. Visualizing and quan-
tifying the glucose metabolism creates an opportunity for early diagnosis and monitoring of
disease progression. New understanding of the impaired metabolic mechanism at an early stage
of disease progression can lead to novel treatment methods [14] [56].
The golden standard of measuring metabolism is ﬂourodeoxyglucose positron emission tomog-
raphy (FDG-PET) [56]. It gives an indication of hypometabolism by measuring the glucose uptake.
However, it provides ambiguous results in the brain that has an intrinsic high glucose uptake
and it is limited to the uptake and cannot measure downstream metabolism. The radioactive
property restricts repetitions of the scanning [11]. With magnetic resonance spectroscopy (MRS)
it is possible non-invasively to measure down-stream metabolites of glucose. It is possible to
distinguish between anaerobic cytosolic metabolism and aerobic metabolism in the mitochondria.
Thermal 13C MRS have been used in the clinic to measure steady-state metabolism in humans
[55]. However, this method has low signal sensitivity and requires long scan times and infusion
of 13C labeled tracer [11].
Hyperpolarized 13C MRS overcomes the sensitivity problem by enhancing the spin polariza-
tion with ﬁve orders of magnitude above thermal Boltzmann polarization [44]. With dissolution
dynamic nuclear polarization (dDNP) the signal to noise ratio (SNR) is enhanced > 10.000 times
and can be applied to molecules in a liquid solution [1]. The dynamics of downstream metabolic
ﬂuxes of 13C labelled tracers can be measured in vivo with a high temporal resolution [1] [32].
High signal sensitivity and spatio-temporal resolution of the metabolites makes the HP 13C

Applying two tracers simultaneously has in other studies been used to gain complementary
information about the metabolism of the tracers. Deja et al. applies non-hyperpolarized 13C and
2H labelled glucose tracers for studying liver metabolism. Information about the metabolites
along the paths of both 13C and 2H respectively was obtained in hepatic cells [13]. Kennedy et al.
combined hyperpolarized 13C and 2H labelling of lactate to study lactate dehydrogenase activity
in tumors. They aimed at measuring the exchange of hydrogen and deuterium [31]. Yang et al.
combined hyperpolarized [1-13C] pyruvate with [3-13C] pyruvate at thermal polarization [55].
From the hyperpolarized signal they got a dynamic view of the reactions with a quantiﬁcation of
the apparent metabolic rates. The thermally polarized tracer gave them a steady-state overview
of the downstream metabolites of pyruvate after six hours of signal recording [55].
1.2
project aim and hypothesis
Both 13C dDNP MRS and 2H thermal MRS share the strengths of being non-radioactive and able
to measure glucose uptake and downstream metabolism in vivo. They have been shown useful
for MRS in the clinic and safe to apply in human brains [11] [45]. Due to low natural abundance
of 13C and 2H highly speciﬁc tracers can be designed. The spatio-temporal resolution and life
time of the signal from the two methods are diﬀerent giving rise to the opportunity of measuring
metabolism at diﬀerent time-scales.

1.3. Thesis Objectives

Project Aim
The aim of this project is to investigate a combined tracer labelled both with 13C for hyper-
polarization and 2H. The tracer is evaluated in the perspective of being clinical applicable for
measuring human cerebral metabolism.
Hypothesis
The hypothesis is that hyperpolarized 13C and 2H MRS can provide complementary metabolic
information by taking advantages of the diﬀerent metabolic proﬁles obtained under the diﬀerent
timescales they operate at.

thesis objectives

2.1
metabolism of the brain
2.1.1
Metabolic Pathways of Glucose
Glucose is the primary energy substrate of the brain. It crosses the blood brain barrier (BBB)
by transporters GLUT-1 and GLUT-3. ATP is synthesized as glucose is catabolized through the
glycolysis, tricarboxylic acid (TCA) and the electron transport chain (ECT). As a result of the
six steps of the glycolysis, glycose is broken down into two pyruvate molecules. Pyruvate is in
rapid exchange with alanine and lactate catalyzed by the enzymes alanine transaminase (ALT)
and lactate dehydrogenase (LDH). Pyruvate is metabolized by oxidative decarboxylation into
acetyl-CoA and carbon dioxide (CO2) which is in equilibrium with bicarbonate. This conversion
is catalyzed by pyruvate dehydrogenase (PDH) [19]. Acetyl-CoA enters the TCA cycle. At the
α−KG step of the TCA cycle, glutamate can be synthesized by glutamate dehydrogenase (GLDH).
Glutamate takes part in the glutamate-glutamine neurotransmitter cycle. GABA, a inhibatory
neurotransmitter, is synthesized from glutamate catalyzed by glutamate decarboxylase (GAD) [12].
In the dehydrogenase steps of the TCA, NAD is used to transport an electron on the to mitochon-
dria. The outcome of the ECT in the mitochondria is synthesized ATP [49]. Various pathological
processes arise from the shift in oxidative metabolism in the mitochondria to anaerobic glycolytic
metabolism with e.g. lactate production [19] [33]. Bicarbonate or glutamate and glutamine
production indicates a ﬂow of pyruvate into the TCA cycle resulting in aerobic metabolism in the
mitochondria [5] [34] [37]. A simpliﬁed illustration of cerebral glucose metabolism is presented
in ﬁgure 2.2.

2.2
magnetic resonance spectroscopy
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance (NMR) was discovered in 1945 and relies on the magnetic properties
and energy of a nuclei. With NMR spectroscopy it is possible to identify and quantify molecules.
In vivo NMR spectroscopy has opened the possibility to study static and dynamic aspects of
metabolism under physiological and pathological conditions [12].
2.2.1
Nuclear Magnetic Resonance
NMR utilizes that all magnetic nuclei with a non-zero spin generates a magnetic moment. When
exposed to an external magnetic ﬁeld low- and high-energy spin states are created being either
in or anti parallel to the magnetic ﬁeld. The energy diﬀerence between the spin states follows
equation 2.1 and is determined by the nuclear Zeeman eﬀect [29] [53].

Excitation pulse
When applying a radio frequency (RF) pulse at the Larmor frequency, the particular nuclei absorbs
the photon and ﬂip from a low-energy state to a high energy state. This phenomena is called
excitation. When the nuclei relax back to the low-energy state, electromagnetic irradiation is
emitted. This energy release correspond to the energy gab in equation 2.1 and is detected as the
NMR signal. Another consequence of excitation is phasing of the spin. They starts to precess in
phase as a bundle of spins when excited [9].
Relaxation
After excitation the nuclei relax back to thermal equilibrium following the Boltzmann distribution.
This process is the spin-lattice relaxation. It has the characteristic exponential time constant T1.
Each nucleus has a distinct T1 depending on the structural and chemical environment [53].
Tumbling describes a molecule chaotically moving and rotating around a liquid. Spin-lattice
relaxation couples the spins to the tumbling of the molecule. This allows a change of energy
between the spin system and the surroundings. After excitation the spin system relaxes back
to thermal equilibrium depending on the T1. Small molecules, low viscosity of the liquid and
high temperature augments the tumbling and prolongs the T1 of the nucleus. The measurable
T1 is a combination of various types of T1 relaxations including chemical shift anisotropy and
paramagnetic relaxation [29]. Nuclei with spin greater than 1/2 have in addition to the magnetic
dipole moment an electric quadrupole moment. The charge distribution in the nucleus of a
quadrupolar nucleus appear ellipsoidal in contrast to a spherical distribution. The quadupolar
nuclei interacts with the electrical ﬁeld gradient of the molecule. These interactions causes
increased spin relaxation, small T1, and broader line widths, small T2 [29]. The phase-coherence
created by the RF pulse is lost with the time constant T2. This is determined the spin-spin
relaxation.

2.2.2
NMR Spectrum
FID and Fourier transform
The RF pulse is a narrow spectrum of RF frequencies that excites the nuclei with the correspond-
ing Larmor frequency. It is emitted by a transmitter coil. When the spin relax back to thermal
equilibrium, the receiver coil quantiﬁes the energy release. This NMR signal is called the free
induction decay (FID). It is detected as sinusoidal waves in the time domain. The FID is converted
to the frequency domain by Fourier Transformation. Each peak in the frequency domain represent
a particular nuclei precessing at a speciﬁc frequency [9].

Chemical shift
The various frequencies from the FID in the frequency domain are peaks separated on an axis of the
dimensionless chemical shift δ. The chemical shift is deﬁned as the diﬀerence between the Lamor
frequency of the nucleus itself and the frequency of a reference nucleus. Tetramethylsilane (TMS)
is conventionally used as the reference for 1H and 13C NMR. δ is referred to in ppm [29], [53] Fac-
tors that aﬀect the chemical shift are the solvent, pH, temperature, and viscosity of the solution [2].
Shielding
The Larmor frequency of a nuclei in a molecule vary marginally. B0 is the magnetic ﬁeld felt
by a nucleus without any electrons. The nuclei is said to be shielded with electrons and feel a
slightly smaller magnetic ﬁeld than B0. This results in a small shift of the Larmor frequency and
chemical shift upﬁeld (left on the spectrum). The local electron distribution of a nucleus depends
on the position in a molecule. With slightly varying Larmor frequencies due to diﬀerences in the
surrounding environment like 1H couplings, the diﬀerent nuclei in a molecule can be separated
using NMR. This phenomenon is denoted shielding. It is highly eﬀected by diamagnetic and para-
magnetic properties. Where paramagnetic properties induces a small local magnetic ﬁeld acting
to augment B0 and giving rise to deshielding following a down ﬁeld chemical shift. Diagmanetic

Couplings
There is not necessarily a single peak per nucleus. The magnetic interaction between the nuclei
results in splitting of the peak into several peaks. The number of peaks is governed by the n +1
rule. If a nuclei has n non-equivalent nuclei neighbors the peak will split into n +1 peaks. These
couplings are quantiﬁed as the diﬀerence between peaks in a split signal. The coupling constant
is referred to as J-coupling and is given in Hz [3]. It is independent of the magnetic ﬁeld strength.
Couplings give information about the molecular structure. In a 13C NMR spectrum couplings
between the carbon atom and the directly bonded 1H would be seen. If the more simple spectrum
is desired, proton decoupled spectra can be obtained. Homonuclear 13C −13C couplings occurs as
well. [29].
Line widths
The sharpness of the peaks are not equivalent. The line width is deﬁned as the width at half
its peaks height [53]. Line broadening is dependent on T2 that reﬂects the homogeneity of the
magnetic ﬁeld. A large T2 will create slow spin-spin relaxation and narrow line widths of the
spectrum [29]. The signal intensity of a peak is therefore not measured as the height, but as the
integral of peak.

2.3
molecular labelling methods
Most biologically relevant molecules have a carbon backbone. 12C is the most abundant isotope
of carbon, but it has no spin and cannot be detected using NMR. Labelling an isotope with 13C
creates a speciﬁc tracer. Since 13C has spin 1/2 it is detectable using NMR and can be used to
track metabolic pathways. The main challenges of 13C MRS is the low sensitivity which demands
long scan times [12], [29]. Methods have been developed to overcome the sensitivity problem
of 13C MRS making it a feasible method for humans in a clinical setting [32]. Another novel
molecular labelling method used to study metabolism is deuterium metabolic imaging (DMI).
DMI has the valuable T1 and T2 ratio making it a clinical viable method [51].
2.3.1
Deuterium MRS
Labeling substrates with deuterium, 2H, is a new approach used to investigate in vivo metabolism.
It is an inexpensive tracer that is easy to administrate and save to use in humans for deuterium
MRS (DMS) and metabolic imaging (DMI). 2H has spin 1 and the quadropolar relaxation shortens
T1 and T2. This creates broader line widths and lower resolution compared to 1H. The 2H
spectra contains a few peaks such the line broadening might not lead to peak overlap [11]. When
acquiring a thermal NMR spectrum the short T1 induced by the quadropole is beneﬁcial to acquire
a large amount of scans quickly. The short recycle time will result in a large SNR after signal
averaging making the 2H tracer detectable [29] [39]. All together DMS is a simple and robust
method and viable in the clinic [11].
2.3.2
Hyperpolarized 13C MRS
Several methods have been developed to overcome the sensitivity problem by enhancing the
polarization described in equation 2.2. These are denoted hyperpolarization methods and among
them is dynamic nuclear polarization (DNP). DNP utilizes microwave irradiation to transfer
electron spin polarization from free radical to nuclear spins [41]. Furthermore, dissolution DNP
(dDNP) have been developed, enabling hyperpolarization of organic molecules in a liquid solution.
After hyperpolarization in the solid phase using DNP, the sample undergoes a rapid dissolution
wile preserving the polarization level. The high polarization enhances the signal to noise ratio over
10.000 times making it applicable to study metabolites in vivo that appear at low concentrations
and would otherwise not be detectable [1].
DNP
The DNP sample consists of the substrate and a paramagnetic agent of organic free radicals. The
spin polarization transfer is best conducted with a homogeneous distribution of electrons and
nuclei ensured by an amorphous sample preparation. The highest polarization of the electrons
yielding 100% is achieved at low temperatures < 1.5K and high magnetic ﬁeld strength > 3T.
This was examined by Meier et al. and their results are shown in ﬁgure ?? [41].

Nuclei as 13C has Larmor frequencies in the RF range. Due to the larger energy level diﬀer-

from the pulse excitation approach described earlier, and diﬀerent mechanisms like thermal
mixing are applied [15]. The overall idea is that when the electron generates a magnetic ﬁeld,
the close by nuclei becomes dipolar-coupled. A combined ﬂip of this nuclei-electron system
occur and brings the nuclei in a high-energy state. The electrons return quickly to thermal
equilibrium due to very low T1 relaxation of approximately 1ms. To create a large population
of nuclei in the high-energy state, many ﬂip of nuclear-electron coupled system must be gained
shortly after another. Spin diﬀusion, where two neighboring nuclei experience mutual ﬂips,
is a spin transport mechanism, that help reaching the nuclei further away. The nuclei relax
back to the low-energy state with their speciﬁc T1 which is in order of seconds. The ﬁnal polar-
ization level will be a compromise between the rate at which the nuclei ﬂips with the electron
transitions, and the T1 relaxation rate of the nuclei. A high level of polarization is around 40% [12].
dDNP
When the level of hyperpolarization saturates dissolution is performed. A heated buﬀer is mixed
with the sample resulting in a rapid dissolution creating a liquid solution. After dissolution the
hyperpolarized signal is nonrenewable and decreases with the T1 relaxation of the nuclei. A long
T1 relaxation time is thereby of interest. A fast transfer of the hyperpolarized sample into the
NMR spectrometer is crucial to acquire the FID before the signal is lost [1]. After one T1, 63% of
the signal is lost. The T1 relaxation in the liquid state is aﬀected by the paramagnetic impurities,
solvent, temperature and pH [2]. The sensitivity problem of 13C NMR is overcome by dDNP
hypolarization. The most prominent challenge is the limited signal life time due to T1 relaxation.
High cost, technical complexity and altered physiological conditions during injection are other
drawbacks of the method [12].

NMR Spectrometer
The external magnetic ﬁeld, B0, is created by a large superconducting magnet. A strong magnet
creates a higher signal sensitivity and better separation of frequencies in NMR spectrum following
equation 2.1. The magnet is cooled down by liquid helium to minimize the electrical resistance
and create superconducting magnets with a higher ﬁeld strength. A stable B0 would avoid drifting

3.2
tracer design
One of the objectives of the project is to design a combined tracer. The tracer will be labelled
with 2H and 13C simultaneously to gain complementary metabolic information. The 2H and
13C labelling will follow diﬀerent metabolic paths when metabolised making it possible to see
diﬀerent metabolites [13]. Which molecule to use as a tracer and how to labelled is important for
the information that can be gained with the two NMR methods.
3.2.1
Clinical tracer demands
The tracer is a molecule labeled with the NMR active isotopes 13C and 2H. The scope is to design a
clinical applicable tracer for brain studies. Therefore it has to fulﬁl some criteria and the setup has
some outer limitations. The section is divided into demands of the tracer in terms of the being in-
jected into the human body, applying thermal MRS and dDNP to obtain spectroscopic information.
Physiological demands
The tracer should ﬁrst of all be biocompatible and non-toxic when injected at concentration of
millimole [30]. Practical considerations about commercially availability and general applicability
of the tracer must be done as well [41]. The tracer should function within the physiological range
of temperature T = 37◦C = 310K and pH = 7.0−7.4. These two conditions eﬀects the chemical
shift separability, spatial resolution, and T1 values of the nuclei [2].
Field strength and sensitivity demands
The magnetic ﬁeld strength of a clinical scanner is 3T. The ﬁeld strength of the spectrometer
applied for research in this project is 11.2T. Increasing the the magnetic ﬁeld strength, will
increase the strength of the NMR signal. The sensitivity should be high enough at both ﬁeld
strength to detect and quantify the signal.
dDNP demands
To gain a high polarization level within a tolerable time range in the solid state, the tracer should
be eﬃciently hyperpolarized. It should be resilient enough to be complete after dissolution
also. Considering the short life time of the hyperpolarized signal, the tracer must undergo fast
distribution, high cellular uptake and fast metabolism on a time scale minutes [30] [38].
3.2.2

a longer time with a longer T1 of 13C. Deuterating the molecule have shown to prolong the
T1 of the 13C nuclei [12]. The deuterium signal is measured by thermal NMR. In this case a
short T1 is preferable. With a fast relaxation back to thermal equilibrium after excitation, it is

3.5.2
Modelling the HP 13C signal
When modelling HP 13C data multiple factors inﬂuence the signal decay. As mentioned before
the hyperpolarization decays with the T1 relaxation of the metabolite. The respective relaxation
rates is simply 1/T1. There is a further signal loss due to application of the RF pulse with tip
angle, α, and repetition time, T R, during signal acquisition. The eﬀective relaxation rate of the
hyperpolarized signal is a combination of these components T1, α and T R. Finally the exchange
of substrate and products is an important factor and will have the eﬀective rate constants k [21].
The aim is to model the HP 13C signal gained from experiments and estimate the conversion rate
k of the reactions. The three decays of the HP 13C signal are:
• 1/T1: decay rate of polarization
• ρ: decay from pulsing with α at T R
• k: rate constant of reaction exchange
Many have successfully modelled the HP 13C signal based on modiﬁed Bloch equations, and
others have applied model-free approaches [23]. A number of modelling approaches have been
compared and evaluated in other studies, but no golden standard have been deﬁned [10] [21].
The models presented in this project build on those ﬁndings. But they are evaluated in relation to
the in vitro experiments set up to represent the enzyme model system in ﬁgure 3.7 and neuron
model system in ﬁgure 3.8 with the chosen tracer design.

4.1.1
Metabolic pathways
The label pathways of C1, C2 and C3 of pyruvate are mapped based on studies that have success-
fully examined cerebral metabolism with the respective isotopic labelling of pyruvate. Grist et al.
used MRI to study human brain metabolism with hyperpolarized [1−13C] pyruvate as tracer [19].
Chung et. al. studied metabolism of the human brain using MRS and [2−13C] pyruvate isotope [5].
Hyperpolarized [3−13C] pyruvate have not yet been used to study human brain metabolism. From
measuring thermally polarized 13C a label ﬂow of cerebral metabolism is illustrated by Degraaf
[12]. By relating this ﬂow to the two studies of the human brain, the [3−13C] label path is mapped.
The 2H labelling of the metabolites of deuterated [6,6′ −2 H2] glucose have been quantiﬁed
in vivo in the mouse rat brain by Lu et al. [37]. DMI of the human brain have been performed
by Feyter et al. using [6,6′ −2 H2] glucose as well [11]. The deuterium labelling of [6,6′ −2 H]
glucose ends up in the methyl group of pyruvate after the glycolysis and downstream information
from there is included. The outcome of mapping the label pathways is an overview of which
metabolites are possible to visualize with the respective tracer design.

4.3
mathematical modelling of the enzyme reaction
Physiological System
The system to be modelled is the enzyme reaction of pyruvate conversion to lactate. One substrate
and one product is present. The data obtained is the signal from [1-13C1,U-2H3] pyruvate and
[1-13C1,U-2H3] lactate. The co-substrates and enzyme is not included. The signal intensities
in arbitrary units are converted to concentrations. This is done by normalizing the signal and
multiplying with the known start concentration of pyruvate being 7.5mM.
Purpose
The purpose was to get a quantiﬁcation of the reaction rate constant kpl in ﬁgure 4.3 for both the
HP 13C and 2H signal. This estimate should ideally be equal, when the reaction was measured
following 1−13C and 2H of pyruvate. The success criteria is a simple transparent models delivering
accurate and robust estimates.
Figure 4.3: Reaction Scheme of the Enzymatic Reaction to be Modelled
Parameter Estimation
The LMFIT package in Python 3.8.3 was used to ﬁt the model to data and estimate the parameters
of the model [46]. First an initial guess of the parameters was made. Based on the initial guess
the system of diﬀerential equations was solved numerically using odeint in Python [7]. An error
function was deﬁned as the diﬀerence between the estimated concentration using the suggested
model and the measured data from the enzyme experiment. The output of the error function
were the residuals. The optimization of the parameters were then performed as an iterative
process applying the lmﬁt minimizer. It minimizes the residuals from the error function and
estimated the optimal parameter values starting from the initial guess. The ﬁtting algorithm used
is Levenberg-Marquardt algorithm. It gives both the estimated parameters with uncertainties
and the error metrics. The system of diﬀerential equations were then solved with the estimated
parameters.
Model Validation and Model Comparison
• The error metrics used for comparing models ﬁtting to the same data was the AIC.
• For model evaluation the R2 and root-mean-squared-error, RMSE, were applied.
• The residuals were assumed to be independent and identically distributed (i.i.d.) following
a normal distribution with zero mean and unit standard deviation ∼N (µ, σ2). QQ-plots

complied.

Modelling the 13C signal
Modelling approaches:
The three HP 13C models are presented below. In the article by Daniels et al. [10] models
describing the pyruvate conversion to lactate are compared. The optimal model in their study
was a two-way reaction model. The assumption of an irreversible reaction is not applied. The
goal is to apply the model for both the enzyme and neuron model systems. Data from the ﬁrst
3 min was used in all models. Both the pyruvate concentration and lactate concentration was
used for ﬁtting the data. Information about the two ﬁrst seconds were missing due to T R = 2sec.
The data was extrapolated with one data point. To do so an exponential decay was ﬁtted to the
pyruvate data and including the pyruvate concentration at t = 0. The corresponding data point
for the lactate signal at t = 0 was set to a NAN value.
• Model (a): One-way reaction with non-equal T1 values of pyruvate and lactate
• Model (b): Two-way reaction with equal apparent T1 values of pyruvate and lactate
• Model (c): Two-way reaction non-equal T1 values of pyruvate and lactate
Assumptions:
Model (a) assumes a one-way reaction in the enzyme model system. The reaction rate of lactate to
pyruvate is assumed klp = 0. This has in many studies been considered a reasonable simpliﬁcation
since most often klp = 1
10kpl [10].
Model (b) is the model that performed best in the article by Daniels et al. It is a two way model
where the T1 values are assumed to be equal. This assumption of using one apparent T1 values
for both pyruvate and lactate reduces the number of parameters to be ﬁtted. It is justiﬁed by the
fast exchange between metabolites[10][35].
Model (c) is a more complex model with separate T1 values of pyruvate and lactate. The assump-
tion of equal T1 values might be a good approximation in terms of modelling the data, but it is

4.4.1
Mouse Neurons
The neurons used in the experiments were taken from healthy NMRI mice approximately 14 days
pregnant. More precise the neurons originate from fetus mice taken from three pregnant mothers
a few days before the estimated day of birth. Neurons at this state has the advantageous of not
having diﬀerentiated. The whole brain was squeezed out of the head that had not yet developed a
skull. All the brains were gathered and chopped to mush. Following the protocol in Supporting
Information G.3 the cell solution was prepared. Neurons from fetuses of three mothers were used
for 50mL solution corresponding approximately to a concentration of 2.75 million cells pr. mL.
This part of the project was done in collaboration with the School of Pharmaceutical Sciences at
the University of Copenhagen and performed carefully by Heidi Marie Nielsen. The cells were
transported to the laboratory at The Technical University of Denmark just after.
Cell culture
All work in the laboratory was done in a laminar ﬂow bench to avoid contamination of the sensitive
neurons. Firstly, everything was disinfected with ethanol to obtain a sterile environment.
• 1 Day before:
The day before getting the neurons, cell culture ﬂasks of 25 cm2 were covered with 5mL of
poly-D-lysin. This increases the binding of the neurons to the ﬂask surface and reduces the
neurons likelihood to clump together.
• Day of receiving cells:
At the day of receiving the cells from Heidi Marie Nielsen, the ﬂasks were ﬂushed with 5mL
of Tyrodes buﬀer twice to remove the poly-D-lysin. 5mL of cell suspension was added to
each ﬂask. All ﬂasks with neurons were then placed in an incubator at 37◦C and 5% CO2.
• 2 Days after:
Two days after 50µL glucose stock solution of 1.2mM was added to each ﬂask as nutrition
for the cells. 50µL cytarabin-solution was added as well to block astrocytal division.
• 5 Days after:
After ﬁve days 50µL glucose stock solution of 1.2mM is added to each ﬂask again.
• 7 Days after:
The neurons were ready for experiments after seven days.

5.1.4
Tracer Design Comparison
Based on the downstream metabolites presented in ﬁgure 5.1, the measured T1 value in table 5.1
and the simulations at B = 11.2 in ﬁgure 5.3 and B = 3T in ﬁgure 5.2 the three tracer designs are
presented.
[1−13C1,U-2H3] Pyruvate
The [1−13C] of pyruvate (172.9 ppm) is converted to lactate C1 (185 ppm), alanine C1 (178ppm)
and bicarbonate C1 (161ppm). This carbon atom does not enter the TCA. However, the production
of bicarbonate would represent oxidative metabolism. The production of lactate and alanine
could reﬂect anaerobic metabolism of pyruvate. Deuterium is not directly attached which has the
beneﬁcial eﬀect of less coupling. Even though deuterium coupled to the carbon atom prolongs
the T1, the T1 of C1 in the pyruvate molecule is the longest of the three carbon atoms. The sample
preparation used for the enzyme and neuron experiments included gadolinium and the deuterated
pyruvic acid. The relevant T1 value in terms of HP signal is T1 = 67s.
For all designs it would be possible to follow deuterium from pyruvate (2.36 ppm) converting
to lactate (1.31 ppm), alanine (1.47 ppm), glutamate (2.33 ppm), glutamine (2.43 ppm) and pyruvate
hydrate based on ﬁgure 5.1. From ﬁgure 5.2 (B) and 5.3 (B) an overlap between pyruvate and
glumatate is seen. At low ﬁeld strengths the glutamine peak would vanish in a large pyruvate-
glutamate-glutamine peak. It is possible to separate alanine and lactate in the simulation. The T1
value of 2H3 in pyruvate is measured to be 2.46s.
[2−13C1,U-2H3] Pyruvate
The [2−13C] of pyruvate (208 ppm) can be followed to lactate C2 (71.2 ppm), alanine C2 (53.4 ppm),
glumatate C5 (183 ppm) and glutamine C5 (180 ppm). The ﬂow into the TCA is reﬂected by the
production of glutamate and glutamine. The anerobic metabolism is again determined to be the
lactate and alanine production. Little coupling is excepted since deuterium is not directly attached
to the C2 of pyruvate. All peaks were nicely separated in the simulation of the 13C spectra at
both ﬁeld strengths in ﬁgure. The 2H spectrum showed one peak for pyruvate, glatamate and
glutamine. The simulations is found in appendix A ﬁgure A.1 and A.2. In protonated pyruvate
the T1 of C2 is the second longest of the three carbon atoms. In a deuterated pyruvate molecule
the T1 value of the C2 is the shortest of the three. For the deuterated pyruvate in a preparation
with gadolinium the T1 value of C2 is 44s.
[3−13C1,U-2H3] Pyruvate
The [3−13 C] of pyruvate (29 ppm) is metabolized to lactate C3 (23 ppm), alanine C3 (19 ppm),
glumatate C4 (36 ppm) and glutamine C4 (34 ppm). Aerobic metabolism through the TCA and
anearobic metabolism with cytosolic production of lactate can also here be followed. The deu-

terium is coupled directly the C3 of pryuvate. This demands decoupling of the system. Not all
spectrometer have that feature. This becomes a problem for the spatial resolution of the 2H
spectrum at low ﬁeld strengths 3T. Peak overlap makes it diﬃcult to distinguish peaks from
diﬀerent metabolites. It is clear that decoupling is needed in the 13C spectra, but peaks are

6.1
discussion of the experiments and results
A part of the project aim was to investigate whether this new NMR method combining 2H and

the experiments and their limitation will be evaluated in terms of answering this.
6.1.1
Combined Tracer design
Evaluation of the combined labelling of Pyruvate with 2H and 13C simultaneously
In the introduction it was mentioned that others have tried to gain information from diﬀerent
labellings of tracer simultaneously. An evaluation of the [1-13C1,U-2H3] pyruvate tracer is evalu-
ated in terms of the information it can provide.
Diﬀerent Metabolic Pathways
In the study by Deja et al. they aim at improving the computational models for quantiﬁcation of
metabolic ﬂuxes in the liver. They point out the problem that the models are simpliﬁed due to the
limited number of tracers giving information about the downstream metabolites. They solve this
problem by applying two tracers of 2H and 13C simultaneously and get information about the
distinctive metabolic path ways.
The brain metabolism of pyruvate was examined for diﬀerent pathways of the three carbon
nuclei and deuterium. The labelling with 13C in the carboxyl group, C1, was found best. This
excluded the measurement of glutamate and glutamine that can give direct information about
pyruvate going into the TCA cycle. However, it was possible to see the aerobic mitochondrial
respiration indirectly with a production of bicarbonate. The anaerobic metabolism could be
followed with the production of alanine and lactate. The pyruvate molecule can only be labelled
with deuterium in the methyl group at C3. Labelling with 2H made it possible to identify lactate
and alanine. Unfortunately the peaks of glutamate and glutamine overlapped with the pyruvate
peak at 3T.
75
==PAGE:90==
76
Chapter 6. Discussion
Steady-state metabolism
In the study by Yang et al., they measured steady-state metabolism of non-hyperpolarized 3-[13C]
labelled pyruvate tracer and dynamics with 1-[13C] labelled pyruvate tracer of living cancer
cells. This was also done with two injection of two tracers. It is raises the question of why intro-
ducing deuterium labelling and not just apply non-HP 13C labelling for the combined tracer design.
With a non-hyperpolarized labelling of either 13 or the 2H, the steady-state metabolism can
be measured on a large time scale. The thermally polarized NMR signal has no limited time
gap, but the sensitivity is low. 13C has a larger γ and thereby a greater sensitivity than 2H. A
good reason for labelling with 2H rather than 13C for steady-state metabolism is the remarkably
short T1 of deuterium compared to the T1 of 13C. For pyruvate the T1 of the carbon atoms was
measured to be 67s, 44s and 57s respectively. The T1 value of deuterium was 2.46s. The spin 1 of
deuterium and quadropolar moment creates the short T1. It is favorably with a short T1 value in
thermal NMR, because the TR can be shorter and the signal acquisition can happen faster with
shorter overall measurement times.
Furthermore, labelling in two carbon atoms in the same molecule will create challenges with
couplings. The 2H is coupled directly to the C3 of pyruvate which created peak overlap in the
simulation due to splitting of the signals. The C1 labelling of pyruvate was chosen above C3,
because no decoupling was needed. This is also the reason for not labelling all carbon atoms.
Dynamics of metabolic ﬂuxes
With hyperpolarized 13C it is possible to pulse with a low α and low T R due to the strongly
enhanced signal. The temporal resolution becomes high enough to measure real-time dynamics
of reactions. But the measuring time is limited to the life time of the hyperpolarized signal that is
decaying exponentially with T1. The long T1 value is therefore desired. The T1 value pyruvate
C1 was the longest in all solutions measured shown in table 5.1. Labelling in C2 has the downside
that a proton is coupled directly in lactate which shortens the T1 signiﬁcantly. No protons were
directly coupled to C1 in any of the downstream metabolites.
Key points
The [1-13C1,U-2H3] double labelling of pyruvate with 2H and 13C for hyperpolarization is novel.
It can potentially show multiple routes and provide dynamic and steady-state information of
pyruvate metabolism.

Design of the enzyme experiment
The choice of a pyruvate concentration at 7.5mM was very high compared to physiological
cerebral concentrations at less than 0.2mM [18]. But this created a large enough 2H sensitivity
to measure dynamics within the 3 minutes of signal acquisition. A noticeably large amount
of NADH was added to secure a one way reaction. Increasing the amount of LDH and NADH
increased the reaction rate, and all pyruvate was converted to lactate within the three minutes in
ﬁgure B.8 and B.9. The T R was set up from 5s til 17s for the deuterium signal acquisition shown
in ﬁgure B.10. The pyruvate signal was suppressed due to too short time to relax back to thermal
equilibrium. The pulsing was done before the full signal could be attained again. The 2H lactate
signal was measured nicely, because of the shorter T1 value of 0.38s.
The 2H and HP-13C signals of the combined tracer were not measured simultaneously. How-
ever, the conditions of for the 2H and 13C signal acquisition was made as similar as possible. Both
HP samples originated from the same batch of labelled pyruvic acid, free radical and gadolinium
complex. The reaction medium and cell suspensions were split into two and applied for each
experiment. After polarization and dissolution, the HP solution was split such that the same
solution was applied when recording the 13C and 2H signals. The hyperpolarization was lost
when the 2H signal was measured, but that made no diﬀerence for the thermal 2H signal.
One diﬀerence arised, when injecting a portion of HP solution right after dissolution for the
HP 13C signal recording. The HP solution was slightly warmer right after dissolution compared
to when it had been lying on the table for a couple of minutes. This had no inﬂuence on the
identiﬁed peaks, because the temperature adjusted quickly to 310K in the spectrometer. However,
it had a consequence when modelling the reactions rates, because it relied on the initial rate of
the reactions. The reaction rate was measured to be lower, when the HP solution was injected
directly after dissolution compared to cooling down at the table ﬁrst.
The pipetting of could be a source of an error. Critical situations are when the HP sample
is going into the HP cup for polarization, measuring the volume of the reaction medium or cell
suspension and taking the HP solution from the receiver container. Diﬀerent volumes in the
NMR tube would results in a diﬀerent sizes of the signals. It would not make a diﬀerence in terms
of comparing the 2H and HP-13C signal, because the sensitivity of the methods are so diﬀerent.
But it can give rise to inter-experimental variations. In ﬁgure 5.5 and 5.7 the shadows show the
standard deviation of the enzyme experiments. They are fairly tight indicating that experiments
have been performed similarly.
Key points
The experiments of the enzyme model system were successful and it was possible to measure the
HP-13C and 2H signals from the combined tracer. The sensitivity and temporal resolution were
high enough to measure the peaks and dynamics of the process within 3 minutes measurement
time with both methods at 11.2T. The model system is not comparable to physiological condition

Mathematical modelling of the reaction dynamics
The simplest model performed best when modelling the 13C and 2H signal respectively.
The 2H signal was modelled best by the ﬁrst order model. It gave the most robust and
interpretative estimates. The Michaelis-Menten models showed good results for the initial rate
constant, but the Km and Vmax were unlikely. A further experiment determining the Km should
be done to apply these models. It was not done in this project due to time limitations.
The 13C signal of the enzyme system was modelled best with the one-way model. It gave
stable results of the reaction rate. The residuals showed a tendency in the beginning of the
reaction though. There could be several causes of this. During the ﬁrst couple of seconds after
injection of pyruvate, the temperature stabilizes at 310K, and the two solutions are mixed. Adding
a temperature term or mixing term might improve the model. Other have done this by adding a
stochastic term to the diﬀerential equation and solving it with a Kalman ﬁlter [50]. For vi vivo
experiments a pyruvate infusion term have been added successfully, but was not considered
relevant for modelling the in vitro experiments of this project [10]. The one-way model showed
a very high R2 suspecting it to overﬁt to any data set given. But this was excluded, when the
model was trained on some of the data sets and tested on the last. The results of the test ﬁt were
good and reliable and did not show overﬁtting.
The modelling approaches of the HP 13C relied on the models presented by Daniels et al. [10].
They found their two-way model with equal apparent T1 values to perform best. Opposite their
results the two-way models performed badly when ﬁtting to the data collected in this project.
The enzyme reaction is forced to be a one-way reaction, but that should in theory just cause
the backwards reaction rate, Klp, to be zero using the two-way models. The experiment in this
project is performed diﬀerent and the way the data is collected is diﬀerent compared to Daniels
study. A striking diﬀerence is that they operate with a 40U LDH compared to the 1.25U in this

Combined tracer design rather than two separate tracers for clinical use
It is possible to do two experiments with two separate tracers labelled with 13C and 2H respectively
like Deja et al. and Yang et al. [13], [55]. Double labelling of the tracer demands implementing two
diﬀerent NMR experiments simultaneously. This becomes challenging in terms of pulse sequence
and the RF coils applied. Morze. et al made a comparison of HP 13C and non-HP 2H labelled
tracers for magnetic resonance imaging to study cerebral glucose metabolism. They applied a
4.7T magnet and two diﬀerent RF coils for the two tracers [44]. The perspective of combining
the two labellings in one molecule is to use it for MRS in the clinic. Having one injection and
one recording of the signal is preferable for the patient, the medical staﬀand from a economical
perspective.
However, in this project the 13C signal acquisition of the HP signal was performed ﬁrst
followed by the 2H signal acquisition. Diﬀerent from Morzes study, one combined tracer was
applied. Up until the signal acquisition step everything was performed similarly for both the 13C
and 2H experiments. It is possible to measure NMR signals from multiple nuclei simultaneously

and interleaved with a clinical scanner [42]. This will be one of the ﬁrst step that should be done

Why choose pyruvate as cerebral tracer?
Altered metabolism of the brain causing various brain disorder is a wide term including mito-
chondrial bioenergetic, oxidative stress, function of transporters, enzyme reactions and BBB
permeability. In the introduction an example of Alzheimer’s disease was given to emphasize the
usefulness of increasing the knowledge on the ﬁeld for an early diagnose and disease or treatment
monitoring. Determining a tracer for brain studies requires that it is non toxic and can cross the
BBB barrier. In a review by Mishowskovsky they summarize ﬁve molecules that live up to these
demands being glucose, acetate, lactate, pyruvate and 2-ketoisocaproic aicd [43]. Not all will be
discussed here.
As glucose is the main source of energy of the brain, it might come oﬀas the most intuitive
tracer. But all carbon nuclei in glucose are coupled directly to a proton resulting in T1 much shorter
compared to pyruvate. This limits the life time of the HP signal. Labelling with deuterium will
increase the T1 and this has been used in in human studies. Feyter et al. successfully performed
deuterium studies on humans with [6,6′ −2H2] glucose as tracer that is infused continuously
into the blood [11]. They were able to detect water, glucose, glumatamate and lactate. The
concentration of glutamate much larger than pyruvate in the brain, so the peak over pyruvate is
hidden the large glutamate signal. The combined tracer design suggested in this project could
further give information about the real-time dynamics by including the HP-13C labelling.
A drawback of applying pyruvate to gain dynamic information from in vivo studies is that it
can only be applied at supra-physiological concentrations. Kennedy et al. applied lactate as their
tracer when studying metabolism of the rat brain, because it can be used under physiological
concentrations [31]. The T1 of 2H on lactate was in my experiments measured to be 0.38s making
lactate a better choice for the thermal 2H NMR experiments. However, the T1 of C1 was measured
to be 22s which much lower than for pyruvate C1 of 50s. It is a compromise between the two
method. Here the longer life time of the HP 13C signal is preferred over a faster 2H signal
acquisitions.
Pyruvate is the most widely used molecule for HP 13C NMR spectroscopy and has successfully
and safely been used in human brain studies [47]. It can cross the BBB and has a fast uptake. It
is a small molecule with a long T1 which is desirable for hyperpolarized signal. Its conversion
to downstream metabolites is within the time frame of the HP signal. It is non-toxic and is
functioning at physiological pH and temperature. It can provide information about aerobic
metabolism by entering the TCA and anaerobic metabolism with the production of e.g. lactate.

CONCLUSION
7
A pyruvate tracer with combined 2H labelling in the methyl group and 13C labelling in the
carboxyl group was designed successfully. This design was chosen above the two other candidates
with labelling of the other carbon atoms of pyruvate. The qualities of the [1−13C1,U-2H3] pyruvate
design included having the longest T1 desired for hyperpolarization signals, creating no need for
decoupling and having the best spatial resolution at magnetic ﬁeld strength of clinical scanners.
It has the potential to show multiple metabolic routes and provide dynamic and steady-state
information of pyruvate metabolism. Two model systems were applied to evaluate the tracer
when combining dDNP experiment to measure the 13C signal and thermal 2D NMR to measure
the 2H signal at a magnetic ﬁeld of 11.2T.
The enzyme model system was developed with successful results. It presented a one-way
reaction with a full conversion of pyruvate to lactate catalyzed by LDH. The pyruvate and lactate
peaks were detectable from both the thermally polarized 2H signal and the hyperpolarized 13C
signal. Furthermore, the temporal resolution of both signals were high enough to measure the
dynamics of the process within three minutes. Multiple mathematical models were tested to get
the most robust and reliable estimate of the reaction rate. A simple ﬁrst-order model for the 2H
signal and a one-way ﬁrst-order kinetic model for the hyperpolarized 13C signal performed best.
They provided similar results of the reaction rate kpl, verifying comparable dynamics from the
diﬀerent signal acquisition methods.
The tracer was further evaluated in a model system of living neurons from mice. With the
hyperpolarized 13C signal it was possible to measure both dynamics and peaks from pyruvate,
lactate and bicarbonate originating from cellular activity within two minutes of scanning. This
result is very exciting since it shows that both aerobic and anaerobic metabolism can be studied
in neurons with the tracer. Higher concentrations of cells is needed to measure the dynamic
from the 2H signal. However, peaks of pyruvate and lactate were successfully detected over a
time period of 1.5 hours. The results from the neuron model system are great since they prove
that measuring steady-state metabolism was enabled by the 2H labelling and real-time dynamics
could be measured with the 13C signal from the same pyruvate tracer.
The feasibility of applying the design as a clinical tracer [1−13 C1,U-2H3] is promising. Pyru-
vate fulﬁlls all requirements of a clinical tracer for cerebral applications. Deuterium magnetic
resonance spectroscopy has been used in human studies with a glucose tracer and so has the
hyperpolarized 13 with a C1 labelled pyruvate tracer [11], [19]. Combining the labellings in one
tracer will limit the number of injection in the patient. It will create more comparable measure-
ment with the two methods which is attractable for modelling the reaction rate of metabolic
ﬂuxes. Simultaneous or interleaved NMR signal acquisition will be needed for the application in

B.2.6
Enzyme Experiment B: Optimal 2H NMR settings
In the desired enzyme experiment all the pyruvate should be converted to lactate. Figure B.10
shows the 2H signal of pyruvate in blue and lactate in green. The blue curve should thereby start
at the same signal intensity as the green ends. In ﬁgure B.10 (A) with TR = 5 s a suppression of
the pyruvate signal is seen. In ﬁgure B.10 (B) the TR is increased to 17 s and α is decreased to
25◦. Decreasing α and most importantly increasing T R secured that the pyruvate signal was
not suppressed. The trade-oﬀwas a decreased number of data points, decreased in temporal
resolution and slight decrease in signal intensity.

PRÆPARATION AF CEREBELLARE NEURONER 
 
Præparation af cerebellare neuroner fra 7/8 dage gamle mus. Bestil mus senest mandag ugen før til 
ankomst onsdag. Musene fra Janvier ankommer typisk mellem 10-12 om onsdagen.  
 
FORBEREDELSE – dagen før 
 
Tjek at der er Krebs buffer og KG-DMEM inden forberedelserne går i gang. Dagen inden musene ankommer 
laves opløsning 1-4 og KG-DMEM + kainsyre + 10 % serum som beskrevet i skema i lab: 
Opløsning 1-4: 
1. 80 mL Krebs buffer (celle køl) 
0,24 g BSA (køl 3) 
0,62 mL 150 mM MgSO4-opl. (celle køl/fryser) 
Anbring BSA pulveret ovenpå Krebs bufferen uden omrøring; det opløses af sig selv 
2. 20 mL opl. 1 
100 µL DNAse-opl. (frys v. døren) 
5 mg trypsin (Sigma beholder, frys v. døren) 
3. 20 mL opl. 1 
100 µL DNAse-opl. (frys v. døren) 
10,4 mg SBTI (trypsin inhib.) (køl 3) 
0,2 mL 150 mM MgSO4-opl. (celle køl/fryser) 
4. 16,8 mL opl. 1 
3,2 mL opl. 3 
Dvs det er en fortynding af opl. 3 i opl. 1 
 
Disse opløsninger laves i alm. lab i målebægere og tages derefter med ind i celle lab, hvor de sterilfiltreres 
ned i 50 mL centrifugerør vha sprøjte og filtrer. Husk at mærke rørene. 
Det samme filter kan bruges til alle 4 opløsninger hvis de filtreres i rækkefølgen 1-2-4-3.
