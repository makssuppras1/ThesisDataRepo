Abstract
Bilateral contracts are a central risk-hedging instrument in modern electricity markets,
and long-term Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) are increasingly critical for securing
renewable energy investments. Yet their negotiation is complex, involving the alloca-
tion of price, volume, and risks. This thesis develops a cooperative game-theoretic
framework to model bilateral PPA negotiations between a renewable generator and a
consumer, using Nash bargaining theory to determine Pareto-efficient strike prices and
contract volumes for both baseload and pay-as-produced (PAP) structures. The frame-
work extends existing models by incorporating stochastic production, price volatility,
capture rate effects, Conditional Value-at-Risk–based risk aversion, bargaining power
asymmetries, and asymmetry of information.
Monte Carlo simulations of prices and production underpin a synthetic case study of
a 30 MW wind farm negotiating with industrial consumers in Denmark’s DK-2 market
(2020–2025 data).
Results show that Nash bargaining solutions deviate systemati-
cally from expected-price benchmarks, as risk aversion drives strike prices towards the
least risk-averse party in negotiations, making the agreement proportionally fair. The
bargaining outcomes highlight the difference in risk allocation between baseload and
pay-as-produced contracts by showcasing different sizes in bargaining sets in different
risk aversion combinations. Under symmetric expectations, price changes are the pri-
mary driver of baseload strike prices, while capture rate changes dominate PAP pricing.
Under asymmetric expectations, the strike-price variation increases substantially, am-
plifying the impact of information divergence when negotiating. Additionally, the party
with greater risk aversion experiences that the lesser risk-averse player’s expectation
dictates a negotiation.
The thesis makes four contributions: (1) extending barter-set theory to stochastic pro-
duction and capture rates in baseload PPAs; (2) the first formal Nash-bargaining formu-
lation for PAP contracts; (3) integrating risk aversion, bargaining power, and information
asymmetry into a unified stochastic optimization model; and (4) conducting elasticity-
based sensitivity analysis across key market parameters. These results provide both
theoretical advances in cooperative bargaining under uncertainty and practical guidance
for structuring equitable PPAs in renewable energy markets.

Introduction
1.1
Context and motivation: Merchant Exposure of VRES in
Electricity Markets
The renewable energy market in Denmark has and is undergoing a substantial transfor-
mation, notably by attempting to shift [1] away from traditional government-supported
support schemes, such as feed-in tariffs and contracts for difference (CfDs), and in-
creasingly embracing full merchant exposure. This evolution places significant reliance
on market revenues and long-term Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs), pivotal for
securing financing and mitigating investment risk. Without these former state support
mechanisms, renewable energy projects must now secure income through market rev-
enues or corporate PPAs to maintain economic viability. Such agreements provide es-
sential financial stability, enabling developers to attract funding from banks by guaran-
teeing stable revenue streams over extended periods [2].
Figure 1.1: Expected growth of renewable energy in Europe [3]
In the day-ahead market, the increased penetration of variable renewable energy sources
(VRES), particularly wind and solar, has introduced significant challenges. These chal-
lenges won’t dissipate in the future with an expected growth of 2.3 times the renewable
energy production in Europe between 2023 and 2030 1.1. Price cannibalization due to
VRES growth is becoming increasingly evident, whereby renewable producers depress
market prices during periods of high generation. The capture prices for wind energy
specifically in Denmark (DK1 and DK2) show a widening gap compared to baseload
prices, i.e., average electricity price, reflecting this price cannibalization [3]. Capture

electricity prices. Furthermore, as renewables often, cannot schedule their production
and rely on the weather, the production average electricity price, capture price, will dif-

1.2
Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs): Challenges and
Opportunities
PPAs are bilateral contracts between two parties, typically the buyer (the consumer)
and the seller (the electricity producer), wherein the price of electricity is locked in
and hedged for both parties in tenors of 5-20 years. Electricity generators enter into
such agreements to ensure stable revenue streams, rather than depending on the of-
ten volatile wholesale electricity market. This is especially important for renewable en-
ergy generators whose revenue is subject to fluctuations in both production and mar-
ket prices. Revenue uncertainty significantly impacts bankability for renewable energy
projects which are characterized by their high capital expenditures (CAPEX). Thus,
PPAs serve as a critical mechanism for securing financing and driving additionality in
new renewable energy development [2]. On the other side, the buyer’s, PPAs act as
a hedge against rising electricity prices and serve as a tool to obtain renewable en-
ergy certificates [7]. These certificates enable consumers to claim the use of renewable
electricity in their carbon footprint which is often linked to environmental, social, and gov-
ernance (ESG) goals. In recent years, PPAs have gained traction among corporations
and heavy industries as a price hedging instrument and to meet sustainability targets
[5].
Following the energy crisis triggered by post-pandemic demand shocks and geopoliti-
cal tensions, particularly the Russian invasion of Ukraine, there has been a global re-
assessment of energy security. The European Commission responded by launching
the REPowerEU plan to eliminate the EU’s dependence on Russian fossil fuels before
2030 [8]. A key measure within this plan has been accelerating and streamlining au-
thorization processes for PPAs, aiming to remove bureaucratic barriers and support the
deployment of renewable energy. As such, PPAs are increasingly recognized as corpo-
rate procurement tools and as instruments of public energy policy in enhancing supply
and fostering investment in RES [8].
Several PPA structures have been developed in order to satisfy different risk profiles
and goals between seller and buyers [9, 10], and further innovation is at the same time
happening with the acceleration of integration of BESS projects seen across Europe
[5]. The two most common types of PPAs are denoted as the ’Pay as Produced’ (PaP)
and ’Baseload’ (BL), and will be the focus of this thesis. PaP denotes a scheme in

PPA Negotiation
Settling on the pricing scheme of Power Purchase Agreements (PPAs) constitutes a
critical aspect, directly influencing their attractiveness and feasibility for both energy
producers and consumers, and is a lengthy process that typically takes 2-12 months
[10]. The pricing mechanisms used in PPAs reflect the broader market conditions, fu-
ture expected market dynamics, and risk considerations inherent in renewable energy
projects.
In general, a fixed-price arrangement is established in which electricity is purchased at a
constant price over the contract duration, providing certainty and enabling risk manage-
ment for both parties. There does, however, exist a variety of alternative pricing struc-
tures, including inflation indexing, hybrid structures, and cap-and-floor mechanisms, de-
signed to align further pricing within the risk appetite of both buyer and seller [9][10]. The
thesis focuses solely on a fixed price settlement scheme where a price is settled upon
and will remain fixed across the whole duration of the tenor.
Strike Price Negotiation
Settling on the strike price is a non-trivial part of the PPA negotiation, and often requires
several rounds of back-and-forth bids between both parties for these to be satisfied. The
parties will bring their expectations about the market outlook, including future electricity
prices, as well as forecasts concerning expected production and consumption. For the
price forecasts, the forward curve serves as a general benchmark. The forward curve
represents the market’s expectations of future electricity prices and is a key input in the
pricing and valuation of PPAs. Derived from traded futures or bilateral forwards, these
curves indicate what the market would pay for electricity in future delivery periods. While
estimating accurate forward curves is a complex process in itself, it falls outside the
scope of this thesis.
Contract Amount Negotiation
The contract volume usually aligns with the expected production capacity of a given
plant. Nevertheless, even with accurate production forecasts, negotiation participants
remain concerned not only with expected net earnings but also with risk, the potential for
actual earnings to deviate adversely from expectations. Thus, negotiations inherently
aim to balance expected profitability against acceptable risk levels, tailored to each par-
ticipant’s risk preferences.
This naturally leads to the question of how the division of risks and benefits in PPA
negotiations can be represented in a formal model, a gap that Nash bargaining theory
is well-suited to address in the context of PPA negotiations.

1.3
Research Questions
Building on the identified challenges and opportunities, this thesis aims to answer the
following research question:
How to predict an (equitable/fair) negotiation outcome in pricing and contract volume in
PPAs?
To address this research question, we focus on developing an analytical framework
for modeling PPA negotiations. Providing realistic representations of the stakeholders
and solution methods to compute the resulting negotiated contract prices and volumes,
assuming both are seeking a successful negotiation. In particular, we aim to answer the
following sub-questions:
1. How can a ’fair’ strike price and contract volume for Baseload and Pay as Pro-
duced PPAs be determined?
2. In what ways do differences in risk aversion and bargaining power of the generator
and load influence the negotiated contract outcomes?
3. How do key stochastic parameters, such as production variability, price expec-
tations, and capture rates, affect the negotiated contract outcomes under both
symmetric and asymmetric information settings?

1.4.1
PPA Design and Pricing: Limitations of Existing Models
Methods in the literature for estimating optimal PPAs include: Gabrielli, Aboutalebi, and
Sansavini [11] is an investment decision problem optimizing the installed capacity of a
portfolio of geographically and technologically diverse assets formulated as a two-stage
stochastic program. The model maximises expected cash-flow from the PPAs while
minimising downside exposure via Conditional Value-at-Risk, tracing an efficient frontier
of risk–return trade-offs. Their case study shows that diversification across solar and
wind can cut financial risk with only a fractional loss in expected returns. In Mendicino
et al. [12] the authors formulate using an extensive expression of the LCOE combining
both renewable-generation costs and the corporate off-taker’s “green-branding” benefit
as a methodology of determining the strike price and contractual length.
These models provide a starting point, but they lack an actionable model that represents
how the negotiation process involves stakeholders with specific preferences and strate-
gic decision-making that deviates from a well-intentioned model. In particular, Gabrielli,
Aboutalebi, and Sansavini [11] assumes a strike price has been agreed upon, and Men-
dicino et al. [12] assumes a predetermined fraction of production has been agreed upon
that will go to the PPA, while also assuming that using LCOE models is a method to
determine a fair price between both parties.

1.4.2
Modeling Strategic Decision-Making
One framework for analyzing strategic decision-making and predicting outcomes of situ-
ations between parties is game theory, often synonymously associated with John Nash’s
’Nash Equilibrium’ in non-cooperative game theory. It is a mathematical approach to an-
alyzing these systems and quantifying the impact of a decision maker’s actions, while
also considering how one’s own decision affects that of another decision maker. It can
be explained more plainly as follows: Game theory is a theory of investigating the mutual
influence of strategies [13]. While Nash’s popularization of the Nash Equilibrium was
introduced in 1951 [14], the general formal definition of game theory was given by John
von Neumann and Oskar Morgenstern in 1944, who also introduced the concept of co-
operative game theory, which was also detailed in Nash’s first paper [15]. The primary
distinction between cooperative and non-cooperative game theory lies in agreements
and enforcement mechanisms in resource allocation problems.
In cooperative games, players can form binding contracts and coalitions, with enforce-
able agreements that focus on distributing gains through cooperation among the partic-
ipants. This approach is beneficial for analyzing negotiations, coalition formation, and
issues such as fair surplus allocation [13]. An industry that has researched the imple-
mentation of cooperative game theory extensively is telecommunications, in allocating
flows within the limited bandwidth accessible [16]. Cooperative theory emphasizes col-
laboration and negotiated outcomes, making it an interesting framework to utilize within
PPAs. A methodology utilizing cooperative game theory is the Nash Bargaining Solution
proposed in Nash’s first seminal paper Nash [15].
Cooperative Game Theory in Energy Systems
Cooperative game theory also has its applications in energy markets, which have grown
substantially in size and complexity due to market liberalizations and the increasing
complexity between agents acting with game-theoretic approaches. Recent research
has applied both cooperative and non-cooperative frameworks to energy trading prob-
lems. Tushar et al. [17] show that a cooperative approach in peer-to-peer energy trading
can achieve better outcomes compared to traditional non-cooperative models. Their
analysis shows that cooperation among distributed energy sources allows better uti-
lization of their resources and higher social welfare (system efficiency). Similarly, Fan,
Ai, and Piao [18] presents a Nash bargaining cooperative framework for energy trading
between demand response aggregators and distribution companies, showing that co-
operative bargaining enables joint determination of trade volumes and payments while
maximizing social welfare. The increase in utility using cooperative approaches is also
supported by Zhang et al. [19] who model an integrated electricity-heat-hydrogen sys-
tem using Nash bargaining theory. Their results demonstrate that cooperative bargain-
ing strategies achieve higher economic benefits and resource utilization compared to
non-cooperative equivalents. Hu et al. [20] restructures an energy trading model of
an aggregator of several smaller renewable assets and flexible loads as an asymmetric
Nash-bargaining game, posing the real￿world fact that parties do not contribute equally.
Bargaining power is tied to their individual contribution to the value function, as such

sign both enlarges the total cooperation surplus and provides a higher allocation to the
most helpful participants, showing how asymmetric bargaining can deliver efficiency and

Cooperative Game Theory in PPAs
For bilateral PPA negotiations, cooperative game theory and especially Nash bargain-
ing theory offers a suitable framework. Yu, Tesfatsion, and Liu [21] establish the first
direct application of Nash bargaining to financial bilateral contracts in wholesale elec-
tricity markets. Yu, Tesfatsion, and Liu [21] presents a Nash bargaining model for ne-
gotiating bilateral contracts between a deterministic baseload producing generator and
a load-serving entity, incorporating risk preferences through Conditional Value at Risk
and information asymmetries through bias in the expectation of prices. They formalize
the ”Barter Set,” which is the set of feasible contract outcomes, laying the theoretical
foundation upon which this thesis builds. Their work shows how negotiated outcomes
systematically depend on relative risk aversion and biases in price expectations.
More contemporary work has focused on including flexibility in Nash bargaining. Fina,
Heidrich, and Krogstie [22] focuses on energy communities with photovoltaic genera-
tion and battery systems, showing how Nash bargaining can determine optimal fixed
prices while accounting for production uncertainty and the addition of risk components
in expectation. Their framework combines long-term production forecasting with histor-
ical price data to optimize both buyer revenues and costs utilizing BESS in only fulfilling
contractual obligations.
Kandpal, Backe, and Granado [23] develops a framework for negotiating 24/7 PPAs that
incorporates demand flexibility through battery storage. Their approach combines Nash
bargaining theory with machine learning methods, demonstrating how demand flexibility
can reduce strike prices while enhancing demand-generation matching.
Chen and Hou [24] develops a Nash bargaining solution in which several buyers bargain
with a developer in signing a PPA. They model the bargaining process as a separate
negotiation, agent-based negotiation, and joint negotiation, deriving closed form solution
for the contract price,size and welfare split. Their simulations show that equal bargaining
power and aligned risk preferences are critical for collective deals to be successful.
Garcia et al. [25] and Garcia et al. [26] proposes an alternative cooperative bargaining
method in bilateral contracts between a risk neutral seller and buyer using the Raiffa-
Kalai-Smorodinsky Bargaining Solution. The approach is compared to the Nash Bar-
gaining Solution, showcasing that by changing the axiom of ’Independence of Irrelevant
Alternatives’ used in Nash bargaining to the monotonicity axiom improves revenues for
both players considering a risk-neutral utility function.
In summary, cooperative game theory, and in particular Nash bargaining, provides a
promising mathematical framework to model PPA negotiations, providing an analyti-
cal solution method to derive fair and mutually beneficial contract outcomes based on

Contributions
This thesis makes the following contributions to the modeling and analysis of Power
Purchase Agreement (PPA) negotiations using cooperative game theory:
1. Extension of barter set frame to stochastic production and capture rates in
baseload PPAs: Building upon the deterministic production formulation of Yu,
Tesfatsion, and Liu [21], a stochastic production, price, and capture rate formu-
lation is made of the barter set considering a modified risk-return function. This
allows the Nash bargaining solution to explicitly account for production variability,
price cannibalization effects, and risk aversion.
2. Formalization of the barter set for pay as produced contracts: While PAP
structures are historically the most prevalent corporate PPA type in practice, they
have not previously been modeled within a Nash bargaining framework.
The
barter set, viable strike price bounds, and the conditions of the PAP considering
stochastic production and prices are derived.
3. Integration of risk aversion, bargaining power, and asymmetric information
into a stochastic optimization model: A mathematical framework combining
Nash bargaining, risk-return utility functions, and asymmetry of information is de-
veloped. This framework captures the joint effects of risk aversion, negotiation
power asymmetry, and biased expectations on negotiated strike prices and con-
tract volumes.

the elasticity of optimal contract terms with respect to changes in price levels, pro-
duction, consumption, and capture rates under both symmetric and asymmetric
expectations. This analysis reveals which parameters most influence bargain-
ing outcomes, providing insights into how parameters affect contract design and
change negotiation strategies.
Collectively, these contributions advance the application of cooperative game theory in

stochastic, risk-aware formulation for prevalent PPA structures, and 3) linking theoretical
negotiation outcomes to practical sensitivities relevant for industry decision-making.

Review of the Power Purchase
Agreement Market
With the power market in recent years involving significantly more renewable energy
generation, price cannibalization has become a significant issue in Europe, affecting not
only assets operating purely in the merchant market but also the pricing of PPAs. pay as
produced contracts are priced lower as the capture rate declines since the buyer’s hedge
requirements diminish. For the Baseload contract, the seller’s risk profile worsens with
declining capture rates and increasing volatility. This occurs because the hours in which
the seller must buy back missing production are generally also those with high electricity
prices, given that renewable energy production is lower [28].
The European Power Purchase Agreement market has developed significantly over the
last decade with the rapid expansion of renewable energy leading to an increasingly
complex environment of new structures and mechanisms that meet the risk needs of
both buyers and sellers [5]. This chapter focuses on describing the content of PPAs and
also their ongoing evolution. These changes ultimately impact how PPAs are negotiated.
The beginning will focus on the elements of PPAs and the ongoing developments in
the industry, including new structures and environmental aspects. Then, the industry
methodology for valuing a PPA, considering the inherent risks associated with it and the
general negotiation procedure between a seller and an off-taker, is explained, providing
context for the usage of cooperative game theory in later chapters.
2.1
Overview of the standard PPA structures
PPAs can be structured in several ways according to various attributes, the key ones
being: generation technology type, physicality of delivery, pricing structure, tenor, and
the counterparty (off-taker). Other attributes include balancing, energy certificates, in-
surance and the commencement of delivery (COD) of the contract [10, 29].
In general, PPAs can be split into two categories: Physical PPAs and Financial/Virtual
PPAs [2]. Physical PPAs refer to the production that is delivered to the offtaker. It can
be split into two further categories: on-site and off-site. On-site projects are smaller
projects, such as PV on rooftops, where the point of connection between generation
and consumption occurs at the same location. If the PPA is off-site, the generation
is located at some physical distance removed from the consumption and needs to be
transmitted across transmission lines. Generally, the PPA also requires that the contract
take place within the same geographic area. In order for the off-taker to ’attain’ the
produced generation from the off-site PPA, the PPA needs to be ’sleeved’ by signing
a contract with an energy provider who buys the production on the market and sells
it back to the offtaker, and then balance potential deficits or surpluses that happen in
the market [10]. A virtual PPA, on the other hand, is a pure financial product where

==PAGE:26==
there is no physical delivery of electricity, and can also be cross-border [10]. The PPA
functions as a ’Contract for Difference’, where parties agree on a fixed strike price. The
energy produced from the project is sold on the wholesale market, and depending on the
difference between the agreed-upon strike price and the market price, cash flows are
exchanged between both parties. A virtual PPA enables the offtaker to obtain energy
certificates, similar to physical ones, but from different projects across borders. The
virtual PPA serves as a price hedge for the seller and a source of certificates for the
buyer[8]. A key contention in negotiating such a contract is determining which wholesale
price to use when both parties are not located within the same geographical region.
Still, delinking the physicality of delivery from production increases the potential pool of
offtakers, which in turn helps the seller in achieving financial close [30]. The offtaker, or
counterparty, in most PPA negotiations can be split into two categories: Corporations
& Industries and utilities, often also called corporate or utility PPA. The main purpose
of the utilty PPA is hedging price volatility, whereas the corporate focus is more on the
energy certificates for their environmental goals. Utility PPAs were previously the main
offtaker, but with the advent of more ambitious carbon reduction goals, corporations
are becoming the primary counterparty. Previously, corporate offtakers were only one
entity ie one company negotiating a PPA for themselves, however multi-buyer PPAs are
emerging [2][5]. These can include corporations with lower overall energy demand and
lower credit ratings. They either sign separate PPAs with the same renewable project
or buy in as a conglomerate. This way, smaller companies lacking the conventionally
required collateral can participate in the PPA market [10].
The technology type of a PPA constitutes a fundamental design element, as it largely
determines both production stability and generation profile. Traditionally, PPAs are cat-
egorized by a single technology type, such as Solar PPAs, Wind PPAs, or biomass
and hydropower. However, with the rise of price cannibalization in electricity markets,
multi-technology PPAs have emerged as a means of mitigating revenue volatility [31].
By combining multiple technologies into a generation portfolio, the resulting production
profile can be shaped to more closely align with the buyer’s consumption pattern [10].
2.1.1
Delivery Schemes
The delivery scheme specifies both the contract’s delivery schedule and the correspond-
ing electricity volume. The most common and straightforward arrangement is the pay
as produced (PAP) scheme, in which a predetermined fraction of generation is delivered
as it is produced and paid for at a fixed price. Under PAP, the buyer bears the majority
of the risk: if the seller’s production does not meet consumption needs, the buyer must
procure the shortfall at the prevailing market price. Conversely, if production exceeds
consumption, the buyer is still obliged to purchase the excess and resell it at market
prices [10]. When production occurs during hours with market prices below the agreed
rate, the buyer effectively pays a premium for electricity. A sub-variant, the As-Forecast
scheme, requires the buyer to purchase a forecasted quantity determined before gen-
eration and communicated in advance. This arrangement allows the buyer to adapt
operational processes to the expected delivery, potentially improving efficiency through

Another common delivery scheme is the Baseload (BL) contract, in which a fixed con-
tract volume is agreed upon and must be supplied every hour. If the seller fails to meet
this obligation, they must procure the shortfall at the prevailing market price. While
baseload contracts are often specified on an annual basis, variants exist that define
volumes on a monthly scale to accommodate seasonal variations in consumption. A
related variant is the Shaped PPA, where the seller delivers a predefined hourly volume
profile rather than a constant amount. This structure shifts risk toward the seller, who
must match the agreed shape and buy back any shortfall at market prices. However, de-
pending on the specific profile, the buyer may also bear significant risk [10]. In contrast,
the Pay-as-Consumed scheme operates as the inverse of the pay as produced model.
Here, the buyer purchases only the amount of electricity they consume, irrespective of
the seller’s generation. This arrangement requires the seller to balance generation with
consumption, a task typically feasible only when supported by a diversified portfolio of
generation assets. Consequently, the Pay-as-Consumed scheme places the majority
of balancing and market risk on the seller [10].
2.1.2
Pricing Structure
The pricing structure refers to how the electricity is priced through the contract. The pric-
ing structure can be categorized into fixed or variable electricity prices over the tenor.
The fixed price is the simplest structure, where the price remains fixed for the entire
duration of the contract. A variation of the fixed-price ’escalation’ PPA exists, where
the initial price either increases or decreases in response to inflation or a fixed percent-
age [10]. By adjusting to the nominal price, the developers are positively affected as it
benefits their bankability, where net present value (NPV) matters substantially.
The other scheme, ’Variable Pricing’, tracks and follows the market price, though often
at a discount. By following the market price, the seller might benefit if prices are rising,
but also be at risk if prices fall. Variable pricing schemes can include a price ’floor’, ’cap’
or a ’collar’ (both combined). The floor guarantees that the price cannot fall below a
specific value; conversely, a ’cap’ ensures that the price cannot exceed a value, provid-

ing security within the range of what the PPA price might be. This structure functions
much like a CfD. A ’reverse’ collar operates the opposite way, if the price is between the
strike price and the floor, then the buyer has to remunerate the seller for the difference.
Conversely, if the price is between strike and cap, the seller has to pay the buyer [10].
Variable pricing is less common for corporate PPAs and is instead meant for buyers with
higher electricity operating costs. Pricing structures with caps, floors, collars, and claw-
back arrangements are less common for corporate PPAs than their fixed counterpart
and provide less price certainty, which might be important in securing financing for a
new renewable energy asset [9].
2.1.3
Duration and Commencement Date
A PPA can be signed for both short-term periods of 2–5 years or long-term periods of
5–20 years. Generally, shorter PPAs command higher prices due to increased exposure
to price and volume risks. Long-term PPAs, by contrast, are often pursued to secure
project financing, as stable and predictable revenue streams are essential for obtaining
investment capital. In this context, the seller bears the implicit burden of securing a PPA
to ensure bankability. In contrast, the offtaker typically has less need to lock in costs
two decades in advance. While short-term markets can be volatile and uncertain, these
risks are mitigated through long-term contracts, which also reduce the relative financial
impact of a single poor weather year. For these reasons, most PPAs used to finance
new projects fall within the 10–20 year range [10]. The Commencement Date (COD)
marks the start of the contract, typically aligning with the beginning of production.
2.1.4
Insurance and Penalties
Insurance
Insurance requirements are a standard feature in PPAs, designed to safeguard both the
seller and the off-taker against unforeseen events that could impact performance and
financial stability. PPAs can feature different forms of insurance to cover unforeseen
events, such as damages to the renewable asset [10].
Penalties
PPA contracts frequently incorporate penalty clauses to address situations where the
seller fails to deliver the contracted amount of energy [12]. Such penalties may be
enforced regardless of whether the shortfall arises from curtailment by the transmission
system operator (TSO) and must therefore be considered during contract structuring
[32]. Additional penalties can apply if the agreed Commencement Date (COD) is not
met due to project delays [9].
In this context, force majeure clauses are critical for addressing events beyond the rea-
sonable control of the parties, such as extreme weather events or armed conflict, that
may prevent fulfillment of contractual obligations.
2.1.5
Trading of PPAs
After a PPA is signed, the offtaker or asset owner typically needs to hedge the price ex-
posure using traded contracts on an exchange such as the European Energy Exchange
(EEX) or Nord Pool. However, there is no liquid market for contracts extending 10 years

or more. EEX has started a 10-year future market with increasing liquidity [33]. Most
trading volume, and therefore liquidity, is concentrated in contracts with terms of 1 to 3
years ahead. A market is considered liquid if large trades can be made without signifi-
cantly moving the price, and if it is easy to find a buyer or seller. In contrast, an illiquid
market means that even relatively small trades can move the price, and executing a
large trade may require paying a premium due to limited participation. To manage this
limitation, traders use a strategy known as stack-and-roll. It involves hedging the total
volume (exposure) by stacking the entirety into blocks and then continuously rolling the
remainder into the following year. For instance, a 20 MW PPA with a 10-year term cor-
responds to a total exposure of 200 MW in one-year contract equivalents. In practice,
it may only be feasible to hedge up to two years ahead, which would involve splitting
the exposure into two 100 MW blocks using futures. As delivery commences for the
first block, part of the volume (e.g., 20 MW) is delivered, while the remaining exposure
(80 MW) is rolled forward into year 3, establishing a new hedge for the smaller residual
volume. This rolling process is repeated annually until year 10, when the final exposure
is delivered. This approach is illustrated in Figure 2.2.

The difference between a forward and a future lies in the way they are traded. For-
wards are privately negotiated over-the-counter (OTC) contracts between two parties,
allowing for flexible terms. Futures are standardized contracts traded on exchanges,
such as EEX, with the exchange acting as the counterparty and requiring daily margin
settlement. Despite these differences, both instruments are used to hedge future price
risks and are settled based on the difference between the contract price and the market
price at the time of delivery or maturity.
While stack-and-roll is a practical solution to hedging long-term exposure in short-term
markets, it is not without limitations. The strategy assumes that the prices of short-term
assets (the liquid part) will move in line with the longer-term (illiquid parts) of the forward
curve. In reality, this is rarely the case. The short end of the power forward curve
tends to be more volatile and reacts more strongly to market events, such as weather or

of supply/demand, policy goals, and the trajectory of future costs. As a result, the two
segments of the curve are not perfectly correlated [34].
This mismatch introduces what is known as basis risk, meaning that the changes in the
value of the hedge might not fully match the changes in the value of the actual PPA
exposure. Because short-term contracts tend to fluctuate more, the volume hedged
may need to be adjusted to avoid covering too much or too little risk. Additionally, every
time the hedge is extended into a new year, practical costs are incurred, such as less
favorable pricing or fewer available trading options. These factors, taken together, mean
that stack-and-roll cannot provide a perfect hedge throughout the entire lifetime of a
long-term PPA.
2.1.6
Emerging Structures
With the challenges of price volatility, cannibalization risk, and intermittent renewable
production outlined in the introduction, a range of innovative ’hybrid’ PPA structures
have emerged in recent years to address these market needs [28]. In general, a ’hybrid’
PPA is an umbrella term for co-locating the asset with storage or combining with other
generation technologies. Mixed technology PPAs all share the strength of more flexiblity
in power deliveries, which results in delivery profiles that are more suitable for price
hedging [5]. A study by Pexapark and Eurelectric found that an optimal mix of wind and
solar in a PPA can lead to an hourly matching of 60% to 70% for a baseload profile.
At the same time, this availability can approach 100% when battery storage is also
included in the project [35]. The interest in hybrid PPA profiles can be reflected in the
volumes of mixed-technology PPAs, which saw a 219% increase in volumes in 2024
compared to 2023 [5]. Another trend in PPAs includes multi-buyer PPAs, where buyers
are aggregated to include corporations with smaller energy demands. By aggregating
demand of smaller companies, it opens up further demand and alleviates the credit risk
smaller corporations might have.
While this thesis does not explicitly analyze these new structures, their increasing adop-
tion indicates a shift in what is becoming important in PPA negotiations.
Shaped PPA
A Shaped PPA is a power purchase agreement where the seller delivers a predefined
delivery profile that mimics the offtaker’s consumption pattern, such as a Fixed-Hourly
Profile or Baseload structure [28]. In a hybrid setting, the technologies of RES and
battery is combined in-order to fulfill that shape and is paid as a pay as produced. Pricing
can be variable, depending on the hours delivered, and may also vary by month. While
the payment structure is PAP, if delivery is missed, the missing position needs to be
settled at market price. As such the profile and volume risk is assumed by the seller, but
can use its storage to shape output accordingly, and in return receive a price premium
[28].
Blended PPA
Another hybrid structure is the ’blended’ renewables and storage PPA, where the renew-
able and storage components operate under the same pay as produced (PAP) contract.

In contrast to shaped delivery, this structure allows the buyer to optimize the whole
system operation, including determining the charge and discharge schedules over the
PPA’s duration [28]. It provides the buyer a good amount of flexibility in choosing when
they want energy. Still, it puts the seller/optimizer at risk of potential opportunity costs

24/7 PPA
The ’24/7’ structure is the ultimate ’pay as consumed’ model, where the goal is to match

model not only enhances the effectiveness of PPAs as a price hedging tool but also
supports corporate decarbonization goals and provides stronger claims for renewable
energy consumption reporting [35, 5]. This method also puts the greatest risk on the
seller, and in return, a higher price premium.
2.1.7
Environmental and Regulation
While previously PPAs were signed by utilities and a few corporations as price hedges
and to address certain long-term energy costs, Environmental, Social, and Governance
(ESG) goals have become an increasingly important factor in the uptake of corporate
PPAs[36]. With the introduction of RE100 in 2014, a group of some of the world’s largest
companies has pledged to meet 100% of their energy requirements from renewable
sources. The global focus on ESG reporting has made PPAs a key part of helping cor-
porations prove their sustainability[36]. In the future, by 2029, due to the Corporate
Sustainability Reporting Directive (CSRD), 50.000 companies within the EU will be re-
quired to report on the origins of their energy usage [37].
Current Framework
Carbon accounting for corporations follows the GHG protocol [38], where corporations
account for their Scope 2 emissions. Scope 2 emissions refer to the emissions result-

based’ and ’market-based’ emissions. In location-based emissions, the consumption of
electricity is multiplied by the grid average emission factor, and in market-based emis-
sions, the emissions are directly related to the procurement of electricity [38]. Under

offset emissions by purchasing energy certificates. In Europe, these energy certificates

bundled with a PPA Lastly, one can use a ’supplier’ emission factor, relying on the fact
that it is a utility with a high share of renewables.
Guarantees of Origin (GOs) constitute part of the revenue provided through a PPA,
as they are certificates that can be bought as proof of renewable energy production,
and it is these specific certificates that allow utilities and corporations to prove what

2.3
PPA Negotiation Process
PPA negotiations represent complex bilateral contracting processes characterized by
substantial customization requirements, extended negotiation periods, and significant
economic stakes. Unlike standardized commodity transactions, these agreements re-
quire extensive bilateral coordination to align generator revenue requirements with off-
taker risk tolerance and renewable energy procurement objectives [10]. This negotia-
tion complexity, combined with the strategic interdependence between parties seeking
mutually beneficial outcomes, makes cooperative game theory a suitable model of mod-
eling.
2.3.1
PPA Negotiation and Cooperative Game Theory
The structural characteristics of PPA negotiations align with cooperative game theory’s
foundational assumptions, making Nash bargaining an optimal analytical framework.
First, PPAs constitute legally enforceable long-term contracts with substantial penalty
mechanisms for non-performance, satisfying cooperative game theory’s binding agree-
ment requirement [15]. Contract enforcement mechanisms distinguish PPAs from in-
formal agreements or spot market transactions, where commitment credibility is less
a concern. Second, PPA negotiations create positive-sum outcomes through risk re-
allocation rather than zero-sum value extraction. Generators obtain revenue certainty,
enabling project financing, while offtakers achieve price stability and renewable energy
compliance [18]. This mutual benefit structure ensures negotiations focus on surplus

division rather than potential competitive value destruction. Third, unlike anonymous
market mechanisms, PPA negotiations involve extensive bilateral communication, due
diligence processes, and information sharing regarding production forecasts, financial
positions, and operational constraints [10]. This direct communication facilitates the
complete information assumption underlying Nash bargaining theory.

Nash Bargaining Solution Concept
Among cooperative solution concepts, Nash bargaining provides the most appropriate
framework due to its foundations in aiming at delivering a proportional fair solution to

3.1
Fundamental Game-Theoretic Concepts
Game theory provides a mathematical framework for analyzing strategic interactions
between rational decision-makers [13, 44]. We begin by establishing the components
that characterize any strategic interaction:
• Players: Decision-making entities seeking to optimize their objectives
• Strategies: The set of feasible actions available to each player
• Payoffs: Functions mapping strategy combinations to numerical outcomes
• Information Structure: Each player’s knowledge about strategies and payoffs
• Order of play: The sequence in which decisions are made
These components must satisfy certain conditions for game-theoretic analysis [13]:
• Finite number of players (n ≥2).
• Every player has a finite set of strategies.
• Interdependent payoffs, each player’s outcome depends on all players’ choices
• Common knowledge of the game structure, knowing strategies of other players.
• Decision making is driven by rationality in the players’ attempts to maximize their
own gains while also considering their interests. The payoff from a strategy must
be numerical or quantifiable.
Games can be categorized along several dimensions that influence the selection of
appropriate solution concepts [44, 45]. Broadly speaking, based on the assumptions
regarding player rationality, game theory can be classified into two main categories:
classical and evolutionary. Classical game theory assumes players possess perfect
rationality, consistently choosing options that maximize their utility. In contrast, evolu-
tionary game theory assumes limited rationality where players adapt strategies based
on experience and limited information [13].
Classical game theory can be further subdivided into non-cooperative and cooperative
categories, distinguished by the presence or absence of explicit collaboration among
players. Additionally, the classification considers the information available to the play-
ers: games can be of complete information, where players have full knowledge of others’

characteristics, or Bayesian-incomplete information, where such knowledge is uncer-
tain. Lastly, the temporal structure of player decisions leads to a distinction between
static games, in which players act simultaneously, and dynamic games, involving se-
quential decision-making over multiple stages [13, 45].

agreement point; however, a main critique and ponderous question remains. If a player
concedes a large portion of their bargaining boundaries or opens up further options,
should a solution not follow this movement of change in preference? The ’Indepen-
dence of Irrelevant Alternatives’ axiom dictates that it does not. Consider this example
Harbig [46]: Assume player 2’s preference relations are C ≫B > A while player 1’s is
A ≫B > C, where the symbol ≫denotes a considerably stronger preference than >.
If option C was not available, the players would agree on A. However, the availability of
option C enables player 2 to advocate for their second-best option B, arguing that their
best option is forfeited. This may lead to the players choosing B in the new situation,
contradicting the principle of the independence of irrelevant alternatives
3.6
Extensions: Asymmetric Nash Bargaining Problems
The standard Nash bargaining solution assumes symmetric bargaining positions be-
tween negotiating parties, reflected in the symmetric Nash product (uG −dG)(uL −dL).
However, real-world negotiations frequently involve asymmetric conditions where par-
ties possess different negotiation advantages due to market position, information ac-
cess, financing constraints, or regulatory environments. It can also be compared to the
fact that the parties might not be able to share information, given potential confiden-
tiality issues. This section extends the Nash bargaining framework to incorporate such
asymmetries through two mechanisms: differential bargaining power and asymmetric
information structures.

3.6.2
Asymmetric Information
In incomplete information settings, parties hold different beliefs about uncertain param-
eters that influence contract value. Unlike the standard Nash bargaining assumption of
complete information, real negotiations often involve significant uncertainty about future
results, with each party potentially having different perspectives.
The asymmetry is modeled in this case by allowing each player to hold a subjective
probability measure, following the approach of [21]. The information asymmetry is mod-
eled as an bias factors for each player, denoted as KG and KL for the generator and
load, respectively. The bias factors capture the factors that are related to ’interpreta-
tion’ or development of parameters involved in the bargaining process. In the context
of a PPA negotiation, several potential information symmetries exist. Each player might
have their own proprietary weather model or buy a different forecast model, which they
use to evaluate the value of a contract. Even with the same model, they might interpret it
differently and draw differing conclusions. The asymmetry of information is interpretable
as the belief a player has in their outcome.

PPAs in a Cooperative Game Framework
Power Purchase Agreement negotiations exhibit key characteristics that we can model

Players: PPA negotiations (often) involve exactly two players[10], not considering out-
side outside players or mediators:
• Generator (G): A renewable energy producer seeking revenue certainty to ensure

• Load-serving entity (L): An offtaker (industrial & corporate consumer or utility)

Unlike spot electricity markets, where anonymity categorizes participants, PPAs involve
identified counterparties in direct negotiations [10].
Strategies: The strategy space in PPA negotiations encompasses contract parameters
such as strike price and volume size, rather than a fixed quantity.
• Strike price: S ∈[Smin, Smax] where bounds reflect the boundary
• For baseload contracts (MW): Contract volume M ∈[0, M max], where Mmax often
represents the capacity.
• For pay as produced: Production share (%) γ ∈[0, 1]
Information and Timing
• Complete information: Parties share production forecasts, price expectations, and
risk tolerances during negotiations[10].
• Dynamic bargaining: Both parties normally propose and revise terms iteratively;
negotiations are as such generally not a simultaneous game. However, for sim-
plicity and implementation of Nash bargaining, we will assume that it is a simul-
taneous game.
Payoffs: Both parties evaluate outcomes through risk-adjusted utility functions.

Risk-Averse Utility Functions
To incorporate risk aversion into the return-risk utility of the players, we use the Con-
ditional Value-at-Risk (CVaR), a risk measure widely applied in both financial and elec-
tricity markets. CVaR evaluates the expected losses that occur beyond a predefined
threshold, specifically the Value-at-Risk (VaR), at a given confidence level (1−α). While
VaR represents the maximum loss not exceeded with confidence α, CVaR considers the
expected shortfall, i.e., the average of the worst-case losses exceeding the VaR thresh-
old.
Figure 4.1: Visual representation of Conditional Value at Risk [47]
Figure 4.1 illustrates this concept graphically on an arbitrary probability density function
(PDF) of potential outcomes. The VaR corresponds to the quantile threshold at (1 −α),
whereas CVaR accounts for the area under the curve to the left of the VaR cutoff. As
such, CVaR not only incorporates the likelihood of extreme losses but also captures their
severity, making it a more informative and conservative risk metric. CVaR will henceforth
be used in evaluating the left-tail of the net earnings, shifting the CVaR to be the right-tail
of a loss distribution would be equivalent.

4.4
Barter Set in PPA Negotiations
This section establishes the mathematical framework for determining the negotiation
space that is mutually beneficial in PPA contracts through Nash bargaining. We first
develop the theoretical foundation through a series of lemmas that extend on [21] to
incorporate stochastic production and pay as produced contracts. These mathematical
properties then allow us to define viable strike price boundaries and characterize the
conditions under which feasible barter sets exist.
4.4.1
Mathematical Foundation of the Barter Set
The construction of the barter set relies on several properties that are extensions to
the framework from [21] to incorporate stochastic production, capture rates, and pay as
produced contract. The following lemmas establish the theoretical foundation for the
bargaining analysis.
Lemma 1 (Utility Space Mapping under Strike Price Variation) The mapping of the
utility curves under varying contract amounts follows the following:
1. Baseload: For any fixed contract volume M ∈[MR, M U], varying the strike price
S from SR to SU maps into a straight line in the utility possibility set U with constant
slope −1.
Nash Bargaining for power purchase agreements
37
==PAGE:53==
2. pay as produced: For any fixed production share γ ∈[γR, γU], varying the strike
price S from SR to SU maps into a curved line in U with variable slope dependent
on γ and the CVaR derivatives.
The constant slope of −1 for baseload contracts simplifies the analysis compared to
[21], where the slope was −(1 + AL)/(1 + AG). For pay as produced contracts, the
interaction between stochastic production and contract parameters creates dependent
utility curves. Detailed derivations are provided in appendices C and D.
Lemma 2 (Concavity of Utility Curves) If the strike price is fixed at SR (or SU), then
the set of points (uG, uL) traced out in U as the contract amount varies determines a
concave curve V1 (or V2):
1. Baseload: As M varies from MR to MU
2. pay as produced: As γ varies from 0 ≡γS to 1 ≡γU
This concavity establishes that the Pareto frontier made by h(uG) = uL is a concave
function. The incorporation of a new utility function 4.5 - 4.6 and stochasticity of param-
eters modifies but preserves the concavity structure (see appendices E.1 and F.1).
Lemma 3 (Monotonicity of Utilities with Respect to Contract Volume) The relation-
ship between strike price boundaries and utility changes as the contract amount varies:
1. Baseload:
• If SR < SR∗(as defined in Equation (4.24)), then with strike price fixed at SR,
as the contract amount M increases: uG decreases and uL increases.
• If SU > SU∗(as defined in Equation (4.25)), then with strike price fixed at SU,
as the contract amount M increases: uG increases and uL decreases.
2. pay as produced:
• If SR < SR∗(as defined in Equation (4.31)), then with strike price fixed at SR,
as the production share γ increases: uG decreases and uL increases.
• If SU > SU∗(as defined in Equation (4.32)), then with strike price fixed at SU,
as the production share γ increases: uG increases and uL decreases.
This monotonicity establishes the direction of the utility changes along the curves V1
and V2, ensuring that the viable strike price boundaries SR∗and SU∗represent the lim-
its beyond which no mutually beneficial contract is possible. When the strike price is
below SR∗, increasing contract amount benefits the load at the generator’s expense.
Conversely, when the strike price exceeds SU∗, increasing contract amount benefits
the generator at the load’s expense. The proof, incorporating stochastic production and
capture rates, is provided in Appendices E.2 - F.2.

5
Methodology
This chapter describes the methodology used to answer the primary research question:
how cooperative game theory can be applied in designing and pricing PPAs. The anal-
ysis employs a synthetic case study of a wind farm developer seeking a PPA contract to
ensure project bankability, providing practical boundaries for the modeling framework.
The methodology combines Nash bargaining for contract negotiations, stochastic opti-
mization for uncertainty modeling, Monte Carlo simulation for scenario generation. This
approach aims to quantify the impacts of risk aversion, negotiation power, and market
parameters on the optimal contract design. The chapter first presents the synthetic case
study to establish realistic parameters. It then develops the mathematical model used
for computational optimization before describing the Monte Carlo simulation approach,
the stochastic process of future price generation, and finally the scenario reduction to
make the model tractable.
5.1
Case Study Design
To illustrate the thesis, a synthetic case is presented of a developer seeking to develop
a wind farm in Ringsted municipality, comprising 10x3 MW turbines, through a PPA
contract with local industries. Ringsted has a high share of Asphalt, Construction &
Building Materials, providing 20% of private employment [48]. Ringsted has committed
to achieving carbon neutrality by 2045 [49] and had an annual electricity consumption of
700 TJ (194 GWh) in 2023 [50]. The PPA aims to help ensure the municipality reaches
its climate neutrality goals and allows the industries to hedge their electricity price. The
wind farm will only consist of wind turbines, and the PPA contract will be an off-grid,
behind-the-meter arrangement in terms of production. Several companies exist within
the municipality, but will be assumed to be a single stakeholder for negotiation on behalf
of all of them.
5.2
Mathematical Modeling Framework
Having established the practical context through the Ringsted case study, we now de-
velop a computational model based from the mathematical models presented through
4.3.1 and 4.3.2 applying Nash Bargaining to analyze the negotiation dynamics between
the wind farm developer and the industries. The framework addresses the research
questions through a stochastic optimization model incorporated with the Nash Bargain-
ing approach that analyzes both BL and PAP PPA structures. This model provides
insight into how different risk profiles, negotiation power dynamics, and market param-
eters influence contract outcomes. The model will be using an annual granularity to
focus on long-term outcomes rather than operational optimization. While this detracts
from seasonal patterns and short-term arbitrage opportunities, it captures the funda-
mental risk-return trade-offs given that the contracts can last over 10-20 years, and the
focus of a PPA is on long-term hedging. The models in 4.3.1 and 4.3.2 are formulated as

Monte Carlo Simulation Method
This section describes the Monte Carlo processes used to generate the scenarios for
the mathematical model. The parameters are all generated through Monte Carlo simu-
lations with adjustments to more accurately represent the distribution.
5.3.1
Monte Carlo Process
Monte Carlo simulation is a numerical technique used to evaluate the expected value
and distribution of outcomes for systems characterized by uncertainty and randomness.
In the context of the thesis, Monte Carlo enables the sampling of the input parame-
ters, baseload prices, wind production, consumption, and capture rates based on their
empirically derived probability distributions from historical data. By generating a large
number of random scenarios and calculating the outcome for each, it becomes possi-
ble to approximate the probability distribution of the revenue and utility. Through Monte
Carlo simulation, the expected value and CVaR can be quantified. Each parameter is
sampled randomly and independently from its sampling method, and combined into a
scenario ω that is assumed to be ’Independent Identically Distributed’ (I.D.D), each with
equal probability 1/N.

Analysis Framework
To address the research questions systematically, five experiments are designed to iso-

Analysis 1: Risk Aversion Impact
Investigation of how varying risk aversion parameters AL, AG affect optimal strike prices,
contract volumes, and revenue outcomes. This experiment maintains equal bargaining

contract structures, and helps answer Questions 1 and 2.
Analysis 2: Negotiation Power Analysis
Analysis of how asymmetric bargaining power τ influences contract terms and surplus
distribution in the base case, but also under different risk aversion combinations. This
analysis systematically shifts negotiation advantage from complete generator domi-
nance τG = 1 to load dominance τL = 1, directly addressing Research Question 2
regarding power asymmetries.
Analysis 3: Asymmetric Information Analysis
Examination of contract outcomes when players hold different expectations about key
parameters, transitioning from complete to incomplete information. This experiment
addresses Research Question 3 by analyzing how expectation biases affect negotiated
outcomes:

Conclusion
This thesis set out to answer the overarching question: How can an equitable negotiation
outcome for pricing and contract amount in Power Purchase Agreements be predicted?
Using Nash bargaining theory as a cooperative game-theoretic framework combined
with stochastic optimization, the study developed and applied a model capable of cap-
turing the strategic interplay between a renewable energy generator and a load entity
under both baseload (BL) and pay as produced (PAP) PPA structures in a synthetic case
study.
The first research question asked how a ‘fair’ strike price and contract amount can be
determined for BL and PAP PPAs. The results show that when risk preferences are
incorporated into the Nash bargaining solution, negotiated outcomes deviate system-
atically from naive expectations based solely on expected prices. The formalization of
the barter set for both contract types provided clear boundaries for feasible negotiations
with the mathematical conditions establishing when mutually beneficial agreements can
exist.
The second research question examined the influence of differences in risk aversion
and bargaining power. For BL contracts, higher load risk aversion leads to higher strike
prices and larger contract volumes, whereas higher generator risk aversion has the op-
posite effect. In PAP contracts, strike prices respond predictably to relative risk aversion,
but contract amounts remain largely constant unless the load is risk-neutral. Bargaining
power shifts the allocation of surplus within the feasible barter set without altering its
size; in BL contracts, this primarily affects strike price, while in PAP contracts, it can
also marginally adjust the contracted share.
The third research question addressed the role of stochastic parameters and asym-
metric information. The sensitivity analysis revealed that for PAP contracts, generator
capture rate is the dominant driver of negotiated price with proportional elasticity in sym-
metric expectations. For BL contracts, differences in price expectations between parties
can substantially alter both strike price and contract size, with asymmetric biases am-
plifying the volatility of the outcome. The feasible contract region shrinks when parties
hold strongly divergent forecasts, underscoring the importance of aligning expectations
or explicitly valuing subjective forecasts in negotiations.
Overall, the framework developed here formalizes the concept of the barter set in PPA
negotiations, extends it to stochastic production and capture rate effects, and demon-
strates how equilibrium outcomes emerge from the interplay of risk attitudes, bargain-
ing power, and parameter uncertainty. The model provides a structured, transparent
method of quantifying “fair” outcomes in long-term renewable energy contracting, offer-
ing both academic insight and practical guidance for PPA design.

8.1
Future Work
While the framework developed in this thesis provides a structured approach to predict-
ing equitable outcomes in bilateral PPA negotiations, several extensions could broaden
its applicability and realism:
• Multi-Party Negotiations: Extend the model to include multiple buyers and sell-
ers, enabling smaller offtakers to acquire a PPA, and increase the pool of potential
offtakers for sellers.
• Further convexification of the model by incorporating decomposition techniques
to increase the tractability of the model.
• Hybrid structures: Integrate battery storage, demand flexibility, and mixed-technology
renewable portfolios into the bargaining framework to assess how flexibility re-
sources shift bargaining power and risk allocation.
• Variable tenor lengths: Compare short- and long-term PPAs to examine how
contract duration influences negotiation outcomes, risk distribution, and the sta-
bility of agreements.
• Alternative utility formulations: Explore different utility functions to represent di-
verse stakeholder priorities, including varying definitions of risk aversion besides
CVaR, alternative disagreement points besides full market exposure such as con-
sidering outside options, and the intrinsic value buyers may place on renewable
energy procurement.
• Ex-post fairness assessment: Apply the model retrospectively to historical PPA
data to evaluate how “fair” past contracts have been given the information and
expectations at the time of signing, and how it compares to the realized future.
• Change in bargaining rule: The framework could also be extended to incorpo-
rate alternative bargaining rules, such as the Kalai-Smorodinsky, to compare how
different axioms influence negotiated outcomes under identical market conditions.
