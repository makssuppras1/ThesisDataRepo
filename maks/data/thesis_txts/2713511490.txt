Abstract 
 
Copepods are a fundamental element of the marine ecosystems with many crucial 
roles to play, such as being the link between the primary producers and higher 
trophic levels and supporting the biological carbon pump. The aim of this project is 
to study the biogeography of copepod biomass, size and the production of 
Particulate Organic Matter (POM) in the global ocean by reviewing and analysing the 
Nutrient – Unicellular – Multicellular (NUM) model. The NUM model follows a trait – 
based approach to describe the planktonic community, using the size of an organism 
as its master – trait. The analysed system consists of a size – structured community 
of unicellular organisms, seven size – structured populations of copepods, a size – 
structured compartment of POM, as well as a pool of nitrogen and DOC. The output 
of the NUM model is tested in both a chemostat and a water column setup. Overall, 
it has been demonstrated that the NUM model manages to sufficiently describe 
fundamental biological and physical dynamics of the planktonic community, such as 
prey – predator interactions and seasonality. Finally, strengths and weaknesses of 
the NUM model approach are discussed.

1. Introduction 
It has been almost 2,300 years since Aristotle first observed and described in his 
“History of Animals” a tiny organism attached under the fins of swordfish and tuna, 
not knowing then that he was introducing the scientific community to the fascinating 
world of copepods (Williamson & Reid, 2009). The scientific research has progressed 
a lot since then and it is well documented now that copepods are one of the most 
abundant and diverse groups of organisms on Earth (Boxshall & Defaye, 2008). They 
can be found in a wide array of aquatic ecosystems, both marine and freshwater, 
living as parasitises or living freely either in the water column (pelagic) or onto 
substrates (benthic) (Williamson & Reid, 2009).  
Copepods form the subclass Copepoda belonging to the subphylum Crustacea and 
the phylum Arthropoda (Tarrant et al., 2019). There is an ongoing debate about the 
classification of copepods into orders, but Khodami et al., 2017 propose that there 
are ten main orders: Platycopioida, Calanoida, Misophrioida, Canuelloida, 
Gelyelloida, Cyclopoida, Harpacticoida, Mormonilloida, Monstrilloida, 
Siphonostomatoida. The diversity in body shapes and lengths is vast among the 
approximately 11,000 copepod species identified so far (Kwok et al., 2015 ; Khodami 
et al., 2017 ; Brun et al., 2017). The life cycle of a copepod starts as an egg and then 
under continuous metamorphoses it passes through six nauplii (larval) and five 
copepodite stages until it reaches the adult stage and sexual maturity in a timescale 
of some days to few weeks (Kwok et al., 2015). Given optimal conditions, copepod’s 
lifetime can last up to a few months (Williamson & Reid, 2009). 
Despite their small size, the contribution of copepods to many ecosystem services is 
of great importance. This is mainly due to their position in the food web comprising 
approximately 80% of the mesozooplankton community (Kiorboe, 1997). Copepods 
may feed on microzooplankton, cannibalize on smaller copepods, exhibit 
coprophagy or predate on phytoplankton and thus they serve as the trophic link 
through which the energy is distributed from the primary producers and consumers 
into higher trophic level organisms (Steinberg & Landry, 2017). Commercial fisheries 
also depend on copepods since they are the main dietary source for marine fish 
larvae and thus they regulate the recruitment of different fish stocks (Tarrant et al., 
2019). Moreover, copepods perform a Diel Vertical Migration (DVM) to avoid 
predation, by migrating in high depths during the day and move to the surface 
during the night to catch their prey (Pinti et al., 2019). Copepods migrating vertically 
in the water column may support the communities which inhabit the mesopelagic

zone by becoming their prey and through respiration and excretion cover bacteria’s 
carbon demands (Kelly et al., 2019).  
In the context of climate change, copepods are a key player in the biological pump 
which influences the capacity of the ocean to act as carbon sink and thus regulate 
the atmospheric CO2 concentration by storing approximately 40% of the 
anthropogenic CO2 emissions since the industrial revolution (Omand et al., 2020). 
The starting point of the biological pump takes place in the photic zone of the ocean 
where photosynthesizing microorganisms utilize nutrients and light to convert 
dissolved inorganic carbon (DIC) into body growth. After this point, most of this 
synthesized organic carbon circulates in the upper ocean layer through a system of 
multiple trophic level interactions, respiration, excretion, and remineralization. 
However, a portion of it manages to escape to deeper water as particulate organic 
matter (POM). The role of copepods in this process is to mediate the production of 
POM through egestion (fecal pellets), the production of mucous feeding webs and 
the discard of their molts (Honjo, 2009 ; Steinberg & Landry, 2017). This content of 
POM has the potential to stick together with other living or non – living particles, i.e., 
phytoplankton and zooplankton carcasses, phytoplankton and bacteria cells, forming 
the so called “marine snow” (Jo et al., 2021). These larger – structure aggregates can 
now sink faster which enables them to avoid remineralization or predation and 
escape below the mixing ocean layer (De La Rocha & Passow, 2013). Moreover, the 
DVM of copepods drives an active carbon export process, in which they ingest 
organic carbon in the surface ocean layer and metabolize it, excrete DIC, egest fecal 
pellets or die in high depths (Le Moigne, 2019). Finally, some copepod species, such 
as Calanus hyperboreus, exhibit overwintering strategies (diapause) by migrating 
down the water column below the permanent thermocline. Their resident time in 
these depths may be up to several months and during this period they directly 
sequester carbon by metabolizing their stored lipids, a process that is referred as 
lipid pump (Visser et al., 2017).   
Regarding the contribution of copepods to the biological pump, it has been proposed 
that size matters (Stamieszkin et al., 2015a). The main argument of this statement is 
that larger copepods produce larger fecal pellets whose sinking velocities are much 
higher than smaller ones. In this sense, fast – sinking particles drastically reduce their 
resident time in the upper ocean layer where they are subjected to bacterial 
degradation and coprophagy and thus increase the efficiency of the biological pump 
by transporting more carbon below the ocean’s “sequestration depth” (Stamieszkin 
et al., 2015a). Garzke et al., 2015 showed that the size of an adult copepod reduces 
drastically under increasing temperatures, mainly due to the increased energy

demands that it must allocate to both basal and active metabolism. Apart from the 
size distribution of the copepod community, the input of the copepods’ fecal pellets 
to the total POM export is controlled by seasonal cycles, regional characteristics and  
depths, the food web structure, the abundance of the copepod community and their 
food availability and quality (Steinberg & Landry, 2017). 
This articulates the importance to enhance the scientific knowledge around the 
spatiotemporal distribution of copepod biomass, body size and POM. Modelling such 
an abundant and diverse group of organisms as copepods may be a trivial task. 
However, the process can be simplified by following a trait – based approach. In trait 
– based models, the function of an ecosystem is governed by interactions between 
individuals which have specific key traits and not between species (Kiørboe et al., 
2018). The fitness of an organism can be determined by how well it performs in 
three main functions: food acquisition, biomass growth and reproduction and overall 
survival (Brun et al., 2017). Fourteen traits have been proposed by (Brun et al., 
2017): clearance rate, ingestion rate and feeding mode associated with feeding, egg 
size, growth rate, spawning strategy, development duration, clutch size and 
fecundity affecting growth and reproduction, myelination, respiration, hibernation, 
and resting eggs essential for survival. The body size of an organism is referred as a 
master – trait which has the potential to affect all three ecological functions. Apart 
from the body size, the feeding mode is of great importance, and it can be classified 
as passive (ambush and particulate feeding), active (feeding currents, cruise 
feeding), mixed or parasitic (Brun et al., 2017). The important aspect of the trait – 
based models is that each trait comes with trade – offs, e.g., if a copepod is an active 
feeder it may increase its chances to capture its prey, but it also attracts predators 
with its movements experiencing higher predation mortality and it has higher 
metabolic needs compared to a passive feeder copepod (Kiørboe et al., 2018).  
In this project, the Nutrient-Unicellular-Multicellular (NUM) framework is 
implemented with the goal to investigate the global biogeography of copepod 
biomass, body size and POM production. In the NUM model, the planktonic 
community is described mechanistically, using the size of an organism as its main 
trait. Thus, the size of the organisms defines all their physiological rates and the 
predator – prey dynamics of the system. The model is a combination of the 
(Andersen & Visser, 2022) generalists’ model and a modified version of (Serra-
Pompei et al., 2020).

2. Methodology 
2.1 The NUM framework 
As shown in figure 1, the NUM model consists of a size – structured community of 
unicellular planktonic organisms, i.e., archaea, bacteria, protists and protozoa, 
referred to as generalists in the context of this project and a number of size – 
structured copepod populations. Regarding the environmental conditions prevailed 
in the system, a pool of dissolved nitrogen, a Dissolved Organic Carbon (DOC) pool 
and a Particulate Organic Carbon (POM) compartment described as a size – spectrum 
are used, as well as variable light intensity and temperature. The model calculates 
how the biomass concentration of all these five compartments develops over time 
(μgC L−1 day−1 or μgN L−1 day−1).  
The generalists can utilize nitrogen and DOC, photosynthesize, and eat smaller 
generalists to gain their energy. Copepods are capable of predating both on 
generalists and smaller copepods and eating POM, exhibiting coprophagy, figure 1. 
Overall, both generalists and copepods compete for limited resources and 
experience predation pressure from higher trophic level (HTL) organisms.  
The nitrogen and DOC pools are updated through remineralization processes and 
mixing of the upper layers with higher depths in the case of nitrogen, section 2.6.3. 
POM is produced by both generalists and copepods, and it is also subject to 
remineralization and sinking processes, section 2.4. 
All the rates presented in the following sections are referring to individual level 
processes and they are extended to the population level by solving a system of 
coupled ordinary equations (ODEs). In the model the majority of the rates are mass – 
specific.

2.7 Size spectra  
The size – structured approach of the NUM model in the representation of each 
population in the plankton community inherits a complexity in the sense that the 
level of biomass in each size – class of a population depends on its size – range.     
Each population in the NUM model is divided into ten logarithmically spaced bins 
and the model’s output corresponds to the geometric mean of each bin. However, 
the width of each size – bin is not constant as it increases moving from the lower to 
the upper size boundary of a population. Hence, the calculated biomasses, one for 
each bin, are referred to different bin widths. To be able to compare the NUM 
model’s output inside a population, through different populations and different 
model setups, the concept of the Sheldon biomass is used.

2.8 Ocean circulation data – Transport Matrix 
In this project, the NUM model’s output is tested in two different setups, a 
chemostat and water column. The chemostat setup represents the photic zone of 
the ocean where there is a mixing of nutrients with a deeper layer. The 
concentration of nitrogen in this deeper layer is 150 μgN. 
In the water column setup of the NUM model, the “Estimating the Circulation and 
Climate of the Ocean” (ECCO) dataset is used, acquired from 
http://kelvin.earth.ox.ac.uk/spk/Research/TMM/TransportMatrixConfigs/. ECCO’s 
goal is to merge advanced ocean general circulation models (GCMs) and provide a 
holistic representation of the hydrography and important oceanic conditions in a 
global scale, such as temperature, light and mixing rates (Wunsch et al., 2009). 
The MITgcm_ECCO covers a 360° x 160° horizontal grid, from east to west and north 
to south respectively. Regarding the water column representation, up to twenty-
three depth bins are included depending on the location. The depth range is divided 
into uneven intervals, progressively larger with depth. The model’s output refers to 
the middle point of each depth interval.

system choices. The overall goal is first to identify crucial parameters that affect the 
outcome of the model and then decide on appropriate values by conducting a 
sensitivity analysis on them.  
3.1 Chemostat  
The chemostat setup of the NUM model is used first as it provides a better control 
on the input parameters which in turn allows us to determine the single effect of the 
investigated parameter. More specifically, important environmental parameters are 
the mixing rate of nutrients, i.e., nitrogen, (day−1 ), the level of light intensity 
(μE/m2/sec) and the temperature (℃). A different combination of these 
parameters can be used to represent a variety of ecosystems. When scaling up to the 
water column setup of the NUM model, these environmental parameters are 
acquired directly from the transport matrices.  
However, the number of copepod populations and the HTL mortality rate (1/day) or 
the HTL mortality coefficient (1/ μgC L−1/ day) in a non-quadratic or a quadratic 
HTL mortality scenario respectively are system parameters whose values will be fixed 
in the model’s water column setup. Hence, it is essential to provide the model with 
relevant values for these parameters.

interpreting the system dynamics. A targeted analysis on the appropriate number of 
copepod population is presented in later chapters. Since in each scenario the same 
parameter has a different meaning, see section 2.3.5, the direct comparison of the 
two scenarios is not straightforward. However, the below presented cases are 
chosen among a wide range of simulations using different environmental parameters

3.1.2 System dynamics  
In this chapter a comparison between a eutrophic, nutrient mixing rate of 0.1 day−1, 
and an oligotrophic system, nutrient mixing rate of 0.001 day−1, is presented. 
Followed by the mass – specific rates that control each population, i.e., generalists 
and five copepod populations, this analysis helps to understand the dynamics of the 
model. The HTL mortality coefficient is 0.2 1/ μgC L−1/ day, light intensity is 60 
μE/m2/sec, the temperature is 10 ℃ . The simulations are run for twenty years in 
both scenarios and both scenarios reach a steady state.  
As shown in figure 12a and figure 12b, the first intuitive difference is that the 
eutrophic environment supports a higher total Sheldon biomass of the whole 
planktonic community, 225.3 μgC L−1, compared to the oligotrophic one, 20.3 
μgC L−1. The copepod community size spectrum in the oligotrophic conditions is 
lower for approximately an order of magnitude in terms of biomass compared to the 
eutrophic environment. This has an important impact on the level of predation 
mortality that the generalists experience in each case. In the oligotrophic scenario 
the predation mortality rates scale down by approximately a factor of ten compared 
to the eutrophic environment, figure 12c and figure 12d. Regarding the predation 
mortality imposed by copepod populations on another copepod population, it is only 
the smaller size classes of the smallest adult – size population, 0.2 μgC, which are 
affected. This makes sense, since the preferred prey – predator ratio in copepods is 
10,000 and hence larger adult – size copepod populations avoid being predated by 
other copepods. 
==PAGE:32==

The losses due to HTL mortality are more pronounced in the eutrophic environment, 
as the Quadratic HTL mortality is density – dependent. Similarly to the scenario of 
the previous chapter, in the eutrophic conditions, figure 12e, the smaller size classes 
of each copepod population do not experience high levels of HTL mortality, which 
allows copepods inside a population to grow into the adult stage and reproduce. The 
same pattern is observed in the oligotrophic environment, but it is not possible to be 
visualized in figure 12f. This scaling of the HTL mortality with the prey – density is 
crucial especially for the larger adult – size copepod population. This is because their 
gains from feeding are substantially lower than the respective ones of the smaller 
adult – size copepods and without this adjustment they would not be able to survive. 
This can be supported by following the growth rate of copepods, which is given by 
subtracting the respiration losses from the gains acquired by feeding, shown with 
grey line in figures 12e,f. Even with very small growth rates these populations are 
coping well, and the reason might be that their losses due to predation from HTL 
organisms are very low. Comparing how the copepod populations respond to the 
HTL selectivity function, see shading area in figure 12a,b, one could argue that the 
top – down control is more pronounced in the oligotrophic environment, as certain 
size – classes of copepod populations that lie below the curve are constrained, 
whereas in the eutrophic environment there is not a lot of deviation for these size – 
classes.  
In regard to the gains of generalists, in the case where nutrients are scarce, 
generalists’ efficiency in photoharvesting reduces drastically based on theory of the 
Liebig’s law of minimum and a larger portion of them are becoming osmotrophs, 
relying on DOC uptake. Passive exudation losses follow the same pattern in both 
cases, in contrast to losses due to viral lysis which are density dependent.

4. Discussion 
This project demonstrated the output of the NUM model consisting of five state 
variables, i.e., a size – structured population of unicellular organisms, seven size – 
structured copepod populations, a size – structured POM component as well as a 
pool of nitrogen and DOC.  
4.1 Copepods’ biomass 
In the section 3.1.4, the annual mean copepods’ biomass in the upper 200m derived 
from the NUM model was compared to the (Moriarty & O’Brien, 2013) study. The 
results indicate a good match in three out of the four examined areas in respect to 
the copepods’ biomass. However, it was noticed that in upwelling areas, such as in 
the Peruvian coastline and the equatorial upwellings, the copepods’ abundance was 
underestimated by the model. Upwelling and frontal zone areas have the potential 
to provide suitable conditions, such as nutrient availability and temperature ranges, 
leading to a fast growth of phytoplankton which in turn can stimulate the growth of 
copepods (Escribano & Hidalgo, 2000 ; Zhang et al., 2015). The NUM model’s 
misrepresentation of these areas might be because in this project only the water 
column setup of the NUM model was used. This means that only a specific water 
column was extracted from the transport matrices and was used as input in the 
model. Since upwelling and frontal zones are very dynamic systems with both 
vertical and horizontal movement of water masses and material, the water column 
setup might lack in capturing these dynamics when only a single water column is 
examined in isolation. Due to time limitation a global simulation setup was not 
possible to be run in this project, but it is appropriate to be executed in further steps 
to test this hypothesis.  
The annual mean total biomass of copepods in the upper 200 m, figure 21, can also 
be compared to (Serra‐Pompei et al., 2022), figure 26. In general, both examined 
transects in this project, Atlantic and Pacific Ocean, provided values in the same 
order of magnitude as (Serra‐Pompei et al., 2022) for both generalists and copepods. 
However, regarding the copepods, there are three important deviations to discuss.  
The first area of interest is the equatorial regions of both transects where the 
copepod community is kept in very low biomass values, whereas the (Serra‐Pompei 
et al., 2022) suggests a vivid copepod community in these areas. Comparing the 
generalists’ output of the two models, the NUM model matches sufficiently the 
latitudinal variability of their biomass, with high annual mean biomass in high 
latitudes followed by reduced values in the case of the subtropical gyres and a not so 
profound increase of the generalists’ abundance in equatorial areas, implying 
==PAGE:55==
51 
 
equatorial upwellings, figure 21. The fact that the copepod community in the NUM 
model does not respond to the generalists’ behaviour in the equatorial region might 
be due to the fact that the HTL mortality is temperature dependent. Hence, the 
mortality rate that copepods experience by HTL organisms might be very 
pronounced throughout the year and this does not give them the chance to thrive. 
The combination of lower 𝑄10 factors for the HTL mortality with a prey – density HTL 
mortality, as used in this study, might release some pressure from the copepods and 
allow them to grow in biomass. Temperature is also affecting the respiration losses 
by a 𝑄10 factor of 2. Since in these areas the temperature is high all year long, the 
basal metabolism of copepods is high as well, decreasing their net energy gains 
available for survival and growth. Another reason might lie in the POM 
remineralization rates which scale up with temperature. One of copepods’ feeding 
sources is POM, coprophagy, which in high temperatures is turned into nitrogen and 
DOC very fast. Off course these can now be used by the generalists which then are 
eaten by copepods but there are many processes involved in this chain and 
copepods might end up with lower gains. Finally, as mentioned above, the equatorial 
upwellings might be better represented when the global setup of NUM model is 
used which could enhance the productivity in these regions.   
The second area of interest is the Southern Ocean where both models might 
overestimate the generalists’ first and the copepods’ biomass as a consequence of 
the first. This is because the Southern Ocean’s primary production is strongly limited, 
apart from light and temperature, by the iron availability (Strzepek et al., 2019), but 
iron as an element is not included in the NUM model yet.  
Finally, the NUM model’s output for the highest latitude in the north Pacific Ocean 
suggests biomass for generalists and copepods almost an order of magnitude larger 
than the subsequent latitudes, figure 21. The area in this latitude refers to the Bering 
strait in the north Pacific Ocean. This is a swallow and highly productive area 
throughout the year governed by a high nutrient content, a complex system of water 
flow through it, without deep water formation processes and periods of sea ice 
coverage (Sambrotto et al., 1984 ; Ferreira et al., 2018). Since the sea ice coverage in 
high latitudes is not implemented in the NUM model and in this project only the 
==PAGE:56==
52 
 
water column setup of the model is used, as discussed previously, implies that the 
model’s output in these areas should be interpreted carefully. 
 
Another interesting study which sought to track the global ocean’s latitudinal 
distribution and abundance of copepods is presented by (Brandão et al., 2021), 
figure 27. In this study, a statistical analysis approach was used by analysing samples 
of plankton acquired from the Tara Oceans expeditions between 2009 and 2013. 
These data were used to fit a Generalized Additive Model based on a variety of 
environmental variables, such as temperature, nutrient concentrations, and oxygen 
levels. Even though the units of abundance are different from the NUM model, the 
pattern of the results in this study highlights the misrepresentation the copepod 
community in the equatorial regions in the NUM model and suggests a lower 
abundance in the southern higher latitudes.

4.2 Copepods’ body size  
The analysis in section 3.3.2 showed that, in both transects, larger copepods have 
the potential to be sustained in higher latitudes whereas copepod communities of 
smaller copepod sizes dominate in the subtropical areas in the Atlantic Ocean 
transect and around the equator in the Pacific Ocean transect, figure 22. In the 
context of the NUM model, this might steam from the dependency of the 
respiration, HTL mortality and POM remineralization on temperature, as discussed in 
the previous section but in a reversed way, as well as on prey – predator dynamics 
and the resulted size – structured community of the generalists with larger 
generalists dominating during blooms. In higher latitudes, lower temperatures 
restrict the losses from basal metabolism and HTL mortality and the slower POM 
remineralization rates might allow more energy gains through coprophagy. Also, the 
pronounced generalists’ blooms in higher latitudes provide sufficient food to sustain 
a long food chain of copepods allowing larger copepods to survive. In section 2.3.3, 
figure 8 shows that the mass – specific respiration rate is higher under higher 
temperatures, given the same food availability, and under higher food uptake, given 
a constant temperature.  
This comes in accordance with the literature. The general metabolic theory suggests 
that the mass – specific metabolic rate, 𝜅, depends on the organism’s size M and 
temperature T, (Brown et al., 2004): 
𝜅 ≡𝛭−1/4 𝑒−𝐸/ 𝑘𝑇   
where E is the temperature correction parameter to separate the effect of the mass 
and k is the Boltzmann’s constant. Copepods are ectotherms and without being able 
to regulate their internal temperature they strongly rely on the temperature of their 
surrounding environment (Brandão et al., 2021). It has been observed that even 
though most of the ectotherms might grow at a slower pace under lower 
temperatures, they can reach larger sizes in their maturity states compared to 
warmer conditions. In contrary, under higher temperatures organisms reach 
maturity faster which leads to an overall lower mean size of the community 
(Angilletta et al., 2004). The Bergmann’s rule supports this statement by suggesting 
that organisms belonging in the same clade are generally larger under colder 
conditions. Focusing on copepods, Campbell et al., 2021 and Evans et al., 2020 
showed that, among other variables, temperature and latitude were the best 
explanatory variables in regard to the copepods’ size, promoting the Bergmann’s 
rule.  
==PAGE:58==
54 
 
Brun et al., 2016 combined empirical data and statistical modelling to investigate the 
biogeography of key copepod traits, such as size, relative offspring size and 
myelination. Figure 28 shows how the mean copepod community size fluctuates in 
the global ocean. Even though figure 22 does not included the mean community 
copepod size and the units are in mass and not length, the main trend of the NUM 
model’s result is similar to (Brun et al., 2016), with higher abundances of larger 
copepods in the higher latitudes and gradually smaller copepods – dominated 
communities moving to the equator. 
 
4.3 Seasonality  
Seasonal cycles of important environmental conditions for life in marine ecosystems, 
such as temperature and light availability, which are the fuelling power for primary 
production are more apparent in high and temperate latitudes (Visser et al., 2020). 
This seasonality in the primary production results in periods of excess food 
availability on the one hand and periods of resources scarcity. During the first case, 
there are the suitable conditions to support a wide array of marine communities, 
from small parasites to big marine mammals and from benthic to pelagic organisms 
(Visser et al., 2020). In the second case, organisms can endure periods of low food 
availability by entering a hibernation state, build lipid reserves during the blooming 
periods or migrate, vertical migration in the case of copepods (Visser et al., 2020). In 
the NUM model, copepods survive through the periods with low abundances of 
generalists by lowering their respiration costs, i.e., basal metabolism is temperature 
dependent and by exhibiting coprophagy.

4.4 POM export 
The implementation of POM in the NUM model is simple but robust. It is based on a 
size – structured compartment, remineralization processes turning POM into 
nitrogen and DOC, coprophagy and a size – dependent sinking velocity. Following the 
behaviour of POM in time, as described in section 3.2.2, the main concept is that 
larger copepods produce larger POM particles through fecal pellets, dead carcasses, 
and reproductive losses. These bigger particles can sink faster and reach higher 
depths by escaping remineralization and predation from larger copepods. Larger 
copepods are more dominant in higher latitudes as discussed in section 4.2, and this 
explains why the model predicts that the carbon export flux is at maximum in both 
examined transects in these areas, figure 25. On the other hand, in areas where 
smaller copepods dominate, such as in the subtropical gyres, the POM size spectrum 
is also characterized by smaller particles which are being locked in the upper part of

the ocean, decreasing the efficiency of the carbon export flux as shown in the middle 
and equatorial latitudes of figures 25a,b. This might, also, explain why the difference 
between the carbon flux export in depth of 120m and 1000m gradually increases 
moving from higher latitudes to the equator, as the larger POM particles are 
exported faster to higher depths limiting their losses. This notion that the carbon 
export flux depends on the community structure of copepods agrees with the 
literature (Uye & Kaname, 1994 ; Stamieszkin et al., 2015b). Smaller copepods play 
an important role as well. The importance of the dominant copepod size in the 
carbon export efficiency is highlighted by (Dubischar & Bathmann, 2002) stating that 
the abundance of smaller, fecal pellet – eater copepods can regulate the amount of 
carbon exported to higher depths. Finally, in the NUM model the sinking speed of 
the POM size structure depends only on its size without including information about 
its composition and density.  Feinberg & Dam, 1998 showed in a laboratory 
experiment that the diet of the copepod Acartia tonsa plays a significant role in the 
sinking rates of the produced fecal pellets by determining their density. Hence, for a 
better representation of the POM dynamics in the model, the incorporation of 
information about the food quality and origin would be a further step. 
The carbon export flux at 200m fluctuations throughout the year, figures 25c,d, 
comes in agreement with the model output of (Serra‐Pompei et al., 2022). In the 
north Pacific the model suggests a strong carbon flux export throughout the year 
which is supported by a constant high abundance of generalists able to sustain in 
turn a vivid copepod community. Contrary, the north Atlantic is characterized by 
deep – water formation periods with strong water column mixing (Ferreira et al., 
2018) which in combination with limitation of nutrients and light cannot sustain a 
strong biomass of generalists and copepods throughout the year.  
In the context of climate change, the mean global ocean surface temperature is 
increasing by 0.28 ± 0.068 ℃ per decade (2010-2019) and the ocean heat content in 
the upper 2,000m follows a warming rate of 0.70 ± 0.07 𝑊𝑚−2 for the same period, 
which is twice compared to the period 1955 – 2019 (Garcia-Soto et al., 2021). Figure 
30 presents the output of NUM model in a chemostat setup under varying 
temperatures. These results indicate that the biomass of larger copepods 
experiences a stronger decrease with increasing temperatures, shifting to planktonic 
communities to be dominated by smaller –  and medium – sized copepods. This in 
turn, as discussed above, may reduce the carbon export flux and potentially feed a 
positive feedback loop where warmer oceans will not be able to sequester large 
amounts of carbon which in turn will lead to higher ocean temperatures. Off course, 
the biological pump encompasses a variety and not fully understood yet physical, 
==PAGE:61==
57 
 
biological, and chemical processes to draw such generalized conclusions like this. 
However, Brun et al., 2019, focusing in the North Atlantic, have demonstrated that 
the carbon export flux induced by copepods has altered under climate change, 
identifying both the copepod biomass and the size – structure of the copepod 
community as important factors. The positive feedback introduced above is 
presented by (Henson et al., 2022). On the other hand, there is an array of other 
negative feedback loops under climate change, such as that warmer oceans will 
result in lower water viscosity and density which in turn has the potential to increase 
the particles’ sinking speed, which makes it challenging to conclude about the final 
net outcome of these loops (Henson et al., 2022).

4.5 Strengths and weaknesses 
The NUM model uses a trait – based approach using the size of an organism as its 
master – trait and then scaling up to the whole community. This has the advantage 
of minimizing the number of the input parameters, as all the important physiological 
rates are size – dependent. In this sense, a variety of species in the planktonic 
community can be represented in the model without the need of introducing 
different parameters for each one. Also, the description of the physical world is 
based on “first principles”, meaning that all the processes that govern the system 
follow fundamental rules, such as fluid dynamics, diffusion principles and 
==PAGE:62==
58 
 
conservation laws (Andersen & Visser, 2022). This gives the benefit to build a model 
where all the processes that govern are easy to understand and interpret from a 
physical and biological point of view. Most importantly, since there is no calibration 
based on a specific area involved on the output of the model, apart from the HTL 
mortality coefficient in this project, the model can easily be used to describe a 
variety of ecosystems and expand to represent the global oceans.  
Regarding the generalists, the inclusion of diatoms would be an important step 
affecting both copepods and the carbon export efficiency. It has been shown that in 
most cases large – cell diatoms dominate in phytoplankton blooms, which in turn has 
the potential to sustain larger copepods. Moreover, diatoms’ siliceous shells and 
once they die they can sink very fast mediating the carbon export flux (Ducklow et 
al., 2001). Consequently, the incorporation of silica as a state variable in the NUM 
model would also be useful to better capture the dynamics of diatoms. As 
mentioned in section 4.1, iron is another crucial element in capturing the dynamics 
of the planktonic community, especially in the Southern Ocean and hence it is 
important to be included in the NUM model. 
Oxygen minimum zones (OMZ) in the ocean are projected to be expanded due to the 
ongoing climate change (Zhou et al., 2022).  Grodzins et al., 2016 investigating how 
the copepod Calanus pacificus behaves under different oxygen levels found that 
there is a threshold of 1.5 mg DO L−1 below which the survival of Calanus pacificus 
decreases leading to total mortality in a period of one hour at oxygen level of 0.9 
mg DO L−1. Similar response has been observed for other calanoid copepods too.  
Consequently, it is appropriate to include the oxygen dynamics in the NUM model.  
Salinity is another important environmental parameter. The ongoing anthropogenic 
climate change has the potential to alter the global water cycle with a projected 
pattern of lower salinities in high and low latitudes and higher salinities to 
subtropical regions (Du et al., 2019). Soulié et al., 2022 showed that the oxygen 
consumption of the copepod Eurytemora affinis increased when exposed to lower 
salinity conditions. Moreover, Sugie et al., 2020 points out the important effect of 
salinity on the structure of the phytoplankton community in the western Arctic 
Ocean and highlights potential synergies with other abiotic factors, such as 
temperature and 𝐶𝑂2 concentration. Hence, the inclusion of the salinity could 
enhance the NUM model’s performance in representing the planktonic community. 
Currently, the NUM model does not cover the Diel Vertical Migration (DVM) 
explicitly. However, DVM is an important strategy of copepods to escape visual 
predators during the day, follow the vertical distribution of their resources and 
==PAGE:63==
59 
 
reduce their exposure to ultraviolet radiation (Sha et al., 2021). Moreover, through 
the DVM copepods mediate an active carbon transport to higher depths, a process 
that the NUM model cannot capture. Hansen & Visser, 2016 suggest that in the 
north Atlantic Ocean carbon exported below the mixed layer through the DVM of 
large copepods accounts for up to 27% of the total carbon export flux in that area. 
Another important survival strategy of copepods that the NUM model does not 
capture is the production of resting eggs. As a response to fast and abrupt 
fluctuations of the environmental conditions, it has been found that embryos of 
calanoid copepods switch into a dormancy state. Diapause resting eggs can also be 
produced directly by female copepods and after reaching the benthos they can rest 
there for up to 70 years (Holm et al., 2018). A further improvement could be to 
introduce passive copepod – feeders in the model, as there are important trade – 
offs involved in each feeding strategy (Kiørboe, 2011). 
Finally, the POM component in the NUM model does not consider for potential 
particle aggregations while sinking. However, such aggregations are important for 
the carbon export since they form larger structures which sink faster (Turner, 2015). 
Moreover, the NUM model does not account for the fragmentation of POM during 
its consumption by copepods, which might create smaller, slow – sinking particles 
being taken up by microbes (Turner, 2015). 
5. Conclusion and future perspectives 
The aim of this project was to investigate the spatiotemporal distribution of biomass 
and size of copepods and POM in the global ocean. This was done mechanistically 
through the NUM model, using the size of the planktonic community as its master – 
trait. It has been shown that the NUM model managed to capture important 
biological properties and dynamics of the planktonic community, such as prey – 
predation interactions, competition for limited resources and seasonality in the 
global ocean. Moreover, it was shown that the size – structure of the planktonic 
community can regulate the POM production and affect the carbon export flux in 
higher depths. In a fast-changing world, knowledge about the geospatial distribution 
of copepod’s biomass and size could be a useful tool in better understanding the 
effect of climate change on the ocean and might be used as an ecosystem health 
indicator. Copepods are a fundamental link in the marine food chains and play a 
crucial role in the overall health of the marine ecosystems. We as humans rely on 
these tiny organisms for many of the ecosystem services we enjoy, from regulating 
the earth’s climate and the efficiency of the carbon pump to provision services, such 
as lucrative fisheries in upwelling areas. Overall, robust models like the NUM model 
==PAGE:64==
60 
 
should work in the direction to be able to incorporate information about future 
climate scenarios. This can provide policy makers a useful insight about the state of 
our ocean in the future and potential tipping points and help them draw the most 
appropriate management strategies in time. Finally, there is a high potential in 
coupling the NUM model with other models specializing in higher trophic level 
dynamics. An example of this is the FishErles Size and functional Type model, FEISTY, 
developed in the Centre for Ocean Life of DTU, which focuses on marine fish food 
webs.
