Abstract
This thesis focuses on the development and characterization of a setup for characterizing polarization-
maintaining (PM) fibers. The primary objective was to verify the alignment of the birefringent axes
with respect to the geometric axis of the PM fibers. The setup was designed to overcome limita-
tions of existing methods, which typically only determines the geometric axis. The initial phase of
the project involved building the setup and ensuring accurate alignment of its components. A cali-
bration procedure was developed and implemented to establish a relationship between the different
components. With the setup in place, measurements were conducted on PM fibers to assess the
alignment of their birefringent axes with respect to the geometric axis. The characterization mea-
surements yielded positive outcomes, indicating good alignment of the birefringent axes with the
geometric axis in different fibers. Repeatability and consistency measurements further validated
the reliability of the setup. Moreover, investigations on a PM fiber with suspected misalignment
revealed significant deviations between the birefringent and geometric axes, thereby confirming
the overall capability of the setup and fulfilling its intended purpose.

1
Introduction
Optical fibers are predominantly used in today‚Äôs world and have diverse applications beyond their
primary use in data communications. Several characteristics of light are utilized while working
with optical fibers and one of them is polarization of light. Conventional optical fibers have a
change in the output state of polarization making them unsuitable for applications where the input
state of polarization needs to be maintained, because internal mechanical stress and heat fluctua-
tion induce birefringence and cross-talk across polarization modes. Hence, the output polarization
state is not controlled. Polarization-maintaining (PM) fibers, a type of specialized fiber, are used to
address this problem. They are based on the fiber‚Äôs purposeful induction of birefringence. External
perturbations are disregarded and the fiber would retain the polarization state of light along its two
birefringent axes since intentional birefringence is greater than birefringence caused by external
forces.
PM fibers are utilized in specialized applications like fiber optic gyroscopes, fiber optic sensing,
interferometry, and quantum key distribution. Since polarized light is needed as input for the mod-
ulator, they are also frequently employed in telecommunications to connect a source laser and a
modulator. Due to its high cost and higher attenuation than single-mode fiber, PM fiber is rarely
employed for long-distance transmission.
After producing any fiber, characterizing it is a crucial step to do. The parameters that are taken
into account when characterizing a PM fiber are similar to those of conventional optical fibers, but
there are also some additional parameters that must be taken into account, including beat length,
polarization extinction ratio (PER), and h-parameter, with the help of which the performance of
the fiber is assessed.
The aforementioned parameters are accurately tested using current industrial standard setups. An
important parameter in the case of a PM fiber is to assess the alignment of the birefringent axes
with respect to the fiber‚Äôs geometric axis. By measuring the fiber‚Äôs geometry and examining its end
face, it is possible to determine the geometric axis which is defined by the stress rods, using image
analysis. Nevertheless, there has been a problem where the birefringent axes are not aligned with
the geometric axis, especially after the fibers have been connectorized in PM connectors using a
ferrule. The misalignment is either due to the fiber manufacturing error which results in a low
internal birefringence of the fiber or due to the rotation of the fiber with respect to the connector
keyway caused by the ferrule.
The thesis‚Äô main goal is to construct a setup to measure the end face geometry of the PM fiber in
order to determine the geometric axis and simultaneously assess how well the birefringent axes
are aligned with respect to the geometric axis. This enables to identify or eliminate the possibility
of birefringent axes misalignment due to low birefringence, resulting from manufacturing errors.
The thesis also includes developing a concept that would allow the setup to be calibrated before
any measurement. There are different types of PM fibers, and these vary depending on how the
stress-applied parts are shaped.

Theory
To be able to design, implement and characterize an experimental setup that is to be used for
characterizing PM fibers, some basic theory behind the science involved in making PM fibers
needs to be understood. The phenomena which pave the way for PM fibers include polarization
of light and birefringence. This section explores the theory behind birefringence, polarization of
light, the different states of polarization, and the important ways in which the polarization of light

Optical Fiber
An optical fiber is a waveguide made of a cylindrical dielectric material, typically silica glass,
with a central core that guides light. The core is surrounded by an outer cladding with a slightly
lower refractive index. When light rays in the core hit the boundary between the core and cladding
at angles with smaller inclinations to the fiber axis, they undergo total internal reflection and are
guided through the core without any loss. However, rays with a greater inclination to the fiber axis
lose some of their power at each reflection and are not guided through the fiber [1]. Figure 7 shows
a schematic of an optical fiber, with core, cladding, and depiction of light propagation through total
internal reflection.
Figure 7: A schematic of an optical fiber with core, cladding, and depiction of light propagation.
The figure shown above indicates the refractive indices of the core and the cladding. The core is
characterized by a refractive index of n2, whereas the cladding has a refractive index of n1. The
refractive index of the core is higher than that of the cladding, which allows for the guiding of
light. In general, light propagates in the form of modes, in both optical fiber and planar waveguide.

Each mode moves along the waveguide‚Äôs axis with its own propagation constant and group veloc-

Optical Modes
The fiber modes are solutions to the wave equations that fit the boundary conditions. Each mode
is characterized by different spatial distributions. The guided modes are usually characterized as
Linearly Polarized (LP) modes and are indexed LPlm, where l = 0,1,2.. is named the azimuthal
mode number and m = 1,2,3... is the radial mode number. In other words, the guided modes corre-
spond to standing waves in the radial and angular direction. The radial mode number corresponds
to the number of nodes in the radial direction and the azimuthal mode number corresponds to the
number of nodes in the azimuthal direction. It should be noted that for l > 0, there are two or-
thogonal degenerate modes per LPlm combination, these are often indexed as LPlma and LPlmb. The
different linearly polarized modes (i.e. different lm-valued modes) are also all orthogonal to each
other. This means that the power of each LPlm-mode is isolated from every other mode traveling
in the fiber. For example, a six-mode fiber may have LP01 (fundamental mode), LP11, LP21, LP02,
LP31 and LP12. As all modes with non-zero l exist with two different orientations, the full number
of spatial modes, counting separately the different orientations, is actually 10. Also counting po-
larization directions, one would arrive at the total number of modes of 20 [1]. An illustration of a

2.3
Birefringence
A standard single-mode fiber is really a two-mode fiber. Above the cut-off wavelength, the fiber
supports two modes of propagation, having orthogonal linear polarization states. In a single-mode
fiber with perfect circularity, the two polarization modes are degenerate, i.e., they have exactly
the same propagation characteristics. Real single-mode fibers possess small asymmetries which
cause the two polarization modes to split, however, the difference remains very small. As the two
polarization modes have similar propagation characteristics it is easy to transfer power from one
mode to the other due to bending or twisting of the fiber or due to any external perturbations, and
hence change the state of polarization of the light propagating in the fiber [5].
2.3
Birefringence
Birefringence is an optical characteristic found in certain materials where the polarization of light
determines the experienced refractive index.
Birefringence naturally occurs only in optically
anisotropic materials, however, optically isotropic materials are also subjected to stress, chemical
regeneration, or electric or magnetic inductions to create the phenomenon. Anisotropic materials
exhibit non-uniform refractive indices throughout their structure. Therefore, when light propa-
gates through such materials, it undergoes double refraction, meaning it experiences two different
refractive indices depending on the direction of polarization of light as shown in the figure 9.
Figure 9: Light propagation through a birefringent material. The extraordinary ray is the one that
gets refracted as it exits the medium, whereas the ordinary ray is the one that exits the medium
unchanged.
According to the fundamentals of light propagation in fibers, there exist two states of polarization
that are orthogonal to each other for a perfect, cylindrical fiber. Ideally, a standard single-mode
fiber should not experience birefringence due to silica being an isotropic material. However, due
to manufacturing problems, practical fiber will be imperfect, e.g. since there are limitations in
creating a fiber with a completely circular symmetric core. The symmetry in the fiber is therefore
broken and the two orthogonal modes will thereby experience different propagation constants.
This imperfection is what will lead to birefringence in the fiber, meaning that the two polarizations
will propagate with different phase velocities and group velocities. This means that there will be a
fast and a slow axis in the fiber (as shown in figure 10) meaning nx Őł= ny, where x and y correspond
to the two axes [4].

mode dispersion (PMD). PMD is quantified in terms of differential group delay (DGD), which is
the average DGD of the smaller sections of the fiber.
To avoid the phenomenon of pulse spreading which is also known as PMD, a PM fiber is used. This
type of fiber has in some way been changed so that the two polarization modes experience signif-
icantly different refractive indices, and hereby a strong birefringence is introduced into the fiber,
which is much stronger than the random birefringence that comes from the fiber imperfections and
external perturbations. This means that the refractive index in one direction, orthogonal to the di-
rection of propagation z, is larger than the refractive index in the other direction over the entire fiber
or at least in the area where the mode propagates, i.e., nx > ny, despite the fiber imperfections [4].
The total birefringence experienced by any fiber is the vectorial sum of the internal and external
birefringence experienced by the fiber. In the case of PM fibers, the internal birefringence is made
very large such that the external birefringence does not really affect the birefringence of the fiber.
Hence, by controlling the fiber‚Äôs birefringence, it becomes feasible to control the polarization state
within the fiber, thereby maintaining the polarization state of light. PM fibers are used if a certain
state of polarization is really important to maintain, or if one wants to avoid pulse broadening or
PMD. More details about the principle of PM fibers and their types are discussed in section 2.5.

A polarizer is a type of optical filter that allows light waves of one polarization to pass through

below.

designed to separate an incident, randomly polarized, monochromatic beam into its horizontal and
vertical linear polarization components as shown in the figure 12. The coating is constructed of
alternating high and low-index materials [13].

have the need for preserving the polarization state of light. The fundamental idea of PM fibers
is to get a significant difference, between propagation constants of each of the two polarization
modes. When the two polarization modes have very different propagation constants it is much
more difficult to couple light between the modes, and hence, if only one polarization mode is
excited, the power stays in that state, and the polarization is maintained throughout the fiber. The
above-mentioned process is done by introducing intentional birefringence to the core, which results
in an orthogonal fast axis and slow axis along the length of the fiber, also known as birefringent
axes together. The different ways in which the birefringence is introduced and the different types
of PM fibers are discussed in this section.
2.5.1
Birefringence in PM fibers
PM fibers are divided into a number of groups as shown in the figure 14. Low- and high-birefringence
fibers make up the two primary categories. Low birefringence fibers are typically used in telecom-
munications and are so sensitive to external disturbances. High-birefringence fibers are further
classified into fibers that support one polarization mode and two orthogonal polarization modes.
High-birefringence, two-mode (polarization modes) fibers are commonly referred to as PM fibers.
There are two essentially distinct approaches for attaining high birefringence. The geometry-
induced birefringence is obtained due to an asymmetric shape of the fiber cross-section. Fibers
using stress-induced birefringence also have non-symmetric layers, but here the geometry induces
an unequal distribution of thermal stress causing birefringence via elasto-optic effect [5].

2.5.2
Birefringence induced by stress
Stress-induced birefringence arises when mechanical stresses are frozen into the fiber during draw-
ing, leading to a change in refractive index due to the elasto-optic effect. Birefringence is created
by introducing heavily doped non-circular layers in the fiber cross-section. In the case of PANDA
fibers, boron doping is used for the stress rods, because boron has a large coefficient of thermal
expansion compared to pure silica. When a fiber is drawn, the asymmetrical stress layers and the
outside silica substrate have different rates of thermal expansion, which result in transverse thermal
stresses in the fiber [5].
The birefringence of the fiber is of course not only dependent on the thermal expansion coefficient
of the stress region but also on the actual shape of the stress region. The bow-tie fiber, elliptical-
cladding fiber, and PANDA fiber are the three most popular designs as shown in the figure 15.
With regard to uniformity of the stress distribution, maximum birefringence, minimal attenuation,
or ease of manufacture, each of these designs offers a unique set of benefits. It is possible to
identify the best parameters for each design‚Äôs size and position of the stress region [5].

An advantage of stress-induced birefringence compared to geometry-induced birefringence is that

As mentioned earlier a PM fiber has two orthogonal polarization modes. The propagation constants
of these polarization modes differ, and the direction of polarization of the mode with the larger
propagation constant is referred to as the slow axis. Conversely, the direction of polarization of the
mode with the smaller propagation constant is called the fast axis. The fast axis and the slow axis
together are referred to as the birefringent axes. Figure 16 shows the fast axis and the slow axis of

to external disturbances, making it more commonly used.
2.5.3
Preform manufacturing
The stress level in a stress-birefringent fiber is raised because the stress layers are made to have
a substantially higher coefficient of thermal expansion than pure silica. Additionally, in order
to simplify the design and production of the fiber, the impact of the stress layers on the optical
properties of the fiber‚ÄĒapart from the birefringence‚ÄĒshould be reduced. The bulk of the typical
dopants utilized in the production of fibers, including GeO2, P2O5, B2O3, and Al2O3, have a higher
thermal expansion coefficient than pure silica. Nevertheless, only B203 has a lower refractive index
than pure silica. This is the main justification for doping the stress layers with boron. As the stress
layers have a low refractive index, light cannot be guided into them, assuring the single-mode
operation of the fiber [5]. The schematic of the refractive index profile of an ideal PANDA-type

2.5.4
Light propagation in PM fibers
An interesting aspect of high-birefringence fibers is the way the state of polarization changes as
the light propagates through the fiber. The refractive indices for x- and y-polarized light in a
single-mode fiber differ, which breaks the degeneracy of the two linearly polarized LP01 modes.
This difference causes the modes to have different propagation constants, resulting in the evolu-
tion of the polarization state along the fiber, if both modes are excited at the input end. Although
all single-mode fibers support two orthogonally polarized LP01 modes, the degeneracy is minimal
in standard single-mode fibers, making the evolution of the polarization state very susceptible to
external perturbations. However, in high-birefringence fibers, the effective refractive index differ-
ence between the x- and y-polarized LP01 modes is high, leading to a predictable evolution of the
polarization state along the fiber [5].

The optical fiber end has to be prepared in a certain way before it could be placed in a setup for

An infinity-corrected tube lens is a type of microscope objective lens that is commonly used in mi-
croscopy applications. The term "infinity-corrected" refers to a specific design of the collimating
lens that produces a parallel beam of light focused at infinity, rather than at a fixed distance. This
design allows for additional optical components, such as filters and dichroic mirrors, to be placed

The infinity-corrected tube lens is typically used in combination with an infinity-corrected ob-

which in turn projects the image onto the camera or detector. The infinity-corrected tube lens is
placed before the camera or detector and collimates or focuses the light depending on the input,
allowing for the addition of optical components without affecting the quality of the image.

Reason for using a SLED
A SLED is a type of semiconductor optical source that combines some of the characteristics of
both laser diodes and light-emitting diodes (LEDs). Like a laser diode, a SLED produces a high-
intensity output with good spectral purity, but it has a broader spectrum than a laser diode.
The short coherence length of a SLED makes it well-suited for measuring the PER of PM fiber
because it reduces the effects of interference caused by reflections from the fiber‚Äôs ends. The broad
spectral width of a SLED also makes it easier to align along the birefringent axis, which is impor-
tant for accurate measurement of the PER.
When a well-polarized light from a broadband source is incident on the fiber with off-axis align-
ment, the light at the end of the fiber may appear to be randomly polarized. This is because the
beat length is wavelength-dependent and therefore, makes the polarization rotate over the broad-
band spectrum of the light source. However, if a narrow spectrum is analyzed, the light appears to
be well polarized even if it is not well aligned with the birefringent axis.
Therefore, it is important to consider the wavelength-dependent and length-dependent nature of
beat-length in PM fibers when aligning the light with one of the birefringent axes, and using a
broadband source like a SLED.

To assess the setup‚Äôs capability to detect misalignments between the birefringent axis and the ge-
ometric axis, a "bad" PM fiber with unsatisfactory geometric parameters, particularly related to
the stress rods, was chosen for the measurement. The selection of such a fiber was based on the
understanding that in cases where the stress rods exhibit uneven stress distribution or stress not
directed towards the core, the birefringence in the fiber becomes low, therefore the birefringent
axes become misaligned with respect to the geometric axis when subjected to external perturba-
tions, additionally, this fiber was earlier suspected of having birefringent axes misalignment. It is
important to note that there is no definitive theory or proof stating that uneven geometry directly
results in low birefringence. In fact, a fiber with seemingly poor geometry can still maintain high
birefringence if the stress exerted by the stress rods is sufficient to counteract external disturbances.
Conversely, a fiber with visually good geometry may still be susceptible to external perturbations
if the stress is either insufficient or not properly directed towards the core. Figure 44 displays the
end-face image of the "bad" PM fiber, clearly showing the unsymmetrical shape of the stress rods.

slow axis are orthogonal to each other. The subsequent evaluation involves comparing the value
of "Biref_cal" with the value of "New_geom_cal" to determine whether the birefringent axis is

Discussion & Outlook
In this section of the thesis, the focus is on providing a comprehensive overview and discussion of
the results obtained in Chapter 4. The objective is to summarize the key findings and observations
from various measurements and place them in a broader context, particularly in relation to their
potential applications and their comparability with existing industrial standard setups for charac-
terizing polarization-maintaining fibers. Furthermore, the discussion will also include an outlook
on what future improvements could be done to enhance the accuracy of the setup and align it more
closely with the standards prevalent in the industry.
As stated in section 3.1.2, all the conducted measurements focused on PM fibers with an 80 ¬Ķm
cladding diameter, and the majority of available 80 ¬Ķm fibers were designed for an operating wave-
length of 830 nm. Moreover, the PER setup was specifically designed for fibers with a center wave-
length of 830 nm. Consequently, it was not feasible to conduct similar measurements on fibers with
a center wavelength of 1550 nm. Although the underlying operating principle remains the same,
it would have been valuable to perform measurements on fibers with a center wavelength of 1550
nm as well, as it would have provided further evidence of the setup‚Äôs capabilities. Based on the
various measurements carried out on 80¬Ķm fibers, it was observed that the setup has demonstrated
the ability to accurately determine the geometric axis and the birefringent axes, even in scenarios
where the birefringent axes were misaligned with respect to the geometric axis. Additionally, the
consistency of the measurements was found to be high, both in the calibration measurements and
the final measurements with the PM fibers. This high level of consistency serves as evidence of
the reliability and repeatability of the measurements conducted using the setup. As previously dis-
cussed, the misalignment of the birefringent axes with the geometric axis is attributed to external
perturbations exploiting the low internal birefringence of the fiber. Consequently, it was expected
that the alignment of the birefringent axes with respect to the geometric axis would change further
with the measurements made by the addition of more external perturbations. However, contrary to
the expectation, it was observed that the misalignment actually decreased. This unexpected result
was attributed to the possibility that the applied loads coincidentally pushed the birefringent axes
closer to the geometric axis instead of further away. Nonetheless, this observation still confirms
the low birefringence of the fiber since there was variation in the value of "‚ąÜGeom_brief" across
different measurements. These measurements provided further evidence of the setup‚Äôs capability
to accurately determine the geometric axis and the birefringent axes under different circumstances.
As mentioned earlier the birefringent axes alignment with respect to the geometric axis is not
definitively linked to the fiber geometry but rather depends on the level of internal birefringence
imposed by the stress rods. This was exemplified by the measurement of a PM fiber with seem-
ingly perfect geometry, yet exhibiting birefringent axes misalignment. Given more time, it would
have been valuable to conduct additional measurements on this fiber by introducing further exter-
nal perturbations. This would have allowed for a comparison with the previous fiber that exhibited
birefringent axes misalignment, providing insights into the similarities or differences in their be-
havior. Furthermore, it would have been beneficial to measure a few more fibers with poor geom-
etry to examine the possibility of well aligned birefringent axes in some or all of them.

Such measurements could have served as additional substantial evidence to support the notion that
fiber geometry lacks a definitive relationship with birefringent axes alignment. However, the un-
availability of such fibers, as they are typically discarded due to their poor geometry, and the time
constraint hindered the realization of these measurements.
Considering the cases where the birefringent axes were well aligned with respect to the geometric
axis. In order to further enhance the measurement accuracy and reduce the value of "‚ąÜGeom_brief",
it is important to address the small variations that still exist. One potential approach to achieve this
is by focusing on reducing the standard deviation of the measurement during the calibration pro-
cess for the rotating polarizer. By improving the stability of the system, particularly in the areas
that directly impact the calibration process, a more stable value for "Pol_cal" can be obtained. One
suggestion is to implement a stable stage in front of the rotating polarizer, securely holding the
polarizing cube in place. Additionally, a stable holder with alignment knobs for the collimator and
the SLED, used for the calibration measurement, would be beneficial. These adjustments would
contribute to the overall stability of the system and subsequently lead to a reduction in the standard
deviation for the value of "Pol_cal". By implementing these measures, the value of "‚ąÜGeom_brief"
can be further minimized, improving the overall accuracy and reliability of the setup.
In addressing the stability and accuracy of the setup, it is important to consider the reflections
observed in the images during the PM fiber end face measurements. Although these reflections,
originating from the non-polarizing beam splitter, may not be of significant concern unless they
interfere with the image analysis process, they have the potential to affect the accuracy of the
measurement results. During the image analysis, it was noticed that the reflections did not have
a substantial impact on the process. However, some minor difficulties were encountered in accu-
rately tracing the cladding and stress rods in certain images. This could be attributed to both a
poor cleave of the fiber and the presence of reflections. It is probable that these reflections con-
tribute to the increase in the value of "‚ąÜGeom_brief". To address this issue and improve the overall
image quality, it is suggested to utilize dichroic mirrors within the appropriate cut-off wavelength
range(525nm), to eliminate the reflections. Implementing this solution would require an additional
green light source for coupling light into the core during the geometry measurement, as the SLED
with a wavelength of 830nm cannot be used for this purpose. However, since coupling light into
the core with birefringent axis alignment is not necessary for the geometry measurement, using a
separate green light source is a viable option. By incorporating dichroic mirrors and mitigating
the impact of reflections, it is expected that the image analysis process will be further improved,
resulting in more accurate measurements and enhanced overall image quality.
Finally, the developed setup for characterizing PM fibers was proven to be a reliable prototype for
verifying the alignment of birefringent axes with the geometric axis. To improve the accuracy of
the results and align the setup with industry standards, the above mentioned suggestions could be
incorporated. Additionally, automating the 3-axis alignment stage and creating an interface having
all the different programs used, would make it more user-friendly.

Conclusion
The objective of the thesis was to develop a setup for characterizing PM fibers and verifying the
alignment of the birefringent axes with respect to the geometric axis. This setup aimed to im-
prove upon existing methods that only determined the geometric axis of the PM fiber. The setup
built during the thesis has the capability of simultaneously determining the geometric axis and the
birefringent axes and verifying the alignment with respect to each other. This was particularly
important because misalignment of the birefringent axes was observed in PM fibers after they are
connectorized into PM connectors. Such misalignment could be attributed to factors like low bire-
fringence resulting from manufacturing errors or rotation of the fiber away from the keyway during
the connectorization process. The setup aimed to identify and address the possibility of misalign-
ment caused by low birefringence arising from manufacturing errors.
The thesis primarily focused on constructing the setup for characterizing PM fibers, followed by
characterizing the setup‚Äôs performance through a series of measurements. Ensuring accurate align-
ment of the setup‚Äôs components was an important aspect during its construction, as it directly im-
pacted measurement accuracy. A method was developed to calibrate the setup, considering the
arbitrary angle of the rotating polarizer and the possibility of camera tilt relative to the horizontal
surface. Initially, the idea was to calibrate the camera and rotating polarizer simultaneously, but
due to the setup‚Äôs optimization for focusing a divergent beam rather than a collimated beam used
during calibration, separate calibration procedures were implemented for both camera and the ro-
tating polarizer. The calibration process successfully demonstrated repeatability and consistency
in the results, enhancing the overall accuracy of the setup.
After successfully calibrating the setup, the measurements with the PM fiber were initiated. The
measurement to determine the light-coupled birefringent axis yielded positive results. However,
during the measurements for the geometric axis, the presence of ambient lighting caused inter-
ference and significantly reduced the image quality of the fiber end face. This interference posed
challenges for the image analysis program to accurately determine the geometric axis. To over-
come this issue, the setup was relocated to a "dark" room, where ambient lighting was eliminated.
This relocation improved the image resolution, enabling the image analysis program to accurately
measure the fiber‚Äôs geometry and determine the geometric axis.
After verifying the accuracy of individual segments of the setup, the next step was to integrate them
to enable measurements on PM fibers. The initial measurement conducted on a PM fiber was suc-
cessful, showing good alignment between the birefringent axes and the geometric axis. Although
there were minor deviations between the calibrated angles of the birefringent axis and the geomet-
ric axis, they were considered acceptable taking into account the small standard deviation in the
calibration measurements and the overall stability of the setup. However, future improvements are
necessary to enhance the accuracy in this aspect. Subsequently, two sets of ten measurements were
performed using the same fiber to assess the consistency and repeatability of the measurements.
The results obtained from these measurements were highly satisfactory, further validating the ef-
fectiveness of the setup.

The setup‚Äôs ability to identify birefringent axes misalignment in PM fibers was an important aspect
to validate its overall capability. To test this, a fiber with poor geometric parameters suspected
of having birefringent axes misalignment was selected for measurement. It should be noted that
there is no conclusive evidence linking fiber geometry with birefringent axes misalignment. The
measurements on the chosen fiber revealed misalignment between the birefringent axes and the
geometric axis. To ensure the consistency of the results, additional measurements were conducted,
which consistently confirmed the misalignment. To further investigate, external perturbations were
applied to the fiber, and it was observed that these perturbations had a significant impact on the
measurements, indicating the presence of low birefringence in the fiber. As a result, the overall
capability of the setup was validated, as it successfully identified the misalignment of the birefrin-
gent axes in the tested fiber, even in the presence of external perturbations.
In conclusion, the developed setup during the thesis provided valuable insights into the character-
ization of PM fibers, demonstrating its capability to verify birefringent axes alignment and iden-
tify misalignment. While the setup proved to be a working model prototype, there are areas for
improvement with respect to improving the overall accuracy of the setup and making it more user-
friendly, thereby aligning it closer to the current industrial standard setups.
