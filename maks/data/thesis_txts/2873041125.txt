Abstract
Thermosensitive hydrogels are a specialized class of hydrogels that undergo sol-to-gel
transition upon heating. As they are flowable at lower temperatures, they offer minimally
invasive delivery and, characteristically for hydrogels, promise localized delivery of drugs
and cells, that are embedded in an ECM-mimicking environment promoting cell adhesion,
migration, and proliferation [1].
Like all hydrogels, thermosensitive hydrogels have highly tunable properties, including
their stiffness and degradation rate — directly affected by their crosslinking mechanism [2,
3]. Compared to physical crosslinking, chemical crosslinking results in irreversible bonds
and is often employed as a secondary strategy to enhance the mechanical properties of
the final hydrogel.
Few studies have explored employing thermosensitive liposomes encapsulating reactive
crosslinking agents, which are released when the system is heated to physiological tem-
peratures to trigger chemical crosslinking as a primary mechanism in injectable and print-
able hydrogels [4]. In this thesis, we investigate the use of thermosensitive liposomes to
encapsulate and release a diazide crosslinker to trigger crosslinking of 4-arm PEG-DBCO.
First, by studying the hydrogel synthesis by mixing the azido-PEG5-azide and 4-arm PEG-
DBCO in different molar ratios to create a 5% w/v PEG hydrogel, we showed that the
precursors rapidly form a uniform gel when mixed in molar ratios of azide to DBCO ranging
from 4:5 to 2:1.
Second, to address the limitations of existing azide detection methods — such as low
sensitivity and high complexity — we designed a new assay to detect azide groups in
aqueous media to quantify the release of the crosslinker from the liposomes. The as-
say combines disrupting the liposomes in 70% acetonitrile with quantifying the di-azide
crosslinker by detecting the click reaction between azide groups and acrlong dbco using
ultraviolet-visible spectrophotometry. Our assay demonstrates specificity and sensitivity,
detecting between 66 𝜇M and 1.5 mM of the crosslinker in the presence of lipids.
Then, to design and characterize a robust thermosensitive liposomes system, we synthe-
sised the liposomes using the interdigitation-fusion method by mixing DPPC and DMPC
in a 9:1 molar ratio to ensure thermotropism in 37°C. Tested by dynamic light scatter-
ing, the liposomes measured 1.7 𝜇m on average. We were able to encapsulate small
amounts of azido-PEG5-azide in the liposomes; however, in higher concentrations, the
method yielded an opaque, white, and viscous solution that does not successfully produce
thermosensitive liposomes. These findings demonstrate that the interaction between the
liposomes and their encapsulated cargo is crucial to make an efficient delivery system.
We argue that altering the chemistry of the di-azide crosslinker to eliminate the use of
polyethylene glycol spacer should be the starting point for further investigating the use
of thermosensitive liposomes to trigger strain-promoted azide-alkyne cycloaddition aided
gelation.

Introduction
Hydrogels are three-dimensional polymeric networks capable of retaining large amounts
of water, resulting in a highly hydrated, tissue-like environment. Owing to their biocom-
patibility and tunable mechanical and physical properties, hydrogels have become central
to applications in tissue engineering, regenerative medicine, and drug delivery [2, 1, 5].
They can be engineered to degrade over time or remain stable, serve as carriers for cells,
growth factors, or therapeutics. They have been shown to support bone regeneration,
wound healing, and sustained release of bioactive compounds.
As hydrogel technologies evolve, the demand for more sophisticated and processable
systems has grown. Two major innovations include injectable and printable hydrogels,
which allow for minimally invasive and spatially defined delivery of therapeutic materials [6,
7]. In particular, extrusion-based bioprinting, and more recently embedded bioprinting, en-
able the deposition of cell-laden hydrogels within support matrices to construct complex
3D tissue-like architectures with high resolution and cell viability [8, 9]. A key challenge
in these systems is developing hydrogels that are processable at room temperature but
rapidly stabilize under physiological conditions. Thermosensitive hydrogels, which un-
dergo a sol-to-gel transition in response to temperature, are especially attractive. Their
ability to flow during injection or printing and solidify upon warming simplifies handling and
promotes in situ gelation [6].
However, thermogelling alone often fails to provide sufficient mechanical strength or long-
term structural integrity. To overcome these limitations, secondary crosslinking strategies
— such as chemical conjugation — are frequently employed to enhance the mechanical
properties and biofunctionality of the final construct. One promising strategy to trigger
crosslinking at physiological temperatures is using thermosensitive liposomes (TSLs) as
temperature-responsive carriers. TSLs can encapsulate reactive crosslinking agents and
release them upon heating, allowing the gelation to be temporally and spatially controlled.
This method minimizes premature crosslinking and enables homogeneous precursor dis-
tribution, which is particularly important for injectable or printed systems. Although initially
introduced by Westhaus et al. [4], this concept remains underexplored.
This thesis focuses on the design of a thermoresponsive hydrogel system utilizing TSLs
loaded with a diazide crosslinker, which undergoes strain-promoted azide-alkyne cycload-
dition upon mild heating. The reaction offers high specificity, rapid kinetics, and minimal
cytotoxicity, making it ideal for biomedical applications.
In the project, we first investigated how the molar ratio of the crosslinker to polymer af-
fects the formation of a gel, aiming to discover crosslinker concentrations needed to trigger
rapid gelation of a polyethylene glycol network. Secondly, we aimed to develop and vali-
date a novel assay for detecting and quantifying diazide encapsulation within liposomes.
Although liposomal drug delivery has been widely studied, quantifying encapsulated small
reactive molecules like diazides remains challenging due to their chemical reactivity and
low UV absorbance. By addressing this analytical gap, the project supports better control
over crosslinker dosage and release kinetics in TSLs-based hydrogel systems. Lastly, we
aimed to design and characterize a robust methodology for creating TSLs to encapsulate
the diazide crosslinker and release it at physiological temperatures.

Theoretical Background
This chapter provides an overview of the research and theoretical concepts relevant to the
study. It establishes the context for the investigation by reviewing key literature, identifies
the gaps that this thesis aims to address, and introduces analytical methods used in the
project.
2.1
Hydrogels
Hydrogels are highly hydrated, tissue-mimicking polymer networks with excellent bio-
compatibility and tunable mechanical and biochemical properties. These characteristics
have made them particularly interesting in diverse tissue engineering and regenerative
medicine applications.
Depending on the desired functionality, hydrogels can be for-
mulated from various polymers with tailored degradation profiles, stiffness, crosslinking
strategies, and bioactivity [2].
2.1.1
Injectable vs Printable
Injectable and printable hydrogels represent two primary strategies for creating versa-
tile platforms for delivering cells, drugs, and signaling molecules in a spatially controlled
and minimally invasive manner. In regenerative medicine and tissue engineering, these
hydrogels can serve as scaffolds to support cell growth, extracellular matrix (ECM) remod-
eling, and even act as load-bearing structures depending on their mechanical properties.
As their versatility enables controlled drug release, defect-specific shaping, and integra-
tion with biofabrication techniques, both platforms are increasingly designed for enhanced
biological activity, responsiveness, and clinical applicability.
Injectable hydrogels are initially liquid or semi-liquid and form a crosslinked network upon
administration. Their injectability allows for easy delivery to target sites with minimal sur-
gical intervention, reduced healing time, and improved conformity to irregular anatomical
shapes. Gelation is typically triggered in situ by physical (e.g., temperature, pH, ionic
strength) or chemical crosslinking mechanisms [7]. Temperature-responsive injectable
systems often use polymers with a lower critical solution temperature (LCST), transition-
ing from sol to gel upon warming to physiological temperatures.
These hydrogels are widely explored in drug delivery because they provide localized, sus-
tained release, thereby reducing systemic side effects [7]. However, burst release, weak
mechanical strength, and potential cytotoxicity from some crosslinking agents are notable
drawbacks. In regenerative medicine, injectable hydrogels serve as ECM-like scaffolds
capable of supporting cell viability, proliferation, and differentiation within complex, defect-
matching geometries. They are also commonly used in space-filling applications, such as
post-surgical cavity filling and vascular embolization, though challenges remain related to
swelling-induced pressure and limited mechanical integrity.
Printable hydrogels, in contrast, are engineered for extrusion or deposition in a layer-
by-layer manner to construct 3D structures [6]. They must exhibit appropriate rheological
properties — shear-thinning behavior to allow smooth extrusion and protect encapsulated
cells, along with rapid recovery of viscosity to maintain structural fidelity after deposition.
Printable hydrogels offer novel solutions for the fabrication of custom tissue constructs,
disease models, and drug testing platforms. For instance, Gao et al. [10] created vascular-
like channels using coaxial nozzle-assisted bioprinting.

2.1.2
PEG Hydrogels
Polyethylene glycol (PEG) is a synthetic polymer, widely used in pharmaceutical and
biomedical applications due to its versatility, safety, and tunable properties. In its na-
tive form, PEG has been used as a solubilizer for hydrophobic drugs, but also to prevent
aggregation and provide stealth coating of nanoparticles to prolong circulation time and
evade immune recognition [14].
PEG-based hydrogels have been extensively studied as matrices delivering drugs and
cells and scaffolds promoting tissue regeneration[15]. Their high water content (typically
>95%) mimics soft tissue environments, facilitating nutrient and waste exchange while
maintaining cell viability. PEG is inherently non-toxic and non-immunogenic, making it
an ideal biomedical backbone. Despite these advantages, PEG hydrogels are often soft,
fragile, and highly permeable, which can lead to burst release or short retention times in
vivo.
Unmodified PEG lacks functional groups or degradation sites. Functionalized PEG deriva-
tives enable the formation of crosslinked hydrogel networks that can be tailored to specific
applications by tuning crosslinking density, polymer structure, and chemistry. Click chem-
istry, such as strain-promoted azide-alkyne cycloaddition, offers a promising route to form
uniform PEG networks without generating toxic byproducts.
2.2
Crosslinking Strategies
No single crosslinking strategy stands out as universally superior. Most physical crosslink-
ing methods — such as thermosensitive gelation, ionic interactions, pH- and electrostatic-
mediated crosslinking — offer gentle, cell-friendly conditions and often rapid gelation.
However, the hydrogels tend to be fragile and suffer from poor mechanical strength. While
the reversibility of physical bonds can be advantageous for degradation, it also renders
the gels sensitive to environmental fluctuations and mechanical stress [3].
Chemical crosslinking can overcome these shortcomings by forming non-reversible co-
valent bonds. Among popular methods, photopolymerization offers spatial control and
near-instant gelation, but may cause cell damage either directly by the UV light or through
cytotoxicity of the photoinitiators. Enzymatic crosslinking comes with high specificity, but
typically involves less tunable reaction kinetics and higher material costs. Click chemistry
— particularly Michael addition or strain-promoted azide-alkyne cycloaddition — offers
high efficiency, tunable kinetics, and compatibility with physiological conditions.
2.2.1
Bioorthogonal Azide-Alkyne Cycloaddition
Click chemistry refers to a class of high-yielding, stereospecific, and straightforward re-
actions, often described as ”molecular Velcro” due to their modularity and reliability [16].
These reactions typically proceed in benign solvents, with byproducts that can be eas-
ily removed. However, conventional click reactions may involve toxic catalysts or show
off-target reactivity with biological molecules.
4

To make click chemistry suitable for use in vivo, the reaction must be bioorthogonal, i.e.,
reacting exclusively with functional groups that are unnatural to the native tissue environ-
ment, such as amines, thiols, or alcohols [17]. Bioorthogonal reactions describe a process
in which the covalent bond formation occurs between two abiotic groups with exclusive
mutual reactivity without perturbing natural biochemical processes.
The azide functional group is particularly attractive in this context — it is highly reactive, yet
stable in aqueous environments and biologically orthogonal. Traditionally used copper(I)-
catalyzed azide-alkyne cycloaddition is a popular choice, but it is unsuitable for biomedical
applications due to copper toxicity. As a result, copper-free alternatives, especially strain-
promoted azide-alkyne cycloaddition (SPAAC) and inverse electron-demand Diels–Alder
(iEDDA) reactions, have emerged as safer options.
In SPAAC, the alkyne is incorporated within a strained cyclooctyne system, which dra-
matically increases intrinsic reactivity and eliminates the need for a metal catalyst [16].
These reactions exhibit impressive selectivity; however, issues with background labeling
and toxicity can still arise and depend on molecular nuances. For instance, hydrophobic
cyclooctynes can strongly interact with serum proteins, leading to aggregation and altered
biodistribution [17].
The term ”strain” in SPAAC refers to geometric distortions in the cyclooctyne ring, which
increase its reactivity by elevating its internal energy [18]. This strain results from several
factors:
Angle strain occurs when the bond angles in a ring deviate from the ideal bond angles
predicted by hybridization (i.e., 109.5 °).
Torsional strain results from eclipsing interactions between adjacent bonds in the ring.
They are most common in smaller rings, where the ring’s geometry forces bonds
into eclipsed conformations.
Steric strain arises when atoms in groups in the ring are forced into close proximity,
leading to repulsion.
Transannular strain is specific to larger rings, where non-adjacent atoms or groups in-
teract across the ring.
Conformational Strain arises from the inability of the molecule to adopt a conformation
that minimizes overall strain.
In this project, PEG hydrogel is created as a result of a reaction between 4-arm PEG-
DBCO (Figure 2.1b) and azido-PEG5-azide (Figure 2.1a).
In DBCO the alkyne is forced into a bent geometry, since the eight-membered ring is not
inherently stable due to both torsional and angle strain. The strain is further amplified
by the presence of fused benzene rings, which introduce additional steric hindrance and
restrict conformational flexibility — resulting in high ring strain and elevated reactivity.

2.3
Liposomes
Liposomes, shown in Figure 2.2, are spherical vesicles composed of one or more con-
centric phospholipid bilayers enclosing an aqueous core [19]. Phospholipids consist of
a hydrophilic head and hydrophobic tails, which drive their self-assembly into bilayers in
aqueous environments. This self-assembly is driven by hydrophobic effects and stabilized
through Van der Waals interactions, hydrogen bonding, and electrostatic forces [20].
Liposomes are highly versatile drug delivery systems due to their ability to encapsulate
both hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs and protect these cargoes from degradation.
They are biodegradable, biocompatible, and can be engineered for site-specific target-
ing, controlled release, and improved pharmacokinetics.

organise forming a bilayer around an aqueous inner core, shielding the hydrophobic tails from the water
contact. Created using BioRender.com
The physicochemical properties of liposomes — such as size, surface charge, lamellarity,
fluidity, and permeability — are primarily determined by lipid composition. While it is pos-

sible to use both naturally derived lipids, such as egg phosphatydylcholine (egg PC), and
synthetic or semi-synthetic alternatives like 1,2-dipalmitoyl-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine
(DPPC) or 1,2-dioleyol-sn-glycero-3-phosphocholine (DOPC), the latter ones are more
predictable and tunable. Thousands of currently existing synthetic and semi-synthetic
lipids differ in fatty acid chain length, degree of saturation, charge, and main phase tran-
sition temperature (𝑇𝑚) — all of which can influence liposome formulation, since chain
length and degree of saturation affect the gel-to-liquid 𝑇𝑚, which in turn influences mem-
brane fluidity. Saturated lipids tend to have higher 𝑇𝑚values, while at the same time they
make the bilayer more rigid.
The choice of head group impacts surface charge, membrane curvature, and interactions
with biological systems. For instance, liposomes with a net negative charge can affect
biodistribution and cellular uptake, and they play an important role in maintaining ion gra-
dients. Cationic lipids form complexes with anionic polymers such as DNA, and are widely
studied for gene delivery purposes. Zwitterionic lipids with neutral net charge are used in
most applications to avoid interactions with plasma serum proteins.
Phospholipid bilayers exhibit distinct thermotropic phase transitions depending on their
𝑇𝑚. Below the 𝑇𝑚, the bilayer adopts a gel phase (L𝛽) characterized by ordered, tightly
packed acyl chains. Above 𝑇𝑚, the bilayer transitions to a liquid-crystalline phase (L𝛼)
with increased fluidity and lateral diffusion. This phase behavior is crucial for liposome
stability and drug release properties, particularly in thermosensitive formulations. It is
also possible for the liposomes to adapt an intermediate ripple phase (P𝛽’), where periodic
ripple structures and limited mobility are observed.

allows for site-specific drug delivery, particularly in combination with localized heating
methods such as radiofrequency ablation or high-intensity focused ultrasound.

2.3.2
Liposomes Synthesis Methods
Lipid preparation method is usually tailored to the final characteristics of liposomes and
is chosen based on physicochemical characteristics of the liposome component, cargo to
be encapsulated, costs, and reproducibility [19, 20].
General steps of liposome synthesis usually involve three key steps: (1) preparation of
lipids for hydration, (2) hydration to form bilayers, (3) size reduction, with an optional fourth
step involving active drug loading.
Most common manufacturing methods are summarized in Table 2.1.
While thin-film hydration remains the most widely used technique due to its simplicity,
solvent injection offers higher encapsulation efficiency. Reverse-phase methods, while
useful, are less suited for delicate cargo, and detergent depletion — though useful for
complex vesicles — remains time-consuming and challenging to scale [24].
Since the liposomes self-assemble, they can acquire various forms when it comes to their
lamellarity and sizes (see Figure 2.4). To achieve homogeneous size distribution and
desired size, the liposomes are usually sized down using methods such as extrusion or
sonication [19]. Extrusion involves forcing the multilamellar vesicles (MLVs) suspension
through a membrane with a specified pore size to make small unilamellar vesicles (SUVs),

Encapsulating active agents in liposomes can be achieved using various methods, which
can generally be divided into passive and active loading [19]. Passive drug loading refers
to the drug being added directly when the lipids are hydrated. This method offers simplicity
and is suitable for hydrophilic compounds; however, it suffers from low encapsulation
efficiency (<10%), may result in lower stability, and burst release upon administration. Low
encapsulation of passive trapping is usually explained by the concept of nonequilibrium
solute distribution effects, which simply put means that the water permeates across the
bilayers faster than the solute [19]. In contrast, active loading describes a process in
which cargo is loaded into already pre-formed liposomes via pH or electrolyte gradient.
Though more complex, active trapping offers higher encapsulation efficiency (as high as
80%), greater stability, and more controlled drug release.
2.3.3
Interdigitation Fusion
For the purpose of drug delivery, the liposomes should usually fit into application-specific
size constraints. Depending on the source, for medical applications, the liposomes should
be between 80–200 nm [19] or 50-450 nm [24]. As such, their internal volume is limited,
and the lipids are not able to carry more or bigger cargo.
For applications beyond drug delivery, such as biomaterial formation, size of the lipo-
somes is less essential, and the internal volume becomes a more attractive parameter
to consider. Thin film hydration produces heterogeneously sized MLVs with internal vol-
umes between 0.5 – 1.0 𝜇l/𝜇mol lipid. The captured volume can be increased by an order
of magnitude employing various techniques, but even then, the internal volumes of lipo-
somes oscillate below 10 𝜇l/𝜇mol lipid. Extremely high internal volumes reaching and
extending beyond 20 𝜇l/𝜇mol lipid are observed in novel liposomes measuring 1–6 𝜇𝑚
that are prepared using the interdigitation fusion technique [25].

such as ethanol or hydrostatic pressure, are then removed, and as a result, the mem-
branes of IFVs are no longer interdigitated. The schematics of this process can be fol-

2.3.4
Liposome Loaded Thermosensitive Hydrogels
In liposome-loaded hydrogels, the liposomes are embedded within a hydrogel matrix [27].
These hybrid systems combine the sustained and localized drug release capabilities of
hydrogels with the encapsulation and delivery benefits of liposomes, making them partic-
ularly appealing for applications in localized drug delivery and tissue engineering.
A key advantage of liposome-loaded hydrogels is their versatility in encapsulating both
hydrophilic and hydrophobic drugs, and the ability to tune the system to the application.
In this setup, the hydrogel acts as a scaffold that physically entraps the liposomes, de-
laying their degradation and enabling sustained, site-specific drug release. Additionally,
hydrogels provide mechanical support and structural integrity, which are essential for im-
plantable or injectable formulations.
Thermosensitive liposome-loaded hydrogels represent a specialized class of these sys-
tems. They remain in a liquid state at room temperature and undergo a sol–gel transi-
tion upon exposure to physiological temperatures [27]. This temperature-triggered phase
change is mediated by thermosensitive polymers within the hydrogel matrix. In such sys-
tems, liposomes primarily serve as drug carriers and do not typically contribute to the
gelation process.
However, some studies have explored the unconventional use of liposomes as active
agents in triggering hydrogel gelation. Westhaus et al. [4] investigated hybrid thermosen-
sitive liposomes composed of DPPC and DMPC for the triggered release of calcium ions,
promoting rapid gelation of alginate hydrogels at 37 °C. Their system achieved 90% re-
lease of encapsulated calcium and formed 2% w/v alginate gels.
Ren et al. [28] used a similar approach, encapsulating horseradish peroxidase (HRP) in
liposomes to catalyze the formation of a hyaluronic acid-based hydrogel scaffold. Building
on this concept, Stowers et al. [29] demonstrated dynamic phototuning of 3D hydrogels by
encapsulating both stiffening and softening agents—alongside gold nanorods for localized
heating—within DPPC liposomes.
The work by Ren et al. was further extended by Tasnim et al. [30], who modified the system
to investigate gelation behavior at different temperatures: 25 °C, 37 °C, and 45 °C.
2.4
Detecting azide-groups
Azides can be detected using several analytical methods. One of the most widely used
techniques is fluorescence-based assays, which rely on the reaction between azides and
fluorophore-labeled alkynes via click chemistry. These assays offer high sensitivity and
exceptional selectivity due to the bioorthogonal nature of the click reaction. However,
they require expensive fluorescent probes, often necessitate immobilizing samples on a
surface, and involve additional washing steps to remove unreacted species [31].
Infrared spectroscopy (IR) is another common method for azide detection, exploiting the
strong absorption band of azide groups around 2100 cm−1. IR spectroscopy provides a
rapid and straightforward measurement, but its sensitivity decreases at low azide concen-
trations. Furthermore, IR is less specific since other functional groups in the sample may
exhibit absorption in similar spectral regions.
Colorimetric assays also exist for azide detection. For example, heating azides with allyl
isothiocyanate or carbon disulfide produces a yellow precipitate, whose color intensity can
be enhanced by adding pyridine. This assay is effective for soluble azides [32]. While
colorimetric methods are simple and rapid, they typically have moderate sensitivity and
require specific reagents.

Dynamic Light Scattering
Dynamic light scattering (DLS), also known as Photon Correlation Spectroscopy, is a
technique used to determine the size distribution profile of particles in suspension. The
principle behind the DLS relies on the fact that light scattering, stemming from the inter-
action of a monochromatic beam of light with a solution containing macromolecules, is a
function of the size and shape of the particles. In dynamic scattering events, the intensity
of scattered light fluctuates because the macromolecules in the solution are subject to
Brownian motion [33]. Analysing the scattered light helps correlate the diffusion coeffi-

Ultraviolet-Visible Spectrophotometry
UV-Vis Spectrophotometry is an analytical technique used to measure sample absorbance
of ultraviolet (100–400 nm) and visible light (400–800 nm) [34]. It is one of the non-
destructive analytical methods that produces rapid and reproducible results.
The method is based on Beer-Lambert Law (Equation 2.2), which describes the relation-
ship between absorbance and the concentration of an absorbing species:

In this equation, 𝐴is the absorbance, 𝐼0 and 𝐼are the intensities of light before and after
passing through the sample, respectively, 𝜖is the molar extinction coefficient, 𝑐is the
concentration of the analyte, and 𝐿is the path length of the cuvette. If a linear relationship
between absorbance and concentration is observed, the concentration of an unknown
sample can be determined from a calibration curve.
Absorbance in the UV–Vis region occurs when 𝜋or non-bonding electrons in a molecule
are excited to a higher energy state. The specific wavelength required for this transition
depends on the molecular structure.
While UV-Vis is a powerful and widely-used tool, certain considerations must be taken
into account to ensure accurate results:
(a) If the sample is a suspension rather than a homogeneous solution, more light is
scattered than absorbed, which might introduce significant noise;
(b) solvent cutoff (i.e., wavelength below the solvent itself absorbs all the light) must be
considered to avoid overlap with the analyte signal;
(c) for the same reason, cuvette material must be appropriate for the spectral range
used, to prevent absorption by the container itself [35].

3.5
Liposome Aynthesis and Characterization
To synthesize the liposomes, DPPC and DMPC in a 9:1 molar ratio were dissolved in 2 ml
of methanol at a concentration of 10 mg/ml. The solution was transferred to a 5 ml round-
bottom flask to form a thin film by evaporating the solvent under vacuum while rotating
the sample in a water bath using the Büchi Rotary Evaporator at 60°C. The sample was
first rotated at 200 rpm, under 250 mbar vacuum for 2 minutes until the thin film was
formed, then the vacuum conditions were adjusted to the lowest setting, and the sample
was further rotated until the residual methanol had completely evaporated (∼10 minutes).
The thin film was hydrated with 1 ml of Mili-Q water containing 100 mM Azido-PEG5-
Azide (60°C, 280 rpm) for 20 minutes. The white liposome solution was then transferred
to a sonicator bath (Ultrasonic Cleaning Batch, VWR@USC) at 60°C and sonicated until
transparent (30–60 minutes). The SUVs solution was then brought to room temperature
and transferred to a 4 ml glass vial.
To prepare the IFVs 0.233 mL of 96% ethanol was added to a total concentration of 4.0
M. The sample was immediately vortexed until it became an extremely viscous, opaque,
white suspension, which indicates interdigitated sheets being formed. This step was fol-
lowed by incubation at room temperature for 15 minutes to stabilize the interdigitated state.
Afterwards, the interdigitated sheets were heated to 60°C and incubated for 15 minutes
to allow the formation of IFVs. The cap was then removed, and the solution was further
incubated at 60°C for 10 minutes to allow for ethanol evaporation. The liposomes were
dialyzed against HBS for 3 days at 4°C.
This protocol was used to create the only functional batch of liposomes. During the course
of the project, the protocol was continuously modified, and the following versions were
tested:
(a) Hydrating the thin-film with 1mL 10 mM HBS pH 7.4;
(b) Adding 200 mM and 100 mM of the Azido-PEG5-Azide to SUVs solution during the
sonication step, after the solution became transparent, and sonicating further in an
attempt to obtain a transparent solution after mixing;
(c) Adding 200 mM of the azido-PEG5-azide after interdigitating the lipid sheets, before
incubating them at 60°C;
(d) Hydrating the thin-film with 1.2 mL of the hydrating buffer instead of 1 mL.
Further, while investigating possible causes of failures in the method, the use of absolute
ethanol and 96% ethanol for interdigitation were compared (0.233 mL, 4 M), and different
incubation times were tested, where the vials were all incubated in room temperature

4.2
Development of the Assay
Before focusing on the synthesis of a liposome system, the work focused on developing
a suitable assay to quantify the release of the diazide crosslinker. Considering the results
of the concentration studies and the limitations of the passive encapsulation methods, the
assay should be able to quantify a wide range of concentrations of the crosslinker, with a
special focus on its sensitivity at and below 40 mM of the diazide.
In the first iteration, we followed a hypothesis that it might be possible to directly detect the
azide groups in the UV-Vis spectrum. The result of testing a dilution series of the diazide
(Figure 4.3a) shows a linear relationship between the concentration and the absorbance
at 229 nm, indicating that the hypothesis was correct.
As the cuvette holder can be heated to 37 °C, we thought it would be possible to study
the release of the crosslinker by measuring absorbance in real-time. However, the spec-
trum obtained from a release test (Figure 4.4a) shows an overlapping signal with a broad
spectrum indicating scattering events.
To test whether the liposomes could have caused light scattering, we recorded the spectra
of two empty liposome samples shown in Figure 4.4b. The wide spectrum seen for all
samples in the region of 200–300 nm confirms that the background scattering disrupts
the measurement of the diazide concentration, and this method could not be pursued
further.
To be able to study the concentration of azide groups, a new method was needed, where
light scattering caused by liposomes would be eliminated. For that reason, the second
approach focused on bursting the liposomes in an organic solvent. This eliminates light
scattering from larger particles, making it possible to record the absorbance of the diazide
crosslinker directly.
To establish how much organic solvent is needed to completely diminish the scattering
caused by liposomes, a fixed concentration of empty liposomes was tested with different
concentrations of acetonitrile (chosen organic solvent). As shown in Figure 4.4c, the scat-
tering peak completely disappears when adding 13 M of acetonitrile which corresponds
to 70% v/v of the solution.

Figures 4.5b. Graphs presenting the absorbance spectra of 0.75–0.047 mM are attached
in Appendix A.
To be able to detect the azide group in lower concentrations, we came up with another
approach, where we would study the spectra of DBCO and indirectly quantify the amount
of the crosslinker. The characteristic bands of DBCO at 292 and 310 nm tend to decrease
when the molecule undergoes click reaction with an azide [37]. Knowing that, it would be
possible to create a reverse calibration curve where different concentrations of the diazide
react with a fixed concentration of DBCO.
The concentration of DBCO was fixed at 1.5 mM as we found that this was the highest
concentration at which the solution didn’t have a white precipitate, meaning that the DBCO
was fully dissolved.
Spectrum of 1.5 mM DBCO before and after the click reaction is shown in the graph
in Figure 4.6a. The reaction was studied in HBS and the spectra were analysed within
seconds of mixing. Our results confirm that azide conjugation causes the DBCO spectrum
to disappear.
Additionally, absorbance of six different concentrations of DBCO were observed in 70%
acetonitrile (Figure 4.6b). As seen in the figure, the absorbance scales with concentration,
which is comparable to literature reports [38], and confirms that it is possible to observe
DBCO spectra in this organic solvent.

5
Discussion
This chapter interprets the significance of the findings presented in Chapter 4 and relates
them to the theoretical framework and the research objectives. Where appropriate, com-
parisons are made with previous studies to contextualize the results and identify areas for
further research.
5.1
Hydrogel Synthesis
Our initial move concerned the qualitative study of the bioorthogonal reaction between
the chosen diazide and DBCO containing PEG polymer by visual observation of gel for-
mation. This analysis revealed that crosslinker concentrations between 52.5–120 mM,
corresponding to molar ratios ranging from 4:5 to 2:1 of azide:DBCO, trigger what seems
to be a quite uniform gelation within seconds of mixing. Even though a 1:2 molar ratio re-
sulted in gel formation after 5 minutes of heat exposure, this concentration was discarded,
as for injectable or printable solutions, it is crucial that the hydrogel is formed in a matter
of seconds. Because of that, it was decided that the synthesis should be able to achieve
encapsulation of a concentration corresponding to a 1:1 molar ratio. Considering the re-
sults, slight fluctuations are acceptable; however, to rapidly form a viscous gel, the molar
ratio of diazide to DBCO should not fall below 4:7. Moreover, the findings of this study
seem to be consistent with what the literature reports about the effect of the crosslinker
concentration on hydrogel properties.
Optimal concentration of the crosslinker should ensure efficient and rapid gelation, uni-
form crosslinking, tunable stiffness, and moderate swelling [39, 40]. While our study was
purely qualitative, and the specifics of the gelation and hydrogel properties, including stiff-
ness and swelling, are unknown, visual observation confirmed rapid gelation. For all the
concentrations, for which the results in Tables 4.1 and 4.2 indicate gel formation, no resid-
ual liquid was observed.
For lower concentrations of the crosslinker, the literature points to slower gelation and
incomplete network formation [41]. These hydrogels have poor mechanical integrity and
are not suitable for load-bearing or long-term implants, which are properties that could be
crucial for injectable hydrogels. In our study, the change in viscosity of the solution points
to an incomplete gelation since there are not enough azide-groups to efficiently connect
all available DBCO groups attached to the PEG polymers.
Only one of the higher concentrations that was studied can be deemed too high, namely
240 mM of the diazide crosslinker. At this concentration, the solution changed into a gel
of low viscosity. While the transition happened rather quickly, gel properties were far from
ideal.
Compared to literature reports, higher concentrations of the crosslinker usually result in
stronger gels with higher stiffness [41]. In our case, however, the excess of the azide
groups is large, and while it might result in complete reaction of the DBCO groups, the ex-
cess crosslinker molecules might affect the mechanical properties of the gel and account
for a more sol-like behavior.
Looking at the results from the DLS, and a surprisingly large size of the crosslinker, we
hypothesised that in high concentrations, the azido-PEG5-azide forms clusters, which

would mean the molecule is less reactive to its environment. This however, is contrast-

widely used in drug delivery field to prevent aggregation of nanoparticles by stealth effect,
and help prolong the circulation time of medicines adimistered intravenously by hepling

best properties. Seeing how the hydrogel can be created by using a range of crosslinker
concentrations, the specific characteristics of each gel should be tested to establish how
the properties could be tailored to specific applications.
5.2
Assay Development
A substantial amount of work has been focused on developing the assay being able to
quantify the diazide crosslinker. Knowing that the liposome system consisting of DPPC
and DMPC is able to release its contents at 37°C, the easiest way of measuring en-
capsulation would be the direct measurement of the concentration of the encapsulated
molecule. This way it would not only be possible to quantify the amount of the diazide in
the IFVs, but also judge the progress of dialysis and further study the kinetics of release.
Direct measurement of the concentration of diazide proved impossible in the UV-Vis spec-
trum, since the liposomes present in the solution introduce scattering. This is caused by
the fact that while at and above the 𝑇𝑚the liposomes become leaky and their membrane
is more permeable, they don’t disassemble and spherical structures are still present in the
solution, causing scattering. Since the liposomes are 2 orders of magnitude larger than
the crosslinker, it is not possible to quantify the absorbance of the diazide.
Light scattering is not the only issue at play — azides have a rather low molar absorption
coefficient (𝜖), and observing their absorbance can be done at high concentrations or with
very sensitive instrumentation. We performed studies that confirm that it is possible to
measure the absorbance of the azide groups in concentrations higher than 15 mM; how-
ever, at lower concentrations, the signal becomes very weak and gradually disappears.
For the assay to be sensitive, it needs to be able to pick up the presence of the azide at
low concentrations.
Our proposed solution offers a sensitive and robust method for quantifying the amount
of diazide present in the solution by observing the absorbance of the DBCO ring, which
decreases after the triazole ring is formed as a result of the click reaction. The assay is
a cheap solution, and can be performed in a couple of hours by heating and stirring the
reaction. Light scattering caused by the liposomes is eliminated by bursting the liposomes
in an organic solvent, which increases membrane fluidity and permeability and, at high
enough concentration causes complete disruption.
It has also been shown that the assay can be performed in the presence of the liposomes,
since studying the course of the reaction with and without the liposome background pro-

A clear limitation of this assay is the inability to detect pure diazide outside the liposomes.
This issue can be tackled using two approaches:
(1) testing the liposomes with 4-arm PEG-DBCO in room temperature — if the solution
does not change viscosity or become a hydrogel, the concentration of the diazide
outside the liposomes can be deemed negligible. This method would only provide
qualitative information, and it would therefore not be possible to verify if any azide
groups are present in the solution.
(2) centrifuging the liposomes and testing the supernatant using the assay. This ap-
proach would not only provide qualitative answer but also help quantify the amount
of the crosslinker outside the liposomes. Here, it could be possible that some of the
diazide would be trapped between sedimented liposomes, because the IFVs are
significantly larger than the crosslinker itself.
As an alternative, the UV-Vis assay could be substituted by using a fluorescent probe
utilizing the click reaction between the azide and DBCO. One solution could be sulfo-
Cyanine5 DBCO [45], which was also considered for suitability in this project. However,
considering the cost and complexity of detection using this probe, fluorescent detection
was discarded. Detecting the azide group using a fluorescent probe means that either
of the reactants has to be anchored to a surface, and extra washing steps would be
necessary to eliminate unreacted species.
One challenge encountered while heating the sample with liposomes was seeing the ab-
sorbance above all other results (Figure 4.13a and 4.13b). In the first experiment, all
samples were heated, but only one of the samples had liposomes in them. Though it was
previously confirmed that the presence of lipids does not alter the result of the study, those
experiments were all conducted at room temperature. Since the result was similar in two
independent assays, it led to a hypothesis that heating the sample introduces some in-
teraction between the liposomes and the solvent, as the interaction of the liposomes and
the click reaction was previously ruled out. When treating the liposomes with 70% v/v
acetonitrile in room temperature, the vesicles burst and white particles are formed. While
heating the sample, the lipid particles seem to be dissolving in acetonitrile, and the white
precipitate reappears after the sample is cooled to room temperature.

5.3
Liposome Design
As expected, the interdigitation fusion synthesis method produces large vesicles. This
was confirmed by multiple DLS measurements of different liposome batches, showing
that the size of IFVs is between 1.2–2.3 𝜇m. Considering the size, the liposomes are
expected to have a high internal volume and be able to encapsulate significantly more
cargo in the same concentrations compared to SUVs.
This was confirmed in the study performed to analyse the suitability of the synthesis
method, since IFVs result in a higher encapsulation efficiency and are able to contain
a significantly higher amount of DTBA (see Table 4.3).
Despite promising advantages, the interdigitation fusion method suffers from some lim-
itations and is sensitive to batch-to-batch variability because of how some variables in-
troduce uncertainties. Looking at the original protocol [4, 46], we identified some areas
where our method stands out, which might be the cause of the inconsistencies we ob-
served.
Firstly, reducing the size of the liposomes using the ultrasonic bath instead of the probe
sonicator means subjecting them to indirect sonication of lower energy. Probe sonicators
offer higher intensity and more focused energy delivery, providing more precise control
over the process. They allow for control over various parameters, including amplitude, and
faster processing. Using a probe sonicator could ensure more reproducible results, since
the size of the SUVs is one of the parameters directly influencing the internal volume of the
IFVs [25]. A clear advantage of using ultrasonic bath is the temperature control function,
allowing to heat the water bath to desired temperature. Moreover, using a probe sonicator
introduces titanium dust into the sample that needs to be centrifuged away before further
processing.
As mentioned, the synthesis protocol involves sonicating the liposome solution until trans-
parent, which is not always a specific time frame. Moreover, discrepancies between the
output power of the device could be observed with the naked eye. As a result, the SUVs
solutions most likely exhibited batch-to-batch variability, producing IFVs of different prop-
erties.
Secondly, the removal of ethanol is based on simple evaporation, and the completion
of the process cannot be confirmed. Removal of ethanol proved crucial, as in some of
the syntheses performed, the liposome solution would go back to a white, opaque state
upon cooling to room temperature. This indicates that residual ethanol was still present
in the solution, forcing the liposomes into a ripple phase (P𝛽’). Westhaus et al. [4] report
removal of ethanol by bubbling the heated solution with dry nitrogen gas, which seems to
be a more reliable method.

Thirdly, removing residual crosslinker from outside the molecules is a lengthy process,
which results in varying amounts of the molecule left in the solution. Removing the resid-

the synthesis, but also provide a more controllable environment, where the supernatant
could easily be tested in situ. Additionally, it would be a lot easier to verify if all the non-
encapsulated crosslinker is removed from the solution.
With those improvements, the method would be more reproducible and suffer from less
variation. Possibly, more control over the synthesis parameters would also influence en-
capsulation efficiency, which is quite low for all systems. As described in Chapter 2, pas-
sive entrapment usually results in quite low encapsulation efficiency numbers, because
of nonequilibrium solute distribution, and varies between the species. For the interdigita-
tion fusion method, encapsulation efficiency values as high as 56% are reported [46], so
possible tweaks in the synthesis could result in a much more efficient system.
5.4
Diazide Encapsulation
Since it was confirmed that interdigitation fusion as a method can produce IFVs of a
large size and encapsulation efficiency of up to 6%, it seems that the failures in the li-
posome synthesis stem from interactions between the lipids and high concentrations of
the crosslinker.
The crosslinker used in this project is a linear molecule, containing two terminal azides
joined by a PEG linker. Conventionally, encapsulating PEG inside liposomes has not
been reported; however, several studies report that PEG is used as a fusion-inducing
agent [47, 48]. The authors study the effect that different concentrations of PEG of differ-
ent molecular weights have on lipid bilayers. PEG is a known dehydrating agent that has
the ability to bind water in a solution, excluding other species from the aqueous phase.
In liposome solutions, PEG dehydrates the membrane surface, modifies the electrostatic
interactions between lipid headgroups, and reduces steric repulsions, promoting aggre-
gation and fusion. Fusion can be optically confirmed by observing a change in color and
viscosity of the solution from optically transparent liquid to white opaque solution [47] —
which is exactly what happened during the synthesis attempts.
Both sources report that aggregation and fusion are a function of PEG concentration and
molecular weight. Higher molecular weights required lower concentrations to induce fu-
sion, and higher concentrations of the same molecular weight PEG resulted in more sig-
nificant fusion. According to the authors, high concentrations of PEG in the molecular
weight range of 0.2—6 kDa caused aggregation and fusion. This also explains why higher
concentrations of the diazide crosslinker would result in this behavior, as the unusual ob-
servations occurred at 40% v/v PEG.
The authors point out that fusion events are more pronounced at temperatures below 𝑇𝑚,
where concentrations as low as 2.5% PEG 6 kDa (wt/wt) would result in bilayer aggre-
gation. This is also similar to the observations of this project, where the solution would
become viscous and lumpy after cooling to room temperature.
Bilayer fusion caused by PEG is reportedly more pronounced for SUVs, which fuse faster
than MLVs. That is why it was hypothesised that adding the diazide crosslinker to the in-
terdigitated sheets would mitigate this issue. This, however, resulted in no encapsulation,
possibly due to insufficient mixing.
PEG induced fusion is also enhanced by the presence of salts [48], since it further reduces
electrostatic repulsions between the head groups, causing tighter packing, and results in

local gradients, further enhancing bilayer dehydration. Considering that, the approach to
synthesis was modified to exclude 10 mM HBS and use Mili-Q water as the hydration
buffer. Using this approach and hydrating the liposomes with a lower concentration of the
crosslinker produced one successful batch of the liposomes. Nonetheless, the synthe-

Further, the molecular crowding studies visually confirmed that the crosslinker seems
to come in interactions with the liposomes. The effect was confirmed both in HBS and

crosslinker used is not suitable for encapsulation in liposomes, and therefore not suitable
for this project.
Since the issues arise from the molecular interactions of PEG, using azide-alkyne click
chemistry should not be discarded as a crosslinking method. Bioorthogonal reaction be-
tween DBCO and an azide group is a non-toxic alternative, able to produce covalently
crosslinked hydrogels offering superb mechanical properties.
Future studies could consider studying the use of a different diazide linker, which does
not use PEG.
A commercially available, ready-to-use solution would be choosing a diazide amine linker
sold by Axis Pharm [49]. This molecule has a similar molecular weight to the azido-PEG5-
azide used in this project, however comes at a significant cost.
A cheaper solution would be to synthesize the molecule in-house by conjugating com-
mercially available reagents. This could be, for example, done by coupling Mal-Azides to
DTBA via thiol-maleimide reaction (Figure 5.1) or linking Azidoacetic Acid NHS Ester to
3-Azido-1-propanamine using NHS coupling (Figure 5.2).
The reaction between thiol and maleimide is a type of Michael addition and is widely used
in bioconjugation. It is a mild and specific reaction offering rapid reaction kinetics. Since
it has been shown in our group that it is possible to encapsulate DTBA inside IFVs, this
dithiol makes the perfect candidate to start the search for a new azide-based crosslinker,
especially considering its net positive charge. While this solution is slightly more complex,
it is still relatively simple and does not require additional reagents or catalysts. Considering
the specifics of the reaction, synthesizing a diazide crosslinker would be fast and effective.
Since both DTBA and Mal-Azide are of similar size, the only concern could be the size
of the final crosslinker. However, considering the internal volume of the IFVs, this should
not be an issue during linker encapsulation.

peptides. In a reaction performed from scratch, the NHS combined with a carboxylic acid
and a coupling agent, forms an NHS ester, which is highly reactive to primary amines.
However, NHS esters of many molecules are readily available, simplifying the reaction
and relieving the need for a coupling agent. The reaction allows for efficient conjugation
under mild conditions and results in a very stable amide bond, making it another suit-

6
Conclusion
From aiding regenerative medicine in minor wounds and cavities to creating whole organs
ex vivo, printable and injectable hydrogels promise to bring these applications from the
lab to the clinic. Creating covalently linked hydrogels in a manner of seconds by utilising
thermosensitive properties paves the way for highly versatile, reliable and simple solutions
in healthcare engineering. Using TSLs as carriers to deliver crosslinking agents to a
polymer solution, ensuring mild gelation at physiological temperatures is an innovative
way of utilizing this said to be perfect drug delivery system in solutions beyond traditional
drug delivery.
In this project we investigated the use of thermosensitive liposomes to encapsulate and
release a di-azide crosslinker at 37°C to trigger gelation of a PEG network via copper free
azide-alkyne cycloaddition.
Our study on how the relative concentrations of azido-PEG5-azide (crosslinker) and 4-
arm PEG-DBCO (polymer) affect the formation of a PEG hydrogel, reveals that to rapidly
create a uniform network the solution should contain molar ratios of azide to DBCO be-
tween 4:5 and 2:1. Lower concentrations of azido-PEG5-azide resulted in slower gelation,
or no gelation at all. Higher concentrations of the crosslinker created a viscous solution
with hydrogel lumps, suggesting that the chosen crosslinker aggregates because of high
concentration of polyethylene glycol.
Moreover, we were able to create a robust assay enabling us to detect azide groups
quickly and specifically. By disrupting the liposomes in an organic solvent we eliminate
light scattering caused by the IFVs, and detect the click reaction between azide groups
and DBCO using ultraviolet-visible spectrophotometry. Using the assay we were able
to detect between 66 𝜇M to 1.5 mM of the crosslinker in the presence of lipids. Despite
specificity and sensitivity, the assay is limited by the solubility of DBCO and slower reaction
kinetics in acetonitrile. We show that the reaction might be sped up by heating to 50°C
and stirring. Doing so, however, introduces interactions between the lipids and the organic
solvent, possibly causing lipid dissolution and amplifying the absorbance of the sample.
In the future this can be bypassed by centrifuging the sample before heating.
In this project, we corroborate earlier findings and show that it is possible to synthesize
thermosensitive liposomes via the interdigitation-fusion technique. By using DPPC and
DMPC in 9:1 molar ratio, we ensure that the liposomes are responsive to the physiological
temperature of 37°C. The synthesized IFVs were of significant size — 1.7 µm on average
measured by DLS. Even though the control syntheses resulted in successful formation
of IFVs, the batch-to-batch variation could be reduced and the encapsulation efficiency
improved by creating a more controlled process and following the initial protocols more
closely. Namely, the liposomes could be sonicated using the probe sonicator to ensure
a more homogeneous size distribution of SUVs and removal of ethanol by simple evap-
oration could be replaced by bubbling with nitrogen gas to ensure a more reliable result.
Centrifuging the liposomes to remove the residual non-encapsulated crosslinker instead
of dialysing the solution would speed up the process and allow for better scalability.
Even though we were able to encapsulate small amounts of azido-PEG5-azide by adding
the crosslinker in lower concentrations at the thin-film hydration step, we discovered that
the interactions of the lipids with their encapsulated cargo are crucial for successful for-
mulation. Higher concentrations of the chosen crosslinker transformed the lipid solution
Thermosensitive cyclo-addition hydrogels
41
==PAGE:58==
into an opaque, white, viscous, and lumpy liquid. This is likely observed because of how
high concentrations of polyethylene glycol cause membrane fusion, making a crosslinker
with a PEG spacer unsuitable for liposome encapsulation.
We propose that by using a di-azide crosslinker without PEG groups, either by purchasing
one directly or synthesizing in-house, it should be possible to encapsulate said crosslinker
in the TSLs. Specifically, the azide groups could be coupled onto DTBA via thiol-malemide
chemistry, creating a crosslinker with a net positive charge, which would reduce the risk
of leakage, or an azide crosslinker could be synthesised via NHS coupling, forming a
molecule with a polar group.
We hope that our findings will be a solid foundation for developing functional thermosensi-
tive liposomes based thermoresponsive hydrogels crosslinked by strain-promoted azide-
alkyne cycloaddition.
